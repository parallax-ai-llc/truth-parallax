## Overview

Sunan Ibn Majah is one of the six canonical hadith collections in Sunni Islam, compiled by Imam Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Yazid ibn Majah al-Qazwini (824–887 CE). The collection contains approximately 4,341 hadiths, of which around 1,339 are unique to this compilation — not found in the other five canonical works. Its inclusion in the canonical six was debated by early scholars, with some preferring the Muwatta of Imam Malik, but it ultimately secured its place through widespread scholarly acceptance.

## Structure

The collection opens with an important introductory book and proceeds through the major domains of Islamic law and spirituality:

1. **Sunnah and Purification** — The authority of prophetic practice, foundational beliefs, and ritual cleanliness
2. **Prayer and Mosques** — The regulations of daily prayer, congregational worship, and mosque etiquette
3. **Fasting and Hajj** — The disciplines of Ramadan, voluntary fasting, and pilgrimage rites
4. **Trade and Inheritance** — Commercial ethics, fair dealing, loans, and estate distribution
5. **Asceticism and Tribulations** — Renunciation of worldly excess, trials of faith, and signs of the end times

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Sunan Ibn Majah holds a particular place across interpretive traditions:

- **Sunni Islam**: Valued for its unique hadiths not found in the other five canonical collections
- **Shia Islam**: Examined critically, with attention to its unique narrations and their chains
- **Sufi traditions**: Cherished for its substantial section on asceticism (*zuhd*) and spiritual detachment
- **Salafi movement**: Consulted with caution due to the inclusion of some weaker narrations
- **Academic scholarship**: Studied for its contribution to understanding the boundaries of the hadith canon
