Salafi scholars engage with Ibn Majah's asceticism and tribulation sections with a blend of devotional appreciation and methodological caution. The zuhd traditions are valued for their prophetic warnings against worldly attachment, but each tradition is verified for authenticity before being used as the basis for moral or theological teaching. Salafi scholars are particularly vigilant in this section, as the themes of asceticism and eschatology have historically attracted weak and fabricated traditions.

The tribulation traditions are applied to the contemporary Muslim situation, with Salafi scholars reading the prophetic warnings about *fitan* as guidance for maintaining correct creed and practice during times of confusion. The signs of the Hour are accepted as prophetic statements of fact, and Salafi scholars insist on their literal interpretation without allegorical reinterpretation. Ibn Majah's unique eschatological traditions are evaluated through al-Albani's grading, with authenticated narrations treated as genuine prophetic prophecies and weak ones set aside to prevent the corruption of Islamic eschatological belief by unreliable material.
