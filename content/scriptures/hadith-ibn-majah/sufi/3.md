Sufi interpreters treasure Ibn Majah's fasting and Hajj traditions for their revelation of the spiritual depths concealed within these pillars of Islam. Fasting is understood as the ultimate practice of *tawakkul* (reliance on God) — by voluntarily renouncing the sustenance that keeps the body alive, the faster declares absolute dependence on God alone. Ibn Majah's traditions on the merits of fasting are read as descriptions of the spiritual rewards that flow from this radical act of trust.

The Hajj traditions are engaged as a symbolic narrative of the soul's journey. The pilgrim's donning of white garments represents the shedding of the ego's adornments; the circumambulation of the Ka'bah mirrors the soul's orbit around the divine center; the standing at Arafat prefigures the standing before God on the Day of Judgment. Ibn Majah's detailed recording of the pilgrimage rites provides the outward structure within which the Sufi pilgrim undertakes the inner journey — a journey from the self to God that transforms the geographical itinerary into a spiritual ascension.
