## Overview

Sufi interpreters hold a particular affinity for Sunan Ibn Majah because of its substantial section on asceticism (*zuhd*) and tribulations (*fitan*). These chapters preserve prophetic traditions on the dangers of worldly attachment, the virtue of spiritual poverty, the patience required in times of trial, and the signs of the end times — themes that resonate deeply with the Sufi path of inner purification and detachment from the ephemeral world.

## Interpretive Approach

The Sufi reading of Ibn Majah gravitates toward the traditions that illuminate the inner life of the believer. His chapters on asceticism are read as a prophetic curriculum for spiritual liberation — teaching the seeker to recognize the transience of worldly pleasure, to cultivate contentment with little, and to orient the heart exclusively toward God. The tribulation traditions are understood as describing the spiritual trials that test and refine the sincere believer.

## Spiritual Themes

For Sufi practitioners, Ibn Majah's zuhd traditions provide prophetic authorization for the path of renunciation. The hadiths on the merits of poverty, the dangers of wealth, and the superiority of the next world over this one affirm the Sufi conviction that true richness lies in nearness to God. These traditions, combined with the collection's unique material on supplication and devotion, make Ibn Majah a valued source for the Sufi devotional repertoire.
