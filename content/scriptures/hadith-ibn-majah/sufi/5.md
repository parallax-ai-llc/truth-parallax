Ibn Majah's concluding sections on asceticism and tribulations represent the spiritual summit of the collection for Sufi readers. The zuhd traditions articulate the prophetic vision of a life liberated from the tyranny of desire — a life in which the heart is so completely filled with God's love that worldly pleasures lose their power to distract. These traditions are read as the voice of the Prophet speaking directly to the spiritual aspirant, calling the seeker away from the illusions of the world and toward the eternal reality of the divine.

The tribulation traditions are understood as descriptions of the spiritual trials that every sincere seeker must endure. The external tribulations — political chaos, moral decay, social fragmentation — mirror the internal tribulations of the soul that struggles to maintain its orientation toward God amid the storms of worldly life. Sufi practitioners read these eschatological traditions not primarily as predictions of future events but as descriptions of the spiritual condition of the world from which the seeker must awaken. The ultimate tribulation is forgetfulness of God, and the ultimate triumph is the heart's permanent establishment in divine awareness.
