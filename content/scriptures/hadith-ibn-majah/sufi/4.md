Sufi engagement with Ibn Majah's commercial and inheritance traditions emphasizes the spiritual accountability that accompanies all worldly dealings. The trade hadiths are read as tests of the believer's integrity — the marketplace, with its temptations of greed and deception, is a mirror that reveals the true state of the heart. The prophetic prohibitions against dishonesty and exploitation are understood not merely as legal rules but as spiritual prescriptions for maintaining the heart's purity in the midst of worldly engagement.

The inheritance traditions speak to the Sufi theme of detachment from material possessions. While Islamic inheritance law provides a detailed framework for distributing wealth, Sufi interpreters note that the truly advanced soul has already released its attachment to worldly goods. The prophetic guidance on just distribution is embraced as divine wisdom for those still engaged in the world of ownership, while the Sufi ideal of voluntary poverty (*faqr*) points beyond the legal framework to a radical freedom from material dependence.
