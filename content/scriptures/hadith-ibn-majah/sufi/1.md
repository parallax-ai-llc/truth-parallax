Sufi interpreters approach Ibn Majah's introductory section on the Sunnah as an affirmation that the Prophet's example encompasses both outward practice and inward spiritual states. The obligation to follow the Sunnah is understood as extending to the Prophet's inner life — his states of the heart, his constant awareness of God, and his compassion for all creation. Ibn Majah's emphasis on the danger of innovation is read by Sufis not as a rejection of spiritual development but as a warning against forms of religion that substitute human invention for prophetic guidance.

The purification chapters are engaged with attention to their spiritual symbolism. Ablution cleanses not only the body but the soul, and each limb washed represents a spiritual faculty being prepared for divine encounter. Sufi masters teach that the mindful performance of ablution, following Ibn Majah's prophetic traditions with full awareness and intention, is itself a form of remembrance (*dhikr*) that draws the practitioner closer to God before the formal prayer has even begun.
