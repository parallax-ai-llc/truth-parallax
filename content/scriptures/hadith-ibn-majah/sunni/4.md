Ibn Majah's sections on trade and inheritance provide Sunni jurisprudence with prophetic traditions governing economic life and the distribution of estates. The commercial traditions address the ethics of buying and selling, the prohibition of usury, the rules governing loans and debts, and the importance of honest measurement. Ibn Majah's unique narrations in commercial law fill important gaps, providing evidence on specific types of transactions not fully addressed by the other canonical collections.

The inheritance chapters record the prophetic traditions that, alongside the Quranic inheritance verses, form the foundation of Islamic succession law. Ibn Majah preserves detailed guidance on the fixed shares (*fara'id*) assigned to various heirs, the treatment of residuary heirs, and the resolution of complex inheritance scenarios. Sunni scholars draw upon these traditions to construct the elaborate mathematical framework of Islamic inheritance distribution, one of the most developed legal sub-disciplines in classical Islamic jurisprudence.
