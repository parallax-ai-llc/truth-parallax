## Overview

The Sunni tradition values Sunan Ibn Majah as the sixth of the canonical hadith collections, appreciating it particularly for the approximately 1,339 hadiths it preserves that are not found in the other five compilations. While some early scholars debated its inclusion in the canon — preferring the Muwatta of Imam Malik or the Sunan of al-Darimi — Ibn Majah's collection ultimately secured its place through widespread scholarly acceptance and use.

## Interpretive Approach

Sunni engagement with Ibn Majah requires careful attention to authentication, as the collection includes a higher proportion of weak and contested traditions than the other canonical works. Scholars such as al-Albani have systematically graded the collection, distinguishing between its reliable and unreliable narrations. Despite this caveat, the collection's unique hadiths fill important gaps in legal and devotional literature, providing evidence on topics not fully covered by the other five compilations.

## Significance

For Sunni scholars, Ibn Majah's introduction (*Muqaddimah*) — which discusses the authority of the Sunnah, the obligation to follow prophetic guidance, and the dangers of innovation — is especially valued as a foundational statement on hadith methodology and religious epistemology. His chapters on asceticism (*zuhd*) also provide rich material for Sunni devotional and ethical literature.
