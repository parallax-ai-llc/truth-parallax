Ibn Majah's concluding sections on asceticism (*zuhd*) and tribulations (*fitan*) constitute one of the most distinctive and influential portions of his collection. The zuhd traditions warn against excessive attachment to worldly pleasures and encourage spiritual contentment with God's provision. These hadiths have profoundly influenced Sunni devotional literature, providing prophetic authorization for the ethic of moderation and spiritual prioritization that characterizes mainstream Sunni piety.

The tribulation sections record prophetic warnings about the trials that will beset the Muslim community — political upheaval, moral decline, and the signs preceding the Day of Judgment. Sunni scholars engage with these eschatological traditions as both warnings and consolations, understanding them as prophetic guidance for maintaining faith during difficult times. Ibn Majah's unique contributions in this area include traditions on specific tribulations and signs of the Hour that have become widely cited in Sunni eschatological literature, shaping Muslim understanding of history's trajectory toward the final divine reckoning.
