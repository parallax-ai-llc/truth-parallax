Shia scholars engage with Ibn Majah's asceticism and tribulation sections with genuine interest, as these themes resonate deeply with Shia spiritual consciousness. The zuhd traditions align with the austere piety exemplified by Ali ibn Abi Talib and the Imams, who are remembered in Shia tradition for their renunciation of worldly power and their devotion to spiritual truth over material comfort.

The tribulation traditions hold special significance for Shia eschatology, which includes the doctrine of the Mahdi — the expected redeemer from the Prophet's family who will restore justice at the end of times. Shia scholars examine Ibn Majah's eschatological narrations for traditions about the Mahdi, the trials preceding his appearance, and the ultimate triumph of justice over oppression. These traditions are compared with Shia eschatological sources, and convergences are cited as evidence that even Sunni canonical collections preserve the prophetic promise of messianic redemption through the Prophet's lineage.
