Shia scholars engage with Ibn Majah's introductory section on the Sunnah with the fundamental proviso that authentic Sunnah, in the Shia understanding, is transmitted and interpreted through the Imams. Where Ibn Majah defines the Sunnah as the collective practice of the companions, Shia scholars note that this definition obscures the distinction between companions who faithfully preserved the Prophet's legacy and those whose reliability Shia tradition questions.

The purification chapters are engaged for their legal content, with Shia scholars comparing Ibn Majah's narrations against Ja'fari jurisprudence on ritual cleanliness. Points of convergence — the general requirements for ablution, the conditions of ritual bathing — are acknowledged, while points of divergence — such as the manner of wiping in ablution — are examined for evidence that Ibn Majah's narrations may contain authentic variants supporting Shia practice. The collection's contested canonical status within Sunni tradition gives Shia scholars additional reason for selective engagement.
