Shia scholars approach Ibn Majah's fasting and Hajj traditions with attention to both legal convergence and the theological significance of pilgrimage events. The fasting traditions are largely compatible with Ja'fari jurisprudence, and Shia scholars acknowledge the shared prophetic foundation while noting specific differences in application and timing.

The Hajj traditions hold particular theological significance for Shia scholars because of the events associated with the Prophet's Farewell Pilgrimage — especially the pronouncement at Ghadir Khumm designating Ali as his successor. Shia readers examine Ibn Majah's pilgrimage narrations for any traditions that corroborate the Ghadir event or record the Prophet's statements about Ali's authority during the Hajj. These traditions are prized as supporting evidence from a Sunni canonical source for the central Shia doctrine of Ali's divinely appointed succession.
