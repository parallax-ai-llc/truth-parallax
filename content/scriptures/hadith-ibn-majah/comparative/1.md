A comparative reading of Ibn Majah's introductory section on the Sunnah examines the concept of authoritative prophetic tradition within the broader phenomenon of oral and textual authority across religions. The Islamic concept of Sunnah as a binding normative source parallels the authority of oral Torah in Rabbinic Judaism, the role of apostolic tradition in Catholic and Orthodox Christianity, and the function of Buddhist vinaya as the authoritative record of the Buddha's disciplinary rulings.

The purification traditions invite cross-traditional comparison with ritual purity systems in other religions. Ibn Majah's detailed prescriptions for physical cleanliness as a prerequisite for worship parallel the elaborate purity regulations of Levitical law in Judaism, the baptismal preparation in Christianity, and the ritual bathing requirements of Hinduism. Each tradition links physical purity with spiritual readiness, developing detailed codes that translate the intuition of sacred preparation into precise bodily practices. The variations within each tradition — between Sunni and Shia, between Ashkenazi and Sephardi — demonstrate the internal diversity characteristic of all living purity systems.
