A comparative reading of Ibn Majah's asceticism and tribulation sections places Islamic spiritual renunciation and eschatological expectation within the broader panorama of world religious thought. The zuhd traditions, which counsel detachment from worldly pleasures, parallel the Buddhist teaching on the dangers of attachment (*upadana*), the Christian counsel of evangelical poverty, the Stoic philosophy of *apatheia*, and the Jain practice of radical non-possessiveness (*aparigraha*). Each tradition identifies attachment to the material world as a source of spiritual suffering and prescribes various forms of detachment as the remedy.

The tribulation and eschatological traditions invite comparison with apocalyptic literature across religions — the biblical books of Daniel and Revelation, the Jewish *midrashic* traditions about the messianic age, the Buddhist prophecies about the decline of the *dharma*, and the Hindu concept of the *kali yuga*. Each tradition develops narratives of historical decline, cosmic crisis, and ultimate divine intervention that simultaneously explain present suffering and promise future redemption. Ibn Majah's contribution to this universal genre is rooted in specifically Islamic themes while participating in patterns shared across the world's eschatological traditions.
