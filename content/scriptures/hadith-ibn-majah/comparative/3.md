A comparative reading of Ibn Majah's fasting and Hajj traditions examines Islamic ascetic discipline and sacred pilgrimage within the broader spectrum of religious practice. The Ramadan fast can be compared with the Lenten fast of Christianity, the Yom Kippur fast of Judaism, and the fasting practices of Jainism and Buddhism. Each tradition understands voluntary deprivation as a means of spiritual purification, though the specific forms and theological rationales differ.

The Hajj traditions invite comparison with pilgrimage practices across world religions — the Jewish pilgrimage to Jerusalem, the Christian pilgrimages to the Holy Land and Rome, the Hindu *tirtha yatra* to sacred rivers and temples, and the Buddhist journeys to the sites of the Buddha's life. Ibn Majah's detailed ritual prescriptions for the Hajj can be compared with the elaborate protocols governing pilgrimage in other traditions, revealing a shared pattern of sacred journey, ritual performance at designated sites, and transformative return. The Hajj's distinctive feature — its requirement as a once-in-a-lifetime obligation for all able Muslims — creates a unique combination of universality and singularity in the comparative pilgrimage landscape.
