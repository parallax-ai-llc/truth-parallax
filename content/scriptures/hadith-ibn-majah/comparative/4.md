A comparative analysis of Ibn Majah's commercial and inheritance traditions examines how religious traditions regulate economic life and intergenerational wealth transfer. The Islamic prohibition of usury documented in Ibn Majah parallels similar prohibitions in Jewish law (*ribit*), medieval Christian canon law, and certain Hindu legal texts. Each tradition grapples with the tension between facilitating economic activity and preventing the exploitation of need, arriving at similar prohibitions through distinct theological frameworks.

The Islamic inheritance system documented in Ibn Majah's traditions can be compared with the succession laws of other religious traditions — the firstborn priority in biblical Israelite law, the varied inheritance customs of Hindu *dharmashastra*, and the absence of fixed inheritance rules in Buddhist vinaya. The Islamic system's distinctive features — its mathematical precision, its guaranteed shares for female heirs, and its integration of Quranic and prophetic prescriptions — represent a unique approach to the universal challenge of distributing wealth across generations within a religious moral framework.
