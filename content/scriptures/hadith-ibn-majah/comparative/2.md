A comparative analysis of Ibn Majah's prayer and mosque traditions situates Islamic worship within the global landscape of structured prayer and sacred architecture. The five daily prayers documented by Ibn Majah parallel the canonical hours of Christian monasticism, the three daily services of Jewish liturgical practice, and the regular meditation sessions of Buddhist monastic life. Each tradition structures the day around prescribed encounters with the sacred.

The mosque traditions invite comparison with the sacred spaces of other religions — the synagogue, the church, the temple, the gurdwara. Each of these spaces embodies its tradition's theology in material form: the mosque's directional orientation toward Mecca parallels the synagogue's orientation toward Jerusalem, while the mosque's relative simplicity contrasts with the iconographic richness of Orthodox Christian churches and Hindu temples. Ibn Majah's traditions on mosque etiquette reflect the universal religious concern with proper behavior in sacred space — a concern expressed through different but analogous behavioral codes across all traditions.
