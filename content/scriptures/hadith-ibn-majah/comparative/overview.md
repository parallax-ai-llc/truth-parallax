## Overview

A comparative approach to Sunan Ibn Majah examines the debates surrounding its canonical status within the broader phenomenon of canonical formation across religious traditions. Every major religion faces the question of which texts belong in its authoritative collection, and the scholarly negotiations surrounding Ibn Majah's inclusion in the Islamic canon illuminate dynamics shared with the formation of the biblical canon, the Buddhist Tripitaka, and the Confucian classics.

## Cross-Traditional Analysis

The debate over Ibn Majah's canonical status parallels similar processes in other traditions: the Christian disputes over the deuterocanonical books, the Rabbinic debates over the canonicity of Ecclesiastes and Song of Songs, and the Buddhist discussions about the relative authority of different sutra collections. Each case reveals that canonical boundaries are products of scholarly negotiation rather than self-evident designations, and that traditions on the margin often provide valuable evidence about the criteria used to define the center.

## Inter-Islamic Comparisons

Within Islam, a comparative reading of Ibn Majah examines how his collection's contested status affects its reception across interpretive communities. The traditions unique to his collection are evaluated differently by Sunni, Shia, Sufi, and Salafi scholars, with each community applying its own criteria of acceptance and relevance. This variation in reception demonstrates how canonical authority is not absolute but is always mediated through the interpretive practices of specific communities.
