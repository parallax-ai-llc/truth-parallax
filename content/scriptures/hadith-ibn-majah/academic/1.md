Academic scholars study Ibn Majah's introductory section on the Sunnah as a key document in the history of Islamic religious epistemology. His articulation of the Sunnah's authority — its relationship to the Quran, its binding character, and the methods for preserving it — reflects the scholarly consensus that had crystallized by the ninth century and provides evidence for how the concept of prophetic authority was constructed and defended in early Islamic intellectual culture.

The purification chapters are examined for their contribution to the history of Islamic ritual practice, with particular attention to the traditions unique to Ibn Majah's collection. Academic researchers analyze why these narrations were not included by other compilers, whether this exclusion reflects methodological judgments about their reliability or simply different selection priorities. The unique traditions provide additional data points for tracing the diversity of early Islamic ritual practice and the processes by which variant practices were either standardized or marginalized.
