Academic scholarship examines Ibn Majah's asceticism and tribulation sections as important sources for the history of Islamic spiritual thought and eschatological belief. The zuhd traditions are studied within the broader context of Near Eastern asceticism — alongside Christian monasticism, Jewish pietism, and Manichaean world-renunciation — examining how Islamic ascetic ideals both drew upon and distinguished themselves from earlier models.

The tribulation traditions are analyzed as evidence of early Islamic eschatological thought and the social anxieties of the early Muslim community. Scholars examine how predictions of future trials reflected contemporary political tensions, how eschatological narratives functioned as vehicles for social criticism, and how the hadith genre of *fitan* literature contributed to the formation of Islamic historical consciousness. Ibn Majah's substantial and partially unique eschatological material provides important evidence for studying the development of Islamic apocalypticism and its relationship to Jewish and Christian eschatological traditions.
