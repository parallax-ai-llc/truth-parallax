Academic scholarship examines Ibn Majah's fasting and Hajj traditions for their contribution to understanding the codification of Islamic ritual obligations and the social history of pilgrimage. The fasting traditions are studied for evidence of how Ramadan observance was standardized, how variant fasting practices were negotiated, and how the fasting discipline related to broader patterns of asceticism in the Near Eastern religious landscape.

The Hajj traditions are particularly valuable for academic study of the pilgrimage as a social, economic, and political phenomenon. Scholars analyze the ritual prescriptions for evidence of pre-Islamic continuities and Islamic innovations, the logistical infrastructure implied by the pilgrimage traditions, and the role of the Hajj in creating a shared identity among geographically dispersed Muslim communities. Ibn Majah's unique pilgrimage narrations provide additional evidence for the diversity of early pilgrimage practice and the processes by which a canonical form of the Hajj was established.
