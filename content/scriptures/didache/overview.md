## Overview

The Didache (Greek for "Teaching"), fully titled "The Teaching of the Lord to the Gentiles through the Twelve Apostles," is among the earliest surviving Christian documents outside the New Testament, likely composed between 50-120 CE. Rediscovered in 1873 in a Constantinople manuscript by Metropolitan Philotheos Bryennios, the Didache provides an unparalleled window into the practices, liturgy, and organization of an early Jewish-Christian community, possibly in Syria.

The text is structured as a manual of instruction for converts and church leaders. It opens with the "Two Ways" teaching — the Way of Life and the Way of Death — presenting a systematic ethical catechesis that draws on both Jewish tradition and the teachings of Jesus. The Didache then provides detailed instructions for baptism, fasting, prayer (including the earliest known form of the Lord's Prayer outside the Gospels), the Eucharist, the reception of traveling apostles and prophets, and the organization of local church leadership.

The Didache's significance extends far beyond its modest length. It preserves liturgical prayers that may predate those in the New Testament, offers the earliest instructions for Christian baptism and eucharistic celebration, and reveals a church still deeply rooted in Jewish practice while developing its own distinctive identity. The text's eschatological conclusion echoes the apocalyptic expectation of the early community and provides evidence for Christian watchfulness teaching.

## Structure

Didache is structured as follows:

1. **Chapters 1-6**: The Two Ways: Ethical catechesis contrasting the Way of Life and the Way of Death
2. **Chapters 7-10**: Liturgical Instructions: Baptism, fasting, prayer, and the Eucharist
3. **Chapters 11-13**: Church Order: Guidelines for receiving apostles, prophets, and teachers
4. **Chapters 14-15**: Community Life: Confession, eucharistic gathering, and the appointment of bishops and deacons
5. **Chapter 16**: Eschatological Conclusion: Watchfulness and the signs of the end

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: One of the most important texts for reconstructing earliest Christian worship, ethics, and community organization
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels with Jewish Two Ways tradition, Qumran community rules, and ethical catechesis across traditions
- **Protestant**: Studied for its witness to early church practice before the development of later hierarchical and sacramental structures
- **Catholic**: Valued as the earliest witness to Christian sacramental practice and the development of church order
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Significant for its liturgical traditions that continue in Orthodox worship and its witness to apostolic practice
- **Interfaith**: A window into the Jewish-Christian matrix that facilitates dialogue about shared ethical foundations and liturgical roots

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Two Ways | The foundational ethical teaching contrasting the path of righteousness with the path of destruction |
| Baptismal Practice | The earliest detailed instructions for Christian baptism, including the preference for living water |
| Eucharistic Prayers | Ancient liturgical prayers over the cup and bread that may preserve pre-Pauline eucharistic tradition |
| Itinerant Ministry | Guidelines for receiving and testing traveling apostles and prophets in the community |
| Church Organization | Early evidence for the appointment of bishops and deacons alongside prophetic ministry |
| Eschatological Watchfulness | The call to readiness for the coming of the Lord and the signs that precede it |
