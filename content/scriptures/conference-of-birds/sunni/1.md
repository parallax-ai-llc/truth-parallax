## The Call to Journey: Quranic Resonances

The hoopoe's summons to the birds echoes the Quranic call to seek God with sincerity and resolve. The Quran identifies the hoopoe as Solomon's trusted messenger (27:20-28), and Attar builds on this Quranic authority to present the hoopoe as a figure of prophetic guidance. For Sunni readers, the assembly of the birds dramatizes the universal human condition described in the Quran: heedlessness (*ghaflah*) and the need for a divine call to awaken the soul from its comfortable slumber.

The birds' objections — the nightingale's love of beauty, the hawk's attachment to royal favor, the parrot's contentment with its cage — represent the worldly distractions that the Quran repeatedly warns against. The hoopoe's responses draw on Quranic themes of divine mercy, the transience of worldly goods, and the ultimate accountability of every soul before God. For Sunni readers, the poem's opening is not merely literary entertainment but a call to *tawba* (repentance) — the turning of the heart from creation toward the Creator.
