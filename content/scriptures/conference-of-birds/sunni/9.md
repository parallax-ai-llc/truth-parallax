## The Simurgh: The Goal of Faith

The poem's climax — the thirty birds discovering that the Simurgh is themselves — is read by Sunni interpreters not as a claim of divine identity but as the realization of *khalifah* (vicegerency): the human being as God's representative on earth (Quran 2:30), created in the divine *fitrah* (primordial nature) and reflecting divine attributes when the veils of ego are removed.

The Sunni reading preserves the ontological distinction between God and creation while affirming the profound intimacy between them. The thirty birds do not become God; they become what they were always meant to be — mirrors of divine attributes, reflecting divine light because the dust of ego has been polished away by the journey. The Simurgh is not the birds; the Simurgh is reflected in the birds. This distinction, crucial for Sunni orthodoxy, preserves both the transcendence of God and the dignity of the human being.
