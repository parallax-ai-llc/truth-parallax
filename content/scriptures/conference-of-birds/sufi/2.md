## The Valley of the Quest: Renunciation and Desire

The first valley that the birds must cross is the Valley of the Quest (*talab*). Here the seeker must renounce everything — wealth, reputation, comfort, even spiritual attainments — and set out with nothing but burning desire for the Simurgh. The valley is vast and trackless, and many birds turn back, unable to endure the uncertainty and hardship.

For the Sufi reader, the Valley of the Quest corresponds to the stage of *tawba* (repentance) and *irada* (spiritual will) — the initial turning away from the world and toward God. The renunciation required is not merely external (giving up possessions) but internal (giving up the ego's claim to know where it is going). The seeker enters the Valley of the Quest not with a map but with a longing — and the longing itself, if genuine, is the only compass needed.

The stories the hoopoe tells in this valley illustrate the cost and the reward of genuine seeking. A king gives up his throne for love; a beggar discovers that his poverty is his greatest wealth. The teaching is consistent: the quest requires everything, but what is given up is nothing compared to what is found.
