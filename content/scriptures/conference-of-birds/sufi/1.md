## The Hoopoe's Call and the Beginning of the Path

The Conference of the Birds opens with the hoopoe (*hudhud*) — the bird mentioned in the Quran (27:20-28) as Solomon's messenger — summoning the birds of the world to undertake a journey to find their king, the Simurgh. Each bird represents a human type, and each raises objections to the journey based on its attachments: the nightingale is attached to the rose (earthly beauty), the parrot to its golden cage (material comfort), the peacock to paradise (spiritual ambition), the hawk to its position at court (worldly power).

For the Sufi reader, the hoopoe represents the *murshid* (spiritual master) — the guide who has already completed the journey and now calls others to undertake it. The birds' objections represent the *nafs* (ego-self) in its various forms: each bird's attachment is a different mask of the same fundamental disease — the preference for the known over the unknown, the partial over the whole, the self over God. The hoopoe answers each objection with a story that exposes the attachment's futility and points toward the greater reality that awaits.

The assembly scene establishes the Sufi hermeneutical key: the poem is not merely a story but a diagnosis. The Sufi reader is invited to identify which bird they most resemble and to recognize their own attachment in the bird's objection.
