## The Simurgh: Thirty Birds See Themselves

The poem's climax is one of the great moments in world literature. Of the thousands of birds that set out on the journey, only thirty (*si murgh*) survive to reach the court of the Simurgh. Exhausted, diminished, stripped of every pretension, they approach the throne — and discover that the Simurgh is themselves. "Si murgh" means "thirty birds" in Persian. The Simurgh they sought was the totality of themselves, purified by the journey. They look into the face of the Simurgh and see their own faces; they look at themselves and see the Simurgh.

For the Sufi reader, this moment of recognition encapsulates the entire teaching of the path. The divine was never elsewhere; it was always here, hidden beneath the layers of ego that the seven valleys progressively stripped away. The journey was real — the suffering, the loss, the bewilderment were real — but the destination was never distant. The Simurgh was present in the first valley as fully as in the last; what changed was not the Simurgh but the birds' capacity to see it.

The poem ends with the thirty birds dissolving into the Simurgh — losing themselves and finding the Real. This is *baqa* (subsistence): the state that follows *fana*, in which the self that was annihilated is reconstituted in God, now seeing with God's eyes, hearing with God's ears, acting with God's hands. The journey is complete — and yet, as Rumi (Attar's spiritual successor) would write: "There is no end to the journey of love." The Simurgh sings through the thirty birds who are no longer thirty, and the song has no end.
