## The Destination Within: The Quest That Ends Where It Began

The poem's climax — the birds discovering that they themselves are the Simurgh — belongs to a widespread pattern in world literature and spirituality: the quest that ends with the discovery that the treasure was at home all along. Dorothy's return to Kansas, the Prodigal Son's return to the Father, the Zen ox-herding pictures' return to the marketplace, and T. S. Eliot's declaration that "the end of all our exploring will be to arrive where we started and know the place for the first time" all express the same paradox.

The comparative insight is that the journey was necessary even though the destination was never distant. The process of seeking transforms the seeker, and it is the transformed seeker — not the original one — who is capable of recognizing what was always present. The thirty birds who reach the Simurgh are not the same birds who set out from the assembly; the journey has polished them into mirrors capable of reflecting the divine light that was shining all along.
