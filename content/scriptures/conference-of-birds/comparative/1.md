## The Call to Adventure: Quest Narratives Across Cultures

The assembly of the birds — a group of seekers summoned by a guide to undertake a dangerous journey to find their king — exemplifies the universal "call to adventure" identified by Joseph Campbell in *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*. The pattern appears across cultures: Odysseus's departure from Ithaca, the Israelites' exodus from Egypt, the Fellowship's departure from Rivendell, the Buddha's departure from the palace.

The birds' initial objections — each offering a reason to stay home — parallel the "refusal of the call" that Campbell identifies as a standard element of the hero's journey. The nightingale's attachment to the rose, the hawk's attachment to royal service, and the parrot's contentment with its cage represent universal forms of resistance to change: attachment to beauty, power, and comfort.
