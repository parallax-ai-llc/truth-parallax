## The Allegorical Frame: Birds, Beasts, and Sufi Pedagogy

Scholars have analyzed the assembly of the birds as a sophisticated deployment of the beast-fable tradition for Sufi pedagogical purposes. The genre — animals gathering, debating, and embarking on a quest — has antecedents in Kalila wa Dimna (the Arabic adaptation of the Panchatantra), the animal fables of Aesop, and the Quranic motif of Solomon's conversation with the animals (27:16-17). Attar transforms this tradition into a vehicle for systematic mystical instruction.

Hellmut Ritter's magisterial study *The Ocean of the Soul* provides the most comprehensive analysis of Attar's narrative technique, demonstrating how the embedded stories (each bird's objection, each valley's parables) create a multilayered text that can be read at progressively deeper levels. The hoopoe's role as guide and storyteller has been compared by scholars to the Sufi master's function in the *sema* (mystical gathering), where stories are deployed not as entertainment but as spiritual catalysts calibrated to the listener's condition.
