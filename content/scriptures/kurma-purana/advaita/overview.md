## Overview

Advaita Vedanta interprets the Kurma Purana through the lens of non-dualism, reading the text's theological affirmations as descriptions of saguna Brahman — Brahman with attributes — which serves as a practical focus for worship while the ultimate reality remains nirguna Brahman, beyond all names, forms, and sectarian distinctions.

For Advaitins, the Purana's devotional framework is a valid but ultimately provisional approach to the highest truth. The narratives and theological claims within the text are understood through the method of adhyaropa-apavada (superimposition and negation), in which attributes are first projected onto Brahman for the purpose of instruction and then negated to reveal the attributeless Absolute.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Saguna and Nirguna Brahman
The deities described in this text are understood as saguna Brahman — Brahman apprehended through the limiting adjuncts (upadhis) of maya. The ultimate reality transcends all personal and impersonal characterizations.

### Vivarta (Apparent Transformation)
The cosmological narratives are read through the doctrine of vivarta — the universe does not truly emerge from Brahman but appears to do so through the power of maya.

### Liberation through Knowledge
While the text may emphasize devotion, Advaitins interpret the ultimate message as pointing toward jnana — the realization that the individual self (atman) is identical with Brahman.

## Theological Emphases

- **Non-duality**: The apparent multiplicity of the universe is ultimately illusory
- **Adhyasa**: Superimposition of attributes onto the attributeless Brahman
- **Vyavaharika and Paramarthika**: The distinction between conventional and ultimate levels of reality
- **Jivanmukti**: Liberation while still embodied through the knowledge of one's true nature
