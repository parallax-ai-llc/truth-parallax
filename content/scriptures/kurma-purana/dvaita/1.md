## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta approaches this section of the Kurma Purana with its characteristic insistence on theological realism and the absolute supremacy of Vishnu. The Kurma avatar, the churning of the ocean, and the cosmological framework — for Dvaitins, these are descriptions of real structures and events in a real universe created and sustained by a real personal God.

Madhvacharya's hermeneutical method involves reading all scriptural passages as ultimately affirming Vishnu's supremacy (Vishnu-sarvottamatva). This section is interpreted through the Dvaita framework of pancha-bheda (five-fold eternal distinction between God, souls, and matter), tattvavada (philosophical realism), and taratamya (the graded hierarchy of beings).

Where this section's theological claims appear to challenge Vishnu's supremacy, Dvaita scholars apply careful hermeneutical strategies to demonstrate that the text's deeper meaning supports the Vaishnava position.

## Key Themes

- **Svatantrya**: Vishnu's absolute independence manifested in the events and structures described
- **Pancha-bheda**: The five-fold eternal distinction between God, souls, and matter
- **Taratamya**: The graded hierarchy of beings reflected in this section's narratives
- **Scriptural Realism**: The described events and structures as referring to real ontological entities
- **Vishnu-sarvottamatva**: The supremacy of Vishnu as the ultimate teaching of this section

## Key Verses and Passages

### Realist Interpretation

Dvaitins insist that the cosmological, mythological, and theological content of this section describes real entities and events. This stands in direct contrast to the Advaita reading, which treats such content as provisional or illusory.

### Anti-Mayavada Polemic

The vivid descriptions of cosmic structures and divine activities in this section are cited by Dvaita scholars as evidence against the Advaita doctrine of maya. A real God creates a real world with real distinctions — this is the Dvaita reading of Puranic cosmology.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's citations from texts like this appear throughout his major works. His student Jayatirtha systematized these readings, while Vyasatirtha mounted vigorous dialectical defenses of the Dvaita interpretation against Advaita and Vishishtadvaita alternatives. Raghavendra Swami's later commentaries made these sophisticated readings accessible to a broader devotional audience.
