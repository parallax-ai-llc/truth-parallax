---
title: "Bhavishya Purana Madhyama Parva (Book 2)"
scripture: "bhavishya-purana"
chapter: 2
---

**1** The Madhyama Parva of the Bhavishya Purana describes the worship of Shiva and Shakti, the sacred Vratas, and the stories that illustrate the power of devotion.

**2** The Purana describes the worship of Shiva in the form of the Linga: "The Linga is the symbol of the formless Brahman. It represents the union of Shiva and Shakti, the masculine and feminine principles of creation. He who worships the Linga with devotion attains the world of Shiva."

**3** The story of the origin of the Linga is narrated. When Brahma and Vishnu quarreled over who was supreme, a blazing pillar of light appeared between them, extending infinitely in both directions. They could not find its beginning or end. That pillar was Shiva, and it became the first Linga.

**4** The twelve Jyotirlingas, the self-manifested lingas of light, are described: Somnath, Mallikarjuna, Mahakaleshwar, Omkareshwar, Kedarnath, Bhimashankar, Vishweshwar, Tryambakeshwar, Vaidyanath, Nageshwar, Rameshwaram, and Grishneshwar.

**5** The Purana describes the worship of Shakti, the divine feminine: "The Goddess is the power of the universe. She is Durga, the invincible; Lakshmi, the source of all prosperity; Sarasvati, the fountain of all knowledge. Without Shakti, Shiva is inert."

**6** The story of Durga slaying the buffalo demon Mahishasura is narrated. When the gods were defeated by Mahishasura, they combined their energies to create the goddess Durga. She rode a lion into battle, fought the demon for nine days, and slew him on the tenth.

**7** The Navaratri festival is described in detail: "For nine nights, the goddess is worshipped with special rituals, offerings, and prayers. On the tenth day, Vijayadashami, the victory of good over evil is celebrated. This festival re-enacts the cosmic battle between dharma and adharma."

**8** The Purana describes the Vrata of Karva Chauth, observed by married women: "On the fourth day after the full moon of Kartika, the wife fasts from sunrise to moonrise for the longevity of her husband. She breaks her fast only after seeing the moon through a sieve."

**9** The story of Queen Veeravati is narrated as the origin of Karva Chauth. By the grace of the goddess, her husband was restored to life after she observed the fast with perfect devotion, despite being tricked into breaking it prematurely by her brothers.

**10** The Purana describes various other Vratas: the Monday fast for Shiva, the Tuesday fast for Hanuman, the Thursday fast for the guru, the Saturday fast for Shani. Each Vrata has its own deity, its own rituals, and its own rewards.

**11** The sage teaches: "Vratas are not mere external observances. They are training for the soul. Through fasting, the body is disciplined. Through prayer, the mind is purified. Through devotion, the heart is opened to the divine."

**12** The Madhyama Parva concludes: "All forms of the divine are worthy of worship. Whether one worships Vishnu, Shiva, Shakti, Surya, or Ganesha, the Supreme Being is pleased, for all these are facets of the one jewel of Brahman."
