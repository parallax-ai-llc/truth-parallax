---
title: "Bhavishya Purana Uttara Parva (Book 4)"
scripture: "bhavishya-purana"
chapter: 4
---

**1** The Uttara Parva of the Bhavishya Purana describes the festivals, sacred observances, and the detailed calendar of religious activities for the devout householder.

**2** The Purana describes the Hindu calendar in detail. The year consists of twelve months, each divided into two fortnights — the bright (Shukla) and the dark (Krishna). Each day has its presiding deity, its auspicious and inauspicious hours, and its recommended activities.

**3** The festival of Makara Sankranti is described: "When the sun enters the sign of Capricorn, the northward journey begins. This day is sacred to Surya. Gifts of sesame and jaggery should be made. The Ganga is especially sanctified on this day."

**4** The festival of Vasanta Panchami, the fifth day of the bright fortnight of Magha, is sacred to Sarasvati: "On this day, the goddess of learning is worshipped with books, musical instruments, and yellow flowers. Students and scholars should begin new studies on this auspicious day."

**5** The Holi festival is described: "On the full moon of Phalguna, the festival of colors celebrates the destruction of the demoness Holika and the triumph of the devotee Prahlada. Bonfires are lit on the night before, and people play with colors on the day itself."

**6** The story behind Holi is narrated: Holika, the sister of Hiranyakashipu, was endowed with a garment that protected her from fire. She took young Prahlada into a bonfire to burn him. But by the grace of Vishnu, the garment flew from Holika to Prahlada, and she was consumed while he emerged unscathed.

**7** The festival of Raksha Bandhan is described: "On the full moon of Shravana, sisters tie a sacred thread on their brothers' wrists, praying for their protection and prosperity. In return, brothers vow to protect their sisters throughout their lives."

**8** The festival of Ganesh Chaturthi is described: "On the fourth day of the bright fortnight of Bhadrapada, Lord Ganesha is worshipped. Clay images of the elephant-headed god are installed and worshipped for one, five, seven, or ten days, then immersed in water."

**9** The Diwali festival is described: "On the new moon of Kartika, the festival of lights celebrates the return of Rama to Ayodhya. Houses are illuminated with lamps. Lakshmi is worshipped for prosperity. Gifts are exchanged, and all beings rejoice."

**10** The Purana teaches: "Festivals are not mere occasions for merriment. They are spiritual opportunities. Each festival commemorates a divine event and offers the devotee a chance to connect with the sacred. He who observes festivals with devotion and understanding progresses on the path of liberation."

**11** The annual cycle of festivals is presented as a spiritual journey: "The year begins with creation at Vasanta Panchami and ends with dissolution at Diwali. Each festival marks a stage in the soul's journey from ignorance to enlightenment."

**12** The sage concluded: "The festivals and observances described in this Purana are the living heart of dharma. Through them, the sacred enters the daily life of every householder. They transform the mundane into the divine and make every day an opportunity for worship."
