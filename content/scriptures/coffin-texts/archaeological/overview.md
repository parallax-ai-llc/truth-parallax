## Overview

Archaeological study of the Coffin Texts focuses on the coffins themselves as material objects — their construction, decoration, provenance, and the relationship between text and image. Unlike the Pyramid Texts (inscribed on walls) or the Book of the Dead (written on papyrus), the Coffin Texts exist primarily on the interior surfaces of rectangular wooden coffins from the Middle Kingdom.

## Key Archaeological Approaches

### Coffin Studies
Archaeologists examine the physical coffins — their wood type, construction technique, painting style, and text layout — to determine provenance, date, and the social status of the deceased.

### Regional Workshops
Material evidence reveals distinct regional coffin-making traditions at sites such as el-Bersha, Asyut, Meir, and Thebes, each with characteristic spell selections and decorative programs.

### Conservation and Documentation
Many Coffin Text inscriptions have deteriorated since their discovery, making early archaeological records invaluable for reconstructing damaged or lost texts.

## Key Archaeological Sites

| Site | Significance |
|------|-------------|
| Deir el-Bersha | Largest corpus; nomarch tombs with the Book of Two Ways |
| Asyut | Important regional tradition with distinctive spells |
| Meir | Well-preserved coffins with extensive texts |
| Beni Hasan | Middle Kingdom tombs with decorated coffins |
| Thebes | Late Middle Kingdom examples |
