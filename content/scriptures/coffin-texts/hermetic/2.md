## Hermetic Reading

Section 2, "Provisioning the Deceased," addresses protective spells that ward the deceased against hostile forces in the underworld. The Coffin Texts (c. 2100-1650 BCE) represent a crucial middle stage in Egyptian funerary literature, democratizing the royal afterlife technology of the Pyramid Texts and making it available to non-royal individuals. For Hermetic interpreters, this democratization parallels the Hermetic tradition's own universalist soteriology: the path to divine union is open to all who possess the requisite gnosis.

## From Royal Privilege to Universal Gnosis

The Coffin Texts' extension of afterlife spells from pharaohs to commoners mirrors the Hermetic movement's extension of salvific knowledge beyond priestly elites. Jan Assmann's *The Mind of Egypt* analyzes this democratization as a theological revolution in which the individual's relationship to the divine became direct rather than mediated through the king. The Corpus Hermeticum reflects the same principle: Hermes Trismegistus teaches all who are prepared to receive, regardless of social station.

## Sympatheia and Theurgic Practice

The spells in this section operate through the Hermetic practitioner's need for spiritual protection during theurgic operations, as described in Iamblichus's *De Mysteriis*. The Coffin Texts' ritual technology — spoken words, visual symbols, and material preparations working in concert — exemplifies the sympatheia (cosmic sympathy) that Hermetic philosophy posits as the mechanism of all effective ritual. Each element of the spell resonates with corresponding realities at higher levels of existence, activating the channels through which divine power flows into the material world.

## The Great Work and Spiritual Alchemy

The transformation encoded in these spells corresponds to the sealing of the alchemical vessel — the creation of a protected space in which transformation can safely occur. Florian Ebeling's *The Secret History of Hermes Trismegistus* documents how early modern alchemists explicitly drew on Egyptian funerary symbolism, recognizing the mummification-and-resurrection pattern as the archetype of alchemical transformation. The Coffin Texts' imagery of food and drink maps directly onto the stages of the opus alchymicum.

## Pneuma and the Breath of Life

The vivifying power invoked in these spells is the pneuma — the divine breath that, in Hermetic cosmology, animates all living things and connects all levels of reality. The Egyptian concepts of *ba*, *ka*, and *akh* represent differentiated aspects of this pneumatic reality, corresponding to what Hermetic philosophy distinguishes as psyche (soul), pneuma (spirit), and nous (divine intellect).
