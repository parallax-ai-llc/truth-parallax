## Zen Buddhist Reading

Volume 28 of the Nihon Shoki recounts the second part of Empress Jito's reign, focusing on administrative consolidation, legal codification, and diplomatic relations. A Zen reading of this material finds within the historical and mythological narrative illustrations of Buddhist principles — impermanence (*mujō*), the suffering born of attachment, and the possibility of awakening within the midst of worldly activity.

The rise and fall of rulers, the reversals of fortune, and the constant flux of political power documented in this volume exemplify the First Noble Truth: all conditioned phenomena are marked by *dukkha* (unsatisfactoriness). The Zen practitioner reads these accounts not for political instruction but as reminders that no worldly achievement is permanent and no temporal power ultimately satisfying.

The *shinbutsu shūgō* (kami-buddha synthesis) tradition provides a framework for reading the kami and human figures of this volume as participants in the universal drama of awakening. The *honji suijaku* theory suggests that the sacred beings who appear in these narratives may be understood as compassionate manifestations of buddhas and bodhisattvas, adapting their teaching to the specific cultural context of Japan.

From Dōgen's perspective, the events narrated here are not merely past occurrences but expressions of *uji* (being-time) — each moment fully containing the totality of existence. The chronicle form, which unfolds linearly, paradoxically points toward the Zen realization that past, present, and future interpenetrate.
