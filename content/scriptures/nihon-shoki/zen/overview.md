## Overview

Zen Buddhist engagement with the Nihon Shoki reflects the long history of *shinbutsu shūgō* (the synthesis of kami and buddhas) in Japanese religious life. While the Nihon Shoki predates Zen's arrival in Japan, Zen thinkers have read its narratives through Buddhist philosophical lenses, finding resonances with teachings on impermanence, interdependence, and the non-duality of sacred and mundane.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Honji Suijaku (Original Ground, Manifest Traces)
This medieval framework identified kami as local manifestations (*suijaku*) of universal buddhas and bodhisattvas (*honji*). Under this reading, the kami of the Nihon Shoki — including Amaterasu — were understood as compassionate emanations of enlightened beings adapting to Japanese cultural forms.

### Impermanence and Ruling
Zen readings of the imperial chronicles emphasize the impermanence (*mujō*) of worldly power. The rise and fall of emperors and the constant political upheaval documented in the text illustrate the Buddhist teaching that all conditioned phenomena are transient.

### Musubi and Śūnyatā
Some interpreters draw parallels between the Shinto concept of *musubi* (creative becoming) and the Buddhist teaching of *śūnyatā* (emptiness) — both pointing to a reality that is dynamic, relational, and without fixed essence.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shinbutsu shūgō**: The creative integration of kami worship and Buddhist practice
- **Mujō**: Impermanence as a lens for reading historical narrative
- **Non-duality**: Overcoming the opposition between sacred and profane, kami and buddha
- **Naturalness (jinen)**: Spontaneous, unforced action aligned with the way of things
