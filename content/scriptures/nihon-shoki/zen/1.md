## Emptiness Before Form

Volume 1's account of the primordial chaos — formless, boundless, and containing all potential — resonates with the Zen Buddhist concept of *śūnyatā* (emptiness, *kū*). The undifferentiated state before heaven and earth separated can be read as pointing toward the ground of being that precedes all distinctions, including the fundamental duality of subject and object.

From a Zen perspective, the emergence of the kami from chaos illustrates the Buddhist teaching of *pratītyasamutpāda* (dependent origination, *engi*): nothing arises independently but only in relation to everything else. The kami do not create from outside but manifest from within the interdependent web of reality.

The *honji suijaku* framework, developed during the medieval period, would later identify the primordial kami with cosmic buddhas. Kūkai (774–835), founder of Shingon Buddhism, interpreted the creation of the cosmos as the self-expression of Dainichi Nyorai (Mahāvairocana), the cosmic buddha whose body is the entire universe.

Dōgen Zenji (1200–1253), while not commenting directly on the Nihon Shoki, articulated a philosophy of *uji* (being-time) in which past, present, and future interpenetrate — a framework that transforms the mythological "beginning" from a distant past event into an ever-present reality accessible in *zazen* (seated meditation).
