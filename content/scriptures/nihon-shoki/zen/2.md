The Izanagi-Izanami narrative, read through a Zen lens, dramatizes the fundamental human encounter with dualism — life and death, purity and pollution, attachment and release. Izanagi's desperate clinging to Izanami, his refusal to accept her death, and his horrified flight from her decaying form in Yomi illustrate the suffering (*dukkha*) that arises from attachment.

The pivotal moment of *misogi* — Izanagi's purification in the river — can be understood as a prototype of the radical letting-go that Zen practice demands. By washing away the pollution of Yomi, Izanagi does not merely cleanse himself physically but undergoes a transformation of consciousness. The three supreme kami born from this purification represent what emerges when attachment to dualistic thinking is released.

Dōgen's concept of *shinjin datsuraku* (dropping off body-mind) resonates with Izanagi's shedding of his garments and immersion in flowing water. The Zen practitioner, like Izanagi, must pass through the confrontation with death and impermanence to reach genuine awakening.

The medieval *honji suijaku* tradition identified Amaterasu — born from Izanagi's purification — with Dainichi Nyorai, suggesting that the supreme kami and the cosmic buddha are two faces of the same reality.
