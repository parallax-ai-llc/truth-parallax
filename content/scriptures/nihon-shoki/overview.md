## Overview

The Nihon Shoki (日本書紀, "Chronicles of Japan"), also known as Nihongi, is the second-oldest book of classical Japanese history, completed in 720 CE under the supervision of Prince Toneri and Ō no Yasumaro. Written in classical Chinese (*kanbun*), it was compiled as an official state history for the imperial court.

## Structure

The Nihon Shoki consists of thirty volumes (*kan*), organized as follows:

1. **Age of the Gods** (Volumes 1–2): Cosmogony, creation myths, and the divine generations of kami
2. **Early Emperors** (Volumes 3–15): From Emperor Jimmu through the fifth century, blending myth and semi-historical narrative
3. **Historical Emperors** (Volumes 16–30): From the sixth century to Empress Jitō (697 CE), increasingly historical in character

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Nihon Shoki holds distinct significance across multiple interpretive frameworks:

- **Shinto Religious**: The foundational narrative of kami, creation, and the divine lineage of the imperial house
- **Philosophical**: A source for understanding Japanese cosmology, ethics, and concepts of governance
- **Confucian**: Reflects Chinese historiographical methods and Confucian political ideals
- **Zen Buddhist**: Read alongside Buddhist cosmology to understand syncretism in Japanese religious thought
- **Academic**: Studied through archaeological, philological, and comparative mythological methods
- **Comparative**: Examined alongside the Kojiki, Chinese dynastic histories, and Korean chronicles

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Cosmogony | The emergence of heaven and earth, and the generations of kami |
| Imperial Legitimacy | The divine descent of the imperial line from Amaterasu |
| Kami | The nature and actions of the gods who shaped Japan |
| Sinicization | The adoption and adaptation of Chinese culture, Buddhism, and statecraft |
| Tennō | The role and authority of the emperor as mediator between heaven and earth |
