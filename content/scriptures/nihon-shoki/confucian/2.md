From a Confucian perspective, Volume 2's account of Izanagi and Izanami models the proper relationship between complementary forces — the active (yang) and receptive (yin) principles that together generate all things. The failed first creation, when Izanami speaks before Izanagi in the marriage ceremony, reflects the Confucian emphasis on proper ritual order (*li*): when hierarchical propriety is violated, the result is defective.

The Confucian reading of the chapter emphasizes the ethical dimensions of the narrative. Izanagi's grief at Izanami's death demonstrates the virtue of conjugal devotion (*gi*). His descent to Yomi shows the depth of human attachment. However, his acceptance of separation and return to the world above models the Confucian virtue of knowing when to act and when to withdraw.

The birth of the three noble children — Amaterasu, Tsukuyomi, and Susanoo — from Izanagi's purification establishes the principle that from proper ritual action comes proper order. Amaterasu's assignment to rule the High Plain of Heaven prefigures the Confucian ideal of a virtuous sovereign whose authority derives from moral cultivation rather than force alone.

Hayashi Razan interpreted Izanagi and Izanami through Neo-Confucian categories as manifestations of *ri* (principle) operating through *ki* (material force).
