## Confucian Interpretation

Volume 8 of the Nihon Shoki recounts Emperor Chuai and Empress Jingu's legendary military expedition to the Korean peninsula, a narrative of significant historiographical controversy. The Confucian reading of this material foregrounds the ethical dimensions of governance, the moral character of rulers, and the relationship between virtuous leadership and social harmony.

The compilers of the Nihon Shoki, trained in Chinese classical learning, structured their narrative according to Confucian historiographical principles. Events are presented not merely as facts but as moral lessons: the virtuous ruler brings peace and prosperity through benevolence (*jin*) and proper ritual (*rei*), while the negligent or tyrannical ruler invites disorder and divine displeasure.

This volume illustrates the Confucian conviction that political order is an extension of moral order. The success or failure of the enterprises described reflects not merely strategic calculation but the moral quality of the actors involved. The concept of *toku* (virtue/德) pervades the evaluation of every reign.

Tokugawa-era Confucian scholars such as Hayashi Razan and Arai Hakuseki read these annals as a mirror for their own age, drawing lessons about proper governance, the duties of ministers, and the relationship between sovereign and subject. The Nihon Shoki thus functioned as a practical handbook of political ethics within the Confucian intellectual tradition.
