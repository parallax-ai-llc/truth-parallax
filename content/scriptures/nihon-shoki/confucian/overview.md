## Overview

The Nihon Shoki was composed in classical Chinese by scholars deeply versed in Confucian learning, and its historiographical framework, moral evaluations, and political ideals bear the unmistakable imprint of Chinese Confucian thought. Confucian readings of the text emphasize the ethical dimensions of rulership, the importance of ritual propriety, and the parallel between cosmic order and social harmony.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historiographical Method
The Nihon Shoki follows the model of Chinese dynastic histories, particularly the *Shiji* (Records of the Grand Historian) and *Han Shu* (Book of Han). This annalistic format itself embodies the Confucian belief that recording the deeds of rulers — both virtuous and corrupt — serves as a moral mirror for posterity.

### Virtue-Based Governance
Confucian interpreters read the imperial annals as moral exempla: good emperors who practiced benevolence (*jin/ren*) and ritual propriety (*rei/li*) brought peace and prosperity, while those who neglected virtue brought disorder.

### Ritual and Music
The Nihon Shoki's accounts of court ceremonies, music, and ritual reflect the Confucian emphasis on *li* (ritual/propriety) as the foundation of social order. The adoption of Chinese court protocols is documented extensively in the later volumes.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tenmei (Mandate of Heaven)**: The concept that heavenly sanction underlies legitimate rule
- **Toku (Virtue)**: Moral cultivation as the essential qualification for governance
- **Chū (Loyalty)**: Faithful service to the sovereign as a cardinal virtue
- **Kō (Filial piety)**: Reverence for ancestors and continuity of lineage
