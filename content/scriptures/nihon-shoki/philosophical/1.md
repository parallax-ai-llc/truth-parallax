## Cosmogony and the Emergence of Order

Volume 1 of the Nihon Shoki opens with a cosmogonic vision: heaven and earth were not yet separated, and the primordial substance resembled an egg, boundless and obscure. From this undifferentiated chaos, the pure and bright element rose to form heaven, while the heavy and turbid settled to become earth. Between them arose the first kami.

This cosmogony presents a philosophical vision fundamentally different from creation *ex nihilo*. The world is not made by a transcendent creator but emerges through internal differentiation — a process of self-organization from chaos to order. The metaphor of the cosmic egg echoes Chinese *hundun* (primordial chaos) and suggests a universe that is inherently generative.

The philosophical implications are significant: order is not imposed from outside but arises naturally from within. The first kami — Kuni-no-tokotachi-no-mikoto (Lord of the Eternal Land) — represents not a maker but a spontaneous crystallization of cosmic potential. This has parallels with process philosophy and organicist thinking.

The text's preservation of multiple variant accounts (*issho*) in this chapter is itself philosophically noteworthy, suggesting that no single narrative can exhaust the meaning of origins.
