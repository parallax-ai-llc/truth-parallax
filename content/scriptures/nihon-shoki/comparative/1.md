## Creation from Chaos: Global Parallels

The Nihon Shoki's opening cosmogony — the world beginning as an undifferentiated, egg-like mass from which heaven and earth separate — belongs to one of the most widespread mythological patterns in the world. The "cosmic egg" motif appears in Hindu (Hiranyagarbha), Chinese (Pangu), Finnish (Kalevala), and Polynesian traditions.

Comparativists note several distinctive features of the Japanese account. Unlike the violent separation found in Greek (Ouranos/Gaia) or Mesopotamian (Marduk/Tiamat) cosmogonies, the Nihon Shoki presents a relatively peaceful differentiation. There is no cosmic battle, no defeated chaos monster. Order emerges organically — closer to the Chinese Daoist model than to the combat myths of the ancient Near East.

The spontaneous generation of kami from the primordial substance parallels the self-manifesting deities of Hindu cosmology (Brahma emerging from the cosmic lotus) and the self-created gods of Egyptian theology (Atum at Heliopolis). In each case, the first divine being represents the transition from potentiality to actuality.

Michael Witzel's "Laurasian" hypothesis places the Nihon Shoki's cosmogony within a macro-narrative pattern shared across Eurasia — from Iceland to Japan — featuring creation, divine generations, a heroic age, and eventual destruction.
