Volume 2 presents the Izanagi-Izanami cycle, which scholars recognize as drawing on multiple source traditions. The island-creation narrative — the divine pair stirring the ocean with a spear — shows parallels with Southeast Asian and Austronesian cosmogonic myths, suggesting deep pre-Yamato cultural layers.

Academic analysis identifies several strata in this chapter. The account of the failed first creation (the leech-child Hiruko, born when Izanami spoke first in the marriage ritual) likely reflects archaic ritual protocols about gendered speech in ceremony. The birth of deities representing natural features — seas, rivers, mountains, wind — follows a pattern of theogonic cataloguing common in ancient Near Eastern and Greek texts.

The Yomi (underworld) narrative closely parallels the Greek myth of Orpheus and Eurydice and the Mesopotamian Descent of Ishtar. Scholars debate whether these parallels reflect cultural diffusion along the Silk Road, common Indo-European heritage, or independent invention from universal psychological patterns. The taboo against looking — Izanagi's fatal glance at Izanami's decaying body — appears in myths worldwide.

The chapter's multiple *issho* variants for key episodes suggest that the compilers were working with competing regional traditions that could not simply be harmonized.
