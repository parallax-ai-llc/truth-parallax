## Source Analysis of Volume 1

Volume 1 of the Nihon Shoki presents the first part of the Age of the Gods narrative, opening with a cosmogonic account that scholars have traced to multiple source traditions. The famous opening — comparing the primordial state to an egg containing undifferentiated chaos — closely parallels passages in the Chinese text *Sanwu Liji* (Historical Records of the Three Sovereign Divinities and the Five Gods) attributed to Xu Zheng (3rd century CE).

Academic analysis reveals a deliberate editorial strategy: the compilers synthesized Japanese oral traditions with Chinese cosmological frameworks to produce a creation account that would be intelligible within the prestigious genre of Chinese dynastic history. The use of *kanbun* (classical Chinese) itself signals this aspiration.

A distinctive feature of Volume 1 is its systematic inclusion of variant traditions (*issho*, "alternate writings"), sometimes numbering six or more for a single episode. Scholars debate whether these variants reflect genuine diversity in oral tradition, different regional cult narratives, or deliberate editorial choices to accommodate competing clan interests at the Yamato court.

Tsuda Sōkichi's groundbreaking analysis argued that the mythological sections primarily served the political interests of the ruling class rather than preserving authentic ancient beliefs — a reading that remains influential though contested.
