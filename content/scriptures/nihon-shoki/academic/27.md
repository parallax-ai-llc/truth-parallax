## Critical Scholarship

Volume 27 of the Nihon Shoki addresses the first part of Empress Jito's reign, including her accession, continuation of Tenmu's reforms, and the establishment of the Fujiwara capital. Academic analysis of this volume employs philological, archaeological, and comparative methods to assess its historical reliability, identify its source materials, and understand its compositional strategies.

Scholars note that the compilers of 720 CE were working with multiple types of source material: earlier court records (*teiki*), clan genealogies (*kuji*), oral traditions, and — for the later volumes — increasingly reliable administrative documents. The challenge for modern scholarship is to distinguish these layers and evaluate each on its own terms.

Archaeological evidence provides an independent check on the text's claims. For the earlier volumes, the correlation between textual narrative and material evidence is often tenuous, while later volumes show increasing correspondence with inscriptional and documentary evidence from Japan, Korea, and China.

The political context of the text's compilation under the early Nara court is essential for understanding its editorial choices. The Nihon Shoki was not a disinterested historical record but a state-sponsored project designed to legitimize the ruling dynasty and establish Japan's place within the East Asian political order. This does not invalidate its historical content but requires critical evaluation of its claims.
