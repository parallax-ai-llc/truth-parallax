## Overview

Within Shinto tradition, the Nihon Shoki is read as a sacred chronicle revealing the origins and nature of the kami, the divine foundations of Japan, and the eternal bond between the imperial house and Amaterasu Ōmikami. Alongside the Kojiki, it forms the scriptural basis of Shinto theology and ritual practice.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Shrine Shinto
Shrines throughout Japan trace their enshrined kami and foundational legends to narratives in the Nihon Shoki. The text provides the mythological charter for shrine rituals (*matsuri*), seasonal festivals, and purification practices.

### Imperial Shinto
The Nihon Shoki's account of the heavenly descent (*tenson kōrin*) of Ninigi-no-Mikoto, grandson of Amaterasu, establishes the theological foundation for the imperial institution. The imperial regalia — mirror, sword, and jewel — derive their significance from these narratives.

### Kokugaku (National Learning)
Scholars such as **Motoori Norinaga** (1730–1801) championed a return to the ancient texts, seeking to recover an authentic Japanese spirituality unclouded by Chinese and Buddhist influences. Norinaga emphasized the emotional, aesthetic response (*mono no aware*) as central to understanding the kami narratives.

## Theological Emphases

- **Kami nature**: The kami are not omnipotent creators but awe-inspiring beings who participate in the unfolding of the world
- **Musubi**: The sacred power of creative becoming that drives all generation and growth
- **Kegare and harae**: Pollution and purification as fundamental spiritual categories
- **Kannagara**: Living in accordance with the way of the kami
