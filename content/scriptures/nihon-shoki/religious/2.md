Volume 2 contains the most sacred narrative in Shinto theology: the divine marriage of Izanagi-no-mikoto and Izanami-no-mikoto, their creation of the Japanese archipelago, and the momentous events that follow Izanami's death. This chapter is the mythological foundation for core Shinto practices and beliefs.

The creation of the islands through the union of the divine pair establishes the sacredness of the land itself. Japan is not merely geographical territory but the product of divine creative love. Every island, mountain, and waterway is the offspring of kami and therefore inherently sacred.

Izanami's death giving birth to Kagutsuchi, the fire kami, and Izanagi's grief-stricken descent to Yomi establish the Shinto understanding of death as a source of ritual pollution (*kegare*). Izanagi's purification in the river upon his return becomes the archetypal act of *misogi* (ritual purification), still practiced at shrines today. From this purification arise Amaterasu (from washing his left eye), Tsukuyomi (right eye), and Susanoo (nose) — the three most important kami.

The narrative teaches that life and death, purity and pollution, are inseparable aspects of existence, and that the proper response to the pollution of death is not avoidance but purification.
