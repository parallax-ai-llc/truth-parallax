## Shinto Religious Reading

Volume 27 of the Nihon Shoki narrates the first part of Empress Jito's reign, including her accession, continuation of Tenmu's reforms, and the establishment of the Fujiwara capital. Within Shinto tradition, this material carries sacred significance, revealing the ongoing activity of the kami in shaping the land, its people, and its institutions.

The events of this volume are commemorated in shrine traditions throughout Japan. The kami who appear and act within this narrative continue to receive worship at specific shrines, where their stories are retold in ritual contexts during annual festivals (*matsuri*). The connection between narrative and living worship distinguishes the Shinto reading from purely historical or literary approaches.

Central to the religious interpretation is the concept of *kannagara* — living in accordance with the way of the kami. The figures in this volume, whether divine beings or human sovereigns, are evaluated by their alignment with this sacred way. Those who act with sincerity (*makoto*), maintain ritual purity (*harae*), and honor the kami bring blessing; those who violate the sacred order bring calamity.

The Shinto reading also emphasizes the sacredness of the Japanese landscape itself. The places named in this volume — mountains, rivers, plains, and coastlines — are not mere geography but sacred sites where the kami's presence can still be encountered.
