## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Proverbs emphasizes the book's practical moral instruction grounded in the fear of the Lord. The Reformers valued Proverbs as a guide for daily Christian living, applicable to all vocations and stations of life.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Wisdom as Divine Gift
Protestant theology reads Proverbs' call to seek wisdom as pointing ultimately to Christ, "in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3). The personification of Wisdom in chapters 1-9 is often read christologically.

### Practical Application
The Reformation emphasis on the "priesthood of all believers" extended to wisdom literature: Proverbs speaks not only to scholars and clergy but to farmers, merchants, and parents. Luther frequently cited Proverbs in his *Table Talk*.

### Law and Gospel
Some Protestant interpreters distinguish between Proverbs' general observations about life (descriptive) and its moral imperatives (prescriptive), relating this to the law-gospel distinction.

## Theological Emphases

- **Fear of the Lord**: The foundational principle (1:7) is linked to justification by faith
- **Two Paths**: The righteous and the wicked as illustrations of the regenerate and unregenerate life
- **Speech Ethics**: The extensive teaching on the tongue informs Protestant pastoral care
- **Work Ethic**: The warnings against laziness shaped the Protestant work ethic
