## Wisdom's Feast and Folly's Snare

Proverbs 9 is part of the extended wisdom discourses of chapters 1-9, where a father instructs his son in the way of wisdom. Protestant interpretation emphasizes both the parental instruction setting and the theological content of these speeches.

## Key Themes

### Two Banquets

The chapter's treatment of two banquets illustrates the Protestant conviction that genuine two banquets is grounded in the fear of the Lord and expressed in practical obedience.

### Wisdom and Folly

The interplay between wisdom and folly in this chapter reflects the Protestant emphasis on the integration of faith and daily life. Calvin's Geneva was deeply shaped by this kind of proverbial instruction.

### The Path of Invitation

The imagery of the "path" or "way" recurs throughout these chapters, reinforcing the two-ways theology that structures the entire book.

## Interpretive Notes

Protestant commentators have noted several significant features of this chapter:

1. The fatherly tone reflects the household as the primary context for moral formation
2. The warnings serve as negative examples — learning from the consequences of folly
3. The promises attached to wisdom serve as motivation, though not as guarantees

| Theme | Application |
|-------|------------|
| Two Banquets | Foundation of moral character |
| Wisdom | Practical outworking of faith |
| Folly | Protection from moral failure |
| Invitation | The goal of wisdom's instruction |

## Theological Significance

These wisdom discourses establish that moral instruction is not legalism but loving guidance. The father's voice anticipates the Father God who instructs His children through His Word — a connection Protestant preachers have drawn consistently.
