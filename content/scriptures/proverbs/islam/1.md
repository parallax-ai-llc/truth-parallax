## Wisdom and the Fear of God in Islamic Perspective

Proverbs 1 opens with themes deeply resonant with Islamic theology: the pursuit of wisdom (*hikmah*), the fear of God (*taqwa*), and the duty of moral instruction. While Islam views the Book of Proverbs through the lens of *tahrif* (textual alteration), many of its ethical teachings find strong parallels in the Quran and Sunnah.

## Quranic Parallels

### "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge" (1:7)

This aligns with the Quranic concept of *taqwa* (God-consciousness), repeatedly identified as the foundation of wisdom and righteousness:

- **Quran 2:197**: "Take provisions, but indeed, the best provision is *taqwa*"
- **Quran 49:13**: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous (*atqakum*)"

### The Warning Against Evil Companions (1:10-19)

The Quran and hadith strongly warn against evil companionship:

- **Quran 25:28-29**: "Oh, woe to me! I wish I had not taken that one as a friend!"
- **Hadith (Abu Dawud)**: "A man follows the religion of his friend, so let each of you consider whom he befriends"

### Wisdom's Call (1:20-33)

Islam presents wisdom as a divine gift available to all: "He gives wisdom to whom He wills, and whoever has been given wisdom has certainly been given much good" (Quran 2:269).

| Proverbs 1 Theme | Quranic Parallel |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Fear of the Lord | *Taqwa* (God-consciousness) |
| Avoiding evil companions | Quran 25:28-29 |
| Wisdom's accessibility | Quran 2:269 |
| Parental instruction | Quran 31:13-19 (Luqman) |

## Theological Significance

Proverbs 1's ethical framework is broadly compatible with Islamic moral theology, though Islam grounds these teachings in the Quran as the final, uncorrupted revelation rather than in the biblical text.
