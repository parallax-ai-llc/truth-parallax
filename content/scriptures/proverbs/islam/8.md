## Wisdom and Divine Sovereignty

Proverbs 8's personification of Wisdom as a figure present at creation raises significant theological concerns from an Islamic perspective, particularly regarding *tawhid* (the absolute oneness of God).

## Islamic Theological Assessment

### The Personification of Wisdom

Islam does not personify divine attributes as independent entities. God's wisdom (*hikmah*) is an attribute of His essence (*dhat*), not a separate being. The portrayal of Wisdom as a figure who "was beside him" during creation (8:30) risks compromising *tawhid*.

### "The Lord possessed me at the beginning" (8:22)

Muslim scholars are aware that Christians identify this Wisdom with Christ. Islamic theology categorically rejects any intermediary in creation: "He is Allah, the One. Allah, the Eternally Self-Sufficient. He neither begets nor is begotten" (Quran 112:1-3).

### Wisdom in Islamic Theology

The Quran affirms God's wisdom without personifying it:

- **Quran 2:269**: "He gives wisdom to whom He wills"
- **Quran 31:12**: "And We gave Luqman wisdom"
- **Quran 6:83**: "That is Our argument which We gave Abraham against his people"

| Proverbs 8 Concept | Islamic Response |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| Wisdom as person | Rejected — attributes are not independent entities |
| Wisdom before creation | God's knowledge is eternal, not a separate being |
| "I was his delight" | God is self-sufficient (*al-Samad*) |
| Wisdom's role in creation | God creates directly by His command: "Be, and it is" (*kun fayakun*) |

## Theological Significance

Proverbs 8 illustrates a fundamental divergence between biblical and Quranic theology. While both affirm divine wisdom, the Quran insists on God's absolute unity and self-sufficiency, without intermediaries or personified attributes.
