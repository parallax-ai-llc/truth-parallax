## The Woman of Valor and Catholic Tradition

Proverbs 31 has deep roots in Catholic devotion and theology, particularly in its portrait of the *Eshet Chayil* (Woman of Valor), which Catholic tradition has applied to both the Blessed Virgin Mary and to the vocation of Christian women.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Queen Mother's Oracle (31:1-9)

The instruction of King Lemuel's mother resonates with the Catholic theology of maternal influence. The Church honors mothers as the first teachers of faith in the domestic church (*ecclesia domestica*).

### The Woman of Valor (31:10-31)

Catholic interpretation has read this passage on multiple levels:

- **Marian**: The Woman of Valor as a type of the Blessed Virgin Mary
- **Ecclesial**: The industrious woman as a figure of the Church
- **Moral**: A model for the virtue of prudence (*prudentia*)
- **Domestic**: The dignity of work within the home

### "Charm is deceitful, and beauty is vain" (31:30)

This verse supports the Catholic emphasis on interior virtue over external appearance, while the sacramental tradition affirms that outward signs can bear inward grace.

| Reading | Application |
|---------|------------|
| Marian | Mary as the perfect exemplar of faithful womanhood |
| Moral | The cardinal virtue of prudence exemplified |
| Social | The dignity of women's work and contribution |
| Eschatological | The woman's "fear of the Lord" as the path to eternal reward |

## Theological Significance

Proverbs 31 brings the book full circle: the fear of the Lord (1:7) is embodied in a life of wisdom, industry, generosity, and worship. Catholic teaching finds here a model of sanctification through daily life.
