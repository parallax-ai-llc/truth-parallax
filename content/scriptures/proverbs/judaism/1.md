## Reshit Hokhmah: The Beginning of Wisdom

In Jewish tradition, Proverbs 1 establishes the foundational principle of all Torah study and ethical life: *Yirat Hashem reshit da'at* — "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge" (1:7). Rashi identifies "wisdom" throughout Proverbs with Torah, so that the entire book becomes a meditation on the life of Torah observance.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge" (1:7)

The Talmud (Shabbat 31b) teaches that when a person is brought before the heavenly court, the first question asked is not about Torah study but about ethical conduct — yet Yirat Shamayim (fear of Heaven) is the treasure-house that contains all other virtues.

Rashi interprets *reshit* (beginning) not merely as a starting point but as the *ikkar* (essential foundation). Without fear of God, all learning is empty.

### Lady Wisdom's Call (1:20-33)

The Vilna Gaon reads Wisdom's public call as the Torah's summons to repentance (*teshuvah*). "How long, O simple ones, will you love being simple?" (1:22) becomes a call to return to God and His commandments.

### "Hear, my son, your father's instruction" (1:8)

The Talmud (Kiddushin 29a) derives from this verse the father's obligation to teach his son Torah. The "mother's teaching" (*torat imekha*) is understood as referring to rabbinic tradition (*derabbanan*).

| Commentator | Reading |
|------------|---------|
| Rashi | Wisdom = Torah throughout |
| Vilna Gaon | Deep mystical-ethical layers |
| Malbim | Systematic distinction between wisdom, understanding, and knowledge |

## Theological Significance

Proverbs 1 frames the entire book as a Torah text — not merely secular prudence but divine instruction for living a life of holiness, justice, and reverence before God.
