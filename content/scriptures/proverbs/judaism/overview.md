## Overview

In Judaism, Proverbs (*Mishlei*) belongs to the Ketuvim (Writings) section of the Tanakh. It is attributed to King Solomon and is deeply embedded in Jewish ethical life. The *mussar* (moral instruction) tradition draws heavily on Proverbs, and the final chapter's *Eshet Chayil* ("Woman of Valor") is recited in Jewish homes every Friday evening before the Shabbat meal.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
Rashi's commentary treats Proverbs primarily as ethical instruction, though he frequently identifies allegorical dimensions where wisdom represents Torah.

### Mussar (Moral Instruction)
The medieval and modern *mussar* movement — from Bahya ibn Paquda's *Duties of the Heart* to the Lithuanian *mussar* yeshivot — found in Proverbs its primary biblical sourcebook for character development.

### Kabbalistic
The Zohar and kabbalistic tradition read Proverbs' Wisdom as an aspect of the divine *sefirot*, particularly *Hokhmah* (Wisdom) and *Binah* (Understanding).

## Theological Emphases

- **Torah as Wisdom**: Proverbs' "wisdom" is identified with Torah study and observance
- **Yirat Hashem**: The "fear of the Lord" as the foundation of Jewish piety
- **Eshet Chayil**: The idealized woman as an allegory for Torah, Shabbat, or the soul
- **Tikkun Middot**: The refinement of character traits

## Liturgical Context

Proverbs features in Jewish liturgical and domestic life:

- **Eshet Chayil** (31:10-31): Recited Friday evenings
- **Proverbs 3:18**: "She is a tree of life" — sung when the Torah scroll is returned to the ark
- **Proverbs 4:2**: "For I give you good doctrine" — associated with Torah study blessings
