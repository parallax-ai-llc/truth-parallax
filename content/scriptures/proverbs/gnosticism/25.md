## Hezekiah's Collection I

Gnostic interpreters read Proverbs 25 on two levels: the surface moral teaching attributed to the Demiurge's order, and a deeper esoteric meaning pointing toward the liberation of the divine spark through gnosis.

## Key Themes in Gnostic Reading

### Kings

The Gnostic reading of kings in this chapter inverts the conventional interpretation. What orthodox readers see as moral instruction, Gnostic readers see as the Demiurge's program of control — or, alternatively, as containing hidden traces of Sophia's wisdom embedded within the text.

### Self-Control and Speech

The interplay between self-control and speech reflects, in Gnostic thought, the ongoing cosmic tension between the Pleroma's light and the Demiurge's darkness. Every moral contrast in Proverbs mirrors the fundamental Gnostic dualism.

### Enemies

The theme of enemies is reinterpreted through the lens of pneumatic (spiritual) versus hylic (material) existence. True enemies comes through gnosis, not through the Demiurge's moral law.

## Gnostic Hermeneutics

| Level | Reading |
|-------|---------|
| Hylic (material) | Surface moral instruction |
| Psychic (soul) | Ethical improvement under the Demiurge |
| Pneumatic (spiritual) | Hidden Sophia teaching leading to gnosis |

## Nag Hammadi Connections

The Nag Hammadi library contains multiple texts that engage with wisdom themes: the *Gospel of Thomas*, the *Gospel of Philip*, and the *Exegesis on the Soul* all reflect the Gnostic reinterpretation of biblical wisdom as pointing toward liberation from the material cosmos.

## Theological Significance

For Gnostic readers, Proverbs' wisdom is a palimpsest: beneath the Demiurge's moral program lies Sophia's call to awakening. The discerning reader — the pneumatic — can hear her voice through the text.
