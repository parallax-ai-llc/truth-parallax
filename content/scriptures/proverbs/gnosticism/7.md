## The Wiles of the Adulteress

Gnostic interpreters read Proverbs 7 on two levels: the surface moral teaching attributed to the Demiurge's order, and a deeper esoteric meaning pointing toward the liberation of the divine spark through gnosis.

## Key Themes in Gnostic Reading

### Temptation

The Gnostic reading of temptation in this chapter inverts the conventional interpretation. What orthodox readers see as moral instruction, Gnostic readers see as the Demiurge's program of control — or, alternatively, as containing hidden traces of Sophia's wisdom embedded within the text.

### Adultery and Seduction

The interplay between adultery and seduction reflects, in Gnostic thought, the ongoing cosmic tension between the Pleroma's light and the Demiurge's darkness. Every moral contrast in Proverbs mirrors the fundamental Gnostic dualism.

### Death

The theme of death is reinterpreted through the lens of pneumatic (spiritual) versus hylic (material) existence. True death comes through gnosis, not through the Demiurge's moral law.

## Gnostic Hermeneutics

| Level | Reading |
|-------|---------|
| Hylic (material) | Surface moral instruction |
| Psychic (soul) | Ethical improvement under the Demiurge |
| Pneumatic (spiritual) | Hidden Sophia teaching leading to gnosis |

## Nag Hammadi Connections

The Nag Hammadi library contains multiple texts that engage with wisdom themes: the *Gospel of Thomas*, the *Gospel of Philip*, and the *Exegesis on the Soul* all reflect the Gnostic reinterpretation of biblical wisdom as pointing toward liberation from the material cosmos.

## Theological Significance

For Gnostic readers, Proverbs' wisdom is a palimpsest: beneath the Demiurge's moral program lies Sophia's call to awakening. The discerning reader — the pneumatic — can hear her voice through the text.
