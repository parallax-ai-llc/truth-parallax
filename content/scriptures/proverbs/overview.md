## Overview

Proverbs (*Mishlei* in Hebrew, meaning "parables" or "similitudes") is one of the central wisdom books of the Hebrew Bible. Traditionally attributed to King Solomon, the book is a collection of moral and practical instructions covering topics from personal ethics to governance, from domestic life to the cosmic order of wisdom.

## Structure

The book divides into several distinct sections:

1. **Chapters 1–9**: Extended discourses on wisdom, including the personification of Lady Wisdom
2. **Chapters 10–22:16**: The "Proverbs of Solomon" — short, two-line sayings
3. **Chapters 22:17–24:34**: "Words of the Wise" — influenced by Egyptian wisdom literature
4. **Chapters 25–29**: More Solomonic proverbs collected by Hezekiah's scribes
5. **Chapter 30**: The words of Agur son of Jakeh
6. **Chapter 31**: The words of King Lemuel and the "Woman of Valor" (*Eshet Chayil*)

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Proverbs occupies a distinctive place in multiple religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: Part of the Ketuvim (Writings); the *Eshet Chayil* poem is recited every Shabbat evening
- **Christianity**: A source for moral theology and catechesis across all branches
- **Islam**: Solomon (*Sulayman*) is a prophet; his wisdom is honored though the biblical text is viewed through the lens of *tahrif*
- **Gnosticism**: Lady Wisdom (Sophia) became a central figure in Gnostic cosmology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Fear of the Lord | The foundational principle of all wisdom (1:7, 9:10) |
| Wisdom vs. Folly | Two paths personified as women calling out to the simple |
| Righteousness | Moral conduct as the path to life and blessing |
| Speech | The power of the tongue for good or ill |
| Discipline | The role of correction and instruction in character formation |
