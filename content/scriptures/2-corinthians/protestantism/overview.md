## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 2 Corinthians has been deeply shaped by the Reformation's emphasis on the theology of the cross. Paul's paradox that divine "power is made perfect in weakness" (12:9) became a cornerstone for Luther's *theologia crucis*, contrasting God's hidden work through suffering with a triumphalistic "theology of glory."

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Theology of the Cross
Luther and his heirs read 2 Corinthians as the definitive statement that God works through suffering, not worldly power. Paul's "thorn in the flesh" (12:7) exemplifies the believer's dependence on grace.

### New Covenant Ministry
Chapter 3's contrast between the "ministry of death" (the old covenant written on stone) and the "ministry of the Spirit" (the new covenant written on hearts) is foundational for Protestant covenant theology. Calvin saw this as demonstrating the superiority of the gospel without denigrating the Old Testament.

### The Collection and Stewardship
Chapters 8–9 have shaped Protestant theology of giving: generosity is rooted in grace (8:9), voluntary rather than compelled (9:7), and modeled on Christ's self-emptying.
