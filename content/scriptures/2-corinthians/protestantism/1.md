## Comfort in Affliction

Paul opens 2 Corinthians with a profound theology of comfort: "Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of mercies and God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction" (1:3-4). Protestant interpretation reads this as establishing the paradigm of the entire letter: suffering is not meaningless but becomes the vehicle through which God's comfort flows to others.

The "affliction in Asia" (1:8-9) — possibly a life-threatening illness, imprisonment, or mob violence — drove Paul to rely entirely on "the God who raises the dead" (1:9). Protestant theology sees this as a model for the Christian life: extremity leads to dependence on God alone, the essence of *sola gratia*.

Paul's discussion of his travel plans (1:15-24) addresses charges of fickleness. He insists that his "yes" is grounded in the faithfulness of God, who in Christ is always "Yes" (1:19-20). Calvin saw this as demonstrating that pastoral flexibility is not inconsistency but responsiveness to the Spirit's leading.
