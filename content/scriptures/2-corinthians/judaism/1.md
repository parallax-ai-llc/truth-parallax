## Comfort and Affliction

Jewish engagement with 2 Corinthians 1 recognizes Paul's opening blessing as deeply rooted in Jewish liturgical tradition. The *berakah* formula — "Blessed be the God and Father" (1:3) — echoes the structure of Jewish benedictions and the tradition of blessing God in all circumstances, including suffering.

The theology of comfort (*paraklesis*) draws on the Hebrew Bible's rich vocabulary of divine consolation, particularly Isaiah 40:1 — "Comfort, comfort my people, says your God." Paul's adaptation of this prophetic tradition to describe his own experience reflects the Second Temple Jewish practice of reading Scripture as personally applicable.

Paul's language of "affliction" (*thlipsis*) in 1:4-8 resonates with Jewish traditions about the "birthpangs of the Messiah" — the idea that suffering precedes redemption. Jewish scholars note that while Paul applies this framework christologically, the underlying structure is deeply Jewish, reflecting the theology of suffering that would later find expression in rabbinic midrash on the righteous who suffer.
