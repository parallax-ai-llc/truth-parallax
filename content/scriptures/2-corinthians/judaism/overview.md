## Overview

Jewish engagement with 2 Corinthians centers primarily on chapter 3, where Paul contrasts the "old covenant" and the "new covenant," the "letter" and the "spirit," and interprets the veil over Moses' face (Exodus 34:29-35). This chapter has been one of the most consequential — and most contested — texts in Jewish-Christian relations.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### The Veil of Moses (Chapter 3)
Paul's claim that "a veil lies over" the minds of Israelites when they read Moses (3:15) has been critiqued by Jewish scholars as a supersessionist misreading of Exodus 34. Jewish interpretation holds that the radiance of Moses' face demonstrated the glory of Torah, not its inadequacy.

### Letter and Spirit
The contrast of "letter" (gramma) and "spirit" (pneuma) in 3:6 has historically been used to characterize Judaism as legalistic. Modern Jewish scholars counter that this dichotomy misrepresents rabbinic Judaism, which has always emphasized the spirit and intention (*kavvanah*) behind commandments.

### Paul's Jewish Identity
Paul's autobiographical passages reveal a complex relationship with his Jewish heritage. His suffering catalogues (11:23-28) and his claim to be "a Hebrew of Hebrews" reflect an identity shaped by, yet departing from, Second Temple Judaism.

## Scholarly Engagement

Jewish scholars such as Daniel Boyarin and Mark Nanos have offered readings of 2 Corinthians 3 that situate Paul within intra-Jewish debates rather than as an opponent of Judaism per se.
