## Divine Comfort in Trials

The opening of 2 Corinthians, with its emphasis on God as "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort" (1:3), resonates with the Quranic emphasis on God's mercy (*rahmah*). The names *al-Rahman* (the Most Merciful) and *al-Rahim* (the Especially Merciful) open every surah of the Quran except one, establishing divine mercy as the foundation of all things.

Paul's account of severe affliction in Asia (1:8-9) and his trust in "the God who raises the dead" parallels the Quranic accounts of prophets who endured extreme trials and placed their trust (*tawakkul*) in God. Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son, Ayyub's (Job's) patience in suffering, and Yunus's (Jonah's) cry from the belly of the fish all exemplify this pattern.

The teaching that comfort received from God flows to others (1:4) aligns with the Islamic understanding that mercy is not merely received but must be extended: "The merciful are shown mercy by the Most Merciful. Be merciful on earth, and you will be shown mercy from above" (Hadith, Abu Dawud).
