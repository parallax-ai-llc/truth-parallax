The Protestant reading of the Bundahishn discovers in Zoroastrian cosmogony a tradition that illuminates the ancient Near Eastern context of biblical creation and eschatology. The concept of creation as a purposeful act of a good God, undertaken in the context of a cosmic struggle against evil forces, resonates with the biblical narrative of creation, fall, and redemption — though the Zoroastrian framework distributes these themes differently than the Christian one.

Protestant scholars since the 19th century have recognized the Bundahishn's importance for understanding the development of concepts central to Christian faith: the cosmic battle between good and evil, bodily resurrection, final judgment, and the renovation of creation. The Bundahishn provides evidence that these concepts, while given definitive expression in the Christian Bible, have roots in a broader Near Eastern theological conversation in which Zoroastrian thought played a significant role.
