The Jewish reading of the Bundahishn engages with Zoroastrian cosmogony as a crucial context for understanding developments in Jewish thought during the Persian and Hellenistic periods. The Babylonian Exile and subsequent Persian period (586–332 BCE) brought Jews into sustained contact with Zoroastrian ideas, and many scholars have traced the development of Jewish concepts of angelology, demonology, resurrection, and cosmic eschatology to this encounter.

The Bundahishn, though compiled centuries after the Persian period, preserves cosmogonic and eschatological traditions that may reflect earlier teachings contemporaneous with the Jewish-Persian encounter. Jewish scholars examine the parallels between the Bundahishn's creation narrative and the Genesis account, between Zoroastrian angelology and the Jewish angelic hierarchies that appear in post-exilic literature, and between the *frashokereti* and Jewish messianic expectations.
