Daoist interpretation of the mourning chapter engages with one of the most provocative contrasts between the two traditions. Zhuangzi's drumming and singing after his wife's death stands in sharp opposition to the Xiaojing's elaborate mourning prescriptions. For Zhuangzi, death is a natural transformation, and grief that clings to the departed fails to understand the Way's ceaseless movement. The three-year mourning period, from this perspective, prolongs attachment rather than facilitating release.

Yet Daoist readers acknowledge that not all practitioners have attained Zhuangzi's equanimity, and that mourning rituals may serve those still bound by conventional attachments.
