## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Classic of Filial Piety reflects a characteristic tension between appreciation for natural family affection and suspicion of codified moral obligations. While Daoist thinkers value the spontaneous love between parent and child, they question whether prescribing and regulating filial conduct transforms a natural sentiment into an artificial performance.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist readers highlight the tension between the text's insistence on prescribed forms of filial behavior and the Daoist ideal of wu wei (non-action). The Zhuangzi's own treatment of mourning — where Zhuangzi drums and sings after his wife's death — represents a radical departure from the elaborate mourning prescriptions of the Xiaojing. Yet Daoist interpreters acknowledge that the text's underlying impulse, the recognition that human beings are embedded in relationships of care and obligation, resonates with the Daoist understanding of the natural Way.

## Points of Convergence and Divergence

While Daoists resist the hierarchical and ritualized framework of the Xiaojing, they share its conviction that authentic moral life must be rooted in genuine feeling rather than external coercion. The Daoist reading of the Classic ultimately challenges Confucian interpreters to distinguish between performative obedience and heartfelt devotion.
