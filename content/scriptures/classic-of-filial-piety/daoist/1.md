Daoist readers approach the opening chapter's elevation of filial piety with characteristic ambivalence. While recognizing the natural love between parent and child as reflecting the spontaneous generosity of the Dao, Daoist interpretation questions whether codifying this love into a moral principle enhances or diminishes it. The Laozi's teaching that "when the great Way is abandoned, benevolence and righteousness appear" suggests that the very need to articulate filial piety as a virtue indicates its natural expression has already been lost.

Yet the passage's emphasis on the body as a gift from one's parents finds resonance with the Daoist concern for bodily cultivation and longevity. The injunction to preserve one's body from harm aligns with Daoist health practices and the principle of nurturing life (yangsheng).
