The five punishments chapter draws sharp Daoist criticism for its reliance on punitive measures. The Laozi's teaching that "the more laws and prohibitions there are, the poorer the people become" suggests that harsh punishments for unfilial behavior indicate a society that has already lost touch with the natural Way. In a well-ordered Daoist society, filial love would not need to be enforced because it would flow naturally from the parent-child bond.

The chapter represents, for Daoist readers, the failure of the Confucian system to trust in the inherent goodness of human nature as it manifests in family affection.
