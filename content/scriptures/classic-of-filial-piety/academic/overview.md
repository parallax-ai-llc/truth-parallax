## Overview

Academic scholarship on the Classic of Filial Piety addresses questions of authorship, dating, textual history, and ideological function. The text exists in two major recensions — the "modern script" (jinwen) version of 18 chapters and the "old script" (guwen) version of 22 chapters — and scholars have debated their relative antiquity and authenticity since the Han dynasty.

## Textual Criticism

Most scholars date the Classic of Filial Piety to the early Han dynasty (3rd-2nd century BCE), though it may incorporate older material. The attribution to Confucius is considered traditional rather than historical. The textual history of the two recensions and the debates between jinwen and guwen schools provide important evidence for understanding the politics of classical learning in imperial China.

## Historical Context

Academic analysis situates the Xiaojing within the broader Han dynasty project of establishing Confucianism as state ideology. The text's emphasis on filial piety as the basis of political loyalty served the interests of centralized imperial authority. Scholars such as Keith Knapp and Alan Cole have examined how the text shaped family structures, gender relations, and political culture across East Asian history.
