Academic analysis of the governance chapter examines the practical implications of the text's political theory. Scholars note that the vision of governance through moral example rather than legal coercion represents a Confucian ideal that was repeatedly invoked but rarely achieved in practice. Historical studies trace the gap between the Xiaojing's idealized portrait of filial governance and the actual practices of Chinese imperial administration.

The chapter's political theory has been compared with Plato's philosopher-king ideal and with Aristotelian notions of the virtuous ruler, revealing both parallels and significant differences.
