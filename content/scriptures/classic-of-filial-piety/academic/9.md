Scholarly treatment of the sages' chapter examines the idealized portrait of sage-kingship and its function within Confucian political mythology. Academic historians note that the sages described in the Xiaojing — Yao, Shun, Yu, and the founders of the Zhou dynasty — serve as normative models whose historicity is less important than their ideological function. The chapter's integration of music, ritual, and moral governance reflects a specifically Han synthesis that earlier Confucian texts do not fully articulate.

Musicological studies examine the chapter's claims about music's moral power in light of cross-cultural evidence for the social functions of music in ancient civilizations.
