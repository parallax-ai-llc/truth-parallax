## Overview

The Classic of Filial Piety has been a focal point in the centuries-long dialogue between Buddhism and Confucianism in China. Chinese Buddhist engagement with the text reflects the tradition's need to address the Confucian charge that monasticism, by requiring disciples to leave their families, violated the fundamental principle of filial piety.

## Interpretive Approach

Chinese Buddhist apologists developed sophisticated responses to the filial piety critique. They argued that monastic practice represents a higher form of filial devotion: by seeking liberation for all sentient beings, the monk or nun serves not only their immediate parents but all beings who have been their parents across countless lifetimes. The Ullambana Sutra, which teaches Maudgalyayana's rescue of his mother from the realm of hungry ghosts, became a key text for demonstrating Buddhism's compatibility with filial values.

## Syncretic Legacy

The Buddhist engagement with the Xiaojing contributed to the development of a distinctively Chinese Buddhist ethic that honored family bonds while maintaining the primacy of the universal compassion ideal. This synthesis remains influential in East Asian Buddhist communities today.
