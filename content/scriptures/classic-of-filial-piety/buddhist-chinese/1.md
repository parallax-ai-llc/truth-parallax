Chinese Buddhist interpretation of the opening chapter engages directly with the text's claim that filial piety is the root of all virtue. Buddhist apologists responded to this challenge by arguing that the bodhisattva's compassion for all sentient beings — all of whom have been one's parents in past lives — represents a deeper and more universal form of filial devotion. The Buddhist understanding of xiao thus transcends the biological family while honoring its foundational importance.

The chapter's injunction to preserve the body received from one's parents raised particular difficulties for Buddhist monastics, whose tonsure and celibacy were criticized as violations of filial duty. Buddhist responses included the argument that preserving the dharma body is more important than preserving the physical body.
