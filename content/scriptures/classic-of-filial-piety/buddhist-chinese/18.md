Chinese Buddhist engagement with the mourning chapter represents one of the most sensitive points of contact between the two traditions. Buddhist funeral practices, including chanting sutras for the deceased, performing merit dedication ceremonies, and observing the Ullambana festival, developed in China partly in response to Confucian mourning culture. While Buddhism does not prescribe a three-year mourning period, it developed elaborate ritual traditions for honoring the dead that complemented and sometimes replaced Confucian observances.

The chapter illustrates the creative synthesis through which Chinese Buddhism adapted to indigenous cultural practices while maintaining its distinctive soteriological orientation.
