Confucian interpretation of the opening chapter treats Confucius's statement that filial piety is "the root of virtue and the source of all teaching" as the foundational axiom of the entire moral system. This chapter establishes the Confucian conviction that ethics begins not with abstract principles but with the concrete relationship between parent and child. The body, hair, and skin received from one's parents must be preserved from harm — a teaching that grounds moral responsibility in gratitude and embodied care.

For Confucian readers, this chapter's elevation of xiao above all other virtues reflects the insight that moral character is formed first in the family. The person who learns reverence, obedience, and care within the household will naturally extend these dispositions to broader social relationships. Filial piety is thus not merely one virtue among many but the generative root from which all other virtues grow.
