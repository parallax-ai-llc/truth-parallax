The chapter on the Son of Heaven's filial piety teaches that the emperor's devotion to his parents sets the moral tone for the entire empire. Confucian interpreters emphasize that the ruler's personal virtue is not a private matter but a public responsibility: when the Son of Heaven models filial devotion, the transformation of society follows naturally. This reflects the Confucian principle that governance by moral example (de) is superior to governance by force or law.

The teaching that the emperor's filial piety causes "the people of the four seas" to respond with reverence connects personal virtue to political harmony through the mechanism of moral influence (ganying). Confucian political thought rests on this conviction that authentic virtue radiates outward, transforming society without coercion.
