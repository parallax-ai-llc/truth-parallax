## Overview

Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Classic of Filial Piety, shaped by Zhu Xi and the Song dynasty revival, reads the text through a metaphysical lens. Filial piety is understood not merely as a social obligation but as an expression of heavenly principle (tianli) inherent in human nature, connecting the domestic virtue of xiao to the cosmic order itself.

## Interpretive Approach

Zhu Xi and other Neo-Confucians incorporated the Xiaojing into their broader philosophical system, interpreting filial devotion as the natural expression of the principle of humaneness (ren) as it manifests within the family. The text's claim that filial piety moves heaven and earth is read literally as reflecting the ontological unity of moral principle across all levels of reality.

## Scholarly Legacy

The Neo-Confucian reading of the Classic of Filial Piety shaped education and governance across East Asia for centuries, informing both the imperial examination system and the daily moral instruction of children. Its integration of filial piety into a comprehensive metaphysical framework gave the text enduring philosophical significance beyond its original social context.
