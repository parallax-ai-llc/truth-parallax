Comparative study of the practice chapter examines mourning and ancestral veneration across traditions. The Jewish traditions of shiva and kaddish, the Christian Requiem Mass, the Hindu shraddha ceremonies, and the Buddhist merit dedication practices all address the obligations of the living to the dead. The Confucian three-year mourning period is distinctive in its duration and intensity, reflecting the Confucian conviction that the parent-child bond is the most important human relationship.

This chapter raises comparative questions about how traditions negotiate the boundary between life and death and about the moral obligations that survive death.
