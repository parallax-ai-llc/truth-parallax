Comparative study of the amplification chapter examines the logic of moral extension across traditions. The Buddhist practice of extending loving-kindness from self to all beings, the Christian command to love one's neighbor, and the Mohist argument for universal love all propose methods for broadening moral concern beyond the family. The Confucian strategy of grounding universal benevolence in particular family relationships distinguishes it from traditions that advocate impartial concern, raising the question of whether particularist or universalist approaches provide a sounder foundation for ethics.

This chapter illuminates one of the most fundamental debates in comparative ethics.
