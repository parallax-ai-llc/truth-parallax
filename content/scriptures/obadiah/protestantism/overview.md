## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Obadiah, though dealing with the shortest book in the Old Testament, draws significant theological principles from its compact oracle. The Reformers and their successors read Obadiah as a case study in divine justice — God's judgment on Edom for pride and betrayal of kinship obligations — with broader applications to all who oppose God's people and purposes.

## Reformation Reading

**Calvin** treated Obadiah as a demonstration that God's justice operates among all nations, not only within the covenant community. Edom's sin was not merely political treachery but theological arrogance — the presumption that their mountain fortresses made them invulnerable to divine judgment. **Luther** drew a pastoral application: as Edom's pride brought destruction, so spiritual pride is the most dangerous sin, and believers must trust in God's justice rather than seeking vengeance themselves.

## Theological Emphases

Protestant commentators emphasize the concluding verse — "the kingdom shall be the LORD's" (v. 21) — as an eschatological declaration pointing toward the universal reign of Christ. Dispensationalist interpreters connect Obadiah to end-times prophecy concerning the nations surrounding Israel. Reformed theologians read it within the broader biblical theology of God's faithfulness to his covenant people through judgment on their oppressors. The brother-against-brother dynamic (Esau/Edom vs. Jacob/Israel) illustrates the theme of election and the consequences of rejecting covenant relationship.
