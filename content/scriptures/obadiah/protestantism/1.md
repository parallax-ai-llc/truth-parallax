Obadiah 1 delivers the shortest prophetic book in the Old Testament — a single chapter of twenty-one verses devoted entirely to the oracle against Edom. Protestant interpreters read the book as a case study in divine justice: Edom's pride, treachery against a brother nation, and exploitation of Judah's catastrophe bring certain judgment. Calvin emphasized that Edom's mountain fortress (likely Petra) represents the false security of those who trust in natural advantages rather than divine grace.

The catalogue of Edom's sins (vv. 10-14) follows a devastating progression: standing aloof while Jerusalem was sacked, gloating over the disaster, entering the gates to loot, cutting off fugitives, and handing survivors over to the enemy. Protestant moral theology draws from this a principle of graduated culpability — passive observation of injustice, active enjoyment of another's suffering, and direct participation in oppression represent escalating degrees of guilt, all of which invite divine retribution.

The principle "as you have done, it shall be done to you" (v. 15) articulates the lex talionis at a national level — proportional divine justice that mirrors the offender's own actions back upon them. The concluding declaration — "the kingdom shall be the LORD's" (v. 21) — transforms a localized oracle against Edom into a universal eschatological promise. Protestant theology reads this as pointing toward the ultimate establishment of Christ's kingdom, when all earthly powers that oppose God's purposes will be brought to judgment.
