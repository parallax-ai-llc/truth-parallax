## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Obadiah draws on the patristic tradition to read this brief oracle as a lens on divine justice, the sin of indifference to the suffering of others, and the ultimate establishment of God's kingdom. Despite its brevity, the book raises themes central to Catholic moral theology: the obligation of solidarity, the sinfulness of exploiting the vulnerable, and the certainty of divine judgment.

## Patristic Reading

**Jerome** produced the earliest Latin commentary on Obadiah, interpreting Edom allegorically as the worldly, flesh-oriented person in contrast to Jacob/Israel as the spiritual person. This spiritual reading was influential throughout the medieval period. **Augustine** drew on Obadiah's concluding vision of God's kingdom in his *City of God*, seeing it as a prophecy of the Church's ultimate triumph over all earthly powers that oppose divine justice.

## Catholic Social and Moral Teaching

Obadiah's catalogue of Edom's sins (vv. 10-14) reads as a moral indictment of indifference and complicity: standing by while others suffer, rejoicing in a neighbor's downfall, looting the dispossessed, and collaborating with oppressors. Catholic social teaching identifies these same sins in modern contexts — the failure of solidarity, the exploitation of refugees, and the complicity of bystanders in injustice. The principle "as you have done, it shall be done to you" (v. 15) expresses the Catholic understanding that divine justice is proportional and inescapable.
