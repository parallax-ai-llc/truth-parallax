Obadiah 1 is a compact oracle against Edom that Catholic tradition reads at multiple levels: historical, moral, and eschatological. Jerome's commentary interpreted Edom allegorically as representing the "carnal" or worldly person, in contrast to Jacob/Israel as the "spiritual" person. This typological reading allowed the Church Fathers to extract universal moral principles from what might otherwise seem a localized historical oracle.

The sins catalogued in verses 10-14 speak directly to Catholic social teaching on solidarity and the obligation to assist the vulnerable. Edom's crime was not only active aggression but the failure of fraternal obligation — standing by while a brother nation suffered, and then profiting from the disaster. The Catechism's treatment of the sin of omission (CCC 1853) finds prophetic support here: the failure to act in the face of injustice is itself a sin that calls for divine judgment.

The eschatological conclusion (vv. 15-21) moves from the particular judgment on Edom to the universal Day of the LORD. The declaration that "the kingdom shall be the LORD's" (v. 21) resonates with the Catholic understanding of the Kingdom of God as both already present in the Church and not yet fully realized. Augustine's *City of God* drew on Obadiah's vision of divine sovereignty triumphing over earthly empires, and this theology of the two cities — the city of God and the city of man — continues to shape Catholic political theology.
