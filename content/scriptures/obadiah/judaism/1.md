Obadiah 1, the shortest book in the Hebrew Bible, holds an outsized place in Jewish tradition. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 39b) identifies the prophet Obadiah as an Edomite convert, making him the ideal messenger to pronounce judgment on Edom — one who knew the nation's pride from within. This identification transforms the book into a witness from the inside, giving the oracle particular moral authority.

The most significant development in Jewish interpretation is the identification of Edom with Rome, and subsequently with Christendom. This reading, established in the Talmudic period, transforms Obadiah from a localized oracle into a prophetic commentary on Jewish experience under gentile domination. Rashi reads the entire book through this lens: Edom's mountain fortress is Rome's imperial power, and the promise of judgment applies to all empires that oppress Israel. This identification gave Jewish communities under Christian rule a theological framework for understanding and enduring their suffering, sustained by the hope of eventual vindication.

The concluding verse — "saviors shall go up to Mount Zion to judge the mountain of Esau, and the kingdom shall be the LORD's" (v. 21) — is a foundational messianic text. The "saviors" (*moshi'im*) are messianic figures or righteous leaders of the end times. The Targum Jonathan renders this passage with explicit messianic language, and the Aleinu prayer, recited at the conclusion of every Jewish worship service, echoes Obadiah's vision: "And the LORD will be king over all the earth; on that day the LORD will be one and his name one." This transforms Obadiah's compact oracle into a statement of ultimate Jewish eschatological hope.
