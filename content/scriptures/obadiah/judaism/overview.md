## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Obadiah (*Ovadyah*) holds a unique place among the Minor Prophets despite — or perhaps because of — its brevity. Rabbinic literature identifies the prophet Obadiah as an Edomite convert (Talmud, Sanhedrin 39b), making him the ideal messenger to pronounce judgment on Edom. This identification transforms the book into a testimony from within: one who knew Edom's pride firsthand now declares its downfall.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The most significant development in Jewish interpretation is the identification of Edom with Rome, and subsequently with Christendom. This reading, established in the Talmudic period, transforms Obadiah from a localized oracle against a neighboring nation into a prophetic commentary on Jewish experience under imperial and ecclesiastical oppression. **Rashi** read the entire oracle through this lens, interpreting Edom's mountain fortresses as Rome's imperial power. **Radak** (David Kimchi) similarly applied the prophecy to the eventual downfall of all empires that oppress Israel.

## Messianic Hope

Obadiah's concluding verse — "Saviors shall go up to Mount Zion to judge the mountain of Esau, and the kingdom shall be the LORD's" — became an important messianic text. The "saviors" (*moshi'im*) are interpreted as messianic figures or righteous leaders who will arise in the end times. The Targum Jonathan expands this verse to describe the messianic age explicitly. Obadiah thus provides a compact summary of Jewish eschatological hope: the end of oppression, the vindication of Israel, and the universal recognition of God's sovereignty.
