## Overview

Obadiah is not referenced in the Quran, but its central theme — God's judgment on an arrogant nation that betrayed its kin and trusted in its own strength — fits squarely within the Quranic pattern of destroyed civilizations. Islamic engagement with Obadiah highlights the parallels between Edom's downfall and the Quranic accounts of nations (*aqwam*) brought low by divine judgment for their pride and injustice.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran repeatedly recounts the destruction of proud civilizations: 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lot, and Pharaoh's Egypt. Obadiah's portrait of Edom — dwelling in mountain clefts, saying "Who will bring me down to the ground?" (v. 3) — closely parallels the Quran's description of Thamud, who "carved houses out of the mountains" (7:74, 26:149). Both texts present the same theological principle: no fortress of human construction can withstand divine judgment. The Quranic refrain "How many a town We have destroyed" (22:45) echoes Obadiah's vision of Edom's total devastation.

## Ethical Principles

Islamic ethics affirms Obadiah's moral logic: betrayal of kinship and exploitation of the vulnerable invite divine retribution. The Quran emphasizes maintaining bonds of kinship (*silat al-rahim*) and warns against those who "cut off what Allah has ordered to be joined" (2:27). Edom's crime — standing by and profiting while a brother nation suffered — represents a violation of the fundamental Islamic principle of solidarity and mutual obligation. The proportional justice of "as you have done, it shall be done to you" (v. 15) aligns with the Quranic principle of proportional recompense.
