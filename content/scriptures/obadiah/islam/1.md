Obadiah 1 delivers an oracle against Edom that Islamic theology reads through the lens of divine justice toward arrogant nations. The portrait of Edom — dwelling in mountain clefts, boasting "Who will bring me down?" (v. 3) — parallels the Quranic descriptions of Thamud, who carved homes from mountains (26:149) and were destroyed for their arrogance and refusal to heed prophetic warning. Both texts affirm the principle that no human fortification can withstand divine judgment.

The catalogue of Edom's sins (vv. 10-14) — standing aloof during a brother's catastrophe, gloating, looting, and betraying refugees — violates the Islamic principle of *silat al-rahim* (maintaining bonds of kinship). The Quran declares: "And give the relative his right" (17:26) and warns against those who "cut what Allah has ordered to be joined" (13:25). Edom's betrayal of kinship obligation represents a fundamental violation of the moral order that both Obadiah and the Quran affirm.

The principle of proportional justice — "as you have done, it shall be done to you" (v. 15) — aligns with the Quranic teaching on divine recompense: "The recompense for an evil is an evil like thereof" (42:40). Islamic eschatology, like Obadiah's vision, culminates in the establishment of divine sovereignty: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth" (3:189), echoing the prophet's declaration that "the kingdom shall be the LORD's." Both traditions affirm that human power structures are temporary and will ultimately give way to the absolute rule of God.
