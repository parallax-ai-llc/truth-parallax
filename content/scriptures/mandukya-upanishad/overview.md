## Overview

The Mandukya Upanishad is the shortest of the principal Upanishads, consisting of just 12 verses, yet it has been called the most philosophically concentrated text in the entire Vedantic tradition. Belonging to the Atharva Veda, it analyzes the sacred syllable AUM and correlates its three phonetic elements (A, U, M) with the three ordinary states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep), culminating in the revelation of *turiya* — the fourth state that is the ground of all three.

## Structure

The 12 verses present a tightly structured argument:

1. **Verses 1-2**: AUM is everything; Brahman is the self with four aspects
2. **Verses 3-7**: The three states — waking (*vaishvanara*), dreaming (*taijasa*), and deep sleep (*prajna*)
3. **Verses 8-12**: AUM's three elements (A, U, M) correlated with the three states, and the silent fourth (*turiya*) beyond them

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: AUM is the sound-manifestation of Vishnu's creative power
- **Shaivism**: The four states map onto Shaiva consciousness theory
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Gaudapada's *Karika* on this Upanishad founded the Advaita school
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: The four aspects demonstrate God's multidimensional sovereignty
- **Academic**: Key text for the study of Indian consciousness theory
- **Comparative**: Parallels with phenomenology and philosophy of mind

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| AUM | The sacred syllable as a map of consciousness |
| Four States | Waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and turiya |
| Turiya | The fourth state — pure consciousness beyond all states |
| Brahman as Self | The self (*atman*) is Brahman in all four aspects |
| Silence | The soundless aspect of AUM pointing to the absolute |
