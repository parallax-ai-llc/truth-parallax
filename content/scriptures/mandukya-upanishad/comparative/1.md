The Mandukya Upanishad's systematic analysis of consciousness states invites comparison with contemplative traditions worldwide. The threefold structure — waking, dreaming, deep sleep — appears in Tibetan Buddhist consciousness theory, where dream yoga and sleep yoga (*yoga nidra*) are advanced practices for maintaining awareness through all three states. The Christian mystic Teresa of Avila's "interior castle" describes progressive states of prayer that move from active engagement to passive receptivity to a state beyond ordinary awareness.

The concept of turiya — the "fourth" state that transcends yet grounds the other three — resonates with several traditions. The Buddhist *nirvana* as the "unconditioned" beyond all conditioned states, the Sufi *fana* (annihilation) followed by *baqa* (subsistence in God), and the Kabbalistic *Ein Sof* (the Infinite beyond all attributes) all point to a reality that cannot be captured by any ordinary state of consciousness. AUM itself, as a sacred syllable encompassing all of reality, invites comparison with the Christian *Logos* ("In the beginning was the Word"), the Islamic *Kun* ("Be!"), and the Sikh *Ik Onkar* — each tradition recognizing that the creative power of the absolute is intimately connected with sound, vibration, or speech.
