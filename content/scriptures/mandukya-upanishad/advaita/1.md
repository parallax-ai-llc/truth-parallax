Shankara considers the Mandukya Upanishad sufficient for complete liberation. Its 12 verses present, in compressed form, the entire Advaita teaching. The opening verse — "AUM is all this" — establishes that the sacred syllable encompasses past, present, future, and whatever transcends time. This is not a claim about a particular sound but about the nature of reality itself: all that exists is Brahman, and AUM is Brahman's sonic form.

The analysis of the three states is the Mandukya's central philosophical contribution. In waking, consciousness appears as the external world of objects; in dreaming, as the internal world of mental images; in deep sleep, as undifferentiated, blissful awareness without objects. Turiya — the fourth — is not another state alongside these three but the awareness in which all three appear and disappear. Shankara insists that turiya is not a mystical experience to be attained but the ever-present reality of pure consciousness that is obscured only by identification with the three states. AUM meditation — sounding A, U, and M while recognizing their correspondence with waking, dreaming, and deep sleep — culminates in the silence that follows, which is turiya itself: non-dual, objectless awareness beyond all description.
