Scholars approach the Mandukya Upanishad's 12 verses as one of the earliest systematic analyses of consciousness in world literature. The text's method — correlating a sound-symbol (AUM) with states of awareness — has been compared with phenomenological reduction: the systematic stripping away of layers of experience to reveal the ground of all experience. Edmund Husserl's concept of "transcendental consciousness" and the Mandukya's turiya share a structural similarity as the non-objectifiable ground of all objectification.

The relationship between the Mandukya and Gaudapada's *Karika* is a major scholarly topic. The *Karika*'s fourth chapter employs arguments strikingly similar to Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka dialectic, leading scholars like Richard King and Thomas Wood to investigate the historical relationship between late Vedanta and Mahayana Buddhism. Whether Gaudapada borrowed from Buddhism, whether both drew on a common philosophical pool, or whether the similarities are coincidental remains actively debated. The Mandukya's dating is uncertain — scholars' estimates range from the 5th century BCE to the early centuries CE — making definitive claims about influence difficult.
