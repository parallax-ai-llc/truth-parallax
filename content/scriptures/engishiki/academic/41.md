## Overview

Academic analysis of Book 41 (Annual Rice Tribute) situates these regulations within the broader institutional, political, and social context of the Heian period. Scholars examine the text for evidence of bureaucratic development, religious policy, economic arrangements, and the practical realities of shrine and state administration in 10th-century Japan.

The Academic Shinto Studies reading of this book draws attention to aspects of the text that illuminate broader questions about ritual, governance, and the relationship between human institutions and sacred reality in the Japanese tradition.

## Key Themes

### Historical Context

Academic study situates Book 41 within the specific historical circumstances of the early 10th century, examining how political rivalries, economic pressures, and changing social conditions shaped the form and content of these regulations. The text's relationship to earlier legal codes and its deviations from them provide evidence of institutional development over time.

### Source Criticism

Scholars examine the textual history of this section, identifying possible layers of composition, later interpolations, and the relationship between the Engishiki's prescriptions and actual administrative practice as evidenced by other historical sources.

## Key Passages

Book 41 contains several passages of particular significance for academic shinto studies interpretation. The regulations concerning annual rice tribute reveal how the Heian court understood and organized this dimension of its responsibilities, providing evidence for broader claims about the nature of Japanese religion, governance, and culture.

## Commentary Tradition

Commentators within the academic tradition have engaged extensively with this section of the Engishiki, reading its prescriptions as evidence for their interpretive frameworks. The detailed specificity of these regulations — their attention to materials, timing, personnel, and procedure — provides rich material for interpretive elaboration and philosophical reflection.
