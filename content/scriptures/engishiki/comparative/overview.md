## Overview

Comparative religious studies approaches the Engishiki as a rich case study in the relationship between state power and religious institution, the codification of ritual practice, and the preservation of liturgical tradition in written form. The text offers valuable material for comparison with other traditions' ritual compilations, such as the Jewish Mishnah, the Roman pontifical texts, and Vedic ritual manuals.

The Engishiki's shrine register system invites comparison with other traditions' classification of sacred sites, while its norito collection provides material for cross-cultural study of prayer, invocation, and liturgical language. The text's treatment of purity and pollution resonates with similar concerns in Zoroastrian, Jewish, and Hindu traditions.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Comparative scholars identify structural parallels and significant differences between the Engishiki's ritual system and those of other traditions. Key comparative themes include the role of the state in regulating worship, the relationship between written and oral liturgical tradition, and the function of calendrical cycles in organizing religious life.
