---
title: "Engishiki Section 15: The Iwashimizu Hachiman Festival"
scripture: "engishiki"
chapter: 15
---

The Iwashimizu Hachiman Shrine, situated south of the capital, venerates the deity Hachiman, identified both as the deified Emperor Ojin and as a protector of the nation. This scroll details the shrine's festivals and the procedures for the Iwashimizu Special Festival, one of the three great festivals of the Heian court.

Hachiman worship represents a distinctive strand in Japanese religion, combining imperial ancestor veneration with warrior deity worship and Buddhist elements. The Engishiki's treatment of Iwashimizu reflects this complexity, prescribing both Shinto offerings and Buddhist ceremonies. The scroll addresses the shrine's dual priesthood, with both Shinto ritualists and Buddhist monks serving the deity.

The festival procession from the capital to the shrine involves hundreds of participants, including court nobles, warriors, musicians, and dancers. The scroll specifies the order of march, the costumes and equipment of each group, and the rituals performed at various points along the route. The Iwashimizu festival became a model for later warrior-class religious observances, and its procedures influenced the development of samurai ceremonial culture.
