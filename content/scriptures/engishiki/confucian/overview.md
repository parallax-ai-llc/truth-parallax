## Overview

The Confucian-Shinto reading of the Engishiki examines how Chinese concepts of ritual propriety (li/rei), social hierarchy, and moral governance were integrated with indigenous Japanese religious practice. The Engishiki itself is a product of this synthesis — a document modeled on Chinese administrative codes yet devoted substantially to the regulation of kami worship, a uniquely Japanese institution.

Scholars in the Suika Shinto tradition (founded by Yamazaki Ansai in the 17th century) and other Confucian-Shinto syncretistic movements found in the Engishiki evidence for the fundamental compatibility of Confucian moral philosophy and Shinto religious practice. The text's emphasis on ritual order, hierarchical organization, and social harmony resonated deeply with Confucian ideals.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Confucian interpreters read the Engishiki's ritual regulations as expressions of the fundamental human need for order, propriety, and moral cultivation. The detailed prescriptions for shrine rank, offering protocols, and festival conduct are understood not merely as religious obligations but as expressions of the Confucian virtue of reverence (jing/kei) — the attitude of seriousness and respect that should permeate all human activity.
