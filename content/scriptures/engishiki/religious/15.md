## Overview

From the religious Shinto perspective, Book 15 (People's Affairs Ministry) preserves sacred prescriptions for the proper conduct of ritual life. These regulations are not merely administrative guidelines but divinely sanctioned procedures whose correct observance ensures the continued blessing and protection of the kami for the Japanese nation.

The Religious Shinto reading of this book draws attention to aspects of the text that illuminate broader questions about ritual, governance, and the relationship between human institutions and sacred reality in the Japanese tradition.

## Key Themes

### Sacred Authority

Religious interpreters understand Book 15's prescriptions as carrying divine authority transmitted through the imperial institution and the hereditary priestly lineages. These are not suggestions but sacred obligations whose fulfillment sustains the spiritual health of the nation and whose neglect risks cosmic disorder and divine displeasure.

### Living Tradition

Many of the ritual forms prescribed in this section continue to shape worship at Japanese shrines today. Religious readers emphasize the continuity between the Engishiki's prescriptions and contemporary practice, seeing in this unbroken transmission evidence of the enduring power and relevance of the ancient ways.

## Key Passages

Book 15 contains several passages of particular significance for religious shinto interpretation. The regulations concerning people's affairs ministry reveal how the Heian court understood and organized this dimension of its responsibilities, providing evidence for broader claims about the nature of Japanese religion, governance, and culture.

## Commentary Tradition

Commentators within the religious tradition have engaged extensively with this section of the Engishiki, reading its prescriptions as evidence for their interpretive frameworks. The detailed specificity of these regulations — their attention to materials, timing, personnel, and procedure — provides rich material for interpretive elaboration and philosophical reflection.
