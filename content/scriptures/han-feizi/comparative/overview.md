## Overview

Comparative political philosophy has found in the Han Feizi one of the most productive texts for cross-cultural dialogue on the nature of political power. Han Fei's political realism — his insistence on seeing political life as it is rather than as it ought to be — invites comparison with Machiavelli, Hobbes, Kautilya, and modern rational choice theory.

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative readers explore structural parallels between the Han Feizi and Western political realism while attending to significant differences in cultural context and philosophical foundation. The comparison with Machiavelli is particularly rich: both thinkers wrote during periods of intense political fragmentation, both distinguished between the moral standards appropriate to private life and the necessities of statecraft, and both were posthumously condemned by the moral establishments of their cultures.

## Key Comparisons

Han Fei's "two handles" (reward and punishment) parallel Machiavelli's teaching that the prince must be both loved and feared. His analysis of the ruler-minister relationship anticipates modern principal-agent theory. His concept of shi (positional authority) resonates with Weber's analysis of legitimate domination. The comparison with Kautilya's Arthashastra reveals how two civilizations independently developed sophisticated theories of statecraft during periods of interstate competition.
