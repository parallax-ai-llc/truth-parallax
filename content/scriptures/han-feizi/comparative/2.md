Comparative reading of Han Feizi section 2, "On Having Standards," places Legalist political theory in dialogue with global traditions of political realism. The arguments presented here invite comparison with Machiavelli's analysis of princely power, Hobbes's theory of sovereignty, and Kautilya's science of statecraft. All four thinkers share the conviction that effective governance requires clear-eyed assessment of human motivation and institutional incentives rather than wishful appeals to moral virtue.

Yet significant differences emerge on closer examination. Han Fei's concept of positional authority (shi) differs from Hobbes's social contract theory in dispensing with any notion of popular consent. His view of law as a tool of state power contrasts with Western traditions of natural law and constitutional limitation. These differences illuminate how different civilizations have grappled with the permanent tensions between power and justice, order and freedom.
