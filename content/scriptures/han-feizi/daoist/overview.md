## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Han Feizi is complicated by Han Fei's own explicit appropriation of Daoist concepts. Two chapters of the Han Feizi are devoted to interpreting the Daodejing, reading Laozi's teachings on wu wei (non-action) and naturalness as prescriptions for the ruler's strategic inscrutability. This Legalist reading of Daoism — in which the ruler governs by remaining empty and passive while the system of laws and rewards does the work — represents one of the most consequential misreadings in Chinese intellectual history.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist readers regard Han Fei's interpretation of the Daodejing as a fundamental distortion. Where Laozi taught wu wei as alignment with the natural Way beyond human contrivance, Han Fei reinterpreted it as a political technique — the ruler's calculated concealment of his intentions to prevent manipulation by ministers. The Daoist sage acts without ulterior motive; Han Fei's ruler acts without apparent action in order to maintain absolute control.

## Significance

Despite rejecting Han Fei's interpretation, Daoists recognize the Han Feizi as evidence of the Daodejing's profound influence on Chinese political culture. The text demonstrates how easily wisdom about naturalness and non-striving can be co-opted into a technology of power — a cautionary tale about the relationship between philosophy and politics.
