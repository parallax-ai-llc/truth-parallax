Daoist reading of Han Feizi section 7, "Inner Congeries of Sayings I," confronts the Legalist appropriation of Daoist concepts. Han Fei's interpretation of non-action (wu wei) as the ruler's strategic passivity represents, from the Daoist perspective, a fundamental corruption of Laozi's teaching. True wu wei arises from alignment with the natural Way; Han Fei's version is a calculated technique of political control that merely mimics the outward form of Daoist wisdom.

Yet this section also reveals the seductive power of Daoist imagery when applied to politics. The ruler who remains still while all things move, who is empty while his ministers are full, who acts without apparent action — these images capture something genuine about effective leadership even as they distort the Daoist meaning. The Daoist reader finds here both a warning about the misuse of philosophical insight and a grudging acknowledgment of the Daodejing's political potency.
