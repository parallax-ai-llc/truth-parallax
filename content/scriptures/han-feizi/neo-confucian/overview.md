## Overview

Neo-Confucian treatment of the Han Feizi is shaped by the historical memory of the Qin dynasty's tyranny, which implemented Legalist policies to devastating effect. Song dynasty thinkers like Zhu Xi and the Cheng brothers regarded Legalism as the philosophical source of political oppression, arguing that Han Fei's rejection of moral cultivation in favor of institutional control represented a catastrophic wrong turn in Chinese political thought.

## Interpretive Approach

Neo-Confucian readers evaluate the Han Feizi against the metaphysical framework of heavenly principle (tian li) and human desire (ren yu). From this perspective, Han Fei's philosophy is a systematic capitulation to human desire — rather than seeking to align governance with heavenly principle, it designs institutions to harness and redirect selfish impulses. This represents, for Neo-Confucians, a fundamental confusion of means and ends.

## Qualified Engagement

Despite their principled opposition, some Neo-Confucian thinkers recognized that effective governance requires attention to institutional design and cannot rely solely on the ruler's personal virtue. The practical challenge of balancing moral aspiration with institutional realism has kept the Han Feizi a relevant, if dangerous, text within the broader Confucian conversation.
