## San Shou (Three Precautions)

The ruler must guard three things above all: his power, his position, and his life.

Power is lost when the ruler entrusts the handles of government to others. Position is lost when the ruler allows ministers to build independent bases of support. Life is lost when the ruler trusts those around him without verification.

The ruler who shares his power with his ministers is like the tiger who lends his claws to another beast. The tiger without claws is prey. The ruler without power is a puppet.

The ruler who allows ministers to build independent followings is planting the seeds of his own overthrow. Every minister with a private army, a personal treasury, or a faction of loyal followers is a potential usurper.

The ruler who trusts without verification is blind. Trust is not a virtue in governance -- it is a vulnerability. The wise ruler verifies everything. He checks the words of one minister against those of another. He compares promises with results.

The three precautions: First, keep the handles of power in your own hands. Second, prevent any minister from becoming too powerful. Third, verify all claims through independent sources.

These precautions are not born of paranoia but of realism. The history of every dynasty confirms that rulers who neglected these precautions were overthrown, while those who observed them maintained order.

The ruler who follows these precautions may sleep soundly. The ruler who ignores them sleeps at the edge of a cliff.
