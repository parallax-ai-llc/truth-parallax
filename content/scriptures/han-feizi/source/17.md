## Bei Nei (Guarding the Interior)

The greatest danger to the ruler comes not from external enemies but from within his own court. Foreign armies can be seen approaching. Court conspiracies are invisible until they strike.

The ruler must guard against several internal threats:

The heir apparent: If the heir is impatient for power, he may conspire against his father. The ruler must balance grooming his successor with preventing premature seizure of power.

The queen and consorts: Those closest to the ruler in private life have the greatest opportunity for manipulation. The ruler must not allow personal affection to influence political decisions.

The ministers: Each minister has his own interests, which may conflict with the ruler's. The ruler must create a system of checks in which ministers watch one another.

The servants and attendants: Those who control access to the ruler control information. The ruler must ensure that no single person or group controls all channels of communication.

The method of guarding the interior: Establish multiple, independent sources of information. Create competing factions that check each other's power. Rotate officials to prevent the accumulation of local influence. Keep the mechanisms of reward and punishment in the ruler's own hands.

The ruler who guards the interior effectively has nothing to fear from the exterior. The ruler who neglects the interior will fall, no matter how strong his walls or how large his army.
