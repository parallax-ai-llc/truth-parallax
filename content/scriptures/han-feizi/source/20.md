## Jie Lao / Yu Lao (Commentaries on the Laozi)

The Laozi says: "The Way that can be spoken of is not the constant Way." This means that the Way is not captured by any doctrine or theory. It is the underlying pattern of all things, beyond the reach of language.

The Laozi says: "When the Great Way is abandoned, there appear benevolence and righteousness." This means that benevolence and righteousness are remedies for a diseased society, not the marks of a healthy one. In a well-ordered state, there is no need to speak of benevolence, because all actions are naturally benevolent.

The Laozi says: "Govern a great state as you would cook a small fish." This means: do not handle it too much. Excessive intervention destroys what it seeks to preserve. The enlightened ruler governs with a light hand, allowing the system of laws and institutions to do the work.

The Laozi says: "The softest thing in the world overcomes the hardest." Water wears away stone. The ruler who appears yielding can overcome the minister who appears strong, because the ruler holds institutional power while the minister holds only personal power.

The Laozi says: "A journey of a thousand li begins with a single step." Great disasters begin with small oversights. The wise ruler attends to small problems before they become large ones. He repairs the dam before the flood, not after.

These teachings of the Laozi, rightly understood, support the principles of Legalism: govern through systems, not through personal virtue; attend to the concrete, not the abstract; prevent problems rather than cure them; and act with the minimum necessary force.
