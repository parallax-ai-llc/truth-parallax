## Shi Guo (Ten Faults)

There are ten faults that bring ruin to a ruler:

The first is to practice petty kindness and thereby neglect the great welfare of the state.

The second is to be swayed by empty rhetoric and thereby neglect practical affairs.

The third is to be fond of fine conduct and thereby neglect matters of real importance. A ruler who values a minister's personal charm over his administrative competence will suffer for it.

The fourth is to enjoy music and women to excess, and thereby neglect the governance of the state.

The fifth is to be exclusively devoted to personal profit, and thereby alienate the hearts of the people.

The sixth is to be infatuated with dancing girls and singers, and thereby lose the respect of the officials.

The seventh is to go on distant travels and hunts while leaving the capital unattended.

The eighth is to ignore loyal admonition and persist in one's errors, thereby provoking the resentment of the officials and the people.

The ninth is to fail to assess one's own strength, provoking powerful neighbors through aggression while the state is weak.

The tenth is to be small and weak yet refuse to show deference to the strong, failing to recognize when submission is the wiser course.

These ten faults are the causes of state ruin. The wise ruler examines himself for these faults daily. He corrects them before they lead to disaster.
