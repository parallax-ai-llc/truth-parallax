## Yang Quan (Wielding Power)

The ruler wields power. The minister wields influence. These two must not be confused.

Power belongs to the position, not to the person. When the ruler sits on the throne, he has power. When he steps down, he is an ordinary man. Therefore, the ruler must understand that his power derives from his position and protect that position accordingly.

The ruler who relies on his personal wisdom rather than his institutional position makes a grave error. Personal wisdom varies -- it may be great or small, sharp or dull. Institutional power is constant -- it does not vary with the individual who holds it.

The analogy of the dragon and the serpent: A dragon rides upon clouds and mist; without clouds and mist, it is no different from a snake crawling on the ground. The clouds and mist are the dragon's power. Similarly, the ruler's institutional position is his power.

When the ruler is elevated in position, his commands are obeyed. When he loses his position, his commands are ignored. It is not that the man has changed -- the position has changed.

Therefore, the wise ruler does not trust to his personal virtue or his personal ability. He trusts to the system -- to clear laws, proper institutions, and the two handles of reward and punishment.

He who relies on the system can govern a large state with ease. He who relies on personal ability can barely manage a household. This is why institutional power surpasses personal power.
