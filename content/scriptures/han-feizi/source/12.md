## Shuo Lin (Collection of Persuasions)

A man of Song had a fine piece of uncut jade. He presented it to Zi Han, the minister. Zi Han refused it. The man said: "I have had it appraised by a jeweler, and he says it is a treasure. That is why I dare to present it to you." Zi Han replied: "You consider jade to be your treasure. I consider not accepting bribes to be my treasure. If I accept your jade, we both lose our treasures. Better that each keeps his own."

The Duke of She told Confucius: "In my village there is a man of upright character. When his father stole a sheep, the son bore witness against him." Confucius said: "The upright men of my village act differently. The father conceals the misconduct of the son, and the son conceals that of the father. Uprightness is to be found in this."

A man of Zheng was about to buy shoes. He first measured his foot and placed the measurement on his seat. When he arrived at the market, he realized he had forgotten the measurement. He went home to fetch it. When he returned, the market had closed. Someone asked: "Why did you not try the shoes on your feet?" He replied: "I trust the measurement more than my own feet."

These stories illustrate the follies of rigid thinking: trusting measures over reality, confusing legalism with justice, and failing to recognize true value.
