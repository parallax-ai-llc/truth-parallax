## You Du (Having Standards)

A state must have standards. Without standards, there is chaos. With standards, even a mediocre ruler can maintain order.

The greatest standard is law. Law is the measure by which all actions are judged. It applies equally to the noble and the base, the rich and the poor. When law is applied equally, the people trust the government. When law is applied unequally, the people despise it.

The carpenter relies on the plumb line, not on his eye. The wheelwright relies on the compass, not on his hand. Why? Because the plumb line and the compass are objective standards that do not vary with the craftsman's mood or fatigue.

So too must the ruler rely on law, not on his personal judgment. Personal judgment varies with mood, with favoritism, with fatigue. Law is constant.

When a man commits a crime, do not ask whether he is a relative of the ruler or a friend of a minister. Ask only: Did he violate the law? If he did, punish him. If he did not, release him. This is the standard.

When a man performs meritorious service, do not ask whether he is noble or base, rich or poor. Ask only: Did he serve the state well? If he did, reward him. If he did not, pass him by.

The state that follows standards grows strong. The state that abandons standards grows weak. This is not theory -- it is the lesson of history, demonstrated by the rise and fall of every dynasty.
