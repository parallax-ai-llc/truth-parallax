## Gu Fen (Solitary Indignation)

The worthy minister finds himself alone and without allies. The treacherous minister has many friends and much influence. Why? Because the worthy minister speaks the truth, which is unpleasant, while the treacherous minister speaks flattery, which is pleasing.

The worthy minister urges the ruler to follow the law and dismiss the unworthy. This makes enemies of all the unworthy. The treacherous minister urges the ruler to relax the law and promote his friends. This makes friends of all the unworthy.

Therefore the worthy minister is isolated and the treacherous minister is powerful. The ruler who does not understand this will dismiss the worthy and promote the treacherous.

This is the source of solitary indignation: The worthy man sees the truth clearly but cannot make others see it. He sees the state heading toward ruin but cannot prevent it. He sees the treacherous prospering but cannot expose them.

The history of the states is filled with examples: Loyal ministers who were executed while treacherous ministers were promoted. Wise advisors who were exiled while foolish favorites were honored.

The root of the problem: Rulers judge ministers by their manner rather than their results. The smooth and pleasing manner of the treacherous wins the ruler's favor. The blunt and unpleasant manner of the loyal earns the ruler's displeasure.

The enlightened ruler reverses this. He values results over manner. He rewards the minister who tells unpleasant truths and distrusts the minister who tells pleasant lies.
