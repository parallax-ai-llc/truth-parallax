## Xian Xue (Prominent Doctrines)

The two most prominent doctrines of the age are those of Confucius and Mo Di. The followers of Confucius are called the Ru. The followers of Mo Di are called the Mo.

Since the deaths of Confucius and Mo Di, the Confucians have split into eight schools and the Mohists into three. Their doctrines contradict each other, yet each claims to represent the true teaching of the master. They cannot all be right. At most, one of them is right; likely, none of them is.

The Confucians say: Love your kin above strangers. The Mohists say: Love all equally. These two principles are contradictory. If one is right, the other must be wrong. Yet both schools flourish, and rulers listen to both.

The problem is that doctrine is evaluated by eloquence rather than by results. The school that argues most persuasively wins the debate, regardless of whether its proposals would actually work in practice.

The Legalist position is different: Evaluate all doctrines by their practical results. Does this teaching, when applied, strengthen the state? Does it increase agricultural production? Does it improve military readiness? Does it maintain order? If yes, adopt it. If no, reject it, regardless of how elegant its arguments may be.

There is no fixed teaching that is right for all times. The sage adapts. The ancient methods suited the ancient world. The present methods must suit the present world. Clinging to antiquity is as foolish as dressing in winter clothes during summer.
