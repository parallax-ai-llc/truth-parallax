## Wu Du (The Five Vermin)

There are five types of men who are vermin to the state:

The first are the scholars who use their learning to undermine the law. They invoke the ancient kings to criticize present policies. They praise propriety and righteousness in order to cast doubt upon military strength and agricultural productivity.

The second are the itinerant persuaders who use clever rhetoric to deceive rulers. They argue both sides with equal facility, caring nothing for truth but only for their own advancement.

The third are the swordsmen who gather followers and use force to violate the prohibitions of the state. They are praised as heroes but they are no better than bandits.

The fourth are those who avoid military service by attaching themselves to powerful patrons. They use personal connections to escape their public obligations.

The fifth are the merchants and artisans who produce luxury goods and accumulate wealth without contributing to agriculture or defense.

These five types of men do not plow or fight. Yet they live well. They consume without producing. They enjoy the benefits of civilization without bearing its burdens.

In the age of the sage kings, the population was small and resources were abundant. Men did not need to compete. But in the present age, the population is large and resources are scarce. Competition is fierce. Therefore the methods of the sage kings -- rule by virtue, government by example -- are no longer sufficient.

The present age requires law, institutional power, and systematic incentives.
