## Nan Yan (Difficulties of Persuasion)

The difficulty in persuading a ruler lies not in having the knowledge to present, nor in the daring to speak frankly, nor in the skill to express one's ideas fully. The difficulty lies in knowing the mind of the person you are persuading and fitting your words to it.

If the ruler values reputation for honor, and you speak to him of great profit, he will consider you base and dismiss you. If the ruler secretly desires profit, and you speak to him of honor, he will consider you impractical and set you aside.

If the ruler is secretly pursuing a project but outwardly pretends otherwise, and you know the truth -- he will feel threatened by you.

If you are too eloquent, the ruler suspects you of manipulation. If you are too blunt, the ruler thinks you are stupid. If you give too many examples, the ruler thinks you are showing off. If you give too few, the ruler thinks you are unprepared.

If the ruler's plan succeeds and you receive the credit, the ruler will resent you. If the plan fails and you are blamed, the ruler will punish you.

Therefore the art of persuasion is the most dangerous of all arts. Many worthy men of ancient times were put to death not for their failures but for their success in speaking the truth.

The wise advisor learns to present the truth in a form the ruler can accept. He makes the ruler feel that the good idea was the ruler's own. This is the highest skill.
