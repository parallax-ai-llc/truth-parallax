## Nan Shi (Difficulties in the Way of Persuasion, Continued)

The art of persuasion requires understanding not only the ruler's mind but the political circumstances of the time. The same argument that succeeds in one context will fail in another.

A man of Wei proposed an alliance with Zhao. His argument was sound, his evidence was strong, his rhetoric was brilliant. Yet the ruler of Wei rejected it. Why? Because the ruler's favorite concubine was from Zhao, and the ruler suspected the advisor of trying to curry favor with her family.

Another advisor, wishing to strengthen the army, compared the state's military to that of its neighbors. The ruler was offended, thinking the advisor was calling him weak. The advisor was dismissed.

The lesson: Logic is not enough. Evidence is not enough. Rhetoric is not enough. The persuader must understand the emotional landscape of the court.

Furthermore, timing is essential. An argument that would have been welcomed yesterday may be rejected today because of a change in the ruler's mood, a shift in factional power, or a new development in foreign affairs.

The greatest persuaders of history were not those with the strongest arguments but those with the deepest understanding of human psychology. They knew when to speak and when to remain silent. They knew how to present a harsh truth in an acceptable form.

The test of a great persuader: His advice is followed without the ruler realizing he has been persuaded.
