Confucian reading of Han Feizi section 11, "Wielding Power," confronts the Legalist challenge to governance by virtue. The Confucian tradition recognizes the analytical power of Han Fei's observations about political dynamics but rejects the conclusion that coercion and manipulation are the only effective tools of governance. The exemplary ruler, in the Confucian view, leads by moral example and transforms the people through education and ritual — a project that Han Fei dismisses as naive but that Confucians regard as the only path to lasting social harmony.

This section reveals the fundamental divergence between Confucian and Legalist anthropology. Where Han Fei sees self-interest as a fixed feature of human nature to be exploited, Confucians see it as a starting condition to be transcended through cultivation. The Confucian response to this section is that a society built entirely on incentives and punishments may achieve temporary order but will inevitably collapse when the mechanisms of control falter.
