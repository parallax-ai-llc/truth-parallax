## Overview

Confucian engagement with the Han Feizi is defined by profound opposition. The Confucian tradition views Legalism as the antithesis of governance by virtue — a system that reduces human beings to objects of manipulation through reward and punishment while abandoning the project of moral cultivation. Mencius's warning that governing by force rather than virtue produces outward compliance without inner transformation applies directly to Han Fei's program.

## Interpretive Approach

Confucian readers acknowledge Han Fei's brilliance as a political analyst while rejecting his fundamental premises. The Confucian critique centers on Han Fei's view of human nature as irredeemably self-interested — a position that, ironically, takes his teacher Xunzi's pessimism to its logical extreme. Where Xunzi believed education and ritual could transform selfish nature, Han Fei abandoned the hope of transformation and designed a system to exploit selfishness for the state's benefit.

## Historical Judgment

The Confucian tradition points to the rapid collapse of the Qin dynasty — which implemented Legalist policies — as historical proof that governance without virtue is unsustainable. The burning of books, the suppression of learning, and the brutal conscription that characterized Qin rule are seen as the inevitable consequences of Han Fei's philosophy.
