## Overview

Chinese Buddhist engagement with the Han Feizi highlights the radical contrast between a philosophy centered on power and one centered on compassion. The Han Feizi's worldview — in which self-interest is universal, trust is foolish, and the state's survival justifies any means — represents the antithesis of Buddhist values. Yet Buddhist thinkers have found the text valuable precisely for articulating with unsparing clarity the logic of samsaric existence: a world driven by craving, aversion, and delusion.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist readers interpret the Han Feizi as a precise description of how political life functions when unilluminated by wisdom and compassion. Han Fei's observations about self-interest, deception, and the struggle for power are not wrong as descriptions of samsaric reality — they are wrong as prescriptions for how human beings should organize their collective life. The Buddhist response is that the cycle of mutual exploitation Han Fei describes can only be broken through the cultivation of wisdom and compassion, not through more sophisticated systems of control.

## Significance

The Han Feizi serves Buddhist pedagogy as a vivid illustration of what the world looks like when governed by the three poisons of greed, hatred, and delusion. Its frank acknowledgment that political life is driven by self-interest provides Buddhist teachers with material for demonstrating why the path of liberation from samsara is necessary.
