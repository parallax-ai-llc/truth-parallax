## The Tragedy of Nadav and Avihu

Leviticus 10 records one of the Torah's most dramatic and theologically challenging episodes: the death of Aaron's eldest sons, Nadav and Avihu, on the very day the Tabernacle service was inaugurated. They brought *eish zarah* ("strange fire") before God, "which He had not commanded them," and fire came forth from God and consumed them. This event introduces fundamental questions about the limits of religious enthusiasm, the dangers of unauthorized worship, and the nature of divine justice.

Aaron's response is captured in two of the most powerful words in the Torah: *Vayidom Aharon* — "And Aaron was silent" (10:3). The Midrash and commentators find extraordinary depth in this silence — not passive resignation but an act of profound faith and acceptance.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Sin of Nadav and Avihu (10:1–2)

What exactly was the *eish zarah*? The commentators offer multiple explanations:

| Source | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Rashi (Sifra) | They entered intoxicated with wine |
| Rashi (Eruvin 63a) | They rendered halakhic decisions in Moshe's presence |
| Vayikra Rabbah 20:10 | They did not consult each other |
| Ramban | They entered the Holy of Holies unbidden |
| Talmud (Sanhedrin 52a) | They awaited Moshe and Aaron's deaths, saying "When will these two die so we can lead?" |

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai taught (Vayikra Rabbah 12:2) that their core sin was entering the innermost sanctuary without being commanded — an excess of passion that overstepped divine boundaries. The Zohar portrays them more sympathetically, as souls so drawn to divine closeness that they could not pull back.

### Aaron's Silence (10:3)

Moshe tells Aaron: "This is what the Lord spoke, saying: 'Through those near to Me I will be sanctified, and before all the people I will be glorified.'" Rashi interprets: God had told Moshe at Sinai that the Tabernacle would be sanctified through God's "intimate ones" — Moshe assumed it would be himself or Aaron, but now realized Nadav and Avihu were greater than both. Hearing this, Aaron accepted the decree and was silent. For this, the Talmud (Zevachim 115b) teaches that Aaron was rewarded with a direct divine communication in the following verses.

### The Prohibition of Wine (10:8–11)

Immediately following the tragedy, God speaks directly to Aaron — one of the rare occasions God addresses Aaron without Moshe as intermediary — prohibiting *kohanim* from drinking wine or intoxicating drink before performing the Temple service. This supports Rashi's view that Nadav and Avihu had entered while intoxicated.

## Halakhic Significance

The prohibition against priestly intoxication during service (10:9) is codified by the Rambam (Hilkhot Bi'at HaMikdash 1:1–7). A *kohen* who performs the service while intoxicated desecrates it and is liable to death at the hands of heaven. The Talmud (Keritot 13b) extends the principle to halakhic decision-making — "Do not render decisions while intoxicated."

The instruction that Aaron's surviving sons must continue the service despite mourning (10:6–7) establishes the halakhic principle that the Temple service overrides personal mourning for *kohanim* on active duty. The Rambam (Hilkhot Bi'at HaMikdash 2:6–8) codifies this, distinguishing between the *kohen gadol* (who serves even while mourning) and ordinary *kohanim*.

## Parashah Context

This chapter continues Parashat Shemini. The devastating shift from chapter 9's joyous climax to chapter 10's tragedy shapes the entire parashah's theological character. The reading of Parashat Shemini often falls near Pesach, and the theme of divine fire — both accepting (9:24) and consuming (10:2) — resonates with the dual nature of divine encounter throughout Jewish thought.
