## Kedoshim Tihyu: You Shall Be Holy

Leviticus 19 is widely regarded as the theological and ethical summit of the Torah. The chapter opens with the sweeping imperative *Kedoshim tihyu ki kadosh ani Hashem Elokeikhem* — "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" (19:2). Rashi, citing the Sifra, notes that this chapter was proclaimed *be-hakhel* — in a full public assembly — because "most of the essential principles of the Torah depend upon it."

The chapter weaves together ritual, ethical, agricultural, judicial, and interpersonal commandments in a dense tapestry that defies categorization, teaching that holiness is not a compartmentalized religious experience but a total way of life.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Parallels to the Ten Commandments

The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 24:5) observes that Leviticus 19 contains parallels to all Ten Commandments:

| Ten Commandments | Leviticus 19 Parallel |
|---|---|
| "I am the Lord your God" | "I am the Lord your God" (19:3) |
| No other gods | "Do not turn to idols" (19:4) |
| Do not take God's name in vain | "Do not swear falsely by My name" (19:12) |
| Remember the Sabbath | "Keep My Sabbaths" (19:3) |
| Honor your parents | "Each person shall revere his mother and father" (19:3) |
| Do not murder | "Do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor" (19:16) |
| Do not commit adultery | Implied in the sexual laws (19:20–22, 19:29) |
| Do not steal | "Do not steal, do not deny, do not lie" (19:11) |
| Do not bear false witness | "Do not go as a talebearer" (19:16) |
| Do not covet | "Love your neighbor as yourself" (19:18) |

### "Love Your Neighbor as Yourself" — *Ve-ahavta le-re'akha kamokha* (19:18)

Rabbi Akiva declared this verse *klal gadol ba-Torah* — "the great principle of the Torah" (Sifra, Kedoshim 2:12). The Talmud (Shabbat 31a) records Hillel's restatement as the negative formulation: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow — this is the entire Torah; the rest is commentary. Go and learn."

Ramban offers a crucial qualification: the command does not require identical emotional intensity toward others as toward oneself (which would be psychologically impossible for most people), but rather that one should wish for others the same good fortune one desires for oneself, without jealousy or reservations.

### Agricultural Justice — *Leket, Shikchah, Pe'ah* (19:9–10)

The commands to leave the corners of the field (*pe'ah*), the gleanings (*leket*), and the fallen grapes (*peret*) for the poor and the stranger institutionalize economic justice within the agricultural system. These laws, elaborated in Tractate Pe'ah, ensure that the poor maintain dignity — they gather for themselves rather than receiving handouts.

### Judicial Ethics (19:15)

"You shall not commit injustice in judgment; do not favor the poor nor honor the great; judge your fellow with righteousness." The Talmud (Sanhedrin 7a) emphasizes that a judge who distorts justice, even in favor of the poor out of sympathy, violates this commandment. Justice must be blind to social status in both directions.

### "Do Not Place a Stumbling Block Before the Blind" — *Lifnei iver* (19:14)

The Talmud (Pesachim 22b) interprets this metaphorically as prohibiting giving inappropriate advice, enabling sin, or placing temptation before someone who cannot resist. This principle (*lifnei iver*) has become one of the most widely applied ethical principles in *halakhah*, governing areas from business ethics to religious guidance.

## Halakhic Significance

Leviticus 19 contains an extraordinary density of mitzvot — the Rambam counts over a dozen distinct commandments within the chapter. The laws span multiple areas of *halakhah*: Yoreh De'ah (dietary mixtures, 19:19), Choshen Mishpat (honest weights and measures, 19:35–36), Even HaEzer (sexual laws), and Orach Chaim (Shabbat). This breadth makes the chapter a microcosm of the entire halakhic system.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 19 is the core of Parashat Kedoshim, often combined with Acharei Mot. Its placement at the center of the Torah (by chapter count) reinforces its centrality to the Jewish moral vision. The chapter's address to the entire assembly (*kol adat bnei Yisrael*) emphasizes that holiness is not the province of priests alone but the calling of every Israelite.
