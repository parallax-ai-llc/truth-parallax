## Zevach Shelamim: The Offering of Wholeness

Leviticus 3 presents the *shelamim* (peace offering, or more accurately, "wholeness offering"), derived from the root *shalem* meaning whole or complete. Unlike the *olah*, the *shelamim* is shared: portions are burned on the altar, portions go to the *kohanim*, and the majority of the meat is eaten by the offerer and his family. It is the most communal of the offerings, representing harmony between God, the priesthood, and the people.

Rashi notes that the name *shelamim* connotes *shalom* — peace — because this offering brings peace to all parties. The Talmud (Zevachim 51a) discusses how the *shelamim* can be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah* (Temple courtyard), unlike the *olah* which must be on the north side, reflecting the offering's more accessible and joyous character.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Three Types of Animals (3:1, 3:7, 3:12)

The chapter permits cattle, sheep, and goats as *shelamim*, but unlike the *olah*, birds are not included. Ramban explains that since the *shelamim* involves a shared meal, there must be sufficient meat for distribution. Both male and female animals are accepted, reflecting the offering's inclusive nature.

### The Prohibition of Fat and Blood (3:17)

The chapter introduces the permanent prohibition against eating *chelev* (certain internal fats) and *dam* (blood): "It is an eternal statute for your generations in all your dwelling places: you shall eat no fat and no blood." The Talmud (Keritot 2a) classifies the consumption of *chelev* as punishable by *karet* (spiritual excision), placing it among the most serious dietary prohibitions.

| Prohibited Element | Hebrew | Punishment | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Certain fats | *Chelev* | Karet | Lev. 3:17, 7:25 |
| Blood | *Dam* | Karet | Lev. 3:17, 7:27 |

The distinction between permitted fat (*shuman*) and prohibited *chelev* is a significant area of *halakhah*, requiring expert knowledge in *nikkur* (the removal of forbidden fats and sinews from kosher meat).

### The Fat Tail of the Sheep (3:9)

The *alyah* (broad fat tail) of the sheep must be offered on the altar. Rashi explains this refers to the entire fat tail up to the backbone. This detail has practical implications for kosher butchering of fat-tailed sheep breeds still common in the Middle East.

## Halakhic Significance

The *shelamim* is the model for festive eating in Judaism. The Talmud (Pesachim 109a) states that there is no *simchah* (joy) without meat and wine, and during Temple times, this joy was expressed through the *shelamim*. On the *shalosh regalim* (pilgrimage festivals), every Israelite was required to bring a *shelamim* known as *shalmei simchah* (peace offerings of joy) and *shalmei chagigah* (festival peace offerings).

The Rambam (Hilkhot Chagigah 1:1) codifies that the joy of the festival is fulfilled through the consumption of *shelamim* meat. In the absence of the Temple, this obligation is understood to be fulfilled through festive meals with meat and wine.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 3 concludes the first triad of voluntary offerings within Parashat Vayikra. The progression from *olah* (wholly for God) to *minchah* (shared with priests) to *shelamim* (shared with all) illustrates an expanding circle of sacred participation.
