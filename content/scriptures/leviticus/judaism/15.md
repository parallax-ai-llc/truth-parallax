## Tumah from Bodily Discharges

Leviticus 15 addresses ritual impurity (*tumah*) arising from bodily discharges, covering four categories: the *zav* (male with abnormal genital discharge), normal male emissions (*keri*), the *zavah* (female with abnormal uterine bleeding), and the *niddah* (regular menstruation). This chapter concludes the purity section that began in chapter 11, establishing the complete framework of *tumah ve-taharah* (impurity and purity) that governs access to the sacred.

The Ramban explains that these laws reflect the principle that the body's uncontrolled losses — whether of reproductive fluids or other emissions — represent a diminishment of life force, and contact with diminished vitality generates *tumah* in a system centered on the God of life.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Zav — Male Abnormal Discharge (15:2–15)

The *zav* is a man experiencing an abnormal genital discharge (*zivah*). He becomes a source of *tumah* that transmits to what he sits on, lies on, or touches. After the discharge ceases, he counts seven clean days, immerses in a *mikveh*, and on the eighth day brings two birds as offerings.

The Mishnah (Zavim 1:1) distinguishes between one, two, and three sightings of the discharge, each carrying different consequences. The Talmud (Niddah 35b) discusses the medical and halakhic criteria for distinguishing pathological discharge from normal emissions.

### Normal Emission — Keri (15:16–18)

A normal seminal emission renders a man *tamei* until evening, requiring immersion. Ezra the Scribe enacted a decree requiring immersion before Torah study and prayer after a seminal emission (*tevilat Ezra*), though the Talmud (Berakhot 22a) records that this enactment was eventually abolished because it had not taken root among the community.

### The Zavah — Female Abnormal Discharge (15:25–30)

A woman who bleeds outside her normal menstrual period becomes a *zavah*. Like the *zav*, she counts seven clean days and brings offerings. The laws of *zavah* are distinct from *niddah* in their requirements and stringencies.

| Category | Duration of Tumah | Offerings Required | Transmits by Contact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zav (male) | 7 clean days after cessation | Two birds | Yes |
| Keri (male) | Until evening | None | Limited |
| Zavah (female) | 7 clean days after cessation | Two birds | Yes |
| Niddah (female) | 7 days from onset | None (in Temple era) | Yes |

### Niddah — Menstrual Impurity (15:19–24)

The *niddah* laws are among the most consequential in Jewish practice. A menstruating woman is *temei'ah* for seven days. The Talmud (Niddah 66a) records that Jewish women voluntarily accepted upon themselves the stringency of counting seven clean days after every uterine bleeding, treating every case as potential *zavah*. This stringency, attributed to the daughters of Israel, became normative *halakhah*.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *niddah* are among the few purity laws that remain fully operative today, as they govern marital relations independent of the Temple. The Shulchan Arukh (Yoreh De'ah 183–200) devotes extensive treatment to these laws under the rubric of *taharat ha-mishpachah* (family purity). Monthly *mikveh* immersion after the conclusion of the *niddah* period remains a central practice in observant Jewish life.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Issurei Bi'ah, chapters 4–11) codifies the laws of *niddah* and *zavah* systematically. The distinction between biblical *niddah* (seven days) and the rabbinic stringency (seven clean days after cessation) is a major topic in halakhic literature.

The chapter's closing verse (15:31) provides the theological rationale: "You shall separate the children of Israel from their impurity, so that they do not die in their impurity by contaminating My Tabernacle that is in their midst." Purity is not asceticism but the maintenance of conditions necessary for the Divine Presence to dwell among the people.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 15 concludes Parashat Metzora. Together with chapters 12–14, it forms a comprehensive treatment of *tumah* originating from the human body — birth, skin afflictions, and discharges. This section prepares for chapter 16 (Yom Kippur), where the ultimate purification of the entire community is accomplished.
