## Mo'adei Hashem: The Appointed Times

Leviticus 23 presents the comprehensive calendar of *mo'adei Hashem* — the appointed times or festivals of the Lord. This chapter is the Torah's most complete festival listing, beginning with Shabbat and proceeding through Pesach, the Omer count, Shavuot, Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Sukkot, and Shemini Atzeret. The festivals are described as *mikra'ei kodesh* (holy convocations), days on which productive labor (*melekhet avodah*) is prohibited.

Rashi notes that Shabbat is listed first (23:3) to teach that whoever desecrates the festivals is considered as if he desecrated Shabbat, and whoever observes the festivals is considered as if he observed Shabbat. The chapter establishes the rhythm of sacred time that structures Jewish life across the year.

### The Omer Count and Shavuot (23:15–16)

"You shall count for yourselves from the day after the Sabbath (*mi-macharat ha-Shabbat*), from the day you bring the wave-offering of the Omer — seven complete weeks." The phrase "the day after the Sabbath" generated the most consequential calendrical dispute in Second Temple Judaism. The Pharisees (and rabbinic tradition) understood *Shabbat* here as the first day of Pesach, while the Sadducees interpreted it as the literal Sabbath, placing Shavuot always on a Sunday.

The Talmud (Menachot 65a–66a) records the detailed arguments between the Boethusians (allied with the Sadducees) and the rabbinic position. The rabbinic interpretation prevailed, fixing Shavuot on 6 Sivan and the Omer count beginning on 16 Nisan.

### The Four Species — *Arba'at HaMinim* (23:40)

"You shall take for yourselves on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree (*etrog*), branches of palm trees (*lulav*), boughs of thick-leaved trees (*hadassim*), and willows of the brook (*aravot*)." The Talmud (Sukkah 35a) identifies each species and elaborates on the requirements for their selection. The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 30:12) interprets the four species as representing four types of Jews — those with Torah and good deeds, those with one but not the other, and those with neither — bound together in unity.

### The Sukkah Dwelling (23:42–43)

"In sukkot you shall dwell for seven days... so that your generations will know that I caused the children of Israel to dwell in sukkot when I brought them out of the land of Egypt." The Talmud (Sukkah 11b) records the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer (who holds these were the actual *Clouds of Glory*) and Rabbi Akiva (who understands them as literal booths). The *halakhah* follows the understanding that the *sukkah* commemorates both physical and spiritual divine protection.

## Halakhic Significance

The festival laws of Leviticus 23 form the backbone of Tractates Pesachim, Sukkah, Rosh Hashanah, Yoma, Chagigah, and Beitzah. The Rambam systematizes them in Hilkhot Shevitat Yom Tov, Hilkhot Chametz u-Matzah, Hilkhot Shofar Sukkah ve-Lulav, and related sections. The Shulchan Arukh (Orach Chaim 242–669) provides the comprehensive practical guide.

The concept of *mo'adim* — divinely appointed times — establishes that the Jewish calendar is not merely commemorative but constitutive: the festivals create sanctified moments in which Israel encounters God anew each year.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 23 is a central section of Parashat Emor. The chapter is also read on the first and second days of Pesach, on Sukkot, and portions are read on the respective festivals throughout the year, making it one of the most frequently encountered chapters in the liturgical cycle.
