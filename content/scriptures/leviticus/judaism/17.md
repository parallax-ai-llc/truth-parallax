## Blood, Life, and the Centralization of Worship

Leviticus 17 serves as a transitional chapter connecting the Yom Kippur laws to the Holiness Code (*Kedoshim*). It addresses three critical matters: the prohibition of slaughtering sacrificial animals outside the Tabernacle (eliminating private altars), the absolute prohibition against consuming blood, and the prohibition of eating *nevelah* (an animal that died naturally) or *tereifah* (a torn animal).

The chapter's central theological declaration is found in verse 11: "For the life (*nefesh*) of the flesh is in the blood, and I have assigned it for you upon the altar to make atonement for your lives; for it is the blood that makes atonement by reason of the life." This verse is foundational to the entire sacrificial theology of the Torah.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Prohibition of Private Altars — *Bamot* (17:3–7)

Any Israelite who slaughters an ox, sheep, or goat outside the Tabernacle precincts without bringing it as a *shelamim* bears the guilt of bloodshed. The Sifra explains this was directed against those who had become accustomed to offering sacrifices to the *se'irim* (goat-demons) in Egypt — the Torah demands that all sacrifice be channeled exclusively to God.

| Period | Status of Private Altars |
|---|---|
| Desert/Mishkan | Forbidden (Lev. 17) |
| After entering the Land (Gilgal, Nov, Givon) | Permitted in certain periods |
| After the Temple was built | Permanently forbidden |

The Talmud (Zevachim 119a) traces the complex history of *bamot* (high places), which were alternately permitted and forbidden throughout Israelite history until the construction of Solomon's Temple permanently centralized worship.

### The Blood Prohibition (17:10–12)

"Any person from the House of Israel or from the strangers who sojourn among them who eats any blood — I will set My face against that soul." The severity of the language — God personally opposing the violator — and the punishment of *karet* underscores the gravity of this prohibition.

Rashi explains that the word *nefesh* (soul/life) is used because blood consumption was believed by ancient peoples to attract demonic forces. The Ramban adds a deeper dimension: consuming blood, which represents the animal soul, could corrupt the human soul, coarsening the spiritual nature of the one who ingests it.

### The Theological Rationale for Blood Prohibition (17:11)

The Rambam (Moreh Nevukhim 3:46) notes that blood consumption was central to pagan rites — pagans believed that eating sacrificial blood created communion with spiritual beings. The Torah's absolute prohibition severs this connection entirely. The blood belongs exclusively to God's altar as the instrument of *kaparah* (atonement).

## Halakhic Significance

The blood prohibition is one of the most fundamental dietary laws, codified extensively in the Shulchan Arukh (Yoreh De'ah 65–78). The process of *melichah* (salting meat) before cooking is specifically designed to extract residual blood. The Rambam (Hilkhot Ma'akhalot Asurot 6:1–10) details the various forms of blood covered by the prohibition.

The centralization of sacrifice established in this chapter provides the conceptual foundation for the prohibition of offering *korbanot* outside the Temple, which the Rambam (Hilkhot Ma'aseh HaKorbanot 18:3) counts as an ongoing prohibition. Even those who advocate rebuilding the Temple acknowledge that offerings cannot be brought at any alternative location.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 17 continues Parashat Acharei Mot. Its placement after the Yom Kippur legislation connects the annual corporate atonement with the daily discipline of proper slaughter and blood avoidance. The chapter's laws apply to both Israelites and *gerim* (resident aliens), foreshadowing the universal ethical demands of the Holiness Code that follows.
