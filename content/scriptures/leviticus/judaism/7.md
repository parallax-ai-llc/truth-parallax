## Concluding the Laws of Korbanot

Leviticus 7 completes the Torah's initial exposition of the sacrificial system, covering the *asham* procedures, the *todah* (thanksgiving offering), the *shelamim* consumption rules, and concluding with the prohibitions against eating *chelev* (forbidden fats) and *dam* (blood). This chapter forms the capstone of the seven-chapter sacrificial code that defines the *avodah* (Temple service).

The chapter introduces the *korban todah* (thanksgiving offering), which Rashi and the Talmud (Berakhot 54b) connect to four categories of people who must give thanks: those who crossed the sea, traversed the desert, recovered from illness, or were released from imprisonment. These correspond to the four occasions requiring *Birkat HaGomel* in contemporary practice.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Todah — Thanksgiving Offering (7:12–15)

The *todah* is a sub-category of *shelamim* accompanied by forty loaves — ten each of four types of bread, including both *chametz* and *matzah* varieties. The large quantity of bread, combined with the requirement to consume the offering by the following morning, necessitates inviting many guests, thereby publicizing God's kindness.

The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 9:1) states that in the messianic era, all offerings will cease except the *todah*, and all prayers will cease except thanksgiving. This teaching elevates gratitude as the most enduring form of worship.

### Time Limits for Consumption — *Notar* (7:15–18)

The *shelamim* may be eaten for two days and the intervening night, while the *todah* must be consumed by the following morning. Any meat remaining beyond its permitted time becomes *notar* (leftover) and must be burned. If at the time of offering the *kohen* intended to eat the meat beyond its time limit, the offering becomes *pigul* (rejected), and whoever eats it bears the sin of *karet*.

| Offering | Time Limit | If Violated |
|---|---|---|
| Todah | Until morning | Notar — must be burned |
| Shelamim | Two days and one night | Notar — must be burned |
| Any offering with improper intent | N/A | Pigul — karet |

The Talmud (Zevachim chapters 2–3) devotes extensive discussion to the laws of *pigul*, which the Mishnah (Zevachim 2:2) calls one of the three fundamental disqualifications of offerings.

### The Chelev and Dam Prohibitions (7:22–27)

The chapter reiterates and expands the permanent prohibition against eating *chelev* and *dam*, applying it universally "in all your dwelling places." Rashi notes that this language extends the prohibition beyond the Temple context to all times and places. The *chelev* prohibition applies specifically to the three sacrificial animal species (cattle, sheep, goats), while the blood prohibition extends to all species including birds and wild animals.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *pigul* and *notar* are pillars of Seder Kodashim, affecting virtually every discussion of sacrificial validity in the Talmud. The Rambam (Hilkhot Pesulei HaMukdashin, chapters 13–18) systematically codifies these disqualifications.

The *todah* principle survives robustly in modern practice through *Birkat HaGomel*, the public thanksgiving blessing recited in synagogue after surviving danger, and through the general emphasis on *hodayah* (gratitude) in Jewish liturgy, most notably in the *Modim* prayer of the *Amidah*.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 7:37–38 provides a formal summary colophon: "This is the Torah of the *olah*, the *minchah*, the *chattat*, the *asham*, the *milu'im*, and the *zevach shelamim*, which the Lord commanded Moshe at Mount Sinai." This closing formula concludes Parashat Tzav and the entire initial sacrificial code.
