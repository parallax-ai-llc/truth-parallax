## Vayikra: The Calling and the Olah

Leviticus opens with the word *Vayikra* ("And He called"), which gives the entire book its Hebrew name. This chapter introduces the *olah* (burnt offering), the most complete form of sacrifice in which the entire animal is consumed on the altar (*mizbeach*). The *olah* represents total dedication to God — nothing is retained by the offerer or the priests.

Rashi notes the significance of the small letter *aleph* in the word *Vayikra* as written in the Torah scroll. According to the Midrash, Moshe wished to describe God's communication with him using the less intimate *Vayikar* ("and He happened upon"), the term used for God's communication with Bilaam. God insisted on *Vayikra*, but Moshe, in his humility, wrote the *aleph* small.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "When a person (*adam*) among you brings an offering" (1:2)

The Talmud (Menachot 110a) derives from the word *adam* that just as Adam, the first man, never offered stolen property (since everything belonged to him), so too must all offerings come from legitimately owned possessions. Rashi emphasizes the voluntary nature of the *olah* — the phrase *yakrivenu* ("he shall bring it") indicates that the individual is encouraged but ultimately brings the offering of his own free will.

### The Laying of Hands — *Semichah* (1:4)

The offerer must lean both hands upon the head of the animal (*ve-samach yado*), a ritual known as *semichah*. The Mishnah (Menachot 9:8) specifies this requires full force with both hands. Ramban explains that the act symbolically transfers the offerer's intention and identification to the animal, creating a powerful moment of self-reflection: the offerer recognizes that what happens to the animal should, in strict justice, happen to him.

### Three Categories of Olah (1:3, 1:10, 1:14)

The chapter presents three tiers of burnt offering:

| Animal | Verse | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Cattle (*bakar*) | 1:3 | The most valuable offering |
| Sheep or goats (*tzon*) | 1:10 | A middle-tier offering |
| Turtledoves or pigeons (*tor/yonah*) | 1:14 | Available to the poor |

The Talmud (Menachot 110a) teaches that regarding the bird offering, the Torah uses the phrase *rei'ach nichoach* ("a pleasing aroma") just as it does for cattle, to show that "whether one offers much or little, it is equally acceptable, provided one directs one's heart to Heaven."

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of the *olah* form a cornerstone of Seder Kodashim in the Talmud, primarily in Tractate Zevachim. While the Temple no longer stands, the Talmud (Berakhot 26b) records the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that the daily prayers (*tefillot*) were instituted to correspond to the daily *tamid* offerings, which were themselves a form of *olah*. Thus the morning *Shacharit* corresponds to the morning *tamid* and the afternoon *Minchah* to the afternoon *tamid*.

The Rambam (Maimonides) in *Moreh Nevukhim* (Guide for the Perplexed 3:32) controversially suggested that sacrifices were a concession to the Israelites' familiarity with pagan worship. Ramban sharply disagrees, arguing that sacrifices embody a deep mystical truth about the relationship between the human soul and God, as evidenced by the offerings of Cain, Abel, and Noah — long before any pagan influence.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 1 opens Parashat Vayikra, the first reading in the book. It is customary in many communities to begin a child's Torah education with Sefer Vayikra, as the Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 7:3) explains: "Let the pure ones (children) come and engage in the study of purity (the laws of offerings)."
