## Arakhim: The Valuation of Sacred Pledges

Leviticus 27 concludes the book with the laws of *arakhim* (valuations), *nedarim* (vows), and *hekdesh* (consecration of property to the Temple). A person who vows the "value" (*erekh*) of a person to the Temple is obligated to pay a fixed sum based on age and gender. The chapter also addresses the consecration and redemption of animals, houses, and fields, and concludes with the laws of tithes and *cherem* (irrevocable consecration).

This final chapter may seem anticlimactic after the dramatic *tochachah* of chapter 26, but the Ramban explains its placement: after hearing the blessings and curses, a person might be moved to make a vow to the Temple. The Torah therefore provides the practical framework for such pledges, channeling religious enthusiasm into structured, sustainable commitment.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Erekh — Fixed Valuations (27:2–8)

When a person says "the *erekh* (value) of so-and-so is upon me," he is obligated to pay a fixed amount determined by the Torah based on age and gender:

| Age Range | Male | Female |
|---|---|---|
| 1 month to 5 years | 5 shekalim | 3 shekalim |
| 5 to 20 years | 20 shekalim | 10 shekalim |
| 20 to 60 years | 50 shekalim | 30 shekalim |
| 60 years and above | 15 shekalim | 10 shekalim |

The fixed-value system is notable: unlike a market valuation (*damim*), the *erekh* is unrelated to the individual's actual economic productivity. The Talmud (Arakhin 4a) derives that these valuations apply regardless of the physical condition of the person being valued. If the person who vowed is too poor to pay the full amount, the *kohen* assesses a reduced payment based on ability (27:8) — the Torah's commitment to economic accessibility even within the voluntary system of vows.

### Consecration of Animals (27:9–13)

An animal consecrated for sacrifice cannot be exchanged. If someone attempts to substitute another animal, both become holy (27:10). The Talmud (Temurah 2a–b) devotes an entire tractate — Tractate Temurah — to this principle, exploring its logical implications and boundary cases.

### Consecration and Redemption of Property (27:14–25)

Houses and fields may be consecrated to the Temple and later redeemed by adding a fifth (*chomesh*) to their assessed value. For ancestral fields (*sedeh achuzah*), the valuation is calculated according to the number of years remaining until the Jubilee. If unredeemed by the Jubilee, the field passes permanently to the *kohanim*. The Talmud (Arakhin 24a–25a) works through the complex calculations and the distinction between ancestral and purchased fields.

### The Three Things That Cannot Be Consecrated (27:26–29)

| Category | Reason | Verse |
|---|---|---|
| Firstborn animal (*bechor*) | Already belongs to God | 27:26 |
| *Cherem* (ban-consecrated property) | Irrevocably dedicated | 27:28–29 |
| Tithe of animals (*maaser behemah*) | Already designated | 27:32–33 |

The *cherem* is the most stringent form of consecration — it cannot be redeemed and goes permanently to the *kohanim* (for property) or constitutes an irrevocable ban (for persons condemned by the court). The Rambam (Hilkhot Arakhin 6:1–5) distinguishes between *cherem* directed to the priests and *cherem* directed to Temple maintenance (*bedek ha-bayit*).

### The Animal Tithe — *Maaser Behemah* (27:32–33)

Every tenth animal passing "under the rod" is consecrated as a tithe. The owner may not select or substitute — the tenth animal, whatever its quality, is the tithe. The Mishnah (Bekhorot 9:1–8) describes the process: animals are placed in a pen with a narrow exit, and as they emerge one by one, every tenth is marked with red paint.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of this chapter are codified primarily in the Rambam's Hilkhot Arakhin va-Charamim and in Tractate Arakhin. While most are not practically applicable without the Temple, the underlying principles — that vows create real obligations, that sacred pledges must be fulfilled, and that consecration carries permanence — continue to inform the halakhic treatment of *nedarim* (vows), codified in the Shulchan Arukh (Yoreh De'ah 203–239).

The chapter's concluding verse — "These are the commandments that the Lord commanded Moshe for the children of Israel on Mount Sinai" (27:34) — serves as the colophon for the entire book of Leviticus, sealing the Sinaitic legislation and marking the completion of the central book of the Torah.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 27 concludes Parashat Bechukotai and the entire book of Vayikra. In the synagogue, the congregation responds to its conclusion with the declaration *Chazak, chazak, ve-nitchazek!* — "Be strong, be strong, and let us strengthen one another!" — marking the completion of the third of the five books of the Torah.
