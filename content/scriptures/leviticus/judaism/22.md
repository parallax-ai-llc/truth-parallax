## Guarding the Sanctity of Offerings

Leviticus 22 addresses two related themes: the conditions under which *kohanim* may or may not eat sacred food (*terumah* and *kodashim*), and the requirements for unblemished animals in sacrifice. The chapter bridges priestly holiness (chapter 21) and the festival calendar (chapter 23), ensuring that the mechanics of sacred consumption and offering meet the standard of holiness demanded by God's presence.

The opening instruction — that the *kohanim* must "separate themselves from the sacred things of the children of Israel" when impure — establishes that even those consecrated for service must maintain vigilance. Holiness is not a permanent status that operates automatically but requires constant attention.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Priestly Impurity and Sacred Food (22:1–9)

A *kohen* who is *tamei* (ritually impure) is forbidden from eating *terumah* or *kodashim*. The chapter specifies various sources of impurity — *tzara'at*, *zav* discharge, corpse contact, seminal emission, contact with *sheratzim* — that temporarily disqualify the *kohen*.

The Mishnah (Terumot 7:1) discusses the practical consequences: a *kohen* who inadvertently eats *terumah* while impure must pay restitution plus a fifth. The verse's warning — "they shall not die thereby when they profane it" (22:9) — indicates that deliberate consumption of sacred food while impure carries the penalty of death at the hands of heaven.

### Who May Eat Terumah (22:10–16)

The chapter delineates precise rules about who shares in priestly food:

| Person | May Eat Terumah? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Kohen's household | Yes | Part of priestly family |
| Kohen's purchased servant | Yes | Considered part of household |
| Kohen's daughter married to non-kohen | No | Joined another household |
| Kohen's widowed/divorced daughter (childless, returned) | Yes | Restored to father's household |
| Non-kohen guest or hired worker | No | Not permanent household member |

The Talmud (Yevamot 70a) elaborates extensively on boundary cases, including the status of a *kohen*'s wife, children of intermarriage, and converts.

### Unblemished Offerings (22:17–25)

No animal with a *mum* (blemish) may be offered on the altar. The chapter lists specific disqualifications: blindness, broken limbs, skin diseases, crushed testicles, and similar defects. The Mishnah (Bekhorot 6:1–12) provides the comprehensive catalogue used by the Temple inspectors who examined each animal before acceptance.

### Minimum Age and Mother-Offspring Rule (22:27–28)

An animal must be at least eight days old before it is eligible for sacrifice (22:27). Additionally, an animal and its offspring may not be slaughtered on the same day (*oto ve-et beno*, 22:28). This law extends beyond sacrificial contexts to all slaughter and is codified in the Shulchan Arukh (Yoreh De'ah 16). The Ramban sees this as an expression of divine compassion — even in the act of slaughter, cruelty must be tempered.

### Kiddush and Chillul HaShem (22:32)

"You shall not profane My holy Name, and I shall be sanctified in the midst of the children of Israel — I am the Lord who sanctifies you." This verse is the primary source for the concepts of *Kiddush HaShem* (sanctification of God's Name) and *Chillul HaShem* (desecration of God's Name). The Rambam (Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah 5:1–4) derives from this verse the obligation to die rather than commit the three cardinal sins, and the broader obligation to conduct oneself in a manner that brings honor to God and Torah.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *terumah* consumption retain practical relevance in the Land of Israel, where *terumah* is still separated from produce (though currently burned rather than consumed, due to universal impurity). The principles of *Kiddush HaShem* and *Chillul HaShem* from verse 32 pervade every area of Jewish law and ethics, from business conduct to public behavior, constituting one of the most consequential verses in the entire Torah.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 22 continues Parashat Emor. The chapter serves as the practical implementation guide for the holiness principles of chapters 21, ensuring that the abstract demand for sanctity translates into concrete behavioral standards in both priestly service and daily conduct.
