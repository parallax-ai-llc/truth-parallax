## Overview

Leviticus (*Vayikra*) is the heart of the Torah in Jewish tradition. Traditionally, Jewish children began their Torah study with Leviticus, not Genesis, because "the pure ones (children) should begin with purity" (Vayikra Rabbah 7:3). The book contains the largest concentration of mitzvot (commandments) in the Torah and forms the basis of vast halakhic literature on sacrifice, purity, kashrut, and ethical conduct.

## Key Interpretive Themes

- **Korbanot (sacrifices)**: Not mere ritual but a means of drawing near (*karov*) to God; the root of the Hebrew word for sacrifice
- **Kashrut**: The dietary laws of chapter 11 form the basis of kosher practice
- **Tumah and taharah**: Ritual impurity and purity as categories of sacred status, not hygiene
- **Yom Kippur**: The Day of Atonement (ch. 16) remains the holiest day of the Jewish year
- **"Love your neighbor"**: Leviticus 19:18 — called by Rabbi Akiva "the great principle of the Torah"
