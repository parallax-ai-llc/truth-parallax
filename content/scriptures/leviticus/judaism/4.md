## The Chattat: Atonement for Inadvertent Sin

Leviticus 4 introduces the *chattat* (sin offering), brought for unintentional transgressions (*shogeg*) of negative commandments punishable by *karet* if done intentionally. The chapter makes a fundamental distinction: this offering addresses *inadvertent* sin — where the person did not know the act was forbidden or forgot the law. Intentional sin (*mezid*) cannot be atoned for through sacrifice alone.

Rashi explains the opening phrase *ki techeta bishgagah* ("when a soul sins unintentionally") as establishing the category of inadvertent violation of Torah prohibitions. The Talmud (Shabbat 69a) explores the parameters of *shogeg*, including cases where the person was unaware of the prohibition's existence versus cases where the person forgot.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Four Categories of Sinners (4:3, 4:13, 4:22, 4:27)

The chapter presents a hierarchy of sin offerings based on the offender's status:

| Offender | Animal | Blood Application |
|---|---|---|
| Anointed Kohen (High Priest) | Bull | Sprinkled inside the Heikhal |
| The entire community | Bull | Sprinkled inside the Heikhal |
| The *nasi* (tribal leader/king) | Male goat | Applied to outer altar |
| An ordinary individual | Female goat or sheep | Applied to outer altar |

Ramban observes that the severity of the offering corresponds to the offender's level of responsibility. The High Priest's sin "brings guilt upon the people" (4:3) because his error implies a failure in teaching or spiritual leadership.

### "When a Leader Sins" — *Asher nasi yecheta* (4:22)

Rashi, citing the Talmud (Horayot 10b), notes the unusual word *asher* ("when" or "fortunate"): "Fortunate (*ashrei*) is the generation whose leader brings a sin offering for his inadvertent error" — meaning a leader humble enough to acknowledge mistakes and seek atonement. This passage establishes the Jewish principle that leaders are held to a higher standard and must publicly acknowledge wrongdoing.

### The Mechanism of Atonement (4:26, 4:31, 4:35)

The recurring phrase *ve-khiper alav ha-kohen* ("and the priest shall make atonement for him") indicates that the offering effects *kaparah* (atonement/covering). The Ramban understands this as a process in which the offerer identifies with the animal, recognizing that the animal's blood and death represent what the offerer deserves in strict justice. The experience is meant to inspire *teshuvah* (repentance).

## Halakhic Significance

The *chattat* is central to Tractate Keritot, which identifies the thirty-six *karet*-liable transgressions for which a *chattat* is brought if committed inadvertently. The Mishnah (Keritot 1:1) enumerates these, including violations of Shabbat, forbidden sexual relations, and consumption of *chelev* or blood.

In the absence of the Temple, the Talmud (Berakhot 12b) teaches that confession (*vidui*) and repentance replace the sacrificial atonement. The *Al Chet* confessional prayer recited on Yom Kippur echoes the *chattat* framework by cataloguing sins and seeking divine forgiveness.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 4 begins a new section within Parashat Vayikra, shifting from voluntary offerings to mandatory ones. This transition reflects a key theological insight: relationship with God requires not only spontaneous expressions of devotion but also structured mechanisms for addressing moral failure.
