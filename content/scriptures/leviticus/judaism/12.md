## Tazria: Impurity Following Childbirth

Leviticus 12, one of the shortest chapters in the Torah with only eight verses, establishes the laws of *tumah* (ritual impurity) following childbirth. A woman who gives birth to a male child is *temei'ah* (ritually impure) for seven days, with an additional thirty-three days of *demei toharah* (blood of purification), totaling forty days. For a female child, the periods are doubled — fourteen days and sixty-six days, totaling eighty days. At the end of the purification period, the mother brings offerings to complete her restoration.

Rashi, following the Talmud (Niddah 31b), explains that the woman's impurity parallels *niddah* (menstrual) impurity — during the pain of labor, a woman may express regret about conception, and the impurity period provides a transitional framework. The Ramban rejects this psychologizing approach, instead connecting the impurity to the metaphysical reality that the departure of a soul from its close proximity to the Divine (the moment of birth as separation) generates *tumah*.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Circumcision on the Eighth Day (12:3)

"On the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised." This verse is the primary Torah source for *brit milah* (circumcision), one of the most fundamental commandments in Judaism. The Talmud (Shabbat 131b–132a) derives from this verse that circumcision overrides Shabbat — it must be performed on the eighth day even if that day falls on Shabbat.

The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 14:2) asks why the Torah placed the commandment of circumcision within the laws of childbirth impurity. The answer: so that the child enters the covenant after the mother has undergone purification, and so the *brit* is a moment of joy for all present.

### The Asymmetry Between Male and Female (12:2–5)

The doubled impurity period for a female birth has generated extensive commentary:

| Commentator | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Talmud (Niddah 31b) | Physiological differences in recovery |
| Ramban | Mystical correspondence to the nature of male and female |
| Abarbanel | Social and psychological considerations for mother and daughter |
| R. Hirsch | The female carries reproductive potential, intensifying the transition |

The Talmud's discussion in Tractate Niddah acknowledges the difficulty and presents multiple approaches without arriving at a single definitive answer, reflecting the Torah's capacity to hold layers of meaning.

### The Purification Offerings (12:6–8)

At the conclusion of her purification period, the mother brings a lamb as an *olah* and a pigeon or turtledove as a *chattat*. If she cannot afford a lamb, she may bring two birds. The Talmud (Keritot 8a) includes the *yoledet* (woman who has given birth) among those who are *mechusar kaparah* — requiring an offering to complete their purification before they may eat sacred food or enter the Temple.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of childbirth impurity are codified in the Rambam's Hilkhot Mechusarei Kaparah and in Tractate Niddah. While the sacrificial aspects are currently inapplicable, the *mikveh* immersion that concludes the impurity period remains a living practice connected to the laws of *niddah* and family purity (*taharat ha-mishpachah*).

The placement of *brit milah* in this chapter connects the beginning of individual Jewish identity to the broader framework of holiness and purity. The eighth day — one day beyond the seven-day natural cycle — symbolizes the transcendence of nature through covenant.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 12 opens Parashat Tazria, which is often combined with Parashat Metzora for the weekly reading. The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 14:1) connects the opening of this parashah to the creation narrative: "Just as the creation of man came after the creation of animals, so the laws concerning humans come after the laws concerning animals" (the dietary laws of chapter 11).
