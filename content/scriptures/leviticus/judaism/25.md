## Behar: The Land's Sabbath and Economic Justice

Leviticus 25 introduces two revolutionary economic institutions: the *Shemitah* (Sabbatical year, every seventh year) and the *Yovel* (Jubilee, every fiftieth year). These laws establish the radical principle that the land belongs ultimately to God and that economic inequalities must be periodically corrected. The chapter opens with the unusual phrase "on Mount Sinai" (*be-Har Sinai*), prompting Rashi's famous question: "What does the *Shemitah* have to do with Mount Sinai?" — answered by the principle that just as *Shemitah* with all its details was given at Sinai, so too were all commandments given in their full detail at Sinai.

The theological foundation is explicit: "The land shall not be sold in perpetuity, for the land is Mine; you are but strangers and sojourners with Me" (25:23). This verse overturns the assumption of absolute property rights and places all economic activity within a covenantal framework.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Shemitah Year (25:1–7)

Every seventh year, the land must rest — no plowing, sowing, pruning, or harvesting. Whatever grows of its own accord is *hefker* (ownerless), available to all. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 39a) responds to those who question how the nation can survive without farming: "The Holy One said to Israel: Sow for six years and release the land in the seventh, so that you will know that the land is Mine."

| Year | Agricultural Status | Economic Provisions |
|---|---|---|
| Years 1–6 | Normal cultivation | Normal commerce |
| Year 7 (Shemitah) | Land lies fallow | Debts released (per Deut. 15) |
| Year 50 (Yovel) | Land lies fallow | Ancestral land returned; slaves freed |

### The Jubilee — Yovel (25:8–17)

After seven cycles of seven years, the fiftieth year is proclaimed with the blast of the *shofar* on Yom Kippur: "You shall proclaim liberty (*deror*) throughout the land to all its inhabitants" (25:10) — a verse famously inscribed on the American Liberty Bell. All ancestral land returns to its original tribal allocation, and Hebrew slaves go free.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Shemitah ve-Yovel 10:3) holds that the Jubilee has not been operative since the Ten Tribes were exiled, because the full quorum of all tribes dwelling in their respective territories is required. The Talmud (Arakhin 32b) discusses this requirement in detail.

### "Do Not Wrong One Another" — *Ona'ah* (25:14–17)

The Torah prohibits both monetary overcharging (*ona'at mamon*) and verbal mistreatment (*ona'at devarim*). The Talmud (Bava Metzia 58b) teaches that *ona'at devarim* is the more severe: one who shames another in public has no share in the World to Come. Specific examples include reminding a convert of his ancestors' practices, or a *baal teshuvah* of his former conduct.

### Redemption of Land and Property (25:25–34)

If an Israelite must sell ancestral land due to poverty, a close relative (*go'el*) has the right of redemption, or the seller himself may redeem it when able. The laws of *geulah* (redemption) are elaborated in Tractate Arakhin, chapter 9. Levitical cities have distinct rules: houses within walled Levitical cities may always be redeemed, and the fields surrounding Levitical cities may never be sold.

### The Hebrew Servant — *Eved Ivri* (25:39–55)

An Israelite who sells himself into servitude due to poverty must be treated not as a slave but as a hired worker. The master may not impose degrading labor (*avodat parech*). At the Jubilee, all Hebrew servants go free. The chapter concludes: "For the children of Israel are servants to Me; they are My servants whom I brought out of the land of Egypt" (25:55) — a Jew cannot be permanently enslaved because he already belongs to God.

## Halakhic Significance

The *Shemitah* remains operative in the Land of Israel today and profoundly affects Israeli agriculture. The Rambam codifies these laws in Hilkhot Shemitah ve-Yovel. The *heter mechirah* (temporary sale of land to a non-Jew) instituted by Rav Kook and the *otzar beit din* (court-supervised distribution) system represent modern halakhic solutions to the practical challenges of observing *Shemitah* in a modern agricultural economy.

The *Shemitah* embodies the principle of *bitachon* (trust in God) — refraining from farming requires faith that God will provide. The Midrash praises those who observe *Shemitah* as *gibborei koach* (mighty in strength), for they watch their fields lie fallow and remain silent.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 25 constitutes the majority of Parashat Behar, often combined with Bechukotai. The parashah's placement near the end of Vayikra signals that the Holiness Code's ultimate concern is not only personal morality but societal justice — holiness requires economic structures that prevent permanent poverty and preserve human dignity.
