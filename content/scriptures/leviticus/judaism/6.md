## Tzav: Instructions to the Priests

Leviticus 6 opens the second major section of sacrificial legislation, shifting from the perspective of the offerer (*Vayikra*) to instructions directed to the *kohanim* themselves. The chapter begins with additional laws of the *olah* and proceeds through the priestly procedures for the *minchah*, the inauguration offering of the High Priest, and the *chattat*.

Rashi, citing the Sifra, notes that the word *tzav* ("command") implies urgency and applies for all generations. Rabbi Shimon adds that the Torah is especially urgent where there is potential financial loss (*chisaron kis*) — since the *olah* is entirely burned with no portion to the priests, extra encouragement is needed.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Eternal Fire — *Eish Tamid* (6:6)

"A perpetual fire shall be kept burning on the altar; it shall not go out." This verse establishes one of the Torah's most evocative images. The Talmud (Yoma 45b) discusses the practical arrangements: although fire descended from heaven, there was a positive commandment to maintain a human-kindled fire as well. Three wood arrangements (*ma'arakhot*) were maintained on the altar.

The *eish tamid* has become a powerful metaphor in Jewish thought. The Zohar interprets the altar fire as corresponding to the fire of Torah study and prayer that must never be extinguished in the Jewish heart. Many synagogues maintain a *ner tamid* (eternal light) as a symbolic continuation of this flame.

### Removal of Ashes — *Terumat HaDeshen* (6:3–4)

The daily removal of ashes from the altar was a priestly service performed at dawn. The *kohen* would first don his linen garments, remove a portion of ash (*terumat ha-deshen*), then change into lesser garments to carry the remaining ashes outside the camp. The Mishnah (Tamid 1:2–2:2) describes the elaborate daily procedure and the eagerness of young priests to perform this service, which once led to a dangerous race up the altar ramp.

### The Minchah of the Kohanim (6:13–16)

The High Priest's daily *minchah* offering — the *chavitin* — consisted of a tenth of an *ephah* of fine flour prepared with oil, half offered in the morning and half in the evening. Unlike the Israelite's *minchah*, the priestly offering was entirely burned: "Every meal offering of a *kohen* shall be entirely consumed; it shall not be eaten" (6:16). Ramban explains that since the *kohanim* are themselves sanctified to serve, their offering requires complete dedication, paralleling the *olah*.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *terumat ha-deshen* are codified by the Rambam (Hilkhot Temidin u-Musafin 2:10–15). The requirement to change garments between the ash removal and the transport teaches, as the Talmud (Shabbat 114a) observes, that "garments in which one cooked for the master should not be used to pour wine" — a principle of respect and propriety in service.

The concept of the *eish tamid* informs the halakhic requirement of regular Torah study. The Talmud (Yoma 21a) lists the miraculous fire among the features of the First Temple, and its absence in the Second Temple is counted among the things lacking.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 6 begins Parashat Tzav, the second parashah in Vayikra. While Parashat Vayikra addresses the Israelites, Parashat Tzav is directed to Aaron and his sons, providing the priestly manual for the sacrificial service. Tzav is traditionally read on the Shabbat before Pesach (Shabbat HaGadol in some years), connecting the priestly preparation to the national preparation for redemption.
