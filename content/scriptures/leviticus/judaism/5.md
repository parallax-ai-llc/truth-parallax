## The Asham and the Sliding-Scale Offering

Leviticus 5 introduces two distinct categories: the *korban oleh ve-yored* (sliding-scale offering, 5:1–13), which adjusts to the offerer's financial means, and the *asham* (guilt offering, 5:14–26), brought for specific categories of trespass. This chapter bridges the *chattat* of chapter 4 with the fuller *asham* laws, demonstrating the Torah's sensitivity to economic disparity while maintaining rigorous standards of accountability.

Rashi notes that the sliding-scale offering applies to three specific cases enumerated at the chapter's opening: failure to testify when one has relevant knowledge (5:1), contact with ritual impurity and then entering the Temple or eating sacred food (5:2–3), and an unfulfilled oath (5:4).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Sliding-Scale Offering (5:6–11)

The Torah provides three tiers based on financial ability:

| Economic Status | Offering | Verse |
|---|---|---|
| Able | Female sheep or goat | 5:6 |
| Cannot afford animal | Two turtledoves or pigeons | 5:7 |
| Cannot afford birds | Tenth of an ephah of flour | 5:11 |

The Talmud (Keritot 27b–28a) discusses the precise threshold at which a person qualifies for the lesser offering. The flour offering brought by the poorest must contain no oil or frankincense — Rashi explains that since this offering addresses sin, it would be inappropriate to adorn it as though it were a festive gift.

### The Asham Me'ilah — Trespass Against Sacred Property (5:15–16)

The *asham me'ilah* is brought when someone unwittingly derives benefit from *hekdesh* (consecrated property). The offerer must bring a ram of specified value, make restitution for the misappropriated sacred property, and add a fifth (*chomesh*) of its value as a penalty. The laws of *me'ilah* are elaborated in the Talmudic tractate of the same name.

### The Asham Talui — The Uncertain Guilt Offering (5:17–19)

When a person suspects but is uncertain whether he committed a sin requiring a *chattat*, he brings an *asham talui* (a guilt offering of uncertainty). Rabbi Eliezer in the Mishnah (Keritot 6:3) was known for bringing an *asham talui* daily, reflecting extreme piety and awareness of the constant possibility of inadvertent sin.

## Halakhic Significance

The *asham* is distinct from the *chattat* in several key ways codified by the Rambam (Hilkhot Shegagot 1:4): it always requires a ram (*ayil*), it involves monetary restitution, and it addresses a narrower category of offenses. The Talmud (Zevachim 5a) classifies the *asham* among the *kodshei kodashim* (most holy offerings), restricting its consumption to male *kohanim* within the *azarah*.

The principle embedded in the sliding-scale offering — that obligation is calibrated to capacity — reverberates throughout *halakhah*, from the laws of *tzedakah* to the festival obligations, ensuring that poverty never becomes a barrier to atonement and spiritual wholeness.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 5 concludes Parashat Vayikra. The parashah thus spans the full range of offerings from voluntary devotion (*olah*) to mandatory atonement (*chattat* and *asham*), providing a comprehensive introduction to the sacrificial system.
