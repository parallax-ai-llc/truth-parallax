## The Milu'im: Inaugurating the Priesthood

Leviticus 8 narrates the seven-day consecration ceremony (*milu'im*) of Aaron and his sons as *kohanim*, fulfilling the instructions given in Exodus 29. The term *milu'im* literally means "fillings," referring to the filling of the priests' hands with the inaugural offerings — a powerful image of being entrusted with sacred responsibility. Moshe serves as the officiant, performing sacrificial functions for the only time in the Torah.

Rashi notes that these events took place on the twenty-third of Adar, seven days before the first of Nisan, when the Mishkan was permanently erected. The Talmud (Megillah 10a) describes the first of Nisan as a day that "received ten crowns" — ten distinctions marking the inauguration of the Tabernacle service.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Public Assembly (8:3)

"Assemble the entire congregation at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting." The Sifra asks how the entire nation could fit in such a limited space and answers that this was one of the occasions when "a small space held a large crowd" — a miraculous compression. This demonstrates that the consecration required public witness, establishing the priesthood's legitimacy before all Israel.

### The Anointing Oil — *Shemen HaMishchah* (8:10–12)

Moshe anointed the Mishkan, its vessels, and Aaron with the sacred anointing oil. The Talmud (Horayot 12a) describes the anointing of the High Priest as being applied in the shape of the Greek letter chi (resembling an X). The Talmud (Keritot 5b) teaches that the original anointing oil prepared by Moshe miraculously persisted and was used throughout the First Temple period.

### The Three Offerings (8:14–29)

Three offerings were brought during the consecration:

| Offering | Animal | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Chattat | Bull | Purification of the altar |
| Olah | Ram | Complete dedication to God |
| Eil HaMilu'im | Second ram | The consecration offering proper |

The blood of the consecration ram was applied to Aaron's right ear, right thumb, and right big toe — symbolizing, as Ramban explains, the sanctification of hearing (to receive God's word), action (to perform the service), and walking (to approach the sacred precincts).

### Seven Days of Seclusion (8:33–35)

Aaron and his sons were commanded to remain at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting for seven days: "You shall not go out from the entrance of the Tent of Meeting for seven days." The Talmud (Yoma 3b) uses this as the model for the High Priest's seven-day seclusion before Yom Kippur, during which he rehearsed every detail of the Day of Atonement service.

## Halakhic Significance

The *milu'im* ceremony established the hereditary priesthood as a permanent institution in Israel. The Rambam (Hilkhot Klei HaMikdash 1:7–12) codifies the anointing procedures and notes that after the anointing oil was hidden, future High Priests were invested through wearing the eight priestly garments (*bigdei kehunah*) rather than anointing.

The seven-day consecration period serves as a paradigm for transitions into sacred roles throughout *halakhah*, including the seven days of mourning (*shivah*) and the seven days of wedding celebration (*sheva berakhot*).

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 8 begins Parashat Shemini (or is the conclusion of Parashat Tzav in some traditions). The narrative of the *milu'im* bridges the legal material of chapters 1–7 with the dramatic events of chapters 9–10, where the priesthood is activated and immediately tested by the tragedy of Nadav and Avihu.
