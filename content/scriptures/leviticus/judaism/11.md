## The Laws of Kashrut

Leviticus 11 establishes the foundational dietary laws (*kashrut*) of the Torah, delineating which animals, birds, fish, and insects may and may not be eaten. This chapter marks the transition from the sacrificial laws to the purity laws (*tumah ve-taharah*), connecting what Israel offers to God with what Israel takes into its own body. The underlying principle is stated clearly: "For I am the Lord your God; sanctify yourselves and be holy, for I am holy" (11:44).

Rashi connects the juxtaposition of dietary laws with the sacrificial code: just as the offerings distinguish between sacred and profane, so the dietary laws distinguish between the permitted (*tahor*) and the forbidden (*tamei*).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Signs of Permitted Land Animals (11:3)

Two signs identify a permitted land animal: it must have fully split hooves (*mafresset parsah*) and chew its cud (*ma'alat gerah*). The Torah enumerates four animals possessing only one sign:

| Animal | Hebrew | Has Split Hooves | Chews Cud |
|---|---|---|---|
| Camel | *Gamal* | No | Yes |
| Hyrax | *Shafan* | No | Yes |
| Hare | *Arnevet* | No | Yes |
| Pig | *Chazir* | Yes | No |

The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 13:5) interprets these four animals allegorically as the four kingdoms that would oppress Israel: Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome. The pig, which displays its split hooves outwardly while lacking the internal sign of cud-chewing, symbolizes hypocrisy — appearing righteous externally while corrupt within.

### Fish: Fins and Scales (11:9–12)

Permitted aquatic creatures must have both *snapir* (fins) and *kaskeset* (scales). The Talmud (Chullin 66b) teaches that every fish with scales also has fins, but not vice versa — the Torah mentions fins to "increase the Torah" and provide additional learning opportunities. The Talmud (Niddah 51b) defines *kaskeset* as scales that can be removed by hand or knife without tearing the skin.

### The Prohibition of Swarming Creatures — *Sheratzim* (11:41–43)

The chapter emphatically prohibits consuming *sheratzim* (swarming creatures), repeating the prohibition multiple times. The Talmud (Makkot 16b) counts the consumption of certain insects as involving multiple prohibitions — eating a single ant can violate up to six negative commandments.

## Halakhic Significance

The dietary laws of Leviticus 11 form the backbone of the *kashrut* system codified in the Shulchan Arukh (Yoreh De'ah 79–86). Tractate Chullin in the Talmud provides the comprehensive halakhic framework, including detailed discussions of species identification, the status of anomalous animals, and the principles of *mesorah* (tradition) for bird identification.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Ma'akhalot Asurot, chapters 1–2) systematizes these laws, while the Ramban (on 11:13) argues that the prohibited animals possess negative spiritual qualities that affect the soul of the one who consumes them. The Rambam, by contrast, tends to emphasize health-related rationales while acknowledging the ultimate inscrutableness of divine decree.

The concept of *kedushat Yisrael* (the holiness of Israel) is inextricable from dietary discipline. Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch interprets the dietary laws as training in self-mastery — the capacity to say no to appetite is the foundation of moral freedom.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 11 continues Parashat Shemini. The placement of dietary laws immediately after the death of Nadav and Avihu suggests that holiness requires not only proper worship but proper daily conduct. The chapter's closing declaration — "I am the Lord who brought you up from the land of Egypt to be your God; you shall be holy, for I am holy" (11:45) — links the Exodus to dietary observance.
