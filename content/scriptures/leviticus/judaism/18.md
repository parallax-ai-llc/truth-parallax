## Arayot: The Sexual Ethics of Holiness

Leviticus 18 enumerates the prohibited sexual relationships (*arayot*), framed by a powerful literary structure: the chapter opens and closes with warnings against imitating the practices of Egypt and Canaan. The *arayot* prohibitions are among the three cardinal sins for which a Jew must accept martyrdom rather than transgress (Sanhedrin 74a), alongside murder and idolatry.

The chapter begins with the foundational principle: "You shall keep My statutes and My laws, which a person shall do and live by them — I am the Lord" (18:5). The Talmud (Sanhedrin 74a) derives from "and live by them" — and not die by them — the principle of *pikuach nefesh*: that preservation of life overrides nearly all Torah commandments.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "According to the Practices of the Land of Egypt... and Canaan" (18:3)

The Sifra explains that Egypt and Canaan were the most morally corrupt of the ancient nations, and Israel dwelt in Egypt and was heading toward Canaan — thus the specific warnings. Rashi adds that the Torah does not merely prohibit imitating their practices generally, but specifically addresses sexual norms that had been normalized in those cultures.

### The Enumerated Prohibitions (18:6–23)

The chapter lists prohibited relationships in a structured sequence:

| Category | Verses | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Parental relations | 18:7–8 | Mother, father's wife |
| Sibling relations | 18:9–11 | Sister, half-sister |
| Extended family | 18:12–16 | Aunt, daughter-in-law |
| Affinity relations | 18:17–18 | Woman and her daughter, two sisters |
| Other prohibitions | 18:19–23 | During niddah, Molech, male intercourse, bestiality |

The Talmud (Yevamot 2a and throughout) extensively discusses the parameters, extensions, and ramifications of each prohibition. The tractate derives that many secondary relatives (*sheniyot*) are also prohibited by rabbinic decree as a safeguard around the Torah prohibitions.

### Molech Worship (18:21)

The prohibition against "passing children through fire to Molech" is placed among the sexual prohibitions. Ramban explains this placement: Molech worship involved a form of spiritual infidelity analogous to sexual betrayal — offering one's children to a false deity is the ultimate violation of the parent-child-God relationship. The Rambam (Hilkhot Avodah Zarah 6:3) describes the Molech ritual and classifies it as a specific form of idolatry.

### The Land's Response (18:25–28)

The chapter concludes with the extraordinary image of the Land of Israel itself vomiting out its inhabitants for sexual immorality: "The land became contaminated, and I visited its iniquity upon it, and the land vomited out its inhabitants." This personification of the land establishes a unique metaphysical link between the moral behavior of the inhabitants and the land's capacity to sustain them.

## Halakhic Significance

The *arayot* prohibitions are codified by the Rambam in Hilkhot Issurei Bi'ah (chapters 1–3) and in the Shulchan Arukh (Even HaEzer 15–16). They constitute one of the foundational pillars of Jewish family law. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 57b–58a) considers some of these prohibitions to be part of the Noahide laws, applicable to all humanity.

Leviticus 18 is read publicly on Yom Kippur afternoon (*Minchah*), underscoring the connection between the day of ultimate spiritual reckoning and the imperative of sexual holiness. The choice to read these laws before the entire community on the holiest day of the year reflects their central importance to the Jewish moral vision.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 18 concludes Parashat Acharei Mot. The movement from Yom Kippur (chapter 16) through blood laws (chapter 17) to sexual ethics (chapter 18) traces a progression from ritual purity to moral purity, establishing that holiness encompasses every dimension of human life.
