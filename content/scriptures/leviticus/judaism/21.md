## Kedushat Kohanim: The Sanctity of the Priesthood

Leviticus 21 establishes the heightened standards of holiness required of the *kohanim* (priests), addressing three areas: restrictions on contact with the dead (*tumat met*), marriage limitations, and physical disqualifications (*mumim*) that prevent a *kohen* from performing the Temple service. The underlying principle is stated clearly: "They shall be holy to their God... for they offer the fire-offerings of the Lord, the bread of their God, and they shall be holy" (21:6).

Rashi observes that the chapter's opening instruction — "Speak to the priests, the sons of Aaron, and say to them" — uses the unusual doubling of "speak" (*emor*) and "say" (*ve-amarta*), which the Talmud (Yevamot 114a) interprets as an admonition to adult priests to educate (*lehazhir*) their children in these laws. This is one of the primary sources for the obligation of *chinuch* (educating minors in mitzvah observance).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Contact with the Dead (21:1–4)

An ordinary *kohen* is forbidden from becoming *tamei* (ritually impure) through contact with a corpse, except for seven close relatives:

| Relative | Hebrew |
|---|---|
| Mother | *Imo* |
| Father | *Aviv* |
| Son | *Beno* |
| Daughter | *Bito* |
| Brother | *Achiv* |
| Unmarried sister | *Achoto ha-betulah* |
| Wife | Derived by the Sifra from "she'ero" (21:2) |

The Talmud (Nazir 43b) establishes the principle of *met mitzvah* — an unattended corpse — which overrides even the High Priest's prohibition against corpse contact, teaching that human dignity (*kevod ha-beriyot*) takes precedence.

### The Kohen Gadol's Stricter Standards (21:10–15)

The High Priest may not become impure for any relative, including parents. He may not let his hair grow loose or rend his garments in mourning. He must marry only a *betulah* (virgin) from the Jewish people — not a widow, divorcee, *chalalah*, or *zonah*.

The Talmud (Yoma 13a) discusses the practical application: during the seven days before Yom Kippur, a backup wife was prepared for the High Priest in case his wife died, so the service would not be interrupted. The Ramban sees the High Priest's restrictions as flowing from his permanent state of nearness to the Divine — one who stands constantly before God cannot be touched by death's impurity.

### Physical Blemishes — *Mumim* (21:17–23)

A *kohen* with certain physical blemishes may not perform the altar service, though he may eat from the sacred portions. The Mishnah (Bekhorot 7:1–6) enumerates 142 specific blemishes. The Rambam (Hilkhot Bi'at HaMikdash 6–8) codifies the complete list.

This is not a statement about the inherent worth of disabled persons — the Torah explicitly states the blemished *kohen* retains his sanctity and may eat sacred food. Rather, the aesthetic wholeness of the service reflects the perfection of the Divine, much as offerings themselves must be unblemished.

## Halakhic Significance

The priestly restrictions regarding corpse impurity remain operative today. Contemporary *kohanim* avoid entering cemeteries, attending funerals (except for the seven close relatives), and being under the same roof as a corpse. These laws are codified in the Shulchan Arukh (Yoreh De'ah 369–374) and are widely observed.

Marriage restrictions also remain in force: a *kohen* today may not marry a divorcee or a *gerushah*. The Shulchan Arukh (Even HaEzer 6–7) codifies these restrictions, which have significant practical implications in Jewish family law and sometimes create painful pastoral situations.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 21 opens Parashat Emor, the ninth parashah in Vayikra. The movement from the universal holiness demanded of all Israel (Kedoshim) to the specific holiness demanded of the *kohanim* (Emor) illustrates the concentric circles of sanctity that structure the Torah's worldview: Israel, Levites, *kohanim*, *kohen gadol* — each circle closer to the Divine and carrying greater responsibility.
