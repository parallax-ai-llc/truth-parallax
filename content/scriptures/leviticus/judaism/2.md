## The Minchah: Offering of the Poor

Leviticus 2 details the *minchah* (meal or grain offering), the only non-animal offering among the primary *korbanot*. The *minchah* consists of fine flour (*solet*), oil (*shemen*), and frankincense (*levonah*). A portion (*kometz*) is burned on the altar, and the remainder is eaten by the *kohanim*.

Rashi, citing the Talmud (Menachot 104b), observes that the Torah uses the word *nefesh* ("soul") specifically in connection with the *minchah* — "When a *nefesh* brings a meal offering." This is because the *minchah* is typically brought by a poor person who cannot afford an animal. God says, as it were, "I consider it as if he offered his very soul."

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Five Types of Minchah (2:1–7)

The chapter delineates five variations of the meal offering:

| Type | Hebrew | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Raw flour | *Solet* | Uncooked fine flour with oil and frankincense |
| Oven-baked | *Ma'afeh tannur* | Unleavened loaves or wafers |
| Pan-fried | *Machavat* | Prepared on a flat griddle |
| Deep-fried | *Marcheshet* | Cooked in a deep pan |
| First fruits | *Bikkurim* | Offering of new grain |

The Talmud (Menachot chapters 5–6) elaborates extensively on the precise preparation requirements, quantities, and ritual procedures for each type.

### The Prohibition of Leaven and Honey (2:11)

No *minchah* may contain *chametz* (leaven) or *devash* (honey). The Sefer HaChinuch (mitzvah 117) explains that leaven symbolizes the evil inclination (*yetzer hara*) — puffed up and inflated — while honey represents excessive sensory pleasure. Offerings to God must embody simplicity and sincerity. The Talmud (Berakhot 17a) echoes this with Rabbi Alexandri's prayer: "Master of the Universe, it is revealed before You that our will is to do Your will. And what prevents us? The leaven in the dough" — a metaphor for the *yetzer hara*.

### The Requirement of Salt (2:13)

Every offering must include salt — the *brit melach* ("covenant of salt"). Ramban explains that salt preserves and endures, symbolizing the eternal nature of God's covenant. The Talmud (Menachot 21a) derives from "the salt of the covenant of your God" that salt must never be absent from the altar. The custom of dipping bread in salt at the Shabbat table is connected to this verse, as the table is compared to the altar (Berakhot 55a).

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of the *minchah* are treated extensively in Tractate Menachot. The procedure of *kemitzah* (taking a fistful of the flour mixture) is one of the most technically demanding priestly actions. The Mishnah (Menachot 1:1) discusses how improper intention during *kemitzah* can invalidate the offering.

In contemporary practice, the *minchah* offering is recalled in the daily *Minchah* prayer. The connection is preserved in the *Korbanot* section recited during morning prayers, where the *minchah* procedures are studied as a form of spiritual service replacing the actual offerings.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 2 continues within Parashat Vayikra. The juxtaposition of the *minchah* immediately after the *olah* teaches that God values the humble offering of the poor equally with the wealthy person's animal sacrifice. This egalitarian principle is foundational to Jewish worship.
