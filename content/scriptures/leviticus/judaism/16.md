## Acharei Mot: The Yom Kippur Service

Leviticus 16 is the theological and liturgical heart of the entire book, prescribing the *avodah* (service) of Yom Kippur — the only day the High Priest (*kohen gadol*) enters the *Kodesh HaKodashim* (Holy of Holies). The chapter opens with reference to the death of Nadav and Avihu, establishing that entry into the innermost sanctuary requires strict divine protocol, not spontaneous enthusiasm.

The Mishnah (Yoma 1:1) begins seven days before Yom Kippur, when the *kohen gadol* is sequestered and trained in every detail of the service. The entire Talmudic tractate Yoma is devoted to this chapter, reflecting its paramount importance. The detailed recounting of the *avodah* during the Yom Kippur *Musaf* prayer remains one of the most dramatic moments in the synagogue liturgy.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Entry into the Holy of Holies (16:2–4)

God warns Aaron: "He shall not come at all times into the holy place." Entry is permitted only once a year, on Yom Kippur, with the incense cloud (*ketoret*) shielding the *kohen gadol* from the divine presence above the *kapporet* (ark cover). The Talmud (Yoma 53a) describes how the *kohen gadol* prepared the incense inside the Holy of Holies, creating a cloud that filled the chamber.

The *kohen gadol* enters wearing simple white linen garments (*bigdei lavan*) rather than his ornate golden vestments. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 26a) explains: "A prosecutor cannot serve as a defender" — gold would recall the Golden Calf and is therefore inappropriate when seeking atonement.

### The Two Goats and Azazel (16:7–10, 16:20–22)

Two identical goats are presented: lots are cast, designating one "for the Lord" (*la-Shem*) as a *chattat*, and one "for Azazel" (*la-Azazel*), sent into the wilderness bearing the sins of the people.

| Goat | Designation | Procedure |
|---|---|---|
| For the Lord | *La-Shem* | Slaughtered as chattat; blood sprinkled in Holy of Holies |
| For Azazel | *La-Azazel* | Confession recited over it; sent to wilderness cliff |

The identity of *Azazel* is debated. Rashi, following the Sifra, understands it as the name of a strong, rocky cliff. Ibn Ezra cryptically hints that the explanation involves mystical knowledge ("when you are thirty-three you will understand"), which Ramban interprets as a reference to the practice of giving a portion to the forces of impurity to satisfy them, so they do not accuse Israel before God.

### The Three Confessions — *Vidui* (16:6, 16:11, 16:21)

The *kohen gadol* confesses three times: first for himself and his household, then for the entire priestly tribe, and finally for all Israel. The Mishnah (Yoma 3:8, 4:2, 6:2) preserves the formula: "Please, O God (*Ana HaShem*), I have sinned, I have transgressed, I have rebelled before You..." When the assembled people heard the *kohen gadol* pronounce the Tetragrammaton (the ineffable Name of God), they would prostrate themselves and respond: "Blessed be the Name of His glorious kingdom forever and ever."

### "For on this day He shall atone for you" (16:30)

This verse establishes Yom Kippur's unique atoning power. The Mishnah (Yoma 8:9) defines its scope: Yom Kippur atones for transgressions between man and God, but for transgressions between man and his fellow, Yom Kippur does not atone until one has appeased the wronged party. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah derives this principle from the verse's specific language.

## Halakhic Significance

The Rambam (Hilkhot Avodat Yom HaKippurim) codifies the entire service in meticulous detail, preserving the procedure for the messianic era. In the absence of the Temple, the Talmud (Yoma 85b) teaches that *teshuvah* (repentance) and the day itself provide atonement, along with prayer, charity, and fasting.

The five *inuyim* (afflictions) of Yom Kippur — fasting from food and drink, abstaining from washing, anointing, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations — are derived by the rabbis from the phrase "you shall afflict your souls" (16:29, 16:31). These are codified in Shulchan Arukh, Orach Chaim 611–618.

## Parashah Context

Leviticus 16 opens Parashat Acharei Mot. It is read as the Torah portion on Yom Kippur morning, making it one of the most frequently heard chapters in the liturgical year. The chapter stands at the structural center of Leviticus, linking the purity laws (chapters 11–15) with the Holiness Code (chapters 17–26).
