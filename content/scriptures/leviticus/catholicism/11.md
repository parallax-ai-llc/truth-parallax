## Clean and Unclean Animals

Leviticus 11 presents the dietary laws (*kashrut*) distinguishing clean from unclean animals. Land animals must chew the cud and have split hooves (vv. 3–8); aquatic creatures must have fins and scales (vv. 9–12); various birds of prey and scavengers are prohibited (vv. 13–19); and among winged insects, only those with jointed legs for hopping are permitted (vv. 20–23). The chapter concludes with regulations about carcass contamination. Catholic theology holds that these ceremonial laws were abrogated by the New Covenant but retains their deeper spiritual significance.

### Abrogation of the Dietary Laws

The Catholic Church teaches definitively that the Mosaic dietary laws are not binding on Christians. This was settled at the Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15), where the apostles, under the guidance of the Holy Spirit, declined to impose the full Mosaic law on Gentile converts. Peter's vision in Acts 10:9–16, in which God declares all animals clean, marks the pivotal moment. The Catechism explains that the ceremonial prescriptions of the Old Law were "abrogated" because they were "fulfilled" in Christ (CCC 1952). The Council of Florence (1442) explicitly taught that Christians who observe the Mosaic ceremonies place themselves "outside the hope of salvation."

### The Spiritual Meaning of the Distinctions

While the dietary laws no longer bind, the Church Fathers consistently drew moral lessons from them. The Epistle of Barnabas (10) offers an early allegorical reading: the prohibition of pork teaches Christians not to associate with those who "live like swine," consuming without giving thanks; the requirement that clean animals chew the cud signifies the duty to meditate on God's word. Origen, Clement of Alexandria, and Augustine all develop similar spiritual interpretations, finding in each clean or unclean animal a lesson about virtue or vice.

### Holiness and Separation

The chapter's concluding exhortation, "Be holy, for I am holy" (v. 44), provides the theological foundation for all the purity laws. Catholic moral theology reads this call to holiness as universal and permanent, even while the specific dietary regulations are set aside. The Second Vatican Council devoted an entire chapter of *Lumen Gentium* (Chapter V) to "The Universal Call to Holiness," teaching that all the baptized — not only clergy and religious — are summoned to the perfection of charity. The Levitical imperative is thus broadened and deepened in Catholic teaching.

### The Body and Sanctification

The dietary laws presuppose that what enters the body matters — a conviction that Catholic theology affirms, even as it reinterprets the principle. Jesus declares all foods clean (Mk 7:19), but He simultaneously teaches that "whatever goes into a person from outside cannot defile him" because what defiles comes from the heart (Mk 7:18–23). Catholic moral theology maintains a robust theology of the body, including teachings on chastity, temperance, and the proper use of material goods. The Catechism affirms that "the body is the temple of the Holy Spirit" (CCC 2519) and that care for bodily integrity is a moral obligation.

### Fasting and Abstinence

Though the dietary laws are abrogated, the Catholic Church retains its own disciplines of fasting and abstinence. The obligation to abstain from meat on Fridays during Lent (and in many countries on all Fridays of the year) echoes the Levitical insight that food choices can be acts of worship and spiritual discipline. The Code of Canon Law (can. 1249–1253) prescribes penitential days and fasting as means of self-discipline, reparation for sin, and solidarity with the suffering.

### Patristic and Magisterial Reflection

Thomas Aquinas, in the *Summa Theologiae* (I-II, q.102, a.6), provides a comprehensive analysis of the dietary laws, identifying three purposes: to distinguish Israel from pagan nations, to cultivate habits of self-discipline, and to symbolize moral and spiritual truths. He argues that while the ceremonial laws have been abrogated in their literal observance, the moral truths they embody remain permanently valid. This threefold analysis remains standard in Catholic biblical scholarship.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 11 teaches that holiness touches every dimension of life, including the most mundane acts of eating and drinking. The Catholic faith preserves this conviction in its sacramental worldview, which sees all of material creation as potentially revelatory of the divine. Paul captures the enduring principle: "Whether you eat or drink, or whatever you do, do everything for the glory of God" (1 Cor 10:31). The dietary laws, though superseded, pointed toward a truth that the Catholic sacramental vision permanently enshrines.
