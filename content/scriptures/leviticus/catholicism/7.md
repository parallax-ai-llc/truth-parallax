## Guilt and Peace Offering Regulations

Leviticus 7 completes the sacrificial instruction section that began in chapter 1. It provides detailed regulations for the guilt offering (vv. 1–10), distinguishes three types of peace offering — thanksgiving, votive, and freewill (vv. 11–21) — prohibits the consumption of fat and blood (vv. 22–27), and specifies the priestly portions (vv. 28–38). The chapter concludes with a summary statement attributing the entire sacrificial system to divine command at Sinai.

### The Thanksgiving Offering

The *todah* or thanksgiving peace offering (vv. 12–15) holds special significance in Catholic theology. It is accompanied by unleavened cakes mixed with oil, wafers, and leavened bread. The Jewish scholar Hartmut Gese and Catholic theologian Joseph Ratzinger (later Pope Benedict XVI) have both argued that the *todah* is the most important liturgical background for the Eucharist. In *The Spirit of the Liturgy*, Ratzinger writes that the Last Supper is best understood as a *todah* meal — a thanksgiving sacrifice offered in the context of deliverance from mortal danger — which Jesus transforms into the perpetual thanksgiving of the New Covenant.

### Time Limits on Sacred Meat

The thanksgiving offering must be eaten on the same day it is offered (v. 15), while votive and freewill offerings may extend to the second day; anything remaining on the third day must be burned (vv. 16–18). Catholic exegetes see in these time restrictions a teaching about the urgency of grace. The Eucharistic species, once consecrated, are to be consumed promptly or reserved with great care for the communion of the sick. The Church's regulations about the proper treatment of the reserved sacrament — including the requirement to consume or properly dispose of the sacred species — echo the Levitical insistence that holy things must not be treated casually.

### The Prohibition of Fat and Blood

Verses 22–27 reiterate the absolute prohibition against eating fat or blood, with the penalty of being "cut off from his people." The severity of this sanction underscores the sacred status of blood as the vehicle of life. Catholic Eucharistic theology stands in deliberate contrast: Christ commands the drinking of His blood precisely because the New Covenant surpasses and fulfills the Old. What was prohibited under the Law becomes the very means of communion with God in the sacrament. As the Catechism states, quoting John 6:53: "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you do not have life in you" (CCC 1384).

### The Priestly Portions

Verses 28–36 assign the breast and the right thigh of the peace offering to the priests. The "wave offering" and "contribution" establish the principle that those who serve at the altar have a right to sustenance from the altar. Paul explicitly cites this principle in 1 Corinthians 9:13–14: "Do you not know that those who are employed in the temple service get their food from the temple? ... In the same way, the Lord commanded that those who proclaim the gospel should get their living by the gospel." Catholic canon law continues this practice through provisions for clerical remuneration and Mass stipends.

### The Conclusion of the Sacrificial Code

Verses 37–38 provide a grand summary: "This is the law of the burnt offering, the grain offering, the sin offering, the guilt offering, the ordination offering, and the sacrifice of peace offerings." The Catholic liturgical calendar, with its intricate system of feasts, fasts, and ordinary time, mirrors the comprehensive structure of the Levitical sacrificial code. Both systems seek to sanctify all of time and all of life through ordered worship.

### Patristic and Scholarly Reflection

Theodoret of Cyrrhus, in his *Questions on Leviticus*, emphasizes that the multiplicity of offerings corresponds to the multiplicity of human needs — thanksgiving, atonement, petition, reparation — all of which find their unity and fulfillment in the single sacrifice of Christ. The Catholic principle that the Mass simultaneously satisfies all four classical purposes of sacrifice (adoration, thanksgiving, petition, and expiation) draws directly on the theology consolidated in Leviticus 7.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 7 closes the sacrificial legislation by demonstrating that biblical worship is both highly structured and deeply personal. The various offerings address every dimension of the human relationship with God — gratitude, repentance, devotion, and communion. The Catholic faith holds that the Eucharist integrates all these dimensions in a single act of worship, fulfilling the rich and complex sacrificial theology that Leviticus articulates. As the Second Vatican Council declares, the Eucharist is "the fount and apex of the whole Christian life" (*Lumen Gentium* 11).
