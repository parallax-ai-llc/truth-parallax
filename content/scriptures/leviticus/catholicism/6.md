## Additional Offering Regulations

Leviticus 6 continues the guilt offering legislation (vv. 1–7) and then provides detailed instructions for the priests regarding the administration of the burnt offering (vv. 8–13), the grain offering (vv. 14–23), and the sin offering (vv. 24–30). The chapter shifts perspective from the layperson's obligations to the priestly duties, establishing rubrics for the daily maintenance of the altar and the proper handling of sacred offerings. Catholic liturgical theology finds in these prescriptions the roots of its own elaborate ritual order.

### Restitution for Fraud and Deception

Verses 1–7 address sins involving deception: fraud, robbery, false oaths regarding lost property, and extortion. The offender must restore the principal plus one-fifth and bring a guilt offering. Catholic moral theology places these offenses under the heading of violations of the seventh commandment. The Catechism teaches that "commutative justice requires the restitution of stolen goods" (CCC 2412) and that "every offense committed against justice and truth entails the duty of reparation." The Levitical text insists that no sacrifice can substitute for justice — a principle the prophets would later proclaim with thundering clarity (cf. Am 5:21–24).

### The Perpetual Fire

"The fire on the altar shall be kept burning on it; it shall not go out" (v. 13). This command to maintain a perpetual flame is one of the most evocative images in Leviticus. Catholic tradition reads it as a type of the sanctuary lamp that burns before the tabernacle in every Catholic church, signifying the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist. The Code of Canon Law (can. 940) prescribes that a special lamp should burn continuously before the tabernacle to indicate and honor the presence of Christ. Origen interprets the perpetual fire spiritually: it represents the flame of prayer and devotion that must never be extinguished in the soul.

### The Priestly Grain Offering

Verses 19–23 prescribe a special grain offering made by the high priest on the day of his anointing and daily thereafter. This offering is entirely burned — the priest cannot eat of his own offering. The principle that the priest's sacrifice is total and unreserved anticipates the Catholic theology of priestly celibacy and self-gift. As John Paul II taught in *Pastores Dabo Vobis* (29), priestly celibacy is a sign of the priest's total configuration to Christ, who gave Himself wholly to the Father and to the Church.

### Holiness and Contagion

The chapter concludes with the remarkable statement that "whatever touches the flesh [of the sin offering] shall be holy" (v. 27). Holiness in Leviticus is understood as a communicable quality — it can be transmitted through physical contact. Catholic sacramental theology operates on an analogous principle: the grace of the sacraments is communicated through material signs. The Catechism teaches that "the sacraments are efficacious signs of grace" (CCC 1131), and the Levitical concept of contagious holiness provides the biblical foundation for understanding how material things can be vehicles of divine power.

### Ritual Precision and Reverence

The meticulous attention to detail in Leviticus 6 — what garments the priest must wear, where the ashes must be deposited, which vessels must be broken or scoured — reflects the conviction that liturgical worship demands care and precision. The Catholic tradition has always insisted on ritual exactitude in the celebration of the sacraments. The *General Instruction of the Roman Missal* prescribes in detail the gestures, postures, and words of the Mass, not from mere legalism but from the conviction that external reverence shapes and expresses interior devotion. As the Second Vatican Council taught in *Sacrosanctum Concilium* (7), the liturgy is "the summit toward which the activity of the Church is directed."

### Patristic Interpretation

Gregory the Great, in his *Pastoral Rule*, draws on the Levitical priestly regulations to argue that those who minister at the altar must maintain the highest standards of moral and spiritual discipline. The priest who tends the perpetual fire must tend the fire of his own heart. Ambrose of Milan similarly reads the priestly duties of Leviticus 6 as a call to interior holiness: the external rites are empty without the corresponding interior dispositions.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 6 reveals that worship is not spontaneous self-expression but ordered service governed by divine command. The Catholic faith holds that authentic liturgy is received from God, not invented by the community — a principle that the Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy affirms when it states that "no other person, not even a priest, may add, remove, or change anything in the liturgy on his own authority" (*Sacrosanctum Concilium* 22). The priestly rubrics of Leviticus 6 stand as the earliest witness to this conviction.
