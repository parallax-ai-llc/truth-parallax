## Laws on Sexual Conduct

Leviticus 18 contains a comprehensive list of prohibited sexual relationships, including incest within various degrees of kinship, adultery, sexual intercourse during menstruation, homosexual acts, and bestiality. The chapter frames these prohibitions with an appeal to holiness and a warning that violation of these laws led to the expulsion of the Canaanites from the land. Catholic moral theology regards this chapter as containing precepts of the natural law that remain permanently binding, even as the ceremonial regulations of Leviticus have been set aside.

### The Distinction Between Ceremonial and Moral Law

Catholic theology distinguishes three categories within the Mosaic law: ceremonial, judicial, and moral. The ceremonial laws (dietary regulations, purity rites) were fulfilled and abrogated by Christ. The judicial laws (civil penalties) applied to the Israelite polity. The moral laws, however, reflect the natural law inscribed in human nature and remain permanently valid (CCC 1952). Aquinas explains in the *Summa Theologiae* (I-II, q.104, a.4) that the sexual prohibitions of Leviticus 18 belong to the moral law because they protect fundamental goods of human nature — marriage, family integrity, and the dignity of the person.

### Incest Prohibitions and the Natural Law

The extensive list of prohibited degrees of kinship (vv. 6–18) reflects the natural law's protection of family bonds. Catholic canon law codifies impediments to marriage based on consanguinity and affinity (Canons 1091–1092), drawing on the Levitical tradition. The theological rationale is that certain relationships are ordered by nature toward familial love rather than conjugal love, and confusion of these categories violates the created order. The Catechism teaches that "incest corrupts family relationships and marks a regression toward animality" (CCC 2388).

### Marriage and Conjugal Integrity

The prohibition of adultery (v. 20) — already present in the Decalogue — is reinforced here within the broader context of sexual holiness. Catholic teaching holds that marriage is an indissoluble covenant between one man and one woman, ordered toward the good of the spouses and the procreation of children (CCC 1601). The sexual prohibitions of Leviticus 18 protect the integrity of this covenant by excluding all sexual activity that falls outside the marital bond. John Paul II's *Familiaris Consortio* (11) describes the conjugal act as a "total" self-gift that is incompatible with any reservation or deviation.

### The Teaching on Homosexual Acts

Verse 22 prohibits sexual relations between men. Catholic moral teaching, while affirming the inherent dignity of every person regardless of sexual orientation, holds that homosexual acts are "intrinsically disordered" because they are not open to the transmission of life and do not reflect the complementarity of man and woman (CCC 2357). The Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, in *Persona Humana* (1975) and subsequent documents, grounds this teaching in both Scripture and natural law. The Catechism simultaneously insists that persons with homosexual tendencies "must be accepted with respect, compassion, and sensitivity" and that "every sign of unjust discrimination in their regard should be avoided" (CCC 2358).

### Molech Worship and Child Sacrifice

Verse 21 prohibits giving one's children to Molech — a practice involving child sacrifice. Catholic pro-life teaching identifies the destruction of innocent human life as an intrinsic evil. The Catechism teaches that "human life must be respected and protected absolutely from the moment of conception" (CCC 2270). The juxtaposition of sexual ethics and the Molech prohibition in Leviticus 18 reflects the Catholic conviction that sexual morality and the protection of children are intimately connected.

### The Land and Moral Consequence

The chapter's conclusion warns that the land itself will "vomit out its inhabitants" if they commit these abominations (vv. 24–28). Catholic social teaching recognizes a connection between moral order and social well-being. John Paul II, in *Veritatis Splendor* (99), teaches that the rejection of moral absolutes leads to social disintegration. The prophetic tradition consistently links Israel's moral failures to national catastrophe — a pattern that Leviticus 18 anticipates with its warning about the fate of the Canaanites.

### Patristic and Magisterial Reflection

Augustine, in *City of God* (XV), reads the sexual prohibitions of Leviticus 18 as reflecting the order of charity (*ordo caritatis*) — the proper ordering of love toward different persons according to their relationship. The Council of Trent reaffirmed the binding character of the Decalogue's sexual ethics, and the Catholic moral tradition has consistently maintained that the prohibitions of Leviticus 18, insofar as they express the natural law, remain normative for all people in every age.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 18 articulates a vision of sexual holiness rooted in the conviction that the human body and its sexual capacities are ordered by God toward specific goods. The Catholic Church reads this chapter as expressing permanent truths of the natural law, while situating these truths within the broader framework of the Gospel's call to love, mercy, and conversion. The chapter's moral vision, properly understood, serves the dignity and flourishing of every human person.
