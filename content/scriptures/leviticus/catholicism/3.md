## Peace Offerings

Leviticus 3 details the *shelamim*, the peace or fellowship offering, which is unique among Levitical sacrifices because portions of the animal are shared among God (the fat burned on the altar), the priests, and the offerer. This communal meal in the presence of God constitutes the closest Old Testament parallel to the Eucharistic banquet. The Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that the Eucharist is "the sacrament of communion" (CCC 1325), and the peace offering provides its most direct typological anticipation.

### The Structure of Communion

The peace offering may be drawn from cattle (vv. 1–5), sheep (vv. 6–11), or goats (vv. 12–16). In each case the offerer lays hands on the animal, slaughters it, and the priests sprinkle the blood and burn the fat on the altar. The remaining meat is eaten by the offerer and his household in a sacred meal. Augustine, in *City of God* (X.6), sees in this tripartite distribution a figure of the communion between God, the ministerial priesthood, and the faithful — the very structure of the Eucharistic liturgy, where the priest offers, God receives, and the congregation partakes.

### The Fat Belongs to the LORD

Verses 16–17 declare emphatically that "all fat is the LORD's" and establish a perpetual statute prohibiting the consumption of fat and blood. In the ancient world, fat represented the richest and best portion. The theological principle is that the best belongs to God. Catholic moral theology, drawing on this instinct, teaches that the virtue of religion requires giving to God what is most excellent — not merely leftovers but the firstfruits of one's time, talent, and treasure. The *Summa Theologiae* (II-II, q.81) identifies religion as the highest of the moral virtues precisely because it directs the whole person toward God.

### The Blood Prohibition

The prohibition against consuming blood (v. 17) runs throughout Leviticus and finds its theological foundation in the principle that "the life of the flesh is in the blood" (Lev 17:11). Catholic exegesis reads this prohibition as pointing forward to the startling reversal in John 6:53–56, where Jesus commands His disciples to drink His blood. What the Old Law forbade as sacred, Christ fulfills by offering His own life — blood now given for consumption because the Eucharistic species communicate divine life itself. The Council of Trent explicitly taught that communion under both species is not necessary for the laity, since Christ is fully present under either species (Session XXI, Chapter 3).

### Covenant Meal Theology

The peace offering is fundamentally a covenant meal — an occasion for joyful fellowship with God. The Catholic liturgical theologian Louis Bouyer argues in *Eucharist* that the peace offering is the most important sacrificial background for understanding the Last Supper. Jesus transforms the Passover meal — itself a form of peace offering — into the New Covenant banquet, establishing a communion that surpasses anything the Levitical system could accomplish. The Catechism states that the Eucharist is "the memorial of Christ's Passover, the making present and the sacramental offering of his unique sacrifice" (CCC 1362).

### Patristic Interpretation

Cyril of Alexandria interprets the peace offering christologically: the peace between God and humanity that the shelamim symbolizes is accomplished definitively by Christ, "our peace, who has made us both one and has broken down the dividing wall of hostility" (Eph 2:14). The fat burned on the altar signifies the total consecration of Christ's divine nature, while the shared meal represents the communion of believers with their Lord. Gregory the Great similarly reads the peace offering as a type of the reconciliation effected through Christ's sacrifice.

### Liturgical Parallels

The Rite of Mass includes the exchange of peace (*Pax*) immediately before Communion, a ritual gesture that directly evokes the theology of the peace offering. The faithful, having been reconciled to God through the Eucharistic sacrifice, now express their reconciliation with one another before sharing in the sacred meal. Pope Francis, in *Evangelii Gaudium* (230), emphasizes that the Eucharist impels the Church toward social reconciliation and justice — extending the peace of the altar into the world.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 3 teaches that sacrifice is ordered not merely toward atonement but toward communion. The Catholic faith holds that the ultimate purpose of Christ's redemptive work is to restore fellowship between God and humanity — a fellowship that is sacramentally realized in every celebration of the Eucharist. The peace offering thus stands as an enduring witness to the convivial heart of biblical religion, fulfilled in the Church's eucharistic life.
