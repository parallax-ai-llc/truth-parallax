## Sabbatical Year and Jubilee

Leviticus 25 prescribes two extraordinary institutions: the Sabbatical year, observed every seven years, during which the land lies fallow (vv. 1–7); and the Jubilee, observed every fiftieth year, when all sold land reverts to its original owners, all Israelite slaves are freed, and the economy undergoes a comprehensive reset (vv. 8–55). Catholic social teaching draws heavily on this chapter, finding in it foundational principles of economic justice, the universal destination of goods, and the preferential option for the poor.

### The Sabbath for the Land

The Sabbatical year extends the Sabbath principle from human rest to the land itself (vv. 2–7). The earth is not a mere commodity to be exploited but a gift of God that requires care and renewal. Catholic social teaching affirms this ecological vision. Pope Francis, in *Laudato Si'* (71), explicitly cites Leviticus 25 as evidence that "the biblical tradition clearly shows that this renewal entails recovering and respecting the rhythms inscribed in nature by the hand of the Creator." The land Sabbath anticipates the Catholic teaching on environmental stewardship.

### The Year of Jubilee

"You shall consecrate the fiftieth year and proclaim liberty throughout the land to all its inhabitants. It shall be a jubilee for you" (v. 10). The Jubilee is one of the most radical social institutions in ancient law. Catholic social teaching identifies it as the biblical foundation for the principle of the universal destination of goods — the teaching that "the goods of creation are destined for the whole human race" (CCC 2402). The Catechism cites the Jubilee as evidence that private property, while legitimate, is always subordinate to the common good.

### Jesus and the Jubilee

In Luke 4:16–21, Jesus inaugurates His public ministry by reading from Isaiah 61 — a passage that draws on Jubilee imagery: "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor... to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor." Jesus identifies Himself as the fulfillment of the Jubilee: He is the one who brings definitive liberation, remission of debts, and restoration. Catholic theology understands salvation itself as the ultimate Jubilee — the eschatological restoration of all things in Christ.

### Land as Divine Gift

"The land shall not be sold in perpetuity, for the land is mine. For you are strangers and sojourners with me" (v. 23). This declaration establishes that God is the ultimate owner of all property, and human beings are tenants and stewards. Catholic social teaching consistently affirms this principle. The Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church (171) teaches that "the right to private property is subordinated to the right to common use, to the fact that goods are meant for everyone." The Jubilee institution guarantees that economic inequality does not become permanent and structural.

### The Redemption of Property and Persons

Verses 25–55 elaborate detailed provisions for the redemption of sold land and the treatment of Israelites who have fallen into debt servitude. A kinsman-redeemer (*go'el*) may buy back land for a relative; an Israelite slave must be treated as a hired worker, not as chattel, and must be released at the Jubilee. Catholic theology sees in the kinsman-redeemer a type of Christ, the ultimate Go'el who redeems humanity from the slavery of sin. The Catechism teaches that "the economy of salvation reveals the definitive plan of divine 'condescension' that is realized in the mystery of Christ" (CCC 1066).

### Prohibition of Usury

"You shall not lend him your money at interest, nor give him your food for profit" (v. 37). The Catholic moral tradition historically condemned usury — the charging of interest on loans to the needy — drawing on this and related biblical texts. While modern Catholic teaching recognizes the legitimacy of reasonable interest in commercial lending, it continues to condemn exploitative lending practices. The Catechism identifies "financial speculation" that harms others as morally wrong (cf. CCC 2409). Catholic microfinance initiatives and credit unions preserve the Jubilee spirit of economic solidarity.

### Papal Jubilee Years

The Catholic Church has celebrated Holy Years or Jubilees since 1300, when Boniface VIII proclaimed the first. The most recent Ordinary Jubilee (2025) directly invokes the Levitical Jubilee's themes of forgiveness, liberation, and renewal. John Paul II, in *Tertio Millennio Adveniente* (13), explicitly connected the Church's Jubilee practice to Leviticus 25, calling for debt relief for developing nations as a concrete expression of the Jubilee's economic justice.

### Patristic and Magisterial Reflection

Ambrose of Milan interprets the Jubilee as God's statement that no human being can be permanently enslaved — a teaching that anticipates the Catholic Church's eventual condemnation of chattel slavery. Leo XIII, in *Rerum Novarum* (1891), drew on the Levitical economic principles to articulate the Church's modern social teaching. The Jubilee vision of periodic economic restoration continues to inspire Catholic advocacy for debt relief, land reform, and equitable distribution of resources.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 25 articulates a comprehensive vision of social justice rooted in the sovereignty of God over all creation. The Catholic faith reads this chapter as a prophetic anticipation of the Kingdom of God, where justice, liberation, and restoration are fully realized. The Jubilee is not merely an ancient Israelite institution but an enduring call to build a society that reflects God's preferential love for the poor, the enslaved, and the dispossessed.
