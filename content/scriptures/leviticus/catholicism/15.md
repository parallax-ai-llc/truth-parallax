## Bodily Discharges

Leviticus 15 addresses ritual impurity arising from bodily discharges — abnormal male discharges (vv. 2–15), seminal emissions (vv. 16–18), menstruation (vv. 19–24), and abnormal female discharges (vv. 25–30). Persons affected become ritually unclean for specified periods, and their impurity is communicable through physical contact. At the chapter's conclusion, purification offerings are required for abnormal conditions. Catholic theology reads these regulations within the framework of its robust theology of the body, recognizing both their historical specificity and their enduring spiritual principles.

### The Body as Sacred

The underlying premise of Leviticus 15 is that the human body, in all its processes, exists within a sacred framework. Catholic theology affirms this conviction emphatically. John Paul II's *Theology of the Body* teaches that the human body is a "sign and instrument" of the person's relationship with God. The Catechism declares that "the body is the temple of the Holy Spirit" (CCC 2519) and that bodily integrity deserves respect and care. While the specific purity regulations of Leviticus 15 are no longer binding, the conviction that bodily life has spiritual significance remains central to Catholic anthropology.

### The Woman with the Flow of Blood

The regulations of Leviticus 15:25–30, concerning the woman with an abnormal discharge, provide essential background for understanding the Gospel story of the woman who touched Jesus' garment (Mk 5:25–34). Her twelve-year hemorrhage would have rendered her perpetually unclean under Levitical law, excluding her from worship and social life. Jesus' healing of this woman — and His commendation of her faith — represents a dramatic inversion of the purity system. Where the Levitical law communicated impurity through touch, Christ communicates holiness. Catholic theology sees in this encounter a revelation of the sacramental principle at its deepest level: divine holiness, far from being contaminated by contact with human brokenness, transforms and heals it.

### Ritual Purity and Sacramental Preparation

The Catholic Church no longer requires the observance of Levitical purity laws, but it does maintain disciplines of sacramental preparation that reflect the same underlying conviction. The Eucharistic fast, the requirement to be in a state of grace before receiving Communion (CCC 1385), and the obligation to confess mortal sins before approaching the altar all express the principle that encounter with the Holy demands appropriate preparation. The Levitical system of purification through washing, waiting, and sacrifice finds its New Covenant analogy in the Catholic disciplines of examination of conscience, confession, and absolution.

### The Distinction Between Clean and Unclean

The chapter concludes with a purpose statement: "You shall keep the people of Israel separate from their uncleanness, lest they die in their uncleanness by defiling my tabernacle that is in their midst" (v. 31). The proximity of the tabernacle — God's dwelling — creates the necessity for purity. Catholic Eucharistic theology applies this logic directly: because Christ is truly present in the tabernacle, the faithful must approach Him with appropriate reverence and spiritual preparation. Paul's warning that whoever eats and drinks "without discerning the body eats and drinks judgment on himself" (1 Cor 11:29) echoes the Levitical concern for the consequences of approaching the sacred in a state of impurity.

### The Equality of Male and Female

It is notable that Leviticus 15 treats male and female discharges with parallel structures and equivalent purification requirements. Catholic anthropology affirms the equal dignity of men and women before God. The Catechism teaches that "man and woman are both with one and the same dignity 'in the image of God'" (CCC 2334). The Levitical text, while operating within an ancient purity framework, already displays a formal equality that Catholic teaching develops into a comprehensive doctrine of complementarity and equal dignity.

### Patristic Reflection

Ephrem the Syrian reads the purification rites of Leviticus 15 as illustrations of the soul's need for continual cleansing through repentance and prayer. John Chrysostom, preaching on the woman with the hemorrhage, emphasizes that Christ's touch reverses the direction of contamination: instead of the unclean defiling the clean, the Holy One purifies the unclean. This reversal, Chrysostom argues, is the essence of the New Covenant's transformation of the Old.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 15 witnesses to the biblical conviction that every dimension of bodily existence stands under God's sovereign care. The Catholic Church, while setting aside the specific purity regulations, preserves the theological vision: the body is not profane matter but a temple of the Holy Spirit, and the faithful are called to honor God in their bodies (1 Cor 6:19–20). The sacramental disciplines of the Church — baptism, Eucharistic preparation, the anointing of the sick — express in New Covenant terms the Levitical truth that bodily life and spiritual life are inseparably connected.
