## The Sanctity of Blood

Leviticus 17 establishes two foundational principles: all legitimate sacrifice must be offered at the Tabernacle (vv. 1–9), and the consumption of blood is absolutely prohibited because "the life of the flesh is in the blood" (vv. 10–16). This chapter marks a theological turning point in Leviticus, providing the interpretive key for the entire sacrificial system and containing the verse most frequently cited in Catholic Eucharistic theology from the Old Testament.

### Centralization of Sacrifice

Verses 1–9 require that all animal slaughter — whether for sacrifice or food — take place at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Animals slaughtered elsewhere are treated as bloodguilt. The purpose is to prevent illicit sacrifice to "goat demons" (*se'irim*, v. 7). Catholic ecclesiology draws a parallel: authentic worship takes place within the visible communion of the Church, under the guidance of legitimate pastors. The Catechism teaches that "the Eucharist is the heart of ecclesial life" (CCC 1407) and that its celebration requires a validly ordained priest acting within the apostolic community. Just as Israel could not legitimately sacrifice outside the Tabernacle, Christians cannot celebrate the Eucharist apart from the Church's communion.

### The Life Is in the Blood

"For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life" (v. 11). This verse is the theological cornerstone of biblical sacrifice. Catholic theology reads it as the foundational explanation for why Christ had to shed His blood: atonement requires the offering of life itself. The Catechism states that "the blood of Christ... is the means by which God makes the new and eternal covenant, definitively binding himself to his people" (cf. CCC 613). Every Mass makes present this saving blood under the appearance of wine.

### The Eucharistic Reversal

The absolute prohibition against consuming blood in the Old Covenant makes Jesus' command in John 6:53–56 all the more startling: "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you." Catholic Eucharistic theology holds that Christ deliberately reverses the Levitical prohibition because His blood is not ordinary blood but the vehicle of divine life. What was forbidden under the Old Law because it belonged to God alone is now given by God Himself as food for eternal life. The Council of Trent affirmed that in the Eucharist, under the species of wine, the faithful truly receive the Blood of Christ (Session XIII, Canon 1).

### Blood and Covenant

The concept of blood-covenant runs throughout salvation history: the covenant with Noah (Gen 9:4–6), the covenant with Abraham through circumcision (Gen 17), the Sinai covenant ratified with blood (Ex 24:8), and finally the New Covenant in Christ's blood (Lk 22:20). Catholic theology understands each successive covenant as building toward the definitive offering of the Cross. At the Last Supper, Jesus declares: "This cup is the new covenant in my blood" (1 Cor 11:25). The Catechism teaches that "the Eucharist is the memorial of Christ's Passover... the making present and the sacramental offering of his unique sacrifice" (CCC 1362).

### The Prohibition Extended to Resident Aliens

Verses 10–12 extend the blood prohibition to resident aliens (*gerim*) living among Israel, indicating that certain moral laws transcend ethnic or national boundaries. Catholic natural law theory affirms that fundamental moral principles are inscribed in human nature and are accessible to all people through reason (CCC 1954–1960). The extension of the blood prohibition to non-Israelites anticipates the universality of the New Covenant moral law.

### Patristic Reflection

John Chrysostom, in his homilies on John's Gospel, devotes extensive attention to the relationship between Leviticus 17 and the Eucharist. He argues that Christ transforms the meaning of blood: no longer a prohibited substance, it becomes the source of spiritual life. "If anyone will not drink blood, he will not have life in him — this is the paradox of the Gospel," Chrysostom writes. Augustine similarly teaches that the Old Law's prohibition prepared Israel to understand the gravity of what Christ would offer: His own life, poured out for the world.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 17 provides the essential hermeneutical key for understanding both the Old Testament sacrificial system and the Catholic Eucharist. The principle that life is in the blood, given by God for atonement, reaches its ultimate fulfillment in Christ's offering of His own blood for the salvation of the world. The Catholic faith proclaims that this blood is made present in every celebration of the Mass, nourishing the faithful unto eternal life. As the Catechism declares, the Eucharist is "the sum and summary of our faith" (CCC 1327).
