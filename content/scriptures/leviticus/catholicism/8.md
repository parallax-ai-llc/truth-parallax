## Ordination of Aaron and His Sons

Leviticus 8 narrates the solemn ordination of Aaron and his sons as priests of Israel, carried out by Moses before the entire assembly. The ceremony involves washing, investiture with priestly garments, anointing with oil, and the offering of a sin offering, a burnt offering, and a ram of ordination. Catholic tradition reads this chapter as the foundational institution of the Old Testament priesthood, which reaches its definitive fulfillment in the sacrament of Holy Orders.

### The Assembly at the Tent of Meeting

Moses gathers the entire congregation to witness the ordination (vv. 1–4), establishing the public and ecclesial character of priestly ministry. Catholic teaching insists that ordination is not a private appointment but a liturgical act of the whole Church. The Catechism states that "the whole Church is a priestly people" (CCC 1591), and the rite of ordination takes place within the Eucharistic liturgy before the assembled faithful. The Levitical precedent confirms that the priesthood is always exercised within and for the community.

### Washing, Vesting, and Anointing

Aaron is washed with water (v. 6), clothed in the sacred vestments (vv. 7–9), and anointed with oil (v. 12). Catholic sacramental theology sees in this threefold ritual a prefiguration of baptism (washing), the investiture with grace (vesting), and the anointing of the Holy Spirit (chrismation). The rite of priestly ordination includes the laying on of hands, the prayer of consecration, the vesting with stole and chasuble, and the anointing of the priest's hands with sacred chrism — gestures that directly echo the Levitical ceremony. Ambrose of Milan, in *De Sacramentis*, draws explicit parallels between the anointing of Aaron and the chrismation of the newly baptized.

### The Anointing of the Tabernacle

Before anointing Aaron, Moses anoints the Tabernacle and all its furnishings (vv. 10–11), sprinkling oil on the altar seven times. The consecration of sacred spaces and objects remains a central practice in Catholicism. The Rite of Dedication of a Church includes the anointing of the altar and walls with sacred chrism, directly continuing the Levitical pattern. The Catechism teaches that "churches are not simply gathering places but signify and make visible the Church living in a particular place" (CCC 1180).

### The Blood on Ear, Thumb, and Toe

In a striking ritual, Moses applies blood from the ordination ram to the right ear, right thumb, and right big toe of Aaron and each of his sons (v. 23–24). Catholic interpreters understand this as the consecration of the whole person for sacred service: the ear for hearing God's word, the hand for doing God's work, the foot for walking in God's ways. Bede the Venerable reads this rite as signifying that the priest must be wholly dedicated — in thought, action, and conduct — to the service of God. The Catholic understanding of priestly character as an indelible spiritual mark (CCC 1582) extends this Levitical insight: ordination transforms the entire person permanently.

### The Seven Days of Ordination

The ordination ceremony extends over seven days (v. 33), during which Aaron and his sons must remain at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Catholic ordination practice historically included extended periods of retreat and preparation. The current requirement of a pre-ordination retreat reflects the Levitical principle that sacred ministry demands sustained preparation and separation. The seven-day period also evokes the seven days of creation, suggesting that ordination inaugurates a new creation — a theological insight developed by Maximus the Confessor.

### Patristic and Magisterial Teaching

The Council of Trent declared that the sacrament of Orders was instituted by Christ and that it imprints an indelible character on the soul, distinguishing the ordained priest from the lay faithful (Session XXIII, Canons 1 and 4). While the Levitical priesthood is superseded by the priesthood of Christ, the Catholic Church reads the ordination of Aaron as a providential preparation for the New Covenant priesthood. John Paul II, in *Pastores Dabo Vobis* (13), teaches that the ordained priest participates in a unique way in the priesthood of Christ, exercising a ministry that is qualitatively distinct from the common priesthood of the baptized.

### Theological Significance

Leviticus 8 demonstrates that the priesthood is a divine institution, not a human invention. The Catholic faith holds that Holy Orders cannot be self-conferred or democratically bestowed; it is received from God through the laying on of hands in apostolic succession. The elaborate ritual of Aaron's ordination — with its washings, vestings, anointings, and sacrifices — witnesses to the gravity of priestly consecration and the holiness required of those who stand between God and His people.
