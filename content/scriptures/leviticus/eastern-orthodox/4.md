## The Sin Offering (Chattat)

Leviticus 4 introduces the sin offering, prescribed for unintentional transgressions against the Lord's commandments. The chapter carefully distinguishes four categories of offender, each requiring a different sacrifice: the anointed priest offers a bull, the whole congregation a bull, a ruler a male goat, and a common person a female goat or lamb. The blood ritual is central — the priest sprinkles blood before the veil of the sanctuary, applies it to the horns of the altar, and pours the remainder at the base of the altar of burnt offering.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "If anyone sins unintentionally in any of the things that the Lord has commanded not to be done" (4:2)

The concept of unintentional sin is significant in Orthodox moral theology. Sin is not merely a legal transgression but a condition — a disease of the soul. Even unintentional sins wound the person and rupture communion with God. St. John Cassian teaches that unconscious sins reveal the depth of fallen humanity's alienation from God; we sin even when we do not realize it, because our very nature is disordered. The sacrament of Confession in Orthodox practice addresses not only conscious transgressions but the whole state of spiritual illness.

### "The priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven" (4:20)

The priestly mediation in the sin offering prefigures Christ's high-priestly ministry as described in the Epistle to the Hebrews. St. Cyril of Alexandria emphasizes that the Levitical priest could only effect a shadow of true atonement — the reality is Christ, who as both priest and victim accomplishes reconciliation in His own person. In the Orthodox sacrament of Confession, the priest stands as a witness and instrument of Christ's forgiveness, pronouncing absolution not in his own authority but in Christ's.

### The Graded Sacrifices (4:3, 4:13, 4:22, 4:27)

The differing requirements for priest, congregation, ruler, and common person reflect the principle that greater knowledge and authority bring greater responsibility. Origen notes that the priest's sin defiles the entire community (hence the more elaborate blood ritual), while the layperson's sin affects primarily the individual. This echoes the Orthodox teaching on the accountability of clergy and the gravity of pastoral responsibility — those who teach and shepherd will face stricter judgment (James 3:1).

## Theological Significance

### Sin as Ontological Wound

Orthodox theology does not view sin primarily in juridical categories — as a crime requiring punishment — but as a disease requiring healing. The sin offering is not a penalty paid to an angry God but a remedy administered by a loving Physician. St. Gregory of Nyssa's understanding of divine punishment is therapeutic: God's "wrath" is the burning away of sin, not vindictive retribution. The blood of the sacrifice symbolizes the life-giving power that restores what sin has damaged.

### The Blood and the Veil

The sprinkling of blood before the veil of the sanctuary (4:6) is read by the Fathers as a type of Christ's blood opening access to the heavenly Holy of Holies. The Epistle to the Hebrews develops this extensively (Hebrews 9-10), and the Orthodox liturgy enacts it: the priest carries the holy gifts through the Royal Doors, passing through the iconostasis (the veil) to offer the Eucharistic sacrifice on the altar (the heavenly throne). The tearing of the Temple veil at the Crucifixion (Matthew 27:51) fulfills this Levitical pattern.

### Corporate and Individual Dimensions

The inclusion of both congregational and individual sin offerings reveals that sin has both communal and personal dimensions. The Orthodox understanding of the Church as the Body of Christ means that every sin wounds the whole body, and every act of repentance heals it. The practice of public penitential prayers in Orthodox liturgy — the prayers of the faithful, the litanies of supplication — reflects this corporate dimension of sin and forgiveness.
