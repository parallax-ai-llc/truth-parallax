## Laws Concerning Bodily Discharges

Leviticus 15 addresses ritual impurity arising from bodily discharges — abnormal male discharges (15:1-15), normal seminal emissions (15:16-18), menstruation (15:19-24), and abnormal female discharges (15:25-30). Each case specifies the duration of impurity, the extent of contagion through contact, and the required purification. The chapter concludes with the purpose statement: "Thus you shall keep the people of Israel separate from their uncleanness, lest they die in their uncleanness by defiling my tabernacle that is in their midst" (15:31).

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "Thus you shall keep the people of Israel separate from their uncleanness" (15:31)

The purpose of these regulations is the protection of the sacred — the tabernacle of God's presence must not be defiled. Orthodox theology understands the human body itself as a temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19), and bodily discipline as a form of liturgical service. The body is not despised but reverenced — precisely because it is the dwelling place of divine grace, it must be treated with care and intentionality.

### The Woman with the Issue of Blood (cf. Mark 5:25-34)

The most significant New Testament engagement with Leviticus 15 is Christ's healing of the woman who had suffered twelve years of hemorrhaging. Under the Levitical code, she was perpetually unclean and anyone she touched became unclean. Yet when she touches Christ's garment, the flow of impurity is reversed: instead of her uncleanness defiling Him, His holiness heals her. St. John Chrysostom sees this as a paradigm of the Incarnation itself — Christ enters the realm of human impurity not to be contaminated by it but to transform it from within.

### "If a man lies with a woman and has an emission of semen, both of them shall bathe in water and be unclean until the evening" (15:18)

The brief impurity associated with marital intercourse is not a condemnation of sexuality but a recognition of its sacred gravity. Orthodox theology, following St. John Chrysostom and St. Maximus the Confessor, holds marriage in high honor as a sacrament. The association of sexual activity with ritual impurity reflects not moral taint but the recognition that procreation participates in the mystery of life and death — the same sacred boundary guarded throughout Leviticus.

## Theological Significance

### The Body in Orthodox Theology

Orthodox Christianity has always maintained a high theology of the body, grounded in the doctrines of creation, incarnation, and resurrection. God created the body and called it good; the Son of God assumed a human body; and the body will be raised in glory. Leviticus 15, far from denigrating the body, treats it as serious — its processes are not trivial or profane but participate in the sacred order of creation. The Orthodox practice of blessing water, food, and homes reflects this conviction that the material world is the arena of divine action.

### Purity and the Sacramental Life

The Orthodox Church has historically debated how the Levitical purity laws apply to baptized Christians. The consensus of the canonical tradition, expressed in the answers of Sts. Dionysius and Timothy of Alexandria, is that baptism fundamentally transforms the Christian's relationship to ritual purity. However, the spiritual principle endures: approaching the holy mysteries requires intentional preparation. The Eucharistic fast, the abstinence from marital relations before communion (a common but not universal practice), and the general discipline of the body all reflect a transformed but continuous Levitical sensibility.

### Christ's Reversal of Impurity

The theological center of gravity for the Christian reading of Leviticus 15 is Christ's sovereign reversal of the impurity dynamic. In the old covenant, impurity was contagious — the unclean contaminated the clean. In Christ, holiness becomes contagious — His touch heals, His presence sanctifies, His body and blood purify. The Orthodox understanding of holy water, blessed oil, relics, and the Eucharist all operate on this reversed dynamic: divine holiness communicates itself through material contact, overcoming and transforming all impurity.
