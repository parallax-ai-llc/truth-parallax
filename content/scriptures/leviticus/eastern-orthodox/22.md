## Priestly Purity and Acceptable Sacrifices

Leviticus 22 addresses two related concerns: the conditions under which priests may eat the holy offerings (22:1-16), and the standards for acceptable sacrificial animals (22:17-33). Priests in a state of impurity — through skin disease, bodily discharge, or contact with unclean things — must refrain from eating the sacred portions. Sacrificial animals must be without blemish: no blindness, broken limbs, running sores, or castration. The chapter concludes with the warning not to profane God's holy name and the reminder, "I am the Lord who sanctifies you" (22:32).

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "They shall therefore keep My charge, lest they bear sin for it and die thereby when they profane it" (22:9)

The profanation of holy things carries lethal consequences — not because God is vindictive but because holiness is real and its violation is genuinely destructive. St. John Chrysostom warns that those who approach the Eucharist carelessly are "like those who pierced Christ's side." The Orthodox practice of careful preparation before communion — fasting from midnight, the recitation of the pre-communion prayers, confession — reflects this Levitical seriousness about encountering the holy.

### "Whatever has a blemish, you shall not offer, for it will not be acceptable for you" (22:20)

The requirement of an unblemished sacrifice is universally read by the Fathers as a type of Christ, "a lamb without blemish or spot" (1 Peter 1:19). St. Cyril of Alexandria develops this at length: Christ's sinlessness is the fulfillment of the Levitical demand for perfection in the sacrifice. The Orthodox proskomedia (preparation of the gifts) involves a careful inspection and cutting of the bread — the priest selects and prepares the Lamb with deliberate care, enacting the Levitical requirement of an unblemished offering.

### "I am the Lord who sanctifies you, who brought you out of the land of Egypt to be your God" (22:32-33)

The chapter's closing formula grounds all holiness in God's act of redemption. Holiness is not a human achievement but a divine gift rooted in the Exodus — and, for Christians, in the Paschal mystery. The Orthodox baptismal liturgy explicitly parallels the Exodus: passage through the waters, liberation from bondage (to sin and death), and entrance into the promised land (the Kingdom of God). Sanctification is always God's work, received by the human person through cooperation.

## Theological Significance

### The Unblemished Offering and Eucharistic Reverence

The Orthodox liturgical tradition surrounds the Eucharistic gifts with extraordinary reverence. The bread must be baked with prayer, the wine must be pure, the vessels must be consecrated. This is not superstitious fussiness but the living application of Leviticus 22 — what is offered to God must be the best, the unblemished, the most carefully prepared. Fr. Alexander Schmemann emphasizes that the liturgical preparation of the gifts is itself an act of theology — it declares the seriousness and reality of what is about to occur.

### Profanation and Sanctification

The chapter repeatedly uses the language of profanation (*chalal* — to make common, to desecrate) in contrast with sanctification (*qadash* — to set apart, to make holy). Orthodox theology holds these as real, ontological categories, not merely symbolic ones. A consecrated church, a blessed icon, a sanctified chalice — these have been genuinely set apart and transformed by divine grace. To treat them as common objects is a real act of profanation with spiritual consequences.

### The Outsider and the Holy

The restrictions on who may eat the holy offerings (22:10-16) — excluding hired workers and guests but including purchased servants and the priest's household — reveal a complex understanding of belonging. In Orthodox Eucharistic practice, only baptized, chrismated, and prepared Orthodox Christians receive communion. This is not exclusion for its own sake but the recognition that the holy mysteries require proper initiation and preparation — the same Levitical logic of progressive access to increasing holiness.
