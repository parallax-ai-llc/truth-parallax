## "Be Holy, For I Am Holy"

Leviticus 19 is widely regarded as the moral heart of the entire book. It opens with the foundational command — "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" (19:2) — and proceeds to a comprehensive ethical code touching worship, family, agriculture, justice, commerce, and interpersonal relations. The chapter recapitulates elements of the Decalogue (sabbath, parents, idolatry) and adds detailed social legislation: gleaning laws for the poor, prohibitions against theft, lying, oppression of laborers, partiality in judgment, and slander. It culminates in the command to love one's neighbor as oneself (19:18).

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" (19:2)

This is the charter verse of Orthodox theosis — the call to participate in God's own holiness. St. Gregory of Nyssa teaches that holiness is not a fixed state but an infinite progression (*epektasis*) — the soul moves ever deeper into the inexhaustible mystery of God without ever reaching an end. The command "Be holy" is not a demand for perfection by human effort but an invitation to unending transformation through participation in the divine energies. St. Peter quotes this verse (1 Peter 1:16) as the foundation of Christian life.

### "You shall love your neighbor as yourself" (19:18)

Christ identifies this as one of the two greatest commandments (Matthew 22:39), and the Orthodox tradition reads it as inseparable from the love of God. St. Maximus the Confessor teaches that love of God and love of neighbor are two aspects of a single reality — one cannot truly love God without loving the neighbor, because every human person bears the image of God. The parable of the Last Judgment (Matthew 25:31-46) makes this identity explicit: to serve the least is to serve Christ.

### "You shall not reap your field right up to its edge... You shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner" (19:9-10)

The gleaning laws establish a structural provision for the poor — not charity from surplus but a right embedded in the agricultural system itself. St. John Chrysostom is famous for his insistence that wealth is not personal property but a trust to be shared: "Not to share our own wealth with the poor is theft from the poor." The Orthodox social tradition draws heavily on these Levitical principles, insisting that economic justice is not optional philanthropy but a requirement of holiness.

### "You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great, but in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor" (19:15)

Justice requires impartiality — neither the sentimental favoritism toward the poor nor the corrupted deference toward the powerful. Orthodox canonical tradition insists on fairness in ecclesiastical courts and pastoral governance. St. Basil the Great established hospitals, hospices, and distribution centers (the *Basiliad*) that served all persons without distinction, embodying the Levitical principle of impartial justice.

## Theological Significance

### The Integration of Worship and Ethics

Leviticus 19 deliberately intermingles cultic regulations (sabbath, sacrifices, idolatry) with ethical commands (justice, honesty, love). This integration is fundamental to Orthodoxy: worship without justice is empty, and justice without worship lacks its divine foundation. The Liturgy of St. Basil prays for "peaceful times, for good seasons, for abundant rains, for fruitful harvests, and for the peace of the whole world" — seamlessly uniting cosmic, social, and spiritual concerns.

### The Stranger and the Sojourner

The command to love the stranger "as yourself" (19:34) extends the scope of love beyond ethnic and religious boundaries. Orthodox theology has always held in tension the particularity of the Church with the universality of the divine image in every person. The monastic tradition of hospitality — receiving every guest as Christ (Rule of St. Benedict, drawing on Matthew 25) — is deeply rooted in this Levitical command. The stranger is not a threat but an icon of Christ.

### Holiness as Participation

The ethical commands of Leviticus 19 are not merely rules to obey but descriptions of the holy life — the life that participates in God's own character. God is just, therefore His people must be just. God loves, therefore His people must love. This participatory logic is the essence of theosis: the human person becomes what God is by grace. St. Athanasius expresses this with maximal clarity: "God became man so that man might become God."
