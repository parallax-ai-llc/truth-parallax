## The Cleansing of the Leper

Leviticus 14 prescribes the elaborate purification ritual for a person healed of *tzaraat*. The priest goes outside the camp to examine the healed person, then performs a two-stage ritual. First, two clean birds are taken — one is killed over running water, and the living bird is dipped in the blood and released into the open field. The person washes, shaves, waits seven days, then on the eighth day presents guilt, sin, and burnt offerings. Blood and oil are applied to the right ear, right thumb, and right toe — the same pattern as priestly ordination. The chapter also addresses *tzaraat* in houses, with a similar two-bird ceremony.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "The priest shall command that one of the birds be killed in an earthenware vessel over fresh water" (14:5)

The two-bird ritual is one of the richest typological images in Leviticus. The Fathers read the two birds as Christ in His two natures or in His death and resurrection. The bird killed over running water represents Christ's death; the living bird dipped in blood and released represents Christ's resurrection and ascension, bearing the marks of His sacrifice into heaven. St. Cyril of Alexandria develops this extensively: the running water represents baptism, the earthen vessel the humanity of Christ, and the release into the open field the liberation accomplished by the resurrection.

### "He shall apply some of the blood of the guilt offering to the lobe of the right ear, the thumb of the right hand, and the great toe of the right foot" (14:14)

The application of blood and oil to ear, thumb, and toe directly parallels the priestly ordination in Leviticus 8. The healed leper undergoes a rite structurally identical to priestly consecration — he is being re-created, re-consecrated, restored to the full dignity of the holy people. Origen sees this as the restoration of the baptismal grace that sin had damaged. In Orthodox practice, the restored penitent is received back into full communion — not as a diminished member but as one fully reconstituted in the Body of Christ.

### "He shall take two birds and cedarwood and scarlet yarn and hyssop" (14:4)

The materials — cedar, scarlet yarn, hyssop — recur in purification contexts (cf. Numbers 19). Hyssop is associated with cleansing in Psalm 50/51 ("Purge me with hyssop and I shall be clean"). The Orthodox Vespers of Forgiveness Sunday uses Psalm 50 as its centerpiece, and the entire Lenten journey is structured as a purification ritual leading to Paschal restoration. The cedar represents incorruptibility, the scarlet yarn the blood of sacrifice, and the hyssop the instrument of application — together they constitute a complete image of sacramental restoration.

## Theological Significance

### Baptismal Typology

The purification ritual of Leviticus 14 is one of the strongest Old Testament types of baptism. The healed leper washes (baptism), is anointed with oil (chrismation), and is marked with blood (Eucharist). The progression mirrors the Orthodox sacraments of initiation. The seven-day waiting period corresponds to the catechumenate — the period of instruction and preparation before full incorporation. The eighth-day completion again evokes the resurrection and the new creation inaugurated in Christ.

### The Healing of Houses

The extension of purification to houses (14:33-53) demonstrates that healing is not limited to persons but extends to their environment. Orthodox practice includes the blessing of homes (especially at Theophany, when holy water is sprinkled throughout the house), the exorcism of places afflicted by spiritual disorder, and the sanctification of buildings through prayer and sacramental acts. The material world is capable of both corruption and sanctification.

### The Priest Goes Outside the Camp

The priest must go outside the camp to meet the healed leper (14:3) — he does not wait for the afflicted person to come to him. This anticipates Christ, who "suffered outside the gate" (Hebrews 13:12), going out to meet sinners where they are. Orthodox missionary and pastoral theology draws from this the imperative of going out — the Church does not merely wait for the lost to arrive but seeks them in their places of exile and isolation.
