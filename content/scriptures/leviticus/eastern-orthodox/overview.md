## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Leviticus reads the book through the lens of the Church Fathers and the liturgical tradition. The sacrificial system is understood as a type of the Eucharistic sacrifice; the holiness laws reveal the nature of God's uncreated holiness; and the entire book is oriented toward theosis — humanity's participation in the divine life. The Orthodox liturgy preserves many Levitical patterns in its structure of sacred time, sacred space, and ritual purity.

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Patristic authority**: Leviticus is read through Origen, Gregory of Nyssa, Cyril of Alexandria, and other Fathers who developed spiritual and allegorical readings
- **Liturgical continuity**: Orthodox worship preserves Levitical patterns — the iconostasis as veil, the altar as holy of holies, the priest as mediator
- **Theosis**: The command "Be holy, for I am holy" (19:2) is understood as a call to deification through participation in God's uncreated energies
- **Mystical interpretation**: The sacrifices are read as symbols of the soul's offering of itself to God
