## The Peace Offering (Shelamim)

Leviticus 3 details the peace offering, or *shelamim*, derived from the root *shalom* — wholeness, completeness, peace. This sacrifice is unique in that portions are shared among God (the fat burned on the altar), the priests, and the offerer, creating a three-way communion meal. Animals from the herd or flock may be offered, male or female, but the fat and blood are absolutely reserved for the Lord. The chapter establishes the perpetual prohibition: "You shall eat neither fat nor blood" (3:17).

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "If his offering is a sacrifice of peace offering" (3:1)

The peace offering is the most communal of all Levitical sacrifices. Origen interprets it as the sacrifice that establishes reconciliation and fellowship — not merely between God and the individual, but within the entire worshipping community. In Orthodox liturgical theology, the Eucharist is understood as the supreme peace offering: it is the sacrifice that reconciles heaven and earth, restores communion between God and humanity, and binds the faithful to one another in the Body of Christ.

### "He shall lay his hand on the head of his offering and kill it at the door of the tabernacle of meeting" (3:2)

The location at the door of the tabernacle is significant. St. Cyril of Alexandria reads this as a type of the threshold between the earthly and heavenly realms. In Orthodox churches, the Royal Doors of the iconostasis function as this threshold — the liturgical action passes between the nave (the world) and the sanctuary (heaven). The peace offering killed at the door signifies that reconciliation occurs at the meeting point of divine and human, a mystery fully realized in the Incarnation.

### "All fat is the Lord's" (3:16)

The fat represents the choicest, richest portion — the best belongs to God. St. John Chrysostom draws from this the principle that Christians must offer God not their leftovers but their first and finest. In the liturgical tradition, the *proskomedia* (preparation of gifts) selects the finest portion of the prosphora to become the Lamb, echoing this Levitical principle. Gregory the Theologian teaches that offering the best is not a burden but a recognition that everything already belongs to God.

## Theological Significance

### Communion as the Heart of Worship

The peace offering reveals that the purpose of sacrifice is not merely propitiation but *koinonia* — communion. Orthodox theology insists that the Eucharist is not a repetition of Calvary but a participation in the one eternal sacrifice of Christ, made present in the liturgical assembly. The shared meal of the peace offering prefigures the Eucharistic banquet where heaven and earth commune at one table. The dismissal prayer of the Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom echoes this: "We have seen the true Light, we have received the heavenly Spirit, we have found the true Faith."

### The Prohibition of Blood

The absolute prohibition against consuming blood (3:17) carries profound theological weight. Blood is the seat of life, and life belongs to God alone. Orthodox theology sees this fulfilled in an astonishing reversal: in the Eucharist, Christ commands His disciples to drink His Blood, because He alone has the authority to give divine life. What was forbidden under the Law is now commanded under grace — not as a violation but as a transcendence of the old covenant by the new.

### Peace as Eschatological Wholeness

The *shalom* of the peace offering is not mere absence of conflict but the positive fullness of divine life communicated to creation. The Orthodox understanding of theosis is precisely this eschatological peace — the complete restoration of the human person and, through humanity, of all creation to communion with God. The liturgical greeting "Peace be to all" carries this full Levitical weight.
