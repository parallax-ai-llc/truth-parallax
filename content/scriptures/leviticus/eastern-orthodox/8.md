## The Consecration of the Priesthood

Leviticus 8 narrates the ordination of Aaron and his sons as priests, fulfilling the instructions given in Exodus 29. Moses washes them with water, vests them in sacred garments, anoints the tabernacle and altar with oil, pours anointing oil on Aaron's head, offers a bull as a sin offering, a ram as a burnt offering, and a second ram as the "ram of ordination." Blood is placed on the right ear, right thumb, and right toe of the ordinands. They are commanded to remain at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting for seven days.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "Moses brought Aaron and his sons and washed them with water" (8:6)

The washing is the first act of priestly consecration. The Fathers unanimously see this as a type of baptism — the foundational sacrament that must precede all others. St. Cyril of Jerusalem, in his *Mystagogical Catecheses*, draws extensively on Levitical consecration imagery to explain the baptismal rite. In Orthodox practice, baptism by triple immersion washes the catechumen and initiates the process that culminates in chrismation and communion — the full priestly consecration of the royal priesthood.

### "He poured some of the anointing oil on Aaron's head and anointed him to consecrate him" (8:12)

The anointing with sacred oil (*chrism*) directly prefigures the Orthodox sacrament of Chrismation, administered immediately after baptism. The priest anoints the newly baptized with the Holy Myron (chrism) on the forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, breast, hands, and feet — a pattern that echoes the Levitical anointing of the whole person. The Myron is consecrated by the Ecumenical Patriarch or the head of each autocephalous church, maintaining an unbroken continuity with the apostolic anointing.

### "He put some of the blood on the lobe of Aaron's right ear and on the thumb of his right hand and on the great toe of his right foot" (8:23)

The application of blood to ear, hand, and foot consecrates the priest's hearing (to receive God's word), his action (to do God's work), and his walking (to follow God's path). Origen reads this as the sanctification of the whole person through specific faculties — the priest is set apart in every dimension of his being. Orthodox ordination prayers echo this holistic consecration, asking that the ordinand's mind, hands, and feet be dedicated to divine service.

## Theological Significance

### Apostolic Succession and Ordination

Orthodox theology insists on the unbroken succession of ordination from the apostles to the present bishops and priests. Leviticus 8 establishes the foundational pattern: consecration is not self-appointed but conferred through an established mediator (Moses) by divine command. The laying on of hands in Orthodox ordination (*cheirotonia*) preserves this principle — no one takes the priesthood upon himself (Hebrews 5:4), but is called and consecrated through the Church's living tradition.

### The Seven Days of Consecration

The seven-day period of remaining at the Tent of Meeting (8:33-35) is a period of liminal transformation — the ordinands are being changed from laymen to priests. St. Gregory the Theologian, in his oration *On the Priesthood*, warns against rushing into sacred ministry without adequate preparation. The Orthodox practice of extended preparation before ordination — seminary formation, diaconal service, spiritual direction — reflects this principle of measured transformation.

### Vestments as Theological Symbols

The detailed vesting of Aaron (8:7-9) establishes the principle that priestly garments are not mere clothing but theological statements. Each vestment signifies an aspect of the priest's ministry and identity. The Orthodox tradition preserves elaborate liturgical vestments — the sticharion (baptismal robe), epitrachelion (stole of pastoral authority), phelonion (covering of divine protection) — each with specific prayers of vesting that articulate their spiritual meaning.

### The Ram of Ordination

The "ram of ordination" (*eil milluim*, literally "ram of filling") fills the priests' hands with their sacred function. The Hebrew root *male* (to fill) gives the ordination its name — the priests' hands are "filled" with their vocation. Orthodox theology understands ordination as the filling of the person with the grace of the Holy Spirit for a specific ministry, not a conferral of personal honor or status.
