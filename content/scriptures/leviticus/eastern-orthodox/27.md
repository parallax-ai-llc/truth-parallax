## Vows, Valuations, and Tithes

Leviticus 27 serves as an appendix to the book, addressing the valuation of persons, animals, houses, and land dedicated to the Lord through special vows. When someone dedicates a person to the Lord by vow, a monetary valuation is assessed based on age and sex, with provision for the poor. Dedicated animals that are clean must be given; unclean animals may be redeemed at their assessed value plus one-fifth. Houses and land follow similar redemption rules. The chapter concludes with legislation on tithes — a tenth of grain, fruit, and livestock belongs to the Lord — and the closing formula: "These are the commandments that the Lord commanded Moses for the people of Israel on Mount Sinai" (27:34).

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "When someone makes a special vow to the Lord involving the valuation of persons" (27:2)

The practice of dedicating persons to God by vow finds its most famous biblical expression in Hannah's dedication of Samuel (1 Samuel 1:11). Orthodox tradition continues this practice in the offering of children to the monastic life or to the Church's service. The *tonsuring* of a new monastic is understood as a fulfillment of the Levitical vow — the person is "dedicated to the Lord" wholly and irrevocably. The monastic vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience are the Christian form of the Levitical dedicatory vow.

### "If he who vowed it wishes to redeem it, then he shall add a fifth to the valuation" (27:13)

The twenty-percent surcharge for redemption teaches that withdrawing a gift from God is costlier than giving it in the first place. Origen applies this principle to the spiritual life: to return to sin after repentance is worse than never having repented, because the soul has tasted grace and rejected it (cf. 2 Peter 2:21). The Orthodox canonical tradition reflects this principle: repeated offenses after confession may receive more stringent penances, not as punishment but as recognition of the increased gravity of knowing the good and choosing its opposite.

### "Every tithe of the land, whether of the seed of the land or of the fruit of the trees, is the Lord's; it is holy to the Lord" (27:30)

The tithe — one-tenth of all produce — belongs to God as a recognition that all blessings originate in divine generosity. Orthodox stewardship theology draws on this principle: giving is not optional charity but the return of what already belongs to God. The offering at the Divine Liturgy, the support of the clergy, and the care for the poor are all expressions of this Levitical mandate. St. John Chrysostom teaches that the tithe was the minimum under the Law; under grace, the standard should be higher, not lower.

## Theological Significance

### The Freedom and Weight of Vows

Orthodox theology takes vows with utmost seriousness. Baptismal vows, monastic vows, and marriage vows are not casual promises but irrevocable consecrations that reshape the person's identity. The Levitical legislation on vows reveals both the freedom of the offerer (vows are voluntary, not compelled) and the binding nature of what is freely given (once offered, it belongs to God). Ecclesiastes 5:4-5 echoes this: "It is better that you should not vow than that you should vow and not pay."

### Valuation and Human Dignity

The monetary valuations assigned to persons in Leviticus 27 — differentiated by age and sex — can appear troubling to modern readers. Orthodox theology reads these not as assessments of human worth (which is infinite and equal in God's sight) but as economic calculations related to the practical value of labor lost when a person is dedicated to sacred service. The deeper theological truth is that every human person, regardless of economic valuation, can be dedicated to God — the sliding scale for the poor (27:8) ensures that no one is excluded from the possibility of consecration.

### The Tithe and Total Offering

The tithe represents a minimum — a formal acknowledgment that everything belongs to God. But the trajectory of Leviticus as a whole points beyond the tithe to total offering: the burnt offering consumes the whole animal, the Holiness Code demands entire sanctification of life, and the call "be holy as I am holy" admits no reservation. Orthodox spiritual theology moves from the discipline of tithing to the freedom of total self-offering — the saints give not a tenth but everything, holding nothing back from God.

### The Book's Conclusion

The closing formula — "These are the commandments that the Lord commanded Moses for the people of Israel on Mount Sinai" (27:34) — ties the entire book to the Sinai revelation. Leviticus is not a collection of arbitrary rules but the continuation of the divine self-disclosure that began with the thunder and fire of Exodus 19. The Orthodox Church reads Leviticus during the liturgical year not as historical curiosity but as the living word of God, addressed to every generation, fulfilled in Christ, and enacted in the sacramental life of the Church.
