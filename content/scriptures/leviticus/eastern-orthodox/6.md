## Priestly Regulations for the Offerings

Leviticus 6 provides detailed instructions for the priests regarding the administration of the sacrifices. It begins with laws on restitution for fraud and false oaths (6:1-7), then prescribes the perpetual fire on the altar that must never be extinguished (6:8-13), the priest's portion of the grain offering (6:14-18), the daily grain offering of the high priest (6:19-23), and regulations for the sin offering (6:24-30). The emphasis shifts from the offerer's perspective (chapters 1-5) to the priestly handling of the sacred rites.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "The fire shall be kept burning on the altar continuously; it shall not go out" (6:13)

The perpetual fire is one of the most potent symbols in Levitical legislation. Orthodox theology reads this as a type of the unceasing prayer that sustains the Church. St. John Cassian identifies the perpetual fire with the injunction to "pray without ceasing" (1 Thessalonians 5:17), and the hesychast tradition of the Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") is understood as the Christian's perpetual altar fire. In monastic practice, the lampada (oil lamp) burning continuously before icons echoes this Levitical command.

### "Every male among the children of Aaron may eat of it. It is a perpetual statute" (6:18)

The priestly consumption of the holy offerings is not mere eating but a liturgical act — participation in the holiness of the sacrifice. St. Cyril of Alexandria reads this as a prefiguration of the clergy's communion at the altar. In the Orthodox Divine Liturgy, the clergy commune before the laity, consuming the reserved portions within the sanctuary, maintaining the Levitical pattern of priestly access to the holiest portions.

### "Whatever touches its flesh shall be holy" (6:27)

The contagious nature of holiness — that which touches the holy becomes holy — is a Levitical principle with profound implications. Orthodox theology affirms the real communication of holiness through material contact: relics sanctify, holy water blesses, the Eucharistic elements transmit divine life. This is not magic but the natural consequence of a creation designed to be a vehicle of divine energy. St. John of Damascus defends the veneration of material things precisely on this basis.

## Theological Significance

### Sacred Order and Liturgical Precision

The detailed priestly regulations of Leviticus 6 reveal that worship is not casual or spontaneous but ordered, precise, and reverent. The Orthodox liturgical tradition inherits this conviction: the Typikon prescribes the exact form of every service, the rubrics govern every gesture, and deviation from the sacred order is understood as a failure of reverence. This is not legalism but love — the care taken with holy things reflects the gravity of encounter with the living God.

### The Priestly Garments and Transformation

The instruction that the priest must change garments when carrying ashes outside the camp (6:10-11) signifies the distinction between sacred and common space. Orthodox clergy vest liturgically before serving and remove vestments afterward — a ritual act of entering and leaving the sacred sphere. The vestments are not decorative but functional: they mark the priest's temporary entry into heavenly service, echoing the angelic vestments described in Isaiah 6 and Revelation.

### The Fire That Never Dies

Patristic and liturgical tradition connects the perpetual fire to the Holy Spirit, who continuously enlivens the Church. The Pentecostal fire that descended on the apostles (Acts 2:3) is the eschatological fulfillment of the altar fire. In the Orthodox paschal celebration, the Holy Fire ceremony in Jerusalem enacts this connection — the fire that emerges from the Holy Sepulchre is received as a sign of the resurrection and the unquenchable life of God.
