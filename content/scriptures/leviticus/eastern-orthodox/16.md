## Yom Kippur: The Day of Atonement

Leviticus 16 stands at the structural and theological center of the book, describing the annual Day of Atonement (*Yom Kippur*). Aaron enters the Holy of Holies only on this day, wearing simple white linen rather than his ornate vestments. He offers a bull for his own sins and the priesthood, then takes two goats — one is sacrificed as a sin offering for the people, its blood sprinkled on the mercy seat (*kapporet*); the other is the scapegoat (*azazel*), upon whose head Aaron confesses the sins of Israel before it is sent into the wilderness. The day is declared "a statute forever" — a sabbath of complete rest and self-denial.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "He shall take the two goats and set them before the Lord at the entrance of the tent of meeting" (16:7)

The two goats represent a single atonement in two aspects. The Fathers consistently read the slain goat as Christ in His death and the scapegoat as Christ bearing away sin. St. Cyril of Alexandria develops this at length: the goat whose blood enters the Holy of Holies represents Christ's sacrificial death that opens heaven, while the goat sent into the wilderness represents Christ's descent into Hades, carrying away the sins of the world to the place of desolation. The Orthodox hymns of Great Saturday celebrate precisely this double movement — death and descent, sacrifice and liberation.

### "He shall sprinkle some of the blood on the mercy seat and before the mercy seat" (16:14-15)

The mercy seat (*kapporet*) — the golden cover of the Ark of the Covenant — is the place where God's presence meets the blood of atonement. The Epistle to the Hebrews identifies Christ as both the true High Priest and the true *kapporet* (Romans 3:25, often translated "propitiation"). Orthodox theology reads the altar table in every church as the eschatological mercy seat — the place where the sacrificial blood of Christ is perpetually present in the Eucharist, mediating reconciliation between God and humanity.

### "Aaron shall lay both his hands on the head of the live goat and confess over it all the iniquities of the people of Israel" (16:21)

The confession of sins over the scapegoat is the most explicit Old Testament instance of substitutionary bearing of sin. Orthodox Christology affirms that Christ truly bears the sins of the world — not in a merely forensic transfer but in an ontological assumption. He takes human sin into Himself and destroys it through the power of His divine life. The Good Friday Vespers hymn echoes this: "Today He who hung the earth upon the waters is hung upon the Cross."

## Theological Significance

### The Great and Holy Friday

The Orthodox liturgical tradition identifies the Day of Atonement with Great and Holy Friday. The Vespers of Great Friday, with its removal of Christ's body from the Cross (the *apokathelosis*) and the procession of the Epitaphios (burial cloth), enacts the atonement drama. Christ is the High Priest who enters the heavenly Holy of Holies "not with the blood of goats and calves, but with His own blood" (Hebrews 9:12). The entire Holy Week liturgical cycle — from the Bridegroom Matins through the Paschal Vigil — is the Church's lived commentary on Leviticus 16.

### The Scapegoat and the Descent into Hades

The scapegoat sent into the wilderness, bearing the sins of the people to a "land of separation" (*eretz gezerah*), prefigures Christ's descent into Hades. Orthodox theology holds that Christ descended into the realm of death not as a victim but as a conqueror — He entered death to destroy death from within. The Paschal icon shows Christ standing on the shattered gates of Hades, raising Adam and Eve. The wilderness destination of the scapegoat becomes, in Christ, the site of ultimate victory.

### The White Linen Vestments

Aaron enters the Holy of Holies not in his glorious vestments but in simple white linen — the garments of humility and purity. The Fathers see in this a type of Christ's kenosis (self-emptying): the divine Logos sets aside His glory and takes the form of a servant (Philippians 2:7). In Orthodox liturgical practice, the celebrant wears white vestments at Pascha and on feasts of the resurrection, recalling both the transfigured Christ and the angels at the empty tomb.

### Once a Year — and Once for All

The annual nature of Yom Kippur — its necessity to be repeated — reveals its provisionality. The Epistle to the Hebrews argues that Christ's sacrifice is "once for all" (*ephapax*), rendering repetition unnecessary (Hebrews 9:12; 10:10). The Orthodox Eucharist is not a repetition of Calvary but a participation in the one eternal sacrifice — the Lamb "slain from the foundation of the world" (Revelation 13:8).
