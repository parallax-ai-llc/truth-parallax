## The Sexual Prohibitions

Leviticus 18 establishes a comprehensive code of sexual prohibitions, framed by the command to avoid the practices of Egypt (where Israel had lived) and Canaan (where Israel was going). The chapter prohibits sexual relations with close relatives (incest in various degrees), intercourse during menstruation, adultery, the offering of children to Molech, male homosexual acts, and bestiality. The land itself is said to be defiled by such acts and will "vomit out" those who practice them, as it vomited out the nations before Israel.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "You shall not do as they do in the land of Egypt... and you shall not do as they do in the land of Canaan" (18:3)

The framing of sexual ethics within a narrative of distinctiveness is theologically significant. Israel is called to be different — set apart, holy. Orthodox moral theology does not derive its ethics primarily from natural observation or social consensus but from divine revelation and the call to theosis. St. John Chrysostom frequently contrasts Christian moral standards with the surrounding pagan culture, insisting that the Church must maintain its prophetic distinctiveness even when — especially when — it is countercultural.

### "You shall keep My statutes and My judgments, which if a man does, he shall live by them" (18:5)

St. Paul quotes this verse in Romans 10:5 and Galatians 3:12, contrasting the "righteousness of the Law" with the "righteousness of faith." Orthodox theology, following the Fathers, does not set law and grace in absolute opposition but sees the Law as a pedagogue leading to Christ (Galatians 3:24). The commandments reveal God's will for human flourishing; grace provides the power to fulfill them. The moral content of the Law remains valid even after its ceremonial aspects have been transcended in Christ.

### "Do not profane the name of your God" (18:21)

The prohibition against offering children to Molech is placed within the sexual ethics legislation, linking child sacrifice to sexual disorder. The Fathers understood Molech worship as the ultimate perversion — the destruction of innocent life for self-serving spiritual power. St. Basil the Great's canonical epistles on the protection of children reflect the ongoing application of this principle, and the Orthodox Church's consistent defense of the sanctity of human life from conception flows from this Levitical foundation.

## Theological Significance

### Natural Law and Divine Command

Orthodox moral theology recognizes that the sexual prohibitions of Leviticus 18 reflect both divine command and the natural moral law accessible through reason. St. John of Damascus articulates a natural law theory grounded in the divine logos — the rational order of creation. The sexual prohibitions are not arbitrary but reflect the structure of human nature as created by God. Violations of this order damage the person and the community, as the chapter's language of the land being defiled and vomiting out its inhabitants vividly expresses.

### The Body and Theosis

Orthodox sexual ethics are rooted in the high theology of the body. The body is destined for resurrection and theosis — it is not a prison to be escaped but a temple to be sanctified. Sexual discipline is therefore not repressive moralism but part of the ascetic training that prepares the whole person — body and soul — for participation in divine life. The monastic tradition of celibacy and the marital tradition of faithful union are both expressions of the same principle: the body is ordered toward God.

### The Land Theology

The remarkable teaching that the land itself is defiled by sexual sin (18:25-28) extends the consequences of moral disorder beyond the individual to the environment. Orthodox theology affirms this cosmic dimension of sin — the whole creation "groans" under the weight of human fallenness (Romans 8:22). The blessing of fields, waters, and homes in the Orthodox liturgical tradition reflects the conviction that the material world participates in both human sin and human redemption.
