## Completion of Sacrificial Legislation

Leviticus 7 concludes the sacrificial instructions begun in chapter 1. It details the guilt offering ritual (7:1-10), elaborates the peace offering into three subtypes — thanksgiving, votive, and freewill (7:11-21) — and restates the absolute prohibitions against eating fat or blood (7:22-27). The chapter closes with the priests' share of the peace offering — the breast (wave offering) and the right thigh (heave offering) — and a formal summary of the entire sacrificial system (7:37-38).

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "If he offers it for a thanksgiving, then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanksgiving unleavened cakes" (7:12)

The thanksgiving peace offering (*todah*) holds a special place in patristic exegesis. The Fathers saw in the *todah* the most direct prefiguration of the Eucharist — the very word *eucharistia* means thanksgiving. St. Irenaeus of Lyon argues that Christ instituted the Eucharist as the new *todah*, the sacrifice of thanksgiving that fulfills and transcends all Levitical offerings. The bread and wine of the thanksgiving offering foreshadow the Eucharistic species.

### "The flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offering for thanksgiving shall be eaten on the day of his offering" (7:15)

The requirement that the thanksgiving offering be consumed the same day emphasizes freshness, immediacy, and presence. Stale offerings are unacceptable. Origen applies this to the spiritual life: spiritual nourishment must be received and lived today, not stored up or delayed. The Orthodox insistence on frequent communion — and the teaching that the Eucharist should be received with fresh preparation through fasting, prayer, and confession — reflects this principle.

### "Every person who eats of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings that belong to the Lord while an uncleanness is on him, that person shall be cut off" (7:20)

Receiving the sacred meal in a state of uncleanness brings not blessing but judgment. This passage directly informs the Orthodox practice of requiring preparation before communion. St. Paul echoes this in 1 Corinthians 11:27-29 — eating and drinking unworthily brings condemnation. The pre-communion prayers, the fasting rule, and the practice of confession before receiving the Eucharist are all rooted in this Levitical warning.

## Theological Significance

### The Eucharist as Todah Offering

Contemporary Orthodox liturgical theology, drawing on the work of scholars like Alexander Schmemann, emphasizes that the Eucharist is fundamentally an act of thanksgiving — the Church's *todah*. The entire structure of the Divine Liturgy moves toward the great Eucharistic Prayer (the *anaphora*), which is a thanksgiving to the Father for creation, redemption, and sanctification. This is not an innovation but the recovery of the oldest layer of sacrificial meaning.

### The Wave and Heave Offerings

The priestly portions — the breast waved before the Lord and the right thigh raised up — involve liturgical gestures of elevation and presentation. Orthodox liturgical practice preserves these gestures: the elevation of the gifts during the Great Entrance, the raising of the Lamb during the anaphora, and the elevation of the chalice and diskos all echo the Levitical movements of wave and heave. Sacred gesture is not decorative but constitutive of worship.

### The Perpetual Prohibition of Blood

The reiteration of the blood prohibition (7:26-27) underscores its permanent significance. Orthodox canon law historically maintained this prohibition in its food regulations, and the Apostolic Decree of Acts 15:29 — which includes abstaining from blood — was understood by the Eastern Church as a binding regulation, not a temporary accommodation. The Eucharistic reversal, where Christ commands the drinking of His Blood, is all the more extraordinary against this background.
