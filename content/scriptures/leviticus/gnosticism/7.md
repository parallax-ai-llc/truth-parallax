## Completing the Sacrificial Legislation

Leviticus 7 concludes the sacrificial legislation begun in chapter 1. It provides additional regulations for the guilt offering, describes three types of peace offerings (thanksgiving, votive, and freewill), establishes rules for eating sacrificial meat within prescribed timeframes, and mandates the severe penalty of being "cut off from the people" (*kareth*) for eating fat or blood. The chapter closes by summarizing all offerings and assigning the priestly portions.

## The Closed System: Every Category Accounted For

The comprehensive summary in verses 37-38 — listing burnt offering, grain offering, sin offering, guilt offering, ordination offering, and peace offering — reveals a closed system in which every conceivable human need, occasion, and transgression has a corresponding sacrificial response. The Gnostic reader sees in this completeness the Demiurge's totalitarian ambition: no aspect of human experience escapes the sacrificial apparatus.

The *Tripartite Tractate* (NHC I,5) describes the Demiurge's creation as a comprehensive counterfeit of the Pleroma, mirroring every aspect of the higher reality in degraded material form. The complete sacrificial system of Leviticus 1-7 is the Demiurge's counterfeit of the spiritual communion that exists naturally within the Fullness.

## Three Kinds of Peace Offering: Multiplying Obligation

The distinction between thanksgiving, votive, and freewill offerings (vv. 12-16) multiplies the occasions for sacrifice. Every positive experience (thanksgiving), every aspiration (vow), and every spontaneous impulse of devotion (freewill) is channeled into the sacrificial system. The Demiurge ensures that even gratitude, hope, and generosity are captured and processed through his altar. Hans Jonas, in *The Gnostic Religion*, describes how the archonic powers colonize every dimension of human interiority — and the three categories of peace offering illustrate this colonization of emotion by ritual.

## Time Limits and Wasted Meat: The Logic of Scarcity

The regulations requiring sacrificial meat to be eaten within strict timeframes — the thanksgiving offering on the same day (v. 15), the votive and freewill offerings within two days (v. 16), with the remainder burned on the third day (v. 17) — introduce artificial urgency into the system. The worshiper must consume quickly or forfeit the offering entirely. This logic of scarcity and waste reflects the Demiurge's material economy, where perishability governs all things.

The *Gospel of Truth* contrasts the transient nature of the material world with the eternal abundance of gnosis: "The Father's knowledge appears, in order that oblivion might vanish and the Father might be known." Gnosis is not subject to time limits or decay.

## Kareth: The Ultimate Archonic Penalty

The penalty of being "cut off from the people" for consuming fat or blood (vv. 25, 27) reveals the Demiurge's ultimate sanction: excommunication from the community of the creator-god's subjects. For the Gnostic, this penalty is paradoxically a liberation. To be cut off from the Demiurge's people is to be freed from the Demiurge's system. The *Testimony of Truth* celebrates those who separate themselves from the false community of the archonic cult.

Birger Pearson notes that many Gnostic communities understood themselves as precisely those who had been "cut off" from the mainstream religious establishment — and who recognized this separation as salvific rather than punitive.

## The Priestly Portions: Systematic Extraction

The final allocation of priestly portions — the breast of the wave offering and the right thigh of the contribution (vv. 31-34) — completes the economic structure of the Levitical system. The priests receive a guaranteed share of every peace offering, creating a permanent revenue stream tied to the continuation of sacrifice. The *Second Treatise of the Great Seth* describes the rulers of this world as parasites who sustain themselves through the religious labor of those they govern. The priestly portions are a concrete expression of this parasitic relationship.

## The Gnostic Counter-Economy

Against the Demiurge's sacrificial economy — with its taxes, time limits, penalties, and priestly extractions — the Gnostic tradition posits an economy of grace and knowledge that is inexhaustible, free, and available to all who awaken. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that true spiritual gifts cannot be measured, divided, or consumed. They belong to the order of the Pleroma, not the order of the altar.

## Significance

Leviticus 7 brings the sacrificial legislation to its comprehensive conclusion, revealing the Demiurge's closed system of material worship in its full scope. Every occasion is covered, every portion allocated, every violation penalized. The Gnostic reads this completeness not as divine generosity but as totalitarian control — a system from which only gnosis provides escape.
