## Penalties for Violations of the Holiness Code

Leviticus 20 prescribes punishments for violations of the laws given in chapters 18-19. The chapter mandates death by stoning for those who give children to Molech, death for cursing one's parents, death for various sexual offenses (adultery, incest, homosexual acts, bestiality), and *kareth* (being cut off) for others. The chapter concludes by reiterating the distinction between clean and unclean, and the call to holiness.

## The Demiurge's Violence Unveiled

If Leviticus 18-19 established the Demiurge's comprehensive legal code, Leviticus 20 reveals the enforcement mechanism: death. The creator-god who demands "Be holy, for I am holy" now prescribes the killing of those who fail to comply. For the Gnostic reader, this chapter is among the most damning in the entire Old Testament. The *Testimony of Truth* asks bluntly: what kind of God kills those who disobey his rules? The answer, for the Gnostic, is clear — not the transcendent Father of light and mercy but a jealous, violent archon.

Hans Jonas, in *The Gnostic Religion*, identifies the Gnostic revulsion at the Old Testament God's violence as one of the movement's foundational impulses. The God who commands stoning is not the God who is love. Leviticus 20, with its catalogue of capital penalties, provides the textual basis for this Gnostic conviction.

## Stoning: Communal Violence in Service of the Archon

The method of execution — stoning, in which the entire community participates — is particularly revealing. The Demiurge distributes the violence among his subjects, making them complicit in the killing. No single executioner bears the burden; the whole community is implicated. The *Apocalypse of Peter* (NHC VII,3) describes how the archonic system conscripts its victims as agents of their own oppression. Communal stoning is a literal enactment of this principle: the people enforce their own bondage by killing those who deviate.

Birger Pearson observes that the Gnostic rejection of institutional violence was a consistent feature of the movement. The communities that produced the Nag Hammadi texts did not practice capital punishment or communal enforcement of religious law — they sought liberation through knowledge, not compliance through terror.

## The Molech Prohibition Revisited: Archonic Rivalry

The death penalty for those who give children to Molech (vv. 2-5) is presented as absolute. But the Gnostic reader continues to observe the structural similarity between the Demiurge's demands and Molech's: both require the destruction of what is valuable (animals, grain, children) in service of a deity. The difference is one of degree, not kind. The Demiurge kills the worshiper who sacrifices to Molech — responding to violence with violence, demonstrating that his moral objection to child sacrifice is less about the welfare of children than about exclusive loyalty.

## "Their Blood Is Upon Them": The Transfer of Guilt

The recurring phrase "their blood shall be upon them" (vv. 9, 11, 12, 13, 16, 27) transfers the guilt for execution from the community to the condemned. The Demiurge absolves his agents of responsibility for the killing: the victim has brought death upon himself. The *Exegesis on the Soul* describes how the archonic powers blame their victims for the suffering they themselves inflict. "Their blood is upon them" is the Demiurge's version of this victim-blaming — a moral inversion in which the one killed bears the guilt for his own death.

## The Clean/Unclean Distinction as Political Tool

The chapter's concluding exhortation to distinguish between clean and unclean animals (v. 25) — immediately following the catalogue of death penalties — reveals the connection between purity classification and social control. Those who violate the boundaries (sexual, ritual, dietary) are killed; those who maintain them are "holy." The entire system of clean and unclean is thus revealed as a political instrument — a means of defining the community's boundaries through the threat of violence.

Michael Williams, in *Rethinking "Gnosticism"*, notes that Gnostic communities defined membership not through purity observance or legal compliance but through the possession of gnosis. The Demiurge's system of classification-plus-enforcement is the exact opposite of the Gnostic principle of inclusion through knowledge.

## Gnosis Against the Death Penalty

The Gnostic alternative to the Demiurge's lethal justice is the recognition that no material transgression — sexual, ritual, or dietary — can harm the divine spark. The spark is inviolable; it belongs to the Pleroma and is merely passing through the material world. The *Gospel of Truth* teaches that when ignorance is dispelled, "anguish and terror" cease. The anguish and terror of Leviticus 20 — the threat of stoning, the fear of being "cut off" — are products of the Demiurge's system of ignorance, and they dissolve in the light of gnosis.

## Significance

Leviticus 20 reveals the Demiurge as a deity who enforces his will through lethal violence, conscripting his own worshipers as executioners. The Gnostic reads this chapter as definitive evidence that the god of Leviticus is not the true God — for the transcendent Father neither demands blood sacrifice nor prescribes death for those who fail to comply with material regulations.
