## Overview

Leviticus (*Vayikra*, "And He called") is the third book of the Bible and the Torah. Situated at the center of the Pentateuch, it provides the legal and ritual framework for Israel's life in the presence of a holy God. The book is primarily concerned with sacrifice, purity, priesthood, and holiness — the conditions under which sinful humanity can dwell with the divine.

## Structure

Leviticus is commonly divided into several major sections:

1. **Laws of Sacrifice** (Chapters 1–7): Burnt offerings, grain offerings, peace offerings, sin offerings, and guilt offerings
2. **Ordination of Priests** (Chapters 8–10): Consecration of Aaron and his sons; the deaths of Nadab and Abihu
3. **Laws of Purity** (Chapters 11–15): Clean and unclean animals, childbirth, skin diseases, bodily discharges
4. **The Day of Atonement** (Chapter 16): Yom Kippur — the annual purification of the sanctuary and the nation
5. **The Holiness Code** (Chapters 17–26): Ethical and ritual laws governing all aspects of communal life
6. **Vows and Tithes** (Chapter 27): Regulations for dedications and valuations

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Leviticus is perhaps the most differently interpreted book across religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: The heart of Torah — the detailed guide for Israel's sacred life; the basis of vast halakhic literature
- **Christianity**: Read typologically as pointing to Christ's sacrifice and priesthood; the Day of Atonement prefigures the cross
- **Islam**: Echoes of purity laws and sacrifice in Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh), though not directly authoritative
- **Gnosticism**: The sacrificial system as evidence of the Demiurge's demand for material appeasement

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Holiness | "Be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy" (19:2) — the central imperative |
| Sacrifice | The system of offerings that mediates between God and Israel |
| Priesthood | Aaron and his descendants as mediators of the divine-human relationship |
| Purity | The distinction between clean and unclean, sacred and profane |
| Atonement | The covering of sin through blood sacrifice, culminating in Yom Kippur |
| Social Ethics | The Holiness Code's vision of justice, neighborly love, and care for the vulnerable |
