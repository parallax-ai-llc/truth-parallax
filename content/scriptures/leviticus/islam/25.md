## Summary of Leviticus 25

Leviticus 25 introduces the Sabbatical year (every seventh year, the land must rest from cultivation) and the Jubilee year (every fiftieth year), when all land returns to its original tribal owners, slaves are freed, and debts are released. The chapter prohibits permanent land sales ("the land is Mine; you are but aliens and tenants"), regulates interest-free lending to the poor, and establishes humane treatment of slaves. The theological foundation is God's sovereign ownership of the land and the people's status as tenants.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 25's economic legislation resonates profoundly with Islamic economic principles. The prohibition on exploitative lending parallels Islam's absolute prohibition of *riba* (usury/interest):

> "Those who consume interest cannot stand [on the Day of Resurrection] except as one stands who is being beaten by Satan into insanity... Allah has permitted trade and has forbidden interest." (Al-Baqarah 2:275)

The Levitical prohibition is limited to lending to fellow Israelites; the Islamic prohibition is universal, applying to all financial transactions regardless of the parties' identities.

The theological principle that the land belongs to God and humans are merely stewards finds a powerful echo in the Islamic concept of *khilafah* (stewardship/vicegerency):

> "It is He who has made you successors (*khala'if*) upon the earth." (Al-An'am 6:165)

All property in Islamic theology belongs ultimately to God; humans hold it in trust (*amanah*). This undergirds the entire Islamic economic system, from *zakat* obligations to inheritance law.

The Jubilee principle — periodic redistribution to prevent permanent inequality — has no direct Islamic institutional equivalent, but its spirit is embodied in Islamic inheritance law (*mirath*), which mandates the division of estates among a broad range of heirs, preventing the concentration of wealth across generations. The Quran specifies fractional shares for parents, spouses, children, and siblings (Al-Nisa 4:11-12), creating a systematic redistribution mechanism.

The manumission of slaves in the Jubilee finds its Islamic parallel in the extensive Quranic and Hadith provisions encouraging and often requiring the freeing of slaves. The Quran makes freeing a slave an expiation for various offenses (Al-Ma'idah 5:89, Al-Mujadilah 58:3) and lists *fi al-riqab* (freeing slaves) among the approved categories of *zakat* expenditure (Al-Tawbah 9:60).

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *riba* is one of the most developed areas of Islamic commercial law. The Hanafi school distinguishes between *riba al-fadl* (excess in exchange of commodities) and *riba al-nasi'ah* (excess due to delay in payment). Both are prohibited. Modern Islamic banking has developed complex financial instruments (*murabahah*, *musharakah*, *ijarah*) to facilitate commerce without interest, representing a practical application of the anti-usury principle shared with Leviticus 25.

The fiqh of *waqf* (charitable endowment) provides an Islamic mechanism for preventing land from being permanently alienated from community use. Like the Jubilee principle, *waqf* removes property from the market permanently, dedicating its income to charitable purposes in perpetuity.

Islamic law on slavery (*ahkam al-riqq*) progressively restricts the institution while stopping short of outright abolition — a trajectory some scholars compare to the Levitical approach, which humanizes slavery without eliminating it. The Prophet Muhammad said: "Your slaves are your brothers whom Allah has placed under your authority... Feed them with what you eat and clothe them with what you wear" (Sahih al-Bukhari).

The Sabbatical year concept — allowing the land to lie fallow — resonates with Islamic agricultural practice. While not religiously mandated, the concept of *ihya al-mawat* (reviving dead land) and the Prophet's discouragement of excessive cultivation reflect a concern for environmental stewardship consistent with the Levitical approach.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The economic justice provisions of Leviticus 25 are among the most favorably viewed portions of the Torah in Islamic scholarship. The prohibition of usury, the concern for the poor, and the principle of divine ownership of land all align with core Islamic values.

Al-Tabari noted that the Torah's economic legislation, including provisions paralleling Leviticus 25, represents the "guidance and light" that the Quran attributes to the original Torah. These provisions are seen as substantially preserved because they align with universal prophetic teaching.

However, some scholars note that the limitation of the usury prohibition to intra-Israelite lending (allowing interest on loans to foreigners) contradicts the universalism of prophetic ethics and may represent a later accommodation to commercial interests.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Qaradawi, in *Fiqh al-Zakat*, draws explicit parallels between the Levitical economic system and Islamic economic principles, arguing that both emerge from the same prophetic tradition of economic justice rooted in divine sovereignty over all wealth and land.

The contemporary scholar Muhammad Umar Chapra, in *Islam and the Economic Challenge*, discusses how the Levitical Jubilee and the Islamic economic system both attempt to solve the fundamental problem of wealth concentration, using different but structurally analogous mechanisms.

Timur Kuran, in *Islam and Mammon*, provides a critical analysis of Islamic economic institutions, noting their historical connections to earlier Abrahamic economic legislation while examining how they have evolved in practice.
