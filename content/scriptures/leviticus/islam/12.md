## Summary of Leviticus 12

Leviticus 12, the shortest chapter in the book, addresses the ritual impurity of a woman after childbirth. After bearing a son, she is impure for seven days (as during menstruation), then continues in "blood purification" for thirty-three days — totaling forty days. For a daughter, the periods are doubled: fourteen days of impurity and sixty-six days of purification, totaling eighty days. At the end, she brings a burnt offering and a sin offering to the priest for atonement.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Islam's concept of *nifas* (postpartum bleeding) provides a direct parallel to Leviticus 12. During *nifas*, a woman is exempt from prayer (*salat*) and fasting (*sawm*) and must refrain from sexual intercourse, similar to the Levitical impurity period. However, there are significant differences.

The duration of *nifas* in Islamic law does not distinguish between male and female children. The maximum period varies by school: the Hanafi school sets it at forty days, the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools at sixty days, and the Maliki school at seventy days. The Levitical distinction — forty days for a son, eighty for a daughter — has no Islamic parallel.

The Quran does not explicitly address postpartum purification, but a Hadith narrated by Umm Salamah states: "A woman in the time of the Prophet used to sit in *nifas* for forty days" (Sunan al-Tirmidhi). The forty-day period for a male child in Leviticus interestingly coincides with this common Islamic ruling.

The Levitical requirement of a sin offering after childbirth is particularly problematic from an Islamic perspective. Islam does not view childbirth as involving any sin or moral culpability. The *nifas* period is understood purely as a physiological state affecting ritual capacity, not a state of spiritual defilement requiring atonement.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *nifas* is discussed extensively in all schools. During *nifas*, a woman may not perform *salat*, fast, touch the Quran (according to the majority), or have sexual intercourse. She must make up missed fasting days but not missed prayers — a distinction unique to Islamic law.

The Hanafi school holds that if bleeding stops before forty days, the woman should perform *ghusl* (full ritual bath) and resume worship. If bleeding exceeds forty days, the excess is treated as *istihadah* (abnormal bleeding), which carries different rules.

The gender-based disparity in Leviticus 12 — twice the impurity period for a female child — finds no support in Islamic law or theology. Islamic scholars who have engaged this text view the distinction as evidence of either cultural attitudes toward gender that do not reflect divine will, or as a textual alteration (*tahrif*).

The concept of *ghusl* after *nifas* parallels the Levitical purification process but without the sacrificial component. The woman simply performs a full body wash with the intention of purification, after which she is fully restored to ritual participation.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The sin offering required after childbirth is a focal point for Islamic critique. The concept that a woman requires atonement for the natural process of giving birth contradicts the Quranic celebration of motherhood:

> "And We have enjoined upon man [care] for his parents. His mother carried him, [increasing her] in weakness upon weakness." (Luqman 31:14)

Muslim scholars argue that the original Torah would not have framed childbirth as requiring atonement, and that this provision reflects priestly interests in maintaining control over life-cycle events.

The doubled impurity period for a female child is viewed as evidence of patriarchal interpolation. Al-Tabari noted that the Quranic message of gender equity in spiritual matters (Al-Ahzab 33:35) makes such distinctions untenable as divine legislation.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah discusses postpartum purification in *Zad al-Ma'ad*, drawing on prophetic practice to establish the Islamic framework. He implicitly contrasts the simplicity of the Islamic approach with what he characterizes as the excessive ritualism of earlier dispensations.

The contemporary scholar Khaled Abou El Fadl has discussed the gender implications of Leviticus 12 in comparative perspective, arguing that Islamic law, while imperfect on gender issues, avoided the specific theological burden of connecting childbirth to sin.

Kecia Ali, in *Sexual Ethics and Islam*, examines how both Levitical and Islamic traditions regulate the female body, noting that while the regulatory impulse is shared, the theological framing differs significantly — Islam treating postpartum states as physiological rather than morally charged.
