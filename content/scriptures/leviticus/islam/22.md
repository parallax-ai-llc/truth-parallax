## Summary of Leviticus 22

Leviticus 22 continues the priestly regulations, addressing two main themes: when priests may and may not eat sacred offerings (they must be ritually pure), and the quality standards for sacrificial animals (they must be without defect — no blindness, lameness, mutilation, or disease). A minimum age requirement is set (at least eight days old), and a mother and its young must not be slaughtered on the same day. The chapter concludes with the imperative not to profane God's holy name.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The requirement that sacrificial animals be free from defects has a remarkably close parallel in Islamic law. The Prophet Muhammad specified the defects that disqualify an animal from *qurbani*:

> "Four [types] are not permitted for sacrifice: an animal that is obviously blind in one eye, an animal that is obviously sick, an animal that is obviously lame, and an emaciated animal that has no marrow in its bones." (Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Sunan Abu Dawud)

This Hadith corresponds precisely to the categories in Leviticus 22:22-24. The shared concern is that offerings to God must represent the best of what one has, not the least valuable.

The minimum age requirement in Leviticus 22:27 (eight days old before an animal can be offered) corresponds to Islamic age requirements for sacrificial animals. The Shariah specifies: at least six months for a sheep (or one year according to some scholars), one year for a goat, two years for cattle, and five years for a camel.

The prohibition against slaughtering a mother and its young on the same day (Lev 22:28) resonates with the broader Islamic ethic of compassion toward animals. The Prophet Muhammad forbade separating a mother animal from its offspring and prohibited causing unnecessary suffering:

> "Allah has prescribed *ihsan* [excellence/compassion] in all things. So if you kill, kill well; and if you slaughter, slaughter well. Let each of you sharpen his blade and spare suffering to the animal he slaughters." (Sahih Muslim)

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *udhiyah* (sacrificial offering) specifies detailed criteria for acceptable animals. The Hanafi school lists the following disqualifying defects: blindness, loss of more than a third of the ear or tail, broken horn (if the breakage reaches the root, according to Abu Hanifah), lameness preventing the animal from walking to the slaughter site, and severe emaciation.

The Shafi'i school adds that any named defect in the Hadith disqualifies the animal absolutely, while unnamed defects may disqualify if they are equivalent in severity. This parallels the Levitical approach of listing specific defects while implying a general standard of soundness.

The concept of *ihsan* (excellence, doing things in the most beautiful way) provides the theological framework for Islamic sacrifice quality standards. The Quran says:

> "Indeed, Allah orders justice and *ihsan*." (Al-Nahl 16:90)

Applied to sacrifice, *ihsan* means selecting the best animal, treating it humanely, and performing the slaughter with skill and compassion — a comprehensive ethic of excellence that encompasses the Levitical concern with physical perfection.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The animal quality standards in Leviticus 22 are generally accepted by Muslim scholars as reflecting authentic divine concern for the dignity of worship. The principle that offerings must represent one's best, not one's surplus or castoffs, is consistent with Quranic teaching.

However, the restriction of sacred food consumption to priests and their households (Lev 22:10-16) is viewed as a priestly privilege that may not reflect the original Mosaic intent. Islamic scholars argue that in the original dispensation, sacred offerings would have been distributed to the needy rather than reserved for a priestly class.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Nawawi, in his commentary on Sahih Muslim, provides detailed analysis of the Hadith on sacrificial animal defects, systematically addressing each category and its implications. His treatment demonstrates the Islamic tradition's parallel concern with the Levitical theme of offering excellence.

The contemporary scholar Mawil Izzi Dien, in *The Environmental Dimensions of Islam*, explores how both Levitical and Islamic sacrificial regulations embed animal welfare concerns within their ritual frameworks, representing early forms of what modern ethicists call "animal rights."

Sami Aldeeb Abu-Sahlieh, in his comparative study of Islamic and Jewish slaughter laws, provides a detailed parallel analysis of the Levitical and Islamic systems, demonstrating extensive structural similarities in animal quality requirements while noting differences in priestly versus communal contexts.
