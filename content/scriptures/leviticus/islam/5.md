## Summary of Leviticus 5

Leviticus 5 addresses the guilt offering (*asham*) and situations requiring it: failing to testify when called as a witness, touching something unclean, making a rash oath, or committing a trespass against holy things. The chapter introduces a sliding scale of offerings based on the offerer's means — from a lamb or goat down to two birds or even a grain offering for the very poor. The guilt offering also requires restitution plus an additional twenty percent penalty when sacred property has been misused.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The sliding scale of offerings in Leviticus 5, which accommodates the financial capacity of the offerer, finds a strong parallel in Islamic jurisprudence's principle of *kaffara* proportional to ability. The Quran explicitly provides graduated options for expiation:

> "And whoever cannot find [or afford it] — then a fast of three days. That is the expiation for oaths when you have sworn." (Al-Ma'idah 5:89)

The Levitical requirement of restitution plus twenty percent for trespass against holy property resonates with Islamic principles of *daman* (liability) and *ta'wid* (compensation). In Islamic commercial law, one who wrongfully takes or damages another's property must restore it in full, and the Hanbali and Maliki schools permit additional compensatory damages in certain circumstances.

The concept of being held accountable for failing to testify (Lev 5:1) closely parallels the Islamic prohibition against concealing testimony:

> "And do not conceal testimony, for whoever conceals it — his heart is indeed sinful." (Al-Baqarah 2:283)

Islamic law makes testimony (*shahadah*) a communal obligation (*fard kifayah*), and deliberately withholding testimony is considered a major sin.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *kaffara* in Islam mirrors the Levitical graduated system. For breaking the fast of Ramadan deliberately, the Hanafi and Maliki schools prescribe (in order): freeing a slave, fasting for sixty consecutive days, or feeding sixty poor people. This graduated structure — from the most demanding to the most accessible — reflects the same principle as Leviticus 5's sliding scale.

The twenty percent penalty for trespass finds an echo in the Islamic law of *ghulul* (misappropriation of public or sacred funds). The Quran sternly warns:

> "It is not for a prophet to take illegally from the war booty... And whoever takes illegally will bring what he took on the Day of Resurrection." (Aal-e-Imran 3:161)

Islamic law requires full restitution for misappropriated communal funds, with additional penalties at the discretion of the judge (*qadi*).

The concept of rash oaths (*lajaj*) is addressed extensively in Islamic law. The Shafi'i school distinguishes between *yamin laghw* (unintentional oaths, which require no expiation), *yamin mun'aqidah* (deliberate oaths, which require *kaffara* if broken), and *yamin ghamus* (false oaths, which are major sins requiring sincere repentance).

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The sliding scale in Leviticus 5, which shows sensitivity to the economic circumstances of worshippers, is generally viewed favorably by Muslim commentators as reflecting authentic divine concern for social equity. This principle aligns with the Islamic legal maxim *la yukallifu Allahu nafsan illa wus'aha* ("God does not burden a soul beyond its capacity," Al-Baqarah 2:286).

However, the designation of specific sacrificial animals and the priestly rituals surrounding them are viewed as later elaborations. Al-Juwayni (d. 1085 CE), the Shafi'i jurist, argued that divine law in its purest form prescribes principles rather than minute ritual details, suggesting that the specific procedures in Leviticus 5 reflect institutional development.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah (d. 1350 CE), in *I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in*, extensively discusses how divine law across all dispensations accommodates human capacity. He cites the Levitical sliding scale as evidence that the original Mosaic law, like Islamic Shariah, embodied the principle of flexibility (*taysir*) in religious obligation.

The contemporary scholar Mohammad Hashim Kamali, in *Principles of Islamic Jurisprudence*, draws parallels between the Levitical and Islamic systems of graduated obligation, arguing that both reflect the universal divine attribute of mercy (*rahmah*) that tempers justice with compassion.

Tariq Ramadan, in his work on Islamic ethics, notes that the Levitical emphasis on restitution — not merely repentance but material compensation for wrongs — aligns with the Islamic insistence that repentance for interpersonal wrongs requires restoring the rights of the injured party, not merely seeking God's forgiveness.
