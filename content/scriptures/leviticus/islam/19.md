## Summary of Leviticus 19

Leviticus 19 is considered the ethical heart of Leviticus, opening with "Be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy." It covers an extraordinary range of moral teachings: honoring parents, keeping the Sabbath, avoiding idols, leaving harvest gleanings for the poor, prohibiting theft, lying, fraud, oppression, and withholding wages, justice for the disabled, impartial judgment, prohibiting slander, the famous command to "love your neighbor as yourself" (v. 18), and various other social and religious duties. It is one of the most admired chapters across all Abrahamic traditions.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 19 reads like a compendium of Islamic ethics (*akhlaq*). The parallels are so extensive that this chapter is perhaps the strongest evidence for shared Abrahamic moral foundations.

The opening command "Be holy" corresponds to the Islamic concept of *taqwa* (God-consciousness), the supreme ethical virtue in the Quran:

> "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous [muttaqi] of you." (Al-Hujurat 49:13)

"Love your neighbor as yourself" (Lev 19:18) is paralleled by the Prophet's teaching: "None of you truly believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself" (Sahih al-Bukhari). This Hadith is considered one of the foundational ethical principles of Islam.

The command to leave gleanings for the poor and the stranger (Lev 19:9-10) corresponds to the Islamic obligations of *zakat* (obligatory charity) and *sadaqah* (voluntary charity). The Quran repeatedly emphasizes the rights of the poor in the wealth of the rich:

> "And in their wealth is a known right for the petitioner and the deprived." (Al-Ma'arij 70:24-25)

The prohibition against withholding wages (Lev 19:13) is echoed in the Hadith Qudsi: "I will be the adversary of three persons on the Day of Resurrection: one who hires a worker and does not pay his wages" (Sahih al-Bukhari).

The command for just weights and measures (Lev 19:35-36) is a recurring Quranic theme, with an entire surah (Al-Mutaffifin, "The Defrauders") devoted to it:

> "Woe to those who give less [than due], who, when they take a measure from people, take in full, but if they give by measure or by weight to them, they cause loss." (Al-Mutaffifin 83:1-3)

The prohibition against cursing the deaf or putting a stumbling block before the blind (Lev 19:14) resonates with the Quranic emphasis on protecting the vulnerable and the Prophet's specific teachings about kindness to the disabled.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

Nearly every ethical injunction in Leviticus 19 has a corresponding ruling in Islamic fiqh. The Shariah addresses: parental respect (*birr al-walidayn*), honest business practices (*mu'amalat*), just wages (*ujrah*), fair judgment (*qada*), protection of the vulnerable, and prohibition of slander (*ghibah*) and tale-bearing (*namimah*).

The Hanafi scholar al-Kasani (d. 1189 CE), in *Bada'i al-Sana'i*, systematically addresses commercial ethics in terms that parallel Leviticus 19's economic provisions. The prohibition against fraud (*ghish*), price manipulation, and exploitation of the ignorant are shared concerns.

The Maliki concept of *sadd al-dhara'i* (blocking the means to evil) provides a theoretical framework for understanding the Levitical prohibitions: they aim not only to prevent specific wrongs but to block the pathways that lead to injustice.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

Leviticus 19 is among the portions of the Torah that Muslim scholars most readily accept as substantially preserving the original Mosaic teaching. The ethical content aligns so closely with Quranic morality that it serves as evidence for the Islamic claim that the Torah originally contained authentic divine guidance.

Al-Tabari cited the ethical provisions of the Torah, including material paralleling Leviticus 19, as examples of the "guidance and light" (Al-Ma'idah 5:44) that the Quran attributes to the Torah. The suggestion is that while ritual legislation may have been altered, the moral core remains largely intact.

Ibn Taymiyyah argued that the ethical teachings shared between the Torah and the Quran represent the universal moral law (*al-shari'ah al-kulliyyah*) that all prophets brought, and that Leviticus 19 preserves this universal law.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Ghazali, in the ethics sections of *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, addresses virtually every theme found in Leviticus 19 — honesty, justice, compassion, neighborly love, economic fairness — demonstrating the deep structural parallels between Levitical and Islamic moral teaching.

The modern scholar Tariq Ramadan, in *Radical Reform*, argues that the shared ethical foundations visible in texts like Leviticus 19 should serve as a basis for inter-Abrahamic ethical cooperation, while acknowledging that theological differences remain.

Khalid Blankinship, in his work on Islamic ethics, traces the concept of social justice in the Quran and Sunnah, demonstrating continuities with earlier Abrahamic ethical traditions including the Levitical Holiness Code, while emphasizing Islam's claim to represent their fullest expression.
