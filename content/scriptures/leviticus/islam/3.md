## Summary of Leviticus 3

Leviticus 3 describes the peace offering (*shelamim*), a sacrifice in which parts of the animal — specifically the fat surrounding the internal organs — are burned on the altar, while the meat is shared in a communal meal among the offerer, the priests, and the community. Animals could be cattle, sheep, or goats, and the chapter strictly prohibits the consumption of fat and blood. The peace offering expresses gratitude, fellowship, and covenant communion with God.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The communal dimension of the peace offering finds its closest Islamic parallel in the *udhiyah* (Eid al-Adha sacrifice) and the *walimah* (wedding feast). The Quranic injunction regarding the Eid sacrifice explicitly mandates communal sharing:

> "So eat of them and feed the miserable and poor." (Al-Hajj 22:28)

The Sunnah specifies that the sacrificial meat should be divided into three portions: one-third for the family, one-third for friends and neighbors, and one-third for the poor. This tripartite distribution mirrors the Levitical peace offering's division among God (the altar portion), the priests, and the offerer.

The Levitical prohibition on consuming blood is strongly echoed in Islam. The Quran states:

> "Prohibited to you are dead animals, blood, the flesh of swine..." (Al-Ma'idah 5:3)

The prohibition on consuming fat (*chelev*) in Leviticus, however, is not replicated in Islamic law. The Quran itself references this as a distinction, noting that certain fat prohibitions were specific to the Israelites:

> "And to those who are Jews We prohibited every animal of uncloven hoof, and of the cattle and the sheep We prohibited to them their fat." (Al-An'am 6:146)

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *aqiqah* (sacrifice at the birth of a child) provides another parallel to the peace offering's function of marking joyful occasions with sacrifice and communal feasting. According to the Hadith in Sunan al-Tirmidhi, two sheep are sacrificed for a boy and one for a girl, with the meat distributed to family, neighbors, and the needy.

The concept of *shukr* (gratitude) in Islam, expressed through voluntary sacrifice and charity, corresponds to the thanksgiving dimension of the peace offering. The Shafi'i school particularly emphasizes *salat al-shukr* (prayer of gratitude) and voluntary charity as expressions of thanks for divine blessings.

Islamic jurisprudence on blood prohibition is unanimous across all four Sunni schools and Shia law: flowing blood (*dam masfuh*) is absolutely haram. The method of Islamic slaughter (*dhabihah*) — cutting the throat, esophagus, and jugular veins — is specifically designed to drain the blood thoroughly, fulfilling the same concern expressed in Leviticus 3:17.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The peace offering's emphasis on communal fellowship and gratitude is generally viewed favorably by Muslim commentators, as it aligns with Islamic values. However, the designation of specific portions for the priestly class is seen as a potential later addition serving Aaronic interests.

Al-Shahrastani observed that the division of sacrificial meat among various parties — a common feature across ancient Semitic religions — likely predates the Mosaic revelation and was codified in Leviticus in a particular institutional form. Islamic scholars argue that the Quranic distribution model (family, neighbors, poor) represents the original divine intent, uncomplicated by priestly claims.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Taymiyyah (d. 1328 CE), in his discussions of pre-Islamic Abrahamic practices, noted that the communal sacrifice and shared meal is a persistent feature of Abrahamic religion, preserved in Islam through the Eid sacrifice. He regarded the Levitical peace offering as evidence that communal worship through sacrifice was always central to God's intended religion.

The contemporary scholar Taha Jabir al-Alwani has explored how the ethics of communal eating and sharing in both Levitical and Islamic law reflect a shared theological conviction: that food is a divine trust (*amanah*) to be distributed equitably, not hoarded or consumed in isolation.

Fazlur Rahman, in *Major Themes of the Quran*, situates the Islamic approach to sacrifice within the broader Quranic critique of ritualism: the outward act is meaningful only when accompanied by genuine piety and social responsibility, a principle he sees as correcting the potential for mere ritualism in the Levitical system.
