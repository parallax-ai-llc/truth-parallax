## Summary of Leviticus 4

Leviticus 4 introduces the sin offering (*chattat*), prescribed for unintentional sins committed by various categories of people: the anointed priest, the entire congregation, a ruler, or a common person. The type of animal required varies by the status of the offerer — a bull for the priest or the congregation, a male goat for a ruler, and a female goat or lamb for a commoner. The chapter emphasizes that atonement requires blood manipulation, with blood sprinkled before the veil of the sanctuary and applied to the horns of the altar.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Islam's concept of *kaffara* (expiation) provides the closest parallel to the Levitical sin offering. The Quran prescribes specific expiatory acts for certain transgressions:

> "Allah will not impose blame upon you for what is meaningless in your oaths, but He will impose blame upon you for [breaking] what you intended of oaths. So its expiation is the feeding of ten needy people... or the freeing of a slave. But whoever cannot find [or afford it] — then a fast of three days." (Al-Ma'idah 5:89)

A fundamental divergence exists, however, in the mechanism of atonement. Leviticus requires blood sacrifice mediated by a priest; Islam insists on direct repentance (*tawbah*) to God without any intermediary:

> "And whoever does a wrong or wrongs himself but then seeks forgiveness of Allah will find Allah Forgiving and Merciful." (Al-Nisa 4:110)

The Quranic concept of *tawbah* involves three conditions articulated by al-Nawawi: ceasing the sin, feeling genuine remorse (*nadam*), and resolving not to repeat it. For sins against other people, a fourth condition is added: restoring the rights of the wronged party. No animal sacrifice is required.

The Levitical distinction between sins of the priest and sins of a commoner has no parallel in Islam, which insists on the equality of all believers before God in matters of repentance and forgiveness.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

Islamic *kaffara* takes several forms depending on the transgression. For breaking an oath, the options are feeding ten poor people, clothing them, or freeing a slave, with fasting as a last resort. For accidental killing, the *kaffara* is freeing a slave and paying blood money (*diyah*), or fasting for two consecutive months (Al-Nisa 4:92).

The Hanafi school classifies sins into those requiring *kaffara* (expiation through specific acts) and those requiring only *tawbah* (repentance). The Levitical sin offering corresponds most closely to the *kaffara* category, though Islamic *kaffara* never involves animal sacrifice for sin — sacrifice is reserved for devotion and celebration.

Al-Ghazali (d. 1111 CE), in *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, discusses repentance at length, arguing that the inner transformation of the heart is the only true atonement. He explicitly contrasts this with what he calls the "externalism" of earlier sacrificial systems, though he does not name Leviticus specifically.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The elaborate blood-manipulation rituals described in Leviticus 4 are viewed by many Muslim scholars as among the most likely candidates for priestly elaboration (*tahrif*). The notion that God requires specific blood rituals to forgive unintentional sins is seen as incompatible with the Quranic portrayal of divine mercy:

> "Say: O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins." (Al-Zumar 39:53)

Ibn Hazm argued that a truly merciful God would not make forgiveness contingent on the death of an animal, and that the sacrificial atonement system represents a corruption of the original Mosaic teaching on repentance.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Tabari, in his *Tafsir*, discusses the Quranic references to the Torah's atonement provisions, noting that while the earlier revelation may have included expiatory acts, the Quran simplified and universalized the path to forgiveness. He sees this as consistent with the principle of progressive revelation (*al-naskh*).

The modern scholar Ismail al-Faruqi argued in *Islam and Other Faiths* that the Levitical sin offering reflects a "sacramental" mentality foreign to authentic Abrahamic monotheism, in which divine forgiveness flows directly from God's mercy in response to human repentance.

Abdulaziz Sachedina, in *The Islamic Roots of Democratic Pluralism*, discusses how both Levitical and Islamic traditions grapple with the tension between divine justice and divine mercy, arriving at different institutional solutions while sharing the underlying theological conviction that God desires human rectification rather than punishment.
