## Summary of Leviticus 27

Leviticus 27, the final chapter, addresses the monetary valuation of persons, animals, houses, and fields dedicated to God through vows. It establishes specific monetary equivalents based on age and gender for persons dedicated to the sanctuary. Animals vowed to God cannot be exchanged; if one tries to substitute, both animals become holy. Houses and fields can be redeemed by paying the valuation plus a fifth. The chapter concludes with laws on tithes — a tenth of produce and livestock — and the declaration that these belong to the Lord.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The concept of *nadhr* (vow) in Islam provides a direct parallel to the Levitical system of dedicatory vows. The Quran recognizes vows as binding:

> "Then let them end their untidiness and fulfill their vows and perform *tawaf* around the ancient House." (Al-Hajj 22:29)

The Prophet said: "Whoever vows to obey Allah, let him obey Him; and whoever vows to disobey Allah, let him not disobey Him" (Sahih al-Bukhari). This Hadith establishes the principle that only vows involving obedience to God are binding — a significant departure from the Levitical system, which treats all vows as absolutely binding regardless of content.

The Levitical tithe system — giving a tenth of one's produce and livestock — corresponds functionally to *zakat*, though the structures differ. Zakat is calculated at 2.5% of accumulated wealth (*nisab*), 10% of rain-fed agricultural produce, 5% of irrigated produce, and 20% of discovered treasure. The rates and bases differ from the flat Levitical tenth, but the underlying principle — that God has a claim on a portion of all wealth and produce — is shared.

The gender-based valuation system in Leviticus 27:1-8, where women are valued at roughly sixty percent of men, has no Islamic parallel. Islamic inheritance law does assign women half the share of men in certain categories (Al-Nisa 4:11), but this is based on different legal and social rationales (men's obligation to provide *nafaqah*/financial support for families) rather than an intrinsic valuation of persons.

The redemption mechanism — paying the valuation plus a fifth to reclaim dedicated property — parallels the Islamic concept of *kaffarah* for broken vows. In Islamic law, one who breaks a vow must expiate through feeding ten poor people, clothing them, or freeing a slave, with fasting as a last resort (Al-Ma'idah 5:89).

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *nadhr* is well-developed across all schools. The Hanafi school classifies vows into: *nadhr tabarrur* (vow of good deed), which is binding; *nadhr lajaj* (vow made in anger or as a conditional threat), which can be fulfilled or expiated through *kaffarah*; and vows involving disobedience, which are invalid and require *kaffarah*.

The Shafi'i school requires that the vowed act be an act of worship (*ibadah*) for it to be binding. A vow to perform a merely permissible (*mubah*) act is not binding, though some scholars recommend fulfilling it. This represents a narrower scope than the Levitical system, which encompasses the dedication of persons, animals, and property without restriction.

The fiqh of *zakat* provides the systematic Islamic equivalent of the Levitical tithe. Al-Qaradawi's *Fiqh al-Zakat* remains the most comprehensive modern treatment, systematically addressing zakat on: gold and silver, commercial goods, agricultural produce, livestock, minerals, and (according to some modern scholars) salaries and professional income. The system is more differentiated than the Levitical flat tithe but serves the same redistributive function.

The concept of *waqf* (charitable endowment) provides another parallel to the Levitical dedication of property. Once a property is designated as *waqf*, it cannot be sold, inherited, or reclaimed — a permanence even more absolute than the Levitical dedication, which allows for redemption at a price.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The gender-based valuation system is viewed critically by Muslim scholars, who see it as reflecting cultural assumptions rather than divine will. The Quran's assertion of spiritual equality — "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (Al-Hujurat 49:13) — is seen as correcting such human valuations.

The tithe system, as a principle, is accepted as authentically Mosaic, since the Quran itself establishes obligatory charity. However, the specific designation of tithes for the Levites and priesthood is viewed as a later institutional arrangement.

Al-Shahrastani, in his analysis of Israelite religion, treated the economic legislation of Leviticus 27 as reflecting a mixed system: authentic prophetic principles of charity and dedication overlaid with institutional provisions serving priestly interests.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Rushd, in *Bidayat al-Mujtahid*, provides systematic treatment of vows and their legal consequences, noting parallels to earlier Abrahamic legislation while explaining the Islamic system's distinctive features.

The contemporary scholar Wael Hallaq, in *An Introduction to Islamic Law*, discusses how the Islamic legal system constructs economic obligations (zakat, *sadaqah*, *nadhr*, *kaffarah*) that serve the same social functions as the Levitical tithe and vow system while operating within a different institutional framework — one without a priestly class or central sanctuary.

Muhammad Akram Khan, in *Islamic Economics and Finance*, examines how the Islamic economic system's obligatory and voluntary wealth-transfer mechanisms (zakat, sadaqah, waqf, inheritance) create a comprehensive redistributive framework that accomplishes what the Levitical tithe and Jubilee system aimed to achieve, representing what he argues is the mature expression of the Abrahamic economic ethic.
