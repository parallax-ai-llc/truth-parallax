## Summary of Leviticus 17

Leviticus 17 serves as a transition between the ritual legislation (chapters 1-16) and the Holiness Code (chapters 17-26). The chapter mandates that all animal slaughter must take place at the Tabernacle — even for food, not just sacrifice — to prevent unauthorized sacrifices "to the goat demons." It reiterates the absolute prohibition on consuming blood, grounding it theologically: "The life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it to you on the altar to make atonement for your souls." Animals found dead or killed by predators must not be eaten.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 17 addresses two themes of profound importance to Islamic law: the prohibition of blood and the requirements for proper slaughter. The parallels are extensive and substantive.

The blood prohibition is one of the most consistent continuities across the Abrahamic traditions. The Quran mirrors the Levitical rationale that blood carries a special status:

> "Prohibited to you are dead animals, blood..." (Al-Ma'idah 5:3)

The prohibition on eating animals found dead (*nevelah* in Hebrew, *maytah* in Arabic) is virtually identical in both systems. The Quran lists multiple categories: animals that die naturally, are strangled, struck to death, fallen, or gored (Al-Ma'idah 5:3). These correspond to the Levitical concern in verse 15.

However, the centralization requirement — that all slaughter must occur at the Tabernacle — has no Islamic parallel. Islam permits slaughter anywhere, provided the proper method and invocation are observed. The Prophet Muhammad said: "What is cut from a living animal is dead meat [and thus prohibited]" (Sunan Abu Dawud), establishing method rather than location as the criterion.

The Islamic method of slaughter (*dhabihah*) requires cutting the throat, windpipe, and blood vessels while invoking Allah's name (*bismillah*). This ensures maximum blood drainage, fulfilling the Levitical concern about blood consumption while freeing the practice from the requirement of priestly supervision or a central sanctuary.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *dhabihah* is one of the most practically relevant areas of Islamic law. The requirements across the four Sunni schools include: the slaughterer must be Muslim or from the People of the Book (*ahl al-kitab*), the animal must be alive at the time of slaughter, the name of Allah must be invoked (obligatory in Hanafi and Hanbali schools, recommended in the Shafi'i school), and the cut must sever the throat and major blood vessels.

The Levitical prohibition against sacrificing to "goat demons" (*se'irim*) finds a parallel in the Islamic prohibition against slaughtering in the name of anyone other than Allah. Meat from animals slaughtered with the invocation of a name other than God's is explicitly haram:

> "And that which has been dedicated to other than Allah." (Al-Ma'idah 5:3)

The Hanafi school permits the meat of People of the Book even without the Islamic invocation, based on the Quranic verse: "The food of those who were given the Scripture is lawful for you" (Al-Ma'idah 5:5). The Hanbali school is more restrictive, requiring certainty that the Christian or Jewish slaughterer invoked God's name.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The centralization requirement — all slaughter at the Tabernacle — is viewed by Muslim scholars as reflecting a specific institutional moment in Israelite history rather than a permanent divine command. The practical impossibility of centralizing all slaughter at one location suggests that this provision served political-religious purposes (preventing syncretistic worship at local shrines) rather than expressing a universal divine principle.

Ibn Kathir, discussing the Quranic dietary laws, argues that the original Mosaic dietary code would have resembled the Islamic model: simple rules about method and invocation, without the centralization requirement that bound slaughter to priestly oversight.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Qaradawi, in *The Lawful and the Prohibited in Islam*, provides an extensive treatment of Islamic slaughter law, noting the shared Abrahamic foundations while explaining Islam's departures. He argues that Islamic law preserved the essential concern (proper method, blood drainage, divine invocation) while removing institutional gatekeeping.

Joe Bradford, in his work on Islamic dietary law, explores how the principle of *tasmiyah* (invoking God's name at slaughter) connects Islamic practice to the Levitical theology that blood represents life and belongs to God.

Muhammad Hamidullah, in *Introduction to Islam*, discusses the Quranic dietary code as a simplification of the Levitical system that maintains the core prohibitions (blood, carrion, invocation of false deities) while eliminating what he characterizes as historically contingent priestly regulations.
