## Summary of Leviticus 16

Leviticus 16 describes the rituals of Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement — the most solemn day in the Israelite calendar. The high priest enters the Holy of Holies (the only person permitted, and only on this day), wearing simple linen garments. He offers a bull for his own sins, then casts lots over two goats: one is sacrificed as a sin offering, and the other (the "scapegoat" or *azazel*) symbolically receives the sins of the people and is driven into the wilderness. Blood is sprinkled on the mercy seat. The day requires fasting, rest from work, and self-denial.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The Day of Atonement connects to Islamic practice through the fast of Ashura (the 10th of Muharram). When the Prophet Muhammad arrived in Medina and found the Jews fasting on this day, he asked about it and was told it commemorated God's deliverance of Musa from Pharaoh. The Prophet responded:

> "We have more right to Musa than you." (Sahih al-Bukhari)

He then fasted and encouraged his followers to fast. While the Islamic understanding of Ashura shifted to commemorate the Exodus rather than atonement, the connection to the Mosaic tradition is explicit.

The fundamental theology of atonement differs radically between the two traditions. Leviticus 16 requires elaborate blood rituals, priestly mediation, and symbolic transfer of sin to the scapegoat. Islam teaches that sincere repentance (*tawbah*) brings direct divine forgiveness:

> "Indeed, Allah loves those who are constantly repentant and loves those who purify themselves." (Al-Baqarah 2:222)

The scapegoat ritual — transferring communal sin onto an animal — has no Islamic parallel and is theologically problematic from an Islamic perspective. The Quran establishes individual moral responsibility:

> "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another." (Al-An'am 6:164)

The concept of the high priest entering the Holy of Holies — a space where only one person may stand in God's presence — contradicts the Islamic principle that all believers have equal access to God. In Islamic worship, every believer in prostration is at the closest point to God: "The closest a servant is to his Lord is when he is in prostration" (Sahih Muslim).

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *tawbah* (repentance) provides the Islamic functional equivalent of Yom Kippur. The conditions for valid repentance, as systematized by al-Nawawi, are: cessation of the sin, genuine remorse, firm resolve not to repeat it, and (for interpersonal wrongs) restoration of the victim's rights. No priest, no blood, no ritual is required.

The fiqh of fasting (*sawm*) on Ashura is well-established. The majority view holds it as *mustahabb* (recommended) rather than obligatory, since the obligation was transferred to Ramadan. The Hanafi school emphasizes fasting the 9th and 10th (or 10th and 11th) to differentiate Islamic practice from Jewish observance, following the Prophet's stated intention.

The concept of collective atonement — the entire community's sins being addressed on one day — finds an echo in the Islamic concept of Laylat al-Qadr (the Night of Power) during Ramadan, described in the Quran as "better than a thousand months" (Al-Qadr 97:3). Worship on this night results in the forgiveness of all previous sins, according to Hadith (Sahih al-Bukhari).

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The Yom Kippur ritual, with its elaborate blood manipulation and the theologically problematic scapegoat mechanism, is viewed by Muslim scholars as substantially altered from any original Mosaic practice. The core principle — designating a day for communal repentance and renewal — is accepted as authentic. The ritual apparatus surrounding it is not.

Ibn Hazm specifically critiqued the scapegoat ritual as irrational, arguing that sin cannot be transferred to an animal and that such a concept undermines genuine moral responsibility. He viewed it as a corruption of the original Mosaic teaching on repentance.

Al-Shahrastani classified the Yom Kippur ritual among the "sacramental" elements of Israelite religion that Islam deliberately excised, returning to what he considered the pure Abrahamic model of direct repentance before God.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Ghazali, in *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, devotes an extensive section to repentance, describing it as one of the "stations" (*maqamat*) on the spiritual path. He argues that genuine *tawbah* accomplishes what the Yom Kippur ritual symbolizes — the complete purification of the self from sin — but through interior transformation rather than external ritual.

The modern scholar Abdulaziz Sachedina explores the theological differences between Levitical atonement and Islamic *tawbah*, arguing that Islam's direct-access model reflects a more optimistic anthropology — humans are capable of genuine repentance without sacerdotal assistance.

Reuven Firestone, in *An Introduction to Islam for Jews*, provides a nuanced comparison of Yom Kippur and Islamic repentance practices, noting both the historical connections (Ashura) and the fundamental theological divergences between vicarious atonement and direct repentance.
