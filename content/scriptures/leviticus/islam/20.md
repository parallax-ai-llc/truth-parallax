## Summary of Leviticus 20

Leviticus 20 prescribes penalties for violations of the laws in chapters 18-19. Death by stoning is mandated for giving children to Molech, cursing parents, adultery, certain incestuous relations, homosexual acts, and bestiality. Lesser penalties include being "cut off" from the people (possibly excommunication or divine punishment) for other offenses. The chapter reiterates the holiness imperative and the mandate to distinguish between clean and unclean.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 20's penal code corresponds structurally to the Islamic *hudud* (fixed divine penalties). Both systems share the conviction that certain offenses are so grave that God Himself prescribes the punishment, removing them from human discretion.

The Levitical death penalty for adultery finds a direct parallel in Islamic law. The Quran prescribes flogging for unmarried fornicators (Al-Nur 24:2), while the Hadith establishes stoning (*rajm*) for married adulterers, a punishment that Islamic scholars explicitly note was also prescribed in the Torah.

The Prophet Muhammad reportedly said regarding an adulterous couple: "I judge between you according to the Book of Allah" (Sahih Muslim), and in some narrations, the Torah's stoning provision was explicitly referenced, demonstrating the conscious continuity between Levitical and Islamic penal law.

However, Islam imposes nearly impossible evidentiary standards for *zina* — four credible eyewitnesses to the actual act of penetration (Al-Nur 24:4). False accusation of *zina* (*qadhf*) is itself a *hadd* crime punishable by eighty lashes. This framework effectively makes conviction almost impossible without confession, reflecting a mercy-oriented application of severe law.

The Levitical penalty for cursing parents corresponds to the Islamic emphasis on parental respect, though Islam does not prescribe capital punishment for this offense. The Quran commands:

> "And your Lord has decreed that you not worship except Him, and to parents, good treatment." (Al-Isra 17:23)

Disrespect to parents (*uquq al-walidayn*) is classified as a major sin (*kabirah*) in Islam but is addressed through social and spiritual sanctions rather than penal law.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The *hudud* in Islamic law include: *zina* (unlawful sexual relations), *qadhf* (false accusation of *zina*), *sariqah* (theft), *hirabah* (armed robbery), *shurb al-khamr* (drinking wine), and *riddah* (apostasy, though this is debated). The Levitical penal system is broader, including offenses like cursing parents and consulting mediums.

A crucial difference lies in the application philosophy. The Islamic legal maxim *idra'u al-hudud bi-l-shubuhat* ("avert the *hudud* by ambiguities") instructs judges to seek any reasonable doubt that would prevent the application of severe penalties. Umar ibn al-Khattab reportedly suspended the penalty for theft during a famine, demonstrating the contextual flexibility built into the Islamic system.

The Hanafi school is particularly known for its restrictive approach to *hudud* application, developing extensive criteria that must all be met before severe penalties can be imposed. This contrasts with the apparently more absolute Levitical formulation.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

Muslim scholars approach Leviticus 20 with a nuanced view. The principle that grave moral transgressions merit severe consequences is accepted as authentically divine. However, the specific catalogue of capital offenses and the apparent lack of evidentiary safeguards are viewed critically.

Al-Jassas (d. 981 CE), the Hanafi jurist, argued that the original Mosaic penal code likely included more protective procedures for the accused than the current text of Leviticus preserves, noting that divine justice requires procedural safeguards.

Ibn Rushd, in his comparative legal analysis, noted that the Levitical and Islamic penal systems share a common structure (divine prescription of penalties for grave offenses) while differing in specifics, suggesting a common origin that was elaborated differently in each tradition.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah, in *I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in*, extensively discusses the philosophy of divine punishment, arguing that *hudud* serve as deterrents and as expressions of the seriousness with which God views certain transgressions. He explicitly draws connections to earlier Abrahamic penal codes.

The contemporary scholar Tariq Ramadan has called for a moratorium on the application of *hudud* penalties in the modern world, arguing that the conditions of justice and social equity required by the Shariah are not met in contemporary Muslim-majority societies.

Mohammad Hashim Kamali, in *Punishment in Islamic Law*, provides a comprehensive analysis of *hudud* theory and application, arguing that the Islamic system's built-in procedural safeguards represent a deliberate humanization of the more austere penal traditions inherited from earlier Abrahamic legislation.
