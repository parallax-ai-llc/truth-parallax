## Summary of Leviticus 7

Leviticus 7 concludes the offering legislation that began in chapter 1. It provides further details on the guilt offering, the thanksgiving offering, and the freewill and vow offerings. The chapter specifies time limits for consuming peace offering meat (same day for thanksgiving, two days for vow and freewill offerings). It reiterates the absolute prohibitions against eating fat (*chelev*) and blood, declaring that violators shall be "cut off from their people." The chapter concludes with a summary of the priestly portions from each type of offering.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The blood prohibition in Leviticus 7:26-27 is one of the most direct parallels between Levitical and Islamic law. The Quran explicitly and repeatedly prohibits the consumption of blood:

> "He has only forbidden to you dead animals, blood, the flesh of swine, and that which has been dedicated to other than Allah." (Al-Baqarah 2:173)

Islamic slaughter (*dhabihah*) is specifically designed to drain blood thoroughly from the animal, fulfilling the same concern as the Levitical prohibition. The method — cutting the throat and blood vessels while the animal's heart continues to pump — ensures maximum blood drainage.

The fat prohibition, however, is unique to Israelite law. The Quran explicitly notes this as a distinction:

> "And to those who are Jews We prohibited every animal of uncloven hoof, and of the cattle and the sheep We prohibited to them their fat, except what adheres to their backs or the entrails or what is joined with bone. [By] that We repaid them for their injustice." (Al-An'am 6:146)

This verse is significant for Islamic exegesis: the fat prohibition is framed as a punitive restriction specific to the Israelites, not a universal divine command. Islamic law permits the consumption of animal fat without restriction, provided the animal was slaughtered properly.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *dhabihah* (Islamic slaughter) provides the most detailed parallel to Levitical blood regulations. All four Sunni schools agree that proper slaughter requires: mentioning the name of Allah (*tasmiyah*), cutting with a sharp instrument, severing the throat and blood vessels, and allowing the blood to drain. The Shafi'i school considers *tasmiyah* recommended but not obligatory, while the Hanafi and Hanbali schools consider it mandatory.

The time restrictions on consuming sacrificial meat in Leviticus 7 have a partial Islamic parallel. The Prophet Muhammad initially prohibited keeping sacrificial meat from Eid al-Adha beyond three days, but later relaxed this restriction, saying: "I had forbidden you to keep the meat of sacrificial animals beyond three days, but now keep and store as much as you wish" (Sahih Muslim).

The Levitical concept of *piggul* (rejected sacrifice due to improper intention) resonates with the Islamic principle that invalid *niyyah* (intention) invalidates an act of worship. In Shafi'i jurisprudence particularly, the intention must be properly formed at the time of slaughter for the meat to be halal.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The concluding summary in Leviticus 7:37-38, which situates the entire offering system at Sinai, is viewed critically by Muslim scholars who argue that the historical attribution masks a longer process of priestly elaboration. The fat prohibition, which the Quran explicitly frames as a punitive measure rather than a universal law, serves as a key example of how Islamic exegesis distinguishes between the original Torah and its current form.

Al-Razi (d. 1210 CE), in his *Tafsir al-Kabir*, discusses the Quranic reference to the fat prohibition extensively, arguing that it demonstrates how the Torah contains both authentic divine legislation (the blood prohibition) and historically contingent punitive measures (the fat prohibition) that were never meant to be permanent.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Rushd (Averroes, d. 1198 CE), in *Bidayat al-Mujtahid*, provides one of the most systematic comparative treatments of animal slaughter laws across legal traditions. He notes the shared Abrahamic concern with blood prohibition while documenting the jurisprudential differences between Levitical and Islamic approaches.

The modern scholar Ali Gomaa, former Grand Mufti of Egypt, has discussed how the Islamic dietary system both continues and simplifies the Levitical framework — maintaining the blood prohibition while eliminating what Islam regards as punitive or culturally specific restrictions like the fat prohibition.

Abdal Hakim Murad (Timothy Winter) has explored in his lectures how the Levitical and Islamic dietary systems both function as spiritual disciplines that transform the act of eating into an act of worship, despite their significant differences in specific regulations.
