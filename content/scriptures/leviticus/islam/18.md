## Summary of Leviticus 18

Leviticus 18 opens the "Holiness Code" (chapters 17-26) with a comprehensive list of prohibited sexual relations. The chapter forbids incest with various relatives (mother, sister, granddaughter, aunt, daughter-in-law, sister-in-law), sexual relations during menstruation, adultery, child sacrifice to Molech, male homosexual acts, and bestiality. The chapter frames these prohibitions as distinguishing Israel from Egyptian and Canaanite practices and warns that the land will "vomit out" those who practice such things.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 18 addresses the same domain as the Islamic law of *mahram* (prohibited marriage partners) and sexual ethics. The overlap is extensive. The Quran provides its own list of prohibited relations:

> "Prohibited to you [for marriage] are your mothers, your daughters, your sisters, your father's sisters, your mother's sisters, your brother's daughters, your sister's daughters, your [milk] mothers who nursed you, your sisters through nursing, your wives' mothers, and your step-daughters under your guardianship [born] of your wives unto whom you have gone in." (Al-Nisa 4:23)

The Quranic list closely parallels the Levitical one, with some notable differences. Islam adds *rada'ah* (milk kinship) — relations established through breastfeeding — which has no Levitical counterpart. The Prophet said: "What is prohibited by reason of kinship is prohibited by reason of suckling" (Sahih al-Bukhari).

The prohibition of adultery (*zina*) is shared emphatically:

> "And do not approach unlawful sexual intercourse. Indeed, it is ever an immorality and is evil as a way." (Al-Isra 17:32)

The Quran uses the phrase "do not approach" (*la taqrabu*) rather than simply "do not commit," indicating a broader prohibition that includes the circumstances leading to the act.

The Levitical condemnation of child sacrifice to Molech finds resonance in the Quranic condemnation of pre-Islamic infanticide:

> "And do not kill your children for fear of poverty. We provide for them and for you." (Al-Isra 17:31)

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *mahram* is extensively developed across all schools. The prohibited categories include: blood relations (*nasab*), marriage relations (*musaharah*), and milk relations (*rada'ah*). The Hanafi school requires a minimum of five breastfeedings to establish milk kinship; the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools also require five, while the Maliki school holds that even one breastfeeding establishes the kinship.

Regarding the Levitical prohibition on marrying a wife's sister during the wife's lifetime, Islamic law agrees: a man may not be married to two sisters simultaneously (Al-Nisa 4:23). However, after divorce or the death of a wife, her sister becomes permissible — a point of agreement between Levitical and Islamic law.

The punishment for *zina* in Islamic law is severe and extensively codified. The Quran prescribes one hundred lashes for unmarried fornicators (Al-Nur 24:2). For married adulterers, the Hadith prescribes stoning (*rajm*), a punishment that Islamic scholars note also appears in the Torah, demonstrating continuity.

The evidentiary standard for *zina* is extraordinarily high — four eyewitnesses to the actual act (Al-Nur 24:4) — making conviction functionally almost impossible without confession, which aligns with the Islamic preference for concealment of sins and private repentance.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The sexual prohibitions of Leviticus 18 are generally viewed favorably by Muslim scholars as reflecting authentic divine moral law. The broad framework of prohibiting incest, adultery, and bestiality aligns with Islamic ethics, and these provisions are among those that Muslims regard as substantially preserved in the Torah.

Al-Tabari, in his discussions of the Mosaic law, treats the sexual ethics of Leviticus as fundamentally sound and consistent with the Quranic message, suggesting that this area of the Torah has been less subject to *tahrif* than the ritual legislation.

However, the framing of these laws as specifically anti-Egyptian and anti-Canaanite (Lev 18:3) is viewed as a later editorial addition that particularizes what should be understood as universal moral principles.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah, in *Ahkam Ahl al-Dhimmah*, discusses the overlap between Levitical and Islamic sexual ethics, noting that both traditions view sexual morality as fundamental to social order and divine obedience.

The contemporary scholar Jonathan Brown, in *Misquoting Muhammad*, discusses the Islamic evidentiary standards for sexual offenses, demonstrating how Islamic law creates a system that is simultaneously severe in theory and merciful in application — a balance he compares to the Levitical approach.

Amira Sonbol, in *Women of Jordan*, examines how both Levitical and Islamic family law traditions construct systems of prohibited relations that serve both to protect family integrity and to regulate women's sexuality, offering a critical feminist perspective on shared patriarchal structures.
