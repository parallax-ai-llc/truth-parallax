## Summary of Leviticus 15

Leviticus 15 addresses ritual impurity caused by bodily discharges. It covers abnormal male discharges (likely gonorrhea or similar conditions), normal seminal emissions, menstruation, and abnormal female bleeding. Each type of discharge renders the person and anything they contact ritually impure. Purification involves waiting periods, washing, and in cases of abnormal discharge, sacrificial offerings. The chapter concludes by framing these laws as necessary to separate the Israelites from impurity so they do not "die in their impurity."

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 15 addresses the same domain as the extensive Islamic jurisprudence on *taharah* (ritual purity). The parallels are among the closest in the entire Torah-Quran comparison.

For seminal emission (*janabah* in Islamic terminology), both systems require purification before resuming worship. The Quran states:

> "O you who have believed, do not approach prayer while you are in a state of... sexual impurity — except those passing through [a place of prayer] — until you have bathed." (Al-Nisa 4:43)

For menstruation (*haidh*), the Quran addresses the topic directly:

> "And they ask you about menstruation. Say: It is harm, so keep away from wives during menstruation. And do not approach them until they are pure." (Al-Baqarah 2:222)

Both Levitical and Islamic law prohibit sexual intercourse during menstruation. However, Islam is less restrictive about other contact. The Prophet Muhammad was asked about associating with menstruating women, and he responded by criticizing the Jewish practice of complete separation: "Do everything except intercourse" (Sahih Muslim). The Prophet reportedly reclined in Aisha's lap while she was menstruating, and she would drink from a cup, then he would drink from the same spot.

The Levitical concept of transmitted impurity — that touching a menstruating woman's bed or seat makes one impure — is explicitly rejected in Islamic practice through the prophetic example.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *taharah* represents one of the most elaborated areas of Islamic law, with entire treatises devoted to it. The classification system includes:

*Hadath asghar* (minor impurity): broken by urination, defecation, flatulence, sleep, or touching the genitals (Shafi'i school). Requires *wudu* (ablution).

*Hadath akbar* (major impurity): caused by sexual intercourse, seminal emission, menstruation (*haidh*), and postpartum bleeding (*nifas*). Requires *ghusl* (full body wash).

The Hanafi school sets the minimum duration of menstruation at three days and the maximum at ten. The Shafi'i school sets the minimum at one day and maximum at fifteen. The Maliki school sets no minimum but a maximum of fifteen days. These discussions parallel the Levitical seven-day standard but with greater variation.

For *janabah* (sexual impurity), all schools agree that *ghusl* is required before prayer. The *ghusl* consists of: intention, washing the entire body, and ensuring water reaches all parts. This is simpler than the Levitical procedure, which involves a seven-day wait and sacrificial offerings for abnormal discharges.

The concept of *tayammum* (dry ablution with clean earth) when water is unavailable provides an Islamic flexibility absent from Levitical law:

> "And if you are ill or on a journey... and you cannot find water, then seek clean earth and wipe over your faces and hands." (Al-Ma'idah 5:6)

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

Muslim scholars generally acknowledge that purity regulations regarding bodily discharges likely formed part of the original Mosaic dispensation, given their extensive presence in Islamic law as well. However, the sacrificial requirements attached to purification (particularly for abnormal discharges) are viewed as priestly additions.

The framework of transferred impurity — making objects and persons impure through contact — is viewed as an exaggeration of the original principle. Al-Shafi'i himself, while developing his own purity jurisprudence, noted that excessive extension of impurity categories tends to create unnecessary hardship, contrary to the divine principle of ease.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Nawawi (d. 1277 CE), in *al-Majmu' Sharh al-Muhadhdhab*, provides the most comprehensive Shafi'i treatment of bodily purity law, systematically addressing each type of discharge with reference to Quran, Hadith, and scholarly consensus. His work represents the mature Islamic alternative to the Levitical system.

The contemporary scholar Kecia Ali examines menstrual purity law in both traditions, arguing that while both systems regulate the female body, Islam's prophetic traditions introduced a humanizing element absent from the Levitical code — treating menstruation as a natural condition rather than a source of contagious impurity.

Marion Katz, in *Body of Text: The Emergence of the Sunni Law of Ritual Purity*, traces the development of Islamic purity law, demonstrating both its indebtedness to the Levitical tradition and its significant departures, particularly in reducing the stigma associated with natural bodily functions.
