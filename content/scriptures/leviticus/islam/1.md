## Summary of Leviticus 1

Leviticus 1 establishes the foundational laws of the burnt offering (*olah*), in which an animal — a bull, sheep, goat, or bird — is entirely consumed by fire on the altar. The offering is described as producing a "pleasing aroma" to the Lord. The offerer lays hands on the animal's head, symbolizing identification and substitution, before the animal is slaughtered, its blood sprinkled on the altar, and the carcass burned completely. The chapter emphasizes voluntary devotion and complete surrender to God.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The concept of animal sacrifice (*qurbani* or *udhiyah*) is central to Islamic practice, particularly during Eid al-Adha. The Quran explicitly links sacrifice to the legacy of Ibrahim (Abraham):

> "And for every nation We have appointed a rite of sacrifice, that they may mention the name of Allah over what He has provided for them of livestock." (Al-Hajj 22:34)

However, Islam diverges from the Levitical model in several key respects. The burnt offering consumes the entire animal, whereas Islamic sacrifice requires distribution of the meat among the poor, relatives, and the household. The Quran states clearly:

> "Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety (taqwa) from you." (Al-Hajj 22:37)

This verse reframes the theology of sacrifice away from the material act toward interior devotion. Where Leviticus 1 speaks of a "pleasing aroma" reaching God, Islam insists that it is the spiritual intention (*niyyah*) rather than the physical substance that God accepts.

The Levitical requirement of laying hands on the animal finds a partial echo in the Islamic practice of the one who offers the sacrifice personally slaughtering the animal or at minimum being present, as recommended in the Hadith literature (Sunan Ibn Majah).

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

In the Hanafi school, *qurbani* during Eid al-Adha is *wajib* (obligatory) for those who meet the financial threshold (*nisab*). The Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali schools consider it *sunnah mu'akkadah* (strongly recommended). The animal must meet specific criteria: it must be of a certain age, free of defects, and slaughtered with the name of Allah (*bismillah, Allahu akbar*).

Unlike Leviticus, which designates specific animals for different categories of offerers (bulls for the wealthy, birds for the poor), Islamic law applies a more uniform standard, though it does acknowledge varying levels of reward for different animals. A camel, for instance, counts for seven shares of sacrifice in Hanafi jurisprudence.

The concept of *taqarrub* (drawing near to God) through sacrifice is shared between Leviticus and Islam. The Arabic root q-r-b, from which *qurbani* derives, carries the same semantic range as the Hebrew *korban*, both meaning "that which is brought near."

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

Islamic scholars approach Leviticus 1 with the understanding that the Torah (*al-Tawrah*) given to Musa (Moses) was originally a divine revelation, but that its text has undergone alteration (*tahrif*) over the centuries. The elaborate priestly regulations found in Leviticus are often viewed by Muslim commentators as later additions reflecting the institutional interests of the Aaronic priesthood rather than the original Mosaic revelation.

Ibn Hazm (d. 1064 CE), the Zahiri scholar, argued in *al-Fasl fi al-Milal* that the detailed sacrificial code was inconsistent with the simplicity of genuine prophetic legislation. Al-Shahrastani (d. 1153 CE) similarly noted in *al-Milal wa al-Nihal* that the sacrificial system as described in Leviticus appears to reflect priestly elaboration rather than direct divine command.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Classical commentators like al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir, when discussing the Quranic references to the Torah, acknowledge the existence of sacrificial law in the earlier dispensation while emphasizing that Islam's sacrificial rites represent a purified continuation. The modern scholar Yusuf al-Qaradawi, in *The Lawful and the Prohibited in Islam*, discusses how Islamic sacrifice preserves the essential spiritual core of earlier Abrahamic practice while removing what Muslims regard as priestly accretions.

Ismail al-Faruqi, in his comparative theological work, argued that the Levitical system's emphasis on priestly mediation stands in tension with the Islamic principle of direct access to God (*tawbah* without intermediary), and that this represents one of the "corrections" Islam brings to earlier Abrahamic practice.

Contemporary scholars like Brannon Wheeler (*Moses in the Quran and Islamic Exegesis*) have explored how Islamic tradition both preserves and reinterprets the Mosaic sacrificial legacy, situating it within the broader Quranic narrative of prophetic continuity.
