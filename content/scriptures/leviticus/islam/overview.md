## Overview

While Leviticus is not a canonical text in Islam, its themes resonate deeply with Islamic jurisprudence and theology. The Quran affirms that the Torah was revealed to Musa (Moses) as divine guidance, and many Levitical concepts — sacrifice, purity, dietary law, and holiness — have parallels in Islamic practice. Islamic scholars engage Leviticus as part of the earlier revelation (*al-Tawrah*) that Islam sees as originally divine but subsequently altered (*tahrif*).

## Key Parallels

| Levitical Theme | Islamic Parallel | Quranic/Hadith Basis |
|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Animal sacrifice | Qurbani / Udhiyah | Al-Hajj 22:34-37 |
| Dietary laws (kashrut) | Halal/Haram food laws | Al-Ma'idah 5:3-5, Al-An'am 6:145 |
| Ritual purity | Taharah / Wudu / Ghusl | Al-Ma'idah 5:6 |
| Day of Atonement | Ashura fasting (for some) | Hadith (Bukhari, Muslim) |
| Holiness imperative | Taqwa (God-consciousness) | Al-Hujurat 49:13 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Continuity and correction**: Islam sees its law (Shariah) as the final, perfected form of the divine legislation that began with earlier revelations including Leviticus
- **Tahrif**: The current text of Leviticus is considered to contain human additions and alterations to the original divine message
- **Universal application**: Where Levitical law is particular to Israel, Islamic law claims universal scope
