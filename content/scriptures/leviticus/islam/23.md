## Summary of Leviticus 23

Leviticus 23 provides the calendar of Israel's sacred festivals: the weekly Sabbath, Passover and Unleavened Bread (1st month), the offering of firstfruits, the Festival of Weeks (Shavuot/Pentecost, seven weeks after firstfruits), the Feast of Trumpets (7th month, 1st day), the Day of Atonement (7th month, 10th day), and the Feast of Tabernacles/Booths (7th month, 15th-22nd days). Each festival has specific rituals, offerings, and requirements for rest from work.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Islam's liturgical calendar is notably simpler than the Levitical one, containing two major festivals: Eid al-Fitr (marking the end of Ramadan) and Eid al-Adha (the Festival of Sacrifice during the Hajj season). The Prophet Muhammad reportedly said upon arriving in Medina:

> "Allah has replaced them [your pre-Islamic festivals] with two better days: Eid al-Adha and Eid al-Fitr." (Sunan Abu Dawud)

The Sabbath (Lev 23:3) has a structural parallel in the Islamic Friday (*Jumu'ah*), though with significant differences. Friday is not a day of rest in the Jewish sense; the Quran explicitly instructs:

> "O you who have believed, when the call to prayer is made on Friday, hasten to the remembrance of Allah and leave trade. But when the prayer is concluded, disperse throughout the land and seek from the bounty of Allah." (Al-Jumu'ah 62:9-10)

Work ceases only during the congregational prayer, not for the entire day. The Islamic tradition views the Sabbath rest as a provision specific to the Israelites, not a universal divine command.

The Day of Atonement (Lev 23:26-32) connects to Ashura, as discussed in Chapter 16. Passover, commemorating the Exodus, is acknowledged in Islam through the Quranic narrative of Musa's deliverance from Pharaoh, though no corresponding festival exists.

The Festival of Tabernacles, with its requirement to dwell in temporary booths (*sukkot*), has a distant echo in the Hajj, where pilgrims camp in temporary shelters at Mina and Arafat. Both festivals involve leaving one's home and dwelling temporarily in simple structures as an act of devotion and remembrance.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of the two Eids is relatively straightforward compared to the elaborate Levitical festival system. Eid prayers (*salat al-eid*) are *wajib* in the Hanafi school and *sunnah mu'akkadah* in the other three schools. The prayers consist of two *rak'ahs* with additional *takbirs* (declarations of God's greatness).

The Hajj — the annual pilgrimage to Mecca — represents Islam's most elaborate ritual, and its timing during Dhul Hijjah creates a season of sacred observance comparable to the Levitical festival cluster in the seventh month (Tishri). Both systems designate specific days within a sacred month for particular ritual acts, creating a rhythm of intensifying devotion.

The concept of *haram* time — periods when certain activities are restricted — exists in both traditions. The four sacred months in Islam (Dhul Qa'dah, Dhul Hijjah, Muharram, and Rajab) during which warfare was traditionally prohibited parallel the Levitical concept of sacred seasons requiring special behavior.

The firstfruits offering (Lev 23:9-14) corresponds functionally to *zakat al-fitr*, the obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan before the Eid prayer. Both represent dedicating the first or best portion of one's provision to God and the community.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

Muslim scholars view the Levitical festival calendar as a combination of authentic commemorations (the Exodus, harvest thanksgiving) and institutional elaborations. The basic principle of marking sacred time with worship, fasting, and charity is affirmed. The elaborate ritual requirements for each festival — specific sacrifices, detailed procedures — are viewed as priestly accretions.

Al-Biruni, who wrote extensively about calendars across civilizations in *al-Athar al-Baqiyah* (Chronology of Ancient Nations), provides one of the most detailed Muslim treatments of the Jewish festival calendar, treating the Levitical festivals as historically significant while viewing their ritual details as culturally specific.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Kathir discusses the Islamic festivals in the context of prophetic history, noting that each prophet's community had its own sacred calendar, and that Islam's simplified two-festival system represents the final, universal form of sacred time.

The contemporary scholar Seyyed Hossein Nasr, in *Islam in the Modern World*, discusses how Islamic sacred time (the daily prayer cycle, the weekly Jumu'ah, the annual Ramadan and Hajj seasons) creates a comprehensive temporal framework that accomplishes what the Levitical festival calendar achieves — the sanctification of time — through different means.

Mustansir Mir, in *Understanding the Islamic Scripture*, examines how the Quranic references to earlier prophetic festivals serve to establish continuity with the Abrahamic tradition while asserting Islam's claim to represent its mature and final form.
