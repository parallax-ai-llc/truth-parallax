## Summary of Leviticus 24

Leviticus 24 contains three distinct sections: instructions for the perpetual lamp (*ner tamid*) and the showbread in the Tabernacle; the narrative of a man who blasphemed the divine Name during a fight and was brought before Moses for judgment; and the resulting legal pronouncement establishing the principle of *lex talionis* (eye for eye, tooth for tooth, life for life). The blasphemer is sentenced to death by stoning, and the same law is declared applicable to both native Israelites and resident aliens.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The blasphemy narrative raises one of the most sensitive topics in comparative Abrahamic law. Islam treats blasphemy (*sabb Allah* or *sabb al-rasul*) as an extremely grave offense. The Quran condemns those who mock God and His Messenger:

> "And if you ask them, they will surely say, 'We were only conversing and playing.' Say, 'Is it Allah and His verses and His Messenger that you were mocking?'" (Al-Tawbah 9:65)

The punishment for blasphemy is debated within Islamic jurisprudence. The Hanafi school generally allows for repentance (*tawbah*) as an alternative to execution. The Maliki and Hanbali schools tend toward execution, particularly for blasphemy against the Prophet. The issue remains one of the most contested in modern Islamic legal discourse.

The *lex talionis* principle (Lev 24:17-22) has a direct Quranic parallel:

> "And We ordained for them therein a life for a life, an eye for an eye, a nose for a nose, an ear for an ear, a tooth for a tooth, and for wounds is legal retribution." (Al-Ma'idah 5:45)

Significantly, the Quran continues: "But whoever gives [up his right as] charity, it is an expiation for him." This addition transforms the strict retaliatory principle into a framework that explicitly encourages forgiveness, marking a departure from the Levitical formulation.

The perpetual lamp and showbread — symbols of God's continuous presence — have no direct Islamic ritual parallel, though the concept of maintaining mosques as spaces of perpetual worship (through five daily prayers, Quran recitation, and *i'tikaf*) serves a similar function.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *qisas* (retaliatory justice) in Islamic law provides the most developed parallel to the Levitical *lex talionis*. The system allows the victim (or their heirs) to choose between: equal retaliation (*qisas*), financial compensation (*diyah*, blood money), or forgiveness (*afw*). This three-option framework goes beyond the Levitical model by formally incorporating mercy and compensation as alternatives to strict retaliation.

The Hanafi school specifies that *qisas* for bodily injuries requires exact equivalence — the retaliatory injury must not exceed the original. If exact equivalence cannot be achieved (e.g., the perpetrator has already lost the equivalent body part), the penalty shifts to *diyah*.

The equal application of law to native and foreigner in Leviticus 24:22 ("You shall have one standard for stranger and citizen alike") resonates with the Islamic principle that Shariah applies equally to all persons within the Islamic polity, regardless of tribal, ethnic, or social status. The Prophet's statement "If Fatimah bint Muhammad stole, I would cut her hand" (Sahih al-Bukhari) illustrates this principle.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The *lex talionis* principle is generally accepted by Muslim scholars as reflecting authentic Mosaic legislation, since the Quran explicitly attributes it to the Torah (Al-Ma'idah 5:45). The blasphemy provision is more contested — while the gravity of blasphemy is accepted, the specific punishment and its application are debated.

Al-Razi, in his commentary on Al-Ma'idah 5:45, discusses the Levitical retaliatory principle as authentic divine legislation that Islam affirms while adding the dimension of encouraged forgiveness. He views the Quranic addition ("whoever gives up his right as charity, it is an expiation for him") as the perfection of the Mosaic principle.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah discusses *qisas* extensively in *I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in*, arguing that the retaliatory principle, properly understood, serves both justice and deterrence while the option of forgiveness serves mercy and social reconciliation.

The contemporary scholar Khaled Abou El Fadl has written extensively on blasphemy law in Islam, arguing that the tradition's internal diversity on this question reflects a genuine theological tension between protecting the sacred and upholding principles of mercy and due process.

Anver Emon, in *Religious Pluralism and Islamic Law*, examines how both Levitical and Islamic legal traditions grapple with the equal application of law across religious and ethnic boundaries, noting that the Levitical provision for equal justice to strangers was a remarkable achievement that Islam sought to extend universally.
