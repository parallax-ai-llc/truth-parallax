## Summary of Leviticus 21

Leviticus 21 sets special holiness requirements for the priests. Ordinary priests must not make themselves ritually impure through contact with the dead (except for close relatives), must not shave their heads, cut their beards, or gash their bodies, and must marry only virgins — not divorced women, widows, or women "defiled." The high priest faces even stricter requirements: no contact with any dead body, no mourning displays, and marriage restricted to virgins from his own people. Priests with physical disabilities are barred from offering sacrifices, though they may eat the priestly portions.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Islam's rejection of a priestly class means that Leviticus 21's regulations have no direct institutional parallel. However, several individual provisions resonate with broader Islamic teachings.

The prohibition on self-mutilation in mourning — shaving the head, cutting the flesh — is shared by Islam. The Prophet Muhammad said: "He is not one of us who slaps his cheeks, tears his garment, and wails like the people of the pre-Islamic era" (Sahih al-Bukhari). The pre-Islamic Arab custom of extreme mourning displays was explicitly prohibited, just as Leviticus prohibits them for priests.

The beard regulations in Leviticus 21:5 echo the Prophet's instruction: "Trim the mustache and let the beard grow" (Sahih al-Bukhari). While the context differs (priestly regulation vs. general Sunnah), the concern with proper grooming as a mark of religious identity is shared.

The exclusion of disabled priests from altar service is deeply problematic from an Islamic perspective. Islam explicitly rejected the notion that physical disability diminishes one's capacity for worship or religious leadership. The Quran rebukes the Prophet himself for turning away from a blind man who sought instruction:

> "He frowned and turned away because there came to him the blind man." (Abasa 80:1-2)

This surah, Abasa, is one of the most significant Quranic passages on disability, establishing that physical condition has no bearing on spiritual worth or capacity.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The question of who may lead prayer (*imamah fi al-salat*) provides the closest Islamic parallel to priestly qualifications. The criteria, however, are based on knowledge and moral character rather than physical attributes or lineage. The Hadith states: "The one who knows the most of the Book of Allah should lead the people in prayer" (Sahih Muslim).

The Shafi'i and Hanbali schools discuss whether a blind person may lead prayer and conclude affirmatively, explicitly rejecting any disability-based exclusion. The famous Companion Ibn Umm Maktum, who was blind, served as the Prophet's appointed leader of Medina (and thus led prayers) when the Prophet was away on military expeditions.

The marriage restrictions for priests have no Islamic parallel. Islamic law permits marriage to widows, divorced women, and women of any tribal or ethnic background, provided they are Muslim (or People of the Book, according to the majority view). The Prophet Muhammad himself married widows and divorced women, establishing by personal example that no stigma attaches to these statuses.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The priestly holiness standards in Leviticus 21 are viewed by Muslim scholars as a particularly clear example of institutional self-legislation. The creation of a hereditary class with special marriage laws, physical requirements, and exclusive ritual privileges runs counter to the Islamic understanding of prophetic religion.

Al-Mawardi argued that the prophetic model of leadership is based on merit, knowledge, and moral character, not on lineage or physical perfection. He viewed the Levitical priestly system as a historical deviation from this prophetic ideal.

Ibn Hazm specifically cited the exclusion of disabled priests as evidence that portions of Leviticus reflect human prejudice rather than divine will, arguing that a merciful God would not exclude His servants from worship based on physical conditions beyond their control.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Ghazali, in his discussions of religious leadership in *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, emphasizes that the essential qualification for leading the community is knowledge and piety, not physical attributes or genealogy. This represents a fundamental departure from the Levitical model.

The contemporary scholar Sherman Jackson examines Islamic approaches to disability and religious participation, demonstrating how the prophetic example and early Islamic practice established an inclusive framework that contrasts with the Levitical exclusion.

Vardit Rispler-Chaim, in *Disability in Islamic Law*, provides a comprehensive analysis of Islamic jurisprudence on disability, noting that the Islamic tradition's inclusive approach represents a conscious departure from the Levitical model of excluding the physically imperfect from religious service.
