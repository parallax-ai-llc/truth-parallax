## Summary of Leviticus 2

Leviticus 2 details the grain offering (*minchah*), a non-animal sacrifice consisting of fine flour, olive oil, and frankincense. The offering could be presented raw, baked, or cooked on a griddle. A portion was burned on the altar while the remainder went to the priests. The chapter prohibits the inclusion of leaven and honey in offerings burned on the altar, and mandates the addition of salt to every grain offering, described as "the salt of the covenant."

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Islam does not prescribe grain offerings in the Levitical sense, but the concept of offering one's provisions to God finds expression in *sadaqah* (voluntary charity) and *zakat* (obligatory alms). The Quran repeatedly connects the giving of one's wealth and sustenance with spiritual purification:

> "Who is it that would loan Allah a goodly loan so He may multiply it for him many times over?" (Al-Baqarah 2:245)

The Levitical grain offering functions as an acknowledgment that all sustenance comes from God. This principle is foundational in Islam, expressed through the concept of *rizq* (divine provision). Every meal in Islamic practice begins with *bismillah* (in the name of God), echoing the dedicatory logic of the grain offering.

The prohibition of leaven in the Levitical offering has no direct Islamic parallel, though some scholars have drawn analogies to the Islamic emphasis on sincerity (*ikhlas*) in worship — leaven, as a fermenting agent, symbolizes corruption or inflation of the self.

The "salt of the covenant" mentioned in verse 13 resonates with the Arabic concept of *milh* (salt) as a symbol of hospitality and covenantal loyalty. In Arab culture, sharing salt creates a bond, and this pre-Islamic custom was absorbed into Islamic ethics.

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

While Islamic law does not prescribe grain sacrifices, the fiqh of *sadaqah* and *zakat* provides a functional equivalent. Zakat on agricultural produce (*zakat al-zuru' wa al-thimar*) requires giving a portion of one's harvest — one-tenth for rain-fed crops and one-twentieth for irrigated crops, according to the Hadith narrated in Sahih al-Bukhari.

The Maliki and Hanbali schools discuss the concept of *kaffarat al-yamin* (expiation for broken oaths), which can include feeding the poor with staple grains, drawing a functional parallel to the dedicatory grain offering.

The priestly portion of the grain offering (*minchah*) finds no direct parallel in Islam, which rejected the concept of a priestly class receiving sacrificial portions. In Islam, religious scholars (*ulama*) have no sacerdotal privileges regarding offerings.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

Muslim scholars view the detailed regulations of the grain offering as likely reflecting later priestly codification. The concept of a priestly share of offerings is particularly suspect from an Islamic perspective, as it suggests institutional self-interest.

Abu al-Qasim al-Khoei, the Shia scholar, argued that the original Mosaic law would have directed all offerings toward the poor rather than toward a priestly class, consistent with the Quranic emphasis on social justice. Al-Biruni (d. 1048 CE), in his comparative study *Tahqiq ma li-l-Hind*, noted that many ancient religious traditions developed elaborate offering systems that served institutional rather than purely spiritual purposes.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Ibn Kathir, in his commentary on Surah Al-Ma'idah, discusses how the Torah originally contained guidance on charity and provision that Islam perfected through the systematic institution of zakat. The grain offering, in this reading, represents an early form of the principle that one's sustenance must be shared with God's community.

The Egyptian scholar Muhammad Abu Zahrah explored in his comparative legal studies how the Levitical offering system and Islamic charity law both emerge from the same Abrahamic imperative to acknowledge divine lordship over all provision, while differing in their institutional structures.

Al-Qaradawi notes in *Fiqh al-Zakat* that the Islamic system of obligatory and voluntary giving represents the mature form of the earlier sacrificial-offering paradigm, redirecting material gifts from the altar to the community of the poor and needy, which he argues better fulfills the original divine intent.
