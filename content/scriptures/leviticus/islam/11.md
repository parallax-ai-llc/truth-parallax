## Summary of Leviticus 11

Leviticus 11 presents the comprehensive dietary code distinguishing clean from unclean animals. Land animals must have split hooves and chew the cud. Aquatic creatures must have fins and scales. The chapter lists specific prohibited birds (mostly raptors and scavengers) and flying insects (except certain locusts). It also addresses impurity from touching animal carcasses. The dietary laws are framed as a holiness requirement: "Be holy, for I am holy."

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Leviticus 11 represents one of the most extensively discussed points of comparison between Levitical and Islamic law. The Quran establishes its own dietary code, which is both simpler and different in scope:

> "Prohibited to you are dead animals, blood, the flesh of swine, and that which has been dedicated to other than Allah, and [those animals] killed by strangling or by a violent blow or by a head-long fall or by the goring of horns, and those from which a wild animal has eaten, except what you [are able to] slaughter [before its death], and those which are sacrificed on stone altars." (Al-Ma'idah 5:3)

The Islamic system is significantly less restrictive than the Levitical one. The Quran permits all seafood without the fins-and-scales requirement (according to the majority opinion):

> "Lawful to you is the game of the sea and its food as provision for you." (Al-Ma'idah 5:96)

The pig/swine prohibition is shared between both systems, representing one of the clearest continuities in Abrahamic dietary law. However, many animals forbidden in Leviticus — such as camels, rabbits, and certain birds — are permissible in Islam.

The Quran explicitly frames the broader Levitical restrictions as punitive measures specific to the Israelites rather than universal divine law:

> "For those who are Jews, We prohibited every animal of uncloven hoof... [By] that We repaid them for their injustice." (Al-An'am 6:146)

## Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

The fiqh of *halal* and *haram* foods is one of the most developed areas of Islamic law. The four Sunni schools differ on specific rulings:

The Hanafi school prohibits shellfish, crabs, and most seafood other than fish, coming closest to the Levitical fins-and-scales rule. The Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali schools permit all seafood, including shellfish, based on the general Quranic permission and the Hadith: "Its water is purifying and its dead are lawful" (Sunan al-Tirmidhi).

Regarding land animals, the schools agree on prohibiting pigs, donkeys, and fanged predators. The Maliki school uniquely permits certain animals that other schools forbid, such as hedgehogs and insects (other than locusts). The Hanafi school is the most restrictive among Sunni schools regarding land animals.

Locusts are explicitly permitted in Islam based on Hadith evidence ("Two dead things and two kinds of blood have been made lawful for us: fish and locusts, liver and spleen" — Sunan Ibn Majah), which interestingly aligns with the Levitical exception for certain locusts (Lev 11:22).

The concept of *najasah* (ritual impurity) from animal contact has parallels to Leviticus 11:24-40. In Shafi'i jurisprudence, dog saliva requires washing seven times, one with earth. The Maliki school is more lenient, considering dogs ritually clean.

## Tahrif and the Original Torah

The extensive dietary restrictions in Leviticus 11 are a key focus of the Islamic *tahrif* discussion. Muslim scholars argue that the original Torah contained simpler dietary guidelines, similar to Islam's concise list, and that the elaborate system of clean and unclean categories reflects later rabbinical and priestly development.

Al-Razi, in his commentary on Al-An'am 6:146, provides an extended discussion of why certain foods were prohibited for the Israelites specifically, arguing that these were disciplinary measures (*ta'zir*) rather than reflections of inherent impurity in the animals themselves.

Al-Qurtubi (d. 1273 CE), in *al-Jami' li-Ahkam al-Quran*, argues that the Quranic dietary code represents the return to the original Abrahamic standard, stripping away the punitive restrictions that had been imposed on the Israelites.

## Scholarly Perspectives

Al-Qaradawi, in *The Lawful and the Prohibited in Islam*, provides the most comprehensive modern treatment of Islamic dietary law in comparative perspective. He argues that Islam's approach embodies the principle of *al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibahah* ("the default regarding things is permissibility"), contrasting this with what he characterizes as Leviticus's more restrictive default.

The scholar Muhammad al-Ghazali (d. 1996 CE, distinct from the medieval al-Ghazali) argued that Islamic dietary law reflects a balanced middle way (*wasatiyyah*) between the extensive Levitical restrictions and the absence of dietary regulation that he perceived in Christianity.

David Freidenreich, in *Foreigners and Their Food*, provides a rigorous academic comparison of Levitical *kashrut* and Islamic *halal* systems, demonstrating both their shared Semitic heritage and their distinct theological frameworks for understanding food purity.
