## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 5 addresses specific cases requiring a guilt offering (*asham*) and extends the sin offering regulations begun in chapter 4. The chapter opens with four particular situations: failing to testify when one could, touching something ceremonially unclean, contact with human uncleanness, or making a rash oath. In each case, the offender must confess the sin and bring a female lamb or goat. If the person cannot afford this, two turtledoves or pigeons may be substituted—one as a sin offering and one as a burnt offering. If even this is beyond the person's means, a tenth of an ephah of fine flour may be brought instead. The chapter then transitions to the guilt offering proper (5:14–19), which addresses unintentional trespass against "the holy things of the LORD" and requires a ram without blemish plus restitution of the value involved, with an additional fifth added.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 5:5 | "He shall confess the sin he has committed" | The necessity of confession; faith includes repentance |
| 5:7 | "If he cannot afford a lamb… two turtledoves or two pigeons" | God's gracious provision for the poor |
| 5:11 | "He shall bring… a tenth of an ephah of fine flour" | Even the poorest may find atonement—grace is not for the wealthy alone |
| 5:16 | "He shall also make restitution… adding a fifth to it" | Restitution as fruit of genuine repentance |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theologians emphasize the graduated sacrifice as evidence of God's condescending grace. God meets the sinner where they are, providing a way of atonement regardless of economic status. Calvin noted that the restitution requirement demonstrated that forgiveness does not negate the obligation to make right what has been wronged. This principle informed Reformed ethics and the Calvinist emphasis on the "third use of the law" as a guide for the sanctified life.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther found in the guilt offering a clear illustration of law and gospel working together. The law exposes sin—even sins committed unknowingly against holy things—while the offering provides the gospel remedy. The confession required in 5:5 demonstrated that true faith is never silent but acknowledges its need before God. The restitution-plus-a-fifth showed that God's justice demands more than mere restoration; it demands abundance, which only Christ ultimately provides.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters highlight that Leviticus 5 teaches both vertical and horizontal reconciliation. The sacrifice addresses the offense against God, while restitution addresses the wrong done to neighbor. This dual dimension anticipates Jesus' summary of the law: love God and love neighbor. The accessibility of the offering to every economic class demonstrates the universality of both sin and the availability of grace.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 5 contributes to Protestant theology in several important ways:

1. **Confession and repentance.** The explicit requirement of confession (5:5) links atonement to acknowledgment of sin. Protestant soteriology insists that saving faith includes repentance—not as a meritorious work but as the sinner's honest response to God's holiness.
2. **God's accommodation to weakness.** The sliding scale of sacrifice—from a lamb to birds to flour—shows God's tender regard for human limitation. This principle undermines any notion that access to God depends on wealth or status.
3. **Restitution as fruit of grace.** The restitution requirement teaches that forgiveness produces tangible change. Zacchaeus' fourfold restoration (Luke 19:8) echoes the Levitical principle that genuine encounter with God transforms economic relationships.
4. **The scope of sin.** Even contact with uncleanness and rash words require sacrifice, exposing the comprehensive reach of human fallenness and the corresponding comprehensiveness of Christ's atoning work.
