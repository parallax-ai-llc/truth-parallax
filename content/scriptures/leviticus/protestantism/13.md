## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 13 is the longest chapter in the book, providing exhaustive instructions for the priestly diagnosis of skin diseases (traditionally translated "leprosy," though the Hebrew *tsara'at* covers a broader range of conditions). The priest examines the afflicted person according to detailed criteria involving the depth of the sore, the color of hair in the affected area, the spread of the condition, and whether raw flesh is present. Suspected cases require a seven-day quarantine, sometimes extended for a second week. Those diagnosed as unclean must tear their clothes, leave their hair disheveled, cover their upper lip, and cry "Unclean, unclean!" while living outside the camp. The chapter also addresses *tsara'at* in garments—mold or discoloration in wool, linen, or leather—which must be shown to the priest and, if persistent, burned.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 13:3 | "The priest shall examine the diseased area… and pronounce him unclean" | The priest's diagnostic role; the law's function of exposing sin |
| 13:45 | "He shall cry, 'Unclean, unclean'" | Public acknowledgment of defilement; the sinner's confession |
| 13:46 | "He shall live alone. His dwelling shall be outside the camp" | Sin's isolating consequences; exclusion from God's community |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed interpreters read *tsara'at* as a vivid symbol of sin's corrupting power. Just as the disease could spread through the body and even into garments, sin permeates every dimension of human existence. Calvin noted that the priest's role was diagnostic, not curative—he could pronounce clean or unclean but could not heal. This paralleled the law's function: it identifies and exposes sin but cannot remedy it. Only Christ, the Great Physician, heals the leper (Matt. 8:1–4), demonstrating His authority over the law's diagnostic limitations.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized the exclusion from the camp as an image of sin's alienating effect. Sin separates the sinner from God's people and from God's presence. The cry "Unclean, unclean!" was a public confession—an acknowledgment before the community that one was defiled. Luther connected this to the first of his Ninety-Five Theses: the entire life of the Christian is one of repentance, constantly acknowledging our spiritual leprosy before the God who alone can heal.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical commentators highlight Jesus' interactions with lepers as the fulfillment of Leviticus 13. Where the law excluded, Jesus touched and healed (Mark 1:40–45). By touching the leper, Jesus reversed the expected contamination: instead of becoming unclean Himself, He made the unclean clean. This is the gospel in microcosm—Christ takes on our defilement and gives us His purity.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 13, despite its seemingly clinical content, carries deep theological meaning for Protestant readers:

1. **Sin as disease.** The elaborate diagnostic procedures symbolize the pervasive, insidious nature of sin, which penetrates the whole person—body, garments, and dwelling. This supports the Reformation doctrine of total depravity.
2. **The law diagnoses but cannot cure.** The priest could identify the disease but had no remedy to offer. This is the law's function according to Paul (Rom. 3:20; 7:7): it reveals sin without providing salvation, driving the sinner to Christ.
3. **Exclusion and restoration.** The banishment from the camp pictured the sinner's alienation from God. The gospel reverses this exclusion, bringing the outcast back into fellowship through Christ's cleansing blood.
4. **Christ's authority.** Jesus' healing of lepers demonstrated His superiority over the Levitical system. He did not merely pronounce clean but actually made clean—fulfilling what the law could only shadow.
