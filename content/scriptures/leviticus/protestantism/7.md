## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 7 concludes the instruction manual for the Levitical sacrifices. It opens with further regulations for the guilt offering (7:1–10), then addresses the peace offering in its three forms: thanksgiving (7:12–15), vow offering, and freewill offering (7:16–18). Strict time limits govern consumption: thanksgiving offerings must be eaten the same day, while vow and freewill offerings may extend to the second day. Any meat remaining on the third day must be burned. The chapter reiterates the absolute prohibition against eating fat and blood (7:22–27), with the penalty of being "cut off" from the people. It concludes by specifying the priestly portions—the breast (wave offering) and the right thigh (contribution)—and summarizing all the offering types addressed in chapters 1–7.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 7:12 | "If he offers it for a thanksgiving, then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanksgiving unleavened loaves" | Gratitude as the proper posture of the redeemed |
| 7:20 | "The person who eats… while an uncleanness is on him, that person shall be cut off" | The seriousness of approaching God in an unworthy manner (cf. 1 Cor. 11:27) |
| 7:26–27 | "You shall eat no blood… whoever eats any blood… shall be cut off" | The sacredness of blood as the God-given means of atonement |
| 7:37–38 | "This is the law of the burnt offering, of the grain offering…" | The comprehensive unity of the sacrificial system |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed interpreters see the thanksgiving offering as the highest expression of the peace offering, since gratitude is the primary motive for the Christian life. The strict eating regulations—consumed on the same day for thanksgiving—taught that worship is timely and urgent, not to be deferred. The summary statement in 7:37–38 confirmed the divine origin of the entire sacrificial system, a point Reformed theologians used to argue that worship must follow God's prescription, not human innovation (the regulative principle of worship).

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther understood the three types of peace offerings as corresponding to different occasions in the believer's life: thanksgiving for blessings received, vow offerings for commitments made, and freewill offerings from sheer love of God. This variety reflected the richness of the Christian's relationship with God. The prohibition of eating in uncleanness anticipated Paul's warnings about unworthy communion (1 Cor. 11:27–29), which informed Lutheran eucharistic practice.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical commentators note that the chapter's closing summary (7:37–38) ties together all five major offerings into a unified system of approach to God. Each offering addressed a different aspect of the divine-human relationship: consecration (burnt), dedication (grain), fellowship (peace), purification (sin), and reparation (guilt). Together they provide a comprehensive picture of what Christ accomplishes in a single act of sacrifice on the cross.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 7 draws the first major section of the book to a close and establishes these Protestant themes:

1. **The centrality of thanksgiving.** The prominence of the thanksgiving offering teaches that redeemed life is fundamentally doxological. The Heidelberg Catechism organizes its entire structure around guilt, grace, and gratitude—the last being the believer's fitting response.
2. **Holy communion foreshadowed.** The consumption regulations and warnings about eating in uncleanness provided the theological groundwork for Paul's eucharistic ethics, which all Protestant traditions engage with seriously.
3. **The blood prohibition.** The repeated insistence that blood must not be consumed underscored its unique atoning function, preparing for the New Testament's proclamation of Christ's blood as the foundation of the new covenant.
4. **Systematic atonement.** The summary of all five offerings in 7:37–38 shows that God's provision for sin and worship is comprehensive, not piecemeal—a pattern fulfilled in the all-sufficient sacrifice of Christ.
