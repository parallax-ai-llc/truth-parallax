## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 11 introduces the purity laws with a comprehensive classification of clean and unclean animals. Land animals must both chew the cud and have split hooves to be clean; the camel, rock badger, hare, and pig are specifically excluded. Water creatures must have both fins and scales. An extensive list of unclean birds is provided, including eagles, vultures, and owls. Winged insects are generally unclean, except for those with jointed legs for hopping (locusts, crickets, grasshoppers). The chapter also addresses defilement through contact with carcasses of unclean animals and concludes with the theological rationale: "For I am the LORD your God. Consecrate yourselves therefore, and be holy, for I am holy" (11:44–45).

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theology classifies the dietary laws as ceremonial legislation, fulfilled and abrogated in Christ (Mark 7:19; Acts 10:9–16). The Westminster Confession (19.3) states that ceremonial laws "are now abrogated under the New Testament." However, Calvin insisted that the underlying moral principle—that God's people must be distinctly holy—remains binding. The clean/unclean distinction taught Israel to think in terms of holiness in every area of life, including eating. This pedagogical function pointed to the gospel's call to comprehensive sanctification.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther firmly held that the dietary laws were abolished by Christ, citing Mark 7:19 and Paul's teaching in Romans 14 and Colossians 2:16. He saw the food laws as part of the old covenant's tutelary function: they trained Israel in obedience and set them apart from the nations during the period before Christ. Once the gospel came to all nations, the wall of separation was torn down (Eph. 2:14). Luther warned against any attempt to reimpose ceremonial regulations on Christian consciences.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical scholars generally agree with the Reformed and Lutheran positions on the abrogation of dietary laws but find enduring spiritual significance in the principles they embody. The call to distinguish between clean and unclean taught discernment—a virtue Paul commends in Philippians 1:9–10. Some evangelical interpreters also note possible hygienic wisdom in the dietary laws, though they caution against reducing the chapter's purpose to health concerns, since holiness, not hygiene, is the stated rationale.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 11 is central to the Protestant understanding of the relationship between Old and New Testaments:

1. **The moral/ceremonial distinction.** This chapter is a primary test case for the Reformed threefold division of the law. The dietary regulations are ceremonial and therefore fulfilled in Christ, but the command to "be holy, for I am holy" is moral and universal (1 Pet. 1:15–16).
2. **Separation unto God.** The dietary laws functioned as boundary markers, distinguishing Israel from the nations. In the new covenant, this separation is expressed not through food restrictions but through moral distinctiveness and spiritual consecration.
3. **Christ's fulfillment.** Peter's vision in Acts 10 explicitly declared all foods clean, signaling that the barrier between Jew and Gentile had been removed in Christ. Protestant theology reads Leviticus 11 through this fulfillment lens.
4. **Christian liberty.** Luther and Calvin both used the abrogation of food laws to defend the Christian's freedom of conscience against ecclesiastical legalism—a principle codified in the Augsburg Confession and the Westminster Standards.
