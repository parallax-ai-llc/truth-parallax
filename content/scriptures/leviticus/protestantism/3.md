## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 3 prescribes the peace offering (*shelamim*), also translated as the "fellowship offering." Unlike the burnt offering, which was entirely consumed on the altar, the peace offering was shared: the fat portions and kidneys were burned for God, specific portions went to the priests, and the remainder was eaten by the offerer and his household. Animals could come from the herd (male or female), from the flock of sheep, or from goats. The chapter repeatedly emphasizes that "all fat is the LORD's" (3:16) and prohibits the eating of fat and blood—a perpetual statute throughout Israel's generations.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 3:1 | "If his offering is a sacrifice of peace offering" | Fellowship with God as the goal of redemption |
| 3:2 | "He shall lay his hand on the head of his offering and kill it" | Identification with the sacrifice; participation in its benefits |
| 3:5 | "Aaron's sons shall burn it on the altar" | The necessity of priestly mediation, fulfilled in Christ |
| 3:16 | "All fat is the LORD's" | The best belongs to God; the principle of giving God the first and finest |
| 3:17 | "You shall eat neither fat nor blood" | Blood carries sacred significance as the means of atonement (Lev. 17:11) |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin identified the peace offering as the most joyful of the Levitical sacrifices because it expressed gratitude and communion rather than primarily addressing guilt. He stressed that the shared meal was a covenant meal—God, priest, and worshipper eating together. Reformed theology sees this as a type of the Lord's Supper, where believers commune with Christ through the elements. The prohibition against eating blood underscored the sacredness of life, which belongs to God alone.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther noted the communal nature of the peace offering as a model for the Christian eucharistic meal. The peace offering was voluntary, offered in thanksgiving, and resulted in fellowship—precisely the qualities Luther emphasized in his reform of the Mass. The offering demonstrated that reconciliation with God produces reconciliation with neighbor, since the meal was shared with family and community.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters emphasize that the peace offering presupposes prior atonement through the burnt and sin offerings. Peace with God is not self-generated but flows from substitutionary sacrifice. The New Testament declares that Christ "is our peace" (Eph. 2:14) and that through His blood believers have "peace with God" (Rom. 5:1). The communal meal dimension anticipates the fellowship of the church gathered around the cross.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 3 enriches Protestant theology in several ways:

1. **Communion with God.** The shared meal signified that the barrier of sin had been dealt with and the worshipper could sit at table with God. This is the ultimate goal of the gospel: not merely forgiveness but fellowship.
2. **Typology of the Lord's Supper.** Protestant traditions—Reformed, Lutheran, and broadly evangelical—have all seen in the peace offering a foreshadowing of the eucharistic meal, where believers partake of Christ's benefits in community.
3. **The priority of God's portion.** The fat—considered the richest part of the animal—was always given to God. This teaches the principle of firstfruits giving and the consecration of one's best to the Lord.
4. **Blood as sacred.** The absolute prohibition against eating blood prepares for the New Testament revelation that Christ's blood is the basis of the new covenant (Matt. 26:28).

The peace offering reminds Protestant readers that salvation is not merely juridical but relational. Justification restores the sinner to fellowship with the living God.
