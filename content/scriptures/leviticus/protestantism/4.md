## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 4 introduces the sin offering (*chattat*), which addresses unintentional sins—violations of God's commandments committed in ignorance or inadvertence. The chapter distinguishes four categories of offenders, each requiring a different grade of sacrifice: the anointed priest must bring a bull (4:3), the whole congregation a bull (4:13–14), a leader a male goat (4:22–23), and a common person a female goat or lamb (4:27–32). In each case, the offerer lays hands on the animal, it is slaughtered, and the priest applies the blood in prescribed ways. For the priest's sin and the congregation's sin, blood is sprinkled before the inner curtain and applied to the horns of the incense altar; for a leader or commoner, blood is applied to the horns of the burnt-offering altar. The fat is burned on the altar, and in the case of the priestly and congregational offerings, the carcass is carried outside the camp and burned entirely.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 4:2 | "If anyone sins unintentionally in any of the LORD's commandments" | Even unintentional sin requires atonement—the depth of human depravity |
| 4:3 | "If it is the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the people" | The inadequacy of human priesthood; the need for a sinless Mediator |
| 4:20 | "And the priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven" | Forgiveness through divinely appointed substitution |
| 4:12 | "He shall carry the bull outside the camp… and burn it" | Christ suffered "outside the gate" (Heb. 13:11–12) |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theology finds in Leviticus 4 powerful confirmation of total depravity. Even unintentional sins are genuine offenses that require blood atonement, demonstrating that sin pervades every dimension of human existence, including the subconscious. Calvin observed that the graduated sacrifice—bull for the priest, goat or lamb for the commoner—reflected the greater responsibility of spiritual leaders, a principle he applied to the accountability of Reformed pastors and elders.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized that the sin offering exposed the futility of self-justification. No one could claim innocence before God, since even unknown transgressions demanded a sacrifice. The fact that the priest himself needed atonement showed the bankruptcy of any sacerdotal system that claims to mediate grace apart from Christ. Luther saw in the burned carcass "outside the camp" a vivid anticipation of Christ's crucifixion outside Jerusalem's walls.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the precision of the blood application as evidence of the intentionality of atonement. God did not leave the method of reconciliation to human invention. The graduated system also demonstrated God's justice: greater privilege brought greater responsibility. Most significantly, the promise "they shall be forgiven" (4:20, 26, 31, 35) assured the penitent that God's prescribed means of atonement truly worked—a foretaste of the "much more" effective blood of Christ (Heb. 9:13–14).

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 4 is foundational for several Protestant doctrines:

1. **Total depravity.** Sin is not limited to deliberate rebellion. The human condition is such that we violate God's will even without knowing it, demonstrating our radical need for grace.
2. **Penal substitutionary atonement.** The hand laid on the animal and the animal's death in the sinner's place constitute the Old Testament's most explicit substitutionary pattern.
3. **The insufficiency of the Levitical system.** Because the priest himself sinned (4:3), the entire system pointed beyond itself to a greater Priest who would be "holy, innocent, unstained" (Heb. 7:26).
4. **Christ outside the camp.** The burning of the sacrificial remains outside the camp is explicitly interpreted by the author of Hebrews as a type of Christ's suffering outside Jerusalem (Heb. 13:11–13).
