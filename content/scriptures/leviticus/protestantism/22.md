## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 22 continues the priestly regulations, addressing two main concerns: the conditions under which priests may eat sacred offerings, and the standards of quality that sacrificial animals must meet. Priests who are ceremonially unclean—through skin disease, bodily discharge, or contact with a corpse or unclean creature—must not eat the sacred offerings until purified. Non-priestly persons (hired workers, visitors) may not eat sacred food, though a priest's household slaves may. The chapter then turns to the animals themselves: sacrificial victims must be without blemish—no blindness, broken limbs, running sores, or crushed testicles. Animals with overly short or long limbs may be used for freewill offerings but not for vow offerings. Newborn animals must remain with their mothers for seven days before being eligible for sacrifice. The chapter concludes: "You shall not profane my holy name, that I may be sanctified among the people of Israel. I am the LORD who sanctifies you" (22:32).

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin emphasized that the blemish-free requirement was not arbitrary but pedagogical: it taught Israel that only perfection is worthy of the holy God. This prepared them to recognize the unblemished Lamb of God, "without blemish or spot" (1 Pet. 1:19). The prohibition against offering defective animals was later echoed by Malachi (1:6–14), who rebuked Israel for offering blind and lame animals—a corruption of worship that the Reformation saw paralleled in medieval practices that dishonored God through superstition and negligence.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther read the quality requirements as an expression of the first commandment: God must be honored above all things, and offering Him anything less than the best was a form of idolatry—placing one's own interests above God's glory. Luther connected this to the principle of giving one's best in all vocations, whether in worship, work, or service to neighbor.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the christological fulfillment: every unblemished animal pointed forward to the sinless Christ. The New Testament makes this connection explicit in 1 Peter 1:18–19 and Hebrews 9:14. The principle also applies to Christian worship: believers are called to offer their best to God, not the leftovers of their time, energy, and resources.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 22 establishes principles that resonate throughout Protestant theology:

1. **The perfection of Christ.** The blemish-free requirement is among the clearest Old Testament pointers to Christ's sinlessness, which qualifies Him uniquely as the atoning sacrifice for the world's sin.
2. **Reverence in worship.** The severe penalties for profaning holy things taught that worship is a serious matter. Protestant worship traditions, from the most liturgical to the simplest, affirm that approaching God requires reverence and sincerity.
3. **God's name at stake.** The command not to profane God's holy name (22:32) links the quality of worship to God's reputation among the nations. Sloppy or corrupt worship dishonors God before a watching world.
4. **Grace as motive.** The chapter grounds its demands in redemption: "I am the LORD who brought you out of Egypt" (22:33). Holiness is not the means of redemption but its fruit—a core Protestant principle.
