## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 20 prescribes the penalties for violations enumerated in chapters 18–19, transforming moral prohibitions into enforceable civil law. The chapter opens with the penalty for child sacrifice to Molech: death by stoning, with the additional warning that if the community fails to execute justice, God Himself will act against the offender and his family (20:1–5). Penalties for consulting mediums, cursing one's parents, adultery, incest, homosexual acts, and bestiality follow—most carrying the death penalty, some the punishment of being "cut off" from the people. The chapter's theological core is the repeated refrain: "You shall be holy to me, for I the LORD am holy and have separated you from the peoples, that you should be mine" (20:26). Israel is called to make the same distinction between clean and unclean that God has made between Israel and the nations.

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theologians distinguish between the moral principles underlying these penalties and the specific civil penalties themselves. The Westminster Confession (19.4) teaches that the judicial laws of Israel expired with the state of Israel, though the "general equity" of their moral content continues. Thus, the moral offenses listed in Leviticus 20 remain sinful, even though the specific penalties (stoning, etc.) belong to Israel's theocratic civil order and are not directly binding on modern states. Calvin applied this distinction carefully, arguing that magistrates should still punish grave moral offenses but according to the forms of their own legal systems.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther distinguished between the two kingdoms—the spiritual and the temporal—in his reading of this chapter. The spiritual kingdom concerns God's governance of believers through Word and Spirit; the temporal kingdom governs civil society through law and magistrates. The civil penalties of Leviticus 20 belonged to Israel's temporal governance and are not directly transferable to Christian states. However, the moral seriousness of the offenses remains, and civil authorities have a God-given responsibility to restrain evil and promote justice.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters grapple with the severity of the penalties, noting that they reflect the seriousness with which God views moral corruption. The death penalty for certain offenses underscored the lethal nature of sin: "the wages of sin is death" (Rom. 6:23). The passage from penalty to grace in the New Testament does not diminish sin's gravity but reveals the magnitude of Christ's atoning sacrifice, which absorbs the penalty that sinners deserve.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 20 addresses some of the most challenging questions in Protestant theology:

1. **General equity.** The Reformed principle of "general equity" allows Protestant ethics to affirm the moral content of Israel's civil laws while recognizing that their specific penalties were shaped for a theocratic context. This hermeneutical tool is essential for applying Old Testament law today.
2. **The seriousness of sin.** The severity of the penalties—primarily death—communicates that sin is not trivial. Every sin merits death (Rom. 6:23), and only the substitutionary death of Christ satisfies this penalty on behalf of believers.
3. **Election and holiness.** Verse 26 links God's election of Israel to the call to holiness. Protestant theology extends this to the church: those whom God has chosen are called to live distinctly (1 Pet. 2:9).
4. **Divine versus human justice.** The warning that God will personally punish when the community fails to act (20:4–5) teaches that human justice, while important, is never the final court. God's justice is ultimate and inescapable.
