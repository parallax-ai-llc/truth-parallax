## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 10 records one of the most sobering events in the Pentateuch. Nadab and Abihu, Aaron's eldest sons, offer "unauthorized fire" (*esh zarah*) before the LORD—fire "he had not commanded them" (10:1). Fire comes out from the LORD and consumes them, and they die in the sanctuary. Moses interprets the event with the words: "Among those who are near me I will be sanctified, and before all the people I will be glorified" (10:3). Aaron is silent. Moses instructs that the bodies be carried out of the camp and forbids Aaron and his remaining sons from public mourning, lest they die and wrath fall on the whole congregation. God then speaks directly to Aaron, commanding that priests abstain from wine and strong drink when entering the Tent of Meeting, so they can distinguish between the holy and the common, the clean and the unclean. The chapter closes with a dispute between Moses and Aaron over the handling of the sin offering, which Moses resolves graciously.

### Reformed Perspective

This chapter has been foundational for the Reformed regulative principle of worship. Calvin cited Nadab and Abihu repeatedly as proof that God will not accept worship offered according to human invention. "Unauthorized fire" was fire that God "had not commanded," and its punishment demonstrated that silence in Scripture is not permission. The Reformed tradition holds that only what Scripture positively warrants may be introduced into worship. Calvin also noted the contrast between the fire of 9:24 and 10:2: the same divine presence that accepts true worship destroys false worship.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther, while not adopting the strict regulative principle, agreed that Nadab and Abihu's sin was presumption—acting on their own authority in matters where God had spoken clearly. Luther applied this to the Roman Mass, arguing that additions to Christ's institution of the Supper were analogous to unauthorized fire. The prohibition of alcohol before service taught that ministers must be sober-minded and competent to handle God's Word rightly.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the severity of God's holiness. The narrative shocks modern readers, but it communicates an essential truth: the holy God is not safe to approach casually. The event also warns against spiritual leaders who substitute their own preferences for God's revealed will. Aaron's silence before the divine verdict (10:3) models submission to God's inscrutable justice, even when it is agonizing.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 10 is a watershed chapter for Protestant theology:

1. **The regulative principle.** The punishment of Nadab and Abihu anchors the Reformed conviction that worship must be governed by divine command, not human creativity.
2. **The holiness of God.** God's nearness does not domesticate His holiness. Greater privilege brings greater accountability—a principle relevant to every pastor and church leader.
3. **Law and grace in tension.** The same fire that accepted the offering in chapter 9 kills the offenders in chapter 10. This stark juxtaposition illustrates that God is both gracious and just, and that His grace must be received on His terms.
4. **Pastoral sobriety.** The prohibition of alcohol for ministers on duty speaks broadly to the spiritual clarity required for handling sacred things, a principle Protestant traditions apply to the qualifications for ministry (1 Tim. 3:3; Titus 1:7).
