## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 19 is one of the most ethically rich chapters in the entire Bible. It opens with the programmatic declaration, "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy" (19:2), and then unfolds a remarkable collection of moral, social, and religious commands. The chapter touches on reverence for parents, Sabbath observance, prohibition of idolatry, proper peace-offering procedure, leaving gleanings for the poor and the foreigner, prohibitions against stealing, lying, swearing falsely, oppressing a neighbor, withholding wages, cursing the deaf or tripping the blind, unjust judgment, slander, hatred, and vengeance. At its heart stands the command that Jesus identified as the second greatest: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself" (19:18). The chapter also includes regulations against mixed breeding of animals, mixed planting, mixed fabrics, and various prohibitions related to divination and pagan mourning practices.

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin considered Leviticus 19 an exposition of the Ten Commandments applied to daily life. The chapter moves systematically through duties to God (Sabbath, no idolatry), duties to parents, and duties to neighbor (justice, honesty, love). This confirmed the Reformed view that the moral law, summarized in the Decalogue, is the permanent standard of righteousness. The command to love one's neighbor was not a sentimental suggestion but a comprehensive ethical obligation with concrete economic, judicial, and relational applications. Calvin's Geneva drew heavily on this chapter for its social ethics.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther was particularly drawn to the social legislation: leaving gleanings for the poor, paying wages on time, not defrauding the vulnerable. He saw these as expressions of the natural law written on every human heart (Rom. 2:14–15), demonstrating that the moral content of Leviticus 19 transcended its Mosaic context. The command "you shall love your neighbor as yourself" was for Luther the summary of the entire second table of the law and the standard by which all works are judged.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters emphasize that Jesus elevated Leviticus 19:18 to the highest ethical principle alongside love for God (Matt. 22:37–40). Paul similarly declared that love fulfills the law (Rom. 13:8–10; Gal. 5:14). The chapter demonstrates that holiness is not merely cultic or ceremonial but profoundly ethical and social. True piety manifests in just business practices, honest courts, generous provision for the poor, and impartial treatment of all people.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 19 is central to Protestant ethics and theology:

1. **The moral law endures.** While ceremonial elements exist in the chapter (mixed fabrics, etc.), the moral commands—love, justice, honesty, care for the poor—are universal and permanent. This chapter is the primary Old Testament basis for the Protestant distinction between moral and ceremonial law.
2. **Holiness as ethical.** The chapter's opening declaration ("Be holy") is immediately followed by ethical commands, not ritual instructions. Protestant theology insists that holiness without justice is impossible—a corrective to both legalism and antinomianism.
3. **The second great commandment.** Jesus' citation of 19:18 as the second greatest commandment gives this verse a normative status unmatched in the Old Testament. It is the lens through which Protestant ethics reads all interpersonal obligation.
4. **Justice for the vulnerable.** The gleanings law, the wage law, and the prohibition of partiality in court establish a robust social ethic that has informed Protestant traditions from Calvinist Geneva to the evangelical social justice movement.
