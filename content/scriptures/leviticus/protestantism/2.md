## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 2 details the regulations for the grain offering (*minchah*), the only offering in the Levitical system that did not involve the shedding of blood. The offering could take several forms: fine flour with oil and frankincense, baked cakes or wafers, or grain roasted in the fire. A portion—the "memorial portion"—was burned on the altar, while the remainder went to the priests. The chapter includes two firm prohibitions: no leaven and no honey were to be placed on the altar fire, though both could accompany firstfruit offerings that were not burned. Every grain offering was to be seasoned with salt, described as "the salt of the covenant" (2:13).

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 2:1 | "When anyone brings a grain offering as an offering to the LORD" | Worship as voluntary response to God's grace, open to all |
| 2:2 | "Its memorial portion… a food offering with a pleasing aroma" | God's gracious acceptance of the worshipper's dedication |
| 2:11 | "No grain offering… shall be made with leaven" | Leaven as a symbol of sin and corruption (1 Cor. 5:6–8) |
| 2:13 | "You shall season all your grain offerings with salt… the salt of the covenant" | The permanence and faithfulness of God's covenantal promises |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed interpreters have noted that the grain offering accompanied the burnt offering, representing the fruit of human labor consecrated to God. Calvin emphasized that because the grain offering involved no blood, it could not atone for sin but instead expressed gratitude and dedication. The prohibition of leaven pointed to the purity God requires—a theme Calvin connected to the need for sincerity in worship (1 Cor. 5:8). The "salt of the covenant" reinforced the Reformed emphasis on covenant theology as the organizing framework of Scripture.

### Lutheran Perspective

Lutheran exegetes stressed that the grain offering illustrated the believer's response to grace rather than a means of securing it. The offering of the firstfruits of labor acknowledged God as the ultimate provider. Luther noted that the memorial portion burned before the Lord signified that God's honor comes first, while the provision for the priests demonstrated the legitimate support of those who serve in ministry (1 Cor. 9:13–14).

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical scholars highlight the relational dimension of the grain offering. It was often presented alongside the burnt and peace offerings, showing that complete worship involves not only atonement but also the dedication of one's daily life and labor to God. The salt requirement speaks to the believer's call to be "the salt of the earth" (Matt. 5:13), preserving and seasoning the world with covenant faithfulness.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 2 contributes several important themes to Protestant theology:

1. **Stewardship and consecration.** The grain offering sanctified ordinary labor. The worshipper brought the product of field and oven, acknowledging that all provision comes from God. This aligns with the Reformed doctrine of vocation—every legitimate calling can be offered to God's glory.
2. **Purity in worship.** The exclusion of leaven and honey guarded against corruption and sensual indulgence in worship. Protestant reformers applied this principle broadly to argue for simplicity and sincerity in church worship, rejecting ceremonies that obscured the gospel.
3. **Covenant permanence.** The salt of the covenant pointed to God's unbreakable commitment to His people. This theme resonates with Protestant assurance of salvation: God's covenant faithfulness, not human performance, is the ground of the believer's security.
4. **Provision for ministers.** The priests received the remainder of the grain offering. Paul explicitly invoked this principle in 1 Corinthians 9:13–14 to support the financial sustenance of gospel workers.

The grain offering's non-bloody character makes it a complement to, not a replacement for, the atoning sacrifices, reminding Protestant readers that justification and sanctification are distinct but inseparable realities.
