## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 18 opens the "holiness code" (chapters 18–27) with comprehensive regulations governing sexual conduct. God prefaces the laws by commanding Israel not to follow the practices of Egypt (where they had lived) or Canaan (where they were going), but to walk in His statutes (18:1–5). The chapter then enumerates prohibited sexual relationships: incest with various family members (mother, stepmother, sister, half-sister, granddaughter, aunt, daughter-in-law, sister-in-law), sexual relations during menstruation, adultery, child sacrifice to Molech, male homosexual intercourse, and bestiality. The chapter concludes with a warning that these are the very practices for which the land is "vomiting out" the Canaanite nations, and that Israel will suffer the same fate if they follow suit.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 18:4–5 | "You shall follow my rules and keep my statutes… I am the LORD your God" | God's authority as the basis for moral law |
| 18:24–25 | "Do not make yourselves unclean by any of these things, for by all these the nations I am driving out before you have become unclean" | Universal moral accountability; these are not arbitrary rules but reflect the created order |
| 18:5 | "If a person does them, he shall live by them" | The law's demand for perfect obedience, which no one can fulfill (Rom. 10:5; Gal. 3:12) |
| 18:25 | "The land vomited out its inhabitants" | Even the land itself recoils from moral corruption |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theologians classify Leviticus 18 as moral law, binding on all people in all ages, not merely ceremonial legislation applicable only to Israel. The proof is in the text itself: these prohibitions applied to the Canaanites, who never received the Mosaic covenant, demonstrating that sexual morality is grounded in creation, not merely in the Sinai legislation. Calvin emphasized that the reference to the land "vomiting out" its inhabitants showed that these violations offend the natural order God established. The Westminster Larger Catechism's exposition of the seventh commandment draws extensively on this chapter.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther read verse 5—"the person who does them shall live by them"—as expressing the law's impossible demand. Paul cites this verse in Galatians 3:12 to argue that justification cannot come through law-keeping, since no one obeys perfectly. The law reveals sin and drives the sinner to Christ. At the same time, Luther upheld the moral content of Leviticus 18 as expressing God's will for human sexuality, rooted in the creation of male and female (Gen. 1:27).

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the theological framework of the chapter: these laws are not cultural artifacts but expressions of God's unchanging moral will. The fact that the Canaanites were judged for violating them—without access to the Mosaic law—demonstrates their universal applicability. Evangelical sexual ethics draws heavily on this chapter, reading it alongside Genesis 1–2 and the New Testament's affirmation of these principles (Rom. 1:24–27; 1 Cor. 6:9–11).

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 18 is among the most discussed chapters in contemporary Protestant ethics:

1. **Moral law and creation order.** The chapter's application to pre-Mosaic nations demonstrates that sexual morality reflects the created order, not merely covenant stipulations. This is foundational to the Protestant argument that these norms transcend cultural context.
2. **The law's impossible demand.** Verse 5, cited by Paul in Romans 10:5 and Galatians 3:12, expresses the covenant of works principle: perfect obedience yields life. Its impossibility for fallen humanity necessitates the gospel of grace.
3. **Land theology.** The personification of the land vomiting out violators teaches that sin has ecological and societal consequences, not merely individual ones. Protestant theology connects this to the broader biblical theme of creation's groaning under sin (Rom. 8:19–22).
4. **Holiness as distinctiveness.** Israel's sexual ethics were to be radically distinct from the surrounding cultures. Protestant social ethics has drawn on this principle to argue for countercultural moral witness.
