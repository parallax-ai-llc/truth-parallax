## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 16 describes the Day of Atonement (*Yom Kippur*), the most solemn day in Israel's liturgical calendar and the theological center of the entire book. After the death of Nadab and Abihu, God instructs Moses that Aaron must not enter the Most Holy Place whenever he chooses, but only on this appointed day and with precise ritual preparation. Aaron bathes, dons plain white linen garments (not the ornate high priestly vestments), and offers a bull as a sin offering for himself and his household. He then takes two goats: one is designated by lot "for the LORD" as a sin offering, and the other "for Azazel" (the scapegoat). Aaron slaughters the LORD's goat and brings its blood behind the curtain, sprinkling it on and before the mercy seat. He then lays both hands on the live goat, confesses over it all the iniquities of Israel, and sends it into the wilderness, bearing their sins away. The ritual also cleanses the altar and the sanctuary itself from accumulated defilement. The chapter concludes by establishing Yom Kippur as a perpetual statute to be observed annually on the tenth day of the seventh month.

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theology regards Leviticus 16 as the single most important Old Testament type of Christ's atoning work. The two goats together picture what Christ accomplished on the cross: the slain goat represents propitiation (the satisfaction of God's wrath through blood), and the scapegoat represents expiation (the removal of sin far from God's people). Calvin emphasized that Aaron's entrance through the veil into the Most Holy Place anticipated Christ's entrance into heaven itself with His own blood (Heb. 9:11–12). The annual repetition of the ceremony—noted in Hebrews 10:1–4—proved that it could never finally resolve the problem of sin; only Christ's once-for-all sacrifice could accomplish what the Day of Atonement merely foreshadowed.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther identified the Day of Atonement as the clearest Old Testament demonstration that salvation is by grace through faith. The people did no work; they rested while the high priest made atonement on their behalf. This passivity of the beneficiary pictured the sinner who receives justification as a gift, apart from works of the law. Luther also stressed the scapegoat's bearing of sins "into the wilderness"—a solitary place where no one would see—as an image of how God removes sin completely: "as far as the east is from the west" (Ps. 103:12).

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters emphasize the comprehensive scope of the Day of Atonement. It addressed not only individual sins but the accumulated defilement of the tabernacle, the altar, and the entire community. Christ's death is similarly comprehensive: it atones for personal sins, restores creation, and reconciles all things to God (Col. 1:20). The detail that Aaron wore plain linen rather than his glorious vestments has been seen as anticipating Christ's humiliation in the incarnation—the King laying aside glory to serve as the sacrifice.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 16 is arguably the single most theologically significant chapter in the Old Testament for Protestant soteriology:

1. **Propitiation and expiation.** The two goats together present the fullness of atonement: God's wrath is satisfied (propitiation) and sin is removed from the people (expiation). This dual aspect is central to the Protestant understanding of the cross.
2. **The once-for-all sacrifice.** Hebrews 9–10 builds its entire argument on the contrast between the annual repetition of Yom Kippur and the finality of Christ's sacrifice. The repeated ceremony was a confession of its own inadequacy.
3. **Access to God.** The veil that restricted access to the Most Holy Place was torn at Christ's crucifixion (Matt. 27:51), opening the way for all believers to approach God directly (Heb. 10:19–22). This is the foundation of the Protestant rejection of a human mediatorial priesthood.
4. **Imputation.** The laying of hands on the scapegoat and the verbal confession of Israel's sins over it constitute the Old Testament's most explicit picture of the imputation of sin to a substitute—the doctrine at the heart of the Reformation.
