## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 9 records the eighth day—the day following the seven-day ordination period—when Aaron begins his active priestly ministry. Moses instructs Aaron to offer a calf for a sin offering and a ram for a burnt offering on his own behalf, and then a male goat for a sin offering, a calf and lamb for a burnt offering, an ox and ram for peace offerings, and a grain offering on behalf of the people. Aaron performs each sacrifice with careful precision. After completing the offerings, Aaron lifts his hands and blesses the people. Moses and Aaron enter the Tent of Meeting and come out to bless the people again. Then the glory of the LORD appears to all the people, and fire comes out from before the LORD and consumes the burnt offering and the fat on the altar. The people shout and fall on their faces in worship.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 9:7 | "Make atonement for yourself and for the people" | The priest's own sinfulness required atonement before he could serve others |
| 9:22 | "Aaron lifted up his hands toward the people and blessed them" | The priestly blessing as mediation of divine grace |
| 9:23 | "The glory of the LORD appeared to all the people" | God's manifest presence as the goal of worship |
| 9:24 | "Fire came out from before the LORD and consumed the burnt offering" | Divine acceptance of the sacrifice; God's initiative in worship |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin saw the divine fire in verse 24 as God's seal of approval on the entire sacrificial system—a visible confirmation that the divinely prescribed worship was accepted. This reinforced the Reformed principle that true worship must follow God's appointment, not human innovation. The fact that Aaron had to atone for himself first highlighted the imperfection of the earthly priesthood and pointed to the superiority of Christ, who had no need to offer for His own sins (Heb. 7:27).

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized the sequence: sacrifice precedes blessing. Aaron could not bless the people until atonement had been made. This order—atonement, then blessing—mirrors the gospel itself: first the cross, then the bestowal of grace. The fire from heaven was God's "Amen" to the sacrifice, confirming that acceptance with God comes through His appointed means, not human works.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters are drawn to the climactic appearance of God's glory. The entire sacrificial system aimed at this moment: the restoration of God's presence among His people. When the people saw the fire and the glory, they responded with awe and worship. This pattern—sin dealt with, God's presence restored, and the people moved to worship—previews the entire gospel narrative and the ultimate reality of Revelation 21:3, where God dwells with His people forever.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 9 marks the climactic inauguration of Israel's sacrificial worship and carries profound theological weight:

1. **Divine acceptance.** The fire from heaven confirmed that God accepted the sacrifices. Protestant theology insists that acceptance with God is always His gift, never our achievement.
2. **The imperfect priest.** Aaron's need to atone for himself first is the strongest argument for the superiority of Christ's priesthood. Hebrews 5:3 and 7:27 build directly on this observation.
3. **The goal of redemption.** The appearance of God's glory shows that atonement serves a larger purpose: the restoration of communion between God and humanity. Forgiveness is the means; fellowship is the end.
4. **Worship as response.** The people's prostration before the divine fire models authentic worship: overwhelmed by God's holiness and grace, the redeemed fall on their faces in reverent awe.
