## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 1 opens the book with God speaking to Moses from the Tent of Meeting, providing detailed instructions for the burnt offering (*olah*). This offering could be brought from the herd (a bull without blemish), from the flock (a sheep or goat), or from birds (turtledoves or young pigeons), accommodating worshippers of varying economic means. The offerer was to lay his hand on the head of the animal, signifying identification and substitution, before slaughtering it. The priests, Aaron's sons, would then sprinkle the blood on the sides of the altar and arrange the pieces on the fire. The entire animal was consumed by the flames—hence the designation "whole burnt offering." The chapter closes by declaring the offering "a food offering, a pleasing aroma to the LORD" (1:17).

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 1:3 | "He shall offer a male without blemish" | Typological foreshadowing of Christ, the unblemished Lamb of God (1 Pet. 1:19) |
| 1:4 | "He shall lay his hand on the head of the burnt offering, and it shall be accepted for him to make atonement" | The doctrine of imputation: sin transferred to a substitute |
| 1:9 | "A food offering, a pleasing aroma to the LORD" | Christ's sacrifice as a fragrant offering to the Father (Eph. 5:2) |
| 1:3 | "He shall offer it… at the entrance of the tent of meeting" | Access to God requires mediation—a truth fulfilled in Christ as the one Mediator (1 Tim. 2:5) |

### Reformed Perspective

John Calvin saw the burnt offering as a comprehensive picture of consecration. Because the entire animal was consumed, it represented the total devotion owed to God. Calvin stressed that the Israelite was not earning merit but receiving a divinely appointed means of grace. The laying on of hands taught that without substitutionary atonement, no sinner could stand before a holy God. The Westminster Larger Catechism (Q. 36) identifies Christ as the substance of all Old Testament sacrificial types.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther understood the Levitical sacrifices as pedagogical: they taught the people their sinfulness and need for a Savior. The burnt offering's complete consumption on the altar demonstrated that partial obedience is insufficient. In his lectures, Luther emphasized that the "pleasing aroma" language showed God's gracious decision to accept a substitute, anticipating the gospel principle that God provides what He demands.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical commentators highlight the accessibility of the burnt offering system. By permitting birds for the poor, God ensured that no one was excluded from worship on economic grounds. This principle of inclusive access foreshadows the gospel's universal invitation. The substitutionary logic of Leviticus 1:4 is foundational for evangelical soteriology: the offerer's hand on the victim's head is the Old Testament's clearest picture of penal substitutionary atonement.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 1 establishes principles that undergird Protestant theology:

1. **Substitutionary atonement.** The animal dies in the place of the worshipper. Protestant theology insists that this pattern finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ's death on the cross (Heb. 10:1–10).
2. **The necessity of blood.** Without the shedding of blood there is no remission of sins (Heb. 9:22). The burnt offering makes this principle visible and tangible.
3. **Total consecration.** Because the entire offering was burned, the burnt offering pictures the believer's call to present their bodies as living sacrifices (Rom. 12:1). Reformed theologians in particular have connected this to the doctrine of sanctification.
4. **Divine initiative.** God prescribes the means of approach. Sinners do not invent their own way to God but come on His terms. This guards against the works-righteousness that the Reformation opposed.

The chapter's emphasis on the "without blemish" requirement anticipates the New Testament's insistence on Christ's sinlessness as a precondition for effective atonement.
