## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 6 falls into two distinct sections. The first (6:1–7) continues the guilt offering instructions from chapter 5, addressing cases of deliberate fraud: deceiving a neighbor about a deposit, stolen property, found objects, or false oaths regarding any of these. Restitution of the full amount plus a fifth is required, along with a ram for the guilt offering. The second section (6:8–30) provides detailed instructions for the priests regarding the administration of the burnt offering, the grain offering, and the sin offering. The fire on the altar must never go out (6:12–13). The grain offering of the high priest's ordination is described, and strict holiness rules govern who may eat the sin offering and where.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 6:2–3 | "If anyone sins and commits a breach of faith against the LORD by deceiving his neighbor" | Sin against neighbor is sin against God |
| 6:5 | "He shall restore it in full and shall add a fifth to it" | Genuine repentance produces restitution |
| 6:12–13 | "The fire on the altar shall be kept burning… it shall not go out" | The perpetual fire as a symbol of unceasing prayer and devotion |
| 6:25 | "In the place where the burnt offering is killed shall the sin offering be killed" | The unity of substitution and propitiation at the same altar |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin drew attention to the fact that deliberate sins of fraud are addressed by the guilt offering system, correcting any notion that only unintentional sins could be atoned for. This demonstrated God's comprehensive provision for human wickedness. The perpetual fire on the altar symbolized the unbroken worship that God's holiness demands and that Christ's intercession now fulfills without ceasing (Heb. 7:25). Reformed worship theology has drawn on this image to emphasize the regularity and constancy of congregational worship.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther saw the perpetual fire as a picture of the Word of God that must never be extinguished in the church. The preaching of law and gospel keeps the altar fire burning. The detailed priestly instructions demonstrated that worship is not casual but ordered, a principle Luther applied to his own liturgical reforms—simplifying Rome's ceremonies while retaining reverent order.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the moral seriousness of fraud in this chapter. Sins against a neighbor require both vertical atonement (the guilt offering) and horizontal restitution (full payment plus a fifth). This dual requirement shows that biblical religion is never merely private or spiritual; it has public, material consequences. The chapter also reinforces the costliness of sin: every wrong must be made right.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 6 contributes to Protestant thought in these ways:

1. **Sin as covenant breach.** Fraud against a neighbor is described as "a breach of faith against the LORD" (6:2), showing that all sin is ultimately vertical, confirming David's confession in Psalm 51:4.
2. **The perpetual fire.** The never-extinguished altar fire is a rich symbol. It speaks of God's constant readiness to receive sinners, Christ's perpetual intercession, and the church's calling to continuous worship.
3. **Holiness in ministry.** The exacting requirements for priestly handling of offerings taught that service to God must be carried out with reverence and care, a principle applied in Protestant ordination standards.
4. **Restitution and justice.** The chapter reinforces that divine forgiveness does not eliminate the obligation to repair earthly harm, balancing the Protestant emphasis on grace with a robust ethic of justice.
