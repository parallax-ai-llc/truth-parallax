## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 14 prescribes the elaborate purification ritual for a person healed of *tsara'at* (skin disease). The ceremony occurs in two stages. On the first day, the priest goes outside the camp to examine the healed person. Two clean birds are brought: one is killed over running water, and the living bird—dipped in the blood of the slain bird along with cedar wood, scarlet yarn, and hyssop—is released into the open field. The healed person washes, shaves, and waits seven days. On the eighth day, a guilt offering, sin offering, and burnt offering are presented. Blood from the guilt offering and oil are applied to the right ear, thumb, and big toe of the cleansed person, mirroring the priestly ordination. A provision for the poor allows substitution of birds for the larger animals. The chapter concludes with instructions for dealing with *tsara'at* contamination in houses, including scraping, replastering, and, if necessary, demolition.

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin saw the two-bird ceremony as a rich christological type. The slain bird represented Christ's death; the living bird dipped in blood and released represented Christ's resurrection, carrying the evidence of the sacrifice into the heavens. The use of cedar, scarlet yarn, and hyssop pointed to the cross (wood), Christ's blood (scarlet), and purification (hyssop, as in Psalm 51:7). The application of blood and oil to ear, thumb, and toe—identical to the priestly ordination—taught that the cleansed person was being reinstated as a full member of God's priestly kingdom.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized that healing alone was insufficient for restoration; atonement was also required. Physical recovery did not automatically restore one to the community of faith. This demonstrated that the barrier between God and humanity is not merely physical but spiritual—it is sin, not sickness, that ultimately separates. The elaborate ceremony of return taught the weight and wonder of being readmitted to God's presence after exclusion.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters highlight that the cleansed leper underwent a ceremony parallel to priestly ordination—blood and oil on the same body parts (cf. 8:23–24). This suggested that restoration was not merely a return to the old status but an elevation to priestly service. Every restored sinner is called to serve. The release of the living bird into the open field is a powerful image of the freedom that comes through Christ's atoning death.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 14 provides Protestant theology with some of its most vivid typological material:

1. **Death and resurrection.** The two-bird ceremony remarkably anticipates the death and resurrection of Christ. One bird dies; the other, bearing the blood of the slain, goes free. This is the gospel in miniature.
2. **Restoration to priestly status.** The parallel between the cleansing ceremony and the ordination of priests (ch. 8) teaches that redeemed sinners are restored not merely to neutral standing but to active priestly service (1 Pet. 2:9).
3. **Atonement beyond healing.** Physical healing does not address the spiritual problem. Only blood atonement restores the person to God's community—a principle that points to the insufficiency of moral reform apart from Christ's sacrifice.
4. **The comprehensive reach of defilement.** The extension of *tsara'at* to houses showed that contamination could pervade the very structures of life, requiring radical intervention—even demolition. Sin's effects are similarly structural and require the radical remedy of the cross.
