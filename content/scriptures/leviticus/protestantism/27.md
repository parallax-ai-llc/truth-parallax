## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 27 forms an appendix to the book, addressing the practical matter of vows and voluntary dedications to the LORD. When a person vowed to dedicate a person to the LORD (presumably for tabernacle service), the priest assigned a monetary valuation based on age and sex, ranging from fifty shekels for a man aged twenty to sixty down to three shekels for a female child under five. Those too poor to pay the standard valuation could have the amount reduced by the priest's assessment. Regulations follow for dedicated animals (clean animals become holy and cannot be exchanged; unclean animals may be redeemed with a surcharge of one-fifth), houses, and fields (with complex calculations based on the Jubilee calendar). The chapter distinguishes between items that can be redeemed and those that are "devoted" (*cherem*)—irrevocably given to God and unredeemable. The book closes with the tithe laws: a tenth of the produce of the land and of the herd belongs to the LORD.

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin drew from this chapter the principle that vows to God must be taken with utmost seriousness. He warned against rash vows—those made in haste without counting the cost (cf. Eccl. 5:4–5)—while affirming that commitments freely made to God carry binding obligation. The provision for the poor to have reduced valuations demonstrated God's equity and compassion. Calvin rejected monastic vows as unbiblical, but he affirmed that the general principle of dedication—offering oneself and one's resources to God—was permanently valid. The prohibition against dedicating the firstborn (since it already belongs to God) taught that we cannot give God what is already His as if it were a meritorious act.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther noted that the chapter's focus on valuation, redemption, and substitution reinforced the book's overarching theme: everything has a cost, and the cost of sin ultimately falls on the divine Substitute. The distinction between redeemable and unredeemable items (*cherem*) taught that some things are irrevocably given to God—a principle Luther applied to baptism and the Christian's irrevocable identity in Christ. Luther also cautioned against the abuse of vows, as seen in the monastic system, arguing that no vow should contradict Christian liberty.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters emphasize the stewardship principles embedded in the chapter. The tithe acknowledged that everything belongs to God and that human beings are stewards, not owners. The complexity of the redemption and valuation system taught that one's relationship with God has material and economic dimensions. Giving is not merely spiritual but tangible and concrete. The chapter also reinforces the sacredness of promises: when a believer commits something to God, that commitment is not casual but holy.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 27 brings the book to a fitting conclusion with several important Protestant themes:

1. **The seriousness of vows.** Protestant theology takes commitments to God seriously while rejecting the elaboration of monastic vows that characterized medieval Catholicism. The chapter teaches that vows should be voluntary, measured, and kept with integrity (Matt. 5:33–37).
2. **Stewardship and tithing.** The tithe laws ground the Protestant theology of giving: everything belongs to God, and returning a portion acknowledges His lordship. While Protestants debate the exact application of the tithe under the new covenant, the underlying principle of generous, proportional giving is universally affirmed.
3. **What cannot be offered.** The prohibition against dedicating what already belongs to God (the firstborn) warns against a transactional approach to religion that imagines we can earn merit by giving God what He already owns. This principle supports the Protestant critique of works-righteousness.
4. **Redemption.** The pervasive concept of redeeming persons, animals, and property points to the greater redemption accomplished by Christ, "who gave himself as a ransom for all" (1 Tim. 2:6). The surcharge of one-fifth on redeemed items suggests the costliness of redemption—fully revealed at the cross.
