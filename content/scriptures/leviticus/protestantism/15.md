## Summary of the Chapter

Leviticus 15 addresses ceremonial impurity arising from bodily discharges—both abnormal and normal—for men and women. The chapter treats four cases in a symmetrical structure: abnormal male discharge (15:2–15), normal male emission (15:16–17), normal female menstruation (15:19–24), and abnormal female discharge (15:25–30). Each case specifies the duration of impurity, the contaminating effect on persons and objects touched, and the purification requirements. Minor impurities require washing and waiting until evening; major or prolonged impurities require a waiting period of seven days after cessation plus offerings (two turtledoves or pigeons). The chapter concludes with the rationale: "Thus you shall keep the people of Israel separate from their uncleanness, lest they die in their uncleanness by defiling my tabernacle that is in their midst" (15:31).

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 15:31 | "Thus you shall keep the people of Israel separate from their uncleanness, lest they die… by defiling my tabernacle" | The danger of approaching God's presence in a state of impurity |
| 15:25 | "If a woman has a discharge of blood for many days" | The background to the woman healed by touching Jesus' garment (Mark 5:25–34) |
| 15:13 | "He shall count for himself seven days… wash his clothes and bathe" | Purification through prescribed means, not self-determination |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin interpreted the impurity laws as teaching that the whole of human bodily existence stands under the shadow of the fall. Bodily functions that are entirely natural still rendered a person ceremonially unfit to approach the holy God. This was not because the body is evil—Calvin firmly rejected dualism—but because the entire creation, including the human body, groans under the consequences of sin (Rom. 8:22). The ceremonial requirements pointed forward to the comprehensive purification that Christ alone provides.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther read this chapter as another demonstration of the law's pedagogical function. The constant cycle of impurity, washing, and restoration kept the awareness of uncleanness ever before Israel, reinforcing their need for a once-for-all cleansing. Luther contrasted this endless repetition with the finality of Christian baptism: where the old covenant required repeated washings, the new covenant offers a single baptism that definitively incorporates the believer into Christ's purifying death.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters are especially attentive to the woman with the twelve-year hemorrhage in Mark 5:25–34, whose condition corresponds to Leviticus 15:25. Her perpetual uncleanness would have excluded her from worship and community for over a decade. When she touched Jesus' garment and was healed, Jesus did not become unclean; instead, His power overcame her impurity. This gospel reversal—the clean making the unclean clean rather than vice versa—is the theological climax toward which Leviticus 15 points.

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 15 carries several important themes for Protestant theology:

1. **The pervasiveness of impurity.** Even involuntary, natural bodily processes rendered a person unfit for worship. This reinforced the comprehensive nature of human need and the impossibility of maintaining purity through one's own effort.
2. **The tabernacle's holiness.** The concluding verse (15:31) reveals the ultimate concern: protecting the sacred space from defilement. God's holiness demands that nothing impure enters His presence—a reality fulfilled in the pure access believers have through Christ (Heb. 10:19–22).
3. **Christ's purifying touch.** The purity laws established the expectation of contagion: the unclean contaminates the clean. Jesus reversed this principle, as His holiness overcomes impurity on contact, the definitive Protestant reading of Christ's interactions with the unclean.
4. **Freedom from ceremonial bondage.** The new covenant liberates believers from the endless cycle of ritual washing, replacing it with the permanent cleansing of Christ's blood (1 John 1:7).
