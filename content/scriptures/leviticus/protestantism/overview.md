## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Leviticus is shaped by the Reformation distinction between the moral, ceremonial, and civil law. While the moral law (supremely the Decalogue) remains binding, the ceremonial and sacrificial laws are understood as fulfilled in Christ. The entire sacrificial system of Leviticus — the burnt offerings, the sin offerings, the Day of Atonement — points typologically to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice on the cross (Hebrews 7–10).

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Typological reading**: Every sacrifice, every priestly act, every purification ritual anticipates the work of Christ
- **The moral/ceremonial distinction**: The Westminster Confession (19.3–4) articulates the classic Reformed position that ceremonial laws "are now abrogated under the New Testament"
- **Justification by faith**: Leviticus demonstrates humanity's inability to achieve holiness through works of the law, pointing to the need for grace
- **The third use of the law**: The moral teachings of Leviticus (especially ch. 19) continue to guide Christian ethics
