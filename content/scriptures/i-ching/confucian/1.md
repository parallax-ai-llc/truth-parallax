The Confucian reading of the first group of hexagrams begins with Qian (The Creative, Heaven) and Kun (The Receptive, Earth), the primal pair whose interaction generates all phenomena. Confucian interpretation, following Cheng Yi and Zhu Xi, reads Qian as the principle of active, initiating moral energy and Kun as the principle of receptive, sustaining moral responsiveness. Together they model the relationship between the creative initiative of the ruler or sage and the responsive cooperation of the minister or student.

The hexagrams that follow — Zhun (Difficulty at the Beginning), Meng (Youthful Folly), Xu (Waiting), Song (Conflict), Shi (The Army), and Bi (Holding Together) — are read as establishing the foundational situations of human social life. The Confucian junzi studies these hexagrams to understand how to act with propriety in each type of situation, cultivating the moral wisdom that enables appropriate response to the full range of human circumstances.
