Confucian interpretation of the second group focuses on themes of social harmony, accumulation of virtue, and the relationship between greatness and humility. Hexagram 11, Tai (Peace), represents the ideal Confucian social order in which heaven and earth, ruler and people, are in harmonious communion. Its paired opposite, Hexagram 12, Pi (Standstill), represents the breakdown of this communion — a warning that the junzi must constantly work to maintain the conditions for social harmony.

Hexagram 15, Qian (Modesty), holds special importance in Confucian reading as the only hexagram in which all six lines are favorable. This reflects the Confucian conviction that modesty is the one virtue that benefits the practitioner in every situation. The junzi who combines genuine accomplishment with genuine humility embodies the Confucian ideal of moral cultivation — a theme that Zhu Xi emphasized as central to the I Ching's teaching.
