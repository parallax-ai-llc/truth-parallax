## Confucian Reading of Hexagram 11

Hexagram 11, Tai (Peace), receives a distinctively moral reading in the Confucian tradition. Confucian interpreters, from the authors of the Ten Wings through the great Neo-Confucian commentators Cheng Yi and Zhu Xi, read this hexagram as instruction for the *junzi* (superior person) in the cultivation of virtue and the ordering of human relationships.

### The Image Commentary

The Image (*Xiang*) commentary for Hexagram 11 draws a lesson for the superior person from the hexagram's natural imagery. The *junzi* observes the pattern of harmony in the natural world and derives from it a principle for moral self-cultivation and social conduct. This connection between natural pattern and moral principle is characteristic of Confucian cosmological ethics.

### Cheng Yi's Commentary

Cheng Yi's *Yizhuan* (Commentary on the Changes) reads Hexagram 11 as an instruction in moral philosophy. He interprets the hexagram's situation as illustrating the proper response of a person of virtue to the conditions of Peace. The emphasis falls on the inner moral quality of the agent rather than on external outcomes, reflecting Cheng Yi's conviction that the I Ching is fundamentally a book of moral wisdom.

### Zhu Xi's Synthesis

Zhu Xi maintained a dual approach: the I Ching is both a divination text and a moral-philosophical treatise. For Hexagram 11, Zhu Xi preserves the oracular dimension while insisting that the deeper meaning concerns the cultivation of *li* (principle) and the rectification of *qi* (vital energy). The hexagram teaches the *junzi* to embody prosperity in order to achieve balance.

### Social and Political Application

Confucian interpreters apply Hexagram 11 to the ordering of the five relationships (ruler-subject, parent-child, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend) and to the governance of the state. The hexagram's counsel regarding harmony is extended to the proper conduct of officials, the management of the household, and the cultivation of social harmony.
