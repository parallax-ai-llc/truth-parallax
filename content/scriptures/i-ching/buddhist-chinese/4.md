Chinese Buddhist readers find in Hexagram 29, Kan (The Abysmal, Water), a powerful image of saṃsāra — the dangerous, ever-moving flow of existence through which the practitioner must navigate. Water fills every hollow it encounters without pause, just as the practitioner must meet each experience fully without becoming trapped. The teaching to maintain sincerity of heart in the midst of danger corresponds to the Buddhist emphasis on maintaining practice in all circumstances.

Hexagram 30, Li (The Clinging, Fire), complements Water as an image of the illuminating quality of awareness (prajñā) that allows the practitioner to see clearly even in the midst of saṃsāra's dangers. Fire depends on fuel and thus represents the conditioned nature of all illumination short of full enlightenment, but it also demonstrates that clarity is possible within the realm of dependent origination.
