Chinese Buddhist reading of the opening hexagrams interprets Qian and Kun through the lens of form and emptiness. Qian's creative energy corresponds to the active manifestation of phenomena from the ground of emptiness (śūnyatā), while Kun's receptive quiescence corresponds to the return to the unmanifest ground. Together they illustrate the Heart Sutra's teaching that form is emptiness and emptiness is form — not as an abstract proposition but as the dynamic reality of every moment.

The subsequent hexagrams of this group — beginning with Zhun (Difficulty at the Beginning) — are read as illustrating the Buddhist teaching of dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda). Each hexagram arises in dependence upon the configuration of yin and yang lines, just as each phenomenon arises in dependence upon its conditions. No hexagram has independent existence; each is defined by its relationship to all the others.
