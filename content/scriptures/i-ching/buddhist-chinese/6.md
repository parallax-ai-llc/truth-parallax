The Buddhist reading of Hexagram 48, Jing (The Well), interprets the inexhaustible well as a symbol of the dharma — the Buddhist teaching that is available to all beings regardless of their condition. Like the well that provides water to all who draw from it, the dharma nourishes all who approach it with receptivity. The hexagram's warning about the rope being too short or the jug breaking represents the obstacles that prevent beings from accessing the dharma despite its universal availability.

Hexagram 46, Sheng (Pushing Upward), is read as an image of the gradual path of spiritual cultivation emphasized in Chinese Buddhist schools like Tiantai. The slow, steady upward growth, like a tree pushing through the earth toward the light, represents the practitioner's patient accumulation of merit and wisdom over many lifetimes. This gradual approach complements the sudden awakening emphasized in Chan, reflecting the creative tension between gradualism and subitism in Chinese Buddhist thought.
