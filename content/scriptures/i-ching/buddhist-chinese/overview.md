## Overview

Chinese Buddhist engagement with the I Ching reflects the broader process of Buddhist adaptation to Chinese culture. As Buddhism entered China, its doctrines were initially understood through the lens of existing Chinese concepts, and the I Ching's emphasis on change (yi) was naturally connected to the Buddhist teaching of impermanence (anitya). This conceptual bridge facilitated the transmission of Buddhist ideas while also transforming how the I Ching itself was understood.

## Theological Emphases

Buddhist thinkers such as Zhiyi (538-597), the founder of the Tiantai school, drew on the I Ching's binary logic of yin and yang to articulate Buddhist philosophical concepts. The hexagram system's representation of all possible combinations of two elements resonated with the Huayan school's philosophy of interpenetration, in which each phenomenon contains and reflects all others. The I Ching's sixty-four hexagrams could be read as illustrating the Buddhist principle that all phenomena arise in mutual dependence.

## Interpretive Context

Chan (Zen) Buddhism developed a more ambivalent relationship with the I Ching, sometimes employing its imagery in koan practice and dharma talks while also insisting that intellectual analysis of hexagrams must yield to direct insight into the nature of mind. The I Ching's teaching that the sage responds spontaneously to changing conditions, without premeditation, resonated with the Chan emphasis on immediate, unmediated awareness.
