Buddhist interpretation of the fifth group reads Hexagram 36, Ming Yi (Darkening of the Light), as an account of how the light of Buddha-nature can be concealed by adverse conditions without being extinguished. The image of brightness entering the earth corresponds to the Tathāgatagarbha doctrine that the Buddha-nature exists fully in every sentient being, even those in the deepest states of ignorance and suffering.

Hexagram 38, Kui (Opposition), with its image of fire and lake moving in opposite directions, is read as illustrating the Buddhist teaching on the non-duality of apparent opposites. What appears as opposition on the conventional level reveals, upon deeper investigation, a hidden unity. This reading draws on the Madhyamaka principle that conventional dualities (saṃsāra and nirvāṇa, self and other, purity and defilement) are ultimately inseparable.
