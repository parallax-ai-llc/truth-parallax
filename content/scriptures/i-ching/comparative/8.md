The I Ching's closing with Wei Ji (Before Completion) invites comparison with how other canonical texts conclude. The Hebrew Bible's placement of 2 Chronicles at the end, calling the people to return, the Qur'an's final sura seeking refuge in God, and the Pali Canon's Mahāparinibbāna Sutta recounting the Buddha's death — each tradition's closing gesture reveals something essential about its understanding of the human situation.

The I Ching's refusal to end with completion sets it apart from most sacred texts. Where the Bible moves toward apocalyptic fulfillment and the Buddhist Abhidharma catalogs reality into exhaustive categories, the I Ching insists that the ultimate truth is ongoing process. This philosophical stance — that reality is fundamentally open-ended and that every completion is also a new beginning — represents one of the I Ching's most distinctive and enduring contributions to world thought.
