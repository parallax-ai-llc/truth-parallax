Comparative study of Hexagram 48, Jing (The Well), connects the I Ching with the widespread use of the well as a religious and philosophical symbol across cultures. The biblical wells of Genesis (Beer-sheba, Beer-lahai-roi), the Hindu concept of the cosmic well of wisdom (jñāna-kūpa), and the Norse Urdarbrunnr (Well of Fate) all invest the well with symbolic significance as a source of life, wisdom, or connection between worlds.

The sixth group's treatment of decrease and increase connects with economic and ethical philosophies across traditions that address the circulation of resources and the relationship between personal sacrifice and communal benefit. The I Ching's teaching that the ruler should decrease personal excess to increase the people's welfare resonates with the Platonic concept of the philosopher-king who rules for the common good, the Buddhist ideal of the bodhisattva who sacrifices personal attainment for others' benefit, and Islamic zakat as obligatory redistribution of wealth.
