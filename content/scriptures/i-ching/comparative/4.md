Comparative analysis of the fourth group's treatment of nourishment (Hexagram 27) connects the I Ching with the universal human concern about the relationship between physical and spiritual sustenance. The parallel between what enters the mouth and what emerges from it — between physical nourishment and verbal expression — appears also in the Hebrew Bible (the dietary laws combined with the prophetic emphasis on righteous speech), in Buddhist mindful eating practices, and in Islamic halal principles that connect physical purity with spiritual state.

Hexagram 32, Heng (Duration), addresses a philosophical question that appears in every major tradition: how to maintain constancy in a world of change. The I Ching's solution — duration is achieved not through rigidity but through responsive adaptation — can be compared with the Aristotelian concept of the mean, the Buddhist Middle Way, and the Confucian zhongyong (Doctrine of the Mean). Each offers a version of the insight that true stability is dynamic rather than static.
