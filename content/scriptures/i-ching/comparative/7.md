Hexagram 49, Ge (Revolution), invites comparison with political philosophies that address the conditions under which regime change is justified. The I Ching's carefully qualified endorsement of revolution — only after multiple demonstrations of necessity and only when the time is truly ripe — can be compared with John Locke's theory of the right of revolution, the Islamic concept of khuruj (rising against unjust authority), and the Christian theory of just revolution developed in the Reformed tradition.

Hexagram 52, Gen (Keeping Still), connects the I Ching with contemplative traditions worldwide that emphasize the spiritual practice of stillness. The comparison extends from Daoist zuowang (sitting in forgetfulness) to Christian hesychasm (the practice of inner stillness), Buddhist samatha (calm abiding meditation), and Hindu dhyana (meditative absorption). The I Ching's distinctive contribution is its insistence that stillness is not a withdrawal from the world of change but a way of perceiving the pattern within change.
