Academic analysis of the first group of hexagrams examines the textual and archaeological evidence for the earliest layers of the I Ching tradition. The hexagram names and judgments for Qian and Kun, along with the line statements, likely originated as oracular responses in Western Zhou divination practice. Scholars debate whether the hexagrams were originally independent oracle texts later organized into a systematic sequence or were composed as part of a planned system from the beginning.

The relationship between the I Ching's hexagram system and the earlier Shang dynasty practice of oracle bone divination (pyromancy using turtle shells and ox scapulae) remains a subject of active scholarly investigation. While both are divination systems, the oracle bone method sought direct answers from ancestral spirits, whereas the I Ching system relies on a symbolic framework of abstract figures. This shift from spirit communication to symbolic interpretation represents a significant intellectual development in ancient Chinese thought.
