## Scholarly Analysis of Hexagram 55

Academic study of Hexagram 55, Feng (Abundance), examines the hexagram's textual history, its original meaning in the Western Zhou context, and the layers of interpretation that have accumulated over nearly three millennia. Modern scholarship applies linguistic, archaeological, and comparative methods to recover meanings obscured by centuries of commentarial tradition.

### Textual Layers

The hexagram text consists of multiple layers: the hexagram name (Feng), likely the oldest element; the Judgment (*tuan*), attributed to King Wen; and the line texts (*yaoci*), attributed to the Duke of Zhou. Academic scholars debate whether these attributions reflect historical authorship or later tradition, with most concluding that the core texts date to the early Western Zhou period (c. 9th century BCE).

### Original Context

In its original context, the imagery of Hexagram 55 likely drew on the agricultural, military, and social life of the early Zhou court. Scholars such as Edward Shaughnessy and Richard Kunst have shown that many hexagram and line texts refer to specific historical events, sacrificial practices, or diplomatic situations that were later universalized through commentarial abstraction.

### Mawangdui Variant

The Mawangdui silk manuscript (168 BCE) preserves a version of this hexagram that differs from the received text in several respects, including variant characters, different line arrangements, and a different hexagram sequence. These variants provide valuable evidence for the text's early transmission history and help scholars distinguish between original readings and later editorial changes.

### Commentarial History

The interpretation of Hexagram 55 has evolved significantly through successive commentarial traditions: the cosmological readings of the Han dynasty, Wang Bi's abstract philosophical approach, the moral-metaphysical readings of Song Neo-Confucianism, and the critical-historical approaches of modern scholarship.
