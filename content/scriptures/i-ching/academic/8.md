Academic analysis of the final group and the Ten Wings examines the relationship between the hexagram texts and the commentarial tradition. The Ten Wings, traditionally attributed to Confucius, are now understood as a collection of texts from different periods, the earliest perhaps dating to the 4th century BCE and the latest to the 2nd century BCE. Their attribution to Confucius, while historically unfounded, reflects the text's incorporation into the Confucian canonical tradition.

The final hexagrams, Ji Ji (After Completion) and Wei Ji (Before Completion), and their placement at the end of the sequence, have been analyzed as a deliberate editorial decision that encodes a philosophical message about the nature of the text itself. Scholars debate whether this arrangement belongs to the earliest stratum of the text or was introduced during the editorial process that produced the received version, but its philosophical implications — the refusal of closure, the insistence on ongoing process — have been recognized as one of the I Ching's most distinctive intellectual contributions.
