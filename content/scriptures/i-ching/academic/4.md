Scholarly examination of the fourth group focuses on the language and imagery of the line statements as windows into Western Zhou culture. Hexagram 26, Da Xu (The Taming Power of the Great), with its references to taming oxen and horses, reflects the importance of animal husbandry and chariot warfare in Zhou society. The transition from literal agricultural and military references to abstract philosophical interpretation can be traced through the layers of commentary.

Hexagram 28, Da Guo (Preponderance of the Great), with its image of a ridgepole sagging under excessive weight, has been analyzed by scholars as reflecting both architectural realities and political metaphor. The identification of such concrete images in the early strata of the text, and their progressive abstraction in later interpretation, illustrates the I Ching's transformation from a practical divination manual to a philosophical classic.
