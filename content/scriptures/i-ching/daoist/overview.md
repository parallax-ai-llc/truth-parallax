## Overview

Daoist interpretation of the I Ching reads the text as a revelation of the Dao's operation through the interplay of yin and yang. The sixty-four hexagrams are understood as mapping the complete cycle of natural transformation — the way in which the Dao manifests through the ceaseless alternation of complementary forces. Each hexagram captures a moment in this eternal flow, and wisdom consists in recognizing the current moment and acting in harmony with its inherent tendency.

## Theological Emphases

The Daoist reading emphasizes the principles of wu wei (non-forcing action) and ziran (naturalness) that the hexagrams implicitly teach. When a situation is at its yang extreme, the wise person recognizes the inevitable turn toward yin and adjusts accordingly, neither resisting the change nor forcing it. This responsive, adaptive wisdom is understood as the practical expression of alignment with the Dao.

## Interpretive Context

Daoist alchemical and meditative traditions have drawn extensively on the I Ching's symbolism, using the trigrams and hexagrams as maps of internal energy transformation. The eight trigrams correspond to organs, energies, and stages of spiritual refinement in Daoist inner alchemy (neidan), making the I Ching not merely a philosophical text but a practical manual for spiritual cultivation.
