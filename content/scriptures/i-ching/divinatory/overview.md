## Overview

The divinatory tradition reads the I Ching primarily as a living oracle — a means of obtaining guidance from a wisdom that transcends ordinary human understanding. The traditional yarrow stalk method, involving the manipulation of fifty stalks through a series of divisions to generate hexagram lines, is understood as a ritual practice that creates the conditions for meaningful communication between the questioner and the cosmic intelligence expressed through the hexagrams.

## Theological Emphases

The simpler three-coin method, which became widespread during the Song dynasty, made the oracle accessible to a broader population. In both methods, the generation of changing lines — lines that transform from yin to yang or vice versa — produces a dynamic reading that moves from a primary hexagram (representing the current situation) to a relating hexagram (representing the situation's development), capturing the I Ching's central insight that all situations are in process of transformation.

## Interpretive Context

The divinatory tradition maintains that the I Ching works not through supernatural intervention but through the principle of synchronicity — the meaningful coincidence between the inner state of the questioner and the outer pattern of the hexagram. This understanding, articulated in modern terms by Carl Jung but implicit in the Chinese tradition, situates the oracle within a worldview in which inner and outer reality are fundamentally connected.
