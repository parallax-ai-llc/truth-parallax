## Overview

The I Ching (Yijing, Book of Changes) is one of the oldest and most influential texts in Chinese civilization, with roots extending back to the Western Zhou dynasty (c. 1046-771 BCE) and layers of commentary accumulated over more than two millennia. At its core, the text presents sixty-four hexagrams — figures composed of six stacked lines, either solid (yang) or broken (yin) — each accompanied by a judgment (guaci) and line statements (yaoci) that offer guidance for human action in a world of ceaseless change.

## Structure

The I Ching as traditionally received consists of the hexagram texts and the Ten Wings (Shiyi), a collection of commentaries attributed to Confucius but likely composed over several centuries:

1. **Heaven and Earth — Hexagrams 1-8**: Beginning with the primal pair of Qian (Heaven) and Kun (Earth), establishing the fundamental dynamics of yang and yin
2. **Lake and Mountain — Hexagrams 9-16**: Exploring themes of accumulation, peace, community, and the power of the small
3. **Fire and Water — Hexagrams 17-24**: Following, adornment, splitting apart, and the critical theme of return
4. **Thunder and Wind — Hexagrams 25-32**: Innocence, great accumulation, nourishment, and the theme of endurance
5. **The Great Treatise Part 1 — Hexagrams 33-40**: Retreat, great power, progress, and the theme of deliverance
6. **Progression and Return — Hexagrams 41-48**: Decrease, increase, breakthrough, and the image of the well
7. **Breakthrough and Gathering — Hexagrams 49-56**: Revolution, the cauldron, arousing, and the wanderer
8. **The Closing — Hexagrams 57-64 and the Ten Wings**: The gentle wind, joyousness, dispersion, and the final hexagrams of completion and incompletion, plus the interpretive commentaries

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The I Ching holds significance across multiple traditions:

- **Confucianism**: One of the Five Classics; studied as a text of moral wisdom and cosmological philosophy
- **Daoism**: A source of yin-yang cosmology, naturalistic wisdom, and the understanding of the Dao's operation
- **Buddhism**: Integrated into Chinese Buddhist thought through syncretic philosophical developments
- **Divination**: The living oracular tradition of consulting the hexagrams through yarrow stalks or coins
- **Academic**: Studied for its textual history, archaeological connections, and philosophical contributions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Change | The fundamental principle that all phenomena are in constant transformation |
| Yin-Yang | The complementary forces whose interaction generates all phenomena |
| Hexagrams | The sixty-four symbolic figures representing archetypal situations |
| Divination | The practice of consulting the oracle for guidance in decision-making |
| Wisdom | Practical and philosophical guidance for navigating a world of change |
| Cosmology | The ordered pattern of the universe reflected in the hexagram system |
