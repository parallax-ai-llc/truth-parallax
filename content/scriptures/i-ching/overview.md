## Overview

The I Ching (易經, "Classic of Changes"), also known as the Yijing, is one of the oldest and most influential texts in world literature. Originating in ancient China around the 9th century BCE and receiving its classical form through centuries of commentary, the I Ching serves simultaneously as a divination manual, a philosophical treatise on the nature of change, and a guide to ethical conduct.

## Structure

The I Ching is built around 64 hexagrams -- six-line figures composed of solid (yang) and broken (yin) lines. Each hexagram represents a fundamental situation or dynamic pattern in the cosmos:

- **The Hexagram System**: 64 hexagrams, each composed of two trigrams stacked together
- **The Judgment (Tuan)**: A brief oracular statement attributed to King Wen
- **The Image (Xiang)**: A commentary on the hexagram's natural imagery
- **The Line Texts (Yao)**: Individual line statements attributed to the Duke of Zhou
- **The Ten Wings**: Commentarial appendices, traditionally attributed to Confucius

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The I Ching occupies a unique position across multiple traditions:

- **Confucian**: One of the Five Classics, a foundation of Confucian learning
- **Daoist**: A source text for yin-yang cosmology and the philosophy of change
- **Buddhist/Zen**: Integrated into East Asian Buddhist thought and practice
- **Academic**: A key text for sinology, comparative philosophy, and the history of science

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Change (Yi) | The fundamental principle that all things are in constant transformation |
| Yin-Yang | The complementary polarity underlying all phenomena |
| Tian (Heaven) | The cosmic order and its moral implications |
| De (Virtue) | The character and conduct appropriate to each situation |
| Shi (Timing) | The crucial importance of acting at the right moment |
