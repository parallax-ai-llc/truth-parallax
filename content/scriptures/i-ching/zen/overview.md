## Overview

Zen Buddhist interpretation of the I Ching draws on the long history of Buddhism's encounter with Chinese culture, in which the I Ching's concepts of change, emptiness, and spontaneous response were integrated with Buddhist teachings on impermanence, *sunyata*, and enlightenment. The synthesis of Yijing thought with Chan/Zen Buddhism produced a distinctive contemplative approach to the hexagrams.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Change as Impermanence

The I Ching's fundamental teaching that all things are in constant transformation resonates directly with the Buddhist doctrine of *anicca* (impermanence). Zen interpreters read the hexagrams as 64 illustrations of the universal truth that nothing remains fixed, encouraging non-attachment and present-moment awareness.

### The Empty Center

Zen interpreters note that each hexagram, while composed of specific yin and yang lines, points beyond its particular configuration to the formless ground from which all patterns arise and into which they dissolve. This resonates with the Zen understanding of *sunyata* (emptiness) as the fertile void from which all phenomena emerge.

### Spontaneous Response

The I Ching's emphasis on responding appropriately to each unique situation aligns with the Zen ideal of spontaneous, non-conceptual response (*mushin*, "no-mind"). The sage who moves with the changes, neither clinging nor resisting, embodies the Zen ideal of flow.
