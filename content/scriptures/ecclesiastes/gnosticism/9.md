## The Common Destiny of All

Gnostic interpreters read Ecclesiastes 9 as further evidence of the material world's fundamental deficiency — the honest testimony of a sage trapped within the Demiurge's cosmos, unknowingly pointing toward the need for gnosis.

## Key Themes in Gnostic Reading

### Death

The chapter's engagement with death confirms, in the Gnostic view, that the material world cannot provide ultimate meaning or satisfaction. The divine spark within the human person yearns for something the Demiurge's creation cannot supply.

### Chance and Enjoyment

The interplay of chance and enjoyment reflects the ongoing tension between the Pleroma's order and the Demiurge's chaos. Kohelet experiences this tension without recognizing its cosmic source.

### Wisdom

The Gnostic reading of wisdom points toward the fundamental inadequacy of life within the material cosmos. What orthodox readers call divine mystery, Gnostic readers call the Demiurge's incompetence or malice.

## Gnostic Hermeneutics

| Level | Reading |
|-------|---------|
| Hylic | Surface despair about worldly things |
| Psychic | Moral reflection on life's limitations |
| Pneumatic | Recognition that the material world is a prison requiring gnosis for escape |

## Nag Hammadi Connections

The *Gospel of Truth* and the *Treatise on the Resurrection* address the anguish and confusion of existence before gnosis in terms that echo Kohelet's frustrations. The difference: the Gnostic texts provide the answer (gnosis) that Kohelet, imprisoned within the Demiurge's worldview, cannot fully articulate.

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 9 functions, in Gnostic reading, as the most authentic voice in the Hebrew Bible — a sage honest enough to admit that the Demiurge's world does not work. The book's failure to provide a satisfying answer (from the Gnostic perspective) only heightens the need for gnosis.
