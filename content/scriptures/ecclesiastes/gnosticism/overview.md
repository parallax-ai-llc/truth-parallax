## Overview

Ecclesiastes' radical critique of life "under the sun" resonated powerfully with Gnostic sensibilities. The declaration that "all is vanity" could be read as confirmation of the Gnostic thesis: the material world, created by the Demiurge, is fundamentally flawed and unsatisfying. Kohelet's frustration becomes, in Gnostic reading, the experience of the divine spark trapped in a deficient cosmos.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### World-Denial as Gnosis
Where orthodox readers see Qoheleth as calling for trust in God despite the world's frustrations, Gnostic readers saw validation of their cosmological pessimism. The world *is* vain because it was made by an ignorant or malicious creator.

### The Limit of Earthly Wisdom
Qoheleth's admission that wisdom itself is "vanity and a striving after wind" (1:17) aligned with the Gnostic distinction between worldly knowledge (mere *sophia*) and salvific *gnosis* — direct experiential knowledge of the divine.

### Liberation Through Knowledge
The book's injunction to "fear God" at its conclusion is reinterpreted: the true God, the transcendent One beyond the Demiurge, is known not through fear but through *gnosis*.

## Nag Hammadi Connections

- **The Gospel of Truth**: Describes the anguish and confusion of existence before gnosis
- **The Treatise on the Resurrection**: The world's impermanence points to a higher, eternal reality
- **Authoritative Teaching**: The soul's entrapment in the material world mirrors Kohelet's frustration

## Theological Significance

| Ecclesiastes Theme | Gnostic Reading |
|-------------------|----------------|
| "All is vanity" | The material world is fundamentally deficient |
| "Nothing new under the sun" | The Demiurge's creation is repetitive and sterile |
| Injustice and oppression (4:1) | The archons' misrule over the material realm |
| "Fear God" (12:13) | Know the true God beyond the Demiurge |
| Death | Liberation of the divine spark from the material prison |
