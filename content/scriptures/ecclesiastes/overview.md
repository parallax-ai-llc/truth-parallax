## Overview

Ecclesiastes (*Kohelet* in Hebrew, meaning "the Preacher" or "the Assembler") is one of the most philosophically provocative books in the Hebrew Bible. Traditionally attributed to King Solomon, the book investigates the meaning of life through the lens of human experience, concluding that all earthly pursuits are *hebel* — a Hebrew word variously translated as "vanity," "breath," or "vapor."

## Structure

The book follows a loosely structured meditation:

1. **Chapter 1**: The thesis — "vanity of vanities, all is vanity"
2. **Chapters 2–6**: Investigations into pleasure, work, wealth, and wisdom
3. **Chapters 7–11**: Practical wisdom amid life's uncertainties
4. **Chapter 12**: The famous poem on aging and death, with the final exhortation to "fear God and keep his commandments"

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Ecclesiastes provokes distinctive responses across religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: Read during Sukkot; Kohelet's tension is resolved by the final verse's call to fear God and keep commandments
- **Christianity**: Interpreted as demonstrating the insufficiency of life apart from God; a precursor to the gospel
- **Islam**: Its themes of worldly transience (*dunya*) deeply resonate with Quranic teaching
- **Gnosticism**: The world's vanity confirms the Gnostic critique of the material cosmos

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Hebel (Vanity) | The transience and ultimate futility of earthly pursuits |
| Time | "A time for every purpose under heaven" (3:1-8) |
| Death | The great equalizer that renders all distinctions meaningless |
| Joy | The enjoyment of life's simple gifts as God's portion for humanity |
| Fear of God | The conclusion that grounds all wisdom in reverence |
