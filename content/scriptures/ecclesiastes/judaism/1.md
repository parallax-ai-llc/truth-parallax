## Havel Havalim

The opening chapter of Kohelet establishes the book's central concept: *havel havalim, hakol havel* — "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" (1:2). Jewish tradition has wrestled with this statement since antiquity, with the Talmud recording that the sages debated whether to include the book in the canon at all.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Vanity of vanities" (1:2)

The Talmud (Shabbat 30b) records that the sages initially sought to suppress (*lignoz*) Kohelet because its words appeared to contradict themselves and to contradict Torah. They ultimately preserved it because "its beginning is words of Torah and its end is words of Torah" — referencing the conclusion in 12:13.

Rashi interprets *havel* as referring to the things in which people toil fruitlessly, not to God's creation itself.

### "What profit has a man from all his toil?" (1:3)

The Midrash (Kohelet Rabbah 1:3) qualifies this: "Under the sun he has no profit, but he does have profit before the sun" — meaning that Torah study and good deeds, which transcend the earthly realm, do yield lasting reward.

### "There is nothing new under the sun" (1:9)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 110a) takes this as a general observation about natural phenomena but notes exceptions in the spiritual realm: repentance (*teshuvah*) is genuinely new, and the Torah's wisdom is inexhaustible.

| Rabbinic Source | Resolution |
|----------------|-----------|
| Shabbat 30b | Book preserved because it begins and ends with Torah |
| Kohelet Rabbah 1:3 | Profit exists "above the sun" — in Torah and mitzvot |
| Midrash | *Havel* refers to fruitless human pursuits, not creation |

## Theological Significance

Jewish interpretation resolves Kohelet's apparent nihilism by insisting that the "vanity" applies to life lived without Torah, not to existence itself. The book's challenge is not to meaning but to false sources of meaning.
