## Oppression and Companionship

Jewish interpretation of Ecclesiastes 4 draws on Talmud, Midrash, and the medieval commentators to read Kohelet's reflections as compatible with — indeed, enriched by — the Torah's worldview.

## Key Themes

### Oppression

Rashi reads the theme of oppression in relation to Torah study and *mitzvot*: Kohelet's frustrations apply to pursuits disconnected from God's commandments, not to the life of Torah observance.

### Envy and Companionship

The Midrash (Kohelet Rabbah) expands on these themes through *mashalim* (parables) and historical illustrations, often connecting Kohelet's observations to moments in Israel's history.

### Loneliness

The Vilna Gaon's commentary finds mystical significance in Kohelet's treatment of loneliness, connecting it to the soul's journey and the divine plan hidden beneath the surface of events.

## Connection to Sukkot

Kohelet is read during Sukkot, the festival of booths. The temporary *sukkah* embodies Kohelet's message: life is impermanent (*havel*), yet joy is commanded. The festival's combination of fragility and celebration mirrors the book's paradox.

## Theological Significance

Jewish tradition resolves Kohelet's tensions not by denying them but by framing them within Torah: life is indeed brief and mysterious, but Torah, *mitzvot*, and the fear of God provide genuine and enduring meaning.
