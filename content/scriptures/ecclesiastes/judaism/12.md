## Zekhor et Borrekha: Remember Your Creator

Ecclesiastes 12 concludes the book with one of the most powerful poems in the Hebrew Bible — the allegory of aging (12:1-7) — and the epilogue that redeems the entire book for the canon (12:13-14).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Remember your Creator in the days of your youth" (12:1)

The Talmud (Shabbat 151b-152a) interprets the allegory of aging verse by verse:

| Image | Talmudic Interpretation |
|-------|----------------------|
| "Before the evil days come" | Old age |
| "The keepers of the house tremble" | The ribs and sides |
| "The grinders cease" | The teeth |
| "Those looking through windows are dimmed" | The eyes |
| "The doors are shut" | The bodily orifices |
| "The almond tree blossoms" | The white hair |
| "The silver cord is snapped" | The spinal column |

### "The dust returns to the earth" (12:7)

This echoes Genesis 3:19 and grounds the Jewish understanding of death in the creation narrative. The spirit (*ruach*) returns to God — a foundational text for the Jewish belief in the soul's immortality (*hasharat hanefesh*).

### "Fear God and keep his commandments" (12:13)

This verse is the canonical key to the entire book. The Talmud (Shabbat 30b) says: "The end of the matter, all having been heard: this is why the sages did not suppress Kohelet." The Midrash adds: "The whole world was created only for this purpose."

### "For God will bring every deed into judgment" (12:14)

This verse grounds Jewish eschatology: every deed is accountable before God. The Mishnah (Avot 2:1) echoes: "Know what is above you — an eye that sees, an ear that hears, and all your deeds are written in a book."

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 12 resolves the book's tensions by pointing to Torah, *mitzvot*, and ultimate accountability before God. Jewish tradition reads this not as a grim warning but as a liberating truth: life has meaning because it is measured by the Eternal.
