## Overview

In Judaism, Ecclesiastes (*Kohelet*) is one of the five *megillot* (scrolls) and is read publicly during the festival of Sukkot. Attributed to King Solomon in his old age, Kohelet's apparent skepticism generated rabbinic debate about its inclusion in the canon. The Talmud records that the sages sought to suppress it until they reconciled its teachings with Torah (Shabbat 30b).

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Rabbinic Resolution
The rabbis resolved Kohelet's tensions by emphasizing its conclusion: "The end of the matter: fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole of humanity" (12:13). This verse redeems the book's apparent heterodoxy.

### Midrashic
Kohelet Rabbah (Midrash on Ecclesiastes) provides verse-by-verse rabbinic commentary, often reading "vanity" as referring to the vanity of neglecting Torah.

### Philosophical
Medieval Jewish philosophers — Ralbag (Gersonides) and others — read Kohelet as a philosophical investigation into the highest good, concluding with the intellectual love of God.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hebel**: Breath, vapor, transience — not nihilism but impermanence
- **Simchah**: Joy as a legitimate response to God's gifts
- **Sukkot Connection**: The temporary booth symbolizes life's impermanence, matching Kohelet's themes
- **Torah as Answer**: Whatever the world's vanity, Torah provides meaning

## Liturgical Context

Kohelet is read on Shabbat during the intermediate days of Sukkot (Chol HaMoed Sukkot), connecting the festival's theme of impermanence with the book's message.
