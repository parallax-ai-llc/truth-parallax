## Remember Your Creator

Ecclesiastes 12 brings the book to its climactic conclusion with the famous allegory of aging and death (12:1-7) and the epilogue's final exhortation (12:13-14).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Remember your Creator in the days of your youth" (12:1)

Protestant interpreters see this as the book's pastoral heart: the time to turn to God is now, before the inevitable decline of old age renders it more difficult. The urgency of conversion is a classic Reformation theme.

### The Allegory of Aging (12:2-7)

The poetic imagery is generally read as an extended metaphor for bodily decline:

| Image | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| Darkened sun and moon | Fading eyesight or joy |
| Trembling keepers | Arms and hands |
| Grinders ceasing | Teeth |
| Windows dimmed | Eyes |
| Silver cord broken | Death itself |

### "The dust returns to the earth" (12:7)

This verse echoes Genesis 3:19 and grounds the book's theology in the creation narrative. The spirit returns to God who gave it — a statement Protestant interpreters connect to the hope of resurrection.

### "Fear God and keep his commandments" (12:13)

The epilogue provides the canonical framework for reading the entire book. Whatever the frustrations of life *under the sun*, the proper human response is reverent obedience to God. Protestant theology reads this as pointing toward Christ, in whom the fear of God and the keeping of commandments find their fulfillment.

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 12 resolves the book's tensions not by answering every question but by redirecting the reader toward God. The Protestant emphasis on *soli Deo gloria* finds profound expression in this conclusion: life's meaning is found not in human achievement but in the fear of God.
