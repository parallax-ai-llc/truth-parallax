## Overview

Ecclesiastes (*Qoheleth*, "the Preacher") has fascinated and troubled Protestant interpreters since the Reformation. Luther valued the book for its realistic assessment of life "under the sun" and its call to enjoy God's gifts with gratitude. The tension between the book's apparent skepticism and its concluding exhortation to "fear God and keep his commandments" (12:13) has generated diverse readings.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Canonical-Theological
Many Protestant interpreters read Ecclesiastes within the canon's larger framework: the "vanity" experienced under the sun points the reader toward the gospel's answer in Christ.

### Wisdom Theology
The book demonstrates the limits of human wisdom apart from revelation, showing that natural reason alone leads to frustration — thus preparing the ground for grace.

### Existential-Pastoral
Modern Protestant readings often emphasize Ecclesiastes' honesty about suffering, doubt, and the human condition, making it a resource for pastoral care.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hebel (Vanity)**: The transience and frustration of earthly pursuits
- **Enjoyment**: God's gift of simple pleasures as genuine goods
- **Sovereignty**: God's inscrutable purposes behind life's apparent injustices
- **Death**: The great equalizer that relativizes all human achievement
