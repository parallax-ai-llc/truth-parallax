## Vanity of Vanities

Ecclesiastes opens with the most famous declaration in wisdom literature: *Havel havalim, hakol havel* — "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" (1:2). Protestant interpretation grapples with this radical statement, reading it within the canonical context of God's sovereignty and the hope of the gospel.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Vanity of vanities" (1:2)

The Hebrew *hebel* literally means "breath" or "vapor." Protestant interpreters debate its precise nuance:

| Translation | Nuance | Proponents |
|------------|--------|-----------|
| Vanity | Worthlessness | KJV tradition |
| Meaningless | Absurdity | NIV |
| Fleeting | Transience | Some modern evangelicals |
| Enigmatic | Mystery | Bartholomew, Longman |

Luther read *hebel* as a critique of human achievement apart from God — not that creation itself is worthless, but that human striving for permanence is futile.

### "What does man gain by all the toil?" (1:3)

The Hebrew *yitron* (profit, advantage) is a commercial term. Qoheleth asks whether life yields any net gain — and finds that the cycles of nature (1:4-7) suggest an answer: the world turns, but nothing advances.

### "There is nothing new under the sun" (1:9)

This famous observation is read by Protestant theologians not as cosmic nihilism but as a statement about life *under the sun* — life viewed from a purely horizontal, earthly perspective. The vertical dimension (God's eternal purposes) is what gives meaning.

## Theological Significance

Protestant theology reads Ecclesiastes 1 as establishing the problem to which the gospel is the answer: apart from God's redemptive purposes, human life is indeed *hebel*. Calvin saw this chapter as driving the reader toward humility before God.
