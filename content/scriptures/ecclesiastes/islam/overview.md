## Overview

Islam does not include Ecclesiastes in its scriptural canon, but the book's themes resonate deeply with Islamic teachings on the transience of worldly life (*dunya*) and the priority of the hereafter (*akhirah*). Solomon (*Sulayman*) is honored as a prophet and king, and the Quranic portrayal of his wisdom and eventual humility complements the themes traditionally associated with Ecclesiastes.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran extensively addresses the fleeting nature of worldly life:

- **Quran 57:20**: "Know that the life of this world is but amusement and diversion and adornment and boasting to one another..."
- **Quran 3:185**: "Every soul will taste death, and you will only be given your full compensation on the Day of Resurrection."
- **Quran 18:45-46**: "Wealth and children are the adornment of the worldly life, but enduring good deeds are better..."

## Key Themes Shared with Islamic Tradition

- **Transience of Worldly Life (*dunya*)**: The world is temporary; only deeds for God endure
- **Zuhd (Asceticism)**: Islamic ascetic tradition mirrors Kohelet's critique of wealth and pleasure
- **Wisdom of Solomon**: Sulayman's divinely granted wisdom is affirmed
- **Death as Equalizer**: A universal theme in Islamic eschatology

## Islamic Reception

| Aspect | Islamic View |
|--------|-------------|
| Solomonic authorship | Plausible; Sulayman was granted *hikmah* |
| "All is vanity" | Compatible with the Quranic view of *dunya* |
| Enjoyment of life | Permitted within Islamic bounds — *halal tayyib* |
| Fear of God | Central to Islam — *taqwa* |
