## Cast Your Bread Upon the Waters

Islamic engagement with Ecclesiastes 11 finds significant thematic parallels between Qoheleth's reflections and the Quranic teaching on the transience of worldly life and the priority of the hereafter.

## Quranic and Hadith Parallels

### Generosity

The Quran addresses themes of generosity in the context of distinguishing between *dunya* (worldly life) and *akhirah* (the hereafter). What Kohelet discovers through observation, the Quran reveals through divine speech.

### Risk and Youth

Islamic moral theology (*akhlaq*) and the *zuhd* (ascetic) tradition engage extensively with risk and youth, themes that Kohelet explores through his characteristic method of experiential investigation.

| Ecclesiastes Theme | Islamic Parallel |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Generosity | Quranic teaching on *dunya* |
| Risk | Islamic *zuhd* (asceticism) tradition |
| Youth | Prophetic example and hadith |
| Joy | Islamic eschatology and divine decree |

## The Wisdom of Sulayman

Solomon (*Sulayman*) is honored in Islam as a prophet who, despite being given extraordinary worldly power and wealth, understood the ultimate primacy of God's will. The reflections attributed to him in Ecclesiastes, insofar as they point to *taqwa* and detachment from *dunya*, are compatible with his prophetic mission.

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 11 illustrates, from an Islamic perspective, the universal human experience of confronting worldly transience. The Quran provides the definitive resolution: meaning is found not "under the sun" but in the worship of Allah and preparation for the hereafter.
