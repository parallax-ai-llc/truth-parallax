## The Transience of Worldly Life

Ecclesiastes 1's declaration that "all is vanity" resonates powerfully with the Quranic theme of *dunya* — the lower, transient worldly life that distracts from the eternal *akhirah* (hereafter).

## Quranic Parallels

### "Vanity of vanities" (1:2)

The Quran repeatedly warns against attachment to worldly life:

- **Quran 57:20**: "Know that the life of this world is but amusement and diversion and adornment and boasting to one another and competition in increase of wealth and children — like the example of a rain whose resulting plant growth pleases the tillers; then it dries and you see it turned yellow; then it becomes scattered debris."
- **Quran 3:14**: "Beautified for people is the love of that which they desire..."

### "What profit has a man?" (1:3)

The Quran answers: true profit is found in deeds for the hereafter. "Whoever desires the harvest of the Hereafter — We increase for him in his harvest. And whoever desires the harvest of this world — We give him thereof, but there is not for him in the Hereafter any share" (Quran 42:20).

### "Nothing new under the sun" (1:9)

Islamic theology affirms that the created world follows *sunnah* (divinely established patterns) while remaining open to God's *qadr* (decree). The regularity of nature reflects God's wisdom, not cosmic futility.

| Ecclesiastes 1 Theme | Quranic Teaching |
|---------------------|-----------------|
| "All is vanity" | *Dunya* is transient (57:20) |
| "No profit" under the sun | True profit in the hereafter (42:20) |
| Cycles of nature | God's *sunnah* in creation |
| Weariness | This world is a test (*ibtila'*) |

## Theological Significance

Islam shares Kohelet's assessment of worldly transience but frames it within a clear eschatological hope: the hereafter provides the meaning and reward that earthly life cannot offer. *Zuhd* (ascetic detachment) is valued precisely because *dunya* is *havel*.
