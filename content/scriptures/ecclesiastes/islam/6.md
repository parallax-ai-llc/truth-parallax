## The Frustration of Desire

Islamic engagement with Ecclesiastes 6 finds significant thematic parallels between Qoheleth's reflections and the Quranic teaching on the transience of worldly life and the priority of the hereafter.

## Quranic and Hadith Parallels

### Desire

The Quran addresses themes of desire in the context of distinguishing between *dunya* (worldly life) and *akhirah* (the hereafter). What Kohelet discovers through observation, the Quran reveals through divine speech.

### Dissatisfaction and Mortality

Islamic moral theology (*akhlaq*) and the *zuhd* (ascetic) tradition engage extensively with dissatisfaction and mortality, themes that Kohelet explores through his characteristic method of experiential investigation.

| Ecclesiastes Theme | Islamic Parallel |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Desire | Quranic teaching on *dunya* |
| Dissatisfaction | Islamic *zuhd* (asceticism) tradition |
| Mortality | Prophetic example and hadith |
| Portion | Islamic eschatology and divine decree |

## The Wisdom of Sulayman

Solomon (*Sulayman*) is honored in Islam as a prophet who, despite being given extraordinary worldly power and wealth, understood the ultimate primacy of God's will. The reflections attributed to him in Ecclesiastes, insofar as they point to *taqwa* and detachment from *dunya*, are compatible with his prophetic mission.

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 6 illustrates, from an Islamic perspective, the universal human experience of confronting worldly transience. The Quran provides the definitive resolution: meaning is found not "under the sun" but in the worship of Allah and preparation for the hereafter.
