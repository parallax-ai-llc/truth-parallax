## Wisdom and Folly in Practice

The Orthodox Church reads Ecclesiastes 10 through the patristic tradition, particularly St. Gregory of Nyssa's homilies, which interpret Qoheleth's reflections as stages in the soul's journey from attachment to worldly things toward union with God.

## Key Themes

### Folly

St. Gregory of Nyssa reads themes of folly in Ecclesiastes as part of the soul's purgative stage — the recognition of the world's insufficiency that prepares for illumination and union.

### Wisdom and Rulers

The Orthodox ascetic tradition, informed by the *Philokalia*, addresses wisdom and rulers as matters of *nepsis* (watchfulness). The monk or layperson who cultivates inner attentiveness can navigate these realities with wisdom.

### Speech

The theme of speech resonates with the Orthodox theology of divine mystery (*apophasis*). God's ways transcend human understanding, and faith means trusting in His providence even when His purposes are hidden.

## Liturgical Context

The themes of Ecclesiastes pervade Orthodox funeral and memorial services. The refrain "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" appears in the *panikhida* (memorial service), reminding the faithful of mortality as an invitation to eternal life.

## Theological Significance

For the Orthodox, Ecclesiastes 10 is not a book of despair but of purification. The world's vanity, honestly acknowledged, clears the ground for the soul's encounter with the Living God — who alone is eternal, unchanging, and infinitely satisfying.
