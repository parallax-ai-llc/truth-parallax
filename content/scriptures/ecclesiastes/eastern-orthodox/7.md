## Wisdom and Folly Compared

The Orthodox Church reads Ecclesiastes 7 through the patristic tradition, particularly St. Gregory of Nyssa's homilies, which interpret Qoheleth's reflections as stages in the soul's journey from attachment to worldly things toward union with God.

## Key Themes

### Wisdom

St. Gregory of Nyssa reads themes of wisdom in Ecclesiastes as part of the soul's purgative stage — the recognition of the world's insufficiency that prepares for illumination and union.

### Sorrow and Patience

The Orthodox ascetic tradition, informed by the *Philokalia*, addresses sorrow and patience as matters of *nepsis* (watchfulness). The monk or layperson who cultivates inner attentiveness can navigate these realities with wisdom.

### Moderation

The theme of moderation resonates with the Orthodox theology of divine mystery (*apophasis*). God's ways transcend human understanding, and faith means trusting in His providence even when His purposes are hidden.

## Liturgical Context

The themes of Ecclesiastes pervade Orthodox funeral and memorial services. The refrain "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" appears in the *panikhida* (memorial service), reminding the faithful of mortality as an invitation to eternal life.

## Theological Significance

For the Orthodox, Ecclesiastes 7 is not a book of despair but of purification. The world's vanity, honestly acknowledged, clears the ground for the soul's encounter with the Living God — who alone is eternal, unchanging, and infinitely satisfying.
