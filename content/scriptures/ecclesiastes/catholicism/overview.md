## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Ecclesiastes has been shaped by the *contemptus mundi* (contempt of the world) tradition, patristic allegorization, and the Thomistic integration of natural and revealed wisdom. The book's declaration that "all is vanity" was read by medieval monastics as an invitation to seek the eternal rather than the temporal.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic-Monastic
St. Jerome's commentary and St. Gregory of Nyssa's homilies on Ecclesiastes set the standard: the world's vanity redirects the soul toward God. The *contemptus mundi* tradition drew heavily on Qoheleth.

### Thomistic Natural Theology
Aquinas read Ecclesiastes as demonstrating that no finite good can satisfy the human heart — an argument that parallels the *desiderium naturale* for the beatific vision.

### Modern Catholic Scholarship
Contemporary Catholic scholars like Roland Murphy approach the text with historical-critical methods while affirming its canonical authority and spiritual depth.

## Theological Emphases

- **Vanitas**: All created goods are insufficient apart from God
- **Memento Mori**: The contemplation of death as a spiritual discipline
- **Joy in God's Gifts**: The Catholic tradition balances world-denial with sacramental appreciation
- **Eschatological Hope**: The final judgment (12:14) points beyond earthly vanity
