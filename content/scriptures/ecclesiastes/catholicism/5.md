## Reverence Before God

Catholic interpretation of Ecclesiastes 5 reads Qoheleth's reflections through the lens of natural theology, the *contemptus mundi* tradition, and the Magisterium's teaching on the relationship between earthly goods and eternal life.

## Key Themes

### Vows

The chapter's treatment of vows aligns with the Catholic understanding that created goods, while genuinely valuable, cannot satisfy the soul's deepest longing for God. Aquinas taught that only the beatific vision — the direct vision of God — can fulfill the *desiderium naturale*.

### Reverence and Wealth

The interplay of reverence and wealth in this chapter reflects the Catholic moral tradition's nuanced assessment of earthly goods: they are to be used (*uti*) for the sake of God, not enjoyed (*frui*) as ultimate ends in themselves (Augustine, *De Doctrina Christiana*).

### Sleep

The theme of sleep connects to the Catholic understanding of divine providence: God permits suffering and injustice within His mysterious plan, which faith trusts will be revealed in its fullness at the last judgment.

## Patristic and Magisterial Commentary

| Source | Teaching |
|--------|---------|

| CCC | The human heart is made for God |

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 5 contributes to the Catholic conviction that honest reckoning with the world's limitations is the beginning of spiritual wisdom. The *contemptus mundi* tradition, properly understood, is not hatred of creation but freedom from idolatry.
