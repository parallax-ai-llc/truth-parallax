## Remember Your Creator

Ecclesiastes 12 delivers the book's climactic message: "Remember your Creator in the days of your youth" (12:1). Catholic interpretation reads this as an urgent call to conversion and a meditation on the *last things* — death, judgment, heaven, and hell.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Allegory of Aging (12:1-7)

Catholic spiritual tradition has used this passage extensively in the practice of *memento mori* — the contemplation of death as a spur to holy living. St. Ignatius of Loyola's Spiritual Exercises include meditation on death as a decisive moment of encounter with God.

### "The dust returns to the earth" (12:7)

This echoes the Ash Wednesday liturgy: "Remember that you are dust, and to dust you shall return." Catholic theology affirms that death is not the end: the spirit "returns to God who gave it," and the body awaits the resurrection.

### "Fear God and keep his commandments" (12:13)

The epilogue provides the canonical key to the entire book. The Catechism connects the fear of God with the gift of the Holy Spirit (CCC 1831) and the keeping of commandments with the life of charity (CCC 2052-2055).

### "For God will bring every deed into judgment" (12:14)

Catholic eschatology teaches both particular judgment (at death) and general judgment (at the end of time). This verse grounds the Catholic moral life in accountability before God.

| Doctrine | Connection |
|---------|-----------|
| Memento mori | 12:1-7 — aging and death |
| Resurrection | 12:7 — the spirit returns to God |
| Moral life | 12:13 — fear God, keep commandments |
| Judgment | 12:14 — every deed judged |

## Theological Significance

Ecclesiastes 12 brings the book to its resolution: whatever the world's vanity, the human person is destined for God. Catholic theology reads this conclusion as fully realized in Christ, who conquers death and brings eternal life.
