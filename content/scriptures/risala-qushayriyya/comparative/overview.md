## Overview

Comparative scholars have found in the Risala al-Qushayriyya a systematic account of mystical experience that invites productive comparison with other contemplative traditions. Qushayri's distinction between stations (maqamat) and states (ahwal) parallels similar distinctions in Christian mystical theology (active/passive purification), Buddhist meditation theory (samatha/vipassana), and Hindu yoga (abhyasa/vairagya).

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative readers explore structural parallels between the Risala's map of the spiritual journey and analogous maps in other traditions. The sequence from repentance through ascetic discipline to love and gnosis mirrors the classic threefold path of purgation, illumination, and union in Christian mysticism. The distinction between acquired stations and gifted states parallels debates in multiple traditions about the relationship between effort and grace.

## Key Comparisons

The Risala's biographical method — presenting the lives and sayings of spiritual masters as exemplars for practitioners — parallels the Desert Fathers' Apophthegmata, the Zen koan collections, and the Hindu guru parampara. Its systematic terminology invites comparison with the Abhidharma's classifications of mental states and with Evagrius Ponticus's catalogues of thoughts (logismoi). These parallels suggest that the human encounter with the transcendent generates remarkably similar experiential structures across cultures.
