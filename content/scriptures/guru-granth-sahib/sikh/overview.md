## Overview

For the Sikh community, the Guru Granth Sahib is not merely a scripture but the living, eternal Guru — the spiritual sovereign of the Khalsa Panth. When Guru Gobind Singh, the tenth human Guru, declared in 1708 that the Granth Sahib would henceforth serve as the Guru of the Sikhs, he established a unique relationship between a religious community and its scripture. The Guru Granth Sahib is treated with the same reverence accorded to a living person: it is ceremonially installed each morning, attended by a *granthi* (reader), fanned with a *chaur sahib*, and respectfully put to rest each evening.

## Liturgical Life

The Guru Granth Sahib structures every aspect of Sikh devotional practice. *Kirtan* (devotional singing of its hymns) is the central act of Sikh worship, performed in every Gurdwara worldwide. The daily prayers (*Nitnem*) are drawn from its pages — Japji Sahib in the morning, Rehras Sahib in the evening, and Kirtan Sohila at bedtime. All Sikh ceremonies — naming, initiation (*Amrit Sanskar*), marriage (*Anand Karaj*), and death — revolve around the Guru Granth Sahib.

## Theological Authority

The Guru Granth Sahib serves as the ultimate authority on all matters of Sikh theology, ethics, and practice. Its message of *Ik Onkar* (One God), *naam simran* (meditation on the Divine Name), and *seva* (selfless service) provides the foundation for Sikh life. The *Hukamnama* — a randomly selected passage read each day — is received as the Guru's direct guidance for the community.
