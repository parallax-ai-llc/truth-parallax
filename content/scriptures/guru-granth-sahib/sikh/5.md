## Sikh Reading

The Sikh tradition receives The Concluding Hymns — Mundavani and Raagmala as the sacred word of the Guru, to be approached with reverence, devotion, and the intention to transform one's life. Within Gurdwara practice, the 15 verses of this section are recited and sung in *kirtan* (devotional music), their melodies prescribed by the raag system that structures the Guru Granth Sahib. For the Sikh devotee, hearing and internalizing these verses is an act of communion with the living Guru, a means of receiving *hukam* (divine command) and cultivating *naam* (awareness of the Divine Name).

The theological teachings embedded in this section reinforce the core Sikh principles of *Ik Onkar* (the oneness of God), *naam simran* (meditation on the Name), *kirat karni* (honest living), *vand chakna* (sharing with others), and *seva* (selfless service). The Sikh community (*sangat*) gathers to hear these words as a collective spiritual practice, understanding that the Guru's wisdom is fully accessible only within the context of community, humility, and love.

In the daily life of observant Sikhs, passages from this section may form part of the *Nitnem* (daily prayers) or be selected as the day's *Hukamnama* (daily reading), providing specific guidance for the challenges and opportunities of each day. The tradition holds that no reading of the Guru Granth Sahib returns empty — each encounter with the Word transforms the listener.
