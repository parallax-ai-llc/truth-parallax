## Overview

The Guru Granth Sahib (Punjabi: ਗੁਰੂ ਗ੍ਰੰਥ ਸਾਹਿਬ) is the central religious scripture of Sikhism, accorded the status of the eternal, living Guru of the Sikh faith. Compiled initially by Guru Arjan Dev (the fifth Sikh Guru) in 1604 CE and finalized by Guru Gobind Singh (the tenth Guru) in 1708 CE, it is unique among world scriptures in containing the compositions of multiple authors from diverse religious backgrounds, including Hindu and Muslim saints alongside the Sikh Gurus.

## Structure

The Guru Granth Sahib comprises 1,430 pages (*angs*, literally "limbs") organized primarily by musical mode (*raag*). The major structural divisions include:

1. **Japji Sahib** (pp. 1–8): The opening composition by Guru Nanak, recited as the morning prayer
2. **Raags** (pp. 14–1353): The main body, organized into 31 musical modes, containing compositions by six Sikh Gurus and fifteen *bhagats* (saints)
3. **Bhagat Bani** (distributed throughout): Hymns of saints including Kabir, Namdev, Ravidas, Sheikh Farid, and others
4. **Vars** (distributed throughout): Ballads of praise set to folk melodies
5. **Concluding Hymns** (pp. 1353–1430): Including the Mundavani (seal) and Raagmala (index of raags)

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Guru Granth Sahib holds distinct significance across multiple interpretive traditions:

- **Sikh**: Revered as the eternal living Guru, treated with the highest ceremonial respect in every Gurdwara
- **Academic**: Studied as a remarkable example of interfaith compilation, multilingual poetry, and musical theology
- **Comparative**: Examined alongside other scriptures for its unique theology of *Ik Onkar* (One God) and its inclusivity
- **Sufi**: Read for the contributions of Sheikh Farid and resonances with Sufi concepts of divine love and *fana*
- **Bhakti**: Appreciated as a culmination of the North Indian devotional movement, containing the poetry of Kabir, Namdev, and Ravidas
- **Interfaith**: Valued as a model of interreligious harmony, incorporating voices from multiple faith traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Ik Onkar | The oneness of God — the foundational theological statement |
| Naam Simran | Meditation on the Divine Name as the path to liberation |
| Seva | Selfless service to humanity as worship |
| Sangat and Pangat | Community gathering and communal dining as expressions of equality |
| Hukam | Divine order and the acceptance of God's will |
| Haumai | Ego as the fundamental barrier to spiritual realization |
| Mukti | Liberation from the cycle of birth and death through divine grace |
