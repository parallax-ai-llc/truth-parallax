## Bhakti Reading

From the Bhakti perspective, The Concluding Hymns — Mundavani and Raagmala with its 15 verses represents the flowering of the North Indian devotional movement's highest spiritual aspirations. The *nirgun bhakti* tradition — devotion to the formless, attributeless divine — finds its most comprehensive scriptural expression in the Guru Granth Sahib, which gathers the voices of saints from across centuries, regions, castes, and religious communities into a single devotional anthology united by the conviction that God is accessible through love, not ritual.

The Bhakti reading of this section emphasizes the experiential and transformative character of devotion. The saints whose compositions appear in the Guru Granth Sahib speak from direct encounter with the divine, not from scholarly learning or priestly authority. Kabir's paradoxes, Namdev's ecstasies, Ravidas's quiet confidence in divine grace — all testify to a mode of knowing God that transcends doctrinal formulation and institutional mediation. This emphasis on personal experience as the ground of spiritual authority is the hallmark of the Bhakti tradition.

The social dimension of Bhakti devotion is equally prominent in this section. The movement's radical rejection of caste hierarchy, gender discrimination, and religious exclusivism is preserved and amplified in the Guru Granth Sahib's compilation, which places the words of low-caste saints alongside those of Gurus and kings, affirming that spiritual realization knows no social boundary.
