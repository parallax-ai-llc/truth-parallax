## Overview

The Bhakti tradition finds extraordinary representation in the Guru Granth Sahib, which includes the compositions of fifteen *bhagats* (saints) from the North Indian devotional movement spanning the 12th to 16th centuries. Among the most prominent are Kabir (541 hymns), Namdev (60 hymns), Ravidas (41 hymns), and Sheikh Farid (134 shaloks). The Guru Granth Sahib thus serves as one of the most important repositories of medieval Bhakti poetry, preserving compositions that might otherwise have been lost or significantly altered in transmission.

## Nirgun Bhakti Theology

The Bhakti saints represented in the Guru Granth Sahib predominantly belong to the *nirgun* (formless) stream of devotion, which emphasizes worship of God beyond all names, forms, and attributes — a theology closely aligned with the Sikh Gurus' own teaching. Kabir's fierce rejection of ritual formalism, Namdev's ecstatic devotion to the Name, and Ravidas's affirmation of divine accessibility regardless of caste all contribute to the Guru Granth Sahib's distinctive theological vision of a God who transcends religious boundaries.

## Social Revolution

The Bhakti movement was profoundly egalitarian, and the Guru Granth Sahib preserves this radical social vision. The inclusion of compositions by saints from traditionally marginalized communities — Ravidas the leather-worker, Kabir the weaver, Sain the barber — embodies the conviction that spiritual realization is available to all, regardless of birth, caste, or social status. This Bhakti inheritance continues to shape Sikh social ethics and the institution of *langar* (communal kitchen).
