## Overview

The Guru Granth Sahib occupies a unique position in comparative religious studies as a scripture that deliberately incorporates the voices of multiple religious traditions. Its inclusion of compositions by Hindu bhakti saints (Kabir, Namdev, Ravidas) and the Muslim Sufi Sheikh Farid alongside the Sikh Gurus makes it an unparalleled document of interreligious literary and theological collaboration. No other major world scripture was compiled with such explicit intention to transcend the boundaries of a single religious community.

## Comparative Theology

The Guru Granth Sahib's central theological affirmation — *Ik Onkar*, "One God" — invites comparison with monotheistic declarations across traditions: the Jewish *Shema*, the Islamic *Shahada*, and the Christian affirmation of one God. Yet the Granth Sahib's conception of the divine resists easy categorization, combining elements of personal theism (God as loving, accessible, responsive to prayer) with monistic mysticism (God as the all-pervading reality, *nirankar*, formless). This theological complexity makes it a rich resource for comparative theological inquiry.

## Social Ethics

Comparative scholars have noted the Guru Granth Sahib's radical egalitarianism — its rejection of caste, gender discrimination, and religious exclusivism — as distinctive among pre-modern religious texts. The inclusion of compositions by low-caste saints such as Ravidas (a leather-worker) and Kabir (a weaver) embodies a social vision that invites comparison with liberation theology, prophetic social criticism, and modern human rights discourse.
