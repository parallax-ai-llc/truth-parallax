## Overview

The Sufi tradition's relationship with the Guru Granth Sahib is mediated primarily through the inclusion of 134 verses (*shaloks*) attributed to Sheikh Farid (Baba Farid Ganj-i-Shakar, 1173–1266 CE), one of the most revered Sufi saints of the Chishti order. The presence of a Muslim mystic's poetry within a Sikh scripture testifies to the profound spiritual exchanges that characterized the religious landscape of medieval Punjab, where Sufi khanqahs and Sikh dharamsals often existed in proximity and mutual respect.

## Sheikh Farid's Poetry

Baba Farid's verses in the Guru Granth Sahib express the quintessential Sufi themes of divine love (*ishq*), the pain of separation from the Beloved, the transience of worldly life, and the urgency of spiritual awakening. His poetry employs the language and imagery familiar to the broader Sufi literary tradition — the lover and Beloved, the wilderness of the soul, the burning of ego — while speaking in the vernacular idiom of Punjab. Sufi readers recognize in these verses the authentic voice of a realized mystic.

## Cross-Traditional Resonance

The inclusion of Farid's poetry within the Guru Granth Sahib demonstrates the permeability of religious boundaries in medieval South Asian mysticism. Sufi scholars note that Farid's message of complete surrender to the divine will (*tawakkul*), the annihilation of the ego (*fana*), and the primacy of inner spiritual experience over external ritual aligns with core Sufi principles while also resonating with the Sikh emphasis on *naam* and the overcoming of *haumai* (ego).
