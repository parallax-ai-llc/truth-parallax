## Sufi Reading

A Sufi reading of Raags — The Musical Compositions discovers within its 30 verses echoes of the mystical themes central to the Sufi tradition: the longing of the soul for union with the Beloved, the purification of the *nafs* (ego-self), the stages of the spiritual journey, and the ultimate dissolution of the boundary between lover and Beloved. The language of divine love (*ishq*) that pervades the Guru Granth Sahib resonates deeply with the literary and spiritual tradition of Rumi, Hafiz, and the great Sufi masters.

The Sufi reader finds particular affinity with the Guru Granth Sahib's insistence that external religious observance without inner transformation is spiritually barren. This critique of formalism — directed equally at Hindu ritualism and Islamic legalism — mirrors the Sufi emphasis on *batin* (inner reality) over *zahir* (outward form). The concept of *naam* (the Divine Name) as the path to spiritual realization parallels the Sufi practice of *dhikr* (remembrance of God) as the means of drawing near to the divine presence.

Reading this section alongside the compositions of Sheikh Farid preserved elsewhere in the Guru Granth Sahib, the Sufi tradition recognizes a shared spiritual vocabulary and a common orientation toward the annihilation of ego in the overwhelming reality of divine love. The boundaries between Sikh and Sufi mysticism, in these verses, become porous.
