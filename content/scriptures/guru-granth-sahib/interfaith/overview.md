## Overview

The Guru Granth Sahib holds singular importance for interfaith dialogue and the study of religious pluralism. As a scripture that deliberately includes the compositions of Hindu, Muslim, and Sikh authors, organized not by religious identity but by musical mode and spiritual theme, it embodies a vision of religious truth that transcends confessional boundaries. Interfaith scholars point to the Guru Granth Sahib as a concrete historical demonstration that deep spiritual insight can be recognized and honored across religious traditions.

## Model of Interreligious Engagement

The compilation of the Guru Granth Sahib by Guru Arjan Dev represents one of history's most remarkable acts of interreligious engagement. The Guru did not merely tolerate the teachings of Hindu and Muslim saints — he canonized them, placing them alongside the compositions of the Sikh Gurus within the same sacred text. This act of inclusion establishes a precedent for recognizing spiritual authority beyond the boundaries of one's own religious community, a principle of profound relevance for contemporary interfaith relations.

## Contemporary Relevance

In an era of religious polarization, the Guru Granth Sahib offers a living example of how multiple religious voices can coexist within a single sacred framework without erasure of difference or forced harmonization. Each contributor speaks from within their own tradition — Kabir as a Bhakti sant, Farid as a Sufi mystic, the Gurus as Sikh teachers — yet their compositions are united by a shared orientation toward the one formless God and the transformative power of the Divine Name. Interfaith practitioners find in this model both inspiration and practical guidance.
