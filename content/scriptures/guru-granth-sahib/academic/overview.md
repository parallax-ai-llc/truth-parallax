## Overview

Academic study of the Guru Granth Sahib encompasses multiple disciplines: textual criticism, historical linguistics, musicology, comparative religion, and South Asian cultural history. The scripture is composed in multiple languages — Punjabi, Braj Bhasha, Sanskrit, Persian, and various regional dialects — transcribed in the Gurmukhi script, presenting significant challenges and opportunities for linguistic analysis. Its compilation history, from the Adi Granth of 1604 to the final recension of 1708, is documented with unusual precision for a pre-modern religious text.

## Textual History

The compilation of the Adi Granth by Guru Arjan Dev in 1604 is one of the best-documented events in the history of scripture formation. The Kartarpur Bir (the original manuscript) survives and has been the subject of scholarly investigation. Academic study traces the editorial decisions that shaped the text — the inclusion of Hindu and Muslim saints' compositions alongside the Gurus' own writings, the organizational principle of *raag* (musical mode), and the system of authorial attribution using the name "Nanak" across multiple Gurus.

## Musicological Significance

The Guru Granth Sahib is organized by *raag* (musical mode), making it one of the few major scriptures whose structure is fundamentally musical. Each composition is assigned to a specific raag, with instructions for melodic performance. Academic musicologists study the scripture as a compendium of North Indian classical and folk musical traditions, preserving raags and compositional forms that might otherwise have been lost.
