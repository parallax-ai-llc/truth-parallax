## Academic Reading

Academic analysis of The Concluding Hymns — Mundavani and Raagmala examines its 15 verses through the lenses of textual criticism, historical linguistics, musicology, and comparative religion. Scholars investigate questions of authorship, dating, linguistic register, and the editorial processes by which these compositions were selected, organized, and transmitted. The multilingual character of the Guru Granth Sahib — employing Punjabi, Braj Bhasha, Khariboli, Sanskrit, Persian, and regional dialects — is particularly evident in this section, offering rich material for sociolinguistic analysis.

The musical structure of this section receives significant academic attention. Each composition is assigned to a specific *raag* with performance instructions, making the Guru Granth Sahib a primary source for the study of North Indian musical traditions in the medieval and early modern periods. Musicologists analyze the relationship between raag, theological content, and emotional affect, exploring how the scripture's musical organization reinforces its spiritual message.

Historical-critical scholars situate this section within the broader context of the Sant and Bhakti movements, the Mughal political environment, and the development of Sikh communal identity. The textual transmission from the original Kartarpur Bir through subsequent manuscripts provides material for stemmatic analysis and the reconstruction of the scripture's compilation history.
