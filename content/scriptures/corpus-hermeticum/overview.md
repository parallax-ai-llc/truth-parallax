## Overview

The Corpus Hermeticum is a collection of eighteen Greek treatises attributed to Hermes Trismegistus ("Thrice-Greatest Hermes"), a legendary figure combining the Greek god Hermes with the Egyptian god Thoth. Composed in Greco-Roman Egypt between the first and third centuries CE, the texts present a synthesis of Greek philosophical thought — particularly Platonism and Stoicism — with Egyptian religious sensibility and elements that parallel Gnostic and Jewish mystical traditions. The Corpus Hermeticum was rediscovered in the Latin West when a manuscript reached Cosimo de' Medici in 1460 and was translated by Marsilio Ficino, sparking an enormous influence on Renaissance philosophy, alchemy, and esotericism.

## Structure

The Corpus Hermeticum comprises eighteen treatises of varying length and form:

1. **Treatises I–IV**: Major cosmogonic and theological dialogues, including the foundational vision of Poimandres and the teaching of the Kratēr (Mixing Bowl)
2. **Treatises V–IX**: Shorter discourses on the nature of God, the Good, ignorance, indestructibility, and the relationship of thought and sense
3. **Treatises X–XIII**: Extended dialogues on the Key to knowledge, the nature of Nous (Mind), and the secret teaching of regeneration
4. **Treatises XIV–XVIII**: Letters, fragments, and shorter pieces on truth, the soul, and the relationship of soul and body

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Hermetic**: The foundational scripture of the Western esoteric tradition, studied as the direct teaching of Hermes Trismegistus
- **Academic**: A crucial primary source for the study of Greco-Egyptian religion, late antique philosophy, and the history of Western esotericism
- **Comparative**: Offers rich parallels with Gnostic cosmogonies, Neoplatonic emanation, Jewish Merkavah mysticism, and Indian metaphysics
- **Gnostic**: Shares the Gnostic emphasis on salvific self-knowledge, the divine spark, and liberation from material ignorance
- **Philosophical**: Engages with fundamental questions of consciousness, the mind-body relationship, emanation, and the nature of the Good
- **Interfaith**: Represents a tradition of universal wisdom (prisca theologia) that has historically served as a bridge between religious and philosophical systems

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Nous (Mind) | The divine Mind as the source of all reality, and human nous as the means of return to God |
| Gnosis | Salvific knowledge — direct, experiential awareness of the divine nature of the self and the cosmos |
| Cosmogony | The creation of the cosmos through emanation from the divine Mind |
| Regeneration | The transformative process by which the soul is reborn and divinized through gnosis |
| The Good | God identified with the supreme Good, beyond all material qualities and limitations |
