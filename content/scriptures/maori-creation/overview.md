## Overview

The Maori creation narratives (Te Orokohanga) form a comprehensive cosmological tradition that traces the origins of all existence from the primordial void (Te Kore) through the long darkness (Te Po) to the world of light (Te Ao Marama). These narratives are not a single unified text but a body of interconnected traditions maintained by different iwi (tribes) and hapu (subtribes) across Aotearoa (New Zealand), each with regional variations that enrich the overall tradition while preserving a common structural framework.

The central narrative arc moves from abstract potentiality through the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) by their son Tane Mahuta, to the creation of humanity and the establishment of mortality, culminating in the exploits of the culture hero Maui. The creation narratives are intimately connected to the practice of whakapapa (genealogy), which traces the descent of all things — gods, humans, animals, plants, geographic features — from common cosmic origins, establishing the kinship of all existence. Some tribal traditions also include the supreme being Io (Io-matua-kore), though the antiquity and universality of the Io tradition remains debated among scholars.

## Structure

| Cycle | Title | Topic |
|---------|-------|-------|
| 1 | Te Kore: The Void and Potential | The primordial nothingness from which all existence emerges |
| 2 | Te Po: The Darkness and Becoming | The long night of cosmic gestation and the embrace of Rangi and Papa |
| 3 | The Separation of Rangi and Papa | Tane's separation of Sky Father and Earth Mother, bringing light to the world |
| 4 | The Children of Rangi and Papa | The gods' domains, conflicts, and the establishment of the natural order |
| 5 | The Creation of Humanity | Tane's creation of the first woman and the origin of human mortality |
| 6 | Maui and the Shaping of the World | The exploits of the trickster hero who shaped the world and confirmed mortality |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: The living foundation of Maori identity, whakapapa, and tikanga (customary practice)
- **Academic**: A major body of study in Polynesian mythology, oral literature, and Indigenous cosmology
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with other Polynesian, Indo-European, and world creation traditions
- **Theological**: Raises questions about divine separation, the origin of mortality, and the nature of creative potential
- **Anthropological**: Central to understanding Maori social organization, kinship, and relationship with the environment
- **Interfaith**: Offers Polynesian perspectives on creation, divine-human relationships, and the meaning of mortality

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Te Kore to Te Ao Marama | The progression from void through darkness to light as a cosmic pattern |
| Whakapapa | Genealogy as the fundamental organizing principle connecting all of existence |
| Separation and Creation | The generative act of separating primordial unity to create space for life |
| Mortality and Loss | The origin of death through Hinetitama's transformation and Maui's failure |
| Kinship of All Being | The understanding that humans, gods, animals, and land share common ancestry |
| Mana and Tapu | Sacred power and restriction as principles governing the relationship between beings |
