The fifth cycle recounts Tane's creation of the first woman, Hineahuone, from the red earth of Kurawaka. Tane breathes life into her and she becomes his wife, bearing a daughter, Hinetitama (Dawn Maiden). When Hinetitama discovers that Tane is both her father and husband, she flees in shame to the underworld and transforms into Hine-nui-te-po, the goddess of death. She declares that she will pull their descendants into the darkness, establishing the reality of human mortality.

Academic analysis of The Creation of Humanity draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
