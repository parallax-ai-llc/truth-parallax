The sixth cycle tells the exploits of Maui-tikitiki-a-Taranga, the great culture hero and trickster of Polynesian tradition. Born prematurely and cast into the sea, Maui is raised by his ancestor Tama-nui-ki-te-rangi. His exploits include fishing up the North Island of New Zealand (Te Ika a Maui), slowing the sun to lengthen the day, obtaining fire from the underworld goddess Mahuika, and his final, fatal attempt to conquer death by entering the body of Hine-nui-te-po — confirming human mortality established in the previous cycle.

Academic analysis of Maui and the Shaping of the World draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
