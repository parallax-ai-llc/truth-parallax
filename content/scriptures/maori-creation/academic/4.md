The fourth cycle describes the activities and domains of the children of Rangi and Papa after the separation. Tawhirimatea (god of storms) attacks his brothers for separating their parents, scattering Tangaroa's children (sea creatures) and threatening Tu-matauenga (god of war and humanity). Tane creates the forests and birds, Rongo governs cultivated plants, Haumia protects wild foods, and Tu-matauenga alone stands against Tawhiri's wrath, establishing humanity's dominion over all the other gods' domains.

Academic analysis of The Children of Rangi and Papa draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
