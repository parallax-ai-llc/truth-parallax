The third cycle narrates the central event of Maori cosmology: the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother). Their children, confined in the darkness between their parents' bodies, debate whether to separate or kill their parents. Tane Mahuta, god of forests and birds, succeeds in pushing his parents apart — bracing his back against the earth and his feet against the sky — flooding the world with light (Te Ao Marama). The tears of the separated parents become rain and mist.

Academic analysis of The Separation of Rangi and Papa draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
