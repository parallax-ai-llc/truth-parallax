The second cycle describes Te Po — the Long Night, the realm of darkness and gestation that follows Te Kore. Like the stages of Te Kore, Te Po progresses through multiple phases of deepening and transformation, from the first night to the great night to the intense night. Te Po represents the period of cosmic gestation during which the creative potential of Te Kore begins to take form. It is in Te Po that Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) come into being and lie in close embrace.

Academic analysis of Te Po: The Darkness and Becoming draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
