---
title: "Maori Creation - Chapter 5"
scripture: "maori-creation"
chapter: 5
---

**1** Then Maui, the trickster, the last-born, the clever one, desired to conquer death for mankind. For he was bold and full of cunning.

**2** Maui had already snared the sun to slow its course across the sky, so that the days were long enough for men to complete their labors.

**3** He had fished up the North Island of Aotearoa from the sea with a magic hook. The island is called Te Ika a Maui, the Fish of Maui.

**4** He had stolen fire from the fingernails of Mahuika, the fire goddess, and given it to mankind. Though Mahuika was wroth, her fire lived on in the wood of the kaikomako tree.

**5** Now Maui desired to make men immortal, so he journeyed with his companions to the edge of the world, to the place where Hine-nui-te-po lay sleeping.

**6** He said to the birds who accompanied him: "When you see me enter the body of the goddess of death, you must not laugh. Be silent until I have passed through her and emerged from her mouth. Then death shall be conquered."

**7** And Maui shed his garments and made himself small. He began to enter the sleeping goddess.

**8** But the little bird Tiwakawaka, the fantail, could not contain himself. Seeing Maui half-swallowed by the sleeping goddess, the fantail burst out laughing.

**9** At the sound of that laughter, Hine-nui-te-po awoke. She felt Maui within her, and she crushed him between her thighs. And so Maui died.

**10** Thus the last great attempt to defeat death was foiled, and death remains the portion of all living things.

**11** But the deeds of Maui live on: the sun still moves slowly enough for men to work, the fire still burns in the hearth, and the great fish that is the island still rises from the sea.

**12** And it is said: "Mate atu he tetekura, ara mai he tetekura" — as one fern frond dies, another rises to take its place. So it is with the generations of men.
