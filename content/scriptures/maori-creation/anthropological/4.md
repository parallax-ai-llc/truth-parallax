The fourth cycle describes the activities and domains of the children of Rangi and Papa after the separation. Tawhirimatea (god of storms) attacks his brothers for separating their parents, scattering Tangaroa's children (sea creatures) and threatening Tu-matauenga (god of war and humanity). Tane creates the forests and birds, Rongo governs cultivated plants, Haumia protects wild foods, and Tu-matauenga alone stands against Tawhiri's wrath, establishing humanity's dominion over all the other gods' domains.

Anthropological study of The Children of Rangi and Papa examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
