The sixth cycle tells the exploits of Maui-tikitiki-a-Taranga, the great culture hero and trickster of Polynesian tradition. Born prematurely and cast into the sea, Maui is raised by his ancestor Tama-nui-ki-te-rangi. His exploits include fishing up the North Island of New Zealand (Te Ika a Maui), slowing the sun to lengthen the day, obtaining fire from the underworld goddess Mahuika, and his final, fatal attempt to conquer death by entering the body of Hine-nui-te-po — confirming human mortality established in the previous cycle.

Anthropological study of Maui and the Shaping of the World examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
