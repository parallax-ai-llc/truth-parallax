The fifth cycle recounts Tane's creation of the first woman, Hineahuone, from the red earth of Kurawaka. Tane breathes life into her and she becomes his wife, bearing a daughter, Hinetitama (Dawn Maiden). When Hinetitama discovers that Tane is both her father and husband, she flees in shame to the underworld and transforms into Hine-nui-te-po, the goddess of death. She declares that she will pull their descendants into the darkness, establishing the reality of human mortality.

Anthropological study of The Creation of Humanity examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
