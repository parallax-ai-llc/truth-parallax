The third cycle narrates the central event of Maori cosmology: the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother). Their children, confined in the darkness between their parents' bodies, debate whether to separate or kill their parents. Tane Mahuta, god of forests and birds, succeeds in pushing his parents apart — bracing his back against the earth and his feet against the sky — flooding the world with light (Te Ao Marama). The tears of the separated parents become rain and mist.

Anthropological study of The Separation of Rangi and Papa examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
