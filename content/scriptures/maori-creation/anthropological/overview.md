## Overview

Anthropological study of Maori creation narratives examines their role in structuring Maori social organization, kinship, ceremonial practice, and relationship with the environment. The creation narratives provide the whakapapa (genealogical) framework that organizes Maori society into iwi (tribes), hapu (subtribes), and whanau (extended families), each tracing their descent from specific Ancestral canoes and ultimately from the gods of the creation narratives.

Key anthropological themes include the relationship between cosmology and social hierarchy (with seniority of descent conferring mana and authority), the role of the creation narratives in the protocols of the marae (meeting ground), the gendered dimensions of the narratives (particularly the significance of the female figures Papatuanuku, Hineahuone, and Hine-nui-te-po), and the ecological knowledge embedded in the narratives' accounts of the natural world.
