The second cycle describes Te Po — the Long Night, the realm of darkness and gestation that follows Te Kore. Like the stages of Te Kore, Te Po progresses through multiple phases of deepening and transformation, from the first night to the great night to the intense night. Te Po represents the period of cosmic gestation during which the creative potential of Te Kore begins to take form. It is in Te Po that Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) come into being and lie in close embrace.

Anthropological study of Te Po: The Darkness and Becoming examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
