The fourth cycle describes the activities and domains of the children of Rangi and Papa after the separation. Tawhirimatea (god of storms) attacks his brothers for separating their parents, scattering Tangaroa's children (sea creatures) and threatening Tu-matauenga (god of war and humanity). Tane creates the forests and birds, Rongo governs cultivated plants, Haumia protects wild foods, and Tu-matauenga alone stands against Tawhiri's wrath, establishing humanity's dominion over all the other gods' domains.

Interfaith engagement with The Children of Rangi and Papa offers opportunities for cross-traditional dialogue on shared human concerns expressed through this tradition's distinctive spiritual vocabulary. The themes addressed in this section — creation, transformation, moral order, the relationship between humans and the natural world — are concerns that resonate across the world's spiritual traditions while being expressed here in uniquely powerful ways.

Respectful interfaith dialogue with this material requires acknowledging both the common ground it shares with other traditions and its irreducible distinctiveness. When approached with humility and genuine openness, this section offers resources for enriching one's own spiritual understanding while deepening appreciation for the breadth and depth of human spiritual experience.
