The second cycle describes Te Po — the Long Night, the realm of darkness and gestation that follows Te Kore. Like the stages of Te Kore, Te Po progresses through multiple phases of deepening and transformation, from the first night to the great night to the intense night. Te Po represents the period of cosmic gestation during which the creative potential of Te Kore begins to take form. It is in Te Po that Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) come into being and lie in close embrace.

Interfaith engagement with Te Po: The Darkness and Becoming offers opportunities for cross-traditional dialogue on shared human concerns expressed through this tradition's distinctive spiritual vocabulary. The themes addressed in this section — creation, transformation, moral order, the relationship between humans and the natural world — are concerns that resonate across the world's spiritual traditions while being expressed here in uniquely powerful ways.

Respectful interfaith dialogue with this material requires acknowledging both the common ground it shares with other traditions and its irreducible distinctiveness. When approached with humility and genuine openness, this section offers resources for enriching one's own spiritual understanding while deepening appreciation for the breadth and depth of human spiritual experience.
