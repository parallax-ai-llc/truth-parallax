The third cycle narrates the central event of Maori cosmology: the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother). Their children, confined in the darkness between their parents' bodies, debate whether to separate or kill their parents. Tane Mahuta, god of forests and birds, succeeds in pushing his parents apart — bracing his back against the earth and his feet against the sky — flooding the world with light (Te Ao Marama). The tears of the separated parents become rain and mist.

Interfaith engagement with The Separation of Rangi and Papa offers opportunities for cross-traditional dialogue on shared human concerns expressed through this tradition's distinctive spiritual vocabulary. The themes addressed in this section — creation, transformation, moral order, the relationship between humans and the natural world — are concerns that resonate across the world's spiritual traditions while being expressed here in uniquely powerful ways.

Respectful interfaith dialogue with this material requires acknowledging both the common ground it shares with other traditions and its irreducible distinctiveness. When approached with humility and genuine openness, this section offers resources for enriching one's own spiritual understanding while deepening appreciation for the breadth and depth of human spiritual experience.
