The third cycle narrates the central event of Maori cosmology: the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother). Their children, confined in the darkness between their parents' bodies, debate whether to separate or kill their parents. Tane Mahuta, god of forests and birds, succeeds in pushing his parents apart — bracing his back against the earth and his feet against the sky — flooding the world with light (Te Ao Marama). The tears of the separated parents become rain and mist.

Comparative analysis of The Separation of Rangi and Papa reveals both parallels with and distinctive departures from similar themes in other world traditions. The motifs and structures found in this section connect to widespread patterns in human religious and mythological expression while retaining features that are specific to this particular cultural and spiritual context.
