The second cycle describes Te Po — the Long Night, the realm of darkness and gestation that follows Te Kore. Like the stages of Te Kore, Te Po progresses through multiple phases of deepening and transformation, from the first night to the great night to the intense night. Te Po represents the period of cosmic gestation during which the creative potential of Te Kore begins to take form. It is in Te Po that Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) come into being and lie in close embrace.

Comparative analysis of Te Po: The Darkness and Becoming reveals both parallels with and distinctive departures from similar themes in other world traditions. The motifs and structures found in this section connect to widespread patterns in human religious and mythological expression while retaining features that are specific to this particular cultural and spiritual context.

Cross-cultural comparison illuminates the universal dimensions of the human religious imagination while also highlighting the unique contributions of this tradition to the global tapestry of spiritual thought.
