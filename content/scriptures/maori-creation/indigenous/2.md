The second cycle describes Te Po — the Long Night, the realm of darkness and gestation that follows Te Kore. Like the stages of Te Kore, Te Po progresses through multiple phases of deepening and transformation, from the first night to the great night to the intense night. Te Po represents the period of cosmic gestation during which the creative potential of Te Kore begins to take form. It is in Te Po that Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) come into being and lie in close embrace.

From an Indigenous perspective, Te Po: The Darkness and Becoming carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
