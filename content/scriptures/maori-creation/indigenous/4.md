The fourth cycle describes the activities and domains of the children of Rangi and Papa after the separation. Tawhirimatea (god of storms) attacks his brothers for separating their parents, scattering Tangaroa's children (sea creatures) and threatening Tu-matauenga (god of war and humanity). Tane creates the forests and birds, Rongo governs cultivated plants, Haumia protects wild foods, and Tu-matauenga alone stands against Tawhiri's wrath, establishing humanity's dominion over all the other gods' domains.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Children of Rangi and Papa carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
