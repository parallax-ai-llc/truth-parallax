The sixth cycle tells the exploits of Maui-tikitiki-a-Taranga, the great culture hero and trickster of Polynesian tradition. Born prematurely and cast into the sea, Maui is raised by his ancestor Tama-nui-ki-te-rangi. His exploits include fishing up the North Island of New Zealand (Te Ika a Maui), slowing the sun to lengthen the day, obtaining fire from the underworld goddess Mahuika, and his final, fatal attempt to conquer death by entering the body of Hine-nui-te-po — confirming human mortality established in the previous cycle.

From an Indigenous perspective, Maui and the Shaping of the World carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
