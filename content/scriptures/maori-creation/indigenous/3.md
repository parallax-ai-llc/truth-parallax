The third cycle narrates the central event of Maori cosmology: the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother). Their children, confined in the darkness between their parents' bodies, debate whether to separate or kill their parents. Tane Mahuta, god of forests and birds, succeeds in pushing his parents apart — bracing his back against the earth and his feet against the sky — flooding the world with light (Te Ao Marama). The tears of the separated parents become rain and mist.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Separation of Rangi and Papa carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
