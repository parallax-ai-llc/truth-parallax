This section tells the story of Hiawatha (Ayenwatha), a man consumed by grief after the loss of his family. The Peacemaker's encounter with Hiawatha and the development of the Condolence Ceremony — using strings of wampum to wipe away tears, open ears, and clear the throat — established one of the most important ritual and diplomatic protocols of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy.

Anthropological study of Hiawatha and the Consolation examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
