This section outlines the qualifications, rights, and responsibilities of the 50 royaneh (chiefs or sachems) who sit on the Grand Council. Chiefs must be of good character, patient, and selfless. They hold their titles through the clans and can be removed by the Clan Mothers if they fail in their duties. The section establishes that chiefs are servants of the people, not rulers over them.

Anthropological study of The Rights and Duties of Chiefs examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
