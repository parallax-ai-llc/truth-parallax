This section details the procedures for deliberation and decision-making in the Grand Council. Issues are introduced by the Mohawk, passed across the fire to the Oneida and Cayuga for consideration, then to the Seneca, and finally confirmed by the Onondaga Firekeepers. This process ensures thorough deliberation and consensus rather than majority rule, reflecting the Great Law's commitment to unity and careful governance.

Anthropological study of The Process of Deliberation examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice.
