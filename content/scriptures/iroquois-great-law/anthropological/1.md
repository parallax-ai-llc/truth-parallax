The opening section of the Great Law recounts the birth and mission of the Peacemaker (Deganawida), who was born among the Huron (Wendat) people and received a divine mandate to bring peace to the warring nations. His journey across the great lake in a stone canoe demonstrated his spiritual authority and set the stage for the transformation of the Five Nations from perpetual warfare to a confederacy of peace.

Anthropological study of The Peacemaker's Journey examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
