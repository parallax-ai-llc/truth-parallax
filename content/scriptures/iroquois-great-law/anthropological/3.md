The third section narrates the confrontation with Tadodaho (Atotarho), the powerful and twisted sorcerer of the Onondaga who opposed the peace. Through a combination of spiritual power, persuasion, and the offer of the position of chief firekeeper of the Confederacy, Tadodaho was transformed from an obstacle to peace into its guardian. This transformation represents the Great Law's principle that even the most resistant forces can be brought into harmony.

Anthropological study of The Defeat of Tadodaho examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
