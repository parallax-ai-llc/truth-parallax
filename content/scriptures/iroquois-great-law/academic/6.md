This section outlines the qualifications, rights, and responsibilities of the 50 royaneh (chiefs or sachems) who sit on the Grand Council. Chiefs must be of good character, patient, and selfless. They hold their titles through the clans and can be removed by the Clan Mothers if they fail in their duties. The section establishes that chiefs are servants of the people, not rulers over them.

Academic analysis of The Rights and Duties of Chiefs draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories.
