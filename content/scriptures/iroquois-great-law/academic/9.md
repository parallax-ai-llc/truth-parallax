This section establishes the protocols for war and peace. War chiefs are distinct from peace chiefs (sachems) and hold authority only in times of conflict. The Great Law prefers peace and diplomacy, establishing procedures for warning enemies before engaging in warfare and for offering peace terms. The section also addresses the adoption of individuals and nations into the Confederacy.

Academic analysis of War and Peace Protocols draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
