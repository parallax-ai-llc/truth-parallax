This section establishes the organizational structure of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy. The Five Nations (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca) are arranged in a specific seating pattern at the Grand Council, with the Mohawk and Seneca as Elder Brothers, the Oneida and Cayuga as Younger Brothers, and the Onondaga as Firekeepers. The clan system and moiety structure provide the basis for representation and decision-making.

Academic analysis of The Structure of the Confederacy draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
