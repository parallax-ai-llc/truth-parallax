This section addresses the procedures for adopting individuals and entire nations into the Haudenosaunee Confederacy. Nations seeking shelter under the Tree of Peace may be admitted with full rights and representation. Individuals from other peoples may be adopted into clans. This section reflects the Confederacy's expansive vision of peace and its openness to incorporating others into its framework of governance.

Academic analysis of Adoption and Citizenship draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
