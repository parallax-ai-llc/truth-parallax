This section tells the story of Hiawatha (Ayenwatha), a man consumed by grief after the loss of his family. The Peacemaker's encounter with Hiawatha and the development of the Condolence Ceremony — using strings of wampum to wipe away tears, open ears, and clear the throat — established one of the most important ritual and diplomatic protocols of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy.

Academic analysis of Hiawatha and the Consolation draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
