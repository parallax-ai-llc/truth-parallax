The opening section of the Great Law recounts the birth and mission of the Peacemaker (Deganawida), who was born among the Huron (Wendat) people and received a divine mandate to bring peace to the warring nations. His journey across the great lake in a stone canoe demonstrated his spiritual authority and set the stage for the transformation of the Five Nations from perpetual warfare to a confederacy of peace.

Academic analysis of The Peacemaker's Journey draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
