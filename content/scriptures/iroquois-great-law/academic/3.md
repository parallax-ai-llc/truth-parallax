The third section narrates the confrontation with Tadodaho (Atotarho), the powerful and twisted sorcerer of the Onondaga who opposed the peace. Through a combination of spiritual power, persuasion, and the offer of the position of chief firekeeper of the Confederacy, Tadodaho was transformed from an obstacle to peace into its guardian. This transformation represents the Great Law's principle that even the most resistant forces can be brought into harmony.

Academic analysis of The Defeat of Tadodaho draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
