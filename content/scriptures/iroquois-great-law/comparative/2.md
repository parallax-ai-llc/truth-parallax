This section tells the story of Hiawatha (Ayenwatha), a man consumed by grief after the loss of his family. The Peacemaker's encounter with Hiawatha and the development of the Condolence Ceremony — using strings of wampum to wipe away tears, open ears, and clear the throat — established one of the most important ritual and diplomatic protocols of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy.

Comparative analysis of Hiawatha and the Consolation reveals both parallels with and distinctive departures from similar themes in other world traditions. The motifs and structures found in this section connect to widespread patterns in human religious and mythological expression while retaining features that are specific to this particular cultural and spiritual context.

Cross-cultural comparison illuminates the universal dimensions of the human religious imagination while also highlighting the unique contributions of this tradition to the global tapestry of spiritual thought. However, comparative analysis must be conducted with care to avoid reducing distinctive traditions to mere instances of universal patterns, thereby obscuring what is most valuable and specific in each tradition.
