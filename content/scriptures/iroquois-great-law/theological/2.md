This section tells the story of Hiawatha (Ayenwatha), a man consumed by grief after the loss of his family. The Peacemaker's encounter with Hiawatha and the development of the Condolence Ceremony — using strings of wampum to wipe away tears, open ears, and clear the throat — established one of the most important ritual and diplomatic protocols of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy.

Theological engagement with Hiawatha and the Consolation raises important questions about the nature of the sacred, the relationship between the divine and the world, and the meaning of human existence within a larger cosmic order. This section's themes invite reflection on fundamental theological questions that transcend any single tradition while being rooted in the specific spiritual vision of this particular culture.

The theological concepts embedded in this section — whether explicit or implicit — offer distinctive perspectives on questions that occupy theologians across traditions: the nature of creation, the relationship between the human and the divine, the meaning of suffering and transformation, and the foundations of moral order.
