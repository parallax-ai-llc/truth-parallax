## Overview

The Great Law of Peace (Gayanashagowa) is the founding constitution of the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy, one of the oldest participatory democracies in the world. Brought by the Peacemaker (Deganawida) and his spokesperson Hiawatha (Ayenwatha), the Great Law transformed five warring nations — the Mohawk (Kanien'kehá:ka), Oneida (Onyota'a:ka), Onondaga (Onöñda'gega'), Cayuga (Gayogo̱hó:nǫ'), and Seneca (Onödowá'ga:) — into a confederacy united under principles of peace, righteousness, and civil authority. A sixth nation, the Tuscarora, joined in 1722.

The Great Law is not merely a political document but a spiritual and moral charter that integrates governance with cosmology, ritual with law, and individual rights with communal responsibility. Its provisions for representative government, separation of powers, checks and balances, women's political authority, and the protection of individual rights have been recognized by scholars as a significant influence on democratic thought, including the development of the United States Constitution. The Haudenosaunee themselves continue to govern according to the Great Law at the Six Nations territories.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: The living constitutional framework of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy, still practiced today
- **Academic**: A foundational text for studies in Indigenous governance, legal theory, and democratic thought
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with other constitutional traditions, federal systems, and democratic innovations
- **Theological**: Integrates spiritual authority, moral law, and governance in a comprehensive sacred charter
- **Anthropological**: Central to understanding Haudenosaunee social organization, kinship, and political culture
- **Interfaith**: Offers Indigenous perspectives on peace, justice, governance, and the sacred foundations of law

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Peace (Skén:nen) | The active creation and maintenance of peace as the highest political and spiritual value |
| Righteousness (Ka'shatstenhsera) | The practice of justice, equity, and moral integrity in governance |
| Power (Ka'nikonhri:io) | The spiritual and civil authority that sustains the Confederacy, rooted in good minds |
| Consensus | Decision-making through deliberation and unanimous agreement rather than majority rule |
| Matrilineal Authority | The central role of Clan Mothers in governance, lineage, and political accountability |
| Living Constitution | The Great Law as a dynamic, adaptive framework rather than a fixed legal code |
