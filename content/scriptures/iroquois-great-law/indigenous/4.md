This section describes the symbolic planting of the Great Tree of Peace (a white pine) at Onondaga, the central fire of the Confederacy. The tree's four white roots extend to the four directions, inviting all nations to follow them to shelter beneath its branches. An eagle sits atop the tree as a sentinel, watching for threats to the peace. Weapons of war were buried beneath the tree's roots.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Planting of the Tree of Peace carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
