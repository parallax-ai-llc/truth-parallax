This section outlines the qualifications, rights, and responsibilities of the 50 royaneh (chiefs or sachems) who sit on the Grand Council. Chiefs must be of good character, patient, and selfless. They hold their titles through the clans and can be removed by the Clan Mothers if they fail in their duties. The section establishes that chiefs are servants of the people, not rulers over them.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Rights and Duties of Chiefs carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
