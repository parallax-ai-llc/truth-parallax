The third section narrates the confrontation with Tadodaho (Atotarho), the powerful and twisted sorcerer of the Onondaga who opposed the peace. Through a combination of spiritual power, persuasion, and the offer of the position of chief firekeeper of the Confederacy, Tadodaho was transformed from an obstacle to peace into its guardian. This transformation represents the Great Law's principle that even the most resistant forces can be brought into harmony.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Defeat of Tadodaho carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
