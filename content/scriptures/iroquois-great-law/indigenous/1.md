The opening section of the Great Law recounts the birth and mission of the Peacemaker (Deganawida), who was born among the Huron (Wendat) people and received a divine mandate to bring peace to the warring nations. His journey across the great lake in a stone canoe demonstrated his spiritual authority and set the stage for the transformation of the Five Nations from perpetual warfare to a confederacy of peace.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Peacemaker's Journey carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
