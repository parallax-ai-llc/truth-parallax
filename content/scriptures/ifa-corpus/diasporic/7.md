Odu 7 — Obara Meji — Speech and the Power of the Word — has been preserved and adapted within African diasporic religious traditions, particularly Cuban Lucumí/Santería and Brazilian Candomblé, where Ifa divination continues as a living practice. Diasporic engagement with this Odu reflects both fidelity to Yoruba origins and creative adaptation to new social, cultural, and linguistic contexts.

In diaspora contexts, Obara Meji retains its fundamental associations with speech and the power of the word while acquiring additional layers of meaning shaped by the historical experience of the African diaspora — the Middle Passage, slavery, resistance, cultural survival, and community building. Diasporic babalawos have maintained the oral transmission of this Odu's verses across generations, often under conditions of severe persecution, testifying to the tradition's resilience and the commitment of its practitioners. Contemporary diasporic practice increasingly engages with West African sources while also affirming the legitimacy of distinctively diasporic interpretive traditions.
