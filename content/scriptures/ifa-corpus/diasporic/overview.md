## Overview

The Ifa corpus has been preserved, adapted, and revitalized across the African diaspora, particularly in Cuba (where it is practiced within the Lucumí/Santería tradition), Brazil (within Candomblé), Trinidad, and increasingly in North America and Europe. The survival and adaptation of Ifa in the diaspora — despite the extreme conditions of the transatlantic slave trade and centuries of colonial suppression — testifies to the tradition's resilience and the determination of its practitioners.

Diasporic Ifa practice has both preserved and transformed elements of the Yoruba tradition. In Cuba, the Ifa system was maintained by enslaved Yoruba practitioners and their descendants, with the Odu and their associated verses transmitted orally through generations of babalawos. The tradition adapted to new social and cultural contexts, incorporating elements of Catholicism (in the well-known syncretism of Orisha with Catholic saints) while maintaining the core structures of Ifa divination. Contemporary diasporic Ifa communities increasingly seek to reconnect with West African sources while also affirming the legitimacy and distinctiveness of their own traditions.
