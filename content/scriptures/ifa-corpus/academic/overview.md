## Overview

Academic study of the Ifa corpus represents one of the most significant areas of African Studies and the study of oral literary traditions worldwide. Scholars have analyzed the corpus from multiple perspectives: as oral literature (examining its poetic forms, narrative structures, and performance contexts), as a knowledge system (exploring its epistemological foundations and its encoding of botanical, medical, historical, and philosophical knowledge), and as a religious practice (studying its ritual dimensions, its role in Yoruba social organization, and its adaptation in diaspora contexts).

Key academic contributions include Wande Abimbola's pioneering work on the literary structure of Ifa verses, William Bascom's anthropological studies of Ifa divination as a system of communication, and more recent scholarship examining the corpus's relevance to African philosophy, indigenous knowledge systems, and decolonial epistemology. The UNESCO recognition of Ifa divination in 2005 as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity has further stimulated scholarly interest.
