Acts 12 narrates the persecution under Herod Agrippa I, a figure well known from Josephus (*Antiquities* 18-19). Agrippa, grandson of Herod the Great and friend of the Roman emperor Claudius, ruled Judea from 41 to 44 CE. His persecution of the Jesus movement — executing James and imprisoning Peter — is historically plausible: Agrippa sought to cultivate Jewish support, and action against a controversial messianic sect would have been politically advantageous.

The reference to Peter's arrest during "the days of Unleavened Bread" (12:3) and Herod's intention to bring him before the people "after the Passover" reflects Jewish festival chronology. Jewish scholars note the irony of persecution during Pesach — the festival celebrating liberation from oppression. The detail about Herod posting four squads of soldiers to guard Peter (12:4) suggests that the Jesus movement was considered a significant security concern, consistent with Roman and Herodian attitudes toward popular movements.

Herod's death (12:20-23) is corroborated by Josephus (*Antiquities* 19.343-350), who describes Agrippa being struck with sudden illness at a public event in Caesarea after being acclaimed as a god. Josephus attributes his death to divine judgment for accepting the acclamation. The convergence of Acts and Josephus on this event provides one of the strongest points of historical correlation between the New Testament and external sources, though the accounts differ in detail.
