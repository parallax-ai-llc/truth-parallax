Acts 6 describes the conflict between Hellenist and Hebrew Jewish believers and the appointment of the Seven. Jewish scholars find this passage illuminating for understanding the diversity within first-century Judaism. The distinction between Hellenistai (Greek-speaking Jews) and Hebraioi (Aramaic/Hebrew-speaking Jews) reflects a well-documented social division in the Jewish community, attested by the presence of numerous Greek-language synagogues in Jerusalem, including those mentioned in Acts 6:9.

The Synagogue of the Freedmen (*Libertinoi*) and the synagogues of Cyrenians, Alexandrians, Cilicians, and Asians (6:9) provide evidence for the diversity of diaspora Jewish communities maintaining distinct identities in Jerusalem. Archaeological evidence, including the Theodotos inscription discovered in 1913, confirms the existence of diaspora synagogues in Jerusalem that served pilgrims and immigrants, providing lodging and ritual baths alongside worship.

The charges against Stephen — that he spoke against the Temple and the Law (6:13-14) — reflect tensions within Judaism about the Temple's permanence and the Torah's interpretation. The Dead Sea Scrolls reveal that the Qumran community had its own sharp critique of the Jerusalem Temple establishment. Jewish scholars note that intra-Jewish debates about the Temple were not unique to the Jesus movement but part of a broader pattern of Second Temple sectarian disagreement.
