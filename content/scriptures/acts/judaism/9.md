Acts 9 narrates the conversion of Saul of Tarsus, a self-described Pharisee from the diaspora. Saul's pre-conversion activity — persecuting the Jesus movement with letters from the high priest (9:1-2) — raises questions about the Sanhedrin's jurisdictional authority over diaspora synagogues. Jewish scholars debate the historicity of this claim: while the high priest had moral authority throughout the diaspora, the extent of his legal jurisdiction, especially for extradition, is uncertain. The Damascus Document from Qumran suggests that the Qumran community also had mechanisms for dealing with members in distant locations.

Saul's Pharisaic identity is of great interest to Jewish scholars. His training "at the feet of Gamaliel" (22:3) would place him among the disciples of the most prestigious rabbinic school of his generation. His zeal for the ancestral traditions (Galatians 1:14) and his claim to be "a Pharisee, a son of Pharisees" (23:6) suggest genuine Pharisaic credentials. Jewish scholars like Daniel Boyarin have argued that Paul remained deeply Jewish even after his conversion, and that his theology — including ideas about the end of the age and the transformation of the covenant — drew on Jewish apocalyptic traditions.

The conversion itself — a visionary encounter with the risen Jesus on the Damascus road (9:3-9) — belongs to a pattern of revelatory experiences attested in Jewish literature. Prophetic call narratives in the Hebrew Bible (Isaiah 6, Ezekiel 1-3, Jeremiah 1), apocalyptic visions in Daniel and 1 Enoch, and merkavah mysticism all describe encounters with the divine that overwhelm and transform the recipient. Paul's experience fits within this broader Jewish pattern of visionary revelation.
