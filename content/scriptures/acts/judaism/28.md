Acts 28 concludes with Paul's arrival in Rome and his final encounter with the Jewish community there. The Roman Jewish community was one of the largest and most important in the diaspora, with multiple synagogues documented by Philo, Josephus, and archaeological evidence including the extensive Jewish catacombs. Paul's invitation to the Jewish leaders (28:17) and his defense — "I had done nothing against our people or the customs of our fathers" (28:17) — represents a final attempt to maintain his Jewish identity within the community.

Paul's citation of Isaiah 6:9-10 (28:26-27) — the hardening of Israel's heart — and his declaration that "this salvation of God has been sent to the Gentiles; they will listen" (28:28) is, from a Jewish perspective, one of the most consequential passages in Christian Scripture. Jewish scholars have traced how this text and its interpretation contributed to the development of Christian supersessionism — the theology that the Church has replaced Israel in God's plan. The passage has been used throughout Christian history to justify the claim that God has rejected the Jewish people, with devastating consequences.

Modern Jewish-Christian dialogue has sought to address this legacy. Jewish scholars like Amy-Jill Levine and Marc Brettler emphasize that the Acts narrative reflects a first-century intra-Jewish debate, not a permanent divine verdict. The open ending of Acts — Paul preaching "without hindrance" in Rome (28:31) — leaves the relationship between the Jesus movement and Judaism unresolved, a tension that the subsequent centuries would transform into full separation. Understanding Acts within its Jewish context remains essential for honest Jewish-Christian encounter.
