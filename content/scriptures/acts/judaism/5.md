Acts 5 contains the story of Ananias and Sapphira and the continued activities of the apostles. The account of sudden divine punishment for deception has parallels in Jewish literature — Achan's sin and punishment in Joshua 7, Nadab and Abihu's unauthorized offering in Leviticus 10. Jewish scholars note that the narrative reflects a theology of immediate divine justice that was more characteristic of certain strands of Second Temple Judaism than of later rabbinic thought, which increasingly emphasized divine mercy and delayed judgment.

Gamaliel's intervention (5:33-39) is of particular interest to Jewish scholars because Gamaliel is a known historical figure — Rabban Gamaliel the Elder, grandson of Hillel and one of the most important early rabbinic authorities. His portrayal in Acts as a voice of moderation and wisdom is consistent with the rabbinic tradition's characterization of him. The Mishnah (*Sotah* 9:15) states that "when Rabban Gamaliel the Elder died, the glory of the Torah ceased." His advice to wait and see whether the movement is of human or divine origin reflects a pragmatic tolerance that some scholars see as characteristic of Pharisaic Judaism's approach to sectarian diversity.

Gamaliel's references to Theudas and Judas the Galilean (5:36-37) place the Jesus movement within the broader context of first-century Jewish messianic and revolutionary movements. Josephus mentions both figures (*Antiquities* 20.97-98 for Theudas; *Jewish War* 2.118 for Judas), though the chronological relationship is debated. Jewish historians use these references to situate the early Christian movement within the larger landscape of Jewish resistance to Roman rule.
