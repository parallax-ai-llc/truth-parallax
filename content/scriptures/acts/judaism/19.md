Acts 19 centers on Paul's ministry in Ephesus, a city with a significant Jewish community. The "twelve disciples" who knew only John's baptism (19:1-7) are of interest to Jewish scholars as evidence for the diversity of Jewish baptismal movements in the first century. John the Baptist's movement had its own followers who did not join the Jesus movement, and traces of this independent Johannine community appear in Mandaean tradition, which claims continuity with John's followers.

The sons of Sceva — described as a "Jewish high priest" (19:14), though no high priest by that name is known — attempt to use Jesus' name as an exorcistic formula (19:13-16). Jewish exorcism is well attested in the Second Temple period. Josephus describes Solomon as the ancestor of exorcistic techniques (*Antiquities* 8.42-49) and recounts a contemporary exorcist named Eleazar who performed exorcisms before Vespasian. The Dead Sea Scrolls contain exorcistic prayers and incantations. The sons of Sceva episode reflects the competitive world of Jewish and Gentile wonder-workers in the first-century Mediterranean.

The burning of magical books worth "fifty thousand pieces of silver" (19:19) reflects the widespread practice of magic in Ephesus, documented in the famous *Ephesia Grammata* — magical formulas associated with the city. Jewish communities in the diaspora existed alongside and sometimes interacted with these magical traditions, despite official prohibitions. The Cairo Genizah has yielded numerous Jewish magical texts from late antiquity, demonstrating that the boundaries between "religion" and "magic" were porous in practice.
