Acts 27 describes Paul's sea voyage to Rome in remarkable nautical detail. Jewish scholars note that the Mediterranean voyage was a common experience for diaspora Jews, who traveled regularly for pilgrimage, commerce, and family connections. Philo describes a delegation of Alexandrian Jews traveling to Rome, and Josephus recounts his own sea voyage to Rome that included a shipwreck (*Life* 13-16), providing an interesting parallel to Paul's experience.

The chapter's detailed description of ancient navigation — sailing along the coast of Asia Minor, the difficulty of the winds near Crete, the use of the ship's tackle and anchors — is valuable for historians of Mediterranean maritime practice. Jewish communities were active in Mediterranean trade, and the diaspora networks depended on sea travel. The Jewish community at Puteoli (28:13-14) was a commercial hub, and Jewish merchants are attested at ports throughout the Mediterranean.

Paul's role during the storm — urging the crew to eat, giving thanks to God, and assuring them of survival (27:33-36) — reflects a pattern of Jewish piety in crisis. The Psalms of distress (Psalm 107:23-32 in particular describes those who go down to the sea in ships) and prayers for travelers are deeply embedded in Jewish liturgical tradition. The safe arrival of all 276 persons fulfills the divine promise and reflects the Jewish theological conviction that God's word, once spoken, is unfailingly accomplished.
