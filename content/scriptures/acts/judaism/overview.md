## Overview

Acts is not a Jewish scripture, but it is of immense significance for Jewish scholarship as a primary source for understanding first-century Judaism. The book portrays synagogue life, Pharisaic theology, Temple worship, Sanhedrin proceedings, and the diversity of Jewish thought in the Second Temple period. Jewish scholars engage Acts critically, both as a historical document and as a text that shaped Christian attitudes toward Judaism for centuries.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### Historical Value
Acts provides evidence for Jewish practices that are otherwise poorly attested:
- Synagogue worship patterns across the Diaspora
- The role of "God-fearers" (sebomenoi/phoboumenoi) — Gentile sympathizers to Judaism
- Pharisaic belief in resurrection (23:6-8)
- Sanhedrin procedures and Temple operations
- Jewish communities in cities like Antioch, Corinth, Ephesus, and Rome

### The Parting of the Ways
Acts narrates a critical period in Jewish-Christian relations. Jewish scholars examine how Acts portrays the progressive separation between the Jesus movement and the synagogue, asking whether this reflects historical reality or later theological projection by the author.

### Representation of Judaism
Jewish scholars have noted that Acts presents Judaism both sympathetically (Gamaliel's wise counsel in 5:33-39, Paul's positive Pharisaic identity in 23:6) and polemically (Stephen's speech in chapter 7, the repeated pattern of Jewish rejection and Gentile acceptance). This ambivalence requires careful historical analysis.

## Theological Emphases

- **Continuity and Discontinuity**: How the early Jesus movement related to broader Judaism
- **The Law**: Acts' portrayal of Torah observance among Jewish believers (15:1-29, 21:20-26)
- **Resurrection**: The Pharisaic doctrine that Paul claims as his own (23:6, 24:15)
- **Messianism**: The christological claims made in Jewish contexts and their reception
- **Diaspora Judaism**: The diversity of Jewish life across the Roman Empire
