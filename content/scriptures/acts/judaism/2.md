Acts 2 describes the Pentecost event, which Jewish scholarship notes falls on the festival of Shavuot — the Feast of Weeks. This is not coincidental. Shavuot commemorates the giving of the Torah at Sinai, and the rabbis associated the event with dramatic divine manifestations: fire, wind, thunder, and the voice of God going out in seventy languages to all the nations (Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 88b). Luke's description of the Spirit's descent with wind and tongues of fire deliberately evokes Sinai imagery, presenting the Spirit as the new Torah written on hearts.

Peter's sermon (2:14-36) is a window into early Jewish-Christian preaching. His citation of Joel 2:28-32, Psalm 16:8-11, and Psalm 110:1 demonstrates the christological hermeneutic that distinguished the Jesus movement from other Jewish groups. Jewish scholars note that these same texts were interpreted very differently in rabbinic tradition: Joel's prophecy was applied to the messianic age in general, and Psalm 110 was read as referring to Abraham or David rather than a divine Messiah. The divergent readings of shared Scripture illuminate the parting of the ways.

The description of the early community (2:42-47) — sharing possessions, meeting in the Temple, breaking bread in homes — portrays a group still functioning within Judaism. They attend Temple worship, observe Jewish customs, and practice a form of communal life that has parallels in the Essene community described by Josephus (*Jewish War* 2.122-127). Jewish historians emphasize that the earliest Jesus movement was a Jewish sect, not a separate religion.
