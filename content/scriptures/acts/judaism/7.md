Acts 7 contains Stephen's speech, a retelling of Israelite history from Abraham to Solomon. Jewish scholars analyze this speech as an example of early Christian reinterpretation of Jewish sacred history. Stephen's selection and arrangement of biblical episodes — emphasizing rejection of God's messengers, the provisional nature of the Temple, and the pattern of Israel's disobedience — reflects a particular hermeneutical strategy aimed at supporting christological claims.

The speech's treatment of Moses is particularly significant. Stephen presents Moses as a prophet rejected by his own people (7:25-29, 35, 39-43), drawing a typological parallel to Jesus. Jewish scholars note that this negative portrayal of Israel's response to Moses stands in tension with the rabbinic tradition, which, while acknowledging the golden calf incident, generally celebrates Moses as Israel's greatest prophet and the faithful shepherd of his people. The Christian reading of Moses-as-rejected-prophet selects from a wider tradition to construct a specific argument.

Stephen's assertion that "the Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands" (7:48) and his accusation that his hearers are "stiff-necked" and "always resist the Holy Spirit" (7:51) have had a troubled legacy in Jewish-Christian relations. Jewish scholars have noted that Stephen's rhetoric, while drawing on prophetic precedent (Isaiah 66:1-2, Jeremiah 7), was later used to justify supersessionist theologies that denied Judaism's ongoing validity. Modern Jewish-Christian dialogue has sought to address this history while respecting the text's original context as an intra-Jewish argument.
