Acts 14 continues Paul's journey through Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe in the region of Lycaonia. Jewish historians note that each city had a Jewish community with a synagogue, reflecting the extensive Jewish diaspora in Asia Minor documented by Josephus and confirmed by archaeological evidence. The Sardis synagogue, one of the largest ancient synagogues discovered, demonstrates the prominence of Jewish communities in this region.

The incident at Lystra, where Paul and Barnabas are mistaken for Zeus and Hermes (14:11-13), illustrates the polytheistic environment in which diaspora Jews lived. Jewish communities in Asia Minor navigated the challenge of maintaining monotheistic faith amid pervasive paganism. The local myth that Zeus and Hermes had once visited the region in human form (recorded by Ovid in *Metamorphoses* 8.611-724) provides the cultural context for the crowd's reaction. Paul's response — calling on the listeners to turn from "vain things to a living God" (14:15) — echoes the standard Jewish polemic against idolatry found in Isaiah 44:9-20 and Wisdom of Solomon 13-15.

The stoning of Paul at Lystra (14:19) and the appointment of elders in each community (14:23) reflect, respectively, a form of mob justice known in both Jewish and Roman contexts, and the organizational patterns of diaspora Jewish communities. Jewish communities were governed by elders (*presbyteroi*, *archontes*), and the early Jesus movement adopted this organizational structure, adapting Jewish communal governance for its own purposes.
