Acts 21 describes Paul's arrival in Jerusalem and his arrest in the Temple. James and the elders inform Paul that thousands of Jewish believers are "all zealous for the law" (21:20) — a detail that Jewish scholars find historically significant. It suggests that the Jerusalem church remained a Torah-observant Jewish community well into the 50s CE, challenging the common assumption that early Christianity quickly abandoned Jewish practice.

Paul's participation in a Nazarite purification rite in the Temple (21:23-26) is one of the most striking details in Acts from a Jewish perspective. The Nazarite vow, described in Numbers 6, involved abstaining from wine and grape products, avoiding contact with the dead, and not cutting one's hair. The completion of the vow required specific sacrifices in the Temple. Paul's willingness to sponsor the purification of four men and to participate himself demonstrates, at minimum, a respectful engagement with Jewish practice that his epistles do not always suggest.

The accusation that Paul brought a Gentile (Trophimus the Ephesian) into the Temple beyond the Court of the Gentiles (21:28-29) reflects the strict purity boundaries of the Temple. Archaeological evidence confirms these boundaries: the Soreg inscription, discovered in 1871, warns in Greek that any non-Jew who passes beyond the barrier does so at the risk of death. Josephus confirms this regulation (*Jewish War* 5.193-194). The charge against Paul, though false (21:29), touches on one of the most sensitive aspects of Jewish sacred space.
