Acts 23 records Paul's appearance before the Sanhedrin and the subsequent conspiracy against him. Paul's opening statement — "Brothers, I have lived my life before God in all good conscience up to this day" (23:1) — and the high priest Ananias' order to strike him (23:2) are evaluated by Jewish scholars in light of Sanhedrin procedure. The Mishnah (*Sanhedrin* 4:1) prescribes careful, respectful treatment of the accused, though the Mishnah may describe ideal rather than actual practice.

Paul's strategic appeal to the Pharisees — "I am a Pharisee, a son of Pharisees. It is with respect to the hope and the resurrection of the dead that I am on trial" (23:6) — exploits the well-documented theological divide between Pharisees and Sadducees. The Pharisaic belief in resurrection, angels, and spirits (23:8) is confirmed by Josephus (*Jewish War* 2.162-163) and became normative in rabbinic Judaism. The Sadducean rejection of these beliefs, tied to their strict adherence to the written Torah alone, disappeared with the destruction of the Temple in 70 CE.

The conspiracy of more than forty men who take an oath to kill Paul (23:12-14) reflects the phenomenon of zealot activism in first-century Palestine. Jewish scholars compare this to the *sicarii* (dagger-men) described by Josephus, who assassinated political and religious targets. The use of an oath (*anathema*) — binding themselves not to eat or drink until they had killed Paul — reflects a pattern of religiously motivated violence that characterized the volatile decades before the Jewish revolt of 66-70 CE.
