Acts 11 describes the reaction to Peter's Gentile mission and the founding of the church at Antioch. The Jerusalem community's initial objection — "You went to uncircumcised men and ate with them" (11:3) — reflects authentic Jewish concerns about table fellowship and purity. The laws of kashrut and the prohibition against eating with Gentiles were not arbitrary restrictions but constitutive practices that defined and maintained Jewish communal identity. Jewish scholars emphasize that the gradual relaxation of these boundaries within the Jesus movement was one of the decisive factors in the separation of Christianity from Judaism.

The Antioch church (11:19-26) is historically significant as the first major center of the Jesus movement outside Palestine. Antioch had a large and diverse Jewish community, described by Josephus (*Jewish War* 7.43-45) as having attracted many Gentile sympathizers. The emergence of a mixed Jewish-Gentile community of Jesus-followers at Antioch, first called "Christians" (11:26), represents a new social formation that transcended the established categories of Judaism and paganism.

The famine relief sent from Antioch to Jerusalem (11:27-30) reflects the networks of mutual aid that connected diaspora Jewish communities. Jewish communities throughout the Roman Empire maintained connections with Jerusalem through the annual Temple tax, pilgrimage, and charitable contributions. The Antioch church's collection mirrors these Jewish patterns of inter-community solidarity while also establishing a distinctly Christian practice of inter-church support.
