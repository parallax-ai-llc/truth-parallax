Acts 15 records the Jerusalem Council, which Jewish scholars consider one of the most consequential moments in the parting of the ways between Judaism and Christianity. The central question — must Gentile believers be circumcised and keep the Law of Moses? (15:1, 5) — is fundamentally a question about Jewish identity: can one belong to the people of God without becoming a Jew?

The council's decision, as articulated by James (15:19-21), imposes four requirements on Gentile believers: abstain from things polluted by idols, from sexual immorality, from things strangled, and from blood (15:20). Jewish scholars have noted the striking similarity between these prohibitions and the rabbinic concept of the Noahide laws — the seven commandments considered binding on all humanity, not just Jews (Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 56a-b). While the exact correspondence is debated, the structural parallel is significant: the Jerusalem Council appears to be applying a Jewish framework for righteous Gentiles to the new situation of Gentile believers.

The council's outcome — Gentiles need not become Jews to be saved — effectively created a new religious category that did not exist in Judaism: righteous Gentiles who are full members of the covenant community without conversion to Judaism. Jewish scholars like Daniel Boyarin have argued that this decision, more than any other single event, marks the emergence of Christianity as a distinct religion separate from Judaism. The implications would unfold over the following centuries as Christianity increasingly defined itself in contrast to, rather than within, Judaism.
