Acts 26 contains Paul's defense before Agrippa, his most detailed account of his conversion and commission. Paul's appeal to Agrippa as one who is "expert in all the customs and controversies of the Jews" (26:3) acknowledges Agrippa's role as a Jewish authority figure. His claim that he is on trial for "the hope of the promise made by God to our fathers" (26:6) frames the entire controversy as an intra-Jewish debate about the fulfillment of Israel's messianic hope.

Paul's description of his former life — "according to the strictest party of our religion I lived as a Pharisee" (26:5) — and his persecution of the Jesus movement (26:9-11) provide valuable information about Pharisaic identity and pre-70 CE sectarian conflict. Jewish scholars note that Paul describes the Pharisees as the "strictest party" (*akribestaten hairesin*), consistent with Josephus' characterization (*Jewish War* 1.110, *Antiquities* 17.41). His account of punishing believers in synagogues (26:11) and pursuing them to foreign cities reflects the synagogue's role as a communal disciplinary institution.

Agrippa's famous response — "In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?" (26:28) — is debated by scholars regarding its tone: sincere interest, polite deflection, or sarcasm. Jewish scholars note that the exchange highlights the moment of decision that the messianic claim posed for every Jew: either Jesus is the promised Messiah, or he is not. The divide between those who accepted and those who rejected this claim would ultimately produce two separate religious traditions from a shared Jewish root.
