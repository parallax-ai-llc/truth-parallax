Acts 24 describes Paul's trial before the Roman governor Felix. The orator Tertullus' prosecution speech (24:1-9) characterizes Paul as "a plague, one who stirs up riots among all the Jews throughout the world, and a ringleader of the sect of the Nazarenes" (24:5). The term "Nazarenes" (*Nazoraioi*) is significant for Jewish scholars: it is the term used for Jewish Christians in later rabbinic literature (*Notzrim*), appearing in the *Birkat haMinim* — the twelfth benediction of the Amidah, which invokes God's judgment against heretics and sectarians.

Paul's defense emphasizes his continuity with Judaism: "I worship the God of our fathers, believing everything laid down by the Law and written in the Prophets, having a hope in God... that there will be a resurrection of both the just and the unjust" (24:14-15). Jewish scholars note that every element of this confession — worship of the God of Israel, acceptance of Torah and Prophets, hope in the resurrection — is compatible with Pharisaic Judaism. The contested point is not these beliefs but their christological application.

Felix's "rather accurate knowledge of the Way" (24:22) and his marriage to Drusilla, "who was Jewish" (24:24), provide evidence for the entanglement of Roman and Jewish interests in first-century Palestine. Drusilla was a daughter of Herod Agrippa I, and Josephus describes her marriage to Felix in detail (*Antiquities* 20.141-144). The Herodian dynasty's intermarriage with Roman officials created a complex web of political and religious relationships that forms the backdrop to Acts' trial narratives.
