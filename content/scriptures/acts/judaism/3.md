Acts 3 describes a healing at the Beautiful Gate of the Temple and Peter's subsequent sermon. The Temple setting is significant for Jewish scholars: the Beautiful Gate (likely the Nicanor Gate between the Court of Women and the Court of Israel) places this event in the heart of Second Temple worship. The lame man's daily presence at the gate for alms-seeking reflects the documented practice of begging at the Temple, attested in rabbinic sources and Josephus.

Peter's sermon in Solomon's Portico (3:12-26) is rich in Jewish theological language. He addresses his audience as "men of Israel," invokes the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and presents Jesus as the prophet like Moses foretold in Deuteronomy 18:15. Jewish scholars note that the "prophet like Moses" expectation appears in the Dead Sea Scrolls (4Q175) and other Second Temple texts, demonstrating that Peter's christological argument operates within a recognizable Jewish framework.

The phrase "times of refreshing" (3:19) and "the time for restoring all the things" (3:21) employ language of eschatological renewal found in Jewish apocalyptic literature. The concept of *tikkun* — cosmic restoration and repair — is central to both rabbinic and mystical Jewish thought. While Christian and Jewish readings of these concepts diverge significantly, the shared vocabulary points to common roots in Second Temple Jewish eschatology.
