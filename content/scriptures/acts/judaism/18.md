Acts 18 describes Paul's ministry in Corinth, where he encounters Aquila and Priscilla — Jewish refugees expelled from Rome by Claudius' edict (18:2). The historian Suetonius records that Claudius "expelled the Jews from Rome because they were constantly making disturbances at the instigation of Chrestus" (*Claudius* 25.4). Jewish scholars debate whether "Chrestus" refers to Christ (suggesting disputes within the Roman synagogues over messianic claims) or to a local agitator by that name. Either way, the passage illustrates the vulnerability of diaspora Jewish communities to imperial action.

Paul's declaration in the Corinthian synagogue — "Your blood be on your own heads... From now on I will go to the Gentiles" (18:6) — marks another instance of the recurring pattern in Acts. Jewish scholars note the biblical resonance of the blood-formula (cf. 2 Samuel 1:16, Ezekiel 33:4) and its subsequent use in Christian anti-Jewish rhetoric. The historical relationship between the Jesus movement and the synagogue was almost certainly more complex and gradual than Acts' dramatic turning points suggest.

The Gallio inscription found at Delphi, dating to 51-52 CE, provides one of the few fixed chronological points for Paul's career. Gallio's refusal to hear the case against Paul (18:14-16) is read by Jewish historians as evidence that Roman officials generally viewed intra-Jewish disputes as matters of internal Jewish jurisdiction. This administrative indifference would later change as Christianity became more clearly distinct from Judaism and required its own legal status.
