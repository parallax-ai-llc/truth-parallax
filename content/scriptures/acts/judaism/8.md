Acts 8 describes the spread of the Jesus movement to Samaria and the conversion of an Ethiopian eunuch. The Samaritan mission is significant for Jewish historians because it crosses one of the most fraught boundaries in Second Temple Judaism. Jewish-Samaritan hostility is well documented in Josephus, the Mishnah, and the Gospels. The Samaritans maintained their own temple on Mount Gerizim (destroyed by John Hyrcanus in 128 BCE) and their own version of the Torah. Philip's mission to Samaria represents a boundary-crossing that was radical even within the context of Jewish sectarian diversity.

The Ethiopian eunuch (8:27-39) is described as having come to Jerusalem "to worship" and reading the prophet Isaiah. Jewish scholars identify him as likely a God-fearer (*yere shamayim*) — a Gentile attracted to Judaism who observed certain practices without full conversion. As a eunuch, he would have been excluded from the congregation of Israel under Deuteronomy 23:1, though Isaiah 56:3-5 promises eunuchs a place in God's house. The tension between these biblical passages reflects ongoing debates within Judaism about the boundaries of the community.

Simon Magus (8:9-24) is a figure who appears in both Jewish and Christian sources. The Jewish-Christian *Pseudo-Clementine* literature elaborates extensively on Simon as a Samaritan wonder-worker and rival of Peter. Jewish scholars note that the first-century Mediterranean world was filled with such figures — healers, exorcists, and charismatic teachers — and the lines between "legitimate" religious authority and "magic" were often contested.
