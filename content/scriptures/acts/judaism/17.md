Acts 17 recounts Paul's preaching in Thessalonica, Berea, and Athens. The pattern of Paul's synagogue preaching — "he reasoned with them from the Scriptures, explaining and proving that it was necessary for the Christ to suffer and to rise from the dead" (17:2-3) — reflects a form of scriptural argumentation familiar from rabbinic debate. The method of opening and explaining (*dianoigon kai paratithemenos*) has parallels in the midrashic practice of expounding Scripture through comparison and juxtaposition of texts.

The accusation at Thessalonica — that Paul and Silas "act against the decrees of Caesar, saying that there is another king, Jesus" (17:7) — reflects the political dimension of messianic claims in the Roman Empire. Jewish communities in the diaspora were vulnerable to charges of disloyalty when messianic movements attracted Roman attention. The Claudian expulsion of Jews from Rome (c. 49 CE, mentioned in Acts 18:2 and by Suetonius) may have been provoked by messianic disturbances within the Roman synagogues. Jewish scholars note that the political fallout of Christian messianic preaching sometimes harmed the broader Jewish community.

Paul's Areopagus speech (17:22-31) is of interest to Jewish scholars as an example of how Jewish monotheistic argumentation was adapted for a pagan audience. The critique of idolatry, the affirmation of one Creator God, and the call to repentance all have deep roots in Jewish apologetic literature, particularly in Hellenistic Jewish writers like Philo of Alexandria and the author of the Wisdom of Solomon. Paul's quotation of Greek poets (17:28) mirrors the practice of Hellenistic Jewish apologists who cited pagan authorities to support monotheistic arguments.
