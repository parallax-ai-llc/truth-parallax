Acts 1 opens with the disciples asking the risen Jesus, "Lord, will you at this time restore the kingdom to Israel?" (1:6). Jewish scholars find this question historically illuminating: it reflects authentic first-century Jewish messianic expectation. The hope for national restoration — the ingathering of exiles, the renewal of Davidic sovereignty, the liberation from Roman rule — was widespread in Second Temple Judaism, attested in the Psalms of Solomon, the Dead Sea Scrolls, and rabbinic literature. The disciples' question places the Jesus movement squarely within this Jewish eschatological framework.

Jesus' response, redirecting attention from political timetables to the coming of the Spirit (1:7-8), represents what Jewish historians see as a critical theological pivot. The messianic hope is not abandoned but redefined: instead of national restoration, the disciples are commissioned as witnesses "to the ends of the earth." This universalizing move — from Israel's restoration to global mission — is one of the defining differences between the Jesus movement and other Jewish messianic movements of the period.

The election of Matthias (1:15-26) through prayer and the casting of lots reflects practices attested in Jewish tradition. The Urim and Thummim were used for divine guidance in the Hebrew Bible, and the casting of lots appears in the selection of the scapegoat on Yom Kippur (Leviticus 16:8). The requirement that the new apostle be a witness to the resurrection (1:21-22) points to the resurrection as the distinguishing claim of the Jesus movement within first-century Judaism.
