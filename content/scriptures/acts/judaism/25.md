Acts 25 describes Paul's appeal to Caesar under the new governor Festus and the introduction of King Agrippa II. Festus' characterization of the dispute — concerning "certain points of disagreement... about their own religion and about a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (25:19) — is historically valuable as a Roman perspective on the Jewish-Christian controversy. The use of the word *deisidaimonia* ("religion" or "superstition," depending on context) reflects Roman attitudes toward Eastern religions.

King Agrippa II (Marcus Julius Agrippa), the last of the Herodian dynasty, is well documented in Josephus. He was granted authority over the Temple and the right to appoint the high priest, giving him significant influence over Jewish religious affairs. His appearance in Acts — showing curiosity about Paul's case and his messianic claims — is consistent with Josephus' portrayal of Agrippa as a figure who navigated between Jewish tradition and Roman allegiance.

Jewish scholars note the irony of Paul's situation: a Pharisee who believes in resurrection and the messianic fulfillment of Israel's Scriptures is judged by a Herodian king whose family's Judaism was always politically calculated, and a Roman governor who finds the entire dispute incomprehensible. The scene illustrates the complex intersection of Jewish theology, Herodian politics, and Roman administration that characterized first-century Palestine.
