Acts 4 records the arrest of Peter and John by the Temple authorities, specifically the Sadducean priestly aristocracy. Jewish historians note that the Sadducees' opposition (4:1-2) was motivated by the apostles' proclamation of resurrection — a doctrine the Sadducees rejected (cf. Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.16). The theological divide between Sadducees and Pharisees on resurrection is well attested in rabbinic literature and provides the backdrop for the conflict in Acts.

The Sanhedrin proceedings described in Acts 4 offer valuable evidence for Jewish legal practice in the Second Temple period, though scholars debate their historical accuracy. The requirement that the apostles be brought before the council, the interrogation procedure, and the deliberation process have parallels in Mishnaic descriptions of Sanhedrin proceedings (*Mishnah Sanhedrin* 4-5), although the Mishnah was codified later and may reflect idealized rather than actual practice.

The description of the community holding "everything in common" (4:32) has drawn comparisons to the Essene community at Qumran. The Dead Sea Scrolls' Community Rule (1QS 6:19-20) describes a similar sharing of property among members. Jewish scholars see this as evidence that the early Jesus movement shared social patterns with other Jewish groups rather than inventing entirely new forms of communal life.
