Acts 16 describes the beginning of Paul's second missionary journey, including the circumcision of Timothy, the Macedonian call, and events at Philippi. Timothy's circumcision (16:3) — performed by Paul "because of the Jews who were in those places" — is a puzzling detail given Paul's argument against circumcision for Gentile believers. Jewish scholars note that Timothy had a Jewish mother and a Greek father (16:1), making him halakhically Jewish by the matrilineal principle that became standard in rabbinic Judaism. Paul circumcises Timothy not to make him a Christian but to regularize his Jewish status.

The reference to a "place of prayer" (*proseuche*) by the river at Philippi (16:13) is significant for Jewish social history. Synagogues in the diaspora were often located near water sources for the purpose of ritual immersion (*mikveh*). Josephus and Philo both mention *proseuchai* (prayer houses) as the standard term for synagogues in certain regions. The small gathering of women at this location suggests a Jewish community too small for a full synagogue, which traditionally required ten adult Jewish males (*minyan*).

The slave girl with a "spirit of divination" (*pneuma pythona* — literally a "Python spirit," 16:16) reflects the widespread phenomenon of oracular practices in the Greco-Roman world. Jewish attitudes toward such phenomena were consistently negative, categorizing them as either demonic or fraudulent. The Mishnah (*Sanhedrin* 7:7) and other rabbinic texts prohibit divination and necromancy, placing them among the most serious violations of Torah. Paul's exorcism of the spirit (16:18) is consistent with Jewish practice of expelling evil spirits through the invocation of a divine name.
