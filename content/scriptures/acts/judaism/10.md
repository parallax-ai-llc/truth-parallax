Acts 10 describes Peter's vision and his visit to Cornelius, a Roman centurion described as a "God-fearer" (*phoboumenos ton theon*). The category of God-fearers is well attested in both literary and epigraphic evidence. The Aphrodisias inscription, discovered in 1976, lists both Jews and "God-worshippers" (*theosebeis*) as donors to a Jewish charitable institution, confirming the existence of Gentile sympathizers attached to synagogues across the diaspora.

Peter's vision of clean and unclean animals (10:9-16) directly addresses the Jewish dietary laws (*kashrut*). The divine command to "kill and eat" what the Torah declares unclean represents, from a Jewish perspective, a radical departure from halakhah. Jewish scholars note that the text itself acknowledges how shocking this is — Peter resists three times, saying "I have never eaten anything that is common or unclean" (10:14). The vision's interpretation is not about food per se but about the inclusion of Gentiles, yet its imagery deliberately evokes the boundary markers that defined Jewish identity.

The episode is foundational for understanding the "parting of the ways" between Judaism and Christianity. The abandonment of kashrut and circumcision as requirements for membership in the community of salvation marks a decisive departure from Jewish practice. Jewish scholars like Shaye Cohen have analyzed how this boundary-dissolution transformed the Jesus movement from a Jewish sect into a distinct religion. The question debated in Acts 10-11 — whether Gentiles must become Jews to be saved — is ultimately answered in a way that separates Christianity from Judaism's communal boundaries.
