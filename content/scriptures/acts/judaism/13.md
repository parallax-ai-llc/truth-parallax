Acts 13 describes the beginning of Paul's missionary activity, starting from Antioch and moving to Cyprus and Pisidian Antioch. Jewish scholars note that Paul's missionary strategy consistently begins with the synagogue (13:5, 14), reflecting his ongoing connection to the Jewish community and the sociological reality that diaspora synagogues provided a ready audience of Jews and God-fearers receptive to messianic claims.

Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch (13:16-41) is addressed to "men of Israel and you who fear God" — Jews and God-fearers together. The sermon follows a pattern recognizable in Jewish homiletical tradition: a retelling of salvation history leading to a theological conclusion. Jewish scholars compare it to the synagogue homily form described in later midrashic literature, though they note that Paul's christological conclusion — identifying Jesus as the fulfillment of the Davidic promise — represents a distinctly sectarian application of the tradition.

The response pattern described at the end of the chapter — initial interest from Jews and God-fearers, followed by Jewish opposition and Paul's turn to Gentiles (13:44-52) — is a recurring motif in Acts that Jewish scholars evaluate critically. Some see it as reflecting historical reality: the Jesus movement's message was attractive to some Jews and God-fearers but divisive within synagogue communities. Others see it as a Lukan literary construction that serves the theological purpose of explaining and justifying the Gentile mission.
