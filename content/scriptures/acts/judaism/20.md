Acts 20 contains Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders and his journey toward Jerusalem. The gathering on "the first day of the week" (20:7) to break bread is noted by Jewish scholars as evidence of the emerging Christian practice of Sunday worship, which would eventually replace Sabbath observance as the primary day of assembly. The transition from Sabbath to Sunday is one of the most significant markers of Christianity's separation from Jewish practice, though the process was gradual and the Sabbath continued to be observed by some Jewish Christians for centuries.

Paul's farewell speech at Miletus (20:17-38) draws on the literary form of the farewell discourse, well established in Jewish literature — Jacob's blessing (Genesis 49), Moses' farewell (Deuteronomy 33), Joshua's final address (Joshua 23-24), and the testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs. Jewish scholars recognize the genre and note that Paul's speech follows the conventions: looking back on a faithful ministry, warning of future dangers, and committing the community to God's care.

Paul's determination to reach Jerusalem for Pentecost (Shavuot) (20:16) reflects his continued participation in the Jewish festival calendar. Jewish scholars point to this and other details — Paul's Nazarite vow (18:18), his Temple participation (21:23-26), his observance of the days of Unleavened Bread (20:6) — as evidence that Paul maintained a Jewish lifestyle even as he argued against requiring Torah observance from Gentile converts. The image of Paul that emerges from Acts is more traditionally Jewish than the Paul of the epistles, a tension that Jewish and Christian scholars continue to debate.
