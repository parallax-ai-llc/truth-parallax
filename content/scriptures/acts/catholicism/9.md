Acts 9 records the conversion of Saul on the Damascus road, an event Catholic tradition commemorates liturgically on January 25 (the Feast of the Conversion of Saint Paul). Catholic theology reads this conversion as a sovereign act of grace that transforms the persecutor into the apostle, while also affirming Paul's free cooperation with that grace. The Council of Trent taught that God's grace precedes, accompanies, and follows the human will, and Paul's conversion illustrates this dynamic.

Ananias' role in Paul's conversion (9:10-19) is significant for Catholic ecclesiology. Paul does not simply receive a private revelation and begin his ministry; he is directed to Ananias, who baptizes him and lays hands on him. Even the greatest apostle enters the Church through the sacramental mediation of another believer. This underscores the Catholic conviction that salvation, while initiated by divine grace, is ordinarily mediated through the Church and its sacraments.

The parallel accounts of Peter's ministry in Lydda and Joppa (9:32-43) — healing Aeneas and raising Tabitha — demonstrate the apostolic power that Catholic tradition sees as continuing in the Church through the sacraments, the intercession of saints, and the charisms of the Holy Spirit. Peter's command to Tabitha, "Get up" (9:40), echoes Jesus' words to Jairus' daughter, reinforcing the Catholic understanding that the apostles exercise Christ's authority in his name.
