Acts 18 records Paul's ministry in Corinth and the introduction of Apollos. Paul's eighteen months in Corinth (18:11) demonstrate the Catholic principle that evangelization requires patient, sustained presence rather than brief proclamation. The Lord's night vision to Paul — "Do not be afraid... for I have many in this city who are my people" (18:9-10) — affirms the Catholic teaching that God's elect are found in every place and culture.

The Gallio incident (18:12-17) — the Roman proconsul's refusal to adjudicate religious disputes — has been significant in Catholic reflection on Church-state relations. While the Catholic Church has historically sought cooperation between spiritual and temporal authority, the Gallio episode illustrates the distinction between religious and civil jurisdiction. The Second Vatican Council's *Dignitatis Humanae* (1965) affirmed religious freedom as a civil right, while maintaining that the Catholic Church possesses the fullness of religious truth.

Apollos' instruction by Priscilla and Aquila (18:24-28) demonstrates the Catholic understanding that catechesis is an ongoing process. Even an eloquent and learned man needs fuller instruction in the faith. The Catholic catechetical tradition — from the Didache and the Catechetical School of Alexandria to the modern RCIA (Rite of Christian Initiation of Adults) — finds in this passage a precedent for the Church's responsibility to form believers progressively in the faith.
