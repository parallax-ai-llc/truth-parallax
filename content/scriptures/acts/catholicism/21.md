Acts 21 describes Paul's journey to Jerusalem, his meeting with James and the elders, and his arrest in the Temple. Catholic interpreters note Paul's willingness to undergo a purification rite in the Temple (21:23-26) as evidence of the Catholic principle of accommodation — the gospel does not require the destruction of cultural and religious practices but their transformation and elevation. Paul is no anarchist; he works within Jewish custom while maintaining the freedom of the gospel for Gentiles.

The prophetic warnings about Paul's suffering in Jerusalem (21:10-14) and his determination to go nevertheless are read in Catholic tradition through the lens of the theology of vocation. Paul's calling includes suffering, and his obedience to that calling mirrors Christ's own journey to Jerusalem. The Catholic theology of redemptive suffering, articulated in John Paul II's *Salvifici Doloris* (1984), finds a model in Paul's willing acceptance of the cross.

Paul's arrest in the Temple (21:27-40) begins his passion narrative, which Catholic typological interpretation reads as a deliberate Lukan parallel to the passion of Christ. Like Jesus, Paul is seized by a mob in the Temple precincts, accused of violating the holy place, and handed over to Roman authority. The parallel underscores the Catholic conviction that the disciple's life conforms to the pattern of the Master — suffering is not a deviation from the Christian calling but its deepest expression.
