Acts 8 describes the expansion of the gospel to Samaria through Philip's ministry, the encounter with Simon Magus, and the baptism of the Ethiopian eunuch. Catholic ecclesiology sees the movement from Jerusalem to Samaria as the progressive realization of the Church's catholicity — its universal mission to all peoples and places.

The Simon Magus episode (8:9-24) is the origin of the canonical crime of simony — the buying or selling of spiritual goods and ecclesiastical offices. Catholic canon law has consistently condemned this practice, and the scene in Acts 8 has been cited in every major reform movement within the Church. The distinction between Philip's baptism and the apostles' laying on of hands (8:14-17) is a key proof-text for the Catholic teaching that Confirmation is a sacrament distinct from Baptism, conferred by the bishop (successor of the apostles) through the imposition of hands.

The Ethiopian eunuch's encounter with Philip (8:26-40) illustrates several Catholic principles: the necessity of authoritative interpretation ("How can I, unless someone guides me?" — 8:31), the christological reading of the Old Testament (Philip explains Isaiah 53 as referring to Christ), and the efficacy of baptism. The eunuch's immediate baptism upon confession of faith demonstrates the Church's understanding that baptism is the gateway to all other sacraments and the necessary beginning of the Christian life.
