Acts 22 presents Paul's defense speech to the Jerusalem crowd, recounting his conversion and calling. Catholic theology reads Paul's testimony as an account of sacramental transformation: the encounter with Christ on the road (22:6-11), Ananias' mediation of healing and the Holy Spirit (22:12-13), and baptism with the washing away of sins (22:16) form a unified sacramental event that incorporates Paul into the Body of Christ.

Paul's account of Ananias' role — "The God of our fathers appointed you" (22:14) — demonstrates the Catholic principle that divine calling is mediated through the Church. Paul's extraordinary experience on the Damascus road does not exempt him from the ordinary means of grace: he must be baptized, he must receive the ministry of another believer, he must be sent by the Church. Even apostolic authority is exercised within the communion of the Church, not independently of it.

Paul's appeal to his Roman citizenship (22:25-29) illustrates the Catholic teaching on the dignity of civil law and the rights of persons. The Church teaches that human rights are grounded in the natural law and in the dignity of the human person created in God's image. Paul's use of his legal rights to protect himself and advance the gospel demonstrates that faith and civic responsibility are complementary, not contradictory — a principle developed in the Second Vatican Council's *Gaudium et Spes*.
