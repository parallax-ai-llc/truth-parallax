Acts 3 presents Peter and John healing a lame man at the Beautiful Gate and Peter's sermon in Solomon's Portico. Catholic sacramental theology sees the healing as an exercise of apostolic authority that prefigures the Church's ministry of healing, including the Anointing of the Sick. Peter's words "In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, rise up and walk" (3:6) demonstrate that the apostles act in the person of Christ (*in persona Christi*), a principle extended to ordained ministers in Catholic theology.

Peter's sermon (3:12-26) presents Jesus as the fulfillment of the Mosaic covenant and the prophetic tradition. His call to repentance "that your sins may be blotted out" (3:19) is connected in Catholic teaching to the sacrament of Reconciliation. The "times of refreshing" that come from the Lord are understood as the grace mediated through the sacraments, which continually renew the faithful.

The Fathers of the Church, particularly St. John Chrysostom, noted that Peter attributes the healing not to his own power or piety but to the name of Jesus (3:12), modeling the priestly role as instrumental — the minister is a channel of divine grace, not its source. This is central to the Catholic understanding of *ex opere operato*: the sacraments are effective by virtue of Christ's action, not the minister's holiness.
