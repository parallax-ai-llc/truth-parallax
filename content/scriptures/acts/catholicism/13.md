Acts 13 marks the beginning of Paul's first missionary journey, inaugurated by the Holy Spirit's command to the church at Antioch: "Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them" (13:2). Catholic missiology sees here the model of missionary vocation: the call comes from the Spirit, is discerned within the worshiping community, and is ratified through liturgical commissioning (fasting, prayer, and the laying on of hands).

Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch (13:16-41) is his first major address and demonstrates the Catholic principle of reading the Old Testament christologically. Paul traces salvation history from the Exodus through David to Jesus, showing that the gospel is the fulfillment of Israel's hope. His declaration that through Jesus "forgiveness of sins is proclaimed" (13:38) is connected in Catholic theology to the sacrament of Reconciliation — the ongoing ministry of forgiveness in the Church.

The pattern of Jewish rejection and Gentile acceptance (13:44-52) raises questions about Israel's place in God's plan. Catholic teaching, following *Nostra Aetate* (4) and subsequent papal statements, affirms that God's covenant with Israel has not been revoked. The turn to the Gentiles is not a replacement of Israel but the extension of God's salvific will to all nations, in fulfillment of the Abrahamic promise.
