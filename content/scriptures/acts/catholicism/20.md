Acts 20 contains Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders at Miletus, a passage of central importance for Catholic pastoral theology. Paul's charge — "Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood" (20:28) — is foundational for the Catholic understanding of the episcopate. The bishops are guardians appointed by the Holy Spirit, responsible for the pastoral care of the Church purchased by Christ's blood.

The "breaking of bread" on the first day of the week at Troas (20:7) is read in Catholic tradition as a reference to the Eucharist celebrated on Sunday. This passage, together with Acts 2:42 and 1 Corinthians 11:23-26, is cited as evidence that the Eucharistic celebration was central to the apostolic Church from its earliest days. The raising of Eutychus after his fall during Paul's lengthy sermon (20:9-12) is a vivid detail that Catholic commentators read both literally and symbolically — the Eucharist is the source of life, even in the midst of death.

Paul's warning about "fierce wolves" entering the flock (20:29-30) has been invoked throughout Catholic history to justify the Magisterium's role in defending orthodoxy against heresy. The teaching authority of the bishops is not a power grab but a pastoral necessity — the flock requires shepherds who will guard the deposit of faith (*depositum fidei*) entrusted to the Church. Paul's example of economic self-sufficiency (20:33-35) also informs Catholic teaching on clerical integrity.
