Acts 25 describes Paul's appeal to Caesar and Festus' consultation with King Agrippa. Paul's appeal (25:10-11) is significant for Catholic political theology. The Catholic tradition, following Thomas Aquinas, teaches that civil authority is ordained by God for the common good, and citizens may legitimately appeal to that authority for the protection of their rights. Paul's appeal to Caesar does not represent a lack of faith in God's protection but a prudent use of the legitimate political order.

Festus' characterization of the dispute as concerning "a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (25:19) is an unwitting summary of the Christian kerygma from an outsider's perspective. Catholic apologetics notes that even hostile witnesses confirm the centrality of the resurrection claim. The gospel is not a system of ethics or a philosophical school but the proclamation of a historical event — the resurrection of Jesus from the dead.

The introduction of King Agrippa and Bernice (25:13) sets the stage for Paul's climactic defense speech. Catholic interpreters observe the irony of the scene: Paul, the prisoner, is the true king who possesses the truth, while the political authorities who sit in judgment are spiritually impoverished. This inversion of worldly power — the last shall be first — is a constant theme in Catholic theology and is embodied in the Church's preferential option for the poor.
