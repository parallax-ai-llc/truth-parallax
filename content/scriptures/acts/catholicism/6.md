Acts 6 narrates the institution of the diaconate — the appointment of seven men to serve the community's material needs. Catholic theology sees this as the origin of the sacrament of Holy Orders in its diaconal grade. The laying on of the apostles' hands (6:6) is the sacramental gesture that confers the grace of ordination, a practice that continues in the Church's ordination rites today. The Second Vatican Council's restoration of the permanent diaconate (*Lumen Gentium* 29) explicitly appealed to Acts 6.

Stephen, the first of the Seven, is described as "full of faith and of the Holy Spirit" (6:5) and "full of grace and power" (6:8). Catholic hagiography venerates Stephen as the protomartyr — the first Christian martyr — and his feast day (December 26) places him immediately after the Nativity of Christ, linking the birth of the Savior to the first witness who gave his life. His face, shining "like the face of an angel" (6:15), is understood as a visible manifestation of sanctifying grace.

The accusation that Stephen spoke against the Temple and the Law (6:13-14) raises the question of the relationship between the old and new covenants. Catholic theology, following *Dei Verbum* (16), teaches that the New Testament is hidden in the Old and the Old is made manifest in the New. Stephen's ministry embodies this transition: he honors the tradition while proclaiming its fulfillment in Christ.
