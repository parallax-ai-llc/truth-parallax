Acts 14 continues the first missionary journey through Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe. The varied responses to the gospel — belief, opposition, attempted idolatry — illustrate the Church's perennial experience in its missionary encounter with diverse cultures.

At Lystra, the healing of a lame man leads the crowd to worship Paul and Barnabas as Zeus and Hermes (14:11-13). Their vigorous refusal — tearing their garments and crying "We also are men, of like nature with you" (14:15) — is cited in Catholic teaching to distinguish proper veneration of saints from idolatry. The saints are honored not as gods but as fellow servants through whom God works. Paul's speech about God's witness in creation (14:15-17) demonstrates what the First Vatican Council (*Dei Filius*) would later define: that God can be known with certainty through the natural light of reason from the things that have been made.

The appointment of elders in every church (14:23) through prayer and fasting is foundational for Catholic holy orders. Catholic theology sees the apostolic appointment of presbyters as the origin of the presbyterate, which, together with the episcopate and diaconate, constitutes the three grades of the sacrament of Holy Orders. Paul's statement that "through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God" (14:22) resonates with the Catholic theology of redemptive suffering.
