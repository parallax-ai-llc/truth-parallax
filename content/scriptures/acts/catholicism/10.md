Acts 10 records Peter's vision and his encounter with Cornelius, the Roman centurion. Catholic interpretation sees this chapter as a decisive moment in the Church's self-understanding: the gospel is for all nations, and the Church is catholic by nature, not merely by aspiration. The Second Vatican Council's *Ad Gentes* (2) grounds the Church's missionary mandate in this universal divine will.

Peter's vision of the sheet with unclean animals (10:9-16) is interpreted in Catholic theology not as the abolition of all Old Testament law but as the removal of the ritual boundary between Jew and Gentile. The dietary laws served their purpose in separating Israel from the nations; now, in Christ, that separation is transcended. The Church teaches that the moral law remains binding, while the ceremonial law is fulfilled and transcended in the new covenant.

The descent of the Holy Spirit on Cornelius' household before baptism (10:44-48) is a unique event that Catholic theology reads as God's extraordinary initiative confirming what the Church would then regularize sacramentally. Peter's command to baptize them (10:48) shows that even when the Spirit acts outside the normal sacramental order, the sacraments remain necessary. The Catechism (CCC 1257) teaches that God has bound salvation to the sacrament of Baptism, but God himself is not bound by his sacraments.
