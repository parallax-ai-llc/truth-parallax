Acts 16 opens the second missionary journey with significant events: the circumcision of Timothy, the Macedonian call, and the founding of the church at Philippi. The Spirit's direction of the mission — forbidding Paul to preach in Asia and redirecting him to Macedonia (16:6-10) — illustrates the Catholic understanding that the Church's mission is not merely a human enterprise but is guided by the Holy Spirit working through the community's discernment.

The conversion of Lydia (16:14-15) — "The Lord opened her heart" — is read in Catholic theology as an illustration of prevenient grace: God's initiative precedes and enables the human response of faith. Lydia's household baptism (16:15) is cited as evidence for the Catholic practice of infant baptism, since "household" (*oikos*) would typically include children and infants. The Catechism (CCC 1252) notes that household baptisms in Acts suggest the inclusion of infants from the earliest period.

The Philippian jailer's conversion and household baptism (16:30-34) further supports this reading. Catholic interpreters also note that Paul and Silas' singing hymns in prison at midnight (16:25) prefigures the Church's Liturgy of the Hours — the sanctification of time through prayer and praise at fixed intervals, including the night office (Matins). The earthquake and the opening of prison doors (16:26) are signs of divine power that confirm the apostolic mission.
