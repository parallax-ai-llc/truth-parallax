Acts 15 records the Council of Jerusalem, which Catholic tradition considers the first ecumenical council and the prototype for all subsequent councils, from Nicaea to Vatican II. The question of Gentile circumcision is resolved through the authoritative discernment of the apostles and elders, guided by the Holy Spirit. The council's declaration — "it has seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (15:28) — is the foundational text for the Catholic understanding of conciliar authority and infallibility.

Peter's role at the council (15:7-11) is read as an exercise of Petrine primacy. Peter speaks first and decisively, declaring that God made no distinction between Jew and Gentile and that salvation comes by grace. James then confirms Peter's testimony and proposes practical measures (15:13-21). Catholic ecclesiology sees here the interplay of papal primacy and episcopal collegiality: Peter leads, the other apostles contribute, and the whole body acts in concert under the Spirit's guidance.

The apostolic decree's four prohibitions (15:29) are understood as pastoral accommodations for the sake of Jewish-Gentile communion, not as binding moral law superseding grace. The council's letter, sent to the churches through delegates (15:22-29), establishes the pattern of conciliar decrees communicated to the universal Church — a practice the Catholic Church has maintained through its ecumenical councils. The council demonstrates that doctrine develops through the Church's lived experience and the Spirit's guidance, not by individual interpretation alone.
