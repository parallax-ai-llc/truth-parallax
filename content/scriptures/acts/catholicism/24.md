Acts 24 presents Paul's trial before the governor Felix. The accusation by Tertullus (24:1-9) that Paul is a "ringleader of the sect of the Nazarenes" reflects the early perception of Christianity as a Jewish sect. Catholic historical theology notes that the gradual differentiation of Christianity from Judaism was a complex process, and the Catholic Church's identity emerged through centuries of doctrinal development guided by the Holy Spirit.

Paul's defense (24:10-21) affirms his continuity with the Jewish faith: he worships the God of the fathers, believes in the resurrection, and maintains a clear conscience before God and men. Catholic moral theology values Paul's emphasis on conscience (24:16) — the Catechism (CCC 1776-1802) teaches that conscience is the inner voice through which God speaks to the human heart, and every person has the duty to follow a well-formed conscience.

Felix's response — trembling at Paul's teaching on "righteousness, self-control, and the coming judgment" (24:25) but ultimately delaying decision — is read in Catholic pastoral theology as a warning about the danger of procrastination in responding to grace. The Catholic tradition of examination of conscience and the sacrament of Reconciliation exists precisely to prevent the hardening of heart that comes from repeated deferral. Felix's hope for a bribe (24:26) represents the corruption that always threatens when temporal power encounters spiritual truth.
