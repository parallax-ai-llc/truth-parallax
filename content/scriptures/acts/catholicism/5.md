Acts 5 presents the judgment on Ananias and Sapphira and the continued growth and persecution of the apostolic community. Catholic moral theology reads the story of Ananias and Sapphira as a warning about sacrilege — the violation of what has been consecrated to God. Their sin is not merely dishonesty but the profanation of a sacred commitment, analogous to the sin of simony that the Church has consistently condemned.

The apostles' healing ministry (5:12-16), including the remarkable detail that Peter's shadow heals the sick, has been cited in Catholic devotional tradition as a precedent for the veneration of relics and the mediation of grace through physical objects and persons. While Protestant critics see superstition, Catholic theology sees the principle of incarnation extended: God works through material means, including the bodies and belongings of the saints.

Gamaliel's counsel (5:33-39) is read in Catholic tradition as an instance of providential wisdom. The Catholic Church has historically applied Gamaliel's logic to its own endurance: institutions of merely human origin fail, but the Church has endured two millennia of persecution and crisis because it is of God. The apostles' response — "We must obey God rather than men" (5:29) — is cited in Catholic moral theology as the basis for conscientious objection when civil law contradicts divine law.
