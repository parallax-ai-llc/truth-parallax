Acts 19 describes Paul's extended ministry in Ephesus, where the gospel encounters both incomplete faith and pagan idolatry. The twelve disciples who had received only John's baptism (19:1-7) and their subsequent baptism in the name of Jesus and reception of the Holy Spirit through Paul's laying on of hands are cited in Catholic sacramental theology as evidence for the distinction between Baptism and Confirmation. The laying on of hands confers a distinct gift of the Spirit beyond what baptism alone provides.

The sons of Sceva episode (19:13-16) illustrates the Catholic distinction between authentic sacramental authority and superstitious imitation. The sacraments are effective because they are performed within the Church by authorized ministers acting in Christ's name, not because of magical formulae. The subsequent burning of magical books (19:19) demonstrates genuine conversion — the incompatibility of Christian faith with occult practices. The Catechism (CCC 2117) condemns all forms of divination and sorcery as contradicting the honor and respect owed to God alone.

The riot of the silversmiths (19:23-41) reveals the social and economic dimensions of evangelization. Catholic social teaching recognizes that the gospel challenges unjust economic systems, including those built on idolatry. The Church's prophetic ministry — calling cultures and economies to conform to divine justice — inevitably provokes resistance from those whose material interests are threatened.
