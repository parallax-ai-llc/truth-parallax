Acts 11 describes Peter's defense of his actions at Cornelius' house and the founding of the church at Antioch. Peter's report to the Jerusalem community (11:1-18) is read in Catholic ecclesiology as an exercise of Petrine authority: Peter acts first on divine instruction, then explains and receives the community's acceptance. His account is not submitted for approval but offered as testimony to what God has done, and the community recognizes God's work through Peter's leadership.

The Antioch church (11:19-26) holds a special place in Catholic tradition as one of the ancient patriarchal sees. Tradition holds that Peter served as the first bishop of Antioch before going to Rome — a claim that connects the Petrine ministry to the Church's expansion beyond Jerusalem. The fact that believers were first called "Christians" at Antioch (11:26) suggests that the movement had become recognizable as a distinct community, not merely a Jewish sect.

The famine relief sent from Antioch to Jerusalem through Barnabas and Saul (11:27-30) exemplifies the communion (*koinonia*) between local churches that Catholic ecclesiology sees as constitutive of the universal Church. Each local church is not an autonomous entity but part of the one Body of Christ, sharing resources and bearing one another's burdens. This principle undergirds the Catholic understanding of the universal Church as a communion of particular churches.
