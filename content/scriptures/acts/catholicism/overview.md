## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Acts reads the book as the foundational charter of the Church. Acts demonstrates the continuity between Jesus' ministry and the apostolic community, establishing the patterns of sacramental life, hierarchical authority, and missionary expansion that the Catholic Church claims to have preserved through apostolic succession. The Magisterium and the Church Fathers provide the authoritative framework for interpretation.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Apostolic Succession
Acts records the appointment of Matthias to replace Judas (1:15-26), the ordination of the Seven (6:1-6), and the appointment of elders (14:23). Catholic theology sees these as the origins of the episcopal, presbyteral, and diaconal orders that continue in the Church today.

### Petrine Primacy
Peter's leadership in the early chapters — presiding over the election of Matthias, preaching at Pentecost, making the first Gentile convert — is read as confirmation of Christ's commission in Matthew 16:18 and the foundation of papal authority.

### Sacramental Life
The breaking of bread (2:42, 20:7), baptism (2:38, 8:36-38), the laying on of hands (8:17, 19:6), and anointing of the sick (implied in the apostolic healings) are seen as the apostolic origins of the seven sacraments.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Church as the Body of Christ**: Acts narrates the birth and growth of the visible, institutional Church
- **Sacred Tradition**: The apostles' teaching (2:42) as the foundation of Tradition alongside Scripture
- **The Eucharist**: The "breaking of bread" as the earliest form of the Mass
- **Confirmation**: The laying on of hands for the reception of the Holy Spirit as a distinct sacrament
- **Magisterial Authority**: The Jerusalem Council (chapter 15) as the prototype of ecumenical councils
