Acts 1 records the ascension of Jesus and the election of Matthias to replace Judas among the Twelve. Catholic theology reads the ascension as Christ's entry into heavenly glory, from which he continues to govern the Church through the Holy Spirit and the apostolic ministry. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 659-667) teaches that the ascension completes Christ's earthly mission and inaugurates his heavenly intercession.

The election of Matthias (1:15-26) is foundational for the Catholic doctrine of apostolic succession. Peter presides over the selection, exercising the primacy entrusted to him by Christ. The requirement that the candidate be a witness to the resurrection and the use of prayer and lots demonstrate that apostolic office is both a divine gift and a communal act of the Church. The Catholic Church sees in this passage the origin of episcopal election — the choosing of successors to carry on the apostolic ministry.

The promise of the Holy Spirit (1:4-8) points toward Pentecost and, in Catholic sacramental theology, toward the sacrament of Confirmation. The apostles are told to wait for power from on high before beginning their mission, establishing the principle that the Church's witness depends not on human effort alone but on the gift of the Spirit.
