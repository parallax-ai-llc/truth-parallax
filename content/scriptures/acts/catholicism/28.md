Acts 28 concludes with Paul's arrival on Malta, his journey to Rome, and his preaching while under house arrest. The viper incident on Malta (28:3-6) is read in Catholic tradition as a sign of apostolic authority and the power of God over nature. Catholic hagiography is filled with similar accounts of saints protected from harm, understood not as magical tales but as manifestations of divine grace working through holy persons.

Paul's meeting with the Jewish leaders in Rome (28:17-28) and his citation of Isaiah 6:9-10 bring Acts' theological narrative to its climax. Catholic theology, following Romans 9-11 and *Nostra Aetate* (4), reads Paul's turn to the Gentiles not as a permanent rejection of Israel but as a mystery within God's providential plan. The Church affirms that God's covenant with the Jewish people has never been revoked and looks forward to the eschatological reconciliation of all God's people.

The book's final image — Paul "proclaiming the kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ with all boldness and without hindrance" (28:31) — is read in Catholic ecclesiology as a description of the Church's permanent mission. The gospel has arrived in Rome, the center of the world, and from Rome the Church will continue its mission to the ends of the earth. The open ending of Acts is not a literary failure but a theological statement: the Church's mission continues through the apostolic succession, the sacraments, and the guidance of the Holy Spirit until the Lord returns.
