Acts 2 narrates the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, which Catholic tradition celebrates as the birthday of the Church. The visible signs — wind, fire, and tongues — manifest the Spirit's transforming power and inaugurate the Church's public mission. The Second Vatican Council's *Lumen Gentium* (4) teaches that the Spirit was sent "on the day of Pentecost in order that He might continually sanctify the Church."

Peter's sermon (2:14-36) is the first exercise of the Petrine teaching office. Standing with the Eleven, Peter authoritatively interprets the event through Scripture and proclaims the kerygma — the death, resurrection, and lordship of Jesus Christ. Catholic ecclesiology sees here the prototype of papal and episcopal teaching: the authorized proclamation of the faith by those ordained to the apostolic office.

The response — "they were cut to the heart" and asked "what shall we do?" (2:37) — and Peter's answer — "Repent and be baptized... for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" (2:38) — is the foundational text for Catholic baptismal theology. Baptism effects the forgiveness of sins and confers the Holy Spirit. The description of the early community devoted to "the apostles' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers" (2:42) establishes the four pillars of Catholic parish life: catechesis, community, Eucharist, and liturgy.
