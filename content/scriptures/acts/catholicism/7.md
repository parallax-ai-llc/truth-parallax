Acts 7 contains Stephen's speech before the Sanhedrin, a sweeping review of Israel's history from Abraham to Solomon. Catholic interpretation, guided by the Church Fathers, reads this speech as a demonstration of salvation history (*heilsgeschichte*) — the continuous unfolding of God's plan through chosen instruments, despite Israel's repeated resistance. The speech's typological structure — Joseph rejected by his brothers, Moses rejected by his people — prefigures the rejection of Christ.

Stephen's declaration that "the Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands" (7:48) is not read in Catholic theology as a rejection of sacred architecture or liturgical worship. Rather, as Chrysostom taught, it corrects the misunderstanding that God is confined to the Temple. The Catholic Church builds churches and cathedrals as houses of prayer and Eucharistic presence, while affirming that God transcends all created spaces. The tabernacle in a Catholic church is understood as a continuation of God's dwelling among his people, not a claim to contain the infinite.

Stephen's martyrdom (7:54-60) is the paradigm of Christian witness in Catholic tradition. His vision of the heavens opened and the Son of Man at God's right hand (7:55-56), his commendation of his spirit to the Lord Jesus, and his prayer for his persecutors (7:60) mirror the death of Christ himself. The Church teaches that the blood of martyrs is the seed of the Church (*sanguis martyrum semen Christianorum*), and Stephen's death leads directly to the gospel's spread beyond Jerusalem.
