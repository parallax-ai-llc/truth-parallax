Acts 17 describes Paul's ministry in Thessalonica, Berea, and Athens. The Bereans' careful examination of Scripture (17:11) is valued in Catholic tradition, though the Catholic reading emphasizes that Scripture is properly interpreted within the living Tradition of the Church and under the guidance of the Magisterium. The Bereans did not interpret privately but searched the Scriptures communally and in light of apostolic preaching.

Paul's Areopagus speech (17:22-31) is a foundational text for Catholic natural theology and the relationship between faith and reason. Paul begins with what his audience already knows — the unknown god, the witness of creation, the insights of pagan poets — and leads them toward the revelation of Christ. The First Vatican Council's constitution *Dei Filius* (1870) affirmed that the existence of God can be known with certainty by the natural light of human reason, and Paul's Athens speech is the scriptural basis for this teaching. The Second Vatican Council's *Gaudium et Spes* (36) further develops this principle of engaging culture and philosophy in service of the faith.

Paul's declaration that God "made from one man every nation of mankind" (17:26) is cited in Catholic social teaching as the basis for the unity and equality of all peoples. *Nostra Aetate* (1) and subsequent papal documents affirm that all people share a common origin and destiny, and that seeds of truth (*semina Verbi*) may be found in non-Christian religions and philosophies, as Paul recognized in Athens.
