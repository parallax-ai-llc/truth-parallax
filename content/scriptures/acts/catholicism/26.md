Acts 26 contains Paul's defense before King Agrippa, his most complete account of his conversion and mission. Paul's statement that he has proclaimed "nothing but what the prophets and Moses said would come to pass: that the Christ must suffer and that, by being the first to rise from the dead, he would proclaim light both to our people and to the Gentiles" (26:22-23) is a concise summary of the Catholic hermeneutical principle: the Old Testament is fulfilled in Christ, and Christ is the key to understanding the whole of Scripture.

Paul's commission to "open their eyes, so that they may turn from darkness to light and from the power of Satan to God, that they may receive forgiveness of sins and a place among those who are sanctified by faith" (26:18) encompasses the full scope of the Church's mission as Catholic theology understands it: enlightenment through catechesis, liberation from evil through the sacraments, forgiveness through Reconciliation, and sanctification through participation in the life of grace.

Agrippa's response — "In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?" (26:28) — and Paul's passionate reply (26:29) illustrate the Catholic understanding of evangelization as a personal encounter. Pope Paul VI's *Evangelii Nuntiandi* (1975) and Pope Francis' *Evangelii Gaudium* (2013) both emphasize that evangelization is not mere information transfer but a witness that invites a response. Paul embodies this: his entire defense is simultaneously a legal argument and a proclamation of the gospel.
