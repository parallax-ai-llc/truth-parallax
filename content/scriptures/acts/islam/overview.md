## Overview

Acts is not part of the Islamic scriptural canon, but its subject matter intersects with Quranic themes at several points. The Quran speaks of Isa's (Jesus') disciples (*hawariyyun*) and their mission, the original purity of the message given to Isa, and the subsequent alteration (*tahrif*) of that message. Islamic engagement with Acts is therefore evaluative: the text is examined for echoes of authentic prophetic teaching while being assessed for theological departures from *tawhid* (divine unity).

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran references the disciples of Isa in several passages:

| Quranic Reference | Content | Relevance to Acts |
|-------------------|---------|-------------------|
| Al-Saff 61:14 | "O you who believe, be supporters of Allah, as when Isa said to the disciples, 'Who are my supporters for Allah?'" | Parallels the apostolic mission in Acts |
| Al-Ma'idah 5:111-112 | The disciples ask for a table spread from heaven | Echoes of communal meals and divine provision |
| Al-Imran 3:52-53 | The hawariyyun declare faith in Allah and submission | The purity of the original community's monotheism |
| Al-Ma'idah 5:116-117 | Isa denies commanding worship of himself and his mother | Islamic critique of christological developments in Acts |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Tawhid as the Standard
Any passage in Acts asserting the divinity of Jesus, the Trinity, or the deity of the Holy Spirit is considered a departure from the original monotheistic message. The Pentecost narrative (chapter 2) is read cautiously — the coming of a spirit is not denied, but its theological interpretation as a "Third Person of the Trinity" is rejected.

### Prophetic Continuity
Islam sees the apostles' mission as continuous with the prophetic tradition. Their preaching of repentance, moral reform, and submission to God's will echoes prophetic patterns recognized in Islam. Paul's theological innovations — particularly regarding the Law and christology — are viewed as the point where deviation from the original message began.

### Tahrif (Textual Alteration)
The text of Acts is considered to have undergone alteration, mixing authentic apostolic memory with later theological elaboration. The speeches attributed to Peter and Paul, for instance, are evaluated against Quranic standards rather than accepted at face value.
