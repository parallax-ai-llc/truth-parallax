Acts 8 describes the spread of the movement to Samaria through Philip and the encounter with the Ethiopian eunuch. Islamic scholars note with interest that the gospel is presented as extending beyond its original Jewish context — a pattern consistent with Islam's teaching that God's message is universal. However, Islam attributes the authentic universal message not to the apostles' theological innovations but to the original teaching of Isa, which Islam identifies with pure monotheism.

The Simon Magus episode (8:9-24) illustrates the danger of corrupting divine gifts through worldly ambition. Islamic theology strongly condemns the commercialization of religion — the Quran warns against those who "exchange the covenant of Allah and their own oaths for a small price" (3:77). Simon's attempt to purchase spiritual power with money has a direct parallel in Islamic ethics: the prohibition against selling religious knowledge or spiritual authority for material gain.

The Ethiopian eunuch's encounter with Philip (8:26-40) is viewed by Islamic scholars as reflecting the universal reach of divine guidance. The eunuch's sincere seeking after truth and his willingness to submit to instruction are qualities that Islam honors. His reading of Isaiah 53 and Philip's christological interpretation, however, represent exactly the kind of scriptural reinterpretation that Islam considers *tahrif* — taking a prophetic text about God's servant and applying it in ways that elevate a human being to divine or quasi-divine status.
