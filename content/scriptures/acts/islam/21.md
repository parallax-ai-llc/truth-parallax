Acts 21 describes Paul's arrival in Jerusalem and his arrest in the Temple. Paul's willingness to participate in a Temple purification rite (21:23-26) is noted by Islamic scholars as evidence that the relationship between the Jesus movement and Jewish law was more complex than Paul's epistles suggest. If Paul truly believed the Law was abolished in Christ, why would he participate in a Temple rite? Islamic scholars see this as evidence of tension between Paul's theology and his practice — a tension that suggests his theological innovations were not universally accepted even among the apostles.

The Jerusalem church's report that "many thousands" of Jewish believers are "all zealous for the law" (21:20) is significant for Islamic scholarship. It confirms that Torah-observant followers of Isa existed alongside Paul's law-free Gentile communities. Islamic theology sees these Torah-observant Jewish believers as closer to the original teaching of Isa, who, according to the Quran, came not to abolish the Law but to confirm it: "I have come to you confirming what was before me of the Torah" (3:50).

Paul's arrest in the Temple by a mob (21:27-36) illustrates the volatile religious atmosphere of first-century Jerusalem. Islamic scholars note the parallel to the opposition Muhammad faced in Mecca — both prophetic figures encountered violent rejection from their own communities. However, Islam maintains a distinction: Muhammad's message was protected and preserved through the Quran's divine preservation, while the message of Isa was progressively altered by his followers.
