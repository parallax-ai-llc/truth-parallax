Acts 11 describes the aftermath of Peter's visit to Cornelius and the founding of the church at Antioch. The Jerusalem community's initial objection to eating with Gentiles (11:3) reflects the dietary and purity boundaries that both Judaism and Islam maintain. Islamic scholars note that the progressive dissolution of these boundaries in early Christianity represents a departure from the prophetic pattern: divine dietary laws are given to be obeyed, not reinterpreted away.

The Antioch community, where believers were first called "Christians" (11:26), represents a critical development. Islamic scholars see the emergence of a distinct "Christian" identity — separate from Judaism — as the social expression of the theological changes that Paul and others introduced. The original *hawariyyun* were Muslims in the Quranic sense — those who submitted to Allah and followed His prophet Isa. The transformation of this prophetic community into "Christianity" marks, in Islamic perspective, a progressive departure from authentic prophetic religion.

The famine relief sent from Antioch to Jerusalem (11:27-30) reflects the virtue of charitable solidarity that Islam strongly values. The Quranic emphasis on caring for the poor, feeding the hungry, and supporting the community of faith (*ummah*) finds an echo in this inter-community generosity. Islamic scholars recognize authentic prophetic values in the social practices of the early community, even where they critique its developing theology.
