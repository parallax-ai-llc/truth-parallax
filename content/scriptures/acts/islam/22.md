Acts 22 presents Paul's defense speech, recounting his Jewish credentials and his conversion. Paul's claim to have studied under Gamaliel and to have been a zealous Pharisee (22:3) is noted by Islamic scholars as evidence that Paul came from a tradition of strict monotheism, making his subsequent theological innovations all the more troubling. A Pharisee trained in the strictest interpretation of Torah would have understood that the elevation of a human being to divine status contradicts the foundational teaching of Judaism — the Shema: "Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4), which corresponds to the Islamic *shahada*.

The Damascus road experience (22:6-11) is evaluated by Islamic scholars within the framework of prophetic validation. In Islamic epistemology, a genuine revelation must be consistent with the core message of all previous prophets — *tawhid*. A vision that leads to the deification of a human being fails this test. Islamic scholars compare Paul's experience unfavorably with the well-attested, publicly verified revelation of the Quran to Muhammad, which was received over twenty-three years in the presence of witnesses and preserved in writing during the Prophet's lifetime.

The crowd's violent reaction to the word "Gentiles" (22:21-22) reveals the deeply contested nature of the universalist turn. Islamic scholars see in this reaction evidence that the broadening of the covenant community to include Gentiles without Torah observance was a radical innovation, not a natural development. Islam offers a different model of universalism: the final revelation through Muhammad is universal by divine design, addressed to all humanity from the beginning (Quran 34:28), not as a secondary development prompted by one group's rejection.
