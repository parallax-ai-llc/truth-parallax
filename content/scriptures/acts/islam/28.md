Acts 28 concludes with Paul's arrival in Rome and his continued preaching. The viper incident on Malta (28:3-6) — where Paul suffers no harm from a deadly snakebite — is consistent with the Islamic understanding that Allah protects His righteous servants when it serves His purposes. Similar accounts appear in Islamic tradition regarding the Prophet Muhammad and various saints (*awliya'*), understood not as magic but as divine protection (*hifz*).

Paul's final encounter with the Jewish leaders in Rome (28:17-28) and his citation of Isaiah 6:9-10 regarding Israel's hardened heart bring the book to its theological conclusion. Islamic scholars note that the theme of a people rejecting their prophet is central to the Quran: "And We had certainly given Musa the Scripture, but it was disagreed upon" (41:45). However, Islam interprets the pattern differently: the rejection of prophets does not justify the theological innovations that followed but rather demonstrates the need for a final, protected revelation — the Quran, which Allah promised to preserve from corruption (15:9).

The final image of Paul preaching "the kingdom of God" in Rome "without hindrance" (28:31) is assessed by Islamic scholars as the culmination of a complex historical process. The message that reached Rome contained, in Islamic perspective, a mixture of authentic prophetic teaching and human theological innovation. Islam sees its own mission as the restoration and completion of the original prophetic message: "This day I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favor upon you and have approved for you Islam as religion" (Quran 5:3). The story that Acts tells is, for Islam, an incomplete chapter in the larger narrative of prophetic history that finds its culmination in the revelation to Muhammad.
