Acts 16 describes Paul's journey to Macedonia and events at Philippi, including the conversions of Lydia and the Philippian jailer. The Holy Spirit's direction of Paul's journey — forbidding entry into certain regions and redirecting him through a vision (16:6-10) — is consistent with the Islamic understanding that Allah guides His servants through various means, including dreams and visions. The Prophet Muhammad received guidance through revelation, dreams, and divine inspiration, and Islamic theology recognizes these as legitimate channels of divine communication.

The conversion of Lydia (16:14) — described as a "worshiper of God" whose heart the Lord "opened" — reflects a pattern that Islamic theology affirms: God guides whom He wills (Quran 28:56). The concept of divine guidance (*hidayah*) opening the heart to truth is central to Islamic spirituality. However, Islam would insist that the truth to which hearts are opened is *tawhid* — the oneness of God — not the christological claims that the Christian mission proclaimed.

Paul and Silas' imprisonment and their singing of hymns at midnight (16:25) exemplify the quality of patient worship in the face of adversity that Islam deeply values. The Quran repeatedly connects worship with trials: "And seek help through patience and prayer" (2:45). The miraculous opening of the prison doors (16:26) is consistent with divine intervention on behalf of His righteous servants. However, the theological content of the jailer's conversion — "Believe in the Lord Jesus" (16:31) — remains the point of Islamic critique.
