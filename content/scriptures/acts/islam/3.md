Acts 3 describes a healing performed by Peter and John at the Temple. Islamic theology affirms that Allah grants prophets and righteous persons the ability to perform miracles (*mu'jizat*) as signs of divine power. The Quran attributes miracles to Isa — healing the blind, curing lepers, raising the dead by Allah's permission (Quran 3:49, 5:110). The healing in Acts 3 is consistent with this pattern, though Islam insists that all miraculous power belongs to Allah alone, not to the human agent.

Peter's attribution of the healing to "the name of Jesus Christ" (3:6) and his sermon presenting Jesus as the fulfillment of Moses' prophecy (3:22) are evaluated against Quranic standards. Islam affirms that Isa was a prophet in the line of Musa (Moses) and Ibrahim (Abraham), but rejects the christological elevation that makes Isa more than a prophet. Peter's call to repentance (3:19) is consistent with prophetic preaching in the Islamic understanding, but the specific content of the christological claims exceeds what Islam considers authentic prophetic teaching.

The concept of "times of refreshing" and the restoration of all things (3:19-21) resonates with Islamic eschatology, which includes the return of Isa before the Day of Judgment. The Quran (43:61) states that Isa is "a sign of the Hour" — his return will herald the final judgment. However, Islam understands this return differently: Isa will come not as the divine Christ of Christian theology but as a prophet who will establish justice and correct the theological errors attributed to him by his followers.
