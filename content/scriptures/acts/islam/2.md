Acts 2 describes the Pentecost event — the coming of wind, fire, and the gift of speaking in other languages. Islamic scholars approach this account cautiously. The coming of a divine spirit to empower believers is not inherently problematic for Islamic theology — the Quran speaks of Allah strengthening Isa with the "Holy Spirit" (*Ruh al-Qudus*, Quran 2:87, 253), generally understood as the angel Jibril (Gabriel). However, the Christian interpretation of the Spirit as a distinct divine Person — the Third Person of the Trinity — is firmly rejected as a violation of *tawhid*.

Peter's sermon (2:14-36), which proclaims Jesus as "both Lord and Christ" (2:36), represents precisely the kind of theological elevation that Islamic theology considers *shirk* (association of partners with God). The Quran explicitly warns: "O People of the Scripture, do not commit excess in your religion or say about Allah except the truth. The Messiah, Isa, the son of Maryam, was but a messenger of Allah" (4:171). From the Islamic perspective, the Pentecost sermon marks the beginning of a theological trajectory that would culminate in the doctrine of the Trinity.

The early community's shared life (2:42-47) — devotion to teaching, fellowship, breaking bread, and prayer — is viewed more sympathetically. Islamic scholars see in this communal devotion an echo of the original Muslim community (*ummah*) at Medina. The sharing of possessions, mutual care, and communal worship reflect values that Islam affirms. The deviation, from the Islamic perspective, lies not in the community's practice but in its developing theology.
