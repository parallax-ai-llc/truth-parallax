Acts 18 describes Paul's ministry in Corinth and introduces Apollos. Paul's extended stay in Corinth and his work as a tentmaker (18:3) reflect the prophetic pattern of self-sufficiency that Islam values. The Prophet Muhammad worked as a merchant before his prophetic call, and Islamic tradition emphasizes that prophets earned their own livelihoods rather than depending on others. Paul's manual labor is seen as consistent with authentic prophetic practice.

The Lord's assurance to Paul — "Do not be afraid... for I have many in this city who are my people" (18:9-10) — reflects the Islamic understanding that Allah guides and encourages His servants. Divine communication through visions and dreams is well established in Islamic tradition, and the content of this particular assurance — that God has faithful people in unexpected places — is consistent with the Quranic teaching that Allah's guidance reaches all nations.

The brief mention of Apollos (18:24-28), who "knew only the baptism of John" and needed further instruction, is noted by Islamic scholars as evidence of theological diversity within the early Jesus movement. The existence of followers who had not adopted the full christological framework suggests that the theological claims about Jesus developed progressively rather than being part of the original message. Islam sees this diversity as confirming that the original teaching of Isa was simpler and more purely monotheistic than what later emerged.
