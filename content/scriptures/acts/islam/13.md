Acts 13 describes the beginning of Paul's missionary journeys from Antioch. Islamic scholars view this as a critical juncture in the history of the original prophetic community. The mission that begins here will carry not just the prophetic message of Isa but the theological innovations of Paul — the divinity of Christ, salvation through the cross, the end of the Law — to the wider world.

Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch (13:16-41) traces Israel's history from the Exodus to David to Jesus. The historical recitation is consistent with the Quranic pattern of prophetic history, and many of the same figures — Ibrahim, Musa, Dawud (David) — appear in both narratives. However, Paul's conclusion — that through Jesus forgiveness of sins is proclaimed and freedom from the Law is achieved (13:38-39) — diverges sharply from Islamic theology, which teaches that forgiveness comes directly from Allah through sincere repentance (*tawbah*), not through any human mediator or sacrificial death.

The pattern of Paul turning from the synagogue to the Gentiles (13:46-47) is seen in Islamic scholarship as the moment when the message begins to be fundamentally altered. The movement away from its Jewish roots and toward a Gentile audience required theological adaptations — particularly regarding the Law, circumcision, and dietary regulations — that, from the Islamic perspective, compromised the original prophetic message. Islam sees itself as the restoration of that original message in its pure, universal form.
