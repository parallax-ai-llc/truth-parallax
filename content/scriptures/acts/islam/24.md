Acts 24 describes Paul's trial before the Roman governor Felix. Paul's defense — that he worships the God of the fathers, believes the Law and the Prophets, and hopes in the resurrection (24:14-15) — is compatible with Islamic theology in its broad outlines. Islam affirms belief in the God of Ibrahim, Musa, and the prophets, accepts the original Torah and Injil as divine revelation, and teaches the resurrection and final judgment. The divergence lies in the christological claims that Paul attaches to these otherwise shared convictions.

Paul's teaching on "righteousness, self-control, and the coming judgment" (24:25) reflects moral and eschatological themes that are central to Islamic ethics. The Quran repeatedly calls for righteousness (*taqwa*), self-discipline, and preparation for the Day of Judgment. Felix's alarmed response and his procrastination illustrate the human tendency to defer repentance — a tendency the Quran warns against: "O you who have believed, repent to Allah with sincere repentance" (66:8).

Felix's hope for a bribe (24:26) represents the corruption of justice that Islamic law condemns in the strongest terms. The Prophet Muhammad stated: "Allah's curse is upon the one who bribes and the one who takes the bribe" (Ahmad, Tirmidhi). The corruption of the judicial process by material interest is a form of injustice (*dhulm*) that the Quran identifies as one of the gravest sins.
