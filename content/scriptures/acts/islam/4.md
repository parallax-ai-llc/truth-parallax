Acts 4 describes the apostles' arrest and their bold proclamation before the Sanhedrin. Islamic theology views the persecution of prophets and their followers as a recurring pattern throughout sacred history. The Quran states: "We gave Musa the Scripture and followed up after him with messengers. And We gave Isa, the son of Maryam, clear proofs and supported him with the Holy Spirit. But is it not that every time a messenger came to you with what your souls did not desire, you were arrogant?" (2:87). The apostles' persecution fits this prophetic pattern.

Peter's declaration that "there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved" (4:12) is a claim that Islam categorically rejects. Salvation in Islamic theology comes through submission to Allah alone, not through any human intermediary. While prophets are honored as guides and their intercession (*shafa'ah*) is acknowledged in some theological schools, the idea that a human person is the exclusive means of salvation contradicts the Quranic emphasis on God's direct relationship with every human being.

The communal sharing described in 4:32-37 reflects values that Islamic tradition honors. The early Muslim community practiced similar solidarity: the institution of *zakah* (obligatory charity), the *waqf* (charitable endowment), and the mutual support system among the *Muhajirun* (emigrants) and *Ansar* (helpers) in Medina parallel the early Christian community's generosity. Islamic scholars see in these social practices an authentic prophetic legacy, even where they disagree with the theological framework.
