Acts 7 contains Stephen's lengthy retelling of Israelite history and his subsequent martyrdom. Islamic scholars find significant common ground in the speech's central theme: the repeated rejection of God's messengers by their own people. The Quran extensively develops this motif — Nuh (Noah), Ibrahim (Abraham), Musa (Moses), and the prophets were all rejected by those to whom they were sent (Quran 2:87, 3:183-184, 5:70). Stephen's survey of Israel's history, emphasizing the rejection of Joseph, Moses, and the prophets, aligns with the Quranic narrative pattern.

Stephen's assertion that "the Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands" (7:48) affirms a principle that Islam strongly endorses. The Quran teaches that Allah cannot be contained in any physical space: "His Kursi extends over the heavens and the earth" (2:255). While Muslims revere the Kaaba as a sacred site and direction of prayer, they affirm that Allah is not confined to it. Stephen's critique of Temple-centered religion is consistent with Islamic theology's emphasis on God's transcendence (*tanzih*).

Stephen's martyrdom (7:54-60) — killed for his testimony — places him among those whom Islam honors as righteous witnesses to truth. The Quran acknowledges that prophets and righteous people were killed by those who rejected them (Quran 2:61, 3:21, 3:112). Stephen's prayer for his persecutors reflects the prophetic quality of mercy that Islam values, and his willingness to die for his convictions mirrors the concept of *shahada* (bearing witness) that is central to Islamic spirituality.
