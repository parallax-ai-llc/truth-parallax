Acts 27 describes the sea voyage to Rome and the dramatic shipwreck. The chapter's vivid narrative of storm, danger, and divine deliverance resonates with Quranic themes. The Quran describes the experience of those at sea: "It is He who enables you to travel on land and sea until, when you are in ships and they sail with them by a good wind and they rejoice therein, there comes a storm wind and the waves come upon them from everywhere and they assume that they are surrounded, supplicating Allah, sincere to Him in religion" (10:22). The pattern of human helplessness at sea leading to sincere prayer echoes Paul's experience.

The angel's assurance to Paul (27:24) that all aboard will be saved reflects the Islamic understanding that Allah may grant safety to many for the sake of one righteous person among them. The Quran suggests this principle in the story of Nuh's ark — those who boarded with the prophet were saved from the flood. Islamic tradition also recounts that cities may be spared destruction because of the presence of righteous people within them.

Paul's leadership during the crisis — encouraging the crew to eat, giving thanks to God (27:33-36) — demonstrates the calm trust in divine providence (*tawakkul*) that Islam values. The Muslim who trusts in Allah remains steadfast in the face of danger, knowing that nothing happens outside of divine will (*qadr*). The safe arrival of all 276 persons confirms that God's word is unfailingly fulfilled — a principle Islamic theology wholeheartedly affirms: "The word of your Lord has been fulfilled in truth and in justice. None can alter His words" (6:115).
