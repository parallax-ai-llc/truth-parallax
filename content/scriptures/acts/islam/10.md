Acts 10 describes Peter's vision and his visit to the household of the Roman centurion Cornelius. The vision of clean and unclean animals (10:9-16) and the command to eat what was formerly prohibited represents, from an Islamic perspective, a significant departure from the divine dietary laws. Islam maintains its own dietary code (*halal* and *haram*), which shares important features with Jewish kashrut, including the prohibition of pork and requirements for the proper slaughter of animals. The abrogation of these laws in Christianity is viewed as an unauthorized human modification of divine commandments.

Peter's declaration that "God shows no partiality" (10:34) and that anyone who fears God and does what is right is acceptable to Him is affirmed by Islamic theology. The Quran states: "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another. Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (49:13). The principle of divine impartiality and the acceptance of all who submit to God is central to Islamic universalism.

However, the specific theological framework through which this universalism is expressed in Acts — faith in Jesus Christ as Lord, baptism in his name, the gift of the Holy Spirit as a divine Person — is rejected by Islam. The universalism of God's mercy and acceptance, Islam teaches, requires submission to God alone (*islam*), not faith in a human being as divine. The Cornelius episode thus illustrates the tension between a principle Islam affirms (God's universal acceptance) and a theological expression Islam rejects (christological mediation).
