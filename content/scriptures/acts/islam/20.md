Acts 20 contains Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders and the journey toward Jerusalem. Paul's pastoral charge — warning about false teachers and committing the community to God's care (20:28-31) — reflects a concern for the preservation of authentic teaching that Islam shares deeply. The Quran warns about those who distort revelation: "Among them is a group who alter the Scripture with their tongues so you may think it is from the Scripture, but it is not from the Scripture" (3:78). Islamic scholars see irony in Paul warning about doctrinal corruption, since Islam considers Paul himself to be a primary agent of theological deviation.

The gathering on "the first day of the week" to break bread (20:7) marks the shift from Sabbath to Sunday worship that Islamic scholars note as a departure from the prophetic pattern. Islam maintains Friday (*Jumu'ah*) as the day of congregational prayer, and the Sabbath is recognized in the Quran as a commandment given to the Children of Israel (16:124). The change from Sabbath to Sunday is seen as a human innovation without divine authorization.

Paul's determination to reach Jerusalem despite warnings of suffering (20:22-24) reflects the quality of steadfastness in God's path that Islam honors. The willingness to face persecution and hardship for the sake of one's mission is central to the Islamic concept of *jihad* in its spiritual sense — the struggle to maintain faithfulness in the face of opposition. Islamic scholars can acknowledge Paul's sincerity and courage while disagreeing with the theological content of his mission.
