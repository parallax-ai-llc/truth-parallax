Acts 1 describes the departure of Isa (Jesus) from his disciples and the selection of a replacement for Judas. Islamic theology affirms that Isa was raised to heaven by Allah (Quran 4:157-158) but rejects the Christian theological interpretation of this event as an "ascension" implying divine status. The Quran states: "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but it was made to appear so to them... Rather, Allah raised him to Himself" (4:157-158). The raising of Isa is an act of divine rescue, not a glorification of a divine being.

The disciples' question about restoring the kingdom to Israel (1:6) is viewed by Islamic commentators as reflecting the authentic prophetic expectation of God's earthly justice — a hope shared by all true prophets. However, the redirection toward a universal mission (1:8) is evaluated critically: while Islam affirms the universality of God's message, it attributes the theological innovations of the later Christian mission — particularly the deification of Isa — to human distortion rather than divine command.

The replacement of Judas by Matthias (1:15-26) is of interest because the Quran references the *hawariyyun* (disciples of Isa) as sincere supporters who declared, "We are the helpers of Allah" (Quran 3:52, 61:14). Islamic tradition honors the original disciples' devotion while maintaining that the theological developments recorded in Acts represent a progressive departure from the pure monotheism that Isa taught.
