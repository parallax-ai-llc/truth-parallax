Acts 12 narrates the persecution of the community under Herod Agrippa, the killing of James, and Peter's miraculous rescue from prison. The persecution and killing of righteous believers is a theme that the Quran addresses extensively. Allah states: "That is because they used to disbelieve in the signs of Allah and kill the prophets without right" (3:112). The killing of James and the imprisonment of Peter fit the pattern of unjust persecution that Allah's servants endure.

Peter's rescue from prison by an angel (12:6-11) is consistent with the Islamic understanding that Allah sends angels to protect and assist His righteous servants. The Quran describes angelic intervention in the lives of the prophets — angels visit Ibrahim (11:69-73), protect Lut (11:77-83), and support the believers at the Battle of Badr (3:123-125). The miraculous nature of Peter's deliverance is not inherently problematic for Islamic theology; the question is whether the theological interpretation attached to such events is consistent with *tawhid*.

Herod's death (12:20-23) — struck down for accepting divine worship — is read by Islamic scholars as a powerful illustration of the sin of pride and the usurpation of God's sovereignty. The Quran repeatedly warns against those who claim divine status or authority: Pharaoh's declaration "I am your most exalted lord" (79:24) brought divine destruction. Herod's fate parallels this Quranic pattern: those who claim God's prerogatives are destroyed by the God whose glory they appropriated.
