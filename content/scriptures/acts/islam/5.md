Acts 5 recounts the divine punishment of Ananias and Sapphira for their deception and the apostles' continued ministry. The narrative of sudden divine judgment for hypocrisy resonates with Islamic theology, which takes the sin of hypocrisy (*nifaq*) with utmost seriousness. The Quran devotes an entire surah to the hypocrites (Surah 63, *Al-Munafiqun*) and warns that they will be in the lowest depths of the Fire (4:145). The story serves as a warning about sincerity (*ikhlas*) that Islamic moral teaching strongly affirms.

Peter's statement that Ananias lied "to the Holy Spirit" and "to God" (5:3-4) is noted by Islamic scholars as a passage that Christians cite in support of the Trinity. The apparent identification of the Holy Spirit with God is read by Muslim commentators as either a later theological interpolation or a misinterpretation — the spirit in question being the angel Jibril or a divine force, not a co-equal divine Person.

Gamaliel's counsel (5:33-39) — to wait and see whether the movement is of human or divine origin — is evaluated by Islamic scholars with interest. From the Islamic perspective, the early Jesus movement contained authentic prophetic elements, but these were progressively distorted. The survival of Christianity does not, in Islamic theology, prove its theological correctness; rather, Islam sees itself as the correction and completion of the prophetic tradition that Christianity preserved only partially.
