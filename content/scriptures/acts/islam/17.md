Acts 17 describes Paul's preaching in Thessalonica, Berea, and Athens. Paul's Areopagus speech (17:22-31) is one of the most extensively discussed passages in Islamic comparative theology because it contains elements of pure monotheistic preaching alongside what Islam considers theological errors.

Paul's critique of idolatry — "The God who made the world and everything in it, being Lord of heaven and earth, does not live in temples made by man, nor is he served by human hands, as though he needed anything" (17:24-25) — is fully consistent with Quranic theology. The Quran makes identical arguments against idol worship: "Indeed, those you invoke besides Allah are servants like you" (7:194). Paul's affirmation that God "made from one man every nation of mankind" (17:26) aligns with the Quran: "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female" (49:13).

However, Paul's conclusion — that God will judge the world through "a man whom he has appointed," proved by raising him from the dead (17:31) — departs from Islamic theology. Islam affirms the Day of Judgment but teaches that Allah judges directly; no human intermediary serves as the appointed judge. The resurrection of Jesus, while not denied in Islam (Isa will return before the Last Day), is interpreted differently: it confirms his prophethood, not his divinity. The Areopagus speech thus illustrates the mixture of authentic monotheism and christological innovation that Islamic scholars identify throughout Acts.
