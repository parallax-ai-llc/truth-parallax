Acts 26 contains Paul's defense before King Agrippa, including his most detailed account of his conversion and mission. Paul's claim to proclaim "nothing but what the prophets and Moses said would come to pass" (26:22) is a claim that Islamic scholars evaluate against the Quranic standard. If Paul truly taught only what the prophets and Moses taught, his message should be consistent with *tawhid*. The specific content of his proclamation — "that the Christ must suffer and that, by being the first to rise from the dead, he would proclaim light" (26:23) — is where Islamic theology diverges.

The concept of a suffering Messiah is foreign to Islamic christology. The Quran presents Isa as a prophet honored by Allah, protected from death on the cross, and raised to heaven. The theology of redemptive suffering — that God's plan required the Messiah to die for humanity's sins — contradicts the Islamic principle that each soul bears its own burden (Quran 6:164, 35:18). Salvation in Islam comes through repentance, faith, and righteous deeds, not through the suffering of another.

Agrippa's response — whether sincere, skeptical, or dismissive — illustrates the challenge of prophetic preaching before political power. Paul's passionate reply (26:29) demonstrates a conviction and zeal that Islamic scholars can respect, even while disagreeing with its theological content. The Quran honors sincere conviction and courageous witness, qualities that Paul undeniably displayed. The Islamic evaluation of Paul thus holds in tension respect for his sincerity and critique of his theology.
