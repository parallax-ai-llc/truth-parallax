Acts 15 records the Jerusalem Council's decision that Gentile believers need not be circumcised or keep the full Mosaic Law. Islamic scholars view this decision as a watershed moment in the history of prophetic religion. The relaxation of divine commandments — circumcision, dietary laws, Sabbath observance — is seen as a human modification of God's Law rather than a legitimate development under divine guidance.

The four prohibitions imposed on Gentile believers (15:20, 29) — abstaining from idolatrous food, sexual immorality, things strangled, and blood — are of particular interest because they overlap with Islamic dietary and moral law. The prohibition of blood and things strangled corresponds to Islamic *halal* slaughter requirements. The prohibition of sexual immorality (*porneia*) aligns with Islamic moral law. Islamic scholars see in these minimal requirements a remnant of the original divine law, reduced from its full scope under pressure from the Gentile mission.

The council's formula — "it has seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (15:28) — raises theological questions from an Islamic perspective. The attribution of the decision to the "Holy Spirit" functioning as a divine guide for the community has a structural parallel in the Islamic concept of *ijma'* (scholarly consensus), which some theologians consider divinely guided. However, Islam maintains that no human council has the authority to abrogate what God has commanded in His revealed Law. The Jerusalem Council's decision, however sincerely made, is seen as exceeding the legitimate bounds of human authority over divine legislation.
