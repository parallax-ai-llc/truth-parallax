Acts 14 describes the missionary journey through Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe. The incident at Lystra, where Paul and Barnabas are mistaken for Zeus and Hermes (14:11-13), illustrates the pagan environment in which the early mission operated. Their vigorous rejection of divine worship — "We also are men, of like nature with you" (14:15) — is wholly consistent with Islamic values. The Quran records similar rejections by prophets: Isa himself will say on the Day of Judgment, "Never did I tell them anything except what You commanded me — to worship Allah, my Lord and your Lord" (5:117).

Paul's speech about the living God who made heaven and earth (14:15-17) echoes Islamic monotheistic preaching. The critique of idolatry, the call to turn from "vain things to a living God," and the appeal to God's witness in creation are central themes of the Quran. Islamic scholars see in this speech an echo of authentic prophetic monotheism that was part of the original message, even as they critique the broader theological framework in which it was delivered.

The stoning of Paul at Lystra (14:19) and his continued preaching demonstrate a perseverance in the face of opposition that Islam respects. The Quranic concept of *sabr* (patient endurance) in the path of God is reflected in Paul's willingness to suffer. The appointment of elders in each community (14:23) mirrors the organizational development of the early Muslim *ummah*, where leaders were appointed for communities of believers. These structural parallels suggest common patterns in the development of prophetic communities.
