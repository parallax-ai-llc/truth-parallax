Acts 19 describes Paul's ministry in Ephesus, including encounters with disciples of John, Jewish exorcists, and the worshipers of Artemis. The twelve disciples who knew only John's baptism (19:1-7) are of interest to Islamic scholars as further evidence of theological diversity in the early movement. These followers of a prophetic figure (John the Baptist, identified with Yahya in the Quran) had not adopted the christological claims associated with Paul's preaching, suggesting that the exaltation of Isa developed over time.

The sons of Sceva episode (19:13-16) — failed exorcists invoking the name of Jesus without genuine faith — illustrates a principle that Islamic theology strongly affirms: divine names and prayers are not magical incantations. In Islam, the efficacy of prayer and supplication (*du'a*) depends on sincerity, faith, and the relationship between the supplicant and Allah. The mere pronunciation of sacred words without genuine submission to God is empty and potentially dangerous.

The riot of the silversmiths over the threat to the cult of Artemis (19:23-41) dramatically illustrates the conflict between monotheism and polytheism that Islam sees as the central struggle of prophetic history. The Quran recounts similar conflicts: Ibrahim smashing the idols (21:57-58), Musa confronting the golden calf (20:88-98). The economic dimension of idolatry — Demetrius' concern for his trade in silver shrines — finds a parallel in the Quran's account of the Meccan opposition to Muhammad, which was partly motivated by the threat to the lucrative pilgrimage trade centered on idol worship at the Kaaba before its purification.
