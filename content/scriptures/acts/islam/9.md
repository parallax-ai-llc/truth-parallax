Acts 9 narrates the conversion of Saul of Tarsus, who would become the apostle Paul. Islamic scholarship views Paul as a pivotal figure in the development of Christianity — and a deeply problematic one. Many Muslim scholars, from medieval thinkers to modern writers like Muhammad Ata ur-Rahim, argue that Paul was the primary agent of theological distortion, transforming the simple monotheistic message of Isa into a complex theology of incarnation, atonement, and Trinity.

The dramatic nature of the Damascus road experience (9:3-9) is not inherently rejected by Islamic theology — Allah communicates with his servants through visions and revelations. However, Islamic scholars question the theological content of Paul's subsequent teaching. The Quranic principle is clear: any revelation that contradicts *tawhid* cannot be from Allah. Paul's later theology — particularly his teaching about Jesus as the Son of God, the atonement through the cross, and the abolition of the Law — is seen as departing from authentic prophetic teaching.

Ananias' role in mediating Paul's conversion (9:10-19) is noted as evidence that even in the canonical account, Paul's authority is mediated through others. Islamic scholars emphasize that Paul was not one of the original *hawariyyun* who accompanied Isa during his earthly ministry. His claim to apostolic authority based on a private vision, rather than on direct companionship with the prophet, is viewed with skepticism — in Islamic terms, his *isnad* (chain of transmission) is weak because it lacks direct prophetic contact.
