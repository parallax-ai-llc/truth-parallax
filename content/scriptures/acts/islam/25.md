Acts 25 describes Paul's appeal to Caesar and the consultation between Festus and King Agrippa. Paul's use of the Roman legal system to protect himself and advance his mission reflects a pragmatic engagement with civil authority that Islamic jurisprudence recognizes. The Prophet Muhammad and the early Muslim community also navigated complex political relationships — the Constitution of Medina, the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, and diplomatic missions to various rulers demonstrate similar strategic engagement with existing power structures.

Festus' characterization of the dispute as concerning "a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (25:19) distills the core claim to its simplest form. Islam's position on this claim is nuanced: the Quran states that Isa was not killed or crucified (4:157), so the question of resurrection from death does not arise in the same way. Isa was raised to Allah alive (4:158) and will return before the Day of Judgment (43:61). The Christian narrative of death and resurrection is seen as a misunderstanding of what actually happened to Isa.

The introduction of Agrippa and the formal hearing (25:23-27) sets up the most complete account of Paul's testimony. Islamic scholars note the irony of the scene: the powerful sit in judgment on a man who claims to bear a divine message. The Quran contains similar scenes — Musa before Fir'awn (Pharaoh), Ibrahim before Nimrod — where the prophet confronts political power with spiritual truth. The pattern is universal in prophetic history.
