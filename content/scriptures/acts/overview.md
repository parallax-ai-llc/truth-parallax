## Overview

Acts of the Apostles is the fifth book of the New Testament and the sequel to the Gospel of Luke, written by the same author. It narrates the founding and expansion of the early Christian church from the ascension of Jesus to Paul's imprisonment in Rome, spanning roughly three decades (c. 30-62 CE).

## Structure

Acts divides naturally into two major sections:

1. **The Jerusalem Church and Peter** (Chapters 1-12): Pentecost, the early community in Jerusalem, Stephen's martyrdom, and the beginnings of Gentile inclusion
2. **Paul's Missionary Journeys** (Chapters 13-28): The spread of the gospel from Antioch through Asia Minor and Greece to Rome

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Acts occupies a distinctive position across religious traditions:

- **Christianity**: The foundational narrative of the church's origin, the gift of the Holy Spirit, and the theological basis for Gentile inclusion
- **Judaism**: A window into first-century Judaism, synagogue practice, and the parting of the ways between Judaism and early Christianity
- **Islam**: Contains narratives of apostles (*hawariyyun*) referenced in the Quran; the early community's monotheism is seen as authentic
- **Gnosticism**: A text both used and contested by Gnostic communities who claimed alternative apostolic traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Holy Spirit | The Spirit as the driving force of the church's mission and identity |
| Witness | The apostles as witnesses (*martyres*) to the resurrection |
| Gentile Inclusion | The progressive opening of salvation to non-Jews |
| Suffering and Persecution | The church grows through opposition and martyrdom |
| The Word of God | The spread of the gospel message across the Roman world |
| Community | The shared life, worship, and mutual care of believers |
