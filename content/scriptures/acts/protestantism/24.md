Acts 24 presents Paul's trial before the Roman governor Felix. The prosecution by the orator Tertullus (24:1-9) characterizes Paul as a troublemaker and ringleader of the "sect of the Nazarenes" — language that reveals how the early Christian movement was perceived from outside. Protestant historians note that the designation as a "sect" (*hairesis*) places the Jesus movement within the recognized spectrum of Jewish groups, not yet as a separate religion.

Paul's defense (24:10-21) affirms continuity with Judaism: he worships the God of the fathers, believes everything in the Law and Prophets, and hopes in the resurrection — all claims a Pharisee could make. His insistence that the true issue is the resurrection of the dead (24:21) reflects the Protestant conviction that the resurrection is the non-negotiable center of the Christian faith. Any theology that sidelines the bodily resurrection departs from apostolic teaching.

Felix's response — he was "well informed about the Way" and delayed judgment, later sending for Paul to hear about "faith in Christ Jesus" while trembling at Paul's discourse on "righteousness, self-control, and the coming judgment" (24:24-25) — is a classic Protestant sermon text about the danger of intellectual interest in Christianity without genuine repentance. Felix's hope for a bribe (24:26) and his political calculation in leaving Paul imprisoned (24:27) illustrate how worldly interests obstruct the gospel.
