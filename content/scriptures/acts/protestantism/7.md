Acts 7 contains Stephen's lengthy speech before the Sanhedrin, the longest speech in Acts. Protestant interpreters see it as a masterful survey of salvation history demonstrating that God's purposes have never been confined to a single place or institution. Stephen traces God's dealings with Abraham, Joseph, Moses, and the prophets, arguing that Israel repeatedly rejected God's messengers — a pattern culminating in the rejection of Jesus, the Righteous One.

The speech's theological center, for Protestant readers, is Stephen's assertion that "the Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands" (7:48), quoting Isaiah 66:1-2. This challenges any theology that locates God's presence exclusively in sacred buildings or rituals, reinforcing the Reformation's rejection of the idea that grace is mediated solely through institutional sacraments. Luther and Calvin both appealed to Stephen's critique of Temple-centered religion.

Stephen's martyrdom (7:54-60) — his vision of the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God, his prayer for his persecutors — is the paradigm of Christian witness (*martyria*) in Protestant tradition. His death, witnessed by Saul of Tarsus (7:58), sets the stage for the great persecutor's transformation into the great apostle. Protestant commentators emphasize the sovereign irony: the blood of the first martyr waters the seed that will produce the church's greatest missionary.
