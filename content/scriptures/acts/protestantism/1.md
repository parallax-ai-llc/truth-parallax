Acts 1 records Jesus' final instructions to the apostles before his ascension, commanding them to wait in Jerusalem for the promised Holy Spirit. Protestant interpreters emphasize Christ's declaration that the apostles would be his witnesses "in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth" (1:8) as the programmatic outline for the entire book and for the church's ongoing mission.

The replacement of Judas by Matthias (1:15-26) raises important questions for Protestant ecclesiology. Reformed commentators note that the casting of lots represents the last instance of this Old Testament practice, soon to be superseded by the direct guidance of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost. The requirement that Matthias be a witness of the resurrection (1:21-22) underscores the foundational Protestant conviction that apostolic authority rests on eyewitness testimony to the risen Christ.

Calvin observed that the disciples' question about restoring the kingdom to Israel (1:6) reveals the persistence of earthly expectations even after the resurrection. Jesus redirects their attention from political timetables to the empowering work of the Spirit — a pattern Protestant preachers have long used to distinguish the spiritual kingdom from temporal power.
