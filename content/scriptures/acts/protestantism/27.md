Acts 27 provides a detailed account of Paul's voyage to Rome and the shipwreck on Malta. Protestant interpreters read this chapter as a narrative demonstration of divine providence. The angel's assurance to Paul — "Do not be afraid, Paul; you must stand before Caesar. And behold, God has granted you all those who sail with you" (27:24) — confirms that God's plan to bring Paul to Rome will be accomplished despite the most extreme natural dangers.

The chapter's nautical detail — the names of winds, the sailing techniques, the progressive abandonment of cargo and tackle — has been praised for its historical accuracy, bolstering confidence in Luke as a reliable historian. F.F. Bruce and other evangelical scholars have pointed to the "we" narrative (27:1ff) as evidence that the author was an eyewitness. This matters for Protestant epistemology: the reliability of Scripture rests in part on the credibility of its human authors.

Paul's leadership during the crisis — urging the crew to eat, giving thanks to God, and assuring them of survival (27:33-36) — models the integration of faith and practical wisdom. Protestant pastoral theology finds here an image of the Christian leader who combines trust in God's promises with attentiveness to immediate human needs. The safe arrival of all 276 persons on shore (27:44) demonstrates that God keeps his word to the last detail.
