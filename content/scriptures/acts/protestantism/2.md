Acts 2 narrates the outpouring of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, one of the most theologically significant events in Protestant interpretation. The visible descent of the Spirit with tongues of fire and the gift of speaking in other languages fulfills Jesus' promise in Acts 1:8. Protestant traditions diverge sharply on the ongoing significance of these phenomena — cessationists view them as unique apostolic signs, while Pentecostals and charismatics see them as normative for all believers.

Peter's sermon (2:14-36) is considered a model of apostolic preaching. He interprets the event through Old Testament prophecy (Joel 2:28-32), presents the death and resurrection of Christ as God's predetermined plan, and calls the hearers to repentance and baptism. Reformed interpreters highlight verse 23 — "delivered up according to the definite plan and foreknowledge of God" — as a key proof-text for divine sovereignty over even the most wicked human actions.

The description of the early community in 2:42-47 — devoted to the apostles' teaching, fellowship, breaking of bread, and prayer — has profoundly shaped Protestant ideals of church life. While Protestants generally do not read the communal sharing of possessions as mandatory, they see these verses as establishing the essential marks of a true church: Word, sacrament, prayer, and mutual care.
