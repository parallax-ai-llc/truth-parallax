Acts 17 records Paul's ministry in Thessalonica, Berea, and Athens. The Bereans' response to Paul's preaching — examining the Scriptures daily to verify his claims (17:11) — has become a Protestant ideal. They are called "more noble" for testing apostolic teaching against Scripture, a practice that embodies *sola scriptura*. Protestant churches frequently invoke the Berean model to encourage Bible study and critical engagement with teaching.

Paul's Areopagus speech in Athens (17:22-31) is the most extensively discussed passage in Protestant missiology. Addressing pagan philosophers, Paul begins with natural theology — the unknown god, God's self-sufficiency, the unity of the human race — and moves toward the proclamation of judgment and resurrection. Reformed interpreters debate whether Paul's approach represents a legitimate contextualization of the gospel or an accommodation that produced limited results (17:32-34).

The speech's declaration that God "made from one man every nation of mankind" (17:26) has been invoked in Protestant social ethics to affirm the unity and equality of all races. Paul's quotation of Greek poets — "In him we live and move and have our being" and "we are indeed his offspring" (17:28) — demonstrates a willingness to find points of contact in non-Christian thought, a practice that has influenced Protestant approaches to apologetics and cultural engagement.
