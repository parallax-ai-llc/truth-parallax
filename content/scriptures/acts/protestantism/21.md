Acts 21 describes Paul's journey to Jerusalem despite repeated prophetic warnings and his arrest in the Temple. The tension between Paul's sense of calling and the Spirit-inspired warnings of the disciples (21:4, 10-14) raises difficult questions about the discernment of divine guidance. Protestant commentators generally read Paul's determination as obedient courage rather than disobedience, comparing it to Jesus' resolve to go to Jerusalem.

Paul's meeting with James and the elders (21:17-26) reveals a Jerusalem church still zealous for the Law, with thousands of Jewish believers who are "all zealous for the law" (21:20). Protestant interpreters use this passage to counter the idea that the early church uniformly abandoned Torah observance. Paul's willingness to undergo a purification rite in the Temple (21:23-26) demonstrates that he was no antinomian, though he refused to impose the Law on Gentile believers.

Paul's arrest by the Roman tribune (21:30-40) begins the long narrative of trials and legal proceedings that will dominate the final quarter of Acts. The mob's cry to kill Paul echoes the crowd's cry against Jesus, and Protestant typological readers see Paul's passion narrative as a deliberate parallel to Christ's. The Roman legal system, though imperfect, provides a measure of justice that protects Paul from mob violence — a point relevant to Protestant discussions of civil government's role in restraining evil.
