## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Acts is shaped by the Reformation conviction that the early church described in Acts represents the normative model for Christian faith and practice. Reformers appealed to Acts to argue that the post-apostolic church had departed from its original simplicity, making Acts a key text in debates over ecclesiology, soteriology, and the work of the Holy Spirit.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Dispensational
Dispensationalist interpreters read Acts as a transitional book between the dispensation of Law and the dispensation of Grace. They distinguish between the Jewish-oriented ministry of Peter (chapters 1-12) and the Gentile-oriented ministry of Paul (chapters 13-28), arguing that certain miraculous gifts were confined to the apostolic era.

### Reformed
Reformed interpreters emphasize the sovereignty of God in Acts — the Spirit directs the mission, opens hearts (Lydia in 16:14), and orchestrates events according to divine purpose. Acts demonstrates the outworking of God's eternal decree through human agency.

### Charismatic/Pentecostal
Pentecostal and charismatic Protestants read Acts as establishing a pattern for ongoing Spirit-baptism, speaking in tongues, and miraculous gifts. The events of Pentecost (chapter 2), Samaria (chapter 8), Cornelius (chapter 10), and Ephesus (chapter 19) are paradigmatic, not merely historical.

## Theological Emphases

- **Justification by Faith**: Peter's sermon in chapter 10 and the Jerusalem Council in chapter 15 as evidence that salvation comes by grace through faith, not through the Law
- **Priesthood of All Believers**: The Spirit is poured out on all flesh, not reserved for a priestly class
- **Church Polity**: Various Protestant groups find support for their polity — congregational, presbyterian, or episcopal — in the structures of Acts
- **Sola Scriptura**: The Bereans (17:11) as the model for testing all teaching against Scripture
