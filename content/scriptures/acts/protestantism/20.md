Acts 20 contains Paul's farewell address to the Ephesian elders at Miletus, one of the most personally revealing passages in Acts. Paul's charge to the elders — "Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers" (20:28) — is foundational for Protestant pastoral theology. The term "overseers" (*episkopoi*) applied to local elders is cited in debates about church polity, with Presbyterians arguing it equates elders and bishops.

Paul's warning about "fierce wolves" who will arise from within the church (20:29-30) has been repeatedly invoked in Protestant heresy controversies, from the Reformation's critique of papal corruption to contemporary debates about doctrinal fidelity. The image of shepherds protecting the flock from wolves is central to the Protestant understanding of pastoral ministry as primarily a teaching and guarding office.

Paul's declaration that he did not shrink from declaring "the whole counsel of God" (20:27) and his economic self-sufficiency through tentmaking (20:33-35) are frequently cited as models for Protestant ministry. The quotation of Jesus — "It is more blessed to give than to receive" (20:35), found nowhere in the Gospels — demonstrates the existence of oral Jesus tradition beyond the written Gospels, a point that complicates strict *sola scriptura* positions.
