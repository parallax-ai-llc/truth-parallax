Acts 11 recounts Peter's defense of his actions at Cornelius' household before the Jerusalem church, and the founding of the church at Antioch. Protestant interpreters note that even Peter, the leading apostle, must give an account to the broader community (11:1-18) — a point used against claims of unaccountable apostolic or papal authority. The Jerusalem believers' response, "Then to the Gentiles also God has granted repentance that leads to life" (11:18), marks a theological watershed.

The Antioch church (11:19-26) holds special significance in Protestant ecclesiology. It is the first church composed of both Jews and Gentiles, the place where believers were first called "Christians" (11:26), and the base for Paul's missionary journeys. Protestant commentators see Antioch as a model of the multiethnic, mission-oriented church that transcends cultural boundaries while maintaining doctrinal unity.

The chapter closes with the church at Antioch sending famine relief to Jerusalem through Barnabas and Saul (11:27-30), demonstrating practical fellowship between congregations. Protestant interpreters highlight this as a model for inter-church cooperation and mutual care, organized voluntarily rather than through hierarchical mandate.
