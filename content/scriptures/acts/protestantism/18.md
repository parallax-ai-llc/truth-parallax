Acts 18 describes Paul's eighteen-month ministry in Corinth and introduces Aquila and Priscilla. Paul's turn from synagogue to Gentile mission in Corinth (18:6) follows the established Lukan pattern, but the Lord's night vision — "Do not be afraid, but go on speaking... for I have many in this city who are my people" (18:9-10) — is a text of great comfort in Reformed theology, affirming that God has an elect people even in the most unpromising contexts.

The Gallio incident (18:12-17) is historically significant as it provides one of the few datable events in Paul's career (c. 51-52 CE). Gallio's refusal to adjudicate a religious dispute establishes an important precedent for Protestant thinking about the relationship between civil and religious authority. Protestant commentators have used this passage to argue for the separation of ecclesiastical and civil jurisdictions, though interpretations vary across traditions.

The brief account of Apollos (18:24-28) — an eloquent Alexandrian who knew only John's baptism until Priscilla and Aquila instructed him more accurately — demonstrates that theological understanding grows progressively. Protestant interpreters note that Priscilla's role as a theological teacher challenges restrictive views of women's ministry, though complementarian interpreters emphasize that the instruction was private rather than public.
