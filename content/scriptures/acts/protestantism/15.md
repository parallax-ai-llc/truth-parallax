Acts 15 records the Jerusalem Council, arguably the most significant ecclesiastical event in Acts for Protestant theology. The question at stake — whether Gentile converts must be circumcised and keep the Law of Moses — strikes at the heart of the gospel. Peter's speech (15:7-11) asserts that salvation comes by "the grace of the Lord Jesus" and not through the Law, a declaration that Protestants read as anticipating the Reformation's *sola gratia*.

James' decision and the apostolic decree (15:13-29) demonstrate conciliar decision-making under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. Protestant interpreters note the phrase "it has seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (15:28) as a model for church governance that is neither autocratic nor merely democratic but pneumatic — guided by the Spirit through collective discernment. The four prohibitions imposed on Gentile believers (15:29) are generally interpreted as practical accommodations for Jewish-Gentile fellowship, not as binding moral law.

The subsequent dispute between Paul and Barnabas over John Mark (15:36-41) — resulting in their separation — is read by Protestant commentators with striking honesty. Even apostles disagree and part ways, yet God uses the separation to multiply the missionary work. This passage challenges any idealization of the early church and affirms that God's purposes advance through imperfect human instruments.
