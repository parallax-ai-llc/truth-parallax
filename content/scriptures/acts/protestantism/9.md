Acts 9 records the conversion of Saul of Tarsus on the Damascus road, one of the most significant events in Protestant theological imagination. The encounter with the risen Christ — "Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting me?" (9:4) — demonstrates that salvation is entirely an act of divine initiative. Reformed theologians point to Saul's conversion as the supreme example of irresistible grace: the most hostile persecutor is transformed into the most devoted apostle by the sovereign call of Christ.

Ananias' reluctance to approach Saul (9:13-14) and the Lord's response — "he is a chosen instrument of mine" (9:15) — reinforces the Protestant emphasis on divine election. God's purposes are not thwarted by human character or history. Luther saw in Paul's conversion a mirror of his own experience: the relentless pursuer of self-righteousness brought low by the grace of God.

The chapter's account of Paul's early preaching in Damascus and Jerusalem (9:19-30) shows the immediate fruit of genuine conversion. Peter's healings at Lydda and Joppa (9:32-43), including the raising of Tabitha, demonstrate that apostolic power continues to confirm the gospel message. Protestant commentators note the deliberate parallel between Peter's miracles and those of Jesus, underscoring the apostles' role as Christ's authorized representatives.
