Acts 22 presents Paul's defense speech to the Jerusalem crowd, recounting his conversion on the Damascus road. Protestant interpreters note that Paul's autobiography emphasizes three elements: his former zeal for the Law (22:3-5), his encounter with the risen Christ (22:6-11), and his commission to the Gentiles (22:14-21). This pattern of testimony — from darkness to light, from self-righteousness to grace — has deeply shaped the Protestant conversion narrative tradition.

Paul's account of Ananias describing God's choice of Paul — "The God of our fathers appointed you to know his will, to see the Righteous One and to hear a voice from his mouth" (22:14) — is significant for Reformed theology's emphasis on divine calling. The encounter is not Paul's initiative but God's appointment. His baptism and the washing away of sins (22:16) is discussed in Protestant sacramental theology, with Baptists reading it as symbolic and Reformed interpreters acknowledging a deeper sacramental connection.

The crowd's violent reaction to the word "Gentiles" (22:21-22) reveals the explosive nature of the inclusion question. Paul's invocation of his Roman citizenship (22:25-29) to avoid scourging introduces the theme of dual citizenship — heavenly and earthly — that has shaped Protestant political theology. The right to fair legal process under civil law is affirmed without compromising the primacy of heavenly allegiance.
