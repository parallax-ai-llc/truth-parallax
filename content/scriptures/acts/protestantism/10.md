Acts 10 records Peter's vision and his visit to the household of Cornelius, a Roman centurion. This chapter is pivotal for Protestant theology as it marks the decisive moment when the gospel breaks through ethnic and religious boundaries to reach the Gentiles. The vision of the sheet filled with unclean animals and the command "What God has made clean, do not call common" (10:15) is interpreted as the abrogation of the ceremonial law — a key distinction in Reformed covenant theology between moral and ceremonial law.

Peter's sermon at Cornelius' house (10:34-43) contains the declaration that "God shows no partiality" (10:34) and that "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins through his name" (10:43). Protestant interpreters see here the clearest apostolic articulation of justification by faith apart from works of the Law — the core of the Reformation's soteriological vision.

The descent of the Holy Spirit on the Gentile hearers before baptism (10:44-48) is theologically momentous. Cessationists note the tongues-speaking as a one-time confirmation of Gentile inclusion; Pentecostals see it as a paradigm for Spirit-baptism. All Protestant traditions agree on the fundamental point: the Spirit is given on the basis of faith, not circumcision, confirming that Gentiles enter the people of God on equal terms with Jews.
