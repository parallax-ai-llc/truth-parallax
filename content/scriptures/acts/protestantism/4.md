Acts 4 describes the arrest of Peter and John by the Temple authorities and their bold defense before the Sanhedrin. Peter's declaration that "there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved" (4:12) is one of the most cited verses in Protestant theology, grounding the doctrine of the exclusivity of salvation through Christ alone (*solus Christus*).

The Sanhedrin's recognition that Peter and John were "uneducated, common men" who "had been with Jesus" (4:13) is a favorite text for Protestant preachers emphasizing that spiritual authority comes from encounter with Christ, not from formal credentials. This resonates with the Reformation's challenge to clerical monopoly on interpretation and preaching.

The prayer of the community in 4:24-31 — calling on the sovereign Lord who made heaven and earth and asking for boldness to speak the Word — is a model of Reformed prayer theology. The shaking of the place and the fresh filling with the Holy Spirit (4:31) demonstrate the ongoing dynamic of Spirit empowerment. The renewed description of communal sharing (4:32-37) reinforces the portrait of the early church as a Spirit-formed community marked by generosity and unity.
