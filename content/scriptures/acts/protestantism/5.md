Acts 5 opens with the dramatic story of Ananias and Sapphira, who lie about the proceeds from a land sale and fall dead at Peter's feet. Protestant interpreters read this as a sobering warning about the holiness of God and the seriousness of hypocrisy within the covenant community. Peter's statement that Ananias lied not to men but "to the Holy Spirit" and "to God" (5:3-4) is cited in trinitarian theology as identifying the Spirit with God.

The continued growth of the church and the apostles' miracles (5:12-16) lead to a second arrest by the Sadducees. The angel's liberation of the apostles from prison (5:19-20) and their resumed preaching in the Temple demonstrate that divine purposes cannot be thwarted by human opposition — a theme dear to Reformed theology.

Gamaliel's counsel to the Sanhedrin (5:33-39) — "if this plan or this undertaking is of man, it will fail; but if it is of God, you will not be able to overthrow them" — has been variously interpreted. Some Protestant commentators see it as providential wisdom; others caution that Gamaliel's pragmatism should not be elevated into a theological principle, since not every movement that endures is necessarily of God.
