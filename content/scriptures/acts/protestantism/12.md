Acts 12 narrates the persecution of the church under Herod Agrippa I, the execution of James the brother of John, and the miraculous deliverance of Peter from prison. Protestant theology reads this chapter as a study in the sovereignty of God: James is killed, but Peter is preserved — not because one is more faithful than the other, but because God's purposes are inscrutable. Calvin noted that God does not always deliver his servants from suffering, but always accomplishes his will through their faithfulness.

The church's earnest prayer for Peter (12:5) and their astonishment at his actual release (12:12-16) provide a realistic and encouraging portrait of early Christian prayer. Protestant preachers frequently use this passage to teach that God often answers prayer beyond what the petitioners themselves expect or believe. Rhoda's joyful recognition and the community's incredulity are among the most humanly vivid scenes in Acts.

Herod's death (12:20-23) — struck down by an angel because he accepted divine honors — serves as a warning against human arrogance and the usurpation of God's glory. Protestant commentators draw a contrast between Herod's self-exaltation and the apostles' consistent refusal to accept worship (cf. 3:12, 14:14-15). The summary statement that "the word of God increased and multiplied" (12:24) despite persecution encapsulates a central theme of Acts.
