Acts 13 marks the beginning of Paul's first missionary journey, commissioned by the church at Antioch under the direction of the Holy Spirit (13:1-3). Protestant ecclesiology finds here a model for missionary sending: the local church, led by the Spirit, sets apart and sends workers. The combination of fasting, prayer, and the laying on of hands (13:3) demonstrates that mission is rooted in the worshiping community, not in individual initiative alone.

Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch (13:16-41) is his first major recorded speech and contains the clearest Pauline articulation of justification by faith in Acts. His declaration that "through this man forgiveness of sins is proclaimed to you, and by him everyone who believes is freed from everything from which you could not be freed by the law of Moses" (13:38-39) is a cornerstone text for the Protestant doctrine of *sola fide*. Luther and Calvin both devoted extensive attention to this passage.

The chapter's conclusion — Jewish rejection leading to the turn toward Gentiles (13:44-52) — establishes a pattern that recurs throughout Paul's missionary work. Paul's citation of Isaiah 49:6, "I have made you a light for the Gentiles" (13:47), is read by Protestant interpreters as demonstrating that Gentile inclusion was always part of God's redemptive plan, not an afterthought prompted by Jewish unbelief.
