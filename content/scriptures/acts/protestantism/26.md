Acts 26 contains Paul's defense before King Agrippa, often considered the climax of the trial narratives. Paul's third retelling of his conversion (26:9-18) adds the commission to open the Gentiles' eyes and "turn them from darkness to light and from the power of Satan to God" (26:18). Protestant interpreters see this comprehensive statement of the gospel's purpose — illumination, liberation, forgiveness, and inheritance — as defining the scope of Christian mission.

Paul's argument that he has said "nothing but what the prophets and Moses said would come to pass: that the Christ must suffer and that, by being the first to rise from the dead, he would proclaim light both to our people and to the Gentiles" (26:22-23) is central to the Protestant hermeneutical principle that Christ is the fulfillment of the Old Testament. The suffering and resurrection of the Messiah and the inclusion of the Gentiles are not Christian innovations but the realization of Israel's own Scriptures.

Agrippa's famous response — "In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?" (26:28) — and Paul's reply that he wishes all his hearers were as he is, "except for these chains" (26:29), have been the subject of countless Protestant sermons. Whether Agrippa spoke sincerely or sarcastically, the exchange dramatizes the moment of decision that Protestant evangelism presses upon every hearer: the gospel demands a response.
