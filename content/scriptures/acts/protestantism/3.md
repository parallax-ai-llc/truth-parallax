Acts 3 records Peter and John healing a lame man at the Beautiful Gate of the Temple, followed by Peter's second major sermon. Protestant commentators emphasize that the healing is performed "in the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth" (3:6), demonstrating that apostolic power is entirely derivative — it flows from Christ, not from the apostles themselves.

Peter's sermon in the Temple colonnade (3:12-26) connects Jesus to the promises made to Abraham and the prophets, presenting him as the fulfillment of Moses' prediction of a prophet like himself (Deuteronomy 18:15). Reformed interpreters see this as evidence of the unity of the covenants — the gospel does not replace God's promises to Israel but fulfills them. The call to repentance "so that times of refreshing may come from the Lord" (3:19) is a central text for Protestant evangelistic preaching.

F.F. Bruce noted that Peter's address to the crowd as "men of Israel" and his appeal to the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (3:12-13) demonstrate that the earliest Christian preaching was thoroughly Jewish in its framework, a point that challenges later supersessionist readings.
