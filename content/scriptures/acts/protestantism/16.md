Acts 16 opens the second missionary journey with the selection of Timothy, the Macedonian call, and the founding of the church at Philippi. The Holy Spirit's guidance is dramatically portrayed: the Spirit forbids Paul from preaching in Asia and Bithynia (16:6-7), then directs him to Macedonia through a vision (16:9-10). Protestant interpreters see here the sovereign direction of the church's mission — the Spirit not only empowers but also redirects and constrains.

The conversion of Lydia (16:14) is a key text in Reformed soteriology. Luke writes that "the Lord opened her heart to pay attention to what was said by Paul." Calvinist interpreters see this as a clear instance of effectual calling — salvation begins not with human decision but with divine initiative that opens the heart to receive the gospel. Arminian interpreters emphasize Lydia's willing response as evidence that God's grace is resistible but genuinely offered.

The Philippian jailer's conversion (16:25-34) provides the famous question and answer: "What must I do to be saved?" — "Believe in the Lord Jesus, and you will be saved" (16:30-31). This exchange is one of the most-preached texts in Protestant evangelistic tradition, encapsulating the simplicity of the gospel offer. Paul and Silas' singing in prison at midnight (16:25) has become an enduring image of faith under persecution.
