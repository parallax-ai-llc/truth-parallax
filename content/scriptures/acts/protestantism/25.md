Acts 25 describes the transition from Felix to Festus as governor and Paul's appeal to Caesar. Festus' reopening of Paul's case at Jewish leaders' request (25:1-5) and Paul's renewed defense (25:6-8) demonstrate the continuing legal impasse. Paul's appeal to Caesar (25:10-11) — "I appeal to Caesar" — is one of the most consequential statements in Acts, redirecting the narrative toward Rome and fulfilling the Lord's promise in 23:11.

Protestant interpreters have drawn diverse lessons from Paul's appeal. Some see it as a legitimate use of civil rights in service of the gospel — Paul employs the legal system to advance his mission to Rome. Others note that Paul's appeal implicitly affirms the legitimacy of the Roman state and its judicial processes, grounding Protestant theories of civil government. Luther's doctrine of the two kingdoms finds a precedent here: the spiritual and temporal realms are distinct but both serve God's purposes.

Festus' consultation with King Agrippa (25:13-27) reveals the embarrassment of a Roman official who has a prisoner but no clear charge against him. Festus' admission that the dispute concerns "a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (25:19) is an unwitting confession of the gospel's core claim. Protestant commentators note the irony: even hostile or indifferent witnesses cannot avoid articulating the resurrection.
