Acts 6 narrates the appointment of the Seven to serve tables, resolving a dispute between Hellenistic and Hebrew Jewish believers over the daily distribution to widows. Protestant ecclesiology finds in this passage a model for the diaconate — a distinct office of service alongside the teaching ministry of the apostles. The apostles' statement that they should not "give up preaching the word of God to serve tables" (6:2) is foundational for the Protestant emphasis on the primacy of Word ministry.

Stephen, one of the Seven, is described as "full of grace and power" and performing "great wonders and signs" (6:8). His opponents from various Hellenistic synagogues cannot withstand his wisdom and the Spirit by which he speaks (6:10). Protestant commentators note that Stephen's ministry blurs the boundary between "deacon" and "evangelist," suggesting that the Spirit's gifting overflows institutional categories.

The false charges brought against Stephen — that he spoke against the Temple and the Law (6:13-14) — foreshadow the central theological question that Acts will wrestle with: the relationship between the old covenant institutions and the new work of God in Christ. Stephen's face, shining "like the face of an angel" (6:15), marks him as a prophetic figure standing in the tradition of Moses.
