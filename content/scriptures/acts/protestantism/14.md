Acts 14 continues Paul's first missionary journey through Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe. The chapter illustrates the full range of responses to the gospel — belief, opposition, confusion, and violence. At Lystra, after healing a lame man, Paul and Barnabas are mistaken for Zeus and Hermes (14:11-13), prompting a vigorous protest and a sermon on natural theology (14:15-17) that appeals to God's witness in creation.

Protestant interpreters find in Paul's Lystra speech (14:15-17) an early example of general revelation theology. Paul argues that God "did not leave himself without witness" among the nations through the gifts of rain, fruitful seasons, and food (14:17). Reformed theologians connect this to Romans 1:19-20, holding that general revelation is sufficient to leave humanity without excuse but insufficient for salvation — the gospel must still be preached.

Paul's stoning at Lystra (14:19) and his immediate return to ministry demonstrate the apostolic willingness to suffer for the gospel. The appointment of elders in every church (14:23) is foundational for Presbyterian polity, which sees the eldership as the normative pattern of church government established by apostolic precedent. Paul's statement that "through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God" (14:22) is a key text for the Protestant theology of suffering.
