Acts 28 concludes with Paul's arrival on Malta, his journey to Rome, and his continued preaching while under house arrest. The episode of the viper on Malta (28:3-6) — the islanders first think Paul is a murderer being punished by divine justice, then reverse their judgment when he suffers no harm — illustrates the fickleness of popular opinion and the sovereignty of God over nature. Protestant commentators connect this to Jesus' promise in Mark 16:18 while debating whether such signs continue today.

Paul's meeting with the Jewish leaders in Rome (28:17-28) constitutes his final appeal to the Jewish community. His citation of Isaiah 6:9-10 — the hardening of Israel's heart — and his declaration that "this salvation of God has been sent to the Gentiles; they will listen" (28:28) brings Acts' theological arc to its culmination. Protestant interpreters debate whether this represents a final rejection of Israel or a temporary hardening, with dispensationalists and covenantalists reaching different conclusions.

The book's ending — Paul "proclaiming the kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ with all boldness and without hindrance" (28:31) — is deliberately open-ended. Protestant scholars have noted that the unresolved narrative invites the reader to see the church's mission as ongoing and unfinished. The word *akolytos* ("without hindrance") is the final word of Acts in Greek, suggesting that despite chains, trials, and shipwrecks, the Word of God cannot be bound. This open ending has profoundly shaped Protestant missiology and the conviction that the church's task continues until Christ returns.
