Acts 8 describes the scattering of the Jerusalem church by persecution and the resulting spread of the gospel beyond Jerusalem — first to Samaria through Philip, then to an Ethiopian eunuch. Protestant interpreters see this as the fulfillment of the programmatic statement in 1:8, as the gospel moves from Jerusalem to Judea and Samaria.

The encounter with Simon Magus (8:9-24) is a critical text for Protestant polemics. Simon's attempt to purchase spiritual power with money gave rise to the term "simony," which Reformers frequently applied to the medieval church's sale of offices and indulgences. Peter's sharp rebuke — "your heart is not right before God" (8:21) — is read as a warning against reducing the Spirit's work to a commodity.

Philip's encounter with the Ethiopian eunuch (8:26-40) is celebrated in Protestant interpretation as a model of personal evangelism and the sufficiency of Scripture. The eunuch reads Isaiah 53 but cannot understand it without a guide; Philip "beginning with this Scripture, told him the good news about Jesus" (8:35). The immediate baptism upon confession of faith, without reference to any institutional process, is cited by Baptists and other free-church traditions as evidence for believer's baptism.
