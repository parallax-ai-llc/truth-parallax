Acts 19 centers on Paul's extended ministry in Ephesus, where the gospel confronts both Jewish exorcism and pagan idolatry. The episode of the twelve disciples who had received only John's baptism (19:1-7) raises questions about the relationship between water baptism and Spirit-baptism that divide Protestant traditions. Pentecostals see here a second experience subsequent to conversion; Reformed interpreters view it as the completion of an incomplete initiation.

The sons of Sceva incident (19:13-16) — where Jewish exorcists attempt to use Jesus' name as a magical formula and are overpowered by the evil spirit — illustrates the Protestant distinction between genuine faith and formal religion. The name of Jesus is not a magical incantation but the expression of a living relationship. The resulting burning of magical books worth fifty thousand pieces of silver (19:19) is a dramatic picture of repentance and the incompatibility of Christian faith with occult practices.

The riot of the silversmiths led by Demetrius (19:23-41) shows the economic and social dimensions of the gospel's impact. The craftsmen's livelihood depended on the worship of Artemis, and the spread of Christianity threatened their trade. Protestant social ethics finds here a paradigm for the conflict between the gospel and economic systems built on idolatry, while also noting the city clerk's appeal to lawful channels (19:38-40) as an endorsement of civil order.
