Acts 11 describes the aftermath of the Cornelius episode and the founding of the Antioch church. Peter's report to the Jerusalem community (11:1-18) is read by Gnostic interpreters as evidence of the institutional church's need to control and authorize all spiritual experience. The Spirit has acted freely at Cornelius' house, but Peter must defend this action before the hierarchy. In Gnostic reading, the pneumatic event precedes and exceeds the institutional response.

The Antioch church (11:19-26) represents, in Gnostic perspective, a more open and cosmopolitan form of the Jesus movement — one potentially more receptive to esoteric teaching than the Jerusalem church with its Torah-observant conservatism. The designation "Christians" (11:26) may mark the emergence of a distinct identity, but Gnostic readers note that this label would eventually become the name of the institutional church that suppressed Gnostic teaching. The original pneumatic diversity of Antioch was, in their view, narrowed into orthodox conformity.

The famine relief sent from Antioch to Jerusalem (11:27-30) is an expression of practical solidarity that even Gnostic interpreters can affirm. While Gnostic theology tends to devalue the material world, it does not necessarily reject acts of compassion toward those who suffer within it. The Valentinian ethic, as described by Clement of Alexandria, included charitable works as expressions of the pneumatic's compassion for the less spiritually advanced.
