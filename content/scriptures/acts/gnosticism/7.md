Acts 7 contains Stephen's speech and martyrdom. Gnostic readers find Stephen's survey of Israelite history (7:2-53) amenable to their own inverted reading of the Old Testament. Stephen's emphasis on Israel's repeated rejection of God's messengers — Joseph sold by his brothers, Moses rejected by his people, the prophets persecuted — supports the Gnostic thesis that the history of the Demiurge's people is a history of failed attempts to control and suppress divine truth.

Stephen's declaration that "the Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands" (7:48) is a key Gnostic proof-text. The Temple, in Gnostic theology, is a creation of the Demiurge — a structure designed to localize and control the divine presence. The true God transcends all material structures, all institutions, all architectural attempts to contain the infinite. Stephen's critique of Temple religion aligns with the Gnostic rejection of all material mediations of the divine.

Stephen's martyrdom and his vision of the heavens opened (7:55-56) are read as a genuine pneumatic experience — a glimpse of the Pleroma that lies beyond the material cosmos. His vision of the Son of Man standing at God's right hand is interpreted not as a christological affirmation within the Demiurge's cosmic order but as a breakthrough to the realm beyond the archons. Stephen's death liberates the divine spark from its material prison. The Gnostic *Testimony of Truth* teaches that the true martyr is not one who dies physically but one who achieves gnosis — yet Stephen's physical death, accompanied by his heavenly vision, may represent both.
