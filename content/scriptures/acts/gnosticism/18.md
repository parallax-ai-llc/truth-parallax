Acts 18 describes Paul's ministry in Corinth and the introduction of Apollos. Gnostic interpreters note that Corinth was a city particularly receptive to diverse spiritual teachings, and Paul's Corinthian correspondence reveals a community with competing claims to spiritual authority and wisdom — the very environment in which Gnostic thought flourished. Paul's language in 1 Corinthians about "wisdom among the mature" and the distinction between spiritual and fleshly believers was foundational for Valentinian theology.

The Lord's night vision to Paul — "I have many in this city who are my people" (18:10) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a reference to the pneumatic elect scattered throughout the world. These are the people who carry the divine spark, who are predestined for gnosis, who will recognize the truth when they hear it. They are God's people not by institutional affiliation but by spiritual nature.

Apollos (18:24-28) — described as eloquent, learned, knowing the Scriptures but only partially instructed — presents an intriguing figure for Gnostic interpreters. His incomplete knowledge and subsequent further instruction by Priscilla and Aquila mirror the Gnostic pattern of progressive illumination. Gnosis is not received all at once but through stages of deepening understanding, guided by those who have advanced further on the spiritual path. The Valentinian system of spiritual grades — from pistis (faith) through gnosis to epignosis (full knowledge) — may find an echo in Apollos' progression.
