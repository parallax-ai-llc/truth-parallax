Acts 4 describes the apostles' arrest and bold testimony before the Sanhedrin. Gnostic interpreters read the Sanhedrin's opposition as the action of archontic forces working through institutional religion to suppress gnosis. The Jewish authorities, in this reading, function as agents of the Demiurge who fears the spread of true knowledge. The pattern of religious authority opposing spiritual awakening is central to the Gnostic narrative: the powers of this world — political, religious, institutional — exist to maintain ignorance and control.

Peter's declaration that salvation comes through no other name than Jesus Christ (4:12) is read by different Gnostic schools in different ways. Valentinians might accept the statement while reinterpreting "Jesus Christ" as the Aeon who descended into the material realm — the name refers not to a historical individual but to the spiritual principle of redemption. Sethian Gnostics might see the statement as an exoteric simplification of a more complex soteriology involving multiple heavenly figures.

The community's bold prayer and the shaking of the place (4:31) are read as authentic pneumatic phenomena. The Spirit's power is real, and the early community's experience of that power is not denied. What Gnostic interpreters reject is the institutional channeling of this power through hierarchical structures and doctrinal formulations. The Spirit blows where it wills; it cannot be controlled by apostolic decree or institutional authority.
