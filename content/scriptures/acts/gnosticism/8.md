Acts 8 describes the mission to Samaria and the encounters with Simon Magus and the Ethiopian eunuch. The Simon Magus episode (8:9-24) is the most contested passage in Acts for Gnostic interpretation. The canonical text presents Simon as a charlatan rebuked by Peter. But Gnostic traditions revered Simon as a divine figure — the Simonians taught that Simon was the incarnation of the Great Power, and Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* 1.23) identifies him as the father of all Gnostic heresies.

The clash between Simon and Peter in Acts 8 is thus read by Gnostics as the foundational conflict between gnosis and institutional authority. Peter represents the emerging hierarchical church that controls access to the Spirit through institutional sacraments (the laying on of hands, 8:17). Simon represents the direct, unmediated encounter with divine power. Peter's rebuke of Simon — "Your heart is not right before God" (8:21) — is reversed in Gnostic reading: it is Peter's institutional authority, not Simon's spiritual ambition, that distorts the true nature of the divine gift.

The Ethiopian eunuch's encounter with Philip (8:26-40) is read sympathetically in Gnostic tradition. The eunuch is an outsider — a foreigner, sexually marginalized — who nonetheless receives illumination. His question, "How can I understand unless someone guides me?" (8:31), affirms the Gnostic principle that gnosis requires a teacher, an initiator who can transmit the secret knowledge. But this guide need not be an institutional authority; Philip is a lay evangelist, not one of the Twelve.
