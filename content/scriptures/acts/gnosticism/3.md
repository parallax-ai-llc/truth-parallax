Acts 3 describes a healing at the Temple and Peter's sermon. Gnostic readers interpret the healing of the lame man (3:1-10) as a demonstration of pneumatic power — the ability of those who possess the divine spark to channel spiritual energy into the material realm. The healing confirms that the material body, though a prison of the soul, can be temporarily overcome by superior spiritual forces. Some Gnostic texts, including the *Acts of John*, describe similar healings as signs of the Savior's continuing pneumatic presence.

Peter's sermon in Solomon's Portico (3:12-26), with its appeal to the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob and to Moses as a prophetic precursor, is read by Gnostic interpreters as an exoteric address to a psychic audience. The God of the patriarchs, in certain Gnostic systems, is the Demiurge — the God of the Old Testament who is not the supreme, unknowable God of the Pleroma. Peter's identification of Jesus with the Mosaic tradition may thus contain an unintended irony: from the Gnostic perspective, the true significance of Christ transcends the Demiurge's covenant with Israel.

The call to repentance for the "blotting out" of sins (3:19) is reinterpreted in Gnostic theology as a call to awakening — the recognition that sin is ultimately ignorance (*agnoia*) rather than moral transgression. The "times of refreshing" (3:19) refer not to a future eschatological event but to the present experience of gnosis, through which the soul is refreshed and restored to its true nature.
