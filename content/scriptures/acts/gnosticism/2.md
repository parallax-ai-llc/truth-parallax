Acts 2 narrates the Pentecost event, which Gnostic interpreters read as a genuine pneumatic experience that the proto-orthodox church subsequently domesticated. The descent of the Spirit with tongues of fire and the gift of supernatural speech represent an authentic encounter with divine power. However, Gnostics distinguish between the Spirit's raw, transformative energy and the institutional interpretations immediately imposed upon it by Peter's sermon.

Peter's sermon (2:14-36), with its christological claims and call to baptism, is viewed by Gnostic readers as the beginning of the exoteric reduction of the Pentecost experience. The true significance of the Spirit's coming is not institutional membership through water baptism but individual awakening to the divine spark within. The Valentinian understanding of three types of humanity — pneumatic (spiritual), psychic (soulish), and hylic (material) — finds confirmation here: the pneumatics receive the Spirit's true meaning, while the psychic majority accept Peter's institutional interpretation.

The early community's shared life (2:42-47) is read with ambivalence. The sharing of possessions and the communal meals may reflect an authentic pneumatic fellowship in which material distinctions dissolve. But the devotion to "the apostles' teaching" (2:42) represents, for Gnostic interpreters, the consolidation of a particular doctrinal line that would eventually suppress the diversity of spiritual experience and esoteric teaching that the Spirit's descent had unleashed.
