Acts 5 presents the story of Ananias and Sapphira and the apostles' ongoing ministry. The sudden deaths of Ananias and Sapphira (5:1-11) are read by Gnostic interpreters as evidence of the psychic church's punitive character. The true pneumatic community operates through illumination and persuasion, not through fear and judgment. The God who kills for financial deception is, in the Gnostic reading, the Demiurge — the jealous, wrathful deity of the Old Testament, not the compassionate Father of the Pleroma.

Peter's growing authority — his shadow healing the sick (5:15) — is viewed with suspicion by Gnostic readers who see in this the beginnings of the cult of personality that would eventually produce the papal institution. The *Acts of Peter* (an apocryphal Gnostic-influenced text) tells a different story of Peter's relationship to power, including his humiliation and eventual crucifixion upside-down as a sign that the world's values must be inverted.

Gamaliel's counsel (5:33-39) receives an unexpected reading in Gnostic tradition. His pragmatic wait-and-see approach is seen as the wisdom of the psychic level — reasonable but lacking the deeper insight of gnosis. The movement will endure not because it is "of God" in Gamaliel's sense but because it carries, however imperfectly, the spark of true gnosis that the archons cannot extinguish.
