Acts 12 narrates Herod's persecution, James' execution, and Peter's escape from prison. Gnostic interpreters read Herod Agrippa as an archontic figure — a ruler of this world who wields power in service of ignorance and oppression. His persecution of the Church mirrors the archons' opposition to gnosis throughout cosmic history. The execution of James (12:2) demonstrates the material cost of bearing witness in the Demiurge's realm.

Peter's liberation from prison by an angel (12:6-11) is read as a symbolic narrative of the soul's liberation from the material prison. The chains that fall from Peter's wrists, the gates that open of their own accord, the angel's guiding light — all correspond to the Gnostic myth of the soul's escape from the archontic spheres. The iron gate that opens "of its own accord" (12:10) is the boundary between the material world and spiritual freedom, which yields to divine power.

Herod's death (12:20-23) — struck down for accepting divine worship — is read as cosmic justice against the archontic presumption. The Demiurge himself makes the same error: he declares "I am God and there is no other beside me" (cf. Isaiah 45:5 and its Gnostic inversion in the *Apocryphon of John*). When mortal rulers imitate the Demiurge's arrogance, they meet the same fate. The closing note that "the word of God increased and multiplied" (12:24) confirms that archontic opposition cannot suppress the spread of gnosis.
