Acts 27 describes the sea voyage and shipwreck en route to Rome. Gnostic interpreters read this chapter as an allegory of the soul's perilous journey through the material world. The sea represents the chaos of material existence — the formless matter (*hyle*) out of which the Demiurge fashioned his inferior cosmos. The ship is the body or the institutional church, a fragile vessel navigating hostile forces. The storm is the assault of the archontic powers on those who carry the divine spark.

The angel's assurance to Paul (27:24) that all aboard will be saved represents the Gnostic teaching that the presence of pneumatic persons benefits even those of lower spiritual grades. The pneumatic does not escape the material world alone; his gnosis radiates outward, creating a sphere of protection that encompasses those around him. The 276 persons saved represent the mixed community of pneumatics, psychics, and hylics who are carried through the storm by the presence of the enlightened one.

The breaking of the ship (27:41) and the swimming to shore (27:44) symbolize the dissolution of the material vehicle and the survival of the essential self. The body perishes; the soul endures. The institutional structures of religion break apart under the pressure of spiritual crisis, but the gnosis they carried survives. The arrival on shore is not the end of the journey but a new beginning — the pneumatic continues toward Rome, toward the heart of the archontic system, carrying the indestructible seed of divine knowledge.
