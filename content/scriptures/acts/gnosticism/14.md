Acts 14 continues the missionary journey through Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe. The incident at Lystra, where Paul and Barnabas are mistaken for Zeus and Hermes (14:11-13), is of interest to Gnostic interpreters because it raises the question of divine appearances in human form. Gnostic christology, particularly in its Docetic forms, taught that the Savior appeared in human guise without being truly human. The Lystrans' mistake is, in Gnostic reading, not entirely wrong — divine beings do appear in human form — but misdirected: Paul and Barnabas are not gods but channels of a power that transcends them.

Paul's speech about the living God who made heaven and earth (14:15-17) is evaluated critically by Gnostic interpreters. The God who made heaven and earth is, in Gnostic cosmology, the Demiurge, not the supreme God. The witness of rain and fruitful seasons (14:17) belongs to the Demiurge's administration of the material world. The true God of the Pleroma is known not through material blessings but through interior gnosis. Paul's speech, in Gnostic reading, addresses the psychic level without touching the pneumatic depths.

Paul's stoning at Lystra (14:19) and his perseverance demonstrate the hostility of the material world toward those who carry a spiritual message. The appointment of elders (14:23) in each community is viewed by Gnostic interpreters as the institutionalization of what began as a pneumatic movement. Each elder represents a fixed authority that replaces the fluid, Spirit-led community with a hierarchical structure — the beginning of the proto-orthodox church that would eventually suppress all alternative voices.
