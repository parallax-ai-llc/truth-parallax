Acts 6 narrates the appointment of the Seven and the emergence of Stephen. Gnostic interpreters note the division between Hellenists and Hebrews (6:1) as evidence of the early community's internal diversity — a diversity that the proto-orthodox church would later suppress in its drive toward institutional uniformity. The Hellenists, with their Greek philosophical background, may have been more receptive to esoteric teaching than the Hebrew-speaking community rooted in Torah observance.

Stephen's characterization as "full of grace and power" and a worker of "great wonders and signs" (6:8) marks him, in Gnostic reading, as a pneumatic figure — one who possesses the divine spark in full measure. His opponents from the Hellenistic synagogues "could not withstand the wisdom and the Spirit with which he was speaking" (6:10). Gnostic interpreters see here a conflict between institutional religious authority and genuine spiritual illumination.

The number seven — the seven appointed servants — resonates with Gnostic cosmology, in which seven archons govern the seven planetary spheres that imprison the soul. Whether this is coincidental or reflects a deeper symbolic structure, Gnostic readers found numerical correspondences significant. The appointment of seven leaders within the community mirrors, and perhaps inverts, the archontic structure of the cosmos.
