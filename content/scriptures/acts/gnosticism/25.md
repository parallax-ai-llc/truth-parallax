Acts 25 describes Paul's appeal to Caesar and Festus' consultation with Agrippa. Paul's appeal to the emperor (25:10-11) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the pneumatic engaging the highest level of archontic authority. Caesar represents the summit of worldly power — the human expression of the Demiurge's rule. Paul's journey toward this encounter is the gnosis-bearing soul's ascent through the planetary spheres, confronting each archon in turn.

Festus' bewilderment — the case concerns "a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (25:19) — illustrates the incomprehension of the hylic mind when confronted with spiritual reality. The material person can perceive only death and life in physical terms. The pneumatic understanding of Christ's "life" transcends the categories of physical existence and death entirely. In Valentinian christology, the true Christ never died because the spiritual Aeon cannot be destroyed; only the material appearance suffered on the cross.

The formal hearing with "great pomp" (25:23) — military commanders, prominent men of the city — is a display of archontic power that serves, ironically, as the stage for the pneumatic's testimony. The pattern recurs throughout Gnostic literature: the powers of this world assemble in their glory, but the humble bearer of gnosis possesses a power that dwarfs their pageantry.
