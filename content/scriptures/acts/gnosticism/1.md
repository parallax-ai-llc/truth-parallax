Acts 1 describes the departure of Christ from the material realm and the reconstitution of the apostolic circle. Gnostic interpreters read the ascension (1:9-11) not as a journey upward within the created cosmos but as a return to the Pleroma — the fullness of divine reality from which the Savior descended. The cloud that receives him (1:9) is understood as the boundary between the material world of the Demiurge and the transcendent realm of the true God. The angels' promise of return (1:11) is reinterpreted as a promise of ongoing spiritual presence accessible through gnosis, not a future physical return.

The disciples' question about restoring the kingdom to Israel (1:6) is read by Gnostic commentators as evidence of the apostles' incomplete understanding. The earthly kingdom is the realm of the Demiurge; the true kingdom is spiritual and interior. Jesus' redirection toward the Spirit (1:8) is understood as pointing beyond political restoration to pneumatic illumination. The Valentinian teacher Heracleon, as reported by Origen, taught that the true apostolic mission was the transmission of secret knowledge, not the establishment of an earthly institution.

The replacement of Judas by Matthias through lot-casting (1:15-26) is viewed ambivalently. Some Gnostic traditions saw Judas not as a traitor but as the instrument of a necessary divine plan — the Gospel of Judas presents him as acting on Jesus' secret instruction. The institutional concern with maintaining the number twelve reflects, in Gnostic reading, the psychic church's fixation on external structures rather than interior illumination.
