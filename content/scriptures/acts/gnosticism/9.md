Acts 9 records the conversion of Saul, a figure of enormous interest to Gnostic interpreters. The Valentinian school claimed Paul as their primary apostolic authority. Valentinus himself claimed to have received secret teaching through Theudas, a disciple of Paul. Paul's language of "wisdom among the mature" (1 Corinthians 2:6), hidden mysteries, and spiritual versus fleshly believers was read as evidence that Paul was a Gnostic teacher who transmitted esoteric knowledge alongside his public preaching.

The Damascus road experience (9:3-9) — a blinding light, a heavenly voice, temporary blindness — is read as a classic Gnostic initiation. The light that blinds Saul is not the light of the material sun but the uncreated light of the Pleroma breaking through the archontic barriers. Saul's blindness represents the death of his old, ignorant self; his restoration of sight through Ananias represents the birth of pneumatic vision. The experience follows the Gnostic pattern of death and rebirth through gnosis.

However, Gnostic readers note the tension in Acts' portrayal of Paul. The canonical text domesticates Paul within the institutional church — he is baptized, he submits to Ananias, he is accepted by the Jerusalem apostles. The Gnostic Paul, by contrast, receives his authority directly from the heavenly Christ (Galatians 1:11-12) and operates independently of the Jerusalem hierarchy. The *Gospel of Truth*, attributed to Valentinus, reflects a Pauline theology freed from the institutional constraints that Acts imposes.
