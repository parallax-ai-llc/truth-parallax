Acts 10 describes Peter's vision and the inclusion of Cornelius. Gnostic interpreters read this chapter as a moment when the material categories that divide humanity — Jew and Gentile, clean and unclean — are transcended by spiritual reality. The vision of the sheet with all kinds of animals (10:11-16) is understood not merely as the abolition of dietary laws but as a symbol of the dissolution of all material distinctions. In the Pleroma, there is no Jew or Gentile, no clean or unclean — only the divine spark that unites all pneumatics.

Peter's declaration that "God shows no partiality" (10:34) is affirmed in Gnostic theology, but with a deeper meaning. The Demiurge shows partiality — he chooses Israel, he establishes laws that distinguish one group from another. The true God of the Pleroma shows no partiality because all who possess the divine spark are equally his children, regardless of ethnic or religious category. The Cornelius episode, in Gnostic reading, reveals a truth that transcends Peter's own understanding.

The spontaneous descent of the Spirit on Cornelius' household before baptism (10:44-48) is a key text for Gnostic pneumatology. The Spirit acts freely, without institutional mediation. No apostle lays hands on Cornelius; no priest administers a sacrament. The Spirit comes directly to those who are ready to receive it. This unmediated pneumatic experience is precisely what the Gnostic tradition affirms and what the institutional church, in its subsequent development, would restrict to sacramental channels controlled by ordained ministers.
