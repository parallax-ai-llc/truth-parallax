Acts 17 describes Paul's engagement with Greek philosophy at Athens. The Areopagus speech (17:22-31) is one of the most significant passages for Gnostic interpretation because it demonstrates the interface between Christian preaching and philosophical inquiry — precisely the intellectual environment in which Gnosticism flourished.

Paul's reference to the "unknown god" (17:23) resonates deeply with Gnostic theology. The true God is, in Gnostic teaching, fundamentally unknown — invisible, incomprehensible, beyond all categories. The Demiurge is a known god who demands worship and obedience; the true God is the Unknown who can be approached only through gnosis. Paul's gesture toward an "unknown god" at Athens is, for Gnostic readers, the moment when apostolic preaching comes closest to the Gnostic vision — though Paul then narrows the unknown into the knowable categories of creator and judge.

Paul's assertion that "in him we live and move and have our being" (17:28), quoting the Greek poet Epimenides, echoes the Gnostic conviction that the divine permeates all reality. The pneumatic dimension of human existence — the divine spark within — is what makes this statement true. Humans "live and move and have their being" in the true God not because they are creatures of the Demiurge but because they carry within them a fragment of the transcendent Pleroma. The Athenians' mixed response (17:32-34) — some mocking, some believing — reflects the Gnostic typology: hylics reject, psychics hesitate, pneumatics recognize the truth.
