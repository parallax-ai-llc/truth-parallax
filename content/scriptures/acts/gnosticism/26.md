Acts 26 contains Paul's defense before Agrippa, which Gnostic interpreters read as the climactic revelation of Paul's pneumatic vocation. His account of the heavenly commission — to open eyes, turn people from darkness to light, from the power of Satan to God (26:18) — is a comprehensive description of the Gnostic salvific program. The opening of eyes is the awakening of gnosis; the turn from darkness to light is the movement from ignorance to illumination; liberation from Satan's power is escape from the archons' dominion.

Paul's claim that he teaches "nothing but what the prophets and Moses said would come to pass" (26:22) is read on two levels. The exoteric meaning connects the gospel to Israelite prophecy. The esoteric meaning, as Valentinian interpreters understood it, is that the prophets and Moses themselves, though serving the Demiurge, occasionally transmitted truths from the Pleroma above — truths that the Demiurge himself did not understand. The Christ's suffering and resurrection reveal the hidden meaning of the prophetic tradition.

Agrippa's response — "In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?" (26:28) — dramatizes the moment of decision between psychic hesitation and pneumatic commitment. Agrippa represents the soul at the threshold: intellectually engaged, emotionally moved, but ultimately unwilling to abandon the security of worldly position for the radical freedom of gnosis. The Gnostic reader recognizes the tragedy: Agrippa could have been awakened, but the pull of the material world was too strong.
