Acts 22 presents Paul's defense before the Jerusalem crowd. His account of the Damascus road experience (22:6-11) — the blinding light, the heavenly voice, the three days of darkness — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a classic description of pneumatic initiation. The light is the uncreated luminosity of the Pleroma penetrating the archontic veil. The blindness is the dissolution of conventional sight that must precede spiritual vision. The three days in darkness mirror the descensus ad inferos — the journey through the underworld that precedes spiritual rebirth.

Paul's description of his commission — to be a witness to "all people" of what he has seen and heard (22:15) — is interpreted by Valentinian Gnostics as including both exoteric and esoteric testimony. To the public, Paul witnesses through preaching and miracles; to the elect, he transmits the secret knowledge revealed to him in his heavenly encounter. The canonical text preserves only the exoteric dimension; the esoteric teaching is known through the Valentinian tradition.

The crowd's violent reaction to the word "Gentiles" (22:21-22) reveals the psychic attachment to ethnic and religious boundaries. The Demiurge's chosen people cannot tolerate the suggestion that his grace extends beyond their exclusive circle. Gnostic universalism, which recognizes the divine spark in all pneumatics regardless of ethnicity, stands in direct opposition to this exclusivism. The riot is thus a manifestation of archontic resistance to the dissolution of the boundaries that maintain the Demiurge's order.
