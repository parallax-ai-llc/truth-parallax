Acts 28 concludes with Paul's arrival in Rome and his continued preaching. The viper episode on Malta (28:3-6) is read by Gnostic interpreters as a demonstration of pneumatic power over the material world's hostility. The serpent — a figure of great ambiguity in Gnostic tradition (the Ophites and Naassenes revered the serpent as a bringer of gnosis) — fails to harm the pneumatic Paul. The islanders' shifting interpretation — first seeing Paul as a murderer, then as a god (28:4-6) — illustrates the fickleness of hylic perception, which oscillates between condemnation and worship without ever achieving understanding.

Paul's final encounter with the Jewish leaders in Rome (28:17-28) and his citation of Isaiah's prophecy of spiritual blindness (28:26-27) are read by Gnostic interpreters as a definitive statement about the limits of the psychic level. Those who cannot see and cannot hear are trapped in the Demiurge's system of law and temple, unable to perceive the spiritual reality that transcends their categories. Paul's turn to the Gentiles (28:28) is read not as a historical shift in missionary strategy but as the gnosis moving beyond the boundaries of the Demiurge's covenant people to reach all who possess the divine spark.

The final verse — Paul preaching "the kingdom of God" in Rome "with all boldness and without hindrance" (28:31) — is the most important sentence in Acts for Gnostic readers. The word *akolytos* ("without hindrance") is the book's last word, and it resonates with the Gnostic conviction that gnosis cannot ultimately be suppressed. Chains, trials, shipwrecks, institutional opposition — none of these can silence the truth. The open ending of Acts is, for Gnostic interpreters, an invitation: the Acts of the true Apostles continue wherever gnosis is sought and found, in every age, beyond the control of any institution.
