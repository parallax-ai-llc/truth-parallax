Acts 13 describes the beginning of Paul's missionary journeys. The Holy Spirit's command — "Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul" (13:2) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a genuine pneumatic commissioning, though they note that the canonical text frames it within institutional worship (fasting, prayer, laying on of hands). The Valentinian Paul would need no institutional authorization; his commission comes directly from the heavenly Christ.

The encounter with the magician Elymas (Bar-Jesus) on Cyprus (13:6-12) is read differently in Gnostic tradition. The canonical text presents Elymas as an opponent who is blinded by Paul's spiritual authority. Gnostic readers note the irony: Paul himself was blinded on the Damascus road. Blindness in Gnostic symbolism can represent both ignorance and the overwhelming encounter with transcendent light. The contest between Paul and Elymas may represent not a simple victory of truth over falsehood but a competition between rival spiritual practitioners — a common theme in the apocryphal Acts literature.

Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch (13:16-41) employs salvation history in a manner that Gnostic readers found partially useful but ultimately limited. The survey of Israel's history is read through the Gnostic lens: the Exodus, the wilderness, the monarchy — all are events within the Demiurge's realm. Paul's christological conclusion, while pointing toward a higher truth, remains trapped within the categories of the Demiurge's covenant. The Gnostic Paul, by contrast, proclaims "wisdom among the mature" — a message that transcends the salvation-history framework entirely.
