## Overview

Gnostic engagement with Acts is paradoxical. On one hand, Gnostic communities produced their own "Acts" literature — the *Acts of John*, *Acts of Peter*, *Acts of Thomas*, and others — suggesting that the canonical Acts' narrative framework was compelling. On the other hand, Gnostic interpreters read canonical Acts as a text that both conceals and inadvertently reveals the conflict between esoteric and exoteric Christianity. The figure of Simon Magus (chapter 8) became a lightning rod for this debate.

## The Gnostic Counter-Narrative

### Simon Magus as Gnostic Hero
Canonical Acts portrays Simon the magician (8:9-24) as a charlatan rebuked by Peter. Gnostic traditions, however, revered Simon as a divine revealer. The Simonians considered him an incarnation of the Great Power. Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* 1.23) identifies Simon as the origin of all Gnostic heresies — a claim that, ironically, elevates Simon's importance. The clash between Simon and Peter in Acts 8 is read by Gnostics as the first battle between gnosis and ecclesiastical authority.

### Paul as Ambiguous Figure
Gnostic readers found Paul both useful and suspect. His language of "wisdom among the mature" (1 Corinthians 2:6), spiritual versus fleshly believers, and hidden mysteries resonated with Gnostic categories. Yet Acts' Paul submits to the Jerusalem authorities and works within institutional structures. Valentinian Gnostics claimed Paul transmitted secret teachings to Theudas, who passed them to Valentinus — a chain of transmission that bypasses the institutional church of Acts.

### Pneumatic vs. Institutional Church
Gnostics read Acts' narrative of church organization — the appointment of deacons, the Jerusalem Council, the establishment of elders — as the hardening of a pneumatic movement into an institutional one. The Spirit-filled freedom of Pentecost gives way to hierarchical control, a trajectory Gnostics viewed as a fall from spiritual authenticity.

## Gnostic Acts Literature

| Text | Date | Key Themes |
|------|------|-----------|
| *Acts of John* | 2nd century | Docetic christology, spiritual Eucharist, polymorphic Christ |
| *Acts of Peter* | 2nd century | Peter vs. Simon Magus in Rome, miraculous contests |
| *Acts of Thomas* | 3rd century | Thomas in India, "Hymn of the Pearl," bridal chamber sacrament |
| *Acts of Paul and Thecla* | 2nd century | Asceticism, women's authority, baptism by women |
| *Acts of Andrew* | 2nd-3rd century | Renunciation of the material world, encratite theology |

## Theological Significance

For Gnostic interpreters, canonical Acts represents the triumph of the institutional church over the pneumatic community. The "true" apostolic teaching was oral and secret, transmitted outside the structures that Acts celebrates. The book is thus read against itself — its silences and tensions revealing what the proto-orthodox author sought to suppress.
