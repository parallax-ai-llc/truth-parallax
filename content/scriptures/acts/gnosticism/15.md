Acts 15 records the Jerusalem Council, which Gnostic interpreters view as a pivotal moment in the institutionalization of Christianity. The debate over circumcision and the Law (15:1-5) is read not as a theological question about salvation but as a power struggle between competing factions within the early movement. The Jerusalem establishment (James, Peter, the elders) negotiates with the Antiochene mission (Paul, Barnabas) to produce a compromise that preserves institutional unity at the cost of spiritual freedom.

The council's decision — imposing four minimal requirements on Gentile believers (15:28-29) — is viewed by Gnostic readers as a half-measure. The pneumatic community needs no external rules; the Spirit guides each person from within. The council's decree, however moderate, represents the principle that institutional authority can legislate for the spiritual community — a principle that would eventually produce the creeds, canons, and inquisitions that suppressed Gnostic teaching.

The formula "it has seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (15:28) is particularly provocative for Gnostic readers. The claim that a human council's decision carries the authority of the Holy Spirit is precisely the kind of institutional co-optation of the pneumatic that Gnostics resisted. The Spirit speaks to individuals in the depths of their souls, not through committee decisions. The Jerusalem Council, in Gnostic reading, marks the moment when the institutional church began to claim monopoly over the Spirit's voice.
