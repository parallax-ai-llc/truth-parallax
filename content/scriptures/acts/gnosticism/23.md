Acts 23 records Paul before the Sanhedrin and the conspiracy against him. Paul's exploitation of the Pharisee-Sadducee division over resurrection (23:6-9) is read by Gnostic interpreters as a shrewd deployment of psychic categories to serve pneumatic purposes. The debate between Pharisees and Sadducees about resurrection, angels, and spirits (23:8) is, in Gnostic perspective, a debate within the Demiurge's system — neither side possesses true gnosis. The Pharisees are closer to the truth because they affirm the reality of the spirit world, but their understanding remains limited to the archontic cosmos.

The Lord's night appearance to Paul — promising testimony in Rome (23:11) — is read as a pneumatic revelation guiding the divine spark toward its destined mission. Rome, the center of worldly power, is also the center of the archontic system. Paul's journey to Rome is a pneumatic penetration of the archons' stronghold — carrying the seed of gnosis into the heart of the material world's ruling power.

The conspiracy of forty men (23:12-22) represents the archontic forces mobilizing against the bearer of gnosis. The number forty recalls the forty days of testing in the wilderness — a period of confrontation between the spiritual and the material. Paul's escape through human means (his nephew's warning, the Roman tribune's protection) demonstrates that the pneumatic does not rely solely on miraculous intervention but navigates the material world with practical wisdom.
