Acts 19 describes Paul's Ephesian ministry, including encounters with incomplete disciples, Jewish exorcists, and the cult of Artemis. The twelve disciples who knew only John's baptism (19:1-7) are of interest to Gnostic interpreters as evidence that multiple baptismal traditions coexisted in the early period. The distinction between John's baptism (water) and baptism in Jesus' name (with the Holy Spirit) resonates with Gnostic sacramental theology, which distinguished between the external rite (accessible to psychics) and the internal pneumatic transformation (accessible only to the elect).

The sons of Sceva episode (19:13-16) illustrates the difference between genuine pneumatic authority and its imitation. The evil spirit's response — "Jesus I know, and Paul I recognize, but who are you?" (19:15) — suggests that spiritual power is personal and relational, not formulaic. Gnostic initiatory practices similarly emphasized the personal transmission of spiritual authority from teacher to student, not the mechanical repetition of sacred words.

The burning of magical books (19:19) is read ambivalently by Gnostic interpreters. While Gnosticism rejected crude magic, it valued esoteric knowledge contained in sacred texts. The destruction of books prefigures, in Gnostic reading, the proto-orthodox church's later destruction of Gnostic scriptures — a campaign of suppression that would culminate in the condemnation of Gnostic texts at various councils and the fortunate concealment of the Nag Hammadi library. The Artemis riot (19:23-41) demonstrates the material world's resistance to spiritual transformation, whether that transformation comes in orthodox or Gnostic form.
