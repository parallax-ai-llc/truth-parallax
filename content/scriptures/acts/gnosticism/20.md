Acts 20 contains Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders. Gnostic readers find this passage both revealing and troubling. Paul's charge to the elders to guard the flock against "fierce wolves" (20:29) is, from the Gnostic perspective, deeply ironic. The proto-orthodox church would use precisely this language against Gnostic teachers, while Gnostics saw the institutional bishops as the wolves who devoured the pneumatic community's spiritual freedom.

The "breaking of bread" on the first day of the week (20:7) is read by Gnostic interpreters through the lens of Gnostic sacramental practice. The Valentinians, according to Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* 1.13), had their own Eucharistic celebrations involving bread, wine, and water, with distinctly Gnostic prayers and invocations. The Gnostic Eucharist was not a memorial of physical death but a participation in the pleroma of spiritual reality. The canonical breaking of bread is, in Gnostic reading, the exoteric form of a deeper sacramental mystery.

Paul's declaration that he proclaimed "the whole counsel of God" (20:27) is questioned by Gnostic interpreters. Valentinians argued that Paul proclaimed the whole counsel only to the pneumatic elect, while offering a simplified version to the psychic majority. The canonical text preserves only the exoteric teaching; the esoteric tradition was transmitted orally from Paul to Theudas to Valentinus. The farewell speech thus represents, in Gnostic reading, only the public face of a two-level apostolic ministry.
