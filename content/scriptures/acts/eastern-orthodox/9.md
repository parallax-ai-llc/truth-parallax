Acts 9 records the conversion of Saul on the Damascus road. The Orthodox Church commemorates this event as a feast and reads the passage as a theophany — a manifestation of the risen Christ in uncreated light. Saul's blindness (9:8-9) is understood not as punishment but as the overwhelming effect of encountering the divine glory — the same glory that blinded the Israelites when Moses descended from Sinai, the same light that shone on Tabor.

Ananias' role in Saul's conversion (9:10-19) demonstrates the Orthodox principle that even the most extraordinary divine encounter is mediated through the Church. Saul is not left alone with his private experience; he is directed to a member of the community who baptizes him and lays hands on him. Orthodox theology insists that the Christian life is ecclesial by nature — there is no salvation outside the communion of the Body of Christ.

The parallel narratives of Peter healing Aeneas at Lydda and raising Tabitha at Joppa (9:32-43) demonstrate the apostolic ministry of healing that the Orthodox Church sees as continuing in its sacramental life. The sacrament of Holy Unction (anointing of the sick) and the prayers for healing offered in the Church's liturgical services are continuations of this apostolic ministry. Peter's command to Tabitha, "Get up" (9:40), echoes Jesus' words and demonstrates that the apostles act in the power of Christ, not their own.
