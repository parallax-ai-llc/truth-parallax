Acts 25 describes Paul's appeal to Caesar and the introduction of King Agrippa. Paul's appeal to the emperor (25:10-11) illustrates the Orthodox understanding that civil authority, when it functions properly, serves as an instrument of divine providence. The Orthodox tradition distinguishes between the Church's spiritual authority and the state's temporal authority, while affirming that both ultimately serve God's purposes. Paul's appeal is not a lack of faith but a legitimate use of the legal order.

Festus' summary of the case — the dispute concerns "a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (25:19) — is an unwitting confession of the Paschal mystery. The Orthodox Easter proclamation resonates: "Christ is risen!" The entire Christian faith stands or falls with this claim. Orthodox theology sees the resurrection not as one doctrine among many but as the foundational reality from which all other doctrines flow — the Incarnation, the sacraments, theosis, and the final resurrection of all humanity.

The setting of Paul's upcoming defense before Agrippa — in the audience hall with "great pomp" (25:23) — illustrates the ironic reversal that the Orthodox tradition celebrates: the prisoner possesses the truth that the powerful lack. This inversion of worldly values — the last shall be first, the foolish things of the world put to shame the wise — is celebrated in the Orthodox hymnography of the martyrs and the fools-for-Christ (*iurodivye*).
