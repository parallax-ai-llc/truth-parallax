Acts 1 is read in the Orthodox Church on the feast of the Ascension and during the Paschal season. The ascension of Christ is understood not merely as a departure but as the glorification of human nature — the God-Man carries humanity into the divine life. St. John Chrysostom teaches that through the ascension, our nature, which had been condemned, now sits at the right hand of the Father. This is the ultimate fulfillment of theosis: human nature is deified in Christ.

The election of Matthias (1:15-26) demonstrates the principle of apostolic succession and conciliar governance that is foundational to Orthodox ecclesiology. The community prays together, discerns together, and acts together under the guidance of the Spirit. There is no solitary authority; decisions are made within the body of the faithful. The replacement of Judas also shows that the apostolic ministry must be maintained — the Twelve must be complete for the witness to be whole.

The promise of the Holy Spirit (1:4-8) anticipates Pentecost, which the Orthodox Church celebrates as one of the Twelve Great Feasts. The apostles are told to wait — a period of ascetical preparation and expectant prayer that the Orthodox tradition sees as a model for the spiritual life. Power for mission comes only after patient preparation in prayer and communion with God.
