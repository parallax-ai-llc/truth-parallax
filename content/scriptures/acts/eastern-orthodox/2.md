Acts 2 describes the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, which the Orthodox Church celebrates as one of the greatest feasts of the liturgical year. The icon of Pentecost depicts the apostles seated in a semicircle with tongues of fire above their heads, receiving the gift that transforms them from fearful disciples into bold witnesses. The hymns of the feast proclaim: "Blessed art Thou, O Christ our God, who hast revealed the fishermen as most wise, by sending down upon them the Holy Spirit."

The Pentecost event reverses the confusion of Babel. Where human pride scattered the nations and confused their speech, the Holy Spirit gathers the nations and enables understanding across all tongues. St. Gregory the Theologian sees Pentecost as the final stage of divine revelation: at Sinai, God revealed the Law; in Christ, God revealed his Son; at Pentecost, God revealed the Spirit in his full power and personality. The Holy Trinity is now fully manifest.

The description of the early community (2:42-47) — devoted to the apostles' teaching, fellowship, the breaking of bread, and prayer — is the Orthodox ideal of the Church. The "breaking of bread" is understood as the Eucharist, celebrated from the very beginning. The community's radical sharing of possessions is not merely an economic arrangement but an expression of the koinonia that flows from Eucharistic communion. St. John Chrysostom devoted some of his most passionate preaching to this passage, calling the early community's life a rebuke to the individualism and greed of his own congregation.
