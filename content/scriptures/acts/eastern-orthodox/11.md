Acts 11 describes Peter's report to the Jerusalem church about Cornelius and the founding of the church at Antioch. Orthodox ecclesiology notes that Peter gives an account to the community (11:1-18) — a pattern consistent with the conciliar nature of the Orthodox Church, where even the chief apostle exercises authority within the communion of the faithful, not above it. The community's response, glorifying God for granting repentance to the Gentiles (11:18), demonstrates the sobornost (conciliarity) that is constitutive of Orthodox governance.

The Antioch church (11:19-26) is of special significance in Orthodox tradition. Antioch is one of the ancient patriarchal sees, and the Orthodox Patriarchate of Antioch traces its origin to this period. The fact that believers were first called "Christians" at Antioch (11:26) gives the city a unique place in the history of the Church's identity. Orthodox tradition holds that Peter was the first bishop of Antioch before moving to Rome, establishing a Petrine connection that the Orthodox Church honors without attributing to it the universal jurisdiction claimed by Rome.

The famine relief sent from Antioch to Jerusalem (11:27-30) exemplifies the communion of love (*agape*) between local churches. In Orthodox ecclesiology, the universal Church is a communion of autocephalous and autonomous churches, each self-governing but bound together in faith, sacraments, and mutual care. The Antioch-Jerusalem exchange is a model of this inter-ecclesial communion.
