Acts 15 records the Council of Jerusalem, which Orthodox tradition regards as the model for all subsequent ecumenical councils. The principle of conciliarity (*sobornost*) — the whole Church discerning truth together under the guidance of the Holy Spirit — is the foundational principle of Orthodox governance. The declaration "it has seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (15:28) is the formula that every ecumenical council echoes: the council's authority is not merely human but pneumatic.

The interplay between Peter, James, and Paul at the council demonstrates the Orthodox understanding of episcopal collegiality. Peter speaks with authority as the chief apostle, but he does not decide unilaterally. James, the bishop of Jerusalem, offers the practical resolution. Paul and Barnabas testify to what God has done among the Gentiles. The decision emerges from communal deliberation, not from a single ruling authority. This is the basis for the Orthodox critique of papal absolutism: the bishop of Rome holds a primacy of honor but not a primacy of universal jurisdiction.

The apostolic decree (15:28-29) demonstrates that the Church has the authority, under the Spirit's guidance, to make binding decisions on matters of faith and practice. Orthodox canonical tradition sees in this decree the precedent for all subsequent conciliar canons — authoritative guidelines issued by the gathered bishops for the life of the whole Church. The letter's delivery by delegates (15:22-23) mirrors the Orthodox practice of communicating conciliar decisions through official channels.
