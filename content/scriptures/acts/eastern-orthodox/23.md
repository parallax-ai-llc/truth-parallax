Acts 23 records Paul's appearance before the Sanhedrin and the conspiracy against his life. Paul's claim to Pharisaic identity and his appeal to the resurrection (23:6) is read in Orthodox theology as evidence that the Christian faith is the true fulfillment of the hopes of Israel. The resurrection of the dead, which the Pharisees affirmed and the Sadducees denied, is the central affirmation of the Orthodox Paschal liturgy: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life."

The Lord's appearance to Paul in the night — "Take courage, for as you have testified to the facts about me in Jerusalem, so you must testify also in Rome" (23:11) — is a divine assurance that shapes the remainder of Acts. Orthodox theology sees in this vision the providential care of Christ for his Church. The gospel must reach Rome — the capital of the world — not by accident but by divine design. The Orthodox tradition affirms that the expansion of the Church to all nations is part of God's eternal plan.

The conspiracy of the forty men (23:12-22) and Paul's nephew's intervention demonstrate the interplay of divine sovereignty and human agency. Orthodox theology holds these in balance: God uses ordinary means — a young man's courage, a Roman tribune's sense of duty — to accomplish his extraordinary purposes. This is the theology of divine providence (*pronoia*) that pervades Orthodox spirituality: nothing happens by chance; all things serve God's saving will.
