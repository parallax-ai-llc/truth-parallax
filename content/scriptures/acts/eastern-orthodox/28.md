Acts 28 concludes with Paul's arrival on Malta, his journey to Rome, and his continued preaching under house arrest. The viper incident on Malta (28:3-6) is read in Orthodox tradition as a demonstration of apostolic sanctity — the holy person is not harmed by what would destroy others, because divine grace protects those who serve God's purposes. Orthodox hagiography contains numerous parallel accounts of saints preserved from natural dangers, understood as signs of God's protective care.

Paul's final encounter with the Jewish leaders in Rome (28:17-28) and his citation of Isaiah 6:9-10 bring Acts to its theological climax. Orthodox theology, following Romans 9-11, does not read this as a permanent rejection of Israel but as a mystery of divine providence. The Orthodox Good Friday liturgy, while lamenting the rejection of Christ, also prays for the illumination of all peoples. The relationship between the Church and the Jewish people remains a subject of ongoing Orthodox theological reflection.

The final verse of Acts — Paul "proclaiming the kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ with all boldness and without hindrance" (28:31) — is the enduring image of the Church's mission. The word *akolytos* ("without hindrance") concludes Acts on a note of triumph: despite imprisonment, persecution, and shipwreck, the gospel cannot be silenced. The Orthodox Church reads this open ending as an invitation: the Acts of the Apostles continue in the life of the Church, in every liturgy, every act of witness, every saint who follows in the apostolic way.
