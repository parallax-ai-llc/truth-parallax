Acts 14 continues the first missionary journey through Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe. The healing of the lame man at Lystra and the crowd's attempt to worship Paul and Barnabas as Zeus and Hermes (14:11-13) illuminate the Orthodox understanding of the distinction between worship (*latreia*), which belongs to God alone, and veneration (*proskynesis*), which may be offered to holy persons and icons. Paul and Barnabas tear their garments and insist they are mere men (14:15), refusing the worship that belongs only to God.

Paul's speech at Lystra (14:15-17) about God's witness in creation resonates with the Orthodox theology of the *logoi* — the rational principles embedded in all created things by the Logos (Christ), through which God communicates his presence. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that every created thing contains a *logos* that points to the Logos. Paul's appeal to rain, fruitful seasons, and food (14:17) as divine witnesses is an expression of this theology in apostolic preaching.

The appointment of elders (*presbyteroi*) in every church (14:23) is foundational for Orthodox ecclesiology. The Orthodox Church maintains the threefold ministry of bishops, priests (presbyters), and deacons as established by apostolic practice. The accompanying prayer and fasting demonstrate that ordination is a sacramental act, not merely an administrative appointment. Paul's exhortation that "through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God" (14:22) resonates deeply with Orthodox theology, which sees suffering as a path of purification and participation in the cross of Christ.
