## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Acts is inseparable from liturgical life. Acts is the appointed reading throughout the Paschal season — from Pascha to Pentecost — making it the Church's primary post-Resurrection text. The Orthodox Church reads Acts through the patristic consensus, particularly the homilies of St. John Chrysostom, and sees the early community as the living icon of what the Church is called to be in every age.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Liturgical Context
Acts is read in its entirety between Pascha and Pentecost. This liturgical placement is theologically deliberate: the outpouring of the Spirit at Pentecost is understood as the completion of Christ's Paschal work. The Church is born not merely as a historical institution but as the Spirit-filled Body of Christ.

### Patristic Exegesis
St. John Chrysostom's 55 *Homilies on Acts* are the foundational Orthodox commentary. Chrysostom emphasized the communal life of the early believers, their radical sharing of possessions, and the transformative power of the Spirit. His readings are both moral and mystical, drawing practical lessons for the Christian life.

### Conciliarity (*Sobornost*)
The Jerusalem Council (chapter 15) is the paradigm for Orthodox conciliar governance. The phrase "it seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (15:28) is the model for how the Church discerns truth — through the Spirit working in the gathered bishops, not through a single supreme authority.

## Theological Emphases

- **Pentecost as the Birthday of the Church**: The descent of the Spirit creates the community of faith
- **Theosis**: The apostles' transformation by the Spirit is a model for deification
- **Koinonia**: The common life of Acts 2:42-47 as the ideal of Christian community
- **Martyrdom and Witness**: Stephen's martyrdom (chapter 7) as the paradigm of Christian witness unto death
- **The Apostolic Faith**: What the apostles taught and practiced is the standard for all subsequent Christian life
