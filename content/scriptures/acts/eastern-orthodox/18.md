Acts 18 records Paul's ministry in Corinth, where he stays eighteen months. The Lord's night vision — "Do not be afraid... for I have many in this city who are my people" (18:9-10) — is a text of comfort and assurance in Orthodox pastoral tradition. God's elect are present even in the most seemingly godless environments, and the Church's mission is to gather them through patient proclamation and faithful presence.

The Gallio incident (18:12-17) raises questions about the relationship between the Church and civil authority. Orthodox political theology, shaped by the Byzantine concept of *symphonia*, envisions a cooperative relationship between the spiritual and temporal powers, each serving distinct but complementary functions. Gallio's refusal to adjudicate a religious dispute represents a differentiation of spheres that the Orthodox tradition generally affirms, while maintaining that the state has a responsibility to support the conditions in which the Church can fulfill its mission.

The account of Apollos (18:24-28) — eloquent, learned, instructed in the way of the Lord, but knowing only John's baptism — illustrates the Orthodox principle that theological formation is an ongoing process. Even the most gifted teacher needs the fuller instruction that comes through the living Tradition of the Church. Priscilla and Aquila's catechesis of Apollos (18:26) demonstrates that theological education is a communal responsibility, not merely an academic enterprise. The Orthodox catechumenate and the tradition of spiritual fatherhood (*starets*) reflect this apostolic pattern.
