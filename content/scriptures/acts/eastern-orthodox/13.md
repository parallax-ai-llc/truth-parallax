Acts 13 records the beginning of Paul's first missionary journey from Antioch. The Holy Spirit's command — "Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them" (13:2) — is read in Orthodox ecclesiology as the paradigm of missionary commissioning. The mission originates in the worshiping community's prayer and fasting, is directed by the Spirit, and is ratified through the laying on of hands. Orthodox missionary practice, from the evangelization of the Slavs to the Alaskan mission, has followed this pattern.

Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch (13:16-41) demonstrates the christological reading of the Old Testament that is central to Orthodox hermeneutics. Paul traces salvation history from the Exodus through David to Jesus, showing that the resurrection fulfills God's promises to Israel. The Orthodox liturgical reading of the Old Testament follows this pattern: the *parimiae* (Old Testament lessons at Vespers) are always selected for their typological connection to the feast being celebrated.

The pattern of Jewish rejection and Gentile acceptance (13:44-52) is read in Orthodox tradition with care. The Orthodox Church does not espouse replacement theology; rather, it affirms that the Church is the continuation and expansion of Israel's calling. The joy of the Gentile believers (13:48) and the filling with the Holy Spirit (13:52) demonstrate that the Spirit's work transcends ethnic boundaries while fulfilling the universal promise made to Abraham.
