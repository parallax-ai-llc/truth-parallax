Acts 8 describes the spread of the gospel to Samaria through Philip and the encounter with Simon Magus and the Ethiopian eunuch. The Orthodox Church reads the Samaritan mission as the beginning of the Church's catholic expansion — the gospel is not confined to Jerusalem but goes out to all peoples, as Christ commanded in Acts 1:8.

The Simon Magus episode (8:9-24) is foundational for the Orthodox canonical condemnation of simony — the purchase or sale of ecclesiastical office or spiritual gifts. The Orthodox Church has historically been vigilant against this abuse, and the canons of the ecumenical councils explicitly forbid it. Peter's rebuke of Simon (8:20-23) is cited as apostolic authority for the Church's disciplinary canons. The distinction between Philip's baptism and the apostles' laying on of hands (8:14-17) is read as reflecting the Orthodox distinction between baptism and chrismation (the sacrament of anointing with holy chrism, normally administered by a priest using chrism blessed by a bishop).

The Ethiopian eunuch's baptism (8:26-40) is a beloved passage in Orthodox tradition, illustrating the universality of the gospel and the necessity of guided interpretation. The eunuch's question, "How can I, unless someone guides me?" (8:31), affirms the Orthodox principle that Scripture is properly understood within the community of faith, under the guidance of the Church's living Tradition. The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church traces its origins to this event, making it one of the most ancient Christian communities in the world.
