Acts 20 contains Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders, a passage of profound importance for Orthodox pastoral theology. The gathering at Troas on "the first day of the week" to "break bread" (20:7) is read as a description of the Sunday Eucharist. The Orthodox Divine Liturgy, celebrated on the Lord's Day, is the continuation of this apostolic practice. Paul's lengthy discourse and the raising of Eutychus (20:9-12) occur within a Eucharistic context, linking the Word and the Sacrament as inseparable aspects of Orthodox worship.

Paul's charge to the Ephesian elders (20:28-31) is foundational for the Orthodox understanding of the episcopal office. The bishops are "overseers" (*episkopoi*) appointed by the Holy Spirit to shepherd the flock. Their primary responsibilities are pastoral care and doctrinal vigilance — guarding against the "fierce wolves" of heresy. The Orthodox Church sees its bishops as standing in unbroken succession from the apostles, and Paul's Miletus speech is the charter of that episcopal ministry.

Paul's emotional farewell — the mutual weeping and the embrace (20:37-38) — reveals the deep personal bonds within the apostolic community. Orthodox monastic tradition especially values this passage, recognizing that spiritual authority is exercised not through institutional power but through relationships of love, trust, and mutual vulnerability. The elder's (or starets') ministry in Orthodoxy is precisely this: a relationship of spiritual fatherhood marked by love, prayer, and transparent truth-telling.
