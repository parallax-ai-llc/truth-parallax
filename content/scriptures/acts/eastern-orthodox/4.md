Acts 4 describes the arrest of Peter and John and the boldness of the apostolic community. The Orthodox Church reads Peter's declaration before the Sanhedrin — that the healing was done "in the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth" (4:10) — as an affirmation of the power of the divine Name. The Orthodox tradition of the Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") finds scriptural grounding in the apostolic practice of invoking Christ's name for healing and salvation.

The statement that the Sanhedrin recognized Peter and John as "uneducated, common men" who "had been with Jesus" (4:13) is a key text in Orthodox spirituality. The source of apostolic authority is not learning or status but communion with Christ. The hesychastic tradition emphasizes that true theological knowledge comes not from academic study but from prayer, asceticism, and direct experience of God — being "with Jesus" in the deepest sense.

The community's prayer (4:24-31) is a model of Orthodox liturgical prayer: it begins with the invocation of God as Creator, recalls the prophetic Scriptures (Psalm 2), interprets present events in light of God's sovereignty, and asks for boldness to continue the mission. The shaking of the place (4:31) and the fresh outpouring of the Spirit demonstrate that prayer is not merely a human activity but an encounter with the living God who acts in response to his people's cry.
