Acts 17 describes Paul's preaching in Thessalonica, Berea, and Athens. The Bereans' examination of the Scriptures (17:11) is valued in Orthodox tradition, though with the qualification that Scripture is properly interpreted within the living Tradition of the Church. The Bereans did not interpret privately or individualistically; they searched the Scriptures communally and in response to apostolic preaching. Orthodox hermeneutics insists that the Bible is the Church's book, properly understood within the liturgical and patristic tradition.

Paul's Areopagus speech (17:22-31) is of particular significance for Orthodox theology. Paul's engagement with Greek philosophy — quoting Epimenides and Aratus, building on the concept of the "unknown god" — anticipates the Orthodox Church's historic relationship with Hellenistic thought. The Greek Fathers, from Justin Martyr to Maximus the Confessor, believed that the best of Greek philosophy contained seeds of the Logos (*logoi spermatikoi*), preparing the way for the full revelation in Christ. Paul's Athens speech is the scriptural foundation for this conviction.

Paul's proclamation that in God "we live and move and have our being" (17:28) resonates with the Orthodox theology of divine presence. God is not an absent clockmaker but the sustaining ground of all existence. This panentheistic emphasis (God in all things, though not identical with all things) is central to the Orthodox understanding of creation, icons, and the sacraments. The material world participates in God's life because God is intimately present to all that he has made.
