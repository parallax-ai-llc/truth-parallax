Acts 5 presents the judgment on Ananias and Sapphira and the continued ministry of the apostles. The Orthodox Church reads the story of Ananias and Sapphira as a warning about the holiness of the Church — the community gathered around the Eucharist is not merely a social organization but the Body of Christ, in which deception is a form of blasphemy against the Spirit. St. John Chrysostom compares their sin to Achan's violation of the ban in Joshua 7: both profane what has been consecrated to God.

The apostles' healing ministry (5:12-16) illustrates the Orthodox understanding of the Church as a place of healing. The faithful brought their sick into the streets hoping that Peter's shadow might fall on them (5:15). Orthodox tradition does not dismiss this as superstition but sees it as an expression of the principle that divine grace permeates the physical world. The relics of saints, holy water, blessed oil, and the touch of a holy person can all be vehicles of God's healing power because the material world has been sanctified by the Incarnation.

Gamaliel's counsel (5:33-39) is read in Orthodox tradition as an expression of divine providence working even through those outside the community of faith. The Orthodox Church has historically taken comfort in Gamaliel's logic: what is of God cannot be overthrown. The Church has endured persecution under Roman emperors, Ottoman sultans, and communist regimes, and its survival is testimony to its divine origin. The apostles' declaration, "We must obey God rather than men" (5:29), is the charter of Orthodox resistance to unjust secular authority.
