Acts 24 presents Paul's trial before the governor Felix. The charges brought by Tertullus (24:1-9) — that Paul is a troublemaker and desecrator of the Temple — represent the worldly perspective on apostolic ministry. Orthodox tradition recognizes that the Church's witness will always be misunderstood and misrepresented by those who judge by external appearances. The saints are often accused of what they most oppose.

Paul's defense (24:10-21) affirms his continuity with the faith of Israel: he worships the same God, believes the same Scriptures, and hopes in the same resurrection. Orthodox theology sees Christianity not as a new religion but as the fulfillment of Israel's vocation. The Orthodox liturgy is saturated with the Psalms, the prophetic readings, and the language of the Hebrew Bible, demonstrating this continuity at the deepest level of worship.

Felix's response to Paul's teaching on "righteousness, self-control, and the coming judgment" (24:25) — he was "alarmed" but postponed decision — is a patristic preaching text about the danger of spiritual procrastination. St. John Chrysostom warned his congregation against the Felix-like tendency to be moved by the truth but unwilling to act on it. Orthodox spiritual direction insists that the moment of grace (*kairos*) must be seized; the spiritual life does not tolerate indefinite delay.
