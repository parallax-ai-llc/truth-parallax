Acts 26 contains Paul's defense before King Agrippa, his most complete account of his conversion and calling. Paul's statement that he proclaims "nothing but what the prophets and Moses said would come to pass" (26:22) affirms the Orthodox hermeneutical principle of christological reading: the whole of Scripture testifies to Christ. The Orthodox lectionary arranges Old Testament readings precisely to demonstrate this continuity — every prophetic word finds its fulfillment in the incarnate Logos.

Paul's commission to "open their eyes, so that they may turn from darkness to light and from the power of Satan to God" (26:18) encompasses the full scope of what the Orthodox Church understands as salvation. Illumination (*photismos*), liberation from demonic powers, forgiveness of sins, and sanctification are not discrete steps but dimensions of the single journey toward theosis. The Orthodox baptismal rite — with its exorcisms, illumination, and chrismation — enacts this entire Pauline description.

Agrippa's response, "In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?" (26:28), and Paul's passionate reply (26:29) dramatize the evangelical urgency that marks Orthodox witness at its best. While the Orthodox tradition emphasizes patience, liturgical beauty, and the slow work of formation, it also recognizes moments of direct appeal. The lives of great Orthodox missionaries — Cyril and Methodius, Innocent of Alaska, Nicholas of Japan — demonstrate the same apostolic boldness that Paul displays before Agrippa.
