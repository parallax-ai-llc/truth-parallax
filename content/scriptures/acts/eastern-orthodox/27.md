Acts 27 recounts Paul's sea voyage and shipwreck on the way to Rome. Orthodox interpretation reads this chapter as a narrative icon of divine providence. The angel's assurance to Paul — "Do not be afraid... God has granted you all those who sail with you" (27:24) — demonstrates that God's care extends not only to his chosen servant but to all those in his company. The Orthodox understanding of intercession is grounded here: the prayers and presence of the righteous bring blessing to those around them.

The scene of Paul taking bread, giving thanks, and breaking it before the entire ship's company (27:35) has liturgical resonance in Orthodox tradition. While not a formal Eucharist, the actions echo the Eucharistic pattern that runs throughout Luke-Acts. Orthodox theology sees the Eucharistic impulse in every act of thanksgiving over bread — the material world becomes a vehicle of divine communion when received with gratitude. The entire cosmos is potentially Eucharistic.

The safe arrival of all 276 persons (27:44) fulfills the divine promise and illustrates the Orthodox conviction that God is faithful. The Akathist hymn to the Theotokos, which originated as a thanksgiving for deliverance from a naval siege, reflects this same trust in divine protection during perilous voyages. Orthodox maritime tradition — the blessing of ships, prayers for those at sea, the invocation of St. Nicholas as patron of sailors — draws on the apostolic experience recorded in this chapter.
