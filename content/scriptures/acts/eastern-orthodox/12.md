Acts 12 narrates the persecution under Herod Agrippa, the martyrdom of James, and Peter's deliverance from prison. The Orthodox Church venerates James the son of Zebedee as a holy apostle and martyr, commemorating his feast on April 30. His death by the sword (12:2) fulfills Christ's prophecy that James would drink his cup (Mark 10:39) and establishes the pattern of apostolic martyrdom that runs through the Church's history.

Peter's deliverance from prison through angelic intervention (12:6-11) while the Church prays ceaselessly (12:5) demonstrates the Orthodox conviction that prayer is effective — it is a real participation in divine action, not merely a psychological exercise. The Orthodox practice of continuous prayer, including the monastic tradition of perpetual prayer and the use of the Jesus Prayer, is grounded in the apostolic community's example. The angel's appearance also illustrates the Orthodox theology of the angelic orders as ministers of God who serve the Church.

Herod's death (12:20-23) — struck down for accepting divine worship — serves as a cautionary tale about the sin of pride and the usurpation of God's glory. Orthodox political theology, following the Byzantine symphonia model, teaches that the ruler's authority is delegated from God and must be exercised in humility and justice. When rulers claim absolute authority, they follow in Herod's footsteps. The chapter's conclusion — the Word of God growing and multiplying despite persecution (12:24) — affirms the Orthodox experience that the Church flourishes through suffering.
