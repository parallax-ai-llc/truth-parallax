Acts 3 records the healing of the lame man at the Beautiful Gate by Peter and John. Orthodox interpretation reads this miracle as a manifestation of the divine energies working through the apostles. The healing power is not Peter's own but the uncreated energy of God flowing through a human instrument — the same principle that underlies the Orthodox understanding of icons, relics, and sacraments. Matter is capable of being a vehicle of divine grace because God has sanctified the material world through the Incarnation.

Peter's sermon in Solomon's Portico (3:12-26) is a proclamation of the Paschal mystery — the death and resurrection of Christ — set within the context of Israel's history. The Orthodox reading emphasizes continuity: Jesus is the fulfillment of what Moses and the prophets foretold. The call to repentance (3:19) is understood as a call to *metanoia* — a radical transformation of mind and heart that is the beginning of the journey toward theosis.

St. John Chrysostom notes that Peter immediately deflects attention from himself: "Why do you stare at us, as though by our own power or piety we have made him walk?" (3:12). This humility is characteristic of the saints in Orthodox tradition — the holy person is transparent to the divine light, not a source of power in his or her own right. The healed man's response — leaping and praising God (3:8) — is an image of the whole creation restored to its proper orientation: doxology, the praise of the Creator.
