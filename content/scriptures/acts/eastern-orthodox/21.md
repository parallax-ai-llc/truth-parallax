Acts 21 describes Paul's journey to Jerusalem and his arrest in the Temple. The prophetic warnings about Paul's impending suffering (21:4, 10-14) and his determination to proceed are read in Orthodox tradition through the lens of the theology of martyrdom. Paul's words — "I am ready not only to be imprisoned but even to die in Jerusalem for the name of the Lord Jesus" (21:13) — echo the resolve of countless Orthodox martyrs, from the early Church to the modern era.

Paul's meeting with James and the Jerusalem elders (21:17-26) reveals a church still deeply rooted in Jewish practice. Orthodox theology reads this sympathetically: the early Jerusalem church maintained Torah observance not out of legalism but out of piety and cultural continuity. The Orthodox Church has always practiced inculturation — receiving the faith within the cultural forms of each people — and the Jerusalem church's continued Jewish practice is an early example of this principle. Paul's willingness to participate in a purification rite (21:23-26) demonstrates the flexibility of pastoral *oikonomia*.

Paul's arrest in the Temple (21:27-40) begins his passion narrative. Orthodox typological reading sees deliberate parallels to Christ's passion: the Temple setting, the mob's violence, the intervention of civil authority. The disciple's suffering mirrors the Master's, and this conformity to Christ through suffering is central to Orthodox spiritual theology. The cross is not merely a doctrine to be believed but a way of life to be lived.
