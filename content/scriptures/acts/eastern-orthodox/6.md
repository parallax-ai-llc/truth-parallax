Acts 6 narrates the appointment of the Seven to serve the community's material needs. Orthodox ecclesiology sees this as the institution of the diaconate — one of the three major orders of ordained ministry alongside the presbyterate and the episcopate. The laying on of the apostles' hands (6:6) is the sacramental act of ordination (*cheirotonia*) that continues in the Orthodox Church today. The deacon serves at the altar, assists the bishop, and cares for the poor — a ministry that reflects the servant-character of Christ himself.

Stephen is described as "full of grace and power" (6:8) — a description the Orthodox tradition reads as indicating advanced theosis. Stephen's face, shining "like the face of an angel" (6:15), recalls Moses' face shining after his encounter with God on Sinai (Exodus 34:29-35). In Orthodox theology, this radiance is not metaphorical but a real participation in the uncreated light of God — the same light that shone on Mount Tabor at Christ's Transfiguration. Stephen has become a living icon of Christ.

The false accusations against Stephen — that he spoke against the Temple and the Law (6:13-14) — foreshadow the theological crisis that the early Church must navigate: the relationship between the worship of the Temple and the worship of the Church. Orthodox liturgical theology sees the Church's worship as the fulfillment of Temple worship, not its abolition. The Divine Liturgy is the true service (*leitourgia*) to which the Temple service pointed.
