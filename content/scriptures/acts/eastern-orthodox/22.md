Acts 22 presents Paul's defense speech to the Jerusalem crowd. His testimony of conversion — the blinding light, the voice of Christ, the mediation of Ananias (22:6-16) — is read in Orthodox tradition as a theophany comparable to Moses' encounter at the burning bush and the disciples' experience on Mount Tabor. The uncreated light that blinded Paul is the same light that transfigured Christ, the same light that the hesychasts seek in prayer. St. Gregory Palamas would later articulate the theology of this experience.

The role of Ananias in Paul's conversion (22:12-16) reinforces the Orthodox conviction that salvation is ecclesial. Even the most dramatic private encounter with Christ leads to the Church: Ananias baptizes Paul, laying hands on him and mediating the Spirit. Orthodox theology holds that there is no Christianity apart from the Church, no spiritual life divorced from the sacramental community. Paul's baptism and the "washing away of sins" (22:16) are sacramental realities, not mere symbols.

Paul's invocation of his Roman citizenship (22:25-29) is understood in Orthodox tradition as a legitimate use of civil rights in service of the gospel. The Orthodox Church has historically navigated complex relationships with various states and empires, and Paul's pragmatic use of his legal status provides a model for engaging civil authority without compromising spiritual integrity. The dual citizenship of the Christian — belonging both to the earthly and the heavenly polity — is a recurring theme in Orthodox political theology.
