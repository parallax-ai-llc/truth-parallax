Acts 19 describes Paul's extended ministry in Ephesus, a city of great significance in Orthodox tradition. The twelve disciples who knew only John's baptism (19:1-7) and their reception of the Holy Spirit through Paul's laying on of hands illustrate the Orthodox distinction between baptism and chrismation. In Orthodox sacramental practice, chrismation (anointing with holy chrism) is administered immediately after baptism and confers the seal of the Holy Spirit — the personal Pentecost of each believer.

The sons of Sceva episode (19:13-16) warns against the reduction of the divine Name to a magical formula. Orthodox theology, particularly in the hesychastic tradition, teaches that the invocation of Christ's name in the Jesus Prayer is effective not as an incantation but as a genuine encounter with the Person of Christ. The Name is powerful because it makes present the One who bears it. The subsequent burning of magical books (19:19) demonstrates the radical break between Christianity and occultism that the Orthodox Church has consistently maintained.

The Artemis riot (19:23-41) reveals the social cost of Christian witness. The Orthodox Church has experienced this dynamic repeatedly — from the destruction of pagan temples in the Roman era to the persecution of Christians under various modern regimes. The gospel threatens vested interests, whether economic, political, or religious. The city clerk's appeal to lawful channels (19:38-40) is noted as a positive example of civil order, but the Orthodox tradition recognizes that legal processes can also be manipulated to suppress the truth.
