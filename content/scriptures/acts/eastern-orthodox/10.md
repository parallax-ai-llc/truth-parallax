Acts 10 records Peter's vision and his visit to the household of Cornelius, the Roman centurion. This chapter is of profound significance for Orthodox ecclesiology: it demonstrates that the Church is catholic by divine intention — not merely Jewish but embracing all nations. The vision of the sheet with all kinds of animals (10:11-16) is read as God's declaration that the distinction between clean and unclean, which had served to separate Israel from the nations, is now transcended in Christ.

Peter's words at Cornelius' house — "Truly I understand that God shows no partiality, but in every nation anyone who fears him and does what is right is acceptable to him" (10:34-35) — are central to Orthodox mission theology. The Orthodox Church has historically evangelized by inculturation, bringing the gospel into the language and culture of each people without imposing a single cultural form. The translation of Scripture and liturgy into vernacular languages — pioneered by Saints Cyril and Methodius among the Slavs — reflects this Petrine principle.

The descent of the Holy Spirit on Cornelius' household before baptism (10:44-48) demonstrates God's freedom to act beyond established patterns. Orthodox theology affirms the necessity of the sacraments as the normative means of grace while recognizing that God is not bound by the sacraments. The Spirit blows where it wills (John 3:8). Peter's command to baptize those who had already received the Spirit shows that sacramental baptism remains essential even when the Spirit has acted extraordinarily.
