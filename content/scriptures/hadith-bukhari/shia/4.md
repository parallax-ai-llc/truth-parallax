The Shia reading of Sahih al-Bukhari, Chapter 4 (Trade and Commerce) engages with economic ethics, fair dealing, and the regulation of commercial life according to prophetic guidance. Through the lens of Imami theological perspective, this section's 25 traditions are examined with attention to the role of Ahl al-Bayt and the scrutiny of narrators. The hadiths address contracts, the prohibition of usury (*riba*), honest weights and measures, and the distribution of wealth — providing a comprehensive framework for just economic relations.

Shia scholars evaluate these traditions through their own biographical dictionaries, assessing narrator reliability and doctrinal consistency. In this chapter, the interplay between specific prophetic directives and broader ethical principles provides rich material for Shia critical analysis. Each hadith is situated within the larger architecture of Sahih al-Bukhari's thematic organization, where its placement illuminates connections to related traditions elsewhere in the collection.

Shia scholarship brings a distinctive critical apparatus to these traditions, cross-referencing them with hadiths preserved in the Shia collections — particularly *al-Kafi*, *Man La Yahduruhu al-Faqih*, *Tahdhib al-Ahkam*, and *al-Istibsar*. Where traditions converge across sectarian lines, they provide powerful evidence for shared prophetic heritage; where they diverge, they illuminate the historical and theological factors that shaped each community's hadith corpus.
