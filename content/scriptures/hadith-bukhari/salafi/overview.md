## Overview

The Salafi movement accords Sahih al-Bukhari the highest authority among hadith collections, treating it as the preeminent source for establishing correct Islamic creed and practice after the Quran. Salafi scholars emphasize a direct, literal engagement with the text, seeking to derive rulings without the intermediary of later theological or legal elaboration that they view as potentially introducing innovation (*bid'ah*).

## Interpretive Approach

Salafi interpretation of al-Bukhari is characterized by a commitment to the apparent (*zahir*) meaning of hadiths, particularly in matters of divine attributes and creed. When al-Bukhari records hadiths describing God's attributes — such as His "hand," His "descending," or His "laughter" — Salafi scholars affirm these descriptions without figurative interpretation (*ta'wil*), following what they consider the methodology of the early Muslim community (*salaf*). This approach extends to legal rulings, where Salafi scholars prefer direct hadith-based evidence over later jurisprudential reasoning by analogy.

## Methodological Emphasis

For Salafi scholars, al-Bukhari's work represents the purest preservation of prophetic guidance. They place great emphasis on the *isnad* (chain of narration) as a uniquely Islamic mechanism for preserving religious knowledge, and they regard al-Bukhari's narrator criticism as a model for scholarly rigor. The collection serves as a primary tool for distinguishing authentic Sunnah from later accretions, cultural customs, and innovations that Salafi thought seeks to purge from Muslim practice.
