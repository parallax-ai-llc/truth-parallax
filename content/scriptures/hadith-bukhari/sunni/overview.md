## Overview

The Sunni tradition regards Sahih al-Bukhari as the most authentic book after the Quran. Imam al-Bukhari's meticulous methodology — requiring an unbroken chain of trustworthy narrators with confirmed contact between each link — set the gold standard for hadith authentication. Sunni scholars have built vast commentarial traditions around this collection, most notably Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani's *Fath al-Bari*.

## Interpretive Approach

Sunni engagement with Sahih al-Bukhari is characterized by deep trust in al-Bukhari's narrator evaluation (*jarh wa ta'dil*) and his arrangement of chapters, which often reflects subtle jurisprudential positions. The four Sunni legal schools — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali — all draw extensively on Bukhari's hadiths, though they may differ in how specific traditions are applied to derive legal rulings.

## Theological Significance

For Sunni Muslims, al-Bukhari's collection provides authoritative evidence for matters of creed (*aqidah*), worship (*ibadah*), and social conduct (*mu'amalat*). The hadiths contained within shape daily religious practice, from the precise manner of performing ablution to the ethics of commercial transactions. The collection's authority is such that questioning the authenticity of hadiths it contains requires substantial scholarly justification.

## Notable Commentaries

- Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, *Fath al-Bari* — the most comprehensive commentary
- al-Ayni, *Umdat al-Qari* — a Hanafi-oriented commentary
- al-Qastallani, *Irshad al-Sari* — widely used in educational settings
