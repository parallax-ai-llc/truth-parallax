Kaivalya Pada's vision of liberation as the "isolation" of pure consciousness raises fundamental comparative questions. Unlike the Buddhist *nirvana*, which extinguishes the illusion of a permanent self, Patanjali's *kaivalya* affirms the eternal existence of individual consciousness in its pure state. Unlike the Christian *beatific vision*, which is relational union with God, kaivalya is the soul's resting in itself. And unlike the Advaita *moksha*, which dissolves all distinction, kaivalya maintains ontological plurality.

These differences illuminate how each tradition's soteriology reflects its fundamental metaphysics. Where the Buddhist denies a permanent self, liberation is freedom from the illusion of self. Where the Christian affirms a relational God, liberation is eternal relationship. Where the Advaitin posits one reality, liberation is recognizing that reality. Patanjali's distinctiveness lies in affirming both individual permanence and isolation as the ultimate good — a vision that has few exact parallels in world religion but resonates with certain Jain and Samkhya conceptions of the liberated soul.
