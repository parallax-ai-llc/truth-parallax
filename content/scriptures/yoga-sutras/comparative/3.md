The supernormal powers described in Vibhuti Pada find parallels in virtually every world religious tradition. Buddhist texts describe *iddhis* (supernatural abilities) attained through meditation; Christian hagiography records levitation, bilocation, and clairvoyance among saints; the Sufi tradition speaks of *karamat* (charismatic gifts); and Daoist masters are attributed powers over nature and the elements.

What is particularly notable in comparative perspective is Patanjali's ambivalence toward these powers. His warning that siddhis obstruct samadhi (3.38) parallels similar cautions across traditions: St. John of the Cross warns against attachment to spiritual consolations; Zen masters dismiss *makyo* (illusory phenomena) during meditation; and the Desert Fathers distinguish between genuine spiritual gifts and demonic deceptions. This cross-traditional pattern suggests a widely recognized tension between extraordinary experiences as signs of spiritual progress and as potential traps for the ego.
