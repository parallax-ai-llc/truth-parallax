## Overview

The Yoga Sutras invite rich comparison with contemplative and meditative traditions across the world's religions. Patanjali's systematic analysis of mental states, obstacles to practice, and stages of absorption provides a framework that resonates with Buddhist jhana practice, Christian contemplative prayer, Sufi meditation, and modern psychological approaches to consciousness.

## Key Comparative Themes

### Meditation and Mental Cultivation
Patanjali's eight limbs parallel the Buddhist Noble Eightfold Path in structure and intent. The progression from ethical foundations (yama/niyama) through physical discipline (asana/pranayama) to mental absorption (dharana/dhyana/samadhi) mirrors the Buddhist threefold training of morality, concentration, and wisdom.

### States of Consciousness
The Yoga Sutras' detailed taxonomy of *samadhi* (with and without seed, cognitive and ultra-cognitive) invites comparison with the jhana stages in Theravada Buddhism, St. Teresa of Avila's interior mansions, and al-Ghazali's stages of Sufi ascent.

### Asceticism and the Body
Patanjali's relationship to the body — as both instrument and obstacle — parallels tensions in Christian monasticism, Buddhist renunciation, and Daoist body cultivation, though each tradition resolves this tension differently.

## Cross-Cultural Reception

- **Buddhism**: Scholars debate whether Patanjali drew from or influenced Buddhist meditation theory
- **Christian Contemplation**: Thomas Merton and Bede Griffiths compared yogic concentration with centering prayer
- **Modern Psychology**: Parallels with William James, Abraham Maslow's peak experiences, and mindfulness-based therapies
- **Global Yoga Movement**: The worldwide adoption and transformation of yoga raises questions about cultural translation
