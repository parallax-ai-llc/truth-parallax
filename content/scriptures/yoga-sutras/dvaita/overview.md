## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta, following Madhvacharya, reads the Yoga Sutras as supporting the fundamental distinction between God, individual souls, and matter. Patanjali's concept of *Ishvara* as a special, distinct *purusha* (1.24) resonates naturally with Dvaita's insistence on God's eternal transcendence. Yoga practice in the Dvaita framework is meditation upon the real attributes of the Supreme Being, never an attempt to merge with or become God.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Madhva's Framework
While Madhva did not write a direct commentary on the Yoga Sutras, his philosophical system incorporates yogic disciplines as devotional practice. Meditation (*dhyana*) is sustained contemplation of Vishnu's real qualities — omniscience, omnipotence, and infinite bliss.

### Jayatirtha and Later Commentators
Later Dvaita scholars situate Patanjali's categories within the *taratamya* (hierarchy) framework, where different souls achieve different levels of yogic attainment based on their innate spiritual capacity (*yogyata*).

## Theological Emphases

- **Real Distinction**: The *purusha-prakriti* dualism of Samkhya-Yoga supports Dvaita's realist metaphysics
- **Ishvara's Independence**: God alone is independently real (*svatantra*); all souls depend on Him
- **Graded Liberation**: Different souls attain different degrees of *samadhi* according to their innate rank
- **Meditation as Worship**: Yogic concentration is a form of devotional service, not self-deification
