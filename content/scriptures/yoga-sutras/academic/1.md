Scholars identify Samadhi Pada as the most philosophically dense section of the Yoga Sutras, drawing on multiple intellectual traditions current in ancient India. The opening definition of yoga as *chitta vritti nirodha* (1.2) reflects a psychology that scholars trace to both Samkhya and Buddhist antecedents. The five types of *vrittis* (1.5-11) constitute a sophisticated epistemology, and debate continues over whether Patanjali's categories were influenced by the Buddhist *Abhidharma* tradition.

The *Ishvara* sutras (1.24-26) present a significant scholarly puzzle, since classical Samkhya is generally atheistic. Some scholars (e.g., Gerald Larson) view the inclusion of Ishvara as an accommodation to theistic currents, while others argue it represents a distinct Yoga theology. The identification of Ishvara with the *pranava* (Om) connects the text to older Upanishadic and ritual traditions, suggesting Patanjali is synthesizing multiple streams of Indian thought into a single systematic framework.
