Scholars have noted that Kaivalya Pada differs in character from the preceding chapters, containing more philosophical argumentation and fewer practical instructions. Some scholars (e.g., Surendranath Dasgupta) have proposed that this chapter was a later addition, written to defend Yoga philosophy against rival systems, particularly the Vijnanavada (Yogachara) school of Buddhism. The chapter's arguments about the relationship between consciousness and its objects closely parallel debates found in Buddhist epistemological texts.

The concept of *kaivalya* itself has generated significant scholarly discussion. Unlike the Buddhist *nirvana* or the Advaita *moksha*, Patanjali's *kaivalya* describes the isolation of individual consciousness in its pure state, presupposing an ontological plurality of selves. Scholars debate whether this represents a genuinely distinct soteriology or whether later commentators (especially Vyasa) softened the radically isolationist implications. Philipp Maas's critical edition has reopened questions about the original meaning by demonstrating the close compositional unity of sutras and *bhashya*.
