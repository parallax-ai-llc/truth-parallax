## Overview

The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali is a collection of 196 aphorisms (*sutras*) that form the foundational text of classical Yoga philosophy. Attributed to the sage Patanjali (c. 2nd century BCE to 4th century CE), this concise work systematizes the theory and practice of yoga into a coherent philosophical framework. The text defines yoga as the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind (*chitta vritti nirodha*) and presents an eightfold path (*ashtanga yoga*) for achieving liberation (*kaivalya*).

## Structure

The Yoga Sutras are divided into four chapters (*padas*):

1. **Samadhi Pada** (Chapter 1): The nature and aims of concentration and meditative absorption
2. **Sadhana Pada** (Chapter 2): The practical means and disciplines for attaining yoga
3. **Vibhuti Pada** (Chapter 3): The supernormal powers arising from advanced practice
4. **Kaivalya Pada** (Chapter 4): The nature of final liberation and the pure consciousness

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Yoga Sutras occupy a unique place across Hindu traditions and beyond:

- **Vaishnavism**: Yoga as devotional discipline directed toward Ishvara (God)
- **Shaivism**: The sutras resonate with Shaiva meditation practices and Shiva as the ultimate yogi
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Yoga as preparation for the direct knowledge of non-dual Brahman
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Yoga practice as service to and meditation upon the Supreme Being
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding Indian psychology, epistemology, and soteriology
- **Comparative**: Widely compared with Buddhist meditation systems, Stoic disciplines, and Christian contemplative traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Chitta Vritti Nirodha | Cessation of mental fluctuations as the definition of yoga |
| Ashtanga Yoga | The eightfold path: yama, niyama, asana, pranayama, pratyahara, dharana, dhyana, samadhi |
| Ishvara Pranidhana | Surrender to God as a means of attaining samadhi |
| Kaivalya | Liberation through the isolation of pure consciousness from material nature |
| Klesas | The five afflictions: ignorance, egoism, attachment, aversion, clinging to life |
