## Overview

Advaita Vedanta treats the Yoga Sutras as a valuable practical manual whose techniques prepare the mind for the ultimate realization of non-duality. While Patanjali's Samkhya-Yoga framework posits a real plurality of *purushas* (conscious selves) and an independent *prakriti* (matter), Advaita reinterprets these categories within its own non-dual ontology. Yoga practice purifies the mind (*chitta shuddhi*), making it fit to receive the liberating knowledge that Atman is Brahman.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Shankara's Yoga
Shankara acknowledged yoga as a valid preparatory discipline. His commentary on the Brahma Sutras (2.1.3) distinguishes between yoga as practice (accepted) and Samkhya-Yoga metaphysics (rejected). The *Yoga Taravali*, attributed to Shankara, presents yoga within an Advaita framework.

### Vidyaranya
Vidyaranya's *Jivanmuktiviveka* integrates Patanjali's yoga with Advaita by arguing that *vasana kshaya* (destruction of latent impressions) and *mano nasha* (dissolution of mind) — both yogic goals — are necessary even after the dawn of self-knowledge.

## Theological Emphases

- **Yoga as Preparation**: Patanjali's practices purify the mind but do not themselves produce liberation
- **Witness Consciousness**: *Kaivalya* is reinterpreted as recognition of the ever-present witness (*sakshi*)
- **Superimposition**: The *kleshas* arise from superimposing the not-self upon the self
- **Integration**: Yoga and Vedanta are complementary, not competing systems
