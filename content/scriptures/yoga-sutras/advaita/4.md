Advaita Vedanta reinterprets Patanjali's *kaivalya* through its non-dual framework. Where classical Yoga sees liberation as the isolation of an individual *purusha* from *prakriti*, Advaita sees it as the recognition that there was never any real bondage to begin with. The Self (*Atman*) is eternally free; bondage is merely the superimposition (*adhyasa*) of limitations upon the limitless. Kaivalya, then, is not an event in time but the timeless recognition of what always already is.

Patanjali's analysis of consciousness and its objects (4.15-24) is read by Advaitins as approaching but not reaching the final truth. The Yoga Sutras correctly recognize that the perceiver is distinct from the perceived, but Advaita takes the further step of recognizing that even the distinction between subject and object is ultimately sublated in non-dual awareness. The final sutra's description of the *gunas* returning to their ground state (4.34) points, for the Advaitin, to the dissolution of all multiplicity into the one Brahman.
