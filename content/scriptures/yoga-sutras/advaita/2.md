Advaita Vedanta reads the practical disciplines of Sadhana Pada as *chitta shuddhi* — purification of the mind that makes it fit to receive the liberating knowledge "I am Brahman." Patanjali's five *kleshas* are understood through the Advaita lens as elaborations of the fundamental error of *adhyasa* (superimposition): mistaking the not-self for the Self, projecting reality onto the unreal.

The eight limbs of yoga serve, in the Advaita framework, as a comprehensive program of mental refinement. *Yama* and *niyama* establish *samadhana* (mental composure); *asana* and *pranayama* calm the body-mind complex; and the inner limbs of *pratyahara*, *dharana*, and *dhyana* progressively withdraw attention from external objects toward the inner witness (*sakshi*). However, the Advaitin maintains that yoga practice, however refined, only removes obstacles — it does not produce liberation, which is the ever-present nature of the Self, requiring only recognition.
