---
title: "Nayadhammakahao - Chapter 3"
scripture: "nayadhammakahao"
chapter: 3
---

**1** Mahavira spoke: "Now hear the story of the monk Keshi and the monk Gautama, and their great debate on the nature of the soul."

**2** "Keshi was a follower of Parshvanatha, the twenty-third Tirthankara, who had taught four vows. Gautama was a follower of Mahavira, the twenty-fourth Tirthankara, who taught five vows."

**3** "Keshi asked Gautama: 'Why does your teacher add a fifth vow — the vow of celibacy — when Parshvanatha taught only four?'"

**4** "Gautama replied: 'The four vows of Parshvanatha and the five vows of Mahavira are not in contradiction. The vow of celibacy is contained within the vow of non-possessiveness, for the body is the greatest possession.'"

**5** "'In Parshvanatha's time, monks had the strength to observe celibacy as part of non-possessiveness. In this age, it must be stated separately, for the strength of practitioners has diminished.'"

**6** "Keshi was satisfied with this answer, and the two traditions were reconciled. They recognized that the teaching of all the Tirthankaras is one."

**7** "Then they discussed the nature of the soul. Keshi said: 'Is the soul eternal or non-eternal?' Gautama replied: 'The soul is both eternal and non-eternal.'"

**8** "'From the standpoint of its substance, the soul is eternal — it was never created and can never be destroyed. From the standpoint of its modes, the soul is non-eternal — it changes from moment to moment.'"

**9** "'This is the doctrine of many-sidedness — anekantavada — which sees truth from multiple perspectives.'"

**10** "'The soul in its pure state is infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, and infinite energy. But covered by karma, it appears as limited, suffering, and bound.'"

**11** "'By removing the karmic coverings through right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct, the soul reveals its true nature and attains liberation.'"

**12** Mahavira said: "Thus did two great monks reconcile their differences through wisdom. Truth has many facets, and the wise see all of them."
