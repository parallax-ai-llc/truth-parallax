## Overview

The Nayadhammakahao (Nāyādhammakahāo), also known as Jnatridharmakatha ("Stories of Knowledge and Dharma"), is the sixth of the twelve Angas of the Shvetambara Jain canon. It is a collection of narrative parables and stories told by Lord Mahavira to illustrate the workings of karma, the virtues of renunciation, and the consequences of moral and immoral conduct. The text is notable for its literary richness and its use of storytelling as a vehicle for doctrinal teaching.

## Structure

The text contains 10 stories or narrative sections:

1. **Story 1 — The Tale of Prince Meghakumara**: Renunciation of royal life
2. **Story 2 — The Tale of Nami**: Patience and endurance under hardship
3. **Story 3 — The Tale of Dhanya**: The futility of worldly attachment
4. **Story 4 — The Tale of the Merchant**: Karma and its consequences
5. **Story 5 — The Tale of the Monk**: The power of austerity and discipline
6. **Story 6 — The Tale of Renunciation**: Leaving the household life
7. **Story 7 — The Tale of Compassion**: Non-violence in action
8. **Story 8 — The Tale of the King**: Impermanence of power and wealth
9. **Story 9 — The Tale of the Ascetic**: Endurance and spiritual victory
10. **Story 10 — The Tale of Liberation**: Achieving moksha through right conduct

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Digambara**: Value the narratives while preserving their own versions
- **Shvetambara**: Cherish the text as Mahavira's own storytelling
- **Academic**: Important for the study of ancient Indian narrative literature
- **Comparative**: Parallels with Buddhist Jataka tales and Hindu Panchatantra

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Narrative Teaching | Using stories to illustrate dharmic principles |
| Renunciation | The spiritual power of giving up worldly attachments |
| Karma | How past actions shape present and future destinies |
| Moral Exemplars | Models of virtue and spiritual heroism |
| Impermanence | The transience of worldly pleasures and power |
