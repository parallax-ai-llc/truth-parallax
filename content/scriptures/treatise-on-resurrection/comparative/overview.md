## Overview

The Treatise on the Resurrection addresses one of humanity's most universal concerns: what happens after death? Its answer — that the true self is already imperishable and that "resurrection" is the recognition of this fact — places it in conversation with traditions worldwide.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Hindu Parallels

The Bhagavad Gita's teaching that the atman is unborn and undying ("Never is it born nor does it die," 2.20) closely parallels the Treatise's insistence that the inner spiritual person is already imperishable. Both traditions locate the problem not in death itself but in the ignorance that identifies the self with the body.

### Buddhist Perspectives

While Buddhism rejects an eternal self, the Mahayana concept of Buddha-nature — the inherent enlightenment already present in all beings — offers a structural parallel to the Valentinian idea that the spiritual person is already resurrected but needs to realize this truth.

### Platonic Tradition

The text's debt to Platonism is direct. Plato's argument in the Phaedo that the soul is immortal and that philosophy is "preparation for death" provides the philosophical framework within which the Valentinian reinterpretation of resurrection operates.
