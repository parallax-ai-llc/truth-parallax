## Overview

For Jewish readers, the Treatise on the Resurrection provides evidence of how Jewish resurrection concepts — rooted in Daniel 12:2, Isaiah 26:19, Ezekiel 37, and Pharisaic theology — were transformed by heterodox Christian movements in the second century.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Jewish Resurrection Origins

The concept of resurrection (*techiyas ha-mesim*) developed within Second Temple Judaism and became a defining doctrine of Pharisaic/rabbinic Judaism. The Treatise's reinterpretation of resurrection as spiritual would have been rejected by the rabbis, who insisted on bodily resurrection as fundamental (Mishnah Sanhedrin 10:1).

### Hellenistic Jewish Context

The Treatise reflects the Hellenistic Jewish intellectual milieu in which Platonic concepts of the soul's immortality intersected with Jewish resurrection faith. Philo of Alexandria developed a sophisticated allegorical reading; the Treatise represents one trajectory within this complex field.

### Body and Soul

The text's devaluation of the body stands in tension with the mainstream Jewish affirmation of bodily existence as inherently good (Genesis 1:31). The rabbinic tradition maintained that the body is not a prison but a partner in divine service.
