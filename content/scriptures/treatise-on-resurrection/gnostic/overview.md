## Overview

For Gnostic readers, the Treatise on the Resurrection represents a clear and authoritative statement of the Valentinian understanding of resurrection: it is not a future reconstitution of the physical body but a present awakening to one's true spiritual nature. The text's teacher explains to Rheginos that the Savior "swallowed death" and "transformed himself into an imperishable aeon" — a model for every pneumatic (spiritual) person who, through gnosis, discovers that they have already risen.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Present Resurrection

The central Gnostic claim is that resurrection has already occurred for those who possess gnosis. The material body is compared to a garment that will be discarded; the true self — the inner spiritual person — is already alive and imperishable. This directly challenges the proto-orthodox insistence on future bodily resurrection.

### The Savior as Paradigm

Christ's resurrection is not unique but paradigmatic. What happened to the Savior demonstrates what is already true of every spiritual person: the inner reality is imperishable, and the apparent death of the body is an illusion to be seen through.

### The Illusion of Death

The text insists that "the world is an illusion" and that those who know this truth have already transcended death. Death is not an event that happens to the spiritual self but a condition of ignorance from which gnosis liberates.
