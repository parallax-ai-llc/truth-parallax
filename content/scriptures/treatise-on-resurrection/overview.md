## Overview

The Treatise on the Resurrection (NHC I,4), also known as the Letter to Rheginos, is a short Valentinian Gnostic epistle discovered among the Nag Hammadi codices in 1945. Written as a letter from an unnamed teacher to a student named Rheginos, the text addresses the fundamental question of whether the resurrection is real. The author argues that spiritual resurrection is not a future physical event but a present reality already accomplished through gnosis — the direct knowledge of one's divine origin and destiny. The text engages extensively with Pauline theology, particularly 1 Corinthians 15 and Romans 6, reinterpreting Paul's language of the "spiritual body" in a distinctly Valentinian direction. Most scholars date the composition to the late second century CE.

## Structure

The text is divided into three sections reflecting its epistolary and didactic character:

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The Reality of the Resurrection | The teacher addresses Rheginos's doubts, affirming that the resurrection is real through the example of the Savior who "swallowed death" |
| 2 | The Nature of the Spiritual Body | Discussion of the inner spiritual person as already resurrected, using the metaphor of a ray of light and engaging with Pauline categories |
| 3 | Exhortation and Practice | Final encouragement to Rheginos to live in the certainty of resurrection already accomplished, with instructions on sharing the teaching |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: A primary Valentinian text demonstrating the reinterpretation of resurrection as present spiritual awakening through gnosis
- **Academic**: Central to scholarly debates about the diversity of early Christian resurrection beliefs and Valentinian Pauline interpretation
- **Comparative**: Illuminates cross-cultural patterns of understanding death as transformation of consciousness rather than bodily annihilation
- **Christian**: Challenges orthodox insistence on bodily resurrection while engaging deeply with the same Pauline texts used by proto-orthodox theology
- **Jewish**: Provides evidence of how Jewish resurrection concepts rooted in Daniel 12, Ezekiel 37, and Pharisaic theology were transformed in heterodox Christian movements
- **Interfaith**: Raises universal questions about the nature of death, the relationship between body and spirit, and the possibility of liberation from mortality

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Spiritual Resurrection | The resurrection is not a future bodily event but a present spiritual awakening through gnosis |
| Valentinian Christology | Christ's resurrection is paradigmatic rather than unique — demonstrating what is already true of every spiritual person |
| The Illusory World | "The world is an illusion" — material reality is transient; spiritual reality is what endures |
| The Spiritual Body | Paul's *soma pneumatikon* is reinterpreted as the already-existing spiritual self, not a transformed physical body |
