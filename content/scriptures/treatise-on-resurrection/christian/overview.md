## Overview

For Christian readers, the Treatise on the Resurrection presents a direct challenge to one of the faith's most fundamental doctrines: the bodily resurrection of the dead. The text's argument that resurrection is a present spiritual reality rather than a future bodily event was explicitly condemned by proto-orthodox authorities (2 Timothy 2:17-18).

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Pauline Question

The Treatise's engagement with Paul is theologically significant because it demonstrates that Paul's resurrection language — particularly the distinction between "natural body" and "spiritual body" in 1 Corinthians 15 — was genuinely susceptible to multiple interpretations.

### Challenge to Creedal Orthodoxy

The Nicene Creed's affirmation of "the resurrection of the body" directly contradicts the Treatise's position. Yet the text's engagement with the same scriptural sources makes it a valuable dialogue partner for Christians examining the foundations of their resurrection faith.

### Realized Eschatology

The Treatise's realized eschatology finds echoes in the Gospel of John ("whoever believes in me has already passed from death to life," John 5:24) and in certain strands of Christian mystical theology.
