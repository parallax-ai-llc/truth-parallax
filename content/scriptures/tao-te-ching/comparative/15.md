Chapter 15 offers fertile ground for comparative analysis of attitudes toward conventional morality across philosophical traditions. The text's critique of artificial virtue parallels Nietzsche's critique of herd morality, the Zen tradition's suspicion of conceptual Buddhism, and the Cynics' rejection of social conventions. In each case, the established moral order is challenged not from amoralism but from a vision of a deeper, more authentic mode of being that transcends conventional categories of good and evil.

Comparative scholars also note the chapter's resonances with Romantic and Transcendentalist thought in the West. Rousseau's critique of civilization, Thoreau's advocacy of simplicity, and Wordsworth's veneration of nature all echo themes found in this chapter. These parallels suggest not direct influence but shared human intuitions about the costs of social complexity and the value of returning to a more natural mode of existence. The global recurrence of these themes across independent traditions is itself a significant datum for comparative philosophy.
