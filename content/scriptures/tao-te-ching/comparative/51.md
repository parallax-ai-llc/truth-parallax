Chapter 51 provides material for comparative analysis of political philosophy across civilizations. The Tao Te Ching's vision of minimal governance through moral example can be compared with anarchist traditions in Western thought, from Proudhon and Kropotkin to contemporary libertarian philosophy. Yet unlike Western anarchism, which typically grounds its critique of the state in the rights of the individual, the Tao Te Ching's political philosophy is rooted in a cosmological vision of natural order that precedes and transcends human institutions.

Comparative scholars also examine how the chapter's governance philosophy relates to the classical Indian concept of dharma and the Confucian ideal of governance through virtue. All three traditions share the conviction that political legitimacy cannot rest on force alone but requires a foundation in moral and cosmic order. The differences lie in how each tradition conceives of that order and the ruler's relationship to it, with the Tao Te Ching uniquely emphasizing the ruler's need to efface himself and allow natural processes to unfold without interference.
