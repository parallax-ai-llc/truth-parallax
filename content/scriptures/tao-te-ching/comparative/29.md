Comparative analysis of chapter 29 reveals significant parallels with process philosophy, particularly Alfred North Whitehead's concept of creativity as the ultimate metaphysical principle. Like the Tao, Whitehead's creativity is not a being but the condition of all becoming, and it manifests through the self-organizing activity of actual entities rather than through the imposition of external form. These structural parallels have led some scholars to speak of the Tao Te Ching as an ancient anticipation of process thought.

The chapter also invites comparison with indigenous philosophical traditions worldwide, many of which share the Tao Te Ching's emphasis on the interconnectedness of all things, the importance of maintaining harmony with the natural world, and the limitations of a purely anthropocentric worldview. Comparative scholars working at the intersection of Taoism and eco-philosophy have found in chapters like this one resources for articulating an environmental ethic grounded in respect for the intrinsic value of natural processes rather than in utilitarian calculations of human benefit.
