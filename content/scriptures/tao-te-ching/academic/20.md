Chapter 20 has attracted significant academic attention for its relationship to contemporary Confucian and Legalist thought. Scholars read the chapter's critique of conventional morality and governance as a specific response to the intellectual debates of the Warring States period, situating Lao Tzu's position within a concrete historical and political context. The anti-Confucian rhetoric, particularly the rejection of ren (benevolence) and yi (righteousness), is analyzed as a deliberate polemic rather than a timeless philosophical stance.

Textual comparison reveals that the Guodian version of chapter 20 differs from the received text in ways that may soften or complicate its apparent anti-Confucian message. Some scholars argue that the earliest layers of the Tao Te Ching were less hostile to Confucian values than the received text suggests, and that the sharpening of the text's polemical edge occurred during later stages of compilation. This hypothesis remains contested, but it illustrates the academic approach of treating the text as a historically situated document rather than a unified authorial creation.
