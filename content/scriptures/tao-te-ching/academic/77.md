The final chapters of the Tao Te Ching, including chapter 77, are examined by scholars for their possible relationship to the utopian and primitivist traditions of ancient Chinese thought. Chapter 80's vision of a small country with few people has attracted particular attention as a possible expression of the 'agriculturalist' (nongjia) school's ideals, while the closing chapter's meditation on the nature of true words connects to broader ancient Chinese debates about the relationship between language and reality.

Academic scholars note that the arrangement of the final chapters may reflect editorial decisions about how best to conclude the text. The progression from the concrete political vision of chapter 80 to the abstract linguistic reflection of chapter 81 creates a deliberate movement from practice to theory, from the particular to the universal. Whether this arrangement is original or the product of later editorial shaping remains a matter of scholarly debate, but its rhetorical effectiveness is widely acknowledged.
