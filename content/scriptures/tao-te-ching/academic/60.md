Academic research on chapter 60 situates its political philosophy within the specific institutional context of Warring States governance. Scholars examine how the chapter's ideals of minimal rule and spontaneous order relate to the actual administrative challenges faced by the competing kingdoms of ancient China. Some have argued that the Tao Te Ching's political chapters were intended as advice for specific rulers, while others see them as utopian thought experiments exploring the logical limits of political philosophy.

Comparative textual studies reveal that chapter 60 shares vocabulary and themes with other Warring States political texts, including the Guan Zi, the Han Fei Zi, and portions of the Lü Shi Chunqiu. These intertextual connections suggest a shared intellectual milieu in which ideas circulated freely across the boundaries of what later became distinct philosophical schools. Academic scholars use these connections to reconstruct the intellectual networks and institutional settings in which the Tao Te Ching was composed, transmitted, and interpreted.
