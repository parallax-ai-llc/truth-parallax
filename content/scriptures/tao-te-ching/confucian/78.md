In the concluding chapters of the Tao Te Ching, Confucian readers find a summation of themes that have been in productive tension with their own tradition throughout the text. Chapter 78's vision of simplicity and return to the natural resonates with the Confucian ideal of sincerity (cheng), which both traditions understand as a stripping away of pretense and artifice to reveal what is genuinely and authentically human.

The Confucian tradition's engagement with chapter 78 illustrates the broader pattern of Chinese intellectual history in which Confucianism and Taoism have functioned not as sealed, opposing systems but as complementary voices in an ongoing conversation about the nature of the good life. Many Chinese intellectuals throughout history have been 'Confucian in office, Taoist in retirement,' drawing on each tradition as circumstances required. The Tao Te Ching's closing chapters speak to the dimension of human experience that Confucianism, with its focus on social responsibility, sometimes neglects: the need for solitude, simplicity, and communion with the natural world.
