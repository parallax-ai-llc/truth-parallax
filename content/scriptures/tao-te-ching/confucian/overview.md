## Overview

The Confucian engagement with the Tao Te Ching is shaped by a long history of both rivalry and mutual influence. Confucian interpreters have read the text alternately as a direct challenge to Confucian values of ritual propriety (li) and humaneness (ren), and as a complementary vision that enriches the Confucian understanding of governance, self-cultivation, and the nature of virtue. The Neo-Confucian synthesis of the Song and Ming dynasties drew significantly on Taoist metaphysics, finding in the Tao Te Ching resources for articulating concepts like the Great Ultimate (Taiji).

## Critique and Appropriation

Confucian readers have noted that the Tao Te Ching's critique of conventional morality (chapters 18–19, 38) directly targets Confucian categories: "When the great Tao is abandoned, benevolence and righteousness appear." Rather than dismissing this critique, some Confucian thinkers have taken it as a corrective against the formalization and emptying of ritual practice, recognizing that genuine virtue cannot be reduced to external conformity.

## Complementary Governance

The text's political philosophy, with its emphasis on the ruler who leads without dominating and governs without interfering, has been read by Confucian statesmen as a necessary complement to active Confucian governance. The ideal of the sage ruler who "does nothing, yet nothing is left undone" has been integrated into Confucian political thought as a principle of restraint and humility in the exercise of power.
