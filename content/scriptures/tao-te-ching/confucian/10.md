Confucian readers approach chapter 10, 'Embracing the One,' with a complex mixture of critique and appreciation. The chapter's metaphysical claims about the Tao as the source of all things are not inherently objectionable to Confucian thought — indeed, the concept of Tao (the Way) is central to the Confucian tradition itself. What distinguishes the Confucian reading is its insistence that the Tao must be understood in relation to human relationships, social institutions, and moral cultivation rather than in abstraction from them.

Neo-Confucian thinkers of the Song dynasty, particularly Zhu Xi, engaged seriously with the metaphysical dimensions of chapter 10, finding resources for articulating the relationship between principle (li) and material force (qi) that became central to Neo-Confucian philosophy. While maintaining that the Tao Te Ching's rejection of ritual and moral cultivation was misguided, these thinkers acknowledged the depth of its metaphysical insight and incorporated Taoist concepts into their own systematic philosophy.
