Confucian engagement with chapter 65 reflects the tradition's complex relationship with themes of warfare, strategy, and conflict resolution. While Confucianism is not a pacifist tradition, it shares with the Tao Te Ching a deep skepticism about the glorification of military force and the use of violence as a first resort. Both traditions advocate for diplomatic solutions and moral persuasion as preferable to armed conflict, though they differ on the metaphysical grounds for this preference.

The Confucian reading of chapter 65 also engages the text's epistemological themes, particularly its valorization of humility and its critique of false confidence. Confucius himself taught that wisdom begins with the recognition of one's own ignorance, a principle strikingly similar to the Tao Te Ching's teaching that 'knowing not-knowing is the highest.' This shared emphasis on intellectual humility represents one of the deepest points of convergence between the two traditions.
