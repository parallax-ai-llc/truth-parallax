Confucian interpreters read chapter 38 of the Te Ching in dialogue with their own tradition's understanding of virtue (de). Where the Tao Te Ching describes te as the spontaneous manifestation of the Tao in individual beings, Confucianism understands virtue as cultivated through study, reflection, and the practice of ritual propriety. Despite this difference in emphasis, both traditions share the conviction that genuine virtue transforms not only the individual but also the social environment.

The Confucian reading of chapter 38 often focuses on its implications for governance and social order. Confucian thinkers find common ground with the text's critique of excessive government intervention and its vision of a society in which people live harmoniously without the need for elaborate legal codes and punitive measures. The disagreement lies in whether this harmonious social order emerges naturally (as the Tao Te Ching suggests) or must be actively cultivated through education and institutional design (as Confucianism maintains).
