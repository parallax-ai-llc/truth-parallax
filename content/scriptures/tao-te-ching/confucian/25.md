Confucian engagement with chapter 25 reflects the tradition's ongoing negotiation between active moral cultivation and receptive openness to the natural order. While Confucianism emphasizes the importance of study, ritual practice, and deliberate self-improvement, the Tao Te Ching's advocacy of naturalness and non-striving has served as a reminder that genuine transformation cannot be forced. Confucian thinkers have drawn on this insight to articulate a vision of moral development that balances effort with grace.

The political dimensions of chapter 25 have been of particular interest to Confucian statesmen and scholars. The Tao Te Ching's vision of sage rulership, in which the ruler leads by moral example rather than by force, closely parallels the Confucian ideal of governance through virtue (de zheng). While Confucians would add that the ruler must also actively promote education, ritual, and institutional reform, they share with the Tao Te Ching the conviction that legitimacy rests on moral authority rather than coercive power.
