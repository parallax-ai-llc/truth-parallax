Chapter 11 presents a direct challenge to Confucian values, and Confucian interpreters have responded with vigorous debate. The text's critique of benevolence (ren), righteousness (yi), and ritual propriety (li) strikes at the heart of the Confucian moral project. Confucian readers argue that Lao Tzu confuses the abuse of these virtues with the virtues themselves: it is not benevolence that is problematic but its hypocritical performance, and the remedy is genuine rather than abandoned virtue.

At the same time, thoughtful Confucian readers have found in chapter 11 a valuable corrective against the danger of moral self-righteousness. The chapter's insistence that true virtue is unselfconscious and unforced resonates with Confucius's own ideal of the person who practices goodness naturally, without calculation or display. The Confucian Mencius similarly argued that genuine moral feeling arises spontaneously from human nature, a position that shares common ground with the Tao Te Ching's vision of effortless virtue.
