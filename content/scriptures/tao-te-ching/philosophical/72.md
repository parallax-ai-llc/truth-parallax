In the closing chapters of the Tao Te Ching, philosophical interpreters find a summation of the text's central arguments. Chapter 72, 'When People Do Not Fear Authority,' brings together themes of naturalness, simplicity, and the limits of language that have been developed throughout the work. The philosophical tradition reads these final chapters as a return to the opening paradox: the Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao, and yet the sage must somehow communicate what cannot be said.

Philosophical commentators note how chapter 72 exemplifies the Tao Te Ching's distinctive method of teaching through paradox and reversal. The text does not present a systematic doctrine to be memorized but rather enacts a way of thinking that loosens the reader's attachment to fixed categories and habitual assumptions. The philosophical value of the Tao Te Ching lies not in any particular proposition but in the transformation of consciousness it effects in the attentive reader.
