Chapter 3, 'Not Exalting the Worthy,' is read by philosophical Taoists as an exploration of the Tao's fundamental nature and its relationship to the world of appearances. The philosophical tradition emphasizes the logical structure of Lao Tzu's argument, finding in these early chapters a systematic ontology that grounds ethics and political philosophy in metaphysical insight. The paradoxes are treated not as poetic flourishes but as precise formulations of the Tao's transcendence of ordinary categories.

Wang Bi and other philosophical commentators draw attention to the way chapter 3 builds on the text's opening distinction between the nameless and the named. The chapter's imagery is interpreted as pointing toward the priority of non-being (wu) over being (you), not as nihilism but as the recognition that the creative source of all things is itself indeterminate and inexhaustible. This reading grounds the ethical ideal of wu wei in an understanding of reality's fundamental openness.
