Philosophical interpreters regard chapter 1 as the foundational statement of Taoist metaphysics. The opening paradox — that the Tao which can be spoken is not the eternal Tao — establishes the text's central epistemological claim: ultimate reality exceeds the capacity of language and conceptual thought. Wang Bi interpreted this as a distinction between the determinate (you, being) and the indeterminate (wu, non-being), arguing that the nameless Tao is the ontological ground from which all named things emerge.

The chapter's paired structure of 'nameless' and 'named,' 'desireless' and 'desiring,' introduces the philosophical method of complementary opposition that pervades the entire text. Rather than choosing one pole over the other, the sage comprehends both as expressions of a single 'mystery' (xuan). Philosophical readers see this not as mystical obscurantism but as a rigorous recognition that reality is more complex than any single conceptual framework can capture.
