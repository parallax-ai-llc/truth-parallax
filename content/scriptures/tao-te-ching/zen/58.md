Zen practitioners read chapter 58 not primarily as political philosophy but as guidance for the inner governance of the mind. The Tao Te Ching's sage ruler who governs without interfering corresponds to the meditator who observes thoughts and emotions without grasping or rejecting them, allowing the mind's natural wisdom to emerge. In both cases, the key insight is that control is best achieved through the relinquishment of control, and order arises most naturally when it is not imposed from above.

The chapter's themes also resonate with the Zen monastery's approach to communal life, in which the roshi (teacher) leads by embodying the practice rather than by issuing commands. The best Zen masters, like the Tao Te Ching's sage rulers, are those whose influence is felt without being seen, who create conditions for awakening without imposing their own understanding on their students. This model of leadership through presence rather than prescription reflects the shared Taoist-Zen conviction that authentic authority flows from being rather than doing.
