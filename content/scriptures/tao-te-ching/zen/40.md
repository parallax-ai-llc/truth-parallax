Chapter 40 of the Te Ching speaks to Zen practitioners about the nature of authentic virtue, which in both traditions arises not from moral effort but from the spontaneous expression of one's true nature. The Tao Te Ching's teaching that 'superior virtue is not virtuous, and therefore has virtue' directly parallels the Zen insight that genuine compassion and wisdom emerge naturally when the ego's interference is removed. The practitioner does not cultivate virtue as an external acquisition but uncovers the virtue that is already present.

Zen readers connect chapter 40's themes to their own practice of zazen (sitting meditation), in which the practitioner sits without goal or expectation, allowing the mind to settle into its natural clarity. This goalless practice mirrors the Tao Te Ching's wu wei: the most profound transformation occurs not through striving but through letting go. Both traditions recognize that the deepest paradox of the spiritual life is that one must abandon the desire for awakening in order to awaken, just as one must abandon the pursuit of virtue in order to be genuinely virtuous.
