Chapter 15 strikes Zen readers as a kindred expression of the insight that conventional knowledge obscures rather than reveals reality. The chapter's injunction to abandon learning and return to simplicity mirrors the Zen master Linji's exhortation to 'kill the Buddha' — not as nihilism but as the radical clearing away of all conceptual obstacles to direct seeing. In both traditions, the accumulation of knowledge and moral refinement can become barriers to awakening if they are mistaken for awakening itself.

Zen practitioners find in chapter 15 an echo of the 'beginner's mind' (shoshin) celebrated by Suzuki Roshi: the open, receptive awareness that precedes expertise and opinion. The Tao Te Ching's sage, like the Zen beginner, approaches each moment freshly, without the burden of preconceptions or the desire to impose order on experience. This shared ideal of radical openness connects the oldest stratum of Taoist thought with the living practice of contemporary Zen, demonstrating the enduring vitality of insights that transcend their historical origins.
