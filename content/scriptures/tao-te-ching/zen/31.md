Zen readers approach chapter 31 as an expression of the non-dual awareness that both traditions regard as the foundation of authentic existence. The chapter's imagery of water, valleys, and the uncarved block resonates with the Zen aesthetic of wabi-sabi — the appreciation of simplicity, impermanence, and imperfection as expressions of the deepest reality. In both traditions, beauty is found not in elaboration and refinement but in the bare, unadorned presentation of things as they are.

The chapter's teaching on wu wei finds its Zen parallel in the concept of mushin (no-mind), the state in which the practitioner acts with complete spontaneity and effectiveness because the discriminating intellect has been transcended. The Zen swordsman who fights without thinking, the calligrapher whose brush moves of its own accord, the tea master who performs the ceremony with effortless precision — all embody the wu wei that chapter 31 describes. This is not the absence of skill but skill so deeply internalized that it operates beyond conscious control.
