In the closing chapters of the Tao Te Ching, Zen readers find a final expression of the wordless truth that both traditions hold most sacred. Chapter 71's meditation on the limits of language and the inadequacy of beautiful words to capture reality mirrors the Heart Sutra's negation of all conceptual categories and the Zen tradition's insistence that the deepest transmission occurs outside scripture, mind to mind, in the living encounter between teacher and student.

Zen practitioners receive chapter 71 as a reminder that all texts, including the Tao Te Ching itself, are ultimately fingers pointing at the moon — useful guides that must not be confused with the reality they indicate. The Tao Te Ching's self-undermining conclusion, in which the sage's words declare the insufficiency of words, enacts the very principle it teaches. In this, it mirrors the Zen tradition's paradoxical use of language to transcend language, a practice that has kept both traditions vitally alive for millennia.
