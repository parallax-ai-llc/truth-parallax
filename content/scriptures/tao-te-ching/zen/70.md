Chapter 70 resonates deeply with the Zen tradition's embrace of paradox, reversal, and the inadequacy of conventional logic. The Tao Te Ching's teaching that the sage knows by not-knowing echoes the Zen master's use of koans — paradoxical questions designed to exhaust the rational mind and precipitate a breakthrough into direct, non-conceptual awareness. In both traditions, the highest wisdom is not a special kind of knowledge but the dissolution of the knower-known duality.

Zen practitioners also find in chapter 70 an affirmation of the 'great doubt' (daigi) that is considered essential to genuine spiritual breakthrough. The Tao Te Ching's sage, who wears rough garments while carrying jade within, embodies the Zen ideal of the realized person whose outer simplicity conceals inner treasure. This theme of hiddenness and humility connects to the Zen tradition of the 'hidden sage' who lives an ordinary life while embodying extraordinary realization, refusing to display spiritual attainment as a badge of superiority.
