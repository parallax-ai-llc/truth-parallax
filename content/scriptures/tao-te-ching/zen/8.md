Zen practitioners read chapter 8, 'The Highest Good Is Like Water,' as a direct pointing at the nature of mind, akin to the wordless transmission that defines the Zen tradition. The chapter's paradoxes are not problems to be solved but koans to be lived, designed to short-circuit the conceptual mind and open a space for direct, non-dual awareness. Where philosophical commentators seek logical coherence, Zen readers seek the rupture of logic that reveals what lies beyond thought.

The resonance between chapter 8 and Zen teaching is not accidental but reflects the deep historical interpenetration of Taoist and Buddhist thought in China. When Bodhidharma brought Chan Buddhism to China, the ground had been prepared by centuries of Taoist cultivation of emptiness, spontaneity, and non-attachment to words. Zen's characteristic directness, its irreverence toward textual authority, and its insistence on immediate experience over doctrinal learning all bear the unmistakable imprint of the Taoist sensibility expressed in chapters like this one.
