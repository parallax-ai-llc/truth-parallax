Religious Taoists read chapter 39 of the Te Ching as an exposition of te — not merely as abstract virtue but as the Tao's indwelling spiritual power, which the practitioner cultivates through disciplined practice. The chapter's teaching on virtue is understood in the context of merit accumulation, a central concern of religious Taoism in which moral conduct, charitable acts, and ritual observance contribute to the practitioner's spiritual advancement and ultimate transcendence of the cycle of birth and death.

The Lingbao (Numinous Treasure) tradition interprets chapter 39 through the lens of universal salvation, finding in the text's vision of the sage who benefits all things without contention a model for the bodhisattva-like aspiration of religious Taoism. The practitioner cultivates te not for personal benefit alone but for the liberation of all sentient beings, a theme that reflects the Buddhist influence on medieval religious Taoism while remaining grounded in the Tao Te Ching's own language of selfless service.
