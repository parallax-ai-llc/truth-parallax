## Overview

Religious Taoism (Daojiao) receives the Tao Te Ching as a sacred scripture revealed by the deified Lao Tzu, who is venerated as Taishang Laojun (the Supreme Old Lord). Within this tradition, the text is not merely philosophical but serves as a foundation for liturgical practice, meditation, inner alchemy (neidan), and the pursuit of spiritual immortality. The Tao is understood not just as an abstract principle but as a living, numinous reality with which the practitioner seeks direct communion.

## Ritual and Devotional Context

In religious Taoist communities, the Tao Te Ching is chanted as part of daily liturgy and ceremonial observance. Specific chapters are recited for protection, purification, and the accumulation of merit. The text's references to the "valley spirit," the "mysterious female," and the "gate of all wonders" are interpreted as descriptions of interior spiritual landscapes accessible through meditation and breath cultivation practices.

## Inner Alchemy and Longevity

Religious Taoists read the text's imagery of water, softness, and returning to the root as coded instructions for the internal alchemical transformation of the practitioner's body and spirit. Concepts like "embracing the One" (chapter 10) and "returning to the state of the uncarved block" (chapter 28) are understood as stages in a systematic program of self-cultivation aimed at transcending mortality and uniting with the Tao.
