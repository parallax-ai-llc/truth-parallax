Within religious Taoism, chapter 18 is read as guidance for the practitioner's spiritual cultivation. The chapter's themes of simplicity, naturalness, and the abandonment of artifice are understood as instructions for purifying the mind and body in preparation for the descent of divine grace. Religious Taoist commentators interpret the text's critique of conventional values as a call to transcend worldly attachments and return to the primordial unity of the Tao.

The Quanzhen (Complete Perfection) school reads chapter 18 as an instruction in inner alchemy, where the practitioner refines the three treasures — jing (essence), qi (vital energy), and shen (spirit) — to achieve spiritual immortality. The chapter's imagery of returning, emptying, and simplifying corresponds to specific stages of the alchemical process in which gross energies are transformed into increasingly subtle forms, culminating in the union of the practitioner's spirit with the Tao.
