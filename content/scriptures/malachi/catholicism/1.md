Catholic interpretation of Malachi 1 centers on verse 11, which the Church has read since the earliest patristic period as a prophecy of the Eucharist: "From the rising of the sun to its setting my name is great among the nations, and in every place incense is offered to my name, and a pure offering." The Council of Trent cited this verse explicitly in its decree on the sacrifice of the Mass (Session XXII, Chapter 1), identifying the "pure offering" (*minchah tehorah*) with the Eucharistic sacrifice that is offered continuously across the globe. This reading is supported by the Didache (14:3), Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, and Augustine.

The contrast between the defective offerings of the Levitical priests (1:7-8) and the pure offering among the nations (1:11) establishes a prophetic basis for the Catholic understanding of the relationship between the Old Covenant sacrifices and the New Covenant Eucharist. The former were shadows and types; the latter is the reality to which they pointed. The priests' contempt for the altar — "the LORD's table is polluted" (1:12) — serves as a perpetual warning to Catholic clergy about the reverence due to the Eucharistic celebration.

The election theology of 1:2-5 — God's love for Jacob and rejection of Esau — is read in Catholic tradition not as a statement of double predestination but as an affirmation of God's sovereign freedom in establishing the covenant. The Catechism (CCC 604) teaches that God's love is universal while acknowledging that He freely chooses the means and instruments of salvation. The emphasis falls on God's initiative in love, not on the exclusion of any person from the possibility of grace.
