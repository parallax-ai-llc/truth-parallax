The prophecy of the messenger (3:1) is read in Catholic tradition on multiple levels: John the Baptist prepares the way, but Christ himself is the "Lord" who suddenly comes to His temple. The Feast of the Presentation of the Lord (February 2) celebrates this fulfillment, when the infant Jesus is brought to the Temple by Mary and Joseph and recognized by Simeon and Anna. The liturgical reading of Malachi 3:1-4 on this feast connects the prophet's vision directly to the mystery of the Incarnation.

The refining fire imagery (3:2-3) is connected in Catholic theology to the doctrine of purgatory. The purification of the "sons of Levi" so that they may "present offerings to the LORD in righteousness" (3:3) suggests a process of post-mortem purification through which the faithful are prepared for the beatific vision. The Catechism (CCC 1030-1032) teaches that those who die in God's grace but are not yet perfectly purified undergo this cleansing fire — an understanding that finds poetic expression in Malachi's imagery.

The tithing passage (3:8-12) supports Catholic teaching on stewardship and the precept of supporting the Church. Canon Law (Can. 222) obliges the faithful to provide for the needs of the Church, and the principle of Malachi — that withholding from God results in spiritual and material impoverishment — undergirds the Church's appeal for generous giving. The promise to "open the windows of heaven" is read not as a prosperity guarantee but as an assurance that generosity toward God is never ultimately a loss.
