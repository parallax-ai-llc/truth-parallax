The "Sun of Righteousness" rising "with healing in its wings" (4:2) is one of the most celebrated messianic images in Catholic tradition. The Advent and Christmas liturgy draws on this solar imagery to present Christ as the true light entering the world's darkness. The ancient *O Antiphons* of Advent echo this symbolism, as does the placement of Christmas near the winter solstice — the return of the light. Catholic art and architecture frequently incorporate solar motifs in monstrances, church windows, and altar designs, reflecting the conviction that Christ is the "sun" whose rising fulfills Malachi's prophecy.

The command to "remember the law of my servant Moses" (4:4) followed by the promise of Elijah (4:5) creates the framework that Catholic theology uses to understand the relationship between law, prophecy, and gospel. The Transfiguration — where Moses and Elijah appear with Christ — is the interpretive key: the Law and the Prophets find their culmination and fulfillment in the person of Jesus. Catholic catechesis uses this structure to teach the unity of the two Testaments and the progressive character of divine revelation.

The final words of the Old Testament — the threat of a curse unless hearts are turned (4:6) — end the prophetic canon on a note of urgency that the Church's liturgical year perpetually renews. The Advent season, in particular, takes up Malachi's tone: the Lord is coming, and the proper response is repentance, reconciliation, and preparation. The Catholic reading thus transforms Malachi's closing threat into an invitation, mediated by the sacraments of Reconciliation and Eucharist, to prepare for the Lord's coming in grace.
