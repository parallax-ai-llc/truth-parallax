Malachi 4 (in the Jewish versification, these verses are 3:19-24, as the chapter division differs from the Christian Bible) represents the closing of the prophetic canon. The Jewish tradition regards these as among the last prophetic words before the onset of the rabbinic era, lending them particular weight. The command to "remember the law of my servant Moses" (4:4) serves as a bridge between prophecy and Torah study: as prophetic revelation ceases, the study and interpretation of Torah becomes the primary means of encountering God's will.

The Elijah prophecy (4:5-6) has generated an enormous body of tradition. In Jewish eschatology, Elijah serves multiple functions: he will herald the Messiah, resolve disputed legal questions (the Talmud uses *teyku* — "let it stand [until Elijah comes]" — to close unresolved debates), restore the dead to life, and reconcile families. The promise that he will "turn the hearts of fathers to their children and the hearts of children to their fathers" (4:6) speaks to the restoration of community and continuity across generations — a value of supreme importance in Jewish tradition.

The concluding verse, with its threat of *cherem* (utter destruction), is considered so harsh that Jewish liturgical tradition repeats verse 23 (4:5) after reading verse 24 (4:6) in synagogue, so that the prophetic reading ends on a note of hope rather than curse. This practice — also applied to the endings of Isaiah, Lamentations, and Ecclesiastes — reflects the rabbinic conviction that God's final word is always mercy, not judgment. The silence that follows Malachi is not empty but pregnant: it is the silence of waiting for redemption.
