## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Malachi holds a unique position as the last of the prophets. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 11a) teaches that prophecy ceased with the deaths of Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi. Whether "Malachi" is a proper name or a title ("my messenger") has been debated since antiquity; the Talmud (Megillah 15a) records the opinion that Malachi was actually Ezra the scribe, while other traditions treat Malachi as an independent prophetic figure. The book's placement at the end of the Trei Asar gives it a valedictory quality — the last prophetic word before the long silence.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The Elijah prophecy (4:5-6) has had enormous influence on Jewish practice and imagination. Elijah's expected return before "the great and awesome day of the LORD" shapes the Passover Seder, where a cup of wine is set aside for Elijah and the door is opened in anticipation of his arrival. The custom of the "chair of Elijah" at circumcision ceremonies likewise derives from the prophetic expectation that Elijah will herald the messianic age. In Jewish eschatology, Elijah serves as the resolver of unanswered legal and theological questions — the word *teyku* (let it stand) in Talmudic debate is traditionally read as an acronym: "Elijah the Tishbite will resolve difficulties and questions."

## Halakhic Significance

The tithing passage (3:8-12) has direct halakhic implications. The obligation of *ma'aser* (tithing) is discussed extensively in the Mishnah and Talmud, and Malachi's dramatic language — "bring the full tithe into the storehouse and test me" — is one of the few passages where God invites Israel to test Him. The Talmud (Ta'anit 9a) plays on the words *aser te'aser* ("you shall surely tithe," Deuteronomy 14:22), reading it as *aser bishvil she-tit'asher* ("tithe so that you may become wealthy").
