Jewish interpretation of Malachi 1 begins with the book's unique literary form: the disputation (*riv*). Each oracle follows a pattern where God makes an assertion, the people challenge it with "How?" (*bammeh*), and God provides detailed evidence. This forensic style is distinctive among the prophets and has led some rabbinic commentators to see Malachi as a skilled teacher (*melamed*) as much as a classical prophet. The Midrash preserves the tradition that Malachi was a member of the Great Assembly (*Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*), the body that bridges the prophetic and rabbinic eras.

The election of Jacob over Esau (1:2-5) is foundational for Jewish self-understanding. Rabbinic tradition identifies Esau with Rome (and later with Christendom), making this passage a statement about Israel's unique election over the imperial powers that dominated Jewish life. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 63:7) elaborates extensively on the Jacob-Esau rivalry, reading it as a paradigm for the relationship between Israel and the nations throughout history.

The critique of defective sacrifices (1:7-14) resonated powerfully with rabbinic concerns about the integrity of Temple worship and, after the Temple's destruction, about the integrity of prayer, which replaced sacrifice. The Talmud (Berakhot 26b) teaches that the daily prayers were instituted to correspond to the daily offerings, and Malachi's insistence on bringing the best — not the blind, lame, or sick — was transferred to the requirement for focused, heartfelt prayer (*kavanah*). The closing rhetorical question, "Would your governor accept such offerings?" (1:8), became a standard rabbinic argument from the lesser to the greater (*kal va-homer*): if you would not insult a human ruler, how much less should you insult the King of Kings.
