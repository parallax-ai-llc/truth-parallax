The covenant of Levi (2:4-7) shapes rabbinic discussion of the priestly and Levitical roles. Though the post-exilic priesthood had historical problems — the Talmud records corruption in the late Second Temple period — the ideal described by Malachi remained normative: a priest who teaches truth, walks in integrity, and turns many from iniquity. After the destruction of the Temple, the rabbi inherited aspects of this priestly teaching function, and the Malachian standard was applied to rabbinic responsibility. The Mishnah's tractate Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) echoes Malachi's ideal when it teaches: "Be deliberate in judgment, raise up many disciples, and make a fence around the Torah" (1:1).

The marriage passage (2:13-16) has been extensively discussed in halakhic literature. The statement "he hates sending away" (2:16) is debated: does God hate divorce categorically, or does He hate a specific kind of faithless divorce? The Talmud (Gittin 90b) records a famous dispute between Beit Shammai (divorce only for sexual immorality) and Beit Hillel (divorce for any cause), with Rabbi Akiva holding that one may divorce even if he finds another more attractive. Malachi's emphasis on the covenantal dimension of marriage supports the more stringent reading, while halakhah ultimately permits divorce as a concession to human hardship.

The concept of "faithlessness" (*bagad*) that runs through this chapter — the priests are faithless, the husbands are faithless — carries covenantal weight in Jewish theology. Faithlessness is not merely individual sin but a rupture of the communal bond between God and Israel. The prophet sees the community's internal betrayals (false teaching, broken marriages) as symptoms of a deeper covenant crisis that only repentance and renewal can address.
