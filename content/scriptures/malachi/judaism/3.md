The messenger prophecy (3:1) is interpreted in Jewish tradition differently from the Christian reading. Rather than identifying the messenger with John the Baptist, rabbinic interpretation connects this figure to Elijah, whose return is promised in 4:5. The Talmud (Eruvin 43b) discusses whether Elijah will come on the eve of the Sabbath or a festival, debating the practical implications of his expected arrival. The figure of Elijah as a messianic herald is deeply embedded in Jewish practice: the cup of Elijah at the Passover Seder, the chair of Elijah at circumcision, and the Havdalah hymn "Eliyahu HaNavi" all reflect this expectation.

The tithing passage (3:8-12) has direct halakhic ramifications. The obligation to tithe agricultural produce is discussed in the Mishnaic tractates Ma'aserot and Ma'aser Sheni. Malachi's dramatic language — "you are robbing God" — underscores the seriousness of this obligation. The invitation to "test me" is remarkable in Jewish theology, where testing God is generally prohibited (Deuteronomy 6:16). The Talmud (Ta'anit 9a) reconciles this by treating tithing as the one exception: in this matter alone, God permits testing because the reward is guaranteed.

The "book of remembrance" (3:16) resonates with the liturgy of Rosh Hashanah, when the Book of Life is traditionally opened and the deeds of every person are recorded. The Unetaneh Tokef prayer — "who shall live and who shall die" — echoes Malachi's distinction between those who fear God and those who do not. The prophet's assurance that God discerns "between one who serves God and one who does not serve him" (3:18) provides comfort to the righteous who feel overlooked and challenges the complacent who assume divine indifference.
