The ideal priest described in Malachi 2:5-7 — one who possesses true knowledge and turns many from iniquity — is appropriated by Gnostic interpreters as a description of the Gnostic teacher or revealer. In Gnostic communities, the one who possesses and transmits *gnosis* occupies a role analogous to the prophet-priest: guiding souls out of ignorance and into salvific knowledge. The critique of priests who "have turned aside from the way" (2:8) is read as a condemnation of the institutional clergy who propagate the Demiurge's religion, keeping the faithful bound to material worship rather than liberating them through knowledge.

The marriage metaphor (2:14-16) is susceptible to a Gnostic allegorical reading. In Valentinian thought, the fundamental drama of the cosmos is the separation and reunion of divine syzygies (pairs). Sophia's separation from her consort produced the Demiurge and the material world; restoration comes through the reunification of separated divine pairs. The prophet's lament over broken marriages can be read as a reflection of this cosmic drama — the "wife of your youth" representing the soul's original divine companion from whom it has been separated by the fall into matter.

The people's complaint — "Where is the God of justice?" (2:17) — has particular resonance for Gnostic theology, which sees the apparent absence of justice in the material world as evidence of the Demiurge's incompetence or malevolence. The true God does not intervene in the material realm through the mechanisms of reward and punishment that Malachi expects; rather, the divine operates through the quiet, hidden work of *gnosis*, which liberates individuals from the Demiurge's system entirely.
