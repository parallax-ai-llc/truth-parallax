Malachi 4, the final chapter of the Old Testament in the Protestant canon, brings the prophetic corpus to a dramatic crescendo. The Day of the LORD will come "burning like an oven" (4:1), consuming the arrogant and evildoers. But for those who fear God's name, "the Sun of Righteousness shall rise with healing in its wings" (4:2). Protestant Christological interpretation identifies the Sun of Righteousness with Christ, the Light of the World whose coming brings healing and liberation — the calves leaping from their stalls (4:2) symbolize the exuberant freedom of salvation.

The double injunction to "remember the law of my servant Moses" (4:4) and to expect the return of "Elijah the prophet" (4:5) creates a theological bridge connecting the Torah, the Prophets, and the coming messianic age. Protestant interpreters note that Moses and Elijah appear together on the Mount of Transfiguration (Matthew 17:1-8), confirming that they represent the Law and the Prophets that find their fulfillment in Christ. John the Baptist came "in the spirit and power of Elijah" (Luke 1:17), fulfilling Malachi's prophecy at least in part.

The final verse — "lest I come and strike the land with a decree of utter destruction" (4:6) — leaves the Old Testament on a note of unresolved tension. The promised reconciliation of fathers and children has not yet occurred; the curse still threatens. Protestant theology reads this as the setup for the gospel: the four hundred years of prophetic silence between Malachi and the coming of John the Baptist create the dramatic pause before God's decisive intervention in Christ. The Old Testament ends with a question that only the New Testament can answer.
