## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Malachi centers on the book's role as the hinge between the Old and New Testaments. The prophet's announcement of a coming messenger (3:1) and the return of Elijah (4:5) are read as directly fulfilled in John the Baptist, making Malachi the immediate prophetic precursor to the gospel. The book's disputational style — God challenging the people's complacency through pointed questions — resonates with the Reformation emphasis on the Word of God as an active, confrontational force that exposes sin and calls for repentance.

## Key Interpretive Themes

The contrast between Jacob and Esau (1:2-3) has been central to Reformed debates about election and predestination, with Calvin and later Reformed theologians citing this passage alongside Romans 9 as evidence of unconditional election. The indictment of corrupt priests (1:6-2:9) was applied by Reformers to the medieval Catholic clergy, while the call to faithful stewardship (3:8-12) continues to shape Protestant theology of tithing and generosity.

## Christological Reading

The "messenger of the covenant" (3:1) is identified with John the Baptist in the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew 11:10, Mark 1:2, Luke 7:27). The "Sun of Righteousness" with "healing in its wings" (4:2) is read as a title for Christ. Protestant eschatology connects the Elijah prophecy both to John the Baptist's first-century ministry and to the end-times expectation of prophetic witness before Christ's return.
