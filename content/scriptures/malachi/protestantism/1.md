Malachi opens with one of the most theologically loaded statements in the prophetic literature: "I have loved you, says the LORD" (1:2). The people's immediate retort — "How have you loved us?" — sets the disputational tone of the entire book. God's answer invokes the election of Jacob over Esau, a passage that became central to Reformed theology's doctrine of unconditional election. Calvin devoted extensive attention to this text, arguing that God's love for Jacob and hatred of Esau (cf. Romans 9:13) demonstrate that salvation originates entirely in divine choice, not human merit.

The indictment of the priests (1:6-14) for offering blind, lame, and sick animals on God's altar strikes at the heart of worship. Protestant interpreters read this not merely as a historical critique of post-exilic Levitical practice but as an enduring principle: God demands the best, not the leftovers. The Reformation's insistence on the integrity and seriousness of worship — against what the Reformers saw as the mechanical ritualism of late medieval Catholicism — found a prophetic warrant in Malachi's devastating question: "When you offer blind animals in sacrifice, is that not evil?" (1:8).

The remarkable verse 1:11 — "from the rising of the sun to its setting my name is great among the nations" — is debated among Protestant interpreters. Some read it as a present-tense statement about God-fearing Gentiles; others, as an eschatological prophecy of the gospel's spread among all nations. Either way, it expands the horizon beyond Israel to a universal worship that transcends the Temple, anticipating the New Testament vision of worship "in spirit and truth" (John 4:23).
