Malachi 3 delivers the book's most dramatic promise: "Behold, I send my messenger, and he will prepare the way before me. And the Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to his temple" (3:1). Protestant interpretation identifies this messenger with John the Baptist, following the explicit citation in the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew 11:10, Mark 1:2). The "Lord" who comes to His temple is Christ himself, whose arrival is described not in comforting terms but as a "refiner's fire" and "fullers' soap" (3:2) — He comes to purify, not merely to comfort.

The tithing passage (3:8-12) is one of the most preached texts in Protestant churches and one of the most debated. The accusation "you are robbing me" and the invitation "test me in this" — the only place in Scripture where God invites testing — have been used to support everything from strict ten-percent tithing to prosperity theology. Mainstream Protestant interpretation holds that the passage teaches a principle of generous, faithful stewardship rather than a legalistic percentage, though many traditions maintain the tithe as a biblical guideline. The promise to "open the windows of heaven" (3:10) echoes the Flood narrative's language, turning destruction into abundance.

The "book of remembrance" written for those who fear the LORD (3:16) is a powerful image of divine attentiveness to the faithful. In a context where the majority complains that "it is vain to serve God" (3:14), the book of remembrance assures the righteous minority that God notices and records their faithfulness. Protestant interpreters connect this to the New Testament imagery of names written in the Lamb's book of life (Revelation 20:12, 21:27).
