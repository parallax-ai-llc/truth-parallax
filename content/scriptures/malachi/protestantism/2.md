Malachi 2 addresses two forms of covenant betrayal: faithless priesthood and faithless marriage. The oracle against the priests (2:1-9) describes the ideal of the Levitical covenant — "true instruction was in his mouth, and no wrong was found on his lips" (2:6) — and contrasts it with the current reality of priests who "have turned aside from the way" and "have caused many to stumble by your instruction" (2:8). Protestant interpreters apply this standard to the pastoral ministry, insisting that those who teach in God's name bear a weighty responsibility.

The marriage passage (2:10-16) contains the famous declaration, often translated "I hate divorce" (2:16). Protestant interpretation has debated whether this represents an absolute prohibition or a statement about God's general disposition. The passage's theological logic connects marriage faithfulness to covenant faithfulness: just as God does not break His covenant with Israel, so husbands should not break faith with their wives. The Reformation, while permitting divorce in certain grave circumstances (adultery, desertion), maintained the covenantal ideal of lifelong fidelity.

The chapter closes with the people's wearying of God through their words: "Everyone who does evil is good in the sight of the LORD, and he delights in them" or "Where is the God of justice?" (2:17). This moral cynicism — the complaint that God does not distinguish between righteous and wicked — sets up the dramatic announcement of chapter 3, where God promises to send a messenger who will indeed bring justice. Protestant preachers note that the same complaint echoes through every generation: if God is just, why do the wicked prosper?
