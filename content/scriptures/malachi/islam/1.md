Islamic engagement with Malachi 1 focuses on the universal prophetic theme of sincere worship versus hollow ritual. The prophet's indictment of priests who offer blemished sacrifices while keeping the best for themselves (1:8) resonates with the Quranic critique of hypocrisy (*nifaq*). The Quran repeatedly distinguishes between outward religious performance and genuine devotion: "Woe to those who pray but are heedless of their prayer, who make a show of piety" (107:4-6). Malachi's God demands wholehearted worship, not leftover observance — a principle shared across the Abrahamic prophetic heritage.

The declaration that God's name is "great among the nations" (1:11) aligns with the Islamic conviction of *tawhid* as a universal truth, not a tribal possession. Islam teaches that every nation has received prophets (*rusul*) calling them to the worship of the one God (Quran 16:36), and the vision of universal worship from "the rising of the sun to its setting" anticipates the Islamic ideal of a global *ummah* united in submission to God. The "pure offering" among all nations is understood not as a specific ritual (Eucharist or otherwise) but as the sincere worship that God accepts from any people who turn to Him.

The election of Jacob over Esau (1:2-5) has less theological significance in Islamic interpretation, which does not build covenant theology around this distinction. Islam affirms the prophethood of Ya'qub (Jacob) and recognizes the twelve tribes as bearers of divine guidance, but does not assign salvific significance to ethnic election. The Islamic emphasis falls instead on individual accountability and the universality of God's mercy.
