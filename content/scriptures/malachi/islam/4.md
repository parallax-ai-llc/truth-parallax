The Day of the LORD "burning like an oven" (4:1) corresponds directly to Quranic descriptions of the Last Day. The imagery of fire consuming the wicked while the righteous rejoice in healing and liberation (4:2-3) closely mirrors the Quranic contrast between the people of the Fire (*ahl an-nar*) and the people of the Garden (*ahl al-jannah*). The Quranic description in Surah Al-Waqi'ah (56) divides humanity into three groups — the forerunners, the people of the right, and the people of the left — in a scheme that echoes Malachi's stark division between those who serve God and those who do not.

The return of Elijah (4:5) has an interesting parallel in Islamic eschatology, which expects the return of Isa (Jesus) rather than Elijah before the Last Day. Both traditions share the expectation that a prophetic figure will return at the end of history to prepare for divine judgment. The Hadith literature describes Isa's return in detail: he will descend in Damascus, pray behind the Mahdi, defeat the Dajjal (Anti-Christ), and establish a period of justice before the final resurrection. While the specific figure differs, the structural pattern — a returning prophet who prepares for the Day of Judgment — is remarkably consistent.

The command to "remember the law of Moses" (4:4) resonates with the Islamic reverence for Musa (Moses), who is mentioned more frequently in the Quran than any other prophet. The Torah (*Tawrat*) is acknowledged as original divine revelation, and the Quranic injunction to "hold firmly to what We have given you and remember what is in it" (2:63) parallels Malachi's closing admonition. The prophetic heritage, in the Islamic view, forms a continuous chain from Adam to Muhammad, with each messenger reinforcing the same essential call to worship God alone and live in justice and mercy.
