The messenger prophecy (3:1) resonates with the Islamic theology of prophetic succession. Islam teaches that God sends messengers to every generation that strays from the straight path, and Malachi's announcement of a coming messenger fits this pattern. The "refiner's fire" (3:2) that purifies worship echoes the Quranic description of how God tests (*yabtaliy*) His servants through trials that refine their faith: "Do people think they will be left alone because they say 'We believe' and not be tested?" (29:2). The purpose of divine testing in Islamic theology is not punishment but purification — the burning away of dross to reveal genuine faith.

The tithing passage (3:8-12) connects with the Islamic pillar of *zakat* (obligatory almsgiving). The accusation "you are robbing God" parallels the Quranic warning against those who hoard wealth and fail to give their due: "Those who hoard gold and silver and do not spend them in the way of God — give them tidings of a painful punishment" (9:34). Both Malachi and the Quran treat withholding from God's cause as a form of theft that disrupts the social and spiritual order. The promise of abundant blessing for faithful givers resonates with the Quranic assurance: "Whatever you spend in the way of God will be repaid to you in full, and you will not be wronged" (8:60).

The distinction between those who fear God and those who do not (3:16-18) aligns with the fundamental Quranic category of *taqwa* (God-consciousness). The "book of remembrance" written for the God-fearing parallels the Quranic concept of the *kitab* (record) in which every deed is inscribed and from which each person will be judged on the Day of Resurrection (17:13-14). In both prophetic traditions, divine record-keeping assures the faithful that no act of devotion goes unnoticed.
