## Overview

Malachi is not named in the Quran and holds no canonical status in Islam. However, the book's themes — divine justice, prophetic warning against religious complacency, the inadequacy of mere ritual observance, and the coming Day of Judgment — resonate deeply with Quranic concerns. Islamic scholarship encounters Malachi primarily through *Isra'iliyyat* traditions and through comparative study of the Abrahamic prophetic heritage.

## Quranic Parallels

Malachi's central complaint — that the people offer God blemished sacrifices while keeping the best for themselves (1:8) — mirrors the Quranic insistence on sincerity in worship. The Quran warns: "Their meat and blood do not reach Allah, but what reaches Him is your piety" (22:37). Both prophetic traditions emphasize that external ritual without internal devotion is worthless. The prophetic call to justice, honest dealing, and covenant faithfulness in Malachi 2-3 aligns with the Quranic vision of a community built on *taqwa* (God-consciousness) and *'adl* (justice).

## Theological Connections

The announcement of a coming messenger who will purify worship (3:1-4) fits the Islamic paradigm of successive prophets sent to reform communities that have drifted from the original divine message. The image of the refiner's fire resonates with the Quranic theme of God testing (*ibtila'*) His servants to purify their faith. The closing promise of a prophet (Elijah) before the Day of the LORD parallels the Islamic expectation of the return of Isa (Jesus) before the Last Day.
