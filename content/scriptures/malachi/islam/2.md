The prophetic ideal of the priesthood described in Malachi 2:5-7 — a teacher who walks with God in integrity and guides people in truth — parallels the Islamic concept of the *'alim* (scholar) whose knowledge is coupled with piety. The Prophet Muhammad said: "The scholars are the heirs of the prophets" (Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi). Malachi's warning that faithless priests "have caused many to stumble" (2:8) echoes the Quranic admonition against religious leaders who misguide their communities: "They took their scholars and monks as lords besides Allah" (9:31). Both traditions hold religious teachers to a high standard of personal integrity and doctrinal fidelity.

The marriage teaching (2:13-16) connects with Islamic jurisprudence on marriage and divorce. Islam permits divorce (*talaq*) but treats it as the "most hated of permissible things" according to a widely cited hadith. The prophetic emphasis on covenant faithfulness in marriage resonates with the Quranic description of spouses as "garments for each other" (2:187) — providing protection, intimacy, and covering. The principle that marital betrayal grieves God is shared across the Abrahamic traditions.

The closing lament — "Where is the God of justice?" (2:17) — finds a powerful echo in Quranic responses to those who doubt divine justice. The Quran addresses this complaint extensively, particularly in the stories of prophets who endured long periods of apparent divine inaction before vindication came. The story of Ayyub (Job), who suffered patiently before being restored, and the story of Yusuf (Joseph), who endured prison before rising to power, both answer the question of delayed divine justice with the assurance that God's timing, not human impatience, determines the moment of reckoning.
