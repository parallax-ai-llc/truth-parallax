Orthodox interpretation of Malachi 1 emphasizes the prophet's theology of worship as the central act of human existence. The divine complaint — "A son honors his father, and a servant his master. If then I am a father, where is my honor?" (1:6) — establishes the fundamental orientation of Orthodox liturgical theology: worship is not optional or auxiliary but the defining relationship between Creator and creature. The defective offerings of the priests represent not mere ritual negligence but a fundamental disorder in the community's relationship with God.

The prophecy of the "pure offering" among all nations (1:11) is read in Orthodox tradition as a direct reference to the Divine Liturgy. From the time of Justin Martyr and Irenaeus, this verse has been understood as foretelling the Eucharistic sacrifice offered throughout the world. The Orthodox understanding of the Eucharist as a true sacrifice — not a repetition of Calvary but a participation in the one eternal offering of Christ — finds its prophetic foundation in Malachi's vision of a worship that transcends the limitations of the Jerusalem Temple.

The election of Jacob over Esau (1:2-5) is read in the patristic tradition not as arbitrary favoritism but as divine pedagogy. The Fathers, following Origen and the Cappadocians, emphasized God's foreknowledge and the mysterious workings of providence rather than a deterministic decree. The Orthodox tradition resists both Calvinist predestination and Pelagian self-sufficiency, maintaining that God's election is real but that human freedom and response remain integral to the economy of salvation.
