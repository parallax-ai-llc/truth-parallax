The messenger prophecy (3:1) occupies a central place in Orthodox theology of the Forerunner (John the Baptist). Orthodox iconography consistently depicts the Forerunner with wings — symbolizing his angelic (messenger) role as prophesied by Malachi. He is commemorated more frequently in the Orthodox liturgical calendar than any other saint, and the Malachi prophecy provides the theological foundation for this singular honor. The Forerunner stands at the boundary between the Old and New Covenants, the last prophet and the first witness to the Incarnate Lord.

The refining fire (3:2-3) is interpreted in Orthodox theology through the lens of *theosis*. The purification that Malachi describes is not punitive but transformative — the burning away of everything in human nature that resists divinization. The Orthodox understanding of salvation is not primarily juridical (acquittal from guilt) but therapeutic (healing from the disease of sin). The refiner's fire is the divine energy (*energeia*) that penetrates the human person, consuming the dross of passion and sin, leaving behind the gold of the divine image restored.

The "book of remembrance" (3:16) written for those who fear the LORD resonates with the Orthodox theology of the communion of saints and the divine memory that holds all creation in being. In Orthodox thought, to be remembered by God is to exist in the fullest sense; to be forgotten is to perish. The book of remembrance is thus not merely a record but an ontological reality — those inscribed in it are preserved in God's eternal love, a theme that echoes through the Orthodox funeral service and memorial prayers.
