## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox tradition reads Malachi through the lens of liturgical theology and the theology of the Forerunner (John the Baptist). The prophecy of a "pure offering" among all nations (1:11) is understood as referring to the Divine Liturgy, the Eucharistic sacrifice that is offered continuously from the rising of the sun to its setting across the Orthodox world. The coming messenger (3:1) is identified with the Forerunner, who holds a place of singular honor in Orthodox iconography and liturgical commemoration.

## Patristic Interpretation

The Church Fathers gave extensive attention to Malachi. Justin Martyr cited 1:11 in his *Dialogue with Trypho* as proof that the Eucharist fulfills the prophetic vision of universal worship. Cyril of Alexandria read the refining messenger (3:2-3) as Christ who purifies the Church through suffering and sacramental grace. The Orthodox liturgical calendar commemorates the Forerunner more frequently than any saint, and Malachi's Elijah prophecy (4:5) provides the prophetic foundation for this veneration.

## Theological Emphases

The theology of priestly vocation in Malachi 2:5-7 — the priest as "messenger of the LORD of hosts" whose lips guard knowledge — informs the Orthodox understanding of the ordained ministry as a teaching and liturgical vocation. The warning against faithless priests resonates with the Orthodox tradition of holding clergy to high standards of personal holiness and doctrinal fidelity.
