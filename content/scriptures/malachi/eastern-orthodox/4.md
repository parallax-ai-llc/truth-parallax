The "Sun of Righteousness" (4:2) is a beloved title in Orthodox Christology and hymnography. The Paschal troparion — "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life" — captures the same solar imagery of light conquering darkness. Orthodox churches are traditionally oriented toward the east, the direction of the rising sun, in anticipation of Christ's second coming as the Sun of Righteousness. The iconographic convention of placing the image of Christ Pantocrator in the dome directly above the congregation embodies this theology of divine illumination descending from above.

The conjunction of Moses and Elijah in the final verses (4:4-5) mirrors their appearance at the Transfiguration (Matthew 17:1-8), an event of paramount importance in Orthodox theology. The Feast of the Transfiguration is one of the great feasts of the Orthodox calendar, and the patristic interpretation sees Moses and Elijah as representing the Law and the Prophets, both of which find their fulfillment in the transfigured Christ. Malachi's closing injunction to "remember the law" and expect Elijah creates the theological structure that the Transfiguration fulfills and completes.

The final verse, with its threat of a curse (4:6), is read in Orthodox tradition not as God's final word but as a penultimate one. The Old Testament ends in suspense; the New Testament begins with resolution. The Orthodox liturgical reading of the prophets during Holy Week and Pascha creates a dramatic arc from judgment to resurrection, from curse to blessing. Malachi's closing word is silence — the four centuries of prophetic silence that the Orthodox tradition understands as the deep breath before the Annunciation, when the angel Gabriel speaks the word that breaks the silence forever.
