## Overview

1 Timothy has a paradoxical relationship with Gnosticism: it contains some of the New Testament's most explicit anti-Gnostic polemic (see 6:20, "what is falsely called *gnosis*"), yet Gnostic interpreters still found material to appropriate. The letter's references to "myths and endless genealogies" (1:4) and "what is falsely called knowledge" (6:20) are widely understood as targeting proto-Gnostic teaching.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### "Falsely Called Knowledge"
The warning against "what is falsely called *gnosis*" (6:20) is the most direct anti-Gnostic statement in the Pauline corpus. Gnostic teachers reversed the polemic, arguing that the Pastoral Epistles represented an institutional Christianity that had suppressed the authentic spiritual teaching of Paul preserved in Gnostic traditions.

### Myths, Genealogies, and Aeons
Gnostic systems featured elaborate genealogies of divine beings (aeons). The Pastoral author's dismissal of "myths and endless genealogies" (1:4) may target early forms of these Gnostic cosmological speculations.

### The Mystery of Godliness
The Christological hymn in 3:16 — "He was manifested in the flesh" — was contested terrain. Orthodox Christians used it to affirm the Incarnation; Gnostic interpreters, particularly Docetists, reread it as describing a spiritual being's temporary appearance in apparent flesh.
