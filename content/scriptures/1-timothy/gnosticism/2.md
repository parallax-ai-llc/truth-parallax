## Instructions on Worship

The instruction to pray "for kings and all who are in high positions" (2:2) is understood by Gnostic interpreters as a strategic concession rather than a theological endorsement. Earthly rulers are reflections of the archontic hierarchy, and prayer for them serves the practical purpose of maintaining a "peaceful and quiet life" (2:2) in which the pneumatic community can pursue gnosis undisturbed. The goal is not to affirm the legitimacy of worldly power but to navigate it skillfully, since the archons and their human agents will persecute those who openly challenge the cosmic order.

The declaration that there is "one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (2:5) carries profound significance in Gnostic christology. The "one God" here is not the Demiurge but the transcendent, unknowable Father. Christ as mediator is the divine aeon who bridges the impossible gulf between the Pleroma and the deficient material cosmos, a gulf that the Demiurge cannot cross because he does not even know it exists. Christ "gave himself as a ransom" (2:6) not to pay a debt to the Demiurge but to penetrate the archontic barriers and plant the seed of liberating knowledge within the prison of flesh.

The passage concerning women's silence and the reference to Eve being "deceived" (2:13-14) was read by many Gnostic schools in a radically different light. In Valentinian and Sethian traditions, Eve is not the origin of sin but a figure of Sophia or Epinoia, the feminine spiritual intelligence who brought knowledge to humanity against the Demiurge's wishes. The Demiurge wanted Adam and Eve to remain in ignorance; it was the serpent, as an emissary of the higher realms, who offered the fruit of gnosis. The restrictions placed on women in this passage are seen as reflecting the Demiurge's fear of the feminine principle of wisdom that threatens to undo his regime of ignorance.
