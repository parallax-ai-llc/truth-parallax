## Warning Against False Teachers

The warning against "different doctrine" and those devoted to "myths and endless genealogies" (1:3-4) is deeply ironic from a Gnostic perspective, since the proto-orthodox church would later use this very passage against Gnostic teachers and their elaborate accounts of aeonic emanations. Gnostic readers, however, reverse the charge: the "false teachers" are those who propagate the Demiurge's law without understanding its hidden meaning, promoting a surface-level religion of commandments that binds souls more tightly to the material order rather than liberating them.

The statement that "the law is good if one uses it lawfully" (1:8) is interpreted as an acknowledgment that the Demiurge's law contains within it, despite its author's ignorance, traces of higher truth that the pneumatic can extract. The law was given by an inferior creator who mimicked patterns from the Pleroma without comprehending them. A lawful use of the law, then, is to read it as a cipher pointing beyond itself to the transcendent Father, not as a literal code of conduct enforcing the Demiurge's regime.

Paul's self-description as the "foremost of sinners" whom Christ came to save (1:15) is understood as a statement about the condition of all divine sparks trapped in flesh. To be a "sinner" in the deepest sense is to be imprisoned in materiality, forgetful of one's origin. Christ's mission is not the satisfaction of a vengeful deity's justice but the rescue operation of Sophia's consort, descending through the archontic heavens to whisper the secret name that awakens the sleeping sparks. The mention of Hymenaeus and Alexander being "handed over to Satan" (1:20) represents souls who, having received gnosis, turned back to the authority of the chief archon, preferring the familiar prison to the terrifying freedom of the Pleroma.
