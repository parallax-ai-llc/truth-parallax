## False Teaching and True Riches

The declaration that "godliness with contentment is great gain" (6:6) expresses the Gnostic insight that true wealth is the possession of gnosis itself. The pneumatic who has awakened to the divine spark within needs nothing from the material world, which is the Demiurge's impoverished domain. The reminder that "we brought nothing into the world, and we cannot take anything out of it" (6:7) underscores the illusory nature of material possession: all that belongs to the hylic realm dissolves when the soul departs the body and begins its ascent through the archontic spheres.

The love of money as "a root of all kinds of evil" (6:10) identifies wealth-accumulation as one of the archons' primary mechanisms for binding divine sparks to matter. Those who pursue riches "have wandered away from the faith and pierced themselves with many pangs" (6:10), meaning they have traded the freedom of gnosis for deeper enslavement within the Demiurge's economy of scarcity and desire. The material world is designed to produce insatiable wanting, and money is its most concentrated expression.

The closing exhortation to "guard the deposit entrusted to you" and avoid "the irreverent babble and contradictions of what is falsely called knowledge" (6:20) is one of the most contested passages in the Gnostic-orthodox dispute. The proto-orthodox used "falsely called knowledge" (pseudonymou gnoseos) as a direct polemic against Gnostic schools. Gnostic readers, however, turn the phrase back: the false knowledge is precisely the Demiurge's counterfeit wisdom, the surface-level theology that mistakes the jealous creator for the supreme God. The "deposit" to be guarded is the authentic gnosis transmitted from the Savior through the apostolic chain, the secret teaching that God "who alone has immortality, who dwells in unapproachable light" (6:16) is not the Demiurge but the unknowable Bythos beyond all archontic heavens.
