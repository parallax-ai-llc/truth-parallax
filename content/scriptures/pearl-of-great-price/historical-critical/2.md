## Historical Context

The Book of Abraham, reportedly translated from Egyptian papyri purchased by Joseph Smith in 1835, has generated more historical-critical controversy than any other Mormon scripture. The surviving papyrus fragments (rediscovered in 1967) have been identified by Egyptologists as common funerary texts (Book of Breathings) dating to the Ptolemaic period, unrelated to Abraham.

## Source Criticism

Robert Ritner, professor of Egyptology at the University of Chicago, has published detailed analyses demonstrating that the papyri contain standard Egyptian funerary spells, not Abrahamic autobiography. The facsimiles published with the text have been identified as conventional vignettes from the Book of the Dead and the Hypocephalus. LDS scholars such as John Gee and Kerry Muhlestein have proposed a "missing scroll" theory or a "catalyst" theory in which the papyri prompted revelation without being the direct source.

## Form Criticism

The text's narrative form — patriarchal autobiography with astronomical instruction — combines Genesis-style narrative with cosmological discourse reminiscent of Hellenistic Jewish texts (cf. Apocalypse of Abraham, Testament of Abraham). The astronomical content (Kolob, governing stars, time reckoning) has been compared by Hugh Nibley to ancient astronomical texts from various traditions, though these comparisons remain contested.

## Sitz im Leben

The text was produced during the Kirtland period (1835-1842) when Smith was studying Hebrew with Joshua Seixas and developing interest in ancient languages. The cultural context includes widespread American interest in Egyptian antiquities following Napoleon's Egyptian expedition and Champollion's decipherment of hieroglyphics.

## Redaction History

The Book of Abraham was published in installments in the *Times and Seasons* (1842), and the relationship between the published text and the earlier Kirtland-era manuscripts is complex. The Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language (GAEL), produced alongside the translation, has been analyzed by scholars including Brian Hauglid as evidence of the translation process.
