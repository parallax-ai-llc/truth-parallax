## Historical Context

Joseph Smith—Matthew is an extract from the JST revision of Matthew 23:39-24:56, produced circa 1831. It presents an expanded version of the Olivet Discourse, Jesus's eschatological prophecy about the destruction of Jerusalem and his Second Coming.

## Source Criticism

The base text is the KJV Matthew 24, with additions and rearrangements. Historical-critical scholars note that the Olivet Discourse is itself a composite text in the Synoptic Gospels, combining authentic Jesus traditions with post-70 CE vaticinia ex eventu (prophecy after the fact) about Jerusalem's destruction. Smith's revision adds further interpretive layers, particularly clarifying the relationship between the destruction of Jerusalem and the Second Coming — a distinction that biblical scholars from Schweitzer onward have also attempted to parse.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The apocalyptic discourse genre is well established in both Jewish (Daniel 7-12, 1 Enoch) and Christian (Mark 13, 2 Thessalonians 2, Revelation) literature. Smith's additions emphasize the imminence of the Second Coming and the need for gathering, reflecting the millenarian Sitz im Leben of the early Restoration. Underwood has documented how early Mormon eschatology was more urgent and specific than later, more cautious formulations.

## Redaction History

As with all JST material, the text was produced through dictation rather than consultation with Greek manuscripts. Wayment's comparative analysis shows that the JST Matthew sometimes follows Luke's wording rather than Matthew's KJV text, suggesting harmonistic editorial tendencies similar to those Tatian employed in the Diatessaron.
