## Historical Context

Joseph Smith—History, canonized as scripture in 1880, is extracted from Smith's 1838 history and provides the official account of the First Vision, Moroni's visits, the translation of the Book of Mormon, and the restoration of the priesthood. Historical-critical scholars have documented multiple earlier versions of the First Vision account, making this text a prime subject for redaction criticism.

## Source Criticism and Redaction History

At least four accounts of the First Vision exist (1832, 1835, 1838, 1842), with significant differences. The 1832 account mentions only one divine personage; the 1838 account specifies two (the Father and the Son). The 1832 account emphasizes personal forgiveness; the 1838 account emphasizes institutional apostasy and restoration. James Allen's groundbreaking 1966 article first documented this developmental trajectory, and subsequent scholarship by Steven Harper and Matthew Grow has expanded the analysis.

## Form Criticism

The First Vision narrative follows the form of a prophetic call narrative while incorporating elements of conversion testimony common in early American revivalism. Richard Bushman situates the account within the "visionary tradition" of rural New England, while Jan Shipps reads it as a foundational myth comparable to Moses at Sinai or Muhammad's revelatory experience.

## Sitz im Leben

The 1838 account was composed during the Missouri crisis, when Smith's authority was being challenged by dissenters including Oliver Cowdery and the Whitmer family. The emphasis on exclusive divine commission can be read as a response to these challenges, a dynamic familiar from biblical texts where prophetic authority is asserted against rivals.

## Historical Evidence

The historical setting described — the 1820 Palmyra-Manchester area revivals — has been debated. Wesley Walters argued that no significant revival occurred in the area until 1824-1825, while Milton Backman and others have identified revivalist activity in the broader region during 1819-1820.
