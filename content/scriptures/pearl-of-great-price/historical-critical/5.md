## Historical Context

The Articles of Faith were composed by Joseph Smith in 1842 as part of the Wentworth Letter, written for John Wentworth, editor of the *Chicago Democrat*. They were canonized as scripture in 1880 when included in the Pearl of Great Price.

## Source Criticism

The thirteen articles draw on multiple sources. Scholars including Richard Howard have identified significant dependence on Orson Pratt's 1840 pamphlet *An Interesting Account of Several Remarkable Visions*, which itself adapted earlier creedal formulations. The articles also show influence from the Methodist Articles of Religion, Alexander Campbell's restorationist theology, and standard Protestant confessional language.

## Form Criticism

The literary form is a creed or confession of faith, a genre with a long history in Christianity (Apostles' Creed, Nicene Creed, Augsburg Confession). The articles function to present Mormonism in terms accessible to mainstream Protestantism, deliberately downplaying distinctive elements such as temple ordinances, plural marriage, and theosis that would emerge as central to later Mormon theology.

## Sitz im Leben

The Wentworth Letter was composed during the Nauvoo period, when Smith was actively courting political allies and public respectability. The articles' irenic tone and conventional Christian language contrast sharply with the more radical theological developments occurring simultaneously in Nauvoo (the King Follett discourse, plural marriage, the temple endowment). This disjunction between public self-presentation and internal theological development is itself a significant datum for historical analysis.

## Compositional Analysis

The brevity and selectivity of the articles have been analyzed as strategic self-representation. What is included (faith, repentance, baptism, spiritual gifts, gathering, millennium) represents the movement's most publicly acceptable claims; what is excluded (priesthood restoration, gold plates, temple rites) represents its most controversial.
