## Historical Context

The Book of Moses is extracted from Joseph Smith's "New Translation" (JST) of the Bible, begun in June 1830. Historical-critical scholars examine it as a creative expansion of Genesis 1-6, comparable to Jewish and Christian *rewritten Bible* traditions (cf. Jubilees, 1 Enoch, Pseudo-Philo).

## Source Criticism

The text depends on the KJV Genesis while introducing substantial additions, including Moses's theophany (chapter 1), expanded creation accounts, Satan's rebellion, and an extensive Enoch narrative. Thomas Römer and other Pentateuchal scholars note that the additions reflect concerns absent from the Hebrew Genesis but present in Second Temple Jewish literature. The Enoch material bears structural similarities to 1 Enoch (which was not available in English translation until 1821, providing a possible source).

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The theophany narrative in Moses 1 follows the form of biblical call narratives (Moses at the burning bush, Isaiah's temple vision) while expanding them with distinctively Mormon theological content, including the plurality of worlds and the identification of God's purpose as bringing to pass "the immortality and eternal life of man." The Sitz im Leben is the early Restoration period's effort to recover a "plain and precious" biblical text that, per Book of Mormon claims, had been corrupted.

## Compositional History

The JST was produced without access to Hebrew or Greek manuscripts, relying on the KJV as base text. Scholars such as Philip Barlow and Thomas Wayment examine it as a revelatory commentary rather than a translation in the conventional sense, comparing it to Targum and Midrash traditions.
