## Faith and Victory

1 John 5:1–5 teaches that faith born of God overcomes the world. Catholic theology reads this within the framework of baptismal regeneration: the faith that conquers is not mere intellectual assent but the living faith infused at baptism and nourished through the sacramental life. This faith is active in love (Galatians 5:6) and finds its ultimate expression in the confession that "Jesus is the Son of God" (5:5).

## The Johannine Comma

1 John 5:7–8 is the site of the famous *Comma Johanneum* — the Trinitarian gloss ("the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit, and these three are one") present in some late Latin manuscripts but absent from the earliest Greek texts. Catholic scholarship has acknowledged that the Comma is not original to the text, though it was included in the Vulgate and cited at the Council of Trent. The Church's Trinitarian faith does not depend on this single textual variant.

## Mortal Sin and Prayer

1 John 5:16–17 distinguishes between "sin that leads to death" and "sin that does not lead to death." Catholic moral theology draws on this passage for its distinction between mortal and venial sin. The instruction to pray for those who commit non-mortal sin informs the Catholic practice of intercessory prayer and the communal dimension of repentance. The letter's closing — "keep yourselves from idols" (5:21) — is a final warning against anything that displaces God from the center of the believer's life.
