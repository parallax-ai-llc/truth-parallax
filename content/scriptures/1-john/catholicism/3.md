## Children of God and Transformation

1 John 3:1–3 declares believers "children of God" and promises that "when he appears we shall be like him." Catholic theology reads this as an affirmation of the transformative power of grace: the believer is not merely declared righteous but is genuinely made a child of God through baptismal regeneration. The future likeness to Christ is the completion of the sanctifying process begun in baptism.

## Sin and the Children of God

1 John 3:4–10, with its assertion that "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning," is read in Catholic theology in light of the distinction between mortal and venial sin. The genuine child of God does not persist in mortal sin, which destroys the life of grace. Venial sins, while real, do not sever the relationship with God and are addressed through the ongoing sacramental life.

## Love in Deed and Truth

1 John 3:16–24 defines love as sacrificial action modeled on Christ. Catholic social teaching draws on this passage to emphasize that love is not mere sentiment but concrete care for the poor and vulnerable. The Works of Mercy — both corporal and spiritual — are the practical expression of the love John describes: "Let us not love in word or talk but in deed and in truth" (3:18).
