## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 1 John draws on the letter's profound theology of love, its teaching on sin and confession, and its defense of the incarnation. The distinction between mortal and venial sin (5:16–17) is foundational for Catholic moral theology and sacramental practice. The letter's declaration that "God is love" (4:8, 16) has been especially influential, shaping Catholic theology from Augustine to Benedict XVI's encyclical *Deus Caritas Est*.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### God Is Love
The declaration "God is love" (4:8, 16) is not merely an attribute of God but a revelation of the divine nature itself. Benedict XVI's first encyclical, *Deus Caritas Est* (2005), takes its title from 1 John 4:16 and develops the letter's theology of love as the foundation for Catholic social teaching and the Church's charitable mission.

### Sin unto Death
1 John 5:16–17 distinguishes between "sin that leads to death" and "sin that does not lead to death." Catholic moral theology has drawn on this distinction to develop the categories of mortal and venial sin. Mortal sin — grave in matter, committed with full knowledge and deliberate consent — severs the soul's relationship with God, while venial sin wounds but does not destroy that relationship.

### Sacramental Life
The letter's teaching on confession of sin (1:9), Christ as advocate (2:1–2), and the anointing with the Holy Spirit (2:20, 27) informs Catholic sacramental theology. The Sacrament of Reconciliation finds scriptural grounding in the letter's assurance that "if we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins" (1:9).

## Theological Emphases

- **Deus Caritas Est**: God's nature as love, the foundation of all theology
- **Mortal and Venial Sin**: The distinction between sins that kill spiritual life and those that wound it
- **Confession and Forgiveness**: 1 John 1:9 as the basis for sacramental reconciliation
- **The Incarnation**: The reality of Christ's flesh as central to faith and salvation
