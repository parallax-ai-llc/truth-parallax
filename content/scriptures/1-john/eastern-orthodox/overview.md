## Overview

Eastern Orthodox theology finds in 1 John some of its most foundational affirmations: "God is love" (4:8, 16), "God is light" (1:5), and the reality of the incarnation as the basis of salvation. The letter's theology of divine love is not merely ethical but ontological — love is the very nature of God, revealed in the Trinity and shared with humanity through the incarnation. The Orthodox reading integrates the letter's teaching on love, light, and truth into the larger framework of theosis.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### God Is Light and Love
1 John's dual affirmation — "God is light" (1:5) and "God is love" (4:8) — shapes the Orthodox understanding of the divine nature. Light and love are not attributes separate from God's being but manifestations of the uncreated divine energies. The faithful encounter this light in prayer and worship, and this love in the sacramental and communal life of the Church.

### The Incarnation and Theosis
1 John's insistence that Jesus came "in the flesh" (4:2) is read as the necessary precondition for theosis. Because the divine Word assumed genuine human nature, human nature is now capable of participating in the divine life. The denial of the incarnation — which the letter identifies as the spirit of antichrist — is therefore the denial of human salvation itself.

### Testing the Spirits
1 John 4:1–6, with its call to "test the spirits," informs the Orthodox tradition of spiritual discernment (*diakrisis*). The desert fathers and the Philokalia tradition teach that not every spiritual experience comes from God, and the criterion for discernment is conformity to the apostolic faith concerning the incarnation.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Love as Ontological**: Love as the nature of the Triune God, not merely a divine attribute
- **The Incarnation**: The reality of Christ's flesh as the foundation of salvation and theosis
- **Light and Darkness**: The spiritual life as a journey from darkness into the uncreated light
- **Communion**: Fellowship with God and with one another as the goal of the Christian life
