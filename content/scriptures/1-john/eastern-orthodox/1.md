## The Word of Life and Communion

Orthodox theology reads the prologue of 1 John (1:1–4) as a declaration of the reality of the incarnation and the communion it makes possible. The Word of life was not only revealed but encountered — seen, heard, and touched. This sensory encounter continues in the Orthodox liturgical life, where the faithful see the icons, hear the Gospel, and receive the Body and Blood of Christ. The fellowship (*koinonia*) described is both divine and ecclesial.

## God Is Light

1 John 1:5 — "God is light, and in him is no darkness at all" — is foundational for Orthodox theology of the divine light. The uncreated light that shone from Christ at the Transfiguration is the same light that illuminates the believer in prayer. The hesychast tradition teaches that the purified heart can perceive this light, which is not a metaphor but a real experience of God's uncreated energies.

## Confession and Healing

1 John 1:8–10 on the reality of sin and the promise of forgiveness through confession shapes Orthodox sacramental practice. The Orthodox sacrament of Confession is understood as spiritual healing — the restoration of the soul's health through the acknowledgment of sin and the reception of divine mercy. The priest functions as a spiritual physician, and the confession is a step in the therapeutic process of salvation.
