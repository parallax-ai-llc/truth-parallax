## The Embodied Word

1 John 1:1–4, with its insistence on the tangible, physical reality of the "Word of life," makes a Christological claim that Muslim theology does not accept. Islam honors Isa (Jesus) as a prophet and the Word of God cast to Mary (Quran 4:171) but does not affirm the incarnation of the divine in human flesh. Muslim scholars engage with the passage as evidence of the early Christian development of Christological doctrine that Islam views as a departure from Jesus's own teaching.

## Walking in Light

1 John 1:5–7, with its metaphor of God as light and the call to walk in the light, resonates with Quranic imagery. The Quran describes God as "the Light of the heavens and the earth" (24:35) in the celebrated Verse of Light (*Ayat an-Nur*). Muslim readers appreciate the shared metaphor while noting that Islam frames the relationship between God and light differently — God is not identified with light but is its source and transcends it.

## Sin and Forgiveness

1 John 1:8–10, teaching that all have sinned and that confession leads to forgiveness, resonates with the Islamic emphasis on *istighfar* (seeking forgiveness). The Quran promises: "And whoever does evil or wrongs himself but then seeks forgiveness of Allah will find Allah Forgiving and Merciful" (4:110). Muslim readers find common ground in the recognition of human sinfulness and the assurance of divine mercy for those who repent.
