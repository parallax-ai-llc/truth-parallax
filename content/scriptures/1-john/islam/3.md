## Children of God and Human Dignity

1 John 3:1 — "that we should be called children of God" — uses language that Muslim theology engages carefully. Islam does not use the concept of divine sonship, considering it incompatible with God's absolute transcendence. However, the Quran affirms the dignity of human beings as God's representatives (*khalifah*) on earth (2:30), and the concept of a special relationship between God and the faithful is shared, even if the vocabulary differs.

## The Example of Cain

1 John 3:12 references Cain's murder of Abel, a narrative also found in the Quran (5:27–32). The Quranic version concludes with the significant declaration: "Whoever kills a soul unless for a soul or for corruption in the land — it is as if he had slain mankind entirely. And whoever saves one — it is as if he had saved mankind entirely" (5:32). Both traditions use the Cain narrative as a foundational moral lesson about the gravity of violence.

## Love in Action

1 John 3:17–18, insisting that love must be expressed in practical care for those in need, aligns with the Islamic obligation of charitable action. The Quran states: "And they give food in spite of love for it to the needy, the orphan, and the captive" (76:8). Both traditions affirm that genuine faith is demonstrated through concrete acts of generosity and compassion, not merely through verbal profession.
