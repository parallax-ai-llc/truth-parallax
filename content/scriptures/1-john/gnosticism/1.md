## The Tangible Word Against Docetism

1 John 1:1–4 insists on the sensory reality of the incarnation: "what we have heard, what we have seen with our eyes, what we looked upon and touched with our hands." This emphatic physicality is directed against Docetic Christology — the Gnostic view that the divine Christ only appeared to have a body. The prologue establishes from the outset that authentic faith confesses a Christ who can be touched, not an ethereal phantom.

## Light and Gnostic Dualism

1 John 1:5 — "God is light" — uses language that Gnostic interpreters could appropriate for their cosmological dualism. The Gnostic tradition identified the transcendent God with light and the Demiurge's material creation with darkness. However, 1 John's light-darkness contrast is moral, not ontological: walking in the light means living truthfully and obediently, not possessing cosmological knowledge about the realms of light.

## Sin and the Gnostic Denial

1 John 1:8 — "If we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves" — may target a Gnostic claim that the pneumatic, being divine by nature, is incapable of sin. Some Gnostic teachers taught that sin pertains only to the body and cannot affect the divine spark within. 1 John counters by insisting on the universal reality of sin and the ongoing need for confession and cleansing.
