## The Word of Life

1 John opens with a prologue (1:1–4) that echoes John 1:1, affirming the reality of the incarnation through emphatic sensory language: "what we have heard, what we have seen with our eyes, what we looked upon and touched with our hands." Protestant interpreters read this as an eyewitness defense of the incarnation against early Docetic denials. The "Word of life" is not abstract theology but a person encountered in history.

## Walking in the Light

1 John 1:5–7 establishes the fundamental metaphor: "God is light, and in him is no darkness at all." Protestant interpreters read the contrast between light and darkness as a moral and spiritual test. Genuine believers "walk in the light" — living in openness, truth, and obedience — while those who claim fellowship with God but walk in darkness are self-deceived.

## Sin and Confession

1 John 1:8–10 addresses the reality of sin in the believer's life. The claim to be "without sin" is self-deception (1:8), but "if we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins" (1:9). Protestant interpreters emphasize that this confession is directed to God, not to a human priest, and that forgiveness rests on Christ's atoning work, not on the act of confession itself.
