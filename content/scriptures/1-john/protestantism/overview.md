## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 1 John has focused on the letter's teaching about assurance of salvation, the tests of genuine faith, and the nature of Christian love. The letter provides three tests by which believers can know they have eternal life: doctrinal (believing in the incarnation), moral (obeying God's commands), and social (loving fellow believers). These "tests of life" have been central to Protestant pastoral theology and the doctrine of assurance.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Tests of Genuine Faith
Robert Law's classic study *The Tests of Life* (1909) identified the three recurring tests in 1 John: the theological test (right belief about Christ), the moral test (obedience to God's commands), and the social test (love for fellow believers). Protestant interpreters use these tests to distinguish genuine from nominal Christianity, providing objective criteria for self-examination.

### Assurance of Salvation
1 John 5:13 — "I write these things to you who believe in the name of the Son of God, that you may know that you have eternal life" — is a key text for the Protestant doctrine of assurance. The letter teaches that genuine believers can have confident knowledge of their salvation, not based on subjective feelings but on the objective evidence of faith, obedience, and love.

### The Love Command
1 John's teaching on love — "God is love" (4:8, 16), "We love because he first loved us" (4:19) — shapes Protestant ethics and devotional life. The love described is not sentimental but sacrificial, modeled on Christ's self-giving and expressed in concrete care for fellow believers.

## Theological Emphases

- **Assurance**: Objective grounds for knowing one possesses eternal life
- **Incarnation**: The non-negotiable confession that Jesus came in the flesh
- **Love as Evidence**: Genuine love for believers as proof of spiritual life
- **Sin and Advocacy**: Christ as the believer's advocate when sin occurs (2:1–2)
