## Keeping the Commandments

1 John 2:3–6 presents obedience as the test of knowing God: "Whoever says 'I know him' but does not keep his commandments is a liar." Protestant interpreters use this as a corrective to antinomian tendencies — the claim that faith in Christ frees one from moral obligation. True faith produces obedience, not as the ground of salvation but as its evidence.

## The New Commandment of Love

1 John 2:7–11 develops the love command as both old and new: rooted in Leviticus 19:18 but renewed in Christ. Protestant interpreters read love for fellow believers as a defining mark of genuine conversion. The one who hates his brother "is in the darkness" and has never truly passed from death to life.

## Warning Against the World and the Antichrist

1 John 2:15–27 warns against love of the world and announces that "many antichrists have come" (2:18). Protestant interpreters identify the "antichrists" as false teachers who departed from the community, denying the incarnation. The "anointing" that teaches believers all things (2:20, 27) is identified with the Holy Spirit, supporting the Protestant emphasis on the Spirit's internal witness to truth.
