## Light and Darkness in Jewish Tradition

1 John 1:5 — "God is light, and in him is no darkness at all" — employs a metaphor deeply rooted in the Hebrew Bible. Isaiah 60:19 declares God to be the "everlasting light," and Psalm 27:1 proclaims, "The Lord is my light and my salvation." Jewish scholars note that the light-darkness dualism in 1 John closely parallels the Dead Sea Scrolls' Community Rule, which describes a cosmic struggle between the "sons of light" and the "sons of darkness," suggesting shared roots in Second Temple Jewish thought.

## Sin and Repentance

1 John 1:8–10, addressing the reality of sin and the promise of forgiveness through confession, resonates with the Jewish concept of *teshuvah* (repentance). The Jewish liturgical tradition, especially the Yom Kippur service, centers on the confession of sin and the assurance of divine forgiveness for those who sincerely repent. Jewish readers recognize in 1 John a familiar pattern of honest self-examination followed by divine mercy.

## Fellowship and Covenant Community

1 John 1:3–4 describes fellowship with God and with one another as the purpose of the letter. Jewish readers connect this to the covenantal relationship between God and Israel — a relationship that is both vertical (between God and the people) and horizontal (among the members of the community). The concept of communal fellowship rooted in shared faith echoes the Jewish understanding of *kehillah* (congregation).
