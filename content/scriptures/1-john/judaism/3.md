## Children of God in Jewish Thought

1 John 3:1 — "that we should be called children of God" — employs language familiar from the Hebrew Bible. Deuteronomy 14:1 declares, "You are the children of the Lord your God," and Hosea 1:10 promises that Israel will be called "children of the living God." Jewish readers note that the concept of divine sonship was applied collectively to Israel before it was individualized in Christian theology.

## Cain and Abel

1 John 3:12 cites Cain as the paradigm of hate: "We should not be like Cain, who was of the evil one and murdered his brother." Jewish interpretive tradition has developed extensive midrash on the Cain and Abel narrative. The rabbinic tradition explores Cain's motives, the nature of his sin, and the implications for human violence. Jewish readers recognize the shared use of Genesis narratives as moral exempla.

## Love of Neighbor in Practice

1 John 3:17–18 asks, "If anyone has the world's goods and sees his brother in need, yet closes his heart against him, how does God's love abide in him?" Jewish readers connect this to the prophetic demand for practical justice and the rabbinic obligation of *tzedakah* (charitable giving). The insistence that love must be expressed "in deed and in truth" aligns with the Jewish priority of action over intention.
