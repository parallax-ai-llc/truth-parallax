## Overview

Jewish scholars engage with 1 John primarily as evidence of intra-communal conflict in early Christianity and as a window into the development of Christian theology out of its Jewish matrix. The letter's language of light and darkness, truth and falsehood, and love and hate bears striking parallels to the Dead Sea Scrolls, suggesting shared roots in Jewish sectarian discourse. The letter's love command, while directed inward to the community, echoes the Torah's command to love one's neighbor.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Sectarian Parallels
The dualistic language of 1 John — "children of God" versus "children of the devil" (3:10), light versus darkness, truth versus falsehood — closely parallels the Community Rule of Qumran. Jewish scholars note that this kind of sectarian rhetoric, with its sharp in-group/out-group boundaries, was a feature of Second Temple Jewish discourse, not a uniquely Christian innovation.

### The Love Command
1 John's repeated command to love one another (3:11, 23; 4:7, 11) is directed toward fellow community members. Jewish readers note that the love command originates in Leviticus 19:18 — "You shall love your neighbor as yourself" — and that its application within the sectarian community parallels the Qumran community's restriction of love to the "sons of light." The question of whether Johannine love extends beyond the community boundary is a subject of ongoing scholarly discussion.

### Christological Development
Jewish scholars observe that 1 John's Christology — particularly its insistence on the incarnation and its identification of Jesus as "the eternal life that was with the Father" (1:2) — represents a significant development beyond the Jewish monotheistic framework. The letter's polemic against those who deny Christ's coming in the flesh illuminates the Christological debates that marked the parting of ways between Judaism and Christianity.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sectarian Rhetoric**: The letter's dualism as rooted in Second Temple Jewish discourse
- **The Love Command**: Continuity with Leviticus 19:18 and its sectarian application
- **Monotheism and Christology**: The tensions between Jewish monotheism and Johannine Christology
- **Community Boundaries**: The definition of insider and outsider in sectarian communities
