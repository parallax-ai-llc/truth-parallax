## The Love Command and Leviticus

1 John 2:7–11, presenting the love command as both "old" and "new," connects directly to Leviticus 19:18: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself." Jewish readers note that this "old commandment" is Torah, and that John's claim to present something "new" raises questions about continuity and innovation. The Jewish tradition has always taught that the love command is central — Hillel's summary of the Torah as "What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor" parallels John's emphasis.

## The Antichrist as Sectarian Rhetoric

1 John 2:18–23, with its language of "antichrists" who have gone out from the community, illuminates the sectarian dynamics of early Christianity. Jewish scholars compare this rhetoric to the Dead Sea Scrolls' characterization of opponents as agents of darkness and deceit. The denunciation of those who "went out from us" reflects the painful process of communal schism familiar from Jewish history.

## The World and Its Passing

1 John 2:15–17 warns that "the world is passing away." Jewish readers connect this to Ecclesiastes' meditation on the transience of earthly things: "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" (1:2). Both traditions counsel against placing ultimate value in the material and temporal, though they differ in their eschatological frameworks.
