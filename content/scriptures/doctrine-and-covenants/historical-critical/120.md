## Historical Context

Section 120, dated to approximately 1834-1838 and associated with Kirtland, Ohio, and Far West, Missouri, belongs to growing tensions within the church and with surrounding communities. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 120 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. Temple theology in the D&C developed progressively, as documented by scholars including David Buerger. The Kirtland temple served different functions than the later Nauvoo temple, and the revelations reflect this evolution.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The endowment concept evolved from a Pentecost-like spiritual outpouring (Kirtland period) to an elaborate ritual ceremony (Nauvoo period), a development documented by David Buerger and Devery Anderson.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

Sealing theology, connecting families across generations, developed in the Nauvoo period and is linked to the temple theology that Samuel Brown has analyzed in *In Heaven as It Is on Earth*.
