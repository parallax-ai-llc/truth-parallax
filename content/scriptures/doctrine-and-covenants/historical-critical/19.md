## Historical Context

Section 19, received in March 1830 and addressed to Martin Harris, contains some of the D&C's most distinctive theological content, including the reinterpretation of "eternal punishment" and a graphic description of the Atonement.

## Source Criticism

The section's claim that "eternal punishment" means "God's punishment" (because "Eternal" is God's name) represents a hermeneutical move without clear biblical precedent. Scholars have compared this exegetical strategy to Origen's universalist reinterpretation of hell and to the broader tradition of allegorical reading. The Atonement passage (vv. 15-19), describing Christ's suffering in first person, is unique in the D&C and has been compared to the Gethsemane tradition in Luke 22:44.

## Form Criticism

The revelation combines multiple forms: theological instruction, personal rebuke, financial directive (Harris is told to pay for printing the Book of Mormon), and eschatological warning. This composite form reflects the practical-theological fusion characteristic of Smith's revelatory style.

## Sitz im Leben

Martin Harris was being asked to mortgage his farm to finance the Book of Mormon's printing — a significant sacrifice that the revelation addresses through both spiritual promise and financial directive. The combination of lofty atonement theology with practical financial instruction is characteristic of the D&C's Sitz im Leben.
