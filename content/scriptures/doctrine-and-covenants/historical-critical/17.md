## Historical Context

Section 17, received in June 1829, formalized the promise that three witnesses would be shown the golden plates. Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris subsequently reported a visionary experience in which they saw the plates and heard God's voice.

## Source Criticism

The revelation's language frames the witness experience in visionary rather than empirical terms: the witnesses will see "by faith" and must "rely upon my word." This language has been analyzed by Brent Metcalfe and Dan Vogel as indicating a subjective spiritual experience rather than the physical examination the term "witness" might imply. Defenders argue that faith-mediated perception does not reduce the reality of the experience.

## Form Criticism

The form is a conditional divine promise (protasis-apodosis structure), echoing biblical covenant formulae. The conditions — faith, obedience, testimony — establish obligations that make the witness experience a covenant transaction rather than a neutral empirical observation.

## Historical Evidence

The Three Witnesses' testimony, published in every edition of the Book of Mormon, has been subjected to extensive historical scrutiny. Martin Harris's various statements about whether he saw the plates "with the spiritual eye" or physically have generated significant scholarly debate. The witnesses' complex later relationships with Smith and the church provide rich material for historical analysis.
