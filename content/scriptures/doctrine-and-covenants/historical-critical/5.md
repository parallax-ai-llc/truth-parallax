## Historical Context

Section 5 was received in March 1829, addressing Martin Harris's desire to see the golden plates. Historical-critical scholars examine this revelation within the context of evidentiary demands and witness testimony that surrounded the Book of Mormon's production.

## Source Criticism

The revelation's language about "three witnesses" who will testify to the plates draws on Deuteronomy 19:15 ("at the mouth of two or three witnesses"), a legal standard deeply embedded in both biblical and early American jurisprudence. Dan Vogel and Grant Palmer have analyzed how the witness paradigm served to preemptively address skepticism about the translation's authenticity.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The form of this revelation — conditional promise contingent on faithfulness — mirrors covenant formulae found throughout the Hebrew Bible. The Sitz im Leben involves Harris's continued vacillation between support and skepticism, documented in multiple contemporary accounts. Harris had already lost the 116 pages, and the revelation can be read as managing a difficult but necessary patron relationship.

## Redaction History

Comparison of the Book of Commandments text with the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals changes that strengthen the prophetic authority claims and clarify the conditions under which witnesses would be shown the plates. These revisions parallel the kind of editorial activity that redaction critics identify in the Deuteronomistic history.
