## Historical Context

Section 106, dated to approximately 1834-1838 and associated with Kirtland, Ohio, and Far West, Missouri, belongs to growing tensions within the church and with surrounding communities. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 106 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. Church organizational revelations document the progressive institutionalization that sociologists of religion, following Weber, identify as the routinization of charisma.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The specification of duties reflects the formalization of roles as the movement grew beyond face-to-face community, a process documented in the Joseph Smith Papers.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

The elaboration of ecclesiastical offices (elder, priest, teacher, deacon, bishop, etc.) developed over time. Prince's work traces how the office structure evolved from a simple two-office system to a complex hierarchy.
