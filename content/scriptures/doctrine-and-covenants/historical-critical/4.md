## Historical Context

Section 4, directed to Joseph Smith Sr. in February 1829, is among the shortest revelations in the collection. Historical-critical scholars examine its function as a call narrative, a genre well established in both biblical and early American religious literature.

## Form Criticism

The section follows the literary form of a prophetic commissioning. The phrase "a marvelous work is about to come forth" echoes Isaiah 29:14, establishing intertextual connections that Philip Barlow has analyzed in detail. The virtues listed (faith, hope, charity, love, with an eye single to the glory of God) draw directly from 1 Corinthians 13 and Moroni 7 in the Book of Mormon, suggesting a composite literary construction from existing scriptural sources.

## Sitz im Leben

The revelation addresses the elder Smith at a moment when the translation project was resuming after the lost manuscript crisis. The agricultural metaphor ("the field is white already to harvest") reflects the rural agrarian context of the Smith family in Harmony, Pennsylvania, while simultaneously echoing John 4:35.

## Compositional Analysis

This section's brevity and formulaic character have led scholars such as H. Michael Marquardt to examine whether it represents a direct revelatory dictation or a composed text drawing on standard biblical language. The near-identical language appearing in sections addressed to different individuals (cf. D&C 11, 12, 14) supports the latter interpretation.
