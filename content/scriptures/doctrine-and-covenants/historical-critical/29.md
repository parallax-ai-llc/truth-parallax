## Historical Context

Section 29, dated to approximately 1830-1831 and associated with Fayette, New York, and Kirtland, Ohio, belongs to the initial organization and expansion of the early church. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 29 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. The Second Coming language reflects the millenarian fervor documented by Grant Underwood as central to early Mormon self-understanding. Prophetic dating attempts in early revelations can be compared with similar phenomena in other millennial movements.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The gathering motif draws on Isaiah's prophecies of Israel's return (Isaiah 11:11-12) while reflecting practical community-building imperatives. Scholars note how theological language served organizational goals.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

Millennial theology in this section participates in the broader premillennialist tradition analyzed by Ernest Sandeen, while adding distinctive Mormon elements including the gathering to Zion.
