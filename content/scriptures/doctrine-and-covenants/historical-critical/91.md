## Historical Context

Section 91, dated to approximately 1832-1834 and associated with Kirtland, Ohio, belongs to the Kirtland temple-building period. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 91 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. The revelatory mode exhibited here — dictated divine speech in first person — has been analyzed by scholars including Kathleen Flake and Terryl Givens as a distinctive literary form that claims prophetic authority through formal features reminiscent of biblical oracle.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The obedience language reflects hierarchical ecclesiology that developed as the church institutionalized. Mark Ashurst-McGee has studied how revelatory authority was progressively centralized.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

The framing of revelations as 'commandments' parallels the Deuteronomic literary convention of divine instruction through a prophetic mediator.
