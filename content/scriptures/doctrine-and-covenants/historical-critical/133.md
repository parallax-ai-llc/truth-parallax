## Historical Context

Section 133, dated to approximately 1838-1843 and associated with Far West, Missouri, and Nauvoo, Illinois, belongs to the Nauvoo period of theological expansion. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 133 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. The gathering motif draws on Isaiah's prophecies of Israel's return (Isaiah 11:11-12) while reflecting practical community-building imperatives. Scholars note how theological language served organizational goals.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The Zion concept evolved from a literal geographical designation (Independence, Missouri) to a more diffuse theological ideal, a development traceable through the D&C's compositional history.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

New Jerusalem language draws on Revelation 21 and reflects the millenarian expectations widespread in Jacksonian America. The shift from a specific Missouri location to a more flexible concept is documented in the revelations' redaction history.
