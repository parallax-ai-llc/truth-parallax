## Historical Context

Section 7 purports to be a translation of a parchment written by the apostle John, received through the Urim and Thummim in April 1829. Historical-critical scholars note the unusual nature of this claim — a translation of a text that has never been independently verified to exist.

## Source Criticism

The content — that John the Beloved was granted power to remain on earth until Christ's Second Coming — draws on John 21:20-23, a passage that generated speculation in early Christianity. The Wandering Jew and similar legends of immortal witnesses represent an ANE and medieval literary tradition. Scholars including Brant Gardner have compared this section to the Book of Mormon's account of the Three Nephites (3 Nephi 28), noting the shared motif.

## Form Criticism

The form of a revealed translation of an ancient document parallels Joseph Smith's other translation projects (Book of Mormon, Book of Abraham, JST). Form-critical analysis identifies this as a distinctive Smithian genre — what Terryl Givens calls "translation as revelation" — in which the claim to translate an ancient text serves as the framework for new prophetic content.

## Historical Evidence

No independent evidence for the parchment's existence has been identified. The theological function of the text — establishing the presence of an immortal apostolic witness — serves to validate the Restoration's claims to continuity with primitive Christianity.
