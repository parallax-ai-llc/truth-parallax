## Historical Context

Section 63, dated to approximately 1831-1832 and associated with Kirtland, Ohio, and Independence, Missouri, belongs to the Missouri-Kirtland period of dual church headquarters. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 63 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. Mission directives reflect the practical needs of a rapidly expanding movement. Historical-critical scholars note how revelatory authority was deployed to motivate and direct missionary labor.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

Preaching instructions employ biblical commission language while reflecting the circuit-riding and camp meeting culture of early American evangelism.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

The harvest metaphor (from John 4:35 and Matthew 9:37-38) was a standard revivalist trope. Its deployment here situates the early Mormon movement within Second Great Awakening rhetorical conventions.
