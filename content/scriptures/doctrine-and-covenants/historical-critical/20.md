## Historical Context

Section 20, known as the "Articles and Covenants of the Church," was adopted at the church's organization on April 6, 1830, in Fayette, New York. It functions as the founding constitutional document of the Church of Christ (later renamed).

## Source Criticism

The text draws extensively on the Book of Mormon, the Bible (particularly the Pauline epistles and Acts), and Methodist organizational precedents. Scott Kenney and others have identified structural parallels with the Articles of Religion in the Methodist Discipline, suggesting that Smith adapted familiar ecclesiastical forms. The sacrament prayers (vv. 77-79) reproduce Book of Mormon language (Moroni 4-5) nearly verbatim.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The section functions as what form critics identify as a community rule, comparable to the Qumran Community Rule (1QS) or the Didache. It establishes baptismal formulae, sacramental liturgy, membership requirements, and governance structures. The Sitz im Leben is the transformation of a charismatic movement into an organized institution — the critical sociological transition that Weber termed the routinization of charisma.

## Redaction History

This section underwent more revision than nearly any other in the D&C. The addition of material about the Melchizedek and Aaronic priesthoods between the 1833 and 1835 editions reflects the development of priesthood theology that occurred during that period. These changes have been exhaustively documented by Woodford and analyzed by Prince.
