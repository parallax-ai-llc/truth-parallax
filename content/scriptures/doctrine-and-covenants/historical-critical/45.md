## Historical Context

Section 45, dated to approximately 1831-1832 and associated with Kirtland, Ohio, and Independence, Missouri, belongs to the Missouri-Kirtland period of dual church headquarters. Historical-critical scholars situate this revelation within the specific social, political, and religious circumstances of its production, examining both the immediate occasion for the revelation and the broader cultural forces shaping its language and concerns.

## Source Criticism and Compositional Analysis

The text of Section 45 shows dependence on biblical language and imagery, a characteristic feature identified by Philip Barlow in *Mormons and the Bible*. The Second Coming language reflects the millenarian fervor documented by Grant Underwood as central to early Mormon self-understanding. Prophetic dating attempts in early revelations can be compared with similar phenomena in other millennial movements.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The enumeration of 'signs of the times' follows a genre well established in Protestant apocalyptic literature. Comparative analysis reveals both conventional and distinctive elements.

## Redaction History

Comparison of manuscript sources (preserved in the Joseph Smith Papers) with the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants reveals editorial changes consistent with the evolving theological and institutional needs of the early church. Robin Scott Jensen and others have documented these textual variants, which provide evidence of the kind of redactional process familiar from biblical criticism.

## Theological Development

New Jerusalem language draws on Revelation 21 and reflects the millenarian expectations widespread in Jacksonian America. The shift from a specific Missouri location to a more flexible concept is documented in the revelations' redaction history.
