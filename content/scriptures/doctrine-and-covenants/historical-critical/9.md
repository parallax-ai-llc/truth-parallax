## Historical Context

Section 9 was received in April 1829 after Oliver Cowdery's failed attempt to translate the Book of Mormon plates. The section provides an explanation for his failure that has become theologically significant: one must "study it out in your mind" before seeking divine confirmation.

## Source Criticism

The epistemological formula — rational study followed by spiritual confirmation ("burning in the bosom") — has no direct biblical parallel and represents a distinctive contribution to revelatory epistemology. Scholars have noted its affinity with Wesleyan "heart religion" and the broader Romantic emphasis on feeling as a mode of knowing, situating it within the intellectual currents of early nineteenth-century America.

## Form Criticism and Sitz im Leben

The section functions as an apologetic explanation for Cowdery's failure while preserving the broader claim of translating through divine power. Form-critical analysis identifies this as a prophetic interpretation of failure — comparable to Deuteronomistic explanations for Israel's defeats — in which setback is attributed to human inadequacy rather than divine absence.

## Historical Evidence

Cowdery's attempt to translate is corroborated in his own later accounts and in Joseph Smith's history. The failure and its explanation became a formative episode for Mormon revelatory theology, influencing the epistemology of spiritual confirmation that remains central to the tradition.
