## Historical Context

Section 10 addresses the lost 116 manuscript pages, providing a theological explanation for why they would not be retranslated. Historical-critical scholars date this revelation's composition to the summer of 1828 or spring of 1829, with the dating itself a subject of scholarly debate.

## Source Criticism

The revelation claims that conspirators planned to alter the lost pages and then challenge Smith to reproduce them identically. This "prophetic foresight" has been analyzed by Fawn Brodie and Dan Vogel as a pragmatic solution to a practical problem, while Richard Bushman reads it as consistent with the text's own claims about divine foreknowledge. The literary solution — substituting the small plates of Nephi for the lost material — involves a remarkable narrative complexity that scholars have analyzed from multiple angles.

## Redaction History

The text underwent revision between the Book of Commandments and the Doctrine and Covenants. Changes include the addition of material about Satan's designs and clarifications of the relationship between the lost pages and the replacement text. These revisions suggest ongoing theological reflection on the episode.

## Sitz im Leben

The lost manuscript crisis threatened the credibility of the entire translation project. The revelation's strategy — explaining why retranslation was unnecessary while maintaining claims of divine guidance — represents what communication scholars would call a framing response to a credibility challenge.
