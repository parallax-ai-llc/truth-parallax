## Historical Context

Section 1 was received on November 1, 1831, at a conference of elders in Hiram, Ohio, where the compilation of revelations for publication as the Book of Commandments was being discussed. Historical-critical scholars such as Richard Bushman and Steven Harper have examined this section as a preface composed after many of the revelations it introduces, raising questions about retrospective framing.

## Source Criticism and Compositional History

The revelation's language draws heavily on biblical idiom, particularly Isaiah and Revelation. Textual analysis reveals significant revisions between the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants, suggesting editorial activity consistent with what Grant Underwood has called "ongoing revelation through revision." The apocalyptic framework echoes contemporaneous early American millenarian rhetoric, placing the text within a broader Sitz im Leben of Second Great Awakening eschatology.

## Redaction History

Comparison of the earliest manuscript (preserved in Revelation Book 1) with printed editions reveals substantive changes. The phrase "only true and living church" was refined across editions. Robin Scott Jensen's work with the Joseph Smith Papers Project has documented how scribal practices and editorial decisions shaped the canonical text, a process analogous to redaction criticism applied to biblical texts.

## Archaeological and Historical Evidence

The historical setting can be confirmed through contemporaneous diaries and letters. William McLellin's journal corroborates the November 1831 conference. The text's references to "wars and rumors of wars" reflect anxieties documented in the period's newspapers, suggesting a Sitz im Leben shaped by Jacksonian-era political and social upheaval.
