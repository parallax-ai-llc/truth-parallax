## Overview

The Book of Breathings (*Shai en Sensen* or *Shai en Seshen*) is an ancient Egyptian funerary text dating primarily from the Ptolemaic and Roman periods (roughly 4th century BCE to 2nd century CE), though it draws on much older traditions extending back to the Pyramid Texts and the Book of the Dead. The text was designed to ensure the deceased's continued existence in the afterlife by magically providing them with breath, sustenance, and the favor of the gods — particularly Osiris, lord of the dead, and Isis, his consort.

Two principal versions of the text are known: the First Book of Breathings (attributed to Isis, composed for the benefit of her brother Osiris) and the Second Book of Breathings (a shorter, later text). The First Book of Breathings is the more important and widely attested version. These texts were typically written on papyrus and placed with the mummy, often near the head or feet, serving as a compact, late-period equivalent of the much longer Book of the Dead.

The Book of Breathings is also notable for its role in the history of Egyptology: the Joseph Smith Papyri, which the founder of the Latter-day Saint movement claimed to translate as the Book of Abraham, were later identified by Egyptologists as copies of the Book of Breathings.

## Structure

|---------|---------|

| 3 | Offerings and Sustenance — provisions for the deceased's eternal nourishment |
| 4 | Vindication and Entry into the Afterlife — the deceased's acceptance among the blessed dead |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: Part of the living religious tradition of ancient Egypt, continuous over three millennia
- **Academic**: Important for understanding late Egyptian funerary religion, Demotic literature, and Ptolemaic theology
- **Comparative**: Parallels with other traditions' concepts of post-mortem breath, soul, and afterlife sustenance
- **Theological**: Expresses sophisticated theology of death, divine intercession, and eternal life
- **Archaeological**: Attested by numerous papyrus finds and tomb deposits throughout Egypt
- **Interfaith**: Connects to universal human concerns about death, breath, and the continuation of consciousness
