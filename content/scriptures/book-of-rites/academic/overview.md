## Overview

Academic scholarship on the Book of Rites applies historical-critical, philological, and archaeological methods to understand the text's composition, dating, and social context. Modern scholars have established that the Liji is a heterogeneous compilation, with individual chapters ranging in date from the early Warring States period (5th century BCE) to the early Han dynasty (2nd century BCE). The work attributed to Dai Sheng represents an editorial selection from an even larger collection, the Da Dai Liji, portions of which survive independently.

## Textual History and Composition

Critical scholarship has identified multiple literary strata within the Liji. Some chapters (such as Qu Li and Shao Yi) preserve genuinely archaic ritual prescriptions that may reflect Zhou-dynasty practice. Others (such as Li Yun and Yue Ji) are philosophical essays from the late Warring States period, representing the work of thinkers associated with the Confucian school but differing significantly in vocabulary and style. The Da Xue and Zhong Yong, elevated by Neo-Confucians, are now recognized as relatively late compositions within the collection.

## Archaeological Corroboration

Excavated texts from Guodian, Mawangdui, and other sites have provided important corroborating evidence for the dating and transmission of Liji materials. Bamboo-slip manuscripts have revealed alternative versions of passages found in the received text, demonstrating that the Liji circulated in multiple recensions before the Han-dynasty standardization. These discoveries have complicated the traditional attribution of the text while confirming the antiquity of many of its ritual descriptions.
