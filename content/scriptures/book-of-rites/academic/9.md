Li Yun has attracted significant academic attention as a text that reveals the political and philosophical debates of the late Warring States period. Scholars identify at least two distinct ideological layers: the utopian vision of Grand Unity (datong), which some scholars associate with Mohist or proto-Daoist political ideals, and the pragmatic defense of institutional ritual (xiaokang), which represents orthodox Confucian theory. The tension between these two visions may reflect not a single author's developmental narrative but the compilation of texts from different intellectual traditions.

Modern scholars have traced the chapter's influence on Chinese political thought from the Han dynasty through the twentieth century, noting that Kang Youwei's reform program and Sun Yat-sen's political theory both drew extensively on the datong ideal. The chapter's remarkable afterlife in modern Chinese political discourse — invoked by reformers, revolutionaries, and communists alike — demonstrates how a ritual text can be reinterpreted to serve radically different political programs across different historical periods.
