Academic scholars approach the Zhong Yong as a composite text likely composed in the fourth or third century BCE, significantly later than its traditional attribution to Confucius's grandson Zisi would suggest. Linguistic analysis reveals vocabulary and conceptual frameworks characteristic of late Warring States philosophical discourse, including the technical use of terms like cheng (sincerity) and zhongyong (centrality and commonality) that appear to reflect engagement with rival schools, particularly the Daoists and the cosmological thinkers.

The discovery of bamboo-slip texts at Guodian has provided new evidence for dating and understanding the Zhong Yong. Several Guodian texts share vocabulary and concepts with the received Zhong Yong, suggesting that the ideas expressed in this chapter circulated in multiple forms before being compiled into the Liji. Scholars continue to debate whether the Guodian materials represent sources for the Zhong Yong or parallel developments within the same intellectual milieu.
