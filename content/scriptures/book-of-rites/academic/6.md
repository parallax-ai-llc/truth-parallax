Academic scholarship has devoted extensive attention to the Yue Ling chapter, which many scholars identify as one of the earliest systematic Chinese texts correlating governmental action with cosmological cycles. The chapter's monthly prescriptions — linking specific colors, foods, sacrifices, and administrative tasks to each month — reflect the correlative cosmology that became dominant during the Warring States and Han periods. Scholars debate whether the Yue Ling preserves genuinely ancient practice or represents a later theoretical systematization projected onto an idealized past.

Comparative analysis with excavated almanac texts from Shuihudi and other sites has revealed both correspondences and discrepancies with the received Yue Ling, suggesting a complex transmission history. The chapter's cosmological framework, organizing the year according to the five phases (wuxing) and their associated qualities, represents a relatively late intellectual development, leading most scholars to date the chapter's composition to the late Warring States or early Han period, despite its attribution to ancient practice.
