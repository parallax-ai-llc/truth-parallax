A comparative reading of Chapter 48, Shen Yi (Deep Robe), places the Liji's treatment of ceremonial dress in dialogue with analogous practices and principles from other world traditions. The chapter's prescriptions reveal assumptions about human nature, social order, and moral formation that become visible only when set alongside alternatives. By comparing the Confucian approach with parallel systems in Hindu dharmashastra, Jewish halakha, Christian canon law, and Islamic fiqh, the comparative interpreter can identify both universal patterns in human ritual behavior and the distinctive features of the Chinese tradition.

The chapter's emphasis on robe symbolism finds resonances in multiple traditions, each of which recognizes that this dimension of human experience requires ceremonial regulation and moral guidance. The similarities suggest that certain human needs — for order, meaning, belonging, and dignity — generate ritual responses across cultures. The differences reveal how each tradition's particular theological and philosophical commitments shape the specific form these responses take. The comparative approach thus enriches understanding of both the Liji and the traditions with which it is placed in dialogue.
