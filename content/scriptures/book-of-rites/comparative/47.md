The comparative study of Chapter 47, San Nian Wen (Three-Year Mourning), reveals how the Liji's prescriptions regarding three-year mourning participate in broader patterns of human ritual behavior while maintaining distinctively Confucian characteristics. Cross-cultural comparison shows that many of the concerns addressed in this chapter — the regulation of filial devotion, the integration of individual conduct with social expectations, the use of ceremony to mark transitions and maintain order — appear across widely separated civilizations, suggesting deep structural commonalities in human social organization.

What distinguishes the Confucian approach from comparable systems is its characteristic combination of humanistic grounding and systematic comprehensiveness. The Liji does not appeal to divine revelation to justify its prescriptions but rather to the accumulated wisdom of sage-kings and the intrinsic requirements of harmonious human relationships. This humanistic orientation can be compared with the Aristotelian emphasis on practical wisdom (phronesis) in the Greek tradition and with the natural law tradition in Western thought, all of which ground ethical norms in human nature and rational reflection rather than in supernatural authority.
