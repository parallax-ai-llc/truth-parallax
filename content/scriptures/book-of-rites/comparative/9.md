Li Yun's vision of the age of Grand Unity (datong) provides rich material for comparative analysis with utopian traditions across cultures. The chapter's description of a world where "the great Way prevailed" and resources were shared by all resonates with Plato's Republic, Thomas More's Utopia, and the prophetic vision of the messianic age in Jewish tradition. Each tradition imagines an ideal social order that transcends the conflicts and inequalities of the present, though they differ dramatically in their understanding of how such an order might be achieved or recovered.

The distinction between datong (Grand Unity) and xiaokang (Lesser Prosperity) parallels similar two-stage narratives in other traditions: the Christian distinction between Eden and the fallen world, the Hindu contrast between Satya Yuga and Kali Yuga, and the Daoist vision of primordial simplicity versus civilized complexity. The comparative perspective reveals that the narrative of decline from an original harmony is a near-universal feature of human moral imagination, though the Confucian version is distinctive in presenting institutional ritual as a dignified and effective response to this decline rather than merely a symptom of it.
