Comparative analysis of Chapter 28, Zhong Yong (Doctrine of the Mean), illuminates how different civilizations have addressed the universal human concern with centrality. The Liji's approach, rooted in the Confucian conviction that ritual propriety (li) is the foundation of civilized life, offers a distinctively Chinese solution to challenges that every complex society must face. By examining how other traditions — from ancient Mediterranean cultures to South Asian religious systems — have addressed similar concerns, the comparative reader gains deeper insight into both the Confucian approach and its alternatives.

The chapter's treatment of harmony is particularly illuminating when compared with analogous systems in other religious and philosophical traditions. The Liji's emphasis on hierarchical differentiation, contextual appropriateness, and the integration of inner feeling with outer form can be contrasted with traditions that emphasize universal equality before God, fixed divine commandments, or the primacy of individual conscience. These comparisons reveal that the Confucian ritual system occupies a distinctive position in the global history of moral thought — one that takes the social dimension of human existence with a seriousness matched by few other traditions.
