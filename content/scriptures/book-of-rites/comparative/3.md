Tan Gong I's elaborate mourning prescriptions invite comparison with death rituals across world cultures and religions. The chapter's graduated mourning system — prescribing different periods and behaviors for different kinship relationships — parallels the Hindu system of ashaucha (ritual impurity following a death), the Jewish practice of shiva (seven days) and sheloshim (thirty days), and the Islamic iddah (waiting period). All these traditions share the insight that grief requires structured temporal expression and that social proximity to the deceased determines the intensity and duration of mourning obligations.

The comparative lens reveals a striking contrast between the Liji's approach and Christian mourning traditions. While the Liji emphasizes extended mourning as an expression of filial piety and social duty, Christian theology tends to view prolonged grief as potentially indicating insufficient faith in resurrection and eternal life. This divergence illuminates fundamentally different anthropologies: the Confucian focus on relational obligation versus the Christian emphasis on individual salvation and divine comfort. The comparison demonstrates how mourning practices encode a culture's deepest assumptions about death, kinship, and the good life.
