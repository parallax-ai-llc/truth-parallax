The Yue Ji's philosophy of music invites comparison with musical theories across world traditions. The chapter's claim that music arises from the human heart's response to external stimuli and that different types of music produce different moral effects parallels Plato's discussion of musical modes in the Republic, where certain modes are banned for their debilitating effects on character. Both the Confucian and Platonic traditions share the conviction that music is not mere entertainment but a powerful moral force that shapes the soul and, by extension, the state.

The comparative perspective extends to Indian musical theory, where the raga system is understood to embody specific emotional and spiritual qualities (rasas), and to the Islamic tradition's debate about the permissibility of music (sama), which revolves around similar concerns about music's power to arouse passions. The Yue Ji's integration of musical theory with cosmology — linking sound to the patterns of yin and yang — finds parallels in the Pythagorean concept of the music of the spheres and in Hindu traditions connecting sound (nada) to the creative principle of the universe.
