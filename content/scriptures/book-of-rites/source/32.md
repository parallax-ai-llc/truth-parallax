## Wen Sang (Questions about Mourning Rites)

After a man has heard of the death of his parent, he wails loudly and gives full expression to his grief. He asks the particulars and then wails again. He then sets out on his journey home, crying aloud all the way.

When one hears of the death of a friend, even though the deceased is in a different state, one should put on mourning garments and go to express condolences.

The rites say: On the day of burial, the host bows to his visitors outside the gate. After the burial, when receiving condolence visits, he positions himself inside the gate.

When condoling with a friend, the visitor weeps first; the mourner responds with tears. When condoling with one who is not a friend, the mourner weeps first; the visitor responds.

The practices of the ancient kings in regard to mourning were all designed to give the fullest expression to the natural feelings of the heart. Where the feelings are deep, the outward expression is extensive; where they are shallow, the expression is slight.

Thus the regulations about the mourning rites were established not to constrain but to guide -- that grief might find its proper expression, and life might be gradually resumed without dishonor to the dead.

Confucius said: "Among my disciples, none observes the rites of mourning better than Yan Yuan."
