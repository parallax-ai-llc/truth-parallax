## Tou Hu (The Game of Pitch-Pot)

The game of pitch-pot was played at feasts. The host took the arrows and, bowing to the guest, invited him to play. The guest declined at first, but upon a second invitation, accepted.

The rules of the game were: The pot was placed at a distance. Each player had four arrows. The one who got the most arrows into the pot was the winner. The loser had to drink a cup of wine as a forfeit.

A director was appointed to oversee the game. He ensured that the rules were followed, that the counting was fair, and that the forfeits were duly exacted.

During the game, music was played. The proceedings went in regular order, governed by rules of propriety even in amusement.

The host, in inviting the guest, said: "I have here the arrows and the pot. I beg that you will amuse yourself with a game." The guest replied: "I have already received the honor of your hospitality. How dare I presume to take part in your game?" The host answered: "The pot and arrows are set out. I beg you not to decline."

This form of courteous exchange typified the rules of propriety as applied even to leisure activities. The ancient rulers believed that amusements should be conducted with decorum, so that even in play, the bonds of propriety and mutual respect were maintained.

The game served to cultivate concentration, composure, and graceful deportment.
