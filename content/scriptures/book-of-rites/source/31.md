## Ben Sang (Questions about Mourning)

The rites of mourning require that when a parent has just died, one should weep without limit. After the dressing of the corpse, one weeps and breathes at intervals. After the coffining, one wails at appointed times.

Immediately after the death: The mourner cries aloud without attending to the modulation of his voice. He stamps with his feet, beats his breast, and leaps.

The mourning garments are donned within the period prescribed. The sash of mourning is worn on the left side. The staff is taken up after the coffining.

The ancient rules of mourning say: After three days, one takes food. After three months, one washes. After a year, one may change one's garments gradually.

The son of the deceased should not leave the side of the coffin day or night. He should weep till he has no tears, and then wail till he has no voice. He should eat nothing pleasant and wear nothing fine.

After the burial, the visits of condolence are returned. The mourner then resumes something of his ordinary life, though still within the bounds of grief.

The meaning of the mourning rites: They provide a graduated expression for the deepest human emotions. Without such rites, grief would know no bounds and the mourner would destroy himself. With them, grief is given its proper form and proper limit, and both the living and the dead are honored.
