## Qu Li II (Summary Rules of Propriety, Part 2)

According to the rules of propriety, a man of rank is called by his designation; an officer, by his name; and a common man, by his personal appellation.

When one is ten years old, we call him a boy; he goes out to school. When he is twenty, we call him a youth; he is capped. When thirty, he has a wife; he begins to discharge the duties of a man. At forty, he is in full vigor; he is employed in office. At fifty, his hair turns grey, and he can give directions about affairs.

In going to take counsel with an elder, one must carry a stool and a staff with him for the elder's use. When the elder asks a question, to reply without acknowledging one's incompetency and without declining to answer is contrary to propriety.

For all sons it is the rule: In winter, to warm the bed for their parents; in summer, to cool it for them; in the evening, to adjust everything for their repose; in the morning, to inquire about their health.

The son of a ruler is called "the son of the ruler's body." When the ruler dies, he is called "the orphan prince." In speaking of himself, a ruler calls himself "I, the small child."

He who is not yet capped does not presume to take a share in the mourning garments of a grown-up man.
