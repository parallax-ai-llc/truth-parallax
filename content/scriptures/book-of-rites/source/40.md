## Guan Yi (The Meaning of the Ceremony of Capping)

The ceremony of capping was the beginning of propriety. In the ancient rites, when a young man reached the age of twenty, he was capped. This was the ceremony by which he was recognized as an adult.

The capping took place in the ancestral temple to give it solemnity. The father presided, and a guest of distinction was invited to perform the capping.

Three caps were placed upon the young man's head in succession: the first was the cap of dark cloth; the second was the cap of white deerskin; the third was the cap with tassels. The three cappings indicated that he was successively qualified for the three duties of service to his state, attendance at the ancestral sacrifices, and military service.

After being capped, the young man was given a new name. This signified his transformation from boy to man, from dependent to responsible member of society.

He was then presented with a cup of sweet spirits and directed to present himself before his mother. Afterwards he went out and was presented to his elders and to the other officers.

The significance of the capping ceremony was: It taught the young man to take on responsibility. It announced to the community that he had come of age. It reminded him that henceforth his actions would carry weight in the world, and he must conduct himself accordingly.
