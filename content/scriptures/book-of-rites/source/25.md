## Ji Tong (A Summary Account of Sacrifices)

Of all the methods of good government, none is more urgent than ceremonies. Ceremonies are of five kinds, and none is more important than sacrifices.

Sacrifice is not something that comes from outside a man -- it issues from within, being born of the heart. When the heart is deeply stirred, expression is given to it in ceremonies. Therefore only men of talent and virtue can give complete exhibition of the idea of sacrifice.

The sacrifices of men of talents and virtue can be described: The preparation lasts several days. Vigilance and purification of the person are maintained. They arrange their temples and halls, set forth their stands and dishes, and present their offerings. They present their prayers, as if the spirits were about to enjoy what is prepared for them.

In sacrificing, a man makes the utmost of his ability, and expends the utmost of his wealth. He displays his loyalty and sincerity. He fully exhibits what ceremonies and music require.

He whose heart is not in his sacrifice, but who merely goes through the motions -- his sacrifice is without acceptance.

The fundamental principle of all sacrifice is this: Reciprocity. Heaven covers all things; Earth sustains all things. Man stands between Heaven and Earth and completes the triad. The sacrificer acknowledges this relationship and gives it visible form.
