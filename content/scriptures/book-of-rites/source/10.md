## Li Qi (Rites in the Formation of Character)

The parrot can speak, and yet is nothing more than a bird; the ape can speak, and yet is nothing more than a beast. Here now is a man who observes no rules of propriety; is not his mind that of a beast? But if men were as beasts, and without the rules of propriety, father and son might have the same mate.

Therefore, when the sages arose, they framed the rules of propriety in order to teach men, and cause them, by their possession of such rules, to make a distinction between themselves and brutes.

The rules as laid down by the ancient kings had their radical element and their outward and elegant form. A true heart and good faith are the radical element. The characteristics of each ceremony according to its meaning are its elegant form. Without the radical element, the elegant form could not be carried out; and without the elegant form, the radical element could not be manifested.

Rites rest on three bases: Heaven and Earth, which are the source of all being; the ancestors, who are the source of the human race; sovereigns and teachers, who are the source of government.

Therefore, to serve the ruler with loyalty, to serve one's father with filial piety, to serve one's elder brother with respect, and to treat the young with kindness -- these are the great articles of the rules of propriety.
