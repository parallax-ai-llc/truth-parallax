## Sang Da Ji (The Great Record of Mourning)

The usages connected with mourning originated in the deep affection of the human heart. The sages regulated those usages by their rules of propriety. Therefore the mourning rites are said to embody what is right.

When a parent is ill, the eldest son should not leave his side. He tastes all the medicines first. He prays to the spirits, saying: "If my parent must die, I beg to die in his stead."

After death, the mourner mounts to the housetop and calls out the name of the deceased, saying: "Return!" This is called the Summoning. One summons the soul because one cannot bear to think the person is truly departed.

Then the body is washed and dressed. The mouth is filled with rice and jade -- this is called the "filling." After this comes the placing in the coffin.

The wailing of the mourner knows no fixed times. Day and night, whenever grief comes, one gives expression to it. When the loss is first known, one is dazed and bewildered, as if the loved one might return. After the dressing of the body, one is fearful and anxious. After the coffining, one begins to feel the permanence of the loss.

These rites allow people to express their grief in measured stages, so that the living are not overwhelmed, and the dead are honored with dignity.
