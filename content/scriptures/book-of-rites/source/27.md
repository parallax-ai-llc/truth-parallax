## Fang Ji (The Dykes)

Confucius said: "The rules of propriety serve as dykes for the people against evil excesses."

Therefore, the sagely man made rules of propriety to teach men, causing them to have a self-restraint, and by their means raising them above the level of the brute creation.

Confucius said: "The small man, when poor, feels the pinch; when rich, becomes proud. Under the pinch of want, he may proceed to steal; being proud, he may proceed to deeds of disorder. The rules of propriety recognize these human feelings and lay down definite rules, to serve as dykes for the people."

Therefore, the sages made the people rich before they demanded duty from them, and honored them before they exacted service from them. This is the great principle of the rules of propriety.

Confucius said: "Under the sky there have been many rulers; but three only among them all were free from anxiety -- they were King Wen, King Wu, and the Duke of Zhou. King Wen had King Ji as his father and King Wu as his son. His father originated the undertaking and his son completed it."

Confucius said: "A man in a high position who is not proud, and one in a low position who is not insubordinate -- these are the men who keep the world from disorder. If all in high positions were proud, and all in low positions insubordinate, the world would not endure for a single day."
