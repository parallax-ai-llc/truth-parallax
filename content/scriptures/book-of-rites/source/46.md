## Sang Fu Si Zhi (The Four Principles of Mourning Dress)

The four principles governing mourning dress are: closeness of kin, honor of rank, name of relationship, and considerations arising from the circumstances of life and death.

Closeness of kin: The nearer the kinship, the heavier the mourning. For parents, the heaviest mourning is worn. For siblings, somewhat less. For cousins and more distant relations, the mourning decreases in proportion.

Honor of rank: When a father holds high office, the mourning practices of the family are adjusted accordingly. The official duties of state cannot be wholly set aside during mourning.

Name of relationship: The specific relationship determines the specific form of mourning. The mourning for a father differs from that for a mother; for an elder brother from that for a younger; for a husband from that for a wife.

Circumstances: If the deceased died in battle or in the service of the state, special honors attend the mourning. If the death occurred under circumstances requiring particular forms of address, these are observed.

These four principles intersect and must be weighed against one another. The sages who established the mourning rites understood that no single principle could govern all cases. Therefore they provided guidance under each of the four heads, so that the living could determine the proper expression of their grief in every circumstance.

The mourning rites thus became a comprehensive system of moral education: teaching kinship, duty, hierarchy, and the proper response to every condition of life and death.
