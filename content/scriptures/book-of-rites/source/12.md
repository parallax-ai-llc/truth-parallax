## Nei Ze (The Pattern of the Family)

The son, from the time he could use his limbs, was taught to use his right hand. At six years old he was taught numbers and the names of the cardinal points. At seven, boys and girls did not occupy the same mat nor eat together. At eight, he was taught when entering or leaving a gate to go after his elders; when beginning to eat, to wait for them to begin first -- the beginning of instruction in yielding to others.

At nine, he was taught how to number the days. At ten, he went to a master outside his home and stayed with him even over the night. He learned writing, arithmetic, and the rules of propriety at this stage.

At thirteen, he learned music, and to repeat the odes, and to dance. At fifteen, he practiced archery and chariot-driving. At twenty, he was capped, and received instruction in the complete observances of propriety.

Sons and daughters-in-law, while their parents and parents-in-law are alive, should not presume to consider their wealth as their own, nor to lay aside anything out of their income.

For the nourishing of the old: There should be rice water, drink, congee, and thick gruel. They should be provided with dates, chestnuts, sugar, and honey to sweeten their dishes; with vegetables suited to the season to harmonize them; with the produce of the waters; and with grain for the solid portion.
