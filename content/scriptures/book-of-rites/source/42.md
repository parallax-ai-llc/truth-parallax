## Xiang Yin Jiu Yi (The Meaning of the Drinking Ceremony in the Districts)

The drinking ceremony in the districts was designed by the ancient rulers to promote proper social relations. It was through this ceremony that the principle of yielding and deference was made manifest.

The host personally went to invite the guest, and the guest came to pay his respects. At the entrance of the gate, the host and guest yielded precedence to each other three times before entering.

They ascended the steps, and at the top they yielded to each other three more times. The host then washed the goblet and offered it to the guest. They pledged each other and drank in turn.

In the ceremony, the host bowed and escorted the guest, yielding to him at every turn. This elaborate courtesy was not mere empty form -- it embodied the principle that social harmony depends upon mutual respect and the willingness to defer to others.

After the formal drinking, the older men sat and the younger stood. The servitors moved about performing their duties. In all of this, the distinctions of age and rank were observed.

The conclusion of the feast came when the rules of ceremony had been completely observed. Then there was singing and music.

The purpose of the ceremony was: that the principle of yielding might be taught; that respect for age might be cultivated; that the people might learn to live in harmony with one another. When this ceremony was well performed, the customs of the district were transformed.
