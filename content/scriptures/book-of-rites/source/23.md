## Ji Fa (The Law of Sacrifices)

The law of sacrifices is this: That of the line of those who sacrificed to Heaven and Earth, the sacrifice should continue to be made by the eldest son generation after generation, and never be discontinued.

Sacrifices are directed to three kinds of being: to the spirits of heaven; to the spirits of the earth; and to the spirits of deceased men.

What the ancient kings valued in sacrificial rites was not the food and drink offered, but the sincerity behind the offering. They looked at the spirit of the sacrifice, and whether the meaning was complete.

A man of perfect virtue may sacrifice to God; a man of merit may sacrifice to the spirits. Sacrifice is what a man does to fulfill his duty -- not what he does to seek happiness.

The Son of Heaven sacrifices to all the spirits under heaven. The feudal princes sacrifice to all within their territories. They sacrifice to what they have received benefit from.

Therefore it is said: "Where there has been no beneficent act, no sacrifice should be offered." Those who labor for the state may receive sacrifices. Those who have died in its defense may receive sacrifices. Those who have warded off great calamities may receive sacrifices. Those who have staved off great afflictions may receive sacrifices.

As examples: The ancient lord Nong taught the people how to farm -- so he receives sacrifice. Hou Ji brought grain to maturity -- so he receives sacrifice.
