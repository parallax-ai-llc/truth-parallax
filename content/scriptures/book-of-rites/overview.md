## Overview

The Book of Rites (Liji, 禮記) is one of the Five Classics of Confucianism, a vast compendium of texts describing the social norms, governmental organization, and ceremonial rites of the Zhou dynasty. Compiled and edited during the Han dynasty by scholars including Dai Sheng (the "Elder Dai") and Dai De, the collection draws on materials dating from the Warring States through the early Han periods. It became one of the most influential texts in East Asian civilization, shaping understandings of propriety (li), education, mourning, sacrifice, and governance for over two millennia.

## Structure

The forty-nine chapters encompass a remarkable range of subjects:

1. **Ritual Propriety** (Chapters 1–2, 17): Rules governing daily conduct, social interactions, and proper behavior
2. **Mourning Rites** (Chapters 3–4, 21–22, 41–42): Elaborate regulations for funeral and mourning practices
3. **Royal Governance** (Chapters 5–6, 16): Regulations for rulers, monthly ordinances, and state administration
4. **Education and Learning** (Chapters 7–8, 18): Theories of pedagogy and moral formation
5. **Philosophical Treatises** (Chapters 9, 26, 30–34): Foundational essays including the Zhong Yong and Da Xue
6. **Ritual Ceremonies** (Chapters 35–40): Detailed descriptions of capping, marriage, archery, and banquet rites
7. **Sacrifice** (Chapters 44–46): The law, meaning, and unity of sacrificial practice

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Book of Rites has been received and interpreted across multiple traditions:

- **Confucianism**: The primary canonical source for ritual theory and the philosophical articulation of li (propriety)
- **Neo-Confucianism**: Source of the Da Xue and Zhong Yong, elevated to the Four Books by Zhu Xi
- **Daoism**: Engaged as both a counterpoint to naturalistic ethics and a shared source for understanding cosmic order
- **Chinese Buddhism**: Read in relation to Buddhist vinaya and monastic discipline
- **Academic**: Subject of extensive textual criticism, archaeological comparison, and historical analysis

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Li (Ritual Propriety) | The system of rites, norms, and ceremonies that orders human relationships |
| Social Hierarchy | The proper differentiation of roles between ruler and minister, parent and child, elder and younger |
| Education | The formation of moral character through learning, ritual practice, and self-cultivation |
| Mourning | Elaborate codes governing grief, funeral rites, and the expression of filial devotion |
| Sacrifice | The rituals connecting the living with ancestors and cosmic forces |
| Governance | The ruler's responsibility to embody virtue and maintain ritual order in the state |
