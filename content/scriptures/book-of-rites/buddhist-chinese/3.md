Chinese Buddhist readers encounter in Tan Gong I the mourning rites that generated the most sustained criticism of Buddhist monasticism in Chinese history. The chapter's vivid portrayals of filial grief and its elaborate prescriptions for mourning conduct challenged Buddhism's teaching that attachment — including attachment to family — is a source of suffering. Buddhist apologists responded by developing the concept of merit transfer, arguing that a monk's spiritual practice benefits deceased parents more effectively than conventional mourning observance.

The chapter's emphasis on the proper expression of grief offers an instructive contrast to the Buddhist approach to death. Where the Liji prescribes graduated periods of mourning calibrated to kinship distance, Buddhism teaches equanimity in the face of impermanence and compassion for all beings without discrimination. Chinese Buddhist thinkers navigated this tension by acknowledging the provisional validity of mourning rites for laypeople while maintaining that monastic practice represents a higher form of devotion that ultimately encompasses and transcends filial obligations.
