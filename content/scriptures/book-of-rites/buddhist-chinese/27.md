Chinese Buddhist interpreters encounter in Chapter 27, Fang Ji (Record of Dykes), material that both challenges and enriches their own tradition's approach to moral restraint. The chapter's prescriptions invite comparison with the Buddhist vinaya (monastic code), which similarly regulates conduct through detailed behavioral norms. Both traditions share the insight that disciplined external conduct shapes internal character, but they differ fundamentally in their goals: the Liji aims at social harmony and the fulfillment of relational obligations, while the vinaya aims at liberation from the cycle of rebirth.

The chapter's treatment of preventive ethics provided Chinese Buddhist thinkers with an opportunity to demonstrate Buddhism's compatibility with Chinese cultural values. By drawing parallels between Confucian ritual propriety and Buddhist monastic discipline, apologists argued that Buddhism did not reject Chinese civilization but offered a complementary path that addressed dimensions of human experience — suffering, impermanence, liberation — that the Liji's ritual framework left unaddressed. This strategy of accommodation shaped the distinctive character of Chinese Buddhism.
