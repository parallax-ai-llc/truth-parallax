Chinese Buddhist readers approach Chapter 7, Zeng Zi Asks (Zengzi Wen), with an awareness of the long history of negotiation between Buddhist and Confucian values in China. The chapter's focus on filial piety represents the kind of worldly concern that Buddhist doctrine identifies as rooted in attachment and conventional truth. Yet Chinese Buddhism's distinctive genius lay in its ability to engage such concerns without dismissing them, finding provisional value in Confucian social norms while maintaining the ultimate truth of emptiness and non-attachment.

The chapter's prescriptions regarding mourning offer a case study in how Chinese Buddhists practiced cultural translation. Monastic communities adopted many elements of Confucian etiquette, adapting them to Buddhist contexts. The Confucian emphasis on hierarchical respect, for instance, was incorporated into Chinese monastic culture in ways that differed from Indian Buddhist practice, reflecting the deep influence of the Liji's vision of social order on all Chinese religious communities, including Buddhist ones.
