From a Chinese Buddhist perspective, Chapter 44, Jing Jie II (Classic Explanation II), presents a comprehensive vision of social order that Buddhism must engage rather than ignore. The chapter's treatment of textual interpretation reflects values deeply embedded in Chinese culture — values that Buddhist missionaries had to address if their teaching was to take root in Chinese soil. The most successful strategy was neither wholesale adoption nor outright rejection but creative reinterpretation.

Chinese Buddhist thinkers found in the chapter's emphasis on canonical meaning resonances with Buddhist concepts of skillful means (upaya) and compassionate engagement with the world. The Liji's insistence on adapting behavior to context and relationship parallels the Buddhist teaching that the dharma must be presented differently to different audiences. This structural similarity allowed Chinese Buddhists to affirm the Liji's social wisdom while reframing it within a Buddhist soteriological context that ultimately points beyond the social order to the liberation of all sentient beings.
