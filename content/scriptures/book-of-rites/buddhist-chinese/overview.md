## Overview

Chinese Buddhism's encounter with the Book of Rites represents one of the most consequential cultural negotiations in religious history. When Buddhism entered China, the Liji's vision of a ritually ordered society centered on filial piety, ancestor worship, and family continuity posed a direct challenge to Buddhist monasticism, which required leaving the family and renouncing worldly ties. Chinese Buddhists developed sophisticated strategies for engaging the Liji's ritual framework, alternately accommodating, reinterpreting, and selectively challenging its prescriptions.

## The Filial Piety Controversy

The Book of Rites' emphasis on filial devotion — mourning rites, ancestral sacrifice, and the duty to produce descendants — generated the most sustained criticism of Buddhism in Chinese history. Buddhist apologists responded by arguing that monastics practiced a higher form of filial piety through merit transfer, benefiting not just living parents but ancestors across countless lifetimes. Texts like the Ullambana Sutra were promoted to demonstrate Buddhism's compatibility with Chinese family values.

## Ritual Comparison

Chinese Buddhist scholars developed productive comparisons between the Liji's ritual system and the Buddhist vinaya (monastic code). Both traditions share an understanding that external conduct shapes internal character, that ceremonial precision matters, and that different social relationships require different behavioral norms. The parallels between Confucian li and Buddhist vinaya facilitated the cultural translation of Buddhism into Chinese terms, even as fundamental differences in cosmology and soteriology remained.
