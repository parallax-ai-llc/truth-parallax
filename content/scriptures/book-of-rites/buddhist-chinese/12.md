The Nei Ze chapter, with its detailed prescriptions for domestic life, food preparation, and service to parents, confronts Chinese Buddhism with the full force of the Confucian family ethic. The chapter describes a household centered on filial service — children rising early to attend to their parents' needs, preparing specific foods according to seasonal requirements, and subordinating their own desires to parental authority. This vision of domestic devotion stands in apparent tension with Buddhism's call to leave household life and pursue liberation from worldly attachments.

Chinese Buddhist interpreters responded by distinguishing between different levels of filial practice. The physical service described in the Nei Ze is honored as appropriate for the lay life, but Buddhists argued that providing spiritual benefit to one's parents through meditation, chanting, and merit transfer constitutes an even higher form of filial devotion. The Nei Ze's prescriptions thus became an occasion for Buddhist thinkers to articulate a comprehensive theory of filial piety that encompasses both Confucian and Buddhist practice.
