Neo-Confucian interpreters read Li Yun through the lens of li (principle, 理) and qi (material force), finding in its account of ritual evolution a cosmological narrative about the relationship between heavenly principle and human history. The chapter's famous description of the age of Grand Unity (datong) is understood not as mere nostalgia but as a portrait of a society perfectly aligned with the moral structure of the universe — a condition that remains permanently available through the recovery of principle.

Zhu Xi and his followers read the transition from datong to xiaokang as illustrating how the obscuration of principle by selfish desire necessitates the institution of ritual. The sage-kings created rites not to constrain human freedom but to provide a structured path back to the original harmony. Neo-Confucian readers find in Li Yun the metaphysical foundation for their program of self-cultivation: by investigating things and extending knowledge, the individual can recover the principle that the sage-kings embodied naturally.
