Neo-Confucian readers regard the Da Xue as the gateway to all learning, the text that establishes the proper sequence and method of moral cultivation. Zhu Xi's reorganization of the Da Xue into a "classic" portion attributed to Confucius and a "commentary" portion attributed to Zengzi became the standard interpretation, and his supplementary chapter on the "investigation of things" (gewu) generated the most consequential philosophical debate in Neo-Confucian history.

The Da Xue's famous "eight items" — investigating things, extending knowledge, making intentions sincere, rectifying the mind, cultivating the person, regulating the family, ordering the state, and bringing peace to the world — provided Neo-Confucianism with its comprehensive program linking individual self-cultivation to political transformation. Zhu Xi read this sequence as implying that moral knowledge must precede moral action, establishing a pedagogical method centered on study and reflection. Wang Yangming's challenge — that knowledge and action are one — reinterpreted the same text to opposite conclusions, demonstrating the Da Xue's inexhaustible philosophical richness.
