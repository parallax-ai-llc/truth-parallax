Neo-Confucian scholars read Chapter 38, Sang Da Ji (Great Mourning Record), as part of the comprehensive ritual system that embodies heavenly principle in human institutions. The chapter's attention to major mourning reflects the Neo-Confucian understanding that principle is not abstract but always manifests in concrete situations and relationships. The Song-dynasty revival of Confucianism emphasized recovering the original meaning of ritual texts like this one, reading them not through the accumulated glosses of Han and Tang commentators but through direct engagement with the moral principles they express.

The chapter's discussion of death rites provided Neo-Confucian thinkers with material for their ongoing debate about the relationship between knowledge and action. Zhu Xi would have read these prescriptions as occasions for the investigation of things — each regulation offering an opportunity to discern the principle at work. Wang Yangming, by contrast, would have insisted that the moral knowledge required to perform these rites correctly is already present in the learner's innate moral consciousness (liangzhi) and need only be extended through sincere practice.
