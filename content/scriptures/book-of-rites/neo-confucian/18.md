The Xue Ji holds a place of special honor in Neo-Confucian pedagogy, providing the classical foundation for the educational theories that Zhu Xi systematized in his commentaries and institutional reforms. Neo-Confucian readers find in this chapter the principles that guided the establishment of academies (shuyuan) throughout the Song, Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties. The text's emphasis on self-directed learning, the gradual progression from fundamentals to advanced understanding, and the teacher's role as moral exemplar all became central tenets of Neo-Confucian education.

The chapter's observation that "teaching is half of learning" was particularly valued by Neo-Confucians who understood moral cultivation as a communal enterprise. Zhu Xi's own pedagogical method, combining close textual study with moral reflection and ritual practice, directly echoes the Xue Ji's integration of intellectual and ethical formation. The chapter's critique of rote memorization without genuine understanding anticipates the Neo-Confucian insistence on "real knowledge" (zhen zhi) as opposed to mere bookish learning.
