## Overview

Neo-Confucian interpreters transformed the reception of the Book of Rites by extracting two of its chapters — the Da Xue (Great Learning, Chapter 34) and the Zhong Yong (Doctrine of the Mean, Chapter 31) — and elevating them to the status of independent classics within the Four Books. Zhu Xi's reordering of the Confucian curriculum placed these texts at the foundation of all learning, making the Liji the source of what became the most influential educational program in Chinese history from the Song dynasty through the end of the imperial examination system.

## Metaphysical Reading of Ritual

Where earlier Confucian interpreters emphasized the social and ethical dimensions of ritual, Neo-Confucians read the Liji's ritual prescriptions as expressions of li (principle, 理) — the cosmic rational order that structures all things. Each ritual act corresponds to a principle inherent in the nature of things, so that performing rites correctly means aligning oneself with the moral structure of the universe. The Li Yun chapter's vision of the "Great Unity" (datong) was read as describing a society perfectly ordered according to heavenly principle.

## Self-Cultivation Framework

Neo-Confucian scholars mined the Book of Rites for its pedagogical and self-cultivation resources. The Da Xue's program of "investigating things" and "extending knowledge" became the basis for competing Neo-Confucian methodologies, with Zhu Xi emphasizing external study and Wang Yangming insisting on innate moral knowledge. The Zhong Yong's concept of "sincerity" (cheng) was elevated to a metaphysical principle linking human moral effort to the creative activity of Heaven itself.
