Neo-Confucian interpreters approach Chapter 27, Fang Ji (Record of Dykes), through the framework of li (principle) and qi (material force) that structures their reading of all classical texts. The chapter's treatment of moral restraint is understood not merely as practical instruction but as an expression of the heavenly principle inherent in human relationships. Each ritual prescription corresponds to a specific principle that can be discovered through careful investigation — the method of "gewu" (investigating things) that Zhu Xi placed at the foundation of Neo-Confucian learning.

For Neo-Confucian readers, the chapter demonstrates that preventive ethics is grounded in the moral structure of the universe itself. The rites described here are not human inventions but discoveries — recognitions of the way relationships ought to be ordered according to the principle endowed by Heaven. When individuals perform these rites with genuine understanding and sincerity, they recover their original nature and align themselves with the transforming power of Heaven and Earth. The chapter thus serves the Neo-Confucian program of self-cultivation by providing concrete occasions for the investigation of principle.
