The Zhong Yong is arguably the single most important text in the Neo-Confucian philosophical canon. Zhu Xi's decision to extract this chapter from the Book of Rites and elevate it to one of the Four Books transformed the trajectory of Chinese intellectual history. Neo-Confucian interpreters read the Zhong Yong as the foundational statement of moral metaphysics, linking human ethical effort to the creative activity of Heaven through the concept of sincerity (cheng).

The text's opening declaration — "What Heaven confers is called the nature; following the nature is called the Way; cultivating the Way is called instruction" — became the cornerstone of Neo-Confucian anthropology. Zhu Xi's commentary establishes that human nature is originally good because it is endowed by Heaven with principle (li), and that moral cultivation is the process of recovering this original nature from the distortions introduced by material force (qi) and selfish desire. The Zhong Yong's vision of the sage who achieves perfect sincerity and thereby participates in the transforming and nourishing power of Heaven and Earth represents the ultimate goal of Neo-Confucian self-cultivation.
