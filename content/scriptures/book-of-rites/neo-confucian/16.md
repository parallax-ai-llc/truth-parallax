The Neo-Confucian reading of Chapter 16, Da Zhuan (Great Treatise), integrates the chapter's ritual prescriptions into the metaphysical framework developed by Zhu Xi, the Cheng brothers, and other Song-dynasty masters. Where earlier Confucian interpreters focused on the practical and social dimensions of cosmology, Neo-Confucians discern in these prescriptions the operation of heavenly principle (tianli) working through the medium of human relationships and ceremonial practice.

This chapter's treatment of grand principles illustrates the Neo-Confucian conviction that every particular thing and event contains principle that can be apprehended through disciplined inquiry. The ritual regulations are not arbitrary but reflect the inherent moral order of the cosmos. By studying these prescriptions and understanding the principles they embody, the learner progresses along the path from "broad learning" to "precise inquiry" to "careful reflection" that the Neo-Confucian tradition prescribes as the method of moral and intellectual cultivation.
