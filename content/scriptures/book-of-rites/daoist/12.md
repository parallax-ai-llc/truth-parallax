Daoist readers approach Chapter 12, Nei Ze (Inner Principles), with the critical eye that the Tao Te Ching and Zhuangzi train upon all systems of codified behavior. The chapter's detailed prescriptions regarding domestic rites exemplify what Daoism identifies as the fundamental error of Confucian civilization: the attempt to replace spontaneous virtue with prescribed conduct. From the Daoist perspective, every additional regulation represents a further departure from the natural simplicity (pu) that characterizes the authentic human condition.

Yet even in their critique, Daoist interpreters recognize certain insights within this chapter that align with their own tradition. The emphasis on family conduct implicitly acknowledges the importance of attunement — of responding appropriately to the situation at hand rather than acting from rigid principle. To the extent that the chapter's prescriptions point toward a deeper sensitivity to context and relationship, they approach the Daoist ideal of wu wei, even if they ultimately fall short by codifying what should remain fluid and spontaneous.
