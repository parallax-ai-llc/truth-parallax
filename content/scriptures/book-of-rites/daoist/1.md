From a Daoist perspective, Chapter 1, Qu Li I (Summary of Rites), illustrates the paradox at the heart of Confucian ritualism: the more precisely human behavior is regulated, the less authentic it becomes. The chapter's treatment of ritual propriety reveals a tradition attempting to manufacture through rules what can only arise naturally — genuine feeling, sincere respect, and spontaneous appropriateness. The Zhuangzi's observation that "ceremonies and regulations are the chaff and bran of the ancients" finds ample illustration in this chapter's meticulous prescriptions.

Nonetheless, Daoist readers interested in the history of Chinese thought find value in studying how the Liji approaches daily conduct. The chapter reveals the underlying assumptions of the Confucian worldview — its faith in education, its trust in hierarchical order, its conviction that human nature requires shaping. By understanding these assumptions, Daoist interpreters can more precisely articulate their own alternative vision: a world in which human goodness emerges not from careful training but from the removal of the artificial constraints that prevent natural virtue from manifesting.
