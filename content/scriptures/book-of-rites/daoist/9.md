Li Yun is the chapter that most engages Daoist readers, for its opening description of the age of Grand Unity (datong) reads like a Daoist paradise. In that primordial era, "the great Way prevailed, the world was shared by all," and people acted from natural goodness rather than imposed regulation. Daoist interpreters recognize in this passage a Confucian acknowledgment of the very condition that Daoism seeks to recover — a world before artifice, before the proliferation of rules and ceremonies that the Liji itself represents.

Yet the chapter's narrative of decline provides fuel for the Daoist critique. The emergence of ritual institutions is presented as a response to the loss of natural virtue — precisely the argument found in the Tao Te Ching and Zhuangzi. Daoist readers note with appreciation that even the Confucian tradition admits that its ritual system is a second-best solution, a necessary accommodation to human imperfection rather than an expression of the highest Way. The chapter thus becomes a site of unexpected agreement between Confucian and Daoist interpretations of human history.
