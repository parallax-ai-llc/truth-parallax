## Overview

The Daoist engagement with the Book of Rites is shaped by the classical Daoist critique of Confucian ritualism. The Tao Te Ching's declaration that "when the great Tao is abandoned, benevolence and righteousness appear" and the Zhuangzi's satirical portrayals of rigid ceremonialism establish the framework through which Daoist readers approach the Liji. Yet the relationship is more complex than simple rejection: Daoists recognize in certain chapters of the Book of Rites — particularly Li Yun — a vision of primordial harmony that resonates with Daoist ideals of naturalness and the uncarved block.

## Critique of Artificial Order

Daoist interpreters view the Liji's elaborate prescriptions for dress, speech, mourning, and governance as symptomatic of a fallen condition. The very need for codified ritual demonstrates that human beings have lost their original connection to the Tao. From a Daoist perspective, the Liji's meticulous regulations represent an attempt to impose artificial order where spontaneous harmony once prevailed. The more detailed the rules become, the further society has strayed from authentic virtue.

## Points of Convergence

Despite this critique, Daoist readers find unexpected resonances. The Li Yun chapter's description of the "age of Grand Unity" (datong), when the world was shared by all and no private property existed, reads remarkably like a Daoist golden age. The Yue Ji's discussion of music's power to harmonize human emotions with cosmic rhythms echoes Daoist cosmology. Several chapters acknowledge that rites must be performed with inner sincerity — a principle Daoists affirm even as they question the external structures.
