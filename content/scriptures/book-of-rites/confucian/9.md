Li Yun is one of the most philosophically significant chapters in the Book of Rites, presenting a sweeping account of the evolution of ritual from the primordial age of Grand Unity (datong) through the subsequent era of Lesser Prosperity (xiaokang). Confucian readers find here the most ambitious theoretical statement of why ritual exists and what function it serves in human civilization. The chapter's famous opening passage, describing a world in which "the great Way prevailed" and all things were shared, has inspired Confucian social idealism for centuries.

The Confucian interpretation emphasizes that the decline from datong to xiaokang was not a catastrophic fall but a necessary adaptation. As human nature grew more complex and self-interested, the sage-kings instituted ritual as a means of channeling natural desires into socially constructive forms. Ritual does not suppress human nature but refines it, creating the conditions for cooperation, trust, and mutual respect. Li Yun thus provides the Confucian tradition with its most comprehensive justification for the ritual order.
