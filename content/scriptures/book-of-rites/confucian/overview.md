## Overview

The Confucian tradition regards the Book of Rites as one of its most essential canonical texts, second only to the Analerta in practical influence on daily life and social organization. For Confucian scholars, the Liji provides the theoretical foundation for li (ritual propriety) — the comprehensive system of norms, ceremonies, and behavioral codes that transforms raw human impulses into refined moral conduct. The text articulates the Confucian conviction that human beings become truly human not through instinct but through the civilizing power of ritual.

## Centrality of Li

Confucian interpreters read the Book of Rites as demonstrating that ritual is not mere formalism but the embodiment of inner moral sentiment. The rites of mourning express genuine grief; the rites of sacrifice manifest authentic reverence; the rites of governance enact the ruler's sincere care for the people. When Confucius said "Restrain yourself and return to ritual," he pointed to the Liji's vision of a world where every human relationship is ordered by appropriate ceremony and mutual respect.

## Moral Formation

The text's chapters on education — particularly Xue Ji (Chapter 18) — are foundational to Confucian pedagogy. Learning is understood not as the acquisition of information but as the transformation of character through the study of rites, music, and the classics. The teacher-student relationship itself is governed by ritual propriety, creating a chain of moral transmission stretching from the ancient sages to the present.
