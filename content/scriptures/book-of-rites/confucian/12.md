Confucian readers approach Chapter 12, Nei Ze (Inner Principles), as an integral part of the ritual system that orders human relationships and social institutions. The chapter's prescriptions reflect the Confucian conviction that every domain of human activity — from the most solemn ceremony to the most routine daily interaction — requires the guidance of ritual propriety (li). The detailed instructions found here are not arbitrary rules but embodiments of moral principles refined by the accumulated wisdom of the sage-kings.

For the Confucian tradition, the material in this chapter demonstrates that domestic rites is inseparable from moral cultivation. The careful attention to proper form in matters of family conduct reflects the understanding that external conduct shapes internal character. A person who habitually acts with propriety develops genuine respect, sincerity, and humaneness — the core Confucian virtues. The chapter thus serves both as practical guidance and as moral philosophy expressed in concrete terms.
