The Royal Regulations chapter presents the Confucian vision of ideal governance, describing how the sage-kings organized the state through ritual institutions. Confucian interpreters read this chapter as a blueprint for legitimate political authority, grounded not in raw power but in the ruler's embodiment of moral virtue and ritual propriety. The regulations for land distribution, taxation, education, and the administration of justice reflect the Confucian conviction that governance is fundamentally an ethical enterprise.

The chapter's description of the enfeoffment system and the gradations of noble rank illustrates the Confucian principle of differentiated social order. Unlike egalitarian political theories, Confucian governance assumes that social hierarchy is natural and necessary — but insists that it must be morally justified. Each rank carries corresponding duties, and the ruler's authority derives from his fulfillment of ritual obligations to Heaven, to the ancestors, and to the people. Wang Zhi thus presents hierarchy as a system of mutual obligation rather than mere domination.
