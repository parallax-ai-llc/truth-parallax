Tan Gong I is one of the most emotionally powerful chapters in the Book of Rites, presenting a collection of mourning narratives and ritual prescriptions that illustrate the Confucian approach to death and grief. Confucian readers find in this chapter the deepest expression of filial piety — the devoted child's response to the unbearable reality of parental death. The stories of exemplary mourners serve as models of how grief should be expressed, contained, and gradually transformed through the structured observances of the mourning code.

The Confucian reading emphasizes that mourning rites are not impositions on natural grief but rather its necessary form. Without ritual structure, grief would either consume the mourner or dissipate prematurely. The graduated mourning periods and prescribed behaviors channel raw emotion into culturally meaningful expression, allowing the bereaved to honor the dead while slowly returning to the duties of the living. This chapter demonstrates the Confucian conviction that ritual is most essential precisely at those moments when human emotion is most intense.
