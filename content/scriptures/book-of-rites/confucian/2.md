Qu Li II continues the systematic presentation of ritual conduct that began in the first chapter, extending its scope to cover additional social situations and ceremonial contexts. Confucian interpreters read this chapter as a practical manual for navigating the complex demands of a ritually ordered society. The prescriptions address conduct during visits, gift-giving, dining with others, and the proper forms of address for different ranks and relationships.

For the Confucian tradition, these rules are not arbitrary conventions but expressions of ren (humaneness) made concrete. Each prescription encodes a recognition of the other person's dignity and social position. The elaborate protocols for gift exchange, for instance, reflect the Confucian understanding that generosity must be tempered by propriety — that giving without regard for appropriate form can shame the recipient rather than honor them. The chapter demonstrates that true courtesy requires both genuine feeling and correct form.
