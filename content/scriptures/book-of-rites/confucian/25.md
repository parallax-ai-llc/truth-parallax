The Confucian tradition reads Chapter 25, Zhong Ni Yan Ju (Confucius at Home), as essential instruction in the proper ordering of human affairs. This chapter addresses Confucius with the systematic thoroughness characteristic of the Liji's approach to ritual regulation. Every prescription serves a dual function: it provides practical guidance for specific situations while simultaneously teaching the broader principles of social harmony and moral refinement.

Confucian interpreters find in this chapter a demonstration of the principle that ritual is the foundation of civilized life. The regulations concerning domestic teaching embody the Confucian understanding that human beings require structured norms to channel their natural impulses into socially constructive expression. Without such guidance, even well-intentioned people would stumble into impropriety, causing disruption to the social order and damage to their own moral development. The chapter's meticulous attention to detail reflects the Confucian commitment to getting things right in practice, not merely in theory.
