## Overview

The *Genesis Apocryphon* (1QapGen, also designated 1Q20) is one of the seven original scrolls discovered in Cave 1 at Qumran in 1947. Written in Aramaic — the vernacular language of Second Temple Jews — it is an expanded retelling of narratives from the Book of Genesis, featuring first-person accounts by the patriarchs Lamech, Noah, and Abraham. The scroll is notable for its literary sophistication, its creative expansion of biblical narrative, and its window into how Second Temple Jews interpreted and reimagined their scriptural heritage.

The scroll was one of the most poorly preserved of the original seven manuscripts, and significant portions remain fragmentary. What survives reveals a work of considerable literary ambition that stands between the biblical text and the fully developed midrashic traditions of later rabbinic literature.

## Structure

The seven reconstructed sections are organized around three patriarchal narratives:

1. **Section 1**: Lamech's account — the birth of Noah and fears about angelic parentage
2. **Section 2**: Lamech's consultation with Methuselah and Enoch
3. **Section 3**: Noah's account — the flood and the division of the earth
4. **Section 4**: The covenant with Noah and the genealogy of nations
5. **Section 5**: Abraham's journey to Canaan and sojourn in Egypt
6. **Section 6**: Sarah's beauty and the encounter with Pharaoh
7. **Section 7**: Abraham's vision and the covenant of the land

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Essene**: Genesis Apocryphon as a document of the community's self-understanding and eschatological hope
- **Judaism**: Witness to the diversity and creativity of Second Temple Jewish thought and practice
- **Protestantism**: Illuminates the Jewish background of the New Testament and early Christian theology
- **Catholicism**: Part of the salvation-historical development that prepared the way for Christ
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Anticipation of the liturgical and mystical realities fulfilled in the Church
- **Academic**: Primary source for understanding Second Temple Jewish religion, literature, and social organization
