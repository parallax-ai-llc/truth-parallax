The sixteenth and final chant brings the genealogical record to its culmination in the person of the ali'i for whom the Kumulipo was composed — traditionally identified as Lono-i-ka-makahiki or Ka-I-i-mamao. This chant completes the cosmic genealogy, demonstrating that the living chief stands at the end of an unbroken chain of descent that begins with the first stirring of life in the primordial darkness. The chief's authority is thus rooted in the very structure of creation itself.

Comparative analysis of Ka-I-i-mamao and the Living Chiefs reveals both parallels with and distinctive departures from similar themes in other world traditions. The motifs and structures found in this section connect to widespread patterns in human religious and mythological expression while retaining features that are specific to this particular cultural and spiritual context.

Cross-cultural comparison illuminates the universal dimensions of the human religious imagination while also highlighting the unique contributions of this tradition to the global tapestry of spiritual thought. However, comparative analysis must be conducted with care to avoid reducing distinctive traditions to mere instances of universal patterns, thereby obscuring what is most valuable and specific in each tradition.
