The second chant continues the pattern of paired births, describing the emergence of various species of fish and their terrestrial plant counterparts. The seaweed is paired with land vegetation, establishing the botanical world alongside the marine world. This chant reflects the Hawaiian observation of ecological relationships and the understanding that land and sea are interdependent realms of a single living system.

Academic analysis of The Birth of Fish and Land Plants draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
