The tenth chant introduces Wakea (Sky Father) and Papa (Earth Mother), whose union produces the Hawaiian Islands and the major lineages of Hawaiian chiefs. The story of Wakea and Papa is one of the most important narratives in Hawaiian tradition, explaining the origin of the kapu (taboo) system, the separation of men and women during eating, and the birth of the taro plant (Haloa) as the elder sibling of humanity. This chant bridges cosmogony and political genealogy.

Academic analysis of The Genealogy of Wakea and Papa draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
