The third chant describes the birth of flying creatures — birds, insects, and bats — paired with their marine counterparts. The expansion into the aerial realm represents a new dimension of creation, as life moves beyond the sea and land into the sky. The careful cataloging of species reflects the Hawaiian tradition of precise natural observation and ecological knowledge.

Academic analysis of The Birth of Flying Creatures draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
