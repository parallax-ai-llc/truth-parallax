The seventh chant describes the birth of the dog ('ilio) and various creatures of the night. The dog holds important cultural significance in Hawaiian tradition, and its appearance near the transition point of the Kumulipo marks the approaching boundary between the era of darkness (Po) and the era of light (Ao). This chant represents the culmination of animal creation in the realm of darkness.

Academic analysis of The Birth of the Dog and Night Creatures draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
