The fifth chant describes the birth of the pig (pua'a) and other larger animals, continuing the progression from simpler to more complex forms of life. The pig holds special significance in Hawaiian culture as a kinolau (body form) of the god Kamapua'a and as an essential element of religious offerings and feasting. This chant marks the transition toward more complex life forms while still within the era of darkness.

Academic analysis of The Birth of Pigs and Other Animals draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
