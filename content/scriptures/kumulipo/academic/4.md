The fourth chant catalogs the birth of creeping and crawling creatures — caterpillars, rats, and various insects — paired with marine counterparts. These creatures of the underbrush and darkness reflect the continuing era of Po and the gradual filling of ecological niches. The chant demonstrates the comprehensiveness of the Hawaiian creation vision, which includes all forms of life, not just those considered noble or beautiful.

Academic analysis of The Birth of Creeping Things draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
