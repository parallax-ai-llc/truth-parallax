The ninth chant, now in the era of Ao (light), introduces La'ila'i, the first woman, and her consorts Ki'i (the first man) and Kane (the god). This triangular relationship between the human woman, the human man, and the divine establishes the pattern of ali'i (chiefly) genealogy that structures the remainder of the Kumulipo. La'ila'i's primacy in the narrative reflects the importance of women in Hawaiian genealogical and political traditions.

Academic analysis of La'ila'i and the First Humans draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
