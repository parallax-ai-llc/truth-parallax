The sixteenth and final chant brings the genealogical record to its culmination in the person of the ali'i for whom the Kumulipo was composed — traditionally identified as Lono-i-ka-makahiki or Ka-I-i-mamao. This chant completes the cosmic genealogy, demonstrating that the living chief stands at the end of an unbroken chain of descent that begins with the first stirring of life in the primordial darkness. The chief's authority is thus rooted in the very structure of creation itself.

Academic analysis of Ka-I-i-mamao and the Living Chiefs draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
