The first chant of the Kumulipo opens in the era of Po (darkness, night, the primordial realm) and describes the birth of the coral polyp (ko'a), the first living creature, and its terrestrial counterpart, the starfish. This pairing of marine and land organisms establishes the fundamental structural principle of the Kumulipo — each sea creature has a corresponding land creature, reflecting the Hawaiian understanding of the interconnectedness of all life and the mirroring relationship between ocean and land ecosystems.

Academic analysis of The Birth of the Coral Polyp and Sea Creatures draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
