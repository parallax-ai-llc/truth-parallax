The tenth chant introduces Wakea (Sky Father) and Papa (Earth Mother), whose union produces the Hawaiian Islands and the major lineages of Hawaiian chiefs. The story of Wakea and Papa is one of the most important narratives in Hawaiian tradition, explaining the origin of the kapu (taboo) system, the separation of men and women during eating, and the birth of the taro plant (Haloa) as the elder sibling of humanity. This chant bridges cosmogony and political genealogy.

Interfaith engagement with The Genealogy of Wakea and Papa offers opportunities for cross-traditional dialogue on shared human concerns expressed through this tradition's distinctive spiritual vocabulary. The themes addressed in this section — creation, transformation, moral order, the relationship between humans and the natural world — are concerns that resonate across the world's spiritual traditions while being expressed here in uniquely powerful ways.

Respectful interfaith dialogue with this material requires acknowledging both the common ground it shares with other traditions and its irreducible distinctiveness. When approached with humility and genuine openness, this section offers resources for enriching one's own spiritual understanding while deepening appreciation for the breadth and depth of human spiritual experience.
