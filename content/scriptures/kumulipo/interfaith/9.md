The ninth chant, now in the era of Ao (light), introduces La'ila'i, the first woman, and her consorts Ki'i (the first man) and Kane (the god). This triangular relationship between the human woman, the human man, and the divine establishes the pattern of ali'i (chiefly) genealogy that structures the remainder of the Kumulipo. La'ila'i's primacy in the narrative reflects the importance of women in Hawaiian genealogical and political traditions.

Interfaith engagement with La'ila'i and the First Humans offers opportunities for cross-traditional dialogue on shared human concerns expressed through this tradition's distinctive spiritual vocabulary. The themes addressed in this section — creation, transformation, moral order, the relationship between humans and the natural world — are concerns that resonate across the world's spiritual traditions while being expressed here in uniquely powerful ways.

Respectful interfaith dialogue with this material requires acknowledging both the common ground it shares with other traditions and its irreducible distinctiveness. When approached with humility and genuine openness, this section offers resources for enriching one's own spiritual understanding while deepening appreciation for the breadth and depth of human spiritual experience.
