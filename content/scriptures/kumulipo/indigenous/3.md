The third chant describes the birth of flying creatures — birds, insects, and bats — paired with their marine counterparts. The expansion into the aerial realm represents a new dimension of creation, as life moves beyond the sea and land into the sky. The careful cataloging of species reflects the Hawaiian tradition of precise natural observation and ecological knowledge.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Birth of Flying Creatures carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
