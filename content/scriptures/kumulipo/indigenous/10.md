The tenth chant introduces Wakea (Sky Father) and Papa (Earth Mother), whose union produces the Hawaiian Islands and the major lineages of Hawaiian chiefs. The story of Wakea and Papa is one of the most important narratives in Hawaiian tradition, explaining the origin of the kapu (taboo) system, the separation of men and women during eating, and the birth of the taro plant (Haloa) as the elder sibling of humanity. This chant bridges cosmogony and political genealogy.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Genealogy of Wakea and Papa carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
