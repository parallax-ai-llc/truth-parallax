The fourth chant catalogs the birth of creeping and crawling creatures — caterpillars, rats, and various insects — paired with marine counterparts. These creatures of the underbrush and darkness reflect the continuing era of Po and the gradual filling of ecological niches. The chant demonstrates the comprehensiveness of the Hawaiian creation vision, which includes all forms of life, not just those considered noble or beautiful.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Birth of Creeping Things carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
