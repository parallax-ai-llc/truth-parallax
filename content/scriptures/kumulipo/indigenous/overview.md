## Overview

The Kumulipo holds profound significance for Native Hawaiian cultural identity, political sovereignty, and spiritual practice. As a royal genealogical chant, it establishes the genealogical connections that define Hawaiian social and political relationships — and by extension, the relationship of the Hawaiian people to their islands, their gods, and the entire natural world. Queen Lili'uokalani's translation of the Kumulipo during her imprisonment after the 1893 overthrow was itself an act of cultural sovereignty, asserting the depth and sophistication of Hawaiian intellectual tradition.

Indigenous engagement with the Kumulipo emphasizes its continuing relevance for Hawaiian cultural revitalization, language preservation, and sovereignty movements. The chant's comprehensive ecological knowledge informs contemporary Hawaiian environmental stewardship, while its genealogical framework continues to structure Hawaiian social and spiritual relationships.
