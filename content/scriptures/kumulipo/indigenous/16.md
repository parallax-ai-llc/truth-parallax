The sixteenth and final chant brings the genealogical record to its culmination in the person of the ali'i for whom the Kumulipo was composed — traditionally identified as Lono-i-ka-makahiki or Ka-I-i-mamao. This chant completes the cosmic genealogy, demonstrating that the living chief stands at the end of an unbroken chain of descent that begins with the first stirring of life in the primordial darkness. The chief's authority is thus rooted in the very structure of creation itself.

From an Indigenous perspective, Ka-I-i-mamao and the Living Chiefs carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
