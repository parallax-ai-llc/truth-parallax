The tenth chant introduces Wakea (Sky Father) and Papa (Earth Mother), whose union produces the Hawaiian Islands and the major lineages of Hawaiian chiefs. The story of Wakea and Papa is one of the most important narratives in Hawaiian tradition, explaining the origin of the kapu (taboo) system, the separation of men and women during eating, and the birth of the taro plant (Haloa) as the elder sibling of humanity. This chant bridges cosmogony and political genealogy.

Anthropological study of The Genealogy of Wakea and Papa examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
