The second chant continues the pattern of paired births, describing the emergence of various species of fish and their terrestrial plant counterparts. The seaweed is paired with land vegetation, establishing the botanical world alongside the marine world. This chant reflects the Hawaiian observation of ecological relationships and the understanding that land and sea are interdependent realms of a single living system.

Anthropological study of The Birth of Fish and Land Plants examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
