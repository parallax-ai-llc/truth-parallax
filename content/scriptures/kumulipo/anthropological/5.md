The fifth chant describes the birth of the pig (pua'a) and other larger animals, continuing the progression from simpler to more complex forms of life. The pig holds special significance in Hawaiian culture as a kinolau (body form) of the god Kamapua'a and as an essential element of religious offerings and feasting. This chant marks the transition toward more complex life forms while still within the era of darkness.

Anthropological study of The Birth of Pigs and Other Animals examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
