The first chant of the Kumulipo opens in the era of Po (darkness, night, the primordial realm) and describes the birth of the coral polyp (ko'a), the first living creature, and its terrestrial counterpart, the starfish. This pairing of marine and land organisms establishes the fundamental structural principle of the Kumulipo — each sea creature has a corresponding land creature, reflecting the Hawaiian understanding of the interconnectedness of all life and the mirroring relationship between ocean and land ecosystems.

Anthropological study of The Birth of the Coral Polyp and Sea Creatures examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
