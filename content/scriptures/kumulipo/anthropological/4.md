The fourth chant catalogs the birth of creeping and crawling creatures — caterpillars, rats, and various insects — paired with marine counterparts. These creatures of the underbrush and darkness reflect the continuing era of Po and the gradual filling of ecological niches. The chant demonstrates the comprehensiveness of the Hawaiian creation vision, which includes all forms of life, not just those considered noble or beautiful.

Anthropological study of The Birth of Creeping Things examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
