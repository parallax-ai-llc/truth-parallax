The eleventh chant begins the detailed genealogical record of the ali'i (chiefly) lineages, tracing the descent from the cosmic and divine ancestors through the generations of human rulers. Each name in the genealogy carries significance, often encoding historical events, migrations, and alliances. This section reflects the Hawaiian understanding that political authority derives from genealogical connection to the gods and the creative forces of the cosmos.

Anthropological study of The Lineage of Chiefs (Part 1) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
