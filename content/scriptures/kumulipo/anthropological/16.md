The sixteenth and final chant brings the genealogical record to its culmination in the person of the ali'i for whom the Kumulipo was composed — traditionally identified as Lono-i-ka-makahiki or Ka-I-i-mamao. This chant completes the cosmic genealogy, demonstrating that the living chief stands at the end of an unbroken chain of descent that begins with the first stirring of life in the primordial darkness. The chief's authority is thus rooted in the very structure of creation itself.

Anthropological study of Ka-I-i-mamao and the Living Chiefs examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
