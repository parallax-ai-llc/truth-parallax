The third chant describes the birth of flying creatures — birds, insects, and bats — paired with their marine counterparts. The expansion into the aerial realm represents a new dimension of creation, as life moves beyond the sea and land into the sky. The careful cataloging of species reflects the Hawaiian tradition of precise natural observation and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological study of The Birth of Flying Creatures examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
