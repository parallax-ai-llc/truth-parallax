The fourteenth chant continues the genealogical sequence, moving closer to the historical period. The names become more recognizable as historical figures, and the genealogical record increasingly intersects with documented Hawaiian history. This chant demonstrates the Kumulipo's function as a living document that connects cosmogony to contemporary political reality.

Anthropological study of The Lineage of Chiefs (Part 4) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
