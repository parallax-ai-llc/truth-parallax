The twelfth chant continues the chiefly genealogies, extending through further generations and incorporating references to historical events, voyaging traditions, and the settlement of the Hawaiian archipelago. The genealogical record serves simultaneously as history, political charter, and spiritual connection, linking the ruling chiefs of each generation to the primordial creative forces described in the opening chants.

Anthropological study of The Lineage of Chiefs (Part 2) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
