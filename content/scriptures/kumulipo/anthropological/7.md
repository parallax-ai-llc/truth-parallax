The seventh chant describes the birth of the dog ('ilio) and various creatures of the night. The dog holds important cultural significance in Hawaiian tradition, and its appearance near the transition point of the Kumulipo marks the approaching boundary between the era of darkness (Po) and the era of light (Ao). This chant represents the culmination of animal creation in the realm of darkness.

Anthropological study of The Birth of the Dog and Night Creatures examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
