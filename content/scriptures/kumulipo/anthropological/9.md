The ninth chant, now in the era of Ao (light), introduces La'ila'i, the first woman, and her consorts Ki'i (the first man) and Kane (the god). This triangular relationship between the human woman, the human man, and the divine establishes the pattern of ali'i (chiefly) genealogy that structures the remainder of the Kumulipo. La'ila'i's primacy in the narrative reflects the importance of women in Hawaiian genealogical and political traditions.

Anthropological study of La'ila'i and the First Humans examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
