The thirteenth chant extends the genealogical record further, documenting the expansion and consolidation of chiefly power across the Hawaiian Islands. The names and relationships encoded in this chant reflect specific historical periods and the complex political dynamics of Hawaiian society. The genealogy serves as a legitimating narrative, demonstrating the unbroken connection between the present rulers and the cosmic origins of creation.

Anthropological study of The Lineage of Chiefs (Part 3) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
