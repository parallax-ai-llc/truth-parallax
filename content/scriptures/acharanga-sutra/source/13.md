---
title: "Acharanga Sutra - Chapter 13"
scripture: "acharanga-sutra"
chapter: 13
---

**1** Mahavira said: The doctrine of non-absolutism, Anekantavada, is the key to understanding reality.

**2** Every object, every truth, every statement has many aspects. To see only one aspect and to claim it as the whole truth is the error of one-sided vision.

**3** The blind men who touched different parts of the elephant — one the trunk, one the leg, one the tail, one the ear — each described the elephant differently, yet each was partially right.

**4** So it is with all truths. The soul exists, and yet it is different from what the Vedantins say. Matter exists, and yet it is different from what the materialists say. God exists, and yet He is different from what the theists say.

**5** The Jain method of describing reality is the doctrine of Syadvada, or conditional predication. Every statement is prefaced with "Syat" — "In some respect" or "From one perspective."

**6** In some respect, a thing exists. In some respect, it does not exist. In some respect, it both exists and does not exist. In some respect, it is indescribable.

**7** This sevenfold predication — the Saptabhangi — is the complete method of describing any reality. It avoids the error of absolutism and embraces the complexity of truth.

**8** The practice of non-absolutism leads to tolerance, for it recognizes that others may see aspects of truth that we have missed.

**9** It leads to non-violence, for violence often arises from the conviction that our view alone is right and that those who disagree are wrong and must be opposed.

**10** He who practises non-absolutism in his thinking becomes open-minded, tolerant, and peaceful. He respects all viewpoints and seeks to understand rather than to condemn.
