## Overview

The Acharanga Sutra (Ācārāṅga Sūtra, "Treatise on Conduct") is the first and oldest text of the Jain Shvetambara canon (Agamas), traditionally regarded as the direct teaching of Mahavira as transmitted through his chief disciple Sudharman. Composed in Ardhamagadhi Prakrit, the text is divided into two books: the first (Shrutaskandha), which is considered the most ancient surviving Jain scripture, and the second, a later compilation of monastic regulations.

The Acharanga Sutra holds a position of supreme importance in Jain literature as the primary source for Mahavira's teaching on ahimsa (non-violence) and the conduct required of Jain monks and nuns. Its first book presents a passionate and uncompromising vision of non-injury to all living beings, extending ethical consideration to earth, water, fire, air, and plant bodies — a radical expansion of moral concern that remains distinctive in world religious literature.

## Structure

The text is organized into sixteen lectures (shrutaskandhas) across two books:

1. **Lectures 1–9 (Book I)**: The ancient core — Mahavira's teachings on non-violence, renunciation, and the nature of suffering
2. **Lectures 10–16 (Book II)**: Later monastic regulations — detailed rules for walking, speaking, begging, receiving, and ascetic discipline

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Shvetambara**: The first Anga of the canonical scriptures, regarded as Mahavira's direct teaching
- **Digambara**: Respected as preserving ancient teachings, though not accepted as canonical scripture
- **Academic**: The oldest datable Jain text, critical for reconstructing early Jain history
- **Comparative**: A foundational text for understanding radical non-violence in world religions
- **Interfaith**: Increasingly studied for its relevance to animal rights, environmental ethics, and peace studies

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Ahimsa | Non-violence extended to all forms of life, including elemental bodies |
| Asceticism | The rigorous physical and mental discipline required for spiritual progress |
| Renunciation | Complete abandonment of worldly attachments and possessions |
| Mahavira's Example | The biographical account of Mahavira's supreme austerities |
| Monastic Conduct | Detailed regulations governing every aspect of mendicant life |
| Karmic Awareness | Constant mindfulness of the karmic consequences of every action |
