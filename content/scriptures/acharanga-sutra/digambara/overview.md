## Overview

The Digambara tradition regards the Acharanga Sutra with deep respect as preserving ancient teachings of Lord Mahavira, while maintaining that the original canonical texts (purvas) were lost during a great famine approximately 160 years after Mahavira's nirvana. The Digambara position holds that the Shvetambara Agamas, including the Acharanga, represent a later reconstruction rather than the verbatim words of the Tirthankara, though they acknowledge that the text contains authentic doctrinal content.

Digambara interpreters read the Acharanga Sutra's teachings on ascetic conduct through the lens of their own more rigorous understanding of monastic discipline, finding in the text support for complete renunciation — including the abandonment of clothing — as the only path to genuine liberation.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Digambara readers emphasize the Acharanga's most austere passages, reading them as evidence that Mahavira's original teaching demanded total renunciation without compromise. The text's vivid descriptions of Mahavira's extreme austerities are taken as the definitive model for monastic life, supporting the Digambara position that authentic asceticism admits no concessions to bodily comfort.
