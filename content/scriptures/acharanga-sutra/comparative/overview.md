## Overview

Comparative religious studies examines the Acharanga Sutra alongside other traditions' foundational texts on ethical conduct, ascetic discipline, and non-violence. The text's radical extension of ahimsa to all forms of life — including earth, water, fire, and air bodies — invites comparison with ethical systems in Buddhism, Hinduism, Christianity, and modern animal rights philosophy.

The Acharanga's monastic regulations provide rich material for comparison with the Buddhist Vinaya, the Rule of Saint Benedict, and other traditions' codifications of renunciant life. Its biographical sections on Mahavira's austerities can be fruitfully compared with Christian hagiography, Buddhist Jataka tales, and Hindu ascetic narratives.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Comparative scholars identify both structural parallels and fundamental differences between the Acharanga's approach to ethics, asceticism, and spiritual liberation and those of other traditions. The Jain emphasis on the materiality of karma and the efficacy of physical austerity distinguishes the Acharanga's soteriology from approaches that emphasize grace, faith, or intellectual understanding.
