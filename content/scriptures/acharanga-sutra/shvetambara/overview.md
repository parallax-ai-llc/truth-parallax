## Overview

The Shvetambara tradition venerates the Acharanga Sutra as the first and most authoritative of the eleven Anga texts that constitute the core of the Jain canonical scriptures. The text is regarded as the direct teaching of Lord Mahavira, transmitted through an unbroken oral lineage from his chief disciple Sudharman Swami, and eventually committed to writing at the Council of Valabhi in approximately 454 CE.

For Shvetambara interpreters, the Acharanga Sutra is both a doctrinal treatise on the nature of non-violence and a practical manual for monastic life. Its first book (Shrutaskandha) is treasured as the oldest surviving expression of Mahavira's radical vision of ahimsa, while its second book provides the foundational rules governing the daily life of monks and nuns.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Shvetambara readers approach the Acharanga Sutra as authoritative scripture whose teachings must be understood in relation to the larger canonical corpus. The text's monastic regulations are interpreted within the Shvetambara framework, which permits monks to wear white garments and use certain minimal possessions — a reading that Shvetambara scholars ground in the broader canonical tradition.
