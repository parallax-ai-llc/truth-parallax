## Overview

Catholic interpretation of James has long celebrated the letter as a counterbalance to any misreading of Paul that would separate faith from moral action. The Council of Trent cited James 2:24 explicitly in its decree on justification, affirming that faith must be active in love and good works. James also provides the primary scriptural foundation for the Sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick (James 5:14–15).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Tridentine Reading
The Council of Trent drew on James 2 to reject the Protestant doctrine of justification by faith alone. Catholic theology insists that James and Paul are fully compatible: Paul teaches that initial justification comes through faith by grace, while James teaches that this faith must grow through works of charity, or it is dead.

### Sacramental Foundation
James 5:14–15, which instructs the sick to call the elders of the church for anointing with oil and prayer, is the principal scriptural basis for the Catholic sacrament of Anointing of the Sick. The passage has been interpreted sacramentally since at least the fifth century.

### Social Teaching
James's denunciation of the rich who exploit laborers (5:1–6) and his command to show no partiality (2:1–9) have been incorporated into Catholic social teaching. Pope Francis has frequently cited James in his calls for economic justice and care for the poor.

## Theological Emphases

- **Faith Perfected by Works**: James 2:22 — Abraham's faith was "completed" by his works
- **Anointing of the Sick**: James 5:14–15 as sacramental institution
- **Preferential Option for the Poor**: James 2:1–9 and 5:1–6 as foundations for social justice
- **Confession**: James 5:16 ("confess your sins to one another") as support for sacramental confession
