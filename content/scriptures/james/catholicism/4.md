## Disordered Desires and Concupiscence

James 4:1–3, which traces conflict to the "passions at war within you," is read by Catholic theology through the lens of concupiscence — the disordered desires that remain even after baptism. While concupiscence is not itself sin, it inclines toward sin and requires the ongoing discipline of the moral and sacramental life to overcome.

## Humility and Grace

James 4:6 — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" — is a foundational text in Catholic spirituality. The call to "draw near to God" (4:8) shapes Catholic teaching on prayer and the interior life. The tradition of spiritual humility, from Benedict to Thérèse of Lisieux, finds resonance in James's insistence that submission to God precedes divine encounter.

## The Uncertainty of Life

James 4:13–17 warns against planning without reference to God's will. Catholic moral theology interprets this as a call to the virtue of prudence — making decisions in light of God's providence rather than presuming on one's own ability to control the future.
