## Wealth and the Prophetic Tradition

Orthodox interpretation of James 5:1–6 connects the letter's prophetic judgment on the rich with the patristic social teaching of Basil the Great and John Chrysostom. Chrysostom taught that hoarding wealth is a form of theft from the poor, echoing James's declaration that the laborers' withheld wages "cry out" to the Lord. Orthodox social ethics draws on this prophetic tradition to affirm the responsibility of the wealthy to share.

## Patient Endurance and Eschatological Hope

James 5:7–11 calls for patience until the Lord's coming. Orthodox eschatology reads this in light of the Paschal mystery: Christ has already conquered death, and the faithful await the full manifestation of this victory. The example of Job as one who endured suffering and saw "the purpose of the Lord" (5:11) is read as a type of the Christian who endures through hope in the resurrection.

## Holy Unction

James 5:14–15 provides the scriptural foundation for the Orthodox sacrament of Holy Unction. Unlike the Western practice of reserving anointing for the dying, Orthodox practice administers Holy Unction more broadly as a sacrament of healing — physical, spiritual, and psychological. The rite involves anointing with blessed oil, the reading of seven epistle and gospel passages, and the prayers of the community.
