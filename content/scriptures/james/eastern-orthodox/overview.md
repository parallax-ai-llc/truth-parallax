## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of James finds in the letter a natural expression of the patristic understanding of salvation as transformation. James's insistence that faith must be active in works aligns with the Orthodox emphasis on synergy — the cooperation of divine grace and human freedom. The letter's practical wisdom is read not as mere ethics but as guidance for the ascetic life of growth toward theosis.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Reading
The Greek Fathers, including Cyril of Alexandria and Oecumenius, read James as consistent with Paul. Orthodoxy has never experienced the Protestant tension between James and Paul because it never adopted the forensic model of justification that created the apparent conflict. Faith and works are united in the single process of salvation understood as healing and transformation.

### Ascetic Tradition
James's teaching on controlling the tongue (3:1–12), resisting passions (4:1–3), and patient endurance (5:7–11) resonates deeply with Orthodox ascetic theology. The desert fathers and monastic tradition drew on these themes as part of the practical struggle against the passions (*pathē*).

### Liturgical Use
James 5:14–15 informs the Orthodox sacrament of Holy Unction, administered not only to the seriously ill but as a regular rite of healing during Holy Week. The letter's emphasis on communal prayer and confession shapes Orthodox pastoral practice.

## Theological Emphases

- **Synergy**: Faith and works as inseparable dimensions of the one salvific process
- **Wisdom as Divine Gift**: James 1:5 as an invitation to the contemplative life of prayer
- **Holy Unction**: James 5:14–15 as the foundation for the sacrament of anointing
- **Taming the Passions**: James 3–4 as practical ascetic guidance
