## Partiality and the Torah's Demand

James 2:1–9 condemns showing favoritism to the wealthy, invoking the "royal law" of Leviticus 19:18: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself." Jewish readers note that James grounds his ethics directly in the Torah, not in distinctly Christian teaching. The prohibition of partiality is deeply rooted in Jewish law: "You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great" (Leviticus 19:15).

## Faith and Works in Jewish Perspective

The debate between faith and works that James 2:14–26 addresses is not a Jewish problem. In Jewish theology, faith (*emunah*) and righteous action (*ma'asim tovim*) have never been separated. The very concept of "faith alone" without deeds is foreign to rabbinic thought. Jewish readers find James's argument — that faith without works is dead — to be simply a restatement of what Judaism has always taught.

## Abraham's Binding of Isaac

James's citation of Abraham's offering of Isaac (2:21) connects to the *Akedah*, one of the most important narratives in Jewish theology. In Jewish interpretation, the Akedah demonstrates Abraham's total devotion to God, and it is commemorated annually in the liturgy of Rosh Hashanah. James's reading of Abraham as justified by faithful action aligns with the Jewish tradition of Abraham as the paradigm of covenant faithfulness.
