## Wisdom and the Jewish Tradition

Jewish scholars note that James 1 reads more like a chapter of Proverbs or Sirach than a Pauline epistle. The opening exhortation to embrace trials and seek wisdom from God parallels the Jewish tradition of wisdom as a divine gift granted to those who ask with pure hearts. The invitation to ask God for wisdom (1:5) echoes Solomon's prayer in 1 Kings 3:9, a foundational text in Jewish wisdom literature.

## The Shema and Wholehearted Devotion

James's contrast between the single-minded person and the "double-minded" waverer (1:6–8) resonates with the Shema's command to love God "with all your heart" (Deuteronomy 6:5). Jewish readers recognize in James the traditional Jewish emphasis on *temimut* (wholeheartedness) and undivided devotion to God.

## True Religion as Deeds of Lovingkindness

James 1:27 defines pure religion as caring for orphans and widows — a definition that aligns perfectly with the Jewish concept of *gemilut chasadim* (acts of lovingkindness). Rabbinic tradition teaches that the world stands on three things: Torah, worship, and deeds of lovingkindness (Pirkei Avot 1:2). James's emphasis on action over mere hearing echoes the Jewish priority of deed over creed.
