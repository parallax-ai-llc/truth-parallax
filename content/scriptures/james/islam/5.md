## Economic Justice

James 5:1–6, with its condemnation of the wealthy who withhold wages, aligns with Islamic economic ethics. The Prophet Muhammad listed withholding a laborer's wages among the gravest sins, and the Quran commands: "Give full measure and weight in justice" (6:152). Muslim readers find in James a prophetic economic ethic fully consistent with Islamic teaching on just commerce and the obligation to pay workers promptly and fairly.

## Patient Endurance

James 5:7–11 calls for patient endurance (*sabr*), one of the most emphasized virtues in Islam. The Quran states: "Indeed, the patient will be given their reward without account" (39:10). James's invocation of the prophets and Job as models of patience resonates with the Islamic reverence for the prophets as exemplars of *sabr* — particularly Ayyub (Job), who is celebrated in the Quran for his endurance through severe affliction (21:83–84).

## Prayer and Healing

James 5:13–16, which instructs believers to pray in all circumstances and to seek communal prayer for healing, is consistent with Islamic teaching on the efficacy of supplication (*du'a*). The Prophet Muhammad encouraged believers to pray for one another's healing, and the Quran describes itself as "a healing for what is in the breasts" (10:57). Muslim readers appreciate that James grounds prayer and healing in communal practice rather than sacramental mediation.
