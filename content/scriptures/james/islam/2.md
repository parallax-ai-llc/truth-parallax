## Against Partiality and Class Distinction

James 2:1–9 condemns favoritism toward the wealthy, a teaching that aligns with the Quranic principle of human equality before God. The Prophet Muhammad declared in his final sermon that "no Arab has superiority over a non-Arab, and no white person has superiority over a black person, except by piety." James's insistence on impartial treatment of the poor reflects the Islamic value of brotherhood and equity within the community.

## Faith Without Works Is Dead

James 2:14–26 — the declaration that faith without works is dead — represents the point of closest alignment between James and Islamic theology. The Quran consistently pairs belief with righteous deeds: "Those who believe and do righteous deeds — they are the best of creatures" (98:7). Muslim scholars have argued that James preserves an understanding of faith closer to Jesus's original teaching, before Paul's doctrine of justification by faith alone altered the trajectory of Christian theology.

## Abraham the Faithful Servant

James's citation of Abraham as justified by his willingness to sacrifice his son resonates deeply with Islamic tradition. The Quran narrates the story of Ibrahim's sacrifice (37:99–111), and it is commemorated annually during Eid al-Adha. Muslim readers see in James's Abraham the same Ibrahim honored in Islam: a servant whose faith was demonstrated through radical obedience.
