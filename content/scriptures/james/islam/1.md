## Trials and Divine Testing

James 1:2–4, which teaches that trials produce endurance and maturity, resonates with the Quranic theme of divine testing. The Quran states: "Do people think they will be left alone because they say 'We believe' and will not be tested?" (29:2). Muslim readers find in James a familiar understanding that God tests the faithful to strengthen and purify them, though Islam frames this within the broader concept of *sabr* (patient endurance) as a supreme virtue.

## Wisdom and Divine Guidance

James 1:5 — asking God for wisdom — aligns with the Islamic emphasis on seeking knowledge and divine guidance. The Quran urges believers to pray for guidance (*hidayah*), and the opening surah, Al-Fatiha, is itself a prayer for divine direction. Muslim readers appreciate James's teaching that wisdom is a generous gift from God, freely available to those who ask in sincerity.

## Pure Religion Defined by Action

James 1:27 defines pure religion as caring for orphans and widows and remaining unstained by the world. This definition resonates powerfully with Islamic teaching. The Quran repeatedly commands care for orphans (*yatama*) and the vulnerable, and the Prophet Muhammad himself was an orphan. Muslim readers note that James's definition of religion aligns more closely with Islam's emphasis on ethical action than with later Christian doctrines of salvation by faith alone.
