## Wealth and the Material Order

James 5:1–6, with its prophetic denunciation of hoarded wealth, could be read by Gnostic interpreters as a critique of attachment to the material order. The Demiurge's world operates on principles of accumulation and exploitation, and the pneumatic who recognizes the illusory nature of material wealth is free from its grip. However, James's concern is with social justice — unpaid wages and the suffering of laborers — not with ontological rejection of matter.

## Patience and the Eschatological Hope

James 5:7–11 calls for patience until the Lord's coming. Gnostic eschatology differs markedly from the apocalyptic framework James assumes. For Gnostics, salvation is not a future cosmic event but a present interior awakening — the gnosis that liberates the divine spark from its material prison. The concept of patient waiting for a future parousia holds little meaning in a system where the goal is the individual's present ascent to the Pleroma.

## Prayer and the Gnostic Alternative

James 5:13–16, with its instructions on prayer and communal anointing, reflects a proto-Catholic ecclesial practice that Gnostic circles would have viewed as belonging to the psychic level of spirituality. Gnostic prayer, as reflected in texts like the *Prayer of the Apostle Paul*, sought mystical ascent and direct encounter with the divine, rather than the communal, practical intercession James describes.
