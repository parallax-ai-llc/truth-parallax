## Passions and the Material Entrapment

James 4:1–3, tracing conflict to the "passions at war within you," could be read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the soul's bondage to the material body and its appetites. The Gnostic tradition viewed the passions as chains binding the divine spark to the Demiurge's creation. Liberation from the passions comes not through moral effort, as James suggests, but through gnosis — the recognition of one's true pneumatic identity.

## Friendship with the World

James 4:4 — "friendship with the world is enmity with God" — resonates with the Gnostic rejection of the material cosmos. For Gnostics, the world is the flawed creation of an inferior deity, and attachment to it is the primary obstacle to spiritual liberation. While James uses "world" in a moral sense (referring to worldly values and behaviors), Gnostic readers would understand it ontologically — the entire material realm is to be transcended through gnosis.

## Humility and the Gnostic Path

James's call to humility before God (4:6–10) sits uneasily with certain Gnostic traditions that emphasized the pneumatic's inherent divinity and superiority. However, some Gnostic texts, including the *Gospel of Thomas*, do value humility as a mark of spiritual maturity, suggesting that humility and gnosis are not necessarily incompatible.
