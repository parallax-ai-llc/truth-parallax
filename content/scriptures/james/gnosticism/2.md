## Partiality and the Demiurge's Social Order

Gnostic interpreters might read James 2:1–9 as a critique of the social hierarchies established by the Demiurge's flawed creation. The material world's valuation of wealth and status reflects the ignorance of the lower creator, while the "royal law" of love points toward the higher realm of the Pleroma where such distinctions dissolve. However, this reading goes well beyond James's own social-ethical intent.

## Faith and Works: A Gnostic Tension

James 2:14–26 presents a direct challenge to any Gnostic soteriology based on knowledge alone. James's insistence that faith without works is dead contradicts the Valentinian teaching that the pneumatic is saved by nature and gnosis regardless of moral conduct. This incompatibility helps explain why Gnostic commentators generally avoided James, preferring Paul's more ambiguous statements on faith, law, and works.

## The Canonical Letter's Anti-Gnostic Potential

Some scholars have suggested that James's emphasis on works over mere verbal profession may itself constitute an implicit polemic against proto-Gnostic tendencies in early Christianity. The insistence that faith must be demonstrated through concrete action challenges any spirituality that claims salvation through interior knowledge while neglecting ethical responsibility.
