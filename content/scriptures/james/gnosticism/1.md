## Testing and the Material World

Gnostic readers might interpret James 1:2–4, with its teaching on trials producing endurance, through the lens of the pneumatic soul's struggle within the material world. The tests faced by the believer are understood as the friction between the divine spark imprisoned in matter and the hostile forces of the Demiurge's creation. However, James's practical, moral framing of trials resists the Gnostic cosmological reinterpretation.

## Wisdom and Gnosis

James 1:5, with its invitation to ask God for wisdom, offers a partial point of contact with Gnostic thought. The Gnostic tradition valued wisdom (*sophia*) as the key to salvation, and the idea that God gives wisdom generously to those who ask could be read as an invitation to seek gnosis. Yet James's wisdom is thoroughly practical — concerned with ethics and conduct — rather than the cosmological knowledge that defines Gnostic *sophia*.

## The Double-Minded and the Material Condition

James's critique of the "double-minded" person (1:8) might be appropriated by Gnostic interpreters to describe the psychic — the person divided between the material world and the spiritual realm, unable to commit fully to the path of gnosis. The pneumatic, by contrast, is single-minded in pursuit of the divine knowledge that liberates from material existence.
