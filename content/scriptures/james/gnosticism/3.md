## The Tongue and the Logos

Gnostic interpreters might connect James 3:1–12 on the power of speech to the Gnostic theology of the Logos — the divine word through which creation (or emanation) occurs. The tongue's capacity to bless and curse simultaneously (3:9–10) could be read as reflecting the ambiguity of the Demiurge's creative word, which produced a flawed material cosmos even as it participated, however imperfectly, in the divine speech of the Pleroma.

## Two Wisdoms as Cosmological Dualism

James 3:13–18, contrasting earthly and heavenly wisdom, offers the most natural point of Gnostic interpretation in the entire letter. The "earthly, unspiritual, demonic" wisdom (3:15) could be identified with the Demiurge's limited knowledge, while the "wisdom from above" corresponds to the divine *Sophia* of the Pleroma. Yet James's description of heavenly wisdom as "peaceable, gentle, full of mercy" emphasizes moral character rather than cosmological insight, resisting full Gnostic appropriation.
