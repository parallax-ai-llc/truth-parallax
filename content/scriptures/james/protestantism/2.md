## The Sin of Partiality

James 2:1–13 condemns the practice of showing favoritism to the wealthy in the assembly. Protestant interpreters read this as a violation of the "royal law" of love (2:8) and a reminder that the gospel levels social distinctions. The passage challenges churches to examine whether their communities genuinely welcome the poor.

## Faith and Works: The Protestant Challenge

James 2:14–26 presents the most direct challenge to the Reformation principle of sola fide. James declares that "faith without works is dead" (2:26) and that Abraham was "justified by works" (2:21). Protestant harmonization typically follows Calvin: Paul speaks of justification before God (by faith), while James speaks of justification before others (demonstrated by works). The two apostles address different errors — Paul combats legalism, James combats antinomianism.

## Abraham and Rahab as Examples

James cites Abraham's offering of Isaac and Rahab's sheltering of the spies as examples of faith made complete by action. Protestant interpreters emphasize that these works were the fruit, not the root, of faith. Abraham believed God first (Genesis 15:6), and his obedient action in Genesis 22 demonstrated the reality of that prior faith.
