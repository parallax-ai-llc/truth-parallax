## Overview

James has occupied an uneasy place in Protestant theology ever since Martin Luther relegated it to the back of his New Testament and called it "an epistle of straw" compared to Romans and Galatians. Luther's objection was that James 2:24 — "a person is justified by works and not by faith alone" — appeared to contradict the Reformation principle of sola fide. Nevertheless, most Protestant traditions have retained James as canonical and valued its practical moral teaching.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Reformation Tensions
Luther's discomfort with James has not been shared by most subsequent Protestant interpreters. Calvin treated James as fully authoritative, arguing that James and Paul address different questions: Paul addresses the ground of justification before God, while James addresses the evidence of genuine faith before others. This harmonization remains the standard evangelical approach.

### Practical Sanctification
Protestant interpreters increasingly read James as a letter about sanctification rather than justification. The letter's concern is not how one is saved but how the saved person lives. Its wisdom sayings on speech, patience, and prayer are treated as guidance for the Christian life after conversion.

### Wisdom Literature
Recent Protestant scholarship emphasizes James's roots in Jewish wisdom literature. The letter draws on Proverbs, Sirach, and the teaching of Jesus, presenting a practical theology of everyday faithfulness.

## Theological Emphases

- **Faith Without Works Is Dead**: James 2:14–26 as a call to authentic, living faith
- **Wisdom from Above**: James 3:13–18 contrasting heavenly and earthly wisdom
- **The Tongue**: James 3:1–12 as a classic text on the ethics of speech
- **Prayer of Faith**: James 5:13–18 as a foundation for healing prayer
