## Covenant at Shechem

Joshua 24 is the great climax of the book: Joshua assembles all Israel at Shechem for a covenant renewal ceremony. He recounts God's saving acts from Abraham to the present, challenges the people to choose whom they will serve, and the people commit themselves to the LORD. The chapter concludes with Joshua's death and burial.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Thus says the LORD, the God of Israel" (24:2)

Joshua recounts salvation history in God's own voice. **Howard** notes that the historical review follows the pattern of ancient Near Eastern suzerainty treaties — the great king recounts his deeds before demanding loyalty.

### "Choose this day whom you will serve" (24:15)

This is one of the most famous verses in the Old Testament. **Luther** used it to emphasize the urgency of faith — there is no neutral ground. **Calvin**, while affirming the sovereignty of grace, acknowledged that the verse presents a genuine call to decision. The verse's popularity in Protestant preaching reflects the evangelical emphasis on personal commitment.

### "As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD" (24:15)

Joshua's personal declaration of loyalty models household faith. **Matthew Henry** emphasized that spiritual leadership begins at home — a leader who cannot govern his own household has no business leading God's people.

### "You are witnesses against yourselves" (24:22)

The people's own testimony becomes self-binding. **Woudstra** notes the legal solemnity: Israel enters a covenant with full awareness of its obligations and consequences.

## Theological Significance

- **Covenant renewal**: Each generation must recommit to God's covenant — faith is not inherited automatically
- **Historical memory**: Recounting God's deeds is the foundation of worship and commitment
- **Personal decision**: "Choose this day" calls for genuine, present-tense faith
- **Household religion**: Joshua models the integration of personal and family devotion
- **The end of an era**: Joshua's death closes the conquest period and opens the uncertain era of the Judges
