## Judah's Territory

Joshua 15 details the boundaries and cities of the tribe of Judah, the royal tribe from which David and ultimately Christ would come. While the chapter is largely geographical, Protestant theology finds significance in the meticulous recording of Judah's inheritance as the territory that would become the heartland of redemptive history.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Judah's Boundaries (15:1-12)

The detailed boundary descriptions establish legal precedent for tribal land claims. **Hess** notes that these descriptions follow ancient Near Eastern boundary list conventions, supporting their historical authenticity.

### Caleb and Othniel (15:13-19)

The narrative of Caleb giving Hebron to his daughter Achsah and her husband Othniel provides a human interest story within the geographical data. **Howard** observes that Othniel later becomes the first judge (Judges 3:7-11), connecting Joshua's era to what follows.

### "As for the Jebusites... Judah could not drive them out" (15:63)

This sobering note acknowledges that the conquest was incomplete. The Jebusites remained in Jerusalem until David conquered it (2 Samuel 5). Protestant commentators see here an honest admission that shapes the "already/not yet" theology of the book.

## Theological Significance

- **Judah's preeminence**: The extensive treatment of Judah's territory reflects its future role as the royal and messianic tribe
- **Geography as theology**: The physical land is the stage for redemptive history
- **Incomplete conquest**: The failure to drive out the Jebusites foreshadows later struggles
- **Women in the narrative**: Achsah's boldness in requesting springs of water models faithful petition
- **Historical reliability**: The detailed boundary lists support the text's historical claims
