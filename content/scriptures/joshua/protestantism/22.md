## The Altar of Witness

Joshua 22 narrates the near-civil-war that erupts when the eastern tribes build an altar by the Jordan. The western tribes assume apostasy, but the eastern tribes explain that the altar is a witness to their belonging — not a rival sanctuary. Protestant theology draws lessons about unity, communication, and the danger of hasty judgment.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Take great care to love the LORD your God" (22:5)

Joshua's farewell charge to the eastern tribes echoes the Shema. **Calvin** noted that love for God is the foundation of all obedience — Joshua does not merely command external conformity but internal devotion.

### "They built an altar... of great size" (22:10)

The altar's impressive scale provokes the western tribes' alarm. **Davis** observes that the crisis arises from a failure to communicate intentions — the eastern tribes build first and explain later.

### "Far be it from us that we should rebel against the LORD" (22:29)

The eastern tribes' explanation reveals that the altar is not for sacrifice but for testimony — a witness that they too belong to the LORD's people. **Howard** notes that the resolution comes through dialogue, not warfare.

## Theological Significance

- **Church unity**: Misunderstanding threatens division; communication preserves unity
- **Zeal without knowledge**: The western tribes' zeal for purity almost leads to unjust war
- **The importance of dialogue**: The crisis is resolved through patient listening and explanation
- **Witness across boundaries**: The altar testifies that God's people are one, even when geographically separated
- **Corporate memory**: The altar ensures future generations remember their common identity
