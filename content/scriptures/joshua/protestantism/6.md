## The Fall of Jericho

Joshua 6 presents the dramatic conquest of Jericho — not by military assault but by a seven-day liturgical procession culminating in a shout of faith. Protestant theology emphasizes that this battle belongs to God, not to human strategy. The walls fall by divine power, and the victory comes through obedience to God's unconventional commands.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "See, I have given Jericho into your hand" (6:2)

Again the perfect tense: the city is *already given*. **Luther** saw here the pattern of all Christian victory — God grants the victory before the battle, and faith appropriates what grace provides.

### The Seven-Day March (6:3-5)

The military absurdity of marching around a city is the point. **Hebrews 11:30** attributes the fall to faith: "By faith the walls of Jericho fell down." Protestant preaching has long used this account to illustrate that God's methods confound human wisdom (1 Corinthians 1:27).

### "Devoted to destruction" (Herem) (6:17-21)

The herem — the total devotion of Jericho to destruction — is one of the most difficult ethical topics in the Old Testament. Protestant responses vary: some emphasize divine judgment on Canaanite sin (Genesis 15:16), others stress the unique redemptive-historical context, and still others highlight that the New Testament transforms holy war into spiritual warfare (Ephesians 6:12).

### Rahab Saved (6:22-25)

Rahab and her household are spared, fulfilling the spies' oath. **Calvin** noted the striking contrast: an entire city falls, but one believing household is preserved — illustrating that faith, not ethnicity, determines salvation.

## Theological Significance

- **Victory belongs to God**: Human strength and strategy do not win the battle
- **Faith as the instrument of victory**: Hebrews 11:30 makes this explicit
- **The herem and divine judgment**: A difficult but necessary aspect of Old Testament theology
- **Grace within judgment**: Rahab's salvation within Jericho's destruction pictures the gospel
