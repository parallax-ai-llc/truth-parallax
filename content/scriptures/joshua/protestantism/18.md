## Remaining Allotments at Shiloh

Joshua 18 records the assembly of Israel at Shiloh, where the tabernacle is set up and the remaining seven tribes receive their territorial assignments through a land survey and lot-casting process. Protestant theology sees significance in both the new worship center and the combination of human effort (surveying) with divine direction (lots).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The whole congregation of the people of Israel assembled at Shiloh and set up the tent of meeting there" (18:1)

The establishment of the tabernacle at Shiloh marks a new phase in Israel's worship. **Woudstra** notes that Shiloh becomes the central sanctuary until its destruction (Jeremiah 7:12-14), prefiguring the later temple at Jerusalem.

### "How long will you put off going in to take possession of the land?" (18:3)

Joshua rebukes the remaining tribes for their delay. **Spurgeon** applied this to the church: God has given spiritual blessings in Christ, yet believers are often slow to appropriate them. The inheritance must be actively claimed.

### Lots Cast Before the LORD (18:6-10)

The use of lots combines divine sovereignty with human responsibility. The tribes survey the land (human agency), but the lots determine the allocation (divine direction). **Howard** sees here a model of how God works through human means while retaining sovereign control.

## Theological Significance

- **Central worship**: The establishment of Shiloh prefigures unified worship centered on God's presence
- **Spiritual lethargy rebuked**: Joshua's rebuke challenges passivity in the face of God's promises
- **Sovereignty and responsibility**: Lots and surveys model the interplay of divine direction and human effort
- **The whole people gathered**: The assembly at Shiloh demonstrates the corporate nature of faith
