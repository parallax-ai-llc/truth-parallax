## Joshua's Divine Commission

Joshua 1 marks the pivotal transition from Moses' leadership to Joshua's. God's repeated command to "be strong and courageous" (1:6, 7, 9) is not mere encouragement — it is a divine imperative grounded in promise. Protestant theology sees here a paradigm of leadership: authority derives not from personal ability but from God's commission and the certainty of His Word.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Moses my servant is dead. Now therefore arise" (1:2)

The bluntness of this declaration has struck Protestant commentators. **Calvin** noted that God does not permit prolonged mourning when work remains to be done — grief is legitimate, but the mission continues. The death of Moses and the rise of Joshua prefigure the passing of the old covenant and the coming of the new.

### "Every place that the sole of your foot will tread upon I have given you" (1:3)

**Matthew Henry** emphasized the perfect tense: God has *already given* the land. The conquest is not uncertain — it is the appropriation of what is already secured. Reformed theology draws a parallel to the believer's position in Christ: the inheritance is certain, but it must be actively entered into.

### "This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth" (1:8)

Protestant emphasis on *sola Scriptura* finds a powerful precedent here. Joshua's success depends not on military strategy but on meditation upon and obedience to God's written word. **Spurgeon** preached extensively on this verse as the secret of spiritual prosperity.

### "Be strong and courageous. Do not be frightened" (1:9)

The threefold repetition of this command (vv. 6, 7, 9) structures the chapter. **David Howard** notes that each occurrence adds a new dimension: strength for conquest (v. 6), strength for obedience (v. 7), and strength from God's presence (v. 9).

## The People's Response (1:16-18)

The tribes' pledge of loyalty to Joshua mirrors their earlier pledge to Moses. Protestant commentators observe that legitimate spiritual authority receives willing submission — a principle applied to church governance in the Reformed tradition.

## Theological Significance

- **Christ as the true Joshua**: Just as Joshua leads Israel into Canaan, Christ leads His people into eternal rest (Hebrews 4:8-9)
- **The Word as the foundation of leadership**: Meditation on Scripture, not personal charisma, is the basis of godly leadership
- **Promise precedes command**: God's promises (vv. 2-5) ground His commands (vv. 6-9)
