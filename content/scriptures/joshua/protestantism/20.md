## Cities of Refuge

Joshua 20 establishes six cities of refuge — three east and three west of the Jordan — where those who accidentally kill someone may flee from the avenger of blood. Protestant theology sees in these cities a profound type of Christ, who is the believer's refuge from the consequences of sin.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Appoint the cities of refuge, of which I spoke to you through Moses" (20:2)

The cities of refuge were first legislated in Numbers 35 and Deuteronomy 19. Joshua now implements what Moses commanded. **Woudstra** notes that this demonstrates the continuity between Mosaic law and its faithful execution.

### "He shall flee to one of these cities... and they shall admit him" (20:4)

The fugitive must be received — the city cannot refuse him. **Calvin** saw a powerful gospel parallel: Christ refuses no one who flees to Him for protection. The cities are open, accessible, and obligated to shelter the refugee.

### The Six Cities (20:7-8)

Three cities west (Kedesh, Shechem, Hebron) and three east (Bezer, Ramoth, Golan) ensure that refuge is geographically accessible from anywhere in the land. **Matthew Henry** observed that God placed refuge within reach of every Israelite, just as the gospel is proclaimed within reach of every sinner.

## Theological Significance

- **Christ as the believer's refuge**: The cities prefigure the safety found in Christ (Hebrews 6:18)
- **Justice and mercy**: The system distinguishes between intentional murder and accidental killing
- **Accessible grace**: The strategic placement of the cities ensures no one is too far from refuge
- **Protection from the avenger**: The law's curse is real, but refuge is provided
- **The death of the high priest**: The manslayer remains until the high priest dies (20:6), which Protestant typology connects to Christ's death securing permanent release
