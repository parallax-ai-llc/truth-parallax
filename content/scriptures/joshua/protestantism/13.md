## Land Still to Conquer

Joshua 13 begins the second major section of the book — the division of the land. God tells the aging Joshua that "very much land remains to be possessed" (13:1), and then the distribution of tribal territories begins, starting with the Transjordan tribes. Protestant theology finds in this chapter the tension between promise fulfilled and promise yet to be fully realized.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You are old and advanced in years, and there remains yet very much land to be possessed" (13:1)

**Calvin** noted the pastoral honesty of this statement: even faithful leaders leave unfinished business. The incompleteness of the conquest points beyond itself to the complete victory that comes only in Christ.

### The Transjordan Allotment (13:8-33)

The tribes of Reuben, Gad, and half-Manasseh receive their inheritance east of the Jordan, as Moses had promised. **Woudstra** observes that honoring Moses' commitments demonstrates covenantal continuity.

### "The LORD God of Israel is their inheritance" (13:33)

The Levites receive no territorial inheritance because God Himself is their portion. **Matthew Henry** saw this as the highest privilege — those who serve God need no earthly estate, for they possess the Giver rather than mere gifts.

## Theological Significance

- **Already/not yet**: The land is given but not fully possessed — a model of the Christian life
- **God as inheritance**: The Levites' portion anticipates the New Testament's spiritual inheritance
- **Faithfulness to prior commitments**: Joshua honors Moses' promises to the eastern tribes
- **Aging and unfinished work**: Even the greatest leaders leave tasks for the next generation
