## Achan's Sin

Joshua 7 presents one of the Old Testament's starkest lessons in corporate holiness: one man's secret sin leads to Israel's defeat at Ai and the death of thirty-six soldiers. Protestant theology draws from this chapter profound truths about the nature of sin, the holiness of God, and the corporate dimensions of individual transgression.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Israel has sinned" (7:11)

God does not say "Achan has sinned" but "Israel has sinned." **Calvin** observed that this reveals the organic unity of the covenant community — one member's disobedience implicates the whole body. Paul's teaching on the Body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12) echoes this principle.

### "I saw... I coveted... I took" (7:21)

Achan's confession follows the pattern of the Fall: seeing, desiring, taking (Genesis 3:6). **Matthew Henry** traced the progression: the eye leads the heart, the heart moves the hand. Protestant moral theology identifies this as the anatomy of all sin.

### The Valley of Achor (7:26)

The Valley of Achor ("trouble") becomes, prophetically, a "door of hope" (Hosea 2:15). Protestant commentators see in this transformation a picture of the gospel: the place of judgment becomes the place of restoration.

## Theological Significance

- **The seriousness of sin**: Secret sin has public consequences
- **Corporate responsibility**: The community is affected by individual transgression
- **The anatomy of temptation**: Seeing, coveting, taking — the universal pattern of sin
- **Divine holiness demands purity**: God will not bless a disobedient people
- **Hope beyond judgment**: The Valley of Achor becomes a door of hope
