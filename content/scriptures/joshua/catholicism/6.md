## The Fall of Jericho and Liturgical Power

Joshua 6 holds special significance in Catholic interpretation: the seven-day liturgical procession around Jericho demonstrates the power of sacred ritual and worship to accomplish what human effort alone cannot. The Church Fathers saw in Jericho's fall a prefiguration of the sacraments' transformative power.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Seven-Day Procession (6:3-5)

**Origen** read the seven circuits as representing the sevenfold gifts of the Holy Spirit and the seven sacraments through which God's saving power operates in the world. The procession of the Ark prefigures Eucharistic processions, where Christ Himself is carried through the streets.

### The Priests and Trumpets (6:4)

The priests lead the procession — not the warriors. Catholic theology emphasizes the primacy of the priestly and liturgical over the military and political. The trumpets prefigure the liturgical proclamation that breaks down the walls of sin.

### The Herem (6:17-21)

Catholic moral theology has engaged extensively with the concept of devoted destruction. **Augustine** argued that divine commands in unique salvation-historical moments do not establish general moral principles. The Catholic just war tradition develops criteria precisely to prevent the misapplication of such texts.

### Rahab's Salvation (6:22-25)

**The Church Fathers** consistently read Rahab's house — marked with the scarlet cord — as a type of the Church: those within are saved, those outside perish. This ecclesiological reading supports the teaching *extra ecclesiam nulla salus* (outside the Church there is no salvation), properly understood.

## Theological Significance

- **Liturgical power**: Sacred ritual accomplishes what human effort cannot
- **The priesthood's role**: Priests lead; the laity follows in faith
- **The Church as ark of salvation**: Rahab's house prefigures the Church's saving role
- **Sacramental prefiguration**: The procession anticipates Eucharistic worship
