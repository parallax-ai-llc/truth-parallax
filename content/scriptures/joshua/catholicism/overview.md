## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Joshua reads the book within the broader framework of salvation history, emphasizing its typological and sacramental significance. The crossing of the Jordan prefigures baptism, the fall of Jericho anticipates the power of liturgical procession, and the conquest of Canaan foreshadows the Church's mission to claim the world for Christ. The Church Fathers — especially Origen, Augustine, and Jerome — saw Joshua as a rich source of allegorical and moral instruction.

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Typology**: Joshua (Jesus Nave in the Septuagint) is a type of Christ leading His people into the promised inheritance
- **Sacramental prefiguring**: The Jordan crossing prefigures baptism; the Passover at Gilgal anticipates the Eucharist
- **Just war tradition**: Catholic moral theology engages seriously with the conquest narratives in developing criteria for just warfare
- **Ecclesiology**: The united twelve tribes prefigure the universal Church; the altar of witness (ch. 22) models ecclesial unity
- **Patristic exegesis**: Origen's homilies on Joshua remain foundational for Catholic spiritual reading of the text
