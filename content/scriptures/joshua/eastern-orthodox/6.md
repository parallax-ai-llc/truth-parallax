## The Fall of Jericho

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Joshua 6 proceeds through the lens of the Church Fathers, emphasizing typology, theophany, and the spiritual meaning embedded within the historical narrative. The conquest section of Joshua is read as part of the divine economy (*oikonomia*) — God's providential plan for the salvation of the world.

## Patristic Interpretation

### Typological Reading

**Theodoret of Cyrus**, one of the primary patristic commentators on Joshua, read the events of chapter 6 as both historically true and typologically significant. The themes of Jericho, faith, divine warfare are understood as shadows of greater realities revealed in Christ and the life of the Church.

### The Spiritual Warfare Interpretation

**Origen**, widely received in Orthodox spiritual theology despite certain theological reservations, interpreted the battles and events of Joshua as allegories of the soul's warfare against the passions. The Jericho, faith, divine warfare themes in this chapter represent aspects of the spiritual combat (*podvig*) that every Christian undertakes through ascetic discipline and prayer.

### Liturgical Connections

The Orthodox Church reads portions of Joshua during specific liturgical seasons, particularly during Great Lent and Holy Week, when themes of warfare, conquest, and entrance into the promised land resonate with the Paschal journey from death to resurrection.

## Theological Themes

### Divine Economy (Oikonomia)

Every event in Joshua 6 serves God's providential plan for universal salvation. Orthodox theology refuses to read any passage in isolation — each chapter participates in the grand narrative stretching from creation to the eschaton.

### Theosis

The human participants in Joshua 6 — whether Joshua, the priests, or the people — model the process of *theosis* (deification): human beings cooperating with divine grace to accomplish God's purposes and become partakers of the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4).

## Theological Significance

- **Typology**: The events prefigure Christ, the Church, and the sacraments
- **Spiritual warfare**: The narrative describes the soul's battle against sin and the passions
- **Divine economy**: Every event serves God's universal salvific plan
- **Theosis**: Human cooperation with divine grace models the path to deification
