## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Joshua emphasizes theophany, typology, and the book's place within the divine economy of salvation. The appearance of the Commander of the Lord's army (5:13-15) is read as a Christophany — a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ. The conquest is understood not primarily as military history but as a revelation of God's power to overcome the forces of evil. Orthodox liturgical tradition draws heavily on Joshua's imagery, particularly the crossing of the Jordan and the fall of Jericho.

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Christophany**: The Commander of the Lord's army (5:13-15) is identified as the pre-incarnate Logos
- **Typology**: Joshua is a type of Christ; the Jordan crossing prefigures baptism; Jericho's fall prefigures the harrowing of Hades
- **Liturgical reading**: The book is read in the context of Great Lent and Theophany, connecting conquest with spiritual warfare
- **Patristic consensus**: The Fathers — especially Origen, Theodoret of Cyrus, and John Chrysostom — guide Orthodox exegesis
- **Divine economy (oikonomia)**: The conquest reveals God's providential ordering of history toward salvation
