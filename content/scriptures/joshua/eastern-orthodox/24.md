## Covenant at Shechem and the Synaxis of the Faithful

Joshua 24 presents a covenant renewal ceremony that Orthodox theology reads as a prototype of the Divine Liturgy — the gathering of the faithful, the recitation of God's saving acts, the call to commitment, and the sealing of the covenant. The chapter reveals the essential structure of Israel's worship, which the Church inherits and transforms.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Put away the foreign gods" (24:14, 23)

**John Chrysostom** applied this to the catechumenate: before entering the covenant community, one must renounce false worship. The Orthodox baptismal rite includes a formal renunciation of Satan that echoes Joshua's demand to put away foreign gods.

### "Choose this day whom you will serve" (24:15)

Orthodox theology reads this not as bare free will but as Spirit-empowered choice. The concept of *synergeia* (cooperation between divine grace and human freedom) is central: God's grace makes genuine choice possible, and human response actualizes what grace offers.

### "We will serve the LORD, for He is our God" (24:18)

The people's response prefigures the congregation's responses in the Divine Liturgy. **Theodoret** noted that this communal confession of faith anticipates the Creed recited by the assembled faithful.

### Joshua's Death (24:29-33)

The death of "Joshua the servant of the LORD" concludes an era. Orthodox tradition venerates Joshua as a righteous forebear of Christ (commemorated September 1). His faithful death models the *dormition* (falling asleep) of the saints — death as peaceful passage, not fearful end.

## Theological Significance

- **Liturgical prototype**: The Shechem assembly prefigures the structure of the Divine Liturgy
- **Baptismal renunciation**: Putting away foreign gods anticipates baptismal renunciation of Satan
- **Synergeia**: Divine grace and human freedom cooperate in covenant commitment
- **Veneration of the righteous**: Joshua is honored among the righteous ancestors of Christ
