## Circumcision, Passover, and the Commander of the Lord's Army

Joshua 5 holds extraordinary significance in Orthodox theology due to the theophany in verses 13-15, where the Commander of the Lord's army appears to Joshua. Orthodox tradition identifies this figure as the pre-incarnate Logos — the Second Person of the Trinity appearing before the Incarnation.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Commander of the Lord's Army (5:13-15)

This Christophany is central to Orthodox theology. The figure accepts worship and commands Joshua to remove his sandals (echoing the Burning Bush of Exodus 3:5), identifying Him as divine. **Theodoret** explicitly identified the Commander as the Logos, the Angel of Great Counsel (Isaiah 9:6 LXX). Orthodox iconography sometimes depicts this scene alongside the Transfiguration, linking Old Testament theophanies to Christ's revealed glory.

### Circumcision at Gilgal (5:2-9)

**Origen** read the flint-knife circumcision as a type of baptism — the cutting away of the old nature. Orthodox baptismal theology draws on this imagery: the catechumen dies to the old life and is raised to new life, just as Israel's reproach was "rolled away" at Gilgal.

### Passover in the Land (5:10-11)

The first Passover celebrated in Canaan connects the Exodus deliverance to the new life in the promised land. Orthodox Paschal theology sees every Passover as a type of Pascha (Easter) — the passage from death to life, from slavery to freedom.

### The Cessation of Manna (5:12)

The manna ceases when the people eat the produce of the land. **John Chrysostom** connected this to the Eucharist: the bread from heaven takes new form in the sacramental bread of the altar, which is the true food of the promised inheritance.

## Theological Significance

- **Christophany**: The Commander is the pre-incarnate Christ, confirming Orthodox trinitarian theology
- **Baptismal typology**: Circumcision at Gilgal prefigures Christian baptism
- **Paschal theology**: Passover in the land anticipates the eternal Pascha
- **Eucharistic foreshadowing**: The transition from manna to the land's produce points to the Eucharist
