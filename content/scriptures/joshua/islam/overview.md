## Overview

Islamic engagement with Joshua (Yusha ibn Nun in Arabic tradition) recognizes him as a righteous servant and successor of Musa (Moses). While Joshua is not explicitly named in the Quran, Muslim scholars identify him as the young man who accompanied Moses in Surah al-Kahf (18:60-65) and as one of the two faithful spies mentioned in Surah al-Ma'idah (5:23). The conquest of the Holy Land illustrates God's faithfulness to those who trust and obey Him, while the Israelites' failures demonstrate the consequences of disobedience.

## Key Quranic Parallels

| Joshua Theme | Quranic Reference | Significance |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Faithful spies (Joshua/Caleb) | Surah al-Ma'idah 5:23 | Two men who urged trust in Allah |
| Entering the Holy Land | Surah al-Ma'idah 5:21 | Moses commands Israel to enter |
| Israel's refusal | Surah al-Ma'idah 5:24-26 | Consequence: forty years of wandering |
| Joshua as Moses' servant | Surah al-Kahf 18:60-65 | Yusha identified as Moses' companion |

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Prophetic succession**: Joshua's leadership after Moses exemplifies the continuity of divine guidance
- **Tawakkul (trust in God)**: The conquest succeeds only through reliance on Allah's power
- **Tahrif**: The biblical text is viewed as containing later alterations to the original revelation
- **Moral lessons**: Stories of obedience (Rahab) and disobedience (Achan) carry universal ethical instruction
