## Overview

Joshua (*Yehoshua*, "The LORD saves") is the sixth book of the Bible and the first of the Former Prophets (*Nevi'im Rishonim*) in the Hebrew canon. It narrates the conquest and settlement of Canaan under Joshua's leadership, fulfilling the promise made to Abraham. The book moves from the crossing of the Jordan through the military campaigns to the division of the land among the twelve tribes, culminating in Joshua's farewell and the covenant renewal at Shechem.

## Structure

1. **Entering the Land** (Chapters 1–5): Commission of Joshua, Rahab and the spies, crossing the Jordan, circumcision and Passover at Gilgal
2. **Conquering the Land** (Chapters 6–12): Jericho, Ai, the southern and northern campaigns, list of defeated kings
3. **Dividing the Land** (Chapters 13–21): Tribal allotments, cities of refuge, Levitical cities
4. **Covenant Renewal** (Chapters 22–24): The altar of witness, Joshua's farewell, covenant at Shechem

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: The transition from Torah to Prophets; Joshua as Moses' faithful successor; the paradigm of inheriting the land
- **Christianity**: Joshua/Jesus share the same name; typological readings of the conquest as spiritual warfare and the "rest" of Hebrews 3-4
- **Islam**: Yusha ibn Nun appears in Islamic tradition as the young companion of Musa and his successor
- **Gnosticism**: The conquest narratives as evidence of the Demiurge's violent character

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Faithfulness | God fulfills every promise made to the patriarchs |
| Obedience | Success depends on strict adherence to God's commands |
| Holy War | The *herem* (devotion to destruction) raises profound theological questions |
| Land | The promised inheritance as both physical reality and theological symbol |
| Leadership | Joshua as model of faithful, courageous leadership |
| Covenant | The book ends with covenant renewal — "Choose this day whom you will serve" (24:15) |
