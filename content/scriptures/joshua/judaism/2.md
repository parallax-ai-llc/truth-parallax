## Rahab and the Spies

Joshua 2 tells of the two spies sent to Jericho and their encounter with Rahab. Rabbinic tradition is remarkably generous toward Rahab, teaching that she converted to Judaism, married Joshua, and became an ancestor of eight prophets and priests, including Jeremiah and the prophetess Huldah (Megillah 14b).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Joshua... sent two men secretly as spies" (2:1)

The Talmud (Megillah 14b) identifies the two spies as Caleb and Phinehas. **Rashi** follows this tradition. Unlike the faithless spies of Numbers 13, these two fulfill their mission with discretion and courage.

### "Rahab the harlot" (2:1)

The word *zonah* is debated. **Rashi** follows the Targum in reading it as "innkeeper" (*pundekita*), while **Radak** accepts the plain meaning. Either way, Rahab's transformation is remarkable — the Talmud lists her among the four most beautiful women in history and teaches that her conversion was sincere and complete.

### "I know that the LORD has given you the land" (2:9)

**Malbim** observes that Rahab's knowledge came from hearing (*shema*) — the nations heard and trembled (cf. Exodus 15:14-15). Her faith contrasts sharply with the Israelite spies of Numbers 13 who saw the land and despaired.

### The Scarlet Cord (2:18)

Rabbinic interpretation connects the scarlet cord to the Yom Kippur ritual (Yoma 67a), where a crimson thread was tied to the scapegoat. The Midrash sees Rahab's cord as a sign of divine mercy extended to the repentant.

## Theological Significance

- **The power of teshuvah (repentance)**: Rahab's transformation demonstrates that sincere conversion is always accepted
- **Ger tzedek (righteous convert)**: Rahab becomes a model of righteous conversion in Jewish tradition
- **Contrast with the first spies**: This episode corrects the failure of Numbers 13
- **Divine mercy extends to all**: Rahab's Canaanite origin is no barrier to God's grace
