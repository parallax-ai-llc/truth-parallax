## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Joshua (Yehoshua) treats the book as the first of the Nevi'im (Prophets), marking the transition from Torah to prophetic history. The Talmud attributes the book to Joshua himself, with the final verses written by Eleazar and Phinehas (Bava Batra 14b-15a). The book's narratives raise profound halakhic and ethical questions about warfare, the status of the Canaanites, and the sanctity of the land. Rabbinic commentary emphasizes both the literal conquest and the spiritual dimensions of entering and possessing the land.

## Key Interpretive Themes

- **Eretz Yisrael**: The land is central to Jewish theology — God's gift, Israel's inheritance, and the place of covenant fulfillment
- **Halakhic questions**: The conquest raises questions about the laws of war, herem (devoted destruction), and relations with non-Israelites
- **Rahab**: The Talmud teaches that Rahab converted and married Joshua, becoming an ancestor of prophets (Megillah 14b)
- **Covenant continuity**: Joshua continues Moses' mission, demonstrating the chain of tradition (mesorah)
