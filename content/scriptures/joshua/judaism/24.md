## Covenant at Shechem

Joshua 24 records the great covenant renewal at Shechem, where Joshua challenges Israel to choose the LORD over the gods of the nations. The chapter concludes with Joshua's death at age 110 — the same age as Joseph (Genesis 50:26), which the Midrash sees as a sign of righteous completion.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Put away the foreign gods that are among you" (24:23)

**Rashi** is troubled by the implication that Israelites still possessed idols. He interprets this as referring to objects taken as spoil from the Canaanites. **Radak** reads it more broadly as a warning against any lingering attachment to pagan culture.

### "Choose this day whom you will serve" (24:15)

The concept of choice (*bechirah*) is fundamental to Jewish theology. **Rambam** (Maimonides) cites free will as a cornerstone of Torah: "Permission is granted to every human being" (Hilchot Teshuvah 5:1). Joshua's call to choose reflects the same principle articulated in Deuteronomy 30:19.

### "We will serve the LORD, for He is our God" (24:18)

**Malbim** notes that the people's declaration echoes the acceptance of Torah at Sinai. Each generation must make the covenant its own — the principle that underlies the Passover Haggadah's teaching: "In every generation, a person must see themselves as if they left Egypt."

### Death of Joshua (24:29-30)

The Talmud (Shabbat 105b) teaches that when Joshua died, the people were so absorbed in dividing the land that they failed to properly eulogize him — a failure the Sages regarded as shameful. This tradition serves as a moral lesson about honoring leaders.

### Joseph's Bones (24:32)

The burial of Joseph's bones at Shechem completes the promise made in Genesis 50:25. **Rashi** notes that this teaches the mitzvah of fulfilling oaths — even obligations undertaken centuries earlier remain binding.

## Theological Significance

- **Bechirah (free choice)**: Every generation must choose to serve God
- **Covenant renewal**: The covenant must be actively renewed, not passively inherited
- **Honoring leaders**: The Sages' criticism of Israel's response to Joshua's death teaches proper gratitude
- **Fulfilling ancestral oaths**: Joseph's burial demonstrates the binding nature of promises across generations
