## Overview

Jewish scholarly engagement with the Drasha d-Yahia focuses on what this text reveals about the diversity of Jewish religious life in the Second Temple and post-Temple periods. The Mandaean tradition's claim to descend from Jewish baptismal communities, its use of an Aramaic dialect closely related to Jewish Babylonian Aramaic, and its references to figures and institutions from Jewish tradition make the Drasha d-Yahia a valuable — if polemically charged — source for Jewish history.

The text's portrait of John the Baptist as a figure critical of the Jerusalem Temple establishment resonates with other evidence for sectarian diversity in Second Temple Judaism, including the Dead Sea Scrolls community at Qumran. Jewish scholars examine the Drasha d-Yahia's anti-Jewish polemics in the context of broader patterns of sectarian boundary-drawing, recognizing that the text's hostile portrayal of "the Jews" likely reflects later Mandaean community self-definition rather than first-century historical reality. The Drasha d-Yahia's Aramaic language and its use of Jewish liturgical and cosmological motifs illuminate the shared cultural world from which both rabbinic Judaism and Mandaeism emerged.
