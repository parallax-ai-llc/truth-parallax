## Jewish Reading

Jewish scholarly engagement with Drasha d-Yahia Section 9, "Yahia's Disputes with the World," focuses on what this text reveals about the diversity of Jewish religious life in the Second Temple and late antique periods. The Mandaean tradition claims roots in Jewish baptismal communities, and this section's treatment of Yahia's Disputes with the World contains linguistic, theological, and ritual elements that shed light on the varieties of Judaism that existed alongside and beyond the rabbinic mainstream.

The section's references to Jewish institutions, practices, and figures — however polemically distorted — provide evidence for the complex religious landscape of ancient Palestine and Mesopotamia. Jewish scholars examine how the Mandaean text reinterprets or inverts Jewish theological categories: the Jordan River as a site of divine revelation (rather than the Temple), baptism as the supreme ritual act (rather than sacrifice), and Yahia as the final prophet (rather than Moses or Elijah). These inversions illuminate the process of sectarian differentiation through which distinct religious communities emerged from a shared Jewish matrix.

## Key Themes

- Second Temple sectarian diversity and its legacy
- Mandaean-Jewish linguistic connections through Aramaic
- Baptismal movements in ancient Judaism
- Polemical reinterpretation of Jewish institutions and figures
- The parting of ways between rabbinic Judaism and Mandaeism

## Key Passages

> "Yahia did not go up to Jerusalem, nor did he offer sacrifice in the Temple of the Jews. He stood in the living water of the Jordan and taught the true worship of the Great Life."

Jewish scholars read this passage as reflecting a sectarian critique of the Jerusalem Temple cult familiar from other Second Temple texts, including the Dead Sea Scrolls. The privileging of water ritual over Temple sacrifice aligns with the practices of various Jewish baptist groups known from ancient sources.

## Commentary Tradition

Jewish scholarship on the Drasha d-Yahia is informed by broader studies of Second Temple Judaism and its sectarian diversity. Key works include those by Joseph Lightfoot, Samuel Krauss, and more recently scholars working at the intersection of Mandaean studies and Jewish studies such as Nathaniel Deutsch and John C. Reeves.
