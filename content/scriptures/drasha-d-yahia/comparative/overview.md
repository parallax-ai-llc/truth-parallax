## Overview

The Drasha d-Yahia provides exceptionally rich material for comparative study, presenting an alternative scriptural tradition centered on a figure — John the Baptist — who appears in Christian, Islamic, and Mandaean sacred texts. Comparative scholars examine how the same historical figure is constructed differently across these traditions: as forerunner to Christ in Christianity, as the prophet Yahya in Islam, and as the supreme prophet of Light in Mandaeism.

The text's baptismal theology invites comparison with Christian baptism, Jewish *mikveh* practices, and the lustrations described in the Dead Sea Scrolls. The Drasha d-Yahia's cosmological framework — with its dualism of Light and Darkness, its celestial intermediaries (*uthras*), and its emphasis on salvific knowledge — is compared with Gnostic texts from Nag Hammadi, Manichaean scriptures, and Zoroastrian cosmology. The polemical passages offer a rare non-Christian perspective on the origins of Christianity, valuable for understanding how new religious movements define themselves against their competitors.
