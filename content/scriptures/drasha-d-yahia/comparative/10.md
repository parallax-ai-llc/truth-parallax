## Comparative Reading

Comparative study of Drasha d-Yahia Section 10, "The Death of Yahia," situates this text within the broader landscape of late antique religious literature and the history of prophetic traditions. The Mandaean account of The Death of Yahia invites comparison with parallel narratives in the New Testament Gospels, the Quran, Josephus's *Antiquities*, and apocryphal Christian and Jewish texts. These comparisons reveal how the same historical figures and events are refracted through different theological lenses, producing strikingly different sacred narratives.

The section's theological themes — baptism as salvific act, the prophet as divine messenger, the struggle between truth and falsehood — find parallels across Gnostic, Christian, Islamic, and Jewish traditions. Comparative scholars resist the temptation to reduce these parallels to simple borrowing, instead analyzing the shared cultural and theological grammar that enabled diverse communities in the ancient Near East to construct distinctive sacred histories from common elements. The Mandaean voice represented in this section enriches the comparative study of Abrahamic and Gnostic religions by providing a perspective that is neither Jewish, Christian, nor Islamic but deeply connected to all three.

## Key Themes

- Parallel accounts of John the Baptist across Mandaean, Christian, and Islamic traditions
- Baptismal theology compared across religious traditions
- Prophetic commissioning narratives: Mandaean, biblical, and Quranic patterns
- Inter-religious polemic as a vehicle for community self-definition
- Shared Aramaic cultural heritage across sectarian boundaries

## Key Passages

> "Yahia said: 'I am not of this world, nor is my teaching from the priests of Jerusalem. My authority comes from the Great Life alone.'"

This declaration of prophetic independence finds parallels in Jesus's sayings in the Fourth Gospel ("My kingdom is not of this world") and in Muhammad's prophetic commissioning narratives, suggesting a shared Near Eastern pattern of prophetic legitimation through claims of direct divine authorization.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholarship draws on the work of scholars who bridge Mandaean studies with broader religious studies: Kurt Rudolph, Jorunn Buckley, Nathaniel Deutsch, and Edmondo Lupieri. The emerging field of comparative Gnostic studies provides new frameworks for understanding the Drasha d-Yahia alongside Nag Hammadi texts, Manichaean literature, and Zoroastrian sources.
