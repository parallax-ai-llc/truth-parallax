Amos 6 denounces the ruling elites who feast in luxury while ignoring the moral collapse around them. Orthodox ascetical theology recognizes the spiritual danger of prosperity: material comfort can deaden the soul's sensitivity to divine promptings and to the suffering of neighbors. The monastic tradition of voluntary simplicity and fasting is, in part, a response to this prophetic warning.

The specific critique that the wealthy "are not grieved over the ruin of Joseph" (6:6) addresses the sin of apatheia in its negative sense — not the healthy detachment from passions taught by the Fathers, but the deadening of moral feeling that makes one indifferent to others' suffering. True *apatheia* in the Orthodox tradition does not eliminate compassion but purifies it, freeing the soul from disordered passions so that authentic love can operate unobstructed.

The announcement that the complacent will "be the first to go into exile" (6:7) illustrates the Orthodox principle of proportional accountability: those who have received most are expected to give most. The wealthy and powerful bear greater responsibility for the common good, and their failure to exercise this responsibility brings correspondingly severe consequences.
