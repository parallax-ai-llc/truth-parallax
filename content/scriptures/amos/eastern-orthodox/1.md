Amos 1 establishes God's universal sovereignty through oracles against the nations surrounding Israel. Orthodox theology reads these judgments as evidence that God's moral governance extends to all peoples, not merely those under the Mosaic covenant. Cyril of Alexandria interpreted the oracles as demonstrating that divine justice operates according to the natural moral law implanted in all human hearts — a law that even pagans can recognize and are held accountable to violate.

The pattern "for three transgressions and for four" indicates the exhaustion of divine patience. Orthodox theology understands this not as an arbitrary limit but as the accumulated weight of unrepented sin reaching a point where the therapeutic purpose of further patience is exhausted. God's judgment is always the last resort, never the first option, but it is a genuine possibility when every invitation to repentance has been refused.

The prophet's identity as a shepherd from Tekoa, called from ordinary life to deliver God's word, illustrates the Orthodox conviction that divine calling transcends social and professional boundaries. The holy fools, desert hermits, and simple monks who became great teachers of the Church exemplify this same principle: God's Spirit blows where it wills, raising up prophetic voices from unexpected quarters.
