Amos 9 opens with the terrifying vision of the altar's destruction and God's inescapable pursuit of the guilty. Orthodox theology reads this through the lens of Psalm 139: God's omnipresence is simultaneously comfort and judgment, depending on the spiritual condition of the person encountered. For the repentant, God's nearness is salvation; for the impenitent, it is terror.

The "sifting" oracle (9:9-10) provides an Old Testament basis for the Orthodox understanding of the Church's passage through tribulation. The genuine members of the community are preserved through judgment like wheat through a sieve, while the chaff is separated and discarded. This image resonates with the Orthodox experience of persecution — from the Roman era through the Soviet period — and the conviction that authentic faith survives and is even purified by adversity.

The restoration promise (9:11-15) is read christologically in Orthodox tradition, following the apostolic interpretation in Acts 15. The "booth of David" raised up is the Church — the renewed Israel that includes all nations — and the abundant harvest imagery anticipates the eschatological renewal of creation. The Orthodox icon of the heavenly feast, depicted in churches around the world, gives visual expression to the vision Amos proclaims: the restored community gathered around the table of God's abundance.
