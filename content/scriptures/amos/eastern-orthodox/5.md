Amos 5 contains the prophetic tradition's most powerful statement of the relationship between worship and justice. The funeral lament over Israel (5:1-3) models the prophetic practice of mourning for the living dead — those who are physically alive but spiritually deceased. Orthodox funeral hymns echo this paradox: "In the midst of life we are in death," and the call to repentance is always a call from death to life.

The rejection of worship divorced from justice (5:21-24) is foundational for Orthodox liturgical theology. The Divine Liturgy is not a self-contained ritual but the source and summit of a life of justice and mercy. When the liturgical community fails to embody the values celebrated in worship — when the offering is accompanied by oppression — the worship itself becomes an offense. John Chrysostom's homilies on this theme are among the most forceful in patristic literature.

The reinterpretation of the Day of the LORD as darkness rather than light (5:18-20) challenges complacent eschatological expectation. Orthodox eschatology holds the tension between hope and judgment: the return of Christ will be salvation for those who have cooperated with divine grace through lives of repentance and charity, but judgment for those who have presumed upon grace without transformation.
