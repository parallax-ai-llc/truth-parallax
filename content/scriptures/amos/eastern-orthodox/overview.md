## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Amos draws on the patristic commentaries and integrates the prophet's message into the broader Orthodox theology of creation, justice, and divine pedagogy. The Greek Fathers read Amos not merely as a social critic but as a theologian of divine sovereignty whose message reveals the inseparable connection between liturgical worship and ethical life.

## Patristic Heritage

**Cyril of Alexandria** commented extensively on Amos, reading the oracles against the nations as demonstrations of God's universal sovereignty — not merely Israel's God, but the Lord of all peoples and all history. **Theodoret of Cyrus** emphasized the pedagogical purpose of judgment: God's punishments are not vindictive but corrective, aimed at turning hearts back to righteousness. The confrontation with Amaziah was read as a paradigm for the independence of prophetic authority from institutional religious power.

## Theological Significance

Orthodox theology draws on Amos's doxologies (4:13, 5:8-9, 9:5-6) — hymnic passages celebrating God as Creator — as evidence that prophetic ethics are rooted in a theology of creation. Justice is not an arbitrary divine command but flows from the very nature of the God who made the Pleiades and Orion. The Orthodox reading insists that social justice and liturgical worship are not alternatives but two dimensions of a single reality: authentic worship transforms worshippers into agents of justice, while injustice renders worship an offense to God.
