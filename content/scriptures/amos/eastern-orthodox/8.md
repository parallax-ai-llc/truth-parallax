Amos 8 develops the theme of impending judgment through the vision of summer fruit and the detailed catalogue of commercial injustice. Orthodox moral theology engages the specific charges — false measures, exploitation of the poor, commodification of human beings — as violations of the image of God in every person. Each act of economic injustice defaces the divine icon that every human being carries.

The prophecy of cosmic mourning — the sun going down at noon, feasts turning to lamentation (8:9-10) — has been read by Orthodox interpreters as a prophecy of the crucifixion, when darkness covered the land from the sixth to the ninth hour (Mark 15:33). The cosmic mourning that Amos describes finds its ultimate fulfillment in creation's response to the death of its Creator — a reading consistent with the Orthodox understanding of the cross as a cosmic event.

The "famine of the word of the LORD" (8:11-12) represents, in Orthodox theology, the most severe form of divine judgment. The withdrawal of divine communication leaves humanity without the means of salvation. Orthodox liturgical life — the reading of Scripture, the preaching of the Word, the eucharistic anaphora — is designed to ensure that the faithful are never deprived of the nourishment of God's word, and the famine Amos describes serves as a warning against any complacency in this regard.
