Amos 2 brings the oracles against the nations to their devastating climax with the indictment of Israel itself. Orthodox theology emphasizes the rhetorical artistry of Amos's approach: having won the audience's agreement to the justice of God's judgment on others, he turns the same standard upon them. The Fathers saw in this a principle of spiritual self-examination — the tendency to see others' sins clearly while remaining blind to one's own.

The specific charges against Israel — selling the righteous for silver, trampling the poor, desecrating the sanctuaries — represent violations of both justice and holiness. Orthodox moral theology insists on the unity of ethical and liturgical life: to exploit the poor and then enter God's sanctuary is not merely hypocritical but sacrilegious, because it treats the holy space as disconnected from the moral order it represents.

The recollection of God's saving acts — the Exodus, the wilderness, the gift of the land (2:9-11) — serves as a reminder that Israel's identity is constituted by divine grace, not human achievement. Orthodox theology of gratitude (*eucharistia*) finds here the foundational principle: right relationship with God begins with the acknowledgment of gifts received, and the corruption of that gratitude into entitlement is the root of moral failure.
