Amos 4 opens with the confrontation of the wealthy women of Samaria and moves through a devastating catalogue of rejected divine disciplines. Orthodox theology reads the five-fold refrain "yet you did not return to me" as a description of the progressive hardening of the heart — each refused invitation to repentance makes the next refusal easier and the eventual judgment more severe.

The Fathers recognized in this pattern the dynamic of *therapeia* (divine healing): God applies increasingly severe remedies to a patient whose disease resists treatment. Famine, drought, blight, plague, and military defeat are not punitive ends in themselves but medicinal interventions aimed at restoring spiritual health. The tragedy is not that God sends affliction but that affliction fails to achieve its therapeutic purpose because of human obstinacy.

The doxology of 4:13 — celebrating God as creator of mountains, wind, and dawn — grounds prophetic judgment in the theology of creation. Orthodox theology insists that the God who judges is the God who creates, and that moral obligation flows from the structure of reality itself. To violate justice is to violate the created order, and the consequences are as natural and inevitable as the physical laws governing the cosmos.
