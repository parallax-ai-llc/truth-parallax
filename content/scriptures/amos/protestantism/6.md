Amos 6 pronounces woe on the complacent elites of both Zion (Jerusalem) and Samaria — those who "lie on beds of ivory and stretch themselves out on their couches" while remaining indifferent to "the ruin of Joseph" (6:4-6). Protestant social ethics identifies this as the sin of affluent indifference: enjoying luxury while ignoring the suffering of the community. The passage has been foundational for Protestant critiques of consumerism and systemic inequality.

The specific luxuries catalogued — ivory beds, choice meats, wine by the bowlful, finest oils (6:4-6) — are not condemned as inherently sinful but as markers of a lifestyle built on exploitation and deaf to the cries of the oppressed. Protestant preachers have consistently applied this principle: wealth itself is not the problem, but wealth that numbs moral sensitivity and insulates the comfortable from the consequences of injustice.

The chapter concludes with the announcement that exile awaits: "they shall now be the first of those who go into exile" (6:7). Protestant theology reads this as the principle of proportional judgment — those who enjoyed the greatest privileges bear the greatest responsibility, and their judgment will be correspondingly severe. This principle extends beyond the historical context to apply to any community of faith that substitutes comfort for covenant faithfulness.
