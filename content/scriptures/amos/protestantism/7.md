Amos 7 contains three vision reports and the dramatic confrontation between Amos and Amaziah the priest of Bethel. The first two visions — locusts and fire — are averted by the prophet's intercession: "O Lord GOD, please cease! How can Jacob stand?" (7:5). Protestant interpreters note the intercessory dimension of the prophetic office — the prophet not only announces judgment but stands in the gap, pleading for mercy.

The third vision — the plumb line — marks a turning point: God will no longer pass by Israel's crookedness. Protestant theology reads the plumb line as a symbol of divine standards that cannot be bent to accommodate human preferences. The Reformers saw in this image a justification for measuring all church practice and doctrine against the straight standard of Scripture.

The confrontation with Amaziah (7:10-17) pits prophetic authority against institutional religious power. Amaziah's command — "O seer, go, flee away to the land of Judah... but never again prophesy at Bethel, for it is the king's sanctuary" (7:12-13) — reveals the subordination of religion to political power. Amos's reply — "I am no prophet, nor a prophet's son; but I am a herdsman" (7:14) — established the principle, central to Protestant ecclesiology, that divine calling transcends institutional credentialing.
