Amos 4 begins with the shocking address: "Hear this word, you cows of Bashan" (4:1) — directed at the wealthy women of Samaria who demand luxury from their husbands at the expense of the poor. Protestant interpreters note Amos's willingness to confront every sector of society, including those who participate in oppression indirectly through their consumption patterns and demands for comfort.

The central section (4:6-11) catalogs a series of divine disciplines — famine, drought, blight, plague, military defeat — each followed by the devastating refrain: "yet you did not return to me, declares the LORD." Protestant theology draws from this passage the principle that God uses providential discipline to call his people to repentance, and that the refusal to respond to escalating warnings leads to final judgment.

The chapter concludes with the doxology: "For behold, he who forms the mountains and creates the wind... the LORD, the God of hosts, is his name" (4:13). Protestant commentators note that Amos grounds his ethical demands in creation theology — the God who demands justice is the God who made the universe, and his moral authority is as absolute as his creative power.
