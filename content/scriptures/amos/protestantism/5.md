Amos 5 contains some of the most quoted and theologically significant verses in the prophetic canon. The chapter opens with a funeral lament for Israel: "Fallen, no more to rise, is the virgin Israel" (5:2). Protestant interpreters debate whether this is an absolute pronouncement or a conditional warning — if Israel does not repent, the fall is irreversible.

The call "Seek me and live" (5:4) offers the alternative to judgment, but it is immediately qualified: seeking God means seeking justice, not performing rituals at Bethel, Gilgal, or Beersheba. The passage 5:21-24 — "I hate, I despise your festivals... But let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream" — is the definitive prophetic statement on the relationship between worship and ethics. Martin Luther King Jr.'s citation of this verse in his "I Have a Dream" speech cemented its place in the Protestant moral imagination.

Amos's reinterpretation of the "Day of the LORD" (5:18-20) as darkness rather than light overturns popular eschatological expectation. Protestant theology draws from this the warning that divine judgment may fall on the complacent faithful as severely as on the openly wicked. Those who eagerly await God's intervention may discover that they themselves are its target.
