Amos 3 opens with one of the most significant theological statements in the prophetic literature: "You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities" (3:2). Protestant theology draws from this verse the principle that election brings greater accountability, not immunity from judgment. The doctrine of covenant responsibility runs directly counter to any presumption of unconditional security.

The series of rhetorical questions in 3:3-8 establishes the logic of prophetic causation: effects have causes, and the prophet speaks because God has spoken. "The Lord GOD has spoken; who can but prophesy?" (3:8). Protestant understanding of the prophetic calling draws from this passage — the prophet is not self-appointed but compelled by divine commission, and the prophetic word is not human opinion but divine communication.

The concluding oracle against Samaria's palaces (3:9-15) targets the wealthy elite who have built their prosperity on oppression. The command to assemble "on the mountains of Samaria" and witness the "great tumults" and "oppressions" within the city invites even Israel's enemies to see the injustice. Protestant social critique has found in this passage a mandate for exposing systemic injustice, regardless of its institutional protectors.
