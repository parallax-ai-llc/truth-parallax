Amos 2 continues the oracles against the nations, condemning Edom, Ammon, Moab, and Judah before finally turning to Israel with the longest and most detailed indictment. Protestant interpreters note the trap Amos has sprung: his northern Israelite audience, having cheered the condemnation of their rivals, must now hear their own sentence pronounced with devastating specificity.

The charges against Israel are primarily social: selling the righteous for silver, trampling the poor, denying justice to the afflicted, sexual exploitation at the shrines (2:6-8). Protestant social ethics draws from this passage the principle that economic oppression is as offensive to God as idolatry — indeed, in Amos's theology, they are inseparable. The God who liberated Israel from Egypt (2:10) will not tolerate the replication of Egyptian oppression within the covenant community.

The chapter concludes with the chilling announcement that neither the swift, the strong, the warrior, the archer, the horseman, nor the hero will escape the coming judgment (2:14-16). Protestant preaching has used this passage to emphasize that no human resource — wealth, power, military might — can provide refuge from divine justice.
