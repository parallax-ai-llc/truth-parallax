## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Amos has been profoundly influential in shaping the tradition's understanding of social justice as integral to authentic faith. From the Reformers to the civil rights movement, Amos's insistence that worship without justice is an abomination has served as a prophetic corrective against religious complacency.

## Reformation and Post-Reformation Reading

**Calvin** emphasized Amos's doctrine of divine sovereignty — that God's judgment extends to all nations, not merely Israel. The shepherd-prophet's confrontation with Amaziah (7:10-17) became a paradigm for Protestant resistance to ecclesiastical authority that suppresses prophetic truth. **Luther** found in Amos's critique of empty ritual (5:21-24) a precursor to the Reformation's rejection of works-righteousness. The plumb line vision (7:7-9) illustrated that God's standard is objective and unchanging, aligning with the Protestant emphasis on Scripture as the sole norm.

## Modern Protestant Engagement

Amos 5:24 — "Let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream" — became the defining text of the social gospel movement and the civil rights era. Martin Luther King Jr.'s frequent citation of Amos placed the prophet at the center of modern Protestant ethical discourse. Evangelical scholars have increasingly recognized that Amos's message integrates personal piety with structural justice, challenging the tendency to separate evangelism from social concern.
