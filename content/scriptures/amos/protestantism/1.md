Amos 1 opens the book with a series of oracles against Israel's neighbors — Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, and others. Each oracle follows the same literary pattern: "For three transgressions... and for four, I will not revoke the punishment." Protestant interpreters note the rhetorical sophistication of this structure: Amos begins with nations his Israelite audience would eagerly condemn before turning the spotlight on Israel itself in chapter 2.

The specific sins condemned — war crimes, slave trading, covenant betrayal — establish that God holds all nations accountable for basic moral standards, not merely those under the Mosaic covenant. This principle has been foundational for Protestant political theology and the development of concepts like natural law and universal human rights rooted in creation rather than special revelation.

The superscription identifies Amos as "among the shepherds of Tekoa" who prophesied during Uzziah's and Jeroboam II's reigns — a period of exceptional prosperity in both Judah and Israel. Protestant commentators emphasize that economic success masked deep moral and spiritual decay, a pattern they frequently apply to contemporary affluent societies.
