Amos 4 opens with the provocative address to the "cows of Bashan" and continues with the catalogue of divine disciplines. The Gnostic reader finds the fivefold refrain "yet you did not return to me" deeply revealing: the Demiurge has used every weapon in his arsenal — famine, drought, plague, military defeat — and none has produced the submission he demands. The archontic system of punishment-as-discipline is fundamentally broken because it addresses symptoms rather than causes.

The escalating severity of punishments — from crop failure to plague to military destruction — reveals the Demiurge's frustration. Each failed attempt provokes a more violent intervention, demonstrating that the archontic god's only tools are fear and pain. The true God of the Pleroma does not coerce through suffering but attracts through the beauty of gnosis. The contrast could not be starker.

The doxology of 4:13 — "He who forms the mountains and creates the wind" — is the Demiurge's assertion of creative authority, his claim that the power to create confers the right to punish. The Gnostic reader notes the implicit threat: the god who made the mountains can also flatten them. Creative power deployed as intimidation reveals the archontic character of this deity — might makes right within the material order, but the pneumatic realm operates by entirely different principles.
