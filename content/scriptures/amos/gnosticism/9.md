Amos 9 opens with the most terrifying image in the book: God standing beside the altar, commanding destruction, and pursuing the guilty to the depths of Sheol and the heights of heaven (9:1-4). The Gnostic reader sees the Demiurge at his most oppressive — the inescapable ruler from whom no corner of creation provides refuge. This is the archontic nightmare: a closed universe with no exit, governed by an all-seeing, all-punishing deity.

The "sifting" oracle (9:9-10) offers a more nuanced picture: not everyone will be destroyed; the divine process will separate the genuine from the false. The Gnostic reader finds here a faint echo of the pneumatic-hylic distinction: the "grain" that is preserved represents the divine sparks that will survive the material world's dissolution, while the "sinners" who fall represent the hylic elements that will perish with the creation.

The restoration promise (9:11-15) presents the greatest interpretive challenge for Gnostic reading. If the "fallen booth of David" is merely the archontic political order restored, the promise offers nothing to the pneumatic seeker. But if David's "booth" symbolizes something more — a sheltering presence that transcends the Demiurge's domain — then the promise of eternal planting and abundant harvest may point, beyond the prophet's intention, toward the Pleroma's ultimate triumph: the reintegration of all scattered light into the fullness of the true divine realm.
