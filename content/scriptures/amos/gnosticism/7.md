Amos 7 presents the prophet's visions and his confrontation with religious authority. The first two visions — locusts and fire averted by intercession — reveal the Demiurge's responsiveness to his prophet's appeals, suggesting that the archontic system is not entirely inflexible. The Gnostic reader notes, however, that the intercession merely delays judgment; it does not address the underlying problem of a world governed by a wrathful deity.

The plumb line vision introduces the concept of divine measurement — the Demiurge's absolute standard against which all creation is evaluated and found wanting. In Gnostic terms, the plumb line represents the archontic law that no creature can satisfy, ensuring permanent guilt and dependence on the system. The true divine reality is not a measurement but a gift — gnosis is not earned by straightness but received by awakening.

The Amos-Amaziah confrontation is rich material for Gnostic reflection. Amaziah represents the fully institutionalized archontic religion — the priesthood that serves the king, the sanctuary that serves the state. Amos's declaration "I am no prophet" (7:14) — his refusal of institutional identity — resonates with the Gnostic suspicion of all religious establishments. Truth comes from outside the system, from the desert and the sheep pens, not from the temple bureaucracy.
