Amos 8 announces the "end" through a wordplay that the Gnostic reader finds richly suggestive. The summer fruit (*qayits*) that signals the end (*qets*) represents the ripeness of the material world — a creation that has reached the limit of what it can produce and now faces dissolution. The Gnostic eschatological hope is precisely this: the material order will reach its endpoint, and the divine sparks it contains will be liberated.

The indictment of fraudulent commerce (8:4-6) reveals the material world's fundamental dishonesty — even the basic instruments of exchange (weights, measures, currency) are corrupted. The Gnostic conclusion: in a world made by a deceiver, deception permeates every level of existence. The merchants who cheat their customers are merely replicating the Demiurge's original fraud — presenting a flawed creation as a good world.

The "famine of the word of the LORD" (8:11-12) is, in Gnostic interpretation, the condition of the material world itself — a realm starved of genuine divine communication. The Demiurge's "word" is not the true Logos but a counterfeit that keeps its hearers in ignorance. The real famine is not the absence of archontic prophecy but the suppression of gnosis — the living knowledge that flows from the Pleroma and can free the imprisoned spark.
