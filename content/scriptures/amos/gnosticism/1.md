Amos 1 presents the Demiurge asserting his jurisdiction over the nations — roaring "from Zion" like a lion over its territorial domain. The Gnostic reader notes that the oracles against the nations reveal the Demiurge's concern with the material world's power politics: wars, slave trades, territorial disputes. This is the domain of the archons, and the Demiurge's "justice" operates entirely within this material framework, unable to address the deeper spiritual enslavement of the divine sparks trapped in flesh.

The formula "for three transgressions and for four" reveals the Demiurge's accounting system — he keeps meticulous records of offenses, dispensing measured portions of wrath. This legalistic precision characterizes the archontic system: every violation is catalogued, every punishment precisely calculated. The true God of the Pleroma, by contrast, does not operate through retributive bookkeeping but through the free gift of gnosis.

The prophet Amos, a shepherd called from the wilderness, presents an ambiguous figure for Gnostic interpretation. On one hand, he serves as the Demiurge's mouthpiece, channeling archontic judgment. On the other hand, his outsider status — not a professional prophet, not trained in the temple system — suggests the possibility that genuine truth can emerge from outside the archontic institutional framework.
