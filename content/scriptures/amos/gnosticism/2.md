Amos 2 brings the oracles to their climax with the indictment of Israel — the Demiurge's own chosen people. The Gnostic reader finds irony in the Demiurge's complaint: the people he selected, liberated, and settled in the land have proven as corrupt as the surrounding nations. The archontic system of election and covenant has failed to produce the obedient subjects the Demiurge demands.

The charges against Israel — selling the righteous, trampling the poor, sexual exploitation at shrines — reveal the dysfunction of the material world the Demiurge has created. Even his "chosen people," operating under the most detailed legal code in the ancient world, cannot resist the gravitational pull of the passions that govern material existence. The Gnostic conclusion: the problem is not insufficient law but the corrupt nature of material existence itself.

The recollection of divine saving acts (2:9-11) functions as the Demiurge's self-justification: "Look what I have done for you!" This is the possessive claim of the creator over his creation — the demand for gratitude that justifies punishment when gratitude is not forthcoming. The Gnostic reader recognizes this pattern from the Apocryphon of John, where Yaldabaoth boasts of his creative acts while demanding worship from beings who owe their true existence to a source beyond him.
