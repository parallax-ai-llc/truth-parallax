## Overview

Gnostic interpretation of Amos finds unexpected resonance in the prophet's blistering critique of institutional religion and social oppression. While Amos himself directs this critique toward Israel's failure to worship God rightly, Gnostic readers can invert the frame: the entire system of temple worship, sacrifice, and covenant obligation represents the Demiurge's apparatus of control, and Amos's prophetic rage exposes its inherent corruption.

## The Critique of Cult as Gnostic Insight

Amos 5:21-24 — "I hate, I despise your festivals... But let justice roll down like waters" — becomes, in a Gnostic reading, a revelation that the Demiurge's own cult is bankrupt. The prophet unknowingly testifies against the religious system he serves. The visions of judgment (chapters 7-9) reveal the Demiurge's destructive rage when his creation fails to conform to his will, exposing the fundamental violence underlying the material order.

## The Shepherd-Prophet and Gnosis

Amos's identity as an outsider — "I am no prophet, nor a prophet's son" (7:14) — resonates with the Gnostic suspicion of institutional religious authority. True knowledge does not come through established channels but breaks in from outside the system. The doxologies praising the God who "made the Pleiades and Orion" (5:8) present a tension: is this the Demiurge boasting of his creation, or a glimpse of the true God whose cosmic scope transcends the petty jealousies of the creator? Gnostic readers would find in Amos both the Demiurge's voice and, hidden beneath it, traces of a higher truth that the prophet himself may not fully comprehend.
