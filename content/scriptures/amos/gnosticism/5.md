Amos 5, with its thunderous demand for justice and its rejection of empty worship, presents a paradox for Gnostic interpretation. On one hand, the call "Seek me and live" is the Demiurge's demand for renewed submission. On the other hand, the rejection of ritual — "I hate, I despise your festivals" (5:21) — undermines the very system the Demiurge has established, creating an opening for a deeper understanding of what it means to "seek" the divine.

The funeral lament over Israel (5:1-3) — "Fallen, no more to rise, is the virgin Israel" — can be read as the Demiurge pronouncing death on his own creation. The Gnostic reader finds a strange mercy in this: the death of the archontic order may be the prerequisite for the birth of something genuinely new. The "virgin Israel" — the system of covenant religion — must die so that the pneumatic reality it obscured can emerge.

The reinterpretation of the Day of the LORD as darkness (5:18-20) is, paradoxically, closer to the Gnostic vision than the popular expectation of divine vindication. The Day of the LORD, in Gnostic terms, is the Demiurge's ultimate assertion of power — a day of archontic triumph that is indeed darkness for the pneumatic soul. Those who eagerly await the Demiurge's victory do not realize that it means the perpetuation of their own enslavement.
