Amos 6 condemns the luxurious complacency of the ruling classes. The Gnostic reader recognizes in this portrait the hylic life in its fullest expression: bodies at ease on ivory beds, appetites satisfied with choice food and wine, senses dulled by music and perfume. The hylic person is not evil in the conventional sense but simply unconscious — so deeply embedded in material existence that the possibility of spiritual awakening never arises.

The charge that the comfortable "are not grieved over the ruin of Joseph" (6:6) applies at the metaphysical level: those absorbed in material pleasure cannot perceive the catastrophe of the pneumatic condition — the divine spark imprisoned in flesh, suffocating under layers of material distraction. The Demiurge condemns this indifference, but from the Gnostic perspective, the entire material system is designed to produce exactly this condition of spiritual anesthesia.

The promise that the complacent will be "first to go into exile" (6:7) represents, in Gnostic reading, the archontic system consuming its own. The hylic rulers who have most fully embraced the Demiurge's material order are the first to be destroyed by it. The irony is structural: the system rewards those who most completely accept its terms, only to destroy them when they have served their purpose.
