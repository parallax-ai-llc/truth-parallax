## Overview

Amos is one of the earliest writing prophets, active in the mid-eighth century BCE during the reign of Jeroboam II of Israel. A shepherd and dresser of sycamore trees from Tekoa in Judah, Amos was called by God to prophesy against the northern kingdom of Israel. His message is one of the most uncompromising in the Hebrew Bible: social injustice and religious hypocrisy will bring divine judgment, and neither election nor ritual observance can substitute for righteousness.

## Structure

1. **Oracles Against the Nations** (Chapters 1–2): Judgment against Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon, Moab, Judah, and finally Israel itself
2. **Prophetic Speeches** (Chapters 3–6): Three discourses introduced by "Hear this word," addressing Israel's privilege, corruption, and false worship
3. **Visions of Judgment** (Chapters 7–9): Five visions — locusts, fire, plumb line, summer fruit, and the shattered sanctuary — interspersed with the confrontation with Amaziah and concluding with a promise of restoration

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: Amos's emphasis on justice (*mishpat*) and righteousness (*tsedaqah*) profoundly shaped prophetic ethics; 5:24 is among the most quoted prophetic verses
- **Christianity**: Martin Luther King Jr. famously cited Amos 5:24; the book grounds the prophetic tradition of social justice within Christian ethics
- **Islam**: The prophetic pattern of a humble man called to confront powerful elites resonates with the Quranic portrayal of prophets like Shu'ayb and Musa
- **Gnosticism**: The critique of institutional religion and external ritual can be read through a Gnostic lens as exposing the Demiurge's empty cult

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Social Justice | The inseparable link between worship and ethical treatment of the poor |
| Judgment | Divine punishment for covenant violations, especially oppression of the vulnerable |
| Election and Responsibility | Israel's chosen status increases rather than excuses accountability |
| Day of the LORD | Recast as a day of darkness, not light, for complacent Israel |
| Remnant | A small surviving group through whom God's purposes continue |
