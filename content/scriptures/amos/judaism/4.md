Amos 4:1 — "Hear this word, you cows of Bashan" — is one of the most provocative addresses in prophetic literature. Rashi explains that the reference is to the wealthy women of Samaria who drove their husbands to oppress the poor in order to sustain their luxurious lifestyles. The Midrash draws from this the principle that those who benefit from injustice share the guilt of those who commit it, even if they do not directly participate.

The catalogue of divine disciplines (4:6-11) — famine, drought, blight, plague, and devastation — followed by the refrain "yet you did not return to me" reflects a theme central to Jewish theology of *teshuvah*. God sends warnings before judgment, providing opportunities for repentance. The failure to respond is not God's failure but Israel's, and the prophetic record of rejected warnings serves as evidence in the divine lawsuit.

The doxology of 4:13 is one of three hymnic passages in Amos (also 5:8-9, 9:5-6) that celebrate God as Creator. Jewish liturgical tradition incorporates similar doxological language in daily prayers, affirming that the God who demands ethical behavior is the God who creates and sustains the universe. The connection between creation and ethics is fundamental to Jewish moral theology: the moral law is as foundational to reality as the physical laws that govern the cosmos.
