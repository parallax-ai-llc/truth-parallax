Amos 6 pronounces woe upon the complacent leadership of both Zion and Samaria. Rashi connects this oracle to the historical period of Jeroboam II's prosperity, when military success and economic growth masked deep social and spiritual decay. The rabbis drew from this a general principle: national prosperity is a dangerous time for the soul because it fosters the illusion that all is well.

The catalogue of luxury (6:4-6) — reclining on ivory beds, eating choice lambs, singing idle songs, drinking wine from bowls — is not condemned as inherently sinful. Jewish tradition permits the enjoyment of God's gifts. The sin is being "not grieved over the ruin of Joseph" (6:6) — the combination of luxury with indifference to communal suffering. The Talmudic concept of *sinat hinam* (baseless hatred) includes this form of callous disregard for others' pain.

The announcement that the "first to go into exile" will be those currently "at ease in Zion" (6:1, 7) inverts the social hierarchy: those at the top will fall the farthest. Jewish historical experience — the destruction of both Temples, the various exiles — has confirmed this prophetic insight: when judgment falls, the privileged and powerful are the most vulnerable because they have the most to lose and have built the least spiritual resilience.
