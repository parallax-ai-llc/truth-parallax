## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Amos occupies a crucial place among the Twelve Minor Prophets (*Trei Asar*). The Talmud (Makkot 24a) records that Amos reduced the entire Torah to a single principle: "Seek me and live" (5:4). His uncompromising demand for justice (*mishpat*) and righteousness (*tsedaqah*) profoundly shaped the prophetic ethical tradition within Judaism.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

**Rashi** explained Amos's oracles against the nations by noting that God judges all peoples according to basic moral standards, but holds Israel to a higher standard because of the covenant. The famous declaration "You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities" (3:2) became a foundational text for the rabbinic concept that election brings greater responsibility, not privilege. **Ibn Ezra** focused on Amos's rhetorical skill, analyzing the literary structure of the oracles as a deliberate progression designed to draw in the audience before delivering the devastating climax against Israel itself.

## Ethical and Liturgical Significance

Amos 5:24 — "Let justice roll down like waters" — is among the most quoted verses in Jewish ethical discourse. The prophetic insistence that ritual without ethics is meaningless shaped the rabbinic emphasis on *gemilut hasadim* (acts of lovingkindness) alongside *avodah* (worship). Amos's vision of the fallen booth of David restored (9:11) became an important messianic text, read as a promise of the Davidic dynasty's renewal. Several passages from Amos serve as haftarah readings, connecting the prophet's message to the weekly Torah cycle.
