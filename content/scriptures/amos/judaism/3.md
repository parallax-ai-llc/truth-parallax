Amos 3:2 — "You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you" — is a foundational text for the Jewish understanding of election and its consequences. The Talmud (Avodah Zarah 4a) discusses this verse in the context of theodicy: Israel's suffering in exile is not evidence of divine abandonment but of divine accountability. Greater intimacy with God brings greater responsibility, and failure to meet that responsibility brings correspondingly severe discipline.

The rhetorical questions of 3:3-8 — "Do two walk together unless they have agreed?" — are interpreted by Rashi as establishing that every effect has a cause, and therefore the prophet's speech has a divine cause. The rabbis extended this principle: nothing in the world happens without divine involvement, and the prophet's role is to make that involvement visible to a community that has become spiritually blind.

The announcement of judgment against Samaria's luxury (3:9-15) targets the "houses of ivory" and multiple residences of the wealthy. Jewish ethical tradition draws from Amos a sustained critique of conspicuous wealth achieved at others' expense. The Talmud discusses the obligations of the wealthy toward the poor, and Amos provides the prophetic foundation for these rabbinic teachings.
