Amos 2 completes the oracles against the nations and delivers the climactic indictment of Israel. Jewish interpreters have long appreciated the rhetorical brilliance of this sequence: Amos first condemns Israel's enemies to gain approval, then condemns Judah to demonstrate impartiality, and finally turns on Israel with a devastating bill of particulars. Rashi notes that each foreign nation is condemned for a specific, egregious crime, while Israel's indictment lists multiple offenses, indicating deeper corruption.

The charges against Israel — selling the righteous for silver, trampling the poor, perverting justice — are violations of specific Torah commandments. Jewish legal tradition (*halakhah*) takes these prophetic denunciations as interpretive guides to the Torah's ethical requirements. The charge of "selling the righteous for silver" (2:6) is connected to the prohibition against perverting justice through bribery (Deuteronomy 16:19).

The recollection of God's saving deeds (2:9-11) — the conquest, the Exodus, the gift of prophets and Nazirites — functions as a covenantal *ribonot* (bill of grievance), establishing the grounds for the lawsuit. Jewish theology emphasizes that covenant violation is doubly grievous because it represents ingratitude: the people who received everything from God now turn against the very institutions God established.
