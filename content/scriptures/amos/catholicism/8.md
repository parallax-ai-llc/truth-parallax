Amos 8 opens with the wordplay vision of summer fruit (*qayits*) / end (*qets*), a prophetic device that Catholic biblical scholarship appreciates as evidence of the inspired author's literary artistry. The dual meaning embedded in the Hebrew reveals how divine communication operates through the full range of human linguistic capacity — reason, imagination, and word-craft all serve the prophetic message.

The detailed indictment of commercial fraud (8:4-6) — false balances, selling the sweepings with the wheat, making the ephah small and the shekel great — addresses the specific mechanisms by which the poor are exploited. Catholic social teaching has applied these principles to modern economic practices: predatory lending, wage theft, misleading advertising, and financial instruments designed to extract wealth from the vulnerable.

The prophecy of a "famine of the word of the LORD" (8:11-12) resonates with Catholic concerns about secularization and biblical illiteracy. The Catechism (CCC 133) insists that access to Scripture is essential for the life of the Church, and the image of people wandering from sea to sea seeking the word but not finding it serves as a warning against any situation in which the faithful are deprived of the nourishment of God's word.
