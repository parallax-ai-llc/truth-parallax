Amos 2 extends the oracles to Moab, Judah, and finally Israel, whose indictment is the longest and most detailed. Catholic moral theology engages the specific charges against Israel — selling the righteous for silver, trampling the poor, exploiting the vulnerable — as descriptions of sins that cry out to heaven, a category emphasized in Catholic tradition (CCC 1867).

The accusation that "a man and his father go in to the same girl, so that my holy name is profaned" (2:7) combines sexual immorality with the desecration of the sacred — a connection Catholic moral theology consistently makes. The violation of persons is simultaneously a violation of God's holiness, because every human being bears the divine image.

The historical recollection of the Exodus and wilderness provision (2:9-11) establishes the context of Israel's ingratitude. Catholic theology of grace draws on this passage: God's saving initiative precedes any human response, and the appropriate response is not entitlement but gratitude expressed through faithful living. The sins of Amos 2 represent the corruption of gratitude into presumption — assuming that divine favor continues regardless of moral conduct.
