Amos 3 articulates the principle that election brings greater responsibility: "You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you" (3:2). Catholic ecclesiology applies this directly to the Church — the covenant community bears a heavier burden of accountability precisely because it has received greater gifts. The Second Vatican Council's *Lumen Gentium* echoes this principle in its teaching on the Church as a sign and instrument of salvation.

The rhetorical questions of 3:3-8 establish the logic of prophetic authority. Catholic theology affirms that the prophet speaks because God has spoken — prophetic charism is not self-generated but divinely commissioned. The Catechism (CCC 64-67) locates prophecy within the economy of revelation, affirming that while public revelation closed with the apostles, the prophetic spirit continues in the Church through the charism of prophecy given to individuals for the building up of the community.

The announcement of judgment against Samaria's palaces (3:9-15) — including the "houses of ivory" and "winter house" and "summer house" — targets the structural expressions of injustice. Catholic social teaching speaks of "structures of sin" (John Paul II, *Sollicitudo Rei Socialis*) — social arrangements that perpetuate exploitation. Amos's attack on the physical infrastructure of luxury is an attack on the system that produces and sustains it.
