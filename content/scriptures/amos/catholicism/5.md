Amos 5 is the theological summit of the book, containing the great prophetic demand for justice. The funeral lament over Israel (5:1-3) uses the language of death to describe a nation that is morally deceased even while physically alive. Catholic theology speaks of mortal sin as spiritual death — the state of the soul separated from grace — and Amos's lament captures the corporate dimension of this condition.

The instruction "Seek me and live" (5:4-6) contrasts genuine seeking of God with attendance at sanctuaries (Bethel, Gilgal, Beersheba). Catholic interpretation does not read this as anti-liturgical but as a demand for the integration of worship and justice. The Eucharist, rightly celebrated, sends the faithful into the world to practice the justice they have received; worship detached from ethical commitment becomes the empty religion Amos condemns.

The climactic declaration (5:24) — "Let justice roll down like waters" — has shaped Catholic social teaching from *Rerum Novarum* through *Laudato Si'*. The image of justice as a perennial stream, not a seasonal flash flood, demands structural and sustained commitment to the common good. Pope Francis has repeatedly invoked this prophetic vision in calling for an economy that serves human dignity rather than abstract profit.
