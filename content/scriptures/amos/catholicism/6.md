Amos 6 pronounces woe on the ruling classes of both Jerusalem and Samaria, condemning their complacency and luxury in the face of national moral crisis. Catholic social teaching's concept of the "social mortgage" — the principle that private property carries social obligations — finds its prophetic roots here. The wealthy who feast while the nation collapses have failed their social responsibility.

The catalogue of luxuries (6:4-6) — ivory couches, imported meats, wine in bowls, expensive perfumes — is not condemned as inherently sinful but as symptomatic of a disordered set of priorities. Catholic moral theology's distinction between the legitimate enjoyment of goods and their sinful excess applies: the issue is not possessing things but being possessed by them, allowing material comfort to deaden awareness of one's obligations to the poor and the community.

The concluding announcement of exile (6:7, 14) illustrates the Catholic principle that social sin has social consequences. The destruction of a nation is not merely the punishment of individuals but the collapse of a social order that has become fundamentally unjust. Catholic liberation theology has drawn on passages like Amos 6 to argue that structural injustice, left unaddressed, inevitably leads to structural collapse.
