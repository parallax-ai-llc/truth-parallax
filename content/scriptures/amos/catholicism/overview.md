## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Amos draws a direct line from the prophet's eighth-century denunciations of economic exploitation to the Church's modern social teaching. Amos's insistence that genuine worship requires justice toward the poor and marginalized has been foundational for papal encyclicals and conciliar documents addressing economic inequality, labor rights, and structural injustice.

## Patristic and Medieval Reading

The Church Fathers read Amos as both a historical prophet and a type of the Christian preacher called to speak truth to power. **Jerome** noted that Amos's humble origins as a shepherd demonstrated that God chooses the lowly to confound the powerful. **Ambrose** drew on Amos's denunciation of the wealthy (6:1-7) in his own preaching on the obligations of the rich. Medieval commentators connected Amos's visions of judgment to the Church's penitential theology, reading the plumb line as the standard of divine law against which all will be measured.

## Catholic Social Teaching

Modern Catholic social teaching has found in Amos a prophetic foundation. *Rerum Novarum* (1891), *Populorum Progressio* (1967), and *Laudato Si'* (2015) echo Amos's concerns about exploitation and the cry of the poor. Liberation theologians in Latin America identified strongly with Amos's message, articulating a "preferential option for the poor" that draws directly from the prophet's denunciation of those who "trample the heads of the poor" (2:7). Pope Francis has frequently invoked Amos's prophetic vision in calling for an economy of inclusion.
