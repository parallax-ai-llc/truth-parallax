Amos 4 catalogs divine disciplines — famine, drought, blight, plague — each followed by the refrain "yet you did not return to me." This pattern aligns precisely with the Quranic portrayal of communities that receive warning after warning but refuse to repent. The Quran describes the people of Pharaoh: "And We had gripped them with suffering and hardship that perhaps they might humble themselves. Then why, when Our punishment came to them, did they not humble themselves?" (6:42-43).

The natural disasters described are understood in both Amos and the Quran as *ayat* (signs) — divine communications through the created order. The Quran states: "And We send not the signs except as a warning" (17:59). The purpose is not destruction for its own sake but the awakening of *taqwa* (God-consciousness) through the recognition of human vulnerability and divine power.

The concluding doxology — "He who forms the mountains and creates the wind" (4:13) — affirms the Islamic principle that the God who commands moral behavior is the God who created the universe. The Quran repeatedly invites reflection on creation as evidence of God's existence and sovereignty: "Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and earth, and the alternation of the night and the day, are signs for those of understanding" (3:190).
