Amos 3 establishes the principle of prophetic causation: the prophet speaks because God has spoken. Islamic theology affirms this through the concept of *wahy* (revelation) — prophets do not speak from their own desire but convey what God has revealed. The Quran declares: "Nor does he speak from his own inclination. It is not but a revelation revealed" (53:3-4), and this principle applies to all genuine prophets, including those in the Israelite tradition.

The statement "You only have I known... therefore I will punish you" (3:2) raises the question of divine election and its consequences. Islamic theology holds that God chose various peoples for specific roles — the children of Israel received the Torah, and their failure to uphold it brought divine discipline. The Quran addresses this directly: "O Children of Israel, remember My favor which I bestowed upon you and that I preferred you over the worlds" (2:47), followed by warnings about the consequences of ingratitude.

The judgment on the wealthy palaces of Samaria (3:9-15) illustrates the Islamic principle that material wealth is a trust (*amanah*) from God, not an absolute possession. Those who accumulate wealth through oppression violate the trust and invite divine retribution. The Quran warns: "And let not those who greedily withhold what Allah has given them of His bounty ever think that it is better for them" (3:180).
