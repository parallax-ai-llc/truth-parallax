Amos 8 announces the "end" through the vision of summer fruit, followed by a detailed indictment of economic exploitation. The merchants' impatience with religious observances (8:5) — eager for the Sabbath to pass so they can resume their dishonest trade — illustrates a hypocrisy that Islamic ethics strongly condemns. The Prophet Muhammad warned against those who pray publicly but cheat in their commercial dealings, describing them as among the worst of people.

The specific fraudulent practices — making the ephah small and the shekel great, selling the refuse with the wheat (8:5-6) — are precisely the kinds of commercial sins the Quran targets. Surah al-Mutaffifin (83) opens with "Woe to those who give less than due" and describes the Day of Judgment awaiting fraudulent merchants. The prophetic denunciation of economic injustice is a shared emphasis across Amos and the Quran.

The "famine of the word of the LORD" (8:11-12) raises the Islamic theological concern about communities that lose access to authentic divine guidance. The Quran addresses this through the concept of *tahrif* — the alteration of previous scriptures — and presents itself as the final, preserved revelation that ensures God's word will never again be lost: "Indeed, it is We who sent down the message, and indeed, We will be its guardian" (15:9).
