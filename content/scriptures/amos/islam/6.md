Amos 6 condemns the complacent wealthy who feast while the nation collapses around them. The Quranic parallel is striking: "Then, is he whom We have promised a good promise which he will obtain like he for whom We provided enjoyment of worldly life but then he is, on the Day of Resurrection, among those presented for punishment?" (28:61). Both texts warn that present comfort is no guarantee of future security.

The specific luxuries described — ivory beds, choice meats, wine in excess, finest oils — are not condemned in Islam as inherently forbidden (except wine, which is *haram*), but the underlying attitude of heedless consumption while others suffer is a grave sin. The Quran warns: "And eat and drink, but be not excessive. Indeed, He does not like those who commit excess" (7:31). Islamic ethics promotes moderation (*wasatiyyah*) and generosity (*sadaqah*) as alternatives to self-indulgent luxury.

The prophet's declaration that the complacent will be "first to go into exile" (6:7) expresses the principle of proportional accountability that the Quran also affirms. The Prophet Muhammad taught: "The one who will be dealt with most severely on the Day of Resurrection is a scholar who did not benefit from his knowledge." Those with the greatest knowledge and privilege bear the heaviest responsibility.
