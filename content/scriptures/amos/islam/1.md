Amos 1 opens with oracles against the nations, establishing that God's moral governance extends to all peoples. This principle resonates deeply with the Islamic understanding of divine sovereignty: Allah is *Rabb al-'alamin* (Lord of all the worlds), and his justice encompasses every nation and community. The Quran affirms: "And never would We punish until We sent a messenger" (17:15), indicating that God's accountability system operates universally.

The specific crimes condemned — ripping open pregnant women, breaking treaties, slave trading — represent violations of basic moral standards that Islamic law (*shari'ah*) also categorically prohibits. The protection of non-combatants, the sanctity of agreements, and the prohibition of slavery (progressively developed in Islamic jurisprudence) all find prophetic support in Amos's denunciations.

The prophet's identity as a humble shepherd called to confront powerful nations mirrors the Quranic emphasis on prophets as ordinary men chosen by God. The Quran states: "And We sent not before you any messenger except that he ate food and walked in the markets" (25:20). Amos's ordinariness — his lack of prophetic pedigree — demonstrates that divine calling depends on God's will, not human credentials.
