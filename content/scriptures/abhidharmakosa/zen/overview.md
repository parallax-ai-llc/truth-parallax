## Overview

Zen Buddhism maintains a famously ambivalent relationship with systematic philosophical texts like the Abhidharmakosa. The Zen tradition's emphasis on direct experience, transmission beyond words and scriptures, and the limitations of conceptual thinking might seem to place it at odds with the Kosa's elaborate analytical framework. Yet historically, Zen monasteries in China, Korea, and Japan included the study of Abhidharma philosophy as part of the broader monastic education, and many great Zen masters were thoroughly versed in its contents.

The Zen perspective on the Abhidharmakosa might be characterized as one of *skillful use* — the text's analysis of consciousness, mental factors, and meditative states provides a valuable conceptual map that supports meditation practice, so long as the map is not confused with the territory. The Kosa's final chapter on the refutation of self (*pudgala*) resonates particularly with Zen's emphasis on the direct realization of no-self (*anatta*/*wu-wo*) beyond all conceptual elaboration.
