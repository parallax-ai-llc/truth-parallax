Chapter 9 of the Abhidharmakosa addresses the refutation of a permanent self (*pudgala*). Zen Buddhism's relationship to this systematic philosophical analysis is characteristically paradoxical: Zen masters have traditionally warned against becoming trapped in conceptual elaboration while simultaneously requiring thorough philosophical training of their students. The *kanna* (koan-studying) Zen tradition in particular presupposes the kind of analytical sophistication that the Kosa develops, even as it seeks to transcend it.

The Zen perspective on Vasubandhu's philosophical arguments against the *Pudgalavada* (Personalist) school's claim that a 'person' exists as a real entity emphasizes the experiential dimension of what the Kosa analyzes conceptually. Where the Abhidharma tradition classifies mental states into elaborate taxonomies, Zen practice aims at direct, non-conceptual awareness of the mind's nature. Yet this direct awareness is not anti-intellectual — it is rather a seeing-through of concepts to the reality they point toward. The Kosa's analysis provides the conceptual framework that makes such seeing-through possible.
