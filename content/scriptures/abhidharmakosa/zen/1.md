Chapter 1 of the Abhidharmakosa addresses the basic elements (*dhatus*) of existence — the 18 elements, 12 sense bases, and 5 aggregates. Zen Buddhism's relationship to this systematic philosophical analysis is characteristically paradoxical: Zen masters have traditionally warned against becoming trapped in conceptual elaboration while simultaneously requiring thorough philosophical training of their students. The *kanna* (koan-studying) Zen tradition in particular presupposes the kind of analytical sophistication that the Kosa develops, even as it seeks to transcend it.

The Zen perspective on Vasubandhu's systematic taxonomy of the fundamental constituents of experience emphasizes the experiential dimension of what the Kosa analyzes conceptually. Where the Abhidharma tradition classifies mental states into elaborate taxonomies, Zen practice aims at direct, non-conceptual awareness of the mind's nature. Yet this direct awareness is not anti-intellectual — it is rather a seeing-through of concepts to the reality they point toward. The Kosa's analysis provides the conceptual framework that makes such seeing-through possible.
