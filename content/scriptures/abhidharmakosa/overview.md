## Overview

The Abhidharmakosa ("Treasury of Abhidharma") is a comprehensive treatise on Buddhist philosophy and psychology composed by Vasubandhu in the fourth or fifth century CE. Written in verse (the *Karika*) with an accompanying prose auto-commentary (the *Bhashya*), it systematizes the teachings of the Sarvastivada Abhidharma tradition while also incorporating critiques from the Sautrantika perspective. The work became one of the most influential philosophical texts in the history of Buddhism, studied across virtually all Buddhist traditions.

The Abhidharmakosa presents a detailed analysis of the constituents of experience (*dharmas*), the nature of consciousness, the mechanics of karma and rebirth, Buddhist cosmology, the stages of meditative absorption, and the path to enlightenment. Its rigorous analytical approach represents Buddhist philosophy at its most systematic and intellectually demanding. Vasubandhu's later turn toward Yogacara (Mind-Only) philosophy adds an additional layer of interest to the text, as the Abhidharmakosa represents his earlier, more realist philosophical position.

## Structure

The nine chapters of the Abhidharmakosa address the following topics:

| Chapter | Title | Topic |
|---------|-------|-------|
| 1 | Dhatu (*Elements*) | The basic constituents of experience — the 18 elements, 12 bases, and 5 aggregates |
| 2 | Indriya (*Faculties*) | The mental and physical faculties, their characteristics, and their functions |
| 3 | Loka (*World/Cosmology*) | Buddhist cosmology — the structure of the universe and the realms of existence |
| 4 | Karma (*Action*) | The theory of karma — intentional action, its types, and its consequences |
| 5 | Anusaya (*Latent Defilements*) | The latent mental afflictions that perpetuate suffering and rebirth |
| 6 | Marga (*Path*) | The noble persons, the stages of realization, and the path to liberation |
| 7 | Jnana (*Knowledge*) | The types of knowledge and wisdom in Buddhist soteriology |
| 8 | Samapatti (*Meditative Attainments*) | The stages of meditative absorption and their characteristics |
| 9 | Pudgala (*Refutation of Self*) | A philosophical refutation of the notion of a permanent self or person |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Theravada**: While the Theravada Abhidhamma is a distinct tradition, the Kosa is studied comparatively as a parallel systematic enterprise
- **Mahayana**: A foundational text for Mahayana philosophical education, especially in East Asian Buddhism
- **Zen**: Studied as philosophical background, though Zen emphasizes direct experience over systematic analysis
- **Tibetan**: A core text in the Tibetan monastic curriculum, studied intensively in all major schools
- **Academic**: One of the most studied texts in Buddhist philosophy, with extensive secondary literature
- **Comparative**: Offers rich material for comparison with Western philosophy of mind, ethics, and metaphysics

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Dharma Theory | The analysis of experience into fundamental constituents (*dharmas*) |
| Consciousness | The nature, types, and functions of consciousness and mental factors |
| Karma | The mechanics of intentional action and its consequences across lifetimes |
| Cosmology | The structure of the Buddhist universe and the realms of rebirth |
| Path | The stages of spiritual development from ordinary being to arahant |
| Non-self | The philosophical refutation of a permanent, unchanging self |
