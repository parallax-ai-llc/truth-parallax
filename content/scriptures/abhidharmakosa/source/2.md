---
title: "Abhidharmakosa Chapter 2: Indriya-nirdesa (Analysis of Faculties)"
scripture: "abhidharmakosa"
chapter: 2
---

**1** The faculties are twenty-two in number. The term "faculty" denotes predominance, for each exercises a ruling influence over its associated states in its own particular domain.

**2** The five sense faculties — eye, ear, nose, tongue, body — exercise predominance in seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, and touching respectively. The mind-faculty exercises predominance over all mental activities.

**3** The female faculty and male faculty exercise predominance in the determination of sex. The life-faculty exercises predominance in maintaining the continuity of the series of mental and material phenomena within one existence.

**4** The five feeling-faculties — pleasure, pain, gladness, sadness, and equanimity — exercise predominance in contamination and purification. Pleasure and gladness lead to attachment; pain and sadness lead to aversion; equanimity is the ground for delusion or wisdom.

**5** The five spiritual faculties — faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom — exercise predominance in purification. They are the antidotes to the five hindrances: faithlessness, laziness, heedlessness, distraction, and ignorance.

**6** The three final faculties — the faculty of "I shall come to know the unknown," the faculty of higher knowledge, and the faculty of one who has fully known — exercise predominance at the three stages of the path: entering the stream, on the path, and at the completion of the path.

**7** All conditioned dharmas have four characteristics: arising, duration, decay, and cessation. These characteristics are themselves conditioned and have their own sub-characteristics.

**8** How many moments does a dharma endure? A dharma endures for one moment only. It arises and immediately ceases. There is no real duration; what we perceive as duration is a rapid series of momentary existences.

**9** Causes are sixfold: efficient cause, co-existent cause, homogeneous cause, conjoined cause, universal cause, and retributive cause. Conditions are fourfold: causal condition, immediately-antecedent condition, object-condition, and predominant condition.

**10** Results are fivefold: retributive result, dominant result, outflowing result, result concordant with the cause, and the result of separation. Through causes and conditions, all conditioned dharmas arise; apart from causes and conditions, nothing conditioned comes into being.
