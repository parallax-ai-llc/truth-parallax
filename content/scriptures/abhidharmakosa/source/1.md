---
title: "Abhidharmakosa Chapter 1: Dhatu-nirdesa (Analysis of Elements)"
scripture: "abhidharmakosa"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Homage to him who has destroyed the darkness: the Buddha who taught the Abhidharma. The Abhidharma is pure wisdom together with its accompaniments. What is this wisdom? It is the discernment of dharmas.

**2** Impure wisdom, and the wisdom associated with it — this is the Abhidharma in the secondary sense of the word. It has the character of leading toward the pure Abhidharma and is therefore called Abhidharma.

**3** Apart from the discernment of dharmas, there is no means of extinguishing the defilements. It is through the defilements that the world wanders in this ocean of transmigration. It is for this reason, they say, that the Abhidharma was taught.

**4** The conditioned dharmas are classified as the five aggregates: form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness. The unconditioned dharmas are three: space, cessation through deliberation, and cessation not through deliberation.

**5** Form is fivefold: the five sense-organs, the five sense-objects, and imperceptible form. The eye is a clear material form derived from the four great elements, having visible form as its object.

**6** The twelve sense-bases and the eighteen elements are another way of classifying all dharmas. The sense-bases are the six internal bases — eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind — and the six external bases — visible form, sound, smell, taste, tangible, and dharmas.

**7** The eighteen elements are: the six sense-organ elements, the six sense-object elements, and the six consciousness elements. Eye-element, visible-form-element, and eye-consciousness-element; and so forth for the remaining five groups.

**8** Why did the Blessed One teach aggregates, sense-bases, and elements? In order to counteract the three types of error: the error regarding self, regarding the experiencer, and regarding the means of experience.

**9** Among all dharmas, some are wholesome, some unwholesome, some indeterminate. The wholesome leads to pleasant results, the unwholesome to unpleasant results, the indeterminate to neither.

**10** The conditioned dharmas arise through causes and conditions. The unconditioned dharmas do not arise, do not endure, and do not cease. They are not produced by causes and conditions but exist as realities.
