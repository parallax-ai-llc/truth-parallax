## Overview

The Abhidharmakosa offers extraordinarily rich material for comparative philosophical analysis. Its detailed taxonomy of mental states invites comparison with Western philosophical psychology from Aristotle through Husserl to contemporary cognitive science. Its analysis of causation resonates with Humean empiricism and process philosophy. Its ethical theory — grounding moral responsibility in intention (*cetana*) — parallels and contrasts with Kantian ethics, utilitarianism, and virtue ethics.

Comparative philosophers have been particularly drawn to the Kosa's treatment of personal identity (Chapter 9), where Vasubandhu's reductionist analysis of the self into impersonal physical and mental processes anticipates arguments later developed by Hume, Parfit, and contemporary neurophilosophy. The text's cosmology, while culturally specific, raises universal questions about the relationship between moral order and the structure of reality that connect to Western natural law theory and contemporary discussions of fine-tuning and cosmic purpose.
