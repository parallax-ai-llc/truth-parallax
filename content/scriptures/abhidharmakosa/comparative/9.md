Chapter 9 of the Abhidharmakosa addresses the refutation of a permanent self (*pudgala*), offering rich material for comparative philosophical analysis. Vasubandhu's philosophical arguments against the *Pudgalavada* (Personalist) school's claim that a 'person' exists as a real entity invites comparison with analogous discussions in Western philosophy, particularly in the areas of philosophy of mind, metaphysics, and moral philosophy. The Kosa's analytical approach — breaking down complex phenomena into their constituent elements and examining the causal relationships between them — resonates with reductionist and empiricist strategies in the Western philosophical tradition.

Comparative analysis of this chapter reveals both striking parallels and illuminating differences between Buddhist and Western approaches. The Abhidharma project of exhaustively classifying the elements of experience anticipates aspects of phenomenology (Husserl's analysis of consciousness), analytic philosophy of mind (the debate between reductionism and emergentism), and cognitive science (the computational theory of mind). At the same time, the Buddhist framework's fundamental commitment to liberation (*moksha*) as the goal of analysis distinguishes it from the more purely theoretical orientation of much Western philosophy.
