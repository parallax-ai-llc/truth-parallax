## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus chapter 1 bridges the patriarchal narratives of Genesis and the liberation story that defines the rest of the Pentateuch. The chapter opens by listing the sons of Jacob who went down to Egypt, numbering seventy persons in all. Joseph has died, and a new Pharaoh arises "who did not know Joseph" (1:8). This new king perceives the rapidly multiplying Israelites as a threat and subjects them to forced labor, setting taskmasters over them to build the store cities of Pithom and Raamses. When oppression fails to curb their growth, Pharaoh commands the Hebrew midwives, Shiphrah and Puah, to kill all male infants at birth. The midwives fear God rather than Pharaoh and refuse to comply. Finally, Pharaoh issues a public decree that every newborn Hebrew boy be cast into the Nile.

| 1:20 | "So God dealt well with the midwives" | Grace rewarding faith-driven obedience |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theologians such as John Calvin emphasized that Israel's affliction in Egypt was not outside God's sovereign plan but was in fact its outworking. God had told Abraham that his descendants would be enslaved for four hundred years (Gen. 15:13), and the events of Exodus 1 fulfill that word precisely. Calvin saw the midwives' civil disobedience as a model for resistance to unjust authority—a theme that would become significant during the Reformation itself.

### Lutheran Perspective

Martin Luther highlighted the contrast between earthly power and divine promise. Pharaoh represents the law's condemning power that cannot ultimately destroy the people of grace. The more Israel is oppressed, the more they multiply—a pattern Luther saw repeated in the church under persecution.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the providential care of God for His covenant people even in the darkest circumstances. The faithfulness of the midwives is held up as an example of practical trust: they acted on their fear of God rather than their fear of man, and God blessed them with families of their own (1:21).

## Theological Significance

Exodus 1 establishes the essential Protestant framework for reading the entire book. The chapter illustrates several core doctrines:

1. **Covenant faithfulness.** God's promise to Abraham that his seed would become a great nation is being fulfilled despite—and even through—oppression.
2. **Providence.** The sovereign God is never absent from the narrative, even when He is not named as an active agent. Protestant theology insists that secondary causes (Pharaoh's policies, the midwives' courage) operate under primary divine governance.
3. **The seed of typology.** The decree to drown Hebrew boys in the Nile anticipates the later judgment on Egypt when its own firstborn are struck down. Protestant commentators see in this reversal a foreshadowing of the gospel pattern: what the world uses for destruction, God turns to salvation.

The chapter also introduces the theme of the "fear of God" versus the fear of man, a distinction central to Reformation ethics. The midwives model what Luther called the freedom of a Christian—bound to God's word above all human commands.
