## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Exodus is shaped by the Reformation's emphasis on grace, faith, and the sufficiency of Scripture. The book is read as a grand typological drama pointing to Christ: the Passover lamb prefigures the Lamb of God, the Red Sea crossing prefigures baptism, and the law given at Sinai reveals both God's holiness and humanity's need for a mediator.

## Key Interpretive Themes

### Law and Gospel
The Reformers drew a critical distinction between the law (which reveals sin and condemns) and the gospel (which offers grace and saves). Exodus is where this dialectic begins: the Ten Commandments expose human sinfulness, while the Passover reveals God's gracious provision.

### Covenant Theology
Reformed Protestantism reads Exodus as a major chapter in the unfolding covenant of grace. The Mosaic covenant is not a separate "works" covenant but a further administration of the same covenant of grace begun with Abraham.

### Typology
Protestant interpreters from Luther onward have seen Exodus as rich with types pointing to Christ and the New Testament:

| Exodus Type | New Testament Antitype |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Passover Lamb | Christ (1 Cor. 5:7) |
| Red Sea crossing | Baptism (1 Cor. 10:1-2) |
| Manna | Bread of Life (John 6:31-35) |
| Water from rock | Christ (1 Cor. 10:4) |
| Tabernacle | Incarnation (John 1:14) |
| Moses as mediator | Christ the one Mediator (1 Tim. 2:5) |
