## The Altar, the Court, and the Accounting

Exodus 38 records the construction of the bronze altar of burnt offering, the bronze laver, and the courtyard enclosure, followed by a detailed inventory of all the materials used in the Tabernacle project. This chapter moves from the sacred furnishings of the interior to the outer structures where the people encounter God through sacrifice, and concludes with a remarkable exercise in financial accountability. Protestant interpreters value this chapter for its theology of sacrifice, its model of transparent stewardship, and its demonstration that redemption has a cost.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Bronze Altar (38:1-7)

The altar of burnt offering is constructed according to the specifications of 27:1-8: acacia wood overlaid with bronze, five cubits square and three cubits high, with horns at each corner. All its utensils — pots, shovels, basins, forks, firepans — are made of bronze.

**Calvin** reiterated that the altar's placement at the entrance to the courtyard is the single most important architectural statement of the Tabernacle complex: there is no way to God that does not pass through sacrifice. Every worshipper, every priest, every offering must begin at this altar. Protestant soteriology makes the same claim about the cross — there is no access to God except through the atoning death of Christ.

The bronze construction, associated throughout Scripture with divine judgment (the bronze serpent, the bronze feet of the glorified Christ in Revelation 1:15), signals that this is the place where God's judgment falls on the substitute. The altar absorbs the fire of divine wrath directed at sin, just as Christ absorbed the full weight of God's judgment on the cross.

### The Bronze Laver (38:8)

A single verse records a striking detail: the laver is made "from the mirrors of the ministering women who ministered at the entrance of the tent of meeting." The women donated their bronze mirrors — precious personal possessions in the ancient world — for the construction of the basin where priests would wash.

| Detail | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| Bronze mirrors donated | Personal vanity surrendered for sacred use |
| Women's voluntary sacrifice | Female participation in worship acknowledged |
| Mirrors become a laver | What reflected self now serves purification |

**Matthew Henry** drew a rich application: what once served self-admiration is transformed into an instrument of cleansing. The gospel performs exactly this work — it takes the self-focused life and reshapes it for service and holiness. Protestant preaching has frequently used this image: conversion turns the mirror into a laver.

### The Courtyard (38:9-20)

The courtyard is constructed with white linen hangings on bronze pillars, forming the perimeter that separates sacred space from common ground. The single entrance on the east side, with its screen of blue, purple, and scarlet yarn, remains the only point of access.

### The Inventory (38:21-31)

The chapter concludes with a detailed accounting of all metals used:

| Metal | Amount | Primary Use |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Gold | 29 talents, 730 shekels | Tabernacle interior, furnishings |
| Silver | 100 talents, 1,775 shekels | Bases for frames and pillars (from atonement money) |
| Bronze | 70 talents, 2,400 shekels | Altar, laver, pillars, pegs |

The silver is explicitly identified as coming from the half-shekel atonement money paid by 603,550 men (38:25-26). The Tabernacle's structural foundation — the silver bases — literally rests on redemption payments. Every socket holding up the sacred structure was funded by the ransom of Israelite lives.

**Luther** saw in this a profound symbol: the house of God stands on atonement. The church is not built on human achievement or generosity alone but on the price paid for every soul. Christ's redemption is the foundation on which the entire structure of the church rests (1 Cor. 3:11).

The inventory itself is also significant. The fact that Moses commissioned a public accounting of every talent and shekel establishes a principle of transparent stewardship. Those who handle resources dedicated to God's purposes are accountable to the community. Protestant church governance has historically insisted on financial transparency, and this passage provides biblical warrant for that practice.

## Theological Significance

- **Sacrifice is the starting point**: The bronze altar's construction confirms that atonement is not optional but foundational. No approach to God bypasses the place of sacrifice.
- **Redemption is the foundation**: The silver bases, funded by atonement money, teach that the church rests on the price of redemption — a price ultimately paid by Christ.
- **Stewardship demands accountability**: The public inventory models transparent handling of resources devoted to God. Protestant church administration draws on this precedent.
- **Transformation of the self**: The mirrors-to-laver detail illustrates the gospel's power to redirect self-focused lives toward holiness and service.
