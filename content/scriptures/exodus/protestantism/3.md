## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 3 records one of the most significant theophanies in Scripture. Moses is tending the flock of his father-in-law Jethro near Horeb, the mountain of God, when he encounters a bush that burns without being consumed. God calls to Moses from the bush, identifies Himself as the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and commissions Moses to lead Israel out of Egypt. Moses raises objections, and God responds by revealing His covenant name: "I AM WHO I AM" (Hebrew: *Ehyeh asher Ehyeh*). God instructs Moses to gather the elders of Israel and to confront Pharaoh with the demand, "Let my people go." God also promises that Israel will not leave Egypt empty-handed but will plunder the Egyptians.

| 3:5 | "Take your sandals off your feet, for the place on which you are standing is holy ground" | The holiness of God—central to Reformed theology |
| 3:14 | "God said to Moses, 'I AM WHO I AM'" | The self-existence and sovereignty of God (aseity) |
| 3:15 | "The LORD, the God of your fathers…this is my name forever" | Covenant continuity from Abraham through Moses to Christ |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin devoted extensive attention to the divine name in 3:14. He argued that "I AM WHO I AM" reveals God's absolute self-existence (*aseitas*)—God depends on nothing outside Himself for His being. This understanding undergirds the entire Reformed doctrine of God: He is sovereign, immutable, and self-sufficient. Calvin also identified the Angel of the LORD as the eternal Word, the second person of the Trinity, an interpretation shared by many Reformed theologians.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized the condescension of God in appearing in a common bush rather than in royal splendor. This is the theology of the cross at work: God reveals Himself not in glory but in lowliness. The bush that burns but is not consumed became for Luther an image of the church under persecution—afflicted but never destroyed.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters frequently highlight the personal nature of God's call. God knows Moses by name, knows the suffering of His people, and has a specific plan of deliverance. The burning bush is often presented as a model of divine encounter: God takes the initiative, calls individuals to participate in His redemptive work, and provides the resources needed to fulfill that calling.

## Theological Significance

This chapter is theologically dense for Protestant interpretation:

1. **The divine name and Christology.** When Jesus declares "Before Abraham was, I am" (John 8:58), He is claiming the name revealed at the bush. Protestant theology sees a direct line from Exodus 3:14 to the Gospel of John's "I AM" sayings, establishing Christ's full deity.
2. **Calling and commission.** The pattern of divine calling established here—God initiates, the human objects, God provides—is normative in Protestant theology. Ministry is not self-appointed but divinely commissioned.
3. **The holiness of God.** The command to remove sandals introduces the theme of God's unapproachable holiness, which will intensify at Sinai. Reformed theology stresses that this holiness demands a mediator—a role Moses fills typologically and Christ fulfills definitively.

| Exodus 3 Theme | New Testament Fulfillment |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Angel of the LORD | Christ, the eternal Word (John 1:1) |
| "I AM WHO I AM" | Jesus' "I AM" declarations (John 8:58) |
| Holy ground | Believers as the temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Cor. 6:19) |
| Moses commissioned as deliverer | Christ as the ultimate Deliverer (Acts 7:35) |
