## The Dwelling Place of God

Exodus 26 provides the architectural blueprint for the Tabernacle itself — the tent structure, its curtains, frames, bars, and the veil separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place. While the detailed measurements and materials may seem technical, Protestant interpreters insist that every element carries theological weight. The Tabernacle is God's answer to the question raised by sin: how can a holy God dwell in the midst of an unholy people?

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Curtains and Coverings (26:1-14)

The Tabernacle is constructed with four layers of covering:

| Layer | Material | Significance |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Inner curtains | Fine linen with cherubim in blue, purple, scarlet | Beauty and holiness visible only from within |
| Second layer | Goat hair | Protective covering, associated with sin offerings |
| Third layer | Ram skins dyed red | Sacrifice and atonement (red = blood) |
| Outer layer | Durable leather/hides | Plain exterior, protection from elements |

**Matthew Henry** observed that the Tabernacle's beauty was hidden from the outside — covered in plain animal skins. Only those who entered saw the glorious inner curtains. Protestant interpreters draw a parallel to Christ: "He had no form or majesty that we should look at him" (Isa. 53:2), yet those who enter into fellowship with Him discover incomparable glory.

The cherubim woven into the inner curtains recall the cherubim set at the entrance to Eden (Gen. 3:24). The Tabernacle is, in a sense, a restoration of Eden — a place where God and humanity dwell together, guarded by angelic figures, with access now mediated through sacrifice rather than permanently barred.

### The Frames and Bars (26:15-30)

Forty-eight acacia frames overlaid with gold form the structural skeleton of the Tabernacle. Each frame stands in silver bases — and silver throughout the Tabernacle comes from the atonement money paid by every Israelite (Exod. 30:11-16). The entire structure literally stands on atonement.

**Calvin** drew attention to the combination of wood and gold: the ordinary and the glorious joined together, just as in Christ the human and divine natures are united in one person. The frames are set in sockets of redemption money, reminding Israel that access to God's presence rests entirely on atonement.

### The Veil (26:31-35)

The most theologically significant feature of the chapter is the veil (*paroket*), a curtain of blue, purple, and scarlet yarn with woven cherubim, separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place where the Ark of the Covenant stands.

Protestant theology gives the veil a central place in understanding the atonement:

- **The veil as barrier**: It represents the separation between a holy God and sinful humanity. Only the high priest could pass beyond it, and only once a year on the Day of Atonement (Lev. 16).
- **The veil torn**: At Christ's death, "the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom" (Matt. 27:51). The tearing from top to bottom indicates divine action — God Himself removes the barrier.
- **Hebrews 10:19-20**: The author of Hebrews identifies the veil with Christ's flesh: "by the new and living way that he opened for us through the curtain, that is, through his flesh." Christ's broken body opens permanent access to God's presence.

This is a cornerstone of Protestant soteriology. There is no longer a restricted holy space, no priestly caste controlling access, no annual limitation on approaching God. Every believer, through Christ, has bold access to the throne of grace (Heb. 4:16).

## Theological Significance

- **God accommodates Himself**: The infinite God condescends to dwell in a tent among nomads. This pattern of divine accommodation culminates in the incarnation.
- **Holiness requires mediation**: The layers of covering and the separating veil teach that sinful humanity cannot casually approach the holy God. Access requires sacrifice and priestly mediation — both fulfilled in Christ.
- **The regulative principle**: Every measurement, material, and placement is divinely prescribed. Protestant worship theology draws from this the conviction that God determines how He is approached.
