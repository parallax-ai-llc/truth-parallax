## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 2 narrates the birth, rescue, and early life of Moses. A Levite woman gives birth to a son and hides him for three months. When she can no longer conceal him, she places him in a waterproofed basket among the reeds of the Nile. Pharaoh's daughter discovers the child, recognizes him as a Hebrew, and adopts him. Through the initiative of Moses' sister, the child's own mother is hired to nurse him. Moses grows up in the Egyptian court but retains awareness of his Hebrew identity. As an adult, he kills an Egyptian who is beating a Hebrew slave, and when the deed becomes known, he flees to Midian. There he marries Zipporah, daughter of the priest Reuel (Jethro), and has a son named Gershom. The chapter closes with a pivotal notice: God hears the groaning of the Israelites, remembers His covenant, and looks upon them with concern.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 2:2 | "The woman conceived and bore a son, and when she saw that he was a fine child, she hid him three months" | Faith acting under persecution (Heb. 11:23) |
| 2:10 | "She named him Moses, 'Because,' she said, 'I drew him out of the water'" | Typology: Moses drawn from water foreshadows baptism and new life |
| 2:12 | "He struck down the Egyptian and hid him in the sand" | Human effort apart from divine calling leads to failure |
| 2:24 | "And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant" | Covenant theology: God's faithfulness initiates redemption |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin stressed that God's "remembering" (2:24) does not imply prior forgetfulness but rather the commencement of visible action in fulfillment of His eternal decree. The entire sequence—Moses' preservation, his flight, and the turning point in verse 24—illustrates the Reformed doctrine that God orchestrates events across decades to accomplish His sovereign purposes.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther saw Moses' failed attempt at liberation (2:11–15) as an illustration of the law–gospel distinction. Moses acts according to human justice—striking the Egyptian—but this produces only exile and further suffering. True deliverance will come only when God Himself acts graciously and calls Moses from the burning bush.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical readings emphasize the personal dimension of God's covenant care. Verses 23–25 form a theological hinge: the verbs "heard," "remembered," "saw," and "knew" reveal a God who is personally attentive to the suffering of His people. This passage is frequently cited in devotional contexts as assurance that God is never indifferent to the cries of the oppressed.

## Theological Significance

Exodus 2 is foundational for Protestant soteriology in several respects:

1. **Grace precedes human action.** Moses does not deliver himself or Israel; he is preserved by grace from infancy. His own attempt to act as deliverer by human strength ends in failure. Protestant theology reads this as a pattern: salvation is of the Lord, not of human effort.
2. **Typological depth.** The infant Moses placed in a basket (Hebrew: *tevah*, the same word used for Noah's ark) is saved through water—a type that Protestant interpreters connect to baptism. Just as God preserved Noah and Moses through water, He delivers believers through the waters of baptism into new life.
3. **Covenant as the ground of redemption.** The critical turning point is not any human initiative but God's remembrance of His covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. This underscores the Reformed emphasis that redemption flows from God's prior promise, not from human merit.

The chapter also illustrates the Protestant theme of the "hidden years" of preparation. Moses spends forty years in Midian before God calls him—a reminder that divine timing is not governed by human impatience.
