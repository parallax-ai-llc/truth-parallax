## The Spirit, the Craftsmen, and the Sabbath

Exodus 31 addresses two seemingly distinct topics that are in fact deeply connected: the appointment of Bezalel and Oholiab as Spirit-filled craftsmen for the Tabernacle's construction, and the restatement of the Sabbath commandment as an eternal sign of the covenant. The chapter concludes with God giving Moses the two stone tablets inscribed by the finger of God. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter a theology of vocation, the Spirit's role in all of life, and the non-negotiable priority of worship rest.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Bezalel and Oholiab: Spirit-Filled Artisans (31:1-11)

God names Bezalel son of Uri, from the tribe of Judah, and fills him "with the Spirit of God, with ability and intelligence, with knowledge and all craftsmanship" (31:3). Oholiab son of Ahisamach, from the tribe of Dan, is appointed as his partner.

| Craftsman | Tribe | Role |
|-----------|-------|------|
| Bezalel | Judah | Lead artisan, filled with the Spirit |
| Oholiab | Dan | Assistant, skilled in engraving and design |

Several Protestant principles emerge from this passage:

- **The Spirit empowers all callings, not only "spiritual" ones**: This is the first explicit mention in Scripture of a person being filled with the Spirit, and it is for craftsmanship, not prophecy or preaching. **Luther's** doctrine of vocation finds strong support here: God calls people to every kind of work, and the Spirit empowers all legitimate labor for God's purposes.
- **Skill is a gift of grace**: Bezalel's abilities are attributed to divine filling, not merely natural talent or training. Even artistic skill, technical knowledge, and craftsmanship are gifts from God.
- **God names His workers**: God does not issue a general call for volunteers but specifically names the individuals He has prepared. Reformed theology sees here a picture of divine election in vocation — God appoints and equips those He has chosen for particular tasks.

**Calvin** noted that the combination of the Spirit with "ability, intelligence, and knowledge" shows that grace does not bypass or replace human faculties but elevates and directs them. The Spirit works through, not around, human skill and learning.

### The Sabbath: Covenant Sign (31:12-17)

Immediately after commissioning the Tabernacle's construction, God reiterates the Sabbath commandment in its most emphatic form. The Sabbath is "a sign forever between me and the people of Israel" (31:17), and its violation carries the death penalty.

The placement of this Sabbath command is theologically significant. Coming directly after the Tabernacle instructions, it teaches that even the most sacred work — building God's house — must cease on the Sabbath. No project, however holy its purpose, overrides the command to rest.

Protestant traditions have interpreted the Sabbath variously:

- **The Puritan/Reformed view**: The Sabbath commandment is a creation ordinance (Gen. 2:2-3) with permanent moral force, transferred to the Lord's Day (Sunday) under the new covenant. The Westminster Confession (21.7-8) teaches that the entire day should be devoted to worship and works of mercy.
- **The Lutheran view**: The Sabbath's ceremonial aspects are fulfilled in Christ, who is our rest (Matt. 11:28). Luther in the Large Catechism taught that Christians are free from Sabbath regulations but should set aside regular time for worship and hearing God's Word.
- **Common ground**: Both traditions agree that the Sabbath principle — rest, worship, trust in God's provision — has enduring significance even if its application differs.

### The Tablets Written by God (31:18)

The chapter closes with a remarkable image: God gives Moses "two tablets of the testimony, tablets of stone, written with the finger of God." The divine authorship of the law is emphasized — these are not Moses' words but God's own inscription.

**Matthew Henry** observed that the "finger of God" conveys both the ease (for God, writing the law requires no more effort than a finger's touch) and the directness (no mediator stands between God and His word) of divine revelation. Protestant commitment to biblical authority finds a symbolic anchor here — Scripture is, at its origin, God's own communication.

## Theological Significance

- **All work is sacred**: The Spirit's filling of craftsmen demolishes the sacred/secular divide. Protestant vocation theology insists that a carpenter or artisan serves God as truly as a priest or pastor.
- **Rest precedes and governs work**: The Sabbath command, placed after the work instructions, establishes that trust in God — expressed through cessation from labor — is more fundamental than any human project.
- **God's Word is God's own**: The stone tablets, inscribed by God's finger, affirm that Scripture carries divine authority, a conviction central to the Reformation's *sola Scriptura* principle.
