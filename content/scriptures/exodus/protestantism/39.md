## Obedience in Every Detail

Exodus 39 describes the fabrication of the priestly garments — the ephod, breastpiece, robe, turban, tunics, and sashes — following the instructions of chapter 28. The chapter is saturated with the refrain "as the LORD had commanded Moses," repeated seven times. It concludes with Moses inspecting the completed work, finding it faithful to the pattern, and blessing the people. Protestant interpreters see in this chapter a powerful model of obedient worship and a literary echo of creation itself.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Garments Constructed (39:1-31)

The narrative follows the order of chapter 28 with precise correspondence:

| Garment | Verses | Key Feature |
|---------|--------|------------|
| The ephod | 39:2-7 | Gold, blue, purple, scarlet; onyx stones with tribal names |
| The breastpiece | 39:8-21 | Twelve stones, each engraved with a tribe's name |
| The robe of the ephod | 39:22-26 | Blue, with bells and pomegranates at the hem |
| The tunics, turban, sashes | 39:27-29 | Fine linen garments for Aaron and his sons |
| The gold plate | 39:30-31 | "HOLY TO THE LORD" engraved and fastened to the turban |

Each section concludes with the same formula: "as the LORD had commanded Moses." This sevenfold repetition is not accidental. **Calvin** observed that the repetition drives home the essential principle of regulated worship — every detail of priestly service must conform to God's revealed will. Human additions, subtractions, or modifications are not merely unnecessary but forbidden.

The breastpiece with its twelve stones, each bearing a tribal name, reiterates the high priest's representative role. He carries all of Israel on his heart before the LORD. Protestant Christology consistently applies this to Christ: He bears the names of His people into the Father's presence, and not one is forgotten or omitted.

The gold plate inscribed "HOLY TO THE LORD" (39:30) is the crowning element. Fixed to the front of the turban, it is the first thing visible as the high priest stands before God. **Luther** noted that this inscription summarizes the entire purpose of the priestly office — and indeed of all worship: consecration to God. Holiness is not a human achievement but a divine claim placed on those called to serve.

### The Completed Work Presented (39:32-43)

When all the components are finished, the people bring everything to Moses for inspection:

> "And Moses saw all the work, and behold, they had done it; as the LORD had commanded, so had they done it. Then Moses blessed them." (39:43)

This verse deliberately echoes Genesis 1:31-2:3: "And God saw everything that he had made, and behold, it was very good.... And God blessed the seventh day." The Tabernacle construction is presented as a new creation — a micro-cosmos where God's presence dwells with His people, just as He walked with them in Eden.

Protestant commentators have drawn several parallels:

- **God saw / Moses saw**: The divine inspection of creation corresponds to the mediator's inspection of the Tabernacle.
- **It was very good / as the LORD commanded**: Both works are pronounced complete and faithful.
- **God blessed / Moses blessed**: Blessing follows faithful completion.
- **Seven days of creation / seven repetitions of obedience**: The literary structure reinforces the creation parallel.

**Matthew Henry** wrote that the blessing Moses pronounced was not merely congratulatory but prophetic — a declaration that God would honor the work done in obedience to His word. The blessing follows the obedience, not the reverse.

### Obedience as Worship

The sevenfold refrain "as the LORD had commanded Moses" constitutes a theology of worship in miniature. Protestant theology, particularly in the Reformed tradition, argues that acceptable worship is defined by divine command, not by human preference or creativity. The craftsmen of Exodus 39 did not add their own artistic improvements; they executed God's design with faithful precision. This is not artistic suppression but artistic submission — the highest form of creative work is that which serves the Creator's stated purpose.

## Theological Significance

- **Faithful obedience, not creative innovation**: The repeated refrain establishes that God is pleased with obedience to His revealed pattern. Protestant worship seeks to follow Scripture's prescriptions, not to improve upon them.
- **The Tabernacle as new creation**: The literary echoes of Genesis 1 position the Tabernacle as the place where God's original intention — dwelling with humanity — is restored in miniature.
- **Christ the faithful High Priest**: The garments that carry Israel's names before God prefigure Christ, who presents His people to the Father and intercedes for them without ceasing.
- **Blessing follows obedience**: Moses' blessing upon the completed work teaches that God honors faithful service, not spectacular performance.
