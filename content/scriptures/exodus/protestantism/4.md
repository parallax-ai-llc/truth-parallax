## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 4 continues the dialogue between God and Moses at the burning bush. Moses protests that the Israelites will not believe him, so God grants him three signs: his staff becomes a serpent and returns to a staff, his hand becomes leprous and is healed, and Nile water poured on dry ground will turn to blood. When Moses further objects that he is not eloquent, God responds in anger but appoints Aaron as his spokesman. Moses departs for Egypt with his family. On the journey, a mysterious and alarming episode occurs: the LORD seeks to put Moses to death, and Zipporah circumcises their son, averting the threat. Moses and Aaron reunite, gather the elders of Israel, perform the signs, and the people believe and worship.

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed interpreters pay close attention to 4:22–23, where God calls Israel His "firstborn son." This is covenant language: Israel's identity as God's son is not based on merit but on election. Calvin argued that this designation foreshadows the believer's adoption in Christ (Gal. 4:4–5). The firstborn language also sets up the theological logic of the tenth plague—Egypt's firstborn for God's firstborn.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther found in Moses' reluctance a portrait of the sinner confronted by a divine calling far exceeding human capacity. Moses' excuses illustrate the weakness of the flesh; God's patient responses (and eventual anger) illustrate the interplay of law and gospel. God does not withdraw His commission but accommodates human weakness by providing Aaron.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical teaching frequently draws on this chapter for lessons in faith and obedience. The question "What is that in your hand?" is a classic sermon text: God does not require extraordinary resources but transforms what is already available. The signs themselves—serpent, leprosy, blood—are understood as previews of God's power over the forces that enslave Israel.

## The Circumcision Crisis (4:24–26)

This enigmatic passage has generated extensive Protestant commentary. The most common interpretation is that Moses had failed to circumcise his son, thereby neglecting the sign of the Abrahamic covenant. God's near-lethal response underscores a critical theological point: the mediator of the covenant must himself be faithful to the covenant. Zipporah's swift action saves Moses' life.

| Element | Theological Significance |
|---------|-------------------------|
| God seeks to kill Moses | Covenant obligations are not optional |
| Zipporah circumcises the son | The covenant sign must be applied to the covenant community |
| "Bridegroom of blood" | Blood is central to covenant ratification—pointing forward to Christ's blood |
| Moses is spared | Grace operates through covenant faithfulness |

## Theological Significance

1. **Accommodation.** God meets Moses in his weakness, providing Aaron as a mouthpiece. Protestant theology sees this as a pattern of divine accommodation—God condescends to work through frail human instruments.
2. **Signs and faith.** The three signs are not magic but covenant confirmations. They authenticate Moses' commission and point to God's sovereign power over creation, disease, and the elements.
3. **The firstborn motif.** The identification of Israel as God's firstborn introduces a theme that will climax in the Passover. Protestant typology connects this to Christ, the true Firstborn (Col. 1:15), whose death redeems all of God's children.
