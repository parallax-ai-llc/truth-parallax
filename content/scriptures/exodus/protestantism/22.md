## Property, Restitution, and Social Responsibility

Exodus 22 continues the Book of the Covenant with laws governing property offenses, restitution, social obligations, and moral conduct. These case laws apply the eighth commandment ("You shall not steal") and the tenth commandment ("You shall not covet") to everyday situations. Protestant interpreters value this chapter as a window into God's view of property, responsibility, and care for the vulnerable.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Laws on Theft and Restitution (22:1-15)

The chapter opens with detailed provisions for theft, negligence, and property damage. A distinctive feature is the emphasis on restitution rather than mere punishment.

| Offense | Restitution Required | Verse |
|---------|---------------------|-------|
| Stealing and slaughtering an ox | Fivefold repayment | 22:1 |
| Stealing and slaughtering a sheep | Fourfold repayment | 22:1 |
| Stolen animal found alive | Double repayment | 22:4 |
| Negligent damage to crops | Best of own field | 22:5-6 |
| Breach of trust with goods | Double if guilty | 22:7-9 |

**Calvin** noted that the principle of restitution — making the victim more than whole — demonstrates that biblical justice is restorative, not merely punitive. The offender must bear the cost of making things right. This principle deeply influenced Protestant legal thought, particularly in Reformed communities where restitution-based justice shaped colonial and early modern legal systems.

The story of Zacchaeus (Luke 19:8) illustrates this principle in the New Testament: the repentant tax collector voluntarily offered fourfold restitution, echoing the very provisions of Exodus 22.

### Social and Moral Laws (22:16-31)

The second half of the chapter shifts from property offenses to broader social and moral obligations:

- **Sorcery prohibited** (22:18): The Reformers took this prohibition seriously as part of the first commandment's demand for exclusive loyalty to God. All forms of occult practice are rejected as attempts to access power apart from God.
- **Oppression of strangers forbidden** (22:21): Israel's experience in Egypt becomes the moral ground for treating foreigners with justice. Protestant social ethics has drawn on this text to argue that national identity does not override the obligation to treat outsiders justly.
- **Care for widows and orphans** (22:22-24): God personally guarantees the protection of the most vulnerable. His anger burns against those who exploit them — a theme that resonated powerfully in Reformed social teaching.
- **Lending without interest** (22:25): Loans to the poor must not carry interest. This provision shaped centuries of Protestant debate about economics and usury, from Calvin's qualified acceptance of commercial interest to the Puritan emphasis on charitable lending.

### "You shall be holy to me" (22:31)

The chapter concludes with a call to holiness, grounding all the preceding laws in Israel's identity as God's consecrated people. Protestant theology reads this as a reminder that obedience flows from identity, not the reverse — Israel obeys because they belong to God, not in order to earn that status.

## Theological Significance

Exodus 22 contributes several enduring principles to Protestant ethical reflection:

- **Property is a stewardship**: The detailed laws assume the legitimacy of private property while simultaneously limiting absolute ownership. Property is held under God's sovereign authority and must be managed justly.
- **Justice must be restorative**: The emphasis on restitution — not merely punishment — reflects God's desire that wrongs be made right. This principle finds its ultimate expression in the atonement, where Christ makes restitution for the debt humanity owes to God.
- **Vulnerability demands protection**: The repeated concern for strangers, widows, orphans, and the poor reveals God's preferential attention to those most easily exploited. Protestant diaconal ministry finds its scriptural warrant in passages like these.
- **Law reveals character**: These civil statutes, while not directly binding on the church, reveal the moral character of the God who gave them. They remain valuable as expressions of divine justice, even when their specific applications belong to ancient Israel.
