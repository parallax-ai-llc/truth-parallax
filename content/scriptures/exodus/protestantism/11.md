## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 11 is a brief but climactic chapter. God tells Moses that one more plague will come upon Pharaoh and Egypt, after which Pharaoh will drive the Israelites out completely. God instructs the people to ask their Egyptian neighbors for silver and gold, and the LORD gives the people favor in the eyes of the Egyptians. Moses himself is regarded as a great man in Egypt. Moses then delivers the final warning to Pharaoh: at midnight, God will go through the midst of Egypt, and every firstborn shall die—from the firstborn of Pharaoh on his throne to the firstborn of the slave girl behind the millstone, and all the firstborn of the livestock. A great cry of anguish will rise from Egypt, unlike anything before or after. Yet among the Israelites, not even a dog will growl, so that all may know the LORD makes a distinction between Egypt and Israel. Pharaoh's servants will come and bow down to Moses, begging the Israelites to leave.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 11:1 | "Yet one plague more I will bring upon Pharaoh" | God's patience has a limit; judgment is certain |
| 11:4–5 | "About midnight I will go out in the midst of Egypt. Every firstborn in the land of Egypt shall die" | God Himself executes judgment—not an angel, not Moses |
| 11:7 | "The LORD makes a distinction between Egypt and Israel" | Election and separation—the ground of all Protestant soteriology |
| 11:8 | "And he went out from Pharaoh in hot anger" | Righteous anger at obstinate unbelief |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin emphasized that the announcement of the tenth plague reveals both God's justice and His grace simultaneously. Justice demands the death of the firstborn as the culmination of Egypt's rebellion; grace spares Israel entirely. The distinction God makes in 11:7 is not based on Israel's merit—they are sinners too—but on God's sovereign election. This is the Exodus-era foundation for the Pauline doctrine that God "has mercy on whomever he wills, and he hardens whomever he wills" (Rom. 9:18).

The plundering of the Egyptians (11:2–3) was interpreted by Calvin as divine reparation—God redistributing the wealth that Israel had earned through centuries of slave labor. This is not theft but justice administered by the sovereign Lord of all possessions.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther read the announcement of the tenth plague as the ultimate expression of the law's condemning power. The law's final word is death. But this death—the death of the firstborn—will be met by the provision of the Passover lamb. The law's condemnation and the gospel's substitution stand side by side in chapters 11 and 12, which Luther saw as inseparable.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters often structure this chapter around the theme of final warning. God does not execute judgment without prior announcement. Moses delivers the warning clearly and publicly, giving Pharaoh one last opportunity to repent. This is cited as a model of evangelistic proclamation: the gospel always includes both the promise of salvation and the warning of judgment.

## The Firstborn Motif

The death of Egypt's firstborn connects to a chain of firstborn references throughout Exodus:

| Reference | Firstborn Theme |
|-----------|----------------|
| 4:22 | Israel is God's firstborn son |
| 11:5 | Egypt's firstborn will die |
| 12:29 | The plague is executed |
| 13:2, 13 | Israel's firstborn are consecrated to God |
| 13:15 | Firstborn redeemed as memorial |

Protestant typology reads this entire motif as pointing to Christ, the "firstborn over all creation" (Col. 1:15) and "firstborn from the dead" (Col. 1:18), whose death redeems all of God's children.

## Theological Significance

1. **The certainty of judgment.** This chapter makes clear that God's warnings are not empty threats. Nine plagues have come and gone with Pharaoh's resistance intact; the tenth will be final and irrevocable. Protestant preaching has consistently used this pattern to warn against presuming on God's patience.

2. **Substitutionary logic foreshadowed.** Although the Passover lamb is not yet introduced (that comes in chapter 12), the announcement of the firstborn's death creates the theological need for a substitute. The logic is: death is certain, but provision will be made. This is the structure of the gospel itself—the wages of sin is death, but God provides a substitute.

3. **Grace within judgment.** Even in announcing the most severe plague, God provides for Israel's enrichment (11:2–3) and safety (11:7). Protestant theology sees this pattern throughout Scripture: judgment on the world is simultaneously salvation for God's people.
