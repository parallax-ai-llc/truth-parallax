## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 9 records three devastating plagues. First, a severe pestilence strikes all Egyptian livestock—horses, donkeys, camels, herds, and flocks—while Israel's animals are spared (9:1–7). Second, Moses and Aaron throw handfuls of soot into the air, and it becomes boils breaking out on both people and animals throughout Egypt. The magicians themselves are afflicted and can no longer stand before Moses (9:8–12). Third, God sends a catastrophic hailstorm, the worst Egypt has ever seen, with thunder, lightning, and fire running along the ground. Those Egyptians who heed God's warning bring their servants and livestock indoors and are spared; those who do not are struck down. Goshen is again exempt. Pharaoh confesses, "I have sinned… the LORD is in the right" (9:27), but once the hail stops, he hardens his heart again.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 9:4 | "The LORD will make a distinction between the livestock of Israel and the livestock of Egypt" | God's sovereign protection of His covenant people |
| 9:12 | "But the LORD hardened the heart of Pharaoh" | First explicit statement that God actively hardens Pharaoh |
| 9:16 | "But for this purpose I have raised you up, to show you my power" | Quoted in Romans 9:17—the linchpin of Reformed soteriology |
| 9:27 | "The LORD is in the right, and I and my people are in the wrong" | Intellectual assent without genuine repentance |

### Reformed Perspective

Verse 9:16 is arguably the most important verse in the entire plague narrative for Reformed theology. Paul quotes it in Romans 9:17 to establish God's sovereign right to show mercy or to harden as He wills. Calvin devoted extensive commentary to this verse, arguing that God does not merely foresee Pharaoh's resistance but actively ordains it to display His power and make His name known. This is not arbitrary cruelty but the outworking of divine justice: Pharaoh is a vessel of wrath fitted for destruction (Rom. 9:22), and his destruction serves the revelation of God's glory.

Verse 9:12 marks a shift in the hardening narrative. Previously the text described Pharaoh hardening his own heart; now God is the explicit agent. Reformed interpreters see this as judicial hardening—God confirming Pharaoh in the rebellion he has freely chosen.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther noted the distinction in 9:20–21 between Egyptians who feared God's word and brought their servants inside, and those who disregarded it. Even within Egypt, the word of God divides hearers into those who respond in faith and those who remain in unbelief. Luther saw this as a miniature picture of the preaching of the gospel: the same word that saves the believing condemns the unbelieving.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters highlight Pharaoh's confession in 9:27 as another instance of false repentance—even more striking because it uses the right theological language ("The LORD is in the right"). Correct doctrine without heart transformation is insufficient. This is frequently cited to distinguish between intellectual assent and saving faith.

## The Plagues as Judgment on Egyptian Gods

| Plague | Egyptian Deity Targeted | Significance |
|--------|------------------------|-------------|
| Livestock disease (5th) | Hathor (cow goddess), Apis (bull god) | YHWH has power over fertility and agriculture |
| Boils (6th) | Sekhmet (goddess of healing), Isis | YHWH inflicts what Egypt's gods cannot heal |
| Hail (7th) | Nut (sky goddess), Seth (storm god) | YHWH controls the heavens |

Protestant interpreters from the Reformation onward have read the plagues as a systematic dismantling of Egypt's religious system. Each plague targets a domain over which an Egyptian deity was believed to have authority, demonstrating that YHWH alone is God.

## Theological Significance

1. **Romans 9 and divine sovereignty.** Exodus 9:16 is the Old Testament foundation for Paul's argument about unconditional election and God's sovereign right to show mercy or harden. This verse has shaped centuries of Protestant debate between Reformed and Arminian traditions.
2. **The escalation of judgment.** The plagues increase in severity, moving from economic damage (livestock) to bodily affliction (boils) to lethal force (hail). Protestant theology sees this as a pattern of divine patience giving way to intensifying judgment when repentance is refused.
3. **Common grace within judgment.** The warning in 9:19–21 shows God offering protection even to Egyptians who will listen. This is an early expression of what Reformed theology calls "common grace"—God's kindness extended to the non-elect.
