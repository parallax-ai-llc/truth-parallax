## The Altar, the Court, and the Perpetual Light

Exodus 27 describes three elements of the Tabernacle complex: the bronze altar of burnt offering, the courtyard enclosure, and the provision of oil for the lampstand. These move from the outermost point of approach (the altar) through the boundary (the court) to the continual light within (the lamp). Protestant interpreters see in this progression a theological geography of redemption — one must pass through sacrifice before entering the presence of God.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Bronze Altar (27:1-8)

The altar of burnt offering stands at the entrance to the courtyard, the first object an approaching worshipper encounters. It is made of acacia wood overlaid with bronze, five cubits square and three cubits high, with horns at its four corners.

| Feature | Description | Significance |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Bronze construction | Durable metal associated with judgment | The altar endures the fire of God's judgment |
| Horns on corners | Projections at each corner | Place of refuge and atonement (cf. 1 Kings 1:50) |
| Grating of bronze | Network inside the altar | Allows the fire to consume completely |
| Position at entrance | First item encountered | No access to God without sacrifice |

**Calvin** stressed that the altar's placement teaches that the first step in approaching God is atonement. Before worship, before fellowship, before any other engagement with the holy God, blood must be shed. The altar of burnt offering is where substitution takes place — the animal dies in the worshipper's place.

Protestant Christology sees the bronze altar as a type of the cross. Just as every Israelite's approach to God began at this altar, so every believer's access to God begins at Calvary. The bronze, associated with judgment in prophetic symbolism (cf. the bronze serpent, Num. 21:9; John 3:14), points to Christ who bore divine judgment on behalf of sinners.

The horns of the altar are especially notable. A person fleeing for his life could grasp the horns of the altar and find sanctuary (1 Kings 1:50). Protestant preachers have long used this image: the cross is the sinner's refuge, the place where justice and mercy meet.

### The Courtyard (27:9-19)

The court is enclosed by linen curtains hung on bronze pillars, forming a rectangle one hundred cubits by fifty cubits. The single entrance is on the east side, marked by a colorful screen of blue, purple, and scarlet yarn.

The white linen boundary symbolizes the holiness that separates the sacred precinct from the common world. Only one entrance exists — there is no alternative way in. Jesus' declaration "I am the door" (John 10:9) and "I am the way" (John 14:6) find a visual precedent here. Protestant theology has consistently insisted on the exclusivity of Christ as the sole means of access to God, and the single-entry courtyard reinforces this architecturally.

The contrast between bronze (in the courtyard) and gold (inside the Tabernacle) is also significant. Bronze is associated with judgment and endurance; gold with glory and divine presence. The worshipper moves from judgment to glory, from the altar of sacrifice to the presence of God — a journey made possible only through atonement.

### Oil for the Lampstand (27:20-21)

The chapter concludes with the command to bring pure beaten olive oil so that the lampstand burns continually "from evening to morning" before the LORD. Aaron and his sons are charged with maintaining the lamp perpetually.

**Matthew Henry** noted that the light must never go out — God's presence in the sanctuary is perpetual, not intermittent. Protestant interpreters see the continual lamp as a figure of Christ, the Light that shines in the darkness and is never extinguished (John 1:5). The oil, often associated with the Holy Spirit in biblical typology, fuels the light that the priests are responsible to maintain.

## Theological Significance

- **No access without sacrifice**: The altar's placement at the entrance establishes the non-negotiable principle that atonement precedes fellowship. Protestant soteriology is built on this logic — justification precedes sanctification.
- **One way in**: The single entrance to the court anticipates the exclusive claims of Christ. Salvation is not a menu of options but a single door.
- **Perpetual light**: God's faithfulness does not waver. The continual lamp testifies that His presence and truth remain constant.
