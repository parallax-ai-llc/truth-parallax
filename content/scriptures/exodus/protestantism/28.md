## Clothed for Glory and Beauty

Exodus 28 details the sacred garments to be made for Aaron and his sons as they serve as priests before the LORD. The garments are made "for glory and for beauty" (28:2) — they are not merely functional but symbolic, communicating theological truths about the priestly office. Protestant interpreters read these garments as a rich typological portrait of Christ, the great High Priest, and of every believer's identity in Him.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Ephod (28:5-14)

The ephod is an elaborate vest-like garment made of gold, blue, purple, and scarlet yarn interwoven with fine linen. On each shoulder is an onyx stone engraved with six names of the tribes of Israel — twelve names in total. Aaron bears the names of Israel on his shoulders before the LORD.

**Calvin** saw in this a picture of Christ the High Priest who carries His people on His shoulders — the place of strength and support. The Good Shepherd who carries the lost sheep on His shoulders (Luke 15:5) is prefigured in the high priest bearing Israel's names into God's presence.

### The Breastpiece of Judgment (28:15-30)

The breastpiece contains twelve precious stones, each engraved with the name of a tribe:

| Row | Stones | Tribes |
|-----|--------|--------|
| First | Sardius, topaz, carbuncle | Reuben, Simeon, Levi |
| Second | Emerald, sapphire, diamond | Judah, Dan, Naphtali |
| Third | Jacinth, agate, amethyst | Gad, Asher, Issachar |
| Fourth | Beryl, onyx, jasper | Zebulun, Joseph, Benjamin |

Aaron bears the names of Israel "over his heart" when he enters the Holy Place (28:29). The shoulder stones and the breastpiece stones together teach that the high priest carries his people both on his shoulders (in strength) and over his heart (in love). Protestant Christology sees here a vivid picture of Christ's intercessory ministry: He bears His people before the Father in both power and affection.

The breastpiece also contains the Urim and Thummim (28:30) — objects used to discern God's will. Their exact nature is uncertain, but their purpose is clear: the priest mediates not only sacrifice but also divine guidance. In Christ, believers have both atonement and the mind of God revealed through His Word and Spirit.

### The Robe, Turban, and Other Garments (28:31-43)

Several additional features carry theological significance:

- **The blue robe with bells and pomegranates** (28:31-35): Golden bells alternate with embroidered pomegranates along the hem, so that Aaron's movements can be heard as he ministers. The bells announce that the priest is alive and active in the sanctuary — he has not been struck down by God's holiness.
- **The gold plate: "HOLY TO THE LORD"** (28:36-38): Fastened to the turban, this plate consecrates Aaron and bears the guilt of Israel's offerings. **Luther** noted the irony: even Israel's holy offerings are tainted and require the priest to bear their guilt. How much more do sinners need a High Priest who bears their iniquity — and Christ bears it not on a gold plate but in His own body on the cross.
- **Linen undergarments** (28:42-43): Even unseen garments must be pure, covering the priests' nakedness. Holiness extends to every detail, visible and invisible.

### "For Glory and for Beauty" (28:2)

This phrase captures the dual purpose of the garments. They glorify the priestly office and display the beauty of holiness. Protestant theology applies this to Christ: His priestly ministry is both glorious (He accomplishes what no one else could) and beautiful (He displays the perfect harmony of justice and mercy).

## Theological Significance

- **Christ our High Priest**: The entire chapter anticipates the argument of Hebrews 7-10. Christ is the priest who bears His people's names, carries their guilt, enters God's presence on their behalf, and lives to intercede for them.
- **The priesthood of all believers**: Because Christ has fulfilled the Aaronic priesthood, every believer now has priestly access to God (1 Pet. 2:9). The garments of glory are no longer worn by a select few — all who are in Christ are clothed in His righteousness.
- **Holiness in every detail**: The meticulous specifications teach that service to God requires reverence and attention to His standards, not casual or self-designed approaches.
