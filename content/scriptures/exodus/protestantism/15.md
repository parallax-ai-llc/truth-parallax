## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 15 divides into two sections. The first (15:1–21) is the Song of Moses and the Song of Miriam, a triumphant hymn celebrating God's victory at the Red Sea. This is one of the oldest poems in the Bible. Moses and the Israelites sing of God as a warrior who has hurled horse and rider into the sea. The song recounts the drowning of the Egyptians, declares God's incomparable majesty, and looks forward to God leading His people to His holy dwelling place. Miriam leads the women in a responsive refrain with tambourines and dancing. The second section (15:22–27) narrates the journey into the wilderness of Shur. After three days without water, they arrive at Marah, where the water is bitter and undrinkable. The people grumble, and God shows Moses a log to throw into the water, making it sweet. God then declares Himself as "the LORD, your healer" (*YHWH Ropheka*). They travel on to Elim, where they find twelve springs and seventy palm trees.

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin treated the Song of Moses as a model of doxological theology—theology expressed in praise. The song does not merely describe what happened; it interprets events theologically, attributing every aspect of deliverance to God's sovereign power and covenant love. Calvin argued that true Reformed worship must follow this pattern: recounting God's acts, attributing glory to God alone, and looking forward in faith to future fulfillment.

The transition from triumphant song (15:1–21) to bitter complaint (15:24) within three days struck Calvin as profoundly instructive. The same people who sang of God's incomparable power immediately doubt His ability to provide water. Calvin used this to warn against spiritual presumption: no past experience of grace guarantees present faithfulness. Ongoing dependence on God is required.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther valued the Song of Moses as the first great hymn of Scripture and saw in it the template for congregational hymnody. Luther's own hymn-writing was inspired by the conviction that the redeemed community should celebrate God's mighty deeds in song. The *Deutsche Messe* and the broader Lutheran chorale tradition trace their theological roots to texts like Exodus 15.

The Marah episode illustrates Luther's law-gospel hermeneutic. The bitter water represents the situation of the sinner confronted by the law's demands—bitter and undrinkable. The log thrown in to sweeten the water is a figure of the cross, which transforms the bitterness of judgment into the sweetness of grace.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpretation emphasizes the personal language of 15:2—"He has become *my* salvation." This is not abstract theology but personal testimony. Evangelical worship draws on this pattern, encouraging believers to move from knowledge about God to personal experience of God's saving power.

| Section | Verses | Content |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Declaration | 15:1–3 | God is a warrior who has triumphed |
| Narration | 15:4–10 | The destruction of Egypt's army |
| Exaltation | 15:11–13 | God's incomparability and steadfast love |
| Anticipation | 15:14–17 | Future nations will tremble; God will plant His people |
| Doxology | 15:18 | "The LORD will reign forever and ever" |
| Response | 15:20–21 | Miriam's antiphonal refrain |

## Theological Significance

1. **Worship as theology.** The Song of Moses establishes the biblical pattern that worship is not merely emotional expression but theological declaration. Protestant worship traditions—from the Genevan Psalter to Lutheran chorales to evangelical praise—all claim roots in this song's integration of doctrine and doxology.

2. **The cross at Marah.** The log that sweetens the bitter water is widely read in Protestant typology as a figure of Christ's cross. Just as the wood transforms undrinkable water into life-sustaining water, the cross transforms the curse of the law into the blessing of the gospel (Gal. 3:13).

3. **YHWH Ropheka.** God's self-identification as "healer" (15:26) introduces a theme that extends through Scripture to Christ's healing ministry. Protestant theology generally interprets this healing as primarily spiritual—God heals the fundamental sickness of sin—while not excluding physical dimensions.
