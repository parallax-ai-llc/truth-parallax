## More Than Enough

Exodus 36 records the beginning of the Tabernacle's construction and contains one of the most remarkable details in the book: the people bring so much that Moses must command them to stop giving. The chapter then describes the fabrication of the curtains, frames, and veil, following the instructions given in chapter 26. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter a powerful illustration of grace-driven generosity, faithful obedience to God's pattern, and the communal nature of worship.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Overflowing Generosity (36:2-7)

Bezalel, Oholiab, and the other skilled workers begin the project, but "the people bring much more than enough for doing the work" (36:5). Moses issues a proclamation: "Let no man or woman do anything more for the contribution" (36:6). The people had to be restrained from giving.

| Observation | Verse | Theological Significance |
|-------------|-------|------------------------|
| Craftsmen report surplus | 36:5 | God's people give beyond what is needed |
| Moses stops the offerings | 36:6 | Generosity must be channeled, not exploited |
| Material was sufficient "and more" | 36:7 | God's provision through His people is abundant |

**Matthew Henry** marveled at this scene: "It is a rare thing to hear that a congregation needs to be restrained from giving." The contrast with the church's frequent experience — struggling to fund its work — is intentional. When hearts are genuinely stirred by God's Spirit, generosity overflows. Protestant teaching on stewardship holds up this moment as the normative pattern: the gospel produces more than enough.

**Calvin** observed that the restraining order protects both the givers and the project. Excess offerings without corresponding need could become occasions for waste or corruption. Biblical generosity is not reckless but purposeful — it meets real needs and builds God's house according to God's plan.

### Construction According to the Pattern (36:8-38)

The remainder of the chapter describes the construction of the Tabernacle structure in detail that closely mirrors the instructions of chapter 26:

- **The inner curtains** (36:8-13): Ten curtains of fine linen with cherubim, joined by gold clasps.
- **The goat-hair covering** (36:14-18): Eleven curtains forming the tent over the Tabernacle.
- **The outer coverings** (36:19): Ram skins dyed red and an outer layer of durable leather.
- **The frames and bars** (36:20-34): Acacia frames overlaid with gold, set in silver bases.
- **The veil and screen** (36:35-38): The inner veil of blue, purple, and scarlet yarn with cherubim, and the screen for the entrance.

The narrative follows the divine instructions with meticulous precision. The repeated phrase from the broader construction narrative — "as the LORD had commanded Moses" — underscores a principle dear to Protestant theology: faithful worship follows God's revealed pattern, not human innovation.

**Luther** noted that obedience in the details, not just the broad strokes, characterizes genuine faithfulness. The craftsmen did not improvise or "improve" upon God's design. They built exactly what God specified. The regulative principle of worship finds practical expression here: every measurement, material, and placement reflects divine intention, not human creativity.

### The Community at Work

The construction is a communal effort involving the entire nation. Skilled craftsmen lead, but the materials come from every household. The Tabernacle is not built by a professional class in isolation but by a people united in purpose.

Protestant ecclesiology sees this as a model for the church. The body of Christ is built through the contributions of every member, each using their gifts and resources under the direction of those called and equipped to lead (Eph. 4:11-16). No one is merely a spectator.

## Theological Significance

- **Grace produces abundance**: The surplus of offerings demonstrates that Spirit-motivated generosity exceeds mere obligation. The gospel creates a people who give more than enough.
- **Obedience to the pattern**: The faithful execution of God's design teaches that worship is not an arena for human creativity but for humble conformity to divine revelation. This conviction undergirds the Protestant commitment to Scripture-regulated worship.
- **The church is built together**: Just as the Tabernacle required the contributions of the entire community — wealthy and ordinary, leaders and artisans — so the church grows as every member contributes.
- **From idolatry to worship**: The gold that had been melted into a calf is now woven into curtains for God's dwelling. Redemption redirects resources from sinful ends to sacred purposes.
