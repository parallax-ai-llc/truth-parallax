## Summary of the Narrative

God responds to Moses' anguished prayer from chapter 5 by reaffirming His covenant commitment. He declares that He appeared to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as God Almighty (*El Shaddai*) but did not make Himself known to them by His name YHWH (6:3). He recounts a series of "I will" promises: He will bring Israel out, rescue them, redeem them, take them as His people, be their God, and bring them to the promised land. Moses relays this message, but the Israelites do not listen because of their "broken spirit and harsh slavery" (6:9). God then sends Moses back to Pharaoh. The chapter includes an extensive genealogy tracing Moses and Aaron's lineage through Levi, establishing their priestly credentials.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 6:3 | "I appeared to Abraham… as God Almighty, but by my name the LORD I did not make myself known to them" | Progressive revelation—a foundational Protestant hermeneutical principle |
| 6:6 | "I will redeem you with an outstretched arm and with great acts of judgment" | Redemption as divine initiative, not human achievement |
| 6:7 | "I will take you to be my people, and I will be your God" | The covenant formula—central to Reformed covenant theology |
| 6:9 | "They did not listen to Moses, because of their broken spirit" | Even the elect can be temporarily deaf to God's promise under suffering |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theology finds in 6:6–8 a comprehensive statement of the covenant of grace. The "I will" declarations constitute an unconditional divine commitment. God does not say "if you obey, I will deliver" but simply "I will." Calvin noted that the covenant formula in 6:7—"I will be your God, and you shall be my people"—is the thread running from Abraham through Moses to the New Covenant (Jer. 31:33; Heb. 8:10).

The question of 6:3 and the divine name has generated substantial Reformed commentary. The dominant interpretation is that while the patriarchs knew the name YHWH, they did not experience its full significance—the redemptive power associated with that name. This is progressive revelation: the same God reveals Himself more fully across the history of redemption.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized that the Israelites' inability to hear the promise (6:9) illustrates the condition of those crushed by the law. The law produces a "broken spirit" that cannot receive the gospel without the Holy Spirit's intervention. Only when God acts externally—through the plagues and the exodus—does the word of promise become believable.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters highlight the pastoral dimension of this chapter. God does not rebuke Moses for his complaint in 5:22 but responds with deeper revelation of Himself. The sevenfold "I will" in 6:6–8 is often presented as a pattern of God's comprehensive salvation, covering deliverance, redemption, adoption, and inheritance.

## The Genealogy (6:14–27)

| Patriarch | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|
| Reuben and Simeon (6:14–15) | Listed first as eldest sons, establishing context |
| Levi (6:16–25) | The focal line—ancestors of Moses and Aaron |
| Amram and Jochebed (6:20) | Parents of Moses and Aaron |
| Aaron and Elisheba (6:23) | Priestly line established |
| Phinehas (6:25) | Future zealous priest (Num. 25:7–13) |

Protestant interpreters see this genealogy as deliberately placed to authenticate Moses and Aaron's authority. It also connects the exodus to the broader redemptive-historical narrative by anchoring it in the covenant lineage.

## Theological Significance

1. **Progressive revelation.** The distinction between *El Shaddai* and YHWH illustrates the Protestant principle that God reveals Himself progressively across redemptive history, with each stage building on and deepening what came before.
2. **Unconditional grace.** The "I will" promises are entirely unilateral. Protestant soteriology sees here the pattern of *sola gratia*—salvation initiated, sustained, and completed by God alone.
3. **The covenant formula.** "I will be your God, and you shall be my people" is the heartbeat of biblical theology in the Reformed tradition, connecting Old and New Testaments in a single redemptive arc.
