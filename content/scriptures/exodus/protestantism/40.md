## The Glory Comes Down

Exodus 40 is the climax of the entire book. Moses erects and assembles the Tabernacle on the first day of the first month — exactly one year after the exodus. He places each furnishing according to God's instructions, anoints the structure and its contents, and consecrates Aaron and his sons. Then, in the final scene, the cloud covers the tent of meeting and the glory of the LORD fills the Tabernacle so powerfully that even Moses cannot enter. The book that began with Israel enslaved in Egypt ends with God dwelling in their midst. Protestant interpreters regard this chapter as one of the great fulfillment texts of the Bible — the goal of redemption achieved, and the pattern for all that follows.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Moses Assembles the Tabernacle (40:1-33)

God commands Moses to erect the Tabernacle and its furnishings in a specific order, and Moses obeys with meticulous faithfulness. The refrain "as the LORD had commanded Moses" appears seven more times, bringing the total across chapters 39-40 to a structured pattern of comprehensive obedience.

| Action | Verse | Significance |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Tabernacle erected | 40:17-19 | God's dwelling takes physical form |
| Ark placed, veil hung | 40:20-21 | God's throne installed, holiness guarded |
| Table set with bread | 40:22-23 | Perpetual fellowship established |
| Lampstand lit | 40:24-25 | Light shines in the sanctuary |
| Incense altar placed | 40:26-27 | Prayer rises before the veil |
| Burnt offering and grain offering made | 40:29 | Atonement and consecration offered |
| Laver filled for washing | 40:30-32 | Ongoing purification provided |

**Calvin** noted that Moses himself performs the initial priestly service — setting the bread, lighting the lamps, burning incense, offering sacrifices. As the mediator who received the covenant and interceded for the people, Moses inaugurates the worship system before handing it to Aaron and his sons. He is prophet, priest, and leader in one person — a combination that finds its ultimate fulfillment only in Christ.

The date is significant: the first day of the first month (40:17), the anniversary of the exodus command. God's redemptive calendar is precise. One year from slavery to sanctuary, from bondage to the presence of God.

### The Glory Fills the Tabernacle (40:34-35)

> "Then the cloud covered the tent of meeting, and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle. And Moses was not able to enter the tent of meeting because the cloud settled on it, and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle."

This is the moment toward which the entire book has been moving. God's purpose declared in 25:8 — "Let them make me a sanctuary, that I may dwell in their midst" — is now realized. The same cloud that led Israel from Egypt, that descended on Sinai, and that spoke with Moses at the tent of meeting now permanently inhabits the structure built for it.

**Luther** was struck by the paradox: Moses, who spoke with God "face to face" (33:11), cannot enter. The glory is so overwhelming that even the greatest mediator of the old covenant is excluded. This signals both the reality and the limitation of the old covenant arrangement. God is truly present, but full access is not yet available. That awaits a greater mediator and a better covenant.

The parallel with Solomon's temple dedication is exact: "The glory of the LORD filled the house of the LORD" so that "the priests could not stand to minister" (1 Kings 8:10-11). And the parallel extends further — to the incarnation: "The Word became flesh and *tabernacled* among us, and we have seen his glory" (John 1:14). What the cloud concealed, Christ reveals.

### The Cloud Guides Israel (40:36-38)

The book concludes with the cloud directing Israel's movements: when the cloud lifts, they journey; when it remains, they stay. The glory is not only a presence to be worshipped but a guide to be followed.

**Matthew Henry** observed that the cloud was visible to all Israel in every stage of their journey — "in the sight of all the house of Israel throughout all their journeys" (40:38). God's guidance is not hidden or ambiguous but publicly manifest. Protestant theology applies this to the Spirit's leading of the church: God guides His people through His Word, visibly and communally, not through private mystical experiences alone.

## Theological Significance

Exodus 40 brings together the great themes of the book into a single, climactic moment:

- **Redemption's goal is God's presence**: The exodus was not merely liberation from slavery but liberation for fellowship with God. The Tabernacle is the destination of redemption. Protestant soteriology insists that salvation's highest good is not heaven as a place but God Himself as the eternal treasure.
- **The incarnation fulfills the Tabernacle**: John 1:14 uses Tabernacle language to describe the incarnation. What was shadowed in Exodus 40 is realized in Christ — God dwelling with humanity not in a tent of animal skins but in human flesh.
- **The old covenant points beyond itself**: Moses' inability to enter the glory-filled Tabernacle signals the incompleteness of the old arrangement. Full access awaits the tearing of the veil (Matt. 27:51) and the ascension of Christ into the true holy of holies (Heb. 9:24).
- **God guides His pilgrim people**: The cloud that fills the Tabernacle also leads the march. Presence and guidance belong together. The God who dwells with His people also directs their steps — a promise that extends to the church in every age.
