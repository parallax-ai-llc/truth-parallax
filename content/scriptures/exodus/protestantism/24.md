## The Covenant Sealed in Blood

Exodus 24 records one of the most solemn and dramatic scenes in the Old Testament: the formal ratification of the Sinai covenant through blood sacrifice. Moses reads the Book of the Covenant to the people, who pledge obedience; sacrifices are offered and blood is sprinkled on the altar and on the people; then Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders ascend the mountain and behold God. Finally, Moses goes up alone into the cloud for forty days and forty nights. For Protestant theology, this chapter is a pivotal text on the nature of covenant, the necessity of a mediator, and the foreshadowing of the new covenant in Christ's blood.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The People's Pledge (24:3, 7)

Twice the people declare, "All the words that the LORD has spoken we will do." The second time adds, "we will be obedient." This solemn pledge is the formal human response to the covenant — and its eventual failure is a central theme in Protestant readings of the Old Testament. The people promise more than they can deliver, revealing the fundamental inadequacy of any covenant that depends on human faithfulness.

### The Blood of the Covenant (24:6-8)

Moses takes the blood of the sacrifices, throws half against the altar (representing God) and half upon the people, declaring: "Behold the blood of the covenant that the LORD has made with you."

| Element | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Blood thrown on the altar | God's commitment sealed |
| Blood thrown on the people | The people bound to the covenant |
| The Book of the Covenant read aloud | Terms made explicit before ratification |
| Burnt offerings and peace offerings | Atonement and fellowship combined |

**Calvin** emphasized that without the shedding of blood there is no covenant, because sin requires atonement before God and humanity can enter into fellowship. The blood simultaneously covers the people's sin and binds them to their obligations.

The connection to the Lord's Supper is unmistakable. Jesus at the Last Supper declares, "This is my blood of the new covenant, poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" (Matt. 26:28). The author of Hebrews explicitly contrasts the blood of Exodus 24 with the superior blood of Christ (Heb. 9:18-22). Protestant theology sees Exodus 24 as the type that finds its antitype in Christ's sacrifice — the old covenant ratified in animal blood, the new covenant ratified in the blood of the eternal Son.

### The Vision of God (24:9-11)

Seventy-four men ascend the mountain and "saw the God of Israel." Beneath his feet was something like a pavement of sapphire stone, clear as the sky. And God "did not lay his hand" on them — they ate and drank in his presence.

This is a startling passage. Protestant interpreters note:

- **They saw a manifestation, not God's full essence**: John 1:18 states that "no one has ever seen God." What the elders beheld was a visible theophany — a real but accommodated revelation of God's glory.
- **Eating in God's presence**: The shared meal after the covenant ratification prefigures the Lord's Supper, where believers commune with God through Christ. Fellowship with God is the goal of the covenant.
- **"He did not lay his hand on them"**: The expected outcome of sinful humans encountering the holy God is death. That they survived is itself an act of grace, made possible by the blood just sprinkled.

### Moses Ascends Alone (24:12-18)

Moses alone is called higher into the cloud. He remains forty days and forty nights, receiving the stone tablets and the Tabernacle instructions. This pattern — the mediator ascending alone into God's presence on behalf of the people — is a powerful type of Christ, who ascended to the Father and entered the heavenly sanctuary as the mediator of a better covenant (Heb. 9:24).

## Theological Significance

- **Covenants require blood**: The Protestant doctrine of the atonement is grounded in the biblical principle that covenant fellowship with God is established and maintained through substitutionary sacrifice.
- **Human promises fail**: Israel's solemn pledge ("we will do") stands under the shadow of the golden calf in chapter 32. The old covenant exposes the need for a new covenant written on the heart (Jer. 31:31-33).
- **Moses as mediator type**: Moses goes where the people cannot, prefiguring Christ who enters the heavenly holy of holies on our behalf.
