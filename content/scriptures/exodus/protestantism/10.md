## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 10 presents the eighth and ninth plagues. God first tells Moses that He has hardened Pharaoh's heart in order that future generations will know His mighty acts (10:1–2). Moses and Aaron warn Pharaoh of locusts. Pharaoh's own servants urge him to let the Israelites go, recognizing that "Egypt is ruined" (10:7). Pharaoh offers a compromise—only the men may go—but Moses refuses. An east wind brings a swarm of locusts that devours every remaining green thing. Pharaoh summons Moses and Aaron, confesses sin, and begs for prayer. God removes the locusts with a west wind, but Pharaoh's heart is again hardened. Then comes the ninth plague: a darkness so thick it can be felt covers Egypt for three days. The Israelites, however, have light in their dwellings. Pharaoh offers another compromise—go, but leave your livestock—and Moses refuses. Pharaoh dismisses Moses with a threat: "Get away from me; take care never to see my face again" (10:28).

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 10:2 | "That you may tell in the hearing of your son and grandson how I dealt harshly with the Egyptians" | Intergenerational testimony—a key Protestant value |
| 10:7 | "Do you not yet understand that Egypt is ruined?" | Even worldly wisdom recognizes the futility of resisting God |
| 10:21 | "A darkness to be felt" | Spiritual blindness made physically manifest |
| 10:26 | "Not a hoof shall be left behind" | Total commitment: God demands all, not a portion |

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin saw God's stated purpose in 10:1–2—"that you may tell your children"—as establishing the principle that redemptive history is didactic. God acts in history not only to save but to teach. This aligns with the Reformed emphasis on catechesis and covenant education. The Heidelberg Catechism and Westminster Shorter Catechism both reflect this conviction that the mighty acts of God must be systematically taught to each generation.

The plague of darkness is interpreted typologically: darkness represents spiritual blindness and separation from God, while Israel's light represents the illumination that comes through covenant relationship. Calvin connected this to 2 Corinthians 4:6—the God who said "Let light shine out of darkness" is the same God who shines the light of the gospel into darkened hearts.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther read the escalation from locusts (economic devastation) to darkness (existential terror) as the intensifying weight of the law. The law strips away every comfort and resource until the sinner stands in total darkness, with no capacity to save himself. It is precisely at this point of utter helplessness that the gospel intervenes—a pattern that will reach its climax in the Passover.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters draw attention to Pharaoh's progressive compromises as a warning against partial obedience:

| Compromise Offered | What Pharaoh Concedes | What He Withholds |
|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| "Sacrifice in the land" (8:25) | Worship is allowed | Separation from Egypt |
| "Don't go far" (8:28) | Some distance | Full departure |
| "Only the men may go" (10:11) | Adult men | Women, children |
| "Leave your livestock" (10:24) | People may go | Possessions, livelihood |

Moses' response in 10:26—"not a hoof shall be left behind"—is a classic evangelical text on total surrender. God does not accept partial devotion.

## Theological Significance

1. **The purpose of divine hardening.** God explicitly states in 10:1–2 that the hardening serves a revelatory and pedagogical purpose. Protestant theology maintains that God's sovereignty is never arbitrary but always purposeful—aimed at making His name known and instructing His people.

2. **Darkness as judgment.** The ninth plague reverses creation itself. In Genesis 1:3, God's first creative act is the separation of light from darkness. In Exodus 10:21–23, judgment returns Egypt to pre-creation chaos while God's people retain light. Protestant typology connects this to John 1:5: "The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it."

3. **No compromise in worship.** Moses' insistence on total departure with all people and all possessions establishes a principle that the Reformers applied directly: worship of the true God cannot be mingled with worldly accommodation. The Reformation's rejection of religious compromise echoes Moses' refusal to negotiate terms of obedience with Pharaoh.
