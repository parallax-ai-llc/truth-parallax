## Summary of the Narrative

Moses and Aaron appear before Pharaoh with the LORD's demand: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast to me in the wilderness" (5:1). Pharaoh responds with contempt: "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice?" (5:2). Far from releasing the Israelites, Pharaoh increases their burden, requiring them to gather their own straw while maintaining the same quota of bricks. The Israelite foremen are beaten when quotas are not met. They appeal to Pharaoh without success and then turn on Moses and Aaron, accusing them of making things worse. The chapter closes with Moses crying out to God in anguish, asking why He has brought this trouble upon His people.

### Reformed Perspective

Calvin read Pharaoh's response in 5:2 as an illustration of total depravity: the unregenerate heart does not merely ignore God but actively defies Him. Pharaoh's question—"Who is the LORD?"—is not a request for information but an assertion of autonomy. Reformed theology sees in this exchange the fundamental human posture apart from grace: rebellion against divine authority.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther noted that the immediate result of God's word being spoken is not liberation but increased suffering. This is the paradox of the theology of the cross: God's saving action often looks like its opposite. The word of promise intensifies the crisis before it resolves it, just as the gospel first convicts of sin before it brings comfort.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical preaching draws from this chapter the lesson that obedience to God's call does not guarantee immediate relief. Moses obeyed, and things got worse. This is frequently used in pastoral contexts to encourage perseverance. Moses' raw prayer in 5:22–23 is presented as a model: faith does not suppress honest questions but brings them directly to God.

## Theological Significance

Exodus 5 introduces themes that are central to the Protestant reading of the entire plague narrative:

1. **The purpose of redemption is worship.** The demand is not merely "let my people go" but "let my people go *that they may serve me*." Protestant theology insists that freedom is not an end in itself but exists for the glory of God. The Westminster Shorter Catechism's first answer—"Man's chief end is to glorify God"—echoes this principle.

2. **Pharaoh as a type of the unregenerate will.** Pharaoh's hardened heart is not yet described in the technical language that appears later (chapters 7–14), but his refusal already illustrates the bondage of the will. Luther's *De Servo Arbitrio* (The Bondage of the Will) drew on the Pharaoh narrative to argue that the human will, apart from grace, is enslaved to sin.

3. **The testing of faith.** The chapter shows that God's redemptive plan includes periods of apparent setback. Reformed covenant theology interprets this as God refining the faith of His people before delivering them—a pattern seen also in Abraham's near-sacrifice of Isaac and the exile of Israel.
