## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 12 is the theological heart of the book. God institutes the Passover: each household is to select a year-old male lamb without blemish on the tenth day of the month and slaughter it on the fourteenth at twilight. The blood must be applied to the doorposts and lintel of the house. The lamb is to be roasted and eaten with unleavened bread and bitter herbs, in haste, with sandals on and staff in hand. God will pass through Egypt at midnight and strike every firstborn, but when He sees the blood, He will "pass over" that house. The Feast of Unleavened Bread is established as a seven-day memorial. At midnight, the LORD strikes all the firstborn of Egypt, from Pharaoh's son to the prisoners' children and all the firstborn of livestock. A great cry goes up. Pharaoh summons Moses and Aaron in the night and demands the Israelites leave. The Egyptians urge them to go quickly, giving them silver, gold, and clothing. About 600,000 men on foot, plus women, children, and a mixed multitude, depart with their flocks and herds. The chapter closes with regulations for future Passover observance.

| 12:13 | "When I see the blood, I will pass over you" | Substitutionary atonement: judgment passes over those covered by blood |
| 12:46 | "You shall not break any of its bones" | Fulfilled in Christ (John 19:36) |

### Reformed Perspective

Reformed theology considers the Passover the single most important Old Testament type of Christ's atoning work. The Westminster Confession of Faith (VII.5) places the Passover within the covenant of grace as one of its sacramental administrations under the Mosaic period. Calvin argued that the Passover lamb is not merely an illustration but a divinely ordained sign pointing forward to the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world (John 1:29).

The application of blood to the doorposts is read as the Old Testament equivalent of faith appropriating Christ's sacrifice. The blood saves—but only when applied. Calvin saw in this the inseparability of Christ's objective work and the believer's subjective appropriation through faith.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized the Passover as the definitive picture of justification by grace through faith. The Israelites did not earn deliverance by their works; they were saved by sheltering under the blood of the lamb. Luther's theology of the Lord's Supper is deeply shaped by Passover theology: just as the lamb was consumed by the family, so Christ gives His body and blood to be received by faith in the sacrament.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpretation of Exodus 12 typically centers on the substitutionary nature of the sacrifice. The lamb dies in place of the firstborn. This is the clearest Old Testament articulation of what Paul states in 1 Corinthians 5:7: "Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed." Evangelistic preaching draws extensively on the imagery of blood on the doorposts as a picture of personal faith in Christ's atoning death.

## Passover-to-Christ Typology

| Passover Element | Christological Fulfillment |
|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Lamb without blemish (12:5) | Christ without sin (1 Pet. 1:19; Heb. 4:15) |
| Blood on doorposts (12:7) | Christ's blood shed for many (Matt. 26:28) |
| "When I see the blood, I will pass over" (12:13) | Justification—no condemnation for those in Christ (Rom. 8:1) |
| No bones broken (12:46) | Fulfilled at crucifixion (John 19:36) |
| Eaten with unleavened bread (12:8) | Sincerity and truth, purging out sin (1 Cor. 5:7–8) |
| Eaten in haste (12:11) | Pilgrims and strangers in this world (1 Pet. 2:11) |
| The mixed multitude (12:38) | Gentiles included in God's people (Eph. 2:11–13) |

## Theological Significance

1. **Substitutionary atonement.** The Passover establishes the principle that death is satisfied by a substitute. God does not simply overlook sin; the penalty must be borne. The lamb bears it in Israel's place, and Christ bears it in the believer's place. This is the foundation of Protestant atonement theology.

2. **Sacramental institution.** Reformed and Lutheran traditions see the Passover as a sacrament of the old covenant, analogous to the Lord's Supper in the new. Jesus instituted the Lord's Supper during a Passover meal (Luke 22:15), explicitly linking the two.

3. **Covenant community.** The Passover is a household event (12:3–4), and the regulations about who may eat it (12:43–49) define the boundaries of the covenant community. This shapes Protestant debates about the sacraments—who may receive communion, and what the relationship is between the visible community and the elect.
