## Grace Greater Than Sin

Exodus 34 records the restoration of the covenant after the catastrophe of the golden calf. God summons Moses back up Sinai with two new stone tablets, proclaims His own name and character in one of the most important theological declarations in the Bible, renews the covenant with warnings against idolatry, and sends Moses back down with a face so radiant that the people are afraid to approach him. Protestant interpreters regard this chapter as the definitive Old Testament revelation of God's character — the text that Jesus, Paul, and the Reformers all rely upon to articulate the relationship between mercy and justice.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Divine Self-Revelation (34:5-7)

God descends in the cloud, stands with Moses, and proclaims His name:

> "The LORD, the LORD, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness, keeping steadfast love for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, but who will by no means clear the guilty, visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children and the children's children, to the third and the fourth generation."

This is arguably the most cited self-description of God in the entire Old Testament. It is echoed in Numbers 14:18, Nehemiah 9:17, Psalms 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joel 2:13, Jonah 4:2, and Nahum 1:3.

| Attribute | Meaning | Protestant Emphasis |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Merciful (*rachum*) | Deeply compassionate | Grace is rooted in God's nature |
| Gracious (*channun*) | Favorable to the undeserving | Sola gratia — grace alone |
| Slow to anger | Patient, long-suffering | God delays judgment to allow repentance |
| Abounding in steadfast love (*chesed*) | Covenant loyalty overflowing | God's faithfulness exceeds human failure |
| Forgiving iniquity | Pardoning rebellion | Justification by grace |
| Will not clear the guilty | Justice is not compromised | The cross satisfies both mercy and justice |

**Calvin** identified this passage as the foundation for understanding the atonement: God is both merciful (forgiving sin) and just (not clearing the guilty). These attributes appear contradictory — how can God forgive and yet not leave the guilty unpunished? The Protestant answer is the cross, where Christ bears the punishment that justice demands so that mercy can be extended to the guilty. Romans 3:25-26 is the New Testament exposition of Exodus 34:6-7.

**Luther** was deeply moved by the order of the attributes: mercy comes first. God leads with grace, not wrath. The law reveals sin, but the gospel reveals the God who is "merciful and gracious" before He is anything else.

### Covenant Renewal and Warnings (34:10-28)

God renews the covenant with specific warnings against entanglement with Canaanite religion:

- No covenants with the land's inhabitants (34:12)
- Destroy their altars, pillars, and Asherah poles (34:13)
- No intermarriage (34:16)
- No molten gods (34:17)

These commands flow directly from God's self-description as "jealous" (34:14). Protestant theology interprets divine jealousy not as petty insecurity but as the righteous zeal of a faithful God who will not share His people's worship with rivals. The marriage metaphor is central: God's jealousy is the passion of a husband for the exclusive love of his bride.

The chapter also reiterates the three annual festivals, the Sabbath, the firstborn redemption, and the prohibition against boiling a kid in its mother's milk — renewal of the same covenant obligations given before the fall.

### Moses' Radiant Face (34:29-35)

When Moses descends, "the skin of his face shone because he had been talking with God." The people are afraid, and Moses begins to wear a veil when not speaking God's word to them.

Paul interprets this passage extensively in 2 Corinthians 3:7-18. The veil, Paul argues, represents the fading glory of the old covenant — a glory that was real but temporary. Under the new covenant, "we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another" (2 Cor. 3:18).

**Calvin** drew the contrast sharply: Moses veiled his face; believers in Christ have the veil removed. The old covenant gave glimpses of glory; the new covenant gives direct access. Moses reflected borrowed light; believers are being transformed into glory itself.

## Theological Significance

- **God's character resolves the paradox of salvation**: Exodus 34:6-7 is the theological problem that the cross solves. Only in Christ can God be simultaneously "just and the justifier" (Rom. 3:26).
- **Grace restores what sin destroyed**: The covenant is renewed — not because Israel deserved it, but because God is who He says He is: merciful, gracious, and abounding in steadfast love.
- **New covenant surpasses old**: Moses' veiled, fading glory points forward to the unveiled, increasing glory available to every believer through the Spirit.
