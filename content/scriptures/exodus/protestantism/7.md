## Summary of the Narrative

God commissions Moses and Aaron to return to Pharaoh, declaring that Moses will be "as God to Pharaoh" and Aaron will be his prophet (7:1). God warns that He will harden Pharaoh's heart and multiply His signs in Egypt. Moses is eighty years old and Aaron eighty-three when they confront Pharaoh. Aaron casts down his staff, and it becomes a serpent; Pharaoh's magicians replicate the feat, but Aaron's serpent swallows theirs. Pharaoh's heart remains hardened. God then initiates the first plague: Aaron strikes the Nile with his staff, and its waters turn to blood. Fish die, the river stinks, and the Egyptians cannot drink. Pharaoh's magicians duplicate this sign as well, and Pharaoh turns away unmoved.

## Key Verses and Protestant Perspectives

| Verse | Text (ESV) | Protestant Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 7:1 | "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh" | Moses as mediator—a type of Christ |
| 7:3 | "But I will harden Pharaoh's heart" | Divine sovereignty in salvation and judgment |
| 7:5 | "The Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD" | God's self-revelation through judgment |
| 7:12 | "Aaron's staff swallowed up their staffs" | God's power supersedes all counterfeit powers |

### Reformed Perspective

The hardening of Pharaoh's heart is one of the most discussed texts in Reformed theology. Calvin distinguished between God's active hardening and Pharaoh's own culpable resistance, arguing that both are true simultaneously. God does not create evil in Pharaoh's heart but sovereignly directs Pharaoh's existing sinfulness to serve His purposes. Romans 9:17–18 cites this passage explicitly to defend the doctrine of divine sovereignty in election and reprobation.

The Reformed tradition identifies three different Hebrew verbs used for hardening throughout the plague narrative:

| Hebrew Verb | Meaning | Typical Usage |
|------------|---------|---------------|
| *chazaq* | To strengthen, make firm | God hardens Pharaoh's heart |
| *kaved* | To make heavy, dull | Pharaoh's heart becomes heavy |
| *qashah* | To make stiff, obstinate | Pharaoh stiffens his heart |

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther similarly affirmed divine sovereignty over Pharaoh's heart but emphasized the pastoral dimension: God's hardening is always a response to prior rebellion. Pharaoh's heart was already set against God; the divine hardening confirms him in the path he has chosen. Luther used this to illustrate his doctrine of the bondage of the will—apart from grace, the sinner cannot choose God.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical commentators often seek to balance sovereignty and responsibility. They note that in early plagues, the text says Pharaoh hardened his own heart, while in later plagues God is the active agent. The cumulative pattern suggests that persistent resistance to God leads to a point where God confirms the sinner in judgment—a solemn warning against presumption.

## The First Plague: Water to Blood (7:14–25)

The turning of the Nile to blood strikes at the heart of Egyptian religion. The Nile was venerated as a source of life; its transformation into blood renders it a source of death. Protestant interpreters see here the beginning of God's systematic dismantling of Egypt's false gods—a theme that will continue through all ten plagues.

Typologically, blood as a sign of judgment anticipates the Passover, where blood becomes a sign of salvation. The same substance that brings death to Egypt will bring life to Israel—a pattern Protestant theology sees fulfilled in the blood of Christ, which is simultaneously judgment (on sin) and salvation (for the believer).

## Theological Significance

1. **Sovereignty and responsibility.** The hardening narrative raises the deepest questions of Protestant theology. The Reformed answer is that God's sovereignty and human responsibility are not contradictory but complementary: Pharaoh is fully responsible for his rebellion, and God is fully sovereign over its outcome.
2. **Judgment as revelation.** The plagues are not arbitrary punishments but revelatory acts. God says, "The Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD" (7:5). Judgment reveals God's character just as surely as mercy does.
3. **Contest with false gods.** The plague narrative is a systematic confrontation between YHWH and the gods of Egypt. Protestant theology sees this as paradigmatic: the true God always triumphs over idols.
