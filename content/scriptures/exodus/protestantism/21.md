## The Book of the Covenant Begins

Exodus 21 opens the section traditionally called the Book of the Covenant (Exod. 21–23), the body of civil and case law that applies the Ten Commandments to concrete situations in Israelite life. Chapter 21 addresses the treatment of servants, personal injuries, and the principle of proportional justice (lex talionis). For Protestant interpreters, these laws reveal God's concern for human dignity even within the social structures of the ancient Near East, and they point forward to the perfect justice fulfilled in Christ.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Laws on Hebrew Servants (21:1-11)

The chapter begins with regulations governing indentured servitude — a system by which a debtor could work off obligations over a six-year period, with mandatory release in the seventh year.

| Provision | Verse | Significance |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Six-year limit on service | 21:2 | Sabbath principle applied to labor contracts |
| Freedom in the seventh year | 21:2 | Echoes creation rest and anticipates jubilee |
| Voluntary permanent service | 21:5-6 | The servant who loves his master chooses to stay |
| Protection of female servants | 21:7-11 | Rights guaranteed even to the most vulnerable |

**Calvin** observed that these laws were not an endorsement of slavery but a regulation of existing economic realities, designed to prevent the worst abuses and protect the vulnerable. The six-year limit embedded the Sabbath principle into economic life, ensuring that debt did not become permanent bondage.

The image of the servant who voluntarily submits to his master's service out of love (21:5-6) has been read by Protestant interpreters as a Christological type. Psalm 40:6-8, quoted in Hebrews 10:5-7, speaks of "ears you have opened" — an allusion to the pierced ear of the willing servant. Christ is the ultimate willing servant who, out of love for the Father and for His people, chose obedience even to death.

### The Lex Talionis (21:23-25)

The famous formula "eye for eye, tooth for tooth" establishes the principle of proportional justice — the punishment must fit the offense, no more and no less.

Protestant interpreters emphasize two key points:

- **Restraint, not revenge**: The lex talionis was designed to limit retaliation, not encourage it. It prevented blood feuds and escalating vengeance by capping punishment at exact equivalence.
- **Jesus' teaching in Matthew 5:38-39**: Christ does not abolish the civil principle but transcends it in the ethics of the kingdom, calling His followers to forgo personal retaliation. Luther's doctrine of the two kingdoms helps distinguish between the civil use of proportional justice (which remains valid for governments) and the personal ethic of turning the other cheek.

### Protection of Human Life (21:12-14, 20-21, 26-27)

The chapter assigns the death penalty for murder (21:12) while distinguishing between intentional killing and manslaughter (21:13-14). Servants who suffer permanent injury — the loss of an eye or tooth — must be set free (21:26-27), giving even the lowest-status person legal recourse.

**Matthew Henry** noted that these provisions demonstrate that in God's economy, no human being is mere property. The life and body of every person, including servants, carries the image of God and is therefore protected by divine law.

## Theological Significance

Exodus 21 raises important questions about the three uses of the law in Protestant theology:

- **The civil use**: These specific statutes governed ancient Israel's legal system. While not directly binding on modern nations, they illustrate principles of justice — proportionality, protection of the vulnerable, accountability — that remain relevant for all civil governance.
- **The pedagogical use**: The detailed case law reveals how far short human societies fall of perfect justice, driving readers to acknowledge their need for grace.
- **The normative use**: The underlying moral principles — human dignity, fair treatment, proportional justice — continue to guide the Christian's understanding of righteousness.

The chapter also reinforces the Protestant conviction that the gospel does not abolish the concern for justice but fulfills it. Christ bore the penalty that justice demanded so that mercy could be extended without compromising righteousness (Rom. 3:25-26).
