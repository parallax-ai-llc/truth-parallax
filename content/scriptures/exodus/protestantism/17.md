## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 17 contains two episodes. In the first (17:1–7), the Israelites camp at Rephidim and find no water. The people quarrel with Moses and demand water, nearly ready to stone him. Moses cries out to the LORD, and God instructs him to take his staff, stand before the rock at Horeb, and strike it. Water gushes out for the people to drink. Moses names the place Massah ("testing") and Meribah ("quarreling") because the people tested God, asking, "Is the LORD among us or not?" In the second episode (17:8–16), the Amalekites attack Israel. Moses sends Joshua to lead the battle while he stands on a hilltop holding the staff of God. When Moses raises his hands, Israel prevails; when he lowers them, Amalek prevails. Aaron and Hur support Moses' arms until sunset, and Joshua defeats Amalek. God declares perpetual war against Amalek, and Moses builds an altar called "The LORD Is My Banner" (*YHWH Nissi*).

### Reformed Perspective

The water from the rock is one of the most theologically significant typologies in the Protestant tradition, because Paul makes the identification explicit: "They drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ" (1 Cor. 10:4). Calvin argued that this is not mere metaphor but sacramental reality: just as the rock, when struck, poured forth life-giving water, so Christ, struck on the cross, pours forth the living water of the Holy Spirit. The rock is struck once (at Horeb in Exodus 17), corresponding to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice.

The battle with Amalek is read by Reformed theologians as an illustration of the necessity of persistent prayer. Moses' upraised hands represent intercession, and the connection between his posture and the battle's outcome demonstrates that spiritual victories are won through prayer sustained by community (Aaron and Hur). Calvin connected this to Christ's ongoing intercessory work at the right hand of God (Heb. 7:25).

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther emphasized the scandal of Israel's complaint. They have witnessed the plagues, the Passover, the Red Sea crossing, and the manna—yet they question whether God is among them. Luther saw this as an indictment of the human heart's incurable tendency to forget God's past faithfulness. The question "Is the LORD among us or not?" is the quintessential expression of unbelief—not the absence of evidence but the refusal to trust.

The struck rock, for Luther, illustrates the theology of the cross. The source of life appears as dead stone; it must be struck—broken—before it yields water. So Christ appears in weakness and suffering, and from His broken body flows the water of salvation.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters draw two primary applications. First, the water from the rock demonstrates that God meets His people's needs even when they approach Him in anger and doubt. Grace is not contingent on the worthiness of the recipients. Second, the Amalek narrative is a model for spiritual warfare: the battle is fought on two fronts simultaneously—Joshua on the battlefield and Moses on the hilltop—illustrating the inseparability of action and prayer.

## Typological Connections

| Exodus 17 Element | Christological Fulfillment |
|-------------------|---------------------------|
| Rock struck at Horeb | Christ crucified once for all (Heb. 9:28) |
| Water from the rock | Living water from Christ (John 7:37–38) |
| "The Rock was Christ" | Paul's explicit identification (1 Cor. 10:4) |
| Moses' upraised arms | Christ's intercession at God's right hand (Heb. 7:25) |
| Joshua defeats Amalek | Jesus (same name) conquers the enemies of God's people |

The name "Joshua" (Hebrew: *Yehoshua*) is the same name rendered "Jesus" in Greek. Protestant interpreters note the significance: it is Joshua/Jesus who fights the battle on the ground while the mediator intercedes above.

## Theological Significance

1. **Christ the smitten Rock.** This typology, established by apostolic authority (1 Cor. 10:4), is foundational for Protestant Christology. The rock's water is freely given to an undeserving people—grace in its purest form.

2. **Intercessory prayer.** Moses' sustained intercession during the battle establishes the pattern of priestly intercession that finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ. The church's prayer, sustained by the community, participates in Christ's ongoing intercession.

3. **Testing God.** The place names Massah and Meribah become permanent warnings in Scripture (Deut. 6:16; Ps. 95:8–9; Heb. 3:8). Jesus quotes "You shall not put the LORD your God to the test" (Matt. 4:7) in resisting Satan's temptation—where Israel failed, Christ succeeds.
