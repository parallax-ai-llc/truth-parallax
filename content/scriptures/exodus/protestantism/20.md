## Summary of the Narrative

Exodus 20 contains the Decalogue—the Ten Commandments—spoken by God directly to the entire people of Israel from Mount Sinai. The chapter opens with the covenantal preamble: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery" (20:2). Then follow the ten words: no other gods, no graven images, no taking God's name in vain, remember the Sabbath, honor your father and mother, no murder, no adultery, no stealing, no false witness, and no coveting. After hearing God's voice accompanied by thunder, lightning, trumpet blasts, and a smoking mountain, the people are terrified and beg Moses to serve as mediator: "You speak to us, and we will listen; but do not let God speak to us, lest we die" (20:19). Moses assures them that God has come to test them, so that the fear of Him may keep them from sinning. The chapter closes with instructions about proper worship: no gods of silver or gold, and altars of earth or uncut stone.

### Reformed Perspective: The Three Uses of the Law

The Reformed tradition, following Calvin, identifies three uses (*usus*) of the moral law as expressed in the Decalogue:

| Use | Latin Name | Function | Application |
|-----|-----------|----------|-------------|
| First | *Usus politicus* | Civil use: restrains sin in society | The commandments serve as the basis for civil law and public morality |
| Second | *Usus elenchticus* | Pedagogical use: convicts of sin | The law is a mirror revealing human sinfulness and driving sinners to Christ (Gal. 3:24) |
| Third | *Usus tertius* / *usus in renatis* | Normative use: guides the believer's life | The law instructs the regenerate in holy living; the "rule of gratitude" |

Calvin regarded the third use as the "principal use" of the law. The believer, justified by grace through faith, turns to the Decalogue not as a means of earning salvation but as a guide for grateful obedience. This is why the Westminster Larger Catechism devotes questions 91–148 to a detailed exposition of the Ten Commandments as the rule of the Christian life.

### Lutheran Perspective

Luther recognized primarily two uses of the law (the civil and the pedagogical), though he acknowledged a function similar to the third use in practice. For Luther, the Decalogue's primary spiritual function is to crush human self-righteousness. The tenth commandment—"You shall not covet"—is especially significant because it reveals that the law addresses the interior disposition, not merely external actions. No human being can claim to have never coveted; therefore, the law condemns universally, driving all people to seek grace.

Luther's *Large Catechism* provides an extensive exposition of each commandment, treating the Decalogue as the definitive summary of the divine will. He emphasized that the commandments begin with faith ("You shall have no other gods") because right relationship with God is the foundation of all other duties.

### Evangelical Perspective

Evangelical interpreters stress the relational context of the commandments. The Decalogue is not a cold legal code imposed on strangers but a covenant charter given by a loving God to His redeemed people. The preamble (20:2) establishes this: God identifies Himself and His saving acts before giving a single command. The commandments describe what life looks like within a restored relationship with God—the first four governing the vertical relationship (God and humanity), the last six governing the horizontal (human and human).

## The Two Tables of the Law

| Table | Commandments | Focus |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| First Table | 1–4 | Duties toward God: exclusive worship, proper worship, reverence for God's name, Sabbath rest |
| Second Table | 5–10 | Duties toward neighbor: family honor, sanctity of life, marital fidelity, property rights, truthful speech, contentment of heart |

Jesus summarized both tables in two commands: love God with all your heart (first table) and love your neighbor as yourself (second table)—Matthew 22:37–40.

## Theological Significance

1. **The indicative-imperative pattern.** The Decalogue begins not with a command but with a declaration: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out." Protestant theology builds on this pattern: the gospel (what God has done) always precedes the law (what God requires). Obedience flows from redemption, not the reverse.

2. **The law and Christ.** Jesus did not abolish the law but fulfilled it (Matt. 5:17). Protestant theology maintains that the moral law as summarized in the Decalogue retains its authority as the standard of righteousness. Christ kept the law perfectly on behalf of believers (active obedience) and bore its penalty on the cross (passive obedience). This double imputation—Christ's righteousness credited to the believer, the believer's sin credited to Christ—is the heart of Protestant soteriology.

3. **The need for a mediator.** The people's terrified request in 20:19—"You speak to us… do not let God speak to us"—reveals the fundamental human problem: sinners cannot stand before a holy God. Moses serves as mediator here, but Protestant theology sees him as a type pointing to Christ, the one Mediator between God and humanity (1 Tim. 2:5).

4. **The tenth commandment and total depravity.** The prohibition against coveting demonstrates that God's law addresses the heart. If the law required only external compliance, some might claim innocence. But the command not to covet—which Paul identifies as the commandment that exposed his own sin (Rom. 7:7)—reveals that all people stand guilty before God. This is a cornerstone text for the Reformed doctrine of total depravity.
