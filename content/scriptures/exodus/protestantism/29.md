## Set Apart for God's Service

Exodus 29 prescribes the elaborate seven-day ceremony for consecrating Aaron and his sons as priests. The ritual involves washing, robing, anointing, and three distinct sacrifices — a bull for a sin offering, a ram for a burnt offering, and a ram of ordination. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter a comprehensive theology of priestly consecration that illuminates Christ's perfect priesthood and the believer's consecration to God.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Preparation (29:1-9)

The ceremony begins with three essential acts:

| Act | Description | Significance |
|-----|------------|-------------|
| Washing with water (29:4) | Ceremonial cleansing at the Tabernacle entrance | Purity required before service |
| Clothing with garments (29:5-6, 8-9) | Aaron and sons robed in priestly vestments | Identity conferred through investiture |
| Anointing with oil (29:7) | Oil poured on Aaron's head | The Spirit's empowerment for service |

**Calvin** observed that the washing, clothing, and anointing follow a deliberate sequence: cleansing precedes consecration, and consecration precedes service. This order mirrors the Christian life — regeneration (washing), justification (clothing in Christ's righteousness), and sanctification (the Spirit's anointing) — all preceding faithful service.

The anointing with oil is particularly significant. The Hebrew word *mashach* ("to anoint") gives us "Messiah" — the Anointed One. Aaron's anointing prefigures Christ's anointing with the Spirit at His baptism (Luke 3:22), which inaugurated His public ministry as the ultimate High Priest.

### The Three Sacrifices (29:10-28)

The consecration requires three sacrifices in sequence, each addressing a different aspect of the priests' relationship to God:

**The bull as sin offering** (29:10-14): Aaron and his sons lay their hands on the bull's head, transferring their sin symbolically to the animal. It is then slaughtered, its blood applied to the altar's horns, and its flesh burned outside the camp. Protestant theology emphasizes the hand-laying as a picture of imputation — sin is transferred from the guilty to the substitute. Christ, the ultimate sin offering, was crucified "outside the gate" (Heb. 13:11-12), fulfilling this type.

**The first ram as burnt offering** (29:15-18): This ram is wholly burned on the altar as "a pleasing aroma to the LORD." The burnt offering represents total consecration — the entire animal is given to God. **Luther** saw in this the principle that the priest must be wholly dedicated, holding nothing back. Christ offered Himself completely, and those who serve Him are called to present their bodies as "living sacrifices" (Rom. 12:1).

**The ram of ordination** (29:19-28): This unique sacrifice involves applying blood to the right ear, right thumb, and right big toe of each priest — consecrating what they hear, what they do, and where they walk. The breast and thigh of this ram become the priests' portion, establishing their ongoing provision through service.

### "I Will Dwell Among the People of Israel" (29:42-46)

The chapter concludes with one of the most sweeping statements of God's purpose in Exodus:

> "I will dwell among the people of Israel and will be their God. And they shall know that I am the LORD their God, who brought them out of the land of Egypt that I might dwell among them."

The entire system — the Tabernacle, the priesthood, the sacrifices — exists for one purpose: God dwelling with His people. **Matthew Henry** commented that redemption from Egypt was not an end in itself but the means to fellowship. God freed Israel so that He could live among them. Protestant theology extends this to the gospel: Christ redeems His people so that God may dwell with them forever (Rev. 21:3).

## Theological Significance

- **Christ's superior consecration**: Unlike Aaron, Christ needed no sin offering for Himself (Heb. 7:27). He was sinless, requiring no washing or purification. His consecration was by the oath of God (Heb. 7:20-22), not by ritual.
- **The believer's consecration**: Peter describes all believers as "a royal priesthood" (1 Pet. 2:9). The consecration pattern of washing (baptism), clothing (Christ's righteousness), and anointing (the Spirit) applies to every Christian.
- **The cost of access**: The elaborate and bloody ceremony underscores that priestly access to God is not casual or cheap. It cost the life of substitutes — and ultimately cost the life of Christ.
