## God Dwells Among His People

Exodus 25 marks a turning point in the book. God instructs Moses to receive voluntary offerings from the people and to build a sanctuary — "that I may dwell in their midst" (25:8). The chapter then gives detailed instructions for the three central furnishings of the Tabernacle's inner rooms: the Ark of the Covenant, the Table of Showbread, and the Golden Lampstand. Protestant interpreters, following the lead of the author of Hebrews, read these furnishings as Christological types of extraordinary richness.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Freewill Offering (25:1-9)

God commands Moses to collect offerings "from every man whose heart moves him" (25:2). The materials include gold, silver, bronze, fine fabrics, animal skins, oil, spices, and gemstones. Two principles emerge:

- **Willing generosity**: God does not coerce; He desires cheerful givers (cf. 2 Cor. 9:7). The Tabernacle is built from the overflow of grateful hearts.
- **"According to the pattern"** (25:9): Every detail is prescribed by God, not invented by human creativity. Reformed theology draws the regulative principle of worship from this insistence — worship must be ordered according to God's Word, not human imagination.

### The Ark of the Covenant (25:10-22)

The Ark is a gold-covered acacia chest containing the tablets of the law, topped by the mercy seat with two cherubim whose wings overshadow it. God declares: "There I will meet with you, and from above the mercy seat... I will speak with you" (25:22).

| Feature | Description | Protestant Typology |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| Acacia wood overlaid with gold | Durable wood covered in glory | Christ's humanity and divinity |
| The law inside | Tablets of the Decalogue | Christ who perfectly fulfilled the law |
| The mercy seat (kapporet) | Cover/place of atonement | The propitiation provided by Christ (Rom. 3:25) |
| Cherubim | Guardians of God's holiness | Angelic witnesses to redemption |
| God's voice from above | The meeting place | Christ as the locus of divine-human encounter |

The mercy seat is especially significant. The Hebrew word *kapporet* is related to *kaphar* ("to atone"). Paul uses the same Greek term (*hilasterion*) in Romans 3:25 to describe Christ as the one "whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood." **Luther** argued that the mercy seat teaches the gospel in miniature: the law is inside the Ark (condemning), but the mercy seat covers it (atoning), and God speaks from above it (forgiving). The sinner meets God not at the place of law but at the place of mercy.

### The Table of Showbread (25:23-30)

Twelve loaves are placed on a gold-covered table before the LORD continually, representing the twelve tribes in perpetual fellowship with God. Protestant interpreters connect this to Christ as the Bread of Life (John 6:35) and to the Lord's Supper, where believers feast in God's presence.

### The Golden Lampstand (25:31-40)

The lampstand (*menorah*), hammered from a single piece of gold, provides light in the Holy Place. Its seven branches, almond-blossom decorations, and perpetual flame have been interpreted as:

- **Christ the Light of the World** (John 8:12): The only source of light in the sanctuary.
- **The church's witness**: In Revelation 1:20, the seven lampstands represent seven churches, their light derived from Christ who stands among them.
- **The Spirit's illumination**: The oil-fed flame suggests the Holy Spirit's role in bringing light to God's people.

## Theological Significance

Exodus 25 establishes the foundational Protestant conviction that the entire Tabernacle system is Christological:

- **God's desire to dwell with humanity** (25:8) is fulfilled in the incarnation: "The Word became flesh and *tabernacled* among us" (John 1:14).
- **The pattern from heaven** (25:9, 40) indicates that the earthly Tabernacle is a copy of heavenly realities (Heb. 8:5), which Christ has now entered on our behalf.
- **Every furnishing points to Christ**: The Ark speaks of His person, the mercy seat of His atoning work, the bread of His sustaining presence, and the lampstand of His illuminating truth.
