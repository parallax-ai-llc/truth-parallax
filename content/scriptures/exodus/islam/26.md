## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 26 provides the detailed specifications for the Tabernacle's curtains, frames, and the veil separating the Holy Place from the Holy of Holies. These architectural details belong to the specific ritual system given to Bani Isra'il and are classified in Islamic scholarship as Isra'iliyyat — historical traditions from the People of the Book that may be acknowledged without being adopted. Islam does not replicate or continue the Tabernacle's design, as the final Shari'ah established its own principles for sacred space.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not contain direct parallels to the Tabernacle's construction details. The closest conceptual parallels relate to sacred space more broadly:

- **Al-Jinn 72:18**: "And indeed, the masjids are for Allah, so do not invoke with Allah anyone." This establishes the Islamic principle of sacred space: it is defined by its dedication to Allah alone, not by architectural elaboration.
- **Al-Baqarah 2:144**: The qiblah (direction of prayer) toward the Sacred Mosque (al-Masjid al-Haram) replaces any previous orientation system, including the Tabernacle.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The concept of a graduated sacred space — an outer court, a Holy Place, and an innermost Holy of Holies accessible only to the high priest once a year — is fundamentally different from Islamic worship architecture. In Islam, every part of a mosque is equally accessible to every Muslim. There is no restricted sacred zone, no priestly class with exclusive access, and no physical barrier between the worshiper and the direction of prayer.

The veil (parokhet) separating the Holy of Holies (Exodus 26:31-33) symbolizes the distinction between ordinary and ultimate sacred space. Islam does not employ this concept; the Ka'bah itself, while containing an interior, is primarily significant as a directional marker (qiblah) rather than as a vessel containing the divine presence. The kiswah (cloth covering the Ka'bah) serves a different function from the Tabernacle's veil.

The elaborate materials — gold, silver, bronze, fine linen, acacia wood — reflect a level of ritual ornamentation that contrasts with the early Islamic emphasis on simplicity in worship spaces. The Prophet Muhammad's original mosque in Madinah was constructed of palm trunks and mud brick, establishing a model of unpretentious worship.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** does not extensively comment on the Tabernacle's architecture, as it falls outside the Quranic narrative. He mentions it briefly in passing when discussing the general history of Bani Isra'il's post-exodus period.

**Al-Tabari** preserves some traditions from earlier authorities about the Tabernacle's construction, treating them as historical narrations without theological commentary. He is characteristically careful to distinguish between what is established by the Quran and what is derived from earlier scriptures.

**Al-Qurtubi** uses the concept of sacred architecture to discuss the Islamic rulings on mosque construction, noting that while beauty in worship spaces is permitted, excessive ornamentation that distracts from worship is discouraged. He draws a principled distinction between honoring Allah through simplicity and honoring Him through elaboration, ultimately favoring the former.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The Tabernacle's layered architecture, with increasing holiness toward the center, raises important questions about Allah's relationship to physical space. Islamic theology resolves this by affirming Allah's complete transcendence — He is not more present in one location than another. Sacred spaces in Islam are honored because of the worship that occurs in them, not because they contain or house the divine. This understanding is a direct application of tawhid: Allah's unity implies His independence from all created things, including the most elaborately constructed sacred structures.
