## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 31 addresses two themes: the appointment of skilled craftsmen (Bezalel and Oholiab) for the Tabernacle's construction, and the reiteration of the Sabbath commandment. The chapter concludes with God giving Musa the two tablets of testimony. Islam engages with the concept of divinely granted skills, acknowledges the Sabbath as a law for Bani Isra'il, and confirms the tablets through Quranic reference.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-A'raf 7:145**: "And We wrote for him on the tablets (al-alwah) from all things — instruction and explanation for all things. So take them with determination and order your people to take the best of it."
- **Al-A'raf 7:154**: "And when the anger subsided in Musa, he took up the tablets; and in their inscription was guidance and mercy for those who are fearful of their Lord."
- **An-Nahl 16:124**: "The Sabbath was only appointed for those who differed over it." This verse contextualizes the Sabbath as a specific obligation for a specific community.
- **An-Nahl 16:68-69**: Allah inspires the bee to build and produce — an example of divinely granted skill in nature, paralleling the concept of God-given craftsmanship.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The divine filling of craftsmen with "the Spirit of God" for artistic work (Exodus 31:3) resonates with the Islamic concept of ilham (divine inspiration) and tawfiq (divine enabling). Islam recognizes that all human abilities are ultimately from Allah — "Allah created you and that which you do" (As-Saffat 37:96). However, Islam does not use the language of being "filled with the Spirit" in the way biblical theology does.

The Sabbath as a "perpetual covenant" and "sign between Me and the people of Israel" (Exodus 31:16-17) is acknowledged in Islam as having been prescribed for Bani Isra'il specifically. Islam explicitly does not observe the Sabbath. The Quran mentions the Sabbath primarily in the context of its violation (Al-Baqarah 2:65, Al-A'raf 7:163-166), where those who transgressed were transformed as punishment — a severe warning about breaking divine law.

The death penalty for Sabbath violation (Exodus 31:14-15) is referenced indirectly in the Quranic punishment stories but is not part of Islamic law. Friday (Jumu'ah) carries no equivalent rest prohibition — Muslims are commanded to attend congregational prayer but may work before and after (Al-Jumu'ah 62:10).

The two tablets of testimony (Exodus 31:18) are confirmed by the Quran (Al-A'raf 7:145, 150, 154), though the Quran does not specify that they were inscribed "by the finger of God." Islamic theology is careful about anthropomorphic descriptions of Allah's actions, preferring to affirm that the tablets were inscribed by divine power without specifying the means.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the tablets at length, noting that they contained guidance, mercy, and instruction for Bani Isra'il. He records traditions about their material — some say emerald, others sapphire — while acknowledging that these are Isra'iliyyat details.

**Al-Tabari** examines the relationship between the Sabbath and the Jewish community, explaining that it was a test specific to Bani Isra'il and that its obligation ended with the final revelation.

**Al-Qurtubi** provides detailed legal discussion about the status of the Sabbath in Islam, concluding that it was abrogated by the Quranic revelation. He discusses the wisdom of Friday being chosen for the Muslim ummah, citing hadith traditions that Muslims are "the last yet the foremost" — given Friday, the day before the Jewish Sabbath and the Christian Sunday.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The divine bestowal of skills for sacred craftsmanship illustrates tawhid through the recognition that all talent comes from Allah. The tablets given to Musa represent the divine origin of law — a core Islamic teaching. The Sabbath's specificity to Bani Isra'il demonstrates the Islamic principle that while aqidah (creed) is universal and unchanging, specific legal rulings (ahkam) may differ between prophetic dispensations according to divine wisdom.
