## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 23 combines ethical injunctions about justice, treatment of enemies, and the Sabbath with instructions for three annual festivals. Islam engages with the ethical content of this chapter while recognizing the festival regulations as specific to the Shari'ah of Musa. The principles of impartial justice ('adl) and compassion (rahmah) articulated here are among the most universally affirmed values in Islamic ethics.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-Ma'idah 5:8**: "O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm for Allah, witnesses in justice, and do not let the hatred of a people prevent you from being just. Be just; that is nearer to righteousness." This directly parallels Exodus 23:1-3's injunctions against false witness and partiality.
- **An-Nisa 4:135**: "O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah, even if it be against yourselves or parents and relatives."
- **Al-Mumtahanah 60:8**: "Allah does not forbid you from those who do not fight you because of religion and do not expel you from your homes — from being righteous toward them and acting justly toward them."
- **Al-Fath 48:29**: The believing community is described as "merciful among themselves."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The three annual festivals — Unleavened Bread, Harvest, and Ingathering (Exodus 23:14-17) — are not observed in Islam. Islam has its own festival calendar: Eid al-Fitr (after Ramadan) and Eid al-Adha (during Hajj). These reflect the Islamic community's distinct identity while serving analogous purposes of communal worship and gratitude.

The command to help one's enemy's donkey (Exodus 23:4-5) resonates with Islamic ethics but goes unmentioned in the Quran specifically. However, the Prophet Muhammad's teachings extensively address kindness to animals and generosity toward adversaries. The hadith tradition includes numerous examples of ethical treatment transcending personal animosity.

The prohibition "Do not cook a young goat in its mother's milk" (Exodus 23:19), which became the basis for Jewish dietary separation of meat and dairy, has no parallel in Islamic dietary law. Islamic halal regulations are derived from independent Quranic and hadith sources and do not include this specific prohibition.

The promise of gradual conquest of the land (Exodus 23:27-33) reflects a specifically Israelite covenantal framework. Islam does not adopt this territorial promise but has its own concept of Allah granting victory and dominion to the faithful as He wills (An-Nur 24:55).

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** highlights the universal ethical principles in this chapter as evidence that divine guidance consistently emphasizes justice and compassion. He connects the Mosaic injunctions against false testimony and judicial corruption to the corresponding Islamic prohibitions.

**Al-Tabari** discusses the three festivals as part of the specific Shari'ah given to Bani Isra'il, noting that each prophetic community received its own rituals while sharing the same ethical foundations.

**Al-Qurtubi** provides extensive treatment of the Islamic concept of 'adl (justice), using the convergence between Mosaic and Quranic ethics to argue that justice is among the most fundamental divine attributes reflected in human law. He emphasizes that justice in Islam extends even to enemies and non-believers.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

Justice and compassion flow from tawhid — because all human beings are created by the one God, all deserve fair treatment regardless of social status, kinship, or personal feelings. The ethical principles in Exodus 23 confirm that Musa's revelation was authentic divine guidance, as its moral content aligns with the eternal truths reaffirmed in the Quran. The different ritual prescriptions reflect the Islamic understanding that each community received its own minhaj (way) and Shari'ah (law) from the same divine source (Al-Ma'idah 5:48).
