## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 15 contains the Song of the Sea — a hymn of praise following the drowning of Fir'awn — and the episode of bitter water made sweet at Marah. Islam affirms the centrality of praising Allah after deliverance, though the specific song is not reproduced in the Quran. The Quranic response to the sea miracle is expressed through its own distinctive form of divine speech, and the theme of gratitude (shukr) after salvation pervades the Musa narrative.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Ash-Shu'ara 26:63-68**: After narrating the drowning, Allah states, "Indeed in that is a sign, but most of them were not to be believers. And indeed, your Lord — He is the Exalted in Might, the Merciful." The Quran praises Allah through its own literary form rather than recording a human hymn.
- **Al-Baqarah 2:50-52**: "And recall when We parted the sea for you and saved you and drowned the people of Fir'awn while you were looking on." The invitation to "recall" (udhkuru) is itself a form of commanded praise and remembrance.
- **Ibrahim 14:5-8**: Musa reminds Bani Isra'il of Allah's favors: "Remember the favor of Allah upon you when He saved you from the people of Fir'awn." This passage also contains the essential teaching: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you; but if you deny, indeed, My punishment is severe."
- **Ad-Dukhan 44:30-33**: "And We certainly saved the Children of Israel from the humiliating torment — from Fir'awn. Indeed, he was a haughty one among the transgressors. And We chose them by knowledge over all the worlds."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Song of the Sea (Exodus 15:1-18) is one of the oldest and most celebrated poems in the Hebrew Bible. The Quran does not reproduce or reference this specific hymn. Islamic theology generally does not attribute extended poetic compositions to prophets, preferring the direct speech of du'a (supplication) and dhikr (remembrance of Allah). The Quran provides its own praise of Allah for the deliverance rather than quoting human words of celebration.

The characterization of God as a "warrior" (Exodus 15:3) differs from Islamic theological language. While Allah is described as having power (qudrah) and might ('izzah), the Quran generally avoids martial metaphors for Allah Himself, instead describing His actions through their effects.

The episode of Marah's bitter waters (Exodus 15:22-25) is not mentioned in the Quran, though the theme of water provision in the wilderness appears in several passages (Al-Baqarah 2:60, Al-A'raf 7:160) in connection with Musa striking the rock. Islamic commentators treat the Marah episode as Isra'iliyyat — permissible to narrate but not part of core Islamic teaching.

Miriam's song and dance (Exodus 15:20-21) has no Quranic parallel. Islamic tradition does not associate Musa's sister (unnamed in the Quran but identified in tradition) with prophetic song, though her role in protecting the infant Musa is acknowledged (Taha 20:40, Al-Qasas 28:11).

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes that the proper response to divine deliverance is gratitude expressed through worship, obedience, and remembrance. He notes that Bani Isra'il's subsequent failures were rooted in forgetting the enormity of Allah's favor at the sea.

**Al-Tabari** discusses the post-deliverance period, recording traditions about the Israelites' initial joy and subsequent complaints. He connects this pattern to the Quranic observation that human beings quickly forget divine favors once immediate danger passes.

**Al-Qurtubi** provides extensive discussion of shukr (gratitude) as a spiritual obligation. He cites Ibrahim 14:7 — "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you" — as the foundational verse connecting gratitude to continued divine blessing, and ingratitude to punishment.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The proper response to divine deliverance is hamd (praise) and shukr (gratitude) — both foundational concepts in Islamic worship. Every unit of prayer begins with "Al-hamdu lillahi Rabb al-'alamin" (All praise belongs to Allah, Lord of the worlds), echoing the spirit of praise after salvation. The episode teaches that tawhid is not merely intellectual assent to God's oneness but a lived response of grateful submission. The Quran's repeated reminder to "remember Allah's favor" transforms the historical event into an ongoing spiritual practice, ensuring that every generation derives the lesson of tawhid from the deliverance at the sea.
