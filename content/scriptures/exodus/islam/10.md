## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 10 describes the plagues of locusts and darkness, two of the most dramatic signs sent to Fir'awn. The Quran explicitly names locusts (al-jarad) among the signs in Al-A'raf 7:133, making this one of the directly confirmed plagues. Darkness, while not named in that verse, is significant in Islamic commentary as a demonstration of Allah's power over the most fundamental elements of creation — light itself.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-A'raf 7:133**: "So We sent upon them the flood and locusts (al-jarad) and lice (al-qummal) and frogs (al-dafadi') and blood as distinct signs (ayat mufassalat), but they were arrogant and were a criminal people."
- **Al-A'raf 7:136**: "So We took retribution from them, and We drowned them in the sea because they denied Our signs and were heedless of them." This verse describes the ultimate consequence of rejecting the accumulated signs.
- **Yunus 10:88**: Musa prays, "Our Lord, You have given Fir'awn and his establishment splendor and wealth in the worldly life, our Lord, that they may lead astray from Your way. Our Lord, obliterate their wealth and harden their hearts so they will not believe until they see the painful punishment."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran confirms the locust plague explicitly but does not detail its duration or negotiation with Fir'awn as the biblical text does. The extended back-and-forth between Moses and Pharaoh about which Israelites may leave (Exodus 10:8-11, 24-26) is absent from the Quranic account. Islam presents the demand as total and non-negotiable — the release of all Bani Isra'il — consistent with the prophetic principle of delivering Allah's command without compromise.

The plague of darkness (Exodus 10:21-23) carries particular resonance in Islamic thought, as light and darkness are powerful Quranic metaphors. Allah is described as "the Light of the heavens and the earth" (An-Nur 24:35), and the removal of light symbolizes spiritual blindness. Though the literal darkness of Egypt is not mentioned in the Quran, Islamic commentators see it as consistent with the pattern of signs targeting every dimension of human existence.

Fir'awn's threat to kill Musa (Exodus 10:28) echoes the Quranic account where Fir'awn says, "Let me kill Musa and let him call upon his Lord" (Al-Mu'min 40:26). In both texts, the threat reveals Fir'awn's desperation as his power proves insufficient against divine signs.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** elaborates on the locusts as a sign that devastated Egypt's agriculture, connecting it to the broader economic collapse that preceded the exodus. He notes that each sign targeted a different aspect of Egyptian life and confidence.

**Al-Tabari** records traditions that the locusts consumed everything — crops, wooden structures, clothing — leaving nothing green in the land. He preserves multiple Isra'iliyyat narrations about the duration of each plague while maintaining appropriate scholarly caution.

**Al-Qurtubi** uses the locust plague to discuss the Quranic concept of signs. He distinguishes between cosmic signs (ayat kawniyyah) that point to Allah's existence in nature and miraculous signs (ayat mu'jizah) that authenticate prophets. The plagues function as both — they demonstrate divine power and validate Musa's mission simultaneously.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The locust and darkness plagues demonstrate that Allah commands every created thing, from the smallest insect to the greatest cosmic force. Nothing in creation operates autonomously. Fir'awn's inability to stop the locusts or restore the light exposes the hollowness of his claim to lordship. A true lord would control his domain — Fir'awn's helplessness proves he is a false god. Musa's steadfastness in the face of death threats affirms prophetic courage ('ismah extends to protection from abandoning the mission), and his continued intercession when asked shows prophetic mercy even toward the oppressor.
