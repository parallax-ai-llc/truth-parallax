## Islamic Perspective

The parting of the sea is one of the most celebrated miracles in Islamic tradition and is narrated in the Quran with dramatic power. This event represents the definitive divine intervention in the Musa narrative — the moment when Allah's promise of deliverance is fulfilled and Fir'awn's pretensions to divinity are irrevocably shattered. The drowning of Fir'awn is among the most frequently referenced events in the Quran, serving as the ultimate warning to all tyrants and a source of hope for all oppressed peoples.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran provides extensive coverage of this event:

- **Ash-Shu'ara 26:61-67**: "And when the two companies saw one another, the companions of Musa said, 'Indeed, we are to be overtaken!' Musa said, 'No! Indeed, with me is my Lord; He will guide me.' Then We inspired to Musa, 'Strike with your staff the sea,' and it parted, and each portion was like a great towering mountain. And We brought near the pursuers. And We saved Musa and those with him, all together. Then We drowned the others."
- **Taha 20:77-78**: "Travel by night with My servants and strike for them a dry path through the sea; you will not fear being overtaken nor be afraid. So Fir'awn pursued them with his soldiers, and there covered them from the sea that which covered them."
- **Al-Baqarah 2:50**: "And recall when We parted the sea for you and saved you and drowned the people of Fir'awn while you were looking on."
- **Yunus 10:90-92**: "And We took the Children of Israel across the sea, and Fir'awn and his soldiers pursued them in tyranny and enmity until, when drowning overtook him, he said, 'I believe that there is no deity except that in whom the Children of Israel believe, and I am of the Muslims.' Now? And you had disobeyed before and were of the corrupters? So today We will save you in body that you may be to those who succeed you a sign."
- **Ad-Dukhan 44:23-33**: Extended account of the pursuit and drowning.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran adds a theologically significant detail absent from the biblical account: Fir'awn's deathbed declaration of faith. In Yunus 10:90-91, Fir'awn declares belief in Allah as he drowns, but Allah rejects this belated conversion — "Now? And you had disobeyed before?" This establishes the critical Islamic principle that repentance must precede the moment of death to be accepted (tawbah is not valid once punishment begins).

The Quran's statement that Fir'awn's body would be preserved "as a sign" (Yunus 10:92) has generated extensive modern discussion, with some scholars connecting it to the discovery of pharaonic mummies. This is unique to the Islamic account and has no biblical parallel.

The biblical account describes the angel of God and the pillar of cloud moving to protect the Israelites (Exodus 14:19-20). The Quran does not mention these specific elements, focusing instead on Musa's act of striking the sea with his staff — the same staff that became a serpent, establishing continuity in the instrument of divine miracles.

The Israelites' fear and complaint (Exodus 14:10-12) is confirmed in the Quran (Ash-Shu'ara 26:61), but Musa's response is framed more powerfully in the Quranic version: "No! Indeed, with me is my Lord; He will guide me" — a declaration of absolute tawakkul that stands as one of the most inspiring statements of faith in scripture.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** provides extensive narration of the sea's parting, describing twelve separate paths (one for each tribe) based on traditions. He discusses the preservation of Fir'awn's body and notes its significance as a lesson for future generations.

**Al-Tabari** records multiple chains of transmission describing the crossing, including the topography and the timing. He preserves traditions about the Israelites' hesitation to enter the sea even after it parted, and how one of them (identified in some traditions as a leader of a tribe) entered first.

**Al-Qurtubi** derives extensive theological lessons from Fir'awn's rejected repentance. He discusses the conditions for valid tawbah (repentance) and why desperation-driven faith is insufficient. He also addresses the question of whether Fir'awn knew the truth earlier and chose to reject it — concluding affirmatively, which makes his punishment just.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The parting of the sea is the supreme demonstration of tawhid in action. No natural force, no human army, no claimed deity can resist Allah's command. The sea obeys Allah; Fir'awn, who claimed to be a god, drowns in it. The contrast could not be more absolute. Musa's faith at the moment of apparent hopelessness — "Indeed, with me is my Lord" — is the essence of prophetic certainty (yaqin) and exemplifies the trust that every Muslim is called to emulate. The preservation of Fir'awn's body transforms a historical defeat into an eternal sign, ensuring that the lesson of tawhid transcends time.
