## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 38 records the construction of the altar of burnt offering, the bronze basin, the courtyard, and a detailed inventory of materials used. The chapter's meticulous accounting of gold, silver, and bronze reflects a principle highly valued in Islam: amanah (trustworthiness) and transparent stewardship of community resources. While the specific structures belong to the Israelite Shari'ah, the underlying ethic of accountability in handling collective wealth is a universal Islamic value.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran emphasizes accountability in handling entrusted resources:

- **An-Nisa 4:58**: "Indeed, Allah commands you to render trusts (amanat) to their owners and when you judge between people to judge with justice."
- **Al-Mu'minun 23:8**: "And they who are to their trusts (amanat) and their promises attentive."
- **Al-Anfal 8:27**: "O you who have believed, do not betray Allah and the Messenger or betray your trusts while you know."
- **Al-Isra 17:34**: "And fulfill every commitment. Indeed, the commitment is ever that about which one will be questioned."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The detailed material inventory in Exodus 38:21-31 — listing exact weights of gold, silver, and bronze — represents a form of financial transparency that Islam strongly endorses. The Prophet Muhammad emphasized accountability for public funds, and the Caliph 'Umar ibn al-Khattab was famous for his meticulous accounting of state resources. The principle is shared even if the institutional context differs.

The altar of burnt offering's construction (Exodus 38:1-7) continues the Israelite sacrificial system that Islam does not replicate. Islamic sacrifice does not require a permanent altar; the act can be performed at any clean location, and the emphasis is on the intention (niyyah) rather than the place or apparatus.

The bronze basin made from mirrors of the serving women (Exodus 38:8) is a detail that Islamic commentators rarely address, as it falls within the category of neutral Isra'iliyyat — neither confirmed nor denied by Islamic sources.

The massive quantities of materials listed — nearly a ton of gold, over three tons of silver — indicate the scale of the Tabernacle project. Islamic tradition records similarly impressive communal efforts in the construction of major mosques, though the theological significance lies not in the quantity of materials but in the sincerity of the offering.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** does not provide detailed commentary on the construction inventory but upholds the general principle that leaders are accountable for how they use community resources — a principle he derives from prophetic practice.

**Al-Tabari** preserves brief traditions about the Tabernacle's material composition, treating them as supplementary historical information.

**Al-Qurtubi** uses the concept of amanah to discuss the broader Islamic framework of fiduciary responsibility. He connects the biblical inventory to the Quranic command to "render trusts to their owners" (An-Nisa 4:58), arguing that public accountability is a divine obligation on all who handle community wealth, whether for sacred or civil purposes.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

Accountability is an expression of tawhid because it acknowledges that all resources ultimately belong to Allah, and those who manage them are His trustees. The detailed inventory in Exodus 38 demonstrates that even in sacred construction, transparency and honesty are required. This aligns with the Islamic teaching that religious authority does not exempt one from financial accountability — in fact, it increases the obligation. The prophetic model of simple living and transparent management of community resources established by Muhammad stands as the Islamic standard for handling what Allah has entrusted to human stewardship.
