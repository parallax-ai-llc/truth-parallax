## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 4 describes the signs given to Musa to authenticate his prophetic mission and the appointment of Harun (Aaron) as his assistant. The Quran confirms the granting of miraculous signs while presenting them within the framework of divine omnipotence. In Islam, miracles (mu'jizat) given to prophets serve as evidence of their truthfulness and are granted solely by Allah's permission — no prophet performs miracles through his own power.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran describes the signs given to Musa in several passages:

- **Taha 20:17-23**: "'And what is that in your right hand, O Musa?' He said, 'It is my staff; I lean upon it, and I bring down leaves for my sheep and I have therein other uses.' Allah said, 'Throw it down, O Musa.' So he threw it down, and thereupon it was a snake, moving swiftly. Allah said, 'Seize it and fear not. We will return it to its former condition. And draw in your hand to your side; it will come forth white without disease — another sign.'"
- **An-Naml 27:10-12**: "Throw down your staff.' But when he saw it writhing as if it were a snake, he turned in flight and did not return. 'O Musa, fear not. Indeed, in My presence the messengers do not fear.'"
- **Al-Qasas 28:31-32**: Similar account of the staff and the white hand.
- **Taha 20:25-36**: Musa's request for Harun: "'My Lord, expand for me my breast, and ease for me my task, and untie the knot from my tongue that they may understand my speech. And appoint for me a minister from my family — Harun, my brother. Increase through him my strength and let him share my task.'"
- **Al-Qasas 28:33-35**: Musa expresses fear of Fir'awn and asks for Harun's support. Allah grants his request.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran does not include the sign of the leprous hand in the same terms as Exodus 4:6-7. Instead, the Quran speaks of Musa's hand emerging "white without disease" (bayda' min ghayri su'), emphasizing that the whiteness was a miraculous radiance, not a skin affliction. Islamic scholars are careful to note that this sign involved no impurity or illness, as prophets are protected from repulsive conditions.

The biblical account of Musa's reluctance (Exodus 4:10-14) and God's anger at his hesitation is treated differently in Islam. While the Quran acknowledges Musa's request for help and his concern about his speech impediment (a knot on his tongue from a childhood incident recorded in tradition), it does not portray Allah as angry with Musa. The prophetic dignity ('ismah) is preserved — Musa's requests are seen as prudent preparation, not disobedience.

The episode of Zipporah circumcising their son (Exodus 4:24-26), one of the most enigmatic passages in the Torah, has no parallel in the Quran or mainstream Islamic tradition. Islamic scholars generally classify such narratives as Isra'iliyyat that need not be affirmed or denied, though many consider the passage theologically problematic from an Islamic standpoint since it seems to portray Allah as attacking His own prophet.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** provides detailed accounts of both signs, explaining that the staff was an ordinary shepherd's staff that became miraculous by Allah's command. He records traditions about the staff's origin, some tracing it to the prophets before Musa.

**Al-Tabari** examines the speech impediment tradition in detail, recording the well-known account that young Musa placed a hot coal in his mouth as a child in Fir'awn's court, resulting in a burn that affected his speech. This is classified as Isra'iliyyat but is widely accepted among Muslim scholars.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the theological implications of miracles, arguing that the transformation of the staff demonstrates Allah's power to alter the nature of created things — a principle relevant to understanding divine omnipotence (qudrah).

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The signs given to Musa reinforce tawhid by demonstrating that true power belongs to Allah alone. The staff does not become a serpent through magic but through Allah's creative command (kun fa-yakun — "Be, and it is"). This distinction between prophetic miracles and sorcery becomes crucial in subsequent chapters when Musa confronts Fir'awn's magicians. The appointment of Harun also illustrates that prophethood is a divine gift, not a human achievement — Allah grants it to whom He wills.
