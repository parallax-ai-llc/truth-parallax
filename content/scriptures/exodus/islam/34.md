## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 34 describes the replacement of the broken tablets, God's self-revelation of His attributes, the renewed covenant, and Moses' radiant face. The Quran addresses the aftermath of the golden calf incident and the restoration of the tablets, framing the episode as an example of divine mercy (rahmah) and the possibility of communal repentance. The renewed covenant demonstrates that Allah's relationship with Bani Isra'il, though severely tested, was not permanently severed.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-A'raf 7:150**: "And when Musa returned to his people, angry and grieved, he said, 'How wretched is that by which you have replaced me after my departure.' ... And he threw down the tablets and seized his brother by the head, pulling him toward him."
- **Al-A'raf 7:154**: "And when the anger subsided in Musa, he took up the tablets; and in their inscription was guidance and mercy for those who are fearful of their Lord."
- **Al-A'raf 7:155-156**: Musa chose seventy men for the appointed meeting with Allah. When an earthquake seized them, Musa prayed, "If You had willed, You could have destroyed them before and me as well. Would You destroy us for what the foolish among us have done? ... My Lord, forgive us and have mercy upon us, and You are the best of the merciful."
- **Al-Baqarah 2:54**: "And recall when Musa said to his people, 'O my people, indeed you have wronged yourselves by your taking of the calf. So repent to your Creator and kill yourselves. That is best for you in the sight of your Creator.' Then He accepted your repentance; indeed, He is the Accepting of repentance, the Merciful."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

God's self-proclamation of attributes (Exodus 34:6-7) — "merciful and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness" — has conceptual parallels with Allah's ninety-nine names (al-asma al-husna). Both traditions affirm divine mercy and compassion as primary attributes. However, the biblical statement that God "visits the iniquity of the fathers on the children and the children's children" (Exodus 34:7) contradicts the Quranic principle that "no bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (Al-An'am 6:164). Islam categorically rejects intergenerational punishment.

Moses' shining face (Exodus 34:29-35), requiring a veil when speaking to the people, is not mentioned in the Quran. Islamic tradition records traditions about light emanating from prophets, but these are not specific to the Sinai encounter. The concept of prophetic luminosity exists in Islamic mysticism (tasawwuf) under the concept of nur Muhammadi (the Muhammadan light).

The specific covenant stipulations renewed in Exodus 34 (festivals, firstborn, Sabbath, prohibition of intermarriage) belong to the specific Shari'ah of Musa and are not applicable to Muslims.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the replacement tablets and the seventy elders chosen by Musa. He interprets the earthquake that struck the elders (Al-A'raf 7:155) as a divine demonstration of power, after which they were restored to life — a sign of both divine wrath and mercy.

**Al-Tabari** examines whether the second set of tablets was identical to the first or contained modified content. He records various opinions, with some scholars suggesting the second set was less comprehensive, symbolizing the diminished spiritual state of the community after the calf incident.

**Al-Qurtubi** focuses on the themes of divine mercy and repentance. He emphasizes that Allah's acceptance of Bani Isra'il's repentance, despite the enormity of their shirk, demonstrates that no sin is beyond forgiveness for those who genuinely return to Allah. He connects this to the hadith qudsi: "O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah."

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The renewed covenant affirms that tawhid is resilient — even a community that has fallen into shirk can be restored through sincere repentance. The replacement of the tablets symbolizes divine mercy overriding human failure. Allah's attributes of mercy and forgiveness, proclaimed in both the biblical and Islamic accounts, are central to understanding tawhid: the one God is not merely powerful but compassionate, not merely just but merciful. Musa's intercession for his people demonstrates the prophetic role of mediation — standing between divine justice and human weakness.
