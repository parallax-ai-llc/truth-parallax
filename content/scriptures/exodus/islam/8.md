## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 8 describes three plagues — frogs, gnats (lice), and flies — sent upon Misr as divine signs. The Quran confirms the sending of multiple signs to Fir'awn and his people, though it does not narrate each plague as a separate episode. In Islamic theology, these signs function as both warnings (indharat) and proofs (hujaj) — they give the oppressor every opportunity to repent while simultaneously establishing the evidence that will justify divine punishment.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran mentions the plagues collectively in a key verse:

- **Al-A'raf 7:133**: "So We sent upon them the flood and locusts and lice and frogs and blood as distinct signs, but they were arrogant and were a criminal people."
- **Al-A'raf 7:134-135**: "And when the punishment descended upon them, they said, 'O Musa, invoke for us your Lord by what He has promised you. If you remove the punishment from us, we will surely believe you, and we will send with you the Children of Israel.' But when We removed the punishment from them for a term which they were to reach, at once they broke their word."
- **Az-Zukhruf 43:48**: "And We showed them not a sign except that it was greater than its sister, and We seized them with affliction that perhaps they might return."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran groups the plagues together rather than narrating them individually, providing a theological summary rather than a chronological account. The five signs mentioned in Al-A'raf 7:133 (flood, locusts, lice, frogs, blood) partially overlap with the ten plagues of Exodus but are not identical in number or order.

The biblical account of Fir'awn repeatedly asking Moses to pray for the plagues to stop and then reneging on his promises is reflected in the Quranic description of broken promises (Al-A'raf 7:134-135). However, the Quran presents this pattern as a unified theological lesson about the nature of arrogant disbelief rather than a series of separate negotiations.

The distinction between the Israelites and Egyptians (Exodus 8:22-23, where God separates the land of Goshen) is not explicitly stated in the Quran, though the general principle that Allah protects the believers from widespread punishment is well established in Islamic theology.

The role of Egyptian magicians admitting "This is the finger of God" (Exodus 8:19) has a parallel in the Quranic account of the sorcerers' conversion, though the Quran places their recognition earlier, at the staff miracle.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses each of the five Quranic signs in turn, drawing on traditions to elaborate on their severity. He notes that each sign was intended to increase in intensity, giving Fir'awn progressively stronger evidence. He records that the frogs entered every aspect of Egyptian life — their food, their beds, their speech.

**Al-Tabari** provides detailed accounts from Isra'iliyyat traditions about the duration and extent of each plague. He treats these traditions as supplementary narration, noting that the Quran's summary is sufficient for extracting the intended lessons.

**Al-Qurtubi** emphasizes the moral pattern: affliction leads to temporary repentance, which is abandoned when comfort returns. He connects this to the human tendency described in Yunus 10:12 — "And when adversity touches man, he calls upon Us, whether lying on his side or sitting or standing; but when We remove his adversity from him, he continues as if he had never called upon Us."

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The plagues demonstrate Allah's absolute control over creation. Every element of the natural world — water, animals, insects — obeys His command and becomes an instrument of His will. This is a direct challenge to the Egyptian polytheistic system, where different gods were believed to control different aspects of nature. By sending plagues that overwhelmed every domain, Allah demonstrated that He alone is the Sovereign of all creation. Fir'awn's repeated broken promises illustrate the Quranic principle that pride prevents genuine submission to tawhid.
