## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 37 describes the construction of the Ark of the Covenant, the table, the lampstand, and the altar of incense — the sacred furniture of the Tabernacle. Among these, only the Ark (al-tabut) receives direct mention in the Quran, and that in a later historical context. The other objects belong to the Israelite ritual system that Islam acknowledges historically without adopting. The chapter invites reflection on how different prophetic communities express devotion to the one God through diverse material forms.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-Baqarah 2:248**: "And their prophet said to them, 'Indeed, a sign of his kingship is that the chest (al-tabut) will come to you in which is assurance (sakinah) from your Lord and a remnant of what the family of Musa and the family of Harun had left, carried by the angels.'" This verse, set during the time of Talut (Saul), confirms the Ark's sacred significance and its connection to Musa and Harun.

The lampstand (menorah) has a metaphorical resonance:

- **An-Nur 24:35**: "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth. The example of His light is like a niche within which is a lamp (misbah), the lamp is within glass, the glass as if it were a pearly white star." The imagery of sacred light connects thematically to the Tabernacle lampstand, though the Quranic verse is metaphorical.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The cherubim on the Ark (Exodus 37:7-9) present a theological challenge from the Islamic perspective. The creation of sculpted angelic figures for a sacred context would conflict with the Islamic prohibition on representational imagery in worship spaces. Islamic scholars generally treat this as part of the Mosaic dispensation that was superseded; alternatively, some argue that the original Ark may have differed from the biblical description, which they view as potentially affected by later editorial additions.

The Ark's function as the locus of divine communication (the space between the cherubim as a "mercy seat") differs from Islamic theology, which holds that Allah communicates with prophets through wahy (revelation) — either through the angel Jibril, through inspiration cast into the heart, or through direct speech (as with Musa). No physical object mediates this communication.

The elaborate craftsmanship of pure gold, with precise measurements and artistic detail, reflects a different aesthetic approach to sacred objects compared with early Islamic practice. Islam's aniconic tradition led to the development of calligraphy, geometric patterns, and arabesque as the primary artistic expressions in sacred contexts.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the tabut primarily in the context of its role during the time of the Israelite judges and kings, noting that it contained relics of Musa's and Harun's era. He describes it as a symbol of divine support (sakinah).

**Al-Tabari** records traditions about the Ark's contents — variously described as including the broken tablets, Musa's staff, remnants of manna, and Harun's rod. He evaluates these traditions for their chains of transmission.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the meaning of sakinah (divine tranquility/assurance) associated with the Ark. He explains that sakinah in the Quran is a spiritual state that Allah casts into the hearts of the faithful, not a substance contained in a physical box. The Ark served as a tangible symbol of this spiritual reality for Bani Isra'il.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The sacred objects of the Tabernacle illustrate a principle important to Islamic theology: the distinction between the sign and the signified. The Ark was a sign of divine presence, not the divine presence itself. When communities confuse the symbol with the reality, they risk falling into shirk. Islam's minimalist approach to sacred objects — no images, no relics in worship, no intermediary objects — reflects a deliberate effort to keep the worshiper focused on Allah alone, without the risk of venerating the created sign instead of the Creator it points to.
