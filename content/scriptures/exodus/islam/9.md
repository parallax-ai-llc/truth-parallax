## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 9 recounts three more plagues — livestock disease, boils, and hail — continuing the escalation of divine signs against Fir'awn and his people. While the Quran does not individually narrate these specific plagues, they fall within the broader Quranic framework of "distinct signs" (ayat mufassalat) sent to demonstrate Allah's power and Fir'awn's obstinacy. Islamic theology views this progressive intensification as an expression of divine justice — Allah gives abundant warning before final judgment.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-A'raf 7:133**: The collective mention of signs including "the flood" (al-tufan), which some commentators interpret broadly enough to encompass various natural disasters including severe storms.
- **Az-Zukhruf 43:48**: "And We showed them not a sign except that it was greater than its sister" — this establishes the principle of escalating severity that corresponds to the biblical sequence.
- **Al-A'raf 7:130**: "And We certainly seized the people of Fir'awn with years of famine and a deficiency in fruits that perhaps they would be reminded." This verse addresses agricultural devastation that relates to the livestock plague and hail's destruction of crops.
- **Al-Mu'min/Ghafir 40:45-46**: References the encompassing punishment that struck Fir'awn's people.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The specific plagues of livestock disease, boils, and hail are not individually named in the Quran. This reflects the Quranic approach of providing theological essence rather than exhaustive historical detail. The Quran's five named signs in Al-A'raf 7:133 serve as representative examples of a larger campaign of divine warnings.

The biblical emphasis on Pharaoh's hardened heart is treated with theological nuance in Islam. The Quran states that Allah "sealed" Fir'awn's heart (Yunus 10:88, Al-Mu'min 40:35), but Islamic theology understands this as a divine response to Fir'awn's own chosen arrogance, not as a predetermined removal of free will. Al-Ash'ari and Al-Maturidi, the two main Sunni theological schools, both maintain that human choice precedes divine sealing.

The detail that some of Pharaoh's servants feared God's word and sheltered their livestock (Exodus 9:20) is absent from the Quran but aligns with the Islamic principle that even within a corrupt society, individuals may possess faith. The Quran mentions a believing man from Fir'awn's family who concealed his faith (Al-Mu'min 40:28), which Islamic tradition considers evidence that truth was recognized even within the oppressor's court.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the broader pattern of divine punishment sent to previous nations (umam), connecting the plagues of Egypt to the destruction of 'Ad, Thamud, and the people of Lut. He argues that Allah's methodology is consistent — signs are sent, then punishment follows if they are rejected.

**Al-Tabari** records Isra'iliyyat traditions detailing the specific effects of each plague, including accounts of the hailstorm's unprecedented ferocity. He treats these as permissible narrations that supplement the Quranic account without contradicting it.

**Al-Qurtubi** draws attention to the agricultural devastation as a form of economic punishment that struck at the material basis of Fir'awn's power. He notes that Allah often undermines tyrants through their worldly sources of strength.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The plagues comprehensively demonstrate tawhid by showing that no aspect of creation operates independently of Allah's will. Livestock, human health, and weather all respond to His command. In Egyptian religion, different deities supposedly controlled different natural domains — the Quran's narrative implicitly negates this by showing a single divine will commanding every element. Musa's role as the one through whom these signs are requested reinforces his status as Allah's authentic messenger and the instrument of divine will.
