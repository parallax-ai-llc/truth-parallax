## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 35 describes Moses assembling the community to collect voluntary offerings for the Tabernacle's construction and the reiteration of Sabbath rest. The chapter emphasizes willing generosity and communal participation in sacred work. While the specific context belongs to Bani Isra'il's wilderness experience, the principles of voluntary contribution and collective devotion resonate strongly with Islamic values of sadaqah (charity) and community building.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not directly reference the Tabernacle offering collection but establishes parallel principles:

- **Al-Baqarah 2:261**: "The example of those who spend their wealth in the way of Allah is like a seed of grain which grows seven spikes; in each spike is a hundred grains. And Allah multiplies His reward for whom He wills."
- **Al-Baqarah 2:267**: "O you who have believed, spend from the good things which you have earned and from that which We have produced for you from the earth."
- **Ali 'Imran 3:92**: "Never will you attain righteousness until you spend from that which you love."
- **At-Tawbah 9:103**: "Take from their wealth a charity by which you purify them and cause them increase."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The biblical freewill offering for the Tabernacle has a structural parallel in Islamic waqf (religious endowment) and sadaqah (voluntary charity). Both systems channel community generosity toward sacred purposes. However, Islamic giving is directed toward diverse causes — mosques, schools, hospitals, the poor — rather than focused on a single elaborate worship structure.

The Sabbath reiteration (Exodus 35:1-3), including the prohibition of lighting fires, belongs to the specific Israelite legal system. Islam does not prohibit work or fire on any day of the week. The Islamic Friday prayer (Jumu'ah) requires attendance at congregational prayer but explicitly permits commerce before and after (Al-Jumu'ah 62:10).

The detailed listing of materials — gold, silver, bronze, fine linen, goat hair, ram skins, acacia wood, gems — reflects the Tabernacle's material requirements. Islamic mosque construction has traditionally favored local materials and simplicity, though Islamic civilization has also produced magnificent architectural achievements (such as the Great Mosques of Cordoba, Istanbul, and Isfahan) funded by community endowments.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** connects the principle of voluntary giving to the Islamic concept of infaq fi sabilillah (spending in the path of Allah), noting that generosity in sacred causes is praised across all prophetic traditions.

**Al-Tabari** records the Tabernacle offering as part of the Israelite narrative, treating it as historical account that illustrates the community's devotion after the crisis of the golden calf.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the Islamic ethics of charitable giving, emphasizing that the best charity is given freely, joyfully, and from one's best possessions — principles that mirror the spirit of Exodus 35's "willing heart" contributions. He cites the hadith: "Charity does not decrease wealth."

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

Voluntary offering expresses tawhid through the recognition that all wealth ultimately belongs to Allah and that giving in His cause is returning a portion of what He has entrusted to His servants. The communal nature of the Tabernacle project — every person contributing according to ability — reflects the Islamic principle of collective responsibility (fard kifayah). The willingness to give generously after the golden calf crisis demonstrates the transformative power of genuine repentance: a community that had fallen into shirk now channels its resources toward tawhid.
