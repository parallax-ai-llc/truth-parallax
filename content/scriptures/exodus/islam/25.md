## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 25 begins the detailed instructions for constructing the Tabernacle (mishkan), including the Ark of the Covenant, the table of showbread, and the golden lampstand. These instructions represent the ritual architecture of Mosaic worship. Islam acknowledges the Tabernacle's existence as part of the Israelite Shari'ah but does not adopt its design or theology. Islamic worship spaces — masjids — follow entirely different principles, centered on simplicity, directional orientation toward the Ka'bah, and the absence of representational imagery.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran makes a brief but significant reference to the Ark:

- **Al-Baqarah 2:248**: "And their prophet said to them, 'Indeed, a sign of his kingship is that the chest (tabut) will come to you in which is assurance (sakinah) from your Lord and a remnant of what the family of Musa and the family of Harun had left, carried by the angels. Indeed in that is a sign for you, if you are believers.'" This verse, while set in the time of Talut (Saul), references the Ark's sacred status and its connection to Musa and Harun.

The Quran does not describe the Tabernacle's construction or design. The detailed architectural instructions in Exodus 25-31 fall entirely within the Isra'iliyyat category — they may be narrated for historical interest but carry no legislative weight in Islam.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The most fundamental difference is theological. The biblical Tabernacle is designed as a dwelling place for God's presence among the Israelites (Exodus 25:8). Islam categorically rejects the idea that Allah dwells in or is contained by any physical structure. Allah is beyond spatial limitation — "He is with you wherever you are" (Al-Hadid 57:4), but this "withness" (ma'iyyah) is understood as knowledge and power, not physical co-location.

The cherubim atop the Ark (Exodus 25:18-20) present another point of divergence. While Islam acknowledges angels, the creation of sculptured angelic figures for ritual use would be prohibited under Islamic law's strict prohibition of representational sacred imagery. This reflects the broader Islamic concern with safeguarding tawhid against any potential pathway to idolatry.

The concept of the "mercy seat" (kapporet) between the cherubim as a place where God communicates (Exodus 25:22) is not recognized in Islam. Allah communicates with prophets through revelation (wahy), angelic mediation (through Jibril), or direct speech (as with Musa), not through a specific ritual object.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** mentions the tabut (Ark) primarily in the context of Al-Baqarah 2:248, discussing its history from Musa's time through its loss and recovery. He records traditions about its contents, including the broken tablets and Harun's staff.

**Al-Tabari** treats the Tabernacle instructions as historical Isra'iliyyat, recording what earlier scholars transmitted about the structure's dimensions and furnishings without endorsing these details as religiously significant for Muslims.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the theological principle that each community's mode of worship is divinely appointed. The Tabernacle served Bani Isra'il; the Ka'bah serves the Muslim ummah. Both represent human orientation toward Allah, though their forms differ by divine design.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

From an Islamic perspective, the Tabernacle instructions illustrate how divine law encompasses all aspects of community life, including the physical space of worship. However, Islam's departure from the Tabernacle model reflects the Quranic emphasis on Allah's transcendence. The simplicity of Islamic worship spaces — devoid of images, statues, or elaborate ritual furniture — is a direct expression of tawhid. The mosque's openness and the absence of a "holy of holies" where God resides symbolize Islam's teaching that Allah is equally accessible everywhere and at all times.
