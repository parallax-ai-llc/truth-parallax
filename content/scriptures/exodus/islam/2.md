## Islamic Perspective

The birth of Musa (Moses) is one of the most celebrated narratives in the Quran, told with remarkable detail in Surah Al-Qasas (Chapter 28). Islam views Musa as one of the five greatest prophets (Ulul 'Azm), and his birth story illustrates Allah's absolute power to protect His chosen servants even within the household of their greatest enemy. The narrative demonstrates that no human stratagem can obstruct the divine will.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran provides an extensive parallel account:

- **Al-Qasas 28:7**: "And We inspired the mother of Musa, saying: 'Nurse him, and when you fear for him, cast him into the river and do not fear and do not grieve. Indeed, We will return him to you and will make him one of the messengers.'"
- **Al-Qasas 28:8-9**: "And the family of Fir'awn picked him up so that he would become to them an enemy and a source of grief. Indeed, Fir'awn and Haman and their soldiers were deliberate sinners. And the wife of Fir'awn said, 'He will be a comfort of the eye for me and for you. Do not kill him. Perhaps he may benefit us, or we may adopt him as a son.' And they perceived not."
- **Al-Qasas 28:10-13**: The Quran describes how Musa's mother's heart became empty with anxiety, how his sister followed the basket, and how Musa refused all wet nurses until he was returned to his own mother — fulfilling Allah's promise.
- **Al-Qasas 28:14-15**: When Musa reached maturity, he entered the city and found two men fighting. He struck the oppressor, accidentally killing him, and said, "This is from the work of Shaytan."
- **Al-Qasas 28:20-21**: A man came running from the far end of the city warning Musa that the chiefs were conspiring to kill him, so Musa fled to Madyan (Midian).
- **Taha 20:37-40**: Allah recounts to Musa how He guided his mother to place him in the river and how He cast love upon Musa so that he would be raised under Allah's watchful eye.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

Several notable differences exist between the Quranic and biblical accounts. The Quran names Fir'awn's wife (identified in hadith as Asiyah) as the one who rescued Musa, whereas Exodus names Pharaoh's daughter. Islamic tradition elevates Asiyah as one of the four greatest women in history, alongside Maryam, Khadijah, and Fatimah.

The Quran explicitly states that Musa's mother received divine inspiration (wahy) to cast her child into the river — this is not prophecy in the technical sense but a form of divine guidance that Islamic scholars distinguish from prophetic revelation. The biblical account does not specify this divine communication.

The incident of killing the Egyptian is treated differently. In Islam, Musa acknowledged his error, sought forgiveness from Allah, and was forgiven (Al-Qasas 28:15-16). This is significant for the doctrine of prophetic infallibility ('ismah) — scholars explain this was an unintentional act, or occurred before his prophetic mission, and that Musa's immediate repentance demonstrates his spiritual purity.

The Quran does not mention Moses being drawn from the water (the Hebrew etymology of his name). Instead, Islamic tradition focuses on the Arabic form "Musa" without providing an etymology.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** elaborates extensively on the birth narrative, weaving Quranic verses with traditions to construct a detailed biography. He emphasizes that Musa was born in the year when male children were being killed, making his survival a direct miracle.

**Al-Tabari** provides multiple chains of narration regarding the circumstances of Musa's upbringing in Fir'awn's palace. He records traditions about Musa's childhood incidents that tested Fir'awn's patience, including the famous account of young Musa grabbing Fir'awn's beard.

**Al-Qurtubi** draws moral lessons about tawakkul (reliance on Allah), noting that Musa's mother exemplifies perfect trust in Allah's promise despite the apparent impossibility of her child's survival.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The birth of Musa powerfully illustrates tawhid in action: Allah alone determines life and death, and the schemes of even the most powerful ruler cannot alter His decree. The irony of Fir'awn raising the very child destined to challenge his false divinity is a theme Islamic scholars cite as proof of Allah's supreme sovereignty and subtle planning (makr, in its positive divine sense as in Al-Anfal 8:30).
