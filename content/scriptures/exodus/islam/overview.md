## Overview

The Exodus narrative is central to Islamic scripture and theology. Musa (Moses) is the most frequently mentioned prophet in the Quran, appearing in over 70 surahs. The confrontation with Fir'awn (Pharaoh) is the Quran's primary example of the eternal struggle between monotheism and tyranny, between divine truth and human arrogance.

## Quranic Parallels

| Exodus Narrative | Quranic Parallel | Key Surahs |
|-----------------|-----------------|------------|
| Birth and rescue of Moses | Musa placed in the river, raised by Pharaoh's wife | Al-Qasas 28:1-13, Ta-Ha 20:37-40 |
| Burning bush | Musa at the sacred valley of Tuwa | Ta-Ha 20:9-24, An-Naml 27:7-12 |
| Plagues of Egypt | Signs sent to Pharaoh | Al-A'raf 7:130-136 |
| Red Sea crossing | Sea parted, Pharaoh drowned | Ash-Shu'ara 26:60-68, Yunus 10:90 |
| Golden Calf | Al-Samiri creates the calf | Ta-Ha 20:83-98 |
| Tablets of the Law | Musa receives the tablets | Al-A'raf 7:142-150 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

- **Prophetic infallibility**: Musa does not have the flaws attributed to Moses in the biblical text
- **Pharaoh as archetype**: Fir'awn represents every tyrant who claims divinity ("I am your lord most high," 79:24)
- **No chosen people exclusivism**: The covenant is with all who submit to God, not an ethnic group
- **Tablets vs. Torah**: Islam holds that the original Torah was revealed to Musa but later altered (*tahrif*)
