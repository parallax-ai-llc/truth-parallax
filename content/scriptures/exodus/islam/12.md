## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 12 describes the institution of Passover and the departure of Bani Isra'il from Misr. While Islam affirms the historical reality of the Israelites' deliverance, it does not recognize the Passover ritual as a continuing obligation. In Islamic theology, the night of deliverance demonstrates Allah's power to save the oppressed and destroy the oppressor. The focus is on the theological lesson — divine salvation through tawhid — rather than the ritual commemoration.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran addresses the departure from Egypt in multiple passages:

- **Al-Qasas 28:6**: "And to establish them in the land and show Fir'awn and Haman and their soldiers through them that which they had feared."
- **Ad-Dukhan 44:23-24**: "Then We inspired to Musa, 'Travel by night with My servants. Indeed, you will be pursued. And leave the sea in stillness. Indeed, they are an army to be drowned.'"
- **Taha 20:77**: "And We had inspired to Musa, 'Travel by night with My servants and strike for them a dry path through the sea; you will not fear being overtaken by Fir'awn nor be afraid.'"
- **Ash-Shu'ara 26:52**: "And We inspired to Musa, 'Travel by night with My servants. Indeed, you will be pursued.'"
- **Al-A'raf 7:137-138**: "And We caused the people who had been oppressed to inherit the eastern regions of the land and the western ones, which We had blessed."

The Quran also references the day of deliverance in connection with fasting:

- In Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim, the Prophet Muhammad, upon arriving in Madinah, found the Jews fasting on the day of 'Ashura (10th of Muharram) in commemoration of Musa's deliverance. He said, "We are more worthy of Musa than you," and commanded the Muslims to fast on that day.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Passover ritual — the sacrificial lamb, the blood on doorposts, the unleavened bread, and the bitter herbs — is not prescribed or described in the Quran. Islam does not adopt or continue the Passover, though it honors the event through the fast of 'Ashura. This reflects the Islamic principle that each ummah (nation) received its own specific rituals while sharing the same core message of tawhid.

The smearing of blood on doorposts to avert the angel of death is not part of the Islamic account. Islamic theology would find the concept theologically redundant — Allah knows His servants and does not require a physical marker to distinguish them. The destruction of the oppressors and the protection of the believers are both achieved through Allah's direct knowledge and command.

The detailed dietary and ritual prescriptions in Exodus 12 (how to prepare the lamb, the requirement of unleavened bread for seven days, the regulations for future observance) are absent from Islam. Islamic law (Shari'ah) has its own comprehensive system of worship, and the specific rituals of previous nations, while historically respected, are not binding on Muslims.

The Quran does not provide the number "600,000 men on foot" (Exodus 12:37). Islamic tradition generally does not specify the population of the exodus, focusing instead on the theological dimensions of the event.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the night departure extensively, connecting it with the command for nocturnal travel (isra) — a term also used for the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey (Al-Isra 17:1). He sees a symbolic parallel between the two divine deliverances.

**Al-Tabari** records various traditions about the timing and circumstances of the departure. He preserves accounts of the Israelites' preparations and the chaos among the Egyptians, while maintaining focus on the Quranic narrative's theological purpose.

**Al-Qurtubi** addresses the fast of 'Ashura in his commentary, discussing its legal status (recommended, not obligatory) and its connection to the historical deliverance of Musa. He notes that the Prophet Muhammad's claim to being "more worthy of Musa" reflects the Islamic teaching that all prophets belong to one spiritual family united by tawhid.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The deliverance of Bani Isra'il is the supreme historical demonstration of Allah's promise: those who uphold tawhid will ultimately be saved, and those who reject it will be destroyed. The event validates Musa's prophetic mission and demonstrates that the seemingly impossible — a nation of slaves escaping the world's most powerful empire — is effortless for Allah. The fast of 'Ashura in Islam connects the Muslim ummah to this legacy, affirming that loyalty to tawhid across prophetic dispensations is the essence of true religion.
