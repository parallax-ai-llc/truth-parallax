## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 7 describes the transformation of Musa's staff into a serpent before Fir'awn and the confrontation with Egyptian sorcerers, followed by the first plague of water turning to blood. The Quran devotes considerable attention to the encounter with the sorcerers, making it one of the best-documented events in the Musa narrative. In Islamic theology, this episode establishes the critical distinction between prophetic miracles (mu'jizat) and sorcery (sihr) — a distinction that has profound implications for Islamic law and creed.

## Quranic Parallels

The confrontation with the sorcerers is narrated in remarkable detail:

- **Al-A'raf 7:107-122**: "And Musa threw his staff, and suddenly it was a serpent, manifest. And he drew out his hand, and suddenly it was white for the observers. The eminent among the people of Fir'awn said, 'Indeed, this is a learned magician.' ... So the magicians came to Fir'awn. They said, 'Indeed, for us is a reward if we are the predominant?' He said, 'Yes, and indeed, you will be among those near to me.' ... So when the magicians came, Musa said to them, 'Throw whatever you will throw.' And when they threw, they bewitched the eyes of the people and struck terror into them, and they presented a great feat of magic. And We inspired to Musa, 'Throw your staff,' and at once it devoured what they were falsifying. So the truth was established, and abolished was what they were doing. And Fir'awn and his people were overcome right there and became debased."
- **Taha 20:56-73**: An extended account where the sorcerers, upon seeing the miracle, prostrate and declare, "We have believed in the Lord of Harun and Musa."
- **Ash-Shu'ara 26:36-51**: Another detailed narration of the same event with the sorcerers' conversion and Fir'awn's threats.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran dramatically expands the conversion of the sorcerers, which is absent from the biblical account. In the Bible, the Egyptian magicians replicate Moses' signs; in the Quran, the sorcerers recognize the difference between their craft and Musa's genuine miracle, leading to their immediate faith. Their conversion despite Fir'awn's threats of crucifixion and amputation (Taha 20:71) becomes one of the most powerful stories of faith in the Quran.

The plague of water turning to blood (Exodus 7:14-25) is referenced in the Quran only in the general mention of "signs" (ayat) sent to Fir'awn. The Quran lists specific plagues in Al-A'raf 7:133 — flood, locusts, lice, frogs, and blood — but does not narrate each one individually as the biblical text does.

The Bible's statement that God "hardened Pharaoh's heart" is addressed differently in the Quran. While the Quran states that Allah seals the hearts of the arrogant (Al-A'raf 7:101), Islamic theology maintains that this sealing is a consequence of the individual's free choice to reject truth, not an arbitrary divine act that removes free will.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** provides extensive detail about the gathering of sorcerers, estimating their numbers in the thousands based on various traditions. He emphasizes that their conversion represents the ultimate proof of Musa's truthfulness — professionals in deception immediately recognized genuine divine power.

**Al-Tabari** records the names and backgrounds of some chief sorcerers from Isra'iliyyat traditions, while noting these specifics are not confirmed by the Quran or authentic hadith.

**Al-Qurtubi** derives important legal rulings about sorcery from this passage, noting that the Quran's distinction between miracle and magic forms the basis for Islamic jurisprudence's prohibition and condemnation of sihr as a form of kufr (disbelief).

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The confrontation with the sorcerers is fundamentally about tawhid versus shirk. The sorcerers' magic relies on jinn or illusion; Musa's miracle flows directly from Allah's creative power. The sorcerers' conversion demonstrates that those who possess genuine knowledge will recognize the truth. Their declaration "We believe in the Lord of the worlds" (Al-A'raf 7:121) is a pure statement of tawhid that cost them their lives but secured their eternal salvation, according to Islamic tradition.
