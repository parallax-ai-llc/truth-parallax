## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 21 presents detailed civil and criminal laws governing slavery, personal injury, and compensation. These laws belong to the Shari'ah of Musa — the specific legal code revealed for Bani Isra'il. While Islam has its own comprehensive legal system, the principles underlying these Mosaic laws often parallel Islamic jurisprudence, particularly in the areas of qisas (just retaliation) and the protection of human life and dignity.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-Baqarah 2:178-179**: "O you who have believed, prescribed for you is legal retribution (qisas) for those murdered — the free for the free, the slave for the slave, and the female for the female. But whoever overlooks from his brother anything, then there should be a suitable follow-up and payment to him with good conduct. ... And there is for you in legal retribution saving of life, O you of understanding."
- **Al-Ma'idah 5:45**: "And We ordained for them therein a life for a life, an eye for an eye, a nose for a nose, an ear for an ear, a tooth for a tooth, and for wounds is legal retribution. But whoever gives up his right as charity, it is an expiation for him."
- **An-Nisa 4:92-93**: Laws concerning accidental and intentional killing, including blood money (diyah).

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The laws of slavery in Exodus 21:1-11 differ significantly from Islamic slave law. While both systems regulated slavery rather than immediately abolishing it, Islamic law imposed substantially different conditions. Islam mandated that slaves be fed and clothed like their masters, prohibited physical abuse, made manumission one of the highest acts of worship, and established multiple legal mechanisms for emancipation (kaffarah, mukataba). The Quran's approach is widely understood as systematically incentivizing abolition.

The lex talionis — "eye for eye, tooth for tooth" (Exodus 21:24) — is confirmed in the Quran (Al-Ma'idah 5:45) as having been prescribed for Bani Isra'il, but Islam adds a crucial modifier: "whoever gives up his right as charity, it is an expiation for him." This addition of a forgiveness option alongside strict justice represents what Islamic scholars view as a refinement consistent with divine mercy.

The laws regarding the goring ox (Exodus 21:28-36) have parallels in Islamic jurisprudence under the category of liability for animal damage (darar). Islamic fiqh developed extensive rulings on owner responsibility for animal-caused harm, though these were derived from hadith and juristic reasoning rather than directly from Mosaic law.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the continuity between Mosaic and Islamic law, noting that the Quran explicitly acknowledges the Torah's legal prescriptions while introducing modifications appropriate to the final revelation.

**Al-Tabari** examines the qisas verses in detail, comparing the Torah's formulation with the Quranic version and noting that the Quranic addition of the forgiveness option demonstrates the progressive refinement of divine law.

**Al-Qurtubi** provides extensive legal analysis of qisas in Islamic jurisprudence, using the comparison with Mosaic law to illustrate how Islamic law balances strict justice with mercy. He discusses the four Sunni madhahib's (legal schools') positions on retaliatory punishment and compensation.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The existence of divine law itself reflects tawhid — only Allah has the right to legislate for humanity. The moral principles embedded in Exodus 21 (protection of life, proportional justice, care for the vulnerable) derive from the same divine source as Islamic Shari'ah. Islam affirms that Musa faithfully transmitted the laws he received, while also teaching that the final revelation through Muhammad perfected and completed the divine legal framework for all humanity.
