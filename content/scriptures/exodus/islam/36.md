## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 36 narrates the actual construction of the Tabernacle, detailing how the skilled craftsmen executed the divine designs with the abundance of community offerings. While the specific construction belongs to Bani Isra'il's sacred history, the Islamic value of itqan (excellence in work) is deeply relevant. The Prophet Muhammad said, "Allah loves that when one of you does a task, he does it with itqan (excellence)." The dedication and precision displayed in the Tabernacle's construction exemplify this universal principle.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not describe the Tabernacle's construction but addresses sacred building in other contexts:

- **Al-Baqarah 2:127**: "And recall when Ibrahim was raising the foundations of the House and Isma'il, saying, 'Our Lord, accept this from us. Indeed You are the Hearing, the Knowing.'" The Ka'bah's construction serves as Islam's primary paradigm of sacred building.
- **At-Tawbah 9:108**: "A mosque founded on righteousness from the first day is more worthy for you to stand in." The spiritual foundation matters more than the physical grandeur.
- **An-Nahl 16:68**: "And your Lord inspired the bee, 'Take for yourself among the mountains, houses, and among the trees and in that which they construct.'" Divine guidance extends to all forms of construction.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The excess of offerings (Exodus 36:5-7) — the people gave so much that Moses had to stop them — has a parallel in Islamic history during the early Muslim community's generosity for military campaigns and mosque construction. The concept of overwhelming community generosity is celebrated in both traditions.

The detailed craftsmanship descriptions — weaving, metalwork, woodwork — reflect a level of artistic elaboration that early Islamic worship spaces deliberately avoided. The Prophet Muhammad's mosque was simple by design, establishing a precedent that prioritized function over form. Later Islamic civilization developed extraordinary artistic traditions (geometric patterns, calligraphy, arabesque designs), but always within the constraint of avoiding representational imagery.

The construction account emphasizes human skill empowered by divine inspiration. Islam similarly recognizes that all human ability is ultimately from Allah while affirming the value of human effort and craftsmanship. The Islamic artistic tradition — from Andalusian architecture to Ottoman tilework to Persian miniatures — demonstrates that devotion to beauty in service of the sacred is fully compatible with tawhid.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** does not extensively comment on the construction details but affirms the principle that obedience to divine command in all aspects of life, including craftsmanship, is an act of worship.

**Al-Tabari** preserves brief traditions about the Tabernacle's construction as part of the broader Israelite narrative, treating them as historical account.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the Islamic ethics of craftsmanship and labor, connecting the biblical narrative to the Prophet's teaching on itqan. He argues that excellence in worldly work becomes worship when performed with the intention of serving Allah and benefiting the community.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The construction narrative demonstrates that tawhid encompasses all human activity. When craftsmen work with divinely inspired skill to create a space dedicated to Allah's worship, their labor itself becomes an act of devotion. Islam extends this principle universally: any permissible work performed with consciousness of Allah (taqwa) and excellence (itqan) is a form of worship. The Tabernacle's construction under Musa's guidance illustrates the prophetic function of directing all aspects of communal life toward the service of Allah.
