## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 24 describes the formal covenant ratification at Sinai, including the blood ceremony, the elders' vision of God, and Musa's ascent for forty days. The Quran confirms the covenant (mithaq) taken from Bani Isra'il and Musa's forty-day absence on the mountain, though the specific covenant ceremony described in Exodus is not reproduced. Islam treats the covenant as a binding divine obligation that Bani Isra'il subsequently violated.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-A'raf 7:142**: "And We appointed for Musa thirty nights and perfected them by the addition of ten; so the term of his Lord was completed as forty nights. And Musa said to his brother Harun, 'Take my place among my people and do right and do not follow the way of the corrupters.'"
- **Al-Baqarah 2:63**: "And recall when We took your covenant and We raised over you the mount, saying, 'Take what We have given you with determination and remember what is in it.'"
- **Al-Baqarah 2:83-84**: "And recall when We took the covenant from the Children of Israel, saying, 'Do not worship except Allah; and to parents do good and to relatives, orphans, and the needy. And speak to people good words and establish prayer and give zakah.' Then you turned away, except a few of you."
- **An-Nisa 4:154**: "And We raised over them the mount for their covenant; and We said to them, 'Enter the gate bowing humbly,' and We said to them, 'Do not transgress on the Sabbath,' and We took from them a solemn covenant."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The biblical account of the elders seeing God and eating and drinking in His presence (Exodus 24:9-11) is theologically problematic from an Islamic standpoint. Islam categorically denies that any human has seen Allah in this world — Musa himself was denied this vision (Al-A'raf 7:143). Islamic scholars would interpret this passage either as a metaphorical description, as a vision of divine light rather than the divine essence, or as an example of textual corruption (tahrif) in the biblical text.

The blood covenant ceremony (Exodus 24:6-8) — sprinkling blood on the altar and on the people — has no Islamic parallel. Islam does not use blood in covenant ceremonies. While animal sacrifice exists in Islam (udhiyah, hadiy), the blood is never ritually applied to people. The Quran states regarding sacrifice: "Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety from you" (Al-Hajj 22:37).

The forty-day period on the mountain is directly confirmed by the Quran (Al-A'raf 7:142), though the Quran specifies it as thirty days plus ten, providing a detail not found in the biblical account. This period becomes the backdrop for the golden calf incident.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the forty nights in detail, explaining that the initial period of thirty nights was extended by ten to complete the preparation for receiving the Torah. He connects this to the importance of spiritual preparation before receiving divine revelation.

**Al-Tabari** addresses the covenant ceremony from multiple angles, recording traditions that elaborate on the nature of the covenant and its specific obligations. He treats the description of the elders' vision as Isra'iliyyat that should be evaluated in light of established Islamic creed.

**Al-Qurtubi** emphasizes that the covenant (mithaq) between Allah and Bani Isra'il was not merely a historical event but a theological paradigm. Every community that receives divine guidance enters into a covenant of obedience, and violation of that covenant brings consequences — a lesson equally applicable to the Muslim ummah.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The covenant at Sinai reinforces tawhid as the basis of the divine-human relationship. The agreement is not between equals but between the Creator and His servants — Allah grants guidance, and the people pledge obedience. The Quran's emphasis on the covenant's violation by Bani Isra'il serves as a warning that divine favor requires sustained faithfulness. Musa's role as covenant mediator prefigures the prophetic function in Islam, where every prophet establishes or renews the covenant of tawhid between Allah and humanity.
