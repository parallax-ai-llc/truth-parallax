## Islamic Perspective

The opening chapter of Exodus describes the oppression of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel) in Misr (Egypt) under a tyrannical Fir'awn (Pharaoh). This narrative occupies a central place in Islamic theology, as the story of Musa (Moses) is the most frequently recounted prophetic narrative in the Quran. Allah mentions Bani Isra'il's suffering under Fir'awn as a paradigm of divine justice overcoming worldly tyranny.

The Quran confirms the essential outline: Fir'awn exalted himself in the land and divided its people into factions, oppressing a group among them by slaughtering their sons and keeping their women alive (Al-Qasas 28:4). This divine account validates the biblical description of infanticide and forced labor while framing it within the broader Quranic theology of Allah's plan to aid the oppressed (mustad'afin).

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran addresses the condition of Bani Isra'il in Misr in several passages:

- **Al-Qasas 28:4-6**: "Indeed, Fir'awn exalted himself in the land and made its people into factions, oppressing a sector among them, slaughtering their sons and keeping their females alive. Indeed, he was of the corrupters. And We wished to confer favor upon those who were oppressed in the land and make them leaders and make them inheritors."
- **Al-Baqarah 2:49**: "And recall when We saved you from the people of Fir'awn, who afflicted you with the worst torment, slaughtering your sons and keeping your females alive."
- **Ibrahim 14:6**: Musa is described as reminding his people of Allah's favor in rescuing them from Fir'awn's oppression.
- **Al-A'raf 7:127-129**: The elders of Fir'awn's people complained about the Israelites, and Fir'awn responded with increased cruelty.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran does not name the specific Pharaoh or provide the genealogical lists found in Exodus 1. Islamic tradition generally identifies the Pharaoh of oppression with the Pharaoh of the Exodus, though some scholars like Al-Tabari distinguish between two rulers. The story of the midwives Shiphrah and Puah does not appear in the Quran; the focus remains on the macro-level divine plan rather than individual human actors other than the prophets.

Islam frames the oppression not merely as political persecution but as a cosmic struggle between tawhid (divine unity) and the false claims of Fir'awn to divinity. Fir'awn's declaration "I am your lord most high" (An-Nazi'at 79:24) represents the ultimate inversion of tawhid, making his oppression of Bani Isra'il an extension of his rebellion against Allah.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** explains that Fir'awn's persecution arose from a dream or a prophecy that a child from Bani Isra'il would overthrow his kingdom. This motivated the systematic slaughter of male infants, which Ibn Kathir connects to both Quranic and Isra'iliyyat sources.

**Al-Tabari** provides extensive historical detail, drawing on earlier traditions to describe the scale of Israelite slavery and the construction projects they were forced to undertake. He notes that Bani Isra'il had multiplied greatly in Misr, causing Fir'awn to view them as a threat.

**Al-Qurtubi** emphasizes the moral lesson: that tyranny, no matter how powerful, is ultimately overturned by Allah's decree. He connects the suffering of Bani Isra'il to the broader Quranic principle that Allah tests those whom He intends to elevate.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The chapter establishes the theological backdrop for Musa's mission. In Islam, every prophet is sent to call people to tawhid, and the oppression of Bani Isra'il represents the consequences of a society ruled by one who rejects Allah's sovereignty. The divine plan unfolds with perfect wisdom — the very decree of infanticide that Fir'awn issues will lead to Musa being raised in his own household, demonstrating that Allah's plan cannot be thwarted by human scheming (Al-Qasas 28:8).

The concept of prophetic infallibility ('ismah) is not directly relevant to this chapter, as Musa has not yet been born. However, the chapter sets the stage for understanding why Allah chose to raise His prophet within the house of the oppressor — a sign of divine irony and absolute sovereignty that Islamic commentators have long celebrated.
