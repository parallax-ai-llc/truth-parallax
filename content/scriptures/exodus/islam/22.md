## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 22 continues the civil law code with regulations concerning theft, property damage, trusteeship, and social responsibility. These laws address the same fundamental concerns that Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) develops through its own sources. Islam views these regulations as valid divine legislation for their time, reflecting eternal principles of justice that the final Shari'ah incorporates in its own distinctive framework.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-Ma'idah 5:38**: "As for the thief, the male and the female, amputate their hands in recompense for what they committed as a deterrent from Allah." Islamic theft law differs in specifics but shares the Mosaic concern for property protection.
- **Al-Baqarah 2:275-280**: Extensive regulations on financial dealings, prohibition of riba (usury/interest), echoing Exodus 22:25's prohibition on charging interest to the poor.
- **Al-Ma'idah 5:1**: "O you who have believed, fulfill all contracts" — the obligation of contractual fidelity underlying property law.
- **An-Nisa 4:10**: "Indeed, those who devour the property of orphans unjustly are only consuming into their bellies fire." This parallels Exodus 22:22's protection of the fatherless.
- **Al-Isra 17:34**: "And do not approach the property of an orphan, except in the way that is best."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The specific penalties differ between Mosaic and Islamic law. Exodus 22:1 prescribes fourfold or fivefold restitution for theft; Islamic law prescribes amputation for theft above a specific threshold (nisab), while requiring restitution as well. The differences reflect distinct legal philosophies, though both prioritize deterrence and victim compensation.

The prohibition of sorcery (Exodus 22:18, "You shall not permit a sorceress to live") has a strong Islamic parallel. Sorcery (sihr) is classified as a major sin in Islam, and some scholars of the Hanbali school considered it a capital offense, aligning with the Mosaic penalty. Other schools prescribed different punishments but universally condemned sorcery.

The laws protecting strangers (Exodus 22:21) resonate with the Islamic concept of dhimmah — the protected status of non-Muslim residents in a Muslim polity. The Quran commands justice toward all people regardless of religion (Al-Ma'idah 5:8), and the Prophet Muhammad established specific protections for non-Muslim communities.

The prohibition of interest (Exodus 22:25) aligns closely with Islam's absolute prohibition of riba. This is one of the clearest convergences between Mosaic and Islamic law, and Islamic economists frequently cite the Torah's prohibition alongside the Quranic injunctions to support the universality of this principle.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the convergence of Mosaic and Islamic property law as evidence of a unified divine legislative will. He notes that while specific penalties may differ, the underlying principles of protecting property, aiding the vulnerable, and maintaining social justice are constant.

**Al-Tabari** treats the Exodus property laws as authentic Isra'iliyyat legislation, comparing them with analogous Islamic rulings to illustrate both continuity and development in divine law.

**Al-Qurtubi** provides detailed comparative analysis, particularly on the prohibition of interest, which he traces through Jewish, Christian, and Islamic legal traditions. He argues that the consistency of this prohibition across revelations demonstrates its divine origin and its importance to social justice.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

Property law in Islam is ultimately rooted in tawhid: all wealth belongs to Allah, and human beings are trustees (khulafa) of the resources in their possession. The laws in Exodus 22 reflect this same theological foundation, even if it is expressed differently. Justice in commercial dealings, protection of the vulnerable, and prohibition of exploitation are manifestations of submission to Allah's will in the social realm. The continuity between Mosaic and Islamic law on these points demonstrates that God's concern for justice is not limited to one nation or era.
