## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 17 contains two significant episodes: water flowing from the rock at Horeb and the battle against Amalek. The water-from-the-rock miracle has direct Quranic confirmation and is one of the most referenced miracles of Musa. The battle against Amalek, however, is not mentioned in the Quran and falls within the category of Isra'iliyyat narratives that Islamic tradition treats with scholarly caution.

## Quranic Parallels

The miracle of water from the rock is clearly attested:

- **Al-Baqarah 2:60**: "And recall when Musa prayed for water for his people, so We said, 'Strike with your staff the stone.' And there gushed forth from it twelve springs, and every people knew its watering place. 'Eat and drink from the provision of Allah, and do not commit abuse on the earth, spreading corruption.'"
- **Al-A'raf 7:160**: "And We divided them into twelve descendant tribes as distinct nations. And We inspired to Musa when his people implored him for water, 'Strike with your staff the stone'; and there gushed forth from it twelve springs. Every people knew its watering place."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran specifies twelve springs — one for each tribe of Bani Isra'il — while Exodus 17 describes a single stream from the rock at Horeb. This Quranic detail emphasizes Allah's comprehensive provision and organizational wisdom, ensuring that each community received its own designated source.

The Quran does not mention the place names "Massah and Meribah" (testing and quarreling) used in Exodus 17:7. However, the Quranic context of complaint and divine response captures the same dynamic of human dissatisfaction meeting divine generosity.

The battle with Amalek (Exodus 17:8-16), including the detail of Moses holding up his hands to ensure victory, has no Quranic parallel. Islamic scholars classify this as Isra'iliyyat. The concept of victory being tied to a prophet's raised hands, while not impossible, is not a typical Islamic framing of divine aid in battle. In the Quran, victory comes from Allah's decree, the believers' faith, and angelic assistance (as at Badr, Ali 'Imran 3:123-125), not from ritualistic physical postures.

The biblical statement "The Lord will have war with Amalek from generation to generation" (Exodus 17:16) has no Islamic equivalent. Islam does not recognize hereditary divine enmity against specific ethnic groups; each individual and generation is judged on its own merits.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the twelve springs in detail, connecting them to the twelve tribes descended from Ya'qub's (Jacob's) sons. He emphasizes the miracle's significance: water flowing from stone demonstrates that Allah can extract sustenance from the most unlikely sources.

**Al-Tabari** records traditions about the specific stone, with some narrators claiming it was a portable stone that traveled with the Israelites. He evaluates these accounts critically, noting the diversity of opinion.

**Al-Qurtubi** derives the principle that complaining to Allah in supplication (du'a) is not blameworthy — it is the appropriate response to hardship. What was blameworthy about Bani Isra'il was their lack of trust in Allah's ability to provide, not the act of asking itself.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The water-from-rock miracle reinforces tawhid by demonstrating that creation's resources are entirely under Allah's command. Water does not flow from rock by natural law; it flows because Allah commands it. The twelve springs for twelve tribes show divine precision and care, refuting any notion that Allah is unaware of His servants' specific needs. Musa's role as intercessor — praying for water on behalf of his people — exemplifies the prophetic function of mediating between the community and the divine, a role that culminates in Muhammad's intercession (shafa'ah) on the Day of Judgment.
