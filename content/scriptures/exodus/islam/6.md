## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 6 contains God's reassurance to Musa after Fir'awn's initial rejection, a reiteration of the covenant with Bani Isra'il, and a genealogical record of Musa and Harun's lineage. In Islam, this chapter affirms the essential Quranic teaching that Allah never abandons His prophets or those who trust in Him. The divine promise to deliver Bani Isra'il is part of Allah's unchangeable decree (qadar), and no human opposition can alter it.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-Qasas 28:5-6**: "And We wished to confer favor upon those who were oppressed in the land and make them leaders and make them inheritors. And to establish them in the land and show Fir'awn and Haman and their soldiers through them that which they had feared."
- **Al-A'raf 7:128**: Musa counsels his people: "Seek help through Allah and be patient. Indeed, the earth belongs to Allah. He causes to inherit it whom He wills of His servants. And the best outcome is for the righteous."
- **Al-A'raf 7:137**: "And We caused the people who had been oppressed to inherit the eastern regions of the land and the western ones, which We had blessed. And the good word of your Lord was fulfilled for the Children of Israel because of what they had patiently endured."
- **Ad-Dukhan 44:30-33**: "And We certainly saved the Children of Israel from the humiliating torment — from Fir'awn. Indeed, he was a haughty one among the transgressors."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The most significant theological divergence concerns the divine name. Exodus 6:2-3 states that God appeared to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as "God Almighty" (El Shaddai) but did not make His name YHWH known to them. Islam rejects this progressive revelation of divine names — in Islamic theology, Allah has always been known by His names and attributes, and all prophets from Adam to Muhammad called upon the same God by the same essential identity. The concept of a previously unrevealed divine name is foreign to Islamic thought.

The extensive genealogical lists in Exodus 6:14-25 have no Quranic counterpart. Islamic tradition generally does not emphasize detailed genealogies of Bani Isra'il, though scholars like Al-Tabari record some lineage information from Isra'iliyyat sources. The Quran is selective in its historical details, providing only what serves its moral and theological purposes.

The Israelites' refusal to listen due to "shortness of spirit" (Exodus 6:9) resonates with Quranic descriptions of human weakness under oppression. However, the Quran does not blame Bani Isra'il for their despair at this stage — the condemnation comes later when they reject clear signs despite having witnessed miracles.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes that Allah's covenant with the descendants of Ibrahim was unconditional in its promise of deliverance but conditional in its spiritual blessings. He connects this to the broader Quranic theme that divine favor requires obedience and gratitude.

**Al-Tabari** records various traditions about the period between Musa's first audience with Fir'awn and the beginning of the plagues, noting that some scholars estimate a period of years during which Musa continued to preach.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the concept of divine patience (hilm) — Allah delays punishment to give the oppressor opportunity to repent, but when the decree comes, it is inescapable. He connects this to the Quranic principle "And your Lord is not ever unjust to His servants" (Fussilat 41:46).

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

This chapter reinforces the Islamic understanding that Allah's promises are certain and His plan unfolds according to His wisdom, not human timelines. The covenant with Bani Isra'il is framed in Islam not as an exclusive ethnic covenant but as part of Allah's universal plan of guidance through prophets. Musa's continued faithfulness despite temporary setbacks exemplifies the prophetic quality of sabr (patience), which is inseparable from complete trust in Allah's decree.
