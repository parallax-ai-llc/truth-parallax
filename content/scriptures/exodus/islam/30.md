## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 30 describes the altar of incense, the census tax (half-shekel), the bronze basin for washing, the anointing oil, and the sacred incense formula. These elements represent the auxiliary ritual infrastructure supporting Tabernacle worship. While incense and purification have roles in various Islamic contexts, the specific forms described here belong to the Shari'ah of Musa and are treated as Isra'iliyyat by Islamic scholars.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not reference these specific ritual objects but addresses the underlying principles:

- **Al-Ma'idah 5:6**: The elaborate washing requirements of the Levitical system are simplified in Islam through wudu — ablution of the hands, face, arms, and feet — establishing ritual purity through accessible means.
- **At-Tawbah 9:103**: "Take from their wealth a purifying charity (sadaqah) by which you purify them and cause them to increase." The census tax of Exodus 30:11-16 finds a conceptual parallel in zakah, Islam's obligatory charitable tax.
- **Al-Baqarah 2:222**: "Indeed, Allah loves those who are constantly repentant and loves those who purify themselves." Purification is a valued Islamic practice, though its forms differ from the Mosaic prescriptions.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The altar of incense (Exodus 30:1-10) has no Islamic equivalent. While incense (bukhur) is used in some Muslim cultures for fragrance, it has no prescribed role in Islamic worship. The Prophet Muhammad appreciated pleasant scents and recommended them, but incense burning is not a component of salah or any obligatory worship.

The half-shekel census tax (Exodus 30:11-16) bears a structural resemblance to zakah but differs in purpose and application. The Torah's tax is a "ransom for one's life" to avert plague; zakah in Islam is an act of worship that purifies wealth and supports the community. The theological framing is distinct even if the practical function overlaps.

The sacred anointing oil (Exodus 30:22-33), with its specific recipe and prohibition against secular use, has no Islamic parallel. Islam does not use anointing oil in any ritual context. Olive oil is valued for health and nutrition (the Quran praises the olive tree in An-Nur 24:35 and At-Tin 95:1), but not as a consecrating substance.

The prohibition against replicating the sacred incense formula (Exodus 30:37-38) reflects a concept of sacred exclusivity that Islam approaches differently. In Islam, acts of worship are defined by their intention (niyyah) and form, not by unique physical substances.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** does not extensively comment on these ritual details, as they fall outside the Quranic narrative. He mentions the Israelite ritual system in general terms when discussing the Torah.

**Al-Tabari** preserves brief traditions about the Tabernacle's furnishings and rituals, categorizing them as historical information about Bani Isra'il.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the Islamic concept of taharah (purification) in comparison with other scriptural traditions. He emphasizes that Islam's purification system is comprehensive yet accessible — requiring only water and sincere intention — in contrast to the elaborate Levitical requirements.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The progressive simplification from the complex Mosaic ritual system to the streamlined Islamic worship reflects what Muslim scholars consider the perfection of divine law. Tawhid demands that the barriers between worshiper and Creator be minimized. While the Mosaic rituals were appropriate for their context and community, the final revelation removed the intermediary layers — the incense, the anointing oil, the sacred formulas — leaving the believer face to face with Allah in the simplicity of prayer, fasting, charity, and pilgrimage.
