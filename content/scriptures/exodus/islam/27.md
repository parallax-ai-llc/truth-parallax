## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 27 describes the construction of the altar of burnt offering, the Tabernacle courtyard, and the provision of oil for the lampstand. The altar occupies a central place in Israelite worship as the site of animal sacrifice. While Islam practices animal sacrifice (particularly during Eid al-Adha), the ritual framework differs entirely from the Tabernacle system. Islamic sacrifice does not require a consecrated altar, a priestly officiant, or a designated sacred precinct.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran addresses sacrifice in principle rather than replicating the Tabernacle's sacrificial system:

- **Al-Hajj 22:34**: "And for every ummah (nation) We have appointed a rite of sacrifice that they may mention the name of Allah over what He has provided for them of sacrificial animals."
- **Al-Hajj 22:36-37**: "And the camels and cattle — We have appointed them for you as among the symbols of Allah. ... Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety (taqwa) from you."
- **Al-Kawthar 108:2**: "So pray to your Lord and sacrifice." This command to the Prophet Muhammad establishes the Islamic practice of sacrifice independent of any altar or temple system.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The bronze altar described in Exodus 27 is a fixed architectural feature with specific dimensions and design. Islamic sacrifice has no such requirement — it may be performed anywhere that is clean and appropriate, reflecting the hadith "The earth has been made for me a place of prayer and a purifier."

The Tabernacle's courtyard system, with its graduated access and specific spatial arrangement, contrasts with the open accessibility of Islamic worship. The Prophet Muhammad deliberately simplified the physical requirements of worship, removing the barriers between the believer and the act of devotion.

The provision of pure olive oil for the perpetual lamp (Exodus 27:20-21) has a conceptual resonance with the famous Light Verse (An-Nur 24:35), where Allah's light is compared to "a niche within which is a lamp, the lamp is within glass, the glass as if it were a pearly star, lit from a blessed olive tree." However, this is a metaphorical parallel rather than a ritual one.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** mentions sacrificial practices of previous nations briefly, noting that each community received its own prescribed ritual while the underlying purpose — devotion to Allah — remained constant.

**Al-Tabari** records traditions about the Israelite sacrificial system as historical background, treating them as part of the narrative of Bani Isra'il's religious life in the wilderness period.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the verse "For every ummah We have appointed a rite of sacrifice" (Al-Hajj 22:34) in detail, arguing that sacrifice has been a universal form of worship across all prophetic dispensations. The specific forms differ, but the act of offering to Allah is constant.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The Quranic statement that "neither their meat nor their blood reaches Allah, but what reaches Him is taqwa from you" (Al-Hajj 22:37) encapsulates the Islamic understanding of sacrifice. The altar, the animal, and the blood are means, not ends. Tawhid demands that the worshiper's intention be directed solely to Allah, and no physical object or ritual structure can substitute for genuine devotion. This principle explains why Islam eliminated the elaborate sacrificial infrastructure of previous dispensations while preserving the essential act of sacrifice as an expression of submission to Allah.
