## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 20 contains the Ten Commandments, the foundational ethical code of the Torah. Islam recognizes these commandments as part of the divine guidance given to Musa and affirms their essential moral content, as the Quran independently establishes the same principles. Islamic scholars have long noted the remarkable convergence between the Decalogue and Quranic ethical teachings, viewing this as evidence of the unified divine source of all prophetic revelation.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran contains direct parallels to each of the Ten Commandments:

1. **No other gods**: "There is no deity except Allah" — the shahada, the foundation of Islam.
2. **No graven images**: "Do not set up with Allah another deity" (Al-Isra 17:22). Prohibition of idolatry is the most emphasized Quranic theme.
3. **Do not take God's name in vain**: "And do not make Allah's name an excuse in your oaths" (Al-Baqarah 2:224).
4. **Remember the Sabbath**: Islam replaced the Sabbath with Friday prayer (Al-Jumu'ah 62:9) while acknowledging the Sabbath was prescribed for Bani Isra'il (An-Nahl 16:124).
5. **Honor parents**: "And your Lord has decreed that you not worship except Him, and to parents, good treatment" (Al-Isra 17:23).
6. **Do not murder**: "And do not kill the soul which Allah has forbidden, except by right" (Al-Isra 17:33).
7. **Do not commit adultery**: "And do not approach unlawful sexual intercourse" (Al-Isra 17:32).
8. **Do not steal**: Theft is prohibited and penalized in Islamic law (Al-Ma'idah 5:38).
9. **Do not bear false witness**: "And do not conceal testimony" (Al-Baqarah 2:283); "avoid false statement" (Al-Hajj 22:30).
10. **Do not covet**: "And do not wish for that by which Allah has made some of you exceed others" (An-Nisa 4:32).

The most striking parallel is Al-Isra 17:22-39, which scholars have called the "Quranic Decalogue" — a sequence of ethical commands closely mirroring the Ten Commandments.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

While the moral principles converge, significant theological differences exist. The Quran does not present these commandments as inscribed on stone tablets given at a specific historical moment in the same way. Rather, the Quran presents them as eternal truths communicated to all prophets. The tablets (al-alwah) are mentioned in Al-A'raf 7:145 — "And We wrote for him on the tablets from all things — instruction and explanation for all things" — but the specific content is not listed as the Ten Commandments.

The Sabbath commandment is the most divergent point. Islam explicitly does not observe the Sabbath. The Quran states that the Sabbath was imposed specifically on those who "differed over it" (An-Nahl 16:124), and the Prophet Muhammad established Friday as the Muslim day of congregation without the rest requirements of the Sabbath.

The biblical framing of "I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt" as a preamble to the Decalogue ties the law to a specific historical event. The Quran ties its ethical commands to the universal reality of Allah's lordship, not to a particular act of national deliverance.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the alwah (tablets) given to Musa, noting that they contained guidance and mercy (Al-A'raf 7:154). He does not limit the tablets' content to ten commandments but sees them as comprehensive divine guidance.

**Al-Tabari** examines the relationship between the Mosaic law and Islamic Shari'ah, noting areas of convergence and divergence. He treats the Decalogue as authentic divine law that was valid for its time and people.

**Al-Qurtubi** provides the most extensive comparison, analyzing each commandment against Quranic parallels. He concludes that the ethical core is universal and eternal, while specific ritual prescriptions (like the Sabbath) were tailored to particular communities.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The first commandment — "no other gods" — is the essence of tawhid and confirms that the Mosaic revelation was fundamentally the same message as Islam. The convergence of the Decalogue with Quranic ethics demonstrates the Islamic principle that all prophets preached the same religion in its essential form. Differences in specific laws (Sabbath vs. Friday, dietary codes, etc.) reflect the Islamic concept of different Shari'ahs for different peoples, while the underlying aqidah (creed) remains constant.
