## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 16 describes the miraculous provision of manna and quails to Bani Isra'il in the wilderness. This is one of the few wilderness narratives with direct Quranic confirmation. The Quran mentions manna (al-mann) and quails (al-salwa) as specific divine blessings granted to the Israelites, making this chapter significant for Islamic engagement with the Exodus narrative beyond the Egyptian episodes.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-Baqarah 2:57**: "And We shaded you with clouds and sent down to you manna and quails, saying, 'Eat from the good things with which We have provided you.' And they wronged Us not, but they were wronging themselves."
- **Al-A'raf 7:160**: "And We shaded them with clouds and sent down upon them manna and quails, saying, 'Eat from the good things with which We have provided you.' And they wronged Us not, but they used to wrong themselves."
- **Taha 20:80-81**: "O Children of Israel, We delivered you from your enemy, and We made an appointment with you at the right side of the mount, and We sent down to you manna and quails. Eat from the good things We have provided for you and do not transgress therein."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The Quran confirms the provision of manna and quails but does not detail the daily collection rules, the prohibition against hoarding, the double portion on the sixth day, or the Sabbath regulations found in Exodus 16. These specifics belong to the Torah's legal framework, and Islam neither affirms nor denies them, treating them as Isra'iliyyat of the neutral category.

The Sabbath institution embedded in this narrative (Exodus 16:23-30) is acknowledged in Islam as a law given to Bani Isra'il but not applicable to Muslims. The Quran references the Sabbath primarily in the context of those who violated it (Al-Baqarah 2:65, Al-A'raf 7:163-166), not as a model for Islamic practice. Friday (Jumu'ah) serves a different function in Islam — it is a day of congregational prayer, not a day of rest.

The Israelites' complaints about food (Exodus 16:2-3) are reflected in the Quran's broader critique of Bani Isra'il's ingratitude. Al-Baqarah 2:61 records them saying, "O Musa, we can never endure one kind of food. So call upon your Lord to bring forth for us from the earth its green herbs and its cucumbers and its garlic and its lentils and its onions." Musa's response — "Would you exchange what is better for what is less?" — captures the Islamic assessment of preferring worldly comforts over divine provision.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** identifies manna as a sweet, honey-like substance that descended upon the Israelites each morning, and quails as small birds that were easily caught. He emphasizes that these provisions required no labor — a pure divine gift that should have inspired profound gratitude.

**Al-Tabari** records various opinions about the nature of manna, ranging from a gum-like substance to heavenly bread. He preserves multiple traditions while noting that the exact nature is less important than the theological lesson of divine sustenance.

**Al-Qurtubi** connects the manna and quails to the broader Islamic concept of rizq (divine provision). He argues that just as Allah provided for Bani Isra'il in an uninhabitable wilderness, He provides for all creation according to His wisdom. The lesson is tawakkul — trusting Allah for sustenance rather than relying on human calculation alone.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The provision of manna and quails demonstrates tawhid through Allah's role as Al-Razzaq (the Provider). No intermediate deity or natural process is credited — the sustenance comes directly from Allah's command. The Israelites' complaint, despite receiving miraculous food, illustrates a recurring Quranic theme: human ingratitude in the face of divine generosity. This serves as a warning to the Muslim ummah to be grateful for Allah's blessings and not to exchange the spiritually superior for the worldly inferior.
