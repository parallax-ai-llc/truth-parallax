## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 11 records the announcement of the final and most devastating plague — the death of the firstborn. This represents the climactic warning before the complete destruction of Fir'awn's power. In Islamic theology, this event embodies the principle that Allah's patience (hilm) has limits, and that persistent rejection of divine signs ultimately leads to catastrophic consequences. The final plague is the point of no return in the divine reckoning with Fir'awn.

## Quranic Parallels

While the Quran does not specifically describe the plague of the firstborn, it provides the theological framework:

- **Al-A'raf 7:136**: "So We took retribution from them, and We drowned them in the sea because they denied Our signs and were heedless of them."
- **Al-Mu'min/Ghafir 40:45**: "So Allah protected him from the evils they plotted, and the people of Fir'awn were enveloped by the worst of punishment."
- **Ad-Dukhan 44:28-29**: "Thus. And We caused to inherit it another people. And the heaven and earth wept not for them, nor were they reprieved."
- **Yunus 10:92**: Allah preserves Fir'awn's body as a sign, indicating the totality of divine judgment against the Egyptian regime.
- **Al-Isra 17:101**: "And We had certainly given Musa nine evident signs" — some scholars count the death of the firstborn among these nine signs.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The death of the firstborn as a specific plague is not narrated in the Quran. Islamic scholars have discussed whether this event is implicitly included in the Quranic account or is part of the biblical traditions (Isra'iliyyat) that may be accepted without being required belief. Most scholars accept its historicity based on the principle that the Quran's silence does not constitute denial.

The concept of God "striking" the firstborn raises theological questions in Islam. Islamic scholars note that collective punishment in the Quran is always tied to the community's collective rejection of guidance (as with 'Ad, Thamud, and others). The death of children who are personally innocent is understood within the framework of divine wisdom that transcends human comprehension — children who die are, in Islamic theology, innocent and admitted to paradise.

The biblical emphasis on the Israelites "plundering" the Egyptians (Exodus 11:2-3) by asking for silver and gold is not reflected in the Quran. Some Islamic scholars consider this detail problematic, as it could imply deception, and prophets in Islam are characterized by absolute honesty. Others interpret it as just compensation for years of unpaid labor.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the escalation of divine punishment as consistent with Allah's sunnah (established practice) with previous nations. He connects the ten plagues to the nine signs mentioned in Al-Isra 17:101, debating which specific signs are included.

**Al-Tabari** records the tradition of the firstborn's death from Isra'iliyyat sources, treating it as a plausible historical event that is neither confirmed nor denied by the Quran. He notes that some scholars count the splitting of the sea as the ninth or tenth sign.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the legal principle of gradual punishment (tadarruj fil-'uqubah), noting that Allah typically sends lighter warnings before severe ones. The death of the firstborn, as the penultimate punishment before drowning, fits this pattern of escalating divine retribution.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

This chapter demonstrates that the consequences of rejecting tawhid ultimately become catastrophic. Fir'awn's persistent claim to divinity brought destruction not only upon himself but upon his entire society — illustrating the Islamic principle that unjust rulers bring ruin upon their peoples. The prophetic mission of Musa was to avert this destruction through the message of tawhid; its rejection sealed Fir'awn's fate. The event also demonstrates that true power over life and death belongs exclusively to Allah, exposing the ultimate impotence of Fir'awn's pretended lordship.
