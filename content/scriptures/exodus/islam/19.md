## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 19 describes the arrival at Mount Sinai and the preparations for receiving the divine law. The Quran references Mount Tur (Sinai) as a place of profound divine encounter and covenant-making. The theophany at Sinai — while understood differently in Islam than in biblical theology — represents a critical moment in sacred history when Allah established His covenant with Bani Isra'il through Musa.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Al-A'raf 7:142-143**: "And We appointed for Musa thirty nights and perfected them by the addition of ten; so the term of his Lord was completed as forty nights. ... And when Musa arrived at Our appointed time and his Lord spoke to him, he said, 'My Lord, show me Yourself that I may look at You.' Allah said, 'You will not see Me, but look at the mountain; if it should remain in its place, then you will see Me.' But when his Lord appeared to the mountain, He made it level, and Musa fell unconscious."
- **Al-Baqarah 2:63**: "And recall when We took your covenant and We raised over you the mount, saying, 'Take what We have given you with determination and remember what is in it that perhaps you may become righteous.'"
- **Maryam 19:52**: "And We called him from the right side of the mount, and We brought him near, confiding to him."
- **At-Tur 52:1**: "By the mount (al-Tur)" — the mountain itself is used as a divine oath, indicating its sacred significance.
- **Al-A'raf 7:171**: "And recall when We raised the mountain above them as if it was a dark cloud and they were certain that it would fall upon them."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The most significant theological difference concerns the nature of the divine encounter. Exodus 19 describes God "descending" upon the mountain with thunder, lightning, thick cloud, and trumpet blasts. Islam categorically rejects the notion of Allah physically descending to or inhabiting a specific location, as this would imply corporeality (tajsim), which contradicts the Quranic declaration "There is nothing like unto Him" (Ash-Shura 42:11).

The Quran addresses the question of seeing Allah directly in Al-A'raf 7:143, where Musa's request to see Allah is denied. When Allah manifests to the mountain, it crumbles — demonstrating that created matter cannot bear the divine manifestation. This is one of the key proof-texts in Islamic theology against the Mu'tazili and other positions on the beatific vision, with Ash'ari scholars arguing that the verse implies seeing Allah is possible in the afterlife (since Musa would not have asked for something absolutely impossible) but not in this world.

The concept of Israel as a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6) is not reflected in the Quran. Islam does not recognize a priestly class within Bani Isra'il as having special ontological status, and the concept of a "chosen nation" is reframed — Bani Isra'il were favored with prophets and guidance but are held to the same standard of obedience as all peoples.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the mountain encounter extensively, emphasizing that Musa heard Allah's speech without mediation — affirming his unique title Kalimullah. He carefully navigates the theological implications, affirming Allah's transcendence while acknowledging the reality of divine speech.

**Al-Tabari** provides detailed accounts of the forty days of preparation, connecting the thirty days plus ten mentioned in Al-A'raf 7:142 to a period of fasting and spiritual purification before receiving the Torah.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the theological controversies surrounding Allah's manifestation (tajalli) to the mountain, presenting the views of various schools. He ultimately affirms the Ash'ari position that Allah's essence is beyond spatial limitation, and that the mountain episode demonstrates the created world's inability to withstand the divine reality.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The Sinai theophany is ultimately about tawhid — Allah's transcendence over all creation. The mountain's destruction upon divine manifestation proves that the Creator is fundamentally different from the creation. This anti-anthropomorphic lesson is central to Islamic theology. Musa's unique privilege of hearing Allah's speech directly, while being unable to see Him, establishes the limits of even prophetic experience in this world while pointing to the promise of the afterlife.
