## Islamic Perspective

The encounter at the burning bush is a pivotal moment in Islamic sacred history. The Quran recounts how Musa, while traveling with his family, perceived a fire on the side of Mount Tur (Sinai) and approached it, only to receive the direct call of Allah. This event marks the beginning of Musa's prophetic mission and is one of the most theologically significant passages in the Quran, as it contains Allah's self-disclosure and the establishment of tawhid as the foundation of Musa's message.

## Quranic Parallels

Multiple surahs describe this encounter in vivid detail:

- **Taha 20:9-14**: "Has the story of Musa reached you? When he saw a fire and said to his family, 'Stay here; indeed, I have perceived a fire. Perhaps I can bring you a torch or find at the fire some guidance.' And when he came to it, he was called, 'O Musa, indeed I am your Lord, so remove your sandals. Indeed, you are in the sacred valley of Tuwa. And I have chosen you, so listen to what is revealed. Indeed, I am Allah. There is no deity except Me, so worship Me and establish prayer for My remembrance.'"
- **An-Naml 27:7-9**: "When Musa said to his family, 'Indeed, I have perceived a fire. I will bring you from there information or will bring you a burning torch that you may warm yourselves.' But when he came to it, he was called, 'Blessed is whoever is at the fire and whoever is around it. And exalted is Allah, Lord of the worlds.'"
- **Al-Qasas 28:29-30**: "When Musa had completed the term and was traveling with his family, he perceived from the direction of the mount a fire. He said to his family, 'Stay here; indeed, I have perceived a fire.' ... But when he came to it, he was called from the right side of the valley in a blessed spot — from the tree, 'O Musa, indeed I am Allah, Lord of the worlds.'"

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The most significant theological difference concerns Allah's self-identification. In Exodus 3:14, God reveals the name "I AM WHO I AM" (YHWH), which became the sacred Tetragrammaton in Judaism. The Quran instead has Allah declare "Indeed, I am Allah. There is no deity except Me" (Taha 20:14) — a direct statement of tawhid rather than an ontological name. Islamic theology rejects the concept of a personal divine name that carries independent metaphysical significance, emphasizing instead Allah's attributes and the unity of His essence.

The Quran does not mention the bush itself burning without being consumed. Rather, the focus is on the fire as a sign and the voice that emanates from the blessed spot near the tree. The miraculous element is the divine speech itself, not the physical phenomenon of an unconsumed bush.

Islam also does not include the extended dialogue about the Israelites' potential disbelief found in Exodus 3:13-22. The Quranic account is more direct — Allah commands, and Musa responds with obedience, though he does express human apprehension addressed in subsequent passages.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** explains that the fire Musa saw was in fact divine light (nur), not ordinary fire. He cites traditions that the location was the sacred valley of Tuwa, which he identifies near Mount Sinai. He emphasizes that Musa's removal of his sandals signified reverence for the sacred ground.

**Al-Tabari** records various opinions about the nature of what Musa perceived. Some early authorities said it was light from Allah's majesty; others understood it literally as fire that served as a means to attract Musa's attention. Al-Tabari treats both views respectfully while preferring the interpretation supported by the strongest chains of transmission.

**Al-Qurtubi** extracts legal and spiritual rulings from this passage, including the propriety of removing shoes in sacred spaces and the obligation of prayer as established in Taha 20:14. He notes that this verse is among the clearest proofs of tawhid in the Quran.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

This chapter is foundational for tawhid. Allah's declaration "There is no deity except Me" (Taha 20:14) is the essence of the shahada and the core of every prophet's message. The burning bush episode demonstrates that Allah communicates with His chosen servants directly — Musa is described as "Kalimullah" (the one who spoke with Allah), a unique title that elevates him among the prophets. Prophetic infallibility is affirmed in Musa's immediate submission to the divine command, despite the overwhelming nature of the encounter.
