## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 39 describes the completion of the priestly garments and the final inspection of all Tabernacle components by Musa. The chapter's recurring phrase — "as the Lord had commanded Moses" — emphasizes exact obedience to divine instruction. While the specific garments and objects belong to the Israelite Shari'ah, the principle of precise obedience (ta'at) to divine commands is a cornerstone of Islamic practice. Islam places immense importance on performing worship exactly as prescribed, a principle captured in the hadith: "Pray as you have seen me pray."

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran emphasizes complete obedience to divine commands:

- **An-Nisa 4:59**: "O you who have believed, obey Allah and obey the Messenger and those in authority among you."
- **Al-Hashr 59:7**: "And whatever the Messenger has given you — take it; and what he has forbidden you — refrain from it."
- **An-Nur 24:51**: "The only statement of the faithful believers, when they are called to Allah and His Messenger to judge between them, is that they say, 'We hear and we obey.'"
- **Al-Ahzab 33:36**: "It is not for a believing man or a believing woman, when Allah and His Messenger have decided a matter, that they should have any option about their decision."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The completion of the priestly garments — ephod, breastpiece, robe, turban, and sash — has no Islamic ritual equivalent. As discussed in the commentary on Exodus 28, Islam does not have priestly vestments. The completion theme, however, parallels the Quranic concept of ikmal (perfection/completion) of religion: "This day I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favor upon you and have approved for you Islam as religion" (Al-Ma'idah 5:3).

The phrase "as the Lord had commanded Moses" appears multiple times in this chapter, establishing a pattern of exact compliance. This echoes the Islamic concept of ittiba' (following precisely) — particularly in worship, where innovation (bid'ah) is discouraged. The Muslim community is expected to follow the prophetic example (sunnah) with the same precision that the Israelite craftsmen followed the divine blueprint.

Musa's inspection and blessing of the completed work (Exodus 39:43) parallels the prophetic function of overseeing and approving communal worship practices. In Islam, the Prophet Muhammad personally demonstrated the correct forms of worship and corrected those who deviated, ensuring that the divine instructions were preserved with precision.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes the importance of obedience to divine commands in their exact form, connecting this to the Islamic principle that worship must follow the prescribed manner rather than human preference or innovation.

**Al-Tabari** records the completion of the Tabernacle project as a milestone in Bani Isra'il's post-exodus history, noting that the community's collective effort after the golden calf incident demonstrated genuine repentance through action.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the concept of bid'ah (religious innovation) in light of the obedience theme. He argues that just as the Israelite craftsmen were required to follow the divine design exactly, Muslims are obligated to worship in the manner prescribed by the Prophet Muhammad without addition or subtraction.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

Precise obedience to divine commands is an expression of tawhid because it acknowledges that Allah alone has the right to determine how He is to be worshiped. Human beings cannot improve upon divine legislation through their own creativity. The completion of the Tabernacle "as the Lord had commanded" demonstrates the ideal relationship between the Creator's command and the creation's response: complete, exact, and unhesitating submission. This is the essence of islam (submission) in its linguistic and theological sense.
