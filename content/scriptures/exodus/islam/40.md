## Islamic Perspective

The final chapter of Exodus describes the erection of the Tabernacle and the descent of God's glory (kavod) in the form of a cloud filling the sacred space. This climactic moment — the divine presence inhabiting a human-built structure — raises profound theological questions that Islamic thought addresses through its own distinctive framework. Islam affirms that Allah's guidance accompanied Bani Isra'il but understands the nature of that accompaniment differently from biblical theology.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran references divine guidance and sakinah (tranquility/divine presence) in ways that connect to this chapter's themes:

- **Al-Baqarah 2:248**: The tabut (Ark) containing sakinah from Allah — the closest Quranic concept to the divine presence dwelling among the Israelites.
- **At-Tawbah 9:26**: "Then Allah sent down His sakinah upon His Messenger and upon the believers." Sakinah descends upon people, not upon structures.
- **At-Tawbah 9:40**: In the cave during the hijrah, Abu Bakr was told "Do not grieve; indeed Allah is with us." Allah "sent down His sakinah upon him and supported him with soldiers you did not see."
- **Al-Fath 48:4**: "It is He who sent down sakinah into the hearts of the believers that they would increase in faith along with their existing faith."
- **Al-Baqarah 2:57**: "And We shaded you with clouds" — divine protection through clouds in the wilderness, resonating with the cloud imagery of Exodus 40.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The concept of God's glory (kavod) filling the Tabernacle so that even Moses could not enter (Exodus 40:34-35) is fundamentally reframed in Islamic theology. Islam does not accept that Allah dwells in or is contained by any physical structure. The Quran states, "His Throne extends over the heavens and the earth" (Al-Baqarah 2:255) — Allah is too great to be contained by any created space.

The Islamic concept of sakinah provides an alternative framework. Rather than divine indwelling, sakinah is a spiritual state of tranquility and assurance that Allah bestows upon His servants. It descends into hearts, not buildings. This distinction preserves divine transcendence while affirming Allah's intimate care for His creation.

The cloud and fire guiding the Israelites' journeys (Exodus 40:36-38) has no direct Quranic equivalent, though the general principle of divine guidance through signs is well established. In Islam, the ultimate form of divine guidance is the Quran itself — "This is the Book about which there is no doubt, a guidance for those conscious of Allah" (Al-Baqarah 2:2).

The setting up of the Tabernacle on the first day of the first month (Exodus 40:2, 17) establishes a sacred calendar marker. Islam has its own sacred calendar — the hijri calendar — with its own foundational dates and sacred months, replacing the Israelite liturgical cycle.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** discusses the completion of the Tabernacle as the establishment of organized worship for Bani Isra'il in the wilderness. He frames it as one stage in the progressive history of sacred spaces culminating in the Ka'bah's restoration as the center of monotheistic worship under Islam.

**Al-Tabari** records traditions about the cloud and fire phenomena, treating them as Isra'iliyyat that describe genuine divine signs granted to Bani Isra'il during their wilderness period. He notes that such extraordinary signs were appropriate for a community in extraordinary circumstances.

**Al-Qurtubi** concludes his engagement with the Tabernacle narrative by discussing the hierarchy of sacred spaces in Islamic thought. He notes that the Prophet Muhammad stated, "The earth has been made for me a masjid and a purifier" — meaning that while specific sacred spaces exist (Masjid al-Haram, Masjid al-Nabawi, Masjid al-Aqsa), every clean place on earth is suitable for worship. This universalization of sacred space represents, in Al-Qurtubi's view, the final expression of the principle that began with the Tabernacle.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

The conclusion of Exodus brings the narrative full circle: from the oppression of a people who had lost their way to the establishment of organized worship of the one God. In Islamic perspective, this trajectory illustrates the prophetic mission at its core — calling people from the worship of creation (Fir'awn's false divinity) to the worship of the Creator. The Tabernacle, whatever its physical form, served the same ultimate purpose as the Ka'bah, the mosque, and the prayer mat: orienting the human soul toward its Creator.

The divine presence "descending" is understood in Islam as Allah's ongoing guidance and care, not as spatial inhabitation. Allah's relationship with His creation is one of knowledge, power, and mercy that transcends physical presence. The completion of the Tabernacle marks the completion of a phase in divine history — the establishment of Bani Isra'il as a worshiping community under the guidance of Musa. The story continues in the Quran, which inherits and reinterprets this legacy as part of the universal narrative of tawhid that culminates in the final message of Islam.
