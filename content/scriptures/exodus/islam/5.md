## Islamic Perspective

Exodus 5 records Musa and Harun's first audience with Fir'awn and his dismissive refusal to release Bani Isra'il. The Quran presents Fir'awn as the archetype of tyrannical arrogance (istikbar) and the quintessential taghut — a false claimant to divine authority. His rejection of Musa's message is not merely political stubbornness but a theological rebellion against the sovereignty of Allah, making this encounter a paradigm of the eternal conflict between truth (haqq) and falsehood (batil).

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran recounts Fir'awn's rejection in multiple passages:

- **Al-A'raf 7:103-104**: "Then after them We sent Musa with Our signs to Fir'awn and his establishment, but they were unjust toward them. So see how was the end of the corrupters. And Musa said, 'O Fir'awn, I am a messenger from the Lord of the worlds.'"
- **Taha 20:49-56**: Fir'awn challenges Musa, asking "Who is your Lord?" Musa responds with a declaration of Allah's universal lordship. Fir'awn then accuses Musa of sorcery and threatens him.
- **Ash-Shu'ara 26:16-29**: "'Go to Fir'awn and say, "We are the messengers of the Lord of the worlds. Send with us the Children of Israel."' Fir'awn responds by reminding Musa of being raised in his palace and accusing him of ingratitude. When Musa declares his Lord, Fir'awn says to those around him, 'Do you not hear?' and threatens imprisonment."
- **Az-Zukhruf 43:51-54**: "And Fir'awn called out among his people and said, 'O my people, does not the kingdom of Misr belong to me, and these rivers flowing beneath me? Then do you not see?'"

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

The biblical account focuses on the practical consequence of Fir'awn's refusal — the increased burden of making bricks without straw. While Islamic tradition acknowledges the intensified oppression, the Quran emphasizes the theological dimensions of the confrontation. The central question is not labor policy but sovereignty: who is the true Lord?

The Quran records Fir'awn's claim to divinity more explicitly than the biblical text. In An-Nazi'at 79:24, Fir'awn declares "I am your lord, most high" (ana rabbukum al-a'la), a blasphemous claim that stands as the opposite of tawhid. This theological framing elevates the narrative beyond a political liberation story to a cosmic drama of faith versus disbelief.

The complaint of the Israelite foremen to Musa (Exodus 5:20-21) finds a partial echo in the Quran (Al-A'raf 7:129), where Bani Isra'il say, "We have been harmed before you came to us and after you have come to us." Musa responds with patience and trust in Allah's plan.

## Classical Commentators

**Ibn Kathir** contextualizes Fir'awn's arrogance within the broader pattern of disbelieving rulers throughout history. He draws parallels between Fir'awn and other Quranic tyrants like Nimrud, who also claimed divinity and was confronted by a prophet (Ibrahim).

**Al-Tabari** records various accounts of the initial meeting, including traditions about the protocol of the Egyptian court and how Musa gained audience with Fir'awn. He notes that Fir'awn's rejection was rooted in pride and the fear of losing political power.

**Al-Qurtubi** extracts the principle that prophets must deliver their message regardless of the anticipated response. Musa's persistence despite Fir'awn's hostility exemplifies the prophetic mandate to convey the truth (tabligh) without compromise.

## Tawhid and Prophetic Themes

This chapter crystallizes the fundamental Islamic teaching that every society faces a choice between tawhid and taghut. Fir'awn represents the ultimate taghut — a human being who arrogates to himself the rights of Allah. Musa's mission to confront this false divinity parallels the mission of every prophet, culminating in Muhammad's confrontation with the polytheism of Quraysh. The prophetic infallibility of Musa is evident in his unwavering commitment to the divine message despite threats and the discouragement of his own people.
