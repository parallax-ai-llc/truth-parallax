## Objects of Worship

Exodus 37 describes Bezalel's construction of the Ark of the Covenant, the table of showbread, the golden lampstand, and the golden incense altar. Orthodox readers see the faithful creation of sacred furniture for God's dwelling. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge's Pleroma reproduced in gold and wood — static material copies of living spiritual realities.

### The Orthodox Reading

Bezalel crafts the Ark of acacia wood overlaid with pure gold, topped by the mercy seat with two cherubim of hammered gold facing each other with wings outstretched. The table of showbread, also gold-overlaid, with its plates, dishes, and bowls, stands ready for the bread of the Presence. The lampstand is hammered from a single piece of gold — seven branches with almond-blossom decorations. The golden incense altar completes the Holy Place's furniture. Each item fulfills the specifications given in chapters 25 and 30.

### The Gnostic Critique

The four objects constructed in this chapter correspond, in Valentinian cosmology, to four aspects of the Pleroma that the Demiurge has captured in material form:

The **Ark** — containing the tablets of the Law — represents the Demiurge's claim to house divine truth. But the "truth" it contains is stone inscription, not living gnosis. The cherubim atop the mercy seat face each other in perpetual stillness — a frozen tableau of the living, dynamic aeons of the Pleroma, reduced to hammered gold.

The **table of showbread** — always bearing twelve loaves — represents the sustenance of the divine realm. In the Pleroma, the aeons receive and radiate the Father's light in an unceasing exchange. The showbread imitates this exchange, but in material form: baked flour, replaced weekly, consumed by priests. Living exchange becomes scheduled rotation.

The **lampstand** — seven branches of gold with almond blossoms — imitates the Pleroma's sevenfold light. But the Pleroma's light is self-generating and inexhaustible. The menorah requires olive oil, wicks, and priestly maintenance. It is dependent light pretending to be autonomous light.

The **incense altar** — burning sacred fragrance before the veil — creates a sensory approximation of the Pleroma's atmosphere. The *Gospel of Truth* describes the Pleroma as filled with "the fragrance of the Father." The incense altar mimics this fragrance with material smoke, producing an olfactory illusion of divine presence.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 37 constructs the Demiurge's imitation Pleroma in miniature. Each object captures a genuine Pleromic quality — truth, sustenance, light, fragrance — and renders it in matter. The result is beautiful, costly, and spiritually inert: a museum of the divine rather than its living presence.
