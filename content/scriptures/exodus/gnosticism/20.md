## The Demiurge's Commands

Exodus 20 contains the Ten Commandments — the Decalogue — the most famous legal code in Western civilization and the foundation of Judeo-Christian ethics. Orthodox readers encounter the moral will of God in its purest form. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge's self-revelation: a jealous, insecure deity who demands exclusive loyalty, regulates behavior through prohibition, and defines himself through negation ("you shall not").

### The Orthodox Reading

God speaks the Ten Commandments directly to Israel. The first four govern humanity's relationship with God (no other gods, no idols, no misuse of the Name, keep the Sabbath); the remaining six govern human relationships (honor parents, no murder, no adultery, no theft, no false witness, no coveting). The Decalogue expresses the natural moral law written on every human heart (Rom 2:15), articulated here in covenant form. Jesus affirms the commandments and deepens them in the Sermon on the Mount.

### The Gnostic Reading

The Decalogue is the Demiurge's constitution, and Gnostic readers analyze it with forensic attention.

**"I am the LORD your God... you shall have no other gods before me" (20:2–3).** The *Apocryphon of John* and the *Hypostasis of the Archons* both seize on the Demiurge's jealousy as self-incriminating. Why would the supreme God forbid other gods unless other gods existed — or unless he feared the discovery of a deity above him? "I am a jealous God" (20:5) is, for Gnostic readers, the confession of a being whose authority is not self-evident and must therefore be enforced by command.

**"You shall not make for yourself a carved image" (20:4).** The prohibition against images is read as the Demiurge's monopoly on representation. He alone defines the interface between the divine and the material — no other image, no other mediation, no other pathway. This forecloses the possibility of direct gnosis, which requires no image and no intermediary.

**"You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain" (20:7).** The protection of the divine name is the protection of the Demiurge's brand identity — consistent with a being who derives power from invocation and worship rather than from intrinsic, self-sustaining plenitude.

The *Testimony of Truth* directly criticizes the Mosaic commandments, arguing that external prohibitions ("you shall not") address behavior without transforming consciousness. Gnosis, by contrast, transforms the knower from within, making external commandments unnecessary. One who knows the Father does not murder, not because of a prohibition, but because the violence has been dissolved at its root.

| Orthodox Reading | Gnostic Reading |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Commandments express God's moral will | The Demiurge regulates behavior without transforming consciousness |
| "No other gods" = monotheistic truth | The Demiurge's jealousy reveals his insecurity |
| The Law is holy, just, and good (Rom 7:12) | The Law is the Demiurge's instrument of control |
| Decalogue as foundation of ethics | External prohibition vs. interior transformation |

### The People's Fear

The chapter ends with the people trembling and standing "far off" while Moses enters the "thick darkness where God was" (20:21). For Gnostic readers, this scene crystallizes the Demiurge's method: the masses are kept at a distance through fear, while a single mediator enters the darkness — the absence of true light — to receive the Demiurge's instructions. Genuine revelation would illuminate everyone; the Demiurge's revelation excludes and terrifies.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 20 is the chapter that most sharply divides orthodox and Gnostic readers. For orthodoxy, the Decalogue is God's gift of moral clarity. For Gnostic interpreters, it is the charter of archonic control — a system of external commandments that substitutes obedience for gnosis, fear for love, and prohibition for transformation.
