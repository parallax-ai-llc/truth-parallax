## The Altar Constructed

Exodus 38 describes the construction of the bronze altar of burnt offering, the bronze laver, the courtyard, and provides a detailed inventory of all metals used — gold, silver, and bronze tallied to the exact weight. Orthodox readers see the completion of the Tabernacle's outer structures and responsible stewardship of sacred resources. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge's killing apparatus constructed and his obsession with material accounting laid bare.

### The Orthodox Reading

The bronze altar — the site of all burnt offerings — is constructed according to specification. The laver, made from bronze mirrors donated by the women who served at the tent of meeting, provides water for priestly washing. The courtyard with its linen hangings and bronze pillars defines the sacred precinct. The chapter concludes with a meticulous inventory: 29 talents and 730 shekels of gold, 100 talents and 1,775 shekels of silver (from the census tax), and 70 talents and 2,400 shekels of bronze. The accounting demonstrates faithful stewardship and transparency.

### The Gnostic Critique

The bronze altar — now physically constructed — is the execution platform of the Demiurge's sacrificial system. Its bronze grating, its horns, its pots and shovels and basins are all instruments for processing animal death into the Demiurge's preferred form of worship. The *Testimony of Truth* condemns the entire sacrificial system as incompatible with a truly divine being, arguing that the slaughter of animals reveals a god who subsists on material life-force.

The laver made from women's bronze mirrors is a detail of particular interest. Mirrors — instruments of self-reflection, of seeing one's own image — are melted down and remade into a basin for ritual washing. In Gnostic terms, the capacity for self-knowledge (symbolized by the mirror) is destroyed and replaced with an instrument of external purification. Gnosis as self-reflection is sacrificed to the Demiurge's preference for ritual cleansing — knowing yourself gives way to washing yourself.

The inventory is the chapter's most revealing section. The Demiurge's accountant lists every talent and shekel of precious metal, tracking material wealth with obsessive precision. The *Gospel of Truth* describes the Father as "immeasurable" — beyond all counting, all quantification. The Demiurge, by contrast, measures everything. His religion is fundamentally auditable, reducible to a balance sheet. This quantitative mentality is the opposite of the Pleroma's qualitative fullness, where abundance is not counted because it is infinite.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 38 reveals the Demiurge as accountant and butcher — meticulously tracking every shekel of silver while constructing the apparatus for systematized animal slaughter. The material precision of the inventory and the utilitarian purpose of the altar embody the archonic reduction of worship to measurable, material transaction.
