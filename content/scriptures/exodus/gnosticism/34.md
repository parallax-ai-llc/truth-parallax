## The Law Renewed

Exodus 34 narrates the renewal of the covenant after the Golden Calf crisis. Moses ascends Sinai again, receives new tablets, and encounters God's self-description: "merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love... but who will by no means clear the guilty, visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children" (34:6–7). When Moses descends, his face shines, and he must veil it. Orthodox readers see divine mercy and covenant restoration. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge's self-contradictory nature and the veil as a symbol of concealed gnosis.

### The Orthodox Reading

God reveals his character in what Jewish tradition calls the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy (34:6–7) — the most comprehensive self-description of God in the Hebrew Bible. The covenant is renewed with fresh tablets and additional laws. Moses' shining face signifies his transformation through intimate contact with God. The veil he subsequently wears becomes, in Paul's reading (2 Cor 3:13–16), a symbol of the old covenant's partial concealment of glory.

### The Gnostic Reading

The Demiurge's self-description in 34:6–7 is, for Gnostic readers, a study in contradiction. He is "merciful and gracious" — and yet "will by no means clear the guilty," punishing children for their parents' sins to the third and fourth generation. Ptolemy's *Letter to Flora* uses precisely this kind of passage to argue that the Demiurge is an intermediate being — not evil, but inconsistent, combining justice with cruelty, mercy with multigenerational vengeance. The true Father, revealed in Christ, is purely good, without admixture.

The declaration "I am a jealous God" (34:14) — here repeated explicitly as God's very name — is the Demiurge's defining confession. The *Apocryphon of John* and the *Hypostasis of the Archons* both identify jealousy as the Demiurge's signature emotion, the proof of his inferiority. A being who is truly supreme has nothing to envy and no rival to fear. Jealousy is the emotion of insecurity, and the Demiurge wears it as his name.

Moses' shining face is the most positive element for Gnostic readers. Something in the encounter on Sinai has left a genuine mark — a luminosity that suggests contact with reality beyond the Demiurge. The *Gospel of Truth* describes those who receive gnosis as becoming "illuminated," radiating the light of the Pleroma. Moses' shining face may represent an authentic trace of this illumination, breaking through despite the Demiurge's filter.

The veil Moses places over his face is then deeply significant. Paul reads it as concealment of the old covenant's fading glory. Gnostic readers see it as the suppression of gnosis: the genuine illumination Moses received must be hidden from the people, who can bear only the Demiurge's mediated instruction, not the direct light. The veil protects the archonic system from the disruptive power of authentic spiritual experience.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 34 reveals the Demiurge's self-contradictory character — merciful and vengeful, gracious and jealous — while simultaneously hinting, through Moses' shining face, that something beyond the Demiurge's system has been glimpsed. The veil ensures that this glimpse remains private, contained, and subordinated to the renewed covenant's institutional demands.
