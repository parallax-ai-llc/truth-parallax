## Darkness Over the Land

Exodus 10 brings the plagues of locusts and darkness — the eighth and ninth plagues. The three days of palpable darkness over Egypt while "all the children of Israel had light in their dwellings" (10:23) is one of the most symbolically rich images in the entire Exodus narrative for Gnostic readers.

### The Orthodox Reading

Locusts devour what the hail spared, completing the destruction of Egypt's agricultural base. The three days of thick darkness demonstrate God's power over Ra, the sun-god at the center of Egyptian religion. The darkness is supernatural — a "darkness that can be felt" — yet Israel has light. God's people are distinguished from the nations by their relationship to divine illumination.

### The Gnostic Reading

The plague of darkness is the plague that most clearly reveals the Gnostic cosmos. In Sethian and Valentinian thought, the material world *is* darkness — the absence of the Pleroma's light. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the material cosmos as emerging from "darkness and water and matter," while the *Gospel of Truth* (Nag Hammadi Codex I,3) describes the condition of ignorance as "fog" and "darkness" in which beings stumble without knowing their origin.

When the Demiurge sends darkness over Egypt, he is, in effect, revealing the true nature of his own creation. For three days, the Egyptians experience what the material world actually is when stripped of its illusions: impenetrable darkness, isolation, paralysis. They cannot see one another, they cannot move — they are trapped in the pure condition of matter without the faintest illumination of gnosis.

That the Israelites have light "in their dwellings" introduces a distinction Gnostic readers interpret pneumatically. The light in Goshen is not physical illumination but the faint glow of the divine spark within the pneumatic seed of Israel. Even under the Demiurge's rule, those who carry the spark are never entirely in darkness. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 24) records Jesus saying: "There is light within a man of light, and he lights up the whole world. If he does not shine, he is darkness." The Israelites in Goshen carry this interior light.

The locusts, which consume every remaining green thing, represent the archonic principle of entropy — the material world consuming itself. Nothing in the Demiurge's creation sustains; everything is devoured. The locusts are not external to the system; they are its logical conclusion.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 10 presents, in Gnostic reading, the most direct symbolic representation of the fundamental human condition: darkness, consumption, and isolation — with a faint, persistent light surviving in those who carry the pneumatic spark. The Demiurge sends the darkness, not realizing that in doing so he reveals the truth about his own cosmos.
