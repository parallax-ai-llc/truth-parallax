## Property Under the Law

Exodus 22 continues the Book of the Covenant with laws concerning theft, property damage, restitution, sorcery, sexual offenses, and social obligations to the vulnerable. Orthodox readers see a comprehensive moral framework for a just society. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge's preoccupation with the material order — defining human worth in terms of property, livestock, and economic transaction.

### The Orthodox Reading

The laws regulate property crimes (theft of livestock, damage to fields, safekeeping of goods), sexual conduct (seduction, bestiality), social obligations (treatment of foreigners, widows, orphans, and the poor), and religious purity (prohibition of sorcery and sacrifice to other gods). The chapter reveals God's concern for economic justice and the protection of the vulnerable, establishing principles that anticipate the prophetic tradition's emphasis on social righteousness.

### The Gnostic Critique

Exodus 22 is, in many ways, the quintessential Demiurgic legislation — a chapter obsessed with material goods and their proper management. The opening verses detail the restitution rates for stolen oxen and sheep with mathematical precision (five oxen for one ox, four sheep for one sheep). The Demiurge's mind operates like a cosmic accountant, tracking material debts and credits with meticulous attention.

The prohibition of sorcery — "You shall not permit a sorceress to live" (22:18) — is significant for Gnostic readers. In the Gnostic framework, certain forms of spiritual practice that the Demiurge labels "sorcery" may represent unauthorized access to spiritual knowledge — gnosis pursued outside the Demiurge's approved channels. The Demiurge tolerates no spiritual practice he has not sanctioned, because any unsanctioned practice threatens to reveal the existence of powers and realities beyond his control.

The social laws regarding foreigners, widows, and orphans (22:21–24) present Gnostic readers with a genuine difficulty. These laws express authentic compassion, and the Demiurge's threat — "my wrath will burn, and I will kill you with the sword, and your wives shall become widows and your children fatherless" (22:24) — mixes ethical concern with characteristically archonic violence. Ptolemy's *Letter to Flora* addresses this directly: the Demiurge's law contains genuine moral insight because the Demiurge is not evil but intermediate — a lesser deity who grasps some truths imperfectly and enforces them through inadequate means.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 22 reveals the Demiurge as a being capable of genuine moral concern but incapable of expressing it without recourse to material calculation and violent enforcement. The laws protect the vulnerable, but they protect them within a system that reduces all value to property and all justice to compensation — never rising to the Pleroma's vision of beings who need no law because they have been transformed by knowledge.
