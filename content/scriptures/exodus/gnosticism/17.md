## Water from the Rock

Exodus 17 contains two episodes: Moses striking the rock at Horeb to produce water, and the battle against Amalek. Orthodox readers see divine provision and protection. Gnostic interpreters find in the water from the rock a powerful symbol of the divine spark hidden within matter, while the battle against Amalek reveals the Demiurge's characteristic resort to violence.

### The Orthodox Reading

Thirsting in the wilderness, Israel grumbles against Moses. God instructs Moses to strike the rock at Horeb, and water flows. Paul later reads this christologically: "the rock was Christ" (1 Cor 10:4). The Amalekite attack follows, and Israel prevails as long as Moses' arms are raised — a scene traditionally read as the power of prayer and intercession.

### The Gnostic Reading

Water from the rock is a symbol that transcends the Demiurge's usual operations. In Gnostic cosmology, the material world is fundamentally dry — lacking the living water of the Pleroma. Rock represents the densest, most inert form of matter. That water — the symbol of life and spirit — flows from within the rock suggests that the divine spark is hidden even in the most apparently dead and material substance. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 77) declares: "Split a piece of wood, and I am there. Lift up the stone, and you will find me there." The water from the rock at Horeb enacts this teaching.

Yet the water comes only when Moses strikes the rock with his staff — the same staff that became a serpent, the same instrument of archonic power. The Demiurge's method is always violent: he does not coax the water forth through gentleness or knowledge but commands it through impact. This is consistent with a deity who knows only command and coercion as modes of interaction with his creation.

The battle with Amalek shifts the chapter entirely into archonic territory. The Demiurge's people are attacked, and the Demiurge responds with warfare. Moses' raised arms — when they drop, Israel loses; when they are held up, Israel wins — reduce divine assistance to a mechanical ritual, a gestural magic that has nothing to do with interior transformation. The *Testimony of Truth* criticizes precisely this kind of external, performative religion, contrasting it with the interior knowledge that constitutes genuine salvation.

The chapter ends with God's declaration of perpetual war against Amalek "from generation to generation" (17:16) — the Demiurge committing himself and his people to endless violence, a cycle that gnosis alone can break.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 17 juxtaposes two modes of the sacred: the water from the rock, which hints at the Pleroma's presence within matter, and the battle with Amalek, which demonstrates the Demiurge's addiction to conflict. The pneumatic reader learns to distinguish between these two registers — seeking the hidden water while refusing the perpetual war.
