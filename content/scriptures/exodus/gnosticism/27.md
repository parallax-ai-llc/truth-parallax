## Altar of Sacrifice

Exodus 27 describes the bronze altar of burnt offering, the courtyard of the Tabernacle, and the provision of oil for the lampstand. Orthodox readers see the establishment of the sacrificial system that will mediate Israel's relationship with God. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge constructing the apparatus of blood religion — the mechanism through which he feeds on the material offerings of his subjects.

### The Orthodox Reading

The bronze altar, five cubits square and three cubits high, is the central feature of the Tabernacle courtyard. Here the burnt offerings, sin offerings, and peace offerings will be presented. The courtyard — enclosed by linen hangings on bronze pillars — defines the sacred space. Pure olive oil is to be provided for the lampstand, maintained by Aaron and his sons "from evening to morning before the LORD." The altar represents the meeting point between sinful humanity and the holy God, where atonement is made through substitutionary sacrifice.

### The Gnostic Critique

The altar of burnt offering is, for Gnostic readers, the most troubling element of the Tabernacle. It is a structure designed for killing — for the systematic slaughter of animals whose blood and burning flesh constitute the Demiurge's preferred form of worship. The *Testimony of Truth* asks with barely concealed contempt: what kind of God requires the smell of burning fat? What kind of deity is pleased by blood poured at the base of a bronze grating?

Several Gnostic texts associate the Demiurge and his archons with the consumption of material offerings. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the archons as beings who derive sustenance from the material world and its creatures. The sacrificial altar is the Demiurge's feeding station — the designated location where material life-force is converted into archonic nourishment through the medium of fire and blood.

The courtyard's linen boundaries define a zone of controlled access — another layer of the Demiurge's architecture of separation. Only priests may approach the altar; only designated animals may be offered; only prescribed rituals are acceptable. The spontaneous encounter with the divine — the hallmark of gnosis — is replaced by regulated, scheduled, mediated transaction.

The provision of oil for the lampstand — kept burning continuously — reveals the Demiurge's constant need for material fuel. The Pleroma's light is self-sustaining; it requires no oil, no wick, no human maintenance. The Demiurge's light is artificial and dependent, an ongoing expense that the people must supply.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 27 establishes the sacrificial infrastructure that will define Israelite worship for centuries. For Gnostic readers, this is the moment when the Demiurge's religion reveals its essence: a system of material transaction in which animals are killed, blood is poured, and fat is burned to sustain a deity who cannot sustain himself.
