## Material Offerings

Exodus 35 opens the construction narrative with the Sabbath commandment reiterated and the collection of materials for the Tabernacle. The people bring gold, silver, bronze, fine yarns, skins, wood, oil, spices, and gemstones — a vast outpouring of material wealth. Orthodox readers see joyful generosity in response to God's call. Gnostic interpreters see the Demiurge extracting material resources from his subjects to build his own dwelling.

### The Orthodox Reading

Moses conveys God's instructions to the assembled people. The Sabbath is reaffirmed. Then Moses invites freewill offerings: "Whoever is of a generous heart, let him bring the LORD's offering" (35:5). The people respond with overwhelming generosity, bringing more than enough for the construction. Bezalel and Oholiab are confirmed as the master craftsmen. The chapter celebrates the willing participation of the entire community in building God's dwelling.

### The Gnostic Critique

The Tabernacle construction section (chapters 35–40) is, for Gnostic readers, the most sustained demonstration of the Demiurge's priorities. Having liberated Israel from one form of forced labor (Egyptian brick-making), the Demiurge immediately redirects their labor toward a new construction project: his own house. The irony is unmistakable — the people who were enslaved to build Pharaoh's cities are now "voluntarily" contributing their wealth and labor to build the Demiurge's Tabernacle.

The voluntariness of the offerings deserves scrutiny. The Demiurge presents the contribution as "freewill" — yet the entire context is one of absolute dependence. Israel is in the wilderness, sustained entirely by the Demiurge's manna and water, guided by his pillars of cloud and fire. In such conditions, a "freewill offering" is as free as a tribute paid to a patron who controls one's food supply. The *Gospel of Philip* observes that the Demiurge's generosity always creates obligation — he gives in order to receive, provides in order to claim.

The materials themselves — gold plundered from Egypt, animal skins, acacia wood — are all products of the material world. The Demiurge builds with what he has: matter. The contrast with the Pleroma, which is constituted by emanations of light and consciousness, could not be starker. Every cubit of fine linen, every ring of gold, every jar of olive oil represents the Demiurge's reduction of worship to material transaction.

### Theological Significance

Exodus 35 inaugurates the final movement of the book: the actual construction of the Demiurge's dwelling. The joyful generosity that orthodox readers celebrate is, for Gnostic interpreters, the willing participation of captive souls in the construction of their own prison — a prison made beautiful, which is the most effective kind.
