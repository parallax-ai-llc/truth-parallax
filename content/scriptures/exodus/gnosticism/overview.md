## Overview

Gnostic interpretation of Exodus is deeply subversive. While the orthodox reading celebrates God's deliverance of Israel and the giving of the Law, Gnostic readers saw the Exodus God (Yahweh/Yaldabaoth) as the Demiurge — a figure more akin to Pharaoh than to the true, transcendent God. The Law given at Sinai is not liberation but a new form of bondage, binding souls to the material world through ritual and regulation.

## The Gnostic Inversion

| Orthodox Reading | Gnostic Reading |
|-----------------|----------------|
| God liberates Israel from slavery | The Demiurge transfers loyalty from one master to himself |
| The plagues show God's power | The archons war against each other |
| Sinai is divine revelation | The Law is the Demiurge's code of control |
| The Tabernacle is God's dwelling | Material worship traps the divine spark |
| Moses is God's servant | Moses serves the Demiurge (or is a partially awakened figure) |
| The Golden Calf is apostasy | The calf reveals the true nature of the Demiurge's religion |

## Key Gnostic Themes in Exodus

### The Law as Bondage
For Valentinian and Sethian Gnostics, the Mosaic law is not salvific but enslaving. True freedom comes through *gnosis* — interior knowledge of one's divine origin — not through external commandments.

### Moses as Ambiguous Figure
Some Gnostic texts treat Moses positively (as a pneumatic who glimpsed the Pleroma at the burning bush) while others view him as the Demiurge's agent. The *Testimony of Truth* criticizes Moses and the Law directly.

### Egypt as Allegory
In Gnostic reading, "Egypt" can represent the material world, and the Exodus can symbolize the soul's liberation from matter — but this liberation comes through gnosis, not through the Demiurge's commands.
