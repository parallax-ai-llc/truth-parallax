The birth and early life of Moses is one of the richest typological narratives in all of Scripture, and the Orthodox Church draws upon it extensively in her hymnography, iconography, and patristic commentary. Moses is the great lawgiver, the prophet par excellence of the Old Covenant, and the foremost type of Christ in the Hebrew Scriptures.

## The Ark of Bulrushes

St. Gregory of Nyssa sees the infant Moses placed in the basket upon the Nile as a figure of baptism. The child is given over to the waters of death — the same river that was meant to drown the Hebrew boys — yet he passes through unharmed. This is the pattern of Christian baptism: we descend into the waters that signify death, and we emerge alive, rescued by divine providence. The "ark" (*tebah*) of bulrushes is the same word used for Noah's ark, and the Fathers do not miss the connection. Both vessels carry the righteous through the flood of destruction into a new life.

Moses' mother Jochebed, who hides her son for three months and then entrusts him to God's care upon the waters, is honored in the Orthodox tradition as a figure of profound faith. St. Ephrem the Syrian compares her act to the faith of the Theotokos, who likewise entrusted her divine Son to the dangers of this world, knowing that God's purposes would prevail. The three months of concealment have been read by some Fathers as a figure of the three days Christ spent in the tomb — hidden from the world, yet destined for resurrection.

## The Women Who Save

Pharaoh's daughter, though a pagan, becomes an instrument of divine providence. The Fathers note God's characteristic irony: the very household that decreed the destruction of the Hebrew children becomes the household that raises the deliverer. St. John Chrysostom marvels at this reversal, calling it a sign that God "makes the plans of the wicked serve His own purposes." Miriam, Moses' sister, who watches over the basket and arranges for his own mother to nurse him, is celebrated in Orthodox tradition as a model of wisdom and courage in youth.

## Moses in Midian

The episode of Moses striking the Egyptian is treated with nuance by the Fathers. St. Gregory of Nyssa sees it as Moses' first imperfect attempt at justice — motivated by righteous anger but carried out through human violence rather than divine power. Moses must flee to Midian, where he spends forty years as a shepherd. This period of exile and obscurity is essential to his formation. St. Maximus the Confessor reads the Midian years as a period of *praxis* — the purification of the passions that must precede *theoria*, the vision of God. Moses cannot encounter the Burning Bush until he has been emptied of self-reliance and worldly ambition.

The well at Midian, where Moses meets Zipporah, recalls the betrothal-type scenes of the patriarchs — Isaac and Rebekah, Jacob and Rachel. The Fathers read these scenes as figures of Christ meeting His Bride, the Church, at the well of baptism. Moses' act of driving away the oppressive shepherds and watering the flock prefigures Christ's pastoral care for His people.

## Liturgical and Iconographic Tradition

Icons of the finding of Moses are well-attested in the Orthodox tradition and often appear in cycles depicting the life of the prophet. The image of the infant on the water, watched over by unseen divine care, speaks to the Orthodox conviction that God's providence is always at work, even when — especially when — the circumstances appear hopeless.

The chapter closes with a pivotal verse: "God heard their groaning, and God remembered His covenant." This "remembering" is not a lapse corrected but a theological declaration that the time of deliverance has arrived. In the liturgical theology of the Orthodox Church, divine *anamnesis* — remembering — is the very heart of the Eucharist. God's remembrance of His people is the ground of all hope.
