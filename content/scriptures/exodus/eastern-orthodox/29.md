Exodus 29 prescribes the elaborate rite for the consecration of Aaron and his sons as priests. The Orthodox Church reads this chapter as the divine institution of ordained ministry and finds in its rituals the prototypes of her own ordination and anointing practices.

## The Washing, Clothing, and Anointing

The consecration begins with washing — the priests are bathed in water before they can be clothed and anointed. St. John Chrysostom connects this directly to baptism, which precedes all other sacraments in the Christian life. One cannot be ordained, anointed, or admitted to the Eucharist without first passing through the waters of regeneration. The washing of the Levitical priests is the Old Testament witness to the necessity of baptismal purification.

The clothing follows the washing — Aaron is dressed in the sacred vestments described in chapter 28. The act of clothing the priest is itself a liturgical act, performed by Moses (the mediator) rather than by Aaron himself. In Orthodox ordination, the bishop clothes the newly ordained priest or deacon in the vestments of his office, enacting this same pattern. The priest does not clothe himself; he receives his vestments from the one who ordains him, signifying that the priesthood is a gift, not an achievement.

The anointing with oil consecrates Aaron as the high priest. The oil poured upon his head (Psalm 133:2) is a type of the Holy Spirit, who empowers the priest for his ministry. The Orthodox sacrament of Chrismation, in which the newly baptized is anointed with holy chrism on the forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, breast, hands, and feet, is the New Covenant fulfillment of this priestly anointing. Through Chrismation, every baptized Christian is made a sharer in the royal priesthood.

## The Three Sacrifices

The consecration involves three offerings: a bull as a sin offering, a ram as a burnt offering, and a second ram as the "ram of consecration." Each addresses a different aspect of the priestly relationship to God.

The sin offering acknowledges that even the priest is a sinner who needs atonement. St. Basil the Great emphasizes that the Orthodox priest does not stand above the congregation in moral perfection; he stands before God as one who himself needs mercy. The Liturgy's preparatory prayers (*proskomedia*) include the priest's confession of his own unworthiness: "No one who is bound by carnal desires and pleasures is worthy to approach or come near or minister to You..."

The burnt offering signifies complete self-dedication — the entire animal is consumed on the altar. The priest's life is to be wholly offered to God. The ram of consecration is unique: its blood is applied to the right ear, thumb, and big toe of each priest, signifying that the priest's hearing, working, and walking are all sanctified for divine service.

## The Wave Offering and the Priestly Portion

The breast and thigh of the consecration ram are set apart as the priestly portion — the share of the sacrifice that sustains the priest's physical life. The Fathers see in this the principle that those who serve the altar are to be sustained by the altar. St. Paul invokes this principle explicitly: "Those who preach the gospel should live from the gospel" (1 Corinthians 9:14). The Orthodox Church's practice of supporting her clergy through the offerings of the faithful is rooted in this ancient provision.

## "I Will Dwell Among the Children of Israel"

The chapter culminates in one of the most magnificent divine promises in Scripture: "I will dwell among the children of Israel and will be their God. And they shall know that I am the LORD their God, who brought them up out of the land of Egypt, that I may dwell among them." The entire elaborate system of sacrifice, priesthood, and Tabernacle serves one purpose: to make possible the dwelling of God with His people.

St. Maximus the Confessor reads this as the ultimate goal of all creation — not merely that God should be worshipped from afar but that He should dwell within and among His creatures. This promise is fulfilled in the Incarnation, renewed in every Eucharist, and will be consummated in the age to come, when "the tabernacle of God is with men, and He will dwell with them" (Revelation 21:3).
