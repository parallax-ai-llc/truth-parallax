Exodus 38 records the construction of the bronze altar of burnt offering, the bronze laver, and the courtyard, as well as an accounting of the materials used. The Orthodox Church finds in these details important teachings on sacrifice, purification, stewardship, and the transparency that should characterize the community of faith.

## The Bronze Altar

The altar of burnt offering, constructed of acacia wood overlaid with bronze, stands at the center of the courtyard as the first and most visible element of the Tabernacle complex. Every worshipper who enters the sacred precinct encounters the altar before anything else. The Fathers consistently identify this altar with the Cross. St. John Chrysostom preaches that just as no one could approach the inner sanctuary without first passing the altar of sacrifice, so no one comes to God except through the Cross of Christ.

The bronze of the altar, unlike the gold of the inner furnishings, can withstand the heat of the perpetual fire. Bronze is a practical material — strong, durable, fire-resistant. The Fathers see in this a theological lesson: the approach to God begins with the hard realities of sacrifice and repentance, with the bronze of ascetic struggle, before the gold of contemplative glory is revealed. The outer life of *praxis* — fasting, prayer, almsgiving — is the bronze altar; the inner life of *theoria* is the golden sanctuary.

## The Bronze Laver

The laver, made from the bronze mirrors of the women who served at the entrance of the Tabernacle, is a detail rich with spiritual meaning. Mirrors — instruments of self-regard — are transformed into a vessel of purification. St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in this a figure of repentance: the person who once looked at themselves in vanity now turns that self-awareness toward self-examination and cleansing. The mirror becomes a laver; self-admiration becomes self-knowledge leading to humility.

The mention of the women who "served at the entrance" is significant. These women, sometimes identified with a tradition of dedicated service later attested in 1 Samuel 2:22, represent the feminine contribution to the worship of God. The Orthodox Church honors the ministry of women in many forms — from the myrrh-bearing women at the tomb to the deaconesses of the early Church to the monastic mothers of the desert tradition.

The laver's placement between the altar and the Tabernacle entrance establishes the sequence: sacrifice, then purification, then entry into the presence. The Orthodox preparation for the Eucharist follows exactly this pattern — the offering of self in repentance (altar), the cleansing of confession (laver), and then admission to the holy mysteries (sanctuary).

## The Courtyard

The courtyard, enclosed by fine linen curtains on pillars, creates the outermost boundary of sacred space. It is open to all Israelites, the space of common worship where the people gather to offer their sacrifices and receive God's blessing. The Orthodox nave, where the faithful stand during the Liturgy, corresponds to this courtyard. It is the space of the assembled Church — not yet the inner sanctuary, but already within the sacred precinct, already participating in the divine worship.

## The Accounting of Materials

The chapter concludes with a precise inventory: 29 talents and 730 shekels of gold, 100 talents and 1,775 shekels of silver, 70 talents and 2,400 shekels of bronze. This detailed accounting may seem mundane, but the Fathers read it as an expression of two important principles.

First, transparency: the community's offerings are publicly accounted for. The resources given to God are not to be handled carelessly or secretly. St. Basil the Great, who managed significant ecclesiastical resources, insisted on careful accounting and public accountability in the administration of Church property. The Orthodox canonical tradition requires bishops and clergy to be responsible stewards of the community's material gifts.

Second, the sheer quantity of material testifies to the generosity of the people. The community that had recently been slaves possessed this wealth because of the "plundering of Egypt" — the gold and silver given to them by their Egyptian neighbors. What was taken from the kingdom of bondage is now consecrated to the kingdom of God. St. Gregory the Theologian sees in this the transformation of worldly wealth into sacred offering — the proper use of material goods, which is not their accumulation but their consecration.
