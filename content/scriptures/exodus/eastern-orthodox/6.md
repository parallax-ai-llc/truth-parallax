In Exodus 6, God renews His commission to Moses with a fuller revelation of His name and purpose. The chapter includes a genealogy of Moses and Aaron, anchoring the narrative in concrete history. The Orthodox Church reads this chapter as a deepening of the divine self-disclosure that began at the Burning Bush.

## The Divine Name Revealed More Fully

God declares to Moses: "I am the LORD. I appeared to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob as God Almighty, but by My name LORD I was not known to them." The Fathers understand this not as a contradiction of Genesis but as a progressive revelation. The patriarchs knew God's power and faithfulness; now Moses receives a deeper knowledge of God's very being. St. Gregory the Theologian insists that divine self-revelation is always accommodated to human capacity — God reveals as much of Himself as the recipient can bear at any given moment.

This principle of divine accommodation (*synkatabasis*) is central to Orthodox theology. God does not overwhelm the creature but draws near gradually, preparing the human person for ever-deeper communion. The entire arc of salvation history, from the patriarchs through the prophets to the Incarnation, is a single movement of divine condescension, reaching its fullness when God Himself takes on human flesh.

## The Fourfold Promise

God makes four declarations using the phrase "I will": I will bring you out, I will rescue you, I will redeem you, I will take you as My people. The Fathers connect these four promises to the four cups of the Passover Seder and, beyond that, to the fourfold action of the Eucharistic liturgy — God takes, blesses, breaks, and gives. Each promise represents a stage in the movement from slavery to communion. St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in this fourfold structure the completeness of divine redemption: God does not merely remove the external oppressor but transforms the inner condition of His people, making them truly His own.

The final promise — "I will be your God, and you shall know that I am the LORD your God" — points toward theosis. The goal of liberation is not mere freedom from oppression but intimate knowledge of God, personal communion with the divine. This is the deepest longing of the human heart, and it is fulfilled not in the Exodus alone but in the Incarnation, when God becomes man so that man might become god, in the famous formulation of St. Athanasius.

## The Genealogy

The genealogical material in this chapter may seem like a dry interlude, but the Orthodox tradition reads it with theological eyes. The listing of the tribes of Reuben, Simeon, and especially Levi roots the narrative in real human history. Moses and Aaron are not mythological figures but concrete persons within a specific lineage. This matters because the Orthodox faith is an incarnational faith — salvation is not an abstract idea but an event that occurs in history, through particular persons, in particular places.

The mention of Amram and Jochebed, Moses' parents, connects back to the faith displayed in chapter 2. The genealogy also establishes Aaron's priestly lineage, which will become crucial when the Tabernacle worship is instituted. The Orthodox Church sees in the Levitical priesthood a type of the Christian priesthood — not identical to it, but pointing toward it.

## Moses' Continued Reluctance

Even after this magnificent divine revelation, Moses protests again: "I am of uncircumcised lips." His persistent sense of inadequacy is, paradoxically, part of what makes him suitable for the task. The Orthodox tradition values this quality. St. John Chrysostom notes that God's power is made perfect in weakness. The greatest instruments of divine purpose are those who know their own insufficiency, because they do not obstruct the work of grace with self-confidence. Moses' stammering speech will carry the word of God precisely because Moses knows the word is not his own.
