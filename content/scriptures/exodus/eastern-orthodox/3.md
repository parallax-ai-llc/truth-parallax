The Burning Bush is among the most theologically significant passages in the entire Old Testament for the Orthodox Church. It is a theophany — a manifestation of God — that reveals fundamental truths about the divine nature, the possibility of human encounter with God, and the mystery of the Incarnation. This chapter is central to Orthodox theology, hymnography, and iconography.

## The Uncreated Fire

The bush that burns without being consumed is the preeminent Old Testament image of the uncreated energies of God. St. Gregory Palamas, defending the hesychast tradition in the fourteenth century, appealed to this passage as evidence that God truly communicates Himself to creation without destroying or annihilating it. The fire is not a created fire — it is the divine light, the same light that shone on Mount Tabor at the Transfiguration. The bush participates in the divine energy yet retains its creaturely nature. This is the theological foundation of theosis: the human person can be filled with divine life without ceasing to be human, just as the bush burned without being consumed.

St. Gregory of Nyssa devotes extensive reflection to this scene in *The Life of Moses*. He reads the approach to the bush as the soul's ascent toward God. Moses must remove his sandals — shedding the "dead skins" of the passions and material attachments — before he can stand on holy ground. The holy ground itself signifies that wherever God reveals Himself, the very earth is sanctified. This is the theological basis of the Orthodox veneration of sacred places, relics, and icons: matter can be a vehicle of divine presence.

## The Theotokos and the Bush

Orthodox hymnography makes an explicit and profound connection between the Burning Bush and the Virgin Mary. The Theotokos received the fire of divinity in her womb yet was not consumed. The hymns of the Nativity and the Annunciation repeatedly invoke this image: "Rejoice, O Bush unburnt!" The icon of the Burning Bush (*Neopalimaya Kupina*) depicts the Theotokos surrounded by flames, holding the Christ Child — she is the living Bush through whom God appeared to the world in flesh. This icon is one of the most beloved in the Russian and Greek traditions.

## "I AM WHO I AM"

The divine name revealed to Moses — *Ehyeh asher Ehyeh*, rendered in the Septuagint as *Ego eimi ho On* ("I am the One Who Is") — is foundational to Orthodox theology proper. The Fathers understand this as a revelation of God's absolute being. God is not one being among others; He is Being itself, the ground of all existence. St. Basil the Great and St. Gregory the Theologian both reflect extensively on this name, insisting that it reveals both God's transcendence (He is beyond all categories) and His immanence (He is the source of every existing thing).

When Christ says "Before Abraham was, I AM" (John 8:58), the Orthodox Church hears an explicit identification with the God of the Burning Bush. The *ho On* ("the One Who Is") is inscribed in the cruciform halo of Christ in every Orthodox icon — a perpetual visual confession that the incarnate Lord is the same God who spoke to Moses from the flames.

## Liturgical Significance

Exodus 3 is read as a *parimia* at Vespers on major feasts of the Theotokos, including the Annunciation and the Dormition. The Paschal connections are also strong: the God who reveals Himself in uncreated fire on Horeb will lead His people through the Red Sea and meet them again on Sinai. The entire trajectory from the Bush to Sinai to Tabor to Pascha is a single arc of divine self-revelation, moving from type to fulfillment.

The command to Moses — "Go, I am sending you" — establishes the pattern of prophetic mission. God does not merely reveal Himself for contemplation; He sends His servants into the world to accomplish liberation. The Orthodox understanding of mission is rooted here: encounter with God must bear fruit in action, in the service of others' freedom.
