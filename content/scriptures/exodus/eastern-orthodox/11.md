This brief but intense chapter records the announcement of the tenth and final plague — the death of the firstborn. It stands as the hinge of the entire Exodus narrative, the moment before the decisive act of liberation. The Orthodox Church reads it with the full weight of Paschal theology.

## The Announcement of the Final Plague

God tells Moses that one more plague will come upon Egypt, after which Pharaoh will not merely permit the departure but will drive the Israelites out. The reversal is complete: the king who said "I do not know the LORD" will now be desperate to remove God's people from his land. St. John Chrysostom sees in this a universal pattern — the powers that oppose God ultimately defeat themselves. Their very resistance becomes the instrument of their undoing.

The death of the firstborn is the most terrible of the plagues, and the Fathers do not minimize its severity. St. Basil the Great acknowledges the anguish of this judgment while insisting that it must be read within the larger narrative of divine patience. Nine plagues have already been sent, each accompanied by the offer of repentance, each followed by Pharaoh's refusal. The final plague comes only after every avenue of mercy has been exhausted by human obstinacy.

## Typology of the Firstborn

Orthodox theology reads the death of Egypt's firstborn in light of a far greater sacrifice. God's own Firstborn — the Only-Begotten Son — will die to bring about the liberation not of one nation but of all humanity. The Paschal troparion declares: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life." The death that strikes Egypt's firstborn is answered by the death that the divine Firstborn freely accepts. The plague reveals the deadly seriousness of sin; the Cross reveals the infinite depth of divine love.

St. Ephrem the Syrian draws a further parallel: just as the firstborn of Egypt die at midnight, Christ descends to Hades at the midnight between Holy Friday and Pascha. The hour of greatest darkness becomes the hour of liberation. The Paschal Canon of St. John of Damascus exults: "Yesterday I was buried with Thee, O Christ; today I rise with Thee in Thy rising."

## The Plundering of Egypt

God instructs the Israelites to ask their Egyptian neighbors for silver and gold. The text notes that the LORD gave the people favor in the sight of the Egyptians. The Church Fathers, beginning with Origen and continued by St. Basil and St. Gregory the Theologian, developed the famous interpretation of the "plundering of Egypt" as a figure for the Christian appropriation of pagan learning. Just as Israel took the wealth of Egypt and later used it to construct the Tabernacle, so the Church takes what is true and beautiful in secular philosophy and culture and consecrates it to the service of God.

This principle has shaped the entire Orthodox intellectual tradition. The great Cappadocian Fathers were educated in the finest Greek schools; St. John of Damascus drew on Aristotelian philosophy in his theological synthesis; the iconographic tradition absorbed and transfigured the artistic techniques of the Hellenistic world. The gold of Egypt, in Orthodox hands, becomes the gold of the sanctuary.

## The Great Cry

Moses announces that at midnight a great cry will go up throughout Egypt — "such as has never been nor ever shall be again." This is the sound of a world breaking apart, the death-cry of an empire founded on oppression. The Orthodox liturgy echoes this language in the hymns of Holy Saturday, where the great silence of the tomb gives way to the triumphant shout of the Resurrection. The cry of Egypt is the cry of death; the cry of Pascha is the cry of life. Between them stands the Lamb, slain from the foundation of the world.

Moses departs from Pharaoh "in hot anger." The Fathers note that this is not sinful wrath but righteous indignation — the anger of one who has seen evil persist despite every divine appeal. Such anger, properly ordered, belongs to the prophetic vocation and to the life of every Christian who stands against injustice.
