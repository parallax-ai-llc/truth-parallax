Exodus 26 provides the detailed instructions for the Tabernacle's curtains, frames, and the great veil separating the Holy Place from the Holy of Holies. The Orthodox tradition reads every measurement and material as laden with spiritual meaning, understanding the Tabernacle as a divinely revealed icon of cosmic and ecclesial reality.

## The Curtains of the Tabernacle

The innermost curtains are of "fine woven linen and blue, purple, and scarlet thread, with artistic designs of cherubim." The Fathers note the symbolism of each color: blue for heaven, purple for royalty, scarlet for blood and sacrifice, white linen for purity. Together they form a vision of the whole divine economy — heavenly kingship manifested through sacrificial love in a world destined for purification. St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in the cherubim woven into the fabric a sign that the angelic and material orders are joined together in the worship of God.

The layers of curtains — linen, goats' hair, rams' skins dyed red, and an outer covering of tough hide — have been read by the Fathers as figures of the different levels of scriptural meaning. The outer covering is rough and unattractive, like the literal surface of the text; the inner curtains are beautiful, like the spiritual meaning revealed to those who enter deeply into the Word. St. Gregory of Nyssa applies this principle throughout his *Life of Moses*, insisting that the beauty of Scripture is hidden beneath its plain exterior, just as the glory of God was hidden within the tent.

## The Frames and Bars

The structural framework of acacia wood overlaid with gold provides stability and form. St. Maximus the Confessor sees in the wooden frames a figure of the saints, who are the living supports of the Church. The gold overlay signifies the grace of God that covers and transforms human nature. The bars connecting the frames represent the bonds of love and communion that unite the members of the Body of Christ.

The precise measurements and proportions of the Tabernacle communicate an important Orthodox principle: sacred space is ordered space. The church building is not designed according to human whim but according to a pattern received from the tradition, which itself reflects the heavenly archetype. The orientation of the altar toward the east, the tripartite division of the building, the placement of icons — all follow a sacred geometry that dates back, in principle, to the pattern shown to Moses on the mountain.

## The Veil

The great veil (*parochet*) separating the Holy Place from the Holy of Holies is one of the most theologically significant elements of the Tabernacle. It is made of the same materials as the inner curtains — blue, purple, scarlet, and fine linen, with cherubim woven into it. The veil represents the boundary between the created and the uncreated, between the visible and the invisible, between God's general presence in the world and His specific, concentrated presence in the innermost sanctuary.

The Epistle to the Hebrews identifies the veil with the flesh of Christ: "through the veil, that is, His flesh" (Hebrews 10:20). At the moment of Christ's death on the Cross, the Temple veil was torn from top to bottom (Matthew 27:51). The Orthodox Church reads this as the decisive act of revelation — the barrier between God and humanity is removed, not from below (by human effort) but from above (by divine initiative). Access to the Holy of Holies — to the very presence of God — is now open to all through Christ.

The Orthodox iconostasis, the icon screen that separates the nave from the sanctuary, is the liturgical descendant of the Tabernacle veil. It is not a barrier but a threshold — the icons upon it depict the saints and angels who stand at the boundary between earth and heaven, welcoming the faithful into the divine presence. The Royal Doors, opened during the Liturgy, enact the tearing of the veil, the opening of heaven that Christ accomplished on the Cross.

## The Church as Tabernacle

St. Maximus the Confessor, in his *Mystagogia*, develops the theology of the church building as a living Tabernacle. The sanctuary is the Holy of Holies, where the Eucharistic mystery is accomplished; the nave is the Holy Place, where the faithful offer their prayers; the narthex is the court, where catechumens and penitents stand. The entire building is an image of the cosmos, oriented toward God and filled with His presence.
