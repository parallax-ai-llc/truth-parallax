The gift of manna in the wilderness is one of the most important Eucharistic types in all of Scripture. The Orthodox Church reads this chapter as a direct anticipation of the heavenly bread given in the Divine Liturgy and as a lesson in radical dependence on divine providence.

## "What Is It?"

The word *manna* comes from the Hebrew *man hu* — "What is it?" The people do not recognize what God is giving them. St. John Chrysostom finds this deeply instructive: divine gifts often come in forms we do not expect or understand. The manna does not look like bread; it does not come through human labor; it appears in the morning dew like something from another world. The Eucharist shares this quality of mystery — what appears to be bread and wine is, in reality, the Body and Blood of Christ. The question "What is it?" is the perennial question of faith before the sacramental mysteries.

## Eucharistic Typology

Christ Himself establishes the connection between manna and the Eucharist in John 6: "Your fathers ate the manna in the wilderness, and are dead. This is the bread which comes down from heaven, that one may eat of it and not die." The manna sustained physical life temporarily; the Eucharistic bread imparts eternal life. St. Maximus the Confessor reads the manna as a figure of the divine *logoi* — the creative words of God that sustain all creation — and the Eucharist as the *Logos* Himself, the Word made flesh, given as food.

The Orthodox Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts, celebrated during Great Lent, carries particular resonance with this chapter. The faithful receive the Body of Christ consecrated at the previous Sunday's Liturgy — bread reserved and given later, as the manna was gathered each morning. The entire Lenten journey is a wilderness walk, and the Presanctified Gifts are the manna that sustains the faithful until Pascha.

## Daily Bread, Daily Trust

The instruction to gather only enough manna for one day — and the failure of those who tried to hoard it — teaches a profound lesson about trust. St. Basil the Great connects this directly to the Lord's Prayer: "Give us this day our daily bread." The Orthodox spiritual tradition insists on living in the present moment, trusting God for each day's provision rather than anxiously accumulating reserves. Hoarding is not merely an economic vice; it is a spiritual one, rooted in the refusal to trust divine providence.

The exception — a double portion on the sixth day, in preparation for the Sabbath — introduces the rhythm of preparation and rest that will be fully articulated in the Sabbath commandment. The Fathers see in this the liturgical principle: the days of labor are oriented toward the day of worship. All of life finds its purpose and culmination in the assembly before God.

## The Sabbath Rest

This chapter contains the first explicit Sabbath instruction in the Exodus narrative. The manna does not appear on the seventh day, and those who go out to gather find nothing. God builds rest into the very structure of provision. St. Gregory of Nyssa reads the Sabbath as an eschatological sign — a foretaste of the eternal rest in God that awaits the faithful. The Orthodox celebration of Sunday (the Lord's Day, the "eighth day" that transcends the weekly cycle) transforms the Sabbath rest into the joy of the Resurrection.

## The Jar of Manna

Moses instructs Aaron to preserve a jar of manna before the Lord as a perpetual memorial. This preserved manna, placed later in the Ark of the Covenant, is understood by the Fathers as a type of the reserved Eucharist. The Orthodox practice of reserving the Holy Gifts for the sick and for the Presanctified Liturgy echoes this ancient provision. The golden jar also prefigures the Theotokos, whom Orthodox hymnography calls the "golden vessel" containing the heavenly bread — Christ Himself.
