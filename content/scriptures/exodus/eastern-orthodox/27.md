Exodus 27 describes the bronze altar of burnt offering, the courtyard of the Tabernacle, and the perpetual lamp fueled by olive oil. These elements, situated in the outer areas of the sacred precinct, govern the approach to God and carry rich significance for Orthodox liturgical theology.

## The Altar of Burnt Offering

The altar, made of acacia wood and overlaid with bronze, stands in the courtyard as the first major object the worshipper encounters. It is the place of sacrifice — where the blood is shed, where the fire consumes the offering, where the sinner meets divine justice and mercy simultaneously. The Fathers read this altar as a type of the Cross, the true altar of sacrifice upon which Christ, the Lamb of God, offered Himself.

St. John Chrysostom draws out the paradox: the Cross, like the bronze altar, stands in the open, visible to all, yet it is the gateway to the hidden mysteries of salvation. One cannot enter the Holy Place without passing the altar; one cannot approach God without encountering the Cross. The Orthodox faithful venerate the Cross before every act of worship — making the sign of the Cross upon entering the church, kissing the Cross after the Liturgy — enacting the approach to God through sacrifice that this chapter establishes.

The fire on the altar, which Leviticus 6:13 commands must never go out, prefigures the perpetual offering of the Church. The Divine Liturgy, celebrated daily in monasteries and regularly in parishes, is the unceasing sacrifice of praise that answers the altar fire of the Tabernacle. St. Maximus the Confessor sees the altar fire as a figure of the Holy Spirit, who sustains the Church's worship and transforms the offerings of the faithful into the Body and Blood of Christ.

## The Court of the Tabernacle

The courtyard, enclosed by curtains of fine linen hung on pillars, creates a sacred boundary between the profane world and the holy precinct. The white linen curtains signify purity and separation — not the separation of exclusion but the separation of consecration. The court is open to all Israelites; it is the space of common worship, of public sacrifice, of communal gathering.

The Orthodox churchyard and narthex serve an analogous function. They are transitional spaces, marking the movement from the ordinary world into the sacred realm. The act of crossing the threshold of the church is never trivial in Orthodox practice — it is accompanied by prayer, by the sign of the Cross, by the awareness that one is entering the presence of God. The courtyard of the Tabernacle established this pattern.

The pillars of the court, set in bronze bases with silver hooks and bands, combine strength with beauty. The Fathers see in the pillars a figure of the apostles and teachers of the Church, who support the fabric of the community and define its boundaries. St. Ephrem the Syrian connects the silver and bronze to the dual nature of the Church's witness — precious and strong, beautiful and enduring.

## The Perpetual Lamp

"You shall command the children of Israel that they bring you pure oil of pressed olives for the light, to cause the lamp to burn continually." This perpetual light, tended by Aaron and his sons, is one of the most enduring elements of biblical worship. The Orthodox Church maintains this practice: the lamp before the altar, the lampada before the icons, the candles lit by the faithful — all are descendants of this commandment.

The oil signifies the Holy Spirit, who illuminates the Church and makes worship possible. The pressing of the olives — the suffering that produces the oil — points to the Cross, through which the light of the Resurrection is released into the world. St. Gregory the Theologian connects the perpetual lamp to the parable of the wise virgins, who kept their lamps trimmed and burning for the coming of the Bridegroom. The Orthodox faithful are called to maintain the inner lamp of prayer and watchfulness, fueled by the oil of the Spirit, burning continually in anticipation of Christ's return.

The instruction that the lamp shall burn "from evening until morning" has liturgical resonance. The Orthodox Vespers service, which begins the liturgical day at sunset, is the moment when the evening lamp is kindled — a daily reenactment of this ancient command, a confession that even in the darkness, the light of God's presence never fails.
