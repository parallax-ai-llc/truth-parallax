Exodus 7 marks the beginning of the ten plagues, the great contest between the God of Israel and the powers of Egypt. The Orthodox Church reads the plagues not merely as historical events but as a cosmic confrontation between the true God and the demonic forces that enslave humanity, with profound implications for the spiritual life.

## Moses as God to Pharaoh

God tells Moses: "I have made you as God to Pharaoh, and Aaron shall be your prophet." St. John Chrysostom reflects on the extraordinary nature of this commission. Moses does not become divine in essence — this would be blasphemy — but he is given divine authority in relation to Pharaoh. This is a figure of the deification (*theosis*) to which all Christians are called: not to become God by nature, but to participate in the divine energies, exercising divine authority over the passions and the powers of darkness. As St. Maximus the Confessor teaches, the human person is called to become by grace everything that God is by nature.

## The Hardening of Pharaoh's Heart

The statement that God "hardened Pharaoh's heart" has been a subject of extensive patristic reflection. The Orthodox Fathers consistently reject any interpretation that would make God the author of evil or that would deny human free will. St. John Chrysostom offers the classic Orthodox explanation: God's actions are like the sun, which by the same rays melts wax and hardens clay. The difference lies not in the sun but in the material. God's self-revelation, His signs and wonders, should have led Pharaoh to repentance. Instead, because of Pharaoh's own disposition, the same divine light that softens a humble heart only hardens a proud one.

St. Basil the Great adds that Scripture's attribution of the hardening to God follows a Hebraic idiom in which God is said to do what He permits. God does not override Pharaoh's freedom; He allows Pharaoh to experience the consequences of his own choices. This reading preserves both divine sovereignty and human moral responsibility — a balance that Orthodox theology insists upon maintaining.

## The Rod and the Serpent

Aaron's rod becoming a serpent and swallowing the serpents of Pharaoh's magicians is rich in patristic symbolism. The serpent recalls the fall of Genesis 3 — the instrument of humanity's original deception. That the rod of God devours the serpents of Egypt signifies the victory of divine power over the demonic. St. Ephrem the Syrian sees here a prefiguration of the Cross, which appears to be folly and weakness (a mere wooden staff) but in reality destroys the power of the ancient serpent.

The fact that the Egyptian magicians can replicate some of the signs is significant. The Fathers note that demonic power is real but limited. It can imitate divine action but cannot match it. The discernment of spirits — distinguishing between genuine divine action and demonic counterfeits — is an essential discipline in the Orthodox spiritual tradition, discussed at length in the *Philokalia*.

## The Nile Turned to Blood

The first plague strikes at the very heart of Egyptian civilization. The Nile was worshipped as a god; now it becomes a source of death. St. Gregory of Nyssa interprets the plagues as a systematic dismantling of idolatry. Each plague targets something the Egyptians worship, demonstrating that these supposed gods are powerless before the true God. The water turned to blood also prefigures the Eucharistic mystery, though in an inverted sense: in Egypt, the life-giving water becomes death; in the Church, what appears ordinary becomes the Blood of Christ, the source of eternal life.

## Liturgical Context

The plagues are recalled throughout Holy Week, as the Church moves toward the celebration of Pascha. The contest between God and Pharaoh is the contest between Christ and death, and each plague is a blow struck against the kingdom of darkness, preparing for the final, decisive victory of the Resurrection.
