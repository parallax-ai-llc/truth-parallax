Exodus 21 begins the Book of the Covenant, the detailed legal code that applies the Decalogue's principles to the concrete circumstances of Israelite life. The laws on servants, personal injury, and property may seem distant from Christian worship, but the Orthodox Fathers find in them profound reflections on justice, human dignity, and the divine concern for the vulnerable.

## The Laws on Servitude

The regulations governing Hebrew servants are striking for their protections. A servant must be freed in the seventh year. A female servant has specific rights that her master cannot violate. A servant who chooses to remain with a beloved master may do so permanently, receiving the mark of a pierced ear at the doorpost. The Fathers read these provisions through the lens of Christ's own servanthood.

St. John Chrysostom sees the pierced ear at the doorpost as a type of Christ, the willing Servant who chose to remain with His people out of love and whose body was pierced on the Cross — the "doorpost" marked with blood at the first Passover. The servant who says "I love my master; I will not go out free" speaks the language of the saints, who choose divine service not from compulsion but from love.

The seventh-year release is connected to the Sabbath principle: just as the land rests every seventh year and time itself is marked by sabbatical rhythms, so human bondage has a divinely appointed limit. St. Basil the Great, in his homilies on social justice, drew on these laws to argue that all forms of human exploitation stand under divine judgment. The Exodus legislation does not endorse slavery; it regulates and limits a pre-existing institution, pressing relentlessly toward the liberation that finds its fulfillment in Christ's declaration that He came "to proclaim liberty to the captives."

## The Lex Talionis

"Eye for eye, tooth for tooth" — the law of proportional retaliation — has often been misunderstood as endorsing vengeance. The Fathers, however, read it as a principle of restraint. In a world where the powerful could inflict unlimited punishment on the weak, this law imposes strict proportionality: the punishment must fit the crime, no more. St. John Chrysostom notes that Christ does not abolish this principle but transcends it, calling His followers to forgiveness and the absorption of evil rather than its replication. The movement from "eye for eye" to "turn the other cheek" is not a contradiction but a progression — from the justice that limits evil to the love that overcomes it.

## The Sanctity of Life

The laws regarding murder, manslaughter, and accidental death reveal a profound concern for the sanctity of human life. The distinction between intentional and unintentional killing — with the provision of cities of refuge for the latter — shows a legal system shaped by theological anthropology. Every human being bears the image of God; therefore, every taking of human life demands an accounting. The Orthodox Church's consistent opposition to capital punishment, abortion, and euthanasia is rooted in this biblical conviction that human life belongs to God.

The specific mention of harm to a pregnant woman and her unborn child (verses 22-25) has been cited throughout Orthodox moral theology as evidence that the unborn child is a full person, protected by divine law. St. Basil the Great states unequivocally: "The woman who deliberately destroys a fetus is answerable for murder."

## The Concern for the Vulnerable

Throughout this chapter, the law pays special attention to those most susceptible to abuse — servants, women, the unborn, even animals (verse 28-36). The Orthodox social tradition draws on this pattern, insisting that the moral quality of a society is measured by how it treats its weakest members. These ancient laws, read in the light of the Gospel, continue to challenge every form of exploitation and indifference to human suffering.
