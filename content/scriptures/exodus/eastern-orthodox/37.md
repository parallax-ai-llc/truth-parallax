Exodus 37 records Bezalel's construction of the Tabernacle's interior furnishings: the Ark of the Covenant, the Table of Showbread, the golden Menorah, and the Altar of Incense. Each object, fashioned with exquisite care under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit, carries layers of typological meaning in the Orthodox tradition.

## Bezalel Makes the Ark

"Bezalel made the ark" — the text credits the Spirit-filled artisan by name, honoring his craftsmanship as an act of worship. The Ark, overlaid inside and out with pure gold, with its golden mercy seat and cherubim with outstretched wings, is the supreme object of Israelite worship. St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in the gold covering the acacia wood a figure of Christ's divinity enveloping His humanity — two natures in one person, without confusion or separation.

The cherubim facing each other, their wings forming a canopy over the mercy seat, establish the pattern for Orthodox altar decoration. The *ciborium* (canopy over the altar) and the angels depicted in the apse or on the icon screen recall these cherubim. The Orthodox priest, standing at the altar during the Eucharist, stands in the position of the mercy seat — at the place where God meets His people, where heaven and earth intersect.

The Ark contained the tablets of the Law, Aaron's rod that budded, and a jar of manna (Hebrews 9:4). Each of these items finds its fulfillment in Christ: He is the living Torah, the resurrection and the life (symbolized by the rod), and the bread from heaven. The Theotokos, who contained Christ within her womb, is called the "New Ark" in Orthodox hymnography — she bore within herself the fullness of what the original Ark merely signified.

## The Table of Showbread

The table, also overlaid with gold, with its border and rings and carrying poles, held the twelve loaves of the bread of the Presence (*lechem hapanim*, literally "bread of the face"). Twelve loaves for twelve tribes, perpetually set before God — this is the most direct Old Testament prefiguration of the Eucharistic table. St. Maximus the Confessor identifies the showbread with the *logoi* of God's creative word, which sustain all things in being. The Eucharistic bread is the *Logos* Himself, the Word through whom all things were made, now given as food for the life of the world.

The Orthodox *prothesis* (table of preparation), where the Eucharistic bread is prepared before the Liturgy, corresponds to the Table of Showbread. The priest cuts and arranges the portions of the prosphora on the diskos, naming the living and the dead, placing each piece in its proper position — a liturgical act that mirrors the careful arrangement of the showbread.

## The Golden Menorah

The menorah, hammered from a single talent of pure gold with its seven branches, almond-blossom cups, and ornamental knobs, is one of the most iconic objects in biblical worship. The seven branches are read by the Fathers as a figure of the sevenfold gifts of the Holy Spirit (Isaiah 11:2). St. John of Damascus connects the menorah to the seven sacraments of the Church, each one a lamp illuminating a different aspect of the Christian life.

The almond blossoms decorating the branches carry their own symbolism. The almond is the first tree to blossom in spring — a sign of watchfulness and early awakening. The Hebrew word for almond (*shaqed*) is related to the word for "watchful" (*shoqed*), a connection made explicit in Jeremiah 1:11-12. The light of the menorah is the light of divine vigilance — God's watchful presence that never sleeps and never dims.

## The Altar of Incense

The small golden altar, with its horns and golden crown, stands before the veil as the nearest object to the Holy of Holies in the outer chamber. Its placement is liturgically significant: incense — prayer — ascends toward God from a position of intimate proximity but not yet full access. The Orthodox practice of censing moves from the altar outward through the church, extending the fragrance of the divine presence throughout the worshipping assembly.

St. Gregory the Theologian reflects on the unity of the Tabernacle furnishings: Ark, table, menorah, and incense altar together create a complete liturgical world — containing the Word of God, the bread of sustenance, the light of truth, and the prayer of the faithful. Every Orthodox church, with its Gospel book, altar table, lampadas, and censers, recapitulates this ancient arrangement.
