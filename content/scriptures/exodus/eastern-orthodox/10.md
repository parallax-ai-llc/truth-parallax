The eighth and ninth plagues — locusts and darkness — bring Egypt to the brink of total ruin. The Orthodox Church reads these final plagues before the Passover as images of the spiritual desolation that awaits those who persist in rejecting God, and as powerful symbols of the darkness that Christ came to dispel.

## The Plague of Locusts

The locusts devour everything the hail has left. Egypt's agricultural abundance, the source of her pride and her geopolitical power, is reduced to nothing. St. Gregory of Nyssa sees in this the inevitable endpoint of a life lived apart from God: every created good, no matter how abundant, is ultimately consumed. Only what is rooted in the eternal endures.

Pharaoh's own servants now plead with him: "Do you not yet know that Egypt is destroyed?" The court officials can see what their king cannot — or will not — acknowledge. St. John Chrysostom remarks that stubborn pride blinds the powerful more thoroughly than the humble. Those closest to power sometimes perceive its futility before the one who wields it. This dynamic plays out repeatedly in the history of the Church, where ordinary believers often grasp spiritual realities that elude the mighty.

Pharaoh offers another compromise: the men may go, but the women and children must remain. Moses refuses absolutely. The Orthodox reading insists that salvation is never partial. God does not liberate individuals in isolation; He saves families, communities, the whole people. The liturgical life of the Orthodox Church reflects this communal understanding of salvation — baptism is a corporate event, the Eucharist is shared, and even personal prayer is set within the framework of the Church's common worship.

## The Plague of Darkness

The ninth plague is the most symbolically rich of all for Orthodox theology. A "darkness that can be felt" covers Egypt for three days, while the Israelites have light in their dwellings. This is not mere absence of sunlight but a palpable, supernatural darkness — what the Fathers call the darkness of spiritual blindness made manifest in the physical world.

St. Gregory of Nyssa interprets this darkness in connection with his theology of divine darkness (*gnophos*). There is a paradox at work: God dwells in "thick darkness" on Sinai (Exodus 20:21), and yet He is also "the Father of lights." The darkness that afflicts Egypt is the darkness of those who have turned away from the light — it is punitive, isolating, terrifying. The darkness that Moses enters on Sinai is the darkness of divine mystery — it is revelatory, communal, and transformative. The same word, two opposite realities. The difference lies in the disposition of the heart.

The three days of darkness inevitably recall the three days of Christ's burial. St. Ephrem the Syrian draws the connection explicitly: Egypt sits in the darkness of death, while Israel enjoys light. On Holy Saturday, all creation sits in the darkness of the tomb, while the light of the Resurrection already burns within it. The Paschal vigil liturgy enacts this movement from darkness to light with extraordinary dramatic power — the faithful stand in a darkened church until the Paschal candle is brought forth, and the cry rings out: "Come, receive the light!"

## Pharaoh's Final Attempt at Compromise

Pharaoh now says the people may go — but their flocks and herds must remain behind. Moses' response is magnificent: "Not a hoof shall be left behind." The Fathers hear in this a confession that everything belongs to God. There can be no negotiation over what is offered to the Lord; He claims all of creation as His own. St. Maximus the Confessor connects this to the Orthodox understanding of the consecration of all creation: matter is not evil, and the whole material world is destined for transfiguration. To leave the animals behind would be to concede that some part of creation lies outside God's redemptive purposes.

Pharaoh's final words to Moses — "Get away from me! The day you see my face you shall die!" — are the last words of the old order. The next time Moses appears before Pharaoh, it will be with the announcement of the final plague, and Pharaoh's power will be broken forever.
