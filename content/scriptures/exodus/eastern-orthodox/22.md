Exodus 22 continues the Book of the Covenant with laws governing property, restitution, social responsibility, and the protection of the vulnerable. The Orthodox tradition reads these regulations as expressions of a divine order in which material goods serve human communion rather than dominating it.

## Property and Restitution

The detailed laws on theft and property damage establish a principle of restorative justice. A thief must repay double or more; if an animal is stolen and slaughtered, the restitution is fourfold or fivefold. The Fathers note that the purpose of these penalties is not merely punitive but restorative — the goal is the healing of the community, the repair of broken relationships. St. John Chrysostom connects this to the Orthodox understanding of repentance: genuine *metanoia* involves not merely confessing sin but making restitution, restoring what was taken, repairing what was damaged.

The case of Zacchaeus, who upon meeting Christ declared "I give half of my goods to the poor, and if I have defrauded anyone, I restore it fourfold" (Luke 19:8), directly echoes the Exodus restitution laws. The Gospel fulfills the Law not by abolishing the demand for justice but by empowering the sinner to exceed it through love.

## The Protection of the Vulnerable

The chapter contains some of the most powerful social legislation in the ancient world. "You shall not mistreat a stranger or oppress him, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt." The memory of suffering is to be transformed into compassion for others who suffer. St. Basil the Great, in his homilies on wealth and poverty, draws heavily on these texts, arguing that the Christian who hoards wealth while others suffer is guilty of the same oppression that Egypt practiced against Israel.

"You shall not afflict any widow or fatherless child" — this is reinforced by a remarkable sanction: "If you afflict them in any way, and they cry at all to Me, I will surely hear their cry." God declares Himself the advocate of the defenseless. The Orthodox tradition takes this with utmost seriousness. The care of widows and orphans is not optional charity but a matter of divine justice. St. John Chrysostom thunders in his homilies that God will hold the wealthy accountable for the suffering of the poor, and that the riches of the Church exist for one purpose: to serve those in need.

## The Prohibition of Usury

"If you lend money to any of My people who are poor among you, you shall not be like a moneylender to him; you shall not charge him interest." The Orthodox Church has historically maintained a strong tradition against usury, rooted in this and similar texts. St. Basil the Great devotes an entire homily to the evils of lending at interest, calling it a form of legalized exploitation that preys upon desperation. While the modern economic system complicates the direct application of this law, the underlying principle — that profit should never come at the expense of the poor — remains a cornerstone of Orthodox social teaching.

## The Cloak as Pledge

The provision that a poor man's cloak, taken as a pledge, must be returned before sunset because "it is his only covering" reveals the deeply personal character of biblical law. God is concerned not with abstract legal principles but with the concrete suffering of real persons. "When he cries to Me, I will hear, for I am gracious." St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in this a revelation of divine tenderness — the God who thundered on Sinai is the same God who listens for the cry of a shivering man whose cloak has been taken.

## First Fruits and Holiness

The chapter closes with instructions on offering first fruits and on the holiness expected of God's people. The call to consecrate the firstborn and to offer the first produce of the land connects back to the theology of Exodus 13 — everything belongs to God, and the offering of the first portion acknowledges this reality. The Orthodox practice of blessing first fruits at the Transfiguration (August 6) and offering them at the altar continues this ancient pattern.
