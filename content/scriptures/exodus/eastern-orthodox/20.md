The Ten Commandments — the Decalogue — are the foundational moral revelation of the Old Testament. The Orthodox Church reads them not as an arbitrary set of rules but as a description of the life that flows naturally from communion with God. They are, in St. Maximus the Confessor's phrase, the divine *logoi* of human existence — the principles according to which human life was designed to function.

## "I Am the LORD Your God"

The Decalogue begins not with a command but with a declaration of identity and a reminder of grace: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage." Before any imperative comes the indicative — God has already acted in love. The commandments are a response to grace, not a condition for receiving it. St. John Chrysostom emphasizes this order: God does not say "Obey me and I will free you" but "I have freed you; now live as free people." The Orthodox understanding of the moral life is grounded in this sequence: ethics flows from salvation, not the reverse.

## The First Commandment and Idolatry

"You shall have no other gods before Me" addresses not merely the worship of pagan deities but every form of idolatry — the elevation of any created thing to the place that belongs to God alone. St. Basil the Great warns that wealth, power, pleasure, and even theological knowledge can become idols when they occupy the center of the heart. The commandment calls for a radical reorientation of desire toward the One who alone can satisfy it.

## The Second Commandment and the Question of Icons

"You shall not make for yourself a carved image" has been the subject of the most consequential theological debate in Orthodox history — the Iconoclastic Controversy of the eighth and ninth centuries. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787) definitively settled the matter: the commandment forbids the worship of idols — images of false gods or images treated as gods — but it does not forbid the veneration of holy images of the true God who has made Himself visible in the Incarnation.

St. John of Damascus, the great defender of icons, argued that the prohibition against images applied before the Incarnation, when God had no visible form. But now that God has taken flesh, become visible, tangible, and depictable, to refuse to depict Him is to deny the Incarnation itself. The icon is not an idol but a window into the divine reality, a visible confession that God has truly entered the material world. The Orthodox Church venerates icons not as objects of worship (*latreia*, which belongs to God alone) but as objects of honor (*proskynesis*), directed through the image to the person depicted.

## The Sabbath Commandment

The commandment to keep the Sabbath holy carries eschatological significance in Orthodox theology. The Sabbath is not merely a day of rest from labor but a participation in God's own rest after creation — and, beyond that, a foretaste of the eternal rest of the Kingdom. The Orthodox celebration of Sunday, the day of Resurrection, transforms the Sabbath into the "eighth day" — the day beyond the weekly cycle that inaugurates the new creation. St. Basil the Great writes that the eighth day is "the image of the age to come."

## The Commandments on Human Relations

The commandments governing human relationships — honoring parents, prohibiting murder, adultery, theft, false witness, and covetousness — are read by the Fathers as expressions of the divine image in human social life. To murder is to assault the image of God; to steal is to violate the communion of persons that God intends; to covet is to refuse gratitude for what God has given. St. Gregory the Theologian insists that the commandments are not restrictions on freedom but descriptions of what genuine freedom looks like — the freedom of persons living in right relationship with God and one another.

## The People's Fear

The chapter closes with the people trembling at the divine presence and asking Moses to mediate. The Fathers see in this the recognition that the Law alone cannot bridge the gap between God and humanity. A Mediator is needed — and the entire Old Testament will strain toward the One who is both God and man, who will stand not at the foot of Sinai but on Golgotha, mediating a new and eternal covenant.
