Exodus 39 details the completion of the priestly vestments and the final assembly of all the Tabernacle components, presented to Moses for inspection. The chapter's most striking feature is the sevenfold repetition of "as the LORD had commanded Moses," echoing the sevenfold divine speech of the creation narrative in Genesis 1.

## Vestments Fashioned in Obedience

The ephod, breastplate, robe, tunics, turban, and linen breeches are all fashioned exactly according to divine instruction. The artisans exercise extraordinary skill — setting precious stones, weaving gold thread into fabric, engraving the names of the twelve tribes — yet every creative act is governed by the pattern revealed on the mountain. The Orthodox tradition of liturgical vesture continues this principle: vestments are rich in beauty and craftsmanship, yet they follow established forms that carry theological meaning.

The ephod of gold, blue, purple, and scarlet, with its shoulder stones bearing the names of Israel's twelve tribes, clothes the high priest as a representative of the entire community. When he enters the sanctuary, all Israel enters with him. St. John Chrysostom applies this to the Orthodox priest: when the celebrant passes through the Royal Doors into the sanctuary, the entire congregation enters mystically with him. The priest's vestments are not his personal adornment but the garments of the whole Church.

## The Breastplate and Its Stones

The twelve stones of the breastplate — sardius, topaz, emerald, turquoise, sapphire, diamond, jacinth, agate, amethyst, beryl, onyx, and jasper — each engraved with a tribal name, form a dazzling mosaic of color and light. The Fathers read the diversity of stones as a figure of the diversity of charisms within the Church. Each tribe (each member of the Body) has its own character, its own beauty, its own irreplaceable contribution, yet all are set together in a single frame of gold, held together by the love of God.

The book of Revelation draws on this imagery when describing the foundations of the heavenly Jerusalem (Revelation 21:19-20), using a similar list of precious stones. The high priest's breastplate is a portable image of the eschatological city — the community of the redeemed, radiant with divine glory, carried upon the heart of the great High Priest, Christ Himself.

## "As the LORD Had Commanded Moses"

The sevenfold repetition of this phrase in the chapter is not stylistic accident but theological design. It deliberately parallels the seven days of creation in Genesis 1, where each divine act is concluded with "and God saw that it was good." The construction of the Tabernacle is a new creation — the building of a microcosm where heaven and earth meet, where divine glory fills a space within the material world. St. Maximus the Confessor develops this at length: the Tabernacle is a model of the cosmos as God intends it — every element in its proper place, every creature fulfilling its purpose, the whole creation ordered toward the divine presence.

The Orthodox Divine Liturgy preserves this creation-language. The Eucharistic prayer recalls the creation of the world alongside the death and resurrection of Christ, recognizing that salvation is not the replacement of creation but its restoration and fulfillment. The Tabernacle, built in obedience to the divine word, is the first fruits of a redeemed creation.

## Moses' Blessing

"Then Moses looked over all the work, and indeed they had done it; as the LORD had commanded, just so they had done it. And Moses blessed them." The inspection and blessing parallel God's inspection of creation in Genesis 1:31: "God saw everything that He had made, and indeed it was very good." Moses, who received the pattern from God, now certifies that the pattern has been faithfully executed. The blessing that follows is both a priestly act and a prophetic confirmation: this work is pleasing to God.

The Orthodox practice of the bishop blessing and consecrating a new church follows this ancient pattern. The bishop inspects the building, the iconography, the furnishings; he certifies that they conform to the tradition; and he blesses the whole, setting it apart for divine worship. The consecration of a church is a reenactment of Moses' blessing of the completed Tabernacle — the recognition that a new sacred space has entered the world.
