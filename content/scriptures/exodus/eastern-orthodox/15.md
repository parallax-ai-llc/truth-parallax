The Song of Moses at the Red Sea is one of the great canticles of the Orthodox Church, incorporated into the daily cycle of worship and forming the first ode of the Paschal Canon. This chapter records the first great hymn of praise in Scripture and, in its closing verses, introduces the theme of testing in the wilderness.

## The Song of Moses

"I will sing to the LORD, for He has triumphed gloriously; the horse and its rider He has thrown into the sea." These opening words, chanted at every Paschal vigil, express the fundamental posture of the redeemed: thanksgiving and praise. St. John Chrysostom observes that Moses does not sing about himself or Israel's courage. The song is entirely directed toward God — "He has triumphed," "He has thrown," "He is my strength and my song." This is the pattern of all authentic Orthodox hymnography: the focus is always on what God has done, never on human accomplishment.

The canticle structure of Orthodox Matins (Orthros) is built upon the biblical canticles, and the Song of the Sea holds pride of place as the first ode. The irmos of each canon in the Octoechos takes its thematic starting point from this song, so that every day of the liturgical year begins, in a sense, at the shore of the Red Sea, celebrating divine deliverance. The Paschal Canon of St. John of Damascus transforms the Exodus imagery into Resurrection proclamation: "It is the day of Resurrection! Let us be illumined for the feast!"

## Miriam's Song

Miriam takes up the timbrel and leads the women in dance, singing: "Sing to the LORD, for He has triumphed gloriously." The Orthodox tradition honors Miriam as a prophetess and a leader of worship. Her dance is not secular entertainment but liturgical celebration — the bodily expression of spiritual joy. The Orthodox tradition retains this understanding of the body's participation in worship through prostrations, processions, the kissing of icons, and the physical movements of the Divine Liturgy.

St. Ephrem the Syrian, himself a master of hymnography, sees in Miriam a figure of the Church's choral tradition. The women singing at the sea prefigure the choirs that will fill Orthodox churches for centuries to come, carrying forward the song of salvation. Ephrem's own practice of composing hymns for women's choirs to sing was inspired, in part, by Miriam's example.

## Marah: The Bitter Waters Made Sweet

The narrative shifts abruptly from triumph to trial. Three days into the wilderness, the people find water — but it is bitter. They grumble against Moses, and God shows him a tree to cast into the water, making it sweet. The Fathers universally read the tree as a type of the Cross. St. Justin Martyr, followed by the entire patristic tradition, sees the wood that sweetens the bitter waters as the wood of Calvary that transforms the bitterness of death into the sweetness of eternal life.

St. Gregory of Nyssa draws the spiritual lesson: the soul that has just experienced a great deliverance (the Red Sea) must not expect the journey to consist entirely of such dramatic interventions. The daily life of faith involves long stretches of wilderness, and the water is often bitter. The Cross — carried daily, as Christ commands — is what transforms the bitterness of ordinary suffering into spiritual nourishment.

## Elim: The Oasis of Grace

The chapter ends at Elim, where there are twelve springs and seventy palm trees. The Fathers note the symbolic numbers: twelve for the tribes (and later the apostles), seventy for the elders (and later the seventy disciples sent out by Christ). Elim is an image of the Church — the place of refreshment and abundance where God's people rest before the next stage of the journey. St. Basil the Great connects the palm trees to the righteous who "flourish like the palm tree" (Psalm 92:12), and the springs to the living water that Christ promises to all who thirst.

The alternation between trial and refreshment, Marah and Elim, characterizes the entire spiritual life. The Orthodox tradition does not promise an easy journey but does promise that God provides resting places along the way.
