## God as Director of History

The chapter opens with God telling Moshe: "See, I have made you a god (*elohim*) to Pharaoh, and Aharon your brother will be your prophet" (7:1). Rashi explains that *elohim* here means "judge and authority" — Moshe would stand before Pharaoh not as a supplicant but as one invested with divine authority. The reversal is deliberate: Pharaoh who considered himself a god now faces one whom the true God has elevated above him. Sforno adds that this appointment was necessary to overcome Moshe's reluctance; he needed to understand that he was acting as God's instrument, not on his own authority.

## The Hardening of Pharaoh's Heart

The theological problem of *"va'ani aksheh et lev Par'oh"* — "I will harden Pharaoh's heart" (7:3) — occupies a central place in Jewish philosophical discussion. Rashi distinguishes between the first five plagues, where Pharaoh hardened his own heart, and the last five, where God hardened it. The initial choices were free; only after Pharaoh repeatedly chose defiance did God lock him into his trajectory as a demonstration of divine justice.

Rambam (Hilkhot Teshuvah 6:3) uses this as a paradigm: sometimes sin becomes its own punishment. When a person persists in wrongdoing, the capacity for repentance is itself taken away. This is not arbitrary cruelty but the natural consequence of moral choices — the soul becomes what it repeatedly does. Nachmanides disagrees, arguing that God restored Pharaoh's courage precisely so that his continued refusal would be genuinely free rather than coerced by fear of the plagues. Without the hardening, Pharaoh would have released Israel not from repentance but from terror.

## The Staff and the Serpent

When Aharon casts his staff before Pharaoh and it becomes a *tannin* (serpent or crocodile), the Egyptian magicians replicate the feat. But Aharon's staff swallows theirs (7:12). The Talmud (Shabbat 97a) emphasizes that the staff swallowed the others *after* it had returned to its wooden form — a miracle within a miracle. Shemot Rabbah 9:7 reads this as a prophetic preview: Egypt's power, impressive as it seemed, would be consumed entirely by the God of Israel.

The Midrash identifies the Egyptian magicians as Yannes and Yambres (referenced in the Talmud, Menachot 85a), who used genuine occult knowledge but operated within created forces rather than transcending them. The distinction between divine miracle and magical manipulation becomes a key theme: the magicians could imitate but never originate, reproduce effects but never exercise sovereignty over nature.

## The First Plague: Blood

The first plague strikes the Nile — the very river into which Hebrew infants had been cast. This measure-for-measure quality (*middah k'neged middah*) is a hallmark of rabbinic interpretation of the plagues. Shemot Rabbah 9:9 explains that Aharon, not Moshe, struck the Nile because Moshe owed a debt of gratitude to the river that had carried his basket to safety. The principle of *hakarat hatov* (recognizing the good) extends even to inanimate objects, teaching that ingratitude corrupts the soul.

The blood affected not only the Nile but all water in Egypt — in vessels of wood and stone (7:19). Rashi notes that Egyptians who purchased water from Israelites found it remained water in Israelite hands but turned to blood in Egyptian cups, an detail that both demonstrated God's precision and enriched the impoverished Israelites. Ibn Ezra adds that the transformation of water to blood was total and literal, not a natural phenomenon like red algae, because the Torah states the fish died — the change was substantive, not merely cosmetic.

The plague lasted seven days (7:25), during which the Egyptians dug around the Nile for groundwater. The Midrash observes that even in judgment, God left a narrow path to survival — a principle that informs the Jewish understanding of divine punishment as corrective rather than annihilating.
