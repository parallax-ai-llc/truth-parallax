## The Thirteen Attributes of Mercy

Chapter 34 contains what Jewish tradition considers the most important revelation about God's nature: the *Shelosh Esreh Middot HaRachamim* — the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy. As God passed before Moshe in the cleft of the rock, He proclaimed: *"Hashem, Hashem, El rachum v'chanun, erekh apayim v'rav chesed v'emet, notzer chesed la'alafim, nosei avon va'fesha v'chata'ah, v'nakeh"* (34:6-7).

The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 17b) declares: "God wrapped Himself in a tallit like a prayer leader and showed Moshe the order of prayer. He said: 'Whenever Israel sins, let them perform this order before Me, and I will forgive them.'" Rabbi Yehudah adds: "A covenant has been made regarding the Thirteen Attributes that they will not return empty-handed." This passage is the foundation of the *Selichot* liturgy — the penitential prayers recited before and during the High Holy Days and on all fast days.

Rashi enumerates the attributes: *Hashem* (compassion before sin), *Hashem* (compassion after sin and repentance), *El* (mighty in mercy), *rachum* (compassionate), *chanun* (gracious), *erekh apayim* (slow to anger), *rav chesed* (abundant in kindness), *emet* (truth), *notzer chesed la'alafim* (preserving kindness for thousands of generations), *nosei avon* (forgiving iniquity), *fesha* (forgiving willful transgression), *chata'ah* (forgiving inadvertent sin), *v'nakeh* (and cleansing). The precise enumeration varies among authorities — the Rambam, Rabbeinu Tam, and Abudraham each count slightly differently — but the theological consensus is uniform: God's default posture toward creation is mercy.

## The Theology of Mercy

The repetition of the Name — *Hashem, Hashem* — with a pause between them (indicated by the *etnachta* cantillation mark) is profoundly significant. The Talmud explains: "I am the same before a person sins and after a person sins and repents." God's compassion is not a reaction to human behavior but an intrinsic attribute. Teshuvah is possible because the divine nature includes the capacity to receive the penitent.

Nachmanides analyzes the progression: *chesed* (kindness) extends beyond what the person deserves; *emet* (truth) ensures that the kindness is not arbitrary but grounded in genuine relationship; the forgiveness of *avon*, *pesha*, and *chata'ah* covers the full spectrum of human failing. The final attribute, *v'nakeh*, is read by some as "and who cleanses [the repentant]" and by others as "but who does not cleanse [the impenitent]" — mercy is always available, but it must be sought.

## The Second Tablets

Moshe carved two new tablets of stone *"ka'rishonim"* (like the first, 34:1), and God inscribed the Ten Declarations upon them. Rashi notes a crucial difference: the first tablets were entirely God's work — both the stone and the writing. The second tablets required human participation: Moshe cut the stone, God wrote. The Talmud (Nedarim 38a) derives that the chips (*p'solet*) from Moshe's carving were sapphire, and God told him to keep them — this was the source of Moshe's wealth.

The second tablets are, in many ways, more precious than the first. They emerged from the ashes of sin and repentance, from the dialogue between divine anger and human intercession. The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 46:1) compares them to a wife who sinned, was forgiven, and rebuilt the marriage — the renewed covenant, tested by betrayal and restored by mercy, is deeper than the original.

## The Renewed Covenant

God establishes covenant conditions: prohibitions against making treaties with Canaanite peoples, against intermarriage, against idolatry (34:12-17). The festivals are restated — Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot — and the prohibition against cooking a kid in its mother's milk is repeated (34:26). Rashi explains the repetition of laws already given: after the sin, the covenant needed to be re-ratified from its foundations.

## Moshe's Radiant Face

When Moshe descended with the second tablets, *"karan or panav"* — the skin of his face radiated light (34:29). He did not know it. The people were afraid to approach, and thereafter Moshe wore a *masveh* (veil) except when speaking with God or transmitting His words (34:33-35). Rashi explains that the radiance came from the proximity to the divine presence during the forty days on Sinai. Ibn Ezra adds that the light was a permanent change, not a temporary effect.

The Sforno offers a striking interpretation: the radiance was itself a gift of the second tablets. The first tablets, given before the sin, did not produce this glow. The light that emerges from repentance and renewed covenant surpasses the light of original innocence — *"gadol ha'or ha'yotzei min ha'choshekh"* — the light that emerges from darkness is greater.
