## Water from the Rock at Massah and Merivah

The people's thirst at Refidim leads to a crisis so severe that Moshe fears for his life: *"Od me'at u'sekaluni"* — "A little more and they will stone me" (17:4). God instructs Moshe to strike the rock at Chorev with his staff, and water flows forth (17:6). The place is named *Massah u'Merivah* — "Testing and Quarreling" — because the people tested God, saying: *"Ha'yesh Hashem b'kirbeinu im ayin?"* — "Is the Lord among us or not?" (17:7).

Rashi observes that the elders accompanied Moshe to witness the miracle so they could testify that the water genuinely came from rock, not from a hidden spring. The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 26:2) adds that the rock traveled with Israel throughout the wilderness — this is "Miriam's Well" (*be'erah shel Miriam*), and when Miriam died (Bamidbar 20:1-2), the water ceased, confirming that it flowed in her merit.

The Talmud (Ta'anit 9a) teaches that three gifts sustained Israel in the wilderness: the manna in Moshe's merit, the clouds of glory in Aharon's merit, and the well in Miriam's merit. The juxtaposition of the water crisis with the manna episode teaches that faith must be renewed at every new challenge — yesterday's miracle does not automatically resolve today's anxiety.

## The War with Amalek

Amalek attacked the stragglers at the rear of the camp (17:8), targeting the weak and exhausted. Rashi, citing Tanchuma, describes Amalek as the paradigmatic enemy who attacks not from strength but from cruelty — preying on those whom others had left vulnerable. Devarim 25:18 adds that Amalek "did not fear God," confirming that the attack was motivated by pure malice rather than territorial dispute.

Moshe appointed Yehoshua to lead the battle — the first military action in Israel's history and Yehoshua's introduction as a leader. The Midrash explains that Moshe chose Yehoshua specifically because the battle against Amalek would span generations, and the younger man would need to internalize its significance for the future. Sforno notes that this moment begins the transition from Moshe's sole leadership to the development of the next generation of commanders.

## Moshe's Hands

The famous image of Moshe sitting on a stone with Aharon and Chur supporting his raised hands (17:11-12) generates one of the Mishnah's most important theological teachings. *"V'hayah ka'asher yarim Moshe yado v'gavar Yisrael"* — when Moshe raised his hands, Israel prevailed; when he lowered them, Amalek prevailed. The Mishnah (Rosh Hashanah 3:8) asks: "Do Moshe's hands make war or break war? Rather, when Israel looked upward and submitted their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they prevailed; when they did not, they fell."

This Mishnah establishes a principle that pervades Jewish thought: physical actions and rituals are not magical but serve to direct the heart. The staff, the raised hands, the supported arms — all function as focal points for the spiritual posture of the community. The battle is won not by the hands of one man but by the collective orientation of the people toward God.

## "Write This as a Memorial"

God commands Moshe: *"Ketov zot zikaron ba'sefer"* — "Write this as a memorial in the book" (17:14). This is the first explicit commandment to write within the Torah itself. The content to be recorded is God's declaration: *"Ki macho emcheh et zekher Amalek"* — "I will utterly blot out the memory of Amalek." The paradox is deliberate: the command to remember (*zakhor*) and the promise to erase (*macho emcheh*) exist in permanent tension. Israel remembers so that God may ultimately erase.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Melakhim 5:5) codifies the commandment to destroy Amalek as one of the 613 mitzvot, though its practical application has been limited since the identity of Amalek's descendants became uncertain. In Jewish thought, Amalek has come to represent the principle of gratuitous cruelty — the force that attacks the vulnerable not for gain but for the satisfaction of inflicting pain. The obligation to "remember" is the obligation to recognize and resist this principle in every generation.

Moshe built an altar and named it *"Hashem Nissi"* — "The Lord is my banner" (17:15). The Targum renders this as "the Lord performed a miracle for us." The altar is not a place of sacrifice but of testimony — a marker in the landscape declaring that victory belongs to God.
