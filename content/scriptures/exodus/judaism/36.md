## The Surplus and the Restraint

Chapter 36 opens with the remarkable scene of Moshe restraining the people from giving more: *"Vayikalei ha'am me'havi"* — "The people were restrained from bringing" (36:6). The Ohr HaChaim explains that the verb *vayikalei* is passive — the people had to be restrained, indicating that their desire to give continued even after the proclamation. This stands as an enduring model in Jewish thought: the ideal of generosity is not merely responding to need but having to be held back from excess.

Rashi notes that the craftsmen *"kol chakham lev b'osei ha'melakhah"* (all the wise-hearted who performed the work, 36:8) were distinct from the donors. The Torah honors both roles: those who give and those who build. The Midrash (Tanchuma, Vayakhel 1) teaches that wisdom in craftsmanship (*chokhmah*) is as sacred as wisdom in Torah when directed toward a holy purpose. The artisan who weaves a curtain for the Mishkan performs a spiritual act no less significant than the scholar who interprets a law.

## The Curtains and Coverings

The construction narrative parallels the instructions given in chapters 25-27 but now describes the actual execution. The ten inner curtains of blue, purple, crimson, and fine linen with woven keruvim (36:8-13) were joined in two sets of five, connected by loops and golden clasps (*korsot*). The fifty golden clasps that joined the two halves of the curtain system correspond, according to the Zohar, to the fifty gates of understanding (*chamishim sha'arei binah*) — the construction of the physical Mishkan mirrors the architecture of divine wisdom.

Over the inner curtains lay eleven curtains of goat hair (36:14-18), joined by fifty copper clasps. The Ramban observes the descending hierarchy of materials: gold clasps for the inner curtains, copper for the outer — reflecting the principle that the innermost layers of sanctity are the most precious, while the protective outer layers use sturdier, less precious materials. This mirrors the structure of holiness itself: the most sacred elements are protected by layers of lesser sanctity.

Above the goat-hair covering were two additional layers: ram skins dyed red and *tachash* skins (36:19). The identity of the *tachash* is debated: the Talmud (Shabbat 28b) describes it as a multicolored animal that existed only during the wilderness period, created specifically for this purpose and then becoming extinct. Whether this is literal or figurative, the teaching is clear: God provides exactly what is needed for sacred work, even creating new realities when necessary.

## The Boards and Bars

The wooden boards (*kerashim*) — each ten cubits tall, set in silver sockets — were erected precisely as commanded (36:20-34). The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 12:9) records that the boards were numbered so that each would always stand in its assigned position: the board on the north always faced north, the one on the south always faced south. The Talmud derives from this a principle of dignity: once an object has been elevated to a sacred function, it should not be demoted to a lesser one (*ma'alin ba'kodesh v'ein moridin*).

The connecting bars (*berichim*) threaded through the boards (36:31-34), with the middle bar miraculously running the full length of each wall. Rashi, citing the Talmud (Shabbat 98b), explains that this bar bent at the corners by itself — a miracle embedded in the construction process. The Mishkan was thus a blend of human skill and divine intervention, neither purely natural nor purely supernatural.

## The Parokhet and the Screen

The *parokhet* (dividing curtain) and the *masakh* (screen for the entrance) were woven as commanded (36:35-38). The parokhet separated the *Kodesh* (Holy) from the *Kodesh HaKodashim* (Holy of Holies), creating the gradient of sanctity that structured all worship. Sforno notes that the gradations — courtyard, Holy, Holy of Holies — correspond to the levels of perception available to different categories of worshippers: the general populace, the ordinary Kohanim, and the Kohen Gadol.

## Execution as Worship

The repeated phrase *"ka'asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe"* — "as the Lord commanded Moshe" — appears throughout these chapters with liturgical regularity. The Ramban counts this phrase and notes its prevalence: the builders followed the instructions exactly, without improvisation or innovation. In the construction of the Mishkan, obedience *was* creativity. The highest artistry consisted not in self-expression but in faithful reproduction of the divine blueprint.

This principle carries halakhic weight: the Rambam rules that the Temple vessels must conform precisely to the Torah's specifications. The Mishkan teaches that sacred architecture is revealed, not invented — a counterpoint to the assumption that human creativity is the highest value. In the realm of the sacred, the craftsman's genius lies in perfect execution of what has been shown.
