## Az Yashir: The Song of the Sea

*"Az yashir Moshe u'Vnei Yisrael"* — "Then Moshe and the children of Israel sang" (15:1). This is the *Shirah*, the Song of the Sea, one of the most exalted passages in the Torah, recited daily in *Shacharit* (the morning service) and chanted with a special melody (*ta'am*) during its public Torah reading on Shabbat Shirah and the seventh day of Pesach.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 91b) notes the future tense form *yashir* ("will sing") rather than the expected past tense *shar* ("sang"). Rabbi Meir derives from this a hint to the resurrection of the dead — Moshe and Israel *will* sing again in the world to come. The Mekhilta adds that the same future tense indicates that the song was spontaneous: the Holy Spirit rested upon the entire people, and they sang in unison, as if with one voice.

The Torah scroll writes this passage in a distinctive format known as *ariach al gabei leveina* — "a half-brick over a whole brick" — resembling the waves of the sea. This special layout, one of only two such passages in the Torah (the other being the song of Devorah in Shoftim 5), signals that the text has a poetic and liturgical character beyond ordinary narrative.

## Content of the Song

The Shirah praises God as a warrior — *"Hashem ish milchamah"* (15:3) — an image that Rashi is careful to qualify: God fights, but not as humans do. The Midrash Tanchuma explains that God appeared at the Sea as a young warrior and at Sinai as an elder sage, to demonstrate that the same God manifests differently according to the occasion.

The song describes the Egyptian forces sinking *"ka'even"* (like a stone, 15:5), *"ka'oferet"* (like lead, 15:10), and *"ka'kash"* (like straw, 15:7). Rashi explains this sequence reflects the varying moral quality of the Egyptians: the most wicked sank like straw, tossed about in agony; the moderately wicked sank like stone; and the best of them sank quickly like lead, with minimal suffering. Even in judgment, God calibrates the consequence to the individual.

## Miriam's Song

After the men's song, *"Vatikach Miriam ha'nevi'ah"* — "Miriam the prophetess took the timbrel" (15:20). She led the women in song and dance with instruments they had brought from Egypt. Rashi cites the Mekhilta: the righteous women of that generation were so confident of redemption that they prepared musical instruments in advance. Their faith surpassed even that of Moshe, who sang only after the event.

The Talmud (Megillah 14a) counts Miriam among the seven prophetesses of Israel. Shemot Rabbah 1:22 records her childhood prophecy that her mother would bear a son who would redeem Israel — a prophecy her father initially doubted but later confirmed. Her role at the Sea completes a trajectory: the girl who watched over the basket now leads the women's celebration of the fulfilled promise.

## Marah: Bitter Waters Made Sweet

The narrative shifts abruptly from triumph to crisis. Three days into the wilderness, the people find water at Marah but cannot drink it because it is bitter (15:23). They complain, and God instructs Moshe to cast a tree into the water, making it sweet (15:25). Rashi, citing the Mekhilta, identifies the tree as itself bitter — God heals bitterness with bitterness, teaching that divine remedy often works through paradox rather than simple opposition.

At Marah, God establishes *"chok u'mishpat"* — statutes and judgments (15:25). The Talmud (Sanhedrin 56b) identifies these as the laws of Shabbat, the red heifer, and civil law — commandments given before Sinai to begin the process of forming Israel into a law-governed community. Sforno explains that the transition from the Sea to Marah represents the essential movement from miraculous redemption to the discipline of daily halakhah: liberation is the beginning, not the end, of the covenantal life.

## God the Healer

The chapter contains the promise *"Ani Hashem rof'ekha"* — "I am the Lord your healer" (15:26). Rashi interprets this as: "I am the one who teaches you Torah and commandments so that you are spared from illness" — prevention through righteous living rather than cure after the fact. The Ramban, by contrast, reads it as a literal promise of divine healing, conditional on obedience.

The arrival at Eilim, with its twelve springs and seventy palm trees (15:27), is read by the Midrash as a sign of providential care: twelve springs for twelve tribes, seventy palms for seventy elders. After the bitterness of Marah, sweetness returns — the pattern of affliction followed by consolation that defines Israel's experience in the wilderness and, by extension, throughout history.
