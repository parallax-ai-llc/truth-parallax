## Sanctification of the Firstborn

The chapter opens with the commandment *"Kadesh li kol bekhor"* — "Sanctify to Me every firstborn" (13:2). Because God struck the Egyptian firstborn and spared the Israelite firstborn, the latter are consecrated to divine service. Rashi explains that this sanctification means the firstborn originally held the priestly role — a status later transferred to the Levites after the sin of the Golden Calf. The ceremony of *pidyon haben* (redemption of the firstborn), still practiced today, derives from this chapter: the father pays five *shekalim* to a Kohen to redeem his firstborn son from Temple service (Bamidbar 18:16), but the Torah source is here.

The Talmud (Bekhorot 46a) codifies the details: the redemption occurs on the thirty-first day of life, the father recites a blessing, and the Kohen accepts the redemption. Nachmanides explains the theology: by redeeming the firstborn, the family acknowledges that its continuation is not natural but owes a debt to God's mercy on the night of the Exodus. Every firstborn son is a living reminder of the tenth plague.

## Matzot and Remembrance

Moshe instructs the people regarding the festival of matzot and the prohibition of chametz for seven days (13:6-7). He introduces the principle of *v'higadeta l'vinkha* — "you shall tell your son" (13:8) — which the Haggadah identifies as the source for the narrative obligation of Seder night. This verse addresses the son who does not know how to ask; the parent must initiate the conversation without waiting to be prompted.

The formula *"ba'avur zeh asah Hashem li"* — "because of this, God did for me" (13:8) — is interpreted by the Talmud (Pesachim 116b) to mean that the story must be told when matzah and maror are placed before you. The demonstrative *zeh* ("this") requires a tangible referent: the telling is not abstract but embodied, connected to the physical symbols on the Seder plate.

## Tefillin

Twice in this chapter (13:9 and 13:16), the Torah uses language that the Oral Tradition interprets as the commandment of tefillin: *"v'hayah l'kha l'ot al yadkha u'l'zikaron bein einekha"* — "it shall be a sign upon your hand and a remembrance between your eyes." Rashi, following the Talmud (Menachot 34b), explains that these verses, along with two parallel passages in Devarim, constitute the four scriptural portions placed inside the tefillin boxes.

The connection between tefillin and the Exodus is intimate: every time a Jew binds tefillin, the parchments inside contain these very verses about the departure from Egypt. The physical act of binding the arm and head creates a daily reenactment of consecration — the body itself becomes a memorial to liberation. Sforno adds that the tefillin on the arm (opposite the heart) and on the head (seat of intellect) signify that both emotion and reason must be directed toward divine service.

## The Bones of Yosef

As the Israelites departed, Moshe personally carried the bones of Yosef (13:19), fulfilling the oath the brothers had sworn generations earlier (Bereishit 50:25). The Talmud (Sotah 13a) praises Moshe for this act: while the rest of Israel was occupied with collecting the gold and silver of Egypt, Moshe was engaged with the mitzvah of honoring the dead. The Midrash adds that Yosef's coffin traveled alongside the Ark of the Covenant throughout the wilderness, and when passersby asked about the two boxes, they were told: "This one fulfilled all that is written in this one" — the righteous man's life embodied the Torah before it was given.

## The Pillars of Cloud and Fire

God led the people by a pillar of cloud by day and a pillar of fire by night (13:21-22). The route chosen was not the shortest path through Philistine territory but a longer wilderness route, because God said *"pen yinachem ha'am"* — "lest the people reconsider when they see war and return to Egypt" (13:17). Rashi explains that God, as it were, anticipated human weakness and planned accordingly. Nachmanides draws a general principle: divine providence accounts for the psychological state of the people, not merely the physical logistics of the journey. Redemption proceeds at the pace the redeemed can sustain.

The cloud and fire never departed (13:22), providing constant visible evidence of God's presence. The Ramban notes that this continuous manifestation — unprecedented and unrepeated in human history — was necessary for a people just emerging from slavery, who needed to learn trust through sustained experience before they could internalize it as faith.
