## The Priestly Garments: Execution

Chapter 39 describes the actual fabrication of the priestly garments — the *ephod*, *choshen* (breastplate), *me'il* (robe), tunics, turbans, trousers, and sash — paralleling the instructions given in chapter 28. The repetition is not redundant but liturgical: the Torah recounts the making of each garment to emphasize that the artisans fulfilled every detail faithfully. The phrase *"ka'asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe"* ("as the Lord commanded Moshe") appears eighteen times in chapters 39-40, corresponding, according to some commentators, to the eighteen blessings of the *Amidah* prayer.

The Ramban offers a profound explanation for the dual account: the instructions (chapters 25-31) represent the divine blueprint — the ideal existing in God's mind — while the execution (chapters 35-39) represents the human realization of that ideal. The Torah honors both the vision and its fulfillment. In Jewish theology, the gap between intention and execution is bridged by faithful action — and the Torah records both to teach that neither alone is sufficient.

## The Ephod and the Stones

The *ephod* was woven of gold, blue, purple, crimson, and fine linen (39:2-7). The gold was hammered into thin sheets and cut into threads, then woven together with the other materials. Rashi marvels at the technical skill: weaving metal thread into fabric was an extraordinarily difficult art. The Talmud (Yoma 72a) notes that each thread of the priestly garments was composed of multiple strands — six threads of fabric twisted with one of gold — creating a textile of remarkable density and beauty.

The two *shoham* (onyx) stones on the shoulder straps, engraved with the names of the twelve tribes (39:6-7), were set in gold filigree. The Ramban explains that the engraving was done *"b'ma'aseh charash even"* — "by the work of a gem engraver" — comparable to the art of signet-ring cutting. The names were permanent, incised into the stone, symbolizing the permanence of God's remembrance of Israel.

## The Choshen

The breastplate (*choshen*) was a span square, doubled, containing twelve precious stones in four rows (39:8-21). Each stone was unique, each bore a tribal name, and together they constituted a complete map of Israel worn over the Kohen Gadol's heart. The Talmud (Yoma 73b) lists the stones and their tribes, though the exact identification of each gem is debated.

The *choshen* was bound to the *ephod* by gold chains and blue cord, ensuring it could not be separated from the garment (39:21). Rashi explains that the detachment of the choshen from the ephod was a Torah prohibition, not merely a practical concern. The Ramban reads this symbolically: judgment (*mishpat*, the choshen's designation) must never be separated from the broader service of God (*avodah*, represented by the ephod). Justice divorced from spiritual consciousness becomes merely technical; service divorced from justice becomes empty ritual.

## The Robe, the Tunics, and the Tzitz

The *me'il* (robe) of pure *techelet* with pomegranates and golden bells on its hem (39:22-26) was completed exactly as commanded. The alternating pattern — bell, pomegranate, bell, pomegranate — is analyzed in the Talmud (Zevachim 88b): the bells that make sound atone for the sin of speech (*lashon hara*); the pomegranates, which are silent and full, atone for silence when one should have spoken.

The *tzitz* (headplate) of pure gold, inscribed *"Kodesh la'Hashem"* (39:30), was attached to the turban with a blue cord. The Talmud (Sukkah 5a) discusses whether the inscription was in one line or two, and the Rambam rules it was in two lines. The inscription declared the Kohen Gadol's total consecration — every thought issuing from his mind was, in principle, dedicated to God.

## The Presentation to Moshe

The culmination of the chapter is deeply moving: *"Vayavi'u et ha'Mishkan el Moshe"* — "They brought the Mishkan to Moshe" (39:33). All the components — the tent, its furnishings, the garments, the vessels — were laid before him for inspection. Rashi explains that the people brought the components but could not erect the structure; its weight was beyond human strength, and God reserved the actual raising for Moshe.

When Moshe saw that the work was completed exactly as commanded, *"vayevarekh otam Moshe"* — "Moshe blessed them" (39:43). Rashi cites the Midrash: his blessing was *"Yehi ratzon she'tishreh Shekhinah b'ma'aseh yedeikhem"* — "May it be God's will that the *Shekhinah* dwell in the work of your hands." This blessing — that human labor might become a dwelling place for the Divine — captures the entire theology of the Mishkan. The construction was complete, but its purpose awaited the descent of God's presence.

The parallel to creation is explicit: just as God completed the work of creation and blessed the seventh day (Bereishit 2:1-3), Moshe completed his inspection and blessed the people. The Mishkan is a new creation — a world in miniature, built by human hands at divine command, awaiting the inhabitation of its Creator.
