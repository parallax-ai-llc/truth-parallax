## The Announcement of the Final Plague

Chapter 11, though only ten verses long, carries enormous theological weight. Moshe announces the tenth and decisive plague: at midnight (*ka'chatzot ha'lailah*), God will go forth through Egypt and every firstborn shall die (11:4-5). Rashi notes the unusual phrasing *ka'chatzot* ("about midnight") rather than *ba'chatzot* ("at midnight"). The Talmud (Berakhot 4a) explains that Moshe deliberately used the approximate term to prevent Pharaoh's astrologers from claiming a discrepancy if their clocks were slightly off. Moshe would not risk God's word appearing inaccurate due to Egyptian timekeeping errors.

The scope of the plague is total: from the firstborn of Pharaoh on his throne to the firstborn of the maidservant behind the millstone, and even the firstborn of the cattle (11:5). Shemot Rabbah 17:5 asks why the maidservant's child must suffer. The Midrash answers that the maidservants had rejoiced in the suffering of the Israelites, saying, "At least we are not the lowest anymore." Complicity in oppression — even passive complicity — brings consequences.

## The Great Cry

Moshe warns that *"v'hay'tah tze'akah gedolah b'chol eretz Mitzrayim"* — "there will be a great cry throughout all the land of Egypt, such as has never been nor will be again" (11:6). This cry mirrors and reverses the cry of the Israelites that had ascended to God (2:23). The Midrash observes the symmetry: those who ignored the screams of enslaved infants thrown into the Nile will now know what it means to lose children. The Torah does not present this as vindictive but as the outworking of cosmic justice — *middah k'neged middah* operating at the deepest level.

## Not a Dog Shall Bark

Against the horror of Egypt's cry, the Torah places a striking contrast: *"u'l'khol Bnei Yisrael lo yecheratz kelev l'shono"* — "but against all the children of Israel, not a dog shall sharpen its tongue" (11:7). Even the animals that normally howl at disturbance will be silent in Israel's neighborhoods. Rashi explains that this distinction demonstrates God's ability to differentiate between nations with surgical precision. The Talmud (Bava Kamma 60b) derives from this the teaching that when destructive forces are released into the world, they do not distinguish between righteous and wicked — but God does.

The Mekhilta adds that the dogs were later rewarded for their silence: the Torah commands that non-kosher meat (*treifah*) be cast to the dogs (22:30), a permanent acknowledgment of their role in the Exodus. This detail illustrates the Jewish principle that no good deed, however small and however performed by the humblest of creatures, goes unrecognized.

## Favor in the Eyes of Egypt

God had already told Moshe that Israel would not leave empty-handed (3:21-22), and now we learn that *"gam ha'ish Moshe gadol me'od b'eretz Mitzrayim"* — "Moshe himself was very great in the land of Egypt, in the eyes of Pharaoh's servants and in the eyes of the people" (11:3). Ibn Ezra notes that this respect was earned through the plagues — the Egyptians recognized Moshe as a genuine man of God, unlike the discredited magicians.

The silver, gold, and garments that the Israelites would receive are described using the language of *sha'al* — borrowing or requesting (11:2). The Talmud (Berakhot 9a) records that God specifically asked Israel to request these gifts so that the righteous Avraham would not complain: "You fulfilled the prophecy of slavery but not the prophecy of great wealth." The gifts constitute back wages for generations of unpaid labor — a concept the rabbis regarded as simple justice rather than divine favoritism.

## Moshe's Departure

The chapter ends with Moshe leaving Pharaoh *"b'chari af"* — in hot anger (11:8). Rashi clarifies that Moshe maintained the dignity of kingship even in his fury, not openly disrespecting Pharaoh but expressing righteous indignation at the suffering caused by his stubbornness. This detail models a principle in Jewish ethics: even when anger is justified, it must be channeled with dignity and restraint.
