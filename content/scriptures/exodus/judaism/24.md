## The Covenant Ceremony

Chapter 24 describes the formal ratification of the covenant between God and Israel — one of the most solemn and mysterious events in the Torah. Moshe wrote down all the words of the Lord (24:4), built an altar at the foot of the mountain with twelve pillars for the twelve tribes, and sent young men to offer burnt offerings and peace offerings. He took half the blood and placed it in basins, and the other half he sprinkled on the altar (24:5-6).

Rashi identifies the "young men" as the firstborn, who still served in the priestly role before the Levites replaced them. The blood ritual created a bond: half on the altar (representing God) and half sprinkled on the people (24:8), with the *Sefer HaBrit* (Book of the Covenant) read between the two acts. The Ramban explains that this bilateral blood ceremony was the formal mechanism of covenant-making in the ancient world — both parties were, as it were, bound by the same blood. The covenant at Sinai was not a unilateral decree but a mutual commitment.

## Na'aseh V'Nishma

When Moshe read the Book of the Covenant to the people, they responded with the immortal words: *"Kol asher diber Hashem na'aseh v'nishma"* — "All that the Lord has spoken, we will do and we will hear/understand" (24:7). The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) celebrates this response extravagantly: "When Israel said *na'aseh* before *nishma*, a heavenly voice declared, 'Who revealed this secret to My children, the secret that the ministering angels use?'" — for the angels, too, do God's will before fully comprehending it.

Rashi explains that *na'aseh* (we will do) precedes *nishma* (we will understand) because Israel committed to obedience before knowing all the details — an act of radical trust. This became the paradigm for the Jewish relationship with Torah: practice precedes full comprehension. One performs the mitzvot and, through their performance, comes to understand them. The doing *is* a form of hearing.

The Midrash (Tanchuma, Re'eh 1) adds that God held the mountain over the people like an inverted barrel (*kafah aleihem har k'gigit*), saying: "If you accept the Torah, good; if not, here will be your burial." This tradition seems to contradict the voluntary acceptance of *na'aseh v'nishma*. The Talmud reconciles them: the initial acceptance was voluntary, but the coercive image teaches that Torah acceptance has cosmic implications — it is not a lifestyle choice but a covenantal obligation with universal consequences.

## The Vision of God

Moshe, Aharon, Nadav, Avihu, and seventy elders ascended the mountain and *"vayechezu et ha'Elokim"* — "they saw God" (24:10). The Torah describes what was "under His feet" — *"k'ma'aseh livnat ha'sapir u'k'etzem ha'shamayim la'tohar"* — "like a work of sapphire brick and like the essence of heaven in purity" (24:10). Rashi, citing the Midrash, explains that the sapphire brick was before God during Israel's enslavement — a memorial of their suffering, as they had been forced to make bricks. Now, in the moment of covenant, the brick has been transfigured into sapphire, a gem of beauty. Suffering is not erased but transformed.

The Talmud (Berakhot 17a) discusses what it means to "see God" since the Torah elsewhere insists that no human can see God's face and live (33:20). Rambam resolves this by interpreting *vayechezu* as an intellectual apprehension, not visual perception. Ibn Ezra similarly reads the passage as describing a prophetic vision, not physical sight. Nachmanides offers a middle path: the elders saw a manifestation of the divine glory, not God's essence.

## Moshe Ascends for Forty Days

Moshe is called up the mountain for forty days and forty nights (24:18). He entrusted the leadership to Aharon and Chur (24:14), saying: "Whoever has a legal matter, let them approach them." The Talmud notes that this delegation proved fateful: in Moshe's absence, the people would turn to Aharon for the Golden Calf.

The cloud covered the mountain for six days, and on the seventh day God called to Moshe from within the cloud (24:16). Rashi explains that the six days of waiting were for purification — even Moshe required preparation before receiving the Torah. The appearance of God's glory was *"k'esh okhelet"* — "like a consuming fire" (24:17) — an image that teaches, according to the Midrash, that Torah study requires the willingness to be consumed by it, to surrender lesser attachments in pursuit of divine wisdom.
