## "I Am the Lord"

Parashat Va'eira opens with God's response to Moshe's anguished complaint: *"Ani Hashem"* — "I am the Lord" (6:2). God then makes a remarkable theological statement: "I appeared to Avraham, to Yitzchak, and to Yaakov as *El Shaddai*, but by My Name YHVH I was not known to them" (6:3). Rashi, following the Talmud, interprets this not as a statement about the patriarchs' ignorance of the Name but about the quality of God's interaction with them. The patriarchs received promises; this generation would witness fulfillment. *El Shaddai* is the God who promises; *YHVH* is the God who delivers.

Nachmanides offers a different reading: *El Shaddai* represents God's governance through nature — miraculous but within the created order — while *YHVH* represents direct supernatural intervention that overrides nature entirely. The plagues and the Exodus would manifest this higher level of divine engagement. Ibn Ezra takes yet another approach, suggesting the verse refers to the experiential dimension of the Name: the patriarchs invoked YHVH but never experienced the full revelation of what it means.

## The Four Expressions of Redemption

Verses 6-7 contain the famous four expressions of redemption: *"V'hotzeiti"* (I will bring you out), *"V'hitzalti"* (I will rescue you), *"V'ga'alti"* (I will redeem you), *"V'lakachti"* (I will take you to Me). These correspond, according to the Talmud Yerushalmi (Pesachim 10:1), to the four cups of wine at the Pesach Seder. Each expression marks a distinct stage: liberation from forced labor, physical rescue from Egypt, spiritual redemption through divine intervention, and the intimate covenantal bond established at Sinai.

Some authorities identify a fifth expression — *"V'heiveiti"* ("I will bring you to the land," 6:8) — corresponding to the Cup of Eliyahu at the Seder, which represents the future, as-yet-unfulfilled dimension of redemption. This dispute over four or five cups reflects a deeper theological question about whether redemption is complete in the present or still awaits its fullness.

## The People's Inability to Hear

When Moshe relayed God's promises, the people did not listen *"mi'kotzer ruach u'mei'avodah kashah"* — "because of shortness of spirit and hard labor" (6:9). Sforno interprets *kotzer ruach* as a spiritual condition: their spirits were so constricted by suffering that they could not expand to receive hope. This is not a moral failure but a psychological and spiritual reality that the Torah records with compassion.

The Kotzker Rebbe famously taught that *kotzer ruach* means they had no patience for a long process — they wanted immediate salvation or nothing at all. The capacity to endure a gradual redemption, with setbacks and delays, is itself a form of spiritual greatness that the people had not yet developed.

## The Genealogy

The chapter includes a genealogy (6:14-25) that appears to interrupt the narrative. Rashi explains its purpose: since Moshe and Aharon are about to be the central figures of the Exodus, the Torah traces their lineage to establish their identity and credentials. The genealogy begins with Reuven and Shimon before arriving at Levi, because skipping the older brothers would have been disrespectful.

Ibn Ezra notes that the genealogy also establishes the priestly and Levitical families that will serve in the Mishkan, connecting the Exodus to the worship that is its ultimate purpose. The listing of Korach's sons alongside Aharon's anticipates both the future rebellion and its resolution — even Korach's descendants will produce psalmists and sacred poets.

## Moshe's Protest Renewed

The chapter ends where it began, with Moshe protesting his inadequacy: "I am of uncircumcised lips" (6:30). The Hebrew metaphor *aral sefatayim* — literally "blocked lips" — recalls the concept of *orlah*, the foreskin that must be removed to reveal what is hidden beneath. Just as circumcision removes a covering to reveal the covenant, so Moshe's speech impediment will be overcome to reveal God's word. The rabbis see in this a principle: the vessel's imperfection does not diminish the message it carries.
