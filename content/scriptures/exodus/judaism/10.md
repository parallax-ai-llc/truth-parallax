## Parashat Bo: "Come to Pharaoh"

Chapter 10 opens Parashat Bo with the command *"Bo el Par'oh"* — "Come to Pharaoh" (10:1). The Zohar famously asks why the Torah says "come" (*bo*) rather than "go" (*lekh*), and answers that God was saying: "Come *with Me* to Pharaoh, for I am already there." The deepest chambers of evil are not beyond God's reach, and Moshe need not face Pharaoh alone. This mystical reading has become a cornerstone of Hasidic teaching about confronting darkness.

The purpose of the plagues is stated explicitly: *"u'l'ma'an tesaper b'oznei vinkha u'ven vinkha"* — "so that you may tell in the ears of your son and your grandson" (10:2). The verb *tesaper* shares a root with *Haggadah*, and this verse is one of the Torah foundations for the Seder night obligation to recount the Exodus. Nachmanides emphasizes that the plagues serve not only as punishment for Egypt but as pedagogy for Israel — each generation must internalize the lessons of divine sovereignty through the retelling.

## The Eighth Plague: Locusts

Pharaoh's own courtiers now beg him to relent: "Do you not yet know that Egypt is destroyed?" (10:7). Pharaoh offers a partial concession — the men may go — but Moshe insists that everyone, including children, must participate. Rashi explains that Moshe's demand — *"bi'n'areinu u'vizkeineinu nelekh"* ("with our youth and our elders we will go," 10:9) — establishes the principle that worship includes the entire community. There is no Judaism without the transmission to children.

The locust swarm was unprecedented: *"lo hayah khen arbeh kamohu"* (10:14). Shemot Rabbah 13:6 describes how the locusts devoured everything the hail had spared, leaving Egypt utterly barren. The Midrash adds a remarkable detail: the Israelites pickled and preserved the locusts for food on their journey, transforming Egypt's curse into Israel's sustenance.

## The Ninth Plague: Darkness

The plague of *choshekh* (darkness) lasted six days, divided into two phases. Rashi, following Shemot Rabbah 14:1-3, explains that during the first three days, the Egyptians could not see one another; during the last three, the darkness was so thick that they could not move from their positions. This was not merely the absence of light but a palpable, oppressive substance — the Midrash calls it the darkness of Gehinnom.

Why this plague? The Midrash offers a startling explanation: during the days of darkness, those Israelites who did not wish to leave Egypt died and were buried unseen by the Egyptians. Rashi records the tradition that four-fifths of the Israelites perished during this period. The darkness concealed this tragedy so that the Egyptians would not say, "They too are being punished." While this number is debated and some authorities reduce the proportion, the tradition conveys a sobering message: not all who dwell in exile desire redemption.

Sforno interprets the darkness differently: it was designed to show Pharaoh that God controls even the fundamental elements of creation. Light, the first thing God created, was now withdrawn — a partial reversal of creation itself. If the plagues demonstrate sovereignty over nature, this one goes furthest, touching the primordial fabric of the cosmos.

## Pharaoh's Final Negotiation

Pharaoh now concedes that families may go but demands the livestock remain behind (10:24). Moshe's response is absolute: *"Gam atah titein b'yadeinu zevachim v'olot"* — "You yourself will provide us with sacrifices" (10:25). Not only will Israel take its own animals; Pharaoh will contribute to the worship. Rashi explains Moshe's boldness: at this stage, the power dynamic has completely reversed. The redeemer now dictates terms to the oppressor.

The chapter concludes with Pharaoh's furious dismissal: "Get away from me! The day you see my face, you will die!" (10:28). Moshe agrees: "You have spoken correctly. I will not see your face again" (10:29). The irony, the rabbis note, is that Pharaoh himself will come to Moshe's door on the night of the tenth plague, begging Israel to leave — but Moshe will have spoken truly: he initiated no further meetings.
