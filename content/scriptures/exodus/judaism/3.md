## The Burning Bush

Moshe's encounter at the burning bush (*sneh*) stands as one of the most theologically charged moments in the Torah. Rashi notes that God chose a lowly thornbush rather than a majestic cedar to demonstrate that He dwells with Israel even in their lowliness and suffering — *"imo anochi b'tzarah"* (Tehillim 91:15). Shemot Rabbah 2:5 elaborates: the bush burns but is not consumed, just as Israel endures the fire of persecution without being destroyed. This image became a foundational symbol in Jewish thought for the indestructibility of the covenant people.

The Midrash asks why Moshe was chosen at this moment. Shemot Rabbah 2:2 offers the famous parable: Moshe was tending his father-in-law's flock when a young kid ran away. He chased it until it reached a pool of water and began to drink. Moshe said, "I did not know you ran because you were thirsty." He carried the kid back on his shoulders. God said, "Because you showed mercy to a mortal's flock, you will shepherd My flock, Israel." Compassion — not military prowess or intellectual brilliance — qualifies the leader.

## "Remove Your Sandals"

God commands Moshe to remove his shoes, for the ground is *admat kodesh* — holy ground (3:5). Ibn Ezra explains that removing footwear signifies removing the barrier between the person and the sanctified earth, an act of humility and submission. This practice persists in the Kohanim removing their shoes before blessing the congregation, and in the custom of removing leather shoes on Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av. Sforno adds that the command taught Moshe that holiness is not confined to temples but can pervade any place where God's presence rests.

## "Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh"

When Moshe asks God's name, the response is the enigmatic *"Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh"* — "I Will Be What I Will Be" (3:14). Rashi interprets this as a message for the suffering people: "I will be with them in this affliction, just as I will be with them in future afflictions." The Talmud (Berakhot 9b) records that Moshe protested — why mention future suffering now? — and God conceded, instructing him to say simply *"Ehyeh"* sent me.

Nachmanides reads the name philosophically: God is the Necessary Existent, the one whose essence *is* existence itself. There is no gap between being and identity in God. Ibn Ezra similarly interprets the name as pointing to God's eternity and self-sufficiency — He does not depend on anything outside Himself for existence.

The revelation of the four-letter Name — the Tetragrammaton (YHVH) — in verse 15 is described as *"zeh shemi l'olam"* ("this is My Name forever"). The Talmud (Kiddushin 71a) notes that the word *l'olam* is written defectively, as if it could be read *l'alem* — "to conceal" — teaching that the Name is to be hidden, not pronounced as written. This is the halakhic foundation for the practice of substituting *Adonai* or *HaShem* in speech and liturgy.

## The Mission

God sends Moshe to confront Pharaoh and to bring the Israelites to worship at "this mountain" (3:12) — Sinai. Rashi observes that the sign God gives Moshe is peculiar: the proof of the mission will only be evident *after* it succeeds, when the people stand at Sinai. Nachmanides explains that this teaches a fundamental principle of faith: sometimes the confirmation of a divine call comes only in retrospect, requiring trust (*emunah*) in the meantime.

The chapter introduces the promise that Israel will not leave Egypt empty-handed. Each woman will ask her Egyptian neighbor for silver, gold, and garments (3:22). The Talmud (Berakhot 9a) presents this as payment of long-overdue wages for centuries of slave labor — not theft, but justice. The Hebrew verb *v'nitzaltem* ("you shall despoil") carries the sense of recovering what is rightfully owed. This motif recurs prominently in the Haggadah's recounting of the Exodus.
