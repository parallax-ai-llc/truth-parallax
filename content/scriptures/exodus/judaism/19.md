## Arrival at Sinai

In the third month after the Exodus, the Israelites arrived at the wilderness of Sinai and encamped before the mountain (19:1-2). The Torah uses the singular form *"vayichan"* ("he encamped") rather than the plural *"vayachanu"*. Rashi's famous comment: *"K'ish echad b'lev echad"* — "as one person with one heart." The unity of Israel at Sinai was the precondition for receiving the Torah. Wherever there is division, the Torah cannot be fully received. This teaching became axiomatic in Jewish thought: communal harmony is not merely a social value but a metaphysical requirement for encountering the Divine.

## "A Kingdom of Priests and a Holy Nation"

God's offer to Israel through Moshe is among the most consequential statements in the Torah: *"V'atem tihyu li mamlechet kohanim v'goy kadosh"* — "You shall be to Me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (19:6). The Mekhilta interprets this as both privilege and responsibility: just as priests serve as intermediaries between the people and God, Israel serves as intermediary between humanity and the Divine. Sforno adds that *goy kadosh* means "a nation set apart" — not inherently superior but functionally distinct, consecrated for a particular purpose.

The Ramban emphasizes the conditional language: *"Im shamo'a tishme'u b'koli"* — "If you will indeed listen to My voice" (19:5). The covenant is bilateral. Israel's election is not unconditional privilege but a partnership that demands ongoing fidelity. The people respond with unanimous consent: *"Kol asher diber Hashem na'aseh"* — "All that the Lord has spoken, we will do" (19:8). This acceptance, before knowing the content of the commandments, is what the Talmud (Shabbat 88a) celebrates as *na'aseh v'nishma* — commitment preceding comprehension.

## Preparation and Boundaries

God instructs Moshe to have the people prepare for three days: wash garments, abstain from marital relations, and set boundaries around the mountain (19:10-13). No person or animal may touch the mountain, on pain of death. Rashi explains that the boundaries served a dual purpose: reverence for the sacred and protection of the people from a force that would overwhelm them if encountered without preparation.

The Talmud (Shabbat 87a) records that Moshe added a third day of preparation on his own authority, and God approved. This is one of three instances where Moshe acted independently and received retroactive divine endorsement — teaching that authentic leadership sometimes requires initiative beyond the explicit command.

## The Theophany

The description of Sinai is overwhelming: thunder, lightning, a thick cloud, and the sound of a *shofar* growing louder and louder (19:16-19). The mountain smoked because God descended upon it *"b'esh"* (in fire), and it trembled greatly (19:18). The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 29:9) states that the entire creation held its breath: no bird sang, no ox lowed, no angel flew, the sea did not move — all of nature fell silent so that the divine voice could be heard.

The Talmud (Zevachim 116a) teaches that the voice at Sinai had a unique quality: it came from no particular direction but filled all of space simultaneously. Rashi cites the Mekhilta that the voice divided into seventy languages, so that all nations could hear. Yet only Israel accepted the Torah — a point that, according to the rabbis, removes any claim that other nations were denied the opportunity.

## Moshe as Mediator

The closing verses describe Moshe ascending and descending the mountain, carrying messages between God and the people. This back-and-forth establishes Moshe's unique role: *"Moshe yedaber v'ha'Elokim ya'anenu v'kol"* — "Moshe would speak and God would answer him with a voice" (19:19). The Rambam considers this verse the Torah's clearest statement about the nature of Moshe's prophecy: unlike all other prophets who received visions and dreams, Moshe conversed with God directly, in waking consciousness, "mouth to mouth" (Bamidbar 12:8).

The warning to the people not to break through to gaze at the Lord (19:21) reflects a sober theological realism: the desire to see God directly, without mediation, is natural but lethal. The boundaries at Sinai are not barriers to keep people from God but guardrails that make the encounter survivable. This principle informs much of later Jewish theology: the mitzvot, the halakhah, the rituals — all are structures that mediate between finite human beings and infinite divinity.
