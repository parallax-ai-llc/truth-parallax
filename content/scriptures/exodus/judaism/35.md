## Parashat Vayakhel: Assembly and Sacred Work

*"Vayakhel Moshe et kol adat Bnei Yisrael"* — "Moshe assembled the entire congregation of Israel" (35:1). Rashi notes that this assembly took place on the day after Yom Kippur — the morning after Moshe descended with the second tablets and the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy. The timing is significant: the people had just been forgiven for the Golden Calf, and the first thing Moshe did was gather them for constructive purpose. The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 51:1) teaches that the Golden Calf was born from the disorder of a leaderless mob; the Mishkan was born from the disciplined assembly of a unified community. Destruction comes from chaos; sanctity from structure.

## Shabbat and Fire

Before discussing the Mishkan, Moshe reiterates the Shabbat commandment, adding a specific prohibition: *"Lo teva'aru esh b'khol moshvoteikhem b'yom ha'Shabbat"* — "You shall not kindle fire in any of your dwellings on the Shabbat day" (35:3). The Talmud (Shabbat 70a) records a dispute about why fire is singled out among the thirty-nine prohibited labors. Rabbi Yosi holds that fire was separated to teach that each *melakhah* is an independent prohibition — violating one does not constitute violating all. Rabbi Natan holds that fire was singled out to indicate a lesser penalty for this particular act. The halakhah follows Rabbi Yosi.

The Karaites, who rejected the Oral Torah, interpreted this verse literally as a prohibition against having any fire burning on Shabbat — sitting in cold and darkness. Rabbinic Judaism, following the Oral Tradition, permits fire kindled *before* Shabbat to continue burning; only the act of *kindling* is prohibited. The Friday night candles — lit just before Shabbat begins — embody this principle: light and warmth are prepared in advance, transforming the prohibition from deprivation into thoughtful anticipation.

## The Outpouring of Generosity

When Moshe called for contributions to the Mishkan, the response was overwhelming. *"Vayavo'u kol ish asher nesa'o libo"* — "Everyone whose heart stirred came" (35:21). The people brought gold, silver, copper, dyed yarns, linen, goat hair, ram skins, acacia wood, oil, spices, and precious stones. The Torah emphasizes that both men and women contributed: *"Vayavo'u ha'anashim al ha'nashim"* — "The men came with the women" (35:22), or alternatively, "the men came upon [in addition to] the women" — the women came first.

Rashi observes that the women's eagerness to contribute to the Mishkan contrasted sharply with their refusal to contribute to the Golden Calf. When their husbands demanded gold earrings for the calf, the women resisted (Pirkei d'Rabbi Eliezer 45). Now, for the Mishkan, they gave willingly and abundantly. The Midrash rewards them for this distinction: Rosh Chodesh (the New Moon) was given to the women as a semi-festival in recognition of their faithfulness.

## Skilled Women

The chapter highlights the women's artisanship: *"V'khol ishah chakhmat lev b'yadeha tavu"* — "Every wise-hearted woman spun with her hands" (35:25). Even more remarkably, verse 26 describes women who spun the goat hair while it was still on the goats — a technique requiring extraordinary skill. Rashi explains that this was a unique craft, and the Talmud (Shabbat 74b) considers it a superior form of spinning. The Torah's recognition of women's intellectual and artistic contributions to the Mishkan is noteworthy: the word *chokhmah* (wisdom) is applied to their work no less than to Betzalel's.

## The Surplus

The generosity was so great that the craftsmen came to Moshe and said: *"Marbim ha'am l'havi"* — "The people are bringing more than enough" (36:5, but the narrative begins here). Moshe had to issue a proclamation stopping further donations (36:6). This is one of the only instances in Jewish communal history where a fundraiser succeeded beyond its goal. The Midrash contrasts this with the meager contributions that later generations sometimes offered, noting with gentle irony that excess generosity is a problem most communities would welcome.

The Sforno draws a spiritual lesson: when the heart is genuinely stirred — when giving flows from inner motivation rather than external pressure — there is no scarcity. The Mishkan was built not by taxation but by love, and the surplus of material reflected the surplus of devotion. The physical structure was saturated with the intentions of its donors, making every beam and curtain a repository of sacred feeling.
