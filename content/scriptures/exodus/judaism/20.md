## The Ten Declarations

Jewish tradition calls these the *Aseret HaDibrot* — the Ten Declarations or Statements — not the "Ten Commandments," because some of them are statements of fact ("I am the Lord your God") rather than commands. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) teaches that the people heard the first two declarations directly from God, while the remaining eight came through Moshe. The direct divine communication was so overwhelming that, according to the Midrash, the people's souls departed at each word and had to be revived by the dew of resurrection.

Rashi notes that the Dibrot were arranged on two tablets: the first five address the relationship between humans and God (*bein adam la'Makom*), the last five address relationships between humans (*bein adam la'chavero*). The Mekhilta draws a parallel between them: "I am the Lord" corresponds to "Do not murder" — denying God diminishes the divine image in which humans were created; "No other gods" corresponds to "Do not commit adultery" — idolatry is spiritual infidelity; and so forth.

## "I Am the Lord Your God"

*"Anochi Hashem Elokekha asher hotzeticha me'eretz Mitzrayim"* (20:2). The Rambam counts this as the first positive commandment: to know that God exists. Significantly, God identifies Himself not as Creator of heaven and earth but as the one "who brought you out of Egypt." Yehudah HaLevi in the *Kuzari* (1:25) explains: the Exodus was witnessed by six hundred thousand people and is historically verifiable in a way that creation is not. God establishes His authority through experienced redemption, not abstract metaphysics.

The Ramban disagrees with the Rambam about whether belief can be commanded: can one be obligated to believe? He resolves this by interpreting the verse as a commandment to *acknowledge* — to live in light of the truth one has witnessed. Faith, in this reading, is not an intellectual proposition but a posture of the whole self.

## Shabbat

The Shabbat commandment (20:8-11) uses the word *zakhor* ("remember"), while the parallel version in Devarim (5:12) uses *shamor* ("guard/observe"). The Talmud (Shevuot 20b) famously teaches: *"Zakhor v'shamor b'dibbur echad ne'emru"* — both words were spoken simultaneously, something the human mouth cannot do and the human ear cannot ordinarily hear. This miraculous utterance demonstrates that the positive aspects of Shabbat (remembering, sanctifying, enjoying) and the negative aspects (refraining from work) are inseparable.

The Mekhilta derives from *zakhor* the obligation to make *kiddush* over wine — to verbally sanctify the day. From *shamor* come the thirty-nine categories of prohibited labor (*melakhot*), derived by the Talmud (Shabbat 73a) from the work performed in constructing the Mishkan. The connection between Shabbat and the Mishkan is not accidental: both create sacred time and sacred space, and the limitations of one define the obligations of the other.

## "Do Not Covet"

The final declaration — *"Lo tachmod"* ("Do not covet," 20:14) — is unique in addressing internal desire rather than external action. The Ibn Ezra's famous parable addresses the obvious question: how can the Torah command an emotion? He compares it to a peasant who does not covet a princess — the gap is so vast that desire does not arise. When a person truly internalizes that everything is allotted by God, coveting another's portion becomes as irrational as a peasant desiring a princess.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Gezeila 1:9-12) distinguishes between *lo tachmod* (do not covet to the point of acquisition) and *lo tit'aveh* (do not even desire), finding two distinct prohibitions. Nachmanides reads the prohibition as the capstone of the Dibrot: if one truly accepts God's sovereignty (first declaration), one will not covet what God has given another (last declaration). The tablets form a complete spiritual arc.

## The People's Fear

After the Dibrot, the people trembled and stood at a distance, asking Moshe to mediate: *"Daber atah imanu v'nishma, v'al yedaber imanu Elokim pen namut"* — "You speak with us and we will listen, but let God not speak with us lest we die" (20:16). Moshe assures them that the experience was intended *"ba'avur nasot etkhem"* — to test them, and so that the awe of God would remain with them to deter sin (20:17). The fear is not something to overcome but to preserve — a permanent orientation of reverence that shapes moral life.
