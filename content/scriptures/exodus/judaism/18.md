## Yitro's Arrival

Yitro, priest of Midian and Moshe's father-in-law, heard about all that God had done for Israel and came to the wilderness (18:1). Rashi asks: what specifically did Yitro hear that motivated him to come? The splitting of the Sea and the war with Amalek. The Sea demonstrated God's power over nature; Amalek demonstrated that God would defend Israel against human enemies. Together, they convinced Yitro that this God was supreme.

The Talmud (Zevachim 116a) debates Yitro's spiritual status. Some authorities hold he was already a monotheist; others maintain he had explored every form of idolatry before concluding their emptiness. His declaration — *"Atah yadati ki gadol Hashem mi'kol ha'elohim"* ("Now I know that the Lord is greater than all gods," 18:11) — carries special weight precisely because it comes from one who had tested the alternatives. The Mekhilta adds: "This is a reproach to Israel — a convert recognized what they took for granted."

Rashi notes the seven names by which Yitro is known in the Torah, each reflecting a different aspect of his character and journey. The name *Yeter* (meaning "he added") was expanded to *Yitro* because he "added" a section to the Torah through his counsel about the judicial system. A non-Israelite's wisdom became part of the eternal Torah — a fact the rabbis emphasize as evidence that truth is not confined to any single nation.

## Moshe's Unsustainable Burden

Yitro observes Moshe judging the people from morning to evening (18:13) and immediately identifies the problem: *"Naval tibol gam atah gam ha'am hazeh"* — "You will surely wear out, both you and this people" (18:18). Sforno explains that Yitro saw not only physical exhaustion but institutional failure — a system dependent on one person is fragile and ultimately unjust, because people waiting in long queues for judgment are effectively denied justice.

The wisdom of Yitro's observation lies in its universality. Nachmanides notes that Moshe, for all his prophetic gifts, could not see this structural problem because he was inside it. The outsider's perspective — the view from Midian — perceived what intimacy with the situation obscured. This is why the Torah preserves Yitro's counsel: it teaches that wisdom can come from unexpected sources, and humility requires receptivity to advice from those outside one's own community.

## The Judicial System

Yitro proposes a hierarchy of judges: rulers of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens (18:21). The qualifications he specifies are moral, not technical: *"anshei chayil, yir'ei Elokim, anshei emet, son'ei vatza"* — "capable men who fear God, men of truth, who hate dishonest gain" (18:21). The Talmud (Sanhedrin 7b) expands on each criterion: "capable men" means wealthy enough to be immune to bribery; "fearing God" means they judge as if standing before the divine tribunal; "men of truth" means their word is reliable; "hating gain" means they despise even their own money when justice is at stake.

Rashi notes that Moshe could find men of all four qualities only for the highest tier. For the lower courts, he found men with three, two, or even one of these qualities. The Midrash teaches that this is the nature of governance: the ideal is rarely fully realized, but the aspiration must remain.

## The Chronological Question

The placement of Yitro's visit before the Sinai revelation raises a famous question, since some details suggest it occurred after the giving of the Torah. Rashi states: *"Ein mukdam u'me'uchar ba'Torah"* — "The Torah does not follow strict chronological order." Ibn Ezra disagrees, placing the visit before Sinai as the text indicates. The Ramban offers a mediating view. This debate, while technical, reflects a deeper hermeneutical principle: the Torah's arrangement is thematic, not merely sequential, and the placement of a passage teaches as much as its content.

## Yitro's Departure

After his counsel is accepted, Yitro returns to Midian (18:27). The Midrash speculates that he went to convert his family. Whether he eventually joined Israel permanently is debated, but his contribution endures: the judicial structure he proposed became the prototype for the *batei din* (courts) that would adjudicate halakhah for millennia. A convert's practical wisdom became embedded in the architecture of Jewish law.
