## The Manna: Bread from Heaven

The complaint about hunger in the wilderness (16:3) leads to one of the Torah's most theologically rich episodes: the provision of *man* (manna). God declares: *"Hinni mamtir lakhem lechem min ha'shamayim"* — "Behold, I will rain bread from heaven for you" (16:4). The manna is more than food; it is a daily lesson in trust. The people must gather only what they need for one day, with a double portion on the sixth day for Shabbat. Anyone who hoarded found it rotting by morning (16:20); anyone who went out on Shabbat found nothing (16:27).

Rashi, following the Mekhilta, describes the manna as adapting to each person's taste — whatever flavor the eater desired, the manna provided. The Talmud (Yoma 75a) elaborates: for the righteous it fell at their doorstep, already prepared; for the average person it fell in the camp and required gathering; for the wicked it fell far away and required laborious collection. The manna, in this reading, was a mirror of spiritual condition.

## Shabbat's First Appearance

Chapter 16 contains the Torah's first practical legislation regarding Shabbat. On Friday, a double portion appeared, and Moshe instructed the people: *"Hu asher diber Hashem: Shabbaton Shabbat kodesh la'Hashem machar"* — "This is what the Lord has spoken: tomorrow is a solemn rest, a holy Shabbat to the Lord" (16:23). The manna that would rot overnight on weekdays miraculously preserved itself over Shabbat.

Rashi notes that Shabbat was given at Marah (15:25) as a concept but is here operationalized through the manna. The people learn Shabbat not through abstract theology but through the rhythm of gathering and resting. The Ramban observes that Shabbat's connection to sustenance teaches that economic anxiety — the fear that resting will lead to deprivation — is fundamentally a failure of trust. The one who provides six days' worth of manna can surely provide for the seventh.

The custom of placing two loaves (*lechem mishneh*) on the Shabbat table derives from the double portion of manna (16:22). The Talmud (Shabbat 117b) codifies this as halakhah. Even the tablecloth and cover over the challah recall the layers of dew above and below the manna (16:13-14), so that the Shabbat table becomes a weekly reenactment of the wilderness provision.

## The Omer and the Measure of Sufficiency

Each person gathered an *omer* per head (16:16) — approximately 2.5 liters. Whether one gathered much or little, each found exactly an omer in their vessel (16:18). This miraculous equalization is cited by the rabbis as a paradigm for economic justice: God provides enough for everyone; inequality results from human hoarding, not divine scarcity.

The Midrash (Tanchuma, Beshalach 22) uses the manna to teach about *parnasah* (livelihood): just as the manna came daily, requiring renewed trust each morning, so a person's sustenance requires daily faith. The anxiety of accumulation — storing more than one needs — is the spiritual equivalent of the manna that bred worms. This teaching profoundly influenced Jewish attitudes toward wealth: sufficiency, not surplus, is the proper aim.

## The Jar of Manna

Moshe instructs Aharon to place an omer of manna in a jar before the Lord as a memorial for future generations (16:33). This jar was eventually kept in the Holy of Holies alongside the Ark of the Covenant. The Talmud (Yoma 52b) records that it was hidden by King Yoshiyahu before the destruction of the First Temple. Its preservation symbolizes the enduring lesson: the God who sustained Israel in the wilderness continues to sustain them in all generations.

## A Test of Faith

God explicitly frames the manna as a test: *"l'ma'an anasenu ha'yelekh b'torati im lo"* — "in order to test them, whether they will walk in My Torah or not" (16:4). Ibn Ezra explains that the test was not of obedience alone but of character: can a people learn to live without surplus? Can they trust tomorrow's provision? The forty years of manna, sustained daily, constituted the longest sustained lesson in faith in human history — and the people's frequent failures along the way are recorded with unflinching honesty.

Sforno draws the connection forward: the manna prepared the people for Sinai. Only a people who had learned to depend on God for daily bread could receive a Torah that demands submission of human autonomy to divine command. Material dependence was the school of spiritual receptivity.
