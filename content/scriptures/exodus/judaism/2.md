## The Hidden Child

The birth of Moshe is narrated with deliberate restraint. The Torah states simply that "a man from the house of Levi went and took a daughter of Levi" (2:1). Rashi, citing Sotah 12a, explains that Amram had divorced Yocheved after Pharaoh's decree, and it was young Miriam who persuaded her father to remarry, arguing that his decree was harsher than Pharaoh's — Pharaoh decreed only against the males, but Amram's separation prevented the birth of daughters as well. This Talmudic tradition elevates Miriam as a prophetic figure whose moral insight set redemption in motion.

When Moshe was born, the Torah says the house was "filled with light" (*vatere oto ki tov hu*, 2:2). The Talmud (Sotah 12a) connects the word *tov* here to the primordial light of creation — *"vayar Elokim et ha'or ki tov"* — suggesting that Moshe's birth carried cosmic significance. Sforno, more modestly, reads *tov* as indicating physical health and vigor, signs that the child might survive.

## The Ark on the Nile

Yocheved placed the infant in a *teivah* — the same word used for Noach's ark. This is no coincidence in rabbinic reading. Shemot Rabbah 1:20 draws the parallel explicitly: just as the ark preserved the future of humanity through the waters of destruction, so this small basket preserved the redeemer of Israel upon the waters of the Nile. The Nile, which Pharaoh had turned into an instrument of death, becomes the channel of salvation.

Miriam stationed herself at a distance to watch (2:4). Ibn Ezra notes the narrative precision: she stood far enough to avoid suspicion but close enough to intervene. Nachmanides sees in Miriam's vigil a model of *bitachon* — trust in God combined with practical human effort.

## Batya and the Act of Compassion

Pharaoh's daughter — called Batya ("daughter of God") in the Talmud (Megillah 13a) — reached out her hand to draw the basket from the water. The Midrash notes the verb *vatishlach* ("she stretched out"), suggesting that her arm was miraculously extended far beyond natural reach. This teaches that when a person stretches toward righteousness, Heaven assists beyond what seems possible.

Batya's compassion crossed every boundary: national, political, familial. She recognized the child as a Hebrew (*mi'yaldei ha'Ivrim zeh*, 2:6) and nevertheless chose to raise him. The rabbis count her among the righteous gentiles and teach that she descended to the Nile specifically to cleanse herself from her father's idolatry (Sotah 12b).

## Moshe in Midian

The narrative leaps forward to Moshe's adulthood: he goes out to see the suffering of his brothers, strikes an Egyptian taskmaster, and flees to Midian. Rashi explains *"vayar b'sivlotam"* ("he saw their burdens") as meaning he set his eyes and heart to share in their distress. Shemot Rabbah 1:27 adds that Moshe physically helped carry their loads, rearranging the labor assignments to ease the burden on weaker workers.

In Midian, Moshe encounters injustice again — shepherds driving away Yitro's daughters from the well — and intervenes. The pattern is clear: the redeemer is one who cannot tolerate oppression in any form, whether against his own people or strangers. This quality of universal moral sensitivity is, for the rabbis, the essential qualification for leadership.

The chapter concludes with a pivotal verse: "God heard their groaning, and God remembered His covenant with Avraham, with Yitzchak, and with Yaakov" (2:24). Nachmanides stresses that *"vayizkor"* does not imply that God had forgotten. Rather, the time decreed for exile was now fulfilled, and divine mercy — always present — now became manifest in historical action. The covenant is the unbreakable thread that connects the patriarchs to the enslaved people, and the enslaved people to the coming redemption.
