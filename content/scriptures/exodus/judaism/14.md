## Parashat Beshalach: The Sea Ahead, Egypt Behind

The splitting of the Sea of Reeds (*Yam Suf*) is the climactic moment of the Exodus narrative. God deliberately leads the Israelites to a position that appears strategically suicidal — the sea before them, the desert on both sides, and Pharaoh's army closing from behind (14:2-3). Rashi explains that God engineered this entrapment so that Pharaoh would say, "They are confused in the land; the wilderness has closed upon them" (14:3), emboldening him to pursue. The trap was set not for Israel but for Egypt.

When the people saw the approaching army, they cried out in terror and turned on Moshe: *"Ha'mibli ein kevarim b'Mitzrayim?"* — "Was it because there are no graves in Egypt that you brought us to die in the wilderness?" (14:11). The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 14:3) identifies four factions: one wanted to fight, one wanted to surrender, one wanted to throw themselves into the sea, and one wanted to cry out to God. Moshe rejected all four: "Stand still and see the salvation of the Lord" (14:13). The correct response to impossible circumstances is neither fight nor flight but faith.

## Nachshon's Leap

The Talmud (Sotah 37a) preserves one of the most celebrated aggadic traditions: while the Israelites stood at the shore debating, Nachshon ben Aminadav of the tribe of Yehudah plunged into the water. The sea did not split until the water reached his nostrils. Only then — when human courage met its absolute limit — did God command the sea to divide. This teaching, though not in the written text, profoundly shaped Jewish theology: God responds to human initiative, and faith must be enacted before it is confirmed.

Rashi cites this tradition in the context of God's rebuke to Moshe: *"Mah titz'ak elai? Daber el Bnei Yisrael v'yisa'u"* — "Why do you cry to Me? Speak to the children of Israel and let them go forward" (14:15). There is a time for prayer and a time for action. The Mekhilta states that God told Moshe: "My children are in distress, the sea is before them, the enemy behind them, and you stand prolonging your prayers?"

## The Splitting

God commanded Moshe to raise his staff and stretch his hand over the sea (14:16). The waters divided, and Israel crossed on dry land with walls of water on both sides (14:22). The Midrash elaborates extensively: the sea split into twelve corridors, one for each tribe; the walls were transparent so the tribes could see one another; fresh water flowed from the walls for the thirsty; fruit trees sprouted from the seabed.

While many of these details are aggadic embellishment, they convey a theological truth: the crossing was not merely an escape but a transformation. The people who entered the sea as refugees emerged as a nation. The Zohar describes the passage through the sea as a collective birth — the narrow passage between the waters echoing the birth canal — and teaches that Israel was born as a people at this moment.

## The Destruction of Egypt

When the Egyptians pursued into the sea, God threw them into confusion — *"vayasar et ofan markevotav"* — removing their chariot wheels (14:25). The Egyptians themselves cried out: "Let us flee from Israel, for the Lord fights for them!" (14:25). But it was too late. The waters returned and covered the entire army (14:28).

The Talmud (Megillah 10b) records a famous teaching: when the ministering angels wished to sing at the destruction of the Egyptians, God rebuked them: *"Ma'aseh yadai tov'im ba'yam, v'atem omrim shirah?"* — "My creatures are drowning in the sea, and you would sing?" This does not mean Israel was wrong to sing — chapter 15 is entirely a song — but that the angelic realm, which stands outside history, must acknowledge the tragedy even within the triumph. Jewish tradition captures this tension in the custom of removing drops of wine from the Seder cup when recounting the plagues: our joy is diminished by the suffering of others, even enemies.

## "And They Believed"

The chapter concludes with the foundational verse: *"Vaya'aminu ba'Hashem u'v'Moshe avdo"* — "They believed in the Lord and in Moshe His servant" (14:31). The Mekhilta elevates this faith to cosmic significance: if the people who witnessed the plagues needed the sea crossing to fully believe, how precious is the faith of subsequent generations who believe without witnessing any miracles at all. This verse is recited daily in the morning prayers (*Shacharit*), embedding the Exodus faith into the rhythm of Jewish life.
