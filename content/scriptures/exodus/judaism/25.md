## Parashat Terumah: "Let Them Make Me a Sanctuary"

The instruction *"V'asu li mikdash v'shakhanti b'tokham"* — "Let them make Me a sanctuary, and I will dwell among them" (25:8) — is the theological foundation of the entire Mishkan (Tabernacle) section. The Kotzker Rebbe made the classic observation: the verse does not say *b'tokho* ("in it" — in the sanctuary) but *b'tokham* ("among them" — among the people). God's dwelling is not in a structure but in the hearts of those who build it. The physical Mishkan is a means, not an end.

Rashi notes that the offerings must come from *"kol ish asher yidvenu libo"* — "every person whose heart moves him" (25:2). The *terumah* (offering) must be voluntary. Coerced sanctity is a contradiction in terms. The Talmud (Bava Batra 9a) teaches that the merit of charity depends on the willingness of the giver, not merely the amount. This principle, established here at the founding of sacred space, pervades Jewish philanthropy.

## The Aron (Ark)

The first vessel described is the *Aron* — the Ark of the Covenant (25:10-22), overlaid with gold inside and outside, with a pure gold *kapporet* (cover) and two *keruvim* (cherubim) facing each other with wings spread. The Talmud (Yoma 72b) derives from the triple layering — gold, wood, gold — that a Torah scholar must be *tokho k'varo* (the inside must match the outside). Outward piety without inner integrity is like an ark with gold only on the surface.

The dimensions of the Ark are given in half-measures: two and a half cubits long, one and a half wide, one and a half high (25:10). Rashi explains: the fractional measurements teach that a Torah scholar must always consider himself incomplete — there is always more to learn. Wholeness in learning is an illusion; the half-measure is the measure of humility.

The *keruvim* faced each other with childlike faces, according to the Talmud (Sukkah 5b). When Israel fulfilled God's will, the faces turned toward each other in love; when Israel sinned, they turned away. The Zohar reads the keruvim as representing the male and female dimensions of the divine, whose union (*zivug*) produces the flow of blessing into the world. This became a foundational image in Kabbalistic theology.

## The Shulchan (Table)

The table for the *lechem ha'panim* (showbread) was overlaid with pure gold (25:23-30). Twelve loaves were placed on it every Shabbat, representing the twelve tribes. The Talmud (Menachot 96b) records the miracle that the bread remained fresh from one Shabbat to the next. Rashi explains that the table represents sustenance — God provides for all Israel — while the Ramban sees it as the channel through which divine blessing of prosperity flows into the world.

The Midrash Tanchuma teaches that whoever hosted guests at their table was considered as if maintaining the Shulchan in the Temple. The table in every Jewish home, then, is a *mikdash me'at* — a small sanctuary — and hospitality is a form of sacred service.

## The Menorah

The Menorah — seven-branched, beaten from a single piece of pure gold (25:31-40) — was the most technically challenging vessel to produce. Rashi, citing the Midrash, states that Moshe could not grasp its complex design until God showed him a model of fire. Even then, the Midrash says, Moshe struggled, and God told him to cast the gold into the fire and the Menorah formed itself — a divine collaboration in craftsmanship.

The seven branches represent multiple things in Jewish tradition: the seven days of creation, the seven lower *sefirot* in Kabbalistic thought, and the wholeness of divine illumination. Sforno interprets the Menorah as representing the intellectual light of Torah that must radiate in all directions from its central shaft. The Rambam (Hilkhot Beit HaBechirah 3:2) codifies the Menorah's precise measurements and emphasizes that no deviation from the Torah's description was permitted — creativity in sacred architecture operates within revealed parameters.

God instructs Moshe: *"U'r'eh va'aseh b'tavnitam asher atah mar'eh ba'har"* — "See and make according to their pattern, which is shown to you on the mountain" (25:40). The Ramban interprets this as meaning that every detail of the Mishkan corresponds to a heavenly archetype. The earthly sanctuary mirrors a celestial reality — a concept that deeply influenced Jewish mysticism and the understanding of ritual as participation in cosmic processes.
