## The Descent into Bondage

Parashat Shemot opens with the phrase *"V'eileh shemot Bnei Yisrael"* — "And these are the names of the children of Israel." Rashi, following Shemot Rabbah 1:3, explains that although the sons of Yaakov were already enumerated in Bereishit, the Torah counts them again by name at the moment of their death to express God's enduring love for them. Just as the stars are called out by name (Yeshayahu 40:26), so too the individuals who descended into Egypt are each precious before the Holy One.

The Midrash draws attention to the seemingly redundant language: the parashah is called *Shemot* ("Names") precisely because the preservation of Hebrew names in Egypt was one of the merits by which Israel was eventually redeemed. Vayikra Rabbah 32:5 teaches that the Israelites did not change their names, their language, or their garments — markers of identity that sustained them through centuries of exile.

## The Rise of a New Pharaoh

The text states that "a new king arose over Egypt who did not know Yosef" (1:8). Rav and Shmuel dispute in the Talmud (Sotah 11a) whether this was literally a new king or the same king who issued new decrees. Either reading underscores a theological point: the amnesia of empires is inevitable, and the covenantal memory of Israel stands in stark contrast to the forgetfulness of worldly power. Ibn Ezra favors the plain reading — a genuinely new dynasty — while Rashbam notes that Egyptian political upheavals often erased previous rulers from official memory.

Pharaoh's strategy of oppression unfolds in stages: first forced labor (*parech*), then instructions to the midwives, and finally the decree to cast male infants into the Nile. Nachmanides observes that Pharaoh acted with calculated political cunning, escalating gradually so that neither the Egyptians nor the Israelites would revolt against the cruelty all at once. This pattern — incremental dehumanization — has become a paradigm in Jewish ethical teaching for recognizing the early signs of tyranny.

## Shifrah and Puah: Moral Courage

The midwives Shifrah and Puah refused Pharaoh's command to kill the male children. The Talmud (Sotah 11b) identifies them as Yocheved and Miriam, though some commentators take them as Egyptian women who converted. Sforno emphasizes that they "feared God" (*yir'at Elokim*) — a universal moral category — and therefore defied a direct royal decree. Their reward was that God "made them houses" (1:21), understood by Rashi as the houses of priesthood, Levite service, and royalty that descended from them.

The Hebrew wordplay in *va'tichyena et ha'yeladim* — "they kept the children alive" — suggests not merely passive refusal but active nurturing. Shemot Rabbah 1:15 describes how the midwives provided food and water to impoverished mothers, sustaining life even at great personal risk.

## Theological Dimensions

The opening chapter of Shemot establishes several themes that resonate throughout the Torah and subsequent Jewish thought. The tension between empire and covenant, the moral agency of individuals within oppressive systems, and the paradox that suffering and multiplication occur simultaneously — *"ka'asher ye'anu oto ken yirbeh"* ("the more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied," 1:12) — all become foundational motifs.

The Zohar reads this chapter as an allegory for the soul's descent into the material world: the seventy souls who enter Egypt correspond to the seventy facets of Torah, and the oppression represents the challenges that refine the spirit. While such mystical readings operate on a different plane than the peshat, they reflect the Jewish conviction that the Exodus narrative speaks to every generation. As the Haggadah insists: *"B'chol dor va'dor chayav adam lirot et atzmo k'ilu hu yatza mi'Mitzrayim"* — in every generation, one must see oneself as having personally gone out of Egypt.
