## The Copper Altar and the Courtyard

Chapter 38 details the construction of the copper altar (*Mizbe'ach ha'Nechoshet*), the laver (*kiyor*), and the courtyard enclosure, followed by a comprehensive accounting of all materials used. The altar, five cubits square and three cubits high (38:1-7), was the central feature of the courtyard — the place where the daily offerings were burned, where atonement was achieved, and where the vertical axis between heaven and earth was most tangible.

Rashi notes that the altar was constructed hollow and filled with earth at each encampment, fulfilling the requirement of *"Mizbe'ach adamah"* (an altar of earth, 20:21). The Midrash observes that the altar consumed offerings daily yet was never itself consumed by the fire — a perpetual miracle that testified to divine acceptance. The Talmud (Chagigah 27a) applies this image to the Torah scholar's table, which "atones like the altar" when guests are welcomed and words of Torah are shared.

## The Kiyor and the Women's Mirrors

The *kiyor* (laver) was made *"b'mar'ot ha'tzov'ot asher tzav'u petach Ohel Mo'ed"* — "from the mirrors of the women who assembled at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (38:8). This verse contains one of the most beautiful Midrashic traditions in the Torah. Shemot Rabbah 51:8 and Tanchuma, Pekudei 9, record that Moshe was reluctant to accept the mirrors, considering them instruments of physical vanity inappropriate for sacred use. God overruled him emphatically: "These are more dear to Me than all other contributions, for through these mirrors the women sustained Israel in Egypt."

The Midrash explains: during the crushing oppression, the men were too exhausted and despairing for intimacy. The women would bring food to the fields, take out their mirrors, and playfully say, "I am more beautiful than you." They aroused their husbands' desire and thereby ensured the continuation of the nation. The very instruments of physical attraction — which Moshe saw as profane — God valued as the most sacred of offerings, because they had served life and continuity in the darkest hour. This teaching is foundational in Judaism's integration of the physical and the spiritual: the body and its desires, rightly directed, are vehicles of holiness.

## The Courtyard

The courtyard's hangings of fine twisted linen, with their pillars of copper, silver hooks, and bands (38:9-20), created the bounded space within which the sacrificial service occurred. The Midrash notes that the courtyard was open to the sky — unlike the Mishkan itself, which was covered. Sforno interprets this architecturally: the open courtyard represents the accessible dimension of worship, where the community gathers; the covered Mishkan represents the hidden dimension, accessible only to the priests; and the Holy of Holies, shrouded behind the *parokhet*, represents the incomprehensible depth of divine being.

## The Accounting — Pekudei

The second half of the chapter provides a meticulous accounting of all materials: 29 talents and 730 shekels of gold; 100 talents and 1,775 shekels of silver; 70 talents and 2,400 shekels of copper (38:24-31). This accounting was conducted under the supervision of Itamar ben Aharon (38:21), establishing the principle that sacred funds require transparent management.

The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 51:1) explains why Moshe himself insisted on the accounting. He overheard mocking whispers — *"ma'u leitzanei ha'dor"* — scoffers of the generation who said, "Look how fat the neck of Ben Amram has become" (implying Moshe enriched himself from the contributions). To silence all suspicion, Moshe conducted a public audit. Rashi draws the lesson: even Moshe, whom God trusted absolutely, submitted to accountability before the people. How much more so must ordinary leaders be transparent with communal funds.

The silver, specifically, came from the *machatzit ha'shekel* (half-shekel) census contribution: 603,550 men contributed, yielding 100 talents for the sockets and the remainder for hooks and bands (38:25-28). The Ramban observes that the Mishkan literally stood on the contributions of every individual — the silver sockets that held the boards were forged from universal participation. No one could say, "This sanctuary is not mine." Every Israelite's half-shekel supported the holy structure.

## Craftsmanship as Testimony

The chapter's detailed inventory serves a purpose beyond mere record-keeping. The Ohr HaChaim teaches that listing every material and its use demonstrated that nothing was wasted and nothing diverted. In a deeper sense, the accounting testified that the physical materials had been fully transformed into sacred purpose. The gold that once adorned Egyptian idols now covered the Ark; the copper from everyday vessels now held the courtyard erect. The accounting was a document of transfiguration — the secular made sacred.
