## The Plague of Frogs

The second plague brings frogs (*tzefarde'im*) upon Egypt. The Torah uses a singular form — *"vataal hatzefardea"* ("the frog came up," 8:2) — which prompts a famous Talmudic discussion (Sanhedrin 67b). Rabbi Akiva taught that one enormous frog emerged and the Egyptians struck it, causing it to swarm into multitudes. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah gently rebuked him: "Akiva, what business have you with *aggadah*? Confine yourself to *nega'im* and *ohalot*." The simple reading is that *tzefardea* is used as a collective noun. Yet the Midrashic image endures: violence against the plague only multiplied it, a parable for the futility of combating divine judgment with human force.

Shemot Rabbah 10:2 describes how the frogs penetrated everywhere — into ovens, into kneading troughs, even into the bodies of the Egyptians. The Talmud (Pesachim 53b) derives from this a remarkable lesson: if frogs, which have no commandment to sanctify God's name, entered fiery ovens out of obedience to their Creator, how much more should Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah be willing to enter Nevuchadnetzar's furnace to sanctify the Name. The frogs become a model of self-sacrifice.

## Pharaoh's Partial Concessions

When Pharaoh begs Moshe to remove the frogs, Moshe graciously offers him the honor of choosing when: "Set for me a time" (8:5). Sforno explains that Moshe's deference demonstrated that the plague's removal was as much under divine control as its onset — it was not a natural phenomenon running its course. When the frogs died and were gathered into heaps, the stench was overwhelming (8:10), yet the moment relief came, Pharaoh hardened his heart again. Rashi comments: this is the pattern of the wicked — they cry out in distress and forget their promises the moment the pressure lifts.

## The Plague of Lice

The third plague — *kinim* (lice or gnats) — came without warning. Rashi, following the Midrash, explains the pattern: each set of three plagues followed a sequence: warning at the Nile, warning at the palace, no warning at all. The escalation in each triad reflects God's pedagogy — instruction, then reminder, then consequence.

Aharon struck the dust of the earth and it became lice (8:13). Once again, Moshe did not strike the earth himself, because the earth had sheltered the body of the Egyptian he killed (Shemot Rabbah 10:7). The principle of gratitude toward the elements of creation is reinforced: one does not harm what has served as a protector.

## "The Finger of God"

The Egyptian magicians attempted to replicate the lice but failed. They declared to Pharaoh: *"Etzba Elokim hi"* — "This is the finger of God" (8:15). Rashi notes that even the magicians acknowledged a power beyond their sorcery. Ibn Ezra explains that the magicians could manipulate existing matter but could not generate life from inorganic dust — the creation of living organisms ex nihilo was beyond any created power.

The Haggadah uses this verse as the basis for Rabbi Yosi HaGelili's calculation: if one finger produced ten plagues in Egypt, then at the Sea — where God struck with a "great hand" — the Egyptians received fifty plagues. This homiletical arithmetic, while playful, makes a serious theological point: human minds cannot fully calculate the scope of divine intervention.

## The Plague of Arov

The fourth plague — *arov* (wild beasts or swarms) — introduces a critical distinction: the land of Goshen, where the Israelites dwell, is spared (8:18). Nachmanides observes that this separation required continuous supernatural maintenance, since animals do not naturally respect political borders. The distinction served to demonstrate that the plagues were not natural catastrophes but targeted divine judgments.

Pharaoh's first genuine concession comes here: "Go sacrifice to your God in the land" (8:21). Moshe refuses, and the negotiation that follows — each partial offer rejected — illustrates a principle that resonates throughout Jewish history: partial freedom is not freedom. The redemption must be complete, encompassing body and spirit, land and worship.
