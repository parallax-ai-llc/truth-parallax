## Three Signs

God grants Moshe three signs to authenticate his mission: the staff becoming a serpent, the hand turning leprous, and water turning to blood. Rashi, drawing on Shemot Rabbah 3:12, explains that each sign also carried a rebuke. When Moshe said "they will not believe me" (4:1), he spoke *lashon hara* (slander) against Israel, doubting a people who were in fact *ma'aminim bnei ma'aminim* — believers and children of believers. The leprous hand, in particular, was a measure-for-measure consequence of evil speech, since leprosy (*tzara'at*) is the Torah's punishment for slander.

The serpent sign bore additional symbolism. The Midrash teaches that when Moshe cast his staff to the ground and it became a *nachash*, this recalled the primordial serpent whose slander in Eden brought suffering to the world. By grasping the serpent's tail and restoring it to a staff, Moshe demonstrated mastery over the forces of deception and falsehood — the essential qualification of a prophet.

## Moshe's Reluctance

Moshe's repeated protests — "I am not a man of words" (4:10), "please send someone else" (4:13) — have generated extensive rabbinic discussion. Rashi interprets Moshe's speech difficulty as a genuine physical impediment, possibly a stammer, rooted in the childhood incident when he placed a hot coal in his mouth (Shemot Rabbah 1:26). Ibn Ezra, however, suggests that Moshe simply lacked rhetorical polish in Egyptian, having spent decades in Midian.

Nachmanides reads Moshe's reluctance more theologically: Moshe genuinely believed he was unworthy and that Aharon, who had been prophesying to the people in Egypt for years, was the more appropriate leader. God's anger (*vayichar af Hashem*, 4:14) at Moshe's final refusal teaches that excessive humility, when it prevents one from fulfilling a divine commission, becomes a form of disobedience.

The appointment of Aharon as spokesman introduces a model of shared leadership unique in the ancient world. Sforno notes that the division — Moshe receiving revelation, Aharon articulating it publicly — anticipates the later relationship between Torah scholars and communal leaders, where different gifts serve complementary functions.

## The Bridegroom of Blood

One of the Torah's most enigmatic passages occurs at the lodging place, where God seeks to kill Moshe (or, according to some readings, his son), and Tzipporah performs an emergency circumcision, declaring *"chatan damim atah li"* — "a bridegroom of blood you are to me" (4:25-26). The Talmud (Nedarim 31b-32a) explains that Moshe had delayed circumcising his son Eliezer, and this delay — even briefly — was a severe transgression for the one about to lead Israel.

Rashi presents two opinions: Moshe delayed because of the dangers of travel, or because he prioritized the urgency of the mission. Regardless, Tzipporah's swift action saved her husband. The Midrash elevates Tzipporah here as a woman of decisive faith and halakhic intuition, understanding that the covenant of circumcision (*brit milah*) could not be deferred even under extraordinary circumstances.

This passage underscores a principle that pervades Jewish law: no one, however great, stands above the commandments. The one who will deliver the Torah must first be subject to it. Ibn Ezra notes that the severity of the threat demonstrates the absolute primacy of *brit milah* as the sign of the covenant — it is one of only two positive commandments whose neglect carries the penalty of *karet* (spiritual excision).

## The Brothers Meet

The chapter concludes with the reunion of Moshe and Aharon at the mountain of God and their joint mission to the elders of Israel. When the people heard that God had remembered them and seen their affliction, "they bowed and prostrated themselves" (4:31). Rashi notes that faith preceded experience — the people believed before witnessing the plagues or the Exodus. This is the *emunah* that the Haggadah celebrates, and the Zohar considers this act of collective faith to be itself a merit that hastened the redemption.
