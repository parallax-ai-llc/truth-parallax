## The Fifth Plague: Pestilence

The plague of *dever* (pestilence) struck Egypt's livestock — horses, donkeys, camels, cattle, and sheep (9:3). Rashi raises the question that recurs throughout the plague narrative: if the livestock died in the fifth plague, how were there animals left for subsequent plagues? He answers that only animals in the field perished; those kept indoors survived. Additionally, animals purchased from Israelites or belonging to God-fearing Egyptians were spared. Ibn Ezra simply states that "all" in the Torah often means "the majority," a principle of biblical rhetoric.

The Midrash emphasizes the *middah k'neged middah* (measure for measure) dimension: Pharaoh had used the Israelites as beasts of burden, so God struck the actual beasts. Shemot Rabbah 11:4 adds that each plague targeted a specific Egyptian deity — in this case, the sacred bulls and cows of Apis and Hathor. The plagues were not only acts of liberation but systematic demonstrations that Egyptian religion was powerless before the Creator.

## The Sixth Plague: Boils

The plague of *shechin* (boils) is the first to afflict the Egyptians' bodies directly. Moshe and Aharon threw soot from a furnace (*kivshan*) toward the sky, and it spread across all Egypt (9:8-10). Shemot Rabbah 11:6 notes the irony: the furnace (*kivshan*) alludes to the brick kilns where the Israelites labored. The instrument of oppression became the instrument of judgment.

The magicians could not stand before Moshe *"ki hayah ha'shechin ba'chartumim"* — "because the boils were upon the magicians" (9:11). Their complete defeat is emphasized: not only could they not replicate this plague, they could not even appear in public. Sforno observes that the boils were the turning point where Pharaoh lost all intellectual justification for resistance — his own advisors were incapacitated and discredited.

## The Seventh Plague: Hail

Before bringing hail, God sends an extended warning that contains one of the most theologically significant statements in the plague narrative: *"Ba'avur zot he'emadticha, ba'avur har'otcha et kochi"* — "For this reason I have preserved you: to show you My power, so that My Name will be declared throughout all the earth" (9:16). Rambam uses this verse to illustrate his teaching on the hardened heart — Pharaoh was sustained in power specifically to serve as the stage upon which divine sovereignty would be demonstrated to the nations.

The warning also introduces a distinction among the Egyptians themselves: *"Ha'yarei et dvar Hashem"* — those who feared God's word brought their servants and cattle indoors, while those who disregarded the warning left them in the field (9:20-21). Rashi identifies the God-fearing Egyptian as Iyov (Job). The Midrash makes a broader point: even within an evil empire, individuals retain moral agency. The Torah does not paint all Egyptians with a single brush.

## The Hail Itself

The hail was miraculous in its composition: fire burned within ice without extinguishing it (9:24). Rashi describes this as *nes b'toch nes* — a miracle within a miracle. The Midrash explains that the fire and ice made peace with each other in order to fulfill their Creator's will, teaching that even opposing forces in nature unite when serving God's purpose. This image became a favorite in Jewish homiletics for demonstrating that apparent contradictions can coexist in the service of a higher unity.

Pharaoh's response is his most dramatic confession: *"Hashem ha'tzaddik, va'ani v'ami ha'resha'im"* — "The Lord is righteous, and I and my people are the wicked ones" (9:27). Yet the confession evaporated the moment the hail ceased. Nachmanides observes that Pharaoh's repentance was motivated entirely by fear, not by genuine moral transformation — and repentance rooted solely in fear of consequences, without inner change, cannot endure.

The chapter notes that the flax and barley were destroyed, but the wheat and spelt were not, because they ripen later (9:31-32). Ibn Ezra draws attention to this naturalistic detail as evidence that the Torah describes real events embedded in the actual agricultural calendar of Egypt, grounding the miraculous within the tangible world.
