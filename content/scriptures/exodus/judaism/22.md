## Theft and Restitution

Chapter 22 continues the civil legislation of Mishpatim with laws governing theft, property damage, and custodial responsibility. The Torah prescribes that a thief who steals an ox pays fivefold and a sheep fourfold (21:37/22:1 in different verse numbering systems). The Talmud (Bava Kamma 79b) asks: why is the ox penalty higher? Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai explains that the ox thief must also carry the animal away on his shoulders, suffering indignity — but the sheep thief has the animal walk on its own. Alternatively, the ox represents lost labor in the field, a more severe economic harm than losing a sheep.

Rashi notes the case of the tunneling thief (*ba ba'machteret*, 22:1): if a homeowner kills a thief who breaks in at night, there is no blood-guilt, because the thief is presumed to be willing to kill. But if the break-in occurs during the day — *"im zarcha ha'shemesh alav"* ("if the sun has risen upon him," 22:2) — killing the thief is murder. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 72a) derives the principle of self-defense (*ha'ba l'horgekha, hashkem l'horgo*) from this passage, while simultaneously limiting its application.

## Bailment and Trust

The laws of the four types of guardians (*shomrim*) — the unpaid guardian, the paid guardian, the borrower, and the renter (22:6-14) — constitute one of the most developed areas of Talmudic civil law. Bava Metzia devotes extensive tractate sections to clarifying the liability of each category. The Torah's framework establishes that responsibility correlates with benefit: the borrower, who benefits most, bears the greatest liability; the unpaid guardian, who receives nothing, bears the least.

Ibn Ezra observes that the oath before God (*shevuat Hashem*, 22:10) required of the guardian in cases of uncertainty elevates a civil dispute into a sacred act. The courtroom becomes a sacred space where God is invoked as witness. Sforno adds that this requirement deters false claims: one who swears falsely before God bears not merely legal penalty but spiritual consequence — a dimension entirely absent from purely secular legal systems.

## Protection of the Vulnerable

The chapter transitions from property law to social ethics with a series of categorical prohibitions. The stranger (*ger*) must not be oppressed, *"ki gerim heyitem b'eretz Mitzrayim"* — "for you were strangers in the land of Egypt" (22:20). This is one of thirty-six (or forty-six) times the Torah commands sensitivity toward the stranger, more than any other ethical commandment. Rashi warns: "Do not taunt the stranger with his origins."

The widow and orphan receive special protection: *"Im aneh te'aneh oto, ki im tza'ok yitz'ak elai, shamo'a eshma tza'akato"* — "If you afflict them, when they cry to Me, I will surely hear their cry" (22:22). The doubled verbs (*aneh te'aneh*, *tza'ok yitz'ak*, *shamo'a eshma*) intensify the threat. Rashi explains: this applies not only to widows and orphans but to any vulnerable person — the Torah specifies the most common cases. The Ramban adds that God takes the side of the powerless as a matter of covenantal commitment, and a society that exploits the vulnerable loses its standing before Heaven.

## Lending and Usury

The prohibition against charging interest (*neshekh*) on loans to the poor (22:24) is absolute. The Talmud (Bava Metzia 75b) extends this to all loans between Jews, establishing that lending at interest between fellow Israelites is fundamentally prohibited. Rashi explains the word *neshekh* — literally "a bite" — because interest bites into the borrower's substance, consuming the debtor gradually.

The commandment to return a pledged garment before sunset (22:25-26) — because it may be the poor person's only covering — reveals the Torah's integration of law and compassion. Nachmanides notes that the creditor has a legal right to the pledge but a moral obligation to return it. Law and mercy are not opposed; in the Torah's system, mercy *is* law.

## Sorcery and Sacred Boundaries

The prohibition *"Mekhashefah lo techayeh"* — "You shall not allow a sorceress to live" (22:17) — is placed amidst civil legislation, not in a ritual context. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 67a) discusses the nature of sorcery extensively, defining it as manipulation of creation through illicit spiritual means. Rambam interprets sorcery as fraud and delusion. The prohibition reflects the Torah's insistence that Israel's relationship with the supernatural must be mediated exclusively through Torah and prophecy, not through occult manipulation.
