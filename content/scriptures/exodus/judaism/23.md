## The Pursuit of Justice

Chapter 23 opens with commandments that form the ethical backbone of Jewish jurisprudence. *"Lo tisa shema shav"* — "Do not bear a false report" (23:1). Rashi interprets this as addressing both the witness who fabricates testimony and the judge who entertains it. The prohibition extends beyond the courtroom: the Talmud (Pesachim 118a) equates accepting *lashon hara* (malicious gossip) with bearing a false report. The integrity of speech is the foundation of justice; where words cannot be trusted, courts collapse.

The command *"Lo tihyeh acharei rabbim l'ra'ot"* — "Do not follow the majority to do evil" (23:2) — generates extensive Talmudic discussion. The Sanhedrin rules by majority vote (Sanhedrin 2a), yet the Torah warns against following a majority to do wrong. Rashi resolves the tension: in capital cases, a simple majority of one is insufficient for conviction — a majority of at least two is required. The Torah acknowledges the democratic principle while tempering it with caution against mob mentality.

The striking commandment to help your enemy's donkey when it collapses under its load (23:5) is analyzed by the Talmud (Bava Metzia 32a) as establishing the priority of mercy over hostility. If you encounter both a friend and an enemy needing help simultaneously, you must help the enemy first — in order to subdue your evil inclination. The mitzvah targets the internal disposition, not just the external act.

## Shemitah and Shabbat

The sabbatical year (*shemitah*) appears here in its agricultural form: in the seventh year, the land lies fallow and its produce is available to the poor and wild animals (23:10-11). Sforno links this to the manna: just as the manna taught dependence on God for daily sustenance, the shemitah teaches dependence for annual harvest. The economic risk of leaving fields uncultivated is an act of national faith.

Shabbat is reiterated (23:12) with the explicit purpose: *"l'ma'an yanuach shorcha va'chamorcha v'yinafesh ben amatcha v'ha'ger"* — "so that your ox and donkey may rest, and the son of your maidservant and the stranger may be refreshed." The verb *yinafesh* (be refreshed) shares a root with *nefesh* (soul). Rashi explains that Shabbat rest restores the *neshamah yeteirah* — the additional soul that accompanies every Jew on Shabbat. Even animals and non-Israelite workers benefit from the sacred pause.

## The Three Pilgrimage Festivals

The chapter establishes the *Shalosh Regalim* — the three pilgrimage festivals: Pesach (Matzot), Shavuot (the harvest), and Sukkot (the ingathering) (23:14-17). Three times a year, all males were to appear before the Lord. Rashi explains that *"v'lo yera'u panai reikam"* ("they shall not appear before Me empty-handed," 23:15) refers to the *korban re'iyah*, the offering brought upon appearing at the Temple.

The prohibition *"Lo tevashel gedi ba'chalev imo"* — "Do not cook a kid in its mother's milk" (23:19) — appears here for the first time. The Talmud (Chullin 115b) derives from its three Torah occurrences three distinct prohibitions: cooking, eating, and deriving benefit from the mixture of meat and milk. This verse is the halakhic foundation for one of Judaism's most distinctive dietary practices — the complete separation of meat and dairy — demonstrating how a seemingly narrow biblical statement generates an entire framework of observance through the Oral Torah.

## The Angel and the Promised Land

God promises to send an angel (*malakh*) before the people to guard and guide them into the land (23:20). Rashi identifies this angel as Metatron, whose name "contains the Name of his Master." The Ramban interprets the angel as the pillar of cloud and fire — a manifestation of divine presence, not a separate being. Either way, the passage warns that the angel "will not forgive your transgressions" (23:21), indicating a level of strict judgment.

The gradual expulsion of the land's inhabitants — *"m'at m'at agarshenu mipanekha"* ("little by little I will drive them out," 23:30) — teaches a principle of historical patience. Rashi explains: if the inhabitants were removed all at once, the land would become desolate and overrun by wild beasts. God's redemptive acts accommodate ecological and demographic realities. The promised future unfolds gradually, requiring sustained effort and faith across generations.
