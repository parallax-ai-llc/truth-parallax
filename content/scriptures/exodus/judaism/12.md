## The First Commandment to Israel as a Nation

Chapter 12 is one of the most halakhically dense chapters in the Torah, containing the laws of the Korban Pesach (Paschal lamb), matzah, and the establishment of the Hebrew calendar. Rashi famously cites Rabbi Yitzchak's question at the very beginning of Bereishit: the Torah should have begun here, with *"Ha'chodesh hazeh lakhem"* ("This month shall be for you," 12:2), since this is the first commandment given to Israel as a collective. The Torah began with creation only to establish God's ownership of the earth, so that no nation could accuse Israel of theft in taking the land of Canaan.

The commandment to establish Nissan as the first month reflects a revolutionary concept: Israel's calendar is not agricultural or astronomical but redemptive. Time itself is sanctified by its connection to the Exodus. The Ramban explains that by counting months from Nissan, every reference to a date becomes an implicit remembrance of the liberation — the Exodus is embedded in the very structure of Jewish time.

## The Korban Pesach

The lamb was selected on the tenth of Nissan and kept until the fourteenth (12:3-6). Rashi explains the four-day interval: the Israelites needed time to separate from the Egyptian worship of sheep, demonstrating their willingness to sacrifice the gods of their oppressors. The Midrash adds that the lamb was tied to the bedpost, and when Egyptians asked about it, the Israelites boldly declared their intention — an act of extraordinary courage.

The blood was placed on the doorposts and lintel with a bundle of hyssop (*ezov*), a humble plant that symbolizes the lowliness required for redemption. The Talmud (Pesachim 96a) derives numerous halakhot from these verses: the lamb must be roasted whole, not boiled or eaten raw; it must be consumed in one house and no bone may be broken; it must be eaten with matzah and bitter herbs (*maror*); and anything remaining must be burned by morning.

## The Night of the Exodus

At midnight, God struck every firstborn in Egypt (12:29). The Haggadah emphasizes: *"Ani v'lo malakh, Ani v'lo saraf"* — "I and not an angel, I and not a seraph." God Himself, without intermediary, executed this judgment. This teaching, rooted in the Mekhilta, underscores the intimacy of divine involvement in the redemption — it was not delegated.

Pharaoh rose at night and found death throughout his land. He sought Moshe and Aharon and begged them to leave: *"Kumu tze'u mi'tokh ami"* — "Rise up, get out from among my people" (12:31). The Midrash records that Pharaoh wandered the streets calling for Moshe, a complete reversal of their earlier confrontations. The one who said "Who is the Lord?" now begs His servants for mercy.

## Matzah: The Bread of Haste and Humility

The Torah explains that the Israelites baked matzot because *"lo yakhlu l'hitmahmeha"* — "they could not delay" (12:39). The dough had no time to rise. This historical explanation is complemented by a symbolic one from the Zohar: matzah is *lechem oni*, the bread of poverty and humility. Chametz (leaven), by contrast, represents the inflated ego. The Seder night is an exercise in spiritual deflation — encountering redemption from a posture of radical simplicity.

The Rambam codifies the laws of Pesach extensively in Hilkhot Chametz u'Matzah, all deriving from this chapter. The prohibition against chametz is so stringent that even the slightest amount is forbidden — unlike nearly all other food prohibitions, where the principle of *bittul* (nullification in a larger mixture) applies. The absolute nature of the chametz prohibition reflects the absolute character of the Exodus itself: the break from slavery must be total.

## The Perpetual Ordinance

The chapter concludes with the instruction that Pesach is *"chukat olam"* — an eternal statute (12:14). When children ask about its meaning (12:26), parents must tell the story. This verse is one of the four questions referenced in the Haggadah's treatment of the Four Sons. The *rasha* (wicked son) asks "What is this service to you?" — excluding himself from the community. The Haggadah's response — "to you and not to him; had he been there, he would not have been redeemed" — is not a rejection but a provocation designed to bring even the alienated child back into the conversation.

The six hundred thousand men, plus families, who departed Egypt (12:37) represent the foundational number of Jewish tradition — the number of souls present at Sinai, the number of letters hinted at in the Torah, the irreducible community of Israel.
