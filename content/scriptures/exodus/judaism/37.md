## Betzalel Builds the Ark

Chapter 37 describes Betzalel's construction of the sacred vessels, beginning with the *Aron* (Ark). The Torah attributes the Ark specifically to Betzalel: *"Vaya'as Betzalel et ha'Aron"* (37:1), while other vessels are described impersonally ("he made"). The Talmud (Berakhot 55a) explains that because Betzalel devoted himself to the Ark with particular intensity — it was the holiest object and contained the tablets of the covenant — it bears his name. The Midrash adds that Betzalel understood instinctively what Moshe had to be told: build the container (the Mishkan) before its contents (the vessels), just as one builds a house before furnishing it. When Moshe presented the instructions in the reverse order, Betzalel respectfully questioned the sequence. Moshe responded: "Perhaps you were in the shadow of God (*b'tzel El*) and you knew."

The Ark was acacia wood overlaid with gold inside and out, with a gold crown (*zer zahav*) around its top (37:2). The Talmud (Yoma 72b) identifies three crowns in the Mishkan: the crown of the Ark (Torah), the crown of the Table (royalty), and the crown of the Incense Altar (priesthood). The crown of Torah is available to all — *"ha'rotzeh litol, yavo v'yitol"* ("whoever wishes to take it, let them come and take it"). Unlike priesthood and royalty, which are hereditary, Torah mastery is open to every person in every generation.

## The Keruvim

The *kapporet* (cover) of pure gold, with two *keruvim* hammered from a single piece (37:6-9), was the locus of divine communication: *"V'dibarti itkha me'al ha'kapporet mi'bein shnei ha'keruvim"* ("I will speak to you from above the cover, from between the two cherubim," 25:22). The keruvim faced each other, their wings spread upward. The Talmud (Bava Batra 99a) notes an apparent contradiction: here they face each other, while in Divrei HaYamim (2 Chronicles 3:13) they face outward. The reconciliation is that when Israel does God's will, the faces turn toward each other in love; when Israel strays, they turn away.

The Zohar reads the keruvim as male and female aspects of the divine — the *mashpi'a* (bestower) and *mekabel* (receiver) — whose harmonious relationship channels blessing into the world. This imagery profoundly influenced Kabbalistic thought about the nature of holiness as fundamentally relational. The most sacred space in Judaism is defined not by a single figure but by a pair in loving encounter.

## The Shulchan (Table)

The table of showbread, overlaid with gold and crowned with a golden rim (37:10-16), was constructed with its vessels: dishes, ladles, supports, and covers. The Talmud (Menachot 96a) describes the table's frame structure that supported the twelve loaves in two stacks of six. Rashi explains that the supports (*menakiyot*) were tube-shaped dividers placed between the loaves to allow air circulation, preventing mold during the week they stood before God.

The Ramban connects the Table to the blessing of sustenance: just as the Menorah channels spiritual light, the Table channels material prosperity. The Talmud (Berakhot 55a) teaches: "Whoever wishes to become wise should face south [toward the Menorah]; whoever wishes to become wealthy should face north [toward the Table]." The Table's position in the Mishkan established the Jewish understanding that material blessing, like spiritual illumination, flows from God through designated sacred channels.

## The Menorah

Betzalel constructed the Menorah from a single *kikkar* (talent) of pure gold, beaten into shape (37:17-24). Seven branches, with cups shaped like almond blossoms (*meshukadim*), knobs, and flowers. Rashi reiterates that this was the vessel Moshe found most difficult to envision, until God showed him a fiery model. The Midrash adds that even with the model, Moshe could not replicate it, so God told him to cast the gold into the fire, and the Menorah emerged of its own accord.

The almond blossom motif (*shaked*) connects to the root meaning "to hasten" — God hastens (*shoked*) to fulfill His word (Yirmiyahu 1:11-12). The Menorah's design thus embodies the principle of divine urgency in bringing light to the world.

## The Golden Altar

The *Mizbe'ach ha'Ketoret* (Altar of Incense) was constructed of acacia wood overlaid with gold (37:25-28), with horns and a golden crown. Its placement inside the Mishkan, near the *parokhet*, made it the closest point of regular priestly service to the Holy of Holies. The Sforno observes that the incense altar represents prayer ascending to heaven — the ketoret was the olfactory counterpart of the Menorah's light, engaging a different sense in the worship of God.

The chapter lists the preparation of the anointing oil and the pure incense (37:29), described as the work of a *roke'ach* (perfumer). The crafting of sacred fragrances was itself a form of sacred art, and the Talmud (Keritot 6a) preserves the Avtinas family's secret techniques for compounding the incense — knowledge they guarded to prevent any secular replication of the sacred blend.
