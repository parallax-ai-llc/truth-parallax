## The Inauguration of the Priesthood

Chapter 29 describes the *miluim* — the seven-day consecration ceremony by which Aharon and his sons were inducted into the priesthood. The process involved washing, dressing in the priestly garments, anointing with oil, and offering a sequence of sacrifices: a bull for a sin offering, a ram for a burnt offering, and a second ram — the *eil ha'miluim* (ram of consecration) — whose blood was applied to the right ear, thumb, and big toe of the priests (29:20).

Rashi explains the symbolism of these three body parts: the ear, to hear God's word; the thumb, to perform sacred service; the toe, to walk in God's ways. The entire person — perception, action, and direction — is consecrated. The Ramban adds that the blood application corresponds to the purification ritual for the *metzora* (leper) in Vayikra 14, suggesting that consecration and purification are mirror images: one enters sacred service as one returns from impurity, through a complete reorientation of the self.

## The Consecration Offerings

The sin offering (*chatat*) of the bull (29:10-14) atoned for any inadvertent sins the priests might have committed. The Talmud (Horayot 12a) discusses the principle that one cannot serve as an atoner while bearing unatoned guilt. The bull was burned outside the camp — its flesh was not eaten — indicating the total surrender required of those entering priestly service.

The burnt offering (*olah*) of the first ram (29:15-18) was entirely consumed on the altar, representing complete self-dedication. Sforno explains that the *olah* — which ascends entirely to God — symbolizes the priest's total availability. Unlike other offerings where portions are eaten, the burnt offering holds nothing back. The priest who offers it learns the posture of service: unreserved, unrestricted, without personal remainder.

The second ram — the *eil ha'miluim* — was unique to this ceremony (29:19-28). Parts of it were waved before the Lord (*tenufah*), parts were burned on the altar, and parts were eaten by the priests. This threefold distribution — God's portion, the altar's portion, the priest's portion — established the pattern for all subsequent offerings. The Ramban sees this as a statement about the priestly role: the Kohen stands between God and the people, participating in both realms, belonging fully to neither.

## The Bread of Consecration

Alongside the ram, a basket of unleavened bread — *challot matzot*, *challot lechem*, and *rekikei matzot* (29:2) — was presented. The Talmud (Menachot 78a) discusses the three types and their preparation in detail. The connection to matzah reinforces the Exodus theme: priestly consecration is rooted in the liberation experience. The one who serves at the altar serves the God who brought Israel out of Egypt; the bread of poverty becomes the bread of consecration.

## The Daily Offering (Korban Tamid)

The chapter concludes with the institution of the *korban tamid* — the daily offering of two lambs, one in the morning and one in the evening (29:38-42). This perpetual offering framed each day with worship, creating a rhythm of sanctification that defined life at the Mishkan and later the Temple. The Talmud (Berakhot 26b) teaches that the three daily prayers — *Shacharit*, *Minchah*, and *Ma'ariv* — were later instituted to correspond to the daily offerings, so that when the Temple was destroyed, prayer replaced sacrifice as the vehicle of daily worship.

## "I Will Dwell Among the Children of Israel"

The chapter's climactic verses contain God's promise: *"V'shakhanti b'tokh Bnei Yisrael v'hayiti lahem l'Elokim"* — "I will dwell among the children of Israel and I will be their God" (29:45). This is the telos of the entire Mishkan project. The Ramban states that the indwelling of the *Shekhinah* among Israel was the restoration of the divine presence that had been in the Garden of Eden before sin drove it away. The Mishkan is, in this reading, a partial repair of the primal rupture between heaven and earth.

The final verse declares: *"V'yad'u ki Ani Hashem Elokeihem asher hotzeiti otam me'eretz Mitzrayim l'shakhni b'tokham"* — "They shall know that I am the Lord their God, who brought them out of Egypt to dwell among them" (29:46). The purpose of the Exodus was not merely political liberation but the establishment of divine intimacy. Freedom from Pharaoh was the precondition for dwelling with God.
