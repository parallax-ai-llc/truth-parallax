## The Copper Altar

The outer altar — the *Mizbe'ach ha'Nechoshet* (Copper Altar) — stood in the courtyard and was the site of all burnt offerings and most sacrifices (27:1-8). Made of acacia wood overlaid with copper, it measured five cubits square and three cubits high, with horns at its four corners. Rashi explains that the horns (*karnot*) served a practical function — the blood of certain offerings was applied to them — but also symbolized divine power. The Midrash (Tanchuma, Terumah 11) connects the four horns to the four directions, signifying that atonement radiates outward to the whole world.

The Talmud (Zevachim 62a) discusses the altar's precise location in the Temple courtyard, noting that its placement was determined by prophetic tradition and could not be altered. Nachmanides emphasizes that the altar was hollow (*n'vuv luchot*, 27:8), filled with earth at each encampment. This recalls the original altar law from Sinai: *"Mizbe'ach adamah ta'aseh li"* — "An altar of earth you shall make for Me" (20:21). Even the elaborate copper structure retained the simplicity of the earthen altar within it.

The Midrash asks why copper rather than gold or silver for the outer altar. Shemot Rabbah 38:8 answers that copper (*nechoshet*) evokes the word *nachash* (serpent) and alludes to the brazenness (*chutzpah*) of sin. The altar, which atones for sin, is made of the very material that symbolizes the human tendency toward transgression. Atonement meets the person where they are — in the rawness of their imperfection.

## The Courtyard

The courtyard (*chatzer*) was enclosed by hangings of twisted fine linen, supported by pillars of copper with silver hooks and bands (27:9-19). The courtyard measured one hundred cubits long and fifty cubits wide, creating a defined sacred precinct around the Mishkan. Sforno observes that the courtyard's linen walls were not solid barriers but translucent hangings — one could sense the sacred space from outside, even if entry was restricted. This mirrors the nature of holiness itself: it calls and attracts without coercing.

The entrance to the courtyard was on the eastern side, marked by a screen of blue, purple, crimson, and fine linen (27:16). Rashi notes that the entrance faced east, toward the rising sun, a deliberate orientation that would be maintained in the Jerusalem Temple. The Midrash interprets this as a polemic against sun worship: Israel faces the sunrise not to worship it but to worship the One who created it.

The measurements of the courtyard are carefully analyzed in the Talmud (Eruvin 23b) for their implications regarding the laws of enclosed spaces (*eruvin*). The Mishkan's courtyard became a halakhic paradigm for defining a *reshut ha'yachid* (private domain) on Shabbat.

## The Ner Tamid — Eternal Light

The chapter concludes with the commandment to bring *"shemen zayit zakh katit la'ma'or"* — "pure olive oil, beaten, for illumination" — to maintain a *ner tamid* (perpetual lamp) in the Tent of Meeting (27:20-21). Rashi explains that *katit* (beaten) means the olives were crushed in a mortar, not ground in a mill, to produce the clearest oil without sediment. Only the first drop was suitable for the Menorah; subsequent pressings could be used for meal offerings.

The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 36:1) asks: does God need light? The One who said "Let there be light" surely does not require human illumination. Rather, the lamp is for Israel's sake — it is a testimony (*edut*) to all the world that the Divine Presence dwells among them. The *ner tamid* that burns in every synagogue today descends from this commandment, a continuous thread linking contemporary worship to the wilderness Mishkan.

The phrase *"me'erev ad boker"* — from evening to morning (27:21) — indicates that the lamp burned through the night, the time of greatest darkness. The Sforno reads this symbolically: the light of Torah is most needed precisely when the world is darkest. The obligation to maintain the lamp *"chukat olam l'dorotam"* (an eternal statute for their generations) extends beyond the Mishkan to every place where Jews gather for worship and study.

Aharon and his sons are charged with tending the lamp *"lifnei Hashem"* — "before the Lord" (27:21). This priestly duty of maintaining light before the divine presence became, in later Jewish thought, a metaphor for the role of every Jew: to keep the light of Torah burning through the long night of exile, tending it faithfully until the dawn of redemption.
