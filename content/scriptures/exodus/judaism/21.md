## Parashat Mishpatim: From Sinai to Civil Law

The transition from the Aseret HaDibrot to the civil laws of Parashat Mishpatim is, according to Rashi, seamless and deliberate. The first word — *"V'eileh"* ("And these") — with the conjunctive *vav* teaches that just as the Dibrot were given at Sinai, so too these civil ordinances were given at Sinai. There is no hierarchy in which ritual law is divine and civil law is merely human convention. The laws governing an injured ox and the laws governing Shabbat have the same Author and the same authority.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 7a) teaches: *"Why is the section on judges placed immediately after the section on the altar? To tell you that the Sanhedrin should sit near the Temple."* Justice and worship are physically and spiritually adjacent. A society that prays but tolerates injustice, or that legislates but does not pray, is fundamentally incoherent.

## The Hebrew Servant

The laws begin with the *eved Ivri* — the Hebrew servant (21:2-6). A person who could not pay a debt or was sold by the court for theft serves for six years and goes free in the seventh. Rashi emphasizes: this is not slavery in the Egyptian sense. The Talmud (Kiddushin 22a) specifies that the master must provide the servant with living conditions equal to his own — *"Kol ha'koneh eved Ivri k'koneh adon l'atzmo"* ("Whoever acquires a Hebrew servant acquires a master for himself"). If there is only one pillow, the servant gets it.

The case of the servant who chooses to remain (21:5-6) — whose ear is pierced against the doorpost — provokes one of Rashi's most powerful comments, citing Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai: "The ear that heard at Sinai 'You shall not steal' and went and stole, or the ear that heard 'They are My servants' and went and acquired another master — let that ear be pierced." The physical mark on the ear is a direct callback to the Sinai revelation.

## "Eye for an Eye"

The phrase *"ayin tachat ayin"* (21:24) — "an eye for an eye" — is perhaps the most misunderstood verse in the Torah. The Oral Torah, unanimously and without exception, interprets this as monetary compensation. The Talmud (Bava Kamma 83b-84a) provides multiple proofs: What if the assailant is blind — do you blind a blind person? What if the injury causes death — then a capital case arises from a property offense? The principle of proportional *financial* restitution is what the Torah mandates.

Rashi cites this Talmudic consensus. The Rambam (Hilkhot Chovel u'Mazik 1:3) codifies it as settled law. Ibn Ezra, while acknowledging the oral tradition, notes that the Torah's stark language serves a pedagogical purpose: the offender deserves to lose his eye; the Torah's mercy commutes the sentence to monetary payment. The severity of the written language preserves the moral weight of the offense even as the oral tradition provides the practical remedy.

## Laws of Damages and Responsibility

The chapter establishes foundational principles of tort law that influenced legal systems far beyond Judaism. The goring ox (21:28-36), the open pit (to be discussed further in chapter 22), and the distinctions between intentional and unintentional harm create a comprehensive framework of liability. The Talmud (Bava Kamma) devotes hundreds of pages to elaborating these principles, developing categories of damage (*nezek*), pain (*tza'ar*), medical expenses (*ripui*), lost income (*shevet*), and humiliation (*boshet*).

Nachmanides observes that these laws immediately follow the revelation at Sinai to teach that building a just society is the primary application of the Torah received there. The mystical experience of God's voice must be translated into the prosaic details of property law and personal injury. Holiness, in the Jewish understanding, descends from the mountain into the marketplace.

## The Value of Human Life

The chapter includes capital offenses — murder, kidnapping, striking or cursing a parent — demonstrating that the Torah places the sanctity of human life and familial honor at the highest level of legal protection. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 37a) derives from the creation of a single human being that destroying one life is as if one destroyed an entire world. These laws in Mishpatim are the practical expression of that theological axiom.
