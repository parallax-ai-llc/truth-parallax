## "Who Is the Lord?"

Moshe and Aharon's first confrontation with Pharaoh yields not liberation but intensified oppression. Pharaoh's response — *"Mi Hashem asher eshma b'kolo?"* ("Who is the Lord that I should listen to His voice?" 5:2) — is, in rabbinic tradition, the paradigmatic statement of arrogance. Shemot Rabbah 5:14 records that Pharaoh consulted his records of deities and declared, "I have searched my books and found no mention of Him." The irony, the Midrash observes, is exquisite: the God who transcends all categories cannot be found in a catalogue of idols.

Rashi notes that Pharaoh's denial was not ignorance but willful defiance. He knew of the God of the Hebrews but refused to submit. This distinction matters halakhically and theologically: Pharaoh's punishment is just precisely because his rejection was informed and deliberate, not the result of honest error.

## Bricks Without Straw

Pharaoh's decree that the Israelites must now gather their own straw while maintaining the same quota of bricks (5:7-8) represents a refinement of cruelty. Sforno explains that this was calculated to destroy hope: if the people were consumed by impossible labor, they would have no energy to dream of freedom or to listen to prophetic voices. The strategy of overwhelming people with survival concerns to prevent spiritual awakening is a pattern the rabbis identified repeatedly in Jewish history.

The Egyptian taskmasters (*nogsim*) and the Israelite foremen (*shotrim*) form a layered system of oppression. The Talmud (Sotah 11a) praises the Israelite foremen who accepted beatings rather than enforce impossible quotas upon their brothers. Shemot Rabbah 5:20 teaches that these same foremen were later rewarded by being appointed to the Sanhedrin of seventy elders, because one who suffers on behalf of the community deserves to lead it.

## The Crisis of Faith

The chapter reaches its emotional nadir when the Israelite foremen confront Moshe and Aharon: "May God look upon you and judge, for you have made us odious in the eyes of Pharaoh" (5:21). The initial euphoria of chapter 4, when the people bowed in faith, has given way to bitter recrimination. Nachmanides observes that this oscillation between faith and despair is entirely natural and not condemned by the text — the people were suffering terribly, and their protest was human.

Moshe himself turns to God with raw anguish: *"Lamah hare'otah la'am hazeh? Lamah zeh shelachtani?"* — "Why have You done evil to this people? Why did You send me?" (5:22). Rashi comments that Moshe was not questioning God's existence or justice but asking for understanding — a form of prayer, not rebellion. Ibn Ezra adds that Moshe's directness with God is itself a mark of prophetic intimacy; only one who stands in genuine relationship with the Divine can voice such complaints.

## Theological Reflections

This chapter introduces a theme central to Jewish theology: the darkening before the dawn. Shemot Rabbah 5:22 offers the parable of a man who was told that a treasure lay buried under his house. He began to dig and found only rocks and mud. The deeper he dug, the worse it seemed — until suddenly the treasure appeared. So too, the redemption was preceded by the intensification of suffering.

The Maharal of Prague develops this idea systematically: genuine transformation requires the complete collapse of the previous state. Egypt's hold on Israel had to become total — the oppression absolute — before the liberation could be equally total. This is not a justification of suffering but a theological grammar for understanding the pattern of exile and redemption that defines Jewish historical experience.

Moshe's cry and God's apparent silence at the chapter's end leave the reader suspended in tension — a literary and spiritual device that the Torah employs deliberately, compelling the reader to continue and to trust that the answer will come.
