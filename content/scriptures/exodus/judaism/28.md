## Parashat Tetzaveh: The Garments of the Kohen Gadol

Parashat Tetzaveh is unique: it is the only parashah from Shemot through the end of Bamidbar in which Moshe's name does not appear. The Vilna Gaon explains that Moshe's selfless plea — *"Mecheini na mi'sifrecha"* ("Erase me from Your book," 32:32) — was partially fulfilled here. A righteous person's words carry consequences even when spoken conditionally. The parashah's focus on Aharon and his sons corresponds to the moment Moshe stepped aside for his brother's glory.

The eight garments of the Kohen Gadol (High Priest) are described *"l'khavod u'l'tifaret"* — "for glory and for beauty" (28:2). The Talmud (Zevachim 88b) teaches that each garment atoned for a specific sin: the *ketonet* (tunic) atoned for bloodshed, the *mikhnasayim* (trousers) for sexual immorality, the *mitznefet* (turban) for arrogance, the *avnet* (sash) for improper thoughts of the heart, the *choshen* (breastplate) for perversion of justice, the *ephod* for idolatry, the *me'il* (robe) for *lashon hara*, and the *tzitz* (headplate) for brazenness. The garments were not merely decorative but functionally redemptive.

## The Ephod and the Choshen

The *ephod* was an apron-like garment of gold, blue, purple, crimson, and fine linen (28:6-14). On its shoulder straps sat two *shoham* (onyx) stones, each engraved with six of the twelve tribal names. Rashi explains that the order of the names followed birth order, so that Aharon bore the tribes on his shoulders before God as a remembrance. The Kohen Gadol did not serve as an individual but as the representative of the entire nation.

The *choshen mishpat* (breastplate of judgment) was the most remarkable of all the garments (28:15-30). It held twelve precious stones, each engraved with a tribal name, arranged in four rows of three. The Talmud (Yoma 73b) identifies each stone with its tribe and its specific color. The Ramban explains that the tribes were arranged not by birth order but by the order of the camp formation — the breastplate was a map of Israel over the Kohen Gadol's heart.

## The Urim V'Tumim

Within (or behind) the choshen were placed the *Urim V'Tumim* — mysterious objects through which God communicated answers to national questions (28:30). The Talmud (Yoma 73b) describes how the letters on the stones would light up to spell out divine responses. Rashi explains that the Urim V'Tumim contained the ineffable Name of God, and it was this Name that caused the letters to illuminate.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Klei HaMikdash 10:11) notes that the Urim V'Tumim were absent from the Second Temple — one of several elements of diminished sanctity. Nachmanides regards the Urim V'Tumim as the highest form of prophetic consultation available to the nation, distinct from individual prophecy in that it served the entire people through the priestly office.

## The Me'il (Robe)

The robe was entirely of *techelet* (blue), with golden bells and embroidered pomegranates alternating on its hem (28:31-35). The bells rang as the Kohen Gadol moved, and the Torah warns: *"v'lo yamut"* — "so that he does not die" (28:35). Rashi explains that entering the sacred space without announcement is presumptuous and potentially fatal. The Talmud (Zevachim 88b) connects the bells to the atonement for *lashon hara*: the tongue that speaks evil is atoned for by the tongue of the bell — sound heals the damage caused by sound.

The pomegranates (*rimonim*) are a rich symbol in Jewish tradition. The Talmud (Berakhot 57a) teaches that even the emptiest among Israel is full of mitzvot as a pomegranate is full of seeds. Their placement alongside the bells represents the unity of beauty and function, silence and sound, in sacred service.

## The Tzitz (Headplate)

The golden headplate bore the inscription *"Kodesh la'Hashem"* — "Holy to the Lord" (28:36). Worn on the Kohen Gadol's forehead, it served to gain acceptance for offerings that had become ritually impure under certain conditions (28:38). The Talmud (Pesachim 77a) debates whether the *tzitz* atones even when not being worn. Beyond its halakhic function, the tzitz was a visible declaration: the Kohen Gadol's every thought, represented by the forehead, was consecrated to God.

The chapter concludes by extending the priestly garments to Aharon's sons, though their garments were simpler — tunic, sash, hat, and trousers. The principle is established: sacred service requires appropriate vestment, and the clothing transforms the wearer. *"Bigdei kehunah mekhadshim"* — the priestly garments sanctify (Sanhedrin 83b).
