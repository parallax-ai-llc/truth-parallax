## Erecting the Mishkan

On the first day of the first month — Rosh Chodesh Nissan, exactly one year after the Exodus — God commanded Moshe to erect the Mishkan (40:2). The Talmud (Shabbat 87b) teaches that this date bore ten unique consecrations: it was the first day of creation (according to Rabbi Yehoshua), the first day of the *nesi'im* (tribal princes) bringing their offerings, the first day of priestly service, the first day of public sacrifices, the first day of fire descending from heaven, and more. The convergence of beginnings on a single date signals that the Mishkan's inauguration was a moment of cosmic renewal.

Rashi, citing the Midrash Tanchuma, explains that Moshe erected the entire Mishkan by himself — a feat that seemed physically impossible given the weight of the boards and coverings. God told him: "Work with your hands, and it will appear as though you are raising it, but it will rise on its own." Human effort and divine power met at the point of sacred construction. The principle embedded in this miracle is that God does not ask the impossible without providing the means — but He requires the gesture of effort.

## Placement of the Vessels

Moshe placed each vessel in its designated position: the Ark in the Holy of Holies, the *parokhet* before it, the Table on the north side, the Menorah on the south, the golden incense altar in the Holy before the curtain, and the copper altar and laver in the courtyard (40:3-33). The Torah describes each placement with the refrain *"ka'asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe"* — "as the Lord commanded Moshe." The Midrash counts this phrase and connects it to the *ma'aseh bereishit* (act of creation): just as God's word created the universe in six utterances (each beginning "Let there be..."), Moshe's obedient placement of each vessel re-created sacred space through faithful execution.

The anointing of the Mishkan and its vessels with the sacred oil (40:9-11) consecrated everything it touched, elevating ordinary materials to the status of *kodesh*. The Rambam (Hilkhot Klei HaMikdash 1:12) codifies that once anointed, the vessels were permanently holy and could never be used for mundane purposes. The moment of anointing was irreversible — sanctity, once conferred, does not dissipate.

## Aharon and His Sons

Moshe then washed, dressed, and anointed Aharon and his sons (40:12-15), initiating the priestly dynasty. God declared that this anointing would serve them *"l'khunat olam l'dorotam"* — "as an eternal priesthood throughout their generations" (40:15). The Ramban observes that the priesthood is the only Torah office that is both hereditary and eternal — it cannot be removed by human authority, only suspended by historical circumstance (as occurred after the Temple's destruction). The Kohanim retain their status to this day, a living link to the Mishkan.

## The Cloud and the Glory

The chapter — and the entire book of Shemot — reaches its climax in verses 34-38: *"Vayekhas he'anan et Ohel Mo'ed u'khvod Hashem male et ha'Mishkan"* — "The cloud covered the Tent of Meeting, and the glory of the Lord filled the Mishkan" (40:34). The purpose of everything — the Exodus from Egypt, the revelation at Sinai, the covenant, the construction — was fulfilled in this moment. God dwelt among His people.

The Ramban sees the descent of the *Shekhinah* upon the Mishkan as the restoration of the presence that had rested on Sinai during the revelation. Sinai was temporary; the Mishkan made the divine presence portable and permanent. The God who spoke from the mountain now spoke from between the keruvim. The people who had trembled at the thunder now lived in proximity to the same Presence, mediated through sacred structure and priestly service.

Remarkably, the glory was so intense that *"v'lo yakhol Moshe lavo el Ohel Mo'ed ki shakhan alav he'anan"* — "Moshe could not enter the Tent of Meeting because the cloud rested upon it" (40:35). Even Moshe, who spoke with God face to face, was excluded when the fullness of the divine glory filled the space. Rashi distinguishes: when the cloud rested, Moshe could not enter; when God wished to speak with him, the cloud would lift, and Moshe would enter. The oscillation of presence and withdrawal, of revelation and concealment, became the rhythm of Israel's ongoing relationship with God.

## The Journey Ahead

The final verses describe the cloud guiding Israel's travels: when it lifted, they journeyed; when it settled, they encamped (40:36-38). The cloud by day and fire by night were visible *"l'einei kol beit Yisrael b'khol mas'eihem"* — "before the eyes of all the house of Israel in all their journeys" (40:38). The Ramban notes that Shemot ends not with arrival but with the beginning of a journey — the book of redemption concludes with the people still in the wilderness, guided by a cloud, moving toward a promise not yet fulfilled.

This open ending is deliberate. The Jewish reading tradition concludes the book by exclaiming *"Chazak, chazak, v'nitchazek!"* — "Be strong, be strong, and may we be strengthened!" The call is to continue: the Mishkan is built, the Presence has descended, but the journey through Vayikra, Bamidbar, and Devarim lies ahead. Redemption is not a single event but an ongoing process, sustained by the presence of God among a people in motion.
