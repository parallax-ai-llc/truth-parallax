## Overview

Exodus 35 marks the transition from divine instruction to human response. Moses assembles the congregation, reiterates the sabbath command, and invites freewill offerings for the construction of the Tabernacle. The people respond with extraordinary generosity — bringing gold, silver, bronze, fabrics, spices, and precious stones. Catholic tradition reads this chapter as a model of stewardship, communal worship, and the joyful offering of material goods for the glory of God.

## The Sabbath Reaffirmed

The chapter opens with a renewed sabbath command (35:1–3), including the striking detail that "you shall kindle no fire in all your dwelling places on the Sabbath day." Catholic interpretation notes that the sabbath is placed first — before the call for offerings — to establish the priority of rest and worship over even the most sacred labor. The Catechism teaches that the Lord's Day is "a day of protest against the servitude of work and the worship of money" (CCC 2172).

This ordering carries practical significance for Catholic Social Teaching. Pope Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* insisted on the worker's right to rest, and Pope John Paul II's *Dies Domini* warned that a culture without sabbath rest inevitably commodifies human beings. The sabbath command in Exodus 35 is not incidental to the Tabernacle project — it frames the entire endeavor in the context of trust in God rather than frantic productivity.

## The Freewill Offering

Moses calls for contributions from "whoever is of a generous heart" (35:5). The offering is voluntary — not imposed by taxation or compulsion. Catholic theology of stewardship draws directly on this principle. The Catechism teaches that the faithful have an obligation to provide for the material needs of the Church (CCC 2043), but this obligation flows from love, not coercion. St. Paul, echoing Exodus, taught: "Each one must give as he has decided in his heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver" (2 Corinthians 9:7).

The inventory of offerings is remarkably diverse: gold and silver, but also yarn, linen, goat hair, animal skins, wood, oil, spices, and gemstones (35:5–9). Every member of the community can contribute something. Catholic ecclesiology emphasizes that the Church is built not by the gifts of the wealthy alone but by the contributions of all the faithful — each according to their means and abilities. The widow's mite (Mark 12:41–44) is the Gospel counterpart of this Exodus principle.

## Women and Men Together

The text explicitly notes the participation of women: "All the women who were skillful spun with their hands" and "all the women whose hearts stirred them used their skill in spinning goat hair" (35:25–26). Catholic tradition affirms the indispensable role of women in the life and mission of the Church. While the ordained priesthood is reserved to men, the contribution of women to the building of the sanctuary — both in Exodus and throughout Church history — is acknowledged as essential and honored.

St. Thérèse of Lisieux, Doctor of the Church, described her vocation as being "love in the heart of the Church." The women of Exodus 35, whose skilled hands created the fabric of the Tabernacle, embody this vocation: they build the dwelling of God through their particular gifts.

## Bezalel and Oholiab Commissioned

The chapter concludes by reaffirming the calling of Bezalel and Oholiab (35:30–35), emphasizing both their Spirit-given ability and their capacity to teach others. Catholic tradition values not only the exercise of charisms but their transmission. The master-apprentice relationship in sacred art and craftsmanship is a form of tradition — handing on what has been received. The Catechism defines Tradition itself in these terms: the living transmission of the faith through teaching, life, and worship (CCC 78).

The Church's patronage of the arts — from the catacombs to the Sistine Chapel — reflects the conviction that beauty serves evangelization. Pope Benedict XVI frequently spoke of the "via pulchritudinis" — the way of beauty as a path to God. The commissioning of Bezalel and Oholiab is the biblical warrant for this conviction: God Himself calls artists and fills them with His Spirit for the work of making the invisible visible.

## Summary

Exodus 35 demonstrates that the dwelling of God is built by the free, joyful, and diverse contributions of an entire community — grounded in sabbath rest and animated by the Holy Spirit. Catholic stewardship, sacred art, and ecclesiology all trace their roots to this chapter's vision of a people united in generous offering.
