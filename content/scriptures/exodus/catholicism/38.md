## Overview

Exodus 38 records the construction of the bronze altar of burnt offering, the bronze basin, and the courtyard, followed by a detailed inventory of all the materials used. Catholic tradition reads this chapter on two levels: typologically, as a continuation of the sacrificial and sacred-space theology developed in earlier chapters; and practically, as a model of transparency and accountability in the stewardship of sacred resources.

## The Bronze Altar Constructed

The bronze altar of burnt offering (38:1–7) is built according to the specifications of Exodus 27. As the first object encountered upon entering the Tabernacle precincts, it establishes that approach to God requires sacrifice. Catholic theology holds that "the altar of the New Covenant is the Lord's Cross" (CCC 1182). Every Catholic altar — whether in a great cathedral or a simple mission chapel — participates in the one altar of Calvary.

The construction of this altar after the golden calf episode carries special significance. The same community that built an idol for false worship now builds an altar for true worship. Catholic sacramental theology recognizes this pattern: the sacrament of Penance restores the sinner to right worship, enabling those who have fallen to approach the altar once more. The Catechism teaches that "individual and integral confession of grave sins followed by absolution remains the only ordinary means of reconciliation with God and with the Church" (CCC 1497).

The bronze altar, built after Israel's lapse, embodies a principle St. Cyprian championed: return to true worship requires the path of repentance.

## The Bronze Basin

The bronze basin (38:8) is made from the mirrors of "the ministering women who ministered at the entrance of the tent of meeting." This striking detail — that women contributed their personal mirrors for sacred use — has drawn comment throughout Catholic tradition. The mirrors, instruments of self-regard, are transformed into a vessel of purification. St. Ephrem the Syrian saw in this a symbol of conversion: vanity is surrendered so that its material can serve holiness.

The basin's function — priestly washing before service at the altar — prefigures baptism and the *lavabo* of the Mass. The Catechism teaches that baptism is "the basis of the whole Christian life, the gateway to life in the Spirit, and the door which gives access to the other sacraments" (CCC 1213). No one approaches the altar of the New Covenant without first passing through the waters of baptism, just as no Levitical priest served at the bronze altar without first washing at the basin.

## The Courtyard

The construction of the courtyard hangings and pillars (38:9–20) establishes the boundary of the sacred precinct. The courtyard is open to all Israelites — unlike the Holy Place (restricted to priests) and the Most Holy Place (restricted to the high priest on the Day of Atonement). Catholic ecclesiology recognizes a similar structure: the church building is open to all, but certain zones and functions are reserved according to the sacramental and hierarchical ordering of the community.

The courtyard also represents the visible boundary of the worshipping community. Catholic teaching holds that the Church has visible boundaries — defined by baptism, profession of faith, and communion with the hierarchy (CCC 836–838) — while also affirming that grace works beyond those boundaries in ways known to God alone (*Lumen Gentium* 16).

## The Inventory

The chapter concludes with a meticulous inventory of materials: twenty-nine talents and 730 shekels of gold; one hundred talents and 1,775 shekels of silver; seventy talents and 2,400 shekels of bronze (38:21–31). The silver came from the census atonement money — a half-shekel per person. This accounting is remarkable in an ancient text and reflects a principle Catholic canon law has codified: those who administer the Church's temporal goods must exercise diligent stewardship and transparent accountability.

Canon 1284 requires Church administrators to act "with the diligence of a good householder." The silver from the atonement money — rich and poor contributing equally — underscores that the Church, like the Tabernacle, is built by all its members.

## Summary

Exodus 38 completes the external structures of the Tabernacle and provides a model of transparent stewardship. Catholic tradition reads the bronze altar as a type of the Cross, the basin as a type of baptism, the courtyard as the visible boundary of the worshipping community, and the inventory as a call to faithful accountability in the administration of sacred resources.
