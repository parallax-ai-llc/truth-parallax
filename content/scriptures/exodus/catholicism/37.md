## Overview

Exodus 37 records the actual construction of the Tabernacle's interior furnishings: the Ark of the Covenant, the table of showbread, the golden lampstand, the altar of incense, and the anointing oil and incense. Bezalel is named as the principal craftsman. While the chapter mirrors the instructions of chapters 25 and 30, Catholic tradition reads the construction narrative as theologically significant in its own right — the moment when divinely intended symbols take material form, prefiguring the Incarnation's principle that spirit and matter are united in God's saving plan.

## The Ark Constructed

Bezalel makes the Ark of acacia wood overlaid with gold, with its rings, poles, and mercy seat crowned by two cherubim with outstretched wings (37:1–9). The construction of the Ark is the construction of the holiest object in Israel's worship — the place where God's presence will dwell on earth.

Catholic Marian typology reaches its fullest expression here. The Litany of Loreto invokes Mary as "Ark of the Covenant" — the living vessel who bore the Word of God in her womb. Just as Bezalel crafted the Ark with the finest materials and utmost care, Mary was prepared by God through the Immaculate Conception to be a fitting dwelling for the Incarnate Word. The Catechism teaches that Mary was "enriched by God with gifts appropriate to such a role" (CCC 490, citing *Lumen Gentium* 56).

The mercy seat (*kapporet*) is the place of atonement — where God meets His people in mercy. Catholic theology identifies Christ as the true mercy seat (Romans 3:25). In every Catholic church, the tabernacle containing the Blessed Sacrament functions as the New Covenant Ark — the place of Real Presence where the faithful come to encounter the living God. The Catechism teaches that Christ is "truly, really, and substantially" present in the Eucharist (CCC 1374).

## The Table of Showbread Constructed

The table of showbread (37:10–16), with its dishes, plates, bowls, and flagons, is constructed for the perpetual display of bread in God's presence. Catholic Eucharistic theology sees this table fulfilled in the altar of every Catholic church. The showbread — twelve loaves representing the twelve tribes, perpetually before God's face — is a type of the Eucharistic bread that becomes the Body of Christ.

The Catechism cites the showbread among the figures of the Eucharist (CCC 1334). What the showbread signified — God's perpetual provision and His people's perpetual gratitude — the Eucharist accomplishes.

## The Lampstand Constructed

The golden lampstand (*Menorah*) with its seven branches (37:17–24) is hammered from a single piece of gold. Catholic tradition has seen in the Menorah multiple layers of meaning: the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit (Isaiah 11:2–3; CCC 1831), the seven sacraments, and Christ who is "the light of the world" (John 8:12).

The fact that the Menorah is beaten from a single piece of gold — not assembled from separate parts — has fascinated Catholic commentators. St. Bede interpreted this as a sign of the Church's unity: though she has many members and diverse gifts, she is one Body, one continuous work of the divine Craftsman. The Catechism affirms: "The Church is one because of her source: 'the highest exemplar and source of this mystery is the unity, in the Trinity of Persons, of one God'" (CCC 813).

## The Altar of Incense and Sacred Oils

The golden altar of incense (37:25–28) and the preparation of the anointing oil and incense (37:29) complete the interior furnishings. The altar of incense stands before the veil — the closest point of approach to God's presence in the Holy of Holies — and represents the prayer that ascends continually to God.

Catholic liturgical practice preserves this function — incense is used at the Gospel, offertory, and elevation of the Eucharist (GIRM 276). The sacred anointing oil prefigures the chrism used in baptism, confirmation, and ordination (CCC 1289).

## Summary

Exodus 37 transforms divine blueprints into material realities. Catholic tradition reads the construction of each furnishing as a step toward the Incarnation — the supreme moment when the invisible God takes material form. The Ark, the table, the lampstand, and the altar of incense all find their fulfillment in the Eucharistic liturgy, where Christ is present in Word, sacrament, and the gathered community.
