## The First Plague

Exodus 7 marks the beginning of the plague narrative, the great contest between the God of Israel and the powers of Egypt. Catholic tradition reads the plagues not merely as historical events but as a theological drama revealing God's sovereignty over creation, the justice of divine judgment, and the progressive unmasking of idolatry.

### Moses as God to Pharaoh

God tells Moses: "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron shall be your prophet" (7:1). The Church Fathers find in this designation a profound Christological type. Moses stands before Pharaoh as God's visible representative, speaking God's word through a prophetic mediator. Origen interprets this as a figure of the Incarnation: just as Moses bore God's authority before Pharaoh, Christ bears the fullness of divinity before the world. The Catechism teaches that Christ is "the mediator and the fullness of all revelation" (CCC 65), the definitive Moses who speaks not merely God's word but is Himself the Word.

### The Hardening of Pharaoh's Heart

God's declaration "I will harden Pharaoh's heart" (7:3) raises one of the most difficult questions in Catholic theology: the relationship between divine sovereignty and human freedom. The Catholic position, developed most systematically by Augustine and Aquinas, holds that God's hardening is not a coercion of the will but a permission — God withdraws the grace that would soften the heart, allowing the natural consequences of Pharaoh's own choices to unfold.

Aquinas explains that God "hardens" in the sense of not preventing hardness, just as the sun is said to "melt" wax and "harden" clay — the same cause producing different effects according to the disposition of the recipient (ST I, q.23, a.3). The Catechism affirms both divine omnipotence and human freedom: "God predestines no one to go to hell; for this, a willful turning away from God (a mortal sin) is necessary, and persistence in it until the end" (CCC 1037).

### The Staff Becomes a Serpent

When Aaron casts down his staff and it becomes a serpent, Pharaoh's magicians replicate the sign (7:10–12). Yet Aaron's serpent swallows theirs — a detail the Fathers read as the triumph of divine power over demonic imitation. Catholic demonology recognizes that evil can produce apparent wonders but cannot match the creative power of God (CCC 395, 2117). The swallowing of the magicians' serpents anticipates the ultimate swallowing of death by life in the resurrection (1 Cor 15:54).

Ambrose of Milan sees in the serpent-staff a figure of the cross: what appears to be an instrument of death (the serpent, the cross) becomes, in God's hands, the instrument of victory. This reading aligns with the Catholic liturgical tradition that celebrates the cross as the *lignum vitae*, the tree of life.

### The First Plague: Water Turned to Blood

The transformation of the Nile into blood (7:17–21) is the first of the ten plagues, and Catholic tradition reads it on multiple levels. Historically, it represents God's judgment on Egypt's dependence on the Nile as the source of life and prosperity — a judgment that reveals the fragility of all merely natural securities. Typologically, the Fathers see in the blood-Nile a contrast with the Eucharistic transformation: in Egypt, water becomes blood as a sign of death; in the Eucharist, wine becomes the Blood of Christ as a sign of life (CCC 1375–1376).

The plague also strikes at Egyptian religion directly. The Nile was worshiped as a deity — Hapi, the god of the annual flood. By turning the Nile to blood, God demonstrates His sovereignty over the natural forces that Egypt had divinized. The Catechism condemns idolatry as "a perversion of man's innate religious sense" that "divinizes what is not God" (CCC 2113). Each plague can be read as a systematic dismantling of the Egyptian pantheon, a revelation that the powers Egypt worships are creatures, not gods.

### The Magicians' Imitation

Pharaoh's magicians replicate the plague on a smaller scale (7:22), and this imitation gives Pharaoh the pretext to harden his heart. Catholic moral theology recognizes this dynamic: the existence of counterfeit spiritual experiences can serve as an excuse for refusing the genuine encounter with God. Discernment of spirits — the ability to distinguish authentic divine action from its imitations — becomes essential, a theme developed extensively by Ignatius of Loyola and enshrined in Catholic spiritual tradition (CCC 2847).

The chapter closes with Pharaoh turning away unmoved. The contest has begun, and it will escalate — not because God delights in destruction, but because Pharaoh's refusal deepens with each demonstration of divine power, necessitating ever more dramatic interventions until the final, devastating plague breaks through his resistance.
