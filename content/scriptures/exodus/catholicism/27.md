## Overview

Exodus 27 describes the bronze altar of burnt offering, the courtyard of the Tabernacle, and the provision of oil for the perpetual lamp. Catholic tradition reads these instructions as further unfolding the theology of sacrifice, sacred space, and God's abiding presence — themes that converge in the Eucharistic liturgy and the ordering of Catholic worship.

## The Altar of Burnt Offering

The bronze altar (27:1–8), positioned in the courtyard before the entrance to the Tabernacle, is the place where sacrificial animals are offered. It is the first object encountered upon entering the sacred precincts — no one approaches God's dwelling without passing through the place of sacrifice.

Catholic theology reads this altar as a type of the Cross. The Catechism teaches that "the altar of the New Covenant is the Lord's Cross, from which the sacraments of the Paschal mystery flow" (CCC 1182). Every Catholic altar is understood as a participation in the one altar of the Cross, and the placement of relics within or beneath the altar (a tradition dating to early Christianity) reinforces the connection between the Eucharistic sacrifice and the sacrificial deaths of the martyrs.

The Council of Trent defined that the Mass is a true sacrifice — not a new sacrifice separate from Calvary, but the same sacrifice made sacramentally present (Session 22, Chapter 2). The bronze altar in the Tabernacle courtyard, where Israel's sacrifices were offered continually, is thus the Old Testament anticipation of every Catholic altar on which the Eucharist is celebrated.

St. John Chrysostom meditated on the altar's significance, teaching that the Christian who approaches the altar approaches Calvary itself. The altar is not furniture; it is a theological statement about how human beings come into communion with God — through sacrifice, not presumption.

## The Courtyard

The courtyard (27:9–19) is bounded by linen hangings supported by bronze pillars. It creates a defined sacred space — open to the sky, yet set apart from the surrounding camp. Catholic ecclesiology sees in this arrangement a symbol of the Church's nature as both visible and spiritual. The Church has boundaries — defined by baptism, creed, and communion with the Bishop of Rome — yet she remains open to all who seek entry.

The graduated holiness of the Tabernacle complex (courtyard, Holy Place, Most Holy Place) corresponds to the traditional arrangement of Catholic churches, where narthex, nave, and sanctuary create a spatial progression toward the altar and the tabernacle containing the Blessed Sacrament. This architectural theology is not accidental but deliberate — a continuation of the Exodus pattern in which approach to God involves movement through zones of increasing holiness.

The fine linen hangings of the courtyard recall the white garments of the baptized. St. Cyril of Jerusalem compared the baptismal garment to the priestly vestments and the Tabernacle curtains, teaching that the newly baptized are clothed in holiness and enter the sacred precincts of the Church as a people set apart for God.

## Oil for the Lamp

The chapter closes with the command to bring "pure beaten olive oil" so that the lamp may burn continually before the LORD (27:20–21). Aaron and his sons are to tend the lamp "from evening to morning" — an unbroken witness to God's presence.

Catholic tradition sees in this perpetual flame the origin of the sanctuary lamp that burns in every Catholic church before the tabernacle containing the reserved Eucharist. The Code of Canon Law (can. 940) requires that a special lamp burn continuously near the tabernacle to indicate and honor the presence of Christ. This practice is a direct continuation of the Exodus command.

The olive oil also carries Christological significance. The word "Christ" means "anointed one" — the one upon whom the oil of the Spirit rests. The Catechism teaches that sacred chrism, perfumed olive oil consecrated by the bishop, is used in baptism, confirmation, and ordination as a sign of the Holy Spirit's anointing (CCC 1241, 1289, 1574). The oil that fuels the Tabernacle lamp thus points to the Spirit who sustains the light of Christ in the Church.

## Summary

Exodus 27 establishes the altar and courtyard as the context for sacrificial worship and introduces the perpetual lamp as a sign of God's enduring presence. Catholic tradition traces a direct line from these instructions to the altar, the sacred space, and the sanctuary lamp of every parish church — visible links in an unbroken chain of worship from Sinai to the present.
