## The Ten Commandments

Exodus 20 contains the Decalogue — the Ten Commandments — the single most important moral text in the Catholic tradition. No other passage of the Old Testament receives more extensive treatment in the Catechism of the Catholic Church, which devotes nearly five hundred paragraphs (CCC 2052–2557) to their exposition. The Decalogue is not merely an ancient legal code; it is, in Catholic teaching, the permanent and universal expression of the natural moral law, inscribed by God on the human heart and proclaimed at Sinai for all generations.

### The Preamble: Grace Before Law

"I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery" (20:2). Before any commandment is given, God identifies Himself as liberator. The Catechism insists on the significance of this order: "The Commandments take on their full meaning within the covenant. According to Scripture, man's moral action takes on its full meaning in and through the covenant" (CCC 2061). The moral life is not a burden imposed from without but a response to a God who has already acted in love. Catholic moral theology is, at its foundation, a theology of gratitude.

### The First Three Commandments: Love of God

The first tablet of the Law governs the human relationship with God. Catholic tradition numbers the commandments differently from the Protestant and Orthodox traditions, combining what others count as the first two commandments (no other gods; no graven images) into a single first commandment, and dividing the final commandment on coveting into two (covetousness regarding a neighbor's wife and covetousness regarding a neighbor's goods).

**The First Commandment** ("You shall have no other gods before me / You shall not make for yourself a carved image") establishes the absolute primacy of God. The Catechism expounds this commandment under the headings of faith, hope, and charity — the theological virtues — and condemns idolatry, superstition, irreligion, and atheism as violations of this fundamental obligation (CCC 2084–2141). The prohibition of images, in Catholic understanding, does not forbid all religious art but only the worship of images as though they were gods. The Second Council of Nicaea (787) definitively settled this question, affirming the veneration of icons and sacred images as legitimate acts of devotion directed ultimately to the persons they represent (CCC 2129–2132).

**The Second Commandment** ("You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain") protects the holiness of the divine name. Catholic teaching extends this to include prohibitions against blasphemy, perjury, and the casual use of God's name. The reverence for the divine name is connected to the theology of naming developed in Exodus 3: because God has revealed His name, it must be treated with the utmost respect (CCC 2142–2167).

**The Third Commandment** ("Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy") establishes the rhythm of work and worship. Catholic teaching transposes the Sabbath obligation to Sunday — the day of the resurrection — and makes participation in the Eucharistic liturgy the central act of Sunday observance. The Catechism teaches that "the Sunday celebration of the Lord's Day and his Eucharist is at the heart of the Church's life" (CCC 2177). The obligation to rest from servile work is maintained as a protection of human dignity and family life (CCC 2184–2188).

### The Fourth Through Tenth Commandments: Love of Neighbor

The second tablet governs human relationships and constitutes the foundation of Catholic social and moral teaching.

**The Fourth Commandment** ("Honor your father and your mother") is the bridge between the two tablets, establishing the family as the fundamental social unit and the first school of virtue (CCC 2197–2257). Catholic social teaching builds on this commandment its entire theology of the family, the duty of children toward parents, and the reciprocal obligations of parents toward children.

**The Fifth Commandment** ("You shall not kill") protects the inviolability of human life from conception to natural death. The Catechism's treatment is extensive, addressing murder, abortion, euthanasia, suicide, scandal, and the conditions for legitimate self-defense and just war (CCC 2258–2330). This commandment grounds the Catholic Church's consistent ethic of life.

**The Sixth and Ninth Commandments** ("You shall not commit adultery" and "You shall not covet your neighbor's wife") together govern the realm of human sexuality. The Catechism treats them as a unified field, presenting Catholic sexual ethics under the headings of chastity, the vocation to marriage, and offenses against the dignity of the conjugal union (CCC 2331–2400, 2514–2533).

**The Seventh and Tenth Commandments** ("You shall not steal" and "You shall not covet your neighbor's goods") together govern economic life and the use of material goods. Catholic social teaching — including the doctrines of the universal destination of goods, the preferential option for the poor, and the just wage — is developed under these headings (CCC 2401–2463, 2534–2557).

**The Eighth Commandment** ("You shall not bear false witness") protects truth in human communication. The Catechism addresses lying, detraction, calumny, and the obligations of media, while also affirming the seal of confession as an absolute obligation (CCC 2464–2513).

### The Decalogue and Christ

Jesus does not abolish the Decalogue but fulfills it: "Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them" (Matt 5:17). Catholic moral theology, following Aquinas, distinguishes between the ceremonial and judicial precepts of the Mosaic law (which were fulfilled and surpassed in Christ) and the moral precepts of the Decalogue (which remain permanently binding because they express the natural law). The Catechism teaches: "The Ten Commandments belong to God's revelation. At the same time they teach us the true humanity of man" (CCC 2070).

### The People's Fear and the Mediator

The chapter closes with the people's terrified response to the theophany: "You speak to us, and we will listen; but do not let God speak to us, lest we die" (20:19). Moses reassures them, but the scene underscores the need for a mediator — someone who can stand in God's presence and transmit His word to the people. Catholic theology finds here a type of Christ, the definitive mediator, and of the Church's teaching office, which receives and transmits the divine revelation for the salvation of all (CCC 85–87).
