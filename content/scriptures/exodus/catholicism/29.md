## Overview

Exodus 29 details the elaborate ritual for the consecration of Aaron and his sons as priests — involving washing, vesting, anointing with oil, and a series of sacrifices including a sin offering, a burnt offering, and a "ram of ordination." Catholic tradition reads this chapter as the Old Testament foundation for the sacrament of Holy Orders and as a rich source of typological connections to Christ's priestly sacrifice and the Eucharistic liturgy.

## Washing and Vesting

The ritual begins with washing Aaron and his sons with water at the entrance to the Tabernacle (29:4), then clothing them in the sacred garments (29:5–9). Catholic ordination rites preserve this structure. The candidate for ordination presents himself before the bishop, and the investiture with stole and chasuble (for a priest) or dalmatic (for a deacon) echoes the vesting of Aaron. The Catechism teaches that the liturgical vestments signify the minister's configuration to Christ and his role of acting *in persona Christi* (CCC 1548).

The initial washing recalls baptism — no one can receive Holy Orders without first having received the foundational sacrament. St. Ambrose taught that the washing of the priests prefigured both Christian baptism and the interior purification required of those who serve at the altar.

## Anointing with Oil

Aaron's head is anointed with oil (29:7), consecrating him as high priest. The anointing with sacred chrism is central to the Catholic rite of ordination for bishops and is reflected in the anointing of the hands of newly ordained priests. The Catechism explains that anointing with chrism "signifies the gift of the Holy Spirit" and confers a special character that configures the recipient to Christ the priest, prophet, and king (CCC 1574).

The title "Christ" — *Christos* in Greek, *Mashiach* in Hebrew — means "the Anointed One." Every priestly anointing in the Old Testament points forward to the supreme anointing of Jesus by the Holy Spirit at His baptism (Luke 3:22; Acts 10:38). The Catholic priest participates in Christ's anointing through the sacrament of Holy Orders, sharing in His priestly mission.

## The Sin Offering

A bull is offered as a sin offering (29:10–14). Aaron and his sons lay their hands on the bull's head before it is slaughtered — a gesture of identification and substitution. Catholic sacramental theology recognizes in this laying on of hands the origin of the gesture that remains central to ordination. The Catechism describes the imposition of hands as "the sign of the outpouring of the Holy Spirit" in ordination (CCC 1573).

The sin offering underscores that priests are sinners who need atonement. The *Confiteor* at the beginning of Mass echoes the logic of the sin offering that preceded Aaron's service.

## The Ram of Ordination

The second ram — the "ram of ordination" (29:19–28) — is unique. Its blood is applied to the right ear, right thumb, and right big toe of Aaron and his sons, signifying the consecration of their hearing (to listen for God's word), their hands (to perform sacred actions), and their feet (to walk in holiness). Blood is also sprinkled on the garments, making the vestments themselves participants in the sacrificial consecration.

Catholic commentators have seen in this threefold application a pattern for priestly formation: the priest must be a man of the Word (ear), of liturgical action (hand), and of holy life (foot). Pope Benedict XVI frequently spoke of the priest's threefold identity as one who listens, celebrates, and witnesses.

## The Perpetual Offering

The chapter concludes with instructions for the daily *tamid* offering — two lambs, one in the morning and one in the evening (29:38–42). God declares: "I will dwell among the people of Israel and will be their God" (29:45). The perpetual sacrifice maintains the covenant relationship and God's presence among His people.

Catholic tradition sees the *tamid* fulfilled in the daily celebration of the Mass throughout the world. From sunrise to sunset, the Eucharistic sacrifice is offered without ceasing — the perpetual offering of the New Covenant. The Catechism calls the Eucharist the "source and summit of the Christian life" (CCC 1324), echoing the centrality of the *tamid* in Israel's worship.

## Summary

Exodus 29 reveals ordination as a process of washing, vesting, anointing, and sacrifice — a pattern preserved in the Catholic rite of Holy Orders. More profoundly, it points to Christ, the true High Priest who consecrated Himself through His own blood, and whose sacrifice is perpetuated in the Mass.
