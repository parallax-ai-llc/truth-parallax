## The Passover

Exodus 12 is arguably the most important chapter in the Old Testament for Catholic theology. The institution of the Passover, the death of Egypt's firstborn, and the departure from Egypt constitute the foundational event that the Church reads as the supreme type of Christ's death and resurrection. The Catechism states unequivocally: "The Eucharist is the memorial of Christ's Passover" (CCC 1339–1340), and every element of the Catholic Eucharistic liturgy is rooted in this chapter.

### The Passover Lamb

God commands each household to select "a lamb without blemish, a male a year old" (12:5), to keep it until the fourteenth day of the month, and then to slaughter it at twilight. The blood is to be placed on the doorposts and lintel; the flesh is to be roasted and eaten with unleavened bread and bitter herbs.

Catholic Christology identifies the Passover lamb with Christ in the most explicit terms. Paul declares: "Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed" (1 Cor 5:7). The Gospel of John coordinates Christ's crucifixion with the slaughter of the Passover lambs in the Temple (John 19:14, 36), and the Catechism teaches that "Jesus knew and embraced his Father's plan of salvation, which the prophets had proclaimed: he is the Lamb who takes away the sin of the world" (CCC 608).

The prescription that no bone of the lamb shall be broken (12:46) finds its typological fulfillment in John 19:36, where the soldiers do not break Jesus' legs on the cross. This is not a coincidental detail but, in Catholic reading, a divine orchestration that confirms Christ as the antitype of the Passover sacrifice.

### The Blood on the Doorposts

"The blood shall be a sign for you, on the houses where you are. And when I see the blood, I will pass over you, and no plague will befall you to destroy you" (12:13). The Church Fathers read the blood on the doorposts as a figure of the sign of the cross. Origen notes that the blood is placed on the two side posts and the lintel — forming the shape of the cross over the entrance. Just as the blood of the lamb protected Israel from the destroying angel, the blood of Christ, applied through baptism and renewed in the Eucharist, protects the faithful from eternal death (CCC 1225).

Augustine sees in the blood-mark a type of the baptismal seal: the *character* or indelible spiritual mark that baptism imprints on the soul (CCC 1272–1274). As the destroying angel recognized the blood and passed over the marked houses, so the judgment of God recognizes in the baptized the mark of Christ and the claim of the covenant.

### The Eucharistic Meal

The manner of eating — in haste, with loins girded, sandals on feet, and staff in hand (12:11) — indicates that this is a meal for pilgrims, people on the move toward freedom. Catholic Eucharistic theology draws heavily on this context. The Eucharist is *viaticum* — food for the journey — not a leisurely banquet but the nourishment of a people in transit from the bondage of sin to the freedom of the Kingdom (CCC 1517, 1524).

The unleavened bread (*matzah*) carries dual significance. Practically, there was no time for the dough to rise (12:39). Theologically, Paul interprets the absence of leaven as the removal of "the old leaven of malice and evil" (1 Cor 5:8). The Roman Canon of the Mass, which remains the first Eucharistic prayer of the Latin Rite, explicitly connects the Eucharistic bread to the Passover: it is offered as the "holy bread of eternal life."

### The Death of the Firstborn

"At midnight the LORD struck down all the firstborn in the land of Egypt" (12:29). Catholic theology confronts this devastating event within the framework of the Paschal mystery. The death of Egypt's firstborn is the consequence of Pharaoh's refusal to release God's "firstborn son" — Israel (4:22). The Catechism reads the entire narrative as pointing toward the one Firstborn whose death will accomplish universal liberation: Christ, "the firstborn of all creation" and "the firstborn from the dead" (Col 1:15, 18; CCC 381).

The great cry that goes up in Egypt — "there was not a house where someone was not dead" (12:30) — is the sound of a world experiencing the full consequence of its rejection of God. Catholic theology holds that this cry is answered, ultimately, by another cry — the cry from the cross (Mark 15:37) — in which the death of God's own Firstborn absorbs and redeems all human suffering.

### The Memorial Command

"This day shall be for you a memorial day, and you shall keep it as a feast to the LORD; throughout your generations, as a statute forever, you shall keep it as a feast" (12:14). The concept of *zikkaron* (memorial) is foundational for Catholic sacramental theology. The Catechism teaches that the Eucharistic memorial is not a mere recollection of past events but a making-present of the saving reality: "When the Church celebrates the Eucharist, she commemorates Christ's Passover, and it is made present: the sacrifice Christ offered once for all on the cross remains ever present" (CCC 1364).

This understanding of memorial — as a liturgical act that transcends time and makes the past salvifically present — is the theological heart of Catholic worship, and it finds its origin here in Exodus 12.

### The Departure from Egypt

The chapter closes with the Israelites' departure: "about six hundred thousand men on foot, besides women and children" (12:37). A "mixed multitude" goes with them (12:38) — non-Israelites who join the exodus. Catholic ecclesiology finds in this detail an early sign of the universal scope of God's salvation: the people of God has never been a purely ethnic or national entity but has always included those drawn by faith from outside the original community (CCC 543–546).
