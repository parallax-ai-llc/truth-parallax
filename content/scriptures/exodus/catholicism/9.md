## Livestock and Boils

Exodus 9 records three plagues — pestilence on livestock, boils on persons, and a devastating hailstorm — escalating the confrontation between God and Pharaoh from economic disruption to bodily affliction to cosmic destruction. Catholic tradition reads this intensification as both a historical narrative and a theological lesson about the consequences of persistent refusal of grace.

### The Plague on Livestock

The fifth plague strikes Egypt's livestock — horses, donkeys, camels, herds, and flocks — while Israel's animals are spared (9:1–7). In the ancient world, livestock represented wealth, military power, and social status. Catholic social teaching recognizes that God's judgment often falls on the structures of wealth and power that sustain injustice. The plague on livestock dismantles the economic foundation of Pharaoh's regime, demonstrating that material prosperity built on oppression has no security.

The Fathers note that the continued distinction between Egypt and Goshen (9:4) reveals God's discriminating justice — He does not punish indiscriminately but targets the instruments of oppression while protecting the vulnerable. This principle informs the Catholic understanding of God's justice as always tempered by mercy and directed toward conversion rather than mere retribution (CCC 1040).

### The Plague of Boils

The sixth plague brings suffering directly upon human bodies: "boils breaking out in sores on man and beast throughout all the land of Egypt" (9:10). The magicians themselves are afflicted and "could not stand before Moses" (9:11). This detail marks their final appearance in the narrative — the representatives of Egypt's religious establishment are reduced to helplessness.

Catholic theology reads the boils as a sign that sin's consequences are not merely external but inscribed on the person. The body itself bears witness to the disorder introduced by the refusal of grace. Gregory of Nyssa, in the *Life of Moses*, interprets the boils allegorically as the visible manifestation of the spiritual corruption that Pharaoh's hardness has produced. This resonates with the Catholic understanding of the effects of sin on the whole person — body and soul — and the corresponding need for integral healing in the sacrament of anointing of the sick (CCC 1520–1523).

### The Hailstorm and Its Theological Significance

The seventh plague is the most dramatic yet: a hailstorm "such as had never been in all the land of Egypt since it became a nation" (9:24). The description includes fire mingled with hail — a theophanic element that recalls God's appearance at Sinai and anticipates the apocalyptic imagery of Revelation. Catholic eschatology draws connections between the Exodus plagues and the cosmic judgments described in the Book of Revelation, understanding both as manifestations of divine sovereignty over creation (CCC 677).

Before sending the hail, God issues an unprecedented warning: "Send therefore, and gather your livestock and all that you have in the field into shelter" (9:19). This detail is theologically significant. Even in judgment, God provides a way of escape. The Catechism teaches that God "desires all men to be saved" (CCC 74, citing 1 Tim 2:4). The warning before the hail reveals that God's plagues are not arbitrary punishments but calls to repentance — opportunities for conversion that Pharaoh consistently refuses.

Notably, "those among the servants of Pharaoh who feared the word of the LORD" brought their servants and livestock inside (9:20). This is the first indication that some Egyptians are responding to God's revelation — a detail Catholic universalism treasures, as it demonstrates that God's saving action is not restricted to the chosen people but extends to all who hear and respond.

### Pharaoh's Confession and Its Limits

Under the hail's devastation, Pharaoh makes his most explicit confession yet: "This time I have sinned; the LORD is in the right, and I and my people are in the wrong" (9:27). This statement contains all the verbal elements of genuine repentance — admission of sin, acknowledgment of God's justice, acceptance of guilt. Yet it proves empty: as soon as the hail ceases, Pharaoh hardens his heart again (9:34–35).

Catholic moral theology distinguishes between verbal confession and interior conversion. The Council of Trent taught that valid sacramental confession requires not only the naming of sins but genuine contrition — sorrow of soul and detestation of sin committed, together with a firm purpose of amendment (CCC 1451). Pharaoh's confession lacks this interior dimension; it is wrung from him by fear and evaporates with the removal of the threat.

### The Purpose of the Plagues

God declares the ultimate purpose of the confrontation: "For this purpose I have raised you up, to show you my power, so that my name may be proclaimed in all the earth" (9:16). Catholic theology reads this not as a statement of divine cruelty but of divine pedagogy. The plagues are God's self-revelation to a world sunk in idolatry — a dramatic demonstration that the gods of Egypt are nothing and that YHWH alone is God. As the Catechism teaches, God's glory consists in the manifestation of His goodness, and all of creation exists to declare that glory (CCC 293–294).
