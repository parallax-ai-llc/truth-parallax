## Overview

Exodus 25 begins the detailed instructions for the Tabernacle and its furnishings — a section that extends through chapter 31. God commands Moses to receive an offering from the people and to build a sanctuary "that I may dwell in their midst" (25:8). Catholic tradition reads these chapters with intense sacramental interest, seeing in every detail a divinely intended prefiguration of Christ, the Church, and the sacraments. The Catechism affirms that the Old Testament liturgical forms were "a preparation for and figure of" the sacraments of the New Covenant (CCC 1093).

## "That I May Dwell in Their Midst"

The foundational purpose of the Tabernacle is stated in 25:8 — God desires to dwell among His people. Catholic theology sees this verse as the key to salvation history. The Word became flesh and "tabernacled" among us (John 1:14, where the Greek *eskenosen* deliberately echoes the Hebrew *mishkan*, "tabernacle"). The Church building, where the Blessed Sacrament is reserved, continues this divine indwelling. The Catechism teaches that "in the liturgy of the New Covenant every liturgical action, especially the celebration of the Eucharist and the sacraments, is an encounter between Christ and the Church" (CCC 1097).

## The Ark of the Covenant

The Ark (25:10–22) — a gold-covered chest containing the tablets of the law, topped by the mercy seat (*kapporet*) and two cherubim — is the holiest object in Israel's worship. Catholic typology reads the Ark in multiple directions simultaneously.

First, the Ark is a type of the Blessed Virgin Mary. Just as the Ark contained the Word of God inscribed on stone, Mary bore the Word of God incarnate. The Catechism and the Litany of Loreto invoke Mary as "Ark of the Covenant." Luke's account of the Visitation (Luke 1:39–56) deliberately echoes 2 Samuel 6, where the Ark travels to the hill country of Judah — paralleling Mary's journey to Elizabeth. St. Athanasius, St. Ambrose, and St. Ephrem all developed this Marian reading.

Second, the mercy seat is a type of Christ. St. Paul calls Christ the *hilasterion* — the "mercy seat" or "place of atonement" (Romans 3:25). The Catechism teaches that Christ is both priest and victim, the one in whom God and humanity are reconciled (CCC 614). The cherubim gazing down at the mercy seat prefigure the adoration that heaven directs toward the crucified and risen Lord.

## The Table of Showbread

The table of showbread (25:23–30) held twelve loaves of bread in the perpetual presence of God. Catholic Eucharistic theology sees here a direct prefiguration of the Eucharist. The bread placed before God's face anticipates the Bread of Life set upon the altar. The Catechism cites the showbread among the Old Testament figures of the Eucharist (CCC 1334). St. Thomas Aquinas drew the connection explicitly: what was mere figure in the Tabernacle becomes reality in the Mass, where bread becomes the Body of Christ (*Summa Theologiae* III, q. 73, a. 6).

## The Menorah

The seven-branched lampstand (25:31–40) was to burn continually before God. Catholic tradition reads the Menorah as a symbol of the sevenfold gifts of the Holy Spirit (Isaiah 11:2; CCC 1831) and of Christ, the light of the world. The sanctuary lamp that burns perpetually before the tabernacle in every Catholic church echoes the Menorah's unceasing flame — a visible sign that the Real Presence abides.

## The Heavenly Pattern

God commands Moses to build "according to the pattern shown you on the mountain" (25:40). The Letter to the Hebrews cites this verse to argue that the earthly Tabernacle was a "copy and shadow of the heavenly things" (Hebrews 8:5). Catholic liturgical theology takes this principle seriously: the earthly liturgy participates in the heavenly liturgy. The Catechism teaches that in the Eucharist, the Church joins "the liturgy celebrated in the holy city of Jerusalem toward which we journey as pilgrims" (CCC 1090).

## Summary

Exodus 25 inaugurates the Tabernacle instructions with objects of extraordinary sacramental density. The Ark, the table, and the Menorah together prefigure the central realities of Catholic worship — the Real Presence, the Eucharistic sacrifice, and the indwelling of the Holy Spirit — establishing a pattern that the Church recognizes as divinely intended from the beginning.
