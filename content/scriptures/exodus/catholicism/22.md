## Overview

Exodus 22 continues the Book of the Covenant with detailed statutes governing property, restitution, lending, and the protection of the vulnerable. Catholic tradition reads these laws as concrete applications of the seventh, eighth, and tenth commandments — "You shall not steal," "You shall not bear false witness," and "You shall not covet" — and as early expressions of the social teaching the Church would develop over centuries. The Catechism grounds its treatment of economic justice in precisely these biblical foundations (CCC 2401–2463).

## Property and Restitution

The opening verses address theft of livestock, property damage, and the obligation of restitution (22:1–15). Catholic moral theology has always insisted that theft creates an obligation to make restitution. The Catechism states plainly: "The seventh commandment forbids theft, that is, usurping another's property against the reasonable will of the owner" and requires that "commutative justice requires the restitution of stolen goods" (CCC 2408, 2412). The Exodus provisions — requiring a thief to repay double or more — embody this principle in its earliest scriptural form.

St. Thomas Aquinas developed a nuanced theology of property from these roots, teaching that private ownership is legitimate but that goods carry a "universal destination" — they exist ultimately for the benefit of all (*Summa Theologiae* II-II, q. 66). Pope Leo XIII drew on this tradition in *Rerum Novarum*, and the Second Vatican Council reaffirmed it: "God intended the earth and everything in it for the use of all peoples" (*Gaudium et Spes* 69). The property laws of Exodus 22 thus stand at the beginning of a long Catholic tradition of balancing private ownership with social responsibility.

## Protection of the Vulnerable

Some of the chapter's most powerful verses concern those whom society might easily exploit: the stranger, the widow, and the orphan (22:21–24). God speaks with fierce directness: "If you do mistreat them, and they cry out to me, I will surely hear their cry." Catholic Social Teaching identifies the "preferential option for the poor" as a principle rooted in passages exactly like this one. The Catechism teaches that "God blesses those who come to the aid of the poor and rebukes those who turn away from them" (CCC 2443).

Pope Francis has emphasized this Exodus imperative repeatedly, calling the Church to hear the cry of the poor as God hears it. The encyclical *Fratelli Tutti* (2020) echoes Exodus 22:21 in its insistence that the treatment of migrants and strangers is a measure of a society's moral character.

## Lending and Usury

The prohibition against charging interest to a fellow Israelite (22:25) became the foundation of the Catholic Church's long-standing teaching against usury. The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) forbade clergy from lending at interest, and subsequent councils extended the prohibition more broadly. While modern Catholic moral theology distinguishes legitimate return on investment from exploitative lending, the principle articulated in Exodus remains: the vulnerable must not be exploited in their need. The Catechism warns against practices that "reduce human beings to servitude" through unjust economic structures (CCC 2414).

## Holiness and Dedication to God

The chapter's closing verses command that the firstborn be dedicated to God (22:29–30) and that the people be holy (22:31). Catholic tradition reads the offering of the firstborn as a type of the consecration that every baptized person makes — and supremely as a prefiguration of the offering of Christ, the "firstborn of all creation" (Colossians 1:15). The call to holiness anticipates the universal call proclaimed at Vatican II: all the faithful, not only clergy and religious, are called to the perfection of charity (*Lumen Gentium* 40; CCC 2013).

## Sorcery and Idolatry

The prohibitions against sorcery (22:18) and sacrificing to other gods (22:20) reflect the first commandment's absolute demand for exclusive worship. The Catechism treats divination, sorcery, and magic as grave offenses against the virtue of religion (CCC 2115–2117), reading these Exodus prohibitions as permanently valid moral norms rather than culturally limited taboos.

## Summary

Exodus 22 reveals a God deeply concerned with economic justice, protection of the weak, and the ordering of society toward holiness. Catholic tradition treasures these laws as seeds of the social doctrine that would flower in the Church's magisterial teaching across two millennia.
