## Moses' Signs

Exodus 4 continues the dialogue between God and Moses at the burning bush, now focusing on the signs that will authenticate Moses' mission. Catholic tradition reads these signs not merely as miraculous credentials but as sacramental anticipations — visible realities that convey invisible grace and point toward the definitive signs Christ will perform.

### The Three Signs and Sacramental Typology

God grants Moses three signs: a staff that becomes a serpent, a hand that becomes leprous and is restored, and water from the Nile that turns to blood on dry ground (4:2–9). The Church Fathers interpret each sign allegorically. Origen reads the serpent-staff as a figure of the cross — the instrument of death that becomes, in God's hands, an instrument of salvation. Just as Moses grasped the serpent by the tail and it became a staff again, Christ took hold of death and transformed it into the instrument of life (cf. CCC 1505).

The hand that becomes leprous and is healed prefigures, in patristic reading, the sacrament of reconciliation — the sinner's condition made visible and then restored through divine power. Augustine connects this sign to the Church's ministry of healing, noting that God not only reveals the wound but provides the remedy. The transformation of water into blood anticipates both the plagues to come and, more profoundly, the Eucharistic mystery in which the fruit of the vine becomes the Blood of Christ (CCC 1375).

The Catholic theology of sacramental signs finds an important foundation in passages like these. The Catechism teaches that "the sacraments of the Church now continue and actualize, for the present, the wonders God wrought for his people in the past" (CCC 1094). Moses' signs are early instances of God using material reality to communicate divine power — the same principle that governs the sacramental economy.

### Moses' Continued Reluctance

Moses protests that he is "slow of speech and of tongue" (4:10). God's response is remarkable: "Who has made man's mouth? ... Is it not I, the LORD?" (4:11). Catholic theology reads this exchange as a revelation of God's sovereign creativity and His power to equip those He calls. The Catechism, reflecting on the charisms given to the baptized, affirms that "grace is first and foremost the gift of the Spirit who justifies and sanctifies us" (CCC 2003).

Yet God also accommodates Moses' weakness by appointing Aaron as his spokesman (4:14–16). This concession illustrates the Catholic principle of divine condescension — God adapts His plan to human limitation without abandoning His purpose. Ambrose sees in the pairing of Moses and Aaron a prefiguration of the relationship between contemplative prayer and active ministry: Moses receives the word from God, while Aaron proclaims it to the people.

### The Mysterious Encounter at the Lodging Place

One of the most enigmatic passages in Exodus occurs at 4:24–26, where the LORD "met him and sought to put him to death." Zipporah circumcises their son and touches Moses' feet with the foreskin, declaring him "a bridegroom of blood." This obscure episode has generated extensive commentary in Catholic tradition.

The Fathers generally read it as underscoring the absolute necessity of the covenant sign. Moses, who is about to lead Israel out of Egypt, must first ensure that his own household bears the mark of the covenant. The Catholic reading draws an analogy to baptism: no one can mediate God's salvation to others while neglecting the sacramental foundations of their own relationship with God. The Catechism teaches that circumcision "prefigured baptism, the sacrament of new birth in the faith of Christ" (CCC 527, cf. Col 2:11–12).

The title "bridegroom of blood" resonates with the nuptial theology that pervades Catholic reading of salvation history. The covenant relationship between God and Israel is consistently figured as a marriage, reaching its fulfillment in Christ the Bridegroom and the Church His Bride (CCC 796). The blood of circumcision, like the blood of the Passover lamb, points forward to the blood of the New Covenant shed on the cross.

### The Return to Egypt

Moses' return to Egypt with the divine commission marks the transition from vocation to mission. Catholic spiritual theology sees in this movement a universal pattern: the encounter with God in prayer (the burning bush) must issue in action on behalf of God's people. As the Catechism teaches, "prayer and Christian life are inseparable" (CCC 2745). Moses does not remain on Horeb; he descends to confront Pharaoh. The contemplative encounter becomes the source of prophetic courage.

Aaron's meeting with Moses and the elders' response of faith — "the people believed" (4:31) — completes the chapter with a note of hope. The community of faith receives the signs, believes the message, and bows in worship. This pattern of sign, faith, and worship anticipates the structure of the Church's own liturgical life, where the sacramental signs evoke the faith of the assembled people and issue in the act of adoration.
