## God Remembers the Covenant

Exodus 6 responds to Moses' anguished prayer at the end of chapter 5 with a solemn divine reaffirmation of the covenant. God's self-revelation deepens, the genealogy of Moses and Aaron is established, and the stage is set for the decisive confrontation with Pharaoh. Catholic tradition reads this chapter as a pivotal moment in the progressive revelation of God's name and nature.

### The Revelation of the Name YHWH

God declares to Moses: "I appeared to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob, as God Almighty (*El Shaddai*), but by my name the LORD (*YHWH*) I did not make myself known to them" (6:3). This verse has generated extensive theological reflection. Catholic exegesis, following the Pontifical Biblical Commission's guidelines, understands this not as a flat contradiction of Genesis (where the name YHWH appears) but as a statement about the deepening quality of revelation. The patriarchs knew God's power; now, through the Exodus, Israel will experience the full significance of the divine name — the God who is, who acts, who saves.

Thomas Aquinas reads this progressive revelation as consistent with the principle that God accommodates His self-disclosure to human capacity (ST II-II, q.174, a.6). The Catechism affirms that "God has revealed himself to Israel progressively and under different names" (CCC 204) and that the full content of the divine name is only revealed in Christ.

### The Fourfold Promise

God's address to Moses in 6:6–8 contains four "I will" declarations: "I will bring you out... I will deliver you... I will redeem you... I will take you as my people." These four promises became, in Jewish liturgical tradition, the basis for the four cups of the Passover Seder. Catholic Eucharistic theology connects this directly to the cup of the New Covenant. When Christ takes the cup at the Last Supper, He fulfills all four promises simultaneously — bringing out, delivering, redeeming, and constituting a new people (CCC 1334, 1340).

The language of redemption (*ga'al*) in particular carries deep Catholic resonance. The *go'el*, or kinsman-redeemer, pays the price to liberate a family member from bondage. Catholic soteriology identifies Christ as the ultimate *Go'el* — the one who, by assuming human nature, became our kinsman and paid the price of our liberation with His own blood (CCC 517, 601).

### The Failure of Israel to Listen

Despite this magnificent promise, "they did not listen to Moses, because of their broken spirit and harsh slavery" (6:9). This poignant detail reveals a truth that Catholic pastoral theology takes seriously: suffering can so devastate the human person that even the word of God cannot penetrate the anguish. The Catechism notes that "the desire for God is written in the human heart" yet acknowledges that this desire can be "forgotten, overlooked, or even explicitly rejected" under the pressure of suffering (CCC 29).

Gregory of Nyssa reads this failure to listen as a spiritual condition — the soul so habituated to bondage that it cannot imagine freedom. The cure, in Gregory's reading, is not argument but action: God must demonstrate His power through mighty deeds before the enslaved heart can believe in the possibility of liberation.

### The Genealogy of Moses and Aaron

The genealogical list in 6:14–25, which traces the lineage of Moses and Aaron through the tribe of Levi, serves a theological purpose beyond historical record. Catholic tradition understands genealogies as instruments of salvation history, situating God's chosen instruments within the concrete web of human relationships and historical continuity. Just as Matthew's genealogy places Christ within Israel's history (Matt 1:1–17), this Levitical genealogy establishes the priestly credentials of the Exodus mediators.

The emphasis on the tribe of Levi anticipates the institution of the Levitical priesthood at Sinai. Catholic theology of holy orders sees the Levitical priesthood as a type of the ordained ministry — a consecrated lineage set apart for the service of God and His people (CCC 1539–1553). Aaron, who appears here as Moses' brother and spokesman, will become the first high priest, a figure the Letter to the Hebrews identifies as a type of Christ the eternal high priest (Heb 5:4–5).

### Commission Renewed

The chapter closes with a renewed commission: Moses and Aaron are sent again to Pharaoh. The repetition underscores a Catholic theological principle: God's purposes are not defeated by initial failure. The divine commission endures through setback and discouragement. This is the pattern of apostolic mission throughout Church history — the gospel is preached, rejected, and preached again, because the word of God "shall not return to me empty" (Isa 55:11; CCC 2584).
