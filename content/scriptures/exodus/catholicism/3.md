## The Burning Bush

Exodus 3 contains one of the most theologically dense passages in the entire Old Testament: the revelation of the divine name at the burning bush. Catholic tradition has found in this chapter foundational material for its understanding of God's nature, the theology of vocation, and the Christological reading of the Hebrew scriptures.

### Holy Ground and the Sense of the Sacred

God commands Moses to remove his sandals, "for the place on which you are standing is holy ground" (3:5). The Catechism draws on this moment to teach about the proper attitude of prayer — reverence, humility, and attentiveness to the divine presence (CCC 2777). The Church Fathers see in the burning bush a manifestation of the divine holiness that sanctifies the material world without destroying it. The bush burns but is not consumed — a paradox that becomes, in patristic and liturgical tradition, one of the most enduring images of the Virgin Mary, who bore the fire of divinity in her womb without being consumed. The Byzantine and Latin liturgical traditions both employ the burning bush as a Marian image, and it appears frequently in Catholic iconography.

### The Revelation of the Divine Name

When Moses asks God's name, he receives the enigmatic response: *Ehyeh asher ehyeh* — "I AM WHO I AM" (3:14). This passage is the cornerstone of Catholic philosophical theology. Thomas Aquinas, in the *Summa Theologiae* (I, q.13, a.11), identifies this self-naming as the revelation that God's essence is His existence — that God alone is *ipsum esse subsistens*, subsistent Being itself. The Catechism draws directly on this tradition: "God revealed himself to his people Israel by making his name known to them. A name expresses a person's essence and identity and the meaning of this person's life. God has a name; he is not an anonymous force" (CCC 203).

The Fourth Gospel's "I AM" sayings of Christ (John 8:58: "Before Abraham was, I am") are understood in Catholic Christology as Jesus' explicit claim to the divine identity revealed at the burning bush. The *ego eimi* declarations are thus not merely assertions of pre-existence but affirmations of ontological identity with the God of Exodus.

### The Call of Moses and the Theology of Vocation

Moses' reluctance — "Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh?" (3:11) — establishes a pattern that recurs throughout salvation history: the prophet's sense of unworthiness before the divine commission. The Catholic theology of vocation holds that God's call always exceeds the natural capacities of the one called and is accompanied by the grace necessary for its fulfillment. The Catechism, in discussing the ordained ministry, notes that every vocation is ultimately rooted in God's initiative, not human merit (CCC 1578).

Gregory of Nyssa devotes extensive attention to Moses' call in the *Life of Moses*, reading it as a paradigm of the spiritual life. The ascent to Mount Horeb represents the soul's journey toward God through progressive purification. The removal of sandals signifies the stripping away of worldly attachments — what the mystics call *apatheia* — so that the soul may stand naked before the divine presence.

### God Who Hears and Acts

God declares: "I have seen the affliction of my people... I have heard their cry... I know their sufferings, and I have come down to deliver them" (3:7–8). This fourfold declaration — seeing, hearing, knowing, descending — encapsulates the Catholic understanding of a God who is not remote but intimately involved in human history. The language of "coming down" anticipates the Incarnation, where God descends definitively into the human condition (CCC 456–460).

Catholic liberation theology draws heavily on this passage. Gustavo Gutierrez, in *A Theology of Liberation*, argues that the God of Exodus reveals Himself primarily in the act of liberating the oppressed. The Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, while cautioning against reductive readings, affirms that "God, in his goodness and wisdom, chose to reveal himself and to make known to us the hidden purpose of his will... by which through Christ, the Word made flesh, man might in the Holy Spirit have access to the Father" (CCC 51, citing *Dei Verbum* 2).

### The Promise of Worship

God tells Moses that the sign of his commission will be that the people "shall serve God upon this mountain" (3:12). The goal of liberation is worship. Catholic theology insists that freedom from oppression is ordered toward freedom for communion with God — a principle that distinguishes the Catholic reading of Exodus from purely political interpretations. As Joseph Ratzinger wrote in *The Spirit of the Liturgy*, "The freedom into which Israel was led was not simply the absence of external coercion; it was directed toward the service of God in worship."
