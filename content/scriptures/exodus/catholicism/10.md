## Locusts and Darkness

Exodus 10 presents the eighth and ninth plagues — locusts that devour every remaining green thing in Egypt and a darkness so thick it can be felt. These plagues intensify the theological drama toward its climax, and Catholic tradition reads them as signs of creation being progressively undone by the rejection of God, anticipating the cosmic darkness that will attend the crucifixion.

### The Plague of Locusts

The locust swarm is described in terms of totality: "They covered the face of the whole land, so that the land was darkened, and they ate all the plants in the land" (10:15). The image of a land stripped bare of vegetation reverses the creative act of the third day, when God commanded the earth to bring forth vegetation (Gen 1:11–12). Catholic theology recognizes in the plagues a progressive uncreation — the systematic reversal of the order God established at the beginning. Where God brought forth life, Pharaoh's refusal brings desolation.

The Church Fathers see in the locusts an image of the destructive power of sin when it is allowed to proliferate unchecked. Ambrose interprets the locusts as symbols of disordered passions that consume the soul's fruitfulness. Augustine, in his commentary on the Psalms, connects the locust imagery to the spiritual devastation of heresy, which strips the Church of its verdant doctrine.

### Pharaoh's Servants Speak

Before the locusts arrive, Pharaoh's own servants urge him to relent: "Do you not yet understand that Egypt is ruined?" (10:7). This intervention is significant in Catholic political theology. The advisors recognize what the ruler refuses to see — that his obstinacy is destroying the commonwealth. Catholic social teaching holds that political authority exists for the common good and that rulers who pursue their own will at the expense of their people betray the trust of their office (CCC 1903–1904, 2234–2237).

Pharaoh's subsequent bargaining — offering to let the men go but not the families (10:11) — represents another partial concession that Moses rejects. The Catholic reading sees in Moses' insistence on the whole community a prefiguration of the Church's understanding that salvation is not an individual affair but a communal reality. The family, not the isolated individual, is the basic unit of God's saving work (CCC 2201–2206).

### The Plague of Darkness

The ninth plague is among the most theologically rich: "There was thick darkness in all the land of Egypt three days. They did not see one another, nor did anyone rise from his place for three days, but all the people of Israel had light where they lived" (10:22–23). Catholic tradition finds multiple layers of meaning in this darkness.

First, the darkness strikes at Ra, the supreme deity of the Egyptian pantheon — the sun god. The Catholic reading sees this as the definitive demonstration of God's supremacy over the most powerful of Egypt's gods. The Catechism teaches that "the divine name 'I Am' or 'He Is' expresses God's faithfulness: despite the faithlessness of men's sin and the punishment it deserves, he keeps 'steadfast love for thousands'" (CCC 211). The God who overcomes the sun overcomes all lesser powers.

Second, the three days of darkness prefigure the three days of Christ's burial. Catholic liturgical tradition draws this connection explicitly in the Easter Vigil, which begins in darkness — the *Exsultet* proclaims: "This is the night when Christ broke the chains of death and rose triumphant from the grave." The ninth plague's darkness anticipates the cosmic darkness at the crucifixion (Luke 23:44–45) and the three days before the resurrection.

Third, Gregory of Nyssa interprets the darkness spiritually in the *Life of Moses* as the soul's entry into the "luminous darkness" of divine mystery — the stage of the mystical journey where rational knowledge gives way to contemplative unknowing. This paradoxical reading, in which darkness becomes a higher form of light, profoundly influenced the Catholic mystical tradition, especially the writings of Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart, and John of the Cross.

### Light in Goshen

The contrast between Egypt's darkness and Israel's light (10:23) is a powerful image that pervades Catholic theology. The Gospel of John opens with language that echoes this Exodus scene: "The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it" (John 1:5). The Catechism teaches that Christ is "the Light of the world" (CCC 748) and that the Church, illuminated by Christ, brings that light to the nations.

The baptismal liturgy employs this Exodus imagery directly. The newly baptized receive a candle lit from the Paschal candle with the words: "Receive the light of Christ." They pass from the darkness of Egypt into the light of Goshen — from death to life, from bondage to freedom, from ignorance to knowledge of God.

### Pharaoh's Final Threat

The chapter ends with Pharaoh driving Moses from his presence with the threat of death: "Get away from me; take care never to see my face again, for on the day you see my face you shall die" (10:28). Moses' reply — "As you say, I will not see your face again" — carries an ominous double meaning. The final plague is imminent, and when it falls, it will not be Moses who dies.
