## Overview

Exodus 21 presents the first detailed legal code given to Israel after the Decalogue — the so-called "Book of the Covenant" (Exodus 21–23). Catholic tradition reads these ordinances not as mere civil regulations of a Bronze Age society, but as an early expression of the natural law that the Decalogue summarized in principle. The Catechism affirms that the Old Law is "the first stage of revealed Law" and is "summed up in the Ten Commandments" (CCC 1961–1962). The practical statutes of Exodus 21 represent the concrete working-out of those commandments in the social order.

## Servant Laws and Human Dignity

The opening verses regulate the treatment of Hebrew servants (21:1–11). While the institution of servitude is not abolished, the regulations introduce striking protections: a servant must be freed after six years; a female servant cannot be treated as disposable property; bodily harm by a master grants the servant freedom (21:26–27). Catholic interpretation sees in these provisions a nascent recognition of the dignity of every human person — a dignity that the Church would later articulate fully.

The Catechism teaches that "every human person, created in the image of God, has the natural right to be recognized as a free and responsible being" (CCC 1738). Pope Leo XIII's encyclical *Rerum Novarum* (1891) and Pope John Paul II's *Laborem Exercens* (1981) stand in continuity with the Exodus concern for workers' rights, insisting that labor must never reduce a person to the status of a commodity. The six-year limit on servitude witnesses to the principle that no economic arrangement may permanently deprive a person of liberty.

## The Lex Talionis

The famous "eye for eye, tooth for tooth" formula (21:23–25) has often been misread as endorsing vengeance. Catholic exegesis, following the Church Fathers, understands it differently. St. Augustine argued that the lex talionis was a *limitation* on vengeance — requiring proportionality where unchecked retribution would escalate. The Catechism echoes this reading: the Old Law "prepares for and disposes toward conversion and faith in God the Savior" (CCC 1964).

Christ's teaching in the Sermon on the Mount does not contradict the lex talionis but fulfills it by calling disciples beyond proportional justice to the perfection of mercy (Matthew 5:38–42). The Catholic tradition thus sees a trajectory from Exodus 21 through the prophets to the Gospel: justice is never abandoned, but it is completed by charity. As St. Thomas Aquinas noted, the Old Law contained moral, ceremonial, and judicial precepts — the moral precepts remain binding, while the judicial precepts (like the lex talionis) expressed principles of justice that now find fuller expression in canon law and Catholic social thought (*Summa Theologiae* I-II, q. 104, a. 3).

## Protection of Life

Verses on homicide (21:12–14), assault (21:18–19), and injury to a pregnant woman (21:22–25) reveal a deep concern for the sanctity of human life. The distinction between intentional murder and accidental killing (21:13) anticipates the moral categories of Catholic ethics, including the principle of double effect. The Catechism affirms that "human life is sacred because from its beginning it involves the creative action of God" (CCC 2258).

The passage addressing harm to a pregnant woman and her unborn child has held particular significance in Catholic moral theology. The Church reads this text as further evidence of Scripture's consistent witness that human life from conception merits legal protection — a conviction reiterated in *Evangelium Vitae* (1995) and CCC 2270–2275.

## Typological Reading

The Church Fathers also read the servant laws typologically. Origen saw the Hebrew servant freed in the seventh year as an image of the Christian set free by Christ in the "sabbath rest" of the New Covenant. The movement from slavery to freedom recapitulates the larger Exodus narrative and points to the liberation that baptism confers — freedom from the slavery of sin (CCC 1221, 1237).

## Summary

Exodus 21, when read within Catholic tradition, is far more than ancient case law. It is a witness to the moral law inscribed in human hearts, an early chapter in the story of God's pedagogy leading humanity toward the fullness of justice and mercy revealed in Christ.
