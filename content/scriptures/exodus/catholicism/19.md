## At Mount Sinai

Exodus 19 narrates Israel's arrival at Mount Sinai, the covenant proposal, and the terrifying theophany that precedes the giving of the Law. Catholic tradition reads this chapter as the decisive moment when God constitutes Israel as a covenantal people — a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (19:6) — and finds in the Sinai event a type of the Church's own constitution at Pentecost.

### The Covenant Proposal

God instructs Moses: "You yourselves have seen what I did to the Egyptians, and how I bore you on eagles' wings and brought you to myself. Now therefore, if you will indeed obey my voice and keep my covenant, you shall be my treasured possession among all peoples... and you shall be to me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (19:4–6). This passage is among the most theologically dense in the Old Testament.

The Catechism draws on it to explain the nature of the covenant: God's initiative precedes any human response. Before commanding obedience, God recounts what He has already done — "I bore you on eagles' wings." Catholic covenant theology insists that law always comes after grace; commandment follows deliverance. The moral life is a response to God's saving action, not a precondition for it (CCC 2060–2063).

The designation "kingdom of priests" is foundational for the Catholic understanding of the priesthood of all believers. The First Letter of Peter quotes this passage directly (1 Pet 2:9), and the Catechism teaches that all the baptized share in the common priesthood of the faithful: "The whole community of believers is, as such, priestly" (CCC 1546). At the same time, Catholic theology distinguishes between the common priesthood and the ministerial priesthood, which differs "in essence and not only in degree" (CCC 1547) — a distinction that emerges more fully when Aaron is consecrated.

### The Preparation of the People

God commands the people to consecrate themselves for three days before the theophany: they must wash their garments, abstain from sexual relations, and set boundaries around the mountain (19:10–15). Catholic sacramental theology sees in this preparation a type of the catechumenate — the period of purification and instruction that precedes the reception of the sacraments.

The requirement to wash garments prefigures baptismal washing. The three-day period anticipates the Easter Triduum — the three days of preparation that culminate in the resurrection and the baptism of catechumens at the Easter Vigil. The Catechism teaches that "the sacraments of initiation — Baptism, Confirmation, and the Eucharist — lay the foundations of every Christian life" (CCC 1212), and the Sinai preparation models the reverence and readiness that should accompany their reception.

The setting of boundaries around the mountain (19:12–13) teaches the Catholic virtue of reverence before the sacred. The holy is not to be approached casually or without preparation. This principle governs Catholic liturgical discipline — the requirement of the Eucharistic fast, the discipline of confession before communion for those in mortal sin, and the general disposition of awe and humility that the tradition cultivates in worship (CCC 1385–1389).

### The Theophany

"On the morning of the third day there were thunders and lightnings and a thick cloud on the mountain and a very loud trumpet blast, so that all the people in the camp trembled" (19:16). The Sinai theophany is one of the most dramatic descriptions of divine manifestation in Scripture. The mountain is wrapped in smoke "because the LORD had descended on it in fire" (19:18), and the whole mountain trembles.

Catholic theology reads the Sinai theophany as a manifestation of God's transcendent holiness — the *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* that Rudolf Otto described and that the Catholic liturgical tradition seeks to communicate. The theophanic elements — fire, cloud, trumpet, earthquake — reappear at Pentecost (Acts 2:1–4), where "tongues as of fire" descend on the apostles. The Catechism draws the Sinai-Pentecost connection explicitly: "Pentecost was the fulfillment of the Sinai theophany" (cf. CCC 696–697).

Gregory of Nyssa, in the *Life of Moses*, interprets the darkness on the mountain as the highest stage of mystical knowledge. God dwells in "thick darkness" (19:9) because His infinite nature transcends all human comprehension. The soul that ascends toward God must pass through the darkness of unknowing — beyond images, beyond concepts — into the direct encounter with the incomprehensible mystery. This interpretation profoundly shaped the Catholic mystical tradition, particularly the apophatic theology of Pseudo-Dionysius and the contemplative teaching of John of the Cross.

### Moses as Mediator

Moses alone ascends the mountain and enters the cloud (19:20). He stands between God and the people, receiving the divine word and transmitting it below. Catholic theology sees in Moses' mediation a type of Christ's unique mediatorial role: "There is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (1 Tim 2:5; CCC 618). Yet Catholic theology also recognizes a participated mediation: the ordained ministry, the intercession of the saints, and the teaching authority of the Church all share, in a subordinate way, in the one mediation of Christ (CCC 1544–1545).

### The Sinai Covenant and the New Covenant

The Sinai covenant, established in this chapter and detailed in the following ones, is central to Catholic salvation history. The Catechism teaches that it is a permanent gift — "the Old Covenant has never been revoked" (CCC 121) — while also affirming that it finds its fulfillment in the New Covenant inaugurated by Christ at the Last Supper. The relationship is one of promise to fulfillment, shadow to reality, preparation to completion (CCC 1964–1967).
