## Song of Moses and Miriam

Exodus 15 contains the great victory hymn sung by Moses and the Israelites after the Red Sea crossing, followed by Miriam's response and the first crisis in the wilderness at Marah. Catholic tradition treasures this chapter as one of the foundational liturgical texts of the Old Testament — a song that the Church sings to this day in the Easter Vigil and in the Liturgy of the Hours.

### The Song of Moses (15:1–18)

"I will sing to the LORD, for he has triumphed gloriously; the horse and his rider he has thrown into the sea" (15:1). This canticle is among the oldest poems in the Bible and occupies a privileged place in Catholic worship. It is sung as the responsorial after the third reading (Exodus 14) at the Easter Vigil, making it the Church's primary song of baptismal triumph. The congregation that has just heard the narrative of the Red Sea crossing now responds with the same words Israel sang on the far shore.

The theological content of the Song is rich. It celebrates God as warrior ("The LORD is a man of war," 15:3), as incomparable deity ("Who is like you, O LORD, among the gods?" 15:11), and as the one who guides His people to His "holy abode" (15:13). Catholic theology reads these themes Christologically: Christ is the divine warrior who conquers death (CCC 2853), the incomparable Son of the Father (CCC 441–445), and the one who leads the Church to the heavenly Jerusalem.

The Song reaches its climax in a vision of God's sanctuary: "You will bring them in and plant them on your own mountain, the place, O LORD, which you have made for your abode, the sanctuary, O LORD, which your hands have established" (15:17). Catholic ecclesiology sees this as a prophetic anticipation of the Temple, the Church, and ultimately the heavenly dwelling — the place where God and His people are united forever (CCC 756, 1042–1050).

### The Structure of Biblical Praise

The Song of Moses establishes a pattern that pervades Catholic liturgical prayer: praise arises from the experience of God's saving action. The Catechism teaches that "praise is the form of prayer which recognizes most immediately that God is God. It lauds God for his own sake and gives him glory, quite beyond what he does, but simply because HE IS" (CCC 2639). Yet this pure praise is characteristically ignited by specific acts of deliverance — the song does not abstract from history but celebrates the concrete event at the sea.

This is why the Catholic liturgical tradition places canticles — including the Song of Moses, the Magnificat, and the Benedictus — at the heart of its daily prayer. The Liturgy of the Hours employs Exodus 15 as a canticle at Morning Prayer, ensuring that the Church begins each day with the language of Exodus liberation.

### Miriam's Song and the Role of Women

"Then Miriam the prophetess, the sister of Aaron, took a tambourine in her hand, and all the women went out after her with tambourines and dancing. And Miriam sang to them: 'Sing to the LORD, for he has triumphed gloriously'" (15:20–21). Miriam is identified as a prophetess — the first woman so designated in Scripture. Catholic tradition honors her as a figure of women's participation in the prophetic and liturgical life of God's people.

The Catechism affirms that "from the beginning, God made man and woman with a vocation to collaborate in the work of creation" (cf. CCC 307, 369). Miriam's leadership in worship anticipates the vital role women have played in Catholic liturgical and spiritual tradition — from the women at the empty tomb who first proclaimed the resurrection to the great mystics and doctors of the Church such as Teresa of Avila and Therese of Lisieux.

### The Waters of Marah (15:22–25)

After three days in the wilderness without water, the Israelites arrive at Marah, where the water is bitter and undrinkable. They grumble against Moses, and God shows him a tree that, when thrown into the water, makes it sweet. The Church Fathers read this episode as a type of the cross. Origen sees the tree as the wood of the cross that transforms the bitter waters of death into the sweet waters of salvation. Augustine develops this further: just as the tree sweetened the bitter water, the cross of Christ transforms the bitterness of human suffering into a source of redemptive grace (CCC 618).

This typology is deeply embedded in Catholic spirituality. The tradition of offering up suffering — uniting personal affliction with the cross of Christ — finds its Old Testament archetype in the tree at Marah. As the Catechism teaches, "by his passion and death on the cross Christ has given a new meaning to suffering: it can henceforth configure us to him and unite us with his redemptive Passion" (CCC 1505).

### God as Healer

At Marah, God reveals Himself as "the LORD, your healer" (*YHWH Rophe'kha*, 15:26). Catholic theology connects this self-revelation to the healing ministry of Christ and the sacrament of the anointing of the sick. The Catechism cites Christ's healing miracles as fulfillments of the Old Testament promises of divine healing and identifies the sacrament of anointing as the continuation of this healing presence in the Church (CCC 1503–1505).

### Elim: The Oasis of Grace

The chapter ends at Elim, where the Israelites find "twelve springs of water and seventy palm trees" (15:27). The Fathers delight in the numbers: twelve springs for the twelve tribes (and later, the twelve apostles), seventy palms for the seventy elders of Israel (and the seventy disciples of Luke 10). Catholic typology reads Elim as a figure of the Church — the oasis of grace in the wilderness of the world, where the weary pilgrim finds refreshment in the sacraments and the fellowship of the faithful.
