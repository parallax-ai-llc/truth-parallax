## Overview

Exodus (*Shemot*, "Names") is the second book of the Bible and the Torah. It narrates the central event of Israelite history: the liberation from Egyptian slavery, the covenant at Sinai, and the construction of the Tabernacle. The book moves from oppression to freedom, from chaos to divine order, and from a family of tribes to a nation constituted by divine law.

## Structure

Exodus is commonly divided into three major sections:

1. **Deliverance from Egypt** (Chapters 1–18): Oppression, Moses' call, the ten plagues, the Passover, crossing the Red Sea, and journey to Sinai
2. **The Covenant at Sinai** (Chapters 19–24): Theophany, the Ten Commandments, the Book of the Covenant
3. **The Tabernacle** (Chapters 25–40): Instructions for the Tabernacle, the Golden Calf, restoration of the covenant, construction and dedication

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Exodus is arguably the most theologically significant narrative in the Hebrew Bible, with profound resonance across traditions:

- **Judaism**: The foundational narrative of Jewish identity; Passover is the central liturgical celebration
- **Christianity**: Typology of redemption — Passover lamb as Christ, Red Sea as baptism, Sinai as the new covenant
- **Islam**: Musa (Moses) is the most frequently mentioned prophet in the Quran; the Exodus narrative (Pharaoh's destruction) is a central theme
- **Liberation Theology**: Exodus as paradigm for God's solidarity with the oppressed
- **Gnosticism**: The Demiurge as Pharaoh-like enslaver; Moses as ambiguous figure

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Liberation | God hears the cry of the oppressed and acts to deliver |
| Covenant | The mutual bond between God and Israel, sealed at Sinai |
| Law | The Torah as divine instruction for communal life |
| Divine Presence | God dwells among the people in the Tabernacle |
| Passover | The foundational ritual of remembrance and redemption |
| Holiness | The call to be "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (19:6) |
