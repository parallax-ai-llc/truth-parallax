## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 21 reads the account of jehoram's wicked reign within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads jehoram's wicked reign as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### Elijah's Letter and Prophetic Witness

The letter from Elijah to Jehoram demonstrates that the prophetic word transcends time and space. Orthodox theology understands the saints as continuing to intercede for the living — the communion of saints bridges the boundary between the living and the departed.

### The Consequences of Apostasy

Jehoram's death "to no one's regret" stands as a solemn warning in Orthodox moral teaching. The Church Fathers use such narratives to illustrate the spiritual death that accompanies persistent rejection of God's grace. True death is not physical but spiritual — the loss of communion with God.
