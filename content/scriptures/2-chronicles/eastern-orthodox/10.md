## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 10 reads the account of the kingdom divided within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads the kingdom divided as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Tragedy of Division

Orthodox ecclesiology reads the division of the kingdom as a type of every schism in the Church. The consequences are devastating: the unity of God's people is shattered, worship is corrupted, and both communities suffer. Orthodox theology maintains that unity is not optional but essential to the Church's nature.

### Divine Providence in Schism

Orthodox theology holds that God permits division without approving it. The division serves the divine economy by demonstrating the consequences of pride and by preserving the faithful remnant in Judah. The pattern — division, suffering, eventual reunion — shapes Orthodox eschatological hope.
