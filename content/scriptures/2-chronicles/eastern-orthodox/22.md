## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 22 reads the account of ahaziah and athaliah within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads ahaziah and athaliah as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### Athaliah and Spiritual Corruption

Orthodox interpretation reads Athaliah as a symbol of corrupted authority — power exercised without reference to God's law. Her attempt to destroy the royal line parallels the enemy's attempt to extinguish the light of faith. The preservation of Joash echoes the preservation of the Church through persecution.

### Providence and Survival

The hiding of the infant Joash by Jehosheba and Jehoiada illustrates the Orthodox conviction that God preserves a remnant through even the darkest periods. The Church survives persecution not through human strength but through divine providence.
