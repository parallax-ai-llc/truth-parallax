## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 9 reads the account of the queen of sheba within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads the queen of sheba as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Nations Drawn to Wisdom

Orthodox reading of the Queen of Sheba's visit emphasizes the universal scope of divine wisdom. The nations are drawn to the light of God's truth, prefiguring the mission of the Church to all peoples. The Orthodox understanding of mission is not conquest but attraction — the beauty and truth of the faith draw seekers from afar.

### Solomon's Glory and Theosis

The magnificent display of Solomon's wisdom and wealth is read through the lens of *theosis*: the human person, united to God, radiates divine glory. Solomon, at his best, manifests the image of God in its fullness — a foretaste of what all humanity is called to become.
