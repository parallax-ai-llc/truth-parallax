## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 34 reads the account of josiah's reforms within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads josiah's reforms as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Recovery of the Word

The discovery of the Book of the Law in the Temple illustrates the Orthodox teaching that Scripture lives within the worshipping community. The book was found in the Temple — it had never left the sacred space but had been forgotten. Orthodox theology insists that Scripture must be read within the liturgical and patristic tradition to be rightly understood.

### The Prophetess Huldah

Orthodox tradition honors women prophets and saints. Huldah's authoritative word to the king demonstrates that the prophetic charism is given according to God's will, not human conventions. The Orthodox calendar commemorates numerous women prophets, martyrs, and teachers.
