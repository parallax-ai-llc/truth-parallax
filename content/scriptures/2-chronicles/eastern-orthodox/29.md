## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 29 reads the account of hezekiah purifies the temple within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads hezekiah purifies the temple as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### Liturgical Renewal as First Priority

Hezekiah's immediate reopening of the Temple confirms the Orthodox conviction that liturgical worship is the foundation of all other renewal. The Divine Liturgy is the source and summit of the Christian life — everything else flows from right worship.

### The Sanctification of the Clergy

Hezekiah's command that the Levites "sanctify yourselves" before cleansing the Temple reflects Orthodox requirements for clerical preparation. The priest prepares for the Divine Liturgy through fasting, prayer, and confession — the inner sanctification must precede the outward ministry.
