## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 1 reads the account of solomon's request for wisdom within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads solomon's request for wisdom as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers

### Ascetical Reading

Orthodox ascetical theology draws practical guidance from this chapter. The struggles of Judah's kings against idolatry and unfaithfulness mirror the soul's combat against the passions. The Philokalia tradition teaches that the virtues demonstrated by faithful kings — prayer, humility, trust in God — are the same virtues required for the spiritual life.

### Iconographic Tradition

Orthodox iconography sometimes depicts scenes from the historical books of the Old Testament, placing them within the broader visual narrative of salvation history. The Temple, the kings, and the prophets all find their place in the iconographic program that surrounds the worshipper in an Orthodox church.
