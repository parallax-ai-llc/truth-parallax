## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 32 reads the account of sennacherib's invasion within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads sennacherib's invasion as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Angel of Deliverance (32:21)

The angel who destroys the Assyrian army is read in Orthodox tradition as a manifestation of divine power in the angelic hierarchy. The Archangel Michael, the defender of God's people, is frequently identified with this intervention. Orthodox iconography depicts the archangels as warriors against the powers of darkness.

### Hezekiah's Humility After Pride (32:25-26)

The pattern of pride followed by humbling illustrates the Orthodox ascetical teaching on *kenosis* — self-emptying. The spiritual life requires constant vigilance against pride, and God in His mercy provides corrective experiences that restore humility.
