## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 12 reads the account of shishak invades judah within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads shishak invades judah as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### Humiliation and Repentance

The pattern of sin, prophetic rebuke, partial repentance, and partial deliverance in chapter 12 illustrates the Orthodox understanding of *metanoia*. Repentance is not a single event but a lifelong process of turning toward God. Rehoboam's humbling is genuine but incomplete — as is much human repentance.

### The Loss of Sacred Objects

The plundering of the Temple's gold shields by Shishak symbolizes the loss of spiritual treasures through unfaithfulness. Orthodox spiritual theology teaches that the gifts of the Spirit, once received, must be guarded through ascetic discipline and prayer.
