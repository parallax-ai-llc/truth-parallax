## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 30 reads the account of hezekiah's passover within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads hezekiah's passover as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Great Pascha

Orthodox reading of Hezekiah's Passover sees a prefiguration of Pascha — the Christian Easter. The invitation extended to all Israel, the joy of celebration, and the extension of the feast all anticipate the universal joy of Christ's resurrection.

### Grace Beyond Law

Hezekiah's prayer for those who eat without proper purification illustrates the Orthodox teaching that God's grace operates beyond the strict requirements of the law. The *oikonomia* principle — pastoral flexibility in applying canonical rules — finds a scriptural basis in this episode.
