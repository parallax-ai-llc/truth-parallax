## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 35 reads the account of josiah's passover and death within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads josiah's passover and death as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Paschal Celebration

Josiah's Passover, the greatest since Samuel, is read in Orthodox typology as the climactic Old Testament anticipation of Pascha. The careful preparation, the Levitical chanting, and the corporate celebration all find their fulfillment in the Great and Holy Week services of the Orthodox Church.

### The Mystery of Josiah's Death

Orthodox theology does not require that every tragedy have a clear explanation. The mystery of Josiah's death at Megiddo is embraced within the larger mystery of divine providence. The laments composed by Jeremiah become part of the tradition of Holy Week lamentation.
