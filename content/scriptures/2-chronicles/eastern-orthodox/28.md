## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 28 reads the account of ahaz's wicked reign within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads ahaz's wicked reign as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Depths of Apostasy

Ahaz's reign represents the complete inversion of covenant faith. Child sacrifice, idolatry, and the closure of the Temple constitute a systematic rejection of everything the Chronicler values. Orthodox moral theology reads this as the progressive logic of sin: each step away from God makes the next step easier.

### The Mercy of Enemies

The prophet Oded's intervention to free Judahite captives demonstrates a theme dear to Orthodox theology: God's mercy operates even through unexpected channels. The northern kingdom, despite its own apostasy, becomes an instrument of divine compassion.
