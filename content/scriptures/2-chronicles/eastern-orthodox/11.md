## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 11 reads the account of rehoboam strengthens judah within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads rehoboam strengthens judah as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### The Levites as Models of Fidelity

The Levites who abandon their property in the north to maintain proper worship in Judah are read in Orthodox tradition as models of monastic renunciation. Like the monks who leave the world to preserve authentic worship, the Levites sacrifice material security for liturgical integrity.

### Fortification and the Church

Rehoboam's fortification of Judah's cities is read typologically as the Church's development of canonical structures and theological boundaries to protect the faith. The walls of the cities correspond to the canons of the councils — boundaries that preserve the integrity of the faith.
