## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 25 reads the account of amaziah's reign within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads amaziah's reign as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### Amaziah's Mixed Legacy

Orthodox spiritual theology reads Amaziah's halfhearted devotion as a warning against spiritual mediocrity. The Fathers teach that lukewarmness is more dangerous than outright rejection — at least the rebel knows where he stands, while the lukewarm believer deceives himself.

### The Irrationality of Idolatry

Amaziah's worship of gods he has just defeated demonstrates what the Fathers call the *logos* of sin — the fundamental irrationality of turning from the Creator to the creature. Sin is never a rational choice but always a disordered will.
