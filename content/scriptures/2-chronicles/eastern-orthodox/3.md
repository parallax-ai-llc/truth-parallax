## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Chronicles 3 reads the account of building the temple within the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene and Cappadocian schools — provide the hermeneutical framework for understanding this narrative within God's divine economy (*oikonomia*).

## Patristic Reading

### Typological Significance

Orthodox exegesis reads building the temple as part of the unfolding plan of salvation leading to the Incarnation. The faithfulness or unfaithfulness of Judah's kings reveals the human condition's need for the divine-human King, Jesus Christ.

### Liturgical Connection

The worship described in Chronicles finds its fulfillment in the Divine Liturgy. The Chronicler's attention to Levitical order, sacred music, and proper sacrifice resonates with Orthodox liturgical theology.

## Theological Significance

- **Theosis**: The goal of human life is participation in the divine nature
- **Divine economy**: God works through history to accomplish salvation
- **Liturgical worship**: True worship participates in the heavenly liturgy
- **Patristic continuity**: The Church reads Scripture with the Fathers


### Sacred Architecture and Iconography

Orthodox church architecture follows principles embedded in the Temple's design. The three-part structure (porch, nave, sanctuary) corresponds to the three-part arrangement of an Orthodox church (narthex, nave, altar). The cherubim in the Holy of Holies anticipate the icons that fill Orthodox churches with the presence of the saints.

### Gold as Uncreated Light

The extensive use of gold overlay in the Temple represents the uncreated light of God's glory. In Orthodox iconography, gold backgrounds symbolize the divine light that transcends created reality — the same theological principle at work in the Temple's decoration.
