## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 35 reads the narrative of josiah's passover and death through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### The Great Passover

Islamic tradition does not observe Passover but recognizes it as a legitimate festival of Bani Isra'il commemorating the Exodus. The Quran narrates the Exodus extensively (e.g., Surah Taha, 20:9-98), and Islamic scholars acknowledge the Passover's connection to this founding event.

### Josiah's Death

The death of the righteous king at Megiddo raises the question of divine justice. Islamic theology maintains that martyrdom (*shahada*) in battle is not a punishment but an honor. Some Islamic commentators read Josiah's death as a form of divine mercy — removing the righteous king before the greater catastrophe of the Babylonian destruction.
