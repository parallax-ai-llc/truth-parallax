## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 3 reads the narrative of building the temple through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### The Construction in Islamic Tradition

The Quran does not describe the Temple's architecture in detail, but Islamic historians like **al-Tabari** and **al-Tha'labi** draw on Isra'iliyyat sources to describe the building. These accounts emphasize the supernatural assistance Sulayman received — jinn cutting stones, birds providing shade — transforming the construction into a miraculous event.

### The Holy of Holies

Islamic tradition acknowledges the sanctity of the Temple's inner chamber while maintaining that no physical space can contain Allah's essence. The Quran states: "And to Allah belongs the east and the west. So wherever you turn, there is the Face of Allah" (2:115). The Temple's holiness derives not from Allah's limitation to a space but from His designation of it as sacred.
