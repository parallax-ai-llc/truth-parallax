## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 33 reads the narrative of manasseh and amon through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### Manasseh's Repentance and Tawbah

Islamic theology affirms that sincere repentance (*tawbah*) is always accepted by Allah: "Say, O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins" (39:53). Manasseh's repentance, even after the most extreme apostasy, illustrates this boundless divine mercy.

### The Conditions of Tawbah

Classical Islamic scholars outline conditions for valid repentance: cessation of sin, remorse, and resolution not to return. Manasseh's story fulfills these conditions — he humbles himself, prays, and is restored, presumably living faithfully afterward.
