## The Fall of Jerusalem and Divine Decree

Islamic interpretation of 2 Chronicles 36 reads the destruction of Jerusalem through the lens of Surah al-Isra' (17:4-8), which describes two occasions when the Children of Israel caused corruption in the land and were punished by God.

## Quranic Framework

### Surah al-Isra' 17:4-8

"And We decreed for the Children of Israel in the Scripture: You will surely cause corruption on the earth twice, and you will surely reach great haughtiness. So when the first of the two promises came, We sent against you servants of Ours, possessing great military might, and they probed even into the homes, and it was a promise fulfilled."

Muslim exegetes, including **al-Tabari** and **Ibn Kathir**, frequently identify the Babylonian destruction as the first of these two corruptions. The destruction is read as divine decree (*qadar*), not merely political misfortune.

### The Edict of Cyrus

Islamic tradition views Cyrus's decree as an expression of Allah's sovereignty over all rulers and nations. Allah can use any instrument — believer or unbeliever — to accomplish His purposes.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine sovereignty**: The fall of Jerusalem is Allah's decree, not historical accident
- **Consequences of disobedience**: Nations that disobey God's prophets face destruction
- **Cyclical history**: The Quran presents a pattern of sin, punishment, and restoration
- **Mercy**: Even after destruction, Allah provides a path of return

### Lessons for the Ummah

Muslim scholars consistently draw lessons from the history of Bani Isra'il for the Muslim community. The patterns of faithfulness and apostasy, divine blessing and punishment, and prophetic warning that characterize 2 Chronicles are understood as universal principles that apply to all communities entrusted with divine revelation.

### The Sunnah of Allah in History

The Quran teaches that Allah's way (*sunnat Allah*) does not change (33:62). The rise and fall of Judah's kings illustrates this unchanging divine practice: nations that obey God's messengers prosper, while those that reject them face consequences. This historical pattern serves as both warning and encouragement.
