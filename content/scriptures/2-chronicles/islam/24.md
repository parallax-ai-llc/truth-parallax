## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 24 reads the narrative of joash restores the temple through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### The Repair of Sacred Spaces

Islamic tradition values the maintenance and repair of mosques as a religious obligation. The Quran states: "The mosques of Allah are only to be maintained by those who believe in Allah and the Last Day" (9:18). Joash's repair of the Temple illustrates this principle, applicable by analogy to the Muslim care of sacred spaces.

### The Murder of the Prophet

The murder of Zechariah in the Temple court is read by Muslim scholars as another instance of Bani Isra'il killing their prophets — a charge repeated in the Quran (2:61, 3:21, 3:112). Islamic tradition regards the killing of prophets as among the gravest sins.
