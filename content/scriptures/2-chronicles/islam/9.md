## The Queen of Sheba (Bilqis) and Sulayman

The visit of the Queen of Sheba to Solomon has a prominent Quranic parallel in Surah an-Naml (27:22-44). Islamic tradition provides an extensive and distinctive account of Bilqis, her kingdom, and her encounter with Sulayman.

## Quranic Account

### Surah an-Naml (27:22-44)

The Quran narrates how a hoopoe bird brings Sulayman news of a queen in Sheba who worships the sun. Sulayman invites her to submit to Allah. Bilqis arrives, is tested by a throne transported miraculously, and ultimately declares: "My Lord, I have wronged myself, and I submit with Sulayman to Allah, Lord of the Worlds" (27:44).

### Differences from the Biblical Account

While 2 Chronicles 9 focuses on the Queen's admiration of Solomon's wisdom and wealth, the Quran emphasizes her theological transformation — from sun worship to *tawhid*. **Al-Tabari** and **al-Tha'labi** provide extensive legendary material about Bilqis.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: The Queen's story is a conversion narrative — from shirk to worship of Allah alone
- **Da'wah**: Sulayman's invitation to Bilqis is a model of prophetic outreach
- **Women and faith**: Bilqis's submission to Allah demonstrates that faith transcends gender
- **Divine signs**: The miracles convince Bilqis of Sulayman's prophetic authority

### Lessons for the Ummah

Muslim scholars consistently draw lessons from the history of Bani Isra'il for the Muslim community. The patterns of faithfulness and apostasy, divine blessing and punishment, and prophetic warning that characterize 2 Chronicles are understood as universal principles that apply to all communities entrusted with divine revelation.

### The Sunnah of Allah in History

The Quran teaches that Allah's way (*sunnat Allah*) does not change (33:62). The rise and fall of Judah's kings illustrates this unchanging divine practice: nations that obey God's messengers prosper, while those that reject them face consequences. This historical pattern serves as both warning and encouragement.
