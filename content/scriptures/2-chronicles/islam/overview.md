## Overview

Islam does not regard 2 Chronicles as a preserved divine text but acknowledges that narratives about the prophets and kings of Israel contain elements of truth. The Quran devotes significant attention to Sulayman (Solomon), who features prominently in 2 Chronicles 1-9, and Islamic tradition engages the broader themes of divine sovereignty, the rise and fall of kingdoms, and the consequences of disobedience to God's prophets.

## Relationship to the Quran

The Quran provides its own account of Sulayman that both parallels and diverges from 2 Chronicles:

| Biblical Narrative | Quranic Parallel |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Solomon's wisdom (ch. 1) | Quran 27:15-16 — Sulayman given knowledge |
| Temple construction (chs. 2-7) | Quran 34:12-13 — Jinn build for Sulayman |
| Queen of Sheba (ch. 9) | Quran 27:22-44 — Bilqis and Sulayman |
| Kingdom's division (ch. 10) | Understood as consequence of disobedience |
| Fall of Jerusalem (ch. 36) | Quran 17:4-8 — Two corruptions of Bani Isra'il |

### Sulayman in Islamic Theology
Sulayman is a prophet-king endowed with extraordinary gifts: command over the wind, authority over jinn, understanding of animal speech (Quran 27:16). His wisdom is a divine grant, not a personal achievement. Islamic tradition emphasizes Sulayman's gratitude and worship despite his power.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Divine Sovereignty
The Chronicler's theme of divine retribution — blessing for obedience, punishment for disobedience — aligns with the Quranic principle that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct (Quran 3:26).

### Isra'iliyyat Caution
Classical scholars accepted historical details cautiously, rejecting anything contradicting the Quran while permitting narration of neutral material.
