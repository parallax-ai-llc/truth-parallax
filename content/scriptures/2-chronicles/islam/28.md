## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 28 reads the narrative of ahaz's wicked reign through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### The Worst of Kings

Ahaz's comprehensive apostasy — including child sacrifice — represents, in Islamic terms, the complete abandonment of *tawhid*. The Quran condemns child sacrifice directly: "And do not kill your children for fear of poverty" (17:31). Ahaz embodies the pre-Islamic *jahiliyyah* (ignorance) that Islam came to abolish.

### Mercy from Unexpected Sources

The prophet Oded's intervention to free captives demonstrates a principle also found in Islamic ethics: mercy and justice are obligations that transcend tribal and national loyalties. The Prophet Muhammad taught that showing mercy to captives is a fundamental Islamic duty.
