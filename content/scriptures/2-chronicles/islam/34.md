## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 34 reads the narrative of josiah's reforms through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### The Discovery of the Book

The finding of the Book of the Law illustrates the Islamic concern with *hifz* (preservation) of divine revelation. The Quran claims divine protection for itself: "Indeed, it is We who sent down the Quran, and indeed, We will be its guardian" (15:9). The loss of the Torah in the Temple serves as a contrast — illustrating why Muslims believe the Quran's preservation is miraculous.

### The Prophetess

Huldah's role as prophetess is engaged cautiously in Islamic tradition. While Islam does not typically recognize female prophets (*nabiyyat*), some scholars acknowledge women who received divine inspiration (*ilham*) without the formal status of prophethood.
