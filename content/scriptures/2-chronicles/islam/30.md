## Overview

Islamic engagement with 2 Chronicles 30 reads the narrative of hezekiah's passover through the principles of *tawhid* (divine unity), prophetic authority, and the Quranic framework for understanding the history of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel).

## Islamic Interpretation

### Quranic Principles

The Quran establishes that God raises and abases nations according to their conduct: "Say, O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Quran 3:26). The events of this chapter illustrate this principle.

### Historical Engagement

Classical Muslim historians like **al-Tabari** include material from 2 Chronicles in their universal histories, drawing on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) while filtering them through Quranic theology. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, accepting only what aligns with the Quran and authentic hadith.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All sovereignty belongs to Allah alone
- **Prophetic warning**: God sends messengers before sending judgment
- **Divine justice**: Allah's dealings with nations are just and purposeful
- **Lessons for the ummah**: The history of Bani Isra'il provides cautionary examples


### The Universal Invitation

Hezekiah's invitation to all Israel — including the separated northern tribes — resonates with the Islamic concept of *da'wah* (invitation). The Quran states: "Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good instruction" (16:125). Hezekiah's couriers carry a message of return and reconciliation.

### Grace for the Sincere

The acceptance of worship from those who had not properly purified themselves illustrates a principle also found in Islamic jurisprudence: Allah accepts worship offered with sincere intention (*niyyah*), even when the external form is imperfect.
