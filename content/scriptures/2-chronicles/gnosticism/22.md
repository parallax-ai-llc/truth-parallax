## Overview

Gnostic engagement with 2 Chronicles 22 applies the characteristic hermeneutical inversion to the narrative of ahaziah and athaliah. While no surviving Gnostic text directly comments on this chapter, the principles established in Gnostic treatments of the Hebrew Bible — the Demiurge, the divine spark, archontic powers, and the primacy of *gnosis* — provide an interpretive framework.

## Gnostic Reading

### The Demiurge's Kingdom

The Chronicler presents the kingdom of Judah as God's chosen political order. Gnostic hermeneutics questions this framing: the God who establishes earthly kingdoms and demands material sacrifices may be the Demiurge, not the true transcendent God.

### Pneumatic and Hylic Figures

Gnostic interpretation classifies the characters of this narrative according to their spiritual orientation. Those who demonstrate knowledge, insight, and transcendence of material concerns are read as pneumatic figures. Those bound to power, ritual, and material wealth are hylic.

## Theological Significance

- **The Demiurge's order**: Earthly kingdoms serve the lower god's purposes
- **Interior knowledge**: True religion is *gnosis*, not institutional worship
- **The divine spark**: Even within the Demiurge's narrative, traces of the true God appear
- **Material impermanence**: All political and religious structures are ultimately temporary


### Athaliah as Material Power

Athaliah's seizure of the throne represents material power at its most ruthless. She destroys even the royal family — the Demiurge's chosen line — to maintain her position. Gnostic interpretation sees this as the self-devouring nature of archontic authority: power unchecked by *gnosis* destroys even its own instruments.

### The Hidden Child

The preservation of the infant Joash in the Temple has Gnostic resonance: the divine spark (represented by the legitimate heir) is hidden within the Demiurge's own house, protected by those who recognize its value. The hidden child represents the hidden *gnosis* preserved within the material-religious system.
