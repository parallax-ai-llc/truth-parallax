## The Collapse of the Demiurge's Order

Gnostic reading of 2 Chronicles 36 interprets the fall of Jerusalem as the inevitable collapse of the Demiurge's political-religious system. The Temple — the Demiurge's dwelling — is destroyed, revealing the impermanence of all material structures and the futility of material worship.

## Gnostic Interpretation

### The End of the Material Temple

The destruction of the Temple is, in Gnostic terms, liberation. The physical structure that localized and controlled access to the divine is removed. True worship — interior *gnosis* — does not require temples, priests, or sacrifices.

### Exile as Awakening

The exile can be read as a Gnostic awakening: stripped of their material supports (land, Temple, monarchy), the people are forced to seek the divine within. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the true Temple is the human body, and the true worship is interior knowledge.

### Cyrus and the Return

The edict of Cyrus presents a problem for Gnostic reading: return to the material homeland and reconstruction of the material Temple seem regressive. Some Gnostic interpreters would read the return as a concession to the hylic (material) impulse, while the truly pneumatic individual recognizes that the real homeland is the *pleroma*.

## Theological Significance

- **The fall of material religion**: Temples built with hands must eventually fall
- **Interior gnosis**: True worship is knowledge, not ritual
- **Exile as liberation**: Loss of material supports can awaken spiritual awareness
- **The tension of return**: The pneumatic transcends the material homeland
