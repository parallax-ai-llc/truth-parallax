## Overview

Gnostic engagement with 2 Chronicles 6 applies the characteristic hermeneutical inversion to the narrative of solomon's prayer of dedication. While no surviving Gnostic text directly comments on this chapter, the principles established in Gnostic treatments of the Hebrew Bible — the Demiurge, the divine spark, archontic powers, and the primacy of *gnosis* — provide an interpretive framework.

## Gnostic Reading

### Solomon's Prayer to the Demiurge

Gnostic interpretation reads Solomon's prayer of dedication with hermeneutical suspicion. The God to whom Solomon prays — who demands a physical house, who links blessing to obedience, who threatens punishment — displays characteristics that Gnostic theology attributes to the Demiurge: a jealous, controlling deity who manages the material world through reward and punishment. The *Testimony of Truth* asks pointedly: "What kind of God is this?" when confronted with such transactional theology.

### The Limitations of Temple Prayer

The concept of praying "toward this house" represents, for Gnostic readers, the localization of the divine — a reduction of the infinite to the spatial. True *gnosis* recognizes that the divine is not directional but is the inner light accessible through contemplation, not orientation. Solomon's own acknowledgment that "the heavens, even the highest heavens, cannot contain you" (6:18) ironically undermines the entire Temple project.

### The Catalog of Suffering

Solomon's prayer catalogs the conditions under which people will pray toward the Temple: famine, plague, defeat, exile. In Gnostic reading, this is the Demiurge's system laid bare — the material world produces suffering, and the Demiurge offers relief only through submission to his institutional worship. The cycle of suffering and supplication keeps the soul bound to the material order.

## Theological Significance

- **Transactional religion**: The Demiurge offers blessing in exchange for obedience
- **Spatial limitation**: Directing prayer toward a building reduces the infinite to the local
- **The suffering cycle**: Material affliction drives people to the Demiurge's Temple, perpetuating bondage
- **Solomon's irony**: The builder himself admits his building cannot contain the divine
