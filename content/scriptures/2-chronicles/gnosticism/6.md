## Overview

Gnostic engagement with 2 Chronicles 6 applies the characteristic hermeneutical inversion to the narrative of solomon's prayer of dedication. While no surviving Gnostic text directly comments on this chapter, the principles established in Gnostic treatments of the Hebrew Bible — the Demiurge, the divine spark, archontic powers, and the primacy of *gnosis* — provide an interpretive framework.

## Gnostic Reading

### The Demiurge's Kingdom

The Chronicler presents the kingdom of Judah as God's chosen political order. Gnostic hermeneutics questions this framing: the God who establishes earthly kingdoms and demands material sacrifices may be the Demiurge, not the true transcendent God.

### Pneumatic and Hylic Figures

Gnostic interpretation classifies the characters of this narrative according to their spiritual orientation. Those who demonstrate knowledge, insight, and transcendence of material concerns are read as pneumatic figures. Those bound to power, ritual, and material wealth are hylic.

## Theological Significance

- **The Demiurge's order**: Earthly kingdoms serve the lower god's purposes
- **Interior knowledge**: True religion is *gnosis*, not institutional worship
- **The divine spark**: Even within the Demiurge's narrative, traces of the true God appear
- **Material impermanence**: All political and religious structures are ultimately temporary


### Solomon's Prayer to the Demiurge

Gnostic interpretation reads Solomon's prayer of dedication with hermeneutical suspicion. The God to whom Solomon prays — who demands a physical house, who links blessing to obedience, who threatens punishment — displays characteristics that Gnostic theology attributes to the Demiurge: a jealous, controlling deity who manages the material world through reward and punishment.

### The Limitations of Temple Prayer

The concept of praying "toward this house" represents, for Gnostic readers, the localization of the divine — a reduction of the infinite to the spatial. True *gnosis* recognizes that the divine is not directional but is the inner light accessible through contemplation, not orientation.
