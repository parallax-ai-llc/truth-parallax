## Overview

Gnostic engagement with 2 Chronicles 31 applies the characteristic hermeneutical inversion to the narrative of hezekiah's religious reforms. While no surviving Gnostic text directly comments on this chapter, the principles established in Gnostic treatments of the Hebrew Bible — the Demiurge, the divine spark, archontic powers, and the primacy of *gnosis* — provide an interpretive framework.

## Gnostic Reading

### Institutional Religion

Hezekiah's establishment of tithing systems and administrative structures illustrates the institutionalization of the Demiurge's religion. The Gnostic critique of organized religion finds its target here: the transformation of spiritual impulse into bureaucratic structure, the routinization of charisma into administration. The detailed lists of officials, storerooms, and priestly divisions reveal religion reduced to logistics.

### Material Support for Material Religion

The generous giving that follows the revival represents, in Gnostic terms, the redirection of material resources toward the Demiurge's system. The pneumatic individual recognizes that spiritual growth requires not material investment but interior transformation. The heaps of grain and produce piled in the Temple courts are a visible symbol of the material-religious exchange: goods offered upward, favor flowing downward.

### The Destruction of Sacred Sites

The people's destruction of high places, sacred stones, and Asherah poles throughout Judah and even into northern territory (31:1) represents the Demiurge's campaign to eliminate competing forms of worship. From a Gnostic perspective, the destruction of diverse sacred sites concentrates all worship at the single Temple — centralizing archontic control and eliminating alternatives.

## Theological Significance

- **Bureaucratized faith**: Tithing systems transform spiritual devotion into administrative function
- **Material investment**: Resources flow to the Demiurge's institution rather than toward inner awakening
- **Centralized worship**: The destruction of local shrines concentrates religious power in one archontic institution
- **The appearance of success**: A well-funded, well-organized religion can look like spiritual health while remaining material
