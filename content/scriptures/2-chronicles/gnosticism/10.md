## Overview

Gnostic engagement with 2 Chronicles 10 applies the characteristic hermeneutical inversion to the narrative of the kingdom divided. While no surviving Gnostic text directly comments on this chapter, the principles established in Gnostic treatments of the Hebrew Bible — the Demiurge, the divine spark, archontic powers, and the primacy of *gnosis* — provide an interpretive framework.

## Gnostic Reading

### The Shattering of the Demiurge's Order

The division of the kingdom exposes the fragility of the Demiurge's political-religious system. The united kingdom, built on material power and religious coercion, cannot sustain itself. In Gnostic terms, all structures of the Demiurge are inherently unstable because they are built on matter rather than spirit. The *Hypostasis of the Archons* teaches that archontic power is always contested and always fragile.

### Rehoboam as Archontic Tyrant

Rehoboam's refusal to lighten the people's burdens embodies the archontic principle: material power maintains itself through coercion. The Gnostic reader sees in Rehoboam's arrogance the characteristic behavior of the archons, who demand submission and punish resistance. His preference for the counsel of the young men over the elders represents the triumph of aggressive material authority over whatever moderating wisdom existed within the system.

### The People's Revolt

The northern tribes' rejection of the Davidic house — "What share do we have in David?" — carries unexpected Gnostic resonance. The refusal to submit to an inherited power structure echoes the Gnostic rejection of the Demiurge's claim to ultimate authority. Yet the revolt does not lead to liberation; it merely creates a second archontic system alongside the first.

## Theological Significance

- **Fragile power**: The Demiurge's political order collapses under the weight of its own coercion
- **Archontic arrogance**: Rulers who increase burdens reveal the true nature of material authority
- **False liberation**: Revolt within the system creates new bondage, not genuine freedom
- **The illusion of divine causation**: The text claims God caused the division — even chaos serves the Demiurge's purposes
