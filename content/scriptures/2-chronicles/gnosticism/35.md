## Overview

Gnostic engagement with 2 Chronicles 35 applies the characteristic hermeneutical inversion to the narrative of josiah's passover and death. While no surviving Gnostic text directly comments on this chapter, the principles established in Gnostic treatments of the Hebrew Bible — the Demiurge, the divine spark, archontic powers, and the primacy of *gnosis* — provide an interpretive framework.

## Gnostic Reading

### The Demiurge's Kingdom

The Chronicler presents the kingdom of Judah as God's chosen political order. Gnostic hermeneutics questions this framing: the God who establishes earthly kingdoms and demands material sacrifices may be the Demiurge, not the true transcendent God.

### Pneumatic and Hylic Figures

Gnostic interpretation classifies the characters of this narrative according to their spiritual orientation. Those who demonstrate knowledge, insight, and transcendence of material concerns are read as pneumatic figures. Those bound to power, ritual, and material wealth are hylic.

## Theological Significance

- **The Demiurge's order**: Earthly kingdoms serve the lower god's purposes
- **Interior knowledge**: True religion is *gnosis*, not institutional worship
- **The divine spark**: Even within the Demiurge's narrative, traces of the true God appear
- **Material impermanence**: All political and religious structures are ultimately temporary


### The Great Festival

Josiah's Passover represents the apex of the Demiurge's worship system — the most perfect material celebration possible. Gnostic interpretation notes that perfection within the material system is still material. The greatest Passover since Samuel is still a meal of lamb and bread, not the spiritual feast of *gnosis*.

### Death at Megiddo

Josiah's death at Megiddo carries Gnostic significance. The righteous king who perfectly serves the Demiurge is killed by a pagan pharaoh who claims to speak "from the mouth of God" (35:22). The confusion of divine voices — which god speaks through which agent? — illustrates the Gnostic observation that the Demiurge's world is a place of confusion and deception.
