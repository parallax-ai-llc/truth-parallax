## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 2 Chronicles 30 reads the account of hezekiah's passover within the framework of Sacred Tradition and the Church's liturgical and moral theology. The Church Fathers provide interpretive keys that connect this narrative to Christ, the Church, and the sacraments.

## Patristic and Magisterial Reading

### Typological Connections

The Fathers read the events of hezekiah's passover as part of the larger divine economy leading to the Incarnation. Every faithful king points toward Christ, the true King; every act of worship anticipates the liturgy of the Church.

### Moral Theology

Catholic moral theology draws lessons from the conduct of Judah's kings and the people's response. The interplay of faithfulness and apostasy illustrates the Church's teaching on the necessity of grace, the reality of human freedom, and the possibility of repentance.

## Theological Significance

- **Christological typology**: The events of this chapter find their fulfillment in Christ
- **Ecclesiological significance**: The Temple and its worship prefigure the Church
- **Moral instruction**: The choices of kings and people provide lasting ethical guidance
- **Sacramental connection**: The worship described here anticipates the Church's sacramental life


### Unity Across Division (30:1-12)

Hezekiah's invitation to the northern tribes illustrates the Catholic understanding of ecumenism — the invitation to unity in worship extended to those who have separated. The varying responses (mockery from some, acceptance from others) mirror the Church's experience in ecumenical dialogue.

### Grace for the Impure (30:18-20)

Hezekiah prays for those who eat the Passover without proper purification, and God heals them. Catholic sacramental theology reads this as an illustration of God's mercy toward those who approach the sacred with sincere hearts, even when external preparations are incomplete.
