## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 2 Chronicles 26 reads the account of uzziah's reign within the framework of Sacred Tradition and the Church's liturgical and moral theology. The Church Fathers provide interpretive keys that connect this narrative to Christ, the Church, and the sacraments.

## Patristic and Magisterial Reading

### Typological Connections

The Fathers read the events of uzziah's reign as part of the larger divine economy leading to the Incarnation. Every faithful king points toward Christ, the true King; every act of worship anticipates the liturgy of the Church.

### Moral Theology

Catholic moral theology draws lessons from the conduct of Judah's kings and the people's response. The interplay of faithfulness and apostasy illustrates the Church's teaching on the necessity of grace, the reality of human freedom, and the possibility of repentance.

## Theological Significance

- **Christological typology**: The events of this chapter find their fulfillment in Christ
- **Ecclesiological significance**: The Temple and its worship prefigure the Church
- **Moral instruction**: The choices of kings and people provide lasting ethical guidance
- **Sacramental connection**: The worship described here anticipates the Church's sacramental life


### Uzziah's Sacrilege (26:16-21)

Uzziah's attempt to burn incense in the Temple — a function reserved for the Aaronic priesthood — is read by Catholic interpreters as a definitive illustration of the distinction between the royal and priestly offices. The Catholic doctrine of the ministerial priesthood finds support in this narrative: the priesthood is a divine appointment, not a function of political authority.

### Leprosy as Judgment (26:19-20)

The immediate onset of leprosy confirms the gravity of Uzziah's transgression. Catholic sacramental theology notes the parallel between Uzziah's visible disease and the invisible spiritual consequences of sacrilege — both require purification and atonement.
