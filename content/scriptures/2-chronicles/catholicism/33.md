## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 2 Chronicles 33 reads the account of manasseh and amon within the framework of Sacred Tradition and the Church's liturgical and moral theology. The Church Fathers provide interpretive keys that connect this narrative to Christ, the Church, and the sacraments.

## Patristic and Magisterial Reading

### Typological Connections

The Fathers read the events of manasseh and amon as part of the larger divine economy leading to the Incarnation. Every faithful king points toward Christ, the true King; every act of worship anticipates the liturgy of the Church.

### Moral Theology

Catholic moral theology draws lessons from the conduct of Judah's kings and the people's response. The interplay of faithfulness and apostasy illustrates the Church's teaching on the necessity of grace, the reality of human freedom, and the possibility of repentance.

## Theological Significance

- **Christological typology**: The events of this chapter find their fulfillment in Christ
- **Ecclesiological significance**: The Temple and its worship prefigure the Church
- **Moral instruction**: The choices of kings and people provide lasting ethical guidance
- **Sacramental connection**: The worship described here anticipates the Church's sacramental life


### Manasseh's Repentance (33:12-13)

This passage is profoundly significant for Catholic moral theology. The *Prayer of Manasseh*, included in the Catholic and Orthodox canons, elaborates on this repentance. The Church Fathers cite Manasseh as evidence that no sin is beyond God's mercy — a principle central to the sacrament of reconciliation.

### The Apocryphal Prayer

The *Prayer of Manasseh*, preserved in some manuscripts of the Septuagint, expands upon 33:12-13 with a penitential prayer that has been incorporated into the Byzantine liturgical tradition. Catholic tradition values this text as a witness to the power of repentance.
