## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 2 Chronicles 35 reads the account of josiah's passover and death within the framework of Sacred Tradition and the Church's liturgical and moral theology. The Church Fathers provide interpretive keys that connect this narrative to Christ, the Church, and the sacraments.

## Patristic and Magisterial Reading

### Typological Connections

The Fathers read the events of josiah's passover and death as part of the larger divine economy leading to the Incarnation. Every faithful king points toward Christ, the true King; every act of worship anticipates the liturgy of the Church.

### Moral Theology

Catholic moral theology draws lessons from the conduct of Judah's kings and the people's response. The interplay of faithfulness and apostasy illustrates the Church's teaching on the necessity of grace, the reality of human freedom, and the possibility of repentance.

## Theological Significance

- **Christological typology**: The events of this chapter find their fulfillment in Christ
- **Ecclesiological significance**: The Temple and its worship prefigure the Church
- **Moral instruction**: The choices of kings and people provide lasting ethical guidance
- **Sacramental connection**: The worship described here anticipates the Church's sacramental life


### The Greatest Passover (35:1-19)

Catholic liturgical theology reads Josiah's Passover as the pinnacle of Old Testament worship, pointing forward to the Eucharist as the New Passover. The meticulous organization, the Levitical service, and the joy of the people provide a model for liturgical celebration.

### Josiah's Death (35:20-25)

The tragedy of Josiah's death at Megiddo illustrates the mystery of suffering: even the most faithful servant of God is not exempt from the consequences of unwise decisions. Catholic theology does not promise temporal reward for righteousness but affirms that all suffering can be united to the Cross.
