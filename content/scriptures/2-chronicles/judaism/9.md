## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 9 engages the narrative of the queen of sheba through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### The Queen of Sheba in Midrash

The Midrash (Targum Sheni on Esther) elaborates extensively on the Queen of Sheba's visit, adding riddles and tests beyond what the biblical text records. **Rashi** notes that the Queen came not merely to admire but to test — her hard questions were genuine intellectual challenges that Shlomo's wisdom could answer.

### The End of an Era

The chapter concludes with Shlomo's death and the assessment of his forty-year reign. Jewish tradition is ambivalent about Shlomo's legacy: the Talmud (Sanhedrin 20b) debates whether Shlomo maintained his righteousness throughout his life. The Chronicler, notably, omits the account of Shlomo's foreign wives found in 1 Kings 11.
