## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 27 engages the narrative of jotham's reign through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Yotam's Brief Record

The brevity of Yotam's (Jotham's) chapter is itself significant in Jewish interpretation. **Radak** observes that the Chronicler has little negative to report — a rare achievement among Judah's kings. The assessment "he became mighty because he ordered his ways before HaShem his God" (27:6) is a model of the connection between faithfulness and strength.

### Learning from a Father

**Ralbag** notes that Yotam learned from Uziyahu's mistake — he "did not enter the Temple of HaShem" (27:2). The capacity to learn from the errors of previous generations is a key rabbinic value, expressed in the principle that the wise person learns from everyone (Pirkei Avot 4:1).
