## Overview

In Judaism, 2 Chronicles (*Divrei HaYamim Bet*) occupies a unique position as the final book of the Tanakh in the traditional Jewish ordering. The book recounts the history of the Davidic monarchy from Solomon through the destruction of the First Temple, concluding with the edict of Cyrus — a note of hope that ends the entire Hebrew Bible on a forward-looking trajectory toward return and restoration.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Position in the Canon
Unlike the Christian ordering, which places Chronicles among the historical books, the Jewish Tanakh places it at the very end of the Ketuvim (Writings). The final verses (36:22-23) — "Whoever is among you of all His people, let him go up" — serve as the closing words of the entire Hebrew Bible, a call to return to the Land.

### Rabbinic Interpretation
The Talmud (Megillah 13a) discusses the Chronicler's method, noting that genealogies and narratives omitted elsewhere are supplied here to complete the record. **Rashi** comments on the theological significance of the Chronicler's emphasis on repentance, particularly Manasseh's *teshuvah* (2 Chronicles 33:12-13), which is extensively discussed in the Midrash.

### Medieval Commentators
- **Rashi**: Concise peshat with midrashic supplements
- **Radak (David Kimchi)**: Grammatical and contextual analysis
- **Ralbag (Gersonides)**: Philosophical and moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*)
- **Abarbanel**: Extended theological and political commentary

## Theological Significance

2 Chronicles addresses questions central to post-exilic Judaism:
- The centrality of the Temple and its worship
- The meaning of divine retribution and repentance
- The hope of restoration embedded in the Cyrus edict
