## The Churban and the Edict of Koresh

Chapter 36 narrates the Churban Bayit Rishon (Destruction of the First Temple) by Nevuchadnetzar and concludes with the edict of Koresh (Cyrus) permitting the return. As the final chapter of the entire Tanakh in the traditional Jewish ordering, these verses carry immense theological weight.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The End of the Tanakh

The Talmud (Bava Batra 14b) places Divrei HaYamim at the end of the Ketuvim. The final words — "let him go up" (*ya'al*) — serve as the closing summons of the entire Hebrew Bible: a call to return to the Land and rebuild. **Rashi** notes that this open ending transforms the Tanakh from a chronicle of loss into an invitation to hope.

### The Seventy Years

The Chronicler interprets the exile as lasting seventy years so the land could observe its Sabbaths (36:21; cf. Leviticus 26:34-35). **Radak** connects this to the Shemitah cycles that Israel failed to observe, calculating that 70 sabbatical years correspond to 490 years of neglect.

## Theological Significance

- **The Churban**: The destruction of the Temple is the defining trauma of Jewish history
- **Shabbat HaAretz**: The land has its own covenant rights
- **Teshuvah and restoration**: The Cyrus edict signals that exile is not permanent
- **The Tanakh's final word**: The Hebrew Bible ends with an invitation, not a verdict


### The Final Kings

**Radak** notes the rapid succession of the last four kings of Judah: Yeho'achaz, Yehoyakim, Yehoyakhin, and Tzidkiyahu. Each reigns only briefly, and each "did what was evil in the eyes of HaShem." The Chronicler compresses this period to convey the inevitability of the approaching catastrophe.

### "Until the Land Had Enjoyed Its Sabbaths" (36:21)

**Rashi** connects the seventy years of exile to the shemitah (sabbatical year) cycles that Israel failed to observe. The land, personified in this verse, has its own covenantal rights — a principle that underlies the halakhic framework of the agricultural laws.
