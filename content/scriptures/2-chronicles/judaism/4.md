## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 4 engages the narrative of temple furnishings through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### The Yam (Sea) of Bronze

The great bronze basin (*yam*) is one of the most distinctive features of Shlomo's Temple. The Talmud (Eruvin 14b) uses its dimensions to calculate the value of pi. **Rashi** notes that the twelve oxen supporting the basin represent the twelve tribes, with each group of three facing one of the four cardinal directions.

### The Menorah and Shulchan

**Radak** observes that Shlomo multiplies the menorah (lampstand) from one to ten and the shulchan (table) from one to ten, expanding the tabernacle's pattern. This multiplication reflects the greater glory of the permanent Temple compared to the portable mishkan.
