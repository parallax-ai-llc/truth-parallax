## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 33 engages the narrative of manasseh and amon through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Menasheh's Teshuvah

Menasheh's (Manasseh's) repentance is extensively discussed in rabbinic literature. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 103a) records a debate about whether Menasheh has a share in the World to Come, with the conclusion that his repentance was accepted. **Rashi** notes that the Chronicler's account of Menasheh's repentance (absent from 2 Kings) demonstrates that no person is beyond the reach of *teshuvah*.

### The Prayer of Menasheh

The apocryphal *Prayer of Manasseh* elaborates on 33:12-13. While not part of the Jewish canon, the prayer reflects themes found in rabbinic *teshuvah* literature: the acknowledgment of sin, the appeal to divine mercy, and the commitment to live differently.
