## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 25 engages the narrative of amaziah's reign through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Amatzyahu's Partial Righteousness

**Rashi** comments on the assessment "he did right in the eyes of HaShem, but not with a complete heart" (25:2), noting that Amatzyahu's (Amaziah's) devotion was sincere but wavering. The Midrash contrasts him with David, whose heart was *shalem* (complete) before HaShem.

### The Edomite Gods

**Radak** expresses astonishment at Amatzyahu's decision to worship the gods of a people he has just conquered: "What possible reason could there be to worship gods that could not save their own worshippers?" The irrationality of idolatry is a recurring theme in prophetic literature.
