## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 6 engages the narrative of solomon's prayer of dedication through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Shlomo's Tefillah (Prayer)

Shlomo's prayer of dedication is one of the foundational texts for Jewish prayer theology. The Talmud (Berakhot 28b-30a) derives from this prayer the principle that one should direct one's prayer toward Yerushalayim and the site of the Beit HaMikdash — the origin of the practice of praying facing east (*mizrach*) in the diaspora.

### The Seven Petitions

**Abarbanel** analyzes the seven specific scenarios Shlomo addresses in his prayer, noting their comprehensive scope: from individual oaths to national emergencies, from drought to military defeat. Each scenario presupposes the Temple as the focal point of prayer — a theological conviction that survived the Temple's destruction and was incorporated into synagogue worship.
