## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 3 engages the narrative of building the temple through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### The Site of the Temple (3:1)

"Then Shlomo began to build the house of HaShem at Yerushalayim on Har HaMoriah." The Talmud (Berakhot 62b) and Midrash extensively discuss the identification of Moriah with the site of the Akeidah (binding of Yitzchak). **Rashi** notes that the Temple stands on the site of the greatest act of devotion in Jewish history, linking sacrifice to worship across the generations.

### The Amot (Measurements)

The Chronicler specifies that the measurements follow "the old standard" (*ba'amah harishonah*). **Radak** explains this as the longer cubit used in the Beit HaMikdash, distinct from the shorter cubit used for secular measurement. The precision of the measurements reflects the halakhic principle that sacred matters require exactness.
