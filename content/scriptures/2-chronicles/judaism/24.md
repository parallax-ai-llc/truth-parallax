## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 24 engages the narrative of joash restores the temple through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Yo'ash and the Temple Repair

**Ralbag** notes that Yo'ash's (Joash's) initiative to repair the Temple demonstrates the principle that the physical maintenance of sacred space is a religious obligation, not merely a practical concern. The deterioration of the Temple during Atalyahu's reign represents both physical neglect and spiritual decay.

### The Murder of Zekharyah (24:20-22)

The murder of Zekharyah ben Yehoyada in the Temple court is extensively discussed in the Talmud (Gittin 57b). His dying words — "May HaShem see and avenge" — are connected by the rabbis to the destruction of the Temple. Jesus refers to this incident (Matthew 23:35), and the rabbinic tradition traces a direct line from this murder to the Churban.
