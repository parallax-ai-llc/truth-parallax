## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 29 engages the narrative of hezekiah purifies the temple through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Chizkiyahu's Temple Cleansing

**Rashi** observes that Chizkiyahu (Hezekiah) opens the Temple doors "in the first month of the first year of his reign" — he does not delay even one day beyond his accession. The Midrash praises this urgency as evidence of genuine devotion: the righteous do not postpone mitzvot.

### The Levitical Music

The Chronicler's detailed account of the musical restoration — with cymbals, harps, lyres, and trumpets — reflects the importance of music in Temple worship. The Talmud (Arakhin 10a-11a) discusses whether the essential element of the Levitical service was instrumental or vocal.
