## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 35 engages the narrative of josiah's passover and death through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### The Great Pesach

**Rashi** observes that the Chronicler calls Yoshiyahu's Pesach the greatest since the time of Shmu'el (Samuel) — placing it even above Chizkiyahu's Pesach (chapter 30). The meticulous organization of the Levitical service reflects the halakhic requirements for the Pesach offering.

### Yoshiyahu's Death at Megiddo

The death of Yoshiyahu at the hands of Pharaoh Necho is one of the most theologically perplexing events in Chronicles. **Abarbanel** offers multiple explanations, including the possibility that Yoshiyahu believed he was fulfilling a prophetic destiny. The Talmud (Ta'anit 22b) and kinot (lamentations) traditions mourn Yoshiyahu's death as a national tragedy second only to the Churban.
