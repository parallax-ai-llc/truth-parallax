## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 32 engages the narrative of sennacherib's invasion through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Sancheriv's Challenge

The confrontation between Chizkiyahu and Sancheriv (Sennacherib) is extensively discussed in the Talmud (Sanhedrin 94a-95b). The rabbis note that Chizkiyahu's merit was so great that he was a candidate for the Mashiach — but the attribute of justice (*middat hadin*) argued against it because he had not composed a song of praise after the miraculous deliverance.

### "With Us Is HaShem Our God" (32:8)

**Radak** contrasts Chizkiyahu's faith with Achaz's fear: where Achaz sought Assyrian alliance, Chizkiyahu trusts HaShem. The Midrash presents this as the defining difference between a righteous king and a wicked one — not ability or intelligence, but trust in HaShem.
