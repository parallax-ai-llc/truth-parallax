## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 10 engages the narrative of the kingdom divided through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### The Division in Jewish Memory

The division of the kingdom is one of the formative tragedies of Jewish history. The Midrash (Seder Olam Rabbah) traces the consequences of this schism through the centuries, ultimately connecting it to the loss of the ten tribes. **Abarbanel** provides an extensive political analysis, arguing that Rechav'am's (Rehoboam's) folly was both avoidable and providential.

### "To Your Tents, O Israel!" (10:16)

This cry of secession echoes an earlier rebellion (2 Samuel 20:1) and marks the permanent fracture of the united monarchy. **Radak** notes the irony: the phrase "to your tents" invokes the nomadic wilderness era, as though the northern tribes are returning to a pre-monarchic state.
