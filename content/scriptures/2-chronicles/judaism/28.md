## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 28 engages the narrative of ahaz's wicked reign through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Achaz's Comprehensive Apostasy

**Abarbanel** provides an extensive analysis of Achaz's (Ahaz's) reign, noting that his sins represent a systematic inversion of covenant theology: he closes the Temple, builds high places, and sacrifices his children. Each action undoes a specific element of the covenant relationship established through Moshe and David.

### The Northern Mercy (28:9-15)

The prophet Oded's intervention to free Judahite captives is a striking narrative. **Ralbag** notes the irony: the schismatic northern kingdom demonstrates greater covenantal sensitivity than the Davidic king. The *to'elet* is that faithfulness to HaShem is not a function of political allegiance but of moral conduct.
