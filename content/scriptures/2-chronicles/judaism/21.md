## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 21 engages the narrative of jehoram's wicked reign through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Eliyahu's Letter

The letter from Eliyahu (Elijah) to Yehoram (Jehoram) is one of the most unusual events in Chronicles. **Radak** discusses the chronological difficulty — did Eliyahu write this before his ascent to heaven, or was it sent supernaturally? The Midrash explores both possibilities, reflecting the rabbinic conviction that prophetic authority transcends ordinary temporal boundaries.

### "To No One's Regret" (21:20)

The Chronicler's terse obituary — "he departed with no one's regret" (*b'lo chemdat*) — is among the harshest in Scripture. **Rashi** contrasts this with the mourning for righteous kings, noting that a king who leads his people into idolatry forfeits not only divine favor but human affection.
