## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Divrei HaYamim Bet chapter 2 engages the narrative of preparations for the temple through the lenses of rabbinic commentary, midrash, and halakhic implication. The Chronicler's distinctive theological perspective — emphasizing the Temple, the Leviim, and the principle of divine retribution — shapes the tradition's reading.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** provides concise peshat (plain sense) commentary, often supplemented by midrashic material that illuminates the theological dimensions of the narrative. **Radak** offers grammatical precision and contextual analysis. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws practical moral lessons (*to'aliyyot*) from the events described.

### The Chronicler's Theology

The Chronicler's emphasis on retribution — blessing for faithfulness, punishment for apostasy — reflects a theological framework that the rabbis engaged critically. The Talmud and Midrash sometimes qualify this framework, noting that righteous suffering and wicked prosperity also exist.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah and kingship**: Kings are measured by their fidelity to the Torah
- **The Beit HaMikdash**: The Temple is the center of Israel's religious life
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, as the Chronicler repeatedly demonstrates
- **Divine justice**: HaShem rewards faithfulness and disciplines apostasy


### Shlomo's Letter to Hiram

**Radak** notes that Shlomo's letter to Hiram (Huram) contains a remarkable theological statement: "The heavens, even the highest heavens, cannot contain Him. Who then am I to build a temple for Him?" (2:6). This awareness of divine transcendence alongside the project of building a physical dwelling illustrates the paradox at the heart of Temple theology.

### The Gerim (Resident Aliens)

The Chronicler specifies that Shlomo uses non-Israelite labor for the Temple construction. **Ralbag** draws a moral lesson about the relationship between Israel and the nations: the nations participate in building the Beit HaMikdash, foreshadowing the prophetic vision that all peoples will come to worship at God's house (Isaiah 56:7).
