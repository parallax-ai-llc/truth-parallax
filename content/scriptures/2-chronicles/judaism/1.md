## Shlomo's Wisdom at Givon

The opening chapter of Divrei HaYamim Bet recounts Shlomo's visit to the mishkan (tabernacle) at Givon, where HaShem appears and grants his request for wisdom. Jewish interpretation emphasizes Shlomo's unique status: the Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 21b) states that wisdom was given to Shlomo to a degree unmatched before or since.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Nature of Shlomo's Wisdom

**Rashi** notes that Shlomo asked specifically for *chokhmah* (wisdom) and *mada* (knowledge) — practical wisdom for governance, not abstract philosophy. The Midrash (Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:1) elaborates that Shlomo's wisdom encompassed knowledge of Torah, natural science, and the languages of all creatures.

### The Chronicler's Purpose

**Radak** observes that the Chronicler begins with Shlomo's wisdom to establish the theological foundation of his reign: the Temple can only be built by a king whose wisdom comes from HaShem.

## Theological Significance

- **Wisdom as divine gift**: True *chokhmah* comes from HaShem alone
- **The king's responsibility**: Wisdom is given for the sake of the people
- **Preparation for the Temple**: Shlomo's wisdom is the prerequisite for building the Beit HaMikdash
- **Humility**: Shlomo's willingness to ask reveals proper relationship to HaShem

### Haftarah and Liturgical Connection

The narratives of Divrei HaYamim, while not directly assigned as haftarah readings, inform the liturgical consciousness of the Jewish community. The themes of Temple worship, divine justice, and the possibility of teshuvah resonate throughout the prayer book, particularly in the High Holy Day liturgy and the kinot of Tisha B'Av.

### Ethical Implications

The Midrash draws from the narratives of Judah's kings practical ethical lessons that apply beyond the royal court. The principles of just governance, faithful worship, and accountability before HaShem are binding on every Jew, not only on rulers.
