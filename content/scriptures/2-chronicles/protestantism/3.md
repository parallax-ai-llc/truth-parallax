## Overview

Chapter 3 of 2 Chronicles continues the Chronicler's account of Solomon's reign, focusing on building the temple. Protestant interpreters read this material through the lens of covenant theology and typological fulfillment.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Context

The Chronicler presents Solomon's activities as the fulfillment of God's promises to David (1 Chronicles 17). Every aspect of the Temple project — from planning to execution — is grounded in divine covenant. Protestant theology emphasizes that human achievement serves God's sovereign purposes.

### Typological Significance

Protestant exegetes read the details of building the temple as types pointing to Christ and the Church. The Temple's construction foreshadows the building of the Church as God's dwelling place (Ephesians 2:19-22).

## Theological Significance

- **Divine faithfulness**: God fulfills His promises to David through Solomon
- **Worship**: The Temple establishes ordered worship as central to Israel's identity
- **Typology**: The earthly Temple points to heavenly realities (Hebrews 9)
- **Obedience**: Solomon's work proceeds according to God's revealed pattern


### The Holy of Holies (3:8-13)

The Most Holy Place, overlaid with fine gold and guarded by cherubim whose wings span the entire width of the room, represents the presence of God in His fullness. **Calvin** notes that the cherubim's outstretched wings suggest both protection and worship — the angelic hosts guard and glorify the divine presence.

### The Veil (3:14)

The veil separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place is made of blue, purple, and crimson yarn. Protestant typology reads the veil as a barrier removed by Christ's death (Matthew 27:51), opening access to God for all believers.
