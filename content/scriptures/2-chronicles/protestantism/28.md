## Overview

Chapter 28 of 2 Chronicles presents the Chronicler's account of ahaz's wicked reign. Protestant interpreters read this material within the framework of covenant theology, emphasizing divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Faithfulness

The Chronicler's presentation of ahaz's wicked reign illustrates the recurring pattern of 2 Chronicles: obedience brings blessing, while apostasy invites judgment. Protestant exegesis reads this not as mechanical retribution but as the outworking of covenant relationship.

### The Davidic Promise

Even through the vicissitudes of individual reigns, God's promise to David endures. The Chronicler preserves the continuity of the Davidic line as evidence of God's faithfulness to His covenant.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine sovereignty**: God directs the course of history through and despite human choices
- **Human responsibility**: Kings are accountable for their response to God's word
- **The prophetic word**: God sends prophets to call rulers to faithfulness
- **Typological significance**: The successes and failures of Judah's kings point to the need for a perfect King


### Ahaz's Comprehensive Apostasy (28:1-4)

Ahaz reverses every reform of his predecessors: he makes images of the Baals, burns incense in the Valley of Ben Hinnom, and sacrifices his children in the fire. The Chronicler presents Ahaz as Judah's worst king before Manasseh — a systematic dismantling of covenant worship.

### The Prophetic Intervention of Oded (28:9-15)

When the northern army captures Judahite prisoners, the prophet Oded confronts them: "Are you not also guilty of sins against the LORD your God?" The northerners release the captives and provide for them. Protestant interpreters note the remarkable irony: the schismatic northern kingdom shows more compassion than the Davidic king.
