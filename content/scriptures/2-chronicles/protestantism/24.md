## Overview

Chapter 24 of 2 Chronicles presents the Chronicler's account of joash restores the temple. Protestant interpreters read this material within the framework of covenant theology, emphasizing divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Faithfulness

The Chronicler's presentation of joash restores the temple illustrates the recurring pattern of 2 Chronicles: obedience brings blessing, while apostasy invites judgment. Protestant exegesis reads this not as mechanical retribution but as the outworking of covenant relationship.

### The Davidic Promise

Even through the vicissitudes of individual reigns, God's promise to David endures. The Chronicler preserves the continuity of the Davidic line as evidence of God's faithfulness to His covenant.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine sovereignty**: God directs the course of history through and despite human choices
- **Human responsibility**: Kings are accountable for their response to God's word
- **The prophetic word**: God sends prophets to call rulers to faithfulness
- **Typological significance**: The successes and failures of Judah's kings point to the need for a perfect King


### Temple Repair Under Joash (24:4-14)

Joash initiates repair of the Temple, collecting offerings through a chest placed at the Temple gate. Protestant interpreters note that Joash's reform is dependent on Jehoiada's guidance — the young king acts faithfully only as long as his mentor lives.

### The Murder of Zechariah (24:20-22)

After Jehoiada's death, Joash turns to idolatry. When Zechariah the priest rebukes the people, Joash orders his execution — in the Temple court. Jesus references this incident in Matthew 23:35. Protestant exegesis reads this as one of Scripture's starkest warnings: the same king who restored the Temple murders a prophet within its courts.
