## Hezekiah's Great Passover

Hezekiah invites all Israel and Judah — including the northern tribes — to celebrate the Passover in Jerusalem. Though many northerners mock the invitation, some from Asher, Manasseh, and Zebulun humble themselves and come. The celebration extends for an additional seven days because of the people's joy.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Invitation to the North (30:6-9)

Hezekiah's couriers carry a message of grace: "Return to the LORD, the God of Abraham, Isaac and Israel, that he may return to you who are left." Protestant interpreters read this as a model of evangelistic outreach and an echo of 7:14.

### Grace for the Unclean (30:18-20)

Many participants eat the Passover without proper ceremonial purification. Hezekiah prays for them, and God heals the people. Protestant theology cites this as evidence that God values the heart's intention over ritual perfection — a principle central to the Reformation's critique of ceremonialism.

## Theological Significance

- **Unity in worship**: Hezekiah reaches across the political division
- **Grace over ritual**: God accepts imperfect worship offered with sincere hearts
- **Revival**: Extended celebration signals genuine spiritual renewal
- **The Passover as center**: Covenant identity is renewed through the Passover meal

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
