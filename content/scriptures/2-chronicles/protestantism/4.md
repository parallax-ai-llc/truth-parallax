## Overview

Chapter 4 of 2 Chronicles continues the Chronicler's account of Solomon's reign, focusing on temple furnishings. Protestant interpreters read this material through the lens of covenant theology and typological fulfillment.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Context

The Chronicler presents Solomon's activities as the fulfillment of God's promises to David (1 Chronicles 17). Every aspect of the Temple project — from planning to execution — is grounded in divine covenant. Protestant theology emphasizes that human achievement serves God's sovereign purposes.

### Typological Significance

Protestant exegetes read the details of temple furnishings as types pointing to Christ and the Church. The Temple's construction foreshadows the building of the Church as God's dwelling place (Ephesians 2:19-22).

## Theological Significance

- **Divine faithfulness**: God fulfills His promises to David through Solomon
- **Worship**: The Temple establishes ordered worship as central to Israel's identity
- **Typology**: The earthly Temple points to heavenly realities (Hebrews 9)
- **Obedience**: Solomon's work proceeds according to God's revealed pattern


### The Bronze Sea (4:2-5)

The massive bronze basin held approximately 17,500 gallons of water and rested on twelve bronze bulls. Protestant interpreters see the twelve bulls as representing the twelve tribes and the water as symbolizing purification. The sheer scale of the basin emphasizes the magnitude of cleansing required for priestly service.

### The Golden Lampstands (4:7)

Solomon makes ten golden lampstands, whereas the tabernacle had only one. **Selman** observes that the multiplication of light symbolizes the increase of divine revelation and presence in the permanent Temple compared to the portable tabernacle.
