## Overview

Chapter 26 of 2 Chronicles presents the Chronicler's account of uzziah's reign. Protestant interpreters read this material within the framework of covenant theology, emphasizing divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Faithfulness

The Chronicler's presentation of uzziah's reign illustrates the recurring pattern of 2 Chronicles: obedience brings blessing, while apostasy invites judgment. Protestant exegesis reads this not as mechanical retribution but as the outworking of covenant relationship.

### The Davidic Promise

Even through the vicissitudes of individual reigns, God's promise to David endures. The Chronicler preserves the continuity of the Davidic line as evidence of God's faithfulness to His covenant.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine sovereignty**: God directs the course of history through and despite human choices
- **Human responsibility**: Kings are accountable for their response to God's word
- **The prophetic word**: God sends prophets to call rulers to faithfulness
- **Typological significance**: The successes and failures of Judah's kings point to the need for a perfect King


### Uzziah's Prosperity (26:4-15)

Uzziah's long and successful reign includes military victories, agricultural development, and technological innovation. The Chronicler attributes this success to the fact that "he sought God during the days of Zechariah, who instructed him in the fear of God."

### Uzziah's Presumption (26:16-21)

At the height of his power, Uzziah enters the Temple to burn incense — a function reserved for priests. When confronted by Azariah the priest and eighty other priests, Uzziah becomes angry and is immediately struck with leprosy. Protestant theology reads this as a defining illustration: no human authority, however successful, may usurp the roles that God has assigned to others.
