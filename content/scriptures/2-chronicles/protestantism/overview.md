## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 2 Chronicles reads the Chronicler's history through the lens of *sola scriptura* and covenant theology. The book's emphasis on temple worship, royal reform, and the consequences of obedience or rebellion aligns naturally with Protestant concerns about the authority of God's word, justification by faith, and the church's calling to continual reformation (*semper reformanda*).

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Temple as Type of Christ
Protestant exegetes read Solomon's Temple not as an end in itself but as a type pointing to Christ. Jesus' declaration "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" (John 2:19) reframes the entire Temple narrative as Christological prophecy.

### The Reformation Pattern
The great reformers of 2 Chronicles — Asa, Jehoshaphat, Hezekiah, and Josiah — are read as models for the Reformation. Their return to the Book of the Law parallels the Reformers' recovery of Scripture as the supreme authority.

### 2 Chronicles 7:14
"If my people, who are called by my name, will humble themselves and pray" — this verse is among the most frequently cited in Protestant devotional tradition, read as a paradigm for revival and national repentance.
