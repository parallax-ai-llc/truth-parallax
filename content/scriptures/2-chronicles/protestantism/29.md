## Hezekiah Cleanses the Temple

Hezekiah becomes king and immediately initiates a sweeping religious reform. In his first month he reopens the Temple doors, summons the priests and Levites, and commands them to consecrate themselves and purify the sanctuary. The cleansing takes sixteen days, after which proper worship is restored with sacrifices, Levitical music, and joyous celebration.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Do Not Be Negligent" (29:11)

Hezekiah's charge to the Levites — "My sons, do not be negligent now, for the LORD has chosen you to stand before him and serve him" — is read by Protestant interpreters as a call to faithful ministry. **Selman** notes that the urgency of Hezekiah's reform contrasts with Ahaz's deliberate closure of the Temple.

### The Priority of Worship

Protestant theology reads Hezekiah's first act as programmatic: national renewal begins with restoring true worship. This parallels the Reformation's insistence that reforming worship is the first task of church reform.

## Theological Significance

- **Reform begins with worship**: The Temple is cleansed before anything else
- **Urgency**: Hezekiah does not delay — reform cannot wait
- **Levitical order**: Proper worship requires properly consecrated leaders
- **Joy**: The restoration of worship produces deep joy (29:36)

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
