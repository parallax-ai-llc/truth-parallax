## Sustaining the Reform

Following the Passover, the people go out and destroy the high places, sacred stones, and Asherah poles throughout Judah and into the northern territories. Hezekiah then reorganizes the Levitical divisions and establishes a system of tithes and offerings to support the Temple ministry.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Generous Giving (31:5-10)

The people respond to Hezekiah's call with such generosity that the priests have more than enough. **Dillard** observes that the Chronicler presents generous giving as the natural fruit of genuine revival — hearts renewed in worship overflow in material generosity.

### Institutional Reform (31:11-19)

Hezekiah creates storerooms and appoints administrators to distribute provisions fairly among the Levites. Protestant interpreters note the combination of spiritual zeal and practical administration — true reform addresses both worship and organizational structures.

## Theological Significance

- **Reform must be sustained**: Destroying idolatry is not enough; positive structures must replace them
- **Generosity as fruit of revival**: Renewed hearts produce open hands
- **Order and administration**: Spiritual vitality requires organizational support
- **The Levitical principle**: Those who serve the Lord should be materially supported

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
