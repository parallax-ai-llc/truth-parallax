## Josiah's Passover and Tragic Death

Chapter 35 records the greatest Passover celebration since the time of Samuel, followed by Josiah's inexplicable decision to confront Pharaoh Neco at Megiddo, resulting in his death. The combination of spiritual triumph and military tragedy creates one of the most poignant narratives in Chronicles.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Greatest Passover (35:18)

"No Passover like it had been kept in Israel since the days of Samuel the prophet." Protestant interpreters note that the Chronicler places Josiah's Passover above even Hezekiah's (ch. 30), emphasizing the completeness of Josiah's devotion to the law.

### Josiah's Fatal Decision (35:20-24)

Neco warns Josiah that he has no quarrel with Judah, claiming to be on a divine mission. Josiah refuses to listen and is mortally wounded. The Chronicler notes that Neco's words were "from the mouth of God" (35:22). Protestant exegesis wrestles with the irony: the king who listened to God's word through Huldah refuses to hear God's word through a pagan pharaoh.

## Theological Significance

- **Faithful worship**: Josiah's Passover represents the pinnacle of reform
- **Human unpredictability**: Even the most faithful king can make a fatal error
- **God speaks through unexpected channels**: Neco's warning comes from God
- **Grief and loss**: Jeremiah composes laments for Josiah — the loss is incalculable

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
