## Overview

Chapter 6 of 2 Chronicles continues the Chronicler's account of Solomon's reign, focusing on solomon's prayer of dedication. Protestant interpreters read this material through the lens of covenant theology and typological fulfillment.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Context

The Chronicler presents Solomon's activities as the fulfillment of God's promises to David (1 Chronicles 17). Every aspect of the Temple project — from planning to execution — is grounded in divine covenant. Protestant theology emphasizes that human achievement serves God's sovereign purposes.

### Typological Significance

Protestant exegetes read the details of solomon's prayer of dedication as types pointing to Christ and the Church. The Temple's construction foreshadows the building of the Church as God's dwelling place (Ephesians 2:19-22).

## Theological Significance

- **Divine faithfulness**: God fulfills His promises to David through Solomon
- **Worship**: The Temple establishes ordered worship as central to Israel's identity
- **Typology**: The earthly Temple points to heavenly realities (Hebrews 9)
- **Obedience**: Solomon's work proceeds according to God's revealed pattern


### Solomon's Extended Prayer (6:14-42)

Solomon's prayer of dedication is one of the longest prayers in Scripture. It systematically addresses seven scenarios in which Israel might call upon God from the Temple: oath-taking, military defeat, drought, famine, individual prayer, foreigner prayer, and warfare. Protestant interpreters read this as a comprehensive theology of prayer.

### The Foreigner's Prayer (6:32-33)

Remarkably, Solomon prays that God will hear even foreigners who pray toward the Temple, "so that all the peoples of the earth may know your name." Protestant missiology cites this as evidence that God's salvific purpose has always been universal.
