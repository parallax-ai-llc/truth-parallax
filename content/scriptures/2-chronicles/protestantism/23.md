## Overview

Chapter 23 of 2 Chronicles presents the Chronicler's account of joash crowned king. Protestant interpreters read this material within the framework of covenant theology, emphasizing divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Faithfulness

The Chronicler's presentation of joash crowned king illustrates the recurring pattern of 2 Chronicles: obedience brings blessing, while apostasy invites judgment. Protestant exegesis reads this not as mechanical retribution but as the outworking of covenant relationship.

### The Davidic Promise

Even through the vicissitudes of individual reigns, God's promise to David endures. The Chronicler preserves the continuity of the Davidic line as evidence of God's faithfulness to His covenant.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine sovereignty**: God directs the course of history through and despite human choices
- **Human responsibility**: Kings are accountable for their response to God's word
- **The prophetic word**: God sends prophets to call rulers to faithfulness
- **Typological significance**: The successes and failures of Judah's kings point to the need for a perfect King


### Jehoiada's Covenant (23:1-3)

The priest Jehoiada orchestrates a carefully planned coup to overthrow Athaliah and restore the Davidic line. Protestant interpreters read this as a model of godly leadership: Jehoiada acts within the covenant framework, first making a covenant with the military commanders "in the house of God."

### "The King's Son Shall Reign" (23:3)

Jehoiada's declaration — "The king's son shall reign, as the LORD promised concerning the sons of David" — grounds the entire action in God's covenant promise. Protestant theology emphasizes that political restoration is secondary to covenant faithfulness.
