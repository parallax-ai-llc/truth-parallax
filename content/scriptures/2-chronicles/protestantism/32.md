## Sennacherib's Invasion and God's Deliverance

After Hezekiah's reforms, Sennacherib of Assyria invades Judah. Hezekiah prepares Jerusalem's defenses and delivers a remarkable speech of encouragement: "With him is only the arm of flesh, but with us is the LORD our God to help us and to fight our battles" (32:7-8). God sends an angel who destroys the Assyrian army, and Sennacherib retreats in disgrace.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "With Us Is the LORD Our God" (32:7-8)

This is one of the great confessions of faith in Scripture. Protestant interpreters read it alongside Isaiah 36-37 as a model of trusting God against overwhelming military power. **Calvin** notes that Hezekiah's confidence is not presumption but faith grounded in covenant promise.

### The Theological Interpretation (32:20-21)

The Chronicler's account is briefer than 2 Kings 18-19 but preserves the essential theology: Hezekiah and Isaiah pray, and the LORD sends an angel. Protestant exegesis emphasizes the primacy of prayer over military strategy.

## Theological Significance

- **Faith over force**: God's power, not military might, determines the outcome
- **Prayer in crisis**: Hezekiah and Isaiah pray rather than panic
- **The vindication of reform**: God protects the king who has honored Him
- **Humility after victory**: Hezekiah's pride (32:25-26) reminds readers that faithfulness must be maintained

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
