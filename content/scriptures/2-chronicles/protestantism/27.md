## Overview

Chapter 27 of 2 Chronicles presents the Chronicler's account of jotham's reign. Protestant interpreters read this material within the framework of covenant theology, emphasizing divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Faithfulness

The Chronicler's presentation of jotham's reign illustrates the recurring pattern of 2 Chronicles: obedience brings blessing, while apostasy invites judgment. Protestant exegesis reads this not as mechanical retribution but as the outworking of covenant relationship.

### The Davidic Promise

Even through the vicissitudes of individual reigns, God's promise to David endures. The Chronicler preserves the continuity of the Davidic line as evidence of God's faithfulness to His covenant.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine sovereignty**: God directs the course of history through and despite human choices
- **Human responsibility**: Kings are accountable for their response to God's word
- **The prophetic word**: God sends prophets to call rulers to faithfulness
- **Typological significance**: The successes and failures of Judah's kings point to the need for a perfect King


### Jotham's Quiet Faithfulness (27:2, 6)

"He did what was right in the eyes of the LORD, just as his father Uzziah had done, but unlike him he did not enter the temple of the LORD." Jotham learned from his father's mistake. The Chronicler's assessment — "Jotham grew powerful because he walked steadfastly before the LORD his God" — is a model of quiet, consistent faithfulness.

### The Brief Chapter

Chapter 27 is one of the shortest in 2 Chronicles, yet Protestant interpreters find significance in its brevity. **Selman** observes that the Chronicler has little to report because Jotham's reign was quietly faithful — no dramatic reforms or catastrophic failures, simply steady obedience.
