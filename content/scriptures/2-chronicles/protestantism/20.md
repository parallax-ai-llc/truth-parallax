## Jehoshaphat's Prayer and Victory

A vast army of Moabites, Ammonites, and Meunites advances against Judah. Jehoshaphat proclaims a fast, gathers the people at the Temple, and prays one of the great prayers in Scripture. God responds through the prophet Jahaziel: "The battle is not yours, but God's." The enemy armies destroy one another, and Judah collects spoil for three days.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Jehoshaphat's Prayer (20:6-12)

"We do not know what to do, but our eyes are on you." Protestant interpreters rank this among the finest prayers in the Old Testament. **Davis** notes that the prayer follows the pattern of covenant appeal: recounting God's past faithfulness, presenting the current crisis, and confessing dependence.

### Praise Before Victory (20:21-22)

Jehoshaphat appoints singers to go before the army, praising God. As they begin to praise, the LORD sets ambushes. Protestant worship theology cites this as evidence that praise is a weapon of spiritual warfare.

## Theological Significance

- **Prayer in crisis**: The model response to overwhelming odds is prayer, not strategy
- **Corporate worship**: The entire community gathers to seek God
- **The battle is the LORD's**: Human inability is the context for divine power
- **Praise as faith**: Singing before the battle demonstrates trust in God's word

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
