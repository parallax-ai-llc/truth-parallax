## Jehoshaphat's Faithful Administration

Chapter 17 introduces Jehoshaphat as a king who walked in the early ways of David, removing the high places and Asherah poles. He sends officials, Levites, and priests throughout Judah to teach the Book of the Law — a program of national religious education unprecedented in Chronicles.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Teaching the Torah (17:7-9)

Protestant interpreters regard Jehoshaphat's teaching program as a model for the church's educational ministry. The Reformers saw in this passage a biblical precedent for the Protestant emphasis on biblical literacy. **Dillard** notes that the Chronicler presents Torah education as the foundation of national strength.

### Fear of the LORD Among the Nations (17:10)

The surrounding nations do not attack Judah because "the fear of the LORD fell on all the kingdoms of the lands." Protestant theology reads this as confirmation that spiritual faithfulness produces tangible blessings.

## Theological Significance

- **The authority of Scripture**: National renewal begins with teaching God's Word
- **The Reformation principle**: Reform requires education, not just legislation
- **God's protection**: Faithfulness brings security
- **Leadership by example**: Jehoshaphat walks in God's ways before requiring it of others

### Historical Context

Protestant historical-critical scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Chronicler's post-exilic theology. Writing after the return from Babylon, the Chronicler shaped the narrative to address the needs of the restored community — particularly questions about the Temple, the Davidic dynasty, and the conditions for divine blessing. Understanding this authorial context enriches the theological reading without diminishing the text's authority.

### Application

The Protestant tradition of applying Old Testament narratives to the life of the church finds rich material here. The patterns of faithfulness and failure, divine initiative and human response, judgment and mercy all speak directly to the church's ongoing experience.
