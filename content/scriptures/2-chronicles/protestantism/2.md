## Overview

Chapter 2 of 2 Chronicles continues the Chronicler's account of Solomon's reign, focusing on preparations for the temple. Protestant interpreters read this material through the lens of covenant theology and typological fulfillment.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Covenant Context

The Chronicler presents Solomon's activities as the fulfillment of God's promises to David (1 Chronicles 17). Every aspect of the Temple project — from planning to execution — is grounded in divine covenant. Protestant theology emphasizes that human achievement serves God's sovereign purposes.

### Typological Significance

Protestant exegetes read the details of preparations for the temple as types pointing to Christ and the Church. The Temple's construction foreshadows the building of the Church as God's dwelling place (Ephesians 2:19-22).

## Theological Significance

- **Divine faithfulness**: God fulfills His promises to David through Solomon
- **Worship**: The Temple establishes ordered worship as central to Israel's identity
- **Typology**: The earthly Temple points to heavenly realities (Hebrews 9)
- **Obedience**: Solomon's work proceeds according to God's revealed pattern


### Hiram of Tyre (2:3-16)

Solomon's correspondence with Hiram reveals an international dimension to the Temple project. **Dillard** notes that the Chronicler presents the pagan king as recognizing the LORD's sovereignty — "Because the LORD loves his people, he has made you king over them" (2:11). Protestant interpreters see this as evidence that God's glory extends beyond Israel's borders.

### The Alien Workers (2:17-18)

The Chronicler specifies that Solomon conscripts foreign laborers, not Israelites. Protestant exegesis observes the theological distinction: Israel is a kingdom of priests, not a labor force. This principle undergirds the Protestant understanding of Christian vocation.
