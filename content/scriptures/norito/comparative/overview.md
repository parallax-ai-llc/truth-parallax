## Overview

Comparative religious studies examines the Norito alongside liturgical prayers and ritual invocations from other world traditions, identifying structural parallels and illuminating differences. The norito's combination of praise, petition, mythological narrative, and communal purification finds echoes in traditions ranging from Vedic hymns and Hebrew psalms to Roman prayers and Native American invocations.

The comparative approach reveals both the universal dimensions of human prayer — the need to address the divine, to petition for protection and blessing, to purify and renew — and the distinctively Japanese features of the norito tradition, including kotodama theology, the emphasis on communal rather than individual prayer, and the intimate connection between prayer and agricultural life.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Comparative scholars analyze the norito using categories drawn from cross-cultural liturgical studies, examining the prayers' speech acts (invocation, praise, petition, thanksgiving), their ritual contexts, and their theological presuppositions. Particular attention is paid to the norito's treatment of purity and pollution, which invites comparison with similar concerns in Jewish, Zoroastrian, and Hindu traditions.
