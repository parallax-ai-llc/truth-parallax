## Overview

The philosophical reading of the Norito examines these ancient Shinto prayers as expressions of a distinctive Japanese metaphysics of language, sacred communication, and cosmic order. Central to this interpretation is the concept of kotodama — the belief that words possess an inherent spiritual power that, when correctly spoken, can influence the kami and shape reality itself.

Philosophical interpreters analyze the norito's formal structure, rhetorical strategies, and cosmological assumptions to reconstruct the worldview of ancient Japanese religion. The prayers reveal a participatory metaphysics in which human speech acts are not merely symbolic but ontologically effective — the correctly recited prayer does not merely represent a request but constitutes a real connection between the human and divine realms.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Philosophical readers attend to the norito's logic of address, praise, petition, and thanksgiving as revealing fundamental assumptions about the nature of the kami, the structure of the cosmos, and the human capacity for sacred communication. The prayers' avoidance of Chinese loanwords is read as evidence of a conscious commitment to linguistic purity in the sacred domain.
