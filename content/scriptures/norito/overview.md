## Overview

The Norito (祝詞) are the ancient ritual prayers of Shinto, recited by priests (kannushi) during shrine ceremonies to invoke, praise, petition, and communicate with the kami. The most authoritative collection of norito is preserved in Book 8 of the Engishiki (927 CE), though individual prayers in this corpus date to considerably earlier periods, some bearing linguistic features suggesting origins in the 7th or even 6th century CE.

These prayers occupy a unique position in world liturgical literature: composed in classical Japanese (yamato kotoba) with archaic grammatical forms and a distinctive rhythmic structure, the norito embody a theology of word-power (kotodama) in which the correctly intoned prayer is believed to possess intrinsic efficacy in bridging the human and divine realms. The language of the norito avoids Chinese loanwords almost entirely, preserving a purely indigenous Japanese vocabulary for the sacred.

## Structure

The twenty-seven prayers in this collection encompass the full range of Shinto ritual occasions:

1. **Agricultural Prayers (1–4)**: Harvest petitions, wind pacification, and food deity invocations
2. **Purification Prayers (5–7)**: The Great Purification, fire pacification, and road spirit offerings
3. **Imperial Rites (8–12)**: Enthronement, first fruits, and imperial purification ceremonies
4. **Shrine-Specific Prayers (13–18)**: Prayers for Ise, Izumo, Kamo, Matsunoo, Hirano, and Omiwa shrines
5. **Crisis Prayers (19–21)**: Epidemic pacification, drought relief, earthquake pacification
6. **Occasional Prayers (22–27)**: Lustration, accession, longevity, consecration, spirit pacification, year-end purification

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Religious Shinto**: The living liturgical core of shrine worship, still recited in ceremonies today
- **Philosophical Shinto**: Reveals the theology of kotodama (word-spirit) and the metaphysics of divine communication
- **Confucian**: Demonstrates the ritual formality and social hierarchy embedded in Japanese sacred address
- **Zen**: Studied for parallels with mantra traditions and the spiritual power of concentrated speech
- **Academic**: Critical texts for understanding ancient Japanese language, religion, and court ritual

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Kotodama | The belief in the spiritual power of correctly spoken words |
| Purification | Ritual cleansing of pollution (kegare) through prayer and ceremony |
| Kami Invocation | The formal summoning and addressing of divine beings |
| Imperial Authority | The emperor's role as intermediary between kami and people |
| Agricultural Cycle | Prayers tied to planting, growth, harvest, and seasonal transitions |
| Cosmic Order | The maintenance of harmony between the human and divine realms |
