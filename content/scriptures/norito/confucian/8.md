## Overview

The Confucian-Shinto reading of Prayer 8, Great Tasting Festival (Daijosai), highlights the prayer's expression of social hierarchy, ritual propriety, and the proper ordering of relationships between ruler, people, and divine powers. Confucian interpreters find in this prayer evidence for the compatibility of Confucian moral philosophy and Shinto religious practice.

This prayer addresses fundamental concerns of great tasting festival, engaging themes that are central to both the Shinto tradition and the broader human experience of seeking divine aid and blessing.

## Key Themes

### Ritual Propriety

Confucian interpreters read this prayer as an exemplary expression of ritual propriety (li/rei) — the careful attention to form, hierarchy, and procedure that Confucian philosophy considers essential to social harmony and moral cultivation. The prayer's formal language, its respectful mode of address to the kami, and its placement within a carefully ordered ceremony all reflect the Confucian conviction that proper ritual sustains the harmony of heaven, earth, and humanity.

### Social Order

The prayer's implicit social structure — with the emperor as chief ritualist, priests as intermediaries, and the people as beneficiaries — aligns with the Confucian understanding of society as a hierarchical but harmonious order in which each person fulfills their proper role. The prayer's petitions for peace, prosperity, and good harvests reflect the Confucian ideal of benevolent governance.

## Key Verses/Passages

Prayer 8 contains several passages of particular significance for confucian-shinto synthesis interpretation. The invocatory formulas, the mythological references, and the specific petitions reveal how the ancient Japanese priesthood understood the nature of divine-human communication and the role of ritual language in maintaining cosmic harmony.

## Commentary Tradition

The confucian tradition has engaged with this prayer through its distinctive interpretive lens, finding in its language and structure evidence for broader claims about the nature of kami, the power of ritual, and the relationship between human community and sacred reality. Commentators have paid particular attention to the prayer's archaic linguistic features and its placement within the broader liturgical calendar.
