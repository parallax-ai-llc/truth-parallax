## The Chariot in the Temple

Ezekiel 10 reprises the chariot-throne vision of chapter 1 within the temple precincts, identifying the living creatures as cherubim. The glory of the LORD rises from the threshold and departs eastward. The Merkabah tradition found in this chapter a critical complement to chapter 1: the chariot-throne is not stationary but mobile, appearing now in Babylon, now in the temple.

The departure of the *Shekhinah* (divine presence) from the temple is one of the most significant events in Jewish theological history. The Midrash (*Lamentations Rabbah*, Proem 25) traces the departure in ten stages, emphasizing God's reluctance: "The Shekhinah made ten journeys... it lingered, hoping Israel would repent." This midrashic elaboration expresses the rabbinic conviction that God does not abandon His people willingly.

Radak noted that the identification of the living creatures as cherubim connects the vision to the Holy of Holies, where the cherubim overshadowed the Ark. The chariot-throne is thus the mobile counterpart of the fixed sanctuary — God's presence is not abolished but liberated from a single location. This theological insight became foundational for rabbinic Judaism's survival without the temple.
