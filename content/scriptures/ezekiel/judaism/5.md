## The Razor and the Remnant

Ezekiel 5 commands the prophet to shave his head with a sword used as a razor and divide the hair into three portions, symbolizing the threefold fate of Jerusalem's population. A tiny remnant is preserved in the prophet's garment, though even some of these are cast into the fire.

Jewish interpretation of the remnant motif connects to the broader biblical theme of survival through catastrophe — Noah's family, the Exodus generation, the return from exile. The rabbis saw in the remnant theology a divine pattern: Israel is never fully destroyed because God preserves a seed from which renewal is possible. This theological conviction sustained Jewish communal resilience through centuries of persecution and destruction.

Rashi noted that the use of a sword as a razor — an implement of war employed for a personal act — symbolizes the intimate nature of the coming judgment. The destruction of Jerusalem is not a distant military campaign but a personal, devastating act that touches every individual. The detailed accounting of the hair's distribution (a third burned, a third struck, a third scattered) emphasizes divine precision: the judgment is not random but measured and specific.
