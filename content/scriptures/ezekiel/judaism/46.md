## The Prince's Worship

Ezekiel 46 details the prince's liturgical role and regulates inheritance and land transactions. Jewish commentators focused on the identity and authority of the prince (nasi), whom most identified as the messianic king. The regulations limiting his power -- particularly the prohibition against seizing the people's inheritance (46:18) -- were read as constitutional constraints on royal authority.

Rashi connected this prohibition to the historical abuses of Israelite kings, particularly Ahab's seizure of Naboth's vineyard (1 Kings 21). The eschatological prince will not repeat these sins because the constitution of the messianic community prevents it. Radak noted that this represents not the elimination of monarchy but its perfection -- authority exercised within divinely established limits.

The year of liberty mentioned in 46:17, in which land reverts to its original owner, connects the temple vision to the Jubilee legislation of Leviticus 25. Jewish social ethics derives from this principle the conviction that the land ultimately belongs to God, and human possession is always stewardship rather than absolute ownership.
