## Acting Out the Exile

Ezekiel 12 records the prophet's enactment of exile — digging through a wall, carrying baggage with covered face — and confronts popular skepticism about prophecy. The proverbs "every vision fails" and "the vision is for many days hence" are rebuked.

Rashi identified the sign-act as specifically depicting Zedekiah's failed escape from Jerusalem. The covered face represents Zedekiah's blinding by Nebuchadnezzar (2 Kings 25:7) — a detail that could only be recognized in hindsight, lending the prophecy powerful retrospective confirmation.

The people's dismissal of prophecy raised a serious theological question for the rabbis: how can true prophecy be distinguished from false? The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 89a-b) establishes criteria: a prophet who suppresses his prophecy, or who prophesies what he has not heard, or who prophesies in the name of an idol, is guilty. But the practical problem of discernment remains. Maimonides addressed this in *Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* (Laws of the Foundations of the Torah), establishing that a prophet known to be righteous is believed when his words come true, and rejected when they do not.
