## The Siege Day

Ezekiel 24 records the beginning of Jerusalem's siege, marked by the parable of the corroded pot and the death of Ezekiel's wife. The prophet's enforced silence in mourning becomes a prophetic sign.

The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 24a) records that the day of the siege's beginning was divinely communicated to Ezekiel in Babylon — a detail that confirmed his prophetic authenticity when later verified. The precise dating (tenth day of the tenth month, which became the fast of the Tenth of Tevet) demonstrates the prophetic capacity to know events at a distance, a sign of genuine divine communication.

The death of Ezekiel's wife and his commanded silence present one of the most humanly wrenching moments in prophetic literature. Rabbinic tradition treated this episode with reverence, seeing in it the ultimate cost of prophetic vocation. The Midrash notes that Ezekiel did not protest or question God's decree — unlike Abraham or Moses, who argued with God, Ezekiel silently obeyed. This silence itself became his most powerful prophecy: when the news of Jerusalem's fall would reach the exiles, they too would be struck dumb, beyond the capacity for ordinary mourning.
