## The King of Tyre

Ezekiel 28 contains two oracles against the ruler of Tyre. The second (28:11-19), with its Edenic imagery, has generated particular interest in Jewish exegesis. Unlike the dominant Christian reading that identifies the figure with Satan, Jewish interpreters have generally maintained that the passage addresses the human king of Tyre, using mythological language to expose his hubris.

Rashi reads the passage as describing a human ruler whose wisdom and wealth led him to believe he was divine -- a critique of royal self-deification common in the ancient Near East. Radak similarly emphasizes the historical referent while noting the allusion to Eden as literary hyperbole. Malbim developed a more elaborate reading, seeing the nine precious stones (compared to the high priest's twelve) as indicating that the king of Tyre aspired to priestly status without legitimate authority.

The chapter's conclusion, with its brief oracle against Sidon and promise of Israel's security, reflects what Radak identifies as a recurring prophetic pattern: judgment on Israel's neighbors creates the conditions for Israel's restoration. The promise that Israel "will know that I am the LORD their God" connects divine action among the nations to the deepening of Israel's covenantal knowledge.
