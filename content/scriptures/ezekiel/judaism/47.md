## The Living Waters

Ezekiel 47's vision of the river flowing from the temple, deepening as it goes and bringing life to the Dead Sea, has captivated Jewish interpreters across the centuries. The Talmud (Yoma 77b-78a) discusses the river in the context of the blessings of the messianic age, and the Midrash elaborates on its healing properties with characteristic exuberance.

Rashi and Radak both read the river as a literal feature of the messianic temple, consistent with other prophetic descriptions of Jerusalem's waters (Zechariah 14:8, Joel 4:18). The progressive deepening was interpreted as indicating the river's miraculous nature -- unlike natural rivers that grow through tributaries, this river grows from its own source, demonstrating divine rather than natural origin.

The trees bearing monthly fruit with leaves for healing were connected in Jewish tradition to the Tree of Life in Eden. The messianic age thus restores what was lost at the beginning: access to the source of life and healing. The Zohar developed this imagery extensively, reading the river as the flow of divine blessing through the sefirot to the material world, and the trees as the righteous who channel this blessing to others.
