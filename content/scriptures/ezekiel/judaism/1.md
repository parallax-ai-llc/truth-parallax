## The Merkabah Vision

Ezekiel 1 records the prophet-priest's vision of the divine chariot-throne (*Merkabah*) by the Kebar canal in Babylon. This chapter holds a singular place in Jewish tradition: it became the foundation of *Ma'aseh Merkabah* (the "Work of the Chariot"), the earliest strand of Jewish mysticism. The Mishnah (*Hagigah* 2:1) restricts its exposition: "The Merkabah may not be expounded to an individual unless he is a sage who understands of his own knowledge."

Rabbinic tradition wrestled with the tension between the vision's theological significance and its perceived dangers. The Talmud (*Hagigah* 14b) tells of four who "entered the *Pardes*" (orchard of mystical knowledge): Ben Azzai died, Ben Zoma went mad, Aher (Elisha ben Avuyah) became a heretic, and only Rabbi Akiva "entered in peace and departed in peace." This cautionary tale reflects the anxiety surrounding the Merkabah tradition.

The medieval commentators approached the chapter differently. Rashi provided relatively restrained comments, while Radak (David Kimchi) offered more detailed explanations of the living creatures and wheels as representing natural and angelic forces. The Kabbalistic tradition, especially the *Zohar*, mapped the four *chayot* (living creatures) onto the *sefirot* and saw in the vision a revelation of the divine structure underlying all reality. Maimonides, in the *Guide for the Perplexed*, identified the Merkabah with Aristotelian metaphysics, reading the vision as an allegory of the celestial spheres and separate intellects.
