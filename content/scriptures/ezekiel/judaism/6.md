## Against the Mountains

Ezekiel 6 directs oracles against the mountains of Israel where idolatrous worship has been practiced. The destruction of high places (*bamot*), altars, and incense stands is decreed, and the dead will be scattered around their idols.

Jewish commentary connected this oracle to the long struggle against high-place worship documented in Kings and Chronicles. While Solomon's temple was meant to centralize worship, the *bamot* persisted as sites of syncretistic practice. Radak noted that the mountains themselves are addressed because they had been silent witnesses to Israel's infidelity — the landscape itself is implicated in the nation's sin.

The promise that the remnant scattered among the nations will "remember" God (6:9) carries particular resonance in Jewish tradition. *Zakhor* (remember) is a foundational imperative of Jewish life. The exilic experience of remembering — maintaining Jewish identity and divine consciousness in a foreign land — became paradigmatic for subsequent diaspora existence. The ability to remember God outside the land, without the temple, became the foundation of rabbinic Judaism's portability and resilience.
