## The Fate of Edom

Ezekiel 35 pronounces judgment on Mount Seir (Edom) for its perpetual enmity against Israel. Rabbinic tradition invested the Esau-Jacob/Edom-Israel relationship with profound symbolic significance. The Talmud and Midrash frequently use "Edom" as a cipher for Rome, and by extension for any empire that oppresses Israel.

Rashi reads the chapter within this broader typological framework: Edom represents the enduring force of anti-Jewish hostility that manifests in different historical guises. The "perpetual enmity" that Ezekiel condemns is not merely a sixth-century phenomenon but a recurring pattern. This reading gave the chapter particular resonance during periods of persecution.

Radak, while acknowledging the typological tradition, also attended to the historical specifics: the Edomites' seizure of Judean territory during the Babylonian exile was a concrete historical betrayal. The chapter's declaration that "the LORD was there" (35:10) -- even in the land's desolation -- affirms the principle of divine ownership of the land that undergirds the Jewish hope for return and restoration.
