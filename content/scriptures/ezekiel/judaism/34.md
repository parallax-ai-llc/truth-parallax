## The Shepherds of Israel

Ezekiel 34's indictment of false shepherds and promise of God's own shepherding is one of the most important texts in Jewish political theology. The metaphor of the ruler as shepherd appears throughout the Tanakh, and Ezekiel 34 provides its most sustained critique. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 37a) invokes the shepherding principle in discussing the responsibilities of Jewish communal leaders.

Rashi identifies the false shepherds with the kings and officials of Judah who led the people into idolatry and political catastrophe. Radak connects the imagery to specific historical figures. The Midrash (Exodus Rabbah 2:2) draws a broader lesson: God tests potential leaders by observing how they tend their flocks before entrusting them with the care of Israel -- Moses, David, and others passed this test.

The promise of "my servant David" (34:23-24) is interpreted in Jewish tradition as a messianic prophecy. Rashi and other medieval commentators identified this figure with the future Mashiach (Messiah) from the Davidic line. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) includes this passage among the key messianic texts, and the daily Amidah prayer's petition for the restoration of the Davidic throne reflects the hope that Ezekiel 34 articulates.
