## The Dry Bones

Ezekiel 37's vision of dry bones revivified by divine breath is one of the most celebrated passages in Jewish liturgy and theology. The vision serves as the haftarah for the intermediate Shabbat of Passover, connecting Israel's deliverance from exile to the Exodus from Egypt. The bones represent the people's despair -- "our bones are dried up, our hope is lost" -- and God's response is not argument but demonstration.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 92b) records a debate about whether the resurrection of the dry bones was a literal historical event or a prophetic parable. Rabbi Eliezer ben Jose testified that he had seen the bones at the river Dura, while other sages maintained the vision was purely metaphorical. This debate reflects a broader tension in rabbinic thought about the nature of prophetic vision.

The passage became a foundational text for the Jewish doctrine of bodily resurrection (techiyat hametim). Maimonides listed resurrection among his Thirteen Principles of Faith, and the Amidah's second blessing -- "You are mighty, O Lord, who revives the dead" -- draws on the theological conviction that Ezekiel 37 articulates. The joining of two sticks into one (37:15-28) prophesies the reunification of Judah and Israel under the messianic king, a hope that remains central to Jewish eschatological expectation.
