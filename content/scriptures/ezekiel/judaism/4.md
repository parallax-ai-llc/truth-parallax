## Sign-Acts of Judgment

Ezekiel 4 commands the prophet to perform symbolic actions representing the siege of Jerusalem: building a miniature siege, lying on his sides for 390 and 40 days, and preparing bread baked under conditions of ritual impurity. These sign-acts are distinctive in prophetic literature for their duration and physical intensity.

Rabbinic interpretation debated the precise significance of the 390 and 40 days. Rashi connected the 390 years to the period from the division of the kingdom under Jeroboam to the destruction of the First Temple, and the 40 years to the period of Manasseh's most egregious idolatry. Radak offered alternative calculations, noting the difficulty of establishing precise chronological correspondence.

The command to bake bread over human dung (4:12), later modified to cow dung at the prophet's protest (4:14-15), raised halakhic questions about ritual purity. The prophet's anguished protest — "I have never defiled myself" — reveals the priestly identity that undergirds his prophetic vocation. Jewish commentators note that Ezekiel's concern for *tumah* (impurity) and *taharah* (purity) reflects his dual identity as priest and prophet, a tension that runs throughout the book.
