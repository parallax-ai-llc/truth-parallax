## Jerusalem's Infidelity

Ezekiel 16 presents an extended allegory of Jerusalem as an abandoned infant rescued and married by God, only to turn to systematic unfaithfulness worse than Sodom and Samaria. The chapter concludes with a promise of everlasting covenant despite Jerusalem's shame.

Rabbinic tradition was deeply uncomfortable with this chapter's graphic imagery. The Talmud (*Megillah* 25a) restricts its public reading and translation: "The story of [Ezekiel 16] is read but not translated" — meaning it was read in Hebrew during synagogue services but not rendered into the Aramaic vernacular, to prevent scandal among those who might not understand its allegorical intention.

Despite this discomfort, the chapter's theological message — God's unconditional covenant love persisting through radical unfaithfulness — became foundational for the Jewish understanding of divine *chesed* (lovingkindness). The concluding promise that God will remember His covenant (16:60) even when Israel has forgotten it undergirds the liturgical theology of the High Holy Days: God's mercy exceeds human deserving, and the covenant endures because of divine faithfulness, not human merit.
