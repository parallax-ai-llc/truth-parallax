## The Monster in the Nile

Ezekiel 29 portrays Pharaoh as a great tannim (river monster) boasting, "My Nile is my own; I made it for myself." Jewish commentators identify this as the fundamental sin of attributing God's gifts to oneself. Rashi connects the passage to the broader biblical theme of Egypt's persistent opposition to Israel, from the Exodus through the exilic period.

The prophecy of forty years of desolation has been discussed extensively in Jewish chronological scholarship. Seder Olam Rabbah and other rabbinic chronological works attempted to correlate this period with known historical events. Abarbanel offered a detailed historical analysis, while acknowledging the difficulty of precise identification.

The chapter's concluding note -- Nebuchadnezzar receiving Egypt as wages for his siege of Tyre (29:17-20) -- attracted particular rabbinic attention. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 96b) discusses the principle that God rewards even gentile rulers for service rendered, deriving from this passage the broader theological point that divine justice operates with meticulous fairness even outside the covenantal framework.
