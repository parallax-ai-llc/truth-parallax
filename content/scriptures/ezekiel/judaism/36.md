## The Mountains and the New Heart

Ezekiel 36 addresses the mountains of Israel, promising their restoration, and delivers the great promise of interior transformation: a new heart, a new spirit, and cleansing from impurity. This chapter is foundational for the Jewish understanding of teshuvah (repentance) as both human effort and divine gift.

The promise of the new heart and spirit (36:26-27) was read by the Talmudic sages in relation to the messianic era. The Talmud (Sukkah 52a) teaches that in the future age, God will slaughter the evil inclination (yetzer hara), removing the internal obstacle to obedience. Maimonides incorporated this expectation into his description of the messianic age in the Mishneh Torah.

The motivation clause -- "not for your sake but for my holy name" (36:22) -- generated significant theological reflection. Rashi emphasized that Israel's restoration serves God's purposes in the world, not Israel's merit. This principle informed the Jewish philosophical tradition's understanding of election: Israel is chosen not for its own sake but as a vehicle for sanctifying God's name (kiddush Hashem) among the nations. The chapter thus connects national restoration to universal theological significance.
