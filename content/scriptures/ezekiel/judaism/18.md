## The Soul That Sins

Ezekiel 18 refutes the proverb about sour grapes and establishes the principle that each person is judged according to their own conduct. The righteous who turns wicked will die; the wicked who repents will live. The chapter concludes with an impassioned appeal to repent.

This chapter is among the most frequently cited in rabbinic discussions of *teshuvah* (repentance) and divine justice. The Talmud (*Kiddushin* 40b) quotes the principle that "if one was wicked his entire life and repented at the end, his wickedness is not remembered." The chapter's insistence on the possibility of genuine transformation — that a person is not permanently defined by their past — undergirds the entire structure of the High Holy Days penitential liturgy.

Maimonides cited Ezekiel 18 extensively in *Hilkhot Teshuvah* (Laws of Repentance) as demonstrating that free will and the genuine possibility of change are axiomatic in Jewish theology. Against fatalistic or deterministic views, the chapter insists that every person retains the capacity to choose. The concluding question — "Why will you die, O house of Israel?" (18:31) — is read not as rhetoric but as a genuine divine plea, expressing God's desire for human repentance rather than punishment.
