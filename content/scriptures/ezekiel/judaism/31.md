## The Cedar Allegory

Ezekiel 31 compares Assyria to a magnificent cedar, towering above all other trees, envied by the trees of Eden, yet consigned to Sheol for its pride. Jewish commentators read this as a historical parable with enduring moral significance: no empire, however great, is permanent.

Rashi identifies the chapter's message as directed to Pharaoh through the example of Assyria: if even the mightiest empire fell, Egypt should not imagine itself immune to judgment. Radak develops this reading with attention to the historical details of Assyria's rise and fall, noting that the Assyrian Empire's destruction within living memory of Ezekiel's audience would have made the warning particularly powerful.

The midrashic tradition found in the cedar's greatness an occasion to reflect on the nature of hubris. Midrash Tanchuma connects the fall of great powers to the principle that "the Holy One, Blessed be He, humbles the proud and exalts the humble" -- a principle woven throughout biblical narrative from the Tower of Babel to the Magnificat's ancestral echoes in Hannah's prayer.
