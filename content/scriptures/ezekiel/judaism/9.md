## The Mark and the Judgment

Ezekiel 9 describes the marking of the righteous and the execution of all who lack the mark. The *tav* — the last letter of the Hebrew alphabet — is placed on the foreheads of those who grieve over Jerusalem's abominations.

The Talmud (*Shabbat* 55a) offers a remarkable interpretation: God told the angel to write a *tav* of ink on the foreheads of the righteous so that the destroying angels would not harm them, and a *tav* of blood on the foreheads of the wicked so that the destroying angels would attack them. The *tav* stands for both *tikhyeh* ("you shall live") and *tamut* ("you shall die") — the same letter marks both salvation and destruction.

Midrashic tradition connected the marking to the Passover narrative, where the blood on the doorposts preserved Israel from the destroyer. The preservation of the righteous remnant within a broader framework of judgment is a recurring pattern in Jewish theology. The criterion for preservation is not ritual status but moral sensitivity — those who "sigh and groan" over abominations are spared. This emphasis on ethical consciousness as the mark of genuine piety resonates throughout rabbinic ethics.
