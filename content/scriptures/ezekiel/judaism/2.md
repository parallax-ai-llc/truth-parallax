## The Prophetic Calling

Ezekiel 2 presents God's commission of the prophet to speak to a rebellious Israel. The Spirit sets Ezekiel on his feet and he is shown a scroll inscribed on both sides with lamentation, mourning, and woe.

Rabbinic commentary emphasizes the unique nature of Ezekiel's prophetic experience. Unlike Moses, who spoke with God "face to face," and unlike Isaiah, who saw the Lord in the temple, Ezekiel receives his call in exile — in an impure land, outside the holy precincts. The Talmud (*Moed Katan* 25a) notes that the *Shekhinah* (divine presence) accompanied Israel into exile, a principle derived in part from Ezekiel's experience of divine encounter in Babylon.

The scroll written on both sides (*panim v'achor*) was interpreted by the rabbis as containing both the revealed and hidden dimensions of Torah. Midrashic tradition suggests the two sides represent the written Torah (*Torah she-bikhtav*) and the oral Torah (*Torah she-be'al peh*), or alternatively the present age and the world to come. The completeness of the inscription — front and back — indicates that God's message through Ezekiel is comprehensive, leaving nothing unsaid.
