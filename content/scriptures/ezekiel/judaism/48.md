## The Land and the Name

Ezekiel 48 concludes the book with the allocation of land among the twelve tribes, the city's dimensions and gates, and the climactic declaration: "The LORD Is There" (Yahweh Shammah). This chapter brings the prophetic vision to its theological conclusion: the permanent, irrevocable dwelling of God with His people in their land.

The tribal allocation differs significantly from the historical arrangement in Joshua. All twelve tribes receive equal east-west strips, with the sacred district at the center. Rashi and Radak interpreted this as indicating a complete restructuring of national life around the worship of God. Abarbanel noted that the inclusion of all twelve tribes -- including the long-lost northern tribes -- affirms the prophetic hope for the reunification of all Israel.

The name "The LORD Is There" provides the theological climax of the entire book of Ezekiel. The Talmud (Bava Batra 75b) records that in the future, Jerusalem will expand to encompass all of the Land of Israel, and the divine Presence will fill the entire land. The name thus encapsulates the deepest aspiration of Jewish faith: not merely that God exists, nor merely that God acts, but that God dwells -- permanently and tangibly -- among His people. This hope animates the daily liturgy, the messianic expectation, and the enduring Jewish attachment to the Land of Israel.
