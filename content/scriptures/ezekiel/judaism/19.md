## Dirge for the Kings

Ezekiel 19 is a *qinah* (lament) for the Davidic dynasty, employing the images of a lioness and her cubs and a vine uprooted. The *qinah* meter — the characteristic limping rhythm of biblical lament — signals that the monarchy is being mourned as already dead.

Rashi identified the lioness as the people of Israel and the cubs as specific kings: the first cub taken to Egypt is Jehoahaz (2 Kings 23:33-34), and the second taken to Babylon is Jehoiachin. Radak offered the alternative identification of Zedekiah as the second cub. The ambiguity itself may be deliberate — the lament mourns not just individual kings but the dynasty as a whole.

The vine imagery in verses 10-14 — a vine planted by water, flourishing, then torn up and consumed — was connected by the rabbis to the destiny of the Davidic line. The fire from within its own branches (19:14) signals that the dynasty's destruction came through internal corruption, not merely external force. Midrashic tradition saw in the uprooted vine a temporary condition: the roots remain, and the possibility of future growth — the messianic hope — endures beneath the surface.
