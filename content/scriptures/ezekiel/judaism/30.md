## Egypt's Day of Reckoning

Ezekiel 30 announces the "Day of the LORD" against Egypt, naming specific cities and prophesying the breaking of Pharaoh's power. Jewish commentators read this chapter within the larger framework of Egypt's role in salvation history. Egypt represents not merely a political enemy but the paradigmatic place of bondage from which God redeems His people.

Radak identifies the specific cities with care, drawing on geographical knowledge. The targeting of religious centers like Heliopolis (On, the city of the sun) reflects the prophetic polemic against Egyptian polytheism -- a theme that resonates with the Exodus narrative's demonstration of God's sovereignty over Egypt's gods.

The image of Pharaoh's broken arms -- one already shattered, the other about to be -- is read by Malbim as illustrating progressive divine judgment. The first breaking represents Pharaoh Necho's defeat at Carchemish (605 BCE), the second the coming Babylonian invasion. This historically grounded reading demonstrates the Jewish interpretive preference for connecting prophecy to identifiable events while acknowledging their theological significance.
