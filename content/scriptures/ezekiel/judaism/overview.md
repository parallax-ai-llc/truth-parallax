## Overview

Ezekiel holds a unique and sometimes controversial place in Jewish tradition. The book's opening vision of the divine chariot-throne (*Merkabah*) became the foundation of Jewish mysticism, yet was also regarded with such reverence and danger that the rabbis restricted its public reading and teaching. The Talmud records debates about whether to include Ezekiel in the canon, particularly because of apparent contradictions between its temple legislation and the Torah.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Rabbinic (Talmud and Midrash)

The Talmudic sages engaged extensively with Ezekiel, especially the tensions between its ritual prescriptions and Mosaic law. The Babylonian Talmud (*Shabbat* 13b) credits Hananiah ben Hezekiah with reconciling these contradictions, thus preserving the book's canonical status. The opening Merkabah vision was restricted: "One may not expound the Merkabah to an individual, unless he is a sage and understands on his own" (*Mishnah Hagigah* 2:1).

### Medieval Commentators

Rashi, Radak (David Kimchi), and Metzudat David provided line-by-line commentary integrating *peshat* (plain meaning) and *derash* (interpretive meaning). These commentaries remain central to traditional Jewish study of Ezekiel.

### Mystical (Kabbalah)

The Merkabah vision is foundational to both early Jewish mysticism (*Ma'aseh Merkabah*) and later Kabbalistic thought. The *Zohar* draws extensively on Ezekiel's imagery, and the four *chayot* (living creatures) correspond to elements of the Kabbalistic sefirotic structure.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Presence (*Shekhinah*) in Exile**: God's glory departing the Temple and appearing in Babylon demonstrates that God's presence is not bound to a single location
- **Individual Responsibility**: Ezekiel 18 is a cornerstone text for the Jewish doctrine that each person is accountable for their own sins
- **Teshuvah (Repentance)**: The call to repentance in chapters 18 and 33 is central to the High Holy Days liturgy and theology
- **Eschatological Hope**: The dry bones vision (chapter 37) and the new temple vision shape messianic expectation
