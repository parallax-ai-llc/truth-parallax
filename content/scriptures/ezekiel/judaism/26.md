## The Oracle Against Tyre

Ezekiel 26 pronounces judgment on Tyre for rejoicing over Jerusalem's fall. Rabbinic tradition (Megillah 6a) records the principle that Tyre and Jerusalem exist in inverse relationship: "When one is filled, the other is desolate." This observation reflects the economic reality that Tyre stood to benefit from Jerusalem's destruction as a commercial rival.

Rashi and Radak both emphasize the moral dimension: Tyre's sin was not merely opportunism but the spiritual offense of treating sacred catastrophe as a business opportunity. The medieval commentators saw in this a warning against the instrumentalization of others' suffering. Malbim noted that Tyre's punishment -- being scraped to bare rock -- mirrors its own callousness: those who strip others bare will themselves be stripped.

The specificity of the prophecy concerning Nebuchadnezzar's siege has been discussed extensively in Jewish commentary. Abarbanel noted the distinction between the initial siege by Nebuchadnezzar and the ultimate destruction that came later through Alexander, reading the prophecy as encompassing a progressive fulfillment -- an interpretive approach that respects both the prophetic word and historical complexity.
