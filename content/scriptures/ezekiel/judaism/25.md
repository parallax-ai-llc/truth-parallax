## Judgment Among the Nations

Ezekiel 25 opens the series of oracles against foreign nations -- Ammon, Moab, Edom, and Philistia. Jewish interpretation situates these oracles within the framework of God's covenantal relationship with Israel and His sovereign governance of all peoples. The Talmud (Berakhot 32b) notes that God's care extends beyond Israel, but His particular relationship with the covenant people establishes unique obligations for neighboring nations.

Rashi comments that each nation's punishment corresponds to its specific offense against Israel -- a principle of midda k'neged midda (measure for measure) that pervades rabbinic theology. Ammon gloated over the Temple's destruction, Moab denied Israel's distinctiveness, Edom pursued active vengeance, and Philistia acted from ancient hatred.

Radak (David Kimchi) emphasizes the historical context: these nations exploited Judah's weakness during the Babylonian crisis rather than offering solidarity. The prophetic denunciation thus reinforces the ethical principle that neighboring peoples bear responsibility for how they treat the vulnerable -- a teaching that extends beyond the specific historical moment to become a permanent element of Jewish moral reasoning.
