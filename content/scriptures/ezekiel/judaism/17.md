## The Riddle of the Eagles

Ezekiel 17 presents a political allegory of two eagles (Nebuchadnezzar and Pharaoh) and a vine (Zedekiah), followed by a messianic oracle about a tender sprig planted by God on a high mountain.

Rashi and Radak both provided detailed identifications: the first eagle is Nebuchadnezzar, the top of the cedar is Jehoiachin, the seed of the land is Zedekiah, and the second eagle is Pharaoh. The vine's turning toward the second eagle represents Zedekiah's ill-fated alliance with Egypt in violation of his oath to Babylon.

The messianic oracle (17:22-24) was interpreted in rabbinic tradition as referring to the Messiah from David's line. The "tender sprig" planted on the "high mountain of Israel" would become a great cedar sheltering all nations. Radak explicitly identified this with the messianic kingdom. The motif of the low tree exalted and the high tree brought low resonated with the rabbinic expectation that the Messiah would come in humility before being revealed in glory — a theme developed in various midrashic texts about the hidden Messiah.
