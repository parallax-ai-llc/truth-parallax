## The Inner Temple

Ezekiel 41 continues the measurement of the temple's interior spaces, including the nave, the most holy place, and decorative elements of cherubim and palm trees. Jewish commentators paid careful attention to the architectural details, comparing them with descriptions of Solomon's temple and the Second Temple.

Rashi noted the differences between Ezekiel's temple and Solomon's, interpreting these not as contradictions but as indications that the future temple will surpass its predecessors. Radak similarly emphasized the eschatological newness of the vision. The Talmud (Sukkah 41a) discusses the expectation that the Third Temple will be built by God Himself or will descend ready-made from heaven -- a tradition that the elaborate detail of Ezekiel's vision both supports and complicates.

The cherubim and palm tree decorations connect the temple to the Garden of Eden, a connection that Jewish mystical tradition developed extensively. The Zohar reads the temple as a microcosm of creation and a point of contact between the upper and lower worlds. The palm trees represent the righteous (tzaddikim), and the cherubim represent the angelic guardians of divine mystery. Every detail of the temple's adornment communicates a spiritual reality to those who have eyes to see.
