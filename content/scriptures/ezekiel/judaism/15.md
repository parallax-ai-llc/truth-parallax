## The Vine Parable

Ezekiel 15 compares Jerusalem to a vine whose wood is useless for construction and, once charred, even more worthless. The parable strips away the positive associations of the vine image found elsewhere in Scripture (Isaiah 5, Psalm 80).

Rashi and Radak both emphasized the devastating logic of the parable: a vine's only value is its fruit. If fruitless, its wood is inferior to every other tree — too soft, too crooked, too small for any structural use. Applied to Israel, the message is that chosenness without faithfulness confers not privilege but heightened vulnerability to judgment.

The midrashic tradition (*Leviticus Rabbah* 36:2) elaborated: "The vine is the lowliest of trees, yet when it produces fruit, it is the most exalted. So Israel, when they do God's will, there is nothing more exalted; when they do not, there is nothing more lowly." This dialectic of potential greatness and potential degradation captures the existential stakes of covenant relationship as understood in Jewish theology.
