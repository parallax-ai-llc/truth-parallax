## The End Declared

Ezekiel 7 announces the finality of judgment upon the land. Economic, social, and political structures collapse; silver is worthless; violence has grown to its full measure.

Rabbinic midrash connected the "end" (*qetz*) in this chapter with broader speculations about divinely appointed times. The concept of the *qetz* — the end of a period of suffering and the onset of redemption — became central to Jewish messianic expectation. The rabbis cautioned against calculating the *qetz* (Sanhedrin 97b: "Blasted be those who calculate the end"), yet the prophetic declaration that the end has been divinely appointed sustained hope through centuries of persecution.

The declaration that "their silver and gold will not be able to deliver them" (7:19) was interpreted by Jewish ethical thinkers as a warning against placing ultimate trust in wealth. Maimonides, in his discussion of charity (*tzedakah*), emphasized that wealth is a tool for righteousness, not a source of security. When detached from ethical purpose, it becomes not merely useless but an obstacle.
