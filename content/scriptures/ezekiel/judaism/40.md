## The Temple Vision

Ezekiel 40 initiates the extended vision of the future temple that occupies the book's final nine chapters. Jewish interpretation has engaged with this vision with particular intensity, as it touches on some of the most central concerns of Jewish religious life: the temple, sacrifice, priesthood, and the sanctification of the land.

The Talmud (Menachot 45a, Middot) draws on Ezekiel's measurements in discussions of temple architecture, sometimes in tension with descriptions in other biblical sources. The Mishnah tractate Middot describes the Second Temple's dimensions, and discrepancies with Ezekiel's vision were noted and discussed by the sages. Maimonides incorporated elements of Ezekiel's temple into his codification of temple law in the Mishneh Torah.

Rashi and Radak read the vision as describing the Third Temple that will be built in the messianic era. The meticulous detail is understood as prophetic instruction: God is providing the blueprint for the future sanctuary. This interpretation has shaped Jewish messianic hope throughout the centuries, maintaining the expectation that the temple will be rebuilt according to divine specifications. The mystical tradition (Zohar, Ramchal) reads the temple measurements as encoding spiritual realities that correspond to the divine sefirot.
