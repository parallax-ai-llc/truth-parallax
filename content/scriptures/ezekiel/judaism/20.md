## Israel's History of Rebellion

Ezekiel 20 recounts Israel's pattern of rebellion from Egypt through the wilderness to the present, structured around divine gift, human faithlessness, and divine restraint. The chapter concludes with a promise of future restoration through purifying judgment.

The recital of Israel's failures is unsparing: even in Egypt, Israel worshiped idols (20:7-8) — a tradition not explicitly recorded in the Exodus narrative but developed in rabbinic midrash. The Midrash (*Shir HaShirim Rabbah* 2:2) elaborated that Israel in Egypt had abandoned circumcision and sunk to the 49th level of impurity, barely retaining enough merit for redemption. Ezekiel's account supported this darker reading of the pre-Exodus period.

The difficult verse 20:25 — "I gave them statutes that were not good" — generated extensive rabbinic discussion. Rashi interpreted it as referring to God allowing Israel to follow their own evil desires as a form of punishment. The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 64a) connected it to the practice of child sacrifice, which God permitted Israel to adopt as a consequence of their rebellious hearts. Maimonides read the "statutes that were not good" as the idolatrous practices of the nations among whom Israel lived, permitted by God as a natural consequence of Israel's chosen path.
