## The Two Sisters

Ezekiel 23 presents the allegory of Oholah (Samaria) and Oholibah (Jerusalem), whose promiscuous alliances with foreign nations constitute spiritual adultery. The allegory parallels chapter 16 but focuses on the political dimension.

Rashi explained the names: Oholah means "her tent" — she set up her own unauthorized worship tent (the northern shrines). Oholibah means "my tent is in her" — God's tent (the temple) was in Jerusalem. The etymological distinction underscores the theological difference: Samaria's worship was illegitimate from the start, while Jerusalem had the legitimate sanctuary but corrupted it.

The rabbinic tradition was, as with chapter 16, cautious about this chapter's public reading due to its graphic imagery. Yet the theological point was clear and important: political alliances that substitute for trust in God are a form of infidelity. The rabbis applied this principle retrospectively to the Hasmonean dynasty's alliances with Rome and prospectively to any Jewish political arrangement that placed strategic calculation above faithfulness to Torah.
