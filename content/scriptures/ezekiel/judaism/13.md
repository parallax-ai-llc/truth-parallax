## False Prophets and Prophetesses

Ezekiel 13 condemns prophets who speak from their own hearts and prophetesses who practice divination. The false prophets are compared to foxes in ruins; their message of peace is whitewash on a crumbling wall.

The rabbinic tradition took the problem of false prophecy with utmost seriousness. The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 89a) prescribes the death penalty for false prophets — those who prophesy what they have not been told, who report another prophet's message as their own, or who prophesy in the name of false gods. The severity of this penalty reflects the existential danger false prophecy poses to the community.

The prophetesses' divination practices (13:17-23) — sewing bands on wrists and making veils — have been interpreted variously by Jewish commentators. Rashi understood these as magical devices used to bind souls. Radak saw them as tools of fortune-telling used to exploit the vulnerable. The condemnation establishes a boundary between legitimate prophecy and magical manipulation, a distinction that remains relevant in Jewish law's prohibition of divination (*nichush*) and sorcery (*kishuf*).
