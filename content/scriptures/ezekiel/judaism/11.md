## Exile and Promise

Ezekiel 11 pronounces judgment on Jerusalem's leaders and offers the exiles a stunning promise: God Himself has been "a sanctuary in small measure" to them, and will give them one heart, a new spirit, and a heart of flesh in place of stone. The glory departs the city entirely.

The concept of *miqdash me'at* — "a sanctuary in small measure" — became a foundational text for the synagogue. The Talmud (*Megillah* 29a) applies this phrase directly to the synagogues and houses of study in Babylon, establishing the theological legitimacy of non-temple worship. When the physical sanctuary is destroyed, God provides alternative forms of presence. This principle sustained Judaism through the loss of both temples and the long centuries without a central shrine.

The promise of a "new heart" and "new spirit" (11:19) was debated among the rabbis. Some read it as describing the messianic age, when the *yetzer hara* (evil inclination) would be subdued or removed. Others understood it as describing the possibility available in every generation through genuine *teshuvah* (repentance). Maimonides emphasized that the capacity for repentance is always present — no one is irredeemably locked into sin.
