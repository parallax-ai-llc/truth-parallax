## The Watchman's Charge

Ezekiel 33 reestablishes Ezekiel as a watchman and teaches the principle of individual moral responsibility. This chapter holds foundational importance for Jewish ethical theology, particularly the doctrine of teshuvah (repentance). The Talmud (Kiddushin 40b) draws on this chapter's teaching that the wicked who repent will live: "If he repented, we do not mention his previous wicked acts."

Rashi emphasizes that the watchman metaphor applies to all who bear communal responsibility -- not only prophets but teachers, judges, and communal leaders. The obligation to warn is not contingent on the likelihood of the warning being heeded; the watchman's duty is to speak, regardless of response.

The chapter's concluding observation -- that the exiles listen to Ezekiel as entertainment rather than divine address (33:30-33) -- has been cited in the Mussar literature as an illustration of the yetzer hara's (evil inclination's) ability to neutralize Torah by aestheticizing it. Rabbi Yisrael Salanter and other Mussar masters warned that the greatest danger to the serious Jew is not rejection of Torah but its transformation into an intellectual or aesthetic pursuit divorced from moral transformation.
