## Watchman and Responsibility

Ezekiel 3 establishes the prophet as a watchman (*tzofeh*) for the house of Israel. The watchman metaphor carries profound implications for Jewish communal responsibility: the leader who sees danger and fails to warn is held accountable.

This passage shaped the halakhic concept of *arvut* — mutual responsibility among Jews. The Talmud (*Shevuot* 39a) declares "All of Israel are guarantors (*arevin*) for one another," a principle that extends the watchman's individual accountability to the collective. Jewish communal leadership — rabbis, scholars, communal officials — bears a special form of this responsibility, analogous to Ezekiel's unique burden.

The enforced silence at the chapter's end (3:26-27) has been interpreted in Hasidic thought as a model of spiritual preparation. Before genuine prophetic speech is possible, a period of deep listening and inner stillness is required. The Ba'al Shem Tov and his disciples emphasized that authentic Torah teaching arises not from intellectual effort alone but from a prior state of spiritual receptivity (*deveikut*, cleaving to God).
