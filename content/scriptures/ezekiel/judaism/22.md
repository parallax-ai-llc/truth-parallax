## Jerusalem's Catalog of Sins

Ezekiel 22 provides a comprehensive indictment of Jerusalem, listing specific transgressions: bloodshed, idolatry, dishonoring parents, oppressing strangers, profaning the Sabbath, sexual immorality, and bribery. Every level of society — prophets, priests, princes, and common people — is implicated.

The list of sins resonates with the *aseret hadibrot* (Ten Commandments) and the Holiness Code of Leviticus 19. Jewish commentators noted that Ezekiel systematically demonstrates the violation of both ritual (*ben adam la-Makom*, between person and God) and ethical (*ben adam la-chavero*, between person and person) commandments. The comprehensiveness indicates that the social fabric has completely disintegrated.

The concluding verse — "I sought for anyone to stand in the breach... but I found no one" (22:30) — was interpreted by the rabbis as the most devastating indictment of all. In earlier biblical narratives, intercessors arose: Abraham for Sodom, Moses for Israel, Samuel for the people. Now, no one remains who can or will intercede. The Talmud (*Berakhot* 32a) saw in this the exhaustion of human merit and the necessity of direct divine intervention — a precondition for the messianic redemption.
