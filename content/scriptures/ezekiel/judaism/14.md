## Idols and Intercession

Ezekiel 14 confronts elders who maintain idols in their hearts while consulting the prophet. God declares that even the righteousness of Noah, Daniel, and Job would save only those three individuals, not the nation.

The invocation of Noah, Daniel, and Job provoked rabbinic discussion. The Talmud (*Bava Batra* 15a) debated the identity of Daniel in this list — some argued it refers not to the biblical Daniel but to an ancient sage of that name, since Ezekiel lists him between antediluvian (Noah) and patriarchal (Job) figures rather than among his own contemporaries. Modern scholarship has connected this Daniel to the Dan'el of Ugaritic literature.

The principle that individual righteousness cannot avert national judgment was balanced by the rabbinic conviction that the merit of the righteous (*zekhut tzaddikim*) can protect communities. The Talmud (*Yoma* 38b) states that the righteous person is the foundation of the world. Ezekiel 14 represents an extreme case — the corruption is so total that even the paradigmatic righteous cannot intercede. The passage does not deny the general principle of intercessory merit but identifies its limits.
