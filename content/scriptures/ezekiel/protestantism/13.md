## False Prophets Condemned

Ezekiel 13 contains a sustained oracle against false prophets who "prophesy out of their own hearts" (13:2) and "follow their own spirit, and have seen nothing" (13:3). Their message of peace when there is no peace is compared to whitewashing a flimsy wall — it may look solid, but the first storm will destroy it.

The second half addresses false prophetesses who practice divination, sewing magic bands on wrists and making veils for heads to "hunt souls." God declares He will tear away these instruments and deliver His people from their hands.

Protestant interpretation of this chapter has been vigorous and polemical across the centuries. Luther and Calvin both applied the condemnation of false prophets to the papacy and the medieval church hierarchy. In contemporary evangelical contexts, the passage is used to critique prosperity gospel teachers, self-appointed prophets, and those who claim divine authority for messages of comfort that contradict Scripture. The criterion for true prophecy is not popularity or personal conviction but alignment with God's revealed word — a principle that flows directly from *sola scriptura*.
