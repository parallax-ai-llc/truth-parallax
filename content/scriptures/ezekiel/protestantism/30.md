## The Day of Egypt's Doom

Ezekiel 30 intensifies the judgment against Egypt, invoking the "Day of the LORD" language that connects this prophecy to the broader prophetic tradition. The chapter announces that Egypt and its allies will all fall by the sword. Protestant interpreters note the universal scope: the Day of the LORD encompasses not only Israel but all nations.

The detailed prophecy against specific Egyptian cities -- Thebes, Memphis, Pelusium, Heliopolis, and Bubastis -- demonstrates God's intimate knowledge of the geopolitical landscape. Evangelical commentators emphasize that divine judgment is not vague or abstract but concrete and particular.

The breaking of Pharaoh's arms (30:21-26) -- one already broken and the other about to be -- provides a vivid image of incremental judgment. Protestant interpreters read this as illustrating how God's judgment often unfolds progressively rather than in a single catastrophic moment, demonstrating that no earthly power can serve as a permanent alternative to trust in God.
