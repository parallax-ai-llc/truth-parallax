## Individual Responsibility

Ezekiel 18 is one of the most theologically significant chapters in the prophetic literature. It directly addresses the proverb "The fathers have eaten sour grapes, and the children's teeth are set on edge" — the fatalistic belief that the present generation suffers for the sins of their ancestors. God categorically rejects this: "The soul who sins shall die" (18:4). Through three case studies — a righteous father, a wicked son, and a righteous grandson — the chapter demonstrates that each person is judged according to their own conduct.

Protestant theology has found this chapter both foundational and complex. On one hand, it undergirds the Reformation emphasis on personal faith and individual accountability before God. Luther cited it against the notion that inherited institutional membership could substitute for personal repentance. On the other hand, the chapter's apparent emphasis on works-righteousness has required careful harmonization with Pauline soteriology.

Reformed commentators resolve this by distinguishing between the basis of justification (Christ's righteousness alone) and the evidence of genuine faith (righteous living). Ezekiel 18 describes the pattern of life that characterizes the truly righteous person, not the meritorious basis of their acceptance before God. The chapter's concluding appeal — "Repent and turn from all your transgressions... get yourselves a new heart and a new spirit!" (18:30-31) — is read alongside 36:26-27, where God Himself provides the new heart. The command to repent and the promise that God will enable repentance are held together in the tension that defines Protestant soteriology.
