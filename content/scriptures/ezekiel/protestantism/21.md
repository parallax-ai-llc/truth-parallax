## The Sword of the Lord

Ezekiel 21 presents the image of God's unsheathed sword, polished for slaughter, cutting to the right and left without distinction. The prophet is commanded to groan with broken heart and bitter grief before the people, then to prophesy that Nebuchadnezzar stands at the crossroads, using divination to determine whether to march against Jerusalem or Rabbah of Ammon. The lot falls on Jerusalem.

Protestant interpreters draw attention to the sovereignty of God working through and above pagan divination practices. Nebuchadnezzar consults his omens, but the outcome is determined by God. Calvin commented extensively on this providential paradox: God uses even the superstitious practices of unbelieving rulers to accomplish His purposes, without endorsing those practices.

The chapter's most striking declaration is the oracle against Zedekiah: "Remove the turban, take off the crown... it shall be no more, until he comes to whom judgment belongs, and I will give it to him" (21:26-27). Protestant interpreters universally see here a messianic prophecy — the Davidic crown is suspended until the rightful king comes. The passage is linked to Genesis 49:10 ("until Shiloh comes") and fulfilled in Christ, the one to whom all authority and judgment belong.
