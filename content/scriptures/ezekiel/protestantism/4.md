## The Siege of Jerusalem Symbolized

Ezekiel 4 presents the first of several dramatic symbolic acts. The prophet is commanded to inscribe a brick with the city of Jerusalem and lay siege to it in miniature, then to lie on his left side for 390 days for the iniquity of Israel and on his right side for 40 days for the iniquity of Judah. He must also prepare bread baked over dung, symbolizing the unclean food of exile.

Protestant interpreters read these sign-acts as enacted parables — prophetic theater that embodies and anticipates the coming destruction of Jerusalem. The precise significance of the 390 and 40 days has generated extensive debate. Some dispensational commentators calculate them as literal years of punishment; Reformed interpreters tend to read them as symbolic numbers representing the long history of Israel's and Judah's unfaithfulness.

The rationing of food and water (4:9-17) vividly portrays siege conditions. Calvin saw in these acts the condescension of God, who stoops to use physical demonstrations because verbal warnings have been ignored. The passage underscores a recurring Protestant theme: God uses every available means — word, sign, and circumstance — to communicate with a rebellious people.
