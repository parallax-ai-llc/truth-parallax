## Idolaters Condemned

Ezekiel 14 addresses elders of Israel who come to inquire of God while harboring idols in their hearts. God declares He will answer such inquirers directly — but in judgment, not guidance. Even if Noah, Daniel, and Job were present, their righteousness would save only themselves, not the nation.

The threefold invocation of Noah, Daniel, and Job is significant in Protestant exegesis. These three represent the most righteous individuals imaginable — a patriarch who survived universal judgment, a sage of legendary piety, and a model of faithfulness under suffering. Yet even their cumulative merit cannot avert corporate judgment or transfer righteousness to others.

Reformed theology finds here a powerful affirmation of two principles: first, that righteousness is not transferable between persons (contra the medieval doctrine of supererogatory merit and the treasury of merits); second, that individual piety cannot substitute for national repentance. The passage also addresses the pastoral problem of divided loyalty — those who seek God's word while clinging to idols receive not guidance but confrontation. Protestant commentators apply this to the danger of approaching Scripture selectively, seeking confirmation rather than transformation.
