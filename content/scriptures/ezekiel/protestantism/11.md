## Judgment and Promise

Ezekiel 11 contains both a judgment oracle against Jerusalem's leaders and a stunning promise of restoration. The chapter opens with the prophet seeing twenty-five men at the east gate, whose counsel is wicked. They compare Jerusalem to a cooking pot that protects its contents — but God reverses the metaphor: the slain are the meat, and the living will be cast out.

The chapter's turning point comes in verses 14-21, where God addresses the exiles directly. Though Jerusalem's inhabitants say "they have gone far from the LORD; to us this land has been given," God declares that He Himself has been "a sanctuary in small measure" (*miqdash me'at*) to the exiles wherever they have gone. He then promises to gather them, give them "one heart and a new spirit," remove the "heart of stone" and give a "heart of flesh."

Protestant theology regards verses 19-20 as a foundational text for the doctrine of regeneration. The replacement of a stony heart with a living heart of flesh is understood as the work of the Holy Spirit in conversion. Reformed theologians connect this promise directly to Ezekiel 36:26-27 and see both as fulfilled in the New Covenant inaugurated by Christ. The chapter concludes with the glory of the LORD departing from the city entirely, completing the staged withdrawal that began in chapter 10.
