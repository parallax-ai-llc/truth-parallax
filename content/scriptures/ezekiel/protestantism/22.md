## The Sins of Jerusalem

Ezekiel 22 catalogs Jerusalem's offenses in three oracles. The first (22:1-16) lists specific sins: bloodshed, idolatry, violation of parents, oppression of aliens, profanation of the Sabbath, sexual immorality, bribery, and usury. The second (22:17-22) employs the metallurgical image of smelting: Israel has become dross rather than refined silver, and God will gather them into the furnace of His wrath. The third (22:23-31) indicts every level of society — prophets, priests, princes, and the people of the land.

Protestant exegesis highlights the comprehensiveness of the indictment. The sins are not only cultic but social, not only individual but institutional. The passage demonstrates that covenant faithfulness encompasses all dimensions of life — worship, economics, family relations, and civil justice. This holistic vision of covenant responsibility has informed Protestant social ethics from the Reformation onward.

The chapter's conclusion is devastating: "I sought for anyone among them who would repair the wall and stand in the breach before me on behalf of the land, so that I would not destroy it; but I found no one" (22:30). Protestant commentators read this as the ultimate failure of Israel's leadership and, typologically, as pointing to humanity's need for a mediator who can truly stand in the breach — a role fulfilled only by Christ.
