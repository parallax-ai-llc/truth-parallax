## The Useless Vine

Ezekiel 15 is a brief but pointed parable comparing Jerusalem to a vine — not a fruitful vine, but one whose wood is utterly useless. Unlike other trees whose wood can be used for construction or crafting, vine wood serves no structural purpose. Once charred by fire, it is even more worthless.

Protestant interpreters read this as a devastating critique of Jerusalem's assumption of special status. Israel understood itself as God's vine (Isaiah 5:1-7, Psalm 80:8-16), but Ezekiel strips away the positive connotations: a vine's only value lies in its fruit-bearing capacity. Without fruit, it is inferior to every other tree. The passage anticipates Jesus's teaching in John 15:1-8, where fruitless branches are cut off and burned.

The theological point is sharpened by the observation that the vine has already been partially burned — a reference to the first deportation of 597 BCE. The wood that was useless before burning is now doubly worthless. Calvin drew from this the principle that divine judgment on covenant unfaithfulness intensifies rather than diminishes when early warnings go unheeded.
