## The Watchman Recommissioned

Ezekiel 33 marks the book's structural turning point. With Jerusalem's fall confirmed by the arrival of a fugitive, the prophet's role shifts from announcing judgment to proclaiming restoration. The chapter opens by reaffirming Ezekiel's commission as a watchman, now recontextualized for a new phase of ministry.

Protestant interpreters have found the watchman metaphor richly applicable to pastoral ministry. The watchman bears responsibility for warning, but each individual bears responsibility for responding. This balance between corporate accountability and individual moral agency resonates deeply with Protestant theology's emphasis on personal faith and responsibility before God.

The chapter's conclusion addresses the exiles' resistance -- they hear Ezekiel's words as entertainment rather than divine address (33:30-33). Protestant preachers have frequently cited this passage as a warning against treating the proclamation of God's word as aesthetic performance rather than a summons to obedience.
