## The End Has Come

Ezekiel 7 delivers a poem of finality: "The end has come upon the four corners of the land" (7:2). The chapter is marked by urgency, repetition, and escalating intensity. Economic structures collapse — silver is thrown into the streets, the marketplace ceases — and social order disintegrates as the king mourns, the prince is clothed in despair, and the hands of the people go slack.

Protestant interpreters note the comprehensive scope of this judgment: it encompasses economic, political, social, and religious life. No sphere of human activity is exempt. Dispensational commentators have sometimes read this as typological of future eschatological judgment, while Reformed interpreters emphasize its historical fulfillment in the Babylonian destruction and its paradigmatic significance for understanding divine judgment throughout history.

The declaration that "they shall know that I am the LORD" frames even this devastating judgment as revelatory. God's ultimate purpose is not destruction for its own sake but self-disclosure — making known His character as holy, just, and sovereign. This theological lens transforms the reading of judgment oracles from mere doom-saying into purposeful divine communication.
