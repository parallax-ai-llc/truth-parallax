## Unfaithful Jerusalem

Ezekiel 16 is the longest chapter in the book — an extended allegory of Jerusalem as an abandoned infant whom God rescued, raised, adorned, and married, only to have her turn to promiscuous idolatry with every passing nation. The imagery is graphic and deliberately shocking. Jerusalem's unfaithfulness is declared worse than that of Sodom and Samaria, her "sisters."

Protestant exegesis has grappled with this chapter's unflinching language. Puritan commentators read it as a comprehensive theology of grace and ingratitude: God's initiative in salvation (finding the abandoned infant), His lavish provision (adorning the bride), and humanity's inexplicable response of rebellion. The chapter has been foundational for the Reformed doctrine of total depravity — the human heart is not merely weak but actively unfaithful.

Yet the chapter does not end in judgment alone. Verses 60-63 contain a promise that God will remember His covenant and establish an everlasting covenant with Jerusalem, who will then "remember and be ashamed." Protestant commentators see here the pattern of unconditional covenant love that persists despite radical unfaithfulness — a foreshadowing of the gospel's proclamation that God justifies the ungodly. The shame of verses 61-63 is not punitive but restorative, the godly sorrow that leads to repentance (2 Corinthians 7:10).
