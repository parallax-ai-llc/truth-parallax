## The Watchman

Ezekiel 3 continues the prophet's commissioning. Ezekiel eats the scroll and finds it sweet as honey, then sits overwhelmed among the exiles at Tel-abib for seven days. God then appoints him as a watchman over Israel, establishing the paradigm that defines his prophetic ministry: if the watchman warns the wicked and they do not turn, the watchman is delivered but the wicked perish; if the watchman fails to warn, the blood of the wicked is on the watchman's hands.

The watchman metaphor has been profoundly influential in Protestant pastoral theology. It undergirds the Reformed understanding of the minister's obligation to preach both law and gospel faithfully, regardless of popular reception. Puritan and evangelical traditions have drawn on this passage to articulate the urgency of evangelism and the moral accountability of spiritual leaders.

The chapter concludes with Ezekiel being bound and made mute, able to speak only when God opens his mouth. Protestant commentators see this as emphasizing that true prophetic speech originates not in human initiative but in divine command. The prophet is not a freelance commentator but a bound servant of the word.
