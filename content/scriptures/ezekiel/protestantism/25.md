## Judgment on the Surrounding Nations

Ezekiel 25 opens the section of oracles against foreign nations with pronouncements against Ammon, Moab, Edom, and Philistia. Protestant interpreters note that each nation is condemned not merely for political hostility but for a theological offense -- rejoicing over the desolation of God's sanctuary and people. The structure reinforces the Reformed principle that God's sovereignty extends over all nations, not just Israel.

The brevity of these oracles compared to those against Tyre and Egypt has led commentators like Calvin to observe that God's justice is proportional and particular. Each nation receives judgment fitted to its specific sin: Ammon for gloating, Moab for denying Israel's distinctiveness, Edom for active vengeance, and Philistia for perpetual enmity.

Protestant exegesis finds in this chapter a wider theological principle: no nation exists outside God's moral governance. The nations' treatment of Israel becomes a barometer of their relationship to God's purposes in history, reinforcing the Protestant understanding of divine providence operating through and beyond human political arrangements.
