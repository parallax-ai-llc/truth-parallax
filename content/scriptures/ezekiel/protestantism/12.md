## The Exile Symbolized

Ezekiel 12 records two symbolic acts and addresses the problem of prophetic skepticism. The prophet digs through a wall at night, carries his baggage out on his shoulder with his face covered, and leaves — enacting the exile of Zedekiah, who would flee Jerusalem and be captured. A second sign-act involves eating and drinking with trembling, symbolizing the anxiety of those remaining in the land.

Protestant interpreters highlight the response of the people: "The days grow long, and every vision comes to nothing" (12:22) and "The vision that he sees is for many days from now" (12:27). These proverbs reflect the perennial temptation to dismiss prophetic warnings through delay and disbelief. God's response is emphatic: "None of my words will be delayed any longer" (12:28).

This chapter has been important in Protestant discussions of the authority and reliability of Scripture. The people's dismissal of prophecy as failed or irrelevant is answered by divine insistence on fulfillment. Evangelical commentators draw a parallel to 2 Peter 3:3-4, where scoffers question the delay of Christ's return. The principle that divine timing is not human timing, yet divine promises are unfailingly reliable, is a recurring emphasis.
