## The Inner Temple

Ezekiel 41 continues the detailed measurement of the temple, focusing on the inner sanctuary -- the nave and the most holy place. The chapter records dimensions of walls, doorways, and side chambers, along with decorative elements: carved cherubim and palm trees adorning the walls.

Protestant commentators note that the decorative program -- alternating cherubim and palm trees -- echoes Solomon's temple (1 Kings 6:29) while also recalling Eden, where cherubim guarded the way to the tree of life. The temple vision thus presents worship as a return to paradise, a restoration of the communion between God and humanity lost in the fall.

The progressive narrowing of access -- from outer court to inner court to nave to most holy place -- embodies the principle of graduated holiness that Protestant interpreters see fulfilled in Christ's atoning work. Where the old temple's architecture emphasized exclusion and mediation, the New Testament declares that through Christ, all believers have "confidence to enter the holy places" (Hebrews 10:19).
