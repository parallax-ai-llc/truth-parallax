## Judgment on the Mountains of Israel

Ezekiel 6 pronounces judgment against the mountains of Israel, the sites of idolatrous high-place worship. God will destroy the altars and incense stands, scattering the bones of the slain around their idols. The refrain "then you shall know that I am the LORD" appears for the first time in the oracles of judgment, establishing the theological purpose behind divine action.

Protestant interpreters read this chapter as a condemnation of syncretistic worship. The "high places" (*bamot*) represent Israel's persistent failure to worship God exclusively according to His prescribed means — a theme that resonates with the Reformation's own critique of unauthorized worship practices. The Regulative Principle of Worship, central to Reformed theology, finds a prophetic antecedent in Ezekiel's condemnation.

Yet even within judgment, a remnant survives: "I will leave some of you alive" (6:8). These scattered survivors will remember God among the nations and loathe their own abominations. Protestant theology sees here the paradox of exile as both judgment and potential means of grace — removal from the land of temptation becomes an occasion for genuine repentance and spiritual renewal.
