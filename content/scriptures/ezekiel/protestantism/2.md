## The Prophet's Commission

Ezekiel 2 records God's commissioning of the prophet to speak to "a nation of rebels" (2:3). The divine voice commands Ezekiel to stand on his feet, and the Spirit enters him to enable obedience. God warns that the people are "impudent and stubborn" (2:4), yet Ezekiel must speak regardless of whether they listen.

Protestant interpreters emphasize the unconditional nature of the prophetic call. The prophet is not commissioned based on the likelihood of success but on the authority of the one who sends him. This principle has been foundational for Reformed and evangelical theology of preaching — the minister's faithfulness is measured by fidelity to the message, not by results.

The chapter concludes with the vision of the scroll, written on both sides with "words of lamentation and mourning and woe" (2:10). That the scroll is inscribed on both sides signals the completeness and intensity of the coming judgment. Protestant commentators note that Ezekiel's commission to eat the scroll (fulfilled in chapter 3) parallels Jeremiah 15:16 and Revelation 10:9-10, establishing a biblical pattern in which God's word must be personally internalized before it can be faithfully proclaimed.
