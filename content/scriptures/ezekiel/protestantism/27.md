## The Ship of Tyre

Ezekiel 27 presents an elaborate lament over Tyre, depicting the city as a magnificent ship constructed from the finest materials of the known world, manned by skilled mariners, and laden with exotic cargo -- only to be wrecked by an east wind in the heart of the seas. Protestant commentators recognize this as one of the most literarily sophisticated passages in the prophetic corpus.

The detailed trade catalogue (27:12-25) has fascinated Protestant scholars for its geographical and commercial precision. Calvin read the passage as demonstrating that even the most impressive human achievements -- technological sophistication, commercial networks, cultural refinement -- are subject to divine reversal when they become ends in themselves.

The lament's emotional power is unmistakable: the surrounding peoples and merchants weep, wail, and throw dust on their heads at Tyre's fall. Protestant interpreters hear in this an anticipation of Revelation 18's lament over Babylon, where merchants weep because "no one buys their cargo anymore." The typological connection between Tyre and eschatological Babylon has been a staple of Protestant prophetic interpretation.
