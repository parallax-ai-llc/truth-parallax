## A New Heart and a New Spirit

Ezekiel 36 is among the most theologically significant chapters in the entire Old Testament for Protestant theology. After addressing the mountains of Israel, the chapter delivers the great promise: "I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit within you. I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh" (36:26).

Protestant interpreters, particularly in the Reformed tradition, read this passage as the definitive Old Testament articulation of regeneration. Calvin saw here the clearest prophetic statement of what the New Testament calls the new birth. The passage's insistence that this transformation is entirely God's initiative -- "I will sprinkle... I will give... I will remove... I will put" -- undergirds the Protestant doctrines of grace.

God's motivation is stated with startling clarity: "It is not for your sake, O house of Israel, that I am about to act, but for the sake of my holy name" (36:22). Protestant theology finds in this verse a foundation for the doctrine of sola gratia -- salvation originates in God's concern for His own glory, not in human merit.
