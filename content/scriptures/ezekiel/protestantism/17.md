## The Two Eagles and the Vine

Ezekiel 17 presents a political allegory in the form of a riddle. A great eagle (Nebuchadnezzar) takes the top of a cedar (King Jehoiachin) and plants a seed (Zedekiah) that grows into a spreading vine. But the vine turns toward another great eagle (Pharaoh) for sustenance, violating its covenant. God asks: will such a vine prosper?

Protestant commentators emphasize two dimensions. First, the political-historical: Zedekiah's treaty with Egypt against Babylon was a violation of the vassal covenant sworn in God's name, and God treats this political oath as a binding commitment. The theological principle is that covenant-breaking, even in the political sphere, has spiritual consequences because oaths invoke God's name.

Second, the chapter concludes with a messianic oracle (17:22-24): God Himself will take a sprig from the top of the cedar and plant it on a high mountain, where it will become a noble cedar sheltering all birds. Protestant interpreters overwhelmingly read this as a messianic prophecy fulfilled in Christ — the rightful king from David's line who establishes a kingdom that encompasses all nations. The reversal of expectations (the low tree exalted, the high tree brought low) anticipates the gospel pattern of divine action through apparent weakness.
