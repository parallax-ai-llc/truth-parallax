## The Glory and the Cherubim

Ezekiel 10 reprises the chariot-throne vision of chapter 1 within the context of the temple, now identifying the living creatures as cherubim. The figure in linen is commanded to take coals of fire from among the cherubim and scatter them over the city. Most significantly, the glory of the LORD rises from the threshold of the temple and mounts the chariot-throne, pausing at the east gate.

Protestant interpreters regard this as one of the most theologically momentous events in the Old Testament: the departure of God's presence from the temple. The glory (*kavod*) that had filled Solomon's temple (1 Kings 8:10-11) now withdraws, stage by stage, signaling that the temple is no longer a place of divine protection but an empty shell awaiting destruction.

Reformed commentators emphasize that God's presence is not mechanically bound to sacred spaces or institutions. The departure of glory warns against presuming upon divine favor without covenant faithfulness. This principle informed the Reformers' critique of the medieval sacramental system — God's presence is not automatically guaranteed by institutional forms but is freely given and withdrawn according to His sovereign purposes.
