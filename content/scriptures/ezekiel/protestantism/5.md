## God's Razor of Judgment

Ezekiel 5 records another symbolic action: the prophet shaves his head and beard with a sword used as a razor, then divides the hair into three portions — one burned, one struck with the sword, one scattered to the wind. A small number of hairs are bound in the hem of his garment, representing a remnant, though even some of these are cast into the fire.

Protestant commentators highlight the threefold division as representing the three fates of Jerusalem's population: death by pestilence and famine within the city, death by the sword outside the walls, and scattering among the nations. The remnant theology here is sobering — even survival is precarious.

The chapter's second half delivers the theological rationale: Jerusalem has been more rebellious than the surrounding nations, violating the covenant despite being given God's statutes. The judgment is therefore proportional and just. Reformed interpreters emphasize the principle of greater accountability — those who receive greater revelation bear greater responsibility. This passage has been influential in Protestant covenant theology's understanding of the relationship between privilege and obligation.
