## Oracle Against Egypt

Ezekiel 29 begins the extended series of oracles against Egypt. Pharaoh is depicted as a great river monster lurking in the Nile, who boasts "My Nile is my own; I made it for myself." Protestant interpreters identify this as the quintessential expression of autonomous human pride -- the creature claiming the prerogatives of the Creator.

The prophecy of Egypt's desolation for forty years, followed by a diminished restoration as "the lowliest of kingdoms," has prompted extensive discussion among Protestant commentators. Some have sought specific historical fulfillments, while others focus on the theological point: Egypt's reduction demonstrates that God's purpose is not annihilation but the humbling of pride.

The chapter concludes with the remarkable prophecy that Nebuchadnezzar will receive Egypt as wages for his service against Tyre (29:17-21). Protestant exegetes find here a striking illustration of divine sovereignty: even pagan conquerors serve as instruments of God's purposes, and the final promise of a horn springing up for Israel keeps the messianic hope alive amidst judgment.
