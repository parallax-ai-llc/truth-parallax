## Oholah and Oholibah

Ezekiel 23 presents an extended allegory of two sisters: Oholah (Samaria) and Oholibah (Jerusalem), both of whom engage in promiscuous political and religious alliances with surrounding nations — Assyria, Babylon, and Egypt. The allegory parallels chapter 16 but focuses specifically on the political dimension of unfaithfulness: the pursuit of foreign military alliances rather than trust in God.

Protestant interpreters note the theological equation between political trust in foreign powers and spiritual adultery. The sisters' pursuit of Assyrian and Babylonian lovers represents not merely diplomatic pragmatism but a fundamental failure of faith — choosing visible military power over trust in the invisible God. This reading has been influential in Protestant political theology, particularly in Reformed traditions that emphasize the sovereignty of God over nations.

The judgment that falls on both sisters comes through the very nations they pursued: Oholah is destroyed by Assyria, and Oholibah will be destroyed by Babylon. Protestant commentators identify here the principle of poetic justice — the instruments of unfaithfulness become the instruments of judgment. The chapter's severity serves as a warning that the consequences of covenant unfaithfulness are proportional to the privileges received: Oholibah's judgment is worse than Oholah's because she witnessed her sister's fate yet continued in the same pattern.
