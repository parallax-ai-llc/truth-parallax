## Israel's Rebellious History

Ezekiel 20 is a comprehensive recital of Israel's history of rebellion, structured around four stages: Egypt, the wilderness (first generation), the wilderness (second generation), and the land of Canaan. At each stage, the pattern repeats — God gives statutes, Israel rebels through idolatry, God considers destroying them but relents "for the sake of my name" to prevent profanation among the nations.

Protestant interpreters find this chapter significant for several reasons. First, it provides a theology of history grounded in divine patience and self-regard: God preserves Israel not because of their merit but to maintain His own reputation among the nations. This resonates with the Reformed emphasis on God's glory as the ultimate purpose of all divine action.

Second, the chapter contains the striking declaration that God "gave them statutes that were not good and rules by which they could not have life" (20:25), which Protestant commentators have interpreted variously as referring to the judicial hardening of rebellious hearts, the pagan practices God permitted Israel to follow to their own destruction, or the ceremonial law that pointed to its own insufficiency. The chapter concludes with a promise of future restoration through judgment — God will bring Israel "into the wilderness of the peoples" and there purge the rebels, entering into covenant with the faithful. The restoration is wholly of grace, accomplished despite and through judgment.
