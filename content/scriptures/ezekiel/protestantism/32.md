## Lament over Pharaoh and Egypt

Ezekiel 32 contains two related compositions: a lament over Pharaoh as a sea monster dragged from the waters (32:1-16), and a dirge consigning Egypt to Sheol alongside other fallen nations (32:17-32). Protestant interpreters find the chapter remarkable for its vivid depiction of the netherworld, where the mighty nations of history lie in shame.

The catalogue of fallen empires in Sheol -- Assyria, Elam, Meshech-Tubal, Edom, and the Sidonians -- creates a panoramic vision of historical judgment. Each empire that once terrorized the living now lies silent among the uncircumcised slain, demonstrating the relentless equality of death.

Evangelical scholarship has noted that this chapter, while not teaching the developed doctrine of hell, contributes to the biblical trajectory toward final judgment. The shame and dishonor of the fallen nations in Sheol anticipates the New Testament distinction between honorable resurrection and disgrace.
