## The Fall of the Great Cedar

Ezekiel 31 presents an extended allegory of Assyria as a magnificent cedar in Lebanon -- towering above all other trees, nourished by deep waters, sheltering birds and beasts, envied by the trees of Eden. Yet because of its pride, God consigned it to Sheol, and all the nations trembled at its fall.

Protestant interpreters have valued this chapter for its meditation on the relationship between greatness and pride. The cedar's beauty and majesty are genuine -- God does not deny the accomplishments of great empires -- but the transformation of legitimate greatness into self-deifying pride triggers divine judgment. Reformed theology identifies this pattern as the fundamental structure of sin: the misattribution of gifts to self rather than to the Giver.

The descent to Sheol imagery (31:15-18) contributes to the developing Old Testament picture of death and the underworld. The consolation that Pharaoh will "lie among the uncircumcised" offers cold comfort -- a grim leveling that strips away all pretensions of earthly glory.
