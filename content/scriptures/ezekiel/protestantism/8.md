## Idolatry in the Temple

Ezekiel 8 begins a temple vision sequence (chapters 8-11) in which the prophet is transported in vision to Jerusalem. There he witnesses four scenes of escalating abomination within the temple precincts: the image of jealousy at the north gate, seventy elders worshiping idolatrous images in a hidden chamber, women weeping for Tammuz at the north gate, and twenty-five men worshiping the sun with their backs turned to the temple.

Protestant exegesis has consistently read this chapter as demonstrating that the Babylonian exile was not arbitrary but a justified divine response to comprehensive covenant violation. The idolatry is not merely private but institutional, penetrating to the very heart of Israel's worship. The elders' claim that "the LORD does not see us, the LORD has forsaken the land" (8:12) reveals a theology of divine absence used to justify moral license.

Calvin and subsequent Reformed interpreters drew pointed parallels between the temple's corruption and the medieval Church's accommodation of unbiblical practices. The principle that worship must be purified of human invention and conformed strictly to divine prescription emerges as a persistent Protestant application of this chapter.
