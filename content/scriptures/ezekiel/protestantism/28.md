## The Pride of the King of Tyre

Ezekiel 28 contains two oracles against the ruler of Tyre, the second of which (28:11-19) uses Edenic imagery that has generated centuries of interpretive debate. The king is described as having been "in Eden, the garden of God," adorned with precious stones, and as a guardian cherub on God's holy mountain -- until iniquity was found in him and he was cast down.

Many Protestant interpreters, particularly in the Reformed and evangelical traditions, have read the second oracle as referring not merely to the human king but to Satan, seeing in the passage a description of the devil's original glory and fall. Other Protestant scholars insist the passage is purely addressed to the historical ruler, using mythological hyperbole to expose his pretensions.

The chapter concludes with a brief oracle against Sidon and a promise of Israel's restoration -- a structural pattern that Protestant commentators note throughout the oracles against nations: judgment on the nations ultimately serves the vindication of God's people and reinforces God's faithfulness to His covenant promises.
