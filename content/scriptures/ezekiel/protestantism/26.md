## The Fall of Tyre

Ezekiel 26 delivers a devastating oracle against Tyre, the great Phoenician trading city. Protestant interpreters have long noted the remarkable specificity of these prophecies -- Nebuchadnezzar's siege, the scraping of Tyre's soil down to bare rock, and its ultimate reduction to a place for spreading fishing nets. The chapter demonstrates the sovereign God's authority over the most powerful commercial centers of the ancient world.

Reformed commentators emphasize the theological logic: Tyre's sin is declaring "Aha!" over Jerusalem's fall, seeing in it a commercial opportunity rather than a divine judgment. The city's mercantile pragmatism -- reducing sacred events to profit calculations -- represents a form of idolatry that Protestant theology identifies with mammon worship.

The descent imagery at the chapter's conclusion -- going down to the pit, dwelling in the lower parts of the earth -- prefigures the New Testament warnings about the ultimate fate of those who trust in wealth rather than God. Protestant preachers from the Puritans onward have used Tyre as a cautionary type for commercial societies that forget their accountability to God.
