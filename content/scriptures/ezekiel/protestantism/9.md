## The Slaughter of the Idolaters

Ezekiel 9 presents a vision of divine judgment executed within Jerusalem. Six executioners approach the city, accompanied by a figure clothed in linen with a writing case, who is commanded to mark the foreheads of those who "sigh and groan" over the city's abominations. The rest are to be slaughtered without mercy — beginning at the sanctuary itself.

Protestant interpreters find several foundational principles here. First, the distinction between the marked and the unmarked establishes the remnant principle: God preserves those who grieve over sin even in the midst of corporate judgment. This has been influential in Puritan and evangelical thought about the "faithful remnant" within compromised religious institutions.

Second, that judgment begins "at my sanctuary" (9:6) is connected to 1 Peter 4:17: "judgment begins at the household of God." Protestant commentators from Luther onward have used this principle to argue that the Church must continually reform itself, for those closest to God's revelation bear the greatest accountability. The marking of the faithful also prefigures the sealing of Revelation 7:3, creating a canonical arc of divine protection for the righteous within judgment.
