## The Valley of Dry Bones

Ezekiel 37 contains the most famous vision in the book: the valley full of dry bones reconstituted, enfleshed, and revivified by the breath of God. The vision is followed by the sign-act of joining two sticks representing Judah and Israel into one, symbolizing the reunification of the divided kingdom under the messianic David.

Protestant interpretation operates on multiple levels. The primary referent is the restoration of Israel from exile -- the bones represent a nation that considers itself dead and hopeless. But Protestant theology has consistently read the passage typologically as pointing to both spiritual regeneration and bodily resurrection. The sequence mirrors the creation account of Genesis 2, suggesting a new creation.

The Davidic covenant renewal in 37:24-28, with its promise of one shepherd, one kingdom, an everlasting covenant, and God's sanctuary in their midst, represents for Protestant interpreters the fullest Old Testament articulation of the messianic hope -- a trajectory fulfilled in Christ's resurrection, the unity of the church, and the promise of God's eternal presence with His people.
