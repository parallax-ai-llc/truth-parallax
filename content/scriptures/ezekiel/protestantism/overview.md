## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Ezekiel is shaped by the Reformation commitment to *sola scriptura* and the conviction that Old Testament prophecy finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ and the New Covenant. Ezekiel's visions, oracles, and symbolic acts are read as divinely authoritative revelation, with significant debate over how literally to interpret the temple vision of chapters 40-48.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Dispensational

Dispensationalist interpreters read Ezekiel's temple vision as a literal prophecy of a future millennial temple. The restoration promises are understood as applying to national Israel in a future eschatological age, distinct from the Church.

### Reformed/Covenant

Reformed interpreters tend to read the temple vision typologically, seeing it as symbolic of the Church, the heavenly reality, or the new creation. The new covenant promises of chapters 34 and 36-37 are understood as fulfilled in Christ and applied to the Church.

### Evangelical Critical

A growing number of Protestant scholars engage with historical-critical methods while maintaining the theological authority of the text, reading Ezekiel within its exilic context while drawing out its canonical significance.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Sovereignty**: The repeated formula "they shall know that I am the LORD" underscores God's self-revelation through judgment and restoration
- **New Covenant**: Ezekiel 36:26-27 — the promise of a new heart and new spirit — is central to Protestant soteriology
- **Individual Responsibility**: Ezekiel 18 is foundational for Protestant teaching on personal accountability before God
- **Christological Typology**: The Good Shepherd of chapter 34 is read as pointing to Christ (John 10)
