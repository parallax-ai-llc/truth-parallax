## The Return of the Glory

Ezekiel 43 records the climactic moment of the temple vision: the glory of God, which departed from the temple in chapters 10-11, returns from the east and fills the new temple. The chapter also details the altar of burnt offering and its consecration rituals.

The return of the glory reverses the most devastating event in the book -- God's abandonment of His own house. Protestant theology reads this as a type of the Incarnation (John 1:14) and ultimately of the eschatological promise in Revelation 21:3. The movement from departure to return structures the entire biblical narrative of redemption.

The altar consecration rituals (43:18-27) have presented a challenge for Protestant interpretation. If the vision is future, how do animal sacrifices relate to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice? Dispensationalists explain them as memorial offerings, while Reformed interpreters see them as symbolic language pointing to the definitive sacrifice of Christ.
