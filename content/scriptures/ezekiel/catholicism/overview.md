## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Ezekiel is grounded in the Church's broader hermeneutical principles: Scripture is read within the living Tradition of the Church, under the guidance of the Magisterium, and in continuity with patristic and medieval exegesis. Ezekiel's visions have been especially significant for Catholic sacramental theology, ecclesiology, and Marian devotion.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic and Medieval

The Church Fathers read Ezekiel extensively in typological and allegorical terms. Origen, Jerome, and Gregory the Great each produced major commentaries. Gregory's *Homilies on Ezekiel* remain influential, reading the prophet's visions as allegories of the soul's journey toward God and the Church's mission in the world.

### Modern Catholic Scholarship

Since *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943) and *Dei Verbum* (1965), Catholic scholars have embraced historical-critical methods alongside theological reading. The Pontifical Biblical Commission encourages attention to the literal sense as the foundation for spiritual interpretation.

## Theological Emphases

- **Ecclesiology**: The temple vision is read as prefiguring the Church, the true dwelling of God among humanity
- **Sacramental Typology**: The water flowing from the temple (47:1-12) is understood as a figure of baptism and the life-giving sacraments
- **Marian Interpretation**: The closed gate of 44:1-3, through which only the Lord passes, has been read since the patristic era as a type of Mary's perpetual virginity
- **Shepherding and Ministry**: Ezekiel 34 informs Catholic teaching on pastoral authority, the episcopate, and Christ as the Good Shepherd
