## Lament for the Great Ship

Ezekiel 27's elaborate lament over Tyre -- depicted as a splendid ship laden with the world's treasures -- has been read by Catholic commentators as a meditation on the vanity of worldly magnificence. The detailed catalogue of trade goods demonstrates the vast reach of Tyre's commercial empire, only to depict its total destruction.

Catholic moral theology has drawn on this passage to develop its teaching on the right use of material goods. The ship of Tyre is not condemned for trading per se -- Catholic social teaching affirms the legitimacy of commerce -- but for elevating commercial success to an ultimate value. The Church's social encyclicals echo the prophetic concern with economies that serve human flourishing versus those that become idolatrous.

The mourning of surrounding peoples anticipates the lament over Babylon in Revelation 18, a connection that Jerome and Theodoret recognized early. The typological relationship reads Tyre as a prefiguration of every worldly system that places wealth above God.
