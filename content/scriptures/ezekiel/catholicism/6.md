## Judgment on the High Places

Ezekiel 6 pronounces judgment against Israel's mountains and their idolatrous shrines. God will destroy the altars, scatter the bones of worshipers around their idols, and devastate the cities. Yet a remnant will survive among the nations and remember God, loathing their own abominations.

Catholic exegesis reads this chapter within the broader biblical theology of true and false worship. The "high places" represent unauthorized worship — sacrifice offered outside the divinely appointed sanctuary. The Fathers drew parallels to schismatic worship conducted outside the communion of the Church. Cyprian of Carthage's principle *extra ecclesiam nulla salus* (outside the Church there is no salvation), while requiring careful nuance in modern Catholic theology, finds a prophetic antecedent in Ezekiel's insistence that worship must conform to God's established order.

The promise that the scattered remnant will "remember" God (6:9) is significant for Catholic spirituality. Memory — *anamnesis* — is a liturgical and sacramental concept. The Eucharist is an act of remembering that makes present what it recalls. The exiles' remembrance of God initiates a process of conversion that moves from recollection through self-loathing to renewal.
