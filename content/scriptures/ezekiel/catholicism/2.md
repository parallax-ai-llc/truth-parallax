## The Call to Prophetic Ministry

Ezekiel 2 recounts God's commissioning of the prophet to a rebellious house of Israel. The Spirit enters the prophet, enabling him to stand and receive the divine word. Catholic interpretation emphasizes the sacramental dimension of prophetic calling — the prophet does not volunteer but is called and empowered by the Spirit, just as ordained ministers in the Church receive their authority not from personal merit but from divine commission through apostolic succession.

The description of Israel as "impudent and stubborn" (2:4) and the command to speak "whether they hear or refuse to hear" (2:5) resonate with Catholic teaching on the prophetic office of the Church. The Magisterium's responsibility to proclaim truth faithfully, regardless of cultural reception, finds a prophetic warrant in Ezekiel's commission. The Church, like the prophet, must not adapt the message to suit the audience.

The scroll written on both sides with lamentation and woe (2:9-10) has been interpreted by the Fathers as prefiguring the fullness of divine revelation: the Old and New Testaments, or the literal and spiritual senses of Scripture. Origen read the two sides as representing the accessible surface meaning and the hidden spiritual depth that requires divine illumination to perceive.
