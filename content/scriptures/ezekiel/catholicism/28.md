## The King of Tyre and the Memory of Eden

Ezekiel 28's oracle against the king of Tyre, with its Edenic imagery of precious stones, the guardian cherub, and expulsion from the holy mountain, has generated rich Catholic theological reflection. The Church Fathers widely read the passage as referring to the fall of Satan -- a reading that informed Catholic angelology and the doctrine of the devil's original perfection and subsequent rebellion.

Augustine, in The City of God, drew on this passage to articulate the nature of angelic sin: the creature's refusal to acknowledge its creaturely status. Thomas Aquinas developed this further in the Summa Theologiae, identifying the root sin of the fallen angels as pride. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (par. 391-395) reflects this patristic and scholastic tradition.

The brief oracle against Sidon and the concluding promise of Israel's restoration remind Catholic readers that judgment against evil always serves God's redemptive purpose. The Catholic theological principle of felix culpa finds a distant echo here: even the devastation wrought by pride becomes an occasion for God to display His restorative power.
