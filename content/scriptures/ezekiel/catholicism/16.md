## The Allegory of Foundling Jerusalem

Ezekiel 16 tells the story of Jerusalem as an abandoned infant rescued by God, raised to royal beauty, married to Him, and then turned to flagrant unfaithfulness with every passing nation — worse than Sodom and Samaria. Yet the chapter concludes with a promise that God will remember His covenant and establish an everlasting covenant.

Catholic tradition has read this chapter on multiple levels. Patristically, it serves as an allegory of the soul's journey: created by God, nurtured by grace, adorned with spiritual gifts, yet always prone to turning away. The imagery has informed Catholic devotional literature and the theology of spiritual direction, particularly in describing the stages of conversion, growth, and the ever-present danger of spiritual infidelity.

The chapter's conclusion (16:60-63) has been especially important for Catholic covenant theology. God's promise to remember His covenant despite Jerusalem's utter failure establishes the principle of irrevocable grace — God's fidelity does not depend on human faithfulness. This understanding informs the Catholic teaching on the indissolubility of certain sacraments (baptism, confirmation, orders) and the permanence of God's covenant love.
