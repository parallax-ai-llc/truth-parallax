## Against False Prophecy

Ezekiel 13 condemns prophets who speak from their own imagination and prophetesses who practice divination. The false prophets are compared to foxes among ruins and their message to whitewash on a weak wall — cosmetically appealing but structurally useless.

Catholic tradition has read this chapter as underscoring the necessity of authorized teaching. The false prophets speak without being sent; they "have seen nothing" (13:3) yet claim divine authority. The Catholic response to this danger is the Magisterium — the authoritative teaching office that ensures the faithful are not "tossed to and fro by every wind of doctrine" (Ephesians 4:14). The chapter provides a prophetic warrant for institutional discernment of authentic versus false spiritual claims.

The image of whitewashing a wall that will not stand has been applied by Catholic commentators to any theology that offers superficial comfort without addressing underlying spiritual corruption. Gregory the Great used this passage to warn preachers against flattering their congregations rather than speaking difficult truths. The pastoral task requires the courage to proclaim judgment as well as comfort.
