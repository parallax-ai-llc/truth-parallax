## The Justice of God

Ezekiel 18 refutes the fatalistic proverb about sour grapes and declares that each person is judged according to their own conduct. The righteous who turns to wickedness will die; the wicked who repents will live. God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked but desires that they turn and live.

Catholic moral theology engages deeply with this chapter. The principle of individual responsibility accords with the Catholic teaching on personal sin and the necessity of individual repentance through the sacrament of reconciliation. Each person must account for their own actions, and no one is condemned for another's sin (though the effects of original sin are distinguished from personal guilt in Catholic doctrine).

The chapter's emphasis on the possibility of conversion — the wicked can turn and live — undergirds the Catholic sacramental system's provision for ongoing repentance. Unlike some Protestant readings that emphasize a single decisive conversion, Catholic theology reads Ezekiel 18 as affirming the lifelong possibility of both falling away and returning. The concluding appeal "get yourselves a new heart and a new spirit" (18:31) is understood in light of the gift-character of conversion: the command presupposes the grace that enables its fulfillment, a principle central to the Catholic theology of nature and grace.
