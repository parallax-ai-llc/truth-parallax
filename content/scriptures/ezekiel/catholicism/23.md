## The Two Sisters

Ezekiel 23 presents the allegory of Oholah (Samaria) and Oholibah (Jerusalem), two sisters whose promiscuous political alliances with Assyria, Babylon, and Egypt constitute spiritual adultery. Oholibah's sin is worse because she continued despite witnessing her sister's destruction.

Catholic moral theology has read this chapter as illustrating the principle of aggravated culpability — sin committed with full knowledge of its consequences is graver than sin committed in ignorance. Oholibah saw Oholah's fate and persisted, a dynamic that intensifies her guilt. This principle informs the Catholic distinction between sins committed in ignorance and sins committed with full knowledge and deliberate consent, as elaborated in the theology of mortal sin.

The political dimension — the pursuit of foreign military alliances as a substitute for trust in God — has been applied in Catholic social teaching to the temptation of placing ultimate confidence in political or military power rather than in divine providence. The allegory warns against a purely pragmatic approach to power that subordinates faith to political calculation.
