## The Mark of Preservation

Ezekiel 9 presents six executioners and a scribe in linen who marks the foreheads of those who grieve over Jerusalem's abominations. The marked are spared; all others are slain, beginning at the sanctuary. The judgment proceeds without pity — the elderly, young men, women, and children who lack the mark all perish.

Catholic tradition has interpreted the mark on the forehead as a prefiguration of baptismal and chrismation signing. The *tau* mark (the Hebrew letter resembling a cross in ancient script) was read by Origen and other Fathers as a prophetic sign of the Cross. Those sealed with the sign of Christ are preserved through judgment, just as those marked by the angel were preserved through Jerusalem's destruction. This typological reading informed the development of the sacramental theology of character — the indelible spiritual mark conferred by baptism, confirmation, and holy orders.

That judgment begins "at my sanctuary" (9:6) was taken by Gregory the Great as a solemn warning to clergy: those who serve at the altar bear the greatest responsibility and face the most searching judgment. This principle continues to inform Catholic teaching on the accountability of ordained ministers.
