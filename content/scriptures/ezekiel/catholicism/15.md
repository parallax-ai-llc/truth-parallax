## The Worthless Vine

Ezekiel 15 compares Jerusalem to a vine whose wood is useless — fit for no construction, and once partially burned, even more worthless. Catholic interpretation reads this brief parable as a meditation on the relationship between election and fruitfulness. Israel's chosenness, like the vine's nature, finds its sole justification in bearing fruit; apart from this purpose, it has no inherent advantage.

The Fathers connected this passage to Jesus's teaching in John 15:1-8, where Christ declares himself the true vine and warns that branches that bear no fruit are cut off and burned. Catholic theology reads these passages together as establishing the principle that sacramental membership in the Church (the vine) requires actual holiness (fruit-bearing) to be salvific. The sacraments are necessary but not sufficient without the response of faith and charity.

Augustine drew on the vine imagery to argue against Donatist claims that the Church must consist only of the visibly holy. The vine includes both fruitful and fruitless branches; but the fruitless branches face judgment, not the vine itself. The Church remains holy in its Head and sacraments even when individual members fail.
