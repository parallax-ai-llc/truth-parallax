## The Dross of Jerusalem

Ezekiel 22 catalogs Jerusalem's sins across every level of society — prophets, priests, princes, and people — and employs the metaphor of smelting: Israel has become dross, and God will melt them in the furnace of His wrath. The chapter concludes with the devastating observation that God sought someone to stand in the breach but found no one.

Catholic interpretation has drawn on the smelting imagery for its theology of purgation. The furnace of judgment that burns away dross finds an analogy in the Catholic doctrine of purgatory — the purifying fire that removes all that is incompatible with God's holiness. While purgatory is a post-mortem reality in Catholic teaching, the prophetic image provides a biblical analogy for the principle that divine love is itself a purifying fire.

The failure to find an intercessor (22:30) is read Christologically in Catholic tradition. No merely human mediator could stand in the breach; the gap between divine holiness and human sinfulness required a mediator who was both divine and human. Christ alone fulfills what Ezekiel 22 shows to be impossible for any prophet, priest, or king of Israel.
