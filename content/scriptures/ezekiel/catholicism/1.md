## The Vision of the Divine Throne

Ezekiel 1 records the prophet's extraordinary vision of the chariot-throne of God (*Merkabah*) by the Kebar canal. Catholic interpretation, rooted in the patristic tradition, reads this theophany as a revelation of God's transcendent majesty that simultaneously conceals and discloses the divine nature. The four living creatures — later identified as cherubim — became the basis for the traditional symbols of the four Evangelists: the man (Matthew), the lion (Mark), the ox (Luke), and the eagle (John).

Gregory the Great devoted extensive attention to this chapter in his *Homilies on Ezekiel*, reading the four faces as representing Christ's Incarnation (man), resurrection (lion), sacrifice (ox), and ascension (eagle). Jerome similarly connected the vision to the fourfold Gospel. This patristic identification has shaped Christian iconography throughout the centuries and remains prominent in Catholic liturgical art and church architecture.

The figure upon the throne — described as having "the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the LORD" (1:28) — is understood in Catholic theology as a manifestation of the pre-incarnate Logos. The carefully layered language of "appearance" and "likeness" preserves divine transcendence while affirming that God genuinely communicates Himself through created media — a principle foundational to Catholic sacramental theology.
