## The Siege of Tyre

Ezekiel 26 pronounces judgment on Tyre for its callous opportunism. Catholic exegesis, grounded in the patristic tradition, reads the fall of Tyre as an exemplum of how worldly power, however magnificent, is subject to divine judgment. Gregory the Great interpreted Tyre's downfall as illustrating the instability of all earthly prosperity disconnected from God.

The detailed prophecy of Tyre's destruction has been understood within Catholic tradition as fulfilled in stages, from Nebuchadnezzar's siege through Alexander the Great's destruction of the island city. The Pontifical Biblical Commission has affirmed that prophetic fulfillment may occur progressively, and this chapter serves as a prime example.

The descent of Tyre to "the Pit" introduces imagery that Catholic theology connects to its developed understanding of the afterlife. While the Old Testament's Sheol is not identical to Catholic eschatological categories, the passage contributes to the trajectory of revelation that reaches its fullness in New Testament teaching on final judgment.
