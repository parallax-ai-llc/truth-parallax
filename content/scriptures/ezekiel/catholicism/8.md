## Abominations in the Temple

Ezekiel 8 transports the prophet in vision to Jerusalem's temple, where he witnesses four escalating abominations: the image of jealousy, elders worshiping creeping things and idols in secret chambers, women weeping for Tammuz, and men worshiping the sun with their backs to the temple. Each scene penetrates deeper into the temple's sacred space.

Catholic patristic commentary read this chapter as a powerful indictment of hypocrisy within sacred institutions. Jerome, who spent years translating and commenting on Ezekiel, emphasized that the most dangerous idolatry is not external but internal — conducted within the very precincts of worship. The seventy elders worshiping in darkness while claiming "the LORD does not see us" (8:12) represent institutional corruption cloaked in religious authority.

The Fathers also noted the pastoral significance: each abomination is introduced with "you will see still greater abominations" (8:6, 13, 15), indicating that sin, when tolerated, inevitably escalates. This principle has informed Catholic moral theology's understanding of the progressive nature of vice and the importance of addressing spiritual corruption at its earliest stages rather than accommodating it.
