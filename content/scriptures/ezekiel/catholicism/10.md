## The Departure of the Glory

Ezekiel 10 narrates the departure of the glory of the LORD from the temple, mounted upon the chariot-throne of the cherubim. The figure in linen scatters coals of fire over Jerusalem, and the glory pauses at the east gate before departing.

Catholic theology reads this departure as one of the most significant events in salvation history — the withdrawal of divine presence from the temple that had been its earthly dwelling since Solomon's dedication. The staged withdrawal (from the inner sanctuary to the threshold in 9:3, to the east gate in 10:19, and finally from the city in 11:23) demonstrates the patience of God, who does not depart suddenly but reluctantly, in stages.

The patristic tradition connected the departure through the east gate to the Ascension of Christ from the Mount of Olives (east of Jerusalem) and to His promised return. The east-facing orientation of Catholic churches and the celebration of the liturgy *ad orientem* (toward the east) reflect this theology of departure and return. The Eucharistic presence of Christ in the tabernacle is understood as the fulfillment of Ezekiel's vision — God's glory has returned, dwelling now not in a stone temple but in the sacramental body of Christ.
