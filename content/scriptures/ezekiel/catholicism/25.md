## Judgment on the Nations

Ezekiel 25 opens the oracles against foreign nations -- Ammon, Moab, Edom, and Philistia -- each condemned for hostility toward God's people. Catholic interpretation, following the Church Fathers, reads these oracles within the framework of God's universal sovereignty. The Catechism's teaching on natural law resonates with the prophetic assumption that these nations should have known better.

Patristic commentators such as Jerome read the four nations typologically: they represent spiritual enemies that assail the soul. Ammon (pride), Moab (complacency), Edom (envy), and Philistia (hostility) become categories of temptation. This allegorical layer, far from displacing the historical meaning, enriches it by demonstrating that the prophetic word speaks to every generation.

The Catholic liturgical tradition draws on these oracles to affirm that God's justice extends to all nations. The Second Vatican Council's Nostra Aetate implicitly stands in continuity with the prophetic vision: God's concern encompasses all humanity, and His judgment ultimately serves His redemptive purposes for the whole world.
