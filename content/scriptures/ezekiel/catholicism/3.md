## The Watchman's Charge

Ezekiel 3 establishes the watchman metaphor central to prophetic ministry. After eating the scroll, the prophet is appointed as a watchman responsible for warning the wicked. If the watchman warns and the wicked refuse, the watchman is innocent; if the watchman fails to warn, the blood of the wicked is required at his hands.

Catholic theology has applied the watchman image directly to the episcopal office. Gregory the Great, himself a pope, drew extensively on this passage in his *Pastoral Rule*, arguing that bishops and pastors bear the watchman's burden: they must warn, teach, correct, and admonish. Failure to do so constitutes not merely professional negligence but spiritual culpability. The passage has shaped Catholic canon law regarding the obligations of bishops and pastors toward their flocks.

The prophet's muteness at the chapter's end — he can speak only when God opens his mouth — has been read in the Catholic tradition as a model of prophetic obedience. True prophetic speech is not self-generated but received. This principle undergirds the Catholic understanding of magisterial teaching as custodial rather than creative: the Church guards and transmits the deposit of faith rather than inventing new doctrine.
