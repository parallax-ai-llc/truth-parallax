## The Parable of the Eagles

Ezekiel 17 presents a riddle of two eagles and a vine, allegorizing the political maneuvering of Judah's last kings between Babylon and Egypt. The chapter concludes with a messianic promise: God Himself will plant a tender sprig from the cedar on a high mountain, where it will become a magnificent tree.

Catholic interpretation highlights the theological problem of covenant-breaking. Zedekiah's violation of his oath to Nebuchadnezzar is treated as a sin against God because the oath was sworn in God's name (17:19). Catholic moral theology has drawn on this principle in its teaching on the binding nature of oaths and vows — commitments made before God carry divine sanction regardless of their political or secular context.

The messianic oracle (17:22-24) has been read Christologically in the Catholic tradition since the patristic era. The tender sprig planted by God on a high mountain is Christ, the son of David whose kingdom shelters all nations. The image of birds nesting in the cedar's branches parallels the mustard seed parable (Matthew 13:31-32), and the Catholic tradition sees both as describing the universal Church — a kingdom that begins in apparent insignificance but grows to encompass the world.
