## The Limits of Intercession

Ezekiel 14 addresses elders who harbor idols in their hearts while consulting the prophet, and declares that even Noah, Daniel, and Job could save only themselves by their righteousness — not their sons or daughters. The fourfold judgment of sword, famine, wild beasts, and pestilence will come upon the land.

Catholic theology engages this passage in dialogue with the doctrine of the communion of saints and intercessory prayer. While Ezekiel appears to limit the efficacy of intercession, Catholic interpretation distinguishes between the Old Covenant context — where Israel's corporate guilt had reached a point of irrevocable judgment — and the New Covenant economy, where Christ's intercession and the prayers of the saints have genuine intercessory power. The passage demonstrates not the impossibility of intercession but its limits in the face of obstinate and universal impenitence.

The theme of hidden idolatry — idols set up "in their hearts" (14:3) — has been significant for Catholic spiritual theology. The tradition of examination of conscience, central to the sacrament of reconciliation, addresses precisely this danger: the subtle interior attachments that compromise the soul's integrity even while outward religious practice continues.
