## The Cedar and Sheol

Ezekiel 31 compares Assyria to a magnificent cedar -- towering, beautiful, envied by the trees of Eden -- yet consigned to Sheol because of its pride. Catholic theology, drawing on Augustine and Aquinas, identifies pride as the root of all sin, and this chapter provides a vivid prophetic illustration.

The cedar's legitimate beauty and grandeur are not denied -- God acknowledges making it beautiful (31:9). Catholic theology's affirmation of the goodness of creation resonates here: the problem is not greatness itself but the prideful misappropriation of gifts. The Ignatian tradition's teaching on disordered attachments finds a prophetic precursor in the cedar's fatal self-glorification.

The descent to Sheol contributes to the developing biblical theology of death and judgment that Catholic doctrine has systematized in its teaching on the four last things: death, judgment, heaven, and hell. While Sheol is not yet the fully differentiated afterlife of Catholic eschatology, it represents an essential stage in progressive revelation.
