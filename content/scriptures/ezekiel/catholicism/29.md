## Oracle Against Pharaoh

Ezekiel 29 depicts Pharaoh as a great river monster claiming ownership of the Nile. Catholic interpretation identifies this as the sin against the first commandment: the creature attributing to itself what belongs to the Creator. The Catechism's teaching on the virtue of humility finds a negative illustration in Pharaoh's arrogance.

The prophecy of forty years of desolation followed by Egypt's restoration as "the lowliest of kingdoms" has been read by Catholic exegetes as an act of divine mercy within judgment. God does not annihilate Egypt but humbles it, and the forty-year period evokes Israel's own purifying wilderness wandering. Catholic spiritual theology recognizes in this pattern the pedagogy of divine discipline.

The chapter's concluding note about Nebuchadnezzar receiving Egypt as compensation reveals what Catholic theology calls divine providence operating through secondary causes. Even pagan rulers serve God's purposes without knowing it -- a principle that Augustine developed extensively in his theology of history.
