## The Threefold Judgment

Ezekiel 5 commands the prophet to shave his head and divide the hair into three portions — burned, struck with the sword, and scattered — with a small remnant bound in his garment. The chapter then delivers the theological rationale: Jerusalem has been more wicked than the nations, violating God's statutes despite receiving them as a gift.

Catholic interpretation emphasizes the principle of proportional accountability. Those who receive greater grace bear greater responsibility. This principle, articulated by the Fathers and developed in Scholastic theology, undergirds the Catholic understanding of the relationship between sacramental privilege and moral obligation. The baptized who persist in grave sin face sterner judgment than those who never received the sacraments.

The remnant bound in the prophet's garment has been read in Catholic tradition as a figure of the faithful preserved within the Church even during periods of widespread corruption. The Church, though containing sinners, always preserves a holy remnant — an ecclesiological principle that distinguishes Catholic theology from Donatist perfectionism. Yet even some hairs from the remnant are cast into the fire (5:4), a sober warning that membership in the visible community provides no automatic guarantee of salvation.
