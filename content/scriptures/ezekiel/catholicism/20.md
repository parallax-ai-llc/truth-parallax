## The History of Rebellion

Ezekiel 20 recounts Israel's history of persistent rebellion from Egypt through the wilderness to the land, structured around the repeated pattern of divine gift, human rebellion, and divine restraint for the sake of God's name. The chapter concludes with a promise of future purification.

Catholic theology finds in this chapter a comprehensive theology of salvation history. The pattern of grace, rebellion, and patient divine forbearance maps onto the broader narrative that Catholic theology traces from creation through fall, covenant, exile, and redemption. The emphasis that God acts "for the sake of my name" (20:9, 14, 22) resonates with the Catholic understanding that God's glory — not human merit — is the ultimate purpose of salvation history.

The controversial verse 20:25 — "I gave them statutes that were not good and rules by which they could not have life" — has received extensive Catholic commentary. Thomas Aquinas, following earlier interpreters, understood this as referring to God's permissive will allowing Israel to follow their own corrupt inclinations as a form of judicial punishment. Others have read it as referring to the ceremonial law that, while divinely given, was never intended to bestow life in itself but rather to point toward the grace of Christ. This interpretation aligns with the Catholic understanding of the Old Law as a *pedagogue* (Galatians 3:24) rather than a means of justification.
