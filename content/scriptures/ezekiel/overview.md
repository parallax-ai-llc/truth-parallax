## Overview

Ezekiel is one of the major prophetic books of the Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament. Its Hebrew name, *Yechezkel* ("God strengthens"), reflects the prophetic call of its author, a priest exiled to Babylon in 597 BCE. The book is distinguished by its extraordinary visionary experiences, elaborate symbolic actions, and detailed architectural visions.

## Structure

Ezekiel is traditionally divided into four main sections:

1. **Oracles of Judgment against Judah and Jerusalem** (Chapters 1–24): Visions of God's glory, symbolic acts, and pronouncements of doom on a rebellious nation
2. **Oracles against Foreign Nations** (Chapters 25–32): Judgments on Ammon, Moab, Edom, Philistia, Tyre, Sidon, and Egypt
3. **Oracles of Restoration** (Chapters 33–39): The watchman's renewed commission, the shepherd passage, the valley of dry bones, and the Gog and Magog prophecy
4. **The New Temple Vision** (Chapters 40–48): A detailed architectural and liturgical blueprint for a restored temple and land

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Ezekiel holds a distinctive place across multiple religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: A complex text whose opening vision (the Merkabah) became foundational for Jewish mysticism; restrictions historically placed on public reading of chapters 1 and 10
- **Christianity**: Key text for Christological and eschatological interpretation, especially the shepherd imagery, the new covenant, and the dry bones vision
- **Islam**: Ezekiel (identified by some scholars with Dhul-Kifl) appears in Islamic tradition; the dry bones vision parallels Quranic narratives
- **Gnosticism**: The throne-chariot vision and the glory departing the temple offered rich material for Gnostic cosmological speculation

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Glory of God (*Kavod*) | The visible, mobile manifestation of God's presence — departing and returning |
| Judgment and Exile | The theological explanation of Jerusalem's destruction as divine judgment |
| Individual Responsibility | Each person bears their own sin (chapter 18) — a major ethical development |
| Restoration and New Covenant | God's promise to gather, cleanse, and renew Israel with a new heart and spirit |
| Divine Sovereignty | God acts "so that they shall know that I am the LORD" — repeated over 60 times |
