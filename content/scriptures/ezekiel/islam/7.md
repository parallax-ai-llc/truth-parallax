## The End Proclaimed

Ezekiel 7 declares the finality of judgment upon the land with escalating urgency. Economic, social, and political structures collapse. Silver and gold become worthless; violence has reached its full measure.

Islamic eschatological thought finds resonance in this chapter. The Quranic descriptions of the Day of Judgment similarly portray the total collapse of worldly values: "The Day when wealth and children will be of no benefit, except for one who comes to Allah with a sound heart" (Quran 26:88-89). The worthlessness of silver and gold in Ezekiel 7 anticipates this eschatological reality.

Al-Ghazali, in his reflections on death and the afterlife (*Kitab Dhikr al-Mawt*), drew on the prophetic tradition's descriptions of worldly collapse to encourage detachment from material wealth. The prophetic witness — whether Ezekiel, Muhammad, or others — consistently warns that worldly security is illusory and that true security lies only in right relationship with God. The formula "then they shall know that I am the LORD" finds its Islamic parallel in the recognition that all human pretension is stripped away before divine reality.
