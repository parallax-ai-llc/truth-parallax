## The Riddle of the Eagles

Ezekiel 17 presents a political allegory of two eagles and a vine, then concludes with a messianic promise. Islamic interpretation engages primarily with the theological principles rather than the specific historical identifications.

The condemnation of oath-breaking (17:16-19) resonates strongly with Islamic ethics. The Quran commands: "Fulfill the covenant of Allah when you have taken it, and do not break oaths after their confirmation" (16:91). In Islamic jurisprudence, the sanctity of oaths and contracts is fundamental. Zedekiah's violation of his oath to Nebuchadnezzar — sworn in God's name — constitutes both political treachery and theological offense. Islamic law (*fiqh*) treats oath-breaking with comparable severity, requiring expiation (*kaffarah*) for broken oaths (Quran 5:89).

The messianic oracle (17:22-24) — God planting a tender sprig that becomes a great cedar — is interpreted in Islamic tradition within the broader framework of prophetic succession. God continually raises up new prophetic figures to guide humanity, culminating in Muhammad as the seal of the prophets. The great cedar sheltering all birds represents the universal community of faith under divine guidance.
