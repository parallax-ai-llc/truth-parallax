## Sanctuary in Exile

Ezekiel 11 combines judgment on Jerusalem's leaders with a promise to the exiles: God has been "a sanctuary in small measure" to them and will give them a new heart and spirit. The glory departs the city entirely.

The concept of God as a sanctuary wherever the faithful are scattered resonates with the Islamic teaching that the entire earth is a place of worship. A hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari reports the Prophet Muhammad saying: "The earth has been made for me a place of prostration and a means of purification." Islam universalizes the principle that Ezekiel introduces in crisis: God's presence is not confined to a building but accompanies the faithful wherever they go.

The promise of a new heart replacing the heart of stone (11:19) connects to the Quranic concept of *qalb salim* — the "sound heart" that is the prerequisite for salvation on the Day of Judgment (Quran 26:89). Islamic spirituality (*tasawwuf*) devotes extensive attention to the purification of the heart, understanding it as the seat of faith and the organ of divine knowledge. The transformation Ezekiel promises is, in Islamic terms, the replacement of a heart hardened by sin with one receptive to divine guidance.
