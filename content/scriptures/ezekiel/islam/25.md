## Divine Justice Among the Nations

Ezekiel 25 pronounces oracles against Ammon, Moab, Edom, and Philistia. Islamic interpretation approaches these oracles through the Quranic principle that God holds all nations accountable for their conduct. The Quran declares, "We have made you into nations and tribes so that you may know one another" (49:13), and the prophetic denunciation of hostile nations affirms that relations between peoples are subject to divine moral governance.

Classical Muslim commentators who engaged with these passages through the Isra'iliyyat tradition noted the parallel with Quranic accounts of destroyed nations -- 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lot. Each was destroyed for specific moral failures, just as each nation in Ezekiel 25 receives judgment proportional to its offense. The principle of divine justice operating among all peoples, not only the covenant community, is central to Islamic theology.

The chapter reinforces the Islamic understanding that no nation exists outside God's moral order. The Quran's repeated references to the ruins of destroyed civilizations (30:9, 40:21) serve the same function as Ezekiel's oracles: they remind the living that divine judgment is real, inevitable, and perfectly calibrated to the offense.
