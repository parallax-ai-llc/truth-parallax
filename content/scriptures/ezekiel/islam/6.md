## Destruction of the High Places

Ezekiel 6 pronounces judgment on the mountains of Israel where idolatrous worship occurs. God will destroy the altars and scatter bones around abandoned shrines. The condemnation of high-place worship resonates powerfully with Islamic theology's central concern: the absolute prohibition of *shirk* (associating partners with God).

The Quran repeatedly condemns idol worship and the veneration of sacred sites apart from God's command. The parallel to the pre-Islamic Arabian context is direct: the Ka'bah had been surrounded by hundreds of idols before Muhammad's conquest of Makkah and their destruction. Islamic commentators drawing on Isra'iliyyat traditions saw in Ezekiel's condemnation of high places a precedent for Muhammad's purification of the Ka'bah.

The promise that the remnant will "remember" God among the nations (6:9) parallels the Quranic concept of *dhikr* (remembrance of God). Quran 13:28 declares: "Surely in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest." The capacity to remember God in exile — without temple, without homeland — anticipates the Islamic principle that worship of God is not bound to any particular place but is a universal human capacity and obligation.
