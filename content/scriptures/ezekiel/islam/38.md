## The Eschatological Battle

Ezekiel 38's prophecy of Gog's invasion connects to the Islamic eschatological tradition of Ya'juj and Ma'juj (Gog and Magog). The Quran references them in two passages: Surah 18:94-99 (Dhul-Qarnayn's wall containing them) and Surah 21:96-97 (their release as a sign of the approaching Hour). Islamic tradition regards the emergence of Gog and Magog as one of the major signs of the end times.

Classical Muslim commentators, including al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir, and al-Qurtubi, drew on both Quranic references and Isra'iliyyat traditions to elaborate the eschatological scenario. The invasion of a restored and peaceful community by overwhelming hostile forces, followed by decisive divine intervention, matches the pattern described in both Ezekiel and Islamic eschatological hadith.

The theological principle underlying the Gog prophecy -- that even the most threatening forces serve God's purposes -- corresponds to the Islamic affirmation of divine qadar (decree). Nothing occurs outside God's knowledge, permission, and purpose. The Quran states, "Say: Nothing will happen to us except what God has decreed for us; He is our protector" (9:51). Ezekiel 38 illustrates this principle on a cosmic scale.
