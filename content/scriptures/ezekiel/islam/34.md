## The Shepherd and the Flock

Ezekiel 34's condemnation of false shepherds and promise of divine shepherding resonates with Islamic teachings on just governance. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "Each of you is a shepherd, and each of you is responsible for his flock" (Sahih al-Bukhari). The prophetic condemnation of rulers who exploit their people rather than serving them is a central concern of Islamic political theology.

The promise that God Himself will shepherd His people reflects the Islamic understanding of God as al-Ra'uf (the Compassionate) and al-Rabb (the Lord/Sustainer). The Quran's first chapter opens with "All praise belongs to God, Lord of all the worlds" -- the divine shepherding that encompasses all creation. The failure of human rulers to reflect this divine quality constitutes a betrayal of their trust (amanah).

The covenant of peace (34:25-31), with its vision of security and abundance, corresponds to the Quranic description of the righteous community that God blesses: "If only the people of the towns had believed and been mindful of God, We would have opened up for them blessings from heaven and earth" (7:96). The prophetic vision of blessed community is conditional on faithfulness to God -- a principle that Islamic ethics applies to all levels of society.
