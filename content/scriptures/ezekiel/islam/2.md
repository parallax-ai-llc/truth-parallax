## The Prophet's Mission

Ezekiel 2 records the commissioning of the prophet to speak to a rebellious people. Islamic theology affirms that God sends prophets (*anbiya'*) and messengers (*rusul*) to every nation (Quran 16:36), and Ezekiel's commission follows this universal pattern. The prophet does not choose his role but is chosen by God and empowered by the Spirit.

The description of Israel as "impudent and stubborn" resonates with the Quranic portrayal of Bani Isra'il as a people who frequently rejected their prophets. Quran 2:87 states: "Whenever a messenger came to you with what your souls did not desire, you were arrogant — some you denied and some you killed." Islamic commentators read Ezekiel's difficult commission as part of this broader pattern.

The scroll of lamentation and woe represents the prophetic burden. In Islamic understanding, the messages entrusted to prophets are not of their own making but divinely originated. The prophet is a vessel and transmitter, not the author of revelation. This principle underlies the Islamic doctrine of *wahy* (revelation) and the insistence that the Quran is God's word, not Muhammad's composition.
