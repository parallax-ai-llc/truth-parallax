## Sacred Time and Governance

Ezekiel 46's liturgical calendar, with its sabbaths, new moons, and festival observances, resonates with the Islamic structuring of time through the five daily prayers, the weekly Jumu'ah, the monthly fasting of Ramadan, and the annual Hajj. Both traditions affirm that time must be sanctified through regular worship -- that the rhythm of prayer structures life around awareness of God.

The regulations limiting the prince's power over the people's property correspond to Islamic principles of governance. The Quran declares, "O you who believe, do not consume one another's wealth unjustly" (4:29), and the caliphal tradition established that rulers must protect the property rights of their subjects. The second caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab was renowned for this principle, famously holding himself accountable for every citizen's welfare.

The year of liberty mentioned in 46:17 resonates with Islamic teachings on the freeing of slaves and the remission of debts. The Quran repeatedly encourages the freeing of slaves as an act of supreme merit (90:13), and Islamic jurisprudence developed extensive rules for the just treatment and eventual liberation of bonded persons. The prophetic vision of periodic economic reset reflects a shared concern for preventing the permanent concentration of wealth and power.
