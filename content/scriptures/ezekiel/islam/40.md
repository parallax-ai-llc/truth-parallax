## The Vision of the Sacred House

Ezekiel 40's detailed temple vision resonates with the Islamic reverence for sacred architecture. The Ka'bah in Makkah, the Prophet's Mosque in Madinah, and the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem all embody the principle that spaces dedicated to God's worship must be constructed with care, intentionality, and reverence. The meticulous measurements communicate divine concern for the ordering of sacred space.

Islamic interpretation approaches Ezekiel's temple vision with awareness that the Temple Mount (al-Haram al-Sharif) is the third holiest site in Islam. The Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey (Isra) brought him to the "farthest mosque" (al-Masjid al-Aqsa), connecting Islamic sacred geography directly to the site of Ezekiel's vision. The chapter thus touches on a location of profound shared significance.

The angelic guide who measures the temple recalls the Islamic tradition of angels as instruments of divine revelation and governance. The Quran describes angels as "messengers with wings -- two, three, or four" (35:1), who serve God in the ordering and maintenance of creation. The meticulous order of the temple reflects the perfect order of God's creation itself.
