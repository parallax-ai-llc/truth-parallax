## Overview

Ezekiel is not explicitly named in the Quran, but Islamic tradition has frequently identified him with Dhul-Kifl, a figure mentioned twice in the Quran (21:85-86, 38:48) among the righteous. Some scholars dispute this identification, but it remains the majority opinion in classical tafsir. Beyond this identification, several of Ezekiel's key narratives — particularly the vision of the dry bones — have direct Quranic parallels that Islamic commentators have explored extensively.

## Relationship to the Quran

Key Ezekiel narratives with Quranic parallels:

| Ezekiel Narrative | Quranic Parallel | Key Passage |
|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Dry bones vision (37:1-14) | Resurrection of a dead community | Al-Baqarah 2:243, 259 |
| God as Shepherd (34:11-16) | God as guide and protector | Various |
| Judgment on idolatry (8-11) | Condemnation of *shirk* | Throughout the Quran |
| Individual responsibility (18) | No soul bears another's burden | Al-An'am 6:164, An-Najm 53:38 |
| Vision of God's glory (1) | God's majesty beyond comprehension | Ash-Shura 42:11 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Tawhid and Divine Transcendence
The elaborate vision of God's glory in Ezekiel 1 is read through Islamic theology as either a vision of angels or as evidence that the original text has been altered (*tahrif*). Islam insists on divine transcendence: "There is nothing like unto Him" (Quran 42:11).

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
Any passage presenting prophets in morally questionable situations is reinterpreted or attributed to textual alteration.

### Isra'iliyyat
Classical commentators like al-Tabari and al-Tha'labi incorporated Ezekiel's narratives as *Isra'iliyyat* — traditions from Jewish sources evaluated against Quranic norms.

## Theological Emphases

- **Resurrection**: The dry bones vision is the most significant Ezekiel passage in Islamic thought, confirming the Quranic teaching of bodily resurrection
- **Anti-Idolatry**: Ezekiel's condemnation of idolatrous worship resonates deeply with the Quranic campaign against *shirk*
- **Divine Justice**: The principle that each person bears their own sin (Ezekiel 18) aligns with Quran 6:164
- **Exile as Divine Punishment**: The destruction of Jerusalem confirms the Quranic principle that God punishes nations that reject His messengers
