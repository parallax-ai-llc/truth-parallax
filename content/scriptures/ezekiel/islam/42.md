## The Boundaries of the Sacred

Ezekiel 42's priestly chambers and the enclosure wall separating holy from common resonate with the Islamic concept of haram and halal -- the sacred and the permissible, the restricted and the open. Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) is fundamentally organized around these categories, and Ezekiel's architectural embodiment of the principle confirms its deep prophetic roots.

The priestly requirement to change garments before entering common areas reflects a principle similar to the Islamic understanding of ihram -- the sacred state entered for hajj, which involves wearing distinctive garments and observing specific restrictions. The transition between sacred and ordinary states requires physical markers that reflect inner spiritual realities.

The perfect square of the temple enclosure communicates completeness and divine order. In Islamic architecture, the Ka'bah is also cuboid (its name means "cube"), and the emphasis on geometric perfection in Islamic sacred architecture reflects the same theological conviction: God's dwelling is characterized by order, harmony, and intentional design.
