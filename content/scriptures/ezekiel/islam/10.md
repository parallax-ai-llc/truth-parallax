## The Departure of Divine Presence

Ezekiel 10 narrates the departure of God's glory from the temple, mounted on the chariot-throne of the cherubim. Coals of fire are scattered over Jerusalem as the glory pauses at the east gate before withdrawing.

Islamic theology does not accept the concept of divine presence dwelling in a physical structure. God is *al-Samad* (the Self-Sufficient) and does not require a house or abode. However, Islamic tradition does recognize sacred spaces as places of particular blessing (*barakah*) — the Ka'bah, the Prophet's Mosque, and al-Aqsa among them. The withdrawal of blessing from a corrupted sacred space resonates with the Islamic principle that divine favor (*tawfiq*) can be withdrawn from communities that persist in disobedience.

The scattering of coals over the city has been connected by some Islamic commentators to the general prophetic theme of cities destroyed by divine decree. The Quran repeatedly references cities that were destroyed — "How many a city We have destroyed" (7:4) — as warnings to contemporary audiences. The glory departing and fire falling represent the transition from a place of blessing to a place of judgment, a pattern that Islam treats as universal and exemplary.
