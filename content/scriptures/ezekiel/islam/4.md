## Signs of Coming Judgment

Ezekiel 4 describes symbolic actions representing the siege of Jerusalem: a brick siege model, extended periods of lying on the prophet's sides, and rationed, impure food. Islamic interpretation reads these sign-acts as confirming the Quranic principle that God warns before He punishes (Quran 17:15: "We would not punish until We had sent a messenger").

The duration of the prophet's suffering — lying immobile for hundreds of days — illustrates the patience (*sabr*) required of prophets. Islamic tradition emphasizes that prophets endure greater trials than ordinary people. A hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari reports that the Prophet Muhammad said: "The most severely tested people are the prophets, then the next best, then the next best."

The rationed food and conditions of ritual impurity also raise questions relevant to Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*). The principle of *darurah* (necessity) in Islamic law permits what is normally prohibited when survival is at stake. Ezekiel's protest against ritual defilement and God's accommodation (allowing cow dung instead of human dung) illustrates a divine sensitivity to the prophet's conscientious objection — a pattern Islamic scholars note as consistent with God's mercy.
