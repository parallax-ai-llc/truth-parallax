## The Sacred Interior

Ezekiel 41's description of the temple interior, with its graduated holiness and decorative program, resonates with the Islamic understanding that spaces of worship should reflect divine beauty and order. The Quran declares, "God is beautiful and loves beauty" (Sahih Muslim), and Islamic architectural tradition has expressed this through geometric patterns, calligraphy, and arabesques that adorn mosques.

The cherubim and palm trees decorating the temple walls, while not directly parallel to Islamic decorative practice (which avoids representational imagery), share the underlying principle of adorning sacred space with symbols of God's creation. Islamic geometric art achieves through abstract patterns what Ezekiel's temple achieves through figurative motifs: the communication of divine order, harmony, and beauty.

The progressive restriction of access -- from outer court to inner sanctuary -- corresponds to the Islamic understanding of degrees of sacred proximity. The Haram (sacred precinct) of Makkah restricts access to Muslims, and within it, the Ka'bah itself represents the most sacred point. This principle of graduated holiness reflects the universal theological insight that approach to the divine requires preparation and worthiness.
