## The Great Tree Brought Low

Ezekiel 31's allegory of Assyria as a magnificent cedar felled for its pride resonates with multiple Quranic themes. The image of the great tree sheltering all creatures beneath its branches echoes the Quranic parable of the good word as a good tree (14:24-27), while the cedar's fall illustrates the fate of those whose greatness leads to self-deification rather than gratitude.

Islamic theology identifies shukr (gratitude) as the proper response to divine blessings and kufr (ingratitude/disbelief) as the fundamental sin. The cedar's transition from legitimate greatness to self-glorifying pride maps this trajectory precisely. The Quran warns, "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you; but if you are ungrateful, indeed, My punishment is severe" (14:7).

The descent to Sheol reinforces the Islamic teaching on the reality of punishment after death. While Islamic eschatology differs from the Old Testament's conception of Sheol, both traditions affirm that death is not extinction but a transition to a state of divine accounting. The barzakh (intermediate state) in Islamic theology, where the deceased experience a foretaste of their ultimate destination, corresponds functionally to the differentiated Sheol described here.
