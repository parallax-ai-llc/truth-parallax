## The Unfaithful City

Ezekiel 16 presents the extended allegory of Jerusalem as a foundling raised to splendor by God, only to turn to systematic unfaithfulness. Islamic commentary engages with this chapter cautiously, noting both its theological resonance and its points of divergence from Islamic sensibility.

The theological structure — God's gratuitous grace, human ingratitude, and divine judgment — maps onto the Quranic narrative of nations that received divine blessing and then turned to corruption. The pattern of *ni'mah* (divine blessing), *kufr al-ni'mah* (ingratitude for blessing), and *'adhab* (punishment) is foundational to Quranic theology. Surah Ibrahim (14:28-30) describes those who "exchanged the favor of Allah for disbelief and settled their people in the abode of ruin."

The chapter's graphic imagery of sexual unfaithfulness as a metaphor for idolatry reflects a prophetic tradition that Islam shares to some extent — the Quran also uses the language of betrayal and corruption for those who abandon monotheism. However, Islamic commentary tends to apply such imagery more abstractly, emphasizing the theological principle of *shirk* (associating partners with God) rather than dwelling on the metaphorical details.
