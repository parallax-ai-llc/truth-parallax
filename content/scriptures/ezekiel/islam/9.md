## Divine Discrimination in Judgment

Ezekiel 9 describes the marking of those who grieve over abominations and the execution of all who lack the mark. Judgment begins at the sanctuary and spares neither young nor old.

Islamic theology affirms the principle of divine discrimination in judgment — God distinguishes between the righteous and the wicked. The Quran states: "Shall We then treat the Muslims like the criminals? What is wrong with you? How do you judge?" (68:35-36). The marking of the righteous in Ezekiel 9 illustrates this principle: divine judgment is not indiscriminate but precisely targeted.

The criterion for preservation — grieving over abominations rather than merely abstaining from them — resonates with the Islamic obligation of *nahy 'an al-munkar* (forbidding evil). Islamic jurisprudence distinguishes between levels of this duty: with the hand (for those in authority), with the tongue (for those who can speak), and with the heart (the minimum for all believers). Those who grieve in their hearts over evil, even when powerless to prevent it, fulfill the minimum requirement of faith. A hadith in Sahih Muslim records the Prophet Muhammad saying: "Whoever among you sees evil, let him change it with his hand; if he cannot, then with his tongue; if he cannot, then with his heart — and that is the weakest faith."
