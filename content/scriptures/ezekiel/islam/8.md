## Corruption of Sacred Space

Ezekiel 8 transports the prophet to the Jerusalem temple, where he witnesses four scenes of escalating idolatry: the image of jealousy, elders worshiping images in secret, women weeping for Tammuz, and men worshiping the sun.

Islamic commentary reads this chapter as a powerful confirmation of the Quranic theme that religious institutions are vulnerable to corruption. The Quran records that even the Ka'bah — the house built by Ibrahim — was filled with idols that had to be removed. Ezekiel's vision of the temple's internal corruption provides a parallel case: sacred spaces can become sites of the very idolatry they were meant to oppose.

The elders' claim that "the LORD does not see us" (8:12) represents the attitude the Quran calls *ghafla* (heedlessness) — the delusion that God is unaware of human action. Multiple Quranic verses counter this: "He knows the secret and what is yet more hidden" (20:7). Islamic theology insists on God's absolute omniscience as a foundation of moral accountability. The temple corruption demonstrates that institutional religious authority provides no cover from divine judgment.
