## The Resurrection of the Bones

Ezekiel 37's vision of dry bones restored to life holds particular significance for Islamic theology, which regards bodily resurrection as a central article of faith. The Quran addresses doubters directly: "Does man think that We will not assemble his bones? Yes, We are able to proportion even his fingertips" (75:3-4). Ezekiel's vision provides prophetic confirmation of God's power to reconstitute the dead.

Al-Tabari and other classical commentators referenced this passage in discussions of resurrection, noting it as an example from the Israelite prophetic tradition that confirms the Quranic teaching. The Quran's own accounts of resurrection (such as the story of the slain man revived by touching the sacrificed cow in 2:72-73, or the man who slept for a hundred years in 2:259) share Ezekiel's emphasis on the tangible, physical nature of God's life-giving power.

The reunification of the two sticks -- Judah and Israel becoming one -- resonates with the Quranic call for unity among believers: "Hold firmly to the rope of God all together and do not become divided" (3:103). Islamic theology recognizes the recurring prophetic theme that division among God's people is a consequence of sin, and that restoration includes reunification under divinely appointed leadership.
