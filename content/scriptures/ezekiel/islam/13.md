## False Prophets Condemned

Ezekiel 13 condemns prophets who speak from their own imagination and prophetesses who practice divination. Their message of false peace is compared to whitewash on a crumbling wall.

Islamic theology categorically condemns false prophecy (*kizb 'ala Allah* — lying about God). The Quran declares: "Who is more unjust than one who invents a lie about Allah?" (6:93). The tradition of verifying prophetic claims is well established in Islamic jurisprudence: a true prophet is confirmed by miracles (*mu'jizat*), consistency with previous revelation, and moral character.

The image of whitewashing an unstable wall has parallels in Islamic ethical discourse. Al-Ghazali used similar metaphors to describe scholars who flatter rulers rather than speaking truth — a betrayal of the scholarly vocation analogous to the prophetic betrayal condemned by Ezekiel. The Islamic intellectual tradition places great weight on the scholar's independence and willingness to speak truth to power (*kalimat haqq 'inda sultan ja'ir*), identified in a hadith as "the greatest jihad."
