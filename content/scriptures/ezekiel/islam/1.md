## The Vision of Divine Majesty

Ezekiel 1 describes an elaborate vision of God's throne surrounded by four living creatures, wheels within wheels, and a figure of radiant glory. Islamic interpretation approaches this chapter through the lens of absolute divine transcendence (*tanzih*). The Quran declares, "There is nothing like unto Him" (42:11), and any visual depiction of God — even in prophetic vision — is understood as a manifestation of angelic or created realities, not the divine essence itself.

Classical Muslim commentators who engaged with this passage through Isra'iliyyat traditions interpreted the living creatures as angels, comparable to the bearers of the Throne (*hamalat al-'arsh*) mentioned in Quran 40:7 and 69:17. Al-Tabari recorded traditions describing eight angels bearing the divine Throne, and some commentators connected these to Ezekiel's four creatures. The wheels and eyes were understood as symbols of divine omniscience and providential governance.

The theological principle at stake is fundamental to Islamic theology: God communicates with prophets through intermediaries (angels, particularly Jibril) rather than through direct visual manifestation. Whatever Ezekiel saw, Islamic interpretation insists, was not the divine essence but a created manifestation of divine power and authority.
