## Justice and Worship

Ezekiel 45's integration of just measures with liturgical prescriptions resonates deeply with Islamic theology, which refuses to separate worship (ibadah) from social ethics (mu'amalat). The Quran states, "God commands justice and kindness" (16:90), and the prophetic sunna consistently links prayer and fasting with honest dealing and care for the vulnerable.

The demand for honest weights and measures (45:10-12) corresponds directly to multiple Quranic injunctions: "Give full measure when you measure, and weigh with a balance that is straight" (17:35). The people of Shu'ayb in the Quran (7:85) were destroyed for commercial dishonesty, and the prophetic tradition regards fraud in business as a grave sin. Ezekiel's integration of this concern with temple legislation confirms that Islamic ethics' unification of worship and commerce has deep prophetic roots.

The prince's obligation to provide for communal worship reflects the Islamic concept of waqf (religious endowment) -- the dedication of resources to sustain mosques, schools, and charitable institutions. Islamic civilization developed the waqf system into a sophisticated instrument of public welfare, embodying the prophetic principle that leadership entails responsibility for the community's spiritual and material well-being.
