## The Threefold Fate

Ezekiel 5 records the symbolic shaving of the prophet's head and the division of his hair into three portions — burned, struck, and scattered — representing the fate of Jerusalem. A small remnant is preserved but even some of these face judgment.

Islamic interpretation reads this passage as confirming the Quranic narrative of divine judgment upon nations that reject their prophets. The destruction of previous civilizations — 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lot, the people of Shu'ayb — follows a consistent pattern: warning, rejection, and punishment. Jerusalem's threefold fate exemplifies this pattern.

The remnant theology implicit in the passage aligns with the Quranic concept that God always preserves believers through judgment: "We saved those who believed" (Quran 41:18). The survival of a remnant demonstrates both divine justice (the wicked perish) and divine mercy (the faithful are preserved). Islamic eschatology extends this principle to the Day of Judgment itself, where the faithful will be separated from the unfaithful and granted salvation.
