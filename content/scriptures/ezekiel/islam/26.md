## The Fall of a Proud City

Ezekiel 26 announces the destruction of Tyre for its arrogant response to Jerusalem's fall. Islamic interpretation reads this oracle through the Quranic denunciation of istikbar (arrogance) -- the refusal to acknowledge one's dependence on God. Tyre's sin is treating a sacred catastrophe as a commercial opportunity, a failure of moral perception that the Quran identifies as a consequence of worldly preoccupation.

Al-Tabari and other classical commentators noted the similarity between Tyre's fate and the Quranic descriptions of cities destroyed for their corruption. The Quran states, "How many a city We have destroyed that was unjust, and We raised up after them other peoples" (21:11). Tyre joins the long list of prosperous cities whose worldly success could not protect them from divine judgment.

The prophetic emphasis on Tyre being scraped to bare rock resonates with the Islamic concept of ibra (instructive example). The ruins of destroyed civilizations serve as visible reminders of God's power and justice. The Quran frequently commands, "Travel through the land and see the fate of those who came before" (3:137) -- a practice of moral reflection that Ezekiel's oracle invites.
