## The Beginning of the End

Ezekiel 24 marks the beginning of Jerusalem's siege with the parable of the corroded pot and the death of the prophet's wife. The prophet's enforced silence becomes a prophetic sign.

Islamic tradition treats the fall of Jerusalem as part of the broader pattern of divine judgment upon communities that reject their prophets. The Quran references the destruction of the temple (*al-masjid*) in Surah al-Isra' (17:4-8), where Bani Isra'il are told they will cause corruption twice and that God will send servants to punish them. Islamic commentators connected the Babylonian destruction to the first of these two corruptions.

The death of Ezekiel's wife and the prophet's silent grief present a model of *sabr* (patience) and *tawakkul* (trust in God) under extreme personal loss. Islamic tradition holds that prophets endure the greatest trials, and their response of patient submission to God's will (*rida*) serves as a model for the Muslim community. The Quran's own narrative of Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son Isma'il (37:102-107) represents the supreme example of prophetic submission. Ezekiel's silent obedience in the face of personal tragedy follows this pattern of prophetic surrender to divine purpose.
