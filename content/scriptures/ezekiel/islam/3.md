## The Watchman's Duty

Ezekiel 3 establishes the prophet as a watchman responsible for warning his people. This concept resonates deeply with the Islamic understanding of prophetic responsibility. The Quran designates Muhammad as a "warner" (*nadhir*) and a "bearer of glad tidings" (*bashir*) — roles that parallel Ezekiel's watchman commission.

The principle that the watchman who warns is absolved of responsibility, while the one who fails to warn bears guilt, aligns with the Islamic concept of *tabligh* — the obligation to deliver the message faithfully. Every Muslim bears a degree of this responsibility through the duty of *amr bil-ma'ruf wa nahy 'an al-munkar* (commanding good and forbidding evil), which extends the watchman principle to the entire community.

Al-Ghazali, in his *Ihya' Ulum al-Din*, elaborated on the conditions and ethics of communal moral responsibility, drawing on precisely this type of prophetic precedent. The obligation to warn is not unlimited — it requires wisdom, compassion, and awareness of context — but neither can it be abandoned without spiritual consequence.
