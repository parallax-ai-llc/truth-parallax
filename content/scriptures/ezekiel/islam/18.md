## Each Soul for Itself

Ezekiel 18 is perhaps the chapter of Ezekiel most directly resonant with Quranic theology. The principle that "the soul who sins shall die" and that each person is judged according to their own conduct aligns precisely with the Quranic declaration: "Every soul earns only against itself, and no bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (6:164).

Islamic theology explicitly rejects the concept of inherited sin. Adam sinned but repented, and God forgave him (Quran 2:37). No subsequent generation bears Adam's guilt. Ezekiel 18's refutation of the "sour grapes" proverb — that children suffer for their parents' sins — is read in Islamic context as confirming this fundamental principle. Each person stands before God on their own merits and demerits.

The chapter's emphasis on the possibility of repentance is equally central to Islamic soteriology. God says through Ezekiel: "I have no pleasure in the death of anyone who dies... so turn, and live" (18:32). The Quran echoes this sentiment: "Say: O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins" (39:53). Both traditions insist that God desires repentance over punishment, and that the door of *teshuvah*/*tawbah* remains open as long as life endures.
