## Pharaoh's False Claim

Ezekiel 29 depicts Pharaoh as a monster boasting, "My Nile is my own; I made it for myself." Islamic interpretation identifies this as the quintessential expression of shirk -- claiming creative power that belongs to God alone. The Quran recounts that Pharaoh declared to his people, "I am your lord most high" (79:24), and God destroyed him as a sign for all subsequent generations.

The Quranic Pharaoh is one of the most prominent examples of tyrannical disbelief. His refusal to acknowledge God despite clear signs, his persecution of the believers, and his ultimate destruction by drowning constitute a paradigmatic narrative of prophetic confrontation with unjust power. Ezekiel's oracle against the pharaoh of his own era extends this pattern, demonstrating its recurrence across history.

The forty-year desolation of Egypt, followed by its diminished restoration, illustrates the Islamic principle that God's punishment may include humiliation rather than annihilation. The Quran describes how God allows nations to continue in reduced circumstances as a mercy and a warning: "If God were to take people to task for their wrongdoing, He would not leave upon the earth any creature, but He defers them until a stated term" (16:61).
