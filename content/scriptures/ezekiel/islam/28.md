## Pride and the Fall

Ezekiel 28's oracle against the king of Tyre, describing a glorious being cast down from the divine mountain for pride, resonates with Islamic teachings on the fall of Iblis (Satan). The Quran recounts that Iblis refused to prostrate before Adam out of pride, declaring, "I am better than he" (7:12). The king of Tyre's self-deification -- "I am a god" (28:2) -- echoes this primordial arrogance.

Islamic theology distinguishes carefully between the ultimate sin of shirk (associating partners with God) and the related sin of kibr (pride). The king of Tyre commits both: claiming divine status constitutes shirk, while the underlying attitude constitutes kibr. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "He who has in his heart the weight of a mustard seed of pride shall not enter Paradise" (Sahih Muslim).

The Edenic imagery in the second oracle (28:11-19) connects to the Quranic account of the Garden and the fall. While Islamic theology does not typically read this passage as referring to Iblis directly, the thematic parallel is unmistakable: a being granted beauty, wisdom, and proximity to the divine who is cast down for the sin of self-exaltation. The moral lesson is universal: pride invites divine retribution regardless of the offender's original station.
