## The Worthless Vine

Ezekiel 15 compares Jerusalem to a vine whose wood is useless — fit only for burning. The parable strips away any sense of inherent superiority based on election.

Islamic theology finds in this passage a confirmation of its own teaching about the conditional nature of divine favor. The Quran warns Muslims against complacency: "O you who have believed, whoever of you should revert from his religion — Allah will bring forth a people He will love and who will love Him" (5:54). No community possesses an unconditional guarantee of divine favor. Election — whether of Israel, the Christians, or the Muslims — carries obligations, and failure to fulfill those obligations exposes the community to judgment.

The vine metaphor resonates with the Quranic concept of *istikhlaf* — God's appointment of successive communities as stewards on earth. When one community fails in its stewardship, God replaces it with another. This is not ethnic replacement but moral succession: the criterion is faithfulness, not identity. Ezekiel's vine, useless without fruit, illustrates precisely this conditional logic.
