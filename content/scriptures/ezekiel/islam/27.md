## The Vanity of Commerce

Ezekiel 27's elaborate lament over Tyre as a magnificent ship wrecked at sea provides Islamic interpreters with a meditation on the transience of worldly wealth. The Quran warns repeatedly against the deception of material prosperity: "Know that the life of this world is but amusement and diversion and adornment and boasting to one another" (57:20). Tyre's elaborate trade networks and exotic commodities exemplify the dunya (worldly life) that distracts from devotion to God.

The detailed catalogue of trade goods has interested Muslim scholars for its historical and geographical value. Medieval Islamic geographers and historians, including al-Mas'udi and al-Idrisi, drew on biblical and extra-biblical sources to reconstruct the commercial networks of the ancient world, and passages like Ezekiel 27 contributed to this scholarly tradition.

The lament's conclusion -- universal mourning at Tyre's destruction -- illustrates the Islamic concept that worldly attachments produce grief. The Quran teaches that "whatever you have been given is but a passing enjoyment of the worldly life, but that which is with God is better and more lasting" (42:36). The merchants who weep over Tyre's fall are mourning the loss of dunya; the believer recognizes that God's judgment reveals the true value of things.
