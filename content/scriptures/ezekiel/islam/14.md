## The Limits of Righteousness

Ezekiel 14 declares that even Noah, Daniel, and Job could save only themselves by their righteousness — not their children or nation. This principle aligns closely with the Quranic teaching on individual moral responsibility.

The Quran repeatedly affirms that no soul bears another's burden: "No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another" (6:164, 17:15, 35:18, 39:7, 53:38). This is one of the most frequently repeated principles in the Quran, and Ezekiel 14 provides a striking prophetic parallel. Even the most righteous individuals — figures revered across traditions — cannot transfer their merit to save others.

Islamic theology recognizes Noah (*Nuh*) and Job (*Ayyub*) as prophets; Daniel (*Danyal*) appears in Islamic tradition though not named in the Quran. Their invocation as paragons of righteousness who nevertheless cannot intercede for a corrupt nation underscores the Islamic emphasis on personal accountability. While Islam does affirm a form of prophetic intercession (*shafa'ah*) on the Day of Judgment, this intercession operates by divine permission and does not override the principle of individual responsibility.
