## Political Infidelity

Ezekiel 23 presents the allegory of two sisters — Oholah (Samaria) and Oholibah (Jerusalem) — whose political alliances with foreign powers constitute spiritual adultery. Islamic interpretation focuses on the theological equation between political trust in worldly powers and spiritual unfaithfulness to God.

The concept of *tawakkul* (reliance on God) is central to Islamic theology. While Islam permits — and indeed requires — practical action (*tying the camel*), ultimate trust must be placed in God alone. The pursuit of foreign military alliances as a substitute for trust in divine protection represents a failure of *tawakkul* that Islamic political theology has consistently warned against.

The principle of intensified judgment for Oholibah — because she witnessed her sister's fate yet continued in the same pattern — aligns with the Quranic teaching that knowledge increases responsibility. "Are those who know equal to those who do not know?" (39:9). Communities that have access to divine guidance and historical precedent bear a heavier burden than those acting in ignorance. This principle applies directly to the Muslim community, which has received the Quran as a final guidance and is therefore held to the highest standard of faithfulness.
