## A Society's Corruption

Ezekiel 22 catalogs Jerusalem's sins across every social level and concludes with the devastating observation that God sought someone to stand in the breach but found no one.

Islamic social ethics engages directly with the comprehensive nature of this indictment. The sins listed — bloodshed, idolatry, dishonoring parents, oppressing foreigners, profaning sacred times, sexual immorality, bribery — correspond closely to major prohibitions in Islamic law. The Quran addresses each of these categories, and Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) has developed detailed legal frameworks to prevent precisely the societal collapse Ezekiel describes.

The failure to find an intercessor (22:30) is read in Islamic context as illustrating the condition of a community so thoroughly corrupted that no reformer remains within it. Islamic political philosophy, from al-Mawardi's *al-Ahkam al-Sultaniyyah* to Ibn Taymiyyah's *al-Siyasah al-Shar'iyyah*, emphasizes the necessity of maintaining a class of scholars and moral leaders who can serve as the community's conscience. When this group disappears or is co-opted, societal collapse follows — a principle Ezekiel 22 illustrates with devastating clarity.
