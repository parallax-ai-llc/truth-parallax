## The Return of the Divine Presence

Ezekiel 43's account of God's glory returning to fill the temple resonates with the Islamic concept of barakah (divine blessing) that sanctifies certain places. The Quran describes the Ka'bah as "the first House established for mankind... blessed and a guidance for the worlds" (3:96). The notion that God's presence specially inhabits certain locations while remaining transcendent is shared by both traditions.

Islamic theology maintains the principle of divine tanzih (transcendence) -- God is not contained by any physical structure. Yet the tradition also affirms that certain places carry special barakah. The Prophet's statement, "One prayer in my mosque is better than a thousand prayers elsewhere" (Sahih al-Bukhari), reflects this understanding without compromising divine transcendence.

The altar consecration rituals described in Ezekiel 43 correspond functionally to the Islamic rites of consecrating a mosque. While Islamic worship does not include animal sacrifice in the liturgical setting (sacrifice being reserved for Eid al-Adha), the principle of dedicating a space to God through prescribed rituals resonates with the prophetic vision. Every mosque, from the humblest prayer room to the grandest sanctuary, is oriented toward the qiblah in Makkah, creating a global network of sacred orientation that fulfills the prophetic vision of ordered worship.
