## The Fall of the Cedar

Ezekiel 31 depicts Assyria as a magnificent cedar consigned to Sheol for its pride. Orthodox theology reads this as a parable of the spiritual life: legitimate beauty becomes destructive when it generates pride, the primordial sin that the ascetical tradition identifies as the source of all others.

The cedar's beauty is genuine and God-given (31:9). Orthodox theology affirms the goodness and beauty of creation with particular emphasis. The iconographic tradition and the theology of transfiguration witness to God's delight in created beauty. The sin is not beauty itself but the failure to refer beauty back to its source -- what the Fathers call acharistia (ingratitude).

The descent to Sheol carries particular resonance because of the Church's robust doctrine of Christ's descent to Hades. The Great Saturday liturgy proclaims, "Today Hades groans and cries aloud." Every descent described in the Old Testament is read through the lens of Christ's harrowing of hell, which transforms the underworld from captivity to liberation.
