## Personal Accountability

Ezekiel 18 refutes inherited guilt and affirms individual moral responsibility. Each person lives or dies according to their own righteousness or wickedness, and the possibility of repentance remains open to the wicked.

Orthodox theology engages this chapter in its understanding of ancestral sin (*propatoric hamartia*), which differs from the Augustinian concept of inherited guilt. Orthodoxy teaches that while humanity inherits the consequences of Adam's sin — mortality, corruption, and a weakened will — personal guilt belongs only to the individual. Ezekiel 18 supports this distinction: "the son shall not suffer for the iniquity of the father" (18:20).

The call to "get yourselves a new heart and a new spirit" (18:31) is read alongside the promise in 36:26 where God provides this new heart. Orthodox theology holds both together through the concept of *synergia* — divine-human cooperation. God provides the grace; the human person must respond with genuine effort. The sacrament of repentance (confession) embodies this synergy: the penitent brings their will to repent, and God provides absolution and transforming grace.
