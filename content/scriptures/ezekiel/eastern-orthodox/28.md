## The Fall from Paradise

Ezekiel 28's oracle against the king of Tyre, with its Edenic imagery, has been interpreted in Orthodox theology as referring to the fall of Satan. The Fathers -- including Origen, Gregory of Nyssa, and John of Damascus -- read the passage as revealing the original beauty and subsequent rebellion of the highest angelic being.

Orthodox angelology, as articulated by Pseudo-Dionysius in the Celestial Hierarchy, understands the angelic orders as mediating divine light. The fall of the guardian cherub represents a catastrophic disruption of cosmic order -- a being designed to reflect divine glory instead turned inward upon itself. John of Damascus defined this as the essence of demonic sin: the free choice to prefer self over God.

The chapter's promise of Israel's restoration reflects the Orthodox conviction that God's redemptive purpose cannot be defeated by creaturely rebellion. The liturgical celebration of the Exaltation of the Cross proclaims that what the enemy destroyed, Christ has restored and surpassed.
