## The Glory Departs

Ezekiel 10 narrates the glory of the LORD rising from the temple and mounting the chariot-throne of the cherubim. Coals of fire are scattered over Jerusalem, and the glory pauses at the east gate before departing.

This chapter is among the most theologically profound in Orthodox reading. The departure of the divine glory (*kavod*/*doxa*) from the temple represents a cosmic rupture — the withdrawal of God's manifest presence from the place He had chosen. Orthodox theology reads this as a prefiguration of the Crucifixion, when the veil of the temple was torn and the glory departed definitively from the old dispensation.

The eastward departure and the Orthodox expectation of Christ's return from the east are liturgically connected. Orthodox churches face east, and the faithful pray facing east, awaiting the return of the glory that departed. The architecture of every Orthodox temple embodies the hope of Ezekiel's vision reversed — the glory returning through the east gate (as fulfilled in chapters 43-44).
