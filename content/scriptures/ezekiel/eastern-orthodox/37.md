## The Valley of Dry Bones

Ezekiel 37's vision of dry bones revivified by God's breath occupies a central place in Orthodox resurrection theology. The passage is read during Holy Saturday Matins, connecting the vision to Christ's descent to Hades and His raising of the dead. The bones represent not only national restoration but all humanity awaiting resurrection.

The sequence -- bones assembled, sinews and flesh added, then the breath of life -- constitutes what the Fathers call a new creation. Gregory of Nyssa interpreted the vision as demonstrating God's power to reconstitute the human person in the resurrection. The materiality is crucial: bones, sinews, and flesh are restored, affirming that redemption encompasses the whole person.

The joining of two sticks has been read ecclesiologically. The Orthodox commitment to conciliar governance and the communion of autocephalous churches reflects this prophetic ideal: many parts, one body, under one Shepherd, who is Christ.
