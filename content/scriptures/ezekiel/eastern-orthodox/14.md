## Idols of the Heart

Ezekiel 14 confronts elders who harbor idols in their hearts while outwardly seeking God, and declares that even Noah, Daniel, and Job could save only themselves. Orthodox spiritual theology engages deeply with the concept of interior idolatry — the attachment to created things that displaces God at the center of the heart.

The patristic tradition, especially the Desert Fathers and Mothers, developed an extensive vocabulary for describing the interior dispositions that constitute the "idols of the heart" — the *logismoi* (intrusive thoughts) and *pathē* (passions) that distort the soul's orientation toward God. The remedy is not merely behavioral change but the purification of the *nous* (spiritual intellect) through prayer, ascetic discipline, and the sacramental life.

The declaration that even the most righteous individuals cannot save others by their merit alone is balanced in Orthodox theology by the strong tradition of intercessory prayer and the communion of saints. The saints' prayers genuinely avail, but not as a substitute for personal repentance. The passage describes a situation of such comprehensive corporate corruption that individual righteousness, while preserving the righteous person, cannot avert collective judgment.
