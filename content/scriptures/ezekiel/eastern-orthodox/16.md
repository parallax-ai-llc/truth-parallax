## The Foundling Bride

Ezekiel 16 tells the extended allegory of Jerusalem as an abandoned infant adopted by God, raised to queenly splendor, and then turned to systematic unfaithfulness — worse than Sodom and Samaria. Yet the chapter concludes with a promise of everlasting covenant.

Orthodox reading of this chapter emphasizes the condescension (*synkatabasis*) of God, who stoops to adopt and adorn what the world has abandoned. This divine condescension is the pattern that reaches its fullness in the Incarnation, where God takes on the most abject condition of humanity to raise it to divine glory. The Nativity hymns of the Orthodox Church echo this movement from abandonment to exaltation.

The promise of everlasting covenant (16:60-63) despite radical unfaithfulness is read through the Orthodox theology of God's faithfulness. Divine love is not transactional but ontological — rooted in God's nature rather than human deserving. Yet the response to this love is not complacency but shame that leads to transformation: "you will remember and be ashamed" (16:63). Orthodox repentance (*metanoia*) involves precisely this movement: the recognition of grace received and squandered, leading to deep compunction and renewed commitment.
