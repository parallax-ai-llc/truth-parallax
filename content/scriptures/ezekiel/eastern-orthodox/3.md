## The Watchman of Israel

Ezekiel 3 establishes the prophet as a watchman — one who bears personal accountability for the faithfulness of his warnings. Orthodox ecclesiology applies this principle to the episcopal office. The bishop, as chief pastor and teacher, bears the watchman's burden for his flock. The liturgical prayer at episcopal consecration reflects this responsibility.

The prophet's enforced silence (3:26-27) — speaking only when God opens his mouth — resonates with the Orthodox theology of *hesychasm*, the tradition of inner silence and stillness that makes authentic divine speech possible. True prophetic utterance arises from deep interior communion with God, not from human eloquence or initiative.

The seven days of stunned silence at Tel-abib before the prophetic commission resumes have been compared by Orthodox commentators to the period of preparation and purification that precedes genuine spiritual insight. The monastic tradition's emphasis on prolonged silence before speaking reflects this prophetic pattern.
