## The Furnace of Purification

Ezekiel 22 indicts every level of Jerusalem's society and likens Israel to dross in a smelting furnace. The conclusion — God sought someone to stand in the breach but found no one — is among the most poignant in the prophetic literature.

Orthodox theology reads the smelting imagery through the lens of purification. The divine fire that consumes dross is the same fire that refines gold. Orthodox ascetic theology understands trials and sufferings as the furnace in which the soul is purified — a process painful but necessary for the soul's transformation into Christlikeness. The prayer of St. Ephrem the Syrian, prayed daily during Great Lent, asks God to grant "the spirit to see my own faults" — the precondition for genuine purification.

The failure to find an intercessor (22:30) is read Christologically: Christ alone stands in the breach between divine holiness and human sinfulness. The Orthodox understanding of Christ as the unique mediator — celebrated in every Divine Liturgy — is grounded in the recognition that no merely human figure could fulfill this role.
