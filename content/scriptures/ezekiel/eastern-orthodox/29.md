## Against Pharaoh

Ezekiel 29 denounces Pharaoh as a river monster claiming, "My Nile is my own; I made it for myself." Orthodox theology identifies this as the fundamental sin of autarkeia -- self-sufficiency. The ascetical tradition, from Evagrius Ponticus through the Philokalia, consistently identifies self-will as the root of spiritual pathology.

The forty-year desolation of Egypt echoes the purifying function of wilderness experience. Orthodox spiritual direction recognizes periods of desolation as potentially transformative, drawing the soul toward genuine dependence on God. The neptic tradition finds in Pharaoh's fate a warning against the spiritual sleep induced by prosperity.

The chapter's theological insight that God uses pagan rulers as instruments resonates with the Orthodox understanding of divine providence (pronoia). Maximus the Confessor taught that God weaves even human resistance into the tapestry of cosmic redemption.
