## The Pride of Tyre

Ezekiel 26 announces Tyre's destruction for rejoicing over Jerusalem's fall. Orthodox interpretation reads this as a revelation of how God responds to hyperephania (arrogance) -- the spiritual condition in which a creature exalts itself above its proper station. John Chrysostom preached extensively on the dangers of wealth and pride, themes that find prophetic expression here.

The detailed prophecy of Tyre's demolition illustrates what Orthodox theology calls the impermanence of all earthly kosmos (order). The beauty and grandeur of human civilizations, while real, are penultimate. The Orthodox funeral service declares, "All human things are vanity, and do not endure after death."

The descent of Tyre to the Pit introduces themes that Orthodox eschatology develops in its theology of Hades. In Orthodox understanding, Christ's descent to Hades liberated those held in its power. The grim finality of Tyre's descent stands under the transformative light of Pascha -- what seemed permanent has been overcome by Christ's victory over death.
