## A History of Grace and Rebellion

Ezekiel 20 recounts Israel's persistent unfaithfulness from Egypt onward, structured around the pattern of divine gift, human rebellion, and divine restraint "for the sake of my name." Orthodox theology reads this chapter as a paradigm of salvation history illuminated by the concept of divine *oikonomia* — God's wise and patient management of human affairs.

The repeated phrase "for the sake of my name" (20:9, 14, 22) reveals that God's forbearance is grounded not in human merit but in His own character. Orthodox doxology — the glorification of God's name in worship — reflects this theological priority: all of salvation history serves the revelation and glorification of the divine name.

The promise of future restoration through judgment (20:33-44) combines severity and mercy. God will bring Israel "into the wilderness of the peoples" and there enter into judgment, purging the rebels. Orthodox Lenten theology embodies this pattern: the wilderness of fasting and repentance is not punishment but the path to Pascha. God leads through wilderness to resurrection.
