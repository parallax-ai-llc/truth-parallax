## The Return of the Glory

Ezekiel 43 records the return of God's glory to the temple. Orthodox theology regards this as one of the most significant theophanies in the Old Testament, anticipating both the Incarnation and the Transfiguration. The glory is understood as the uncreated divine energies made visible, the same light that shone on Mount Tabor.

Gregory Palamas's Transfiguration theology illuminates this passage: what Ezekiel saw was God's genuine self-communication through uncreated energies. The same glory revealed to Peter, James, and John on the mountain is accessible to the saints through prayer and the sacraments. The Orthodox understanding of theosis holds that believers participate in this very glory.

The altar consecration rituals are read typologically. Every Orthodox altar is consecrated with elaborate rites including the placement of relics, anointing with chrism, and the first Liturgy -- echoing the seven-day consecration described here. The altar is a living symbol of Christ Himself, through whom heaven and earth are united.
