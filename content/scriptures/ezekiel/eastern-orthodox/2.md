## The Prophetic Commission

Ezekiel 2 records the divine command to stand, receive the Spirit, and speak to a rebellious nation. Orthodox theology emphasizes the synergistic character of this commission: the Spirit enters Ezekiel and sets him on his feet, but the prophet must then actively cooperate with divine grace. This pattern reflects the Orthodox understanding of theosis — divine-human cooperation in the work of salvation.

The command to speak regardless of whether the people listen reflects the Orthodox understanding of the Church's prophetic witness in the world. The Church does not calibrate its proclamation to cultural acceptability but faithfully transmits the apostolic tradition, trusting that the truth has its own power. The prophet's commission anticipates the Church's missionary mandate.

The scroll of lamentation and woe (2:9-10) has been read in Orthodox tradition as prefiguring the complexity of divine revelation — containing both judgment and mercy, both the severity and the kindness of God. The liturgical readings of Holy Week embody this duality, moving from the lamentations of Good Friday to the joy of Pascha.
