## The Temple Defiled

Ezekiel 8 transports the prophet to Jerusalem's temple, where he witnesses escalating abominations: the image of jealousy, secret idol worship, weeping for Tammuz, and sun worship. Each scene penetrates deeper into the sacred precinct.

Orthodox patristic interpretation reads this vision as revealing the anatomy of spiritual corruption. The progression from external accommodation (the image of jealousy at the gate) to hidden interior idolatry (the elders in their chambers) to syncretic practice (Tammuz worship) to outright apostasy (sun worship with backs turned to the temple) maps onto the spiritual life's vulnerabilities. Orthodox spiritual fathers use similar progressive analyses in their teaching on the stages of temptation and the development of sinful passions.

The elders' claim that "the LORD does not see us" (8:12) represents what Orthodox theology calls *prelest* — spiritual delusion. The most dangerous spiritual state is not open rebellion but the sincere conviction that one's corruption is invisible to God. The Orthodox tradition of spiritual direction exists precisely to expose and correct such self-deception.
