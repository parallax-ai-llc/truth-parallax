## The Siege Begins

Ezekiel 24 marks the day of Jerusalem's siege with the parable of the corroded pot and the death of the prophet's wife. The prophet's enforced silence in mourning becomes a sign of the coming devastation.

Orthodox theology reads the death of Ezekiel's wife as a profound revelation of the cost of prophetic vocation. The prophet's personal grief is subsumed into his public mission. Orthodox monasticism, with its call to renounce even the most legitimate human attachments for the sake of the Kingdom, finds a prophetic precedent in Ezekiel's experience. The monastic tradition does not devalue human love but subordinates it to the absolute claim of God upon the whole person.

The corroded pot that cannot be cleansed (24:6-13) speaks to the Orthodox understanding of sin as a deep ontological distortion, not merely a legal infraction. When corruption becomes integral to the vessel itself, no surface cleaning suffices — only the fire of divine judgment and the re-creation of grace can address it. This insight undergirds the Orthodox insistence that salvation involves genuine transformation (*theosis*), not merely forensic declaration.
