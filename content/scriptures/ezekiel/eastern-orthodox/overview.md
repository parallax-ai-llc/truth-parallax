## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Ezekiel is deeply rooted in the patristic tradition and liturgical life of the Church. The book's extraordinary theophanies — the chariot-throne vision, the glory departing and returning to the temple — are read as revelations of the uncreated divine energies central to Orthodox theology. Ezekiel occupies a prominent place in Orthodox hymnography, iconography, and the theology of worship.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Consensus

Orthodox exegesis privileges the *consensus patrum* — the common witness of the Church Fathers. Key patristic interpreters of Ezekiel include Origen, Theodoret of Cyrrhus, and Gregory the Great (recognized in Orthodoxy as Gregory the Dialogist). These readings emphasize the spiritual and ecclesial dimensions of the text.

### Liturgical Hermeneutic

Ezekiel's visions are encountered primarily through liturgical worship. The chariot-throne vision informs the iconography of Christ in Majesty (*Pantocrator*), while the closed gate of chapter 44 features in Theotokos hymnography, especially in the feasts of the Mother of God.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theophany and Divine Energies**: The glory (*kavod*) of God in Ezekiel is understood through the Palamite distinction between God's essence and energies — what Ezekiel saw was the uncreated divine energy, not the divine essence
- **Theosis**: The promise of a new heart and spirit (36:26-27) is read as a prophecy of human participation in the divine nature (*theosis*)
- **Ecclesiology**: The temple vision prefigures the Church as the locus of God's presence in the world
- **Iconographic Tradition**: The four living creatures of Ezekiel 1 became the standard symbols of the four Evangelists in Christian iconography
