## The History of Captivity

Ezekiel 20's recital of Israel's rebellious history was read by Gnostic interpreters as a coded account of the divine sparks' progressive entrapment in matter. Each stage of the narrative — Egypt, the wilderness, the land — represents a deeper immersion in the Demiurge's system, punctuated by moments of rebellion that Gnostic readers saw as glimpses of the pneumatic impulse resisting material bondage.

The enigmatic verse 20:25 — "I gave them statutes that were not good" — was a favorite Gnostic proof-text. It appeared to confirm the Gnostic claim that the Demiurge's law is itself a form of bondage rather than liberation. The "statutes that were not good" are the totality of the Demiurge's legal system, which cannot give life because it operates entirely within the material realm. Only *gnosis* of the true God, who dwells beyond the Demiurge's reach, can bestow genuine life.

The promise of future restoration through purifying judgment (20:33-44) was read ambiguously. If the restoration returns the people to the Demiurge's land under the Demiurge's rule, it merely continues the cycle of captivity. If, however, a deeper voice speaks through the prophecy, the restoration may point to the ultimate redemption — the return of all divine sparks to the Pleroma when the material world is finally dissolved.
