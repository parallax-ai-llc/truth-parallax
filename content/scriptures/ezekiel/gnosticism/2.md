## The Demiurge's Commission

Ezekiel 2 records God's commissioning of the prophet to speak to a rebellious people. Gnostic readers reinterpreted this commission ambiguously: is Ezekiel being sent by the true God or by the Demiurge? The answer depended on the Gnostic school.

Valentinian interpreters tended to see in the prophets a mixed inspiration — partially from the Demiurge (who desired obedience to his law) and partially from Sophia (who smuggled genuine wisdom into the prophetic message without the Demiurge's knowledge). The scroll of lamentation and woe (2:9-10) could thus be read on two levels: the Demiurge's threat of punishment for disobedience, and Sophia's genuine grief over the entrapment of divine sparks in matter.

The description of Israel as "a nation of rebels" was inverted by some Gnostic traditions: their rebellion was not sin but the stirring of the divine spark within them, the instinctive resistance of pneumatic souls to the Demiurge's demand for blind obedience. In this reading, the "rebellion" Ezekiel condemns is actually the first movement toward spiritual awakening — the refusal to submit entirely to a god who is not the true God.
