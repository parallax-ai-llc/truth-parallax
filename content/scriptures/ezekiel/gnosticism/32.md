## The Underworld Geography

Ezekiel 32's catalogue of fallen nations in Sheol provides Gnostic interpreters with a map of the underworld's archonic structure. Each nation occupies its designated region, governed by its particular archon, bearing shame proportional to the violence it wielded in the upper world. The ordered arrangement of Sheol mirrors the ordered arrangement of the celestial spheres above -- the cosmos is a prison both upward and downward.

The repeated emphasis on shame and uncircumcision among the fallen reveals the Demiurge's obsession with ritual purity and hierarchical distinction -- categories that persist even in the underworld. The Gnostic recognizes these as marks of the archonic system, not genuine spiritual realities.

For the pneumatic reader, the chapter's most important revelation is that the afterlife described here is not the true destiny of the divine spark. Sheol is another region of the archonic cosmos, not the Pleroma. The soul possessing gnosis does not descend to Sheol but ascends through the archontic spheres to reunite with the divine Fullness. The grim underworld of Ezekiel 32 is the fate of those who remain bound to the Demiurge's system.
