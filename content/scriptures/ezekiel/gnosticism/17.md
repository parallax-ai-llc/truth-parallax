## Eagles and Archons

Ezekiel 17's riddle of two eagles and a vine was read by Gnostic interpreters as an allegory of archonic politics. The eagles represent the archons — powerful beings who manipulate human kingdoms for their own purposes. The vine — the human community — is caught between competing archonic forces, unable to escape the system of cosmic power politics.

The tender sprig planted by God on a high mountain (17:22-24) received more positive Gnostic treatment. The "high mountain" was identified with the Pleroma or its boundary, and the sprig was read as a Gnostic redeemer figure — an emanation from the true God who descends to establish a community of awakened souls beneath the protection of genuine divine power, beyond the archons' reach.

The reversal of high and low trees (17:24) resonated with the Gnostic inversion of worldly values. What the world considers exalted — the Demiurge's institutions, the archons' power structures — will be brought low, while what the world despises — the hidden knowledge of the Gnostic community — will be exalted. This pattern of cosmic inversion is central to Gnostic soteriology.
