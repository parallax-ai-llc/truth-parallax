## The Cosmic Tree and Its Fall

Ezekiel 31's allegory of Assyria as a great cedar -- towering above all trees, envied by the trees of Eden, yet consigned to Sheol -- reads in Gnostic interpretation as a myth of the cosmic tree, a common motif in Gnostic and Hermetic literature. The tree represents the archonic world-structure itself, rooted in the waters of primordial chaos and reaching toward the heavens.

The cedar's beauty is genuine but derivative -- "I made it beautiful," the Demiurge declares (31:9). Gnostic theology consistently maintains that the material world possesses a reflected beauty borrowed from the Pleroma. The cedar's fatal error is the same as the Demiurge's: confusing reflected glory with self-generated splendor.

The descent to Sheol represents the inevitable collapse of archonic structures that lack genuine spiritual substance. The trees of Eden "were consoled in the earth below" (31:16) -- in the Gnostic reading, the archonic powers find their natural level in the lower regions of the cosmos, far from the light of the true God. The passage reveals the ultimate trajectory of all material power: downward, toward dissolution.
