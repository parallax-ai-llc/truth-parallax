## The Abandoned Spark

Ezekiel 16's allegory of the foundling infant was read by Gnostic interpreters as a parable of the divine spark's history in the material world. The infant abandoned in the field represents the pneumatic element cast out into matter — naked, exposed, and helpless. But rather than God's rescue, Gnostic readers saw the Demiurge's co-optation: the spark is "rescued" by the Demiurge, who adorns it with material attractions — wealth, beauty, status — binding it more tightly to his world through the very gifts he provides.

The "unfaithfulness" that follows is, in Gnostic perspective, a dim memory of the spark's true origin breaking through. The foundling's turn to "lovers" represents not sin but a confused search for the true divine home — the Pleroma from which the spark originated. The search is misdirected (it turns to material powers rather than the transcendent God), but the impulse is genuine: the spark knows, at some level, that the Demiurge is not its true source.

The chapter's conclusion — the promise of an everlasting covenant — was read by Gnostic interpreters with suspicion. If this is the Demiurge's promise, it represents another binding: an eternal covenant with the prison warden. If a trace of Sophia speaks through the text, the everlasting covenant might refer to the Pleroma's unfailing commitment to reclaim every lost spark, no matter how deep the entanglement in matter.
