## Beyond Intercession

Ezekiel 14 declares that even Noah, Daniel, and Job cannot save others by their righteousness. Gnostic interpreters found this passage revealing: it demonstrates the limits of the Demiurge's system. Even the most righteous individuals within his moral framework cannot save others, because righteousness within the Demiurge's law is not genuine salvation — it is merely compliance with the rules of the prison.

The three figures — Noah, Daniel, and Job — were reinterpreted in Gnostic tradition. Noah's survival of the flood was sometimes read as preservation through a catastrophe the Demiurge unleashed when he feared humanity's growing knowledge. Daniel's wisdom was seen as a form of proto-*gnosis*. Job's suffering illustrated the Demiurge's arbitrary cruelty toward even his most faithful servants.

The chapter's deepest implication, for Gnostic readers, is that salvation cannot be achieved within the Demiurge's moral system at all. No accumulation of righteous deeds, no matter how perfect, can break the soul free from material entrapment. Only *gnosis* — the direct recognition of one's true divine origin — accomplishes what righteousness cannot. This is not antinomianism (the Demiurge's law serves a limited purpose) but a recognition of the law's fundamental inadequacy.
