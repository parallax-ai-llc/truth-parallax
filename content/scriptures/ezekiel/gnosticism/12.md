## The Exile as Liberation

Ezekiel 12 enacts the departure from Jerusalem and confronts skepticism about prophetic fulfillment. Gnostic reading inverted the conventional meaning: exile from the Demiurge's city is not punishment but liberation. The prophet's act of digging through the wall and departing at night becomes a parable of the Gnostic soul's escape from the material prison.

The covered face (12:6) — traditionally interpreted as representing Zedekiah's blinding — was read by Gnostic interpreters as the necessary veiling of the soul during its passage through the archonic realms. Gnostic ascent texts describe the soul passing through the spheres ruled by the archons, concealing its true nature until it reaches safety beyond their jurisdiction. The covered face is not a sign of shame but of strategic concealment.

The people's dismissal of prophecy — "every vision comes to nothing" — was read ironically by Gnostic exegetes. The people are right that the Demiurge's prophecies will come to nothing, because his entire order is destined for dissolution. But they fail to perceive the deeper truth: the true prophetic vision concerns realities beyond the Demiurge's cosmos, and this vision will indeed be fulfilled when the material world is finally dissolved.
