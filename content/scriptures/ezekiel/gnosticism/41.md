## The Inner Architecture of Illusion

Ezekiel 41's description of the temple interior -- the nave, the most holy place, and the cherubim and palm tree decorations -- reveals to the Gnostic interpreter the innermost structure of the archonic system. The progressive restriction of access, with the most holy place at the center, represents the layered concealment of the Demiurge's own nature from his worshippers.

The cherubim carved on the walls are read as representations of the archonic guardians who prevent unauthorized access to higher realms. In the Apocryphon of John, each heavenly gate is guarded by archons who demand passwords and seals from ascending souls. The decorative program of the temple walls is a map of these barriers.

The wooden altar described as "the table that is before the LORD" (41:22) represents the archonic demand for offerings -- the continuous extraction of psychic energy from worshippers that sustains the Demiurge's realm. The Gnostic recognizes that true communion with the divine requires no altar, no sacrifice, no mediation -- only the direct knowledge of one's own divine origin.
