## The False Shepherd

Ezekiel 34's condemnation of false shepherds and promise of divine shepherding presents Gnostic interpreters with a complex text. The Demiurge condemns the rulers who failed to care for the flock -- but the Gnostic notes that the Demiurge himself is the ultimate false shepherd, one who claims ownership of a flock that does not truly belong to him.

The promise of "my servant David" as messianic shepherd is read in some Gnostic traditions as an unwitting prophecy of the true Christ -- the revealer from the Pleroma who comes not to rule the material flock but to awaken the divine sparks within it. The Gospel of Philip distinguishes between the good shepherd who feeds the sheep and the Demiurge who feeds on them.

The covenant of peace (34:25-31), with its promises of material abundance, reveals the Demiurge's limited imagination. He can only conceive of blessing in material terms -- safety from wild beasts, agricultural prosperity, freedom from slavery. The Gnostic recognizes that true peace transcends the material entirely: it is the return of the pneumatic self to the Pleroma of Light.
