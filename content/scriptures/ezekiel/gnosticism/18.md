## Beyond the Demiurge's Justice

Ezekiel 18's declaration of individual moral responsibility was read by Gnostic interpreters as both valid and insufficient. The Demiurge's justice is real within his system — each person reaps what they sow under his law — but this entire system of reward and punishment operates within the material prison. True liberation does not come through perfecting one's performance within the Demiurge's moral framework but through transcending it entirely.

The "new heart and new spirit" of 18:31 was read more positively by some Gnostic interpreters as describing the moment of *gnosis* — the awakening that transforms the soul's orientation from material to spiritual. The "heart of stone" is the consciousness trapped in materiality; the "heart of flesh" (somewhat paradoxically, given Gnostic suspicion of flesh) represents the living, responsive spiritual capacity that *gnosis* restores.

The declaration "I have no pleasure in the death of anyone" (18:32) raised a question for Gnostic theology: whose voice is this? If the Demiurge, then his claim is dubious — the entire material world involves death and suffering. If the true God speaks through the text despite the Demiurge's mediation, then the passage offers a genuine glimpse of divine compassion breaking through the archonic overlay. Some Valentinian interpreters favored this reading, seeing in the prophets an unstable mixture of Demiurgic and authentic divine inspiration.
