## Prophets of the Demiurge

Ezekiel 13 condemns false prophets who speak from their own hearts and prophetesses who practice divination. Gnostic interpretation offered a radical rereading: the "false prophets" condemned by the Demiurge's spokesperson are actually those who fail to serve the Demiurge's agenda — they prophesy from their own hearts rather than from the Demiurge's dictation, which means they may be accessing genuine spiritual insight.

The whitewashing of walls (13:10-16) was read by Gnostic interpreters as a description of orthodox religious teaching itself: a cosmetic covering applied to the fundamentally unstable structure of the Demiurge's cosmos. The promise of peace within a system built on cosmic ignorance is, in Gnostic perspective, the ultimate false prophecy. True peace comes only through awakening and escape.

The prophetesses' magical practices (13:17-23) — sewing bands and making veils — received varied Gnostic interpretation. Some saw these as describing archonic binding magic that keeps souls trapped in matter. Others read them as distorted versions of genuine Gnostic ritual practice: the use of sacred formulas and ritual objects to facilitate the soul's liberation, corrupted by practitioners who did not understand their true significance.
