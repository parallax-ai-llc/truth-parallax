## The Watchman and False Freedom

Ezekiel 33's reestablishment of the prophet as watchman and its teaching on individual responsibility are read by Gnostic interpreters as revealing the Demiurge's system of moral surveillance. The watchman represents the archonic mechanism of monitoring and controlling human behavior through law and guilt -- the very system from which gnosis liberates.

The chapter's insistence that the righteous who sin will die and the wicked who repent will live describes the Demiurge's transactional economy of merit. Gnostic theology rejects this framework entirely: salvation comes not through moral performance but through knowledge of one's true divine origin. The Demiurge's system of rewards and punishments keeps souls trapped in an endless cycle of striving and failing.

The exiles who treat Ezekiel's words as entertainment (33:30-33) are, ironically, closer to the Gnostic posture than those who take the words with deadly seriousness. The pneumatic recognizes that the Demiurge's threats and promises operate within a system that has no ultimate authority over the divine spark within.
