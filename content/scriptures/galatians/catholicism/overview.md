## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Galatians navigates between affirming Paul's critique of works-righteousness and preserving the role of love, sacraments, and good works in the life of faith. The key verse for Catholic reading is 5:6: "faith working through love" (*fides caritate formata*), which the Council of Trent cited as demonstrating that justification involves not faith alone but faith animated by charity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Faith Working Through Love
Catholic theology reads Galatians 5:6 as Paul's own qualification of his faith-language: genuine faith is not mere intellectual assent but trust that expresses itself in love and works of mercy. This reading integrates Paul's polemic with the teaching of James 2.

### Freedom and Moral Responsibility
Catholic interpreters emphasize that Christian freedom (5:1) is not autonomy but freedom for love and service. The "law of Christ" (6:2) involves moral norms, sacramental life, and ecclesial belonging.

### Paul and the Law
Catholic scholars increasingly distinguish between "works of the law" (Jewish identity markers) and moral good works, following the New Perspective on Paul. The law's role in salvation history is respected, not denigrated.

## Theological Emphases

- **Justification**: Initial and ongoing, involving faith, hope, and love
- **Baptism**: "Clothed with Christ" in baptism (3:27) is sacramental, not merely metaphorical
- **New Creation**: The ultimate goal transcending the law/gospel dichotomy (6:15)
