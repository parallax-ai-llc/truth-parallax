## Overview

Galatians is one of the most challenging texts in the New Testament from a Jewish perspective. Paul's rhetoric against circumcision, his apparent denigration of Torah, and his assertion that the law was a temporary "guardian" (3:24) until Christ's coming have made this letter central to Jewish-Christian dialogue — and to historical anti-Jewish sentiment.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### Torah and Salvation
Paul's claim that "by works of the law no one will be justified" (2:16) has been interpreted in Jewish scholarship not as a fair critique of Judaism but as a caricature. Rabbinic Judaism never taught that salvation was "earned" through mechanical law-observance; the covenant relationship with God was foundational.

### Abraham and the Promise
Paul's reading of Abraham as justified by faith before circumcision (ch. 3) competes with the rabbinic reading in which Abraham kept the entire Torah before it was given (Kiddushin 4:14). Jewish interpreters note that Paul selectively reads Genesis to support his argument.

### The "Curse of the Law"
Paul's citation of Deuteronomy 27:26 — "cursed is everyone who does not abide by all things written in the book of the law" (3:10) — is read in Jewish tradition as a call to repentance, not as an indictment of the law itself.

## Modern Jewish Scholarship

Scholars like Daniel Boyarin situate Paul within Second Temple Judaism's diversity, arguing that his position reflects intra-Jewish debates about Gentile inclusion rather than a rejection of Judaism per se.
