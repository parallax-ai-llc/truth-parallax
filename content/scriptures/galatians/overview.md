## Overview

Galatians is Paul's most passionate defense of the gospel of justification by faith apart from works of the Mosaic law. Written to churches in the Roman province of Galatia, the letter confronts rival teachers who insisted that Gentile believers must be circumcised and observe Torah to be fully included in God's people. Luther called Galatians "my epistle" and it became a cornerstone of Reformation theology.

## Structure

The letter follows a clear argumentative arc:

1. **Chapters 1–2**: Autobiographical defense — Paul's gospel came by revelation, not human tradition
2. **Chapters 3–4**: Theological argument — the promise to Abraham, fulfilled in Christ, precedes and supersedes the law
3. **Chapters 5–6**: Ethical exhortation — freedom from the law does not mean license but life in the Spirit

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Galatians generates intense cross-traditional engagement:

- **Protestantism**: The magna carta of justification by faith alone (*sola fide*)
- **Catholicism**: Read with nuance — faith working through love (5:6) balances the letter's polemic
- **Judaism**: A central and often painful text in Jewish-Christian relations due to its rhetoric about the law
- **Islam**: The tension between law and grace maps onto Islamic discussions of *shari'ah* and divine mercy
- **Gnosticism**: The spirit/flesh contrast was read by Gnostics as confirming their dualism

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Justification by Faith | Righteousness through trust in Christ, not Torah observance |
| Freedom | Liberation from slavery to the law and the elements of the world |
| Law and Gospel | The proper roles of Torah and promise in salvation history |
| Fruit of the Spirit | The ethical character of Spirit-led life (5:22-23) |
| New Creation | The ultimate reality that transcends circumcision and uncircumcision |
