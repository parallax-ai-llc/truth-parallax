## Overview

Islamic engagement with Galatians centers on the letter's themes of divine law, the legacy of Abraham, and the tension between legal obligation and divine mercy. While Islam does not accept Paul's apostolic authority, the theological questions he raises about the relationship between law and grace find parallels in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.

## Quranic Resonances

### Abraham as Model
Paul's appeal to Abraham's faith (ch. 3) resonates with the Quran's presentation of Ibrahim as the archetype of pure monotheism (*hanif*). However, Islam sees Abraham as a Muslim who submitted to God — not as a figure whose faith supersedes the law.

### Law and Mercy
The tension between law and grace in Galatians maps onto Islamic discussions of *shari'ah* (divine law) and *rahmah* (divine mercy). Islam affirms both: the law is God's mercy, not its opposite.

### Freedom and Submission
Paul's theme of freedom (5:1) contrasts with the Islamic understanding that true freedom is found in submission (*islam*) to God's will. Liberty from divine law is not a virtue in Islamic theology.

## Points of Divergence

| Galatians Theme | Islamic Perspective |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Justification by faith alone | Faith and works are inseparable |
| The law as temporary guardian | The law (*shari'ah*) is God's permanent guidance |
| Freedom from the law | Freedom through the law |
