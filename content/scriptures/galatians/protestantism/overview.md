## Overview

Galatians has been called the Magna Carta of Protestant theology. Luther's lectures on Galatians (1531/1535) laid the foundation for the Reformation's understanding of justification by faith alone (*sola fide*). The letter's insistence that "a person is not justified by works of the law but through faith in Jesus Christ" (2:16) became the central axis of Protestant soteriology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Justification by Faith Alone
The heart of Protestant reading: Paul teaches that righteousness is received through faith, not earned through Torah observance or moral effort. Luther's distinction between "law" and "gospel" was built largely on Galatians.

### Christian Freedom
"For freedom Christ has set us free" (5:1) is read as liberation from both legalism and antinomianism. The believer is free from the curse of the law but bound by the "law of Christ" (6:2) — love.

### The Fruit of the Spirit
The contrast between "works of the flesh" and "fruit of the Spirit" (5:16-26) shapes Protestant ethics: genuine transformation is the Spirit's work, not human moral striving.
