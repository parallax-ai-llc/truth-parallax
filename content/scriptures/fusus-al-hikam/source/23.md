## The Bezel of the Wisdom of Virtue in the Word of Luqman

**1.** Luqman was given wisdom by God, not prophethood. He was a sage, not a prophet—and this distinction contains a profound teaching.

**2.** God gives wisdom to whom He wills, and whoever is given wisdom has been given a great good. But wisdom without prophethood means that the wise man speaks from experience, not from revelation.

**3.** Luqman said to his son: "O my son, do not associate anything with God. Indeed, association with God is a great injustice."

**4.** The first teaching is unity. Before all virtues comes the recognition that there is no reality but the Reality, no being but the Being.

**5.** "O my son, even if it be the weight of a mustard seed in a rock, or in the heavens, or in the earth, God will bring it forth. God is the Subtle, the Aware."

**6.** Nothing is hidden from God, not even the weight of an atom. This knowledge is the foundation of virtue, for the one who knows he is always seen behaves differently from the one who thinks he is unseen.

**7.** "O my son, establish prayer, and enjoin what is right, and forbid what is wrong, and be patient with what befalls you. Indeed, that is of the matters requiring determination."

**8.** Prayer, justice, patience—these are the three pillars of virtue. Without prayer, there is no connection. Without justice, there is no order. Without patience, there is no endurance.

**9.** "And do not turn your cheek in contempt toward people, and do not walk through the earth exultantly. Indeed, God does not love any self-conceited boaster."

**10.** Humility is the garment of the virtuous. Pride is the garment of the ignorant. The man who walks proudly upon the earth has not yet looked at the sky.

**11.** "And be moderate in your pace, and lower your voice. Indeed, the most disagreeable of sounds is the voice of the donkey."

**12.** Moderation is the sign of wisdom. The one who rushes misses the path. The one who shouts drowns out the whisper of God.

**13.** Luqman's wisdom was the wisdom of the good father: practical, gentle, and rooted in the daily life of the soul.

**14.** He did not speak of high metaphysics or cosmic mysteries—he spoke of how to walk, how to speak, how to treat others, and how to remember God in every moment.

**15.** The wisdom of Luqman is that the greatest virtue is not the grand gesture but the daily practice of humility, patience, and awareness before the Subtle, the Aware.
