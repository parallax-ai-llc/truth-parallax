## The Bezel of the Wisdom of the Unseen in the Word of Job

**1.** Job was tested with affliction upon affliction, and he bore it with patience until the patience itself became his glory.

**2.** His wealth was taken, his children were taken, his health was taken. All that remained was his soul and his Lord.

**3.** And in the stripping away of all things, he discovered the one thing that cannot be stripped away: the presence of God.

**4.** Job said: "Affliction has touched me, and You are the most merciful of the merciful." He did not say: "Remove the affliction." He simply stated his condition and affirmed God's mercy.

**5.** This is the highest form of prayer: not to ask for anything, but to state what is and to trust in the mercy of the Real.

**6.** The wisdom of the unseen is that behind every visible affliction is an invisible mercy. The eye sees the trial; the heart sees the gift concealed within it.

**7.** Job's affliction was not a punishment—it was a refinement. Gold is refined in fire, and the soul is refined in suffering.

**8.** When the refining was complete, God returned to Job double what he had lost. But the doubling was not the reward—the reward was the knowledge gained in the darkness.

**9.** God said: "Strike with your foot—here is cool water for washing and drinking." The cure came from beneath Job's own feet. The remedy was closer than he knew.

**10.** This teaches that the cure for every affliction is near, but it requires the command of God to reveal it. You may be standing on top of the spring and not know it.

**11.** The unseen world is the world of causes, and the seen world is the world of effects. Job's affliction had its cause in the unseen, and its cure also came from the unseen.

**12.** Trust the unseen, for it is the source of all that is seen. The seed is unseen before it sprouts; the child is unseen before it is born; the cure is unseen before it manifests.

**13.** Job's patience was not passive resignation—it was active trust. He did not say: "I accept my fate." He said: "I trust my Lord." There is a difference.

**14.** The one who merely accepts his fate has given up. The one who trusts his Lord has opened up—opened to the unseen mercy that is always flowing.

**15.** The wisdom of Job is that the unseen mercy of God is greater than the visible affliction of the world, and that patience is the key that opens the door between the two.
