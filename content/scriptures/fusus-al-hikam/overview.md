## Overview

The *Fusus al-Hikam* (Bezels of Wisdom) is the masterwork of Muhyi al-Din Ibn al-Arabi (1165–1240 CE), the greatest metaphysician of Islamic mysticism. Composed in Damascus in 1229, Ibn Arabi claimed the work was given to him in a vision by the Prophet Muhammad himself. The text consists of twenty-seven chapters (fusus, literally "bezels" or "ring-stones"), each dedicated to a particular prophet and the unique divine wisdom (hikma) that prophet embodies.

The Fusus represents the summit of Ibn Arabi's metaphysical system, articulating the doctrine of the Unity of Being (wahdat al-wujud) through the lens of prophetic typology. Each prophet is understood as a unique "word" (kalima) of God — a particular modality through which the divine self-discloses in creation. Together, the twenty-seven prophets constitute a complete map of the possibilities of divine manifestation in human form, culminating in the seal of prophecy, Muhammad, who unites all prophetic wisdoms in a single comprehensive reality.

## Structure

The twenty-seven bezels follow the prophetic lineage from Adam to Muhammad:

1. **Bezels 1–4**: Adam, Seth, Noah, Enoch — the primordial prophets and the foundations of divine wisdom
2. **Bezels 5–9**: Abraham, Isaac, Ishmael, Jacob, Joseph — the patriarchal prophets and the mysteries of love, sacrifice, and beauty
3. **Bezels 10–14**: Hud, Salih, Shuayb, Lot, Uzayr — Arabian and biblical prophets and the wisdoms of unity, opening, and destiny
4. **Bezels 15–18**: Jesus, Solomon, David, Jonah — prophets of spirit, mercy, existence, and the soul
5. **Bezels 19–24**: Job, John, Zachariah, Elijah, Luqman, Aaron — prophets of patience, majesty, and intimacy
6. **Bezels 25–27**: Moses, Khalid, Muhammad — the climactic prophets of exaltation, uniqueness, and singularity

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Sufi**: The supreme philosophical expression of Sufi metaphysics, studied in every major tariqa as the definitive statement of wahdat al-wujud
- **Sunni**: Controversial — revered by those who accept Ibn Arabi's sainthood, condemned by literalists as heretical
- **Shia**: Embraced for its emphasis on the Perfect Human (al-insan al-kamil) and the esoteric dimensions of prophecy
- **Academic**: Central to the study of Islamic philosophy, comparative mysticism, and the history of metaphysical thought
- **Interfaith**: The prophetic typology offers a framework for understanding multiple religious traditions as diverse manifestations of a single divine reality

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Wahdat al-Wujud | The Unity of Being — all existence is a self-disclosure of the one divine reality |
| Al-Insan al-Kamil | The Perfect Human who mirrors all divine names and attributes |
| Prophetic Wisdom | Each prophet embodies a unique modality of divine self-disclosure |
| Tajalli | Divine self-manifestation through the forms of creation |
| Imagination | The creative imagination (khayyal) as the faculty that perceives divine realities in sensible forms |
| Barzakh | The isthmus or intermediate realm between the divine and the created |
