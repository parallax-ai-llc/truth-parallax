## Overview

Interfaith engagement with the Fusus al-Hikam has been drawn to Ibn Arabi's remarkable openness to other religious traditions. His teaching that every prophet embodies a unique divine wisdom — and that the diversity of religious forms reflects the infinite modes of divine self-disclosure rather than human error — offers a sophisticated theological framework for religious pluralism that has few parallels in pre-modern thought.

## Interpretive Approach

Interfaith readers find in the Fusus a model for honoring religious diversity without relativism. Ibn Arabi does not teach that all religions are the same but rather that each tradition captures a genuine aspect of the infinite divine reality that no single tradition can exhaust. His famous poem — "My heart has become capable of every form: a pasture for gazelles, a monastery for monks, a temple for idols, the Ka'ba of the pilgrim" — expresses the spiritual capacity that results from recognizing God's self-disclosure in all forms.

## Significance for Dialogue

The Fusus offers interfaith dialogue a metaphysical framework in which religious diversity is not a problem to be solved but a reflection of divine generosity. Each prophet's unique wisdom is necessary for a complete understanding of the divine, and the elimination of any tradition would impoverish humanity's collective knowledge of God.
