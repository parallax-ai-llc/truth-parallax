## Overview

Comparative philosophy and theology have found in the Fusus al-Hikam one of the richest texts for cross-traditional dialogue on mystical metaphysics. Ibn Arabi's doctrine of the Unity of Being invites comparison with Advaita Vedanta's teaching on Brahman, Neoplatonic emanationism, Kabbalistic conceptions of the Ein Sof, and Meister Eckhart's theology of the divine ground.

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative readers explore structural parallels between Ibn Arabi's metaphysical system and other mystical traditions while respecting the irreducible differences that arise from distinct theological commitments. The comparison with Shankara's Advaita Vedanta has been particularly productive: both thinkers argue that the world of multiplicity is ultimately grounded in a single reality, though they differ on the ontological status of the phenomenal world.

## Key Comparisons

Ibn Arabi's concept of the Perfect Human (al-insan al-kamil) has been compared with the Hindu concept of the avatara, the Christian doctrine of the Incarnation, and the Kabbalistic Adam Kadmon. His theory of imagination (khayyal) as the creative faculty that mediates between the divine and the sensible invites comparison with Coleridge's "primary imagination" and Corbin's concept of the mundus imaginalis. These comparisons illuminate how different traditions have articulated the relationship between ultimate reality and its manifestation in human consciousness.
