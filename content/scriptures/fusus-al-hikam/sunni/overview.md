## Overview

Sunni engagement with the Fusus al-Hikam has been characterized by intense debate between those who revere Ibn Arabi as the Greatest Master (al-Shaykh al-Akbar) and those who condemn his teachings as heretical deviations from orthodox tawhid. This controversy has persisted for eight centuries and remains one of the most contentious issues in Sunni intellectual life.

## Interpretive Approach

Sunni scholars who accept the Fusus read it within the framework of Ash'ari or Maturidi theology, interpreting wahdat al-wujud as consistent with the absolute transcendence of God. They argue that Ibn Arabi does not identify God with creation but rather teaches that creation has no independent existence apart from God — a distinction between pantheism and what might be called "panentheism" or "acosmism."

## Critics and Defenders

Critics such as Ibn Taymiyya (d. 1328) argued that the Fusus contains statements that contradict the Quran and Sunnah, particularly regarding the nature of divine unity and the status of non-Islamic religions. Defenders such as Jami, al-Qashani, and al-Nabulusi produced elaborate commentaries demonstrating the text's compatibility with orthodox theology. This debate reflects broader Sunni tensions between mystical and scholarly approaches to religious knowledge.
