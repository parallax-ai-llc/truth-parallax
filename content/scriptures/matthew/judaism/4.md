## The Temptation and Torah

Jewish scholars observe that Jesus' responses to Satan's temptations are drawn exclusively from Deuteronomy (8:3, 6:16, 6:13) — the Torah itself. This places Jesus squarely within the Jewish tradition of relying on Torah as the guide for righteous living. The forty-day period echoes Moses' forty days on Sinai (Exodus 34:28) and Israel's forty years in the wilderness, suggesting that Matthew presents Jesus as a new Moses figure.

## The Wilderness Tradition

The wilderness (*midbar*) holds deep significance in Jewish thought. It is the place of Israel's formation, the setting of the giving of the Torah, and the location of prophetic encounter with God. Jewish scholars read Jesus' withdrawal to the wilderness as participation in a distinctively Jewish spiritual geography — the liminal space between the settled world and the divine presence.

## Galilee of the Gentiles

Matthew's citation of Isaiah 9:1-2, referring to "Galilee of the Gentiles," reflects the socio-religious reality of first-century Galilee as a region of mixed Jewish and Gentile population. Jewish archaeological evidence from the period — synagogues, mikva'ot, stone vessels — demonstrates that Galilean Jews maintained their religious identity and practice. The scholarly question of Jesus' Galilean context continues to illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism.
