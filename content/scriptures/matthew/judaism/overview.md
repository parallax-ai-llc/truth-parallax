## Overview

Jewish engagement with the Gospel of Matthew occupies a unique position in interfaith scholarship. Matthew is simultaneously the most Jewish of the Gospels — rooted in Torah, halakhic debate, and Messianic expectation — and the text most frequently cited in the history of Christian anti-Judaism (particularly Chapter 23 and 27:25). Modern Jewish scholars have reclaimed Matthew as a valuable witness to first-century Jewish life, theology, and sectarian debate.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historical-Critical
Jewish scholars such as David Flusser, Amy-Jill Levine, and Daniel Boyarin approach Matthew as a first-century Jewish text, illuminating its halakhic arguments, its relationship to Pharisaic Judaism, and its place within the diverse landscape of Second Temple Judaism.

### Interfaith Dialogue
Since *Nostra Aetate* (1965), Jewish and Christian scholars have collaborated on reading Matthew in ways that resist supersessionist interpretations and acknowledge the Gospel's Jewish roots.

### Anti-Judaism Critique
Jewish scholars have critically analyzed Matthew's polemical passages — the Woes against the Pharisees (Chapter 23) and the blood cry (27:25) — tracing their reception history and their role in anti-Jewish violence.

## Theological Emphases

- **Jesus as Jewish Teacher**: Matthew's Jesus engages in recognizably rabbinic modes of argument (e.g., *qal va-homer*, "light and heavy")
- **Torah Interpretation**: The antitheses of the Sermon on the Mount ("You have heard... but I say") as intra-Jewish halakhic debate
- **Messianic Expectation**: Matthew's fulfillment quotations as evidence for first-century Jewish Messianic interpretation of the Hebrew Bible
- **The Parting of the Ways**: Matthew as a window into the gradual separation of Christianity from Judaism
