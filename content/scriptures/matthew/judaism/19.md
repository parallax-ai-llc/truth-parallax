## The Divorce Debate: Shammai vs. Hillel

Jesus' teaching on divorce (19:1-9) enters directly into a well-documented rabbinic debate. The school of Shammai held that divorce was permitted only for sexual immorality (*ervat davar*, Deuteronomy 24:1), while the school of Hillel allowed it for any cause. Jesus' position — restricting divorce to cases of *porneia* (19:9) — aligns closely with the school of Shammai. Jewish scholars recognize this as a halakhic ruling within the spectrum of legitimate Jewish legal opinion.

## Celibacy and Jewish Values

Jesus' saying about those who become "eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom" (19:12) stands in tension with the strong Jewish emphasis on marriage and procreation. The command to "be fruitful and multiply" (Genesis 1:28) is regarded in rabbinic Judaism as the first commandment. While individual figures like Jeremiah were called to celibacy, and the Essenes reportedly practiced it, voluntary celibacy was exceptional rather than normative in Jewish life.

## Wealth, Poverty, and the World to Come

The rich young man's encounter with Jesus (19:16-22) raises the question of the relationship between wealth and piety. Jewish tradition offers a complex view: wealth can be a divine blessing (Deuteronomy 28:1-14) or a dangerous temptation. The rabbinic concept of *olam ha-ba* (the world to come) provides the framework for Jesus' promise that those who leave everything will receive "a hundredfold" and "eternal life" (19:29).
