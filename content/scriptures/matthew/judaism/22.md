## The Sadducees and Resurrection

The Sadducees' question about resurrection (22:23-33) reflects a genuine theological debate within Second Temple Judaism. The Sadducees denied resurrection, accepting only the written Torah as authoritative. The Pharisees affirmed resurrection based on their broader canon and oral tradition. Jewish scholars note that Jesus' argument — citing Exodus 3:6 to prove resurrection from the Torah itself — employs a distinctly Pharisaic hermeneutical strategy, placing him closer to the Pharisaic camp on this issue.

## The Greatest Commandment and the Shema

Jesus' identification of the greatest commandment (22:34-40) — love God (Deuteronomy 6:5) and love neighbor (Leviticus 19:18) — is deeply rooted in Jewish tradition. The Shema ("Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one") is the central confession of Jewish faith, recited twice daily. Rabbi Akiva identified Leviticus 19:18 as "the great principle of the Torah" (Jerusalem Talmud, Nedarim 9:4). Jewish scholars note that Jesus' answer, combining these two commandments, reflects a recognizable Jewish synthesis rather than a novel innovation.

## David's Son or David's Lord?

Jesus' question about the Messiah as David's son and David's lord (22:41-46) raises a puzzle drawn from Psalm 110:1. Jewish scholars note that Psalm 110 was widely interpreted as Messianic in the Second Temple period. The question of how the Messiah can be both David's descendant and David's lord touches on the tension between Messianic humility and Messianic authority — a tension that remains unresolved in Jewish Messianic thought.
