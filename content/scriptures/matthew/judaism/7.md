## The Golden Rule and Hillel

The Golden Rule (7:12) — "Whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them" — has a famous parallel in the teaching of Hillel the Elder (c. 110 BCE - 10 CE). When asked to teach the entire Torah while standing on one foot, Hillel replied: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor. That is the whole Torah; the rest is commentary. Go and learn it" (Shabbat 31a). Jewish scholars note that Hillel's negative formulation and Jesus' positive formulation are complementary rather than contradictory, and both reflect the centrality of Leviticus 19:18 in Jewish ethics.

## Discernment and False Prophets

The warning against false prophets (7:15-23) resonates with the Torah's own criteria for evaluating prophets (Deuteronomy 13:1-5; 18:15-22). Jewish scholars note that the question of distinguishing true from false prophecy was urgent in the Second Temple period, with multiple competing prophetic and Messianic claimants. Matthew's Jesus applies traditional Jewish criteria — the test of "fruits" — to this perennial problem.

## The Wise Builder

The parable of the wise and foolish builders (7:24-27) employs a teaching method — the *mashal* (parable) — that is one of the most characteristic forms of rabbinic instruction. Jewish scholars observe that the image of building on rock versus sand has parallels in rabbinic literature (Avot de-Rabbi Natan 24) and reflects the common Jewish emphasis on the unity of study and practice: learning Torah without observing it is like building without a foundation.
