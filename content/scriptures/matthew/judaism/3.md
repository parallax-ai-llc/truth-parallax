## Ritual Immersion in Jewish Practice

John's baptism has clear antecedents in Jewish purification practices. The *mikveh* (ritual immersion pool) was central to Second Temple Jewish life, used for purification after various forms of impurity. The Qumran community practiced regular ritual immersion as part of their covenantal piety. Jewish scholars read John's baptism as a prophetic adaptation of existing Jewish practice — a public, one-time immersion signifying repentance and preparation for divine action.

## The Prophetic Tradition

John's role as described in Matthew — the voice crying in the wilderness (Isaiah 40:3) — places him squarely within the Jewish prophetic tradition. His dress (camel's hair, leather belt) deliberately echoes Elijah (2 Kings 1:8). Jewish scholars note that the expectation of Elijah's return before the Messianic age (Malachi 4:5-6) was widespread in Second Temple Judaism and remains part of Jewish liturgical practice (the cup of Elijah at the Passover Seder).

## The Heavenly Voice

The *bat qol* ("daughter of a voice") — the heavenly voice that declares "This is my beloved Son" (3:17) — has parallels in rabbinic literature. The Talmud (Berakhot 3a, Sotah 33a) records instances of the *bat qol* as a form of post-prophetic divine communication. Jewish scholars note that while the *bat qol* is recognized in rabbinic tradition, it does not carry the same authority as direct prophecy.
