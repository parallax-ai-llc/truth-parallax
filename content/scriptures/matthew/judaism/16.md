## Messianic Confession in Jewish Context

Peter's confession — "You are the Christ" (16:16) — employs the term *Mashiach* (Messiah, "Anointed One"), a concept with deep roots in Jewish tradition. Jewish Messianic expectation was diverse in the Second Temple period: some expected a Davidic king, others a priestly Messiah, others a prophetic figure, and some combined these roles. Jewish scholars note that Peter's confession reflects a specific Jewish hope, not a generic religious claim.

## "The Son of the Living God"

The title "Son of the living God" (16:16) is read by Jewish scholars in light of its Jewish background. In the Hebrew Bible, "son of God" can refer to angels (Genesis 6:2), Israel as a nation (Exodus 4:22), or the Davidic king (2 Samuel 7:14, Psalm 2:7). The term does not necessarily imply ontological divinity in Jewish usage. The christological interpretation that developed in later Christian theology represents a significant departure from this Jewish semantic range.

## Suffering and Messiahship

Jesus' prediction of suffering and death (16:21) contradicts the dominant Jewish Messianic expectation of a victorious, conquering figure. Jewish scholars note that while the concept of a suffering Messiah (*Mashiach ben Yosef*) appears in later rabbinic literature (Sukkah 52a), it was not the mainstream expectation. Peter's rebuke (16:22) reflects the natural Jewish reaction to the idea that God's anointed would be killed by the very powers he was expected to overthrow.
