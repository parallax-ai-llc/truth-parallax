## The Woes and Anti-Judaism

Matthew 23 is the most contested chapter in Jewish-Christian relations. The seven woes against "the scribes and Pharisees" have fueled centuries of anti-Jewish polemic and violence. Modern Jewish scholars, including Amy-Jill Levine, have argued that these woes must be understood as intra-Jewish prophetic critique — comparable to Jeremiah's critique of priests or Amos' critique of the wealthy — rather than as a blanket condemnation of Judaism. The prophetic tradition of harsh rhetoric against religious leaders is well-attested in the Hebrew Bible.

## Historical Context and Sectarian Polemic

Jewish scholars emphasize that the rhetoric of Matthew 23 reflects the bitterness of the "parting of the ways" — the gradual separation of early Christianity from its Jewish matrix in the late first century. The Matthean community was likely a Jewish-Christian group in conflict with the emerging rabbinic leadership after the destruction of the Temple (70 CE). The intensity of the polemic is proportional to the proximity of the communities: one criticizes most harshly those closest to oneself.

## The Blood Cry (27:25) and Its Reception

Although technically in Chapter 27, the trajectory of Matthew 23's rhetoric culminates in the crowd's cry, "His blood be on us and on our children" (27:25). Jewish scholars have traced the catastrophic reception history of this verse, from patristic invocations through medieval pogroms to modern antisemitism. The Second Vatican Council's *Nostra Aetate* (1965) represented a watershed: repudiating the charge of collective Jewish guilt and calling for a new relationship of mutual respect.
