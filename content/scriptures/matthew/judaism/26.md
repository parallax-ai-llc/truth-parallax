## The Passover Context

The Last Supper (26:17-30) takes place during Passover, the most important of the Jewish pilgrimage festivals. Jewish scholars have debated the relationship between the Last Supper and the Passover Seder. The Synoptic Gospels, including Matthew, present the meal as a Seder, while John places the crucifixion on the day of preparation, before the Seder. The bread and wine of the supper have parallels in the Passover meal's *matzah* and the four cups of wine, though the christological reinterpretation of these elements marks a departure from Jewish practice.

## Gethsemane and Jewish Prayer

Jesus' anguished prayer in Gethsemane (26:36-46) employs Jewish forms of address ("Abba, Father") and Jewish modes of prayer — prostration, petition, and submission to divine will. Jewish scholars note that the prayer, "Not as I will, but as you will" (26:39), echoes the rabbinic teaching of acceptance of divine decree (*kabbalat ol malkhut shamayim*). The scene is thoroughly Jewish in its piety and emotional texture.

## The Arrest and Jewish Legal Process

Jewish legal scholars have examined the trial proceedings described in Matthew 26:57-68 and noted significant irregularities according to Mishnaic law: capital trials were not to be held at night, not during festivals, and required at least two consistent witnesses. These observations have led to debates about whether the Mishnaic rules were in effect during Jesus' lifetime or were later codifications. The question of Jewish legal responsibility remains sensitive and complex.
