## Sinai Typology

Jewish scholars recognize the Transfiguration (17:1-8) as heavily dependent on Sinai imagery: the high mountain, the cloud, the divine voice, and the shining face all echo Moses' experience on Mount Sinai (Exodus 24, 34:29-35). The presence of Moses and Elijah represents the Torah and the Prophets — the two major divisions of the Jewish canon. Matthew's narrative presents Jesus as standing in continuity with, and surpassing, the greatest figures of Jewish tradition.

## The Divine Voice and Jewish Theology

The Father's declaration, "This is my beloved Son, listen to him" (17:5), echoes Deuteronomy 18:15, where Moses predicts a future prophet: "The Lord your God will raise up for you a prophet like me... you shall listen to him." Jewish scholars note that the identification of Jesus with this Mosaic prophet-figure was a claim intelligible within Jewish categories, even if its christological development exceeded Jewish expectations.

## The Temple Tax

The temple tax episode (17:24-27) provides evidence of Jesus' relationship to Jewish institutional life. The half-shekel tax (*shekalim*) was an annual obligation for adult Jewish males to support the Temple service (Exodus 30:11-16). Jewish scholars note that Jesus pays the tax, affirming his participation in Jewish communal obligations, while simultaneously asserting a unique filial relationship with God that exempts him in principle.
