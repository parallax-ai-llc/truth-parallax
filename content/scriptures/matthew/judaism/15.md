## Oral Torah and Written Torah

Jesus' debate with the Pharisees over handwashing (15:1-20) engages the central halakhic question of the authority of the Oral Torah. The Pharisaic tradition held that Moses received both a Written Torah and an Oral Torah at Sinai, the latter transmitted through an unbroken chain of tradition (Avot 1:1). Jesus' critique — "you have made void the word of God because of your tradition" (15:6) — challenges the authority of specific oral traditions, though Jewish scholars debate whether he rejects the principle of oral tradition itself.

## The Canaanite Woman and Jewish Particularism

The encounter with the Canaanite woman (15:21-28) reflects the tension between Jewish particularism and universalism. Jesus' initial refusal — "I was sent only to the lost sheep of the house of Israel" (15:24) — expresses a mission focused on Jewish restoration. Jewish scholars note that this particularist statement is remarkably candid and may preserve an authentic tradition about Jesus' self-understanding as a figure within Judaism, not the founder of a separate religion.

## Purity from the Heart

Jesus' teaching that defilement comes from the heart rather than from food (15:10-20) has been read as a radical departure from Jewish dietary law (*kashrut*). However, Jewish scholars note that the distinction between moral and ritual purity is present within Jewish tradition itself. The prophets consistently prioritized ethical conduct over ritual performance (Isaiah 1:11-17, Micah 6:6-8), and rabbinic literature recognizes that interior disposition (*kavvanah*) is essential to the validity of ritual action.
