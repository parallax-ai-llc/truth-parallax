## Community Discipline and Jewish Precedent

The procedure for addressing sin within the community (18:15-17) — private confrontation, witnesses, then communal adjudication — follows patterns attested in Jewish community practice. The Qumran Rule of the Community (1QS 5:25-6:1) describes a strikingly similar procedure for dealing with offenders. Jewish scholars see this as evidence that the Matthean community operated within recognizably Jewish institutional frameworks.

## Forgiveness and Jewish Ethics

Peter's question about forgiving "seven times" (18:21) and Jesus' answer of "seventy-seven times" (or "seventy times seven") engage the Jewish tradition of measuring forgiveness. Rabbinic sources discuss the question of how many times one must forgive an offender (some suggest three times, based on Amos 1:3). Jesus' hyperbolic answer pushes beyond any calculable limit, reflecting the Jewish theological conviction that God's mercy is infinite (*middah k'neged middah* — measure for measure, but divine mercy always exceeds).

## The Parable of the Unforgiving Servant

The Parable of the Unforgiving Servant (18:23-35) employs the Jewish concept of debt as a metaphor for sin — a concept found in the Lord's Prayer and widespread in rabbinic literature. Jewish scholars note that the parable's logic is consistent with the Jewish principle that human forgiveness is a prerequisite for divine forgiveness, as expressed in the Yom Kippur liturgy: the Day of Atonement atones for sins against God, but sins against fellow human beings require interpersonal reconciliation first (Mishnah Yoma 8:9).
