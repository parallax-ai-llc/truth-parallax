## The Sermon and Rabbinic Teaching

Jewish scholars have extensively compared the Sermon on the Mount with rabbinic ethical teaching. David Flusser demonstrated that many of Jesus' sayings have close parallels in rabbinic literature. The Beatitudes, for example, echo themes found in the Psalms and Wisdom literature. "Blessed are the peacemakers" parallels Hillel's teaching in Avot 1:12: "Be of the disciples of Aaron, loving peace and pursuing peace."

## The Antitheses and Halakhic Debate

The antitheses ("You have heard... but I say to you," 5:21-48) are recognized by Jewish scholars as a form of halakhic interpretation familiar from rabbinic disputes. Jesus' method of intensifying Torah commands — prohibiting not just murder but anger, not just adultery but lustful intent — employs the rabbinic technique of "building a fence around the Torah" (*seyag la-Torah*). The question is whether Jesus operates within or outside the bounds of legitimate halakhic discourse.

## "Be Perfect" and Jewish Ethics

The command "Be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect" (5:48) echoes Leviticus 19:2, "Be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy." Jewish scholars note that the concept of imitating God (*imitatio Dei*) is a central principle of Jewish ethics. The Talmud (Sotah 14a) teaches: "Just as God clothes the naked, visits the sick, and buries the dead, so should you." Matthew's Jesus stands within this tradition of ethical aspiration rooted in the divine character.
