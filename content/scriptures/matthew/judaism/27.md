## The Trial and Roman Authority

Jewish scholars emphasize that the crucifixion was a Roman form of execution, carried out by Roman authority, for a political charge (claiming to be "King of the Jews," 27:37). Crucifixion was not a Jewish penalty. The involvement of the high priestly establishment — the Sadducean aristocracy collaborating with Rome — must be distinguished from the Jewish people as a whole. Jewish scholarship has consistently argued that responsibility for Jesus' death lies primarily with the Roman imperial system.

## The Blood Cry and Its Legacy

The crowd's cry, "His blood be on us and on our children" (27:25), has been the single most destructive verse in Jewish-Christian history. Jewish scholars have traced its use from John Chrysostom's *Adversus Judaeos* sermons through the Crusades, the Inquisition, the pogroms, and into modern antisemitism. The verse, read as a divine curse on all Jews for all time, has been repudiated by mainstream Christian scholarship and by the Catholic Church's *Nostra Aetate*. Jewish scholars continue to call for responsible interpretation of this text.

## Death and Atonement

Jewish theology offers its own framework for atonement that does not depend on vicarious sacrifice. Repentance (*teshuvah*), prayer, and acts of loving-kindness replace sacrifice after the Temple's destruction (Hosea 14:2, "We will offer the words of our lips as bulls"). The Christian interpretation of Jesus' death as atoning sacrifice represents a significant theological departure from mainstream Jewish soteriology, though some scholars find precedents in the concept of the suffering righteous whose death benefits others (cf. 4 Maccabees 6:28-29).
