## The Entry into Jerusalem

The triumphal entry (21:1-11) deliberately enacts Zechariah 9:9: "your king is coming to you, humble and mounted on a donkey." Jewish scholars note that this prophetic text envisions a peaceable king, in contrast to the warrior-king expectations associated with other Messianic traditions. Jesus' deliberate staging of this entry represents a prophetic-symbolic action (*ma'aseh navi*) — a tradition in which prophets perform visible signs to communicate divine messages (cf. Jeremiah 19, Ezekiel 4).

## The Temple Action

The cleansing of the temple (21:12-17) has been interpreted by Jewish scholars in multiple ways. Some see it as a prophetic critique of commercial exploitation within the sacred space, consistent with Jeremiah's "den of robbers" oracle (7:11). Others, like E.P. Sanders, interpret it as a symbolic enactment of the temple's destruction and eschatological replacement — a more radical claim that goes beyond reform to replacement.

## Parables of Authority

The parables that follow (21:28-46) — the Two Sons and the Tenants — employ the vineyard imagery of Isaiah 5 and raise the question of legitimate religious authority. Jewish scholars note that the claim that "the kingdom of God will be taken away from you and given to a people producing its fruits" (21:43) reflects the rhetoric of sectarian conflict within Judaism, not an external judgment by a separate religion.
