## Apocalyptic Discourse and Jewish Literature

The Olivet Discourse has extensive parallels in Jewish apocalyptic literature — Daniel 7-12, 1 Enoch, 4 Ezra, and 2 Baruch. Jewish scholars read Matthew 24 within this rich tradition of eschatological speculation that flourished in the Second Temple period. The imagery of cosmic upheaval (24:29), the coming of the Son of Man (24:30), and the gathering of the elect (24:31) are drawn directly from Daniel 7:13-14 and related Jewish texts.

## The Destruction of the Temple

Jesus' prediction of the Temple's destruction (24:1-2) is a central issue in historical Jesus research. Jewish scholars note that warnings about the Temple's fate were not unique to Jesus — Josephus records several other prophetic figures who predicted Jerusalem's destruction. The question is whether Matthew's account reflects an authentic prediction or a post-70 CE composition shaped by knowledge of the Temple's actual destruction.

## The Son of Man

The title "Son of Man" (24:30) is one of the most debated terms in Jewish-Christian scholarship. In Jewish Aramaic, *bar enasha* can simply mean "a human being." In Daniel 7:13-14, the "one like a son of man" receives dominion from the Ancient of Days. Jewish scholars debate whether this figure was understood as an individual Messiah, a symbol of Israel, or an angelic being. Jesus' use of the title remains a crucial intersection of Jewish and Christian interpretation.
