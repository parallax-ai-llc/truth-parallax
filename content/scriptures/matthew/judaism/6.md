## Tzedakah, Prayer, and Fasting

The three practices addressed in Matthew 6 — almsgiving (*tzedakah*), prayer (*tefillah*), and fasting (*ta'anit*) — are the pillars of Jewish piety. Jewish scholars note that Jesus' instruction to practice them sincerely rather than ostentatiously parallels rabbinic warnings against performative piety. The Talmud (Berakhot 17a) teaches that prayer should be offered with *kavvanah* (directed intention), not as rote performance.

## The Lord's Prayer and Jewish Liturgy

The Lord's Prayer has extensive parallels with Jewish liturgical prayers. "Our Father who art in heaven" echoes the *Avinu Malkeinu* ("Our Father, our King"). "Hallowed be thy name" parallels the *Kaddish* ("May His great name be exalted and sanctified"). "Give us this day our daily bread" resonates with the daily provision acknowledged in the *Birkat Hamazon* (Grace after Meals). Jewish scholars read the Lord's Prayer as a thoroughly Jewish composition that draws on existing liturgical traditions.

## Anxiety and Trust in God

Jesus' teaching on anxiety (6:25-34) parallels the rabbinic concept of *bitahon* (trust in God). The Mishnah (Sotah 9:15) describes the quality of those who trust in God's provision even in difficult times. Jewish wisdom literature, particularly Psalms and Proverbs, addresses the tension between human effort and divine providence that Jesus navigates in this passage.
