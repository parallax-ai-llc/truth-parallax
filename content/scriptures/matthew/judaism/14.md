## Herod Antipas and Jewish Politics

The execution of John the Baptist by Herod Antipas (14:1-12) is attested by Josephus (*Antiquities* 18.5.2), who attributes Herod's motivation to political fear rather than the personal grudge described in the Gospels. Jewish scholars note that Josephus' account and Matthew's are not necessarily contradictory — political and personal motivations often intertwine. The episode illuminates the dangerous intersection of prophetic speech and political power in first-century Judea.

## Feeding Miracles and the Manna Tradition

The feeding of the five thousand (14:13-21) evokes the manna tradition of Exodus 16. Jewish Messianic expectation included the belief that the Messiah would repeat Moses' miracle of providing bread from heaven (2 Baruch 29:8). Jewish scholars read the feeding miracle as Matthew's deliberate presentation of Jesus as a new Moses, a claim that would have been both recognizable and controversial within the Jewish context.

## Walking on Water and Divine Power

The walking on water (14:22-33) draws on biblical imagery of God's sovereignty over the sea (Job 9:8, Psalm 77:19). Jewish scholars note that in the Hebrew Bible, power over the waters is an exclusively divine prerogative. Matthew's narrative, by attributing this power to Jesus, makes an implicit claim about his identity that would have been provocative in a Jewish monotheistic context.
