## Parables of Judgment and Jewish Ethics

The parables of Matthew 25 — the Ten Virgins, the Talents, and the Sheep and Goats — address themes of readiness, responsibility, and righteous action that resonate deeply with Jewish ethical tradition. The image of the bridegroom arriving at midnight echoes Jewish wedding customs and the rabbinic theme of the unpredictability of divine visitation. The Talmud asks, "What day will the Messiah come?" and answers with references to the need for constant readiness.

## The Sheep and the Goats

The judgment scene (25:31-46) is one of the most ethically compelling passages in the Gospels from a Jewish perspective. The criteria of judgment — feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, visiting the sick and imprisoned — correspond precisely to the Jewish concept of *gemilut hasadim* (acts of loving-kindness). The Talmud (Sotah 14a) lists these same acts as expressions of *imitatio Dei*. Jewish scholars note that this passage, more than any other in Matthew, presents an ethic that is recognizably and authentically Jewish.

## Ethical Monotheism

Jewish scholars have observed that the ethical vision of Matthew 25 — in which the ultimate criterion of divine judgment is care for the vulnerable — represents the highest expression of Jewish ethical monotheism within the New Testament. The identification of the divine with the suffering neighbor ("as you did it to one of the least of these, you did it to me," 25:40) has parallels in rabbinic teaching that God's presence (*Shekhinah*) dwells among the suffering.
