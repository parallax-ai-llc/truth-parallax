## Resurrection and Jewish Belief

The concept of resurrection (*tehiyat ha-metim*) was a matter of debate within Second Temple Judaism. The Pharisees affirmed bodily resurrection; the Sadducees denied it. The claim that Jesus rose from the dead (28:1-10) would have been intelligible to Pharisaic Jews, though they would have expected a general resurrection at the end of days, not the resurrection of a single individual in the middle of history. Jewish scholars note that this represents a significant innovation within Jewish eschatology.

## The Polemical Conclusion

Matthew 28:11-15 describes the chief priests bribing the guards to say that the disciples stole the body — a polemic that reflects the ongoing Jewish-Christian debate about the resurrection. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 43a) and the medieval *Toledot Yeshu* literature preserve Jewish counter-narratives about Jesus' death and the fate of his body. Jewish scholars read these exchanges as evidence of the continued proximity and mutual engagement of the two communities.

## The Parting and the Legacy

Matthew's Gospel concludes with the Great Commission to "make disciples of all nations" (28:19), which marks the decisive turn toward Gentile mission. For Jewish scholars, this moment represents the beginning of the theological "parting of the ways" that would eventually produce two distinct religions from a common matrix. The legacy of Matthew for Jewish-Christian relations is ambivalent: it preserves the most Jewish portrait of Jesus while also providing the textual basis for centuries of anti-Jewish interpretation. The ongoing work of Jewish-Christian dialogue seeks to honor the first dimension while repudiating the second.
