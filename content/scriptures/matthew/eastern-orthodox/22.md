## The Wedding Feast of the Kingdom

The Parable of the Wedding Banquet (22:1-14) is read in Orthodox liturgical theology as an image of the Divine Liturgy — the heavenly banquet made present on earth. The invitation goes out to all, but the wedding garment — understood as the "garment of light" received in baptism — must be maintained through repentance and the life of virtue. Orthodox baptismal liturgy includes the vesting of the newly baptized in a white garment, symbolizing this eschatological readiness.

## Render unto Caesar

Jesus' teaching on Caesar and God (22:15-22) has shaped the Orthodox understanding of the relationship between Church and state — historically expressed in the Byzantine concept of *symphonia* (harmony between imperial and ecclesiastical authority). While this ideal has been imperfectly realized, the theological principle remains: temporal authority is legitimate but subordinate to the kingdom of God.

## The Greatest Commandment

The double commandment of love (22:34-40) is the summary of Orthodox moral theology. Love of God is expressed supremely in worship — the Divine Liturgy as the offering of all creation back to its Creator. Love of neighbor is expressed in works of mercy and in the recognition of every human person as an icon of God, bearing the divine image regardless of condition or circumstance.
