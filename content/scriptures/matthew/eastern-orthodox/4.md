## The Temptation and Great Lent

The temptation narrative (4:1-11) provides the theological foundation for Great Lent in Orthodox practice. Jesus' forty-day fast is the model for the forty days of Lenten preparation before Pascha. The three temptations are understood as attacks on the three fundamental aspects of human vulnerability: bodily appetite (bread), vainglory (the temple pinnacle), and the desire for worldly power (the kingdoms of the earth).

## Spiritual Warfare

Orthodox ascetical theology, rooted in the Desert Fathers, reads this passage as the paradigm of spiritual combat. Every Christian faces temptation, and Jesus' weapon — the Word of God — is the believer's primary defense. The Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") functions in Orthodox practice as a continuous invocation of Christ's victory over the tempter.

## The Light of Galilee

Jesus' move to Galilee and the beginning of his public ministry (4:12-17) fulfill Isaiah's prophecy of light shining in darkness. Orthodox theology reads this as the uncreated Light of God entering the world — the same Light that will blaze forth on Mount Tabor at the Transfiguration. The calling of the first disciples (4:18-22) models the radical surrender that characterizes the monastic vocation, which Orthodoxy regards as the most complete form of Christian discipleship.
