## Discernment and Spiritual Fruits

Orthodox moral theology reads Jesus' teaching on judgment (7:1-5) through the lens of the patristic distinction between judging actions and judging persons. St. John Climacus warns that the impulse to judge others is itself a sign of spiritual immaturity. The test of "fruits" (7:15-20) provides the criterion for discerning true from false spiritual teachers — a principle applied in the Orthodox tradition to the evaluation of spiritual fathers and elders.

## The Narrow Way

The narrow gate (7:13-14) resonates deeply with Orthodox ascetical theology. The monastic tradition, from St. Anthony of Egypt to the Athonite fathers, embodies this narrow path through fasting, vigil, and unceasing prayer. Yet Orthodox theology insists that the narrow way is not reserved for monks alone — every Christian is called to the ascetical struggle appropriate to their state of life.

## Building on Rock

The parable of the wise and foolish builders (7:24-27) concludes the Sermon with a call to praxis — not merely hearing but doing. Orthodox theology resists any separation of faith from works: salvation is the living union of belief and practice, sustained by grace and enacted in the liturgical and moral life of the Church. The crowds' astonishment at Jesus' authority (7:28-29) points to the uniqueness of Christ as the incarnate Word — the source, not merely a transmitter, of divine truth.
