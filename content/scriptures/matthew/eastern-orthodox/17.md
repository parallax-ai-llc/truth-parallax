## The Transfiguration on Mount Tabor

The Transfiguration (17:1-8) is one of the twelve Great Feasts of the Orthodox Church, celebrated on August 6/19. It holds a theological importance in Orthodoxy unparalleled in Western Christianity. The light that shone from Christ on Tabor is understood not as created light but as the uncreated divine energies — the same energies by which God created the world and by which the saints are deified. St. Gregory Palamas (14th century) defended this doctrine against the nominalist critique of Barlaam, establishing the Tabor light as a pillar of Orthodox theology.

## Theosis and the Uncreated Light

The Transfiguration reveals humanity's ultimate destiny: to be transformed by the uncreated light of God. The disciples' vision of Christ in glory is a foretaste of the eschatological transformation of all creation. Orthodox iconography depicts the Transfiguration with mandorla rays emanating from Christ, touching the three disciples — the light is not merely seen but participates in transforming the beholder.

## The Epileptic Boy and Prayer

The healing of the epileptic boy (17:14-21) after the disciples' failure teaches the necessity of prayer and fasting — the twin pillars of Orthodox ascetical life. Jesus' words, "This kind never comes out except by prayer and fasting" (17:21, in some manuscripts), ground the Orthodox practice of regular fasting as a spiritual discipline inseparable from prayer.
