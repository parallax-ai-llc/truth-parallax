## Great and Holy Friday

The trial and crucifixion of Jesus (27:1-56) are the subject of the most solemn Orthodox liturgical observances. On Great and Holy Friday, the faithful venerate the cross, and the *Epitaphios* (burial shroud) is processed through the church. The hymns of the day contemplate the paradox: the Creator of all things hangs upon the cross; the Lord of life submits to death; the one who cannot be contained is laid in a tomb.

## The Cry of Dereliction

Jesus' cry, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (27:46), is read in Orthodox theology not as evidence of the Father's abandonment but as Christ's voluntary entry into the full depth of human alienation from God. By descending into the furthest reaches of human suffering — even the experience of godforsakenness — Christ sanctifies and redeems that suffering from within. The *kenosis* (self-emptying) of the cross is the ultimate expression of divine love.

## Death, Burial, and Descent into Hades

The tearing of the temple veil (27:51), the earthquake, and the opening of tombs (27:52-53) signal the cosmic scope of the crucifixion. Orthodox theology reads Jesus' death as the defeat of death itself. The burial by Joseph of Arimathea prepares for the central mystery of Holy Saturday: Christ's descent into Hades, where he tramples down death by death and liberates Adam and Eve — an event celebrated as the beginning of the resurrection.
