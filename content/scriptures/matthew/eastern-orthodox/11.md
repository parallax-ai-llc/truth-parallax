## The Holy Forerunner

Orthodox veneration of John the Baptist as the "Holy Forerunner and Baptist" is reflected in the placement of his icon on the iconostasis — to the left of Christ, paired with the Theotokos on the right. Jesus' praise of John as the greatest "born of women" (11:11) confirms his unique status in the Orthodox calendar, where he is commemorated more frequently than any saint except the Theotokos.

## Divine Wisdom Revealed to the Humble

Jesus' prayer of thanksgiving (11:25-27) — revealing that divine wisdom is hidden from the wise and given to "little children" — resonates with the Orthodox tradition of holy fools (*yurodivy*) and the spiritual wisdom of simple monastics. Knowledge of God comes not through intellectual accomplishment but through humility, prayer, and purity of heart.

## Rest and the Easy Yoke

The invitation "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden" (11:28) is heard in Orthodox worship as Christ's perpetual call, extended through the Divine Liturgy and the sacraments. The "easy yoke" is understood as the life of grace within the Church — demanding, yet lighter than the burden of sin and self-reliance. St. Seraphim of Sarov's greeting, "Christ is risen, my joy!" embodies this experience of lightness in the midst of ascetical struggle.
