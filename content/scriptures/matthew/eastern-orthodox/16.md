## The Confession of Faith

Peter's confession at Caesarea Philippi (16:13-20) is the climactic christological moment of the Gospel. Orthodox theology affirms that "the rock" on which the Church is built is the faith that Peter confesses — faith in Jesus as the Christ, the Son of the living God. While honoring Peter's primacy of honor among the apostles, Orthodoxy does not derive from this passage a doctrine of papal jurisdiction. The authority of binding and loosing belongs to the apostolic community as a whole, exercised through the conciliar structure of the Church.

## The Cross and Self-Denial

Jesus' first passion prediction (16:21-23) and the call to take up the cross (16:24-28) define Christian discipleship in Orthodox terms as the path of *kenosis* — self-emptying love modeled on Christ's own journey to Golgotha. The sign of the cross, made constantly by Orthodox Christians, is a physical confession that the way of the cross is the way of life.

## Losing and Finding Life

The paradox of losing life to find it (16:25) is central to Orthodox spiritual theology. The monastic tradition, in particular, embodies this paradox: by renouncing worldly security, the monk gains the freedom to pursue union with God. But the principle applies to every Christian: genuine life is found only in self-surrendering love.
