## Eschatology and the Age to Come

Orthodox interpretation of the Olivet Discourse is shaped by the patristic consensus that apocalyptic imagery should be read with spiritual sobriety, not sensationalist speculation. The Fathers generally distinguished between the destruction of Jerusalem (fulfilled in AD 70) and the final coming of Christ (still awaited). Orthodox theology affirms the Second Coming as a central article of the Creed: "He will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead, and his kingdom will have no end."

## Signs, Tribulation, and Watchfulness

The "signs" of Matthew 24 — wars, earthquakes, famines, false prophets — describe the ongoing character of the present age, not a sequence of countdown events. Orthodox theology resists date-setting and apocalyptic calculation, emphasizing instead the call to constant spiritual watchfulness (*nepsis*). Each generation lives in the eschatological tension between the "already" of Christ's first coming and the "not yet" of his return.

## The Coming of the Son of Man

The image of the Son of Man coming "on the clouds of heaven with power and great glory" (24:30) is the hope that sustains Orthodox worship. Every Divine Liturgy is an anticipation of the parousia — the heavenly kingdom made present in the Eucharistic assembly. The Liturgy's exclamation, "Maranatha — Come, Lord!" captures this posture of eager, joyful expectation.
