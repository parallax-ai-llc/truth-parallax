## The Forgiveness of Sins

The healing of the paralytic (9:1-8) is significant in Orthodox theology because it demonstrates that the Son of Man has authority to forgive sins on earth. Orthodox sacramental theology sees in this passage a foundation for the Mystery of Confession — the Church's ongoing ministry of reconciliation and healing. The link between physical healing and forgiveness of sins reflects the Orthodox holistic understanding of the human person as a unity of body and soul.

## The Calling of Matthew

The tax collector's immediate response to Jesus' call (9:9) exemplifies the Orthodox understanding of *metanoia* — a complete turning of the whole person toward God. The feast with "tax collectors and sinners" (9:10-13) reveals that Christ's table is open to all who repent. Orthodox liturgical practice reflects this radical hospitality: the Eucharistic liturgy is understood as the heavenly banquet made present on earth.

## New Wine and the Kingdom

The parable of new wine in old wineskins (9:16-17) is read in Orthodox theology not as a rejection of tradition but as an affirmation that the new covenant, while fulfilling the old, requires a transformed vessel — the renewed human person, the Church, the sacramental life. The harvest metaphor (9:36-38) grounds the Orthodox missionary imperative and the tradition of monastic evangelism that brought Christianity to the Slavic peoples.
