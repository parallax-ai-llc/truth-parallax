## The Mystical Supper

The Last Supper (26:26-29) is known in Orthodox tradition as the "Mystical Supper" and is depicted in one of the most prominent icons in every Orthodox church. Jesus' words — "This is my body... this is my blood" — are understood in Orthodox theology as effecting a real transformation of the bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ, though Orthodoxy avoids the scholastic term "transubstantiation," preferring the language of mystery and divine action through the Holy Spirit (*epiclesis*).

## Gethsemane and the Agony

Jesus' agony in Gethsemane (26:36-46) reveals the fullness of his human nature — capable of fear, sorrow, and the desire to avoid suffering. Orthodox Christology, defined at Chalcedon (451), affirms two complete natures in one Person. The prayer "Not as I will, but as you will" (26:39) is the supreme act of human obedience, reversing Adam's disobedience in the Garden of Eden. Orthodox Holy Thursday services reenact Gethsemane's vigil.

## Betrayal and Denial

Judas' betrayal (26:47-50) and Peter's denial (26:69-75) frame the Passion as a moment of total human failure. Orthodox Holy Week hymns dwell on the contrast between Judas, who despaired, and Peter, who wept and was restored. The message is clear: the path from sin to salvation runs through repentance, not despair.
