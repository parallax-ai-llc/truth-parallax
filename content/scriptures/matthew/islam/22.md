## God's Sovereignty

The Parable of the Wedding Banquet (22:1-14) is read in Islamic thought as illustrating God's sovereign invitation and human accountability. Those who reject God's invitation face consequences; those who accept must come prepared. The Quran describes a similar dynamic: "Invite to the way of your Lord" (16:125), and those who refuse divine guidance bring judgment upon themselves. The guest without a wedding garment represents those who approach God without sincerity (*ikhlas*).

## Render unto Caesar and Islamic Governance

Jesus' teaching on paying taxes to Caesar (22:15-22) is compared by Muslim scholars with Islamic principles of political authority. Islam recognizes legitimate civil authority while insisting on God's ultimate sovereignty. The Quran states: "Obey Allah, and obey the Messenger, and those in authority among you" (4:59), establishing a hierarchy in which political obedience is subordinate to divine command.

## The Greatest Commandment and Tawhid

Jesus' identification of the greatest commandment — love God with all your heart (22:37) — is enthusiastically affirmed by Muslim scholars as an expression of *tawhid*, the absolute oneness and priority of God. The Shema ("Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one"), which underlies Jesus' answer, corresponds directly to the Islamic declaration of faith (*shahada*): "There is no god but Allah." The command to love the neighbor (22:39) parallels the Islamic obligation of *ihsan* — excellence in conduct toward others.
