## The Temptation and Prophetic Integrity

The temptation narrative (4:1-11) is read in Islamic context as consistent with the concept of prophetic trial (*ibtila'*). The Quran affirms that Satan (*Iblis/Shaytan*) is the sworn enemy of humanity (2:168, 7:27) and seeks to lead people astray. Jesus' resistance to Satan's temptations demonstrates the quality of *ismah* (prophetic protection from sin) that Islam attributes to all prophets — they may face trials but are divinely protected from falling into sin.

## Reliance on Scripture

Jesus' responses from Deuteronomy align with the Islamic emphasis on *tawakkul* (reliance on God) and the authority of revealed scripture. Muslim scholars note approvingly that Jesus appeals to God's word rather than to personal power or status. This is consistent with the Islamic view that prophets are servants of God's revelation, not independent sources of authority.

## The Beginning of Prophetic Ministry

Jesus' proclamation, "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (4:17), is interpreted in Islam as a prophetic call to *tawbah* (repentance) and submission to God's will. The "kingdom of heaven" is understood not as a christological claim but as the sovereignty of God that all prophets proclaim. The calling of disciples parallels Muhammad's gathering of companions (*sahabah*) — a recognized prophetic pattern.
