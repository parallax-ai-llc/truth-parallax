## The Resurrection and Ascension

Islam does not accept the resurrection of Jesus as described in Matthew 28. Instead, Islamic theology teaches that Jesus was raised alive to heaven (*rafa'ahu Allahu ilayhi*, "Allah raised him to Himself," 4:158) before or instead of crucifixion. The post-resurrection appearances described in the Gospels are understood as either accounts of Jesus after his divine rescue (before his ascension) or as later theological constructions designed to support the doctrine of bodily resurrection.

## The Great Commission and Islamic Critique

The Great Commission (28:18-20) — commanding baptism "in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" — is identified by Muslim scholars as evidence of *tahrif* (textual alteration). The Trinitarian formula is understood as a later theological addition that Jesus, as a strict monotheist, would never have uttered. Muslim scholars argue that the original *Injil* commissioned disciples to teach the oneness of God and righteous living, not a Trinitarian theology.

## Jesus in Islamic Perspective: A Summary

Matthew's Gospel, read through an Islamic lens, presents a complex portrait. Muslims affirm Jesus as a prophet of extraordinary power and moral beauty, born of a virgin, worker of miracles by God's permission, and teacher of profound ethical wisdom. They reject the theological framework that makes him divine, the narrative that has him crucified, and the Trinitarian formula that concludes the Gospel. For Islam, the true Jesus is the one described in the Quran: *Isa ibn Maryam, al-Masih* — the Messiah, son of Mary, a servant and messenger of God.
