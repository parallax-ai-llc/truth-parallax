## Divine Justice and Mercy

The Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard (20:1-16) illustrates God's sovereign generosity, a theme central to Islamic theology. The Quran repeatedly emphasizes that God's mercy encompasses all things (7:156) and that God rewards not according to strict accounting but according to his bounty (*fadl*). The parable's message — that divine generosity transcends human calculations of fairness — aligns with the Islamic understanding that God's mercy always exceeds his justice.

## Service and Humility

Jesus' teaching on servant leadership (20:25-28) resonates with the Islamic concept of leadership as stewardship (*amanah*). The Prophet Muhammad is described in the hadith as the servant of his community, mending his own clothes and milking his own goats. Muslim scholars see in Jesus' rejection of domineering authority a prophetic principle shared by all God's messengers.

## The Ransom Saying

The statement that the Son of Man came "to give his life as a ransom for many" (20:28) is the point of sharpest divergence between Islamic and Christian soteriology. Islam rejects the concept of vicarious atonement. Each person is accountable for their own deeds: "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (6:164). Muslim scholars read the "ransom" saying as a later theological addition to the original prophetic teaching.
