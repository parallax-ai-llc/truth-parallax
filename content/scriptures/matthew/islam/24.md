## The End Times and Islamic Eschatology

The Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24) has significant parallels with Islamic eschatology. Islam teaches that the end times will be preceded by signs (*ashrat al-sa'ah*): wars, earthquakes, the appearance of false prophets (*dajjal*), and moral corruption. These correspond closely to the signs described by Jesus in Matthew 24:4-14. Islamic eschatology is deeply engaged with the figure of Jesus, who is expected to return at the end of days.

## The Return of Isa

Islamic tradition holds that Jesus was not crucified but was raised by God to heaven (4:157-158) and will return before the Day of Judgment. This belief, drawn from hadith literature (Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim), envisions Jesus descending in Damascus, defeating the *Dajjal* (Antichrist), breaking the cross, abolishing the *jizyah* (tax on non-Muslims), and establishing justice on earth. The Olivet Discourse's language about the "coming of the Son of Man" (24:30) is interpreted by Muslim scholars as a prophecy of this return.

## Watchfulness and the Day of Judgment

Jesus' call to watchfulness (24:42-44) aligns with the Quranic emphasis on being prepared for the Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*): "They ask you about the Hour: when is its arrival? Say, 'Its knowledge is only with my Lord'" (7:187). The shared insistence on constant readiness and the impossibility of knowing the exact time reflects a common prophetic message.
