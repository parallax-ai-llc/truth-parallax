## Critique of Religious Hypocrisy

Jesus' woes against the scribes and Pharisees (23:1-36) are read by Muslim scholars as a prophetic critique applicable to religious leaders of every tradition. The Quran similarly warns against scholars who distort God's word for personal gain: "Woe to those who write the book with their own hands, then say, 'This is from Allah,' to exchange it for a small price" (2:79). Muslim commentators have applied both Jesus' woes and this Quranic verse to corrupt clergy, scholars, and leaders within all religious communities, including Islam.

## Sincerity Over Display

The recurring charge of hypocrisy (*nifaq*) resonates powerfully with Islamic ethics. The Quran devotes extensive attention to the *munafiqun* (hypocrites) who profess faith outwardly while concealing disbelief. Surah al-Munafiqun (63) addresses this theme directly. Muslim scholars see in Jesus' critique a prophetic warning that applies whenever religious practice becomes performative rather than sincere.

## The Lament over Jerusalem

Jesus' lament over Jerusalem (23:37-39) expresses prophetic grief over a city that rejected God's messengers. The Quran records similar prophetic grief: Noah laments over his unbelieving son (11:42-47), and Muhammad grieves over those who reject his message (18:6). Muslim scholars see in this lament evidence of Jesus' authentic prophetic compassion — a quality that transcends doctrinal boundaries.
