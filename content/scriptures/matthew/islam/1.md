## The Lineage of Isa ibn Maryam

The Islamic perspective on Matthew's genealogy (1:1-17) acknowledges Jesus' (*Isa*) Davidic and Abrahamic lineage as consistent with the Quranic identification of Jesus as an Israelite prophet. However, Islam does not require a genealogical claim to establish prophetic authority — prophethood is bestowed by God's will alone. The theological significance that Christians derive from the genealogy (Messianic kingship, fulfillment of covenant promises) is reframed in Islamic thought: Jesus is honored not as a king but as a prophet and messenger (*rasul*).

## The Virgin Birth

Islam affirms the virgin birth of Jesus as a miraculous act of divine power. Surah Maryam (19:16-34) and Surah Al Imran (3:45-47) provide detailed accounts of the annunciation and birth. Mary (*Maryam*) is the most honored woman in the Quran, with an entire surah bearing her name. However, Islam categorically rejects the theological conclusion Christians draw from the virgin birth — that Jesus is the Son of God. The virgin birth demonstrates God's power (*qudrah*), not Jesus' divinity.

## Divergence on Joseph and Immanuel

Islamic tradition does not assign Joseph the theological role that Matthew's narrative gives him. The name "Immanuel" ("God with us," 1:23) is reinterpreted: God is with humanity through his prophets, his revelation, and his mercy, but this does not imply incarnation. The concept of God taking human form contradicts the absolute transcendence of *tawhid*.
