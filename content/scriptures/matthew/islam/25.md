## Accountability Before God

The parables of Matthew 25 — the Ten Virgins, the Talents, and the Sheep and Goats — all address the theme of accountability before God, which is central to Islamic theology. The Quran emphasizes that every person will be held accountable: "Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it" (99:7-8). The Parable of the Talents, in which stewardship of God's gifts is evaluated, resonates with the Islamic concept of *amanah* (trust/stewardship).

## The Sheep and the Goats

The judgment scene (25:31-46), in which the criterion of judgment is care for the hungry, thirsty, naked, sick, and imprisoned, aligns remarkably with Islamic ethical teaching. Feeding the hungry and caring for the orphan are repeatedly emphasized in the Quran (76:8-9, 107:1-3, 90:12-16). The hadith tradition records that God will say on the Day of Judgment: "I was sick and you did not visit me... My servant was sick and you did not visit him" (Sahih Muslim) — a striking parallel to Matthew 25:43-44.

## Justice and Mercy

Muslim scholars have frequently cited the Sheep and Goats passage as evidence that Jesus taught an ethical monotheism consistent with Islamic values. The identification of service to the poor with service to God echoes the Sufi principle that the divine is encountered in the neighbor. Ibn Arabi taught that every human being is a *tajalli* (manifestation) of the divine — a concept that resonates with Jesus' teaching, "as you did it to one of the least of these, you did it to me" (25:40).
