## Overview

Islamic engagement with the Gospel of Matthew is shaped by the Quran's affirmation of Jesus (*Isa ibn Maryam*) as one of the greatest prophets and the recipient of the *Injil* (Gospel) — a divine revelation that Muslims believe has been partially preserved but also altered (*tahrif*) in its present textual form. Muslim scholars read Matthew critically, affirming passages that align with Quranic theology while identifying points of divergence as evidence of textual or doctrinal corruption.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Tahrif (Alteration)
Muslims hold that the original *Injil* revealed to Jesus was a unified divine message, not four Gospels written by later authors. The canonical Gospels, including Matthew, are understood as human compositions that contain fragments of authentic revelation alongside theological additions — particularly the doctrines of divine sonship, Trinity, and atonement.

### Quranic Corrective
The Quran serves as the criterion (*furqan*) for evaluating Matthean content. Where Matthew's Jesus teaches monotheism, charity, humility, and prayer, Muslims recognize echoes of the original prophetic message. Where Matthew attributes divinity to Jesus, Muslims identify *tahrif*.

## Theological Emphases

- **Jesus as Prophet**: Affirmed as *al-Masih* (the Messiah), a mighty prophet, but categorically not divine
- **The Virgin Birth**: Accepted (cf. Quran 3:45-47; Matthew 1:18-25) as a miraculous sign of God's power
- **Ethical Teaching**: The Sermon on the Mount's emphasis on sincerity, charity, and trust in God resonates with Islamic ethics
- **Rejection of Crucifixion**: Islam teaches that Jesus was not crucified (Quran 4:157); Matthew's Passion narrative is read as inaccurate
