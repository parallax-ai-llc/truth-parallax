## Marriage in Islam

Jesus' teaching on marriage and divorce (19:1-9) is compared by Muslim scholars with Islamic marital law. Islam permits divorce (*talaq*) under certain conditions, viewing it as the "most hated of permissible things" (Abu Dawud). The Quranic approach balances the ideal of marital permanence with the recognition that some marriages become untenable. Muslim scholars note that Jesus' strict prohibition of divorce (except for *porneia*) differs from the Islamic allowance, but both traditions affirm the sanctity of marriage.

## Celibacy and Islam

Jesus' saying about those who become "eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven" (19:12) diverges significantly from Islamic teaching. Islam strongly encourages marriage and views celibacy with suspicion. The Prophet Muhammad is reported to have said: "Marriage is my way; whoever turns away from my way is not of me" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Christian monasticism and celibacy are critiqued in the Quran: "We prescribed for them monasticism, which they invented; We did not prescribe it for them" (57:27).

## Wealth and the Afterlife

The encounter with the rich young man (19:16-22) and the teaching about wealth and eternal life resonate with Islamic eschatology. The concept of *akhirah* (the afterlife) and the danger of worldly attachment (*dunya*) are central to Quranic teaching. The Quran warns: "Know that the life of this world is but amusement and diversion and adornment and boasting to one another" (57:20). Jesus' counsel to prioritize eternal life over material wealth is fully consistent with Islamic values.
