## Parables and Prophetic Teaching

Jesus' use of parables in Matthew 13 is understood in Islam as consistent with the prophetic method of teaching through stories and metaphors. The Quran itself employs parables extensively: "Allah does not hesitate to set forth a parable" (2:26). The Parable of the Sower's varied soils parallels the Quranic observation that people respond to divine guidance differently: "Among the people is he who worships Allah on an edge; if good touches him, he is reassured; but if trial strikes him, he turns on his face" (22:11).

## The Kingdom of Heaven

Jesus' teaching about the "kingdom of heaven" is reinterpreted in Islamic terms as the sovereignty of God (*hukm Allah*) over all creation. Islam does not adopt the specifically Christian eschatological framework of a kingdom inaugurated by the Messiah. Rather, God's sovereignty is eternal and unconditioned — it does not "arrive" but is the permanent reality that humans are called to recognize and submit to.

## Hidden Wisdom

The notion that parables conceal truth from the unworthy (13:11-13) resonates with the Sufi concept of layered meaning in sacred texts. Sufi scholars have read Jesus' parables alongside Quranic verses as containing both an outer (*zahir*) and inner (*batin*) meaning. The pearl of great price (13:46) has been interpreted in Sufi tradition as *ma'rifah* — direct experiential knowledge of God, worth sacrificing everything to attain.
