## The Sermon on the Mount and Islamic Ethics

The Sermon on the Mount contains ethical teachings that resonate deeply with Islamic moral theology. The emphasis on sincerity over ostentation, the priority of interior piety, and the call to trust in divine provision all find parallels in Quranic teaching. Muslim scholars such as al-Ghazali (d. 1111) in his *Ihya Ulum al-Din* quoted Sermon on the Mount teachings approvingly as examples of prophetic wisdom preserved in the Christian tradition.

## The Beatitudes and Islamic Virtues

Many of the Beatitudes align with Islamic virtues: the poor in spirit (*tawadu'*, humility), the merciful (*rahmah*), the peacemakers (*islah*), and the pure in heart (*ikhlas*, sincerity). The Quran's own beatitudes include: "Successful indeed are the believers who are humble in their prayers" (23:1-2). Islamic scholars see in the Beatitudes evidence of the authentic prophetic teaching that Jesus brought.

## Points of Divergence

However, Islamic theology diverges from the Sermon's Matthean context. The antitheses ("You have heard... but I say to you") are read cautiously: Islam honors Jesus' prophetic authority to interpret God's law but does not accept the christological implication that Jesus possesses authority equal to or surpassing God's. The command to "be perfect as your heavenly Father is perfect" (5:48) is reframed: striving for moral excellence (*ihsan*) is a duty, but the language of divine "fatherhood" is rejected as incompatible with *tawhid*.
