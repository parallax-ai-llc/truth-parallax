## Charity, Prayer, and Fasting in Islam

Matthew 6's three pillars — almsgiving, prayer, fasting — correspond precisely to three of Islam's Five Pillars: *zakat* (obligatory charity), *salat* (prayer), and *sawm* (fasting during Ramadan). Muslim scholars see in Jesus' teaching a prophetic instruction entirely consistent with Islamic practice. The emphasis on performing these acts with sincerity rather than for public display resonates with the Islamic concept of *ikhlas* (sincerity of intention).

## The Lord's Prayer and Islamic Parallels

Several elements of the Lord's Prayer (6:9-13) parallel Islamic prayer concepts. "Hallowed be thy name" echoes the constant Islamic invocation of God's names (*al-Asma al-Husna*). "Thy kingdom come, thy will be done" corresponds to the Islamic concept of submission to God's will (*islam*). "Give us this day our daily bread" parallels the trust in divine provision (*rizq*). However, the address "Our Father" is rejected in Islamic theology — God is addressed as *Rabb* (Lord), *Allah*, or by his beautiful names, never as "Father."

## Trust in Divine Provision

Jesus' teaching on anxiety (6:25-34) closely parallels the Quranic concept of *tawakkul* — trusting in God's provision. The Quran states: "And whoever relies upon Allah — then He is sufficient for him" (65:3). Muslim ascetics and Sufis have drawn on these Matthean teachings alongside Quranic verses to develop a rich theology of dependence on God. Al-Ghazali's discussion of *tawakkul* in the *Ihya* explicitly cites similar prophetic teachings.
