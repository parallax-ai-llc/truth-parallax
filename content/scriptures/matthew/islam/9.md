## Forgiveness and Prophetic Authority

The healing of the paralytic (9:1-8), in which Jesus forgives sins, raises a critical theological issue for Islam. In Islamic theology, only Allah forgives sins (3:135, 39:53). Muslim scholars interpret Jesus' pronouncement of forgiveness as conveying God's message of forgiveness to the individual — an act of prophetic mediation, not an exercise of personal divine authority. The distinction between conveying God's forgiveness and claiming the power to forgive is theologically essential.

## Mercy and Compassion

Jesus' declaration, "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" (9:13), resonates strongly with Islamic theology. The Quran describes Allah as *al-Rahman al-Rahim* (the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful) — attributes invoked at the beginning of every surah. The priority of mercy over ritual formalism is a shared prophetic principle. Muslim scholars have frequently quoted Jesus' compassionate sayings as evidence of his authentic prophetic character.

## The Harvest and Da'wah

Jesus' compassion for the crowds and his call for workers (9:36-38) parallels the Islamic concept of *da'wah* — the invitation to God's path. The Quran instructs: "Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good instruction" (16:125). Muslim scholars see in Jesus' compassion the same urgency that characterizes Muhammad's mission: the people are in need of divine guidance, and prophets and their followers must bring it to them.
