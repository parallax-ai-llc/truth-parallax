## The Martyrdom of Yahya

The execution of John the Baptist (14:1-12) by Herod is regarded in Islam as the martyrdom of a prophet — an event of great gravity. The Quran acknowledges that the Israelites killed prophets unjustly (2:61, 3:21). Muslim commentators include Yahya's death in the *Qisas al-Anbiya'* (Stories of the Prophets) as a sobering example of worldly power opposing divine truth. The moral lesson — that faithfulness to God may cost one's life — is a recurring theme in Islamic tradition.

## The Feeding Miracle

The feeding of the five thousand (14:13-21) is understood as a *mu'jizah* (prophetic miracle) granted by God's permission. The Quran records a related tradition: the disciples of Jesus asked for "a table spread from heaven" (*ma'idah*), and God sent it as a sign (5:112-115). Muslim commentators see the feeding miracles as confirmation of Jesus' prophethood, demonstrating God's power to provide through his chosen messenger.

## Walking on Water

Jesus walking on water (14:22-33) is accepted as another miracle by divine permission. Muslim scholars emphasize that such miracles do not indicate divinity but prophetic distinction. The Quran records comparable miraculous events for other prophets: Moses parting the sea (26:63), Solomon commanding the wind (34:12). Each miracle serves to authenticate the prophet's message, not to establish the prophet's divine nature.
