## The Sabbath and Islamic Jurisprudence

The Sabbath controversies (12:1-14) are read in Islamic context through the Quranic references to the Sabbath. The Quran mentions the Sabbath as a commandment given specifically to the Jews (16:124) and records the punishment of those who violated it (7:163-166). Islam does not observe the Sabbath; Friday (*Jumu'ah*) is the day of congregational prayer but not a day of rest in the Sabbath sense. Jesus' assertion of authority over the Sabbath is read as a prophetic prerogative to interpret divine law for his community.

## Signs and Prophetic Authentication

The Pharisees' demand for a sign (12:38) and Jesus' response about the sign of Jonah parallel the Quranic accounts of peoples demanding signs from their prophets. The Quran records that the Quraysh demanded miracles from Muhammad (17:90-93), and God's response was that the Quran itself is the sufficient sign. Muslim scholars see a parallel in Jesus' refusal to perform signs on demand — true faith does not rest on spectacle but on recognition of divine truth.

## Blasphemy Against the Holy Spirit

Jesus' teaching about blasphemy against the Holy Spirit (12:31-32) is reinterpreted in Islamic theology. The "Holy Spirit" (*Ruh al-Qudus*) in Islam refers to the angel Jibril (Gabriel), not a divine person. Blasphemy against God's signs and messengers is a grave sin in Islam (2:87-88), but the Trinitarian framework of the "unforgivable sin" is not applicable within Islamic theology.
