## The Temple as the Demiurge's House

The cleansing of the temple (21:12-17) is read in Gnostic exegesis as the Christ's direct assault on the Demiurge's central institution. The Jerusalem temple was the Demiurge's earthly seat of power — the place where his law was administered and his cult maintained. Jesus' action is not reform but prophetic judgment: the Demiurge's system is corrupt at its core, and the true worship of the transcendent Father has no need for temples, sacrifices, or priesthoods.

## The Fig Tree and Fruitless Religion

The cursing of the fig tree (21:18-22) symbolizes the barrenness of the Demiurge's religion. Despite its outward appearance of life (leaves), it produces no spiritual fruit (*gnosis*). The institutional religion of the Old Testament God — its laws, rituals, and prophecies — promises nourishment but delivers none. Only the Christ's teaching bears the fruit of genuine spiritual awakening.

## The Tenants and the Archons

The Parable of the Tenants (21:33-46) is read as an allegory of cosmic history: the vineyard is the material world; the owner is the transcendent Father (not the Demiurge); the tenants are the archons who have usurped control; the servants are the prophets who brought fragments of truth; and the Son is the Christ-aeon sent to reclaim what belongs to the Father. The tenants' murder of the Son is the archons' attempt to destroy the Pleromatic intruder — an attempt that, in Gnostic theology, ultimately fails.
