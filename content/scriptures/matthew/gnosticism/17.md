## The Transfiguration as Pleromatic Revelation

The Transfiguration (17:1-8) is one of the most theologically significant passages for Gnostic Christology. The radiant light that shines from Jesus is understood as the uncovering of his true Pleromatic nature — the divine Christ-aeon made visible through the material veil. For a moment, the three disciples glimpse reality beyond the Demiurge's domain: the light of the Pleroma that the archons' darkness cannot comprehend.

## Moses, Elijah, and the Transcended Dispensation

The presence of Moses and Elijah is interpreted ambiguously. In one reading, they represent the highest achievements of the Demiurge's dispensation — the Law and the Prophets — now subordinated to the Christ. The Father's command, "Listen to him" (17:5), supersedes the authority of both. In another reading, Moses and Elijah are themselves pneumatic figures who had already attained *gnosis* and appear as the Christ's spiritual equals, confirming the unity of all who have escaped the Demiurge's domain.

## The Disciples' Fear

The disciples' terror (17:6) and Jesus' reassuring touch illustrate the Gnostic theme that encountering the divine is initially overwhelming. The archon-conditioned mind cannot immediately absorb the Pleromatic light. The instruction to "tell no one" (17:9) reinforces the secrecy that Gnostic communities practiced: the deepest revelations are not for public consumption but for those prepared to receive them.
