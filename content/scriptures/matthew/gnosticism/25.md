## The Oil of Gnosis

The Parable of the Ten Virgins (25:1-13) is read in Gnostic terms as contrasting those who possess *gnosis* (the wise virgins with oil) and those who do not (the foolish virgins without oil). The oil represents the divine knowledge that sustains the soul through the darkness of the material world. It cannot be shared or borrowed at the last moment — each person must acquire their own *gnosis* through personal awakening and initiation. The closed door represents the finality of the cosmic separation between the pneumatic and the non-pneumatic.

## The Talents and Spiritual Gifts

The Parable of the Talents (25:14-30) is allegorized as the distribution of pneumatic gifts. Each person carries a measure of the divine spark, and the expectation is that this spark will be cultivated through the pursuit of *gnosis*. The servant who buries his talent represents the pneumatic person who, out of fear of the archons, suppresses their divine nature and conforms to the Demiurge's world. The master's anger reflects the Pleromatic imperative: the divine spark must not be hidden but must shine forth.

## The Sheep and the Goats

The judgment of the sheep and goats (25:31-46) is reinterpreted in some Gnostic traditions as the cosmic separation at the end of the age. The "sheep" are the *pneumatikoi* who recognized and served the divine spark in others ("the least of these my brothers"). The "goats" are those who remained blind to the divine presence within the material world. The criterion of judgment is not moral action in the conventional sense but the recognition of the hidden divine within the apparently lowly — itself a form of *gnosis*.
