## Forgiveness and the Authority Above the Demiurge

The healing of the paralytic (9:1-8) and Jesus' claim to forgive sins are read in Gnostic theology as evidence of an authority that surpasses the Demiurge's. The scribes' objection — "This man blasphemes!" (9:3) — is correct from their perspective: within the Demiurge's system, only the Demiurge can forgive sins. But Jesus operates from above that system, wielding the authority of the true Father. The physical healing confirms the spiritual reality of liberation from the archons' power.

## The Call of Matthew as Awakening

The calling of Matthew the tax collector (9:9) is read as a paradigmatic moment of *gnosis* — the divine call that awakens the sleeping spark. Matthew's immediate response, leaving everything to follow Jesus, represents the pneumatic person's recognition of their true identity. They are not tax collectors, sinners, or righteous — they are divine sparks trapped in the material world, and the Christ has come to remind them who they truly are.

## New Wine and the Old Cosmos

The parable of new wine in old wineskins (9:16-17) has powerful resonance in Gnostic thought. The "old wineskins" represent the Demiurge's creation — the material world and its religious systems — which cannot contain the transformative power of *gnosis*. The "new wine" of the Pleromatic revelation bursts the old containers. The Gnostic community understood itself as the new vessel, distinct from both Judaism and orthodox Christianity.
