## The Temptation as Archonic Challenge

The temptation narrative (4:1-11) is read in Gnostic exegesis as a confrontation between the divine Christ and the archonic powers that govern the material world. Satan, in some Gnostic systems, is identified with the Demiurge or as one of his chief archons. The three temptations represent the Demiurge's attempt to bring the Christ under his authority: through material need (bread), through spectacle (the temple), and through worldly dominion (the kingdoms).

## The Victory of Gnosis

Jesus' victory over the tempter is accomplished not through moral willpower but through superior knowledge (*gnosis*). His quotations from Scripture are read not as proof-texts from the Demiurge's Torah but as words that, when properly understood, contain hidden truths that even the Demiurge who inspired them did not comprehend. The Gnostic Christ turns the Demiurge's own scriptures against his agents.

## The Wilderness and the Inner Journey

The wilderness, in Gnostic interpretation, symbolizes the soul's passage through the material world — a place of testing and potential awakening. The forty days of fasting represent the ascetical discipline that strips away material attachment and prepares the pneumatic person for the reception of *gnosis*. The Gnostic appropriation of asceticism is directed not toward pleasing the creator-god but toward escaping his domain.
