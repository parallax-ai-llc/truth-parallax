## Material Renunciation

The encounter with the rich young man (19:16-22) is a paradigmatic Gnostic text. The command to "sell what you possess" is read not merely as an ethical instruction but as a radical call to renounce attachment to the material world — the Demiurge's domain. Wealth, as a product and instrument of the material cosmos, binds the soul to the archonic order. Only by releasing all material attachments can the divine spark begin its return to the Pleroma.

## Celibacy and Escape from Matter

Jesus' saying about those who become "eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven" (19:12) resonated deeply with Gnostic asceticism. Many Gnostic groups practiced sexual abstinence as a means of refusing to participate in the Demiurge's scheme of material reproduction. By not begetting children, the Gnostic refused to create new material prisons for divine sparks. The *Gospel of the Egyptians* quotes Jesus: "I have come to destroy the works of the female" — a saying interpreted as opposing biological reproduction.

## The Camel and the Needle's Eye

The saying about the camel and the needle's eye (19:24) reinforces the Gnostic conviction that the material world and its rewards are obstacles to salvation. "With God all things are possible" (19:26) is read as the assurance that the transcendent Father can accomplish what the Demiurge's system renders impossible: the liberation of the divine spark from the prison of matter.
