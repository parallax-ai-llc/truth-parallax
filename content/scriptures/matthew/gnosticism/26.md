## The Last Supper and Sacramental Mystery

The Last Supper (26:26-29) receives diverse treatment in Gnostic traditions. Some Valentinian groups practiced a Eucharistic rite, interpreting the bread and wine as symbols of the Pleromatic Christ's presence within matter — the divine hidden in the material. Others, particularly encratite groups, rejected wine in favor of water, refusing to participate in the material substance of the Demiurge's creation. The *Gospel of Philip* offers an extended Gnostic sacramental theology connected to the Matthean institution narrative.

## Gethsemane and the Agony of the Divine in Matter

Jesus' agony in Gethsemane (26:36-46) creates a significant problem for docetic Gnostics. If the divine Christ does not truly suffer, what is happening in the garden? Some Gnostic interpreters distinguished between the human Jesus, who genuinely agonizes, and the Christ-aeon, who observes from above. Others read the scene as the moment when the divine Christ prepares to separate from the human Jesus before the crucifixion — anticipating the cry of dereliction on the cross.

## The Betrayal and Archonic Complicity

Judas' betrayal (26:47-56) is read in some Gnostic traditions paradoxically: Judas, by handing Jesus over to the authorities, enables the cosmic drama that the Christ came to enact. The *Gospel of Judas* (a Sethian text) presents Judas as the most enlightened disciple, acting on Jesus' instructions to facilitate the liberation of the divine spark from the material body. This radical reinterpretation transforms the betrayer into the instrument of salvation.
