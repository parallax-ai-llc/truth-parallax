## The Crucifixion and Docetism

The crucifixion narrative (27:33-56) is the most theologically fraught passage for Gnostic Christology. Orthodox Christianity insists on the reality of Christ's suffering; Gnostic traditions generally deny it. In the *Second Treatise of the Great Seth* (Nag Hammadi), the risen Christ declares: "I did not die in reality but in appearance... It was another who drank the gall and the vinegar; it was not I... It was another, Simon, who bore the cross on his shoulder."

## The Cry of Dereliction

Jesus' cry, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (27:46), is interpreted in Gnostic theology as the moment when the divine Christ-aeon separates from the human Jesus. The human Jesus cries out because he has been abandoned by the divine power that inhabited him since the baptism. What dies on the cross is the material body, not the Pleromatic Christ. In the *Apocalypse of Peter* (Nag Hammadi), Peter sees the living Christ standing above the cross, laughing at the ignorance of those who think they are killing him.

## The Tearing of the Veil

The tearing of the temple curtain (27:51) is given cosmic significance in Gnostic reading. The veil separates the holy of holies — the Demiurge's inner sanctum — from the outer world. Its tearing signifies the breaking of the boundary between the Demiurge's domain and the realm beyond. The Demiurge's most sacred barrier has been breached by the Pleromatic power, exposing the emptiness behind the veil and opening a path of escape for the divine sparks trapped within the material cosmos.
