## The Woes and the Demiurge's Servants

Gnostic interpreters read Jesus' woes against the scribes and Pharisees (23:1-36) as a condemnation of the Demiurge's religious establishment — those who administer the law and cult of the lesser god. The charge of hypocrisy takes on cosmic dimensions: the religious leaders sincerely serve the Demiurge while mistakenly believing they serve the true God. Their blindness is not merely moral but ontological — they cannot see beyond the Demiurge's domain.

## The Tombs of the Prophets

The accusation that the scribes and Pharisees build the tombs of the prophets while being "sons of those who murdered them" (23:29-31) is read as evidence that the Demiurge's religion has consistently opposed the messengers of the true God. The prophets, in some Gnostic readings, were pneumatic figures who brought fragments of Pleromatic truth into the Demiurge's world and were destroyed for it. The institutional religion honors them after death while having silenced them in life.

## The Lament as Divine Grief

Jesus' lament over Jerusalem (23:37-39) expresses the grief of the Pleromatic Christ over the divine sparks trapped in the Demiurge's city. Jerusalem, the center of the Demiurge's cult, is the place where the archons' power is most concentrated. The image of the hen gathering her chicks is the transcendent God's desire to draw the scattered sparks back to the Pleroma — a desire that the archonic system frustrates.
