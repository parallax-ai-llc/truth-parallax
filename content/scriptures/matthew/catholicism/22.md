## The Wedding Banquet

The Parable of the Wedding Banquet (22:1-14) is read in Catholic sacramental theology as an image of the Eucharistic feast. The king's invitation represents God's call; the refusal of the invited guests parallels Israel's rejection of the prophets; the gathering of all from the highways anticipates the universal Church. The guest without a wedding garment (22:11-14) is understood as a warning that participation in the sacraments requires genuine conversion — receiving Communion in a state of mortal sin profanes the gift.

## Render unto Caesar

Jesus' teaching on paying taxes to Caesar (22:15-22) is foundational for Catholic social teaching on the relationship between Church and state. Catholic tradition affirms the legitimate autonomy of the temporal order (*Gaudium et Spes* 36) while insisting that all human authority is ultimately subject to divine law. The distinction is not between two equal realms but between a subordinate temporal order and God's absolute sovereignty.

## The Greatest Commandment

Jesus' identification of love of God and love of neighbor as the greatest commandments (22:34-40) is the summary of Catholic moral theology. The *Catechism* (CCC 2055) identifies these as the foundation of the Decalogue and all Christian ethics. Catholic social teaching's integration of justice and charity flows directly from this passage: love of neighbor demands not only personal kindness but structural justice.
