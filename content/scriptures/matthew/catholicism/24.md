## Eschatology and the Signs of the Times

Catholic interpretation of Matthew 24 is guided by the Magisterium's caution against apocalyptic speculation. The *Catechism* (CCC 673-677) teaches that the Church does not claim to know the specific timing of Christ's return. The "signs of the times" — wars, famines, false prophets — describe the ongoing character of history between Christ's ascension and his parousia, not a checklist of imminent events.

## The Destruction of the Temple and the End of the Age

Catholic exegesis distinguishes between the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 (fulfilled prophecy) and the eschatological coming of the Son of Man (still awaited). The theological point is vigilance: the Christian lives in the tension between the "already" of Christ's first coming and the "not yet" of his return. This tension shapes Catholic liturgical time, which moves cyclically through Advent, Christmas, Lent, and Easter.

## Watchfulness

Jesus' parables of watchfulness — the faithful and unfaithful servants (24:45-51) — ground the Catholic spiritual tradition of constant readiness. The practice of frequent confession, daily Mass, and the Liturgy of the Hours embodies this posture of spiritual alertness. The Advent season, with its readings from Matthew 24, liturgically enacts the Church's waiting for Christ's return.
