## The Sermon on the Mount and Catholic Moral Theology

The Sermon on the Mount is treated in Catholic tradition as the magna carta of Christian ethics. The *Catechism of the Catholic Church* devotes extensive attention to it, particularly the Beatitudes (CCC 1716-1729), which are understood as the portrait of Christ himself and the map of the Christian moral life. Unlike some Protestant readings that emphasize the Sermon's impossibility apart from grace, Catholic teaching holds that the Sermon describes a genuine, grace-enabled human perfection.

## The Beatitudes and the Evangelical Counsels

Catholic tradition connects the Beatitudes to the evangelical counsels of poverty, chastity, and obedience. "Blessed are the poor in spirit" grounds the religious vow of poverty. "Blessed are the pure in heart" undergirds the vow of chastity. The Beatitudes are not merely eschatological promises but describe the present life of holiness that the saints have embodied.

## The Antitheses and Natural Law

The antitheses (5:21-48) are read by Catholic moral theologians as Jesus revealing the full depth of the natural moral law inscribed in creation. The command "Be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect" (5:48) is understood not as an impossible ideal but as the vocation of every baptized person, achievable through cooperation with grace. St. Thomas Aquinas argued that the New Law of the Sermon fulfills and perfects the Old Law by addressing the interior dispositions of the heart.
