## Forgiveness of Sins

The healing of the paralytic (9:1-8) is significant in Catholic sacramental theology because Jesus explicitly links physical healing to the forgiveness of sins. Catholic interpreters see in this passage a prefiguring of the Sacrament of Reconciliation (Confession), where the Church exercises the authority to forgive sins that Christ entrusted to the apostles (cf. John 20:23).

## The Call of Matthew and the Mercy of God

The calling of Matthew (9:9-13) — a tax collector, considered a public sinner — is read by Catholic tradition as a paradigmatic act of divine mercy. Pope Francis adopted his episcopal motto, *Miserando atque eligendo* ("by having mercy and by choosing"), from a homily by the Venerable Bede on this very passage. Jesus' declaration, "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" (9:13, quoting Hosea 6:6), is central to Catholic pastoral theology.

## New Wine and New Wineskins

The parable of new wine in old wineskins (9:16-17) is read in Catholic ecclesiology not as a justification for breaking with tradition but as an affirmation that the new covenant, while continuous with the old, requires an expanded vessel — the universal Church. The harvest imagery (9:36-38) grounds the Catholic theology of vocations: the Church prays for laborers, and each baptized person is called to share in the harvest.
