## The Ten Virgins and Preparedness

The Parable of the Ten Virgins (25:1-13) is read in Catholic spirituality as a call to maintain the oil of grace through the sacramental life. The closed door and the bridegroom's reply, "I do not know you," warn against presumption. Catholic pastoral practice emphasizes the importance of receiving the sacraments regularly — particularly Reconciliation and the Eucharist — as the means of sustaining the life of grace.

## The Talents and Stewardship

The Parable of the Talents (25:14-30) informs the Catholic theology of stewardship — the understanding that all gifts are entrusted by God and will be accounted for. Catholic social teaching extends this principle beyond personal finance to include time, talent, and the goods of creation. The U.S. Catholic Bishops' pastoral letter *Stewardship: A Disciple's Response* (1992) drew directly on this parable.

## The Last Judgment and the Works of Mercy

The judgment of the sheep and goats (25:31-46) is the scriptural foundation for the Catholic tradition of the corporal and spiritual works of mercy. Feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, visiting the sick and imprisoned — these are not optional charity but the criteria of final judgment. Catholic social teaching, from *Rerum Novarum* (1891) to *Laudato Si'* (2015), consistently returns to this passage as the mandate for the Church's engagement with poverty, injustice, and human suffering.
