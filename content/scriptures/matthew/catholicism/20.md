## The Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard

Catholic interpretation of the Workers in the Vineyard (20:1-16) emphasizes God's sovereign generosity, which cannot be measured by human standards of fairness. The parable resists a works-based economy of salvation: God gives according to mercy, not strict accounting. This reading supports the Catholic teaching that grace is primary and that human cooperation with grace, while real, never earns salvation as if by contract.

## Servant Leadership

The request of Zebedee's sons (20:20-28) and Jesus' response — "whoever would be great among you must be your servant" — are foundational for Catholic teaching on ordained ministry. The ordained priest or bishop exercises authority not as domination but as *diakonia* (service). Jesus' self-description as the one who came "to give his life as a ransom for many" (20:28) is central to Catholic soteriology and is quoted in the Eucharistic Prayer.

## The Healing at Jericho

The healing of two blind men at Jericho (20:29-34) — their cry "Lord, have mercy on us, Son of David" — anticipates the *Kyrie eleison* of the Mass. Catholic liturgical theology sees in their persistent plea the paradigm of all Christian prayer: recognition of need, invocation of Christ's mercy, and the transformative encounter that follows.
