## The Indissolubility of Marriage

Catholic teaching on marriage draws heavily on Matthew 19:1-12. Jesus' appeal to the creation ordinance — "What God has joined together, let not man separate" (19:6) — is the scriptural basis for the Catholic doctrine that a sacramental marriage between two baptized persons is absolutely indissoluble. The "exception clause" (19:9, *porneia*) is interpreted by Catholic scholars not as grounds for divorce and remarriage but as referring to invalid unions (a reading supported by the annulment process).

## Celibacy for the Kingdom

Jesus' saying about those who "have made themselves eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven" (19:12) is the primary scriptural foundation for priestly and religious celibacy in the Catholic tradition. The calling to celibate life is understood as a charism — a gift freely given and freely received — that witnesses to the eschatological kingdom where there is no marriage (cf. 22:30).

## The Rich Young Man and Evangelical Poverty

The encounter with the rich young man (19:16-22) and Jesus' command to "sell what you possess and give to the poor" are read by Catholic tradition as the foundation of the evangelical counsel of voluntary poverty. Religious orders from the Franciscans to modern communities take this passage as their charter. Jesus' words about the camel and the needle's eye (19:24) do not condemn wealth absolutely but warn that attachment to possessions can obstruct the path to God.
