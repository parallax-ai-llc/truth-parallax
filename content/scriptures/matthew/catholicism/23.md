## The Woes and Prophetic Critique

Catholic interpretation of Jesus' woes against the scribes and Pharisees (23:1-36) has evolved significantly since the Second Vatican Council. *Nostra Aetate* (1965) rejected the use of these passages to justify anti-Jewish sentiment. Contemporary Catholic scholarship reads the woes as intra-Jewish prophetic critique — Jesus speaking as a Jewish reformer within his own tradition, not as the founder of an opposing religion.

## Hypocrisy and Authentic Piety

The charge of hypocrisy — saying but not doing, tithing herbs while neglecting justice and mercy (23:23) — applies universally. Catholic moral theology holds that the external practices of religion (sacraments, liturgy, devotions) must be animated by interior charity. The saints are presented as models of authentic piety: Francis of Assisi, who embraced poverty; Catherine of Siena, who challenged corrupt clergy.

## The Lament over Jerusalem

Jesus' lament (23:37-39) expresses God's compassion even in judgment. Catholic Mariology has seen in the image of the mother hen an echo of Mary's maternal care for the Church. More broadly, the passage expresses the Catholic conviction that God's saving will is universal — he desires the salvation of all (1 Timothy 2:4) — even as human freedom can resist divine grace.
