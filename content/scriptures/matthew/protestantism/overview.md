## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Matthew is shaped by the Reformation principles of *sola scriptura* and *sola fide*. The Gospel's emphasis on Jesus' teaching authority, the nature of the kingdom of heaven, and the Great Commission have made it central to Protestant theology, missions, and ecclesiology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Dispensationalist
Dispensational Protestants distinguish between the "kingdom of heaven" as an offer to Israel (which was rejected) and the Church age. The Sermon on the Mount is sometimes viewed as kingdom ethics for a future millennial reign rather than direct Church-age instruction.

### Reformed/Covenantal
Reformed interpreters read Matthew within a covenant-theological framework, seeing Jesus as the mediator of the new covenant. The Sermon on the Mount describes the ethical life of the covenant community, and the five discourses are understood as the new Torah given by the greater Moses.

### Evangelical
Evangelical Protestants emphasize the Great Commission (28:18-20) as the marching orders of the Church. Matthew's Gospel has been foundational for the modern missionary movement and for evangelical understandings of discipleship.

## Theological Emphases

- **Fulfillment**: Matthew's formula quotations ("This was to fulfill...") demonstrate Jesus as the promised Messiah
- **Ecclesiology**: Matthew 16:18 ("on this rock I will build my church") is a key text in Protestant-Catholic debate — Protestants generally identify "the rock" as Peter's confession of faith, not Peter himself
- **The Lord's Prayer**: Central to Protestant devotion and catechesis (6:9-13)
- **Justification and Works**: The tension between faith and the Sermon on the Mount's ethical demands is a perennial Protestant discussion
