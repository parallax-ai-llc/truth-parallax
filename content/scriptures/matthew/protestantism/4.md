## The Temptation in the Wilderness

Protestant exegesis of the temptation narrative (4:1-11) emphasizes Jesus' use of Scripture to combat Satan. Each temptation is answered with a quotation from Deuteronomy, demonstrating *sola scriptura* in practice. Luther saw the temptation as paradigmatic for the Christian life: the devil attacks through appetite, spectacle, and power, and the believer's defense is the Word of God.

## Jesus as the New Israel

Where Israel failed in the wilderness for forty years, Jesus triumphs in forty days. Protestant typological reading sees Jesus succeeding at every point where Adam and Israel fell — resisting the temptation to doubt God's provision, to test God's faithfulness, and to seize power apart from God's will.

## The Beginning of Galilean Ministry

Jesus' relocation to Capernaum in Galilee (4:12-16) fulfills Isaiah 9:1-2. Protestant interpreters note that Jesus begins his ministry not in Jerusalem but in "Galilee of the Gentiles," signaling the universal scope of the gospel. The calling of the first disciples (4:18-22) models the immediacy and totality of Christian discipleship: they leave everything and follow.
