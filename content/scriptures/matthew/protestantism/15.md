## Tradition and the Heart

Jesus' confrontation with the Pharisees over handwashing traditions (15:1-20) is a key text for the Protestant principle of *sola scriptura*. Jesus accuses the Pharisees of nullifying God's commandment for the sake of human tradition (15:3-6), a critique that the Reformers applied directly to medieval Catholic practices they considered extra-biblical. Jesus' declaration that defilement comes from the heart, not from external observance (15:17-20), reinforces the Protestant emphasis on interior transformation.

## The Canaanite Woman

The encounter with the Canaanite woman (15:21-28) is one of the most discussed passages in Protestant commentary. Her persistent faith despite apparent rejection ("even the dogs eat the crumbs," 15:27) is celebrated as a model of tenacious trust. Jesus' commendation — "O woman, great is your faith!" — extends the boundaries of the kingdom beyond Israel, anticipating the Great Commission.

## Feeding the Four Thousand

The second feeding miracle (15:32-39) reinforces the themes of the first while taking place in predominantly Gentile territory. Protestant interpreters see in this a deliberate pattern: Jesus' ministry extends from Israel to the nations, from the five thousand to the four thousand, from Jewish to Gentile contexts.
