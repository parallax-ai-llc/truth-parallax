## The Healing of the Paralytic

The healing of the paralytic (9:1-8) is significant in Protestant theology because Jesus first forgives the man's sins and then heals his body, demonstrating his divine authority. Protestant interpreters emphasize that the visible miracle confirms the invisible reality of forgiveness — a pattern that anticipates the relationship between the proclaimed Word and its effects.

## The Call of Matthew

The calling of Matthew the tax collector (9:9-13) is a paradigmatic conversion story in Protestant preaching. Jesus' declaration, "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" (9:13), encapsulates the Protestant understanding of grace as unmerited divine favor extended to the undeserving. The Pharisees' objection to Jesus eating with "tax collectors and sinners" highlights the tension between grace and religious respectability.

## New Wine in New Wineskins

Jesus' parable of new wine in old wineskins (9:16-17) has been influential in Protestant ecclesiology. Reformation-era interpreters read it as justification for breaking with institutional forms that could not contain the gospel. The passage concludes with Jesus' compassion for the crowds, "harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd" (9:36), and his call for laborers — a verse central to Protestant missionary theology.
