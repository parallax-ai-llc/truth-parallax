## The Last Supper

The institution of the Lord's Supper (26:26-29) is central to Protestant sacramental theology. Jesus' words, "This is my body... this is my blood of the covenant," have generated centuries of debate. Lutheran theology affirms the real presence of Christ "in, with, and under" the bread and wine. Reformed theology teaches a spiritual presence received by faith. Zwinglian and Baptist traditions understand the Supper as a memorial and act of obedience. Despite these differences, all Protestant traditions regard the Supper as a means of grace instituted by Christ himself.

## Gethsemane

Jesus' anguish in Gethsemane (26:36-46) — "My soul is very sorrowful, even to death" — reveals the full humanity of the incarnate Son. Protestant devotion has drawn deep comfort from the knowledge that Christ enters human suffering from the inside. His prayer, "Not as I will, but as you will" (26:39), models the surrender of human will to divine purpose. The disciples' failure to stay awake is read as a warning against spiritual complacency.

## The Arrest and Peter's Denial

Judas' betrayal with a kiss (26:47-50) and Peter's three denials (26:69-75) frame the Passion as a moment of total human failure. Protestant interpreters emphasize that Jesus is abandoned by all his followers, bearing the cross alone. Peter's bitter weeping after the rooster crows is read as genuine repentance — the gateway to restoration that will come in Chapter 28.
