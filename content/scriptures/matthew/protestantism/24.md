## The Olivet Discourse Begins

Matthew 24 contains Jesus' teaching about the destruction of the temple and the end of the age. Protestant interpretation of this chapter varies dramatically across eschatological frameworks. Preterists read most of it as fulfilled in the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70. Futurists see it as primarily describing events before Christ's second coming. Historicists trace the prophecies through the sweep of church history.

## Signs of the End

Jesus warns of false messiahs, wars, famines, and earthquakes (24:4-14) as "the beginning of birth pains." Protestant interpreters across traditions emphasize the command to "not be alarmed" (24:6) and the missionary imperative: "this gospel of the kingdom will be proclaimed throughout the whole world as a testimony to all nations, and then the end will come" (24:14). This verse has fueled Protestant missions movements from William Carey to the present.

## The Coming of the Son of Man

The vivid imagery of the Son of Man coming "on the clouds of heaven with power and great glory" (24:30) is the foundation of Protestant hope. The parable of the fig tree (24:32-35) and the warning that "no one knows the day or hour" (24:36) establish the tension between watchfulness and date-setting that has characterized Protestant eschatology. Jesus' command to "stay awake" (24:42) frames the proper posture as alert readiness, not speculative calculation.
