## The Parable of the Ten Virgins

The Ten Virgins parable (25:1-13) warns against spiritual unpreparedness. Protestant interpreters read the oil as genuine faith or the Holy Spirit's indwelling — something that cannot be borrowed at the last moment. The closed door and the bridegroom's reply, "I do not know you" (25:12), soberly underscore that not all who profess faith will enter the kingdom.

## The Parable of the Talents

The Parable of the Talents (25:14-30) has been enormously influential in Protestant theology, shaping the concept of stewardship, vocation, and accountability. Each servant is entrusted according to his ability and judged according to his faithfulness. The servant who buries his talent out of fear is condemned. Protestant interpreters draw from this parable the ethic of industrious faithfulness — using one's gifts in active service rather than in fearful self-protection.

## The Sheep and the Goats

The judgment scene (25:31-46) presents a striking criterion: care for "the least of these my brothers." Protestant interpreters debate whether "the least of these" refers to all people in need or specifically to persecuted Christians. Either way, the passage insists that authentic faith produces tangible compassion. Luther used this passage to argue that good works are the evidence, not the basis, of salvation — faith alone saves, but the faith that saves is never alone.
