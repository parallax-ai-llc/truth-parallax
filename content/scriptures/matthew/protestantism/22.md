## The Parable of the Wedding Banquet

The Parable of the Wedding Banquet (22:1-14) continues Matthew's series of judgment parables. The invited guests refuse to come, so the king sends servants to invite anyone they find. Protestant interpreters read this as the gospel going from Israel to the nations. The man without a wedding garment (22:11-14) warns that entering the kingdom requires genuine transformation, not mere outward association.

## Render unto Caesar

The question about paying taxes to Caesar (22:15-22) has been foundational for Protestant political theology. Jesus' answer — "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and to God the things that are God's" — has been read as establishing the "two kingdoms" (Luther) or "two spheres" (Calvin) doctrine, recognizing legitimate but limited civil authority alongside God's ultimate sovereignty.

## The Greatest Commandment

Jesus' identification of the greatest commandment — love God with all your heart, soul, and mind, and love your neighbor as yourself (22:34-40) — provides the hermeneutical key for the entire Law. Protestant interpreters see this as the summary of the moral law that remains binding on Christians, even as the ceremonial and civil aspects of Torah are fulfilled in Christ.
