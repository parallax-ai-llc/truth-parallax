## The Sermon on the Mount Begins

The Sermon on the Mount (Chapters 5-7) is the most extensively commented-upon passage in Protestant theology. Luther devoted an entire lecture series to it; Calvin treated it as the definitive exposition of the Law's true meaning. The setting — Jesus ascending a mountain to teach — deliberately echoes Moses on Sinai, presenting Jesus as the authoritative interpreter (and fulfiller) of Torah.

## The Beatitudes (5:3-12)

Protestant interpretation of the Beatitudes varies significantly. Lutheran readings emphasize the Beatitudes as descriptions of those who receive grace, not prescriptions for earning it. Reformed readings see them as the character traits of kingdom citizens empowered by the Spirit. Anabaptist and Wesleyan traditions read them as genuine ethical imperatives that the community of disciples is called to embody.

## Law and Gospel

The antitheses ("You have heard... but I say to you," 5:21-48) raise the central Protestant question of the relationship between Law and Gospel. Luther distinguished between the "civil," "pedagogical," and "normative" uses of the Law. Calvin argued that Jesus does not abolish or replace the Law but reveals its original, deeper intention. The concluding command — "Be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect" (5:48) — is understood not as achievable human effort but as the standard that drives believers to grace.
