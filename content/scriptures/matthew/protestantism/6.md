## The Practice of Piety

Matthew 6 addresses the three pillars of Jewish piety — almsgiving, prayer, and fasting — and Jesus' instruction to practice them "in secret" (6:1-18). Protestant interpreters emphasize the contrast between external religiosity and authentic interior devotion. Luther saw this chapter as a direct attack on the performative piety he associated with medieval Catholicism.

## The Lord's Prayer (6:9-13)

The Lord's Prayer is central to Protestant worship and catechesis. Luther's *Small Catechism* and the Westminster Shorter Catechism both provide detailed expositions. Protestant interpreters note the prayer's structure: three petitions concerning God (name, kingdom, will) followed by three concerning human need (bread, forgiveness, deliverance). The doxology ("For yours is the kingdom...") is included in most Protestant traditions though absent from the earliest manuscripts.

## Treasures in Heaven and Anxiety (6:19-34)

Jesus' teaching on wealth and anxiety has been a touchstone for Protestant ethics. Calvin emphasized the legitimate use of earthly goods within a framework of stewardship. The Puritan tradition developed a complex theology of vocation and prosperity rooted in this passage. The command "seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness" (6:33) encapsulates the Protestant emphasis on prioritizing God's reign over material security.
