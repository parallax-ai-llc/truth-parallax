## The Death of John the Baptist

Herod's execution of John the Baptist (14:1-12) serves as a somber interlude in Matthew's narrative. Protestant interpreters read it as a foreshadowing of Jesus' own death at the hands of political power and as a reminder that faithfulness to God's word can cost everything. John's courage in confronting Herod's unlawful marriage is frequently cited in Protestant ethics of prophetic witness to civil authority.

## Feeding the Five Thousand

The feeding miracle (14:13-21) is the only miracle besides the resurrection recorded in all four Gospels. Protestant interpreters emphasize both its Christological significance — Jesus as the bread of life, the greater Moses providing manna in the wilderness — and its practical implications for Christian generosity. The disciples' inadequacy ("We have only five loaves and two fish") and Jesus' superabundant provision model the dynamic of grace.

## Walking on Water

Peter's attempt to walk on water (14:28-31) is a favorite passage in Protestant devotion. Peter's initial faith, his fear when he sees the wind, and Jesus' rescuing hand illustrate the Protestant understanding of faith as trust in Christ that falters when focused on circumstances. Jesus' gentle rebuke — "O you of little faith, why did you doubt?" — frames the Christian life as a continual return to trust.
