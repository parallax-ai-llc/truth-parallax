## The Visit of the Magi

Protestant interpretation of the Magi's visit emphasizes the universal scope of Christ's kingship. The Magi, as Gentile seekers of truth, foreshadow the Great Commission's reach to "all nations" (28:19). Their gifts — gold, frankincense, and myrrh — are traditionally read as symbolic of Jesus' kingship, divinity, and coming death.

## Herod's Massacre and the Flight to Egypt

The slaughter of the innocents (2:16-18) and the flight to Egypt are understood within Matthew's fulfillment theology. The citation of Hosea 11:1 ("Out of Egypt I called my son") presents Jesus as recapitulating Israel's history. Protestant commentators note that Jesus succeeds where Israel failed — passing through the wilderness temptation (Chapter 4) without sin.

## Fulfillment Quotations

Chapter 2 contains three formula quotations (2:6, 2:15, 2:18), establishing Matthew's characteristic pattern. Protestant exegetes debate whether these represent direct prediction, typological fulfillment, or analogical correspondence, but all affirm that they demonstrate God's sovereign control over the events surrounding Jesus' birth.
