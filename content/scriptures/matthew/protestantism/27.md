## The Trial Before Pilate

Jesus' trial before Pilate (27:1-26) raises profound questions about guilt and complicity. Pilate's handwashing (27:24) has become proverbial for the evasion of moral responsibility. Protestant interpreters note that Roman authority and Jewish leadership both share in the condemnation, reflecting the universal human complicity in sin that the cross addresses.

## The Crucifixion

Matthew's crucifixion narrative (27:33-56) is the theological center of the Gospel for Protestant theology. The cry of dereliction — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (27:46) — is read as Jesus bearing the full weight of divine judgment against sin, experiencing the separation from God that sinners deserve. Luther's *theologia crucis* takes this moment as the revelation of God's hidden nature: God is most present precisely where he seems most absent.

## The Death and Burial

The tearing of the temple curtain (27:51) symbolizes, for Protestant interpreters, the end of the old sacrificial system and direct access to God through Christ. The opening of tombs and resurrection of saints (27:52-53) — unique to Matthew — anticipates the general resurrection. Joseph of Arimathea's burial of Jesus (27:57-61) fulfills Isaiah 53:9 and confirms the reality of Jesus' death, essential for the reality of his resurrection.
