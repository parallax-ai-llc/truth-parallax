## The Workers in the Vineyard

The Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard (20:1-16) is a cornerstone of Protestant grace theology. Workers hired at the eleventh hour receive the same wage as those who labored all day. Protestant interpreters read this as a vivid illustration of grace that cannot be earned or measured — God's generosity overturns human calculations of merit. Luther cited this parable against the system of indulgences and the notion that accumulated works earn a greater heavenly reward.

## The Third Passion Prediction

Jesus' third prediction of his death and resurrection (20:17-19) adds specific details: he will be handed over to the Gentiles, mocked, flogged, and crucified. Protestant commentators note the increasing specificity of these predictions as evidence of Jesus' deliberate, voluntary movement toward the cross — not as a victim of circumstance but as the one who "came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" (20:28).

## The Request of James and John

The request of Zebedee's sons for positions of honor (20:20-28) occasions Jesus' teaching on servant leadership. The statement that the Son of Man came "to give his life as a ransom for many" (20:28) is foundational for Protestant atonement theology, supporting the substitutionary understanding of Christ's death.
