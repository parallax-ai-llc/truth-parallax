## The Resurrection

The resurrection narrative (28:1-10) is the vindication of everything Jesus taught and did. Protestant interpreters emphasize the bodily, physical nature of the resurrection against both ancient docetism and modern demythologization. The angel's message — "He is not here, for he has risen, as he said" (28:6) — confirms Jesus' predictions and establishes the resurrection as historical fact, not metaphor.

## The Great Commission

The Great Commission (28:18-20) is arguably the single most important passage for Protestant missions and ecclesiology. Three elements structure the commission: (1) the universal authority of Christ — "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me"; (2) the threefold task — "make disciples of all nations, baptizing them... teaching them"; and (3) the promise of presence — "I am with you always, to the end of the age." This final promise forms an *inclusio* with the name "Immanuel" (1:23), framing the entire Gospel.

## Theological Significance

Protestant theology reads Matthew 28 as the foundation of the Church's mission. The command to baptize in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit provides the Trinitarian formula used in virtually all Protestant baptismal liturgies. The emphasis on "teaching them to observe all that I have commanded" ensures that the Great Commission is not merely about initial conversion but about ongoing discipleship. The Gospel ends not with closure but with commission — the story of Jesus continues in the mission of his Church.
