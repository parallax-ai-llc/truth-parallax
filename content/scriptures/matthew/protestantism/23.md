## The Seven Woes

Matthew 23 contains Jesus' most sustained polemic — seven woes against the scribes and Pharisees. Protestant interpreters have historically read this chapter as a critique of religious hypocrisy in general, applicable to any tradition that substitutes external observance for interior righteousness. The Reformers applied these woes to the medieval Catholic hierarchy, though modern Protestant scholarship is more cautious about anti-Jewish readings.

## Hypocrisy and Self-Examination

The recurring charge of hypocrisy — doing for public show what should flow from genuine devotion — is treated in Protestant ethics as a warning to all believers. Jesus condemns tithing mint and dill while neglecting justice, mercy, and faithfulness (23:23). Protestant interpreters note that Jesus does not abolish tithing but demands that external observance be grounded in the "weightier matters" of the Law.

## Lament over Jerusalem

Jesus' lament, "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, the city that kills the prophets" (23:37-39), concludes the chapter with grief rather than anger. Protestant interpreters read this as evidence of Jesus' genuine compassion even in judgment. The image of the hen gathering her chicks portrays divine love that is offered but can be refused — a key text in Arminian-Calvinist debates about the extent of God's saving will.
