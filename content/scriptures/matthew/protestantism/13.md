## The Parables of the Kingdom

Matthew 13 is the central parable chapter in the Gospel. Protestant exegesis has developed extensive theories of parabolic interpretation. Calvin and the Reformers generally favored finding a single main point in each parable, warning against allegorizing every detail. Modern evangelical scholars follow this principle while recognizing that some parables (like the Sower) invite detailed allegorical reading because Jesus himself provides the interpretation.

## The Sower and the Soils

The Parable of the Sower (13:1-23) and its explanation have been foundational for Protestant preaching on the varied responses to the gospel. The four soils represent different heart conditions, and Protestant interpreters emphasize that only the "good soil" — hearing and understanding — produces lasting fruit. The passage raises questions about predestination and human responsibility that have divided Calvinist and Arminian interpreters.

## Wheat and Tares, Mustard Seed, and Hidden Treasure

The Parable of the Wheat and Tares (13:24-30, 36-43) addresses the coexistence of genuine believers and pretenders within the visible church — a theme central to Protestant ecclesiology since Augustine. The Mustard Seed (13:31-32) and Leaven (13:33) parables describe the kingdom's surprising growth from small beginnings. The parables of Hidden Treasure and the Pearl of Great Price (13:44-46) emphasize the surpassing worth of the kingdom, demanding total commitment.
