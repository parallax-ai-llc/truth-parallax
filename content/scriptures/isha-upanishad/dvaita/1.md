Madhva's reading of the Isha Upanishad's 18 verses systematically establishes the Dvaita position that God and the individual soul are eternally distinct. The opening verse — "All this is pervaded by the Lord" — affirms Vishnu's omnipresent sovereignty, not the identity of God and world. The Lord pervades creation as its controller (*niyamaka*), not as its substance. The instruction to "enjoy through renunciation" means to use the world's goods in God's service, recognizing His ownership of everything.

Madhva reads the verses describing the self (4-8) as distinguishing between two "selves": the Supreme Self (Vishnu) who is truly all-pervading and beyond description, and the individual self (*jiva*) who is limited, dependent, and in need of liberation. The *vidya/avidya* passages (9-14) are interpreted as teaching that both knowledge of God's nature and knowledge of the material world are necessary — exclusive pursuit of either leads to incomplete understanding. The closing prayers are directed to Vishnu in His cosmic forms, seeking liberation through divine grace at the hour of death.
