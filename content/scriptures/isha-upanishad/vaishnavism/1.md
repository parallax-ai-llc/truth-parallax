The Isha Upanishad opens with the declaration that all of existence is pervaded by the Lord (*Isha*), whom Vaishnava commentators identify as Vishnu or Narayana. Prabhupada's commentary in *Sri Isopanisad* emphasizes that verse 1 establishes God's proprietorship over the entire creation — nothing belongs to the individual soul, and true enjoyment comes through recognizing this divine ownership. The instruction to "not covet anyone's wealth" means to use material resources in God's service rather than for selfish enjoyment.

The Upanishad's middle verses (4-8) describing the self as simultaneously moving and unmoving, near and far, are read by Vaishnavas as describing Vishnu's paradoxical nature as both transcendent and intimately present within every atom as the Supersoul (*Paramatma*). The closing prayer (verses 15-18) — asking the sun-deity to reveal the face of truth — is understood as a prayer to Vishnu in His solar aspect, requesting the removal of material illusion so that the devotee may behold God's true form at the moment of death.
