Shankara's commentary on the Isha Upanishad is foundational to Advaita Vedanta. He interprets the opening verse as establishing that Brahman alone is real and that the phenomenal world of names and forms is a superimposition upon this one reality. The injunction to "enjoy through renunciation" (*tena tyaktena bhunjitha*) is read as the call to abandon false identification with the body-mind complex and abide as the self. For Shankara, this is not ethical advice about sharing but a metaphysical instruction about the nature of reality.

Shankara's reading of the *vidya/avidya* section (verses 9-14) has been intensely debated. He interprets *avidya* as Vedic ritual knowledge and *vidya* as meditation on deities — both of which are "lower knowledge" compared to the direct realization of Brahman. Those who pursue either exclusively enter into "darkness." Only the integration of both, leading ultimately to transcendent self-knowledge, leads to immortality. The closing prayer — "O Pushan, remove the covering of truth" (verse 15) — is read as the aspirant's plea for the removal of ignorance that conceals the self's identity with Brahman.
