The Isha Upanishad's opening verse — "All this is pervaded by the Lord" — stands as one of the world's most concise expressions of divine immanence. It resonates with the Quran's declaration "Wherever you turn, there is the face of God" (2:115), the Psalmist's question "Where can I flee from your presence?" (Psalm 139:7), and the Stoic concept of *logos* permeating all things. Each tradition arrives at a similar intuition through different paths: the world is not separate from the divine but is pervaded, sustained, and given meaning by it.

The Upanishad's teaching on *vidya* (knowledge) and *avidya* (ignorance) in verses 9-14 — where exclusive pursuit of either leads to darkness — finds remarkable parallels in other traditions. The Christian mystical concept of *docta ignorantia* (learned ignorance), developed by Nicholas of Cusa, similarly teaches that true wisdom transcends both ordinary knowing and not-knowing. The Zen koan tradition deliberately undermines both knowledge and ignorance to provoke a breakthrough beyond conceptual categories. The Isha Upanishad's resolution — that one must traverse both knowledge and ignorance to reach immortality — anticipates these later dialectical approaches by centuries.
