## Overview

The Isha Upanishad's vision of the divine pervading all reality resonates with mystical and philosophical traditions across the world. Its opening declaration — "All this is pervaded by the Lord" — invites comparison with panentheistic theologies in Christianity (Meister Eckhart), Islam (Ibn Arabi's *wahdat al-wujud*), and Judaism (Hasidic notions of divine sparks in all things).

## Comparative Themes

The Upanishad's paradoxical descriptions of the self — moving and unmoving, far and near (verse 5) — parallel the apophatic theology of Pseudo-Dionysius and the coincidence of opposites (*coincidentia oppositorum*) in Nicholas of Cusa. The reconciliation of knowledge and ignorance (verses 9-14) resonates with the Zen Buddhist concept of "beginner's mind" and the Christian mystical tradition of "learned ignorance" (*docta ignorantia*). The closing prayers for guidance through death (verses 15-18) compare with the Egyptian Book of the Dead and the Tibetan Bardo Thodol.
