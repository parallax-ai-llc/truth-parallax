---
title: "Rígsþula (The Lay of Rig)"
section: 22
---

In olden days, | they say, did Rig
Along green paths | walk on the earth;
Mighty and old, | the god strode forth,
Strong and sturdy, | upon his way.

He came to a dwelling, | the door was ajar;
In went he, a fire | was burning within.
There sat a couple, | grey-haired both,
Ai and Edda, | in garments old.

Rig knew well | wise counsel to give;
There in the midst | of the bed he lay,
And on either side | the couple sat.

Then Edda bore | a swarthy boy,
And with water they sprinkled him, | Thrall they called him.
He began to grow | and to gain in strength,
Of his hands the skin | was shriveled and rough,
Knuckles knotty, | and fingers thick;
His face was ugly, | his back was humpy,
His heels were long. | He began straightway
To lay his strength | and to loosen bark,
He bore on his back | great bundles of wood.

Then came to the dwelling | a damsel walking,
Mud on her soles, | and her arms were sunburned,
Her nose was flat; | her name was Thir.

There in the midst | of the bench she sat,
And beside her sat | the son of the house;
They chatted and whispered, | the bed they made,
Thrall and Thir, | and the time passed on.

Children they had, | they lived and were happy,
Their names, I think, | were Noisy and Rough,
Stumpy and Stout, | and Sluggard and Grey,
And Lout and Leggy | and Lean and Pale.
They laid fences, | spread dung on the fields,
Tended the swine, | and herded the goats,
And dug the peat.
