---
title: "Völundarkviða (The Lay of Völund)"
section: 19
---

There was a king in Sweden named Nidud. He had two sons and one daughter; her name was Bothvild. There were three brothers, sons of a king of the Finns: one was called Slagfith, another Egil, and the third Völund. They went on snowshoes and hunted wild beasts.

They came to Ulfdalir and there they built themselves a house. There was a lake there which is called Ulfsjar. Early one morning they found on the shore of the lake three women, who were spinning flax. Near them were their swan-garments, for they were Valkyries.

Now Nidud, king of the Njars, heard that Völund sat alone in Ulfdalir. At night went men in studded corslets; their shields glistened in the waning moon.

They entered the hall and saw | rings strung on bast,
Seven hundred in all, | that the warrior had;
They took them off, | they put them on,
Save one alone | which they left behind.

Then came Völund | home from the hunt,
From a weary way, | the skillful bowman,
A brown bear's flesh | would he roast with fire;
The wind-dry wood | of the fir burned well,
Before Völund | the wind-dried fir.

He sat on a bear-skin | and counted his rings,
The prince of the elves; | but one he missed;
He thought that Hlothver's | daughter had it,
The all-wise maid, | and that she had come back.

So long sat he | that he fell asleep,
And waking found | heavy bonds on his hands;
On his feet were fetters, | firmly wrought.

Völund spake:
"Who are they who have | laid upon me
Bonds of bast, | and bound me thus?"
