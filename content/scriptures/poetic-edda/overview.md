## Overview

The Poetic Edda is the most important primary source for Norse mythology and the pre-Christian religion of Scandinavia. This collection of Old Norse poems, preserved primarily in the Codex Regius manuscript (c. 1270 CE), contains mythological narratives about the Norse gods and cosmology, as well as heroic legends rooted in the Migration Period of European history. Though the manuscript dates to the thirteenth century, many of the poems are believed to originate centuries earlier, reflecting oral traditions that may reach back to the Viking Age or even the Migration Period.

The Poetic Edda presents the Norse cosmos from its creation to its destruction at Ragnarök, the fate of the gods, the wisdom traditions associated with Odin, and the heroic deeds of legendary figures such as Sigurd the Dragon-Slayer. The poems employ a variety of meters — including fornyrðislag, ljóðaháttr, and málaháttr — and use vivid imagery, dramatic dialogue, and mythological allusions that presuppose a knowledgeable audience deeply familiar with the tradition.

## Structure

The 29 poems of the Poetic Edda are traditionally divided into mythological poems and heroic poems:

| Poem | Title | Subject |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Völuspá | The creation and destruction of the world as told by a seeress to Odin |
| 2 | Hávamál | Wisdom sayings, ethical counsel, and runic knowledge attributed to Odin |
| 3 | Vafþrúðnismál | A wisdom contest between Odin and the giant Vafthrudnir |
| 4 | Grímnismál | Odin reveals cosmological knowledge while tortured between fires |
| 5 | Skírnismál | Freyr's servant Skirnir woos the giantess Gerd on his master's behalf |
| 6 | Hárbarðsljóð | A flyting (verbal contest) between Thor and Odin disguised as a ferryman |
| 7 | Hymiskviða | Thor's fishing expedition to catch the Midgard Serpent |
| 8 | Lokasenna | Loki insults the gods at Aegir's feast, revealing their hidden shames |
| 9 | Þrymskviða | Thor disguises himself as Freyja to recover his stolen hammer |
| 10-15 | Miscellaneous Mythological | Various poems on Volund, Alvis, Baldr, Rig, Hyndla, and Svipdag |
| 16-29 | Heroic Poems | The Völsung-Nibelung cycle and related heroic legends |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: Central to Old Norse philology, comparative mythology, and medieval Scandinavian studies
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with Indo-European mythology, Germanic folklore, and world creation myths
- **Philosophical**: Explores fate, wisdom, honor, and the nature of cosmic order and chaos
- **Literary**: A masterwork of medieval poetry with enduring influence on Western literature
- **Archaeological**: Corroborated by runestones, iconography, and material culture from the Viking Age
- **Interfaith**: Offers perspectives on polytheistic cosmology, eschatology, and the sacred in nature

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Cosmology | The creation, structure, and ultimate destruction of the Norse cosmos |
| Wisdom | The pursuit and price of knowledge, especially as embodied by Odin |
| Fate | The inescapable destiny governing gods and mortals alike |
| Heroism | The ideals of courage, honor, and loyalty in the face of certain doom |
| Ragnarök | The prophesied end of the world and its renewal |
| Shape of the Sacred | The relationship between gods, giants, humans, and the natural world |
