## Overview

In traditional understanding, the Sama Veda represents the musical dimension of eternal revelation. While the Rig Veda provides the words (*rik*) and the Yajur Veda the ritual formulas (*yajus*), the Sama Veda supplies the melodies (*saman*) that transform spoken mantras into sung liturgy. The Udgatr priest and his three assistants — the Prastotr, Pratiharttr, and Subrahmanya — are responsible for the Samavedic chanting during the Soma sacrifice.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Ritual Function
Each saman is not merely a song but a ritual instrument with specific efficacy. The traditional system classifies chants by their place in the sacrificial sequence: the Prastava (prelude), Udgitha (main chant), Pratihara (response), Upadrava (after-song), and Nidhana (conclusion). These five parts constitute the complete saman.

### Sacred Syllables
The Sama Veda introduces special syllables — *hau, hai, atha, iha, u* — called *stobha*, which are inserted into the Rig Vedic text when it is set to melody. These syllables, though semantically empty, are considered spiritually potent and essential to the chant's efficacy.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sound as Revelation**: The melody itself carries spiritual power beyond the meaning of the words
- **Cosmic Harmony**: Samavedic chanting sustains the cosmic order by harmonizing the human and divine realms
- **Discipleship**: Mastery of saman requires years of oral training under a qualified teacher (*acharya*)
- **Soma Connection**: The chants are primarily associated with the Soma sacrifice, the most elaborate of Vedic rituals
