The Mahaprasthanika Parva narrates the Pandavas' final renunciation of their kingdom and their great journey toward Mount Meru, during which Draupadi and four of the five brothers fall dead one by one, leaving only Yudhishthira and a faithful dog to reach the summit. For Vaishnavas, this parva is a supreme teaching on detachment and the soul's ultimate journey toward God.

Each companion's fall is attributed to a specific moral failing: Draupadi's partiality toward Arjuna, Sahadeva's intellectual pride, Nakula's vanity, Arjuna's boastfulness, and Bhima's excessive appetite. Vaishnava commentators, particularly in the Madhva tradition, interpret these "failings" carefully. They do not indicate that these great souls were condemned but rather that the narrative uses their falls to teach that even the subtlest attachments must be surrendered before one can stand in God's presence. Madhva's reading specifically defends Bhima's spiritual status, arguing that Bhima's fall is only apparent and that as Vayu's incarnation, he attains the highest liberation.

Yudhishthira's refusal to abandon the dog — who is revealed as Dharma in disguise — encapsulates the Vaishnava virtue of universal compassion rooted in seeing God in all beings. Yet Vaishnava interpreters emphasize that Yudhishthira's true qualification is not merely his compassion but his unwavering devotion to Krishna throughout every trial. The journey to Meru symbolizes the soul's progressive stripping away of material identity until nothing remains but the pure devotional relationship with the Lord. Yudhishthira's arrival at heaven's gate represents the culmination of a life lived in surrender to divine will.
