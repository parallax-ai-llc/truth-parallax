The Adi Parva opens the grand narrative of the Mahabharata, and for Vaishnavas it establishes the divine framework within which all subsequent events unfold. The parva recounts the origins of the Kuru dynasty, the births of the Pandavas and Kauravas, and the early signs of the conflict that will consume an entire generation. From a Vaishnava perspective, every birth and event in this parva is orchestrated by the Lord's will to set the stage for Krishna's future intervention.

The miraculous births of the Pandavas — Yudhishthira from Dharma, Bhima from Vayu, Arjuna from Indra, and the twins from the Ashvins — are understood as divine arrangements. These are not random celestial blessings but part of Vishnu's cosmic plan to place righteous warriors on earth who will serve as instruments of dharma's restoration. The Kauravas' birth from a mass of flesh divided into one hundred portions signals an adharmic origin that foreshadows their eventual destruction.

The Adi Parva also introduces Draupadi's svayamvara and the Pandavas' early exile, events that Vaishnava commentators read as divine tests designed to strengthen the Pandavas' dependence on God. Krishna's first significant appearance — at Draupadi's svayamvara and during the burning of the Khandava forest — reveals His role as the Pandavas' supreme friend and protector. The establishment of Indraprastha with Krishna's guidance demonstrates that all prosperity flows from alignment with the Lord's will. Madhva's Tatparya Nirnaya emphasizes that even seemingly mundane political events encode Vishnu's sovereign purpose.
