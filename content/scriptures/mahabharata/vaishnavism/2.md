The Sabha Parva narrates one of the most pivotal and emotionally charged episodes in the Mahabharata: the dice game in which Yudhishthira loses his kingdom, his brothers, and Draupadi to Duryodhana's treachery. For Vaishnavas, this parva reveals both the devastating consequences of adharma and the Lord's mysterious protective grace operating even in moments of apparent catastrophe.

Draupadi's desperate prayer to Krishna during her attempted disrobing is a defining moment in Vaishnava theology. When Duhshasana attempts to strip her in the assembly, Draupadi releases her grip on her garment and calls out to Krishna — an act of complete surrender (sharanagati). Krishna responds by providing an endless supply of cloth, demonstrating that the Lord always protects those who take exclusive refuge in Him. This episode is cited across Vaishnava traditions as the supreme example of divine responsiveness to a devotee's cry.

The Sabha Parva also exposes the moral failure of the Kuru elders — Bhishma, Drona, and Vidura — who witness the outrage but cannot prevent it. Vaishnava commentators note that even great souls bound by worldly obligations cannot match the protective power of the Supreme Lord. Vidura's protests, while morally admirable, prove insufficient; only Krishna's direct intervention saves Draupadi. Madhva reads this as confirming that worldly dharma, however noble, is inadequate without devotion to Vishnu. The exile that follows is understood not as defeat but as divine preparation for the Pandavas' ultimate victory through Krishna's grace.
