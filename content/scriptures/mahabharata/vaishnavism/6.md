The Bhishma Parva is the theological heart of the Mahabharata for Vaishnavas, containing the Bhagavad Gita — Krishna's supreme teaching to Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. This parva covers the first ten days of the war under Bhishma's command, but its eternal significance lies in Krishna's systematic revelation of karma yoga, jnana yoga, and above all bhakti yoga as the paths to liberation. The Gita's final instruction — "Abandon all varieties of dharma and surrender unto Me alone" (18.66) — is regarded by Vaishnavas as the supreme conclusion of all scripture.

Krishna's choice to serve as Arjuna's charioteer rather than fight is itself a profound theological statement. The Supreme Lord of the universe willingly assumes a subordinate position to demonstrate that divine love expresses itself through service. Vaishnava acharyas emphasize that Krishna's charioteer role reveals God's saulabhya — His astonishing accessibility to those who love Him. The Lord who controls all creation allows Himself to be directed by His devotee's reins.

The war narrative itself demonstrates Krishna's strategic omniscience. Under Bhishma's command, the Kaurava forces are devastating, yet Krishna guides the Pandavas through each crisis. The climactic fall of Bhishma — achieved through the controversial use of Shikhandi as a shield — raises complex moral questions that Vaishnava commentators resolve by appealing to Krishna's transcendent authority. What appears as adharma by conventional standards is sanctioned by the Lord whose will supersedes all human moral calculation. Bhishma's own recognition of Krishna's supremacy, expressed in the Vishnu Sahasranama he later recites from his deathbed, confirms that even the greatest warriors find their ultimate refuge in devotion to the Lord.
