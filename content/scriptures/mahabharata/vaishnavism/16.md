The Mausala Parva recounts the self-destruction of the Yadava dynasty through internecine warfare with iron clubs, fulfilling Gandhari's curse and the sages' curse provoked by the Yadava youths' mockery. For Vaishnavas, this brief but devastating parva addresses one of the most challenging theological questions: why does Krishna allow His own clan to perish?

Vaishnava theology offers a multi-layered response. First, the Yadavas' destruction demonstrates that even those closest to the Lord in physical proximity are not exempt from karmic consequences when they act with pride and irreverence. The youths' mocking of the sages — dressing Samba as a pregnant woman and asking if he would bear a son — represents the arrogance that inevitably follows prolonged material prosperity. Second, and more profoundly, Madhva and other Vaishnava commentators explain that the Yadava destruction was part of Krishna's pre-determined plan for withdrawing His earthly lila. The Lord's purpose on earth — establishing dharma and speaking the Gita — was complete, and the removal of the Yadava dynasty signals the approaching end of the Dvapara Yuga.

Krishna's calm observation of His clan's annihilation reveals His absolute transcendence. He is not diminished by these events but stands as the eternal witness, unaffected by the phenomena He Himself orchestrates. The iron pestle that becomes the clubs of destruction — born from the sages' curse — symbolizes how adharma carries within itself the seeds of its own annihilation. For Vaishnavas, this parva ultimately deepens rather than diminishes Krishna's glory: a God who can withdraw His own manifestation with equanimity demonstrates sovereignty beyond human comprehension.
