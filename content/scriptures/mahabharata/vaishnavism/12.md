The Shanti Parva is the Mahabharata's longest book, containing Bhishma's vast teachings on dharma, governance, and liberation as he lies on his deathbed of arrows. For Vaishnavas, this parva demonstrates that even the greatest human teacher ultimately points toward devotion to Vishnu as the supreme path. Bhishma, who fought on the wrong side yet remained a devotee of Krishna, teaches from a position of purified wisdom, his martial attachments burned away by the arrows of Arjuna.

The Rajadharma section, covering principles of statecraft and royal duty, is read by Vaishnavas as establishing that all legitimate governance derives its authority from alignment with divine will. A righteous king rules as God's representative, and his primary duty is to create conditions in which dharma — and therefore devotion — can flourish. Yudhishthira's reluctance to rule after the carnage is overcome through Bhishma's teaching that abandoning one's divinely appointed duty is itself adharmic.

The Moksha-dharma section, with its extensive philosophical dialogues, is interpreted by Vaishnavas as consistently pointing toward bhakti as the highest realization. While the section contains Samkhya, Yoga, and proto-Vedantic teachings, Vaishnava commentators argue that these diverse philosophical streams find their resolution in surrender to the Supreme Person. Madhva's reading particularly emphasizes passages where Narayana is explicitly identified as the supreme reality beyond all philosophical categories. Bhishma's final teaching, culminating in the Vishnu Sahasranama (which technically falls in the Anushasana Parva), confirms that the knowledge-tradition of the Mahabharata finds its apex in devotion to Vishnu.
