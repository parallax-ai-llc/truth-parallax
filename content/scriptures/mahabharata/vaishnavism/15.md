The Ashramvasika Parva narrates the retirement of Dhritarashtra, Gandhari, Kunti, Vidura, and Sanjaya to the forest, and their eventual deaths in a forest fire. For Vaishnavas, this parva teaches the inevitability of renunciation and the ultimate futility of worldly attachment. Even the blind king who clung so desperately to his sons' cause must finally release everything and seek the divine.

Vidura's death is particularly significant in Vaishnava theology. As an incarnation of Dharma (or in Madhva's reading, a specially empowered soul), Vidura merges his life-force with Yudhishthira in a mystical transfer witnessed by Dhritarashtra. Vaishnava commentators see this as demonstrating the continuity of dharmic consciousness across individuals — the righteous wisdom Vidura embodied does not perish but flows into the king who will carry it forward.

Kunti's choice to remain with Dhritarashtra and Gandhari rather than enjoy the comforts of her sons' kingdom reveals the Vaishnava ideal of vairagya (dispassion). Having witnessed Krishna's divine activities and the war's devastation, Kunti understands that worldly comfort is transient. Her death in the forest fire, alongside the others, is read not as tragedy but as liberation — the burning away of the last vestiges of material existence. Vaishnava interpreters emphasize that Kunti, as a great devotee who composed famous prayers to Krishna (preserved in the Bhagavata Purana), attained the Lord's supreme abode. The parva's somber tone reminds the reader that the Mahabharata's ultimate teaching is not about worldly victory but about transcending the material world through devotion.
