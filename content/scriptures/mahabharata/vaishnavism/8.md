The Karna Parva narrates the sixteenth and seventeenth days of battle under Karna's command, culminating in the titanic duel between Karna and Arjuna. For Vaishnavas, Karna's story is a tragic illustration of how even extraordinary virtue and valor cannot prevail against divine will when one aligns with adharma. Karna is acknowledged as a warrior of supreme skill and generosity, yet his choice to stand with Duryodhana places him in opposition to Krishna's purpose.

Vaishnava interpreters treat Karna with considerable nuance. His legendary generosity — giving away his divine armor and earrings to Indra, honoring every request even at personal cost — embodies genuine virtue. Yet Madhva's reading insists that virtue misaligned with Vishnu's will remains incomplete. Karna's loyalty to Duryodhana, while humanly admirable, represents attachment to personal relationships over cosmic dharma. Krishna's earlier revelation of Karna's true parentage offered him the chance to join the righteous side; his refusal, though motivated by honor, sealed his fate.

The climactic duel itself is laden with Vaishnava theological significance. When Karna's chariot wheel sinks into the earth and he appeals to Arjuna's sense of fair play, Krishna reminds Arjuna of every injustice the Kauravas committed — Draupadi's humiliation, Abhimanyu's murder, the poisoning of Bhima. Krishna's command to strike the defenseless Karna shocks conventional morality but affirms the Vaishnava principle that divine judgment encompasses a totality invisible to human perspective. Karna's death, in this reading, is not unjust but the culmination of accumulated karmic consequences, executed under the Lord's sovereign authority.
