The Anushasana Parva continues Bhishma's deathbed instructions, focusing on dharma in its practical dimensions — charity, vows, conduct, and devotion. For Vaishnavas, this parva contains one of the most sacred texts in all of Hindu literature: the Vishnu Sahasranama, the thousand names of Lord Vishnu recited by Bhishma in the presence of Krishna Himself. This hymn is regarded as the culminating revelation of Bhishma's wisdom, demonstrating that all his teachings on dharma find their ultimate purpose in glorifying the Supreme Lord.

The Vishnu Sahasranama occupies a unique theological position. Bhishma, the greatest warrior of the age, uses his final breaths not to recount martial exploits but to chant the names of Vishnu. Vaishnava commentators — Shankara, Parashara Bhattar, and Madhva all composed commentaries — emphasize that each name reveals an aspect of the Lord's infinite nature. The hymn's frame narrative, in which Yudhishthira asks Bhishma "Who is the one supreme deity?" and receives Vishnu-Krishna as the answer, is taken as the Mahabharata's definitive theological statement.

The parva's teachings on dana (charity), vrata (vows), and achara (proper conduct) are understood by Vaishnavas as preparatory disciplines that purify the devotee for the supreme practice of bhakti. Giving, self-discipline, and ethical living create the conditions in which love of God can flourish, but they are not ends in themselves. Madhva's interpretation insists that every dharmic act described in this parva gains its ultimate value only when performed as an offering to Vishnu. Without this devotional orientation, even the most meritorious actions yield only temporary results within the cycle of samsara.
