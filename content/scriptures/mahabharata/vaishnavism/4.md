The Virata Parva recounts the Pandavas' final year of exile, spent in disguise at the court of King Virata. For Vaishnavas, this parva illustrates the principle that devotees of the Lord can endure any humiliation when sustained by divine grace. The Pandavas assume lowly identities — Yudhishthira as a dice-playing courtier, Bhima as a cook, Arjuna as the eunuch dance-teacher Brihannala, Draupadi as a queen's attendant — yet their inner nobility, rooted in devotion, cannot be permanently concealed.

The parva's climax comes when the Kauravas attack Virata's kingdom and Arjuna, still disguised as Brihannala, single-handedly defeats the entire Kaurava army. Vaishnava interpreters see this episode as a prefiguration of the Kurukshetra War: Arjuna's victory is possible only because of the divine weapons he received through Krishna's arrangement and the Lord's unseen protection. The recovery of the Pandavas' hidden weapons from the Shami tree carries symbolic significance — dharma and divine power, though temporarily concealed, inevitably reassert themselves.

Draupadi's suffering at the hands of Virata's general Kichaka, and Bhima's wrathful destruction of Kichaka, demonstrate another Vaishnava theme: the Lord arranges for the protection of devotees through other devotees. Bhima's fierce devotion to Draupadi mirrors the protective relationship between the Lord and His bhaktas. Madhva's reading particularly emphasizes Bhima's role in this parva as Vayu's incarnation, the embodiment of devoted service whose strength flows directly from his unwavering connection to Vishnu.
