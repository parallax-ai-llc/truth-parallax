The Shalya Parva covers the eighteenth and final day of the Kurukshetra War, with Shalya as the Kauravas' last commander before Duryodhana's solitary stand. For Vaishnavas, this parva represents the inexorable completion of Krishna's divine plan — the systematic dismantling of the adharmic order so that righteousness may be restored. The rapid destruction of the remaining Kaurava forces demonstrates that no military power can withstand the Lord's sovereign will.

Shalya's brief command and swift death at Yudhishthira's hands carries theological weight. Vaishnavas note that Yudhishthira — the embodiment of dharma who had been reluctant to fight — finally acts with decisive warrior fury, demonstrating that even the gentlest soul must fulfill its kshatriya duty when empowered by divine sanction. Krishna's guidance transforms reluctance into righteous action.

The parva climaxes with the mace duel between Bhima and Duryodhana at the lake where Duryodhana hides after his army's annihilation. Bhima's controversial blow to Duryodhana's thigh — forbidden in mace combat — is orchestrated by Krishna, who signals Bhima by slapping his own thigh. Vaishnava commentators, particularly in the Madhva tradition, celebrate this moment as the fulfillment of Bhima's sacred vow to break Duryodhana's thigh for his crimes against Draupadi. Bhima acts as Vayu's incarnation, the instrument of divine justice. Balarama's outrage at the foul blow represents conventional dharmic thinking, but Krishna's higher authority sanctifies the act. The war ends with dharma restored through means that transcend human moral categories.
