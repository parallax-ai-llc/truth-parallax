The Svargarohana Parva, the Mahabharata's concluding book, presents Yudhishthira's final test and his ascent to heaven. Upon arrival, Yudhishthira finds Duryodhana enthroned in celestial glory while the Pandavas and their allies suffer in hell. Outraged, Yudhishthira chooses to remain in hell with his loved ones rather than enjoy heaven with the wicked — a final act of dharmic solidarity that dissolves the illusion, revealing that both heaven and hell were maya, and all the warriors dwell in their rightful celestial abodes.

For Vaishnavas, this conclusion carries profound theological significance. The illusory hell vision is understood as Yudhishthira's final purification — the last trace of pride and judgment burned away before he can perceive reality as it truly is. Vaishnava commentators emphasize that the "heaven" described here is not the supreme spiritual realm (Vaikuntha) but the temporary celestial abode within the material cosmos. The Pandavas' ultimate destination, in Vaishnava theology, transcends even this celestial reward.

Madhva's reading of the Svargarohana Parva is particularly distinctive. He interprets the hierarchical arrangement of souls in heaven as confirming the Dvaita doctrine of taratamya — the eternal gradation of souls according to their intrinsic nature and devotion. Krishna's departure from the earthly realm and the Pandavas' ascent together mark the end of an age and confirm the epic's central message: those who align themselves with Vishnu's will, endure suffering with faith, and act according to divine guidance attain an eternal destination beyond the cycles of samsara. The Mahabharata thus concludes, in Vaishnava reading, as an extended demonstration that bhakti — loving devotion to the Supreme Lord — is the ultimate purpose of all human existence.
