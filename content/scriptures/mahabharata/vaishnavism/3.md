The Vana Parva chronicles the Pandavas' thirteen years of forest exile, a period that Vaishnava interpreters understand as divinely orchestrated spiritual preparation. Far from being mere punishment, the exile deepens the Pandavas' devotion and equips them with the celestial weapons and alliances necessary for the coming war. Krishna visits the Pandavas in the forest, reinforcing that the Lord never abandons His devotees even in their darkest hours.

This parva contains some of the Mahabharata's most beloved embedded narratives, including the stories of Nala and Damayanti, Savitri and Satyavan, and Rama's adventures. Vaishnava commentators read these embedded tales as illustrating key devotional principles: Nala's story demonstrates how divine grace rescues those who maintain faith through adversity; Savitri's conquest of death through devotion to her husband mirrors the devotee's victory over mortality through devotion to God.

Arjuna's journey to heaven to obtain divine weapons — including the Pashupatastra from Shiva and weapons from Indra — is understood in Vaishnava theology as part of Krishna's larger plan. While Arjuna receives boons from multiple deities, Vaishnavas emphasize that these gods themselves act under Vishnu's supreme authority. The Vana Parva also contains the Yaksha Prashna episode, where Yudhishthira answers the questions of a mysterious Yaksha (revealed as Dharma himself). Vaishnava readers note that Yudhishthira's wisdom here flows from his alignment with divine will, prefiguring the righteousness that will ultimately secure the Pandavas' restoration through Krishna's intervention.
