The Ashvamedhika Parva recounts Yudhishthira's performance of the Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice) to establish his sovereignty and atone for the war's violence. For Vaishnavas, this parva illustrates the principle that all Vedic rituals find their true fulfillment in devotion to Vishnu. The sacrifice itself is an act of cosmic ordering, but its spiritual value depends entirely on the performer's recognition of the Lord's supremacy.

This parva contains the Anugita, a philosophical dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna that revisits themes from the Bhagavad Gita. When Arjuna requests Krishna to repeat His battlefield teaching, Krishna famously replies that He cannot recall the exact words, as they were spoken in a state of divine inspiration. Vaishnava commentators interpret this not as limitation but as emphasizing the unique, unrepeatable nature of the Gita's revelation — it was a singular act of grace suited to a specific cosmic moment. The Anugita, while valuable, is understood as a secondary teaching that reinforces rather than replaces the Gita's supreme message.

The horse sacrifice's successful completion, with the sacrificial horse wandering unchallenged across the earth, confirms Yudhishthira's universal sovereignty — a sovereignty that Vaishnavas understand as deriving entirely from Krishna's grace. The appearance of a mongoose who declares that a poor Brahmin's handful of barley flour offered with pure devotion exceeded the merit of Yudhishthira's grand sacrifice is a powerful Vaishnava teaching: external grandeur means nothing compared to heartfelt offering to the Lord. This episode encapsulates the epic's consistent message that sincere devotion surpasses all ritual accomplishment.
