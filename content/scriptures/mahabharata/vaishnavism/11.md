The Stri Parva presents the devastating aftermath of the Kurukshetra War through the lamentations of the women who lost husbands, sons, and fathers. For Vaishnavas, this parva offers profound lessons about the nature of attachment, grief, and the limits of worldly relationships when viewed against the eternal reality of the soul's relationship with God.

Gandhari's grief and her curse upon Krishna — that His own Yadava clan will destroy itself within thirty-six years — represents one of the epic's most theologically complex moments. Vaishnava commentators approach this episode with great care. Krishna accepts Gandhari's curse without resistance, demonstrating His transcendent equanimity. The Lord is not diminished by curses or blessings; He stands beyond all dualities. Madhva interprets Krishna's acceptance as part of His pre-determined plan to withdraw His dynasty from the earth when His mission of dharma-restoration is complete.

The women's lamentations over the battlefield corpses bring the war's human cost into sharp focus. Vaishnavas do not dismiss this grief as illusion but recognize it as a natural response to loss, while also teaching that ultimate consolation lies only in understanding the soul's immortality as Krishna teaches in the Gita. Yudhishthira's own anguish and guilt over the slaughter reveal that even the victors suffer. Vaishnava interpreters emphasize that this suffering itself purifies and deepens devotion — the Pandavas' post-war grief drives them toward the renunciation and spiritual realization that characterizes the epic's final books.
