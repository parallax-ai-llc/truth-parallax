## Overview

For Vaishnavas, the Mahabharata is fundamentally the story of Krishna's divine activity (lila) on earth. The epic is not merely a historical record of dynastic conflict but a revelation of how the Supreme Lord orchestrates events to establish dharma, protect devotees, and destroy the wicked. Krishna's multifaceted roles — as cousin, friend, counselor, charioteer, and ultimately the Supreme Personality of Godhead — demonstrate that God participates intimately in human affairs.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Gaudiya Vaishnavism
The Gaudiya tradition reads the Mahabharata as revealing Krishna's progressive self-disclosure. From political advisor in the Sabha Parva to cosmic lord in the Bhagavad Gita's Chapter 11, each stage deepens the audience's understanding of Krishna's supreme nature. The epic's conclusion — where dharma ultimately triumphs through divine grace — confirms that surrender to Krishna is the highest path.

### Sri Vaishnavism
Ramanuja's tradition emphasizes the Mahabharata's teachings on *prapatti* (surrender) and God's accessibility (*saulabhya*). Krishna's willingness to serve as Arjuna's charioteer illustrates the Lord's gracious condescension toward His devotees.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Orchestration**: Every event in the epic unfolds according to Krishna's supreme will
- **Devotee Protection**: The Pandavas' survival through impossible circumstances demonstrates divine grace
- **Dharma-sthapana**: Krishna descends specifically to re-establish righteousness (Gita 4.7-8)
- **Bhakti as Liberation**: The epic demonstrates that those devoted to Krishna attain the highest destination
