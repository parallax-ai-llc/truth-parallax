---
title: "Mahabharata Mausala Parva (Book 16)"
scripture: "mahabharata"
chapter: 16
---

**1** Thirty-six years after the great war, ill omens appeared throughout the land. Violent winds blew, the quarters of the sky were ablaze, and animals circled cities from left to right — all signs of impending destruction.

**2** The curse of Gandhari began to manifest among the Yadavas, the clan of Krishna. The young men of the race became arrogant and disrespectful. They mocked the sages and brought upon themselves a terrible curse.

**3** The sages Vishvamitra, Kanva, and Narada visited Dwaraka. The Yadava youths, in sport, dressed Samba, Krishna's son, as a woman and asked the sages: "This woman is with child. Tell us whether she will bear a son or a daughter."

**4** The enraged sages cursed: "This one shall bring forth an iron mace that will destroy the entire Yadava race." In due time, Samba produced an iron mace. The Yadavas, terrified, ground it to powder and cast it into the sea. But the powder grew into reeds along the shore.

**5** Krishna, knowing that the destruction of his race was ordained, declared that all Yadavas should go on a pilgrimage to the holy site of Prabhasa. There the warriors feasted and drank wine in great excess.

**6** Inflamed by drink, the Yadavas began to quarrel among themselves. Old grievances surfaced, insults were exchanged, and soon they seized the iron reeds that grew along the shore and began to slaughter one another.

**7** Satyaki slew Kritavarma, and in the melee that followed, every great warrior of the Yadava race fell. Pradyumna was slain, and Aniruddha, and Samba himself. The shore of Prabhasa was drenched in the blood of Krishna's kinsmen.

**8** Krishna himself took up the iron reeds and slew many warriors in his fury. When it was over, he said to Balarama: "Brother, the curse has been fulfilled. Our race is destroyed." Balarama sat in meditation beneath a tree, and his life-breath departed in the form of a great white serpent.

**9** Krishna, alone among the dead, sat beneath a tree in the forest, his foot exposed. A hunter named Jara, mistaking the sole of his foot for a deer, shot an arrow that pierced it. When he approached and saw what he had done, the hunter fell at Krishna's feet in horror.

**10** Krishna said: "Fear not, O hunter. You have done what was ordained. By this arrow I depart from this mortal world." Then the Lord of all, who had sustained the universe, left his mortal body and ascended to his eternal abode. The age of Dvapara ended, and the dark age of Kali began.

**11** Arjuna came to Dwaraka and found the city engulfed in mourning. He performed the funeral rites for Krishna and Balarama and led the surviving women and children of the Yadava clan toward Indraprastha. But on the road, they were attacked by robbers, and Arjuna found that his divine weapons no longer answered his call. The age of heroes was over.
