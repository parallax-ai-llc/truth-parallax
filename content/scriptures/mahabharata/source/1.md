---
title: "Mahabharata Adi Parva (Book 1)"
scripture: "mahabharata"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Om! Having bowed down to Narayana and Nara, the most exalted male being, and also to the goddess Sarasvati, must the word Jaya be uttered.

**2** Ugrasrava, the son of Lomaharshana, surnamed Sauti, well-versed in the Puranas, while present in the forest of Naimisha, at the twelve-year sacrifice of Saunaka, surnamed Kulapati, stood before the Rishis in attendance.

**3** Having studied the entire Vedas and the historical scriptures, with all their appendices, Sauti addressed those holy men assembled there, saying: "I will recite to you the sacred history called the Mahabharata, composed by the illustrious Vyasa."

**4** Janamejaya, the son of Parikshit, was the ruler of the Kuru kingdom. He performed a great sacrifice known as the Snake Sacrifice, and during that sacrifice he heard the story of his ancestors from the sage Vaishampayana.

**5** In the beginning, Vyasa composed the Mahabharata, a hundred thousand verses in extent, comprising the Upakhyanas, together with the Harivamsa. Then he made an abridgment in twenty-four thousand verses, which is known as the Bharata.

**6** Vyasa first taught this sacred history to his son Shuka, and afterwards to his other disciples. Narada recited it to the gods, and Asita-Devala to the Pitris. Shuka told it to the Gandharvas, the Yakshas, and the Rakshasas.

**7** This history is sacred and excellent. It is equivalent to the Vedas. He who listens to it obtains fame, long life, and heaven. In this history are contained the science of morality, the science of government, and the science of spiritual liberation.

**8** In the race of Bharata were born many mighty kings. Of these, the Pandavas and the Kauravas were the most illustrious. The sons of Pandu were five: Yudhishthira, Bhima, Arjuna, Nakula, and Sahadeva. The sons of Dhritarashtra numbered one hundred, of whom Duryodhana was the eldest.

**9** Bhishma, the son of Shantanu and Ganga, renounced the throne and took a terrible vow of lifelong celibacy for the sake of his father's happiness. He was known as Devavrata, but for this sacrifice he was called Bhishma, the terrible.

**10** Kunti, the wife of Pandu, by the grace of the sage Durvasa, possessed a mantra by which she could invoke any deity. Through this power, Yudhishthira was born from Dharma, Bhima from Vayu, and Arjuna from Indra.

**11** After the death of Pandu in the forest, his queen Kunti brought the five Pandavas to Hastinapura. There they were raised alongside the hundred sons of Dhritarashtra. But jealousy grew in the heart of Duryodhana from that day forward.

**12** The Pandavas excelled in every art and skill. Arjuna surpassed all in archery. Bhima was the mightiest in strength. Yudhishthira was unmatched in virtue. This caused Duryodhana to burn with envy and plot their destruction.
