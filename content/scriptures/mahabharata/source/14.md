---
title: "Mahabharata Ashvamedhika Parva (Book 14)"
scripture: "mahabharata"
chapter: 14
---

**1** After the death of Bhishma, Yudhishthira performed the Ashvamedha sacrifice, the great horse sacrifice, to purify himself and his kingdom of the sin of the war. Krishna advised him: "O king, the Ashvamedha is the greatest of all sacrifices. It will cleanse you of all guilt."

**2** A horse of great beauty was consecrated and set free to wander the earth. Arjuna followed with a mighty army, for the rule was that any king who seized the horse must fight or submit to Yudhishthira's sovereignty.

**3** Arjuna encountered many kings and warriors during his journey with the sacrificial horse. He fought battles across the length and breadth of Bharatavarsha, defeating all who challenged his brother's supremacy.

**4** At Manipura, Arjuna encountered his own son Babhruvahana, born of Princess Chitrangada. The young prince, fulfilling the dharma of a Kshatriya, challenged Arjuna and struck him down with a powerful arrow, rendering him unconscious.

**5** Ulupi, the Naga princess and another wife of Arjuna, revived him with a celestial gem. Arjuna embraced his son and blessed him, acknowledging that the boy had acted correctly in defending his territory.

**6** The horse wandered freely for one year, and at the end Yudhishthira performed the great sacrifice with magnificent rituals. Gold flowed like water, and gifts were distributed to Brahmins, the poor, and all who came seeking charity.

**7** During the sacrifice, a mongoose appeared whose body was half golden. It rolled on the sacrificial ground and then spoke: "This great sacrifice of Yudhishthira does not equal the gift of a single handful of barley flour given by a poor Brahmin family in Kurukshetra."

**8** The mongoose narrated the story: "A Brahmin, his wife, his son, and his daughter-in-law were starving during a famine. When they obtained a small quantity of barley flour, a guest arrived. The Brahmin gave his portion. The guest was still hungry, so the wife gave hers, then the son, and finally the daughter-in-law."

**9** "That family died of starvation, but their sacrifice was so pure and selfless that when I, a mongoose, rolled on the ground where that flour had fallen, half my body turned to gold. Since then I have visited every great sacrifice, but none has been pure enough to turn my other half golden."

**10** The assembled sages were humbled by this story. They understood that true dharma lies not in grand ceremonies but in selfless giving, in the sacrifice of the poor who give all they have, not in the abundance of the rich who give from their surplus.
