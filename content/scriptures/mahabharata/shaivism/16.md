The Mausala Parva's account of the Yadava dynasty's self-destruction through drunken fratricidal warfare is approached by Shaiva interpreters as a culminating demonstration of Rudra's dissolution principle. The iron clubs fashioned from the cursed iron pestle become instruments of mutual annihilation, and the scenes of former allies slaughtering each other mirror the cosmic dissolution that Shiva presides over at the end of each kalpa.

Shaiva commentators note that the Yadavas' destruction arises from their own accumulated pride and moral decay — the youths' mockery of sages represents the kind of spiritual arrogance that inevitably triggers Rudra's corrective wrath. The curse mechanism itself operates according to the Shaiva principle that the cosmos is self-regulating: when a community's dharmic foundations erode, the forces of dissolution activate automatically, without requiring external intervention. This is Shiva's governance operating through natural law rather than personal decree.

Krishna's departure from the mortal world — struck by the hunter Jara's arrow while reclining in the forest — carries specific Shaiva resonance. The forest setting, the association with a hunter (recalling the Kirata episode), and the element of death through an apparently random act all connect to Shaiva themes of the divine operating through the wild, the marginal, and the unexpected. Shaiva interpreters read Krishna's death not as the end of a Vaishnava deity but as the withdrawal of one divine manifestation, after which Shiva's ongoing cosmic governance continues. The era's end (yuga-anta) that follows Krishna's departure evokes Shiva's role as Mahakala — the Great Time that outlasts all individual manifestations of the divine.
