The Svargarohana Parva's conclusion — in which Yudhishthira experiences illusory hell before realizing that all souls have reached their rightful celestial destinations — is read by Shaiva interpreters as the epic's final teaching on the nature of maya and the transcendence of duality. The vision of hell that dissolves into heavenly reality mirrors the Shaiva understanding that suffering and bondage are ultimately products of limited perception, not ontological realities.

Kashmir Shaiva philosophy finds particular resonance in this parva. The moment of recognition (pratyabhijna) when Yudhishthira realizes that the apparent hell was an illusion parallels the central Kashmir Shaiva teaching that liberation consists not in traveling somewhere new but in recognizing what has always been the case — that consciousness is infinite, free, and identical with Shiva. Yudhishthira's "hell" was never real; his recognition of its illusory nature is itself the liberating act.

The hierarchical arrangement of souls in heaven, each in their appropriate station, reflects the Shaiva understanding that liberation takes different forms according to the soul's spiritual development. Shaiva Siddhanta distinguishes multiple levels of liberation — from salokya (dwelling in Shiva's realm) to sayujya (union with Shiva) — and the Svargarohana Parva's celestial hierarchy can be read as mapping these gradations. The epic's final verses, which praise the Mahabharata as equivalent to the Vedas, affirm its status as a comprehensive revelation — one that, in Shaiva reading, ultimately points to Mahadeva as the supreme reality toward which all the epic's characters, conflicts, and teachings converge.
