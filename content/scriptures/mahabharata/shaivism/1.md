The Adi Parva lays the foundation for the Mahabharata's cosmic drama, and Shaiva interpreters identify several key elements that prefigure Shiva's decisive role in the epic's outcome. While the surface narrative focuses on the births and early rivalries of the Pandavas and Kauravas, Shaiva readings attend to the underlying cosmological framework — the cycle of creation and destruction, the interplay of tapas (austerity) and karma, and the presence of Rudra-Shiva as the ultimate power behind all transformations.

The Adi Parva recounts the churning of the ocean (samudra-manthana), in which Shiva swallows the halahala poison to save creation — an act that establishes Him as the supreme protector willing to absorb the cosmos's destructive forces. Shaiva interpreters note that this cosmogonic event sets the pattern for the entire epic: creation involves destruction, and only Mahadeva can hold both forces in balance. The Kuru dynasty's origins in sacrifice, curse, and divine intervention reflect this Shaiva principle of creative destruction.

The births of the Pandavas through divine fathers are read by Shaiva commentators as indicating the broader divine involvement in earthly affairs, of which Shiva's contributions are paramount. The Pandavas' martial training under Drona, a Brahmin who embodies the Shaiva synthesis of asceticism and warrior power, foreshadows the role of Shaiva blessings in the coming war. The burning of the Khandava forest, often read as a Vaishnava episode, also demonstrates the Shaiva principle that old orders must be consumed by fire so that new creation can emerge — a theme that Shiva as Destroyer and Regenerator embodies throughout the epic.
