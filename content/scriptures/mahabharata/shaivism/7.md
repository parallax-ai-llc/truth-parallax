The Drona Parva intensifies the war's violence and moral complexity, and Shaiva interpreters find in its escalating chaos a reflection of Rudra's destructive dance reaching its crescendo. Drona, a Brahmin-warrior who embodies the Shaiva synthesis of knowledge and power, commands the Kaurava forces with devastating effectiveness. His mastery of astras (celestial weapons) gained through rigorous tapas exemplifies the Shaiva path of acquiring transcendent power through ascetic discipline.

Abhimanyu's death in the Chakravyuha — the labyrinthine military formation he could enter but not escape — carries symbolic resonance in Shaiva philosophy. The Chakravyuha mirrors the cosmic web of maya from which the individual soul struggles to escape. Abhimanyu's partial knowledge (he knows how to enter but not exit) parallels the spiritual seeker who has gained preliminary insight but lacks the complete knowledge necessary for liberation. In Shaiva terms, he possesses diksha (initiation) but has not yet achieved the full recognition (pratyabhijna) that would free him from the cycle.

The Narayaniya section embedded in this parva contains the most explicit Shiva-Vishnu theological dialogue in the Mahabharata. Here, the relationship between Nara-Narayana and Shiva is explored in depth, with passages that Shaiva interpreters cite as affirming Shiva's supreme status or, at minimum, the essential unity of Shiva and Vishnu as complementary aspects of one ultimate reality. The parva's treatment of Drona's death — felled through deception while meditating — evokes the Shaiva understanding that the warrior-ascetic's highest attainment comes at the moment of releasing the body through yogic concentration, transcending the battlefield's violence in a final act of spiritual ascent.
