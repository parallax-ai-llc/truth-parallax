The Sabha Parva's account of the dice game and Draupadi's humiliation is read by Shaiva interpreters as a demonstration of how adharma accumulates destructive karmic force that must eventually be resolved through cosmic transformation. The assembly hall itself — Maya Danava's magical construction — represents the deceptive nature of material power and political sovereignty, themes central to Shaiva philosophical reflection on maya and its transcendence.

Shaiva readings emphasize the structural role of the dice game in generating the conditions for the Kurukshetra War, which functions as a cosmic dissolution (pralaya) on the human plane. Duryodhana's adharmic seizure of power through gambling mirrors the cosmic forces of disorder that Shiva periodically destroys through His tandava dance. The humiliation of Draupadi — Shakti incarnate in many Shaiva-Shakta readings — represents an assault on the feminine divine principle that demands cosmic retribution.

The inability of the assembled elders to prevent Draupadi's suffering reflects a key Shaiva insight: conventional dharmic structures, when corrupted by the accumulation of adharmic power, become ineffective. Only transcendent divine intervention can restore balance. While the narrative credits Krishna's protection of Draupadi, Shaiva commentators note that the deeper principle at work — the inevitable destruction of adharma by forces beyond human control — aligns with Shiva's role as the great destroyer and transformer. The exile that follows is understood as a period of tapas, the ascetic purification through which the Pandavas acquire the spiritual power necessary for dharma's restoration.
