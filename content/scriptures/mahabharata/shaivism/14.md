The Ashvamedhika Parva's account of Yudhishthira's horse sacrifice is read by Shaiva interpreters as a Vedic ritual that, while establishing worldly sovereignty, ultimately points toward the transcendence of all ritual in the realization of Shiva. The Ashvamedha is the supreme royal sacrifice, yet Shaiva philosophy consistently teaches that external ritual, however elaborate, is preparatory to the internal sacrifice of the ego in the fire of divine knowledge.

The Anugita contained in this parva, while framed as Krishna's teaching, includes philosophical passages that Shaiva commentators identify as consonant with proto-Shaiva thought. The dialogue's treatment of the Samkhya categories, the nature of the gunas, and the relationship between purusha and prakriti aligns with the Shaiva philosophical framework that later systematized these categories under Shiva's supreme lordship. In Kashmir Shaiva reading, the Anugita's metaphysics are understood as pointing toward the recognition that consciousness (Shiva) and its power (Shakti) constitute the totality of reality.

The episode of the mongoose who critiques the horse sacrifice's merit — declaring that a poor Brahmin's sincere offering surpassed its value — resonates with the Shaiva tradition's characteristic suspicion of ritualism divorced from genuine spiritual realization. Shaiva teachers from the Pashupatas to the Virashaivas have consistently taught that external ceremony without internal transformation is spiritually empty. The mongoose's critique, in Shaiva reading, affirms that the true sacrifice is the offering of one's limited selfhood into the infinite consciousness of Shiva, not the performance of elaborate public rituals.
