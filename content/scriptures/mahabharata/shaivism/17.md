The Mahaprasthanika Parva's account of the Pandavas' final journey toward Mount Meru is profoundly resonant with Shaiva spirituality. The Himalayas are Shiva's abode — Kailasa, the cosmic mountain where Mahadeva dwells with Parvati — and the Pandavas' northward journey represents the soul's ascent toward Shiva's realm. In Shaiva geography, the Himalayas are not merely physical mountains but the axis mundi connecting the earthly and divine planes, and to walk toward them is to walk toward liberation.

The sequential falling of Draupadi and the Pandava brothers is interpreted by Shaiva commentators through the framework of spiritual purification. Each fall represents the shedding of a subtle attachment — a remaining bond (pasha) that must be released before the soul can enter Shiva's presence. The specific failings attributed to each character (pride, vanity, boastfulness, partiality) correspond to the inner obstacles that Shaiva practice identifies as barriers to realization. Only when all these bonds are dissolved can the seeker stand before Mahadeva.

Yudhishthira alone reaches the summit, accompanied by a dog who is Dharma in disguise. Shaiva interpreters note that Shiva himself is associated with dogs — the Bhairava form of Shiva is accompanied by a dog, and in South Indian Shaiva tradition, the dog is a sacred animal connected to the lord of the cremation ground. Yudhishthira's refusal to abandon the dog reflects the Shaiva value of compassion that extends to all beings, including those considered impure by orthodox standards. The ascent to Meru's peak symbolizes the final stage of Shaiva sadhana — the dissolution of individual identity into the infinite consciousness of Shiva.
