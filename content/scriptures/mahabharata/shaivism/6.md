The Bhishma Parva, containing the Bhagavad Gita and the first ten days of war, is approached by Shaiva interpreters through a distinctive lens. While the Gita is most commonly associated with Vaishnava theology, Shaiva commentators — notably Abhinavagupta in his Gitartha Sangraha — read it as expressing universal truths fully compatible with Shaiva non-dualism. In Abhinavagupta's reading, Krishna's teaching reveals the nature of absolute consciousness (Paramashiva) expressed through the terminology of the Vaishnava tradition.

Abhinavagupta interprets the Gita's central concepts through Kashmir Shaiva categories. The "field" and "knower of the field" (kshetra/kshetrajna) of Chapter 13 correspond to Shiva and Shakti. The yogic states described in Chapter 6 align with the stages of Shaiva meditation practice. Most significantly, the Gita's teaching on the identity of the individual self with the supreme Self resonates with the Shaiva doctrine of recognition (pratyabhijna) — the realization that one's own consciousness is identical with universal divine consciousness.

The war itself, beginning under Bhishma's command, manifests Rudra's destructive power on the human plane. Shaiva interpreters note that Kurukshetra (literally "field of the Kurus") functions as a sacrificial ground where the old order is offered into the fire of cosmic transformation. Bhishma, the grand patriarch whose death initiates the old order's dissolution, embodies the Shaiva principle that even the most venerable structures must yield to the force of temporal change. His ten-day battle is a protracted sacrificial offering that opens the way for the new dharmic order to emerge from the war's ashes.
