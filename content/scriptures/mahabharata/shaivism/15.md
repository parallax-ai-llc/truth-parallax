The Ashramvasika Parva's account of the elder generation's retirement to the forest and their death by fire resonates with fundamental Shaiva themes of renunciation, asceticism, and purification through destruction. Dhritarashtra, Gandhari, and Kunti abandon the palace for the vanaprastha (forest-dwelling) stage of life, entering the realm that Shaiva tradition associates with Shiva himself — the great lord of the forests and mountains who dwells beyond civilization's boundaries.

The forest fire that consumes the aged royals is read by Shaiva interpreters not as accidental tragedy but as a final act of cosmic purification. Fire is Agni, Shiva's own element, and death by fire represents the most complete form of bodily dissolution — a return of the material elements to their source. In Shaiva cremation theology, the funeral pyre is the gateway to liberation, and the Ashramvasika fire functions as a collective funeral rite that releases these souls from their remaining karmic bonds.

Vidura's mysterious death — his life-force transferring into Yudhishthira through yogic power — exemplifies the Shaiva concept of the accomplished yogi's ability to transcend ordinary death. Shaiva tradition preserves numerous accounts of realized beings who leave their bodies consciously through specific yogic techniques (utkranti), choosing the moment and manner of their departure. Vidura's yogic passing demonstrates mastery of the forces that bind consciousness to the body, a mastery that Shaiva practice aims to cultivate. The parva's overall movement — from palace to forest, from worldly power to ascetic simplicity, from embodiment to dissolution — traces the archetypal Shaiva journey toward ultimate liberation.
