The Shanti Parva's encyclopedic teachings on dharma, governance, and liberation contain substantial Shaiva philosophical content, particularly in the Moksha-dharma section. Shaiva interpreters regard this parva as preserving some of the earliest Pashupata and proto-Shaiva teachings in Sanskrit literature, embedded within the broader framework of Bhishma's deathbed discourse. The Moksha-dharma's philosophical dialogues, which explore the nature of Brahman, the soul, and liberation, contain passages that resonate with both Shaiva Siddhanta and Kashmir Shaiva philosophy.

The Pashupata teachings within the Moksha-dharma describe a system of spiritual practice centered on Pashupati (Lord of Creatures, a name of Shiva) as the supreme deity who liberates bound souls (pashus) from their fetters (pashas). This theological framework — the lord (pati), the bound soul (pashu), and the bonds (pasha) — became foundational for later Shaiva Siddhanta. Shaiva interpreters regard these Mahabharata passages as evidence of Shaivism's deep antiquity and its recognition as a legitimate path to liberation within the epic's own framework.

The Rajadharma sections, while apparently focused on practical governance, are read by Shaiva commentators through the lens of Shiva's cosmic sovereignty. The ideal king mirrors Shiva's role as Ishvara — the lord who governs creation with justice while remaining ultimately detached from its fluctuations. Bhishma's teaching that a king must sometimes employ danda (punishment) reflects Rudra's disciplinary aspect, while his emphasis on compassion and protection mirrors Shiva's anugraha (grace). The Shanti Parva thus presents a Shaiva vision of kingship as the human reflection of divine governance.
