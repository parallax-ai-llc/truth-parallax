The Virata Parva's narrative of concealment and revelation resonates deeply with Shaiva theology, which emphasizes the hidden nature of the divine and the necessity of penetrating appearances to reach ultimate reality. The Pandavas' year of disguise at Virata's court mirrors Shiva's own characteristic mode of appearing in unexpected, often lowly forms — as the Kirata hunter, as the naked ascetic, as Bhairava.

Arjuna's disguise as Brihannala, the eunuch dance-teacher, carries particular Shaiva significance. Shiva as Ardhanarishvara (the half-male, half-female deity) transcends gender categories, and Arjuna's assumption of a feminine-coded identity can be read as reflecting the Shaiva ideal of transcending binary distinctions. The warrior who dances embodies the union of Shiva's two primary modes — the fierce destroyer (Rudra) and the graceful dancer (Nataraja).

When Arjuna finally reveals himself and defeats the Kaurava forces single-handedly, the weapons he employs include the Pashupatastra granted by Shiva in the Vana Parva. The recovery of weapons from the Shami tree is symbolically linked to the revelation of hidden divine power — just as Shiva conceals Himself within apparent ordinariness, the supreme weapons of dharmic restoration lay hidden in a tree, awaiting the proper moment for manifestation. Shaiva interpreters read the entire Virata Parva as an extended meditation on the relationship between concealment (tirobhava) and revelation (anugraha), two of Shiva's five cosmic functions alongside creation, preservation, and destruction.
