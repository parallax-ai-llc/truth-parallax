The Vana Parva is the most important parva for Shaiva interpretation of the Mahabharata, containing the celebrated Kiratarjuniya episode in which Arjuna encounters Shiva disguised as a Kirata (mountain hunter). This encounter is the epic's definitive Shaiva moment: Arjuna, performing severe tapas to obtain divine weapons, unknowingly fights Shiva over a slain boar. When Arjuna exhausts all his weapons and physical strength against the invincible hunter, he fashions a linga from clay and worships it — only to find the flowers he places on the linga appearing on the hunter's head.

The Kiratarjuniya reveals Shiva's nature as the ultimate source of martial and spiritual power. Pleased by Arjuna's valor and devotion, Shiva grants him the Pashupatastra — the most devastating weapon in existence, capable of destroying the universe itself. Shaiva interpreters emphasize that without this weapon, the Pandavas' eventual victory would be impossible. Thus Shiva's blessing is the indispensable foundation upon which the entire war's outcome rests.

The episode also illustrates key Shaiva theological principles. Shiva appears not in regal splendor but as a wild forest hunter, embodying His nature as the lord of the margins, the untamed divine who transcends social conventions. Arjuna must fight to the point of complete exhaustion — surrendering all his Vaishnava and Indra-given resources — before Shiva reveals Himself. This narrative structure teaches that Shiva is accessible only to those who push beyond all conventional resources into the raw confrontation of self with the Absolute. The Vana Parva's forest setting reinforces Shiva's association with the wilderness, asceticism, and transformative spiritual practice beyond the boundaries of civilization.
