The Sauptika Parva is the most explicitly Shaiva parva in the Mahabharata. Ashwatthama, consumed by grief and rage after his father Drona's death, performs a desperate nighttime worship of Shiva and receives the god's terrifying blessing. Entering the Pandava camp under cover of darkness, Ashwatthama becomes possessed by Rudra's destructive energy and slaughters the sleeping warriors — the Panchalas and the five sons of Draupadi — in a massacre that horrifies even the hardened survivors of eighteen days of war.

Shaiva interpreters approach this episode with theological seriousness rather than moral condemnation alone. Ashwatthama's invocation of Shiva and the subsequent possession represent the most primal manifestation of Rudra's power in the epic — raw destruction without discrimination, the cosmic fire that consumes everything in its path. The text explicitly describes terrifying beings (bhutas, pretas, and rakshasas) assisting Ashwatthama, and the night raid unfolds as a ritual of Bhairava-like annihilation.

The parva's critical moment occurs when Ashwatthama attempts to destroy the Pandava lineage entirely by launching the Brahmastra at Uttara's womb. This overreach transforms Shiva's devastating but purposeful destruction into nihilistic annihilation — and it is here that the narrative draws the line. Even Rudra's destructive power serves cosmic purpose; purposeless annihilation exceeds its mandate. Ashwatthama's eternal curse — wandering wounded and immortal — represents the fate of one who channels destructive divine power without wisdom or cosmic authorization. Shaiva theology distinguishes between Shiva's controlled cosmic dissolution and the chaos of ungoverned rage, and Ashwatthama's fate illustrates this distinction with terrible clarity.
