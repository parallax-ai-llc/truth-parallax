The Stri Parva's lamentations of the widowed women over the battlefield dead represent, in Shaiva interpretation, the voice of Shakti mourning the destruction wrought by Rudra's cosmic dance. The women's grief is not mere emotional reaction but a sacred utterance — the primal cry of the creative feminine principle confronting the reality of dissolution. In Shaiva-Shakta theology, Shakti and Shiva are inseparable; creation and destruction, life and death, are complementary aspects of one divine reality, and the Stri Parva makes this complementarity painfully visible.

Gandhari's curse upon Krishna carries particular weight in Shaiva interpretation. Her accumulated tapas — decades of voluntary blindness and ascetic self-denial — gives her words irresistible power. Shaiva commentators note that tapas generates a force independent of sectarian allegiance; Gandhari's asceticism, though performed within a Vaishnava cultural framework, operates according to the universal Shaiva principle that concentrated spiritual energy (tejas) transforms reality. Her curse succeeds because it draws on the same cosmic force that empowers Shiva's own transformative acts.

The battlefield itself, strewn with corpses, evokes the imagery of Shiva's cremation ground — the shmashana where the great god dwells and dances. Shaiva ascetics have long identified cremation grounds as places of ultimate spiritual transformation, where the illusions of worldly life are literally reduced to ash. The Stri Parva transforms Kurukshetra into a vast shmashana, and the women's lamentations become a form of Shaiva mourning ritual that acknowledges death's reality while pointing toward the transcendence that lies beyond all loss.
