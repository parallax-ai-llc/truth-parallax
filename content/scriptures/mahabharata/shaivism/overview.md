## Overview

Shaiva readings of the Mahabharata highlight the extensive Shaiva content embedded within the epic, including the Shiva Sahasranama (Anushasana Parva), Arjuna's encounter with Shiva as the Kirata (hunter), and numerous hymns glorifying Mahadeva. While the epic's surface narrative centers on Krishna and the Pandavas, Shaiva interpreters argue that Shiva's supremacy is consistently affirmed through these embedded sections and that the epic's theological framework ultimately points to Shiva as the supreme reality.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Kiratarjuniya Episode
Arjuna's encounter with Shiva disguised as a mountain hunter (Vana Parva) is a central Shaiva passage. Shiva tests Arjuna's valor and then grants him the devastating Pashupatastra weapon, without which the Pandavas' victory would be impossible. This episode establishes Shiva as the ultimate source of martial and spiritual power.

### Shiva Sahasranama
The Anushasana Parva contains the thousand names of Shiva, recited by Bhishma, which describe Shiva as the supreme lord of all creation, preservation, and dissolution.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shiva as Supreme Power**: The Pashupatastra and Shiva's boons are decisive in the war's outcome
- **Tapas and Asceticism**: The epic valorizes the ascetic ideal that is central to Shaiva practice
- **Cosmic Dissolution**: The Kurukshetra War mirrors Shiva's role as destroyer and transformer
- **Complementarity**: Krishna and Shiva are understood as aspects of one supreme reality in some Shaiva readings

## Notable Shaiva References in the Epic

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Vana Parva (Kiratarjuniya) | Shiva tests and blesses Arjuna |
| Drona Parva (Narayaniya) | Shiva-Vishnu philosophical discourse |
| Anushasana Parva | Shiva Sahasranama and Shiva Gita |
| Shanti Parva (Moksha-dharma) | Pashupata and proto-Shaiva teachings |
