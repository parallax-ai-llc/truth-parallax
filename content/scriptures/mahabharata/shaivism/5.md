The Udyoga Parva's account of failed diplomacy and the marshaling of armies for war reflects the Shaiva understanding that destruction is sometimes the necessary precondition for cosmic renewal. While the parva foregrounds Krishna's peace embassy, Shaiva interpreters focus on the underlying inevitability of the conflict — a cosmic necessity that aligns with Shiva's role as the agent of dissolution who clears away exhausted structures so that new creation can emerge.

The Sanatsujatiya, embedded in this parva as a philosophical dialogue during the night before Krishna's embassy, contains significant proto-Shaiva content. Sanatsujata's teachings on the transcendence of death through knowledge and asceticism resonate with Shaiva soteriology, which emphasizes jnana (knowledge) and yoga as paths to liberation from the cycle of death and rebirth. The dialogue's emphasis on the imperishable Brahman that underlies all phenomena parallels Shaiva non-dualism, particularly the Kashmir Shaiva understanding of Paramashiva as the undifferentiated ground of reality.

The parva's war preparations also reflect Shaiva cosmological themes. The gathering of armies on both sides mirrors the cosmic forces that assemble before a pralaya (dissolution). Shaiva interpreters note that the war's approaching inevitability — despite every effort at peace — demonstrates a principle central to Shaiva theology: transformation cannot be forestalled. When the accumulated weight of adharma reaches critical mass, Rudra's destructive aspect must manifest. The Udyoga Parva thus depicts the moment before the cosmic storm, when even the wisest counselors recognize that the forces of dissolution have become irreversible.
