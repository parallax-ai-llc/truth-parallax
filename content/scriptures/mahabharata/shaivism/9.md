The Shalya Parva brings the Kurukshetra War to its devastating conclusion, and Shaiva interpreters read its final battles as the completion of Rudra's cosmic dance of destruction. The war's eighteenth day, with Shalya's brief command and rapid death, followed by the annihilation of nearly every remaining warrior, mirrors the accelerating pace of dissolution at the end of a cosmic cycle. What began as organized warfare has devolved into raw, primal destruction — the domain of Rudra.

The mace duel between Bhima and Duryodhana at Dvaipayana Lake carries deep Shaiva symbolism. The mace (gada) is associated with both Shiva and Vishnu, and combat with this weapon represents the most elemental form of battle — two bodies in direct physical contest without the mediation of arrows or astras. Shaiva interpreters see this primal combat as a return to Rudra's fundamental destructive energy, stripped of all sophisticated martial artifice.

Duryodhana's final moments, lying broken on the battlefield, produce one of the epic's most complex moral reflections. He claims to have lived as a true kshatriya and will ascend to heaven, while the Pandavas achieved victory through repeated deception. Shaiva commentators note the partial validity of Duryodhana's critique — the war's moral compromises reflect the inherent violence of cosmic dissolution, which spares no one's purity. In Shaiva theology, Rudra's destruction is amoral in the conventional sense; it operates beyond good and evil as a cosmic necessity. The war's end, with both sides morally scarred, demonstrates that dissolution purifies through fire rather than through ethical perfection.
