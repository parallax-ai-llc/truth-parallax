The Karna Parva presents the tragic arc of Karna, a figure whose generous spirit and warrior prowess are undone by fate and cosmic forces beyond his control. Shaiva interpreters approach Karna's story through the lens of prarabdha karma — the portion of accumulated karma that has already begun to bear fruit and cannot be averted, even by the gods. Karna's doom, despite his extraordinary virtues, illustrates the Shaiva teaching that individual merit operates within a larger cosmic framework governed by Mahadeva's will.

Karna's legendary act of giving away his divine armor (kavacha-kundala) to Indra, knowing it will cost him his life, resonates with the Shaiva ideal of the supreme renunciant. In Shaiva asceticism, the willingness to strip away every protection — physical, psychological, and spiritual — is the prerequisite for encountering the naked truth of Shiva. Karna's self-disarmament, even if motivated by warrior honor rather than spiritual seeking, mirrors the ascetic's voluntary vulnerability before the Absolute.

The duel between Karna and Arjuna, which culminates in Karna's death, is read by Shaiva commentators as the collision of two cosmic forces. Arjuna, bearing the Pashupatastra and empowered by Shiva's blessing from the Kiratarjuniya encounter, wields divine Shaiva power. Karna, despite his Surya-given armor and Parashurama-taught skills, lacks Shiva's specific blessing. The outcome confirms, in Shaiva reading, that Shiva's grace is the decisive factor in all cosmic conflicts. Without Mahadeva's sanction, no amount of personal valor or accumulated merit can prevail in the ultimate contest.
