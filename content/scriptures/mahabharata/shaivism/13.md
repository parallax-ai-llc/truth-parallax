The Anushasana Parva is of supreme importance for Shaiva interpretation of the Mahabharata because it contains the Shiva Sahasranama — the thousand names of Lord Shiva — recited by Bhishma alongside the more famous Vishnu Sahasranama. Shaiva interpreters regard the inclusion of both hymns as the epic's acknowledgment of Shiva's co-equal or supreme status, and they point to the Shiva Sahasranama as one of the most authoritative Shaiva texts in the entire itihasa tradition.

The Shiva Sahasranama reveals Mahadeva's infinite aspects: creator, preserver, and destroyer; ascetic and householder; the one who dwells in cremation grounds and the lord of the cosmic dance; the benevolent Shankara and the terrifying Bhairava. Each name encodes a theological teaching about Shiva's nature, and Shaiva commentators have produced extensive exegetical literature unpacking these names' philosophical implications. The hymn's inclusion in Bhishma's final teachings places Shiva-worship on the highest plane of dharmic practice.

This parva also contains the Shiva Gita and extensive passages on Shiva's greatness, including episodes where even Vishnu and Brahma acknowledge Shiva's supremacy. Shaiva interpreters regard these passages as the Mahabharata's clearest theological statements about the divine hierarchy. The parva's teachings on dana, vrata, and prayaschitta (expiation) are read through a Shaiva lens as practices that purify the soul's bondage (pasha) and prepare it for the liberating grace of Pashupati. The Anushasana Parva thus represents, in Shaiva reading, the epic's comprehensive presentation of dharma as a path leading ultimately to Shiva-realization.
