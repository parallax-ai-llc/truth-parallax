The Virata Parva's year of incognito exile tests the Pandavas' endurance and provides further evidence, in Dvaita reading, of their sattvic natures. Madhva interprets the Pandavas' ability to maintain disguises while preserving their essential dharmic character as demonstrating that the soul's intrinsic nature (svarupa) cannot be permanently suppressed by external circumstances. No matter how humble their assumed roles, the Pandavas' innate nobility — rooted in their status as liberated souls (mukti-yogya) — inevitably manifests.

Bhima's exploits during the Virata period receive extensive treatment in Madhva's Tatparya Nirnaya. His destruction of Kichaka, who attempted to assault Draupadi, is celebrated as Vayu's wrathful protection of dharma's honor. Madhva emphasizes the ferocity and completeness of Bhima's response — he not only kills Kichaka but disfigures the body beyond recognition — as appropriate to the cosmic significance of the crime. An assault on Draupadi (who, in some Dvaita readings, represents Bharati-devi, the consort of Vayu) demands the most decisive response from her divine protector.

Arjuna's single-handed defeat of the Kaurava army, while impressive, is interpreted by Dvaita as secondary to Bhima's achievements in this parva. Madhva's consistent elevation of Bhima over Arjuna reflects the Dvaita hierarchical principle: Vayu ranks above Indra in the cosmic order, and therefore Bhima's devotional service surpasses Arjuna's martial prowess. The Virata Parva's narrative confirms this hierarchy — Bhima acts from pure devotional motivation (protecting Draupadi), while Arjuna acts from warrior pride (defeating the Kauravas). Both serve Vishnu's purpose, but Bhima's service is more perfectly aligned with the supreme devotional ideal.
