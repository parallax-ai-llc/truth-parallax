The Karna Parva presents Dvaita Vedanta with a character whose theological classification requires considerable subtlety. Madhva recognizes Karna's extraordinary virtues — his unparalleled generosity, his martial prowess, his fidelity to his word — while maintaining that these virtues, disconnected from devotion to Vishnu, cannot produce ultimate spiritual benefit. Karna represents the tragic case of a soul whose excellent qualities are misdirected by association with demonic forces.

Madhva's Tatparya Nirnaya devotes significant attention to the Karna-Arjuna duel, providing a theological commentary on each phase of the combat. The contest between Karna and Arjuna is not merely a personal rivalry but a cosmic confrontation between forces aligned with and against Vishnu. Arjuna fights with Krishna as his charioteer — literally directed by God — while Karna fights with Shalya, who (in some Dvaita readings) subtly undermines Karna's confidence through Krishna's own arrangement. The outcome is therefore predetermined by divine will, not by the warriors' relative skill.

Karna's appeals for fair play as his chariot wheel sinks — and Krishna's rejection of those appeals by cataloguing the Kauravas' crimes — is interpreted by Dvaita as the definitive demonstration of God's comprehensive justice. Vishnu does not judge isolated moments but the entire arc of cosmic history. Karna's present helplessness is the accumulated fruit of his past choices: his participation in Draupadi's humiliation, his goading of Duryodhana, and his refusal of Krishna's offer of reconciliation. Dvaita insists that divine justice is always perfectly proportioned, even when it appears harsh by limited human standards. Karna's death is not unjust but precisely calibrated to his total karmic account.
