The Mahaprasthanika Parva's account of the Pandavas' final journey is read by Dvaita Vedanta through the lens of Madhva's hierarchical theology. The sequential falling of the companions is not random but reflects the precise spiritual hierarchy of each soul. In Madhva's interpretation, the order and manner of falling correspond to each character's rank in the cosmic hierarchy — a practical demonstration of the taratamya doctrine that is Dvaita's most distinctive teaching.

Madhva's treatment of Bhima's fall is particularly noteworthy. While the surface text attributes Bhima's fall to gluttony, Madhva argues that this attribution is the text's exoteric meaning (bahir-artha), concealing the esoteric truth (antar-artha) that Bhima, as Vayu's incarnation, does not genuinely fall at all. His apparent fall is either a voluntary act of compassion (choosing solidarity with his fallen brothers) or a narrative device concealing his direct ascent to the highest celestial station. Madhva refuses to accept that Vayu's incarnation — the supreme devotee after Brahma — could be genuinely impeded by a moral failing as trivial as excessive eating.

Yudhishthira's loyalty to the dog — and the dog's revelation as Dharma — is interpreted by Dvaita as confirming Yudhishthira's position in the hierarchy: he embodies dharma itself, but he is not the supreme devotee. In Madhva's system, Yudhishthira's status, while exalted, is subordinate to Bhima's because Dharma is subordinate to Vayu in the cosmic hierarchy. The Mahaprasthanika Parva thus provides a final demonstration of the eternal gradation of souls that structures all of reality according to Vishnu's design.
