The Udyoga Parva's diplomatic negotiations are read by Dvaita Vedanta as Vishnu's final offer of grace to those who still possess the capacity to accept it, combined with the just condemnation of those whose tamasic nature renders them incapable of righteous choice. Madhva interprets Krishna's peace mission not as a genuine attempt at compromise — the omniscient God already knows Duryodhana will refuse — but as a cosmic legal proceeding that establishes the full justification for the war to follow.

Krishna's revelation of His cosmic form (vishvarupa) in the Kaurava assembly is, for Dvaita theology, an unambiguous demonstration of Vishnu's absolute supremacy over all beings. Unlike the Gita's later, more philosophical theophany, this revelation is primarily a display of sovereign power intended to terrify the wicked and confirm the faithful. Duryodhana's attempt to bind Krishna and his subsequent terror at the cosmic form perfectly illustrate the Dvaita teaching that demonic souls (tamo-yogya) simultaneously hate and fear the divine — they cannot surrender because their intrinsic nature prevents them from recognizing God's supremacy.

Krishna's revelation to Karna of his true parentage is approached by Madhva with characteristic theological precision. Karna's refusal to join the Pandavas, despite being offered the kingship, demonstrates that individual choice operates within the constraints of one's innate spiritual nature. Karna is not a tamo-yogya soul — his generosity and honor place him in a higher category — but his accumulated karma and attachments from previous lives bind him to the wrong cause. Dvaita philosophy thus explains Karna's tragedy without either condemning him absolutely or excusing his opposition to Vishnu's devotees.
