The Anushasana Parva's Vishnu Sahasranama is the single most important text for Dvaita Vedanta's Mahabharata interpretation. Madhva's commentary on the thousand names of Vishnu, composed with meticulous care, establishes each name as revealing a genuine attribute of the personal God. Unlike Shankara's Advaita commentary, which reads the names as descriptions of attributeless Brahman, Madhva insists that each name denotes a real, distinct perfection of Vishnu's infinite personality — His omniscience, omnipotence, beauty, compassion, justice, and sovereignty are all genuinely and eternally real.

Bhishma's answer to Yudhishthira's question — "Who is the one supreme deity?" — is taken by Madhva as the Mahabharata's definitive theological pronouncement. Bhishma's response, identifying Vishnu-Krishna as the supreme lord of all, is not merely one opinion among many but the authoritative declaration of the epic's greatest teacher, spoken at the moment of maximum spiritual clarity (on the brink of death, with Krishna physically present). Madhva argues that this passage settles all theological disputes: any other interpretation of the Mahabharata that does not acknowledge Vishnu's absolute supremacy contradicts its own most authoritative statement.

The Anushasana Parva's practical teachings on charity, vows, and proper conduct are interpreted by Dvaita as practices that generate divine grace (Vishnu-prasada) when performed with devotional intent. Unlike the Advaita reading, which subordinates these practices to jnana, or the Vaishnava-bhakti reading, which subordinates them to emotional devotion, Madhva's Dvaita treats them as genuine means of cultivating Vishnu's favor. Good actions, properly motivated, produce real spiritual merit that draws the soul closer to God — though liberation itself ultimately depends on Vishnu's sovereign grace rather than on accumulated merit alone.
