The Vana Parva's forest exile is read by Dvaita Vedanta as a divinely orchestrated period of preparation in which Vishnu strengthens His devotees for the coming cosmic conflict. Madhva emphasizes that the exile was not punishment but part of God's sovereign plan — the Pandavas needed to acquire specific weapons, alliances, and spiritual maturation that could only be obtained through the experiences of exile. Every event in the forest, from Arjuna's celestial journey to the Yaksha Prashna episode, serves Vishnu's meticulously detailed purpose.

The embedded narratives of the Vana Parva are interpreted by Madhva as encoding theological lessons about the hierarchy of souls and the supremacy of devotion to Vishnu. The story of Nala, who loses his kingdom through a dice game (paralleling Yudhishthira) but is restored through divine grace, demonstrates that Vishnu rescues those who maintain faith through adversity. Madhva's reading identifies hidden references to Vishnu's supremacy even in stories that do not explicitly mention Him, employing his distinctive hermeneutic of multiple meaning-layers.

Bhima's encounters during the forest exile receive particular attention in Dvaita interpretation. His meeting with Hanuman — identified as another incarnation of Vayu — is a pivotal episode where the two manifestations of the same divine being recognize each other. Madhva reads this meeting as confirming the continuity of Vayu's devotional mission across cosmic ages: Hanuman served Rama (Vishnu's previous incarnation) just as Bhima serves Krishna (Vishnu's current incarnation). The Vana Parva thus reveals the deep structure of cosmic history as a repeated pattern of Vishnu's incarnation accompanied by Vayu's devoted service.
