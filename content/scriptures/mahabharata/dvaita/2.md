The Sabha Parva's dice game and Draupadi's humiliation are interpreted by Dvaita Vedanta as a decisive demonstration of the eternal conflict between divine and demonic natures. Madhva's philosophy teaches that souls possess intrinsic natures (svarupa-yogyata) that determine their spiritual trajectory, and the Sabha Parva reveals these natures with unflinching clarity. Duryodhana, Duhshasana, and Shakuni act according to their tamasic essence, while the Pandavas and Draupadi demonstrate the qualities of sattvic souls even in extremity.

Draupadi's appeal to Krishna during her attempted disrobing is a central Dvaita proof-text for the doctrine of absolute divine protection of devotees. Unlike Advaita, which might interpret this episode metaphorically, Dvaita insists on its literal reality: Krishna personally and directly intervened to provide infinite cloth because He is a real, personal God who responds to the genuine cries of His devotees. The miracle is not symbolic but actual, demonstrating Vishnu's omnipotence and His special relationship with those who depend upon Him.

Bhima's sacred vows in the assembly hall — to drink Duhshasana's blood and break Duryodhana's thigh — are treated by Madhva as divinely inspired utterances that carry the force of cosmic law. As Vayu's incarnation, Bhima's words are not mere emotional outbursts but prophetic declarations that Vishnu will ensure are fulfilled. Madhva reads these vows as establishing the moral and cosmic framework for the entire war: every major event in the subsequent parvas moves toward the fulfillment of Bhima's declarations, confirming that God's justice operates through His foremost devotee with absolute certainty.
