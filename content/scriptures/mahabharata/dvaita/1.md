The Adi Parva's narrative of origins is read by Dvaita Vedanta through Madhvacharya's interpretive lens as establishing the cosmic hierarchy (taratamya) that governs the entire epic. The births of the Pandavas are not random events but the incarnation of specific celestial beings according to their intrinsic spiritual rank. Yudhishthira embodies Dharma, Bhima embodies Vayu (the wind god and Vishnu's foremost devotee), Arjuna embodies Indra, and the twins embody the Ashvins — each occupying a precise position in the eternal hierarchy of souls that Madhva's philosophy identifies as the fundamental structure of reality.

Madhva's Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya begins its retelling with the Adi Parva, emphasizing that every detail of the epic encodes theological meaning. The Kauravas' birth from a mass of flesh — unnatural and collective rather than individual — signals their tamasic (dark) nature in the Dvaita classification of souls. Madhva divides all souls into three categories: those destined for liberation (mukti-yogya), those destined for eternal samsara (nitya-samsarin), and those destined for eternal damnation (tamo-yogya). The Kauravas exemplify the third category, making the conflict not merely political but cosmically predetermined.

Bhima's role in the Adi Parva receives special emphasis in Dvaita interpretation. His extraordinary strength, his early conflicts with Duryodhana, and his unwavering loyalty to dharma mark him as Vayu's incarnation — the supreme devotee of Vishnu whose power flows directly from his divine master. Madhva elevates Bhima above Arjuna in spiritual status, reading the Adi Parva's accounts of Bhima's feats as evidence of Vayu's inherent superiority in the cosmic hierarchy, second only to Brahma and Sarasvati among created beings.
