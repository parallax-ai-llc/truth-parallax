The Svargarohana Parva's conclusion is the final proof-text for Madhva's taratamya doctrine. The celestial hierarchy that Yudhishthira observes — souls arranged in their proper stations according to their spiritual status — directly demonstrates the Dvaita teaching that souls are eternally differentiated by intrinsic nature and that liberation consists not in the dissolution of these differences but in their perfected, blissful experience under Vishnu's sovereign governance.

Yudhishthira's initial vision of hell — finding his allies suffering while Duryodhana enjoys heaven — is interpreted by Madhva as a test designed to burn away Yudhishthira's last trace of moralistic pride. The vision's dissolution reveals that apparent injustice was illusion, but this does not (as Advaita would argue) negate the reality of the celestial hierarchy. Instead, it demonstrates that human moral judgment, operating with limited knowledge, often misperceives the perfectly just arrangement that Vishnu has established. The final vision, in which all souls occupy their rightful stations, confirms rather than dissolves the principle of hierarchical differentiation.

Madhva's reading of the Mahabharata's conclusion emphasizes three final doctrinal points. First, Vishnu's sovereignty is absolute: every event in the epic, from the Adi Parva's beginnings to this celestial conclusion, unfolded according to His will. Second, souls are eternally individual: the warriors of Kurukshetra retain their distinct identities in their celestial stations, each experiencing bliss appropriate to their nature and rank. Third, devotion to Vishnu — supremely exemplified by Bhima — is the highest attainment possible for any created being. The Mahabharata thus concludes, in Dvaita reading, as a comprehensive demonstration that reality is personal, hierarchical, and centered on the eternally real, infinitely perfect Supreme Person, Vishnu-Narayana.
