The Ashramvasika Parva's narrative of the elder generation's retirement and death is approached by Dvaita Vedanta with attention to the different spiritual destinations of the departed souls. Madhva's hierarchical cosmology assigns each soul its appropriate post-mortem state based on its intrinsic nature (svarupa), accumulated karma, and degree of devotion to Vishnu. The deaths described in this parva thus have differential spiritual significance.

Vidura's conscious departure — transferring his vital essence to Yudhishthira — is interpreted by Dvaita as the departure of a supremely righteous soul who has fulfilled his cosmic mission. In Madhva's reading, Vidura's special status (as an incarnation of Dharma or Yama, depending on the interpretive line) places him high in the celestial hierarchy. His yogic death demonstrates the powers available to souls who have cultivated deep devotion and spiritual discipline under Vishnu's grace.

Dhritarashtra and Gandhari's deaths in the forest fire present a more complex case for Dvaita theology. Their spiritual destinations depend on their inner relationship with Vishnu at the moment of death. Madhva's tradition teaches that Dhritarashtra, despite his lifelong moral failures — his blindness to his sons' crimes, his inability to act on Vidura's counsel — retained a residual connection to dharma that, purified by his final period of forest asceticism, secured him an honorable (though not supreme) celestial station. Gandhari's accumulated tapas, despite being directed partly by grief and anger, also generated significant spiritual merit. The parva thus illustrates the Dvaita principle that Vishnu's justice is precisely calibrated: every soul receives exactly the post-mortem state appropriate to its total spiritual account.
