The Stri Parva's lamentations are read by Dvaita Vedanta as revealing the suffering that inevitably follows from association with adharmic forces. The Kaurava women's grief, while humanly genuine, is understood in Madhva's framework as the consequence of their husbands' and sons' choices to oppose Vishnu's devotees. Dvaita theology insists on the reality of suffering — unlike Advaita, which treats it as ultimately illusory — and therefore takes the women's grief seriously as a real consequence of real moral choices within a divinely governed cosmos.

Gandhari's curse upon Krishna — that His clan will destroy itself — is handled by Madhva with characteristic theological rigor. Krishna's acceptance of the curse does not indicate vulnerability or guilt but demonstrates His supreme equanimity and His pre-existing plan to withdraw the Yadava dynasty. Madhva argues that Gandhari's curse has no actual power over Vishnu — the Lord is beyond all curses and blessings — but He graciously accepts it because the events it predicts align with His already-determined plan. The curse functions within Vishnu's sovereign will, not against it.

The Stri Parva's cataloguing of the dead warriors, with the women identifying each fallen hero's virtues and accomplishments, provides Madhva with evidence for his theological reading of the epic's characters. Each warrior's spiritual status can be discerned from the manner of their death, their final thoughts, and the trajectory of their souls — information that Madhva systematically analyzes in the Tatparya Nirnaya. The Dvaita hierarchy of souls extends even to the afterlife: those who died fighting alongside the Pandavas (and therefore alongside Vishnu) attain higher celestial stations than those who opposed them.
