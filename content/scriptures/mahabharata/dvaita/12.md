The Shanti Parva's vast teachings are interpreted by Dvaita Vedanta as consistently pointing toward Vishnu's supremacy and the soul's eternal dependence upon Him. Madhva reads the philosophical dialogues of the Moksha-dharma section through his distinctive hermeneutic, identifying passages that establish the five fundamental differences (pancha-bheda) that define Dvaita metaphysics: the differences between God and soul, God and matter, soul and matter, one soul and another, and one form of matter and another.

The Rajadharma sections are approached by Dvaita with practical seriousness. Madhva's tradition does not treat political teachings as merely metaphorical but as genuine guidance for worldly governance under divine authority. A righteous king derives his authority from Vishnu and governs as Vishnu's representative on earth. The detailed instructions on taxation, justice, warfare, and diplomacy are understood as expressing Vishnu's will for an orderly human society in which dharma can flourish and devotion can develop.

The Moksha-dharma's discussions of liberation are read by Madhva as describing the Dvaita concept of moksha: the soul's eternal, blissful experience of its own nature as a knowing, willing servant of Vishnu, accompanied by direct vision (aparoksha-jnana) of the Lord's infinite perfections. Madhva emphatically rejects the Advaita interpretation that these passages teach the soul's merger with Brahman. Instead, he identifies key statements affirming the soul's eternal individuality and its permanent relationship of dependence upon God. The Shanti Parva's philosophical richness is thus read as supporting Dvaita's core conviction: reality is fundamentally plural, hierarchical, and centered on the personal supremacy of Vishnu-Narayana.
