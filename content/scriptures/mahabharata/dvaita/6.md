The Bhishma Parva, containing the Bhagavad Gita, is the theological pinnacle of the Mahabharata for Dvaita Vedanta, just as it is for all Hindu traditions. However, Madhva's Gita commentary (Gita Bhashya and Gita Tatparya) reads the text through radically different philosophical premises than Shankara's Advaita interpretation. For Dvaita, the Gita's supreme teaching is not the identity of the self with Brahman but the eternal, real distinction between God (Vishnu), individual souls (jivas), and matter (prakriti), combined with the soul's absolute dependence on God for liberation.

Madhva interprets the Gita's "I am the Self seated in the hearts of all creatures" (10.20) not as asserting ontological identity between God and souls but as declaring Vishnu's indwelling presence as the inner controller (antaryamin) of all beings. The soul is real, individual, and eternally distinct from God, but it depends on God for its very existence, knowledge, and capacity to act. Liberation (moksha) consists not in merging with Brahman but in the soul's eternal, blissful awareness of its own nature as a dependent servant of Vishnu.

The war narrative surrounding the Gita reveals, in Dvaita reading, the concrete operation of taratamya. Bhishma, despite his personal greatness and his devotion to Krishna, fights on the wrong side — yet his spiritual status remains secure because his inner devotion to Vishnu is genuine. Madhva explains that Bhishma's external position with the Kauravas was determined by his oath, not by his spiritual allegiance. His recognition of Krishna's supremacy, demonstrated through his later recitation of the Vishnu Sahasranama, confirms that the soul's ultimate destiny is determined by its inner devotion rather than its external circumstances.
