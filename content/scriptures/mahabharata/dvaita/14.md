The Ashvamedhika Parva's horse sacrifice is read by Dvaita Vedanta as the proper Vedic culmination of the Pandavas' divinely sanctioned victory. Madhva treats the Ashvamedha as a genuine ritual act that produces real cosmic effects — not merely symbolic — confirming Yudhishthira's sovereignty as Vishnu's appointed ruler. The sacrifice's elaborate procedures are understood as reflecting the intricate order that Vishnu has established for the cosmos, and their faithful performance honors and strengthens that order.

The Anugita's philosophical content is interpreted by Dvaita through Madhva's hermeneutical principles. Where the text appears to teach Advaitic non-dualism, Madhva applies his interpretive rules — particularly the principle that statements seemingly denying distinction between God and soul should be read as expressing intimate relationship rather than ontological identity. Thus "I am in all beings" means "I dwell within all beings as their inner controller," not "I am identical with all beings."

The mongoose episode is given a distinctive Dvaita reading. The mongoose's critique of the Ashvamedha is not a rejection of ritual per se but an assertion that sincerity of devotion matters more than scale of performance. Madhva's Dvaita tradition values both ritual and devotion — they are not opposed but complementary. The poor Brahmin's offering surpassed Yudhishthira's sacrifice in merit because it was performed with absolute self-surrender to Vishnu, the attitude that Dvaita identifies as the soul's highest possible state. The episode teaches that Vishnu evaluates the heart's orientation, not the offering's material value, while simultaneously affirming that proper Vedic ritual, performed with devotion, retains its spiritual efficacy.
