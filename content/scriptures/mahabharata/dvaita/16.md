The Mausala Parva's account of the Yadava destruction is interpreted by Dvaita Vedanta as the divinely pre-ordained conclusion to Krishna's earthly mission, not as a genuine catastrophe. Madhva insists that Krishna, as the Supreme Lord, orchestrated every aspect of the Yadava withdrawal according to His own timeline and purpose. The curses — from Gandhari and from the sages — merely provided the apparent mechanism for events that Vishnu had already determined.

Madhva's treatment of Krishna's physical departure from the world is theologically precise. Unlike Advaita, which might describe Krishna's death as the dissolution of a phenomenal appearance, Dvaita insists that Krishna's body is not material — it is His eternal, divine form (divya-mangala-vigraha) that He chose to withdraw from human perception. Krishna does not die; He returns to His eternal abode (Vaikuntha) where He has always existed in His full divine glory. The hunter Jara, who strikes Krishna with an arrow, acts unknowingly within Vishnu's plan — even this apparently random event is precisely orchestrated.

The Yadavas' self-destruction through intoxicated violence is explained by Dvaita as the consequence of accumulated karmic debt that Vishnu permitted to ripen at the moment of His planned withdrawal. The Yadavas were not ordinary souls but celestial beings who incarnated to assist Krishna's mission; their return to their original celestial states merely required the dissolution of their earthly forms. Madhva's reading thus transforms an apparently tragic narrative into a demonstration of Vishnu's comprehensive sovereignty: the Lord who arranges incarnations also arranges departures, and both serve His eternal, inscrutable purpose.
