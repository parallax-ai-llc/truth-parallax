The Shalya Parva's conclusion of the Kurukshetra War is read by Dvaita Vedanta as the complete execution of Vishnu's cosmic judgment against the forces of adharma. Madhva's interpretation emphasizes the systematic nature of the divine plan: each Kaurava commander was specifically destined to fall at a specific time and by a specific hand, all according to Vishnu's pre-ordained design. The war is not chaos but a precisely orchestrated divine operation.

The mace duel between Bhima and Duryodhana is the supreme moment of Dvaita Mahabharata interpretation. Madhva celebrates Bhima as the epic's greatest devotee-hero, and the fulfillment of his vow to break Duryodhana's thigh is presented as the climactic triumph of devotion over demonic pride. Krishna's signal — slapping His own thigh to indicate where Bhima should strike — demonstrates the intimate coordination between God and His foremost devotee. Bhima does not act independently but as Vishnu's instrument, guided by the Lord's direct command.

Balarama's protest against the foul blow is acknowledged by Madhva but dismissed as reflecting a limited understanding of dharma. Balarama judges by conventional martial rules, but Krishna operates by transcendent divine law. The Dvaita position is uncompromising: whatever Krishna sanctions is righteous, regardless of how it appears by conventional standards. Duryodhana's fall, lying broken on the battlefield with his thigh shattered, represents the ultimate defeat of adharmic power — not through superior strategy or skill but through the irresistible force of Vishnu's will expressed through His devotee Bhima, the incarnation of Vayu.
