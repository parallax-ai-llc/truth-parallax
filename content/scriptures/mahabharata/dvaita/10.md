The Sauptika Parva's night massacre is interpreted by Dvaita Vedanta as revealing the consequences when a powerful soul acts under the influence of tamas (darkness) rather than divine guidance. Ashwatthama, despite his high birth and extraordinary capabilities, aligns himself with destructive forces and perpetrates an act of supreme cowardice. Madhva classifies Ashwatthama's spiritual status with precision: he is not among the liberated souls (mukti-yogya) but a being whose enormous power is misdirected by demonic impulse.

Madhva's reading of the night raid emphasizes its contrast with the Pandavas' divinely guided warfare. Every action the Pandavas took during the war, however morally problematic by conventional standards, was sanctioned by Krishna. Ashwatthama's massacre, by contrast, is self-authorized — driven by personal grief and rage rather than divine command. This distinction is fundamental to Dvaita ethics: the rightness of an action depends not merely on its content but on whether it is performed in alignment with God's will. Identical actions can be righteous (when God-directed) or sinful (when self-directed).

Krishna's protection of the unborn Parikshit from the Brahmastra directed at Uttara's womb demonstrates the Dvaita doctrine of God's invincible sovereignty. No weapon, however powerful — even the Brahmastra, which can destroy the universe — can overcome Vishnu's protective will. Madhva interprets this episode as confirming the absolute hierarchy: Vishnu stands supreme over all astras, all devas, and all cosmic forces. Ashwatthama's eternal punishment — cursed to wander three thousand years with a festering wound — exemplifies the Dvaita teaching that those who attack Vishnu's devotees face consequences proportionate to the cosmic severity of their offense.
