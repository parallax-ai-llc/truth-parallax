The Adi Parva's narrative of origins — the births of the Pandavas and Kauravas, the rivalries and alliances that set the epic in motion — is read by Advaita Vedanta as illustrating the fundamental human predicament of avidya (ignorance). The entire Kuru dynasty's entanglement in desire, attachment, and conflict arises from the failure to recognize the non-dual reality of Brahman that underlies all apparent distinctions between self and other, friend and enemy, gain and loss.

From the Advaita perspective, the miraculous circumstances surrounding the Pandavas' births — divine fathers, supernatural conceptions — do not establish literal divine genealogies but illustrate the Upanishadic teaching that all beings are ultimately expressions of one Brahman. The apparent multiplicity of characters, motivations, and cosmic forces is itself the play of maya, the creative power that projects the appearance of diversity onto the undifferentiated ground of pure consciousness. The Kuru family's internal divisions mirror the illusory fragmentation of the one Self into competing individual selves.

Shankara's philosophical framework invites the reader to look through the Adi Parva's dramatic surface to its deeper teaching: that the attachments driving these characters — to dynasty, honor, kingdom, and family — are rooted in superimposition (adhyasa), the fundamental error of identifying the eternal atman with the transient body-mind complex. The entire epic trajectory, from the Adi Parva's beginnings to the Svargarohana Parva's conclusion, traces the progressive recognition and dissolution of this error. Every conflict that emerges in this opening book is ultimately a symptom of non-recognition of the Self, and the epic's resolution will lie in knowledge, not in victory.
