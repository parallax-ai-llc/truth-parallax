The Sabha Parva's dice game and Draupadi's humiliation are approached by Advaita Vedanta as a dramatic demonstration of the suffering that arises from identification with the phenomenal world. Yudhishthira's compulsive gambling — staking his kingdom, his brothers, himself, and finally Draupadi — exemplifies the bondage of a soul entangled in the web of maya. Despite being renowned for wisdom and dharma, Yudhishthira falls prey to the same fundamental error that afflicts all unenlightened beings: attachment to worldly possessions and relationships mistaken for the Self.

The assembly hall itself, constructed by the asura architect Maya with supernatural illusion, serves as a potent metaphor for the Advaita concept of maya. Just as Maya Danava's hall confuses perception — making water appear solid and solid ground appear liquid — so the phenomenal world confuses the unenlightened soul into mistaking the transient for the eternal. Duryodhana's initial humiliation in this illusory hall foreshadows his later role as the supreme embodiment of ahamkara (ego) whose destruction is necessary for dharma's restoration.

Draupadi's crisis in the assembly reveals, in Advaita reading, the moment when conventional dharmic structures collapse and only transcendent reality remains. The elders' paralysis before Draupadi's suffering demonstrates that worldly knowledge and authority cannot resolve the deepest human crises. From the Advaita perspective, Draupadi's appeal to a higher power represents the soul's turning from the phenomenal world toward Brahman — the recognition that no finite resource can provide ultimate protection and that only the infinite Self is truly real and dependable.
