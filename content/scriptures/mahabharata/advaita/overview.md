## Overview

Advaita Vedanta approaches the Mahabharata as an *itihasa* (historical narrative) that serves as a vehicle for the highest philosophical teaching: the identity of the individual self (atman) with the absolute reality (Brahman). While the epic's dramatic surface recounts war and political struggle, Advaita interpreters — beginning with Shankara — read its philosophical embedded texts (the Bhagavad Gita, the Moksha-dharma, and the Anugita) as pointing toward non-dual realization as the supreme goal.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Shankara's Framework
Shankara did not compose a commentary on the entire Mahabharata, but his Gita Bhashya establishes the interpretive lens: the epic's conflicts arise from *avidya* (ignorance) and the solution is *jnana* (knowledge of the non-dual self). Arjuna's despair exemplifies the confusion caused by superimposing bodily identity onto the eternal, unchanging atman.

### The Moksha-dharma
The Shanti Parva's Moksha-dharma section (chapters 174-365) contains extensive philosophical dialogues that Advaita interpreters regard as articulating proto-Vedantic ideas about the unreality of phenomenal distinctions and the primacy of Brahman-knowledge.

## Theological Emphases

- **Ignorance as Root Cause**: The entire Kuru conflict stems from attachment rooted in *avidya*
- **Knowledge as Liberation**: True resolution comes not through war but through self-knowledge
- **Maya and Appearance**: The epic's dramatic world illustrates the play of *maya*
- **Sannyasa Ideal**: The Pandavas' final renunciation (Mahaprasthanika Parva) represents the culmination of spiritual life
