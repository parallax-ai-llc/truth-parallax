The Mahaprasthanika Parva is the Mahabharata's most explicitly renunciatory book, and Advaita Vedanta reads it as the narrative culmination of its philosophical teaching. The Pandavas' decision to abandon their kingdom and walk toward death represents the ultimate vairagya — dispassion toward worldly existence — that Advaita identifies as the essential precondition for liberation. Having fulfilled all worldly duties, the Pandavas enact the highest form of sannyasa: the great departure (mahaprasthana) from which there is no return.

The sequential falling of Draupadi and the brothers is interpreted by Advaita as the progressive dissolution of the pancha-kosha — the five sheaths that, according to Taittiriya Upanishad teaching, cover the true Self. Each character's fall represents the shedding of one layer of phenomenal identity: the physical body (annamaya kosha), the vital breath (pranamaya kosha), the mind (manomaya kosha), the intellect (vijnanamaya kosha), and finally the bliss sheath (anandamaya kosha). Only when all five sheaths are transcended does the pure Self — symbolized by Yudhishthira, the embodiment of dharma — stand naked before the Absolute.

Yudhishthira's solitary ascent with only a dog for companion represents the jnani's final journey — stripped of all relationships, possessions, and identities, accompanied only by dharma itself. Advaita commentators note that this radical aloneness (kaivalya) is not loneliness but the fullness of self-knowledge in which all apparent others are recognized as the one Self. The mountain summit toward which Yudhishthira walks is Brahman itself — the ultimate reality that the Mahabharata's entire trajectory has been pointing toward from the Adi Parva onward.
