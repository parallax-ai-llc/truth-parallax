The Ashramvasika Parva's narrative of withdrawal and death enacts the Advaita teaching on the ashrama system as a progressive movement toward liberation. Dhritarashtra's retirement to the forest represents the vanaprastha stage that, in Advaita understanding, prepares the soul for the final stage of complete renunciation (sannyasa). Even the blind king, whose attachment to his sons caused immeasurable suffering, is finally compelled by the logic of human existence to release his worldly bonds.

Vidura's death through yogic withdrawal — transferring his life-force to Yudhishthira — exemplifies the Advaita concept of the realized soul's conscious departure from the body. Unlike ordinary death, which is governed by accumulated karma and experienced in confusion, the jnani's death is a deliberate act of consciousness. Vidura's life-force merging with Yudhishthira symbolizes, at the Advaita level, the dissolution of individual consciousness into the universal Self — not a transfer between two separate beings but the recognition that what appeared as two was always one.

The forest fire that consumes the elder generation is read as a metaphor for the fire of knowledge (jnanagni) that burns away the residual karma (prarabdha) sustaining the body of an embodied liberated being (jivanmukta). In Advaita teaching, even after self-knowledge dawns, the body continues to exist until its prarabdha karma is exhausted — like a potter's wheel that continues spinning after the potter's hand is removed. The forest fire represents the final exhaustion of this remaining karma, releasing the souls from their last connection to phenomenal existence and establishing them in the unconditioned reality of Brahman.
