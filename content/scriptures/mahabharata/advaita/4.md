The Virata Parva's central theme of disguise and concealed identity resonates profoundly with Advaita Vedanta's teaching on the atman hidden within the layers of phenomenal appearance. The Pandavas' assumption of false identities at Virata's court mirrors the universal condition described in Advaita philosophy: the true Self (atman), which is pure consciousness identical with Brahman, is concealed beneath the superimposed identities of body, mind, social role, and personal history.

Each Pandava's disguise illustrates a different aspect of phenomenal concealment. Arjuna as Brihannala — the mighty warrior hidden beneath the appearance of a eunuch dance-teacher — most dramatically demonstrates the Advaita principle that the Self's nature bears no necessary relation to its apparent form. The atman is neither male nor female, neither warrior nor dancer; it is pure consciousness that merely appears to be this or that particular individual through the power of maya.

The parva's conclusion, when the Pandavas shed their disguises and reveal their true identities, provides an analogy for the Advaita experience of liberation (moksha). Just as the Pandavas were always themselves beneath their assumed identities, so the atman is always Brahman beneath the superimposed layers of name and form. Liberation is not the acquisition of something new but the recognition of what has always been the case — the removal of the concealing power of ignorance that prevented self-knowledge. The dramatic revelation scene, where Arjuna defeats the entire Kaurava army while his true identity blazes forth, symbolizes the moment of enlightenment when the infinite Self shines through the dissolving veils of avidya.
