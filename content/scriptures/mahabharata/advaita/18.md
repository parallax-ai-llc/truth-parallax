The Svargarohana Parva's conclusion — Yudhishthira's vision of hell dissolving into the reality of heaven — is the Mahabharata's most perfect Advaita parable. The apparent experience of hell, with its suffering and injustice, proves to be entirely illusory; what is real is the unchanging, blissful state in which all souls abide according to their true nature. This narrative structure mirrors the Advaita teaching on liberation with remarkable precision: awakening from the dream of samsara reveals that bondage was never real, that the Self was always free, and that what appeared as suffering was the product of ignorance alone.

Yudhishthira's moral outrage at finding Duryodhana in heaven and his allies in hell represents the last flaring of dualistic judgment — the final barrier to non-dual realization. His choice to remain in hell with his loved ones rather than enjoy heaven without them, while morally admirable, is itself transcended when the entire distinction between heaven and hell dissolves. Advaita interpreters read this as teaching that even the noblest ethical sentiments must ultimately yield to the recognition that reality is one, undivided, and beyond all moral categories.

The Mahabharata concludes with the affirmation that dharma, artha, kama, and moksha are all taught within its compass — but Advaita Vedanta insists that moksha is the supreme goal that renders the other three meaningful. The entire epic, read through Advaita eyes, is a vast demonstration of the inadequacy of action, desire, and even conventional righteousness to provide lasting happiness, and the necessity of self-knowledge as the sole means to liberation. The Svargarohana Parva's dissolving illusions are the epic's final gift: the revelation that what was sought was never lost, that the Self was always Brahman, and that the long journey from the Adi Parva to this moment was, in the deepest sense, no journey at all.
