The Bhishma Parva, containing the Bhagavad Gita, is the philosophical centerpiece of the Mahabharata for Advaita Vedanta. Shankara's Gita Bhashya established the Gita as one of the three foundational texts (prasthanatraya) of Vedanta, alongside the Upanishads and the Brahma Sutras. In Shankara's reading, the Gita's ultimate teaching is the identity of the individual self (atman) with the supreme Self (Brahman), and all the paths it describes — karma yoga, bhakti yoga, raja yoga — are preparatory stages leading to this supreme knowledge (jnana).

Arjuna's crisis on the battlefield is interpreted by Advaita as a crisis of ignorance rather than mere emotional weakness. His attachment to family members, his fear of sin, and his grief over the prospect of killing kinsmen all arise from the false identification of the Self with the body-mind complex. Krishna's response — beginning with "The wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead" (2.11) — initiates the Advaita teaching that the real Self is unborn, undying, and untouched by the body's activities. This is not cold indifference but the highest knowledge: when one knows the Self to be imperishable, the apparent tragedy of war is seen in its true perspective.

Shankara reads Chapter 13's distinction between kshetra (the field, i.e., the body-mind-world) and kshetrajna (the knower of the field, i.e., pure consciousness) as the Gita's clearest Advaita statement. The knower of all fields is one — Brahman — and the apparent multiplicity of individual knowers is the product of upadhi (limiting adjuncts). When the limiting adjuncts are removed through knowledge, the individual self recognizes its identity with the universal Self, and liberation is attained without any action or movement whatsoever.
