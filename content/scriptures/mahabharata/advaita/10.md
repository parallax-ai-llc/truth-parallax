The Sauptika Parva's night massacre, perpetrated by Ashwatthama in a frenzy of grief and rage, represents the darkest consequence of avidya in the Mahabharata. Advaita interpreters read Ashwatthama's rampage as demonstrating what happens when powerful beings act under the complete domination of the kleshas — afflictions of ignorance, ego, attachment, aversion, and clinging to life — identified in both Yoga and Advaita psychology as the roots of suffering.

Ashwatthama's state of mind before the night raid is described as a burning confusion of grief, rage, and desperation — a textbook case of what Advaita philosophy calls avarana (the concealing power of maya) and vikshepa (the projecting power that creates distorted perceptions). Under these powers' influence, Ashwatthama cannot distinguish between legitimate warrior action and murderous rampage. His inability to perceive the moral reality of his situation — killing sleeping, unarmed warriors including children — demonstrates the complete obscuration of discriminative wisdom by emotional turbulence.

The Brahmastra launched against the unborn Parikshit represents the attempt to destroy the future itself — to extend destruction beyond the present moment into posterity. Advaita commentators note that this ultimate act of nihilism is the logical endpoint of ego-driven action: when the self is identified with its grievances, satisfaction requires the annihilation of everything. The reversal of this destruction — Parikshit's revival — affirms the Advaita teaching that the real Self (atman) cannot ultimately be destroyed. Ignorance can produce immense suffering within the phenomenal world, but it cannot touch the eternal, unchanging consciousness that is the true nature of all beings.
