The Drona Parva's escalating violence and moral compromise are read by Advaita Vedanta as demonstrating the inevitable degradation that occurs when beings act from ignorance rather than knowledge. The war's progressive abandonment of ethical norms — the violation of rules of engagement, the killing of helpless opponents, the resort to deception — illustrates the Advaita teaching that dharma at the phenomenal level is inherently unstable and cannot provide ultimate resolution.

Abhimanyu's death in the Chakravyuha provides a powerful metaphor for the Advaita understanding of samsara. The Chakravyuha — a labyrinthine formation that can be entered but not escaped without specific knowledge — mirrors the soul's entrapment in the cycle of birth and death. One enters samsara through the momentum of past karma but cannot escape without the discriminative knowledge (viveka) that distinguishes the real Self from its phenomenal coverings. Abhimanyu's heroic but doomed struggle within the formation represents the bound soul fighting valiantly but futilely within samsara's structures without possessing the liberating knowledge.

The deception used to kill Drona — Yudhishthira's half-truth about Ashwatthama's death — raises questions about the nature of truth that Advaita philosophy addresses at a fundamental level. From the Advaita perspective, all statements within the phenomenal world partake of relative truth (vyavaharika satya) rather than absolute truth (paramarthika satya). The absolute truth — Brahman alone is real — renders all phenomenal events, including the war's moral dilemmas, part of the dream-like play of maya. This does not excuse immorality but recontextualizes it within a framework where ultimate resolution lies in awakening from the dream, not in perfecting conduct within it.
