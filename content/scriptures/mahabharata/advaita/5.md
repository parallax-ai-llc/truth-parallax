The Udyoga Parva's failed peace negotiations are read by Advaita Vedanta as demonstrating the ultimate impossibility of resolving conflicts rooted in ignorance through merely worldly means. Diplomacy, however skilled, cannot overcome the fundamental error of self-identification with the body and its possessions. Duryodhana's refusal to concede even five villages to the Pandavas stems from the deepest form of avidya — the identification of self with territory, power, and ego that makes compromise appear as self-destruction.

The Sanatsujatiya, a philosophical dialogue embedded in the Udyoga Parva, is of particular importance for Advaita interpretation. Sanatsujata's teaching to Dhritarashtra on the nature of death, the self, and Brahman anticipates key Advaita doctrines. His declaration that death does not truly exist — that what appears as death is merely the transformation of phenomenal forms while the eternal Self remains unchanged — echoes the Upanishadic teaching that Shankara would later systematize. The Sanatsujatiya's insistence that Brahman-knowledge alone liberates the soul from the cycle of birth and death places it squarely within the Advaita tradition.

Vidura's counsel throughout this parva represents the voice of practical wisdom (vyavaharika satya) operating within the phenomenal world. Advaita interpreters note that Vidura's advice, while morally impeccable, cannot prevent the war because it operates at the level of conventional reality. The deeper problem — humanity's bondage to ignorance — cannot be resolved through political negotiation. The Udyoga Parva's tragic conclusion, with war becoming inevitable, demonstrates that the suffering of samsara can only be transcended through knowledge of the non-dual Self, not through rearranging the phenomenal world's power structures.
