The Anushasana Parva's practical teachings on charity, vows, and conduct are approached by Advaita Vedanta as descriptions of preparatory disciplines (sadhana chatushtaya) that purify the mind for the reception of Brahman-knowledge. Shankara's philosophical system identifies four prerequisites for self-inquiry: discrimination between the real and unreal (viveka), dispassion toward worldly enjoyment (vairagya), the six virtues of mental discipline (shama, dama, uparati, titiksha, shraddha, samadhana), and the intense desire for liberation (mumukshutva). The Anushasana Parva's ethical teachings are read as cultivating these qualities.

Shankara's commentary on the Vishnu Sahasranama, contained in this parva, provides a remarkable example of Advaita hermeneutics. While the hymn appears to praise Vishnu as a personal deity distinct from other gods and from individual souls, Shankara interprets each name as describing an attribute of nirguna Brahman — the attributeless Absolute that is the true Self of all beings. "Vishnu" itself is etymologized as "the all-pervading one," which for Advaita refers not to a personal deity but to the infinite consciousness that permeates all existence without distinction.

The parva's teachings on dana (giving) are reinterpreted by Advaita as pointing toward the ultimate gift — the relinquishment of the false self. All external charity is preparation for the supreme renunciation in which the ego surrenders its claim to separate existence and recognizes its identity with Brahman. Bhishma's final instructions thus move from the practical to the philosophical, from worldly dharma to ultimate liberation, tracing the path that Advaita identifies as the soul's journey from ignorance to self-knowledge.
