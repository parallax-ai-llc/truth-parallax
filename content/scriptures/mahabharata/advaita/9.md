The Shalya Parva's depiction of the war's final day and its aftermath brings the Advaita theme of suffering through ignorance to its climax. The near-total destruction of both armies — leaving only a handful of survivors from the millions who fought — starkly demonstrates the futility of action driven by ego-identification. All the ambitions, grievances, and heroic aspirations that fueled the war are reduced to a field of corpses, a result that Advaita interpreters present as the inevitable outcome of samsaric existence untransformed by self-knowledge.

Duryodhana's final stand, alone at a lake after his entire army's destruction, is a powerful image of the ego stripped of all its supports. The king who refused to share five villages now has nothing — no army, no kingdom, no allies. Advaita commentators see in Duryodhana's isolation the predicament of the ego (ahamkara) when its projections collapse. Yet even in this extremity, Duryodhana does not attain self-knowledge; he remains defiant, proud, identified with his warrior self to the last. His claim that he will ascend to heaven as a hero demonstrates that the ego's illusions can persist even in the face of total material destruction.

The mace duel's conclusion through a blow forbidden by dharmic convention illustrates the Advaita teaching that conventional morality, while necessary at the phenomenal level, cannot provide absolute guidance. The war's resolution through rule-breaking reveals the inherent limitations of all relative frameworks. From the paramarthika (absolute) standpoint, the distinction between fair and foul, dharma and adharma, is itself part of the phenomenal play. Only Brahman-knowledge, which transcends all dualities, offers genuine resolution — a teaching the Mahabharata will complete in its final books.
