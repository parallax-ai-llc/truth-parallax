The Vana Parva's forest exile is read by Advaita Vedanta as an extended period of spiritual preparation through vanaprastha — the withdrawal from worldly life that creates conditions for self-knowledge. The Pandavas' forced departure from civilization into the forest mirrors the aspirant's voluntary renunciation of worldly attachments in pursuit of Brahman-knowledge. The forest, in Upanishadic tradition, is the original setting of the highest philosophical teachings (the Aranyakas — "forest texts").

The embedded narratives of the Vana Parva are particularly rich for Advaita interpretation. The Yaksha Prashna episode, where Yudhishthira answers the questions of a disguised Yaksha, contains several responses that align with Advaita philosophy. When asked "What is the greatest wonder?" Yudhishthira replies that although every creature sees others dying, no one believes they will die — a teaching on the universal delusion (moha) that conceals the impermanent nature of phenomenal existence while the immortal Self remains unrecognized.

The story of Savitri, who rescues her husband from death through determination and wisdom, illustrates the Advaita principle that knowledge conquers death. Savitri's victory over Yama is achieved not through martial power but through discriminative wisdom (viveka) — the ability to distinguish the real from the unreal, the eternal from the transient. Arjuna's acquisition of celestial weapons during this parva is treated by Advaita commentators as secondary to the philosophical teachings embedded in these narratives. Weapons destroy bodies, which are transient; knowledge destroys ignorance, which is the root of all bondage. The Vana Parva thus presents both paths but consistently points toward jnana as the higher resolution.
