The Stri Parva's lamentations present the full weight of samsaric suffering — the grief of mothers, wives, and daughters over the dead of Kurukshetra. Advaita Vedanta approaches this parva with philosophical rigor while acknowledging the emotional reality of the suffering depicted. The women's grief is real at the phenomenal level (vyavaharika), and the epic makes no attempt to minimize it. Yet Advaita interpretation simultaneously points to the deeper truth: the Self that the women mourn as dead is, in reality, unborn and imperishable.

This parva echoes and deepens the Bhagavad Gita's opening teaching. Just as Krishna told Arjuna that the wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead, the Stri Parva demonstrates what happens when this teaching is not realized — the inconsolable suffering of those who identify the Self with the body. The women's grief is not morally wrong; it is the natural consequence of avidya. Advaita does not condemn grief but diagnoses its root cause and prescribes self-knowledge as the ultimate remedy.

Gandhari's curse upon Krishna — born from decades of accumulated ascetic power — raises the Advaita question of whether spiritual practice performed without self-knowledge can lead to liberation. Advaita Vedanta's answer is clear: tapas, vows, and asceticism, however powerful, remain within the realm of karma so long as they are performed by an ego that identifies with the body-mind complex. Gandhari's tremendous spiritual power produces a devastating curse, not liberation, because her practice was motivated by grief and attachment rather than the pursuit of Brahman-knowledge. Her example illustrates the Advaita distinction between spiritual power and spiritual liberation — they are not identical.
