The Mausala Parva's account of the Yadava dynasty's self-destruction poses a distinctive challenge for Advaita interpretation. If Krishna represents Brahman manifest in human form, what does His clan's destruction and His own death signify? Advaita Vedanta resolves this by distinguishing between the eternal, attributeless Brahman (nirguna) and its temporary manifestation within the phenomenal world (saguna). The destruction of the Yadavas and Krishna's physical departure do not affect Brahman itself, which remains eternally unchanging and all-pervading.

The Yadavas' intoxicated mutual slaughter demonstrates the Advaita principle that material prosperity without self-knowledge inevitably leads to destruction. The Yadavas enjoyed the highest possible worldly advantage — proximity to the divine incarnation — yet this proximity could not save them from the consequences of their own moral decay. Advaita interpreters draw the lesson that external association with sacred reality, without internal transformation through knowledge, provides no lasting benefit. Only the internalization of self-knowledge — the direct recognition of Brahman as one's own Self — constitutes genuine spiritual attainment.

Krishna's departure from the phenomenal world is read by Advaita as the dissolution of a particular manifestation of Brahman, not the death of the Absolute. Just as the space within a pot is not destroyed when the pot breaks — it merely merges with the infinite space that was always its true nature — so the consciousness that appeared as Krishna returns to the infinite consciousness that it always was. For Advaita Vedanta, this understanding transforms the Mausala Parva from a narrative of loss into a teaching on the imperishable nature of reality itself.
