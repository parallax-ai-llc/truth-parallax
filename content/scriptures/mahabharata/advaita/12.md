The Shanti Parva is the most philosophically important parva for Advaita Vedanta after the Bhishma Parva's Bhagavad Gita. Its Moksha-dharma section contains extensive philosophical dialogues that articulate proto-Vedantic ideas about the nature of Brahman, the self, and liberation — ideas that Shankara and subsequent Advaita thinkers would systematize. These dialogues between sages, kings, and celestial beings explore the questions that define Advaita inquiry: What is ultimately real? What is the nature of the self? How is liberation attained?

The Moksha-dharma's teaching on the three levels of reality — paramarthika (absolute), vyavaharika (conventional), and pratibhasika (illusory) — can be traced in embryonic form through its philosophical exchanges. Passages describing Brahman as the unchanging substratum of all phenomenal change, the self as identical with this Brahman, and ignorance as the sole cause of bondage articulate the core Advaita position centuries before Shankara's formal systematization. Advaita interpreters regard these passages as evidence that their philosophical tradition has roots in the most ancient strata of Hindu thought.

The Rajadharma section's teachings on governance are interpreted by Advaita as applying to the inner kingdom of the self as much as to external political authority. The king who governs wisely mirrors the awakened being who has mastered the senses, mind, and ego. The political chaos that necessitated the Kurukshetra War resulted from failures of inner governance — the inability of the Kuru elders to control their own attachments and fears. Advaita reading thus transforms the Shanti Parva's political teachings into a spiritual psychology of self-mastery that culminates in the recognition of the non-dual Self.
