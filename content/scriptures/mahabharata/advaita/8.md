The Karna Parva's tragic narrative centers on a character whose entire life is shaped by misidentification — a problem that lies at the very heart of Advaita Vedanta. Karna, raised as a charioteer's son while actually being Kunti's firstborn and the Pandavas' eldest brother, lives and dies under a false identity. His story is the epic's most poignant illustration of the Advaita concept of adhyasa (superimposition): just as the rope is mistaken for a snake, Karna mistakes his social identity for his true nature, and this error determines the entire course of his life.

Advaita interpreters note that Karna's tragedy is not that he lacked virtue or ability but that his actions were oriented by a false understanding of who he was. His loyalty to Duryodhana, his enmity toward the Pandavas, and his relentless pursuit of martial glory are all based on a superimposed identity that does not correspond to his actual nature. When Krishna reveals Karna's true parentage, offering him the chance to assume his rightful identity, Karna refuses — choosing to maintain the superimposed identity rather than accept the truth. This refusal mirrors the spiritual seeker's resistance to Brahman-knowledge, clinging to familiar but false self-conceptions.

Karna's death on the battlefield, his chariot wheel stuck in the earth, represents the moment when the consequences of misidentification become inescapable. Advaita philosophy teaches that ignorance generates karma, and karma generates suffering — a chain that can only be broken through knowledge. Karna's virtues, however admirable, could not save him because they operated within the framework of a false self, illustrating the Advaita insistence that no amount of good action (karma) can substitute for the liberating knowledge (jnana) of one's true nature as the non-dual Self.
