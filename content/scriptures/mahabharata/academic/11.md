The Stri Parva has attracted increasing scholarly attention as a key text for understanding the Mahabharata's representation of women, its treatment of grief and mourning, and its literary relationship to other epic lamentation traditions. The parva's catalogue of women grieving over identified corpses has been compared to similar scenes in the Iliad (Andromache's lament for Hector), Greek tragedy (Euripides' Trojan Women), and other world traditions of war literature.

Scholars including Sally Sutherland Goldman and Alf Hiltebeitel have analyzed the Stri Parva's women's speeches as possessing a distinctive rhetorical authority that challenges the epic's predominantly male perspective. The women's laments do not merely express personal grief but articulate a systematic critique of the war's moral justifications. Gandhari's accusation of Krishna — holding him responsible for a war he could have prevented — has been studied as the epic's most direct challenge to the divine plan that is presented elsewhere as unchallengeable.

The parva's ritual dimensions have also been examined. The women's procession to the battlefield, their identification and lamentation of the dead, and the subsequent cremation rites reflect actual ancient Indian funeral practices documented in the Grihya Sutras and other ritual texts. Scholars have noted that the Stri Parva gives detailed attention to the practical mechanics of mass cremation, suggesting familiarity with actual post-battle conditions. The parva's compressed but intense emotional register — described by some scholars as the Mahabharata's most emotionally powerful section — has been analyzed as a deliberate counterpoint to the preceding books' detailed celebration of martial prowess.
