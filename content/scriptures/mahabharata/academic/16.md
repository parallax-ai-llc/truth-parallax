The Mausala Parva is one of the Mahabharata's shortest books, and its compressed narrative of the Yadava dynasty's self-destruction has been studied by scholars for its literary, theological, and possibly historical implications. The parva's account of an entire clan destroying itself through drunken violence has been compared to other ancient narratives of civilizational collapse, including the Norse Ragnarok and the destruction of Sodom in the Hebrew Bible.

Krishna's death — struck by a hunter's arrow while resting in the forest — has attracted considerable scholarly attention. The seemingly unheroic manner of the Supreme Lord's death has been analyzed as a deliberate narrative choice that resists conventional expectations of divine invulnerability. Scholars including Alf Hiltebeitel have argued that Krishna's death scene echoes and inverts the death of the Vedic god Prajapati, connecting the avatar's departure to deep mythological patterns of divine sacrifice and cosmic renewal.

The Mausala Parva's relationship to the broader Mahabharata narrative has been debated. Some scholars regard it as belonging to the epic's oldest narrative core, arguing that the tradition of the Yadava destruction is ancient and that later Vaishnava editors would have suppressed rather than created such a narrative. Others suggest it was added to explain the absence of the Yadavas from the post-epic political landscape. The parva's terse style — notably different from the expansive narration of the didactic parvas — has been interpreted as either archaic compression or deliberate literary restraint in the face of events too terrible for elaboration.
