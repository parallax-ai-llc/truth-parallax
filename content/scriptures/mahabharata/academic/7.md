The Drona Parva covers the central phase of the Kurukshetra War and is one of the longest books of the Mahabharata. Scholarly analysis identifies this parva as containing both archaic battle narratives and substantial later additions, including the Narayaniya section — a major theological treatise on the relationship between Vishnu-Narayana and Shiva that scholars regard as a relatively late interpolation reflecting sectarian debates of the early centuries CE.

The Narayaniya has received extensive scholarly attention as a key document in the history of Vaishnavism and Hindu theology more broadly. Scholars including R.G. Bhandarkar and Suvira Jaiswal have analyzed it as evidence for the development of the Pancharatra and Bhagavata religious movements, tracing the theological evolution from Vedic religion to classical Vaishnavism. The section's treatment of the Shiva-Vishnu relationship has been studied as evidence for the negotiation between rival devotional movements in the early centuries of the Common Era.

The military narrative of the Drona Parva raises important questions about the epic's treatment of martial ethics. The progressive breakdown of the rules of engagement — culminating in the deception used to kill Drona — has been analyzed by scholars as reflecting a deliberate literary and philosophical strategy. Kevin McGrath and others have argued that the epic intentionally portrays the degradation of dharmic warfare to make a larger philosophical point about the impossibility of "clean" violence. The Chakravyuha episode and Abhimanyu's death have been studied as examples of the epic's tragic mode, comparable to the deaths of Patroclus in the Iliad and Roland in the Chanson de Roland.
