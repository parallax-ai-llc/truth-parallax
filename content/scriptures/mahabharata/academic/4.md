The Virata Parva is one of the shortest and most narratively focused books of the Mahabharata, and scholars have suggested that its relatively compact structure may indicate that it suffered less editorial expansion than longer, more didactically oriented parvas. The narrative's tight focus on plot — the Pandavas' disguises, their adventures at Virata's court, and the climactic battle — has characteristics associated with the epic's older narrative layers.

The motif of the hero in disguise is well attested in world epic traditions, and scholars have drawn parallels with Odysseus's return to Ithaca, various folk-tale traditions of the disguised prince, and the broader Indo-European pattern of the hero's temporary abasement before his triumphant return. The year of incognito exile has been analyzed as a liminal period — a ritualized interval of non-identity between the loss of sovereignty (Sabha Parva) and its recovery (the war).

Arjuna's disguise as Brihannala, a eunuch dance-teacher, has attracted considerable scholarly attention. Ruth Vanita and others have examined this episode in relation to ancient Indian conceptions of gender and sexuality, noting that the text treats Arjuna's temporary gender transformation with relative matter-of-factness. The Kichaka episode — his attempted sexual assault on Draupadi and his subsequent killing by Bhima — has been studied as reflecting ancient Indian attitudes toward women's vulnerability and male protective obligations. The Virata Parva's battle scene, in which Arjuna single-handedly defeats the Kauravas, employs the heroic aristeia pattern common to Sanskrit and Greek epic literature.
