The Vana Parva is the Mahabharata's second-longest book and the one most extensively augmented through the insertion of sub-narratives. Scholarly analysis identifies the exile narrative as a relatively thin framework upon which an enormous body of pilgrimage literature, didactic tales, and mythological material has been grafted. The parva's manuscript tradition shows exceptionally high variation, suggesting that it was a particularly active site of editorial expansion.

The embedded narratives — Nala-Damayanti, Savitri-Satyavan, Rama's story (an independent Ramayana retelling), and many others — have been studied both as literary works in their own right and as evidence for the process of epic growth. Scholars such as John Brockington have demonstrated that the Vana Parva's Rama narrative represents an early version of the Ramayana tradition that differs in significant details from Valmiki's text, providing evidence for the fluid relationship between India's two great epics.

The Kiratarjuniya episode (Arjuna's encounter with Shiva disguised as a hunter) has attracted scholarly attention for its theological implications. Peter Bisschop and other scholars of early Shaivism have analyzed this episode as evidence for the integration of Shaiva devotion into the originally Vaishnava-inflected epic, reflecting the growing prominence of Shiva worship in the period of the epic's expansion. The tirtha-yatra (pilgrimage) sections embedded in the Vana Parva have been studied as among the earliest surviving guides to Indian sacred geography, providing evidence for pilgrimage networks and the sanctification of landscape in ancient India.
