The Karna Parva has attracted scholarly attention both for its martial narrative and for the complex characterization of Karna, whom many scholars regard as the Mahabharata's most psychologically compelling figure. The parva's detailed account of the Karna-Arjuna rivalry has been analyzed through multiple lenses: as heroic aristeia comparable to Homeric combat, as a narrative exploration of fate versus free will, and as a study in the tragic consequences of social exclusion.

Karna's identity crisis — raised as a charioteer's son despite being a kshatriya by birth — has been studied by scholars including Simon Brodbeck and Kevin McGrath as the epic's most sustained engagement with the tensions within the varna (caste) system. Karna's repeated humiliations on account of his perceived low birth, and his compensatory pursuit of martial excellence, reflect genuine social anxieties about birth-status versus achieved merit that the epic both dramatizes and critiques.

The ethical dimensions of Karna's death — killed while defenseless, with his chariot wheel stuck — have been extensively debated. Scholars have noted that the text itself presents multiple moral perspectives on the event: Krishna's utilitarian justification (cataloguing the Kauravas' prior crimes), Karna's appeal to fair play, and the narrator's implicit ambivalence. This multiperspectival approach to ethical dilemmas has been identified by scholars like Bimal Matilal and Martha Nussbaum as one of the Mahabharata's distinctive contributions to world ethical thought — presenting genuine moral complexity without resolving it into a single authoritative judgment.
