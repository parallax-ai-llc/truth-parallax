The Ashvamedhika Parva has been studied by scholars both for its account of the Ashvamedha ritual and for its embedded philosophical text, the Anugita. The horse sacrifice itself is one of the most extensively documented Vedic rituals, attested in Brahmana texts and performed by historical Indian kings well into the Gupta period (4th-5th century CE). Scholars have compared the Mahabharata's account with the ritual prescriptions in the Shatapatha Brahmana and other Vedic sources.

The Anugita — a post-war philosophical dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna — has received less scholarly attention than the Bhagavad Gita but is increasingly recognized as an important philosophical text in its own right. Krishna's disclaimer that he cannot reproduce his Gita teaching has been interpreted by scholars as a narrative device acknowledging the uniqueness of the Gita while introducing a related but distinct philosophical discourse. The Anugita's content has been analyzed as reflecting a somewhat later philosophical milieu than the Gita, with greater emphasis on Samkhya categories and less on bhakti.

The mongoose episode has fascinated scholars as a narrative subversion of the very ritual the parva celebrates. The half-golden mongoose who declares that a poor Brahmin's selfless gift surpassed the merit of Yudhishthira's grand sacrifice has been interpreted as a critique of ritualism that scholars connect to broader anti-ritualist currents in Indian religious history, from the Upanishadic internalization of sacrifice to Buddhist and Jain critiques. The episode's inclusion within a parva ostensibly celebrating the Ashvamedha creates a deliberate tension that scholars regard as characteristic of the Mahabharata's refusal to endorse any single perspective without qualification.
