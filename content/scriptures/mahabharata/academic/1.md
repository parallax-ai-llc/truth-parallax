The Adi Parva is the Mahabharata's longest introductory section, establishing the genealogical, mythological, and narrative framework for the entire epic. Scholarly analysis identifies this parva as one of the most stratified portions of the text, containing material ranging from what may be among the epic's oldest narrative cores to relatively late interpolations including cosmogonic myths, sage-lineage lists, and didactic sub-narratives.

The Critical Edition prepared by V.S. Sukthankar and his colleagues at the Bhandarkar Oriental Research Institute demonstrated significant variation in the Adi Parva across manuscript traditions. The Southern recension contains substantially more material than the Northern, including expanded versions of several sub-narratives and additional didactic passages. These variations provide evidence for the epic's growth through regional editorial processes over several centuries.

Key scholarly questions surrounding the Adi Parva include the historicity of the genealogical material, the relationship between the Kuru dynasty as depicted and actual Vedic-period polities known from other sources, and the function of the elaborate frame narrative (Ugrasravas reciting to the sages at Naimisha forest, who heard it from Vaishampayana at Janamejaya's snake sacrifice). The frame narrative's multiple layers — story within story within story — have been analyzed by scholars including Alf Hiltebeitel and Madeleine Biardeau as reflecting both oral performance conventions and a sophisticated literary strategy for embedding diverse material within a unified narrative structure. The Adi Parva's sub-stories (Shakuntala, Yayati, the churning of the ocean) function as thematic prefigurations of the main narrative's central conflicts.
