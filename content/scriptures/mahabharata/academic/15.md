The Ashramvasika Parva has been studied by scholars for its treatment of the ashrama system (the four stages of life), its narrative of generational transition, and its depiction of the complex emotional aftermath of the war. The parva's account of Dhritarashtra's retirement to the forest reflects the vanaprastha ideal documented in dharmashastra literature, providing a narrative illustration of the transition from householder responsibilities to ascetic withdrawal.

Scholars have noted that the Ashramvasika Parva presents a remarkably nuanced psychological portrait of the relationship between the victors and the defeated. Bhima's expressed resentment toward Dhritarashtra — reminding the blind king of his sons' crimes — and the tension this creates within the Pandava family have been analyzed as evidence of the epic's refusal to present a sanitized post-war reconciliation. The emotional complexity of the interactions between Yudhishthira (who respects Dhritarashtra), Bhima (who resents him), and Dhritarashtra (who harbors suppressed fury toward Bhima) has been compared by literary scholars to the psychological realism of modern fiction.

Vyasa's summoning of the dead warriors from the Ganges — allowing the living to see and speak with their fallen relatives one final time — has been analyzed as a liminal ritual bridging the worlds of the living and dead. Scholars have compared this episode to necromantic traditions in Greek epic (Odyssey Book 11) and other cultures. The episode's emotional impact — the brief, bittersweet reunion followed by permanent separation — has been read as the epic's acknowledgment that narrative closure cannot fully heal the wounds of war.
