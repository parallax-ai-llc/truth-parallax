The Bhishma Parva is dominated by the Bhagavad Gita (chapters 25-42 of the parva), the most extensively studied text in all of Indian philosophy. Academic scholarship on the Gita addresses questions of dating, compositional history, philosophical coherence, and reception. The broad scholarly consensus places the Gita's composition between the 3rd century BCE and the 2nd century CE, though significant disagreement persists about more precise dating.

The question of whether the Gita is a unitary composition or a multi-layered text assembled over time has generated extensive debate. Richard Garbe and E.W. Hopkins proposed that an original theistic poem was progressively expanded with Samkhya, Yoga, and Vedantic material. More recent scholars, including Angelika Malinar and Robert Minor, have argued for greater compositional unity, reading the text's philosophical diversity as intentional synthesis rather than accidental accretion. The relationship between the Gita and the surrounding Mahabharata narrative remains a key scholarly question: is the Gita an organic part of the epic or a later interpolation?

The war narrative in the Bhishma Parva has been studied for its military content, with scholars examining the described battle formations, weapons, and tactics for evidence of actual ancient Indian warfare. The vyuha (formation) descriptions have been compared with Kautilya's Arthashastra and other military texts. The description of Kurukshetra itself has been correlated with the known geography of Haryana, where the site is traditionally identified. Archaeological evidence from the region, while not conclusive, is consistent with significant settlement and political importance during the late Vedic period.
