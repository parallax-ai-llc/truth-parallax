## Overview

Academic scholarship approaches the Mahabharata as one of the most complex textual traditions in world literature. The Bhandarkar Oriental Research Institute's Critical Edition (1919-1966), which collated over 1,200 manuscripts, remains the foundational scholarly text. Scholars debate the epic's composition history, dating, relationship to historical events, and literary structure, generally viewing it as a multi-layered work that grew over centuries from a shorter martial narrative (*Jaya*) to its present encyclopedic form.

## Key Scholarly Approaches

### Composition and Dating
The epic is generally dated between 400 BCE and 400 CE in its final form, with a core narrative possibly reaching back to the late Vedic period (c. 900-800 BCE). V.S. Sukthankar's work on the Critical Edition demonstrated the existence of a "shared archetype" underlying all manuscript traditions, while also revealing massive regional variation.

### Oral and Literary Traditions
Scholars such as John Brockington and John Smith have examined the Mahabharata's relationship to oral-formulaic composition, comparing it with Homeric epic traditions. The epic shows evidence of both oral performance conventions and literate editorial intervention.

## Research Themes

- **Stratigraphy**: Identifying the oldest and youngest layers within the text
- **Historical Kernel**: Whether the Kurukshetra War reflects actual historical events (c. 900 BCE?)
- **Didactic Expansion**: How philosophical, legal, and devotional material was incorporated into the narrative frame
- **Regional Recensions**: Significant differences between Northern and Southern manuscript traditions
- **Performance Traditions**: Living recitation and dramatic traditions across South and Southeast Asia
