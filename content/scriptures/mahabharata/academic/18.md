The Svargarohana Parva, the Mahabharata's final book, has been studied by scholars as one of the most philosophically and literarily sophisticated conclusions in world epic literature. The narrative of Yudhishthira's experience of illusory hell — finding Duryodhana in heaven and his own allies in torment, then discovering that this vision was a final test — has been analyzed as an epistemological parable about the unreliability of appearances and the limits of human moral judgment.

Scholars have debated the parva's relationship to the epic's broader moral framework. Some read the dissolving hell vision as affirming cosmic justice: the apparent injustice was only an appearance, and true justice prevails in the end. Others, including Matilal and Doniger, argue that the episode deliberately unsettles any simple moral calculus — if even Duryodhana attains heaven through his warrior's death, what was the point of the war? This interpretive ambiguity has been identified as characteristic of the Mahabharata's distinctive approach to ethical complexity.

The epic's concluding verses — which summarize its contents and assert its equivalence with the Vedas — have been studied as evidence of the text's self-conscious canonization. The famous verse "dharma, artha, kama, and moksha are all found here" represents the epic's claim to encyclopedic comprehensiveness. Scholars have compared this self-referential conclusion to similar devices in other ancient texts and analyzed it as evidence of the Mahabharata's transition from oral performance tradition to fixed literary canon. The Svargarohana Parva's meta-narrative dimension — the story commenting on its own significance — has been studied as one of the most sophisticated instances of literary self-reflection in ancient world literature.
