The Shanti Parva is the Mahabharata's longest book and the focus of some of the most important recent academic work on the epic. James Fitzgerald's translation and analysis (University of Chicago Press, 2004) has demonstrated the parva's philosophical sophistication and its central importance for understanding ancient Indian thought on governance, ethics, and liberation. The Shanti Parva is widely regarded by scholars as one of the epic's latest major additions, incorporating material from diverse philosophical and legal traditions.

The Rajadharma section has been studied alongside the Arthashastra of Kautilya, the Manusmriti, and other dharmashastra texts as evidence for ancient Indian political thought. Scholars have identified both continuities and tensions between the Shanti Parva's idealistic vision of righteous kingship and the more pragmatic (some would say cynical) approach of Kautilyan statecraft. The section on danda-niti (the science of punishment) has been analyzed as reflecting genuine debates about the use of coercive state power in maintaining social order.

The Moksha-dharma section is of enormous scholarly importance for the history of Indian philosophy. It contains early formulations of Samkhya, Yoga, Pashupata, and proto-Vedantic thought, often in dialogue form, that predate the classical systematizations of these schools. Scholars including Erich Frauwallner and Gerald Larson have mined the Moksha-dharma for evidence of pre-classical philosophical positions that were later absorbed into or displaced by the formal darshana systems. The section's philosophical pluralism — presenting multiple, sometimes contradictory positions without resolving them into a single system — has been interpreted by scholars as reflecting the genuinely open intellectual climate of its period.
