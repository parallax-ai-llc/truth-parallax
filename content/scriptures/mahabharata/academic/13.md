The Anushasana Parva continues the didactic project of the Shanti Parva and has been studied by scholars as a compendium of dharmic instruction that draws on multiple genres: legal (dharmashastra), devotional (stuti/stotra), narrative (itihasa), and philosophical (darshana). The parva's encyclopedic scope — covering topics from the proper treatment of Brahmins to the theology of the Vishnu and Shiva Sahasranamas — reflects the Mahabharata's self-understanding as a comprehensive guide to dharma.

The Vishnu Sahasranama and Shiva Sahasranama contained in this parva have been studied by scholars as important documents in the history of Hindu devotion. The inclusion of hymns to both Vishnu and Shiva within a single textual context has been analyzed as evidence for the Mahabharata's broadly inclusive theological stance — or, alternatively, as evidence of competing sectarian interpolations, with Vaishnava and Shaiva editors each inserting their preferred material. The commentary traditions on the Vishnu Sahasranama (Shankara, Parashara Bhattar, Madhva) have been studied as documenting sectarian interpretive strategies applied to a shared text.

The Anushasana Parva's teachings on dana (gift-giving) have been analyzed by scholars including Maria Heim as reflecting and shaping actual social practices of patronage, religious endowment, and reciprocal obligation in ancient Indian society. The detailed prescriptions regarding what gifts to give, to whom, and under what circumstances provide evidence for the economics of religious life and the material support systems that sustained Brahminical culture.
