The Udyoga Parva's extended diplomatic negotiations have been studied by scholars as reflecting genuine ancient Indian practices of inter-state relations, alliance formation, and pre-war diplomacy. The detailed descriptions of embassy protocols, messenger conventions, and the rhetoric of war and peace provide valuable evidence for the political culture of the late Vedic and early classical periods, though scholars caution against treating the epic as a straightforward historical source.

Krishna's embassy to the Kaurava court has been analyzed as a masterpiece of diplomatic rhetoric. R.P. Goldman and others have examined the speeches delivered by Krishna, Bhishma, Drona, Vidura, and Duryodhana as examples of sophisticated political argumentation that draws on multiple rhetorical traditions. The Sanatsujatiya, a philosophical dialogue embedded in the parva, has been studied as an early Upanishadic-style text that may have been incorporated into the epic from an independent source.

The Udyoga Parva's military catalogues — lists of warriors, armies, and alliances — have been compared by scholars to similar catalogues in Homer's Iliad (the Catalogue of Ships) and other world epics. These lists may preserve genuine clan and tribal names from the late Vedic period, though the extent to which they reflect historical realities is debated. The parva's treatment of the ethics of war — particularly the debate over whether peace or war is the appropriate response to Duryodhana's intransigence — has been studied alongside ancient Indian treatises on statecraft (arthashastra) and dharma, revealing tensions between pragmatic political reasoning and ethical idealism.
