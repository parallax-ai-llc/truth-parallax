The Mahaprasthanika Parva is the Mahabharata's penultimate book and one of its most discussed sections in scholarly literature. The Pandavas' renunciation of their kingdom and their northward journey toward Mount Meru — during which all but Yudhishthira die — has been analyzed as combining multiple literary and religious motifs: the final pilgrimage, the journey to the otherworld, and the testing of the hero.

The sequential deaths of Draupadi and the Pandava brothers have been interpreted by scholars through multiple frameworks. One approach reads the deaths as a moral accounting, with each character's "failing" representing a genuine moral judgment by the epic's authors. Another approach, advocated by scholars such as Wendy Doniger, reads the attributed failings ironically — as the epic's acknowledgment that no human being, however righteous, is without flaw. A third approach, drawing on anthropological parallels, interprets the deaths as a ritual shedding of companions necessary for the hero's solitary passage to the otherworld.

The loyal dog who accompanies Yudhishthira has been studied as one of the Mahabharata's most symbolically rich motifs. The dog's association with the god Dharma, combined with its ritual impurity in Brahmanical tradition, creates a deliberate paradox that scholars have connected to the epic's broader interrogation of conventional dharmic categories. Yudhishthira's refusal to abandon the dog — choosing loyalty to a despised animal over entry into heaven — has been compared to similar ethical test narratives in world literature and analyzed as the epic's final statement on the primacy of compassion over ritual purity.
