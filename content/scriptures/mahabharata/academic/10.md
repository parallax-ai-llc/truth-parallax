The Sauptika Parva's night massacre represents the Mahabharata's most extreme transgression of martial dharma, and scholars have analyzed its narrative function, theological implications, and literary structure. The parva's association of Ashwatthama's rampage with Shiva's destructive power has been studied as evidence for the integration of Shaiva theology into the epic's later strata, with some scholars identifying the Shiva-possession narrative as a sectarian addition.

The parva's literary structure — moving from Ashwatthama's anguished vigil through the invocation of Shiva to the systematic slaughter — has been analyzed as following the pattern of a perverted sacrifice. Several scholars, including Madeleine Biardeau, have identified structural parallels between the night raid and Vedic ritual, arguing that Ashwatthama's massacre functions as a dark inversion of the war-sacrifice that the Kurukshetra battle represents. The sleeping warriors become unwitting sacrificial victims in a rite that produces not cosmic renewal but sterile destruction.

The Brahmastra episode — Ashwatthama's weapon directed at the Pandava lineage's unborn heir — has been studied in the context of ancient Indian weapons literature and the concept of astronomical or supernatural weapons (astras). Scholars have debated whether these weapons reflect mythologized memories of actual military technologies, symbolic representations of cosmic forces, or purely literary devices. The intervention that saves Parikshit has been analyzed as narratively essential: the future king is needed for the frame narrative (Janamejaya, Parikshit's son, is the audience for the Mahabharata's recitation), creating a self-referential loop that scholars have compared to similar meta-narrative devices in other literary traditions.
