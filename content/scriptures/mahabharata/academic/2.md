The Sabha Parva's dice game episode is widely regarded by scholars as belonging to the epic's oldest narrative stratum. The motif of a king losing his kingdom through gambling is attested in Vedic literature (the Rig Veda's gambler's hymn, 10.34) and may reflect actual practices in early Indian royal culture. Scholars such as J.C. Heesterman and David Shulman have analyzed the dice game as a ritual inversion of the royal consecration (rajasuya), in which the king ritually stakes and recovers his sovereignty.

The Draupadi disrobing scene has received extensive scholarly attention as a crux of the epic's moral and narrative architecture. Alf Hiltebeitel has argued that the scene functions as a pivotal sacrifice — Draupadi's humiliation generates the moral and cosmic energy that drives the entire war. The legal debates in the assembly — whether Yudhishthira had the right to stake Draupadi after having already lost himself — reflect genuine concerns of ancient Indian jurisprudence and have been analyzed in relation to dharmashastra literature.

The construction of Maya Danava's assembly hall, with its architectural illusions, has been studied as evidence of Indian awareness of sophisticated architectural and artistic traditions. The rivalry between Yudhishthira's and Duryodhana's courts reflects the competitive royal culture documented in Vedic and post-Vedic sources. Scholars have noted that the Sabha Parva's political narrative — alliance building, tributary relationships, and imperial expansion through the rajasuya — provides valuable (if idealized) evidence for understanding the political structures of early India, though caution is required in extracting historical data from a literary text.
