The Shalya Parva concludes the war narrative proper and has been studied by scholars for its treatment of the war's devastating aftermath. The rapid narration of the final day — Shalya's brief command and death, the massacre of the remaining Kaurava forces, and Duryodhana's solitary retreat — has been analyzed as a deliberate narrative acceleration reflecting the exhaustion of both the armies and the epic's own capacity to find meaning in continued violence.

The mace duel between Bhima and Duryodhana has received significant scholarly attention as a climactic encounter laden with ritual significance. David Shulman and others have interpreted the duel as a form of ritual combat that resolves the conflict through a controlled, witnessed encounter between the two parties' champions — a practice with parallels in many ancient cultures. The controversial blow to Duryodhana's thigh has been analyzed in relation to symbolic body geography in ancient Indian thought, where the thigh carries associations with sexuality and sovereignty.

Duryodhana's final speech — claiming moral victory despite military defeat — is regarded by many scholars as one of the epic's most powerful literary moments. His argument that the Pandavas' victory was achieved through systematic cheating raises questions that the text deliberately refuses to resolve fully. Scholars have noted that the epic gives Duryodhana eloquent final words rather than silencing him, suggesting that the narrator intends the audience to grapple with the moral ambiguity of the entire war rather than simply celebrating the Pandavas' triumph. This literary strategy has been compared to the complexity of Greek tragic characterization.
