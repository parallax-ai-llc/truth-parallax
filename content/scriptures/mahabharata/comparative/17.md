The Mahaprasthanika Parva's final journey toward the cosmic mountain belongs to one of humanity's most ancient narrative patterns: the ascent to heaven or the otherworld. Parallels include Elijah's ascension in a chariot of fire (2 Kings 2), Enoch's journey through the heavens (Book of Enoch), Muhammad's Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj), Dante's ascent through Purgatorio to Paradiso, and the Daoist journey to the Kunlun mountains. In each tradition, the journey upward combines physical movement with spiritual transformation.

The sequential falling of companions along the journey resonates with the motif of progressive stripping found in initiation rituals and mystical texts worldwide. Inanna's descent through seven gates (each removing an article of clothing/power), Dante's progressive purification on the mount of Purgatory, the Sufi concept of successive stations (maqamat) along the spiritual path, and the Buddhist progression through jhana states all describe a process of shedding layers before the ultimate encounter with the divine. The Pandavas' companions fall away like these shed layers, leaving Yudhishthira increasingly alone before the Absolute.

The faithful dog who accompanies Yudhishthira — and who turns out to be Dharma in disguise — connects to the worldwide tradition of the divine test disguised as a compassion challenge. Abraham's hospitality to the three strangers (Genesis 18), the Buddhist Jataka tales of the Bodhisattva's compassion toward animals, and the Sufi tradition of the saint who serves the despised all test the protagonist's willingness to extend kindness beyond conventional boundaries. Yudhishthira's refusal to abandon the dog affirms compassion as the supreme virtue — a teaching shared, in different forms, by virtually every major religious tradition.
