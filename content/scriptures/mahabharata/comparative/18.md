The Svargarohana Parva's dissolving vision of hell — apparent torment revealed as a final illusion before the bliss of heaven — invites comparison with other traditions' visions of the afterlife and their relationship to earthly justice. Dante's Commedia presents a stable, hierarchical afterworld where justice is fixed and eternal; the Tibetan Bardo Thodol describes intermediate states shaped by the dying person's consciousness; the Quranic descriptions of paradise and hellfire serve as moral incentives; and Platonic eschatology (the Myth of Er) presents afterlife experiences as educative rather than punitive. The Mahabharata's approach is uniquely subversive: its afterlife vision dissolves upon examination, suggesting that heaven and hell themselves may be pedagogical devices rather than fixed realities.

Yudhishthira's final test — his outrage at finding Duryodhana in heaven — raises the question of divine justice that every religious tradition must address. The parable of the workers in the vineyard (Matthew 20), the Buddhist teaching that even Devadatta will eventually attain Buddhahood, and the Islamic tradition of divine mercy extending to unexpected recipients all challenge the human expectation that cosmic justice should mirror human moral intuitions. The Mahabharata's conclusion, where all warriors attain celestial stations regardless of which side they fought on, represents perhaps the most radical version of this challenge.

As a conclusion to the world's longest epic, the Svargarohana Parva can be compared to the endings of other great narrative traditions. The Odyssey's peaceful resolution, the Aeneid's ambiguous final scene, the Gilgamesh epic's melancholy acceptance of mortality, and the Divine Comedy's beatific vision each offer a different answer to the question of how suffering resolves. The Mahabharata's answer — that the distinction between suffering and bliss is itself an illusion that dissolves in the light of ultimate reality — stands as one of the most philosophically ambitious conclusions in world literature, inviting comparison with the great mystical traditions of every civilization.
