The Stri Parva's women's laments over the dead of Kurukshetra belong to one of the most ancient and universal genres of world literature: the war lamentation. Comparable texts include the Sumerian "Lament for Ur," the Trojan women's laments in Homer and Euripides, the Irish keening tradition, and the biblical Book of Lamentations. In each tradition, women's voices articulate the human cost of violence that male-dominated war narratives often minimize or heroicize.

The women's perspective on the war fundamentally challenges the heroic narrative that precedes it. Where the battle parvas celebrate martial prowess and strategic cunning, the Stri Parva confronts the reader with the broken bodies and shattered families that heroism produces. This juxtaposition is shared by other traditions: the Iliad's final book, where Priam retrieves Hector's body and the women lament, functions similarly as a corrective to the preceding books' celebration of Achilles' wrath. The Mahabharata's treatment is more extensive, devoting an entire parva to the women's grief.

Gandhari's curse upon Krishna — holding the divine protector responsible for a war he could have prevented — raises a theological question common to monotheistic and theistic traditions alike: the problem of theodicy. If an omnipotent, benevolent God exists, why does suffering persist? The Hebrew Bible's Job, the Quran's treatment of divine testing, and Buddhist reflections on suffering (dukkha) all engage this question. Gandhari's accusation is particularly striking because it is directed not at an absent deity but at God physically present, making her challenge both personal and cosmic.
