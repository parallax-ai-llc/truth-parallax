The Adi Parva's genealogical and mythological foundations invite comparison with origin narratives across world traditions. The Kuru dynasty's origins — marked by divine births, curses, boons, and sibling rivalry — share structural features with the patriarchal narratives of Genesis (Jacob and Esau, Joseph and his brothers), the founding myths of the Greek royal houses (the House of Atreus), and Norse dynastic sagas. In each tradition, a family's founding story establishes the moral and cosmic parameters within which future conflict will unfold.

The Pandavas' miraculous conception through divine fathers echoes the widespread motif of semi-divine heroic birth found in Greek epic (Heracles, Perseus), Norse mythology (Sigurd), and the Hebrew Bible (Samson). Yet the Mahabharata's treatment differs from these parallels in its systematic theological framework: each divine father corresponds to a specific cosmic principle (Dharma, Vayu, Indra, the Ashvins), creating a hero-team whose combined powers represent a complete divine intervention in human affairs. This structural approach contrasts with the more individualistic heroic births of Western traditions.

The rivalry between the Pandavas and Kauravas — five righteous brothers against one hundred antagonists — establishes a numerical and moral asymmetry that resonates across cultures. The biblical David-and-Goliath pattern, the Arthurian contrast between the Round Table knights and their enemies, and the Norse opposition between the Aesir and the giants all employ similar structural contrasts. The Mahabharata's distinctive contribution is the moral complexity it introduces from the very beginning: the Kauravas are not simply evil but deeply human in their jealousies and insecurities, a characterological depth that anticipates modern literary treatment of antagonists.
