The Anushasana Parva's detailed instructions on charity, vows, and proper conduct invite comparison with the practical ethical teachings found across world religious traditions. The parva's detailed prescriptions on dana (gift-giving) parallel the Torah's ma'aser (tithing) requirements, the Quran's zakat obligation, Buddhist dana practice, and the Christian tradition of almsgiving. Each tradition treats generous giving as simultaneously a social obligation, a spiritual practice, and a mechanism for cosmic balance.

The hymnic tradition represented by the Vishnu Sahasranama and Shiva Sahasranama has parallels in the Ninety-Nine Names of Allah in Islamic tradition, the various litanies of divine names in Catholic and Orthodox Christianity (Litany of the Saints, Akathist Hymn), and the Buddhist practice of reciting the names of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas. In each tradition, the enumeration and recitation of divine names is understood as both an act of worship and a method of contemplation that progressively reveals the divine nature to the devotee.

The deathbed teaching scenario — Bhishma instructing from his bed of arrows — connects to the widespread tradition of the dying sage's last words. Socrates' final teachings in the Phaedo, the Buddha's last discourse (Mahaparinibbana Sutta), Jacob's blessings of his sons in Genesis, and the Prophet Muhammad's farewell sermon all share the premise that proximity to death confers a unique authority and clarity on the speaker's words. The Mahabharata extends this motif more than any other tradition, devoting two entire parvas to Bhishma's deathbed discourse — a testament to the epic's conviction that wisdom is best transmitted at the boundary between life and death.
