The Mausala Parva's account of a mighty dynasty destroying itself from within resonates with traditions of civilizational collapse found across cultures. The fall of Troy, the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah, the Norse Ragnarok, and the Buddhist narrative of the Shakyas' destruction all portray the annihilation of once-great peoples through a combination of moral decay and cosmic necessity. The Yadava destruction shares with these narratives the theme that no earthly power, however divinely favored, is permanent.

Krishna's death — struck by a hunter's arrow while resting under a tree — invites comparison with the deaths of divine or semi-divine beings in other traditions. The death of Baldr in Norse mythology (killed by an unexpected missile), the crucifixion of Christ (divinity suffering human death), and the parinirvana of the Buddha (the enlightened one's physical dissolution) all address the paradox of divinity experiencing mortality. Each tradition resolves this paradox differently: Norse mythology treats it as tragedy, Christianity as redemptive sacrifice, Buddhism as demonstration of impermanence, and Hinduism as the voluntary withdrawal of a cosmic manifestation.

The self-inflicted nature of the Yadava destruction — they kill each other with weapons fashioned from a cursed iron pestle — connects to the theme of auto-destruction through hubris found in Greek tragedy, the Tower of Babel narrative, and the Buddhist Jataka tales about proud kingdoms brought low. The Mahabharata's distinctive treatment combines the auto-destruction motif with the cosmic withdrawal motif: the Yadavas destroy themselves because the era itself is ending, not merely because of individual moral failures.
