The Ashvamedhika Parva's horse sacrifice invites comparison with sovereignty rituals across ancient cultures. The Roman triumph, the Pharaoh's sed-festival, the Chinese imperial Heaven sacrifice, and the medieval European coronation all serve the same fundamental purpose: ritually establishing and renewing the ruler's legitimate authority. The Ashvamedha's specific mechanism — a consecrated horse wandering freely for a year, with the king's army following to challenge anyone who impedes it — has no precise parallel but shares with other traditions the association of sovereign power with territorial command and divine sanction.

The tension between ritual grandeur and spiritual sincerity — crystallized in the mongoose episode — resonates with critiques of religious institutionalism found across traditions. The Hebrew prophets' denunciation of empty sacrifice ("I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — Hosea 6:6), the Buddha's rejection of Vedic ritualism, Jesus's critique of Pharisaic legalism, and Sufi critiques of externalized Islamic practice all assert the primacy of inner sincerity over outward performance. The Mahabharata's mongoose story places this critique within the ritual text itself, creating a self-subverting narrative structure that is characteristically Indian.

The Anugita's relationship to the Bhagavad Gita — a secondary teaching that acknowledges the unrepeatable nature of the original revelation — parallels the relationship between primary and secondary revelations in other traditions. The Hebrew Bible's distinction between Torah and subsequent prophecy, the Islamic distinction between the Quran and hadith, and the Buddhist distinction between the Buddha's direct teachings and later commentaries all address the question of how secondary discourse relates to originary revelation.
