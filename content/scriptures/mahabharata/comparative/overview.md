## Overview

Comparative approaches to the Mahabharata situate it within global traditions of epic literature, moral philosophy, and sacred narrative. Its sheer scope — encompassing war, law, philosophy, cosmology, and devotion — makes it a uniquely rich text for cross-cultural analysis. Scholars and readers across traditions have drawn parallels with Homer's Iliad and Odyssey, the Norse sagas, the Arthurian legends, and the great narratives of the Abrahamic traditions.

## Key Comparative Approaches

### Epic Literature
The Mahabharata's kinship conflict, heroic aristeia (battle exploits), and divine intervention invite comparison with the Iliad. Both epics treat war as a crucible of moral values, yet the Mahabharata's philosophical depth — particularly the Bhagavad Gita — distinguishes it from purely martial narratives.

### Just War Theory
The epic's extended deliberation over the ethics of warfare (especially in Udyoga and Shanti Parvas) parallels Western just war traditions from Augustine to Aquinas, as well as discussions in Islamic jurisprudence and Confucian statecraft.

## Comparative Themes

- **Heroic Code vs. Moral Law**: Tension between warrior honor and transcendent duty, comparable to Greek tragedy and Norse sagas
- **Divine Intervention**: Krishna's role parallels but also differs significantly from Greek divine participation in mortal affairs
- **Exile and Return**: The Pandavas' forest exile echoes the exile motif in the Odyssey, the Hebrew Bible, and Arthurian legend
- **Apocalyptic War**: Kurukshetra as cosmic destruction parallels Ragnarok, Armageddon, and other end-time conflicts
- **Wisdom Literature**: The embedded didactic sections compare with Ecclesiastes, the Analects, and Plato's dialogues
