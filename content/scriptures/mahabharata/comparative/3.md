The Vana Parva's exile narrative belongs to one of the most widespread patterns in world literature: the hero's temporary banishment and eventual return. Odysseus's decade of wandering, the Israelites' forty years in the wilderness, Rama's fourteen-year forest exile, and the Arthurian knights' questing adventures all employ the exile motif as a period of testing, transformation, and preparation for the hero's triumphant restoration. The Pandavas' thirteen-year exile combines elements of all these patterns.

The forest as a space of spiritual transformation connects the Vana Parva to the wilderness traditions of multiple religions. The Hebrew prophets encounter God in the desert; the Buddha attains enlightenment in the forest at Bodh Gaya; Jesus faces temptation in the wilderness; Celtic saints seek God on remote islands. In each tradition, withdrawal from civilization enables a deeper encounter with the divine. The Pandavas' forest exile similarly combines physical hardship with spiritual deepening, and the embedded pilgrimage narratives establish the forest as sacred geography.

The Yaksha Prashna episode — Yudhishthira answering a mysterious being's riddles to save his brothers' lives — belongs to the worldwide tradition of the wisdom contest or trial by questioning. Parallels include the riddle of the Sphinx in the Oedipus cycle, Solomon's reputation for answering all questions, the Norse Vafthrudnismal (Odin's wisdom contest with a giant), and Zen koan practice. In each case, the ability to answer correctly demonstrates not mere cleverness but profound understanding of reality's deepest structures, and the stakes are life and death.
