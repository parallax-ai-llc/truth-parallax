The Shalya Parva's depiction of the war's final day and Duryodhana's last stand invite comparison with other traditions' portrayals of defeated antagonists. Duryodhana's solitary retreat to the lake, his defiant challenge to the Pandavas, and his eloquent dying speech recall Hector's final confrontation with Achilles, Roland's death at Roncesvalles, and the Norse Ragnarok's doomed last battles. In each tradition, the defeated warrior's courage in the face of inevitable destruction confers a dignity that complicates simple moral judgments.

The mace duel as a decisive combat resolving a larger conflict belongs to the tradition of judicial combat or trial by battle found across Indo-European cultures. The David-Goliath encounter, the Horatii-Curiatii combat in Roman tradition, and medieval European judicial duels all rest on the premise that a single combat between champions can legitimately determine the outcome of a larger dispute. The Mahabharata's version is complicated by the foul blow to Duryodhana's thigh, which introduces the question of whether legitimate ends justify illegitimate means — a question central to political philosophy from Machiavelli to modern just war theory.

Duryodhana's dying claim that he lived gloriously and will ascend to heaven while the Pandavas won through dishonor resonates with a perennial question in ethics: whether means matter as much as ends. The Mahabharata's refusal to silence Duryodhana's critique — allowing the defeated antagonist to articulate a genuinely powerful moral objection to the victors' methods — has been compared to Shakespeare's practice of giving his villains compelling speeches and to the moral complexity of modern war literature.
