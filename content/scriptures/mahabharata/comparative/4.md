The Virata Parva's narrative of heroes in disguise connects to a pervasive motif in world literature: the sovereign hidden in humble form. Odysseus returns to Ithaca disguised as a beggar; King Alfred hides among Danish peasants; Harun al-Rashid wanders Baghdad incognito; the Chinese emperor Qianlong travels in disguise to understand his people's condition. In each case, the disguise tests both the hero's patience and the surrounding society's capacity to recognize true worth beneath appearances.

Arjuna's specific disguise as Brihannala — a eunuch who teaches dance to royal women — introduces the theme of gender transformation that appears across world traditions with varying degrees of seriousness. Greek mythology's Achilles hiding among women on Skyros, the Norse Thor disguised as a bride to recover his hammer, and various trickster traditions' gender-crossing episodes all explore the boundary between masculine and feminine identity. The Mahabharata's treatment is comparatively matter-of-fact, presenting gender fluidity as one element of a divinely arranged plan rather than as comedy or scandal.

The Virata Parva's climactic battle — one hero defeating an army — participates in the aristeia tradition shared by multiple epic cultures. Arjuna's solo victory parallels Achilles' rampage in Iliad Book 20-22, Roland's stand at Roncesvalles, and Beowulf's solitary combats. Yet the Mahabharata introduces a distinctive element: the hero fights not for personal glory but to maintain his disguise until the proper moment of revelation, making the battle simultaneously a military triumph and a spiritual discipline of concealment.
