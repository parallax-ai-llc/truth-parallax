Karna is frequently compared to tragic heroes across world literature. His combination of extraordinary virtue and fatal positioning — allied with the wrong side through bonds of loyalty and honor — parallels Hector in the Iliad, who fights valiantly for a cause doomed by Paris's transgression. Both warriors possess genuine nobility, both are aware of their likely defeat, and both choose to fight out of loyalty to their people rather than flee. The difference lies in self-knowledge: Hector knows his identity while Karna's is concealed from him, adding an Oedipal dimension to his tragedy.

Karna's lifelong struggle against social exclusion — denied recognition as a warrior because of his apparent low birth — connects to a universal theme of merit versus ascription that appears in traditions from Cinderella to the Arthurian Sword in the Stone. The question of whether birth or ability should determine social standing is explored across cultures, from Confucian debates about hereditary versus merit-based governance to European Enlightenment arguments against aristocratic privilege. Karna's story gives this universal theme a specifically tragic inflection: he proves his merit through extraordinary achievement but is destroyed because social structures prevent his true identity from being recognized until too late.

The final duel between Karna and Arjuna — the two greatest warriors of their generation, revealed to be brothers — echoes the fratricidal combat motif found in traditions from Cain and Abel to the Irish saga of Cuchulain fighting Ferdiad. The tragic irony of brothers killing brothers in war is a theme that the Mahabharata shares with the American Civil War narrative and the mythologies of divided nations worldwide.
