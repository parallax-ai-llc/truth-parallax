The Bhagavad Gita, embedded in the Bhishma Parva, is the most widely compared Indian text in world philosophical and religious studies. Its dialogue between a divine teacher and a morally troubled warrior on the eve of battle has been set alongside the Sermon on the Mount, the Dhammapada, Plato's dialogues, and the Tao Te Ching as one of humanity's foundational spiritual texts. Each comparison illuminates both similarities and distinctive features.

The Gita-Sermon on the Mount comparison, popularized by Mahatma Gandhi among others, highlights contrasting approaches to violence and non-violence. While Jesus teaches radical non-resistance ("turn the other cheek"), Krishna counsels righteous warfare. Yet both teachings share a deeper structural similarity: both subordinate external action to inner disposition. Jesus emphasizes purity of heart over outward conformity; Krishna emphasizes detachment from results (nishkama karma) over avoidance of action. Both relocate morality from the external act to the agent's internal orientation.

The battlefield setting of divine teaching has parallels in other traditions — the Buddha's Fire Sermon, Muhammad's farewell sermon, and Confucius's teachings amid political chaos — yet the Gita's specific pairing of ultimate philosophical discourse with imminent mortal combat is unique. The text's synthesis of multiple philosophical paths (knowledge, action, devotion) into a single framework has been compared to the synthetic achievements of Thomas Aquinas's Summa Theologica and Zhu Xi's Neo-Confucian synthesis, each of which attempted to unify diverse intellectual traditions into a coherent whole.
