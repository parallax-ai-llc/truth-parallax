The Shanti Parva's extensive teachings on governance and dharma invite comparison with the great political-philosophical texts of other civilizations. Bhishma's deathbed discourse on statecraft parallels Plato's Republic and Laws, Aristotle's Politics, Kautilya's Arthashastra, Confucius's political teachings in the Analerta, and the Islamic mirrors for princes tradition. Each tradition addresses the fundamental question of how power should be organized and exercised for the common good.

The Rajadharma sections resonate particularly with the Confucian ideal of governance through virtue (de) and the Platonic concept of the philosopher-king. Like Confucius, Bhishma emphasizes that the ruler's personal virtue is the foundation of political order — a corrupt king produces a corrupt society. Like Plato, the Mahabharata recognizes that wisdom, not merely power, qualifies one to rule. The distinctive Indian contribution is the integration of political teaching with cosmological and soteriological frameworks: good governance is not merely pragmatically beneficial but cosmically significant.

The Moksha-dharma section's philosophical dialogues invite comparison with Plato's dialogues, the Upanishadic conversations between teacher and student, Buddhist suttas featuring the Buddha in dialogue with questioners, and the Confucian Analects' question-and-answer format. All share the assumption that philosophical truth emerges through dialogue rather than monologue, and that the questioner's confusion is as important as the teacher's wisdom. The Moksha-dharma's philosophical pluralism — presenting multiple, sometimes contradictory positions — anticipates the Tibetan Buddhist debate tradition and contrasts with the more systematic approaches of later Indian and Western philosophy.
