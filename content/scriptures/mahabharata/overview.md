## Overview

The Mahabharata is the longest epic poem in world literature, comprising approximately 100,000 verses (shlokas) across 18 major books (parvas). Attributed to the sage Vyasa, it narrates the dynastic struggle between the Pandavas and Kauravas — two branches of the Kuru royal family — culminating in the cataclysmic Kurukshetra War. Far more than a war narrative, the Mahabharata encompasses law, philosophy, devotion, cosmology, and statecraft, earning its description as "what is found here may be found elsewhere; what is not found here is found nowhere" (Adi Parva 1.56.33).

## Structure

The 18 parvas trace the complete arc of the conflict:

1. **Adi Parva** (Chapters 1-19): Origins, births, and early rivalries
2. **Sabha–Udyoga Parvas** (Books 2-5): Political intrigue, exile, and failed diplomacy
3. **Bhishma–Shalya Parvas** (Books 6-9): The eighteen-day Kurukshetra War
4. **Sauptika–Stri Parvas** (Books 10-11): Aftermath and lamentation
5. **Shanti–Anushasana Parvas** (Books 12-13): Teachings on dharma and governance
6. **Ashvamedhika–Svargarohana Parvas** (Books 14-18): Final rites and ascent to heaven

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Mahabharata holds profound importance across Hindu traditions and beyond:

- **Vaishnavism**: Krishna's role as divine guide, friend, and supreme lord pervades the epic
- **Shaivism**: Contains major Shaiva sections including the Shiva Sahasranama and Anushasana teachings
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The embedded Bhagavad Gita and Moksha-dharma sections are foundational texts
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Madhva composed the Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya to reveal the epic's hidden meaning
- **Academic**: Central to Indological studies of ancient Indian society, law, and literary composition
- **Comparative**: Studied alongside the Iliad, Odyssey, and other world epics for structural and thematic parallels

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Dharma | The complex, context-dependent nature of righteous duty |
| Karma | Actions and their inescapable consequences across lifetimes |
| Kingship | The responsibilities and moral dilemmas of sovereign authority |
| War and Peace | The ethics of violence, just war, and reconciliation |
| Fate and Free Will | The interplay between destiny and human agency |
