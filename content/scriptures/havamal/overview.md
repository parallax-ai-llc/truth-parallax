## Overview

The Hávamál ("Sayings of the High One") is one of the most important poems of the Poetic Edda, attributed to Odin (the "High One") and presenting a comprehensive body of Norse wisdom literature. Preserved in the Codex Regius manuscript (c. 1270 CE), the Hávamál is actually a composite work, bringing together several originally independent poems that share the theme of wisdom and knowledge associated with Odin. The text ranges from practical advice about hospitality and social conduct to profound meditations on the nature of wisdom, the acquisition of runic knowledge through self-sacrifice, and the magical power of sacred speech.

The Hávamál occupies a unique position in world wisdom literature. Its gnomic verses on friendship, generosity, moderation, and the value of a good reputation resonate with proverbial traditions worldwide, while its account of Odin's self-hanging on the World Tree to gain the runes represents one of the most powerful images of sacrificial wisdom-seeking in any religious tradition. The poem bridges the practical and the transcendent, the ethical and the magical, offering a vision of wisdom that encompasses both everyday conduct and cosmic mystery.

## Structure

The six sections of the Hávamál address the following topics:

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Gestaþáttr | Practical wisdom about hospitality, travel, and guest-host relationships |
| 2 | Gnomic Verses | General wisdom sayings on friendship, moderation, and the human condition |
| 3 | Loddfáfnismál | Direct counsel addressed to Loddfáfnir on ethical and practical matters |
| 4 | Rúnatal | Odin's account of his self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil to gain the runes |
| 5 | Ljóðatal | A catalog of eighteen magical spells or charms known to Odin |
| 6 | Deception of Gunnlöð | The narrative of Odin's quest to obtain the mead of poetry |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: A central text for the study of Old Norse wisdom literature, ethics, and religion
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with wisdom traditions from Proverbs to the Tao Te Ching
- **Philosophical**: Articulates a practical ethics of reciprocity, moderation, and wisdom-seeking
- **Literary**: A masterpiece of gnomic and narrative poetry in the Old Norse tradition
- **Archaeological**: Connected to runic inscriptions and evidence of Odinic cult practices
- **Interfaith**: Offers a distinctive vision of wisdom, sacrifice, and the sacred

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Wisdom | The supreme value of knowledge and the sacrifice required to attain it |
| Hospitality | The ethics of guest-host relationships in Norse society |
| Moderation | The virtue of temperance in drinking, speech, and conduct |
| Sacrifice | Odin's self-sacrifice on the World Tree as the price of wisdom |
| Runes | The sacred knowledge of runic writing and its magical power |
| Reputation | The enduring value of a good name beyond death |
