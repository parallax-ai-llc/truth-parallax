---
title: "Havamal - Chapter 1"
scripture: "havamal"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Within the gates, ere a man shall go, full long let him look about him; for uncertain is the knowing, when foes may sit within, before one in the foreroom.

**2** Hail, ye Givers! A guest is come; say, where shall he sit within? Much hasteth he who fain on the hearth would find him warmth and weal.

**3** He hath need of fire, who now is come, numbed with cold to the knee; food and clothing the wanderer craves who has fared o'er the frosty fell.

**4** He craves for water, who comes for refreshment, drying and friendly bidding, marks of good will, fair fame if 'tis won, and welcome once and again.

**5** He hath need of his wits who wanders wide, aught simple will serve at home; but a byword he is who has naught to say, when he sits with the sage and the wise.

**6** A man shall not boast of the breadth of his mind, but keep a wary watch. The sage and silent come seldom to grief, when they fare to a friendly house. No better burden can a man bear on the way than a store of common sense.

**7** The wary guest who comes to the feast, keeps silent with listening ears; with his eyes he observes, with his ears he tries, and thus every wise man watches.

**8** Happy is he who wins for himself fair fame and kindly words; but uneasy is that which a man must own while it lies in another's breast.

**9** Happy is he who hath in himself praise and wisdom in life; for ill counsel is often given by those of evil heart.

**10** A better burden may no man bear for wanderings wide than wisdom; it is better than wealth on unknown ways, and in grief a refuge it gives.

**11** A better burden may no man bear for wanderings wide than wisdom; worse food for the journey he brings not afield than an over-drinking of ale.

**12** Less good than they say for the sons of men is the drinking oft of ale; for the more they drink, the less can they think and keep a watch o'er their wits.

**13** A bird of unmindfulness flutters o'er ale feasts, wiling away men's wits; with the feathers of that fowl I was fettered once in the garths of Gunnlod below.

**14** Drunk I was, I was over-drunk, at that cunning Fjalar's. 'Tis the best of banquets when every man gets back his reason anew.

**15** Silent and thoughtful and bold in strife the prince's child should be. Joyous and generous let each man show him until he shall suffer death.

**16** The witless man weens he will aye live if he but shuns the fray; but old age gives him no peace, though spears may spare his life.

**17** A fool will gape when he goes to a friend, mumbling only or moping; but all at once, if he gets a drink, the man's mind is laid bare.

**18** He alone is aware who has wandered wide and far has fared on the way; the mind of a man who has travelled much is known by what he knows.
