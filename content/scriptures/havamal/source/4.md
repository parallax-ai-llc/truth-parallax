---
title: "Havamal - Chapter 4"
scripture: "havamal"
chapter: 4
---

**1** Better it is to live than to lie a corpse; the living man catches the cow. I saw flames rise for a rich man's pyre, and before his door he lay dead.

**2** The lame can ride a horse, the handless can drive a herd, the deaf can fight and win; better to be blind than buried, for a dead man does naught of use.

**3** A son is better, even though born late, after his father's death; seldom will standing stones be raised at the road's side except by kinsmen for their kin.

**4** Two can vanquish one, the tongue is the bane of the head. In every furry cloak a fist I look for.

**5** He is happy who for himself obtains fame and favour in his lifetime; for ill counsels are often taken when men sit at the counsel of another's breast.

**6** He is happy who in himself possesses fame and wit while living; for evil counsels have oft been taken from another's breast.

**7** Of his wisdom a man should never boast, but rather keep watch on his wits; cautious and silent let him enter a dwelling. To the watchful, harm seldom comes, for a man can never have a more faithful friend than a good store of wit.

**8** The gift-greedy guest, when he comes to the feast, sits silent and seldom speaks; with his ears he listens and with his eyes he watches, so every wise man looketh about.

**9** For him that takes a gift a giver he hath found; of his own he gives naught to none. His meat he hides, miserable man, when he comes to another's home.

**10** With many a man my mind agrees not, though at feasts he be fair of speech; it ever befalls that the bolder man will make the worse exchange.

**11** About his affairs let no man be anxious, save in measure; but none can take away that anxiety of mind which the care-worn carry.

**12** A man I knew, rich in his mind, who was destroyed by a woman's words. A lying tongue brought him to his death, and in no righteous cause.

**13** A friend should show himself a friend and give gift for gift; men should meet laughter with laughter, and let lies come in return for lies.

**14** A friend should be a friend to his friend and to his friend's friend. But a friend should never be a friend to his friend's enemy.

**15** If thou hast a friend whom thou fully trustest, and wishest to win him for thee, lay bare thy heart to him and give him gifts, and fare to find him oft.

**16** If another thou hast whom thou trustedst ill, yet wouldst have good from him, smooth thy words should be but false thy thoughts, and give lies in return for lies.

**17** The young fir that falls and rots having neither needles nor bark, so is the fate of the friendless man; why should he live long?

**18** Praise day at even, a wife when dead, a weapon when tried, a maid when married, ice when 'tis crossed, and ale when 'tis drunk.
