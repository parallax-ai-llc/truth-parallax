---
title: "Havamal - Chapter 2"
scripture: "havamal"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Drink not too much ale, be not ever speaking; none shall find fault with thee if soon thou seekest rest. The greedy man, if his mind be not wary, eats to his own undoing.

**2** Herds know the hour of their going home and leave their grazing then; but the foolish man can never learn the measure of his own maw.

**3** The miserable man and of evil mind sneers at all things ever; one thing he knows not, which he ought to know: that he is not free from faults.

**4** The witless man is awake all night thinking of many things. When the morning comes he is weary with care, and all is a burden as ever.

**5** The unwise man thinks all who smile upon him to be his friends; he thinks not that they speak him ill, when he sits among the wise.

**6** The unwise man thinks all who smile upon him to be his friends; but when he comes to the Thing, he finds few who will speak in his favor.

**7** The unwise man thinks he knows all if he sits in a sheltered nook; but he knows not what to answer when put to the test by men.

**8** For the witless man it is best to be mute when he comes among folk; none is aware of his lack of wit if he wastes not too many words; he who knows nothing knows not even this, that he knows not aught.

**9** Wise in measure let each man be, but let him not wax too wise; for never the happiest of men is he who knows much of many things.

**10** Wise in measure should each man be, but let him not wax too wise; seldom a heart will sing with joy if the owner be all too wise.

**11** Wise in measure should each man be, but ne'er let him wax too wise; for he who looks not forward to learn his fate unburdened heart will bear.

**12** A brand from a brand is kindled until it burns to ashes; man becomes wise by speaking with man, but a fool by keeping still.

**13** He must rise early who would have another's life or wealth; the slumbering wolf wins not the prize, nor the sleeping man a victory.

**14** He must rise early who has few folk to work for him; much is lost by the man who sleeps in the morn; half of wealth depends on energy.

**15** Of dry planks and roof-bark a man knows the measure, and of the wood that may last through a season; likewise of fodder that may suffice through the winter.

**16** Washed and fed a man should ride to the Thing, though his garments be not too fair; let none be ashamed of shoes or shoon, nor of his horse, though a good one he have not.

**17** Like an eagle swooping over old ocean, snatching after his prey, so comes a man into court who finds there are few to defend his cause.

**18** Each man who is wise and would wise be called must ask and answer aright; one may know a secret, but never a second; if three, a thousand shall know.
