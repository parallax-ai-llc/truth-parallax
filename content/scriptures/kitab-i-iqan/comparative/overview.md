## Overview

Comparative study of the Kitab-i-Iqan places its hermeneutical method alongside other traditions' approaches to prophecy, scripture interpretation, and the relationship between old and new revelation. The Iqan's central argument — that prophetic language is symbolic and that divine revelation is progressive — engages directly with interpretive traditions in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Comparative Hermeneutics
The Iqan's symbolic reading of eschatological prophecy parallels methods found across traditions:
- **Jewish midrash**: Creative, multi-layered interpretation of Torah
- **Christian typology**: Old Testament events as "types" prefiguring Christ
- **Sufi ta'wil**: Inner, esoteric meaning behind the outer text
- **Buddhist upaya**: Teachings adapted to the capacity of the audience

### Progressive Revelation Compared
The Baha'i concept of progressive revelation finds partial parallels in other traditions — the Christian fulfillment of the "Old Covenant," the Islamic concept of supersession, the Hindu concept of avatars — but is more systematic and explicit than any of these.

### The Problem of Recognizing the New
Every tradition grapples with the question of how to recognize authentic divine revelation. The Iqan's analysis of why people reject new prophets — attachment to tradition, literal reading of prophecy, clerical authority — applies across religious history.
