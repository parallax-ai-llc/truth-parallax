## Part One (Proof from Past Scriptures)

This part of the Kitab-i-Iqan addresses the interpretation of past scriptures — Quran, Gospels, and Hebrew Bible — to demonstrate that prophetic language is symbolic and that divine revelation is continuous, using examples of how each new Manifestation was rejected by the followers of the previous one.

Part One of the Iqan is itself a work of comparative theology — perhaps the first systematic attempt to read the Hebrew Bible, the Gospels, and the Quran within a single interpretive framework. Comparative scholars note both the novelty of this approach and its debts to prior traditions of inter-scriptural reading.

The hermeneutical method — symbolic reading of eschatological prophecy — has parallels across traditions. Jewish midrash finds multiple layers of meaning in Torah. Christian patristic allegory (Origen, Gregory of Nyssa) reads the Old Testament as pointing beyond its literal sense. Sufi *ta'wil* discovers inner meanings beneath the outer text. The Iqan's innovation lies in applying these methods systematically across scriptural boundaries.

The Iqan's central thesis — that each new revelation both fulfills and transcends the previous — invites comparison with the Christian concept of 'fulfillment' (Matthew 5:17) and the Islamic concept of supersession. The comparative reader asks whether these are genuinely parallel phenomena or fundamentally different claims.
