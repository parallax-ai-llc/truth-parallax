## Part Two (The New Revelation)

This part of the Kitab-i-Iqan addresses the establishment of the Bab's station and the principles by which a new Manifestation of God can be recognized, including the qualities of the true seeker and the proofs of divine authority.

Part Two's theology of the Manifestation of God invites comparison with parallel concepts across traditions. The Hindu doctrine of *avatara* (divine descent), the Buddhist concept of *trikaya* (three bodies of the Buddha), and the Christian doctrine of incarnation all address the question of how the divine communicates with humanity through a specific human figure.

The Iqan's concept occupies a distinctive position: the Manifestation is more than a prophet (who merely receives messages) but less than God incarnate (who is fully divine). This 'mirror' theology — the Manifestation perfectly reflects divine attributes without being the divine essence — offers a middle way that comparative theologians find productive for cross-traditional dialogue.

The Iqan's description of the true seeker's qualities parallels spiritual prerequisites found across traditions: the Buddhist 'beginner's mind,' the Christian 'poverty of spirit,' the Sufi 'fana' (self-annihilation), and the philosophical *epochē* (suspension of judgment).
