## Overview

The Kitab-i-Iqan (Persian/Arabic: كتاب ايقان, "Book of Certitude") was revealed by Baha'u'llah in Baghdad in 1861, two days in response to questions posed by Haji Mirza Siyyid Muhammad, a maternal uncle of the Bab. It is the preeminent theological work of the Baha'i Faith, presenting a comprehensive framework for understanding scripture, prophecy, and progressive revelation.

## Structure

The Kitab-i-Iqan is divided into two main parts:

1. **Part One — Proof from Past Scriptures**: Interprets passages from the Quran, the Gospels, and the Hebrew Bible to demonstrate the continuity of divine revelation and the symbolic nature of prophetic language
2. **Part Two — The New Revelation**: Establishes the station of the Bab and Baha'u'llah and articulates the principles of recognizing a new Manifestation of God

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Kitab-i-Iqan holds distinct significance across multiple interpretive frameworks:

- **Baha'i**: The foundational theological text, establishing hermeneutical principles for understanding all scripture
- **Islamic Studies**: A radical reinterpretation of Quranic eschatology and the concept of the Seal of the Prophets
- **Christian Studies**: Its interpretation of biblical prophecy invites comparison with Christian eschatological hermeneutics
- **Academic**: Studied as an example of innovative scriptural hermeneutics emerging from a Shia milieu
- **Comparative**: Analyzed alongside other traditions' methods of scriptural interpretation
- **Interfaith**: A key text for understanding the Baha'i approach to religious pluralism

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Progressive Revelation | God's truth unfolds through successive divine messengers |
| Symbolic Interpretation | Prophetic language uses symbols that must be spiritually discerned |
| Tests and Trials | Each new revelation tests the followers of the previous one |
| True Seekers | Spiritual prerequisites for recognizing divine truth |
| Unity of the Prophets | All Manifestations of God share one essential purpose |
