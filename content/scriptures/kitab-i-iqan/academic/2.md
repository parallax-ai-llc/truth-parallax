## Part Two (The New Revelation)

This part of the Kitab-i-Iqan addresses the establishment of the Bab's station and the principles by which a new Manifestation of God can be recognized, including the qualities of the true seeker and the proofs of divine authority.

Part Two shifts from hermeneutical argument to theological construction. Scholars analyze Baha'u'llah's concept of the Manifestation of God as an innovative theological category that draws on but transforms Islamic *nubuwwa* (prophetology), Christian incarnational theology, and Neoplatonic emanation theory.

The epistemological argument is notable: Baha'u'llah contends that the Manifestation's authority is self-authenticating — proven by the transformative power of his revelation rather than by conformity to prior expectations. This circularity is a feature, not a bug: just as the sun proves its existence by shining, not by external certification, the Manifestation proves his station by the effects of his revelation.

Scholars also note the text's sophisticated sociology of religious opposition: Baha'u'llah analyzes the institutional, psychological, and hermeneutical mechanisms by which religious establishments resist new claims to authority. This analysis anticipates modern sociological studies of religious innovation and resistance.
