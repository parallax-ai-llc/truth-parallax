## Part Two (The New Revelation)

This part of the Kitab-i-Iqan addresses the establishment of the Bab's station and the principles by which a new Manifestation of God can be recognized, including the qualities of the true seeker and the proofs of divine authority.

Part Two's Christological implications are significant for Christian readers. The concept of the Manifestation of God offers an alternative to the Chalcedonian definition (fully God and fully human) that Baha'is consider more coherent and more applicable across traditions. The 'mirror' analogy — the Manifestation perfectly reflects God without being God — resonates with some subordinationist and Logos Christologies in early Christianity.

The description of the true seeker parallels the spiritual attitudes commended in the Gospels — poverty of spirit (Matthew 5:3), becoming like children (Matthew 18:3), and seeking with the whole heart (Jeremiah 29:13). Christian readers may find these spiritual prerequisites familiar even while questioning whether they lead to the conclusions the Iqan draws.

The fundamental Christian question remains: is Jesus unique as the incarnation of God, or is he one in a series of divine Manifestations? The Iqan's answer — that all Manifestations share the same essential station — directly challenges Christian claims of Christ's absolute uniqueness.
