## Part One (Proof from Past Scriptures)

This part of the Kitab-i-Iqan addresses the interpretation of past scriptures — Quran, Gospels, and Hebrew Bible — to demonstrate that prophetic language is symbolic and that divine revelation is continuous, using examples of how each new Manifestation was rejected by the followers of the previous one.

Christian readers find in Part One a sustained engagement with biblical prophecy — particularly the Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24), the Book of Revelation, and Isaianic prophecy. Baha'u'llah's interpretation of 'the sun shall be darkened' and 'the stars shall fall' as symbolic of the decline of religious leadership rather than cosmic catastrophe resonates with some Christian interpretive traditions (preterism, idealism) while conflicting with others (futurism, dispensationalism).

The Iqan's argument that the Jewish leaders' rejection of Jesus — despite the apparent non-fulfillment of messianic prophecy in literal terms — parallels the situation facing those who encounter any new Manifestation. This argument has particular force for Christians, who recognize that Jesus' messiahship was not what most first-century Jews expected.

The question for Christian readers is whether the analogy holds: does the pattern of symbolic fulfillment apply to the prophecies of Christ's return, or is the Second Coming fundamentally different from Christ's first appearance?
