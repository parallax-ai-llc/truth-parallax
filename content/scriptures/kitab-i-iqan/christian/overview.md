## Overview

Christian engagement with the Kitab-i-Iqan centers on its interpretation of biblical prophecy — particularly passages concerning the return of Christ, the Day of Judgment, and the signs of the end times. The Iqan's argument that these prophecies are fulfilled symbolically rather than literally creates a direct dialogue with Christian eschatological expectations.

## Key Interpretive Issues

### Biblical Prophecy
The Iqan interprets passages from Matthew 24 (the Olivet Discourse), the Book of Revelation, Isaiah, and other prophetic texts. Its symbolic reading of "the sun shall be darkened," "the stars shall fall," and "the Son of Man coming in the clouds" challenges the literal expectations of many Christian traditions while resonating with some preterist and amillennialist approaches.

### Christological Questions
The Iqan's concept of the Manifestation of God offers a Christology that differs from both Chalcedonian orthodoxy (two natures, one person) and Arian subordinationism. For Christian readers, this raises fundamental questions about the uniqueness and finality of Christ's incarnation.

### Hermeneutical Resonance
Despite theological disagreements, the Iqan's method has affinities with established Christian hermeneutical traditions — patristic allegory (Origen, Clement of Alexandria), medieval four-fold interpretation, and modern narrative theology. The question is whether these methods warrant the Iqan's specific conclusions.
