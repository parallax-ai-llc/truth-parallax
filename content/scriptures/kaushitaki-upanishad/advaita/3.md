For Advaita, Indra's teaching in Chapter 3 represents a crucial identification: prana (breath) and prajna (consciousness) are one, and that one is the self (atman). Shankara reads this as a progressive teaching leading to the recognition of non-dual Brahman. Indra's statement "know me alone" does not point to Indra as a personal deity but to the universal self speaking through him.

The key philosophical move is the identification of the vital force with consciousness and both with the self. This collapses the apparent multiplicity of the inner world — breath, thought, perception — into a single reality, which is ultimately Brahman. The chapter thus serves as an important step in the Upanishadic progression from the many to the One.
