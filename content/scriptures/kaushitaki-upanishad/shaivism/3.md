Indra's teaching in Chapter 3 — "Know me as prana-prajna, as life-consciousness" — is read by Shaiva interpreters as Shiva's self-revelation through the deity Indra. The identification of prana (vital force) with prajna (consciousness) confirms the Shaiva teaching that life and awareness are one: consciousness is not an epiphenomenon of life but its very essence.

The imperative "know me alone" resonates with the Shaiva emphasis on recognition (*pratyabhijna*): liberation consists in recognizing that the consciousness animating one's own breath is none other than Shiva. The chapter's refusal to separate breath from consciousness — insisting that they are one self (*atman*) — mirrors the Shaiva rejection of the Samkhya dualism between spirit and matter.
