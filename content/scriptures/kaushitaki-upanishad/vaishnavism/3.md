Chapter 3 presents Indra's revelation to Pratardana: "Know me alone — this is what I consider most beneficial for a human being." Vaishnava commentators read this as the Supreme Lord (operating through Indra) commanding exclusive devotion. Indra's identification of himself as *prana-prajna-atman* (the breath-consciousness-self) is understood as a description of Vishnu's presence as the inner controller of all vital and cognitive functions.

The chapter's teaching that one who knows prana-prajna attains a full life span and fame is read as confirming the rewards of knowing God. The narrative framework — a warrior (Pratardana) receiving divine knowledge after battle — mirrors the Bhagavad Gita's setting, where Krishna reveals supreme knowledge to the warrior Arjuna.
