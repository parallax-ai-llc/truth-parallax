The Indra-Pratardana dialogue in Chapter 3 is one of the most philosophically significant passages in the Kaushitaki. Scholars debate whether the identification of prana with prajna represents a genuine philosophical position (vitalism-idealism) or a transitional formulation that later thinkers refined. Joel Brereton argues that the Kaushitaki's prana-prajna identification represents an early attempt to bridge the gap between physiological and psychological explanations of selfhood.

The narrative frame — Indra rewarding Pratardana for his valor in battle — connects philosophical teaching to the kshatriya (warrior) context, suggesting that Upanishadic wisdom was not exclusively the preserve of Brahmins. Indra's instruction "Know me alone" may reflect a period when individual deities were being identified with the absolute, before the full abstraction of Brahman as an impersonal principle.
