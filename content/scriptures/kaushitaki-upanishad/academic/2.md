The prana contest in Chapter 2 belongs to a genre of faculty-competition narratives found across the Upanishadic corpus (cf. Brihadaranyaka 6.1, Chandogya 5.1). Scholars note that these narratives served both pedagogical and rhetorical purposes — establishing the primacy of breath over the senses through a dramatic, memorable story.

The emphasis on prana as the supreme faculty reflects an early Indian preoccupation with breath as the link between body and consciousness. Paul Deussen noted that the Kaushitaki's prana teachings represent a philosophical stage in which the principle of life had not yet been clearly distinguished from the principle of consciousness — a distinction that later Upanishads and the Samkhya system would sharpen.
