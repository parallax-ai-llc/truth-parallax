## Overview

Tibetan Buddhism engages with the narrative of the Buddha's passing through its own canonical versions and through the lens of its distinctive teachings on death, rebirth, and the continuity of enlightened activity. The account of the Buddha's death is foundational to Tibetan teachings on impermanence and the practices surrounding death and dying.

## Impermanence as Foundation

The Buddha's passing serves as the supreme example in Tibetan teachings on impermanence (mi rtag pa), one of the "four thoughts that turn the mind" toward dharma practice. If even the fully awakened Buddha's physical body is subject to dissolution, how much more urgent is practice for ordinary beings? This reflection is a standard preliminary practice across all Tibetan lineages.

## Continuity of Enlightened Activity

Tibetan Buddhism's tulku system — the recognition of reincarnated teachers — can be understood as a response to the crisis posed by the Buddha's death. While the Pali sutta presents the Buddha's passing as final, the Tibetan tradition developed the understanding that enlightened beings voluntarily return to guide sentient beings, ensuring the continuity of the teaching lineage.
