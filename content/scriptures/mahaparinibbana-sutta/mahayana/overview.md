## Overview

Mahayana Buddhism engages with two related but distinct texts concerning the Buddha's passing: the Pali Mahaparinibbana Sutta and the much longer Mahayana Mahaparinirvana Sutra. While acknowledging the historical account in the Pali text, Mahayana interpretation emphasizes the doctrinal elaborations found in its own expanded version.

## The Mahayana Expansion

The Mahayana Mahaparinirvana Sutra transforms the narrative of the Buddha's death into a vehicle for teaching Buddha-nature (tathagatagarbha). In this expanded version, the Buddha's passing is itself a form of skillful means — the eternal Tathagata only appears to die in order to inspire urgency among his followers. This reinterpretation shifts the emphasis from impermanence to the eternal, blissful, pure nature of the Buddha.

## Doctrinal Significance

The Mahayana text's declaration that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature became one of the most influential teachings in East Asian Buddhism. The contrast between the Pali sutta's emphasis on impermanence and the Mahayana sutra's teaching of an eternal Buddha-nature represents one of the most significant doctrinal divergences in Buddhist history.
