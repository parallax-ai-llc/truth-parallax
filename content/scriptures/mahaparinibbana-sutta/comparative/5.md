The death of the Buddha invites comparison with the deaths of other religious founders, each of which carries distinctive theological meaning. The Buddha dies peacefully, in full meditative control, teaching impermanence through his own passing. Jesus dies violently on a cross, his death understood as redemptive sacrifice. Muhammad dies of illness, surrounded by his community, his death marking the seal of prophethood. Socrates drinks the hemlock with philosophical composure. Each death encapsulates the tradition's central teaching: impermanence, redemption, prophetic finality, or the philosopher's equanimity before death.
