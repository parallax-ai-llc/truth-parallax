The Buddha's passing through the jhanas is read in Zen as a demonstration of complete freedom — freedom even from the highest attainments. The progression through all states and the final release from the fourth jhana shows a mind that clings to nothing, not even to the most refined spiritual experiences. Zen's teaching on the 'great death' (daishi) — the complete extinction of ego-clinging that precedes awakening — finds its ultimate example here. The world's response to the Buddha's death — earthquakes, celestial flowers — is understood not as supernatural event but as the natural expression of the interdependence of all things: when the teacher passes, the entire universe responds.
