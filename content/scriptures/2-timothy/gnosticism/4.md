## Preach the Word

The charge to "preach the word; be ready in season and out of season" (4:2) commissions the pneumatic teacher to transmit gnosis regardless of circumstances. The word to be preached is not the Demiurge's law but the liberating revelation of the transcendent Father. "In season and out" recognizes that the archons create conditions both favorable and hostile to the spread of gnosis, and the pneumatic must be prepared for both. To "reprove, rebuke, and exhort" (4:2) is to shatter the illusions of those still sleeping in material consciousness, sometimes gently and sometimes with the sharp force required to cut through archontic conditioning.

The warning that people will "accumulate for themselves teachers to suit their own passions" and "turn away from listening to the truth and wander off into myths" (4:3-4) describes the inevitable drift of psychic believers toward comfortable religion. The Demiurge accommodates this tendency by providing an endless supply of teachers who reinforce the cosmic illusion, offering salvation through obedience to law, ritual, and institutional authority rather than through the terrifying freedom of gnosis. The myths they prefer are not pagan stories but the Demiurge's own narrative: that he is the supreme god, that the material world is good, that the body will be literally resurrected.

Paul's declaration "I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith" (4:7) is his pneumatic testament, the confession of a soul about to shed its material garment and ascend through the archontic spheres. The "crown of righteousness" awaiting him (4:8) is reunion with the Pleroma. The statement that "the Lord stood by me and strengthened me" (4:17) records a direct theophany of the true God, not the Demiurge but the transcendent Father whose presence sustained Paul through his imprisonment in both literal chains and the larger prison of the material cosmos. That he was "rescued from the lion's mouth" (4:17) symbolizes deliverance from the chief archon, often depicted as a lion-faced being in Gnostic iconography of Yaldabaoth.
