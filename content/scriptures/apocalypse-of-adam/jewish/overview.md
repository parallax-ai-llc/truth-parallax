## Overview

Jewish readers encounter in the Apocalypse of Adam a sophisticated use of the Jewish testament genre — a dying patriarch revealing hidden knowledge to his descendants (compare the Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs, the Testament of Moses). The text transforms Adam and Seth from Genesis figures into vehicles for Gnostic revelation, and the thirteen kingdoms passage may draw on Jewish traditions of competing origin stories for messianic figures. The text's possible pre-Christian character suggests that Gnostic speculation may have roots in Jewish sectarian thought of the late Second Temple period.
