## Gaius: A Model of Faithful Hospitality

The elder writes to Gaius, expressing joy that he is "walking in the truth" (v. 3). Protestant interpreters hold up Gaius as a model for ordinary believers: his faithfulness is not expressed through extraordinary spiritual gifts or theological sophistication but through the practical virtue of hospitality. By welcoming traveling missionaries — "strangers" to him — Gaius becomes a "fellow worker for the truth" (v. 8).

## Diotrephes: A Warning Against Autocratic Leadership

Diotrephes "likes to put himself first" and refuses to acknowledge the elder's authority (v. 9). He slanders the elder, refuses to welcome the missionaries, and expels those who do. Protestant interpreters read this as a cautionary tale about the dangers of autocratic church leadership. The Reformation itself was in part a response to ecclesiastical authoritarianism, and Diotrephes serves as a biblical example of the kind of leader the Reformers opposed.

## Demetrius and the Imitation of Good

Verse 11 — "Beloved, do not imitate evil but imitate good" — is applied to the contrast between Diotrephes (evil example) and Demetrius (good example). Demetrius "has received a good testimony from everyone, and from the truth itself" (v. 12). Protestant interpreters use this as a reminder that the Christian life is ultimately about imitating Christ through practical goodness, not seeking power or prominence.
