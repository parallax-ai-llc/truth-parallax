## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 3 John draws practical lessons about church leadership, hospitality, and the support of itinerant missionaries. The letter's contrast between Gaius (faithful, hospitable) and Diotrephes (domineering, inhospitable) has been used to critique autocratic leadership and promote the ideal of servant leadership. The letter also supports the Protestant emphasis on congregational involvement in supporting gospel workers.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Servant Leadership vs. Authoritarianism
Diotrephes, "who likes to put himself first" (v. 9), serves as the paradigmatic negative example of church leadership in Protestant pastoral theology. His refusal to welcome traveling missionaries, his slander of the elder, and his excommunication of those who disagree with him represent the kind of authoritarian leadership that Protestantism has historically opposed. Gaius, by contrast, models the generous, truth-loving believer who supports gospel work.

### Missionary Support
3 John 5–8 provides the theological rationale for supporting traveling missionaries: "We ought to support people like these, that we may be fellow workers for the truth" (v. 8). Protestant mission theology has drawn on this passage to encourage churches to support itinerant preachers, missionaries, and evangelists as a form of partnership in the gospel.

## Theological Emphases

- **Servant Leadership**: The contrast between Gaius and Diotrephes as models of leadership
- **Hospitality as Mission**: Supporting traveling teachers as partnership in the truth
- **Walking in Truth**: Faithfulness to the truth as the measure of Christian maturity
- **Congregational Responsibility**: The church's duty to support gospel workers
