## Hospitality as Encounter with Christ

The elder commends Gaius for his hospitality toward traveling missionaries. Orthodox theology reads this as an expression of the fundamental Christian calling to welcome Christ in the stranger. The monastic tradition of receiving every guest as Christ himself (following the Rule of St. Benedict, which echoes Orthodox practice) finds scriptural support in 3 John's commendation of Gaius.

## Diotrephes and Spiritual Pride

Diotrephes's desire to "put himself first" represents the spiritual disease of pride (*hyperēphania*), which the desert fathers identified as the most dangerous of all the passions. Orthodox spiritual tradition teaches that pride corrupts even genuine spiritual gifts and ecclesiastical authority. Diotrephes serves as a practical warning that leadership without humility becomes tyranny.

## Truth and the Good

The letter's closing moral — "Whoever does good is from God; whoever does evil has not seen God" (v. 11) — encapsulates the Orthodox understanding that moral conduct reveals the state of the soul. The person who lives in communion with God naturally produces the fruit of goodness, while the person who does evil, regardless of their title or position, demonstrates that they have not truly encountered the living God.
