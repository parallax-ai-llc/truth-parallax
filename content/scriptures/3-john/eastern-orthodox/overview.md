## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 3 John draws on the letter's themes of hospitality, humility, and the proper exercise of pastoral authority. Diotrephes's love of preeminence is seen as a warning against the spiritual sickness of *philautia* (self-love) that the desert fathers identified as the root of all vice. Gaius's faithful hospitality, by contrast, exemplifies the Orthodox virtue of receiving Christ in the stranger.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Humility and Philautia
Diotrephes "likes to put himself first" (v. 9), exemplifying the vice of *philautia* — disordered self-love that places one's own will above the community's good. The Orthodox ascetic tradition identifies self-love as the root of the passions, and Diotrephes serves as a practical illustration of how this spiritual disease corrupts ecclesiastical leadership.

### Hospitality as Christological Practice
The Orthodox tradition reads hospitality as an encounter with Christ in the stranger, based on Matthew 25:35 ("I was a stranger and you welcomed me"). Gaius's reception of traveling missionaries is not merely a social courtesy but a spiritual practice that embodies the Church's calling to welcome Christ in every person.

## Theological Emphases

- **Humility vs. Pride**: Diotrephes as a warning against ecclesiastical pride
- **Hospitality**: Welcoming strangers as a form of receiving Christ
- **Walking in Truth**: Gaius's faithfulness as the model of Christian life
- **Pastoral Accountability**: The elder's willingness to address Diotrephes's behavior
