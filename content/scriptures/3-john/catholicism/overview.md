## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 3 John engages with the letter's portrayal of authority and communion within the early church. The elder writes with an authority that Diotrephes rejects — a situation Catholic interpreters read as an early example of the tension between local and universal pastoral authority. The letter's commendation of hospitality and its condemnation of Diotrephes's autocratic behavior inform Catholic teaching on the proper exercise of ecclesiastical authority.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Authority and Communion
The elder's complaint that Diotrephes "does not acknowledge our authority" (v. 9) is read by Catholic interpreters as an early instance of the tension that would eventually be resolved by the development of hierarchical structures of authority. Catholic ecclesiology emphasizes that local leadership must be exercised in communion with the broader Church, and Diotrephes's rejection of the elder represents a break in this communion.

### Hospitality and the Universal Church
The letter's commendation of Gaius for welcoming traveling missionaries reflects the early Church's practice of maintaining communion across geographic distance through hospitality. Catholic theology reads this as an expression of the Church's catholicity — its universal reach maintained through bonds of love, shared faith, and mutual support.

## Theological Emphases

- **Ecclesiastical Authority**: The elder's authority as a model of pastoral oversight
- **Communion**: Hospitality as an expression of the Church's universal communion
- **Diotrephes**: The danger of local leaders who sever communion with the broader Church
- **Doing Good**: "Whoever does good is from God" (v. 11) as a moral principle
