## Authority and Communion

3 John illustrates the tension between local and broader ecclesiastical authority. The elder writes with an authority that Diotrephes refuses to recognize. Catholic interpreters read this as a precursor to the development of the hierarchical structure of the Church, in which local leaders exercise authority in communion with the wider body. Diotrephes's refusal to acknowledge the elder represents a break in ecclesial communion — a situation the Church's hierarchical structure was designed to prevent.

## Hospitality and the Missionary Church

The elder's commendation of Gaius's hospitality (vv. 5–8) is read by Catholic interpreters as an expression of the Church's missionary nature. Supporting those who travel "for the sake of the name" (v. 7) is a form of participation in the Church's universal mission. This principle undergirds Catholic religious orders, missionary societies, and the broader tradition of support for those who leave home to serve the gospel.

## Imitating Good

Verse 11 — "Whoever does good is from God; whoever does evil has not seen God" — articulates a moral principle that Catholic theology develops extensively. The good life is not merely ethical compliance but a participation in God's own goodness. Those who do good reveal that they are in living relationship with the God who is the source of all goodness.
