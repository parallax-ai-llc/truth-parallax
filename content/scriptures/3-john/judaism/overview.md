## Overview

Jewish scholars find in 3 John a revealing snapshot of the social dynamics of early Christian communities — dynamics with close parallels to Jewish communal life. The letter's central tension between hospitality and authority, between welcoming outsiders and maintaining communal order, resonates with challenges Jewish communities have faced throughout history.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Hospitality as Sacred Duty
The commendation of Gaius's hospitality (vv. 5–8) resonates with the Jewish value of *hachnasat orchim*. The rabbinic tradition teaches that hospitality is greater even than receiving the divine presence (Shabbat 127a). The letter's emphasis on welcoming traveling strangers as a religious duty reflects a shared Abrahamic ethic.

### Leadership and Authority
Diotrephes's domineering behavior (v. 9) illustrates a problem familiar to Jewish communal life: the local leader who abuses authority for personal aggrandizement. Rabbinic tradition warns against leaders who "lord it over the community" (Pesachim 113b) and insists that communal leadership is a form of service, not domination.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hospitality**: The sacred duty of welcoming strangers and supporting travelers
- **Leadership Ethics**: The contrast between humble service and domineering authority
- **Community Dynamics**: The universal challenge of maintaining order without tyranny
- **Truth-Walking**: Ethical conduct as the measure of faithfulness
