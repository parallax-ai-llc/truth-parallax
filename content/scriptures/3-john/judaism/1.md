## Hospitality in the Jewish Tradition

3 John's central theme — the commendation of Gaius's hospitality and the condemnation of Diotrephes's inhospitality — resonates deeply with the Jewish tradition. Abraham's eager reception of three strangers (Genesis 18) is the paradigmatic act of *hachnasat orchim*, and the Talmud places hospitality among the deeds whose "fruits a person enjoys in this world while the capital is laid up for him in the world to come" (Shabbat 127a). Gaius embodies this virtue.

## Leadership Ethics

Diotrephes's domineering behavior finds parallels in rabbinic warnings against communal leaders who abuse their authority. The Mishnah teaches: "Do not seek greatness for yourself, and do not covet honor" (Avot 6:5). The contrast between Gaius and Diotrephes mirrors the rabbinic distinction between leaders who serve the community and those who exploit it for personal prestige.

## Walking in Truth

The elder's joy that Gaius is "walking in the truth" (v. 3) resonates with the Jewish concept of *halakhah* — literally "walking," the practical path of Torah observance. Jewish readers recognize in 3 John the familiar emphasis on faith as a way of life — a practical walk — rather than merely a set of beliefs.
