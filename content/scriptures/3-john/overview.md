## Overview

3 John is a personal letter from "the elder" to a believer named Gaius, addressing a conflict over hospitality and authority within the early church. The letter commends Gaius for his faithful hospitality to traveling missionaries, condemns Diotrephes for his refusal to welcome them and his domineering leadership, and commends Demetrius as a trustworthy figure. The letter provides a remarkable window into the social dynamics, power struggles, and hospitality practices of early Christian communities.

## Structure

The letter's fifteen verses divide into four sections:

1. **Greeting to Gaius** (1–4): Joy at Gaius's faithfulness to the truth
2. **Commendation of Hospitality** (5–8): Supporting traveling missionaries
3. **Condemnation of Diotrephes** (9–10): A leader who rejects the elder's authority
4. **Commendation of Demetrius** (11–12): A trustworthy believer
5. **Closing** (13–15): Plans for a personal visit

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Protestantism**: A case study in church leadership, authority, and the dangers of autocratic rule
- **Catholicism**: The elder's authority and the relationship between local and universal leadership
- **Eastern Orthodoxy**: The importance of hospitality and the danger of ecclesiastical pride
- **Judaism**: Hospitality ethics and the dynamics of communal leadership
- **Islam**: The emphasis on hospitality and the critique of arrogant leadership
- **Gnosticism**: The conflict between institutional and charismatic authority
