## Overview

Islamic engagement with 3 John focuses on its ethical teaching about hospitality, leadership, and the danger of arrogance. The letter's critique of Diotrephes, who "likes to put himself first," resonates with the Islamic condemnation of *kibr* (arrogance), which the Prophet Muhammad identified as a barrier to paradise. The commendation of Gaius's hospitality aligns with the Islamic emphasis on generous reception of guests and support for those who serve God's cause.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Hospitality and Islamic Ethics
3 John's commendation of hospitality resonates with the Prophet Muhammad's instruction: "Whoever believes in Allah and the Last Day should honor his guest" (Bukhari and Muslim). The Quran and hadith repeatedly emphasize the virtue of generous hospitality, and the letter's portrayal of Gaius as a model host aligns with this Islamic value.

### The Critique of Arrogant Leadership
Diotrephes's self-aggrandizing behavior resonates with the Islamic critique of leadership driven by ego rather than service. The Prophet Muhammad said: "The leader of a people is their servant" (Abu Dawud). The Quran warns against those who are "self-deluded" and "arrogant" (31:18). Muslim readers find in the Diotrephes/Gaius contrast a clear illustration of the prophetic distinction between humble service and prideful domination.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hospitality**: The duty of generous welcome as a mark of faith
- **Humble Leadership**: Leadership as service, not domination
- **Against Arrogance**: Diotrephes as a warning against *kibr*
- **Doing Good**: "Whoever does good is from God" (v. 11) as a universal moral principle
