## The Virtue of Hospitality

3 John's commendation of Gaius's hospitality resonates with the profound Islamic emphasis on welcoming guests. The Prophet Muhammad taught that hospitality is a right of the guest and a duty of the host: "Whoever believes in Allah and the Last Day should honor his guest" (Bukhari and Muslim). The Quran praises Ibrahim (Abraham) for his generous welcome of strangers (51:24–27). Gaius represents the ideal host that both Islamic and Christian traditions celebrate.

## Against Arrogant Leadership

Diotrephes, who "likes to put himself first" and refuses to recognize the elder's authority, exemplifies the kind of arrogant leadership that Islam categorically condemns. The Prophet Muhammad said: "He who has in his heart the weight of a mustard seed of arrogance shall not enter Paradise" (Muslim). The Quran warns against those who "strut arrogantly through the land" (31:18). Muslim readers find in Diotrephes a universal cautionary figure — the leader corrupted by pride.

## Doing Good as the Sign of Faith

Verse 11 — "Whoever does good is from God" — articulates a moral principle that resonates with Islamic teaching. The Quran states: "Whoever does righteousness — it is for his own soul" (45:15). Both traditions affirm that genuine relationship with God is demonstrated through ethical conduct, not through titles, positions, or claims to authority.
