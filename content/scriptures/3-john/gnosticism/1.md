## The Authority Conflict

3 John presents a conflict between the elder (itinerant authority) and Diotrephes (local institutional authority). This dynamic is relevant to understanding the Gnostic-orthodox split. Gnostic teachers typically operated as itinerant, charismatic figures claiming direct spiritual authority, while the emerging proto-orthodox structure centralized authority in local bishops. Diotrephes's rejection of the elder's authority could be read as an early instance of the institutional consolidation that would eventually marginalize Gnostic voices.

## Hospitality and Access to Truth

The elder's emphasis on hospitality — welcoming traveling teachers who "went out for the sake of the name" (v. 7) — reflects a model of community in which truth circulates freely through traveling teachers. Gnostic communities, which valued the transmission of hidden knowledge through personal initiation, would have resonated with this model of truth-sharing through personal encounter rather than institutional control.

## Doing Good and Seeing God

Verse 11 — "Whoever does good is from God; whoever does evil has not seen God" — uses the language of "seeing God" that resonates with the Gnostic concept of gnosis as a vision of the divine. For Gnostics, seeing God is the supreme spiritual experience. However, John ties this vision to moral conduct rather than cosmological knowledge — those who do good have "seen God," while those who do evil, regardless of their spiritual claims, have not.
