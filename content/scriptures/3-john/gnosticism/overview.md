## Overview

3 John provides a window into the kind of authority conflict that may have contributed to the Gnostic-orthodox split. The tension between the elder and Diotrephes — between itinerant charismatic authority and settled institutional leadership — reflects a dynamic that shaped the early Christian centuries. Gnostic groups generally favored charismatic, Spirit-led authority over institutional hierarchy, and the letter's portrayal of Diotrephes as a domineering local leader resonates with Gnostic critiques of proto-orthodox ecclesiastical power.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Charismatic vs. Institutional Authority
The elder represents a form of authority based on personal relationship, tradition, and itinerant ministry. Diotrephes represents the emerging power of the settled, local leader who controls access to the community. Gnostic groups typically sided with charismatic authority — the teacher who claimed direct spiritual insight — against the institutional structures that would eventually become normative in the orthodox Church.

### Hospitality and Control
Diotrephes's refusal to welcome the elder's messengers and his expulsion of those who do (vv. 9–10) represents an exercise of institutional control over community boundaries. Gnostic communities, which valued open access to hidden knowledge and resisted institutional gatekeeping, would have seen Diotrephes as an example of the ecclesiastical authoritarianism they opposed.

## Theological Emphases

- **Authority Conflict**: The elder vs. Diotrephes as a paradigm of charismatic vs. institutional authority
- **Institutional Control**: Diotrephes's behavior as proto-orthodox ecclesiastical power
- **Open Access**: The elder's preference for hospitality as resonant with Gnostic openness
- **Doing Good**: The moral criterion (v. 11) as transcending institutional boundaries
