The Rainbow Serpent is one of the most widespread and significant Ancestral Beings across Aboriginal Australia, known by many names in different language groups — Ngalyod (Gunwinggu), Yurlungur (Yolngu), Waugal (Noongar), Karia (Boulia), among many others. The Rainbow Serpent is associated with water, rain, fertility, and the creation of rivers, waterholes, and landscape features. The serpent's movements across the land during the Dreaming created the topography of the Australian continent, and the Rainbow Serpent continues to inhabit sacred waterways, enforcing the law and punishing transgressors.

Anthropological study of The Rainbow Serpent examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
