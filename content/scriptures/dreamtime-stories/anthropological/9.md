The Dreaming narratives encode a comprehensive system of law and morality that governs every aspect of Aboriginal life. These laws — covering kinship obligations, marriage rules, territorial boundaries, resource management, ceremonial responsibilities, and social behavior — are not human inventions but were established by the Ancestral Beings during the Dreaming. Transgression of these laws in the narratives invariably results in consequences (transformation into stone, exile, death), establishing precedents for the maintenance of social order.

Anthropological study of Law and Morality in the Dreaming examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
