Aboriginal Australian astronomical knowledge is among the oldest in the world, with the night sky serving as a vast map of Dreaming narratives. The stars, constellations, planets, and celestial phenomena are understood as Ancestral Beings or as the marks of their actions. The Pleiades are often associated with a group of women (the Seven Sisters, known as Kungkarungkara in Pitjantjatjara tradition) pursued across the sky. The dark spaces between stars (dark constellations, such as the Emu in the Sky) are as significant as the bright stars themselves.

Anthropological study of The Sky Ancestors and Celestial Dreaming examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
