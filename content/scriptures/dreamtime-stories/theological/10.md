The Dreaming is not a distant past but a continuing reality that coexists with the present. Through ceremony, song, dance, art, and the proper maintenance of sacred sites, Aboriginal people participate in the ongoing creative processes of the Dreaming. The Ancestral Beings are not dead or departed but remain present in the landscape, in the ceremonial life of the people, and in the totemic relationships that connect all living things. This final cycle addresses the responsibility of each generation to maintain the Dreaming through proper custodianship of country, knowledge, and ceremony.

Theological engagement with The Continuing Dreaming raises important questions about the nature of the sacred, the relationship between the divine and the world, and the meaning of human existence within a larger cosmic order. This section's themes invite reflection on fundamental theological questions that transcend any single tradition while being rooted in the specific spiritual vision of this particular culture.

The theological concepts embedded in this section — whether explicit or implicit — offer distinctive perspectives on questions that occupy theologians across traditions: the nature of creation, the relationship between the human and the divine, the meaning of suffering and transformation, and the foundations of moral order. Engaging with these concepts enriches theological reflection by expanding the range of perspectives brought to bear on enduring questions.
