The Dreaming narratives encode a comprehensive system of law and morality that governs every aspect of Aboriginal life. These laws — covering kinship obligations, marriage rules, territorial boundaries, resource management, ceremonial responsibilities, and social behavior — are not human inventions but were established by the Ancestral Beings during the Dreaming. Transgression of these laws in the narratives invariably results in consequences (transformation into stone, exile, death), establishing precedents for the maintenance of social order.

Theological engagement with Law and Morality in the Dreaming raises important questions about the nature of the sacred, the relationship between the divine and the world, and the meaning of human existence within a larger cosmic order. This section's themes invite reflection on fundamental theological questions that transcend any single tradition while being rooted in the specific spiritual vision of this particular culture.

The theological concepts embedded in this section — whether explicit or implicit — offer distinctive perspectives on questions that occupy theologians across traditions: the nature of creation, the relationship between the human and the divine, the meaning of suffering and transformation, and the foundations of moral order.
