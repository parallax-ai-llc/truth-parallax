Aboriginal Dreaming narratives about death address both the origin of human mortality and the journey of the spirit after death. Death is typically understood as having been introduced through a specific event during the Dreaming — often through the actions or choices of an Ancestral Being. After death, the spirit may journey to a specific place in the landscape, return to the Dreaming, or be reborn. The relationship between the living and the dead is maintained through ceremony, and the spirits of the deceased remain connected to their country.

Theological engagement with Death and the Spirit World raises important questions about the nature of the sacred, the relationship between the divine and the world, and the meaning of human existence within a larger cosmic order. This section's themes invite reflection on fundamental theological questions that transcend any single tradition while being rooted in the specific spiritual vision of this particular culture.

The theological concepts embedded in this section — whether explicit or implicit — offer distinctive perspectives on questions that occupy theologians across traditions: the nature of creation, the relationship between the human and the divine, the meaning of suffering and transformation, and the foundations of moral order.
