Aboriginal Dreaming narratives about death address both the origin of human mortality and the journey of the spirit after death. Death is typically understood as having been introduced through a specific event during the Dreaming — often through the actions or choices of an Ancestral Being. After death, the spirit may journey to a specific place in the landscape, return to the Dreaming, or be reborn. The relationship between the living and the dead is maintained through ceremony, and the spirits of the deceased remain connected to their country.

Interfaith engagement with Death and the Spirit World offers opportunities for cross-traditional dialogue on shared human concerns expressed through this tradition's distinctive spiritual vocabulary. The themes addressed in this section — creation, transformation, moral order, the relationship between humans and the natural world — are concerns that resonate across the world's spiritual traditions while being expressed here in uniquely powerful ways.

Respectful interfaith dialogue with this material requires acknowledging both the common ground it shares with other traditions and its irreducible distinctiveness. When approached with humility and genuine openness, this section offers resources for enriching one's own spiritual understanding while deepening appreciation for the breadth and depth of human spiritual experience.
