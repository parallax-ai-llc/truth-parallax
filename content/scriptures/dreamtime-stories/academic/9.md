The Dreaming narratives encode a comprehensive system of law and morality that governs every aspect of Aboriginal life. These laws — covering kinship obligations, marriage rules, territorial boundaries, resource management, ceremonial responsibilities, and social behavior — are not human inventions but were established by the Ancestral Beings during the Dreaming. Transgression of these laws in the narratives invariably results in consequences (transformation into stone, exile, death), establishing precedents for the maintenance of social order.

Academic analysis of Law and Morality in the Dreaming draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
