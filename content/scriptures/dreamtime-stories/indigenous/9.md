The Dreaming narratives encode a comprehensive system of law and morality that governs every aspect of Aboriginal life. These laws — covering kinship obligations, marriage rules, territorial boundaries, resource management, ceremonial responsibilities, and social behavior — are not human inventions but were established by the Ancestral Beings during the Dreaming. Transgression of these laws in the narratives invariably results in consequences (transformation into stone, exile, death), establishing precedents for the maintenance of social order.

From an Indigenous perspective, Law and Morality in the Dreaming carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
