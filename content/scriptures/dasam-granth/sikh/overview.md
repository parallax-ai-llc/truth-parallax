## Overview

For the Sikh community, the Dasam Granth occupies a complex and sometimes contested position. While the Guru Granth Sahib is universally acknowledged as the eternal living Guru, specific compositions from the Dasam Granth are integral to daily Sikh practice and the Khalsa identity. The Jaap Sahib and portions of the Akal Ustat are recited as part of the *Nitnem* (daily prayers), and the *Amrit Sanskar* (Khalsa initiation ceremony) draws upon Dasam Granth compositions.

The Sikh community's relationship with the Dasam Granth reflects broader questions about the nature of scripture, authorship, and canonical authority in Sikhism. While some Sikhs accept the entire Dasam Granth as the authentic work of Guru Gobind Singh, others restrict their acceptance to specific compositions (particularly the Jaap Sahib, Akal Ustat, and Zafarnama), and some regard the *Puranic* narratives as the work of court poets rather than the Guru himself. Despite these debates, the Dasam Granth's influence on Sikh identity — particularly the martial, courageous spirit of the Khalsa — is undeniable.
