## Overview

The Dasam Granth ("Book of the Tenth Master") is a collection of texts attributed to Guru Gobind Singh (1666-1708), the tenth and last human Guru of the Sikhs. Compiled in the early eighteenth century, the Dasam Granth is a complex, multi-genre work comprising hymns of praise, mythological narratives drawn from Hindu *Puranic* traditions, autobiographical accounts, ethical tales, and letters of political significance. Its 1,428 pages contain compositions in Braj Bhasha, Hindi, Persian, Punjabi, and Sanskrit.

## Structure

The Dasam Granth contains numerous compositions, of which five major sections are most commonly studied and liturgically significant:

1. **Jaap Sahib** — A hymn of divine praise, listing the many names and attributes of God
2. **Akal Ustat** — "Praise of the Immortal," a philosophical composition on the nature of God
3. **Bachitar Natak** — "Wondrous Drama," an autobiographical account of Guru Gobind Singh's divine mission
4. **Chandi Charitar** — Narratives of the Hindu goddess Chandi (Durga), reinterpreted through Sikh theology
5. **Zafarnama** — "Letter of Victory," a Persian epistle addressed to the Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Sikh**: Portions are integral to daily Sikh liturgy and the Khalsa initiation ceremony
- **Academic**: A major subject of textual criticism, authorship debates, and literary analysis
- **Comparative**: Offers unique examples of inter-traditional literary and theological engagement
- **Sufi**: The Persian compositions, particularly the Zafarnama, engage with Sufi and Islamic literary traditions
- **Bhakti**: The devotional hymns connect to the broader North Indian bhakti movement
- **Interfaith**: Demonstrates creative theological engagement across Hindu, Sikh, and Islamic traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Divine Sovereignty | God as all-powerful, formless, and beyond human comprehension |
| Martial Spirit | The righteousness of armed resistance against tyranny and injustice |
| *Bir Ras* | The heroic sentiment (*rasa*) as a devotional and literary mode |
| Divine Names | The countless names and attributes of the one formless God |
| Dharma Yudh | Righteous warfare as a last resort in defense of justice |
| Khalsa Identity | The spiritual and martial identity of the Sikh community |
