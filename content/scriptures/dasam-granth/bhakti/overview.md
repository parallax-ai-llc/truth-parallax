## Overview

The Bhakti reading of the Dasam Granth situates the text within the broader tradition of North Indian devotional poetry. The hymns of divine praise — particularly the Jaap Sahib and Akal Ustat — employ the devotional language and emotional intensity characteristic of the bhakti movement, while the Guru's personal relationship with the divine echoes the intimate, passionate devotion of bhakti saints like Kabir, Ravidas, and Mirabai.

Bhakti scholars note that the Dasam Granth's theology of divine names — the extended litanies of God's attributes found in the Jaap Sahib — resonates with the bhakti practice of *nama-smarana* (remembrance of the divine name) as the path to liberation. The text's insistence on God's formlessness and transcendence, however, distinguishes it from bhakti traditions centered on particular divine forms (*saguna* bhakti), aligning it more closely with the *nirguna* (formless) bhakti of Kabir and Guru Nanak.
