---
title: "Asclepius Section 2"
scripture: "asclepius"
chapter: 2
---

**1** But it is time for me to explain these things to you. God, the Father and Lord of all, who alone is all things, willingly reveals himself to all.

**2** Not that he shows to any person his place, or his quality, or his greatness, but he enlightens man solely with the intellectual insight of the mind, when the darkness of error is cast away, and man perceives the truth of the light.

**3** He mingles himself with the mind alone, which is divine by nature. The intellectual perception of the mind that is closest to God is to know God, and it is like a divine light illuminating the entire thought.

**4** Indeed, such a perception of the mind frees it from the bodily senses that hold it in the darkness of error. Not all men, O Asclepius, have attained true perception, but only those who do not rush to an understanding that they seize with reckless haste.

**5** Rather, knowledge demands the slow pace of learning. And when it has traversed the full course of learning, then it reaches the mind and illumines it throughout with the light of understanding.

**6** Now concerning the nature of the divine: God is not mind, but is the cause of the existence of mind. God is not spirit, but is the cause of the existence of spirit. God is not light, but is the cause of the existence of light.

**7** Therefore, one must honor God by these two names which belong to him alone and to no other. For none of the other beings called gods, nor any man, nor any demon, can be in any degree good, save God alone.

**8** And this is what he is and nothing else. All other things are separable from the nature of the good. For the body and the soul have no room in them for the good.

**9** For the good is as great as the existence of all things, both corporeal and incorporeal, both sensible and intelligible. This is the good, this is God.

**10** You must not, then, call anything else good, for that would be impious. Nor must you ever call God anything but good, for that too would be impious.

**11** In word, indeed, the good is spoken of by all, but all do not understand what it is. Not only is God not understood by all, but through ignorance both the gods and some men are called good, though they can never either be or become good.
