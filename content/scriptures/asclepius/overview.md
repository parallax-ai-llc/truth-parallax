## Overview

The Asclepius (NHC VI,8) is a Coptic translation of sections 21-29 of the Latin Hermetic dialogue known as Asclepius (or the Perfect Discourse), in which Hermes Trismegistus instructs his student Asclepius about the nature of humanity, the cosmos, and the gods. The most celebrated passage is the "Lament for Egypt" — a dramatic prophecy that Egypt's sacred traditions will be abandoned, her temples will become tombs, and the gods will depart the earth, after which a time of darkness will precede an eventual cosmic restoration. The text presents humanity as a "great miracle" (*magnum miraculum*) — a being uniquely positioned between the divine and the material, capable of ascending to the gods or descending to the beasts. The Latin version of the full Asclepius survives among the works attributed to Apuleius; the Coptic Nag Hammadi version provides a crucial witness to the text's transmission in Egypt.

## Structure

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Humanity the Great Miracle | The nature of the human being as a microcosm bridging the divine and material realms |
| 2 | The Living Cosmos | The cosmos as a living, ensouled being, animated by the divine breath |
| 3 | The Nature of the Gods | The relationship between the transcendent God, the cosmic gods, and the gods made by human beings |
| 4 | The Prophecy of Egypt's Decline | The prediction that Egypt's sacred traditions will be abandoned and the gods will depart |
| 5 | The Lament for Egypt | Hermes weeps for the coming desolation of the sacred land |
| 6 | The Restoration of All Things | The promise that after the time of darkness, God will restore all things to their original order |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Read as a Hermetic revelation about the divine nature of humanity and the cosmic drama of decline and restoration
- **Academic**: Central to the study of Hermetism, the transmission of Hermetic texts, and the relationship between Latin and Coptic versions
- **Comparative**: The "great miracle" concept parallels Islamic and Renaissance celebrations of human dignity; the decline-and-restoration schema parallels Hindu, Buddhist, and Zoroastrian cyclical cosmologies
- **Christian**: Influenced Renaissance Christian humanism (Pico della Mirandola's *Oration on the Dignity of Man* draws on Asclepius)
- **Jewish**: Engages with Jewish concepts of humanity as the image of God and the prophetic tradition of decline and restoration
- **Interfaith**: Addresses universal questions about human dignity, cosmic purpose, and the relationship between decline and renewal

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Humanity as Great Miracle | The human being is uniquely positioned between god and beast, capable of both |
| The Living Cosmos | The universe is not dead matter but a living, divine organism |
| The Lament for Egypt | A powerful prophecy of spiritual decline — the sacred land will become a wasteland |
| Cosmic Restoration | After the darkness, God will restore the cosmos to its original perfection |
