## Overview

Comparative study of the Bustan places it within the global tradition of didactic poetry — literature that uses the power of verse to convey moral and philosophical instruction. Parallels include Virgil's *Georgics*, Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura*, the Sanskrit *Hitopadesha*, Dante's *Divine Comedy*, and the Chinese *Shijing*. Each of these works demonstrates the distinctive capacity of poetry to make abstract ethical principles vivid, memorable, and emotionally compelling.

The Bustan's organization around cardinal virtues invites comparison with Aristotelian virtue ethics, Confucian moral cultivation, and the Buddhist *paramitas* (perfections). Scholars examine how different civilizations have used narrative poetry as a vehicle for moral education, noting both convergent strategies (exemplary tales, memorable maxims, appeals to emotion) and culturally specific features (Sa'di's characteristically Persian blend of mystical aspiration and worldly pragmatism).
