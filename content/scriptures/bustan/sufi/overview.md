## Overview

The Sufi reading of the Bustan encounters in Sa'di's verse a poetic map of the mystical journey (*suluk*). Each chapter corresponds to a station (*maqam*) or state (*hal*) on the Sufi path, from the outer discipline of justice to the inner transformation of repentance and prayer. The Bustan's verse form — sustained *masnavi* couplets — itself mirrors the Sufi journey: a continuous, flowing movement toward the Divine that cannot be divided into discrete, self-contained units.

Sa'di's poetic mastery serves the Sufi purpose of opening the heart through beauty. Where prose instructs the intellect, poetry bypasses rational defenses to plant seeds of spiritual longing directly in the soul. The Bustan's stories of lovers, mystics, and seekers function as spiritual teaching tales (*hikayat*) designed to awaken the reader's own desire for the Beloved. The intoxication Sa'di celebrates is not merely metaphorical but points to the authentic Sufi experience of *sukr* — the ecstatic overwhelming of the rational self by divine love.
