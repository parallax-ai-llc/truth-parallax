## The Living Jesus and the Apostolic Witness

Logia 1-10 open the Gospel of Thomas with the claim to record "secret sayings" of Jesus transmitted through Didymos Judas Thomas. The Eastern Orthodox tradition evaluates this claim against the criterion of apostolic witness as received and confirmed in the liturgical and patristic life of the Church. The canonical Gospels were recognized as authoritative because they faithfully transmitted the apostolic preaching (*kerygma*) as proclaimed in the worshipping community; Thomas's claim to secrecy stands in tension with this public, liturgical criterion.

Logion 1's promise that discovering the interpretation of these sayings grants freedom from death recalls, but fundamentally alters, the Orthodox understanding of how death is conquered. In Orthodox theology, death is overcome through Christ's own death and resurrection — the paschal mystery celebrated in every Divine Liturgy. The Paschal troparion proclaims: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death." Thomas replaces this cosmic, ontological victory over death with an intellectual achievement — finding the correct interpretation of sayings.

Logion 5, which promises that what is hidden will be revealed, resonates with the Orthodox understanding of divine revelation as a progressive manifestation of divine truth. However, Orthodox theology holds that this revelation reaches its fullness in the person of Christ as confessed in the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed and interpreted by the Ecumenical Councils, not in a collection of enigmatic aphorisms requiring private interpretation.
