## Overview

Eastern Orthodox theology engages the Gospel of Thomas through the interpretive lens of the Holy Fathers, the Ecumenical Councils, and the lived liturgical tradition of the Church. Orthodoxy holds that the canon of Scripture was not arbitrarily determined but organically recognized by the Church as it was guided by the Holy Spirit. Thomas's exclusion from the canon reflects the consensus of the Fathers that it failed to accord with the apostolic faith as received and transmitted in the worshipping community.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### Patristic Assessment

The Greek-speaking Fathers were aware of gospels circulating under apostolic names that did not belong to the received tradition. Origen listed a "Gospel according to Thomas" among texts rejected by the churches. Eusebius of Caesarea categorized such works among the "spurious" (*notha*) writings. The Orthodox tradition follows these patristic judgments, understanding them not as mere historical opinions but as expressions of the Church's Spirit-guided discernment of authentic apostolic teaching.

### Christological Concerns

Orthodox theology holds that authentic knowledge of Christ is inseparable from the full narrative of Incarnation, Crucifixion, Resurrection, and Ascension. Thomas presents Jesus as a revealer of hidden sayings but lacks any account of the paschal mystery that stands at the heart of Orthodox worship and theology. The text's apparent docetism — its discomfort with materiality and embodiment — conflicts with the Orthodox insistence, formulated at Chalcedon, that Christ is fully divine and fully human, with a real body that suffered, died, and was raised.

### Theosis and Gnosis

While Orthodoxy affirms that the goal of the Christian life is *theosis* (deification — participation in the divine nature), it distinguishes sharply between authentic theosis and Gnostic *gnosis*. Theosis is achieved through the sacramental and ascetical life of the Church, in communion with other believers, and through the grace of the Holy Spirit. The Gnostic model of salvation through private, intellectual self-knowledge, which Thomas appears to advocate, bypasses the ecclesial, sacramental, and communal dimensions that Orthodoxy regards as essential.

### The Role of Tradition

Orthodox theology understands Scripture and Tradition as a single, unified source of divine revelation. A text that was never received into the liturgical, catechetical, or theological tradition of the Church cannot be considered authoritative, regardless of its antiquity. Thomas's absence from the lectionary, the patristic commentarial tradition, and the hymnographic heritage of Orthodoxy confirms its status as an extracanonical document.
