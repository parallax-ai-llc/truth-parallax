## The World and Orthodox Asceticism

Logia 86-95 present statements of world-rejection that Orthodox theology distinguishes from its own ascetical tradition. Logion 87's lament over the "wretched body" is foreign to Orthodoxy, which celebrates the body as destined for resurrection and glorification. The Orthodox funeral service proclaims the hope of bodily resurrection: "Give rest, O Christ, to the soul of Thy servant... where there is neither sickness, nor sorrow, nor sighing, but life everlasting." The body is honored even in death — washed, anointed, and laid in the grave with the expectation of being raised incorruptible.

Logion 90's invitation to find rest under Jesus's easy yoke closely parallels Matthew 11:28-30, a text read liturgically in the Orthodox Church. This saying resonates with the Orthodox understanding of Christ as the gentle shepherd and the giver of sabbath rest. When heard in its canonical liturgical context, it evokes the whole mystery of Christ's compassionate love; in Thomas, it stands as an isolated invitation without the supporting framework of incarnation, cross, and resurrection.

Logion 93's warning against giving sacred things to the unworthy connects to the Orthodox liturgical practice of restricting communion to baptized, chrismated Orthodox Christians who have prepared through fasting and confession. The deacon's cry before communion — "The holy things for the holy" — expresses this principle of sacred reserve. However, Orthodox sacred reserve operates within the open, public worship of the Church, not as an esoteric restriction of knowledge to a spiritual elite.
