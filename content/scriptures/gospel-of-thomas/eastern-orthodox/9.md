## The Uncreated Light

Logia 77-85 present Thomas's most developed teaching on light, which Orthodox interpreters evaluate in relation to the Palamite theology of the uncreated light. Gregory Palamas (1296-1359), one of Orthodoxy's greatest theologians, taught that the light of Christ's Transfiguration on Mount Tabor was the uncreated divine energy — a real participation in God's own life, not a created symbol. Logion 77's declaration "I am the light that is over all things" superficially resembles this theology but differs in crucial ways.

Thomas's light appears to be an impersonal force pervading all matter ("Split a piece of wood; I am there"), while the Orthodox uncreated light is a personal divine energy communicated through Christ in the Holy Spirit within the ecclesial community. The hesychast tradition of prayer — stillness, the Jesus Prayer, and the vision of the uncreated light — is embedded in the sacramental and monastic life of the Church, not in the private interpretation of enigmatic sayings.

Logion 83-84's teaching on images and the hidden light connects to the Orthodox theology of the icon. Orthodox iconography is grounded in the Incarnation: because God became visible in Christ, Christ can be depicted in images that communicate divine grace. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787) defended the veneration of icons against the iconoclasts. Thomas's distinction between visible images and hidden light, while superficially similar, lacks the incarnational foundation that makes Orthodox icon theology possible.
