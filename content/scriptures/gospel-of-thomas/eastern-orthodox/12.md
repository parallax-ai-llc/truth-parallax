## Transformation and Theosis

Logia 106-114 conclude the Gospel of Thomas with teachings on union and transformation that Orthodox theology evaluates against its own rich doctrine of theosis. Logion 108's promise that the one who drinks from Jesus's mouth "will become like me" resembles the language of 2 Peter 1:4 — becoming "partakers of the divine nature" — which is foundational to the Orthodox doctrine of deification. However, Orthodox theosis preserves the ontological distinction between Creator and creature: the human person participates in the divine energies, not in the divine essence. Thomas's language of identity ("I myself shall become that person") risks collapsing this distinction.

Logion 111 states: "The heavens and the earth will roll up in your presence." This apocalyptic language echoes Isaiah 34:4 and Revelation 6:14, texts that the Orthodox tradition interprets within its eschatological framework. Orthodox eschatology awaits the Second Coming of Christ, the general resurrection, and the transformation of all creation — events that are cosmic and communal, not merely individual and interior. Thomas's apparent individualization of these cosmic events reflects a fundamentally different understanding of the end.

Logion 114 has been the subject of significant discussion in Orthodox theology, which affirms the full spiritual dignity of women. The Orthodox tradition venerates the Theotokos (Mother of God) as "more honorable than the Cherubim and beyond compare more glorious than the Seraphim." Women saints — martyrs, ascetics, teachers, and confessors — fill the Orthodox calendar. The suggestion that a woman must "become male" to attain spiritual life contradicts the Orthodox understanding that the image of God is equally present in men and women and that both are called to theosis without any change of gender.
