## Parables and the Liturgical Reading of Scripture

Logia 68-76 contain parables and beatitudes that Orthodox interpreters compare with their canonical forms as read in the liturgical cycle. The Orthodox Church reads the canonical Gospels according to a fixed lectionary that assigns specific passages to specific days, seasons, and feasts. This liturgical context shapes how the parables are understood: they are not free-floating wisdom sayings but proclamations heard within the worshipping community as part of the Church's ongoing encounter with the living Christ.

Logion 76, the pearl of great price, is read in the Orthodox tradition in connection with the Matthean context (Matthew 13:45-46) and the broader teaching on the kingdom. The liturgical hymns and patristic commentaries that surround the canonical reading provide layers of interpretation that Thomas, as an isolated text without liturgical embedding, cannot replicate. Orthodox hermeneutics is inherently communal and liturgical — Scripture is read aloud in the assembly, not studied privately for esoteric insight.

Logion 70 — "If you bring forth what is within you, what you have will save you" — is sometimes cited in popular Orthodox spirituality as an expression of the inner life. Patristic commentators might connect this to the teaching that the image of God (*eikon tou Theou*) is present in every person and must be activated through the spiritual life. However, strict Orthodox theology would add that the image is activated not by human effort alone but by the grace of the Holy Spirit received through baptism, chrismation, and the Eucharist.
