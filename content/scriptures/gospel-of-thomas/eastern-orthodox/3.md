## Body, Spirit, and the Icon of Christ

Logia 21-29 address the body-spirit relationship in ways that Orthodox theology finds deeply problematic. Logion 22's call to make "the two into one" and to transform the body into a spiritual reality resonates superficially with the Orthodox doctrine of transfiguration — the teaching that the whole human person, body and soul, is destined for glorification. However, Thomas's direction of movement is toward the dissolution of the body's distinctiveness, while Orthodox theology moves toward its transformation and glorification. The icon of Christ's Transfiguration on Mount Tabor reveals a body radiant with uncreated light, not a body absorbed into spirit.

Logion 28's incarnational language — "I stood in the midst of the world, and in flesh I appeared to them" — is assessed against the Chalcedonian definition, which confesses Christ as truly God and truly human, with each nature preserved in its fullness. Thomas's subsequent lament that humanity is universally "drunk" suggests a Christology in which the Incarnation is more diagnostic than redemptive. Orthodox Christology, by contrast, holds that the Incarnation itself accomplishes the healing and deification of human nature.

Logion 29's astonishment that spirit dwells in flesh conflicts with the Orthodox celebration of the body as a temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19). The veneration of relics, the theology of the icon, and the doctrine of bodily resurrection all testify to Orthodoxy's conviction that matter is capable of bearing and communicating divine grace — a conviction that Thomas's body-spirit dualism fundamentally undermines.
