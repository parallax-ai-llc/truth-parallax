## The Kingdom and the Liturgical Life

Logia 11-20 present Thomas's teaching on the kingdom in terms that Orthodox theology evaluates carefully. Logion 13, where Thomas is elevated above Peter and Matthew, is particularly significant for Orthodoxy, which honors the apostles as the foundation of the Church and maintains that no single apostle's witness can be isolated from the apostolic community as a whole. The Orthodox tradition venerates Thomas the Apostle as the evangelizer of India, but his authority is understood within the college of the Twelve, not as a privileged recipient of esoteric knowledge.

Logion 14's apparent rejection of fasting, prayer, and almsgiving directly contradicts the Orthodox ascetical tradition, which regards these practices as essential to the Christian life. The Orthodox liturgical calendar includes four major fasting periods (Great Lent, Apostles' Fast, Dormition Fast, Nativity Fast) along with weekly fasts on Wednesdays and Fridays. Prayer, particularly the Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner"), is the heartbeat of Orthodox spirituality. Thomas's dismissal of these practices reveals a theology fundamentally alien to Orthodox piety.

Logion 18's teaching about standing "at the beginning" to avoid death is evaluated by Orthodox interpreters against the patristic teaching on *theosis*. While the Fathers speak of the restoration of humanity to its original state through Christ, this restoration is accomplished through the sacramental and ascetical life of the Church, not through private esoteric knowledge. Gregory of Nyssa's teaching on *epektasis* — the eternal progress of the soul toward God — presupposes the ecclesial context that Thomas entirely lacks.
