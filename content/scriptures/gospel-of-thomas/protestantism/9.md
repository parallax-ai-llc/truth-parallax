## Light, Logos, and the Limits of Thomas

Logia 77-85 contain some of Thomas's most theologically elevated material, which Protestant interpreters evaluate with particular care. Logion 77, in which Jesus declares "I am the light that is over all things... Split a piece of wood; I am there," presents a cosmic Christology that superficially resembles the Johannine Logos hymn (John 1:1-18). However, Protestant scholars note a critical difference: John's Logos is personal, incarnate, and historically identified with Jesus of Nazareth, while Thomas's light-Christ tends toward an impersonal, pantheistic principle pervading all matter.

Logion 82 states: "Whoever is near me is near the fire, and whoever is far from me is far from the kingdom." This saying, also attributed to Jesus by Origen, has been cited as potentially preserving authentic tradition. Protestant scholars are open to the possibility that individual Thomas logia may reflect genuine Jesus sayings preserved outside the canonical tradition, while maintaining that such sayings require evaluation against the canonical witness as the norm.

Logion 83-84 distinguish between visible images and the hidden "light of the father," introducing a Platonic dualism between appearance and reality that Protestant theology generally resists. The Reformation's affirmation of creation's goodness and the doctrine of the resurrection of the body stand against any theology that devalues the visible, material order as mere shadow.
