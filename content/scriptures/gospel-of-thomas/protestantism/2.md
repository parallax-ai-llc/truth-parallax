## The Kingdom Redefined

Logia 11-20 develop Thomas's distinctive theology of the kingdom, which Protestant interpreters evaluate against the canonical witness. Logion 13 is particularly significant: Thomas is singled out as the one disciple who truly understands Jesus, receiving private revelation that he cannot share with the others. Protestant theology resists the implication of a spiritual hierarchy among believers, affirming instead the priesthood of all believers and the accessibility of the gospel to all through Scripture.

Logion 14 instructs the disciples: "If you fast, you will bring sin upon yourselves; and if you pray, you will be condemned; and if you give alms, you will harm your spirits." This apparent rejection of core Jewish and Christian spiritual practices sharply contradicts Jesus's teaching in the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 6), where fasting, prayer, and almsgiving are commended when practiced with the right intention. Protestant interpreters regard this logion as evidence of Thomas's departure from authentic Jesus tradition toward an antinomian or encratite theological program.

Logion 20, the mustard seed parable, appears in a form shorter than its Synoptic parallels (Matthew 13:31-32; Mark 4:30-32; Luke 13:18-19). Some Protestant scholars argue that this shorter form may be earlier and closer to Jesus's original parable, while others contend that Thomas has stripped away the narrative context and Christological implications that give the canonical versions their full theological meaning.
