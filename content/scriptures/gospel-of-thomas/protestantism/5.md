## Authority and the Keys of Knowledge

Logia 39-48 raise questions about religious authority that resonate with, but ultimately diverge from, Protestant concerns. Logion 39 accuses the Pharisees and scribes of hiding "the keys of knowledge," a saying with a close parallel in Luke 11:52. Protestant interpreters can sympathize with the critique of religious leaders who obstruct access to divine truth — this is precisely the Reformation's complaint against the medieval church. However, Thomas's solution is esoteric knowledge available only to the spiritual elite, whereas the Reformation sought to make Scripture accessible to all believers through translation, literacy, and the preaching ministry.

Logion 44 warns about blasphemy against the Holy Spirit, paralleling Mark 3:28-29 and its Synoptic counterparts. This is one of the few Thomas logia that mention the Holy Spirit, yet it offers no developed pneumatology. Protestant theology holds that the Spirit's work of illumination is essential to understanding Scripture (1 Corinthians 2:10-16); Thomas appears to locate understanding in the individual's capacity for self-knowledge rather than in the Spirit's gracious gift.

Logion 47 presents the parable of serving two masters (parallel to Matthew 6:24 / Luke 16:13) and the image of new wine in old wineskins (parallel to Mark 2:22). These sayings have rich canonical contexts that Protestant preaching and theology have developed extensively. Thomas presents them in isolation, stripped of their narrative settings, which Protestant interpreters view as a loss of essential meaning.
