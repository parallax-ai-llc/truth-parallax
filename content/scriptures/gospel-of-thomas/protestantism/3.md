## Flesh, Spirit, and Incarnation

Logia 21-29 address the relationship between body and spirit in ways that Protestant theology finds deeply problematic. Logion 22's call to make "the two into one" and to make "male and female into a single one" echoes Galatians 3:28 but in a direction that moves toward the dissolution of created distinctions rather than their redemptive transformation. Protestant theology, following the creation narratives of Genesis 1-2, generally affirms the goodness of embodied, gendered existence as part of God's original creative intent.

Logion 28 presents Jesus saying: "I stood in the midst of the world, and in flesh I appeared to them. I found them all drunk; I found none of them thirsty." While the language of incarnation ("in flesh I appeared") resonates with canonical testimony (John 1:14; 1 Timothy 3:16), the characterization of all humanity as spiritually "drunk" without any offer of redemptive grace stands in tension with the Protestant understanding of the gospel as God's initiative to save sinners. In the canonical Gospels, Jesus comes not merely to diagnose humanity's condition but to remedy it through his death and resurrection.

Logion 29 expresses astonishment that spirit would dwell in flesh — a sentiment that, for Protestant interpreters, tilts dangerously toward a docetic Christology that devalues the material body. The Reformation's insistence on the real, bodily suffering of Christ and the future resurrection of the body stands in sharp contrast to Thomas's apparent discomfort with embodiment.
