## The Final Sayings and the Question of Canonicity

Logia 106-114 bring the Gospel of Thomas to a close with sayings about union, transformation, and the final destiny of the disciple. Logion 108 identifies the one who drinks from Jesus's mouth with Jesus himself — a claim to mystical union that Protestant interpreters compare cautiously with the canonical language of being "in Christ" (Galatians 2:20; 2 Corinthians 5:17). The canonical formulation maintains the distinction between Creator and creature even within the language of union, whereas Thomas appears to dissolve that distinction entirely.

Logion 113 addresses the coming of the kingdom: "It will not come by watching for it. It will not be said, 'Look, here!' or 'Look, there!' Rather, the father's kingdom is spread out upon the earth, and people do not see it." This echoes Luke 17:20-21 but with a crucial difference: Thomas presents the kingdom as a purely present, spatial reality, while the canonical Jesus teaches that the kingdom is both "already" and "not yet" — present in his person and ministry but awaiting future consummation. The loss of eschatological tension is, for Protestant interpreters, one of Thomas's most significant theological deficiencies.

Logion 114, the final saying about making Mary "male," raises profound concerns for contemporary Protestant interpreters committed to the full dignity and equality of women in the church. While some scholars read this as a symbolic statement about transcending duality, the plain sense of the text subordinates the feminine to the masculine in a way that contradicts both the canonical witness to women's roles in Jesus's ministry and the Reformation's recovery of the baptismal equality articulated in Galatians 3:28.
