## Seeking Without the Cross

Logia 30-38 focus on seeking and finding, themes that appear throughout the canonical Gospels. However, Protestant interpreters note a critical absence: what is sought and found in Thomas is self-knowledge rather than the saving knowledge of God in Christ crucified. The canonical Jesus invites people to "seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness" (Matthew 6:33); Thomas's Jesus invites seekers into a journey of interior discovery that has no apparent connection to the cross or to the covenant faithfulness of God.

Logion 34 states: "If a blind person leads a blind person, both of them will fall into a pit." This saying appears in nearly identical form in Matthew 15:14 and Luke 6:39. Conservative Protestant scholars view such close parallels as evidence that Thomas has borrowed from the Synoptics, simply extracting individual sayings from their canonical narrative context. More critical scholars counter that the saying may circulate independently, noting that Thomas's version lacks the Synoptic editorial framework.

Logion 38 warns that there will come a time when the disciples will seek Jesus and not find him. Protestant interpreters connect this to the canonical theme of Jesus's departure and the sending of the Holy Spirit (John 14-16), but note that Thomas contains no pneumatology — no doctrine of the Holy Spirit's ongoing presence with the community of faith. This absence leaves the reader dependent on the text itself as the sole mediator of Jesus's teaching, in contrast to the Protestant understanding of Scripture as interpreted within the community of faith under the Spirit's guidance.
