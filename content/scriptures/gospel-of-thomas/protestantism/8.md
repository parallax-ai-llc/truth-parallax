## Parables in Isolation

Logia 68-76 contain several parables with Synoptic parallels, providing Protestant interpreters with a clear basis for comparing Thomas's use of Jesus tradition with the canonical versions. Logion 76, the pearl of great price, parallels Matthew 13:45-46 but adds: "You too, seek his treasure that does not perish, that endures, where no moth comes to eat and no worm destroys." The addition echoes Matthew 6:19-20, suggesting to some scholars that Thomas's author had access to the Gospel of Matthew and freely combined its materials.

Logion 68 blesses those who are hated and persecuted, echoing the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:10-12). However, Protestant interpreters note that in the canonical Beatitudes, persecution is endured "for righteousness' sake" or "because of me" — that is, for the sake of Christ and his kingdom. Thomas's version shifts the emphasis from Christocentric faithfulness to the inner spiritual state of the persecuted, consistent with the text's overall tendency to interiorize Jesus's teaching.

Logion 72 records someone asking Jesus to tell his brothers to divide possessions with him, paralleling Luke 12:13-14. Jesus's refusal — "Who made me a divider?" — is identical in both versions. Protestant interpreters observe that while the saying itself may be authentic, its placement in Thomas (without Luke's parable of the rich fool as commentary) leaves it as an isolated aphorism rather than part of a coherent teaching about the dangers of greed and the sovereignty of God.
