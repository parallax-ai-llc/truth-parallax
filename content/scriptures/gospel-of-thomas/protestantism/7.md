## Suffering Without Redemption

Logia 58-67 address suffering and blessing in ways that Protestant interpreters find incomplete. Logion 58 blesses the one who "has labored and has found life," but this labor is directed toward self-discovery rather than toward trust in God's saving action. Protestant theology, particularly in its Lutheran expression, distinguishes sharply between human works and divine grace: "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God" (Ephesians 2:8-9). Thomas's soteriology of spiritual labor inverts this relationship.

Logion 63, the parable of the rich man who dies before enjoying his wealth, parallels Luke 12:16-21. In Luke, the parable concludes with God's rebuke — "You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you" — and Jesus's exhortation to be "rich toward God." Thomas presents the parable without this divine address, leaving it as a wisdom observation about the futility of material accumulation. Protestant interpreters note that the canonical version grounds the parable in the God-human relationship, while Thomas's version remains anthropocentric.

Logion 67's warning that "one who knows the All but fails to know oneself lacks everything" summarizes Thomas's anthropocentric soteriology. For Protestant theology, the opposite is true: self-knowledge apart from knowledge of God and God's grace in Christ is ultimately futile. Calvin's *Institutes* opens with the observation that knowledge of God and knowledge of self are inseparable, but always with God's self-revelation as the prior and determining factor.
