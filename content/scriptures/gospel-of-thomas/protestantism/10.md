## The World's Rejection and the Gospel's Absence

Logia 86-95 intensify Thomas's rejection of the material world, culminating in statements that Protestant interpreters regard as fundamentally incompatible with the Christian gospel. Logion 87 declares: "Wretched is the body that depends on a body, and wretched is the soul that depends on these two." Protestant theology affirms the psychosomatic unity of the human person as created good by God, acknowledges the effects of sin on the whole person, and looks forward to the redemption of the body (Romans 8:23) — not its dismissal as "wretched."

Logion 90 presents Jesus saying: "Come to me, for my yoke is easy and my lordship is gentle, and you will find rest for yourselves." This close parallel to Matthew 11:28-30 is one of the most beautiful sayings in Thomas, and Protestant interpreters are willing to acknowledge that it may well reflect authentic Jesus tradition. Yet even here, the context differs: in Matthew, the saying follows Jesus's thanksgiving to the Father and his claim to unique revelatory authority (Matthew 11:25-27), while Thomas presents it in isolation.

Logion 93 warns against giving holy things to dogs or casting pearls before swine (parallel to Matthew 7:6). In Thomas, this supports the text's esoteric program: sacred knowledge must be protected from the unworthy. Protestant theology, with its commitment to the public proclamation of the gospel to all people, reads the canonical saying differently — as a caution about discernment in ministry, not as a justification for restricting the gospel to a spiritual elite.
