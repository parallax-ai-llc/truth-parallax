## Gender, Interiority, and the Rule of Faith

Logia 96-105 address themes of interiority and the transcendence of conventional categories, including gender. Logion 97, the parable of the woman with the leaking jar, has no Synoptic parallel and is unique to Thomas. Protestant scholars have debated whether it represents an authentic Jesus parable preserved only in Thomas or a Gnostic composition. Its pessimistic tone — the kingdom is like something that is lost without one's knowledge — contrasts with the parables of growth and discovery in the canonical Gospels.

Logion 99 redefines Jesus's family: "Those here who do the will of my father are my brothers and my mother. It is they who will enter the kingdom of my father." This parallels Mark 3:31-35 closely, and Protestant interpreters have no difficulty affirming the saying's content — the primacy of spiritual kinship over biological relation is a consistent theme in the canonical Gospels. Such sayings demonstrate that Thomas does contain material consonant with canonical teaching, even if the overall theological framework differs substantially.

Logion 100, the question about paying taxes to Caesar, closely parallels the Synoptic account (Mark 12:13-17). Thomas adds a third clause: "Give me what is mine." Protestant interpreters note that this addition shifts the emphasis from the canonical teaching about dual obligations (to God and to the governing authorities, per Romans 13) toward a Christocentric claim that could be read in either a canonical or a Gnostic direction.
