## The Closing Logia and the Question of Unity

Logia 106-114 bring the Gospel of Thomas to a close and raise critical questions about the text's compositional unity. Scholars debate whether Thomas was composed as a unified document by a single author/editor or grew through successive accretions of tradition. April DeConick's "rolling corpus" model proposes that an early "kernel" of sayings (c. 50-70 CE) was augmented by later "accretions" in the early second century. Simon Gathercole has critiqued this model, arguing that the methodological criteria for distinguishing layers are insufficiently rigorous.

Logion 108's identification of the one who drinks from Jesus's mouth with Jesus himself has been analyzed in relation to ancient theories of mystical identification and possession. Scholars such as Richard Valantasis have interpreted this saying within the context of Greco-Roman practices of self-transformation through philosophical speech, while others connect it to Jewish traditions of the sage who so fully embodies Torah that he becomes a living Torah scroll.

Logion 114, the final saying about making Mary "male," has generated an enormous scholarly literature. Key contributions include those of Marvin Meyer (who connects it to ancient gender theory), Jorunn Buckley (who reads it as evidence of patriarchal ideology), and Antti Marjanen (who places it within the broader context of Thomas's representation of women). The saying is often compared to parallel formulations in the Gospel of the Egyptians (preserved by Clement of Alexandria) and in the Naassene Preaching (preserved by Hippolytus). Its placement as the final logion gives it programmatic significance — whether that program is understood as the transcendence of gender duality, the subordination of the feminine, or the restoration of androgynous unity remains among the most debated questions in Thomas scholarship.
