## The Monachos and Social Formation

Logia 49-57 introduce the *monachos* (solitary/single one), a term that has generated significant scholarly discussion. The word appears in Thomas and in a few other early Christian texts, and its precise meaning is debated. Scholarly proposals include: (1) the celibate or sexually continent person; (2) the spiritually unified individual who has overcome internal duality; (3) the "only one" — a person of unique spiritual status; (4) a precursor to the later Christian "monk." Arthur Voobus and Giulia Sfameni Gasparro have connected the term to the Syrian Christian tradition of the *ihidaya* (solitary one), the baptized celibate who imitated Christ's singleness.

Logion 50's cosmological passage — the soul's encounter with cosmic powers and its testimony to its origin in light — has been analyzed by scholars of ancient cosmology, including Karen King and Birger Pearson. The passage has parallels in Gnostic texts (the *Apocryphon of John*, *On the Origin of the World*) but also in Jewish merkabah (throne-chariot) mysticism and Platonic descriptions of the soul's descent and ascent. The question of which tradition provides the closest parallel bears on the larger question of Thomas's religious identity.

Logion 57, the parable of the wheat and weeds, is shorter than the Matthean version (Matthew 13:24-30) and lacks the allegorical explanation (Matthew 13:36-43). Scholars who argue for Thomas's independence note that the allegorical explanation is widely regarded as a Matthean addition; Thomas's version, without it, may preserve an earlier form. Those arguing for dependence counter that Thomas may have simply omitted material that did not suit his theological purposes.
