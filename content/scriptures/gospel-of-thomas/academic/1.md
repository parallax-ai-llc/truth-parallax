## The Prologue and the Hermeneutical Frame

Logia 1-10 establish the literary and theological framework of the Gospel of Thomas. The Prologue identifies the collector as "Didymos Judas Thomas," a figure whose significance has been debated extensively in scholarship. "Didymos" (Greek) and "Thomas" (Aramaic *T'oma*) both mean "twin," suggesting a symbolic rather than historical identification. The Thomas tradition is particularly strong in Syrian Christianity, where Thomas was venerated as Jesus's twin brother and the apostle to the East. Scholars such as Helmut Koester and April DeConick have argued that Thomas originated in eastern Syria (Edessa/Urfa), a hypothesis supported by linguistic and theological parallels with other Syrian Christian literature.

Logion 1 sets the hermeneutical program: "Whoever discovers the interpretation of these sayings will not taste death." This framing device makes interpretation itself the means of salvation — a feature that James M. Robinson connected to the *logoi sophon* (sayings of the wise) genre, which he traced from Jewish wisdom literature through Q to Thomas. The genre-critical approach remains influential in Thomas scholarship, though it has been challenged by scholars who question whether "sayings gospel" constitutes a genuine literary genre.

Logion 2 (seeking, finding, being troubled, being astonished, ruling over the All) exists in a significantly different form in the Greek Oxyrhynchus fragment (P.Oxy. 654), which adds an intermediate stage of "resting." This textual variation between the Greek and Coptic versions is a key datum for scholars reconstructing the text's transmission history and compositional layers.
