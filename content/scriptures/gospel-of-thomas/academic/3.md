## Anthropology and Encratism in Logia 21-29

Logia 21-29 contain material that scholars have connected to encratite (sexually ascetic) Christianity, a movement particularly strong in second-century Syria. Logion 22's teaching about making "the two into one" and making "male and female into a single one" has been compared to similar language in 2 Clement 12:2 and the Gospel of the Egyptians, both of which are associated with encratite theology. The question of whether Thomas's anthropology is properly "Gnostic" or "encratite" has been debated by scholars including Stevan Davies, who argues for an encratite reading, and April DeConick, who sees a more complex layered composition.

Logion 27, which enjoins fasting from the world and keeping the sabbath, is significant for scholars studying Thomas's relationship to Judaism. The sabbath reference is unexpected in a text usually classified as Gnostic, and some scholars take it as evidence of an early Jewish-Christian layer of tradition underlying the present text. The conjunction of asceticism ("fast from the world") with sabbath observance places this logion at the intersection of Jewish practice and early Christian ascetical innovation.

Logion 29's astonishment at spirit dwelling in flesh has been analyzed by scholars of ancient anthropology as reflecting a body-soul dualism with both Platonic and Jewish antecedents. Scholars such as Gerard Luttikhuizen have argued that Thomas's anthropology is closer to Middle Platonic dualism than to the elaborate mythological systems of Valentinian or Sethian Gnosticism. This observation supports the view that Thomas occupies a position between Jewish wisdom, Hellenistic philosophy, and Gnostic theology.
