## Conflict Traditions in Logia 39-48

Logia 39-48 contain conflict sayings and community rules that scholars analyze for evidence of Thomas's social setting (*Sitz im Leben*). Logion 39's critique of the Pharisees and scribes (parallel to Matthew 23:13 / Luke 11:52) reflects the kind of intra-Jewish polemics well documented in Second Temple literature. Scholars debate whether this polemic reflects the historical Jesus's engagement with other Jewish teachers, the early Jesus movement's competition with emergent rabbinic Judaism, or a later Christian appropriation of anti-Jewish rhetoric.

Logion 42 — "Become passers-by" (*shpe erētn n-parageōn*) — is one of Thomas's most difficult sayings. The Coptic phrase has been variously translated and interpreted. Some scholars connect it to the Aramaic concept of *abar* (to pass over/through), suggesting an original Semitic saying. Others see in it a Stoic or Cynic ideal of detachment from the world. The saying has no Synoptic parallel, making it a candidate for material unique to the Thomas tradition.

Logion 47's cluster of sayings (two masters, old/new wine, old/new garments, new wine in old wineskins) appears in various combinations in the Synoptics (Matthew 6:24, 9:16-17; Mark 2:21-22; Luke 5:36-39, 16:13). The way Thomas combines these sayings — and the specific details that match or diverge from the Synoptic versions — has been the subject of detailed redaction-critical analysis by scholars including Klyne Snodgrass and Risto Uro.
