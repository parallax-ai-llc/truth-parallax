## Source Criticism and Logia 30-38

Logia 30-38 provide important evidence for source-critical analysis of Thomas. Logion 31 combines two proverbial sayings (the prophet without honor and the physician who cannot heal his own) that appear separately in the Synoptics. The combination parallels Luke 4:23-24, which has led some scholars to argue for Thomas's dependence on Luke. However, others note that proverbial sayings naturally cluster in oral tradition and that the combination may be pre-Lukan.

Logion 36, on anxiety about clothing, exists in a significantly shorter form in the Coptic Thomas than in the Greek Oxyrhynchus fragment (P.Oxy. 655), which is much closer to the Matthean version (Matthew 6:25-34). This textual situation is unusual — the Greek version is longer and more Synoptic-like than the Coptic. Scholars have proposed various explanations: the Coptic translator abbreviated, the Greek copyist harmonized with Matthew, or different recensions of Thomas circulated independently. This logion is one of the most important data points for understanding the text's transmission history.

Logion 34's saying about the blind leading the blind (parallel to Matthew 15:14 / Luke 6:39) is a simple proverbial saying that could easily circulate independently of any written source. Scholars use sayings like this to test their methodological assumptions: if Thomas and the Synoptics share a simple proverb, does that prove literary dependence or merely access to common oral tradition? The answer one gives shapes one's entire reconstruction of the text's origins.
