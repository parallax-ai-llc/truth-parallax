## Gender, Asceticism, and Community

Logia 96-105 have been the focus of extensive scholarly work on gender and asceticism in early Christianity. Logion 97, the parable of the woman with the leaking jar, has no canonical parallel and is one of the sayings most often identified as potentially preserving independent tradition. Scholars have debated its meaning extensively: is it a parable of the kingdom (like the woman with leaven in Logion 96), a warning about spiritual loss, or an anti-parable that subverts the expectations of the kingdom parable genre? John Dominic Crossan and Mary Ann Tolbert have offered influential readings.

Logion 99's redefinition of Jesus's family (parallel to Mark 3:31-35) is studied by scholars interested in the social dynamics of the early Jesus movement. The replacement of biological kinship with spiritual kinship has been analyzed by scholars such as Halvor Moxnes (*Putting Jesus in His Place*, 2003) as a strategy for constructing alternative social identity in a Mediterranean honor-shame culture. Thomas and the Synoptics agree on this point, suggesting it was a widespread feature of Jesus tradition.

Logion 101's double saying about father and mother — hating biological parents, loving divine parents — has been analyzed by April DeConick as evidence of a two-stage compositional process. She argues that the first half (parallel to Luke 14:26) belongs to an early layer of Thomas, while the second half (the "true mother" who gives life) was added in a later Gnostic revision. This kind of redactional analysis attempts to distinguish earlier from later strata within the text.
