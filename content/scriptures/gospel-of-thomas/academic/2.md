## Form and Tradition in Logia 11-20

Logia 11-20 contain material that has been central to scholarly debates about Thomas's relationship to the Synoptic tradition. Logion 13, the confession scene, is a critical test case. In the Synoptic parallels (Mark 8:27-30; Matthew 16:13-20), Peter gives the correct answer; in Thomas, Peter and Matthew give inadequate answers while Thomas receives private revelation. Scholars debate whether this represents an independent tradition, a deliberate reversal of the Synoptic narrative to elevate Thomas, or a later Gnostic reworking.

Logion 14 combines material that appears separately in the Synoptics: rejection of fasting (cf. Mark 2:18-22), dietary laws (cf. Mark 7:15), and a mission charge (cf. Luke 10:8-9). The combination of disparate traditions into a single logion is taken by some scholars (Mark Goodacre, Simon Gathercole) as evidence that Thomas's author had access to the Synoptic Gospels and freely recombined their material. Others (Stephen Patterson, Risto Uro) argue that the combination reflects an independent oral tradition in which these themes were naturally associated.

Logion 20, the mustard seed parable, is particularly important for Q scholarship. Thomas's version is shorter than both Matthew 13:31-32 and Luke 13:18-19, raising the question of whether Thomas preserves a form prior to the Synoptic elaboration or has independently simplified a longer tradition. John Dominic Crossan argued that Thomas's shorter version is closer to the original; Simon Gathercole has countered that brevity alone is not evidence of priority.
