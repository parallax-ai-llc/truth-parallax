## Beatitudes and Chreia in Logia 58-67

Logia 58-67 contain beatitudes, dialogues, and parables that scholars analyze using the tools of form criticism. Logion 58's beatitude ("Blessed is the person who has labored and has found life") represents a macarism (blessing formula) that has parallels in both Jewish wisdom literature and Greco-Roman philosophy. The form-critical question is whether this beatitude derives from the same tradition as the Synoptic Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12 / Luke 6:20-23) or represents an independent wisdom tradition.

Logion 61's dialogue with Salome has been studied for its evidence of women's roles in the Thomas community. Scholars including Antti Marjanen (*The Woman Jesus Loved*, 1996) and Jorunn Buckley have examined Thomas's representation of women disciples, noting that while Salome and Mary Magdalene appear as interlocutors, their roles are limited and ultimately subordinated to a male-oriented theological vision (especially in Logion 114). The tension between women's presence and their theological marginalization may reflect debates within the community that produced or used this text.

Logion 65, the parable of the wicked tenants, is particularly important for Jesus Seminar scholarship. The Fellows of the Jesus Seminar voted Thomas's version pink (probably authentic), arguing that its shorter form, without the allegorical overlay of the Synoptic versions, is closer to a parable Jesus might actually have told. Critics of the Jesus Seminar's methodology (Dale Allison, Birger Gerhardsson) have argued that brevity is not a reliable criterion of authenticity.
