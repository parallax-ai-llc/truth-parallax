## Parabolic Tradition and Oral Transmission

Logia 68-76 contain parables that provide key evidence for scholarly theories about oral tradition and the transmission of Jesus's teaching. Logion 76, the pearl of great price, has been studied by John Dominic Crossan, Bernard Brandon Scott, and Klyne Snodgrass as a test case for distinguishing between oral and literary transmission. The question is whether Thomas's version of the pearl parable shows signs of literary editing (suggesting dependence on Matthew) or preserves features of oral performance (suggesting independent tradition).

Logion 73 — "The harvest is great but the laborers are few" (parallel to Matthew 9:37 / Luke 10:2, a Q saying) — is significant for Q scholarship. If Thomas independently attests a Q saying, this strengthens the hypothesis that Q existed as a real document or stable oral tradition. If Thomas depends on Matthew or Luke, the saying tells us about Thomas's use of the canonical Gospels but not about Q. The assessment of each such parallel saying is contested and methodologically consequential.

Logion 72, the request to divide an inheritance (parallel to Luke 12:13-14), is notable because it preserves a brief narrative frame — a feature rare in Thomas, which usually presents sayings without narrative context. Scholars have debated whether the occasional narrative elements in Thomas represent residual traces of a fuller tradition that was stripped down to a sayings format, or whether they were added to originally free-standing sayings to provide minimal context.
