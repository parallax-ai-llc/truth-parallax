## World-Rejection and Social Context

Logia 86-95 contain material that scholars analyze for evidence of Thomas's social and ideological context. The theme of world-rejection that pervades these sayings has been connected by scholars to various social locations: (1) itinerant radicalism (Gerd Theissen's model of wandering charismatics); (2) Syrian Christian asceticism; (3) Gnostic sectarian withdrawal; (4) philosophical renunciation in the Cynic tradition. The social location one attributes to Thomas's community shapes one's reading of these sayings.

Logion 86, the "Son of Man has no place to lay his head" (parallel to Matthew 8:20 / Luke 9:58), is important for Son of Man scholarship. In Thomas, the phrase "child of humanity" (*p-shēre m-p-rōme*) lacks the apocalyptic overtones associated with Daniel 7:13 and is closer to the Aramaic idiomatic use of *bar enasha* as a self-referential or generic term for "a person." Some scholars (Helmut Koester, John Dominic Crossan) take this as evidence that Thomas preserves an early, non-apocalyptic use of the expression, before it was invested with the Christological significance it carries in the Synoptics.

Logion 95's prohibition of lending at interest, while having clear Torah roots (Exodus 22:25; Deuteronomy 23:19-20), is presented in Thomas without the covenantal framework that gives the biblical prohibition its meaning. Scholars note this as an example of Thomas's tendency to extract individual ethical or wisdom sayings from their Jewish legal context, repurposing them as general spiritual counsel. This decontextualization is a feature of the sayings genre that distinguishes it from the narrative Gospel form.
