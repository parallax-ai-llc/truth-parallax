## Light and the Hidden Radiance

Logia 77-85 develop the theme of light, which has deep resonance in Jewish mystical and aggadic tradition. Logion 77's declaration that light pervades all things recalls the Jewish teaching about the *or ha-ganuz* — the primordial light of creation that God hid away for the righteous in the world to come. Genesis Rabbah 3:6 teaches that this light, which shone for the first thirty-six hours of creation, was concealed because the wicked were unworthy of its use. The Zohar extensively develops this tradition, seeing the hidden light as accessible to those who study Torah with mystical intention.

Logion 83's distinction between visible images and the hidden "light of the father" connects to the Jewish understanding of *tzelem* (image) in Genesis 1:27. Kabbalistic interpretation understands the *tzelem* as a luminous spiritual form — the divine image that each person bears but that is normally hidden beneath the surface of material existence. While the theological frameworks of Thomas and Kabbalah differ significantly, both share the conviction that visible reality conceals a deeper, luminous truth.

Logion 84's teaching about pre-existent images that "neither die nor become visible" parallels the Jewish concept of the *neshamah* (higher soul) that exists in the divine realm before entering the body. The Talmud (Hagigah 12b) teaches that God showed Adam all the generations that would descend from him. The idea of pre-existent souls waiting to be born is found in various forms throughout rabbinic and kabbalistic literature. Thomas's language of pre-existent "images," while embedded in a different theological framework, engages a question that also occupied Jewish thinkers.
