## Suffering and Blessing in Jewish Perspective

Logia 58-67 address suffering and blessing, themes deeply embedded in Jewish theology. Logion 58's blessing on the one who has labored and found life echoes the rabbinic emphasis on the effort required for Torah study. The Talmud (Berakhot 5a) discusses "afflictions of love" (*yissurin shel ahavah*) — sufferings sent by God not as punishment but as a means of spiritual refinement. However, Jewish theology always frames suffering within the context of the God-Israel relationship, while Thomas frames it as a solitary journey of self-discovery.

Logion 63, the parable of the rich man who dies suddenly, has strong parallels in Jewish wisdom literature. Ecclesiastes (Qohelet) repeatedly warns about the futility of accumulating wealth: "As he came from his mother's womb he shall go again, naked as he came, and shall take nothing for his toil" (Ecclesiastes 5:15). The parable also resembles rabbinic stories about wealthy men who neglect Torah study — a genre well attested in the Talmud and Midrash. Jewish scholars note that Thomas's version of this parable operates within a recognizably Jewish moral framework.

Logion 65, the parable of the wicked tenants, is significant for Jewish-Christian relations because the canonical Synoptic versions (especially Matthew 21:33-46) have historically been read as supersessionist parables about God's rejection of Israel. Thomas's shorter version, which lacks the allegorical elements that support this reading, is of particular interest to Jewish scholars studying the development of anti-Jewish interpretation in early Christianity.
