## Parables and the Mashal Tradition

Logia 68-76 contain several parables that Jewish scholars analyze within the context of the *mashal* tradition. Logion 76, the merchant who finds a pearl, has a close parallel not only in Matthew 13:45-46 but also in rabbinic parables about the supreme value of Torah. The Midrash (Song of Songs Rabbah 1:1) compares Torah to a priceless pearl, and numerous rabbinic parables use the merchant-and-treasure motif to illustrate the surpassing worth of wisdom and Torah study.

Logion 69's beatitude on the hungry — "Blessed are the hungry, for the belly of the one who desires will be filled" — echoes the biblical promise of Psalm 107:9 ("For he satisfies the thirsty, and the hungry he fills with good things") and the prophetic vision of a time when hunger will be satisfied (Isaiah 55:1-2). Jewish scholars note that hunger and thirst as metaphors for spiritual longing are deeply rooted in the Hebrew Bible and continue throughout rabbinic literature.

Logion 74, the enigmatic saying about the well and the lack of anyone to draw water, employs imagery drawn from the Jewish world. Wells are sites of encounter and revelation throughout the Hebrew Bible (Genesis 24, Exodus 2, Numbers 21). The rabbinic tradition reads the well of Miriam (Numbers 21:16-18) as a symbol of Torah, and the act of drawing water is associated with drawing wisdom. Thomas's use of well imagery, however spare, draws on this rich reservoir of Jewish symbolism.
