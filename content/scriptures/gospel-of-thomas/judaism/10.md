## The World and Ascetic Withdrawal

Logia 86-95 contain statements about the world and its rejection that Jewish scholars evaluate against Judaism's generally affirmative stance toward the created order. While Judaism includes ascetic streams (the Nazirite vow, the Essenes, certain kabbalistic practices), the dominant rabbinic tradition celebrates the material world as God's creation. The Talmud (Kiddushin 82a) declares that "a person will have to give account on the Day of Judgment for every good thing which he might have enjoyed and did not" — a statement diametrically opposed to Thomas's characterization of the world as a "corpse."

Logion 86 states that "foxes have their dens and birds have their nests, but the child of humanity has no place to lay his head and rest" (parallel to Matthew 8:20 / Luke 9:58). Jewish scholars note the use of the phrase "child of humanity" (*bar enasha* in Aramaic), which in Jewish usage can mean simply "a human being" (as in Psalm 8:4) or can carry the apocalyptic associations of Daniel 7:13. The saying's resonance with Psalm 8 — which asks "What is man that you are mindful of him?" — places it within a recognizably Jewish meditation on human vulnerability.

Logion 95's injunction against lending at interest echoes the Torah's prohibition on usury (*neshekh*) among Israelites (Exodus 22:25; Leviticus 25:35-37; Deuteronomy 23:19-20). This prohibition is elaborated extensively in rabbinic literature (Bava Metzia 5:1-11) and remains a concern in Jewish business ethics. Thomas's version of the saying, while embedded in a Gnostic context, preserves a teaching with clear roots in Torah legislation.
