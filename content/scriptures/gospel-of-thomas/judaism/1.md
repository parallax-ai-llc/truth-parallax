## Wisdom Forms and the Teacher-Disciple Model

Logia 1-10 open the Gospel of Thomas with a literary form deeply rooted in Jewish tradition: the collection of a revered teacher's sayings transmitted by a named disciple. This parallels the rabbinic model of *Torah she-be'al peh* (oral Torah), in which the teachings of great sages are preserved and transmitted through chains of discipleship. *Pirke Avot* ("Ethics of the Fathers"), the Mishnah's collection of wisdom sayings attributed to named rabbis, represents the closest structural parallel to Thomas's literary form.

Logion 2's pattern of seeking, finding, and being astonished has parallels in rabbinic literature. The Talmud (Megillah 6b) teaches: "If a person says, 'I have labored and found,' believe them." The metaphor of Torah study as a process of seeking and discovery is pervasive in rabbinic culture. However, Jewish tradition understands the object of seeking as Torah and the will of God, while Thomas directs the seeker inward toward self-knowledge — a significant difference in orientation.

Logion 9, the parable of the sower, employs the *mashal* form that is among the most characteristic features of Jewish rhetoric. Jewish scholars have noted that Thomas's version of the sower parable is shorter and less allegorized than the Synoptic versions, potentially preserving a form closer to the original Jewish rhetorical context. The image of seeds, soil, and harvest is deeply embedded in biblical and rabbinic agricultural metaphor.
