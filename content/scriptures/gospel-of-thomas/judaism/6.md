## The Chosen and the Stranger

Logia 49-57 introduce the concepts of the "solitary" and the "elect," language that Jewish scholars examine in light of Jewish traditions of chosenness and separation. The concept of election (*bechirah*) in Judaism refers to God's choice of Israel as a covenant people — a communal, national election, not an individual spiritual status. Thomas's individualized notion of election, in which isolated spiritual adepts discover their divine origin, differs markedly from the covenantal, communal election that defines Jewish identity.

Logion 53's teaching on circumcision — that "the true circumcision in spirit has become profitable in every respect" — reflects a polemic against physical circumcision that is incompatible with Jewish teaching. Circumcision (*brit milah*) is the foundational covenantal sign in Judaism, commanded by God to Abraham (Genesis 17:10-14) and observed faithfully throughout Jewish history. While the prophets spoke of "circumcision of the heart" (Deuteronomy 10:16; Jeremiah 4:4), this was understood as an addition to, not a replacement for, physical circumcision. Thomas's (and Paul's) spiritualization of circumcision marks a decisive departure from Jewish practice.

Logion 57, the parable of the wheat and the weeds, employs agricultural imagery drawn from Palestinian farming. Jewish scholars note that the concern about weeds among wheat (probably *zizania*, darnel) reflects real agricultural challenges in the Galilee. The parable form itself — a brief narrative drawn from everyday life that illuminates a spiritual truth — is characteristic of the *mashal* tradition used by the rabbis.
