## Gender, Family, and the Jewish Household

Logia 96-105 address themes of gender, family, and the interior life that Jewish scholars evaluate in light of rabbinic teachings on these subjects. Logion 96, the parable of the woman making bread with leaven (parallel to Matthew 13:33 / Luke 13:20-21), draws on an image from the Jewish household. Bread-making was a domestic activity associated with women, and the rabbis connected it to the three mitzvot specifically incumbent on women: separating *challah* (a portion of dough), lighting Shabbat candles, and observing the laws of *niddah* (family purity). The parable's use of a woman protagonist reflects the Jewish domestic world that Jesus inhabited.

Logion 100, the question about paying taxes to Caesar (parallel to Mark 12:13-17), is situated in the historical context of Roman occupation of Jewish Palestine. Jewish resistance to Roman taxation was a politically and religiously charged issue, culminating in the Great Revolt of 66-70 CE. The question of whether Jews could legitimately use Roman coinage bearing the emperor's image engaged halakhic (legal) reasoning about idolatry and political obligation. Jewish scholars read this saying as evidence of the thoroughly Jewish context of Jesus's ministry.

Logion 101's teaching about hating and loving one's father and mother engages the Jewish commandment to honor parents (*kibbud av va-em*, Exodus 20:12), which is one of the Ten Commandments and is extensively developed in rabbinic literature. The tension between family loyalty and the demands of Torah study is a recognized theme in rabbinic tradition: the Talmud (Kiddushin 29b-30a) discusses when a son's obligation to study Torah takes precedence over his obligation to honor his parents.
