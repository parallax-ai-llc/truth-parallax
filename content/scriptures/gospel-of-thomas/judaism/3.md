## Body and Soul in Jewish Anthropology

Logia 21-29 engage the relationship between body and spirit, a topic on which Jewish and Gnostic perspectives diverge sharply. Jewish anthropology, rooted in Genesis 2:7, understands the human person as an integrated unity of body (*guf*) and soul (*neshamah*), both created by God and both destined for redemption. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 91a-b) records a famous parable of the blind man and the lame man, teaching that body and soul will be judged together at the resurrection. Thomas's devaluation of the body is foreign to this integrated Jewish vision.

Logion 22's teaching about making "male and female into a single one" can be compared to rabbinic midrash on the creation of Adam. Genesis Rabbah 8:1 records the teaching that the first human was created androgynous (*androginos*) and was subsequently divided into male and female. While this midrash shares Thomas's interest in the original unity of the human being, rabbinic Judaism does not treat the division into male and female as a fall to be reversed but as a divinely intended step in creation — "male and female He created them" (Genesis 1:27).

Logion 25's command to "love your brother like your soul; guard that person like the pupil of your eye" echoes the Torah's commandment to "love your neighbor as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18), which Rabbi Akiva called "the great principle of the Torah" (Sifra, Kedoshim 4:12). This saying demonstrates the Jewish ethical core that persists in Thomas beneath its Gnostic overlay.
