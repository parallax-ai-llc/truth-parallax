## Final Sayings and the Legacy of Jewish Context

Logia 106-114 conclude the Gospel of Thomas with sayings that Jewish scholars evaluate as evidence of the text's complex relationship with its Jewish origins. Logion 106's promise that making "the two into one" confers the ability to move mountains echoes the rabbinic expression for a brilliant Torah scholar: "an uprooter of mountains" (*oker harim*, Berakhot 64a). The hyperbolic image of moving mountains as a metaphor for extraordinary spiritual or intellectual achievement is thoroughly at home in Jewish rhetoric.

Logion 113's teaching that the kingdom is "spread out upon the earth" but unseen resonates with certain strands of Jewish messianic thought. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) records various opinions about when the Messiah will come, including the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that the Messiah is already present among the suffering at the gates of Rome, waiting to be recognized. The idea that redemption is present but unrecognized is a recognizable Jewish motif, even if Thomas develops it in a non-Jewish direction.

Logion 114, the final and most controversial saying about making Mary "male," has no parallel in rabbinic literature. Jewish scholars note that while rabbinic tradition sometimes expressed the view that men were spiritually privileged (the morning blessing thanking God "who did not make me a woman"), it never proposed that women could or should become men as a condition of spiritual life. The saying is best understood against the background of Hellenistic Gnostic gender theory rather than Jewish tradition. It marks the point at which Thomas's Jewish roots give way most clearly to its Gnostic theological framework.
