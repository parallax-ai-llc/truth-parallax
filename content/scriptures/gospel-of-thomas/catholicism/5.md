## Authority, Keys, and Ecclesiology

Logia 39-48 address religious authority in terms that intersect with Catholic ecclesiology. Logion 39's accusation that the Pharisees and scribes have hidden "the keys of knowledge" inverts the Catholic reading of Matthew 16:19, where the keys are given to Peter as the foundation of ecclesial authority. Catholic interpreters see in Thomas's version an anti-institutional polemic that rejects mediating structures between the individual and divine knowledge — a position incompatible with the Catholic understanding of the Church as the sacrament of salvation.

Logion 43 records the disciples asking Jesus, "Who are you to say these things to us?" and Jesus responding, "You do not know who I am from what I say to you." Catholic Christology, developed through the Ecumenical Councils, holds that Jesus's identity is known through the fullness of revelation — his words, deeds, death, and resurrection, as interpreted by the Church under the Spirit's guidance. Thomas's suggestion that Jesus's identity remains hidden even to his disciples after hearing his words reflects a Gnostic Christology of ongoing concealment.

Logion 48 promises that those who "make peace with each other in this one house" will have power to move mountains. Catholic interpreters read the "house" as the Church — the household of God (1 Timothy 3:15) — and the peace-making as the reconciliation that takes place within the ecclesial community through the sacrament of Reconciliation and the common life of charity. Thomas's individualistic framework lacks this communal, sacramental dimension.
