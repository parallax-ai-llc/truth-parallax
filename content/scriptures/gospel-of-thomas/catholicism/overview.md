## Overview

The Catholic Church's engagement with the Gospel of Thomas is grounded in its theology of canon and tradition. The text was never part of the biblical canon recognized by Catholic councils (Hippo in 393, Carthage in 397, and confirmed at Trent in 1546), and several Church Fathers explicitly identified Thomasine literature as heterodox. Catholic scholarship approaches Thomas primarily through the lens of patristic reception, canon history, and doctrinal evaluation, while acknowledging its value as a historical witness to the diversity of early Christianity.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### Patristic Testimony

Several Church Fathers reference a "Gospel of Thomas" or "Gospel according to Thomas," though it is not always certain they are referring to the same text discovered at Nag Hammadi. Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235) associated Thomasine sayings with the Naassene Gnostics. Origen (c. 185–253) mentioned a "Gospel of Thomas" among texts used by heterodox groups. Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 313–386) explicitly warned catechumens against reading it, distinguishing it from the four canonical Gospels. These patristic judgments form the historical basis for the Catholic assessment of Thomas as an apocryphal, non-authoritative text.

### Canon and Magisterium

Catholic theology holds that the canon of Scripture was discerned by the Church under the guidance of the Holy Spirit, through the Magisterium's exercise of teaching authority. The exclusion of Thomas is understood not as an arbitrary historical accident but as a Spirit-guided act of ecclesial discernment. The text's incompatibility with the rule of faith (*regula fidei*), its lack of liturgical use in the early churches, and its association with Gnostic communities all contributed to its non-reception.

### Doctrinal Concerns

From a Catholic doctrinal standpoint, Thomas is deficient in several critical areas. It contains no account of the Incarnation, no sacramental theology, no ecclesiology, and no soteriology rooted in the paschal mystery. Its emphasis on individual, esoteric knowledge contradicts the Catholic understanding of salvation as mediated through the Church, its sacraments, and the communion of the faithful. The text's apparent devaluation of the body conflicts with Catholic affirmation of the goodness of creation and the doctrine of bodily resurrection.

## Contemporary Catholic Scholarship

Modern Catholic scholars such as Jean-Daniel Dubois and Pheme Perkins have contributed significantly to Thomas research within the framework of historical-critical method, treating the text as an important source for understanding second-century Christianity while maintaining that its theological content does not rise to the level of inspired Scripture. The Pontifical Biblical Commission's documents on Scripture and interpretation provide the framework within which such scholarship proceeds.
