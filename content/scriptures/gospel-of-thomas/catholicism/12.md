## Transformation and the Fullness of Revelation

Logia 106-114 conclude the Gospel of Thomas with teachings on union and transformation that Catholic theology engages with both critical distance and occasional points of contact. Logion 108's promise that whoever drinks from Jesus's mouth "will become like me" echoes the Catholic doctrine of divinization (*theosis*), which holds that the baptized participate in the divine nature through grace (2 Peter 1:4). The Church Fathers, especially the Greek Fathers (Athanasius, Gregory of Nazianzus), taught that "God became man so that man might become God." However, this Catholic understanding of divinization always preserves the Creator-creature distinction and is mediated through the sacramental life of the Church.

Logion 113 teaches that the kingdom is "spread out upon the earth" but unseen. Catholic theology affirms both the present and future dimensions of the kingdom: the Church itself is "the kingdom of Christ already present in mystery" (*Lumen Gentium* 3), while the eschatological fullness of the kingdom awaits Christ's return. Thomas's exclusively realized eschatology — the kingdom is entirely present, merely unrecognized — represents an impoverishment of the full canonical witness to the kingdom's "already and not yet" character.

Logion 114, the final saying about making Mary "male," has been the subject of extensive Catholic feminist scholarship. Catholic teaching on the equal dignity of women and men, grounded in the *imago Dei* and reaffirmed by modern papal teaching (*Mulieris Dignitatem*, 1988), rejects any formulation that makes the masculine normative for spiritual life. The Catholic tradition's veneration of Mary the Mother of God, the Magdalene, and countless women saints testifies to the full spiritual dignity of women without the requirement of "becoming male."
