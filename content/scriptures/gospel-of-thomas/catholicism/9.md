## Light, Image, and the Incarnation

Logia 77-85 present Thomas's most developed theological language about light, image, and the divine presence in creation. Logion 77's declaration — "I am the light that is over all things... Split a piece of wood; I am there" — has been compared to the Catholic doctrine of divine omnipresence and to the theology of creation's participation in God (Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologiae* I.8). However, Catholic theology carefully distinguishes between God's presence in creation through sustaining power and the pantheistic identification of God with created matter that Thomas 77 might imply.

Logion 83-84's teaching on images, light, and the pre-existent heavenly form engages questions that Catholic theology addresses through the doctrine of the *imago Dei* — the teaching that human beings are created in God's image (Genesis 1:27). Catholic anthropology understands the *imago Dei* as a real quality of the human person, not merely a Platonic participation in a heavenly archetype. The Second Vatican Council (*Gaudium et Spes* 22) taught that the mystery of the human person is fully illuminated only in the mystery of the Incarnate Word.

Logion 82 — "Whoever is near me is near the fire, and whoever is far from me is far from the kingdom" — is a saying that Origen also attributed to Jesus, lending it some claim to antiquity. Catholic interpreters note its compatibility with canonical Christology: Christ is the way to the Father and the kingdom. This is one of those Thomas logia that Catholic scholarship can affirm as potentially reflecting authentic Jesus tradition.
