## The Kingdom and the Church

Logia 11-20 develop Thomas's teaching on the kingdom of God in ways that Catholic theology finds both intriguing and deficient. Logion 13, in which Thomas is elevated above Peter and Matthew, challenges the Catholic understanding of Petrine primacy. In the canonical tradition (Matthew 16:13-19), Peter's confession of Jesus as Messiah is met with the bestowal of the "keys of the kingdom" and the authority to bind and loose. Thomas inverts this hierarchy, privileging the esoteric insight of Thomas over the public confession of Peter — an inversion that the Catholic tradition reads as a hallmark of Gnostic anti-ecclesiastical polemic.

Logion 14's apparent condemnation of fasting, prayer, and almsgiving contradicts not only the canonical Jesus's teaching but also the Catholic Church's long tradition of these practices as essential expressions of Christian piety. The Church Fathers consistently taught that such practices, rightly ordered, are means of grace and growth in holiness. Thomas's rejection of them aligns with encratite or libertine tendencies that the Fathers identified and opposed.

Logion 20, the mustard seed parable, appears in a form that Catholic New Testament scholars have studied carefully in comparison with the Synoptic versions. Catholic scholarship, following the historical-critical method endorsed by *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943) and *Dei Verbum* (1965), is open to the possibility that Thomas preserves early parabolic forms while maintaining that the canonical context provides the definitive theological interpretation.
