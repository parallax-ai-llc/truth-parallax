## Parables and the Treasury of Faith

Logia 68-76 contain parables and beatitudes that Catholic interpreters examine in light of the Church's living tradition of scriptural interpretation. Logion 76, the pearl of great price, is read in the Catholic tradition as an image of the kingdom of God and of the supreme value of the faith. The Council of Trent used the imagery of seeking and finding treasure to describe the discovery of justifying grace; Thomas uses similar imagery but directs it toward interior self-discovery rather than the reception of divine grace through the sacraments.

Logion 69's double beatitude — blessed are the persecuted in heart, blessed are the hungry — parallels the Matthean and Lukan Beatitudes that are central to Catholic moral and spiritual teaching. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 1716-1729) presents the Beatitudes as the charter of the Christian life, fulfilled in the lives of the saints. Catholic interpreters note that Thomas's interiorized version ("persecuted in their hearts") shifts the focus from concrete social and moral commitment to a purely inward spiritual state.

Logion 70 — "If you bring forth what is within you, what you have will save you" — has been widely quoted in popular spirituality, including by some Catholic writers. However, official Catholic theology would insist that what ultimately saves is not something within the person but the grace of God received through faith and the sacraments. The saying's popularity illustrates the appeal, but also the theological risk, of Thomas's anthropocentric soteriology.
