## The Secret Sayings and Apostolic Tradition

Logia 1-10 open the Gospel of Thomas with a theological program that Catholic interpreters assess against the criteria of apostolic tradition and the rule of faith. The claim to record "secret sayings" of Jesus immediately raises concerns within a tradition that understands divine revelation as public, entrusted to the apostles, and transmitted faithfully through the Magisterium. Cyril of Jerusalem warned catechumens against the Gospel of Thomas precisely because its claim to secrecy conflicted with the public nature of the apostolic witness.

Logion 1 promises that "whoever discovers the interpretation of these sayings will not taste death." Catholic theology locates the authority for scriptural interpretation in the Church — specifically in the Magisterium's exercise of its teaching office under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. Thomas, by contrast, places the burden of interpretation on the individual reader's capacity for spiritual insight, a hermeneutical approach that the Catholic tradition regards as insufficient and potentially dangerous.

Nevertheless, Catholic scholars note that several sayings in this section (Logion 5 on hidden things revealed, Logion 9 on the sower) have close canonical parallels and may preserve early forms of Jesus tradition. The Pontifical Biblical Commission's instruction on the historical truth of the Gospels (1964) allows for the careful, critical study of extracanonical sources within the framework of the Church's teaching authority.
