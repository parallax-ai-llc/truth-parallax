## The World and the Goodness of Creation

Logia 86-95 contain some of Thomas's starkest statements of world-rejection, which Catholic theology evaluates in light of its robust doctrine of creation. Logion 87's description of the body as "wretched" contradicts the Catholic affirmation, rooted in Genesis 1 and reaffirmed by the Councils, that the material world is fundamentally good. The Catechism teaches that the human body "shares in the dignity of the image of God" (CCC 364) and that bodily existence is destined not for dissolution but for resurrection.

Logion 90 — "Come to me, for my yoke is easy and my lordship is gentle" — closely parallels Matthew 11:28-30, a text that has been central to Catholic spirituality and devotion. The Sacred Heart tradition, in particular, draws on this saying to present Christ as the gentle, compassionate Lord who invites all the weary and burdened to find rest in him. Catholic interpreters note that even in a text they consider non-canonical, this logion resonates with the heart of the gospel message.

Logion 93's injunction not to cast pearls before swine has been interpreted in Catholic tradition as a guide for pastoral discernment — the wisdom of adapting teaching to the capacity of the audience, a principle that underlies the Church's catechetical practice and the graduated formation of the catechumenate. Thomas's use of the saying to justify restricting knowledge to a spiritual elite represents a fundamentally different application.
