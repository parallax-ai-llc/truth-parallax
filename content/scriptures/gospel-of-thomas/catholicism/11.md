## Interiority and the Communion of Saints

Logia 96-105 explore themes of interiority, family, and spiritual identity. Logion 97, the parable of the woman with the leaking jar, is unique to Thomas and has no canonical parallel. Catholic scholars have studied it as a possible authentic Jesus parable, noting its thematic similarity to parables of loss (the lost coin, the lost sheep) in Luke 15. However, unlike the Lukan parables of loss, which emphasize God's joy in finding the lost, Thomas's parable presents loss without recovery — a notable absence of the redemptive note that characterizes the canonical parables.

Logion 99 redefines Jesus's family as those who do the will of his Father, closely paralleling Mark 3:31-35. Catholic theology has developed this theme extensively through the doctrine of the communion of saints — the spiritual kinship that unites all believers, living and dead, in the Body of Christ. The Church itself is this new family, united not by blood but by baptism and the common life of faith, hope, and charity. Thomas's individualistic framework does not develop this communal dimension.

Logion 101's double saying about hating and loving one's father and mother has a partial parallel in Luke 14:26. Catholic interpreters, following the patristic and scholastic tradition, understand Jesus's call to "hate" parents not as literal animosity but as the relativization of earthly bonds in light of the absolute claim of the kingdom. The Catholic tradition of religious life — in which men and women leave family to follow Christ in community — embodies this teaching within an ecclesial framework that Thomas lacks.
