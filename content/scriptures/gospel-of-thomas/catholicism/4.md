## Seeking and the Sacramental Life

Logia 30-38 center on seeking and finding, themes that Catholic theology addresses through the lens of the sacramental and liturgical life of the Church. While Thomas presents seeking as an individual, interior journey toward self-knowledge, Catholic theology understands the search for God as guided by the Church, nourished by the sacraments, and oriented toward communion with God in Christ. The Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that "the desire for God is written in the human heart" (CCC 27), but this desire is fulfilled through the public means of grace, not through private gnosis.

Logion 37's instruction to "strip without being ashamed" and trample on garments like children recalls the imagery of baptismal stripping in the early Church. Cyril of Jerusalem, in his *Mystagogical Catecheses*, describes the baptismal candidate's removal of garments as a symbolic stripping of the old self. Catholic interpreters note the irony that Thomas uses baptismal imagery while lacking any sacramental theology — the ritual act that gives the image its meaning is absent from the text's theological framework.

Logion 38's warning that there will be days when the disciples seek Jesus and cannot find him takes on particular significance in Catholic theology, which holds that Christ remains perpetually accessible through the Eucharist, the sacraments, and the life of the Church. The Catholic response to Thomas's warning is the assurance of Christ's abiding sacramental presence: "I am with you always, to the end of the age" (Matthew 28:20).
