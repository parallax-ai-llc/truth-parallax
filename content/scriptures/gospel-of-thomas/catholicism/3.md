## The Body in Catholic Theology

Logia 21-29 address the relationship between body and spirit, a theme on which Catholic theology has developed extensive and nuanced teaching. Logion 22's instruction to make "the two into one" and to transcend the distinction between male and female raises questions about Thomas's anthropology. Catholic teaching, rooted in the theology of creation (Genesis 1-2) and developed through the tradition from Augustine through Thomas Aquinas to John Paul II's Theology of the Body, affirms the goodness of sexual differentiation as part of God's creative design.

Logion 28, in which Jesus says he "stood in the midst of the world and in flesh appeared to them," uses incarnational language that Catholic interpreters evaluate carefully. The Council of Chalcedon (451) defined that Christ is truly God and truly human, with a real human body. Thomas's subsequent characterization of this embodiment as an encounter with universal spiritual drunkenness, without any mention of redemptive purpose, falls short of the Catholic understanding of the Incarnation as the means of humanity's salvation.

Logion 29 expresses wonder that spirit should dwell in flesh. Catholic theology, by contrast, celebrates the union of spirit and matter in the human person and, supremely, in the Incarnation. The sacramental principle — that grace is mediated through material signs (bread, wine, water, oil) — depends on the conviction that matter is capable of bearing divine presence. Thomas's apparent discomfort with embodiment undermines the entire sacramental economy.
