## Labor and Grace

Logia 58-67 address the relationship between human effort and divine blessing. Logion 58 blesses the one who "has labored and has found life." Catholic theology affirms the cooperation of human effort and divine grace (*synergeia*), but insists that grace is always prior and enabling. The Council of Trent (Session 6, 1547) taught that justification is not by works alone but by grace received through faith and expressed in works. Thomas's saying, divorced from any doctrine of grace, risks the Pelagian error of suggesting that human spiritual effort alone is sufficient for salvation.

Logion 62 states that Jesus tells his mysteries "to those who are worthy of his mysteries" — a statement that Catholic interpreters connect to the *disciplina arcani* (discipline of the secret), the early Church's practice of withholding certain teachings and sacramental rites from catechumens until they were properly prepared. However, the *disciplina arcani* was a pedagogical practice within the public Church, not an esoteric restriction of knowledge to a spiritual elite. The Catholic catechumenate aims to prepare all who seek baptism, not to separate an inherently worthy few from an unworthy majority.

Logion 65, the parable of the wicked tenants, parallels the Synoptic version (Mark 12:1-12) that Catholic tradition has long read as an allegory of salvation history: the vineyard is Israel, the tenants are its leaders, the servants are the prophets, and the son is Christ. Thomas's shorter version lacks the allegorical elements that support this reading, which Catholic interpreters take as evidence of deliberate theological editing.
