## Overview

The Gospel of Thomas is a collection of 114 logia (sayings) attributed to Jesus, preserved in a Coptic manuscript discovered in 1945 near Nag Hammadi, Upper Egypt. Unlike the canonical Gospels, it contains no narrative framework — no birth account, no miracles, no passion, and no resurrection story. Instead, it presents itself as a record of "the secret sayings that the living Jesus spoke and Didymos Judas Thomas recorded" (Prologue). The text exists in its fullest form in Coptic (Nag Hammadi Codex II, Tractate 2), though three Greek fragments from Oxyrhynchus (P.Oxy. 1, 654, and 655), dating to roughly 200 CE, attest to an earlier Greek version.

## Structure

The Gospel of Thomas consists of 114 individual logia, each typically introduced by the formula "Jesus said" or by a disciple's question followed by Jesus's response. The sayings range from brief aphorisms to short parables and dialogues. There is no obvious thematic or chronological organization, though clusters of related sayings appear throughout. For the purposes of this resource, the logia are divided into 12 chapters of approximately 9–10 sayings each.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Gospel of Thomas occupies a unique position at the intersection of multiple religious and scholarly traditions:

- **Gnosticism**: Read as a primary witness to early Christian esotericism and the pursuit of self-knowledge (*gnosis*)
- **Protestantism**: Engaged critically as a text that raises questions about canon formation and the nature of Jesus's teaching
- **Catholicism**: Examined in light of patristic responses (Hippolytus, Origen, Cyril of Jerusalem) and the history of the biblical canon
- **Eastern Orthodoxy**: Assessed through the lens of patristic theology and the criteria of apostolic authenticity
- **Judaism**: Studied for its preservation of Jewish wisdom forms, parallels to rabbinic parables, and evidence of early Jewish-Christian interaction
- **Academic**: Central to debates about the historical Jesus, the hypothetical Q source, and the diversity of early Christianity

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Secret Knowledge | Salvation comes through understanding the hidden meaning of Jesus's words |
| Kingdom Within | The kingdom is not a future event but a present, interior reality |
| The Solitary (Monachos) | The ideal disciple is "single" or "solitary," unified within |
| Light and Darkness | Dualistic imagery of inner light versus the darkness of the world |
| World-Rejection | The material world (*kosmos*) is a "corpse" to be transcended |
| Self-Knowledge | "Know what is before your face, and what is hidden will be revealed" |

## Relationship to the Synoptic Gospels

Approximately half of Thomas's sayings have parallels in the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke), though often in shorter or more enigmatic form. Scholars debate whether Thomas drew from the Synoptics, whether both traditions share a common oral or written source (possibly related to Q), or whether Thomas preserves independent early tradition. The relationship remains one of the most contested questions in New Testament scholarship.
