Zen interpretation of Chapter 14, 'Inquiring Words,' discovers beneath the Huainanzi's encyclopedic scope a spiritual insight that resonates with Zen's own emphasis on direct experience and spontaneous action. While this chapter addresses its subject within the framework of systematic cosmology and political philosophy, Zen readers discern an underlying teaching about the nature of authentic engagement with reality — an engagement that arises not from conceptual knowledge but from a state of inner stillness and clarity.

The Huainanzi's treatment of wuwei (non-action) as the highest mode of engagement parallels the Zen ideal of mushin (no-mind): both traditions teach that the most effective and authentic action arises not from deliberation and calculation but from a consciousness that has been cleared of the obstructions of ego, desire, and fixed opinion.
