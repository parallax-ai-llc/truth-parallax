## Lan Ming Xun (Peering into the Obscure)

What is called the Way is vast and formless. Its virtue is soft and yielding. It is flat and easy, broad and spreading, flexible and pliant.

Those who peer into the obscure seek to understand what lies beyond the surface of things. The ordinary person sees the visible; the sage perceives the invisible. The ordinary person hears sounds; the sage perceives silence. The ordinary person knows the present; the sage understands the patterns that connect past, present, and future.

The sage looks at the small and perceives the large. He examines the near and understands the distant. He observes the present and comprehends the ancient.

An ant hole can cause the collapse of a great dike. A tiny spark can set ablaze a vast forest. Events that seem insignificant may lead to enormous consequences. The sage perceives these connections and acts accordingly.

The clear-sighted person is not the one who sees what everyone sees, but the one who sees what no one else sees. The wise person is not the one who knows what everyone knows, but the one who knows what no one else knows.

The sage examines the beginning to know the end. He studies the root to understand the branch. He observes the subtle to anticipate the manifest.

This is the meaning of peering into the obscure: to see the hidden patterns that govern events, to perceive the seeds of the future in the present moment, and to act before problems become crises.
