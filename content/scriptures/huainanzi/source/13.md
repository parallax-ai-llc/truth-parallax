## Fan Lun Xun (Boundless Discourses)

The Way is boundless in its application. It manifests differently in different contexts, yet it remains one.

In the individual, the Way appears as virtue. In the family, it appears as harmony. In the state, it appears as good governance. In the world, it appears as peace. The manifestation changes, but the principle is the same.

The sage does not cling to one application of the Way. He adapts it to every situation. In war, he is strategic. In peace, he is humane. In prosperity, he is generous. In adversity, he is resilient.

The boundlessness of the Way means that it cannot be captured by any single doctrine. The Confucians perceive one aspect. The Mohists perceive another. The Legalists perceive yet another. Each sees truly, but none sees completely.

The wise man learns from all schools and clings to none. He takes what is useful from each and discards what is not. He does not ask: "Is this Confucian or Legalist?" He asks: "Is this true? Is this useful? Does it accord with the Way?"

The Way is like water: it takes the shape of whatever vessel contains it, yet its nature remains constant. Pour it into a round vessel and it becomes round. Pour it into a square vessel and it becomes square. But in all cases it remains water.

The sage is the same: he adapts to every circumstance yet his inner nature remains unchanged. This adaptability is not weakness but the highest strength, not inconsistency but the truest consistency.
