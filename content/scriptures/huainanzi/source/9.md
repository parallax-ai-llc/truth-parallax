## Zhu Shu Xun (The Techniques of the Ruler)

The ruler occupies the position of non-action. He delegates to those with ability. He does not try to do everything himself.

The ruler's technique: Establish clear standards. Assign responsibilities. Evaluate results. Reward success. Punish failure. Beyond this, the ruler should remain empty and still.

When the ruler is empty, the ministers reveal their true nature. When the ruler is still, the ministers exert themselves. The ruler who is always busy doing things himself leaves nothing for his ministers to do. The ruler who is always expressing opinions leaves no room for his ministers to think.

The sage ruler uses the technique of non-interference: He sets the wheel in motion and then steps back. The wheel turns by itself. He plants the seed and then waits. The plant grows by itself.

But non-interference does not mean neglect. The ruler must be constantly watchful, even while appearing inactive. He observes the performance of his ministers silently. He detects problems early, when they are still small. He acts decisively when action is needed, but only when it is needed.

The ruler who interferes too much exhausts himself and his ministers. The ruler who interferes too little allows disorder to grow. The correct technique is to intervene at the right moment with the minimum necessary force.

This is the meaning of wu wei -- not "doing nothing" but "doing nothing unnecessary." The sage ruler accomplishes the most by doing the least.
