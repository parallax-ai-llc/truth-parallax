## Ben Jing Xun (The Root of the Classic)

In high antiquity, the true people breathed the essence and spat out the old. They abandoned wisdom and discarded cleverness. They returned to the great simplicity and embraced the uncarved block.

In that age, there were no roads and bridges, for the people did not travel far. There were no boats or carriages, for there was no need. There were no walls or moats, for the people had no enemies. There were no weapons or armor, for the people had no quarrels.

The people ate their food and found it sweet. They wore their clothes and found them fine. They were content in their dwellings and happy in their customs. Neighboring states could see each other, and the sounds of roosters and dogs could be heard across the borders, yet the people grew old and died without traveling back and forth.

Then the sages arose. They hobbled the people with rites and music, dangled benevolence and righteousness before them, and stirred up their desires with the lure of profit. The natural simplicity was destroyed.

This is the root of the classic teaching: That the original nature of man is good and complete. It requires no addition. The problem is not a lack of virtue but an excess of artificial contrivance.

The way back to the root is not through more rules and institutions but through less. Simplify. Reduce. Return to the natural. This is the teaching that underlies all other teachings.
