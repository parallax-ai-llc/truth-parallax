## Quan Yan Xun (Interpretation of the Way)

Words can point toward the Way but cannot capture it. The finger that points at the moon is not the moon.

The teachings of the ancients are like maps: they show the territory but they are not the territory itself. The man who confuses the map with the territory will go astray.

The Way cannot be taught by words alone. It must be experienced directly. The master can point the student in the right direction, but the student must walk the path himself.

Consider: The taste of honey cannot be conveyed by description. No matter how eloquently you describe sweetness, the listener who has never tasted honey will not truly understand. Only by tasting does one know.

Similarly, the Way can be described in words, but only through practice and direct experience does one truly know it.

The ancient sages used different words and different methods to convey the Way. Some used ritual. Some used music. Some used poetry. Some used parables. The form varied, but the content was the same.

The danger of words: Once the Way is captured in words, people cling to the words and forget the Way. They debate about the words and lose sight of the reality the words were meant to convey.

Therefore the sage uses words sparingly. He teaches more by example than by precept. He demonstrates the Way rather than describing it. And when he does use words, he reminds his listeners that the words are only fingers pointing at the moon.
