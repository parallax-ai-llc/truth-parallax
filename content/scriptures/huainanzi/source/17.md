## Shuo Lin Xun (A Forest of Persuasions)

A forest of persuasions: stories and arguments gathered from every quarter.

Duke Huan of Qi asked Guan Zhong: "What is the greatest worry of a state?" Guan Zhong replied: "The greatest worry is what I call the 'club moss on the shrine.' The club moss grows on the shrine, and nobody dares to remove it. If you try to burn it, you will damage the shrine. If you try to flood it, the walls will dissolve. The club moss survives because of its position."

So too with corrupt ministers: They survive because they are too close to the ruler to be removed without damaging the state.

A man of Lu kept a pet monkey. When times were hard, he told the monkeys: "I will give you three chestnuts in the morning and four in the evening." The monkeys were furious. He said: "Then I will give you four in the morning and three in the evening." The monkeys were delighted.

The total was the same, but the perception changed. The wise ruler understands that perception matters as much as reality. How a policy is presented may be as important as the policy itself.

A man who lost his axe suspected his neighbor's son of stealing it. He watched the boy: the boy's walk, his expression, his manner of speaking -- everything about him seemed to say "thief." Later the man found his axe where he had left it. He looked at the neighbor's son again: nothing about him seemed like a thief anymore.

Suspicion distorts perception. The ruler must guard against letting preconceptions color his judgment.
