## Miu Cheng Xun (Erroneous Designations)

Things are often called by names that do not correspond to their reality. Actions that appear virtuous may be harmful. Actions that appear harmful may be beneficial.

A man of Song was digging a well and found a man in it. He reported: "I have obtained a man by digging a well." The story spread, and people said: "A man was created by digging a well." The reality was that he found a stowaway, but the name given to the event transformed its meaning entirely.

The farmer of Song who waited by a stump for another rabbit: He had once seen a rabbit run into the stump and kill itself. Thereafter he abandoned his plowing and waited by the stump, hoping to catch another rabbit. He became the laughingstock of the state.

The man who marked the side of his boat to show where his sword fell into the river, then searched at the mark after the boat had moved -- he did not understand that circumstances change while marks remain fixed.

These stories illustrate a fundamental error: mistaking the name for the reality, the past for the present, the surface for the substance.

Good government requires that names correspond to realities. When a minister is called "loyal" but acts treacherously, the name is erroneous. When a policy is called "benevolent" but harms the people, the designation is wrong.

The sage corrects erroneous designations. He examines the reality beneath every name. He tests claims against evidence. He judges by results, not by labels.
