## Xiu Wu Xun (Cultivating Effort)

Effort without direction is wasted. Direction without effort is empty. The sage combines both: he knows where to go and he works to get there.

The cultivation of effort begins with the cultivation of the self. He who cannot govern himself cannot govern others. He who cannot order his own household cannot order the state.

The stages of cultivation: First, still the mind. Second, clarify the intentions. Third, order the daily habits. Fourth, extend this order to the family. Fifth, extend it to the community. Sixth, extend it to the state.

This is not a matter of grand gestures but of small, consistent actions. The man who practices virtue every day, in small matters, builds a character that is solid and enduring. The man who practices virtue only on great occasions builds a character that is hollow and unreliable.

Effort must be proportional to the task. The farmer who plants too thickly will harvest nothing, for the plants will choke each other. The ruler who governs too strictly will lose his people, for they will feel oppressed.

The right amount of effort is like the right amount of water for a plant: enough to nourish but not enough to drown. Too little effort and the task fails. Too much effort and the task is spoiled.

The sage knows when to exert himself and when to rest. He works when work is needed and waits when waiting is needed. His effort is always appropriate to the circumstance.
