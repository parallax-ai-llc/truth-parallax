## Shuo Shan Xun (A Mountain of Persuasions)

A mountain of persuasions: gathered wisdom from many sources, arranged for the instruction of rulers and the guidance of the people.

A man of Qi had a fine horse that refused to gallop. He asked a horse expert for advice. The expert said: "Set a wolf behind the horse." The man did so, and the horse galloped magnificently. But afterward, the horse was so exhausted and frightened that it could never be ridden again.

This illustrates the danger of governing through fear. Fear produces immediate results but destroys long-term capacity. The people driven by fear will perform in the short term but will be exhausted and resentful in the long term.

A skilled cook was cutting up an ox. His knife had been in use for nineteen years, yet it was as sharp as if newly ground. He said: "I follow the natural structure of the animal. I cut through the spaces between the joints. I never force the blade through bone. Therefore my knife never dulls."

This illustrates the principle of governing with minimal force: Follow the natural structure of society. Work through existing channels. Do not force policies against the grain of human nature.

A man was trying to lengthen his shadow by running toward it. The faster he ran, the faster the shadow moved ahead. Someone told him: "Stand under a tree." He stood under a tree, and his shadow grew long and still.

This illustrates the futility of pursuing goals through direct effort when indirect means would succeed effortlessly.
