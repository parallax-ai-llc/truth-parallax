## Overview

*4QMMT* (Miqtsat Ma'ase ha-Torah, "Some of the Works of the Torah") is one of the most significant legal documents found among the Dead Sea Scrolls. Reconstructed from six fragmentary manuscripts (4Q394–399), it takes the form of a letter — possibly from the Teacher of Righteousness to the leader of the Jerusalem establishment — cataloguing approximately twenty halakhic (legal) disputes between the Qumran community and the Jerusalem Temple authorities.

4QMMT is crucial for understanding the sectarian origins of the Qumran community, as it appears to document the specific legal disagreements that led to the community's separation from mainstream Judaism. The text has also become central to debates about Paul's phrase "works of the law" (erga nomou) in his letters to the Romans and Galatians, as 4QMMT uses the Hebrew equivalent (ma'ase ha-torah) in a context that illuminates the relationship between law-observance and righteousness.

## Structure

The six sections of 4QMMT are organized as follows:

1. **Section 1**: The calendar — the 364-day solar calendar versus the Jerusalem lunar calendar
2. **Section 2**: Purity laws concerning sacrificial offerings
3. **Section 3**: Purity regulations regarding the Temple and its precincts
4. **Section 4**: Laws concerning forbidden mixtures and priestly portions
5. **Section 5**: Additional halakhic rulings and their scriptural basis
6. **Section 6**: The concluding exhortation — a call to righteousness and the consequences of obedience

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Essene**: 4QMMT as a document of the community's self-understanding and eschatological hope
- **Judaism**: Witness to the diversity and creativity of Second Temple Jewish thought and practice
- **Protestantism**: Illuminates the Jewish background of the New Testament and early Christian theology
- **Catholicism**: Part of the salvation-historical development that prepared the way for Christ
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Anticipation of the liturgical and mystical realities fulfilled in the Church
- **Academic**: Primary source for understanding Second Temple Jewish religion, literature, and social organization
