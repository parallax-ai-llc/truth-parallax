## Overview

The Orphic Hymns represent a distinctive genre of ancient Greek literature — the liturgical hymn composed for actual ritual performance. As literary works, they stand in the tradition of earlier Greek hymnic poetry (the Homeric Hymns, Callimachus's Hymns, the Hymns of Proclus) while exhibiting their own distinctive features: their brevity, their consistent liturgical structure (invocation, attributes, petition), their rich accumulation of divine epithets, and their specification of incense offerings.

The literary analysis of the Orphic Hymns reveals sophisticated poetic techniques: the use of compound epithets (*polonymos*, many-named; *pantokrator*, all-ruling), ring composition (returning to the opening invocation at the close), catalogic enumeration of divine attributes and functions, and allusive compression of mythological narrative into epithetical phrases. Their hexameter verse connects them to the epic tradition while their content and purpose align them with the shorter hymnic forms of Greek worship.
