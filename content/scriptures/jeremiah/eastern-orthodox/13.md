The symbolic action of the linen belt in Jeremiah 13 is read by Orthodox interpreters as a parable of the intimate union between God and His people that sin has destroyed. The belt clinging to the waist (13:11) represents the closeness of the divine-human relationship that the Orthodox tradition calls *theosis* — the participation of humanity in the divine nature. When this intimate union is broken by sin, the result is not merely moral failure but ontological corruption, symbolized by the rotting of the belt. Orthodox theology understands sin primarily as a sickness of the soul that distorts the image of God within.

The journey to the Euphrates and the belt's subsequent ruin point toward the exile as the concrete consequence of spiritual corruption. Orthodox interpreters note that the physical displacement mirrors an interior displacement — alienation from God, from one's true self, and from the community of faith. The Orthodox understanding of repentance (*metanoia*) as a "return" to one's true nature resonates with the imagery of the belt that was meant to cling to God but instead was abandoned in a foreign land and destroyed.

The rhetorical question about the Ethiopian's skin and the leopard's spots (13:23) is read in Orthodox anthropology not as a statement of ontological determinism but as a description of the depth of habitual sin from which only divine grace can liberate. The Orthodox understanding of the passions (*pathē*) — deeply ingrained patterns of disordered desire — acknowledges that long-established habits of sin become quasi-natural, requiring sustained ascetic effort and divine grace to overcome. The passage thus serves as an argument for the necessity of the sacramental and ascetic life of the Church.
