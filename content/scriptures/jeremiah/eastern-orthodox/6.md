The invitation to "ask for the ancient paths" (6:16) resonates deeply with Orthodox ecclesiology, which grounds itself in the continuity of apostolic and patristic tradition. The Orthodox understanding of Tradition (with a capital T) is not mere conservatism but the living transmission of the faith once delivered to the saints. Jeremiah's call to seek the ancient way validates the Orthodox conviction that faithfulness to God requires rootedness in the tested wisdom of the past, not the restless pursuit of novelty.

The refiner's fire imagery (6:27-30) — Jeremiah appointed to test the people's metal, only to discover it irredeemably corrupt — connects with the Orthodox understanding of ascetic purification. The spiritual life, in Orthodox theology, is a process of refining — removing the passions (*pathē*) that obscure the image of God within. When this refining process is resisted or rejected, the result is what Jeremiah describes: "rejected silver." The Orthodox ascetic tradition's insistence on the necessity of *praxis* (active spiritual discipline) before *theoria* (contemplative vision) reflects this prophetic insight.

The false cry of "Peace, peace, when there is no peace" (6:14) has been applied by Orthodox theologians to the modern phenomenon of cheap ecumenism — a superficial unity that papers over genuine theological differences without the hard work of truth-seeking. The Orthodox commitment to conciliar truth-seeking, rooted in the model of the Ecumenical Councils, insists that genuine peace requires genuine agreement on the apostolic faith. Jeremiah's condemnation of false peace serves as a prophetic warrant for this insistence.
