Jeremiah's arrest and humiliation by Pashhur (20:1-6) and the agonized lament that follows (20:7-18) are read in Orthodox tradition as a profound anticipation of Christ's Passion. The prophet who is beaten and placed in stocks by the chief Temple officer prefigures Christ before the Sanhedrin. The Orthodox hymns of Holy Week draw on precisely this typological connection, seeing in Jeremiah's suffering a pattern that reaches its fulfillment in the suffering of God incarnate.

The extraordinary confession of 20:7 — rendered in some translations as "O Lord, you have seduced me" — represents the most daring expression of the prophet's relationship with God. Orthodox interpreters, following the Fathers, understand this not as an accusation against God but as the expression of one who has been overwhelmed by divine love. The mystics of the Orthodox tradition, from St. Symeon the New Theologian to the hesychast Fathers, recognized that the experience of God's presence can be simultaneously ecstatic and agonizing — the fire of divine love that both illumines and consumes.

The compulsion to speak (20:9) — "there is in my heart as it were a burning fire shut up in my bones" — connects to the Orthodox theology of the *logikos* nature of human beings. Humanity is created as the rational, speaking creature whose vocation is to articulate the divine Word. The prophet cannot suppress the word because to do so would be to deny his own nature as a being created for communion with and proclamation of the divine Logos. Orthodox theology of preaching and witness is grounded in this understanding of speech as fundamental to human identity and vocation.
