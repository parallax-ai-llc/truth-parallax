Jeremiah 22 evaluates the kings of Judah and establishes the prophetic principle that kingship is justified only by justice. The declaration that to know God is to do justice for the poor and needy (22:16) is foundational to the Orthodox understanding of *theognosia* — knowledge of God as inseparable from ethical practice. Orthodox theology, following the Johannine tradition ("If anyone says, 'I love God,' and hates his brother, he is a liar," 1 John 4:20), refuses to separate contemplative knowledge of God from active love of neighbor.

The condemnation of Jehoiakim for building his palace with forced labor (22:13-17) resonates with the Orthodox social tradition's emphasis on the dignity of labor and the rights of workers. St. John Chrysostom's homilies on wealth and poverty, which are among the most radical social critiques in Christian literature, draw on prophetic passages like this to argue that exploitation of workers is not merely an ethical failure but a fundamental denial of the image of God in every human person.

The oracle against Coniah (22:24-30), declaring that none of his descendants will sit on David's throne, creates a Christological puzzle that Orthodox interpreters resolve through the theology of the Theotokos. The Orthodox tradition, while acknowledging the genealogical complexity, emphasizes that Christ's royal authority derives not merely from biological descent but from the hypostatic union — the union of divine and human natures in one person. Mary's role as the one through whom the eternal Son enters David's lineage is thus central to the resolution of the prophetic tension between curse and promise.
