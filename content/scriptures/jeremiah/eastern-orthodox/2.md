Orthodox exegesis of Jeremiah 2 reads the covenant lawsuit against Israel through the patristic theology of spiritual marriage. The image of Israel as a faithful bride who has become a harlot resonates with the Orthodox understanding of the soul's relationship to God — created for intimate communion but continually tempted to seek fulfillment in created things rather than the Creator. St. Maximus the Confessor would later develop this insight systematically in his teaching on the *logoi* of creation, which are properly ordered only when they lead the soul back to God.

The metaphor of the broken cisterns (2:13) — Israel forsaking the fountain of living waters for containers that cannot hold water — is read in Orthodox liturgical theology as an image of the consequences of turning from the sacramental life of the Church. The living waters flow through baptism, the Eucharist, and the other mysteries (*mysteria*); the broken cisterns represent any attempt to find spiritual sustenance apart from the Church's sacramental economy. This imagery is echoed in the baptismal liturgy, where the candidate is brought to the "fountain of living water."

The patristic reading of this chapter emphasizes that Israel's apostasy is not merely a moral failure but an ontological distortion — a turning away from being itself, since God alone is the source of all being. Theodoret of Cyrrhus, in his commentary on Jeremiah, argues that idolatry is fundamentally a confusion of categories: treating finite creatures as though they possessed the infinite attributes that belong to God alone. This ontological analysis of sin remains central to Orthodox theology.
