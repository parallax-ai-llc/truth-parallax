Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 44 reads the confrontation over the "Queen of Heaven" worship through the patristic understanding of idolatry as a fundamental distortion of the human capacity for worship. The Fathers taught that the *imago Dei* includes an inherent orientation toward worship, and when this orientation is directed away from the true God, it inevitably attaches to created beings or cosmic forces. The Egyptian community's devotion to the Queen of Heaven represents not the absence of religious impulse but its catastrophic misdirection.

The community's argument that their prosperity coincided with their idolatrous worship (44:17-18) is analyzed by the Orthodox Fathers as an instance of spiritual blindness — the inability to perceive the true causes of events because the *nous* (spiritual intellect) has been darkened by the passions. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the fallen intellect consistently misreads the relationship between cause and effect in the spiritual realm, interpreting God's patience as approval and His judgment as abandonment.

Jeremiah's final oracle to the Egyptian community (44:24-30) functions in Orthodox reading as the last offer of repentance before judgment becomes irrevocable. The Orthodox tradition, following the Fathers, maintains that God's patience is immense but not infinite in its historical manifestations — there comes a point at which a community's accumulated refusals reach a critical mass. The Liturgy of St. Basil prays for deliverance from "hardness of heart," recognizing that the greatest spiritual danger is not external punishment but the internal calcification that makes repentance impossible.
