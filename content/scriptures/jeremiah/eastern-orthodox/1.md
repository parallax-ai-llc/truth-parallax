Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah's call (1:4-19) reads the passage through the lens of *theosis* — God's purpose in calling Jeremiah is not merely to commission a messenger but to draw a human being into participation in the divine life. The declaration "Before I formed you in the womb I knew you" (1:5) is understood by the Fathers as expressing God's eternal knowledge of each person and His desire for their deification. St. John Chrysostom emphasized that divine foreknowledge does not negate human freedom but rather reveals the depth of God's personal engagement with His creatures.

The prophet's reluctance and God's reassurance — "I am with you to deliver you" (1:8) — establish a pattern that Orthodox theology recognizes in all authentic vocation. The divine call always exceeds human capacity, requiring the synergy (*synergia*) of divine grace and human cooperation that is central to Orthodox soteriology. Jeremiah's weakness becomes the vessel through which divine power operates, a principle that St. Paul would later articulate as "my power is made perfect in weakness" (2 Corinthians 12:9).

The two inaugural visions — the almond branch and the boiling pot from the north — are read in the Orthodox tradition as instances of prophetic *theoria* (contemplation), in which God opens the spiritual eyes of the prophet to perceive realities hidden from ordinary sight. The liturgical reading of Jeremiah's call during the ordination of clergy in some Orthodox traditions underscores its paradigmatic significance: every minister of the word stands in continuity with the prophetic vocation first articulated here.
