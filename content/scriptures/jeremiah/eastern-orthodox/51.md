Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 51 reads the final oracle against Babylon as a cosmic drama in which the powers of the material world are overthrown by divine justice. The comprehensive nature of the destruction — military, cultural, ecological — is understood by the Fathers as anticipating the ultimate transformation of the cosmos that Orthodox eschatology envisions. Not merely the destruction of the old order but its transfiguration into the new creation is the goal of divine judgment, and Babylon's fall clears the ground for this renewal.

The creation theology embedded in the oracle (51:15-19) is read through the Orthodox doctrine of *logoi* — the divine principles or intentions that underlie all created things. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that every creature exists according to its *logos* in the mind of God, and when creatures or civilizations deviate from their *logoi*, they move toward dissolution. Babylon's fall is the consequence of its radical departure from the divine intention — the empire that was meant to serve God's purposes (as the instrument of Judah's discipline) instead served its own pride.

The sinking of the scroll in the Euphrates (51:63-64) is interpreted in Orthodox liturgical theology as a prophetic act that participates in the reality it proclaims. The Orthodox understanding of icons, liturgical actions, and blessed objects holds that material things can become bearers of spiritual reality through divine consecration. Seraiah's act of casting the word-laden scroll into the river represents the intersection of word and world, prophecy and history, that the Orthodox tradition celebrates in its liturgical life.
