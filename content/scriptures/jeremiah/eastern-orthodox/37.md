Jeremiah 37 narrates the prophet's arrest and imprisonment, and Orthodox interpreters read this passage as a typological anticipation of Christ's own arrest and trial. The false accusation — that Jeremiah was deserting to the enemy — mirrors the false charges brought against Christ before the Sanhedrin and Pilate. The Orthodox reading of the prophets is consistently christological: Jeremiah's sufferings are not merely historical parallels but genuine prefigurations of the Passion, part of the single narrative of salvation that finds its climax in Christ's death and resurrection.

Zedekiah's secret meeting with Jeremiah (37:17) is read in Orthodox spiritual theology as an image of the soul that knows the truth but fears to embrace it publicly. The hesychastic tradition calls this condition *acedia* — a spiritual paralysis that recognizes the good but cannot summon the will to pursue it. Zedekiah's tragedy is not ignorance but cowardice: he has access to the prophetic word and even believes it, but he cannot act on it because he is enslaved to the opinions of his officials. Orthodox ascetical theology addresses this condition through the cultivation of *parrhesia* — the boldness to speak and act truthfully regardless of consequences.

The transfer of Jeremiah from the dungeon to the court of the guard (37:21), where he receives a daily ration of bread, illustrates the Orthodox theology of divine providence operating through human agents. Even in imprisonment, God provides for His prophet, using the king's grudging mercy as an instrument of sustenance. The Orthodox tradition sees in this provision a foreshadowing of the Eucharist — the daily bread that sustains the faithful through every form of earthly suffering and confinement.
