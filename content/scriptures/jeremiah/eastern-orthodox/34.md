Jeremiah 34 narrates the broken covenant of liberty, in which Jerusalem's inhabitants freed their slaves during the siege and then re-enslaved them — an act of false repentance that Orthodox theology reads as a paradigm of *metanoia* that fails to take root. The Orthodox understanding of repentance insists that genuine *metanoia* involves a permanent reorientation of the will, not a temporary behavioral change motivated by fear. The Jerusalemites' reversal demonstrates what the Fathers call "repentance of the lips" — an outward show of conversion that evaporates when external pressure is removed.

The covenant ceremony of passing between the halves of a cut calf (34:18-19) carries profound typological significance in Orthodox reading. This ancient ritual of self-imprecation — in which the parties invoke upon themselves the fate of the slaughtered animal if they break the covenant — foreshadows the ultimate covenant sacrifice of Christ, who took upon Himself the curse that faithless humanity deserved. The Orthodox theology of atonement, articulated by the Fathers, sees in every covenant ritual a shadow of the Cross, where the ultimate covenant is sealed in blood.

The ironic proclamation of "liberty" to sword, pestilence, and famine (34:17) illustrates the Orthodox conviction that freedom divorced from truth and justice is not authentic freedom but a deeper form of bondage. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that true freedom (*eleutheria*) is the capacity to choose the good in accordance with one's nature, while the freedom to choose evil is actually a deficiency — a falling away from the fullness of human personhood. Jeremiah's ironic wordplay expresses this same principle in prophetic terms.
