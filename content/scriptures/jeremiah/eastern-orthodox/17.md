The diagnosis of the heart as "deceitful above all things" (17:9) resonates with the Orthodox tradition's sophisticated understanding of the nous — the spiritual intellect that, in its fallen state, is clouded by the passions and incapable of perceiving reality clearly. Orthodox spiritual theology, following the Philokalia tradition, teaches that the healing of the nous through *nepsis* (watchfulness), *prosoche* (attention), and the Jesus Prayer is essential for the recovery of authentic self-knowledge and knowledge of God. Jeremiah's diagnosis of the heart's deceptiveness provides the prophetic foundation for this entire ascetic tradition.

The contrasting images of the cursed shrub and the blessed tree (17:5-8) illustrate the Orthodox teaching on the two fundamental orientations of the human will — toward God or toward self. The tree planted by water, whose roots draw sustenance from hidden sources even during drought, represents the believer whose life is grounded in prayer and the sacraments. The cursed shrub in the desert represents the person who trusts in human strength alone. Orthodox spiritual directors use this imagery to emphasize the necessity of maintaining a life of prayer as the root system that sustains visible Christian life.

The sabbath oracle (17:19-27) is interpreted in Orthodox theology through the lens of the Lord's Day and the eschatological rest that it anticipates. The Orthodox Sunday celebration — centered on the Divine Liturgy as the weekly Pascha (resurrection feast) — transforms the sabbath principle from a day of cessation to a day of participation in the risen life of Christ. The blessing promised for faithful observance is understood not as contractual reward but as the natural consequence of entering into the rhythm of divine rest that God has established for human flourishing.
