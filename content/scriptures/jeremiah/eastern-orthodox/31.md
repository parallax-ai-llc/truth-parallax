Jeremiah 31 contains the new covenant prophecy (31:31-34), which Orthodox theology reads as the prophetic foundation for the entire economy of salvation realized in Christ and communicated through the sacramental life of the Church. The promise that God will write the law "on their hearts" is understood by the Fathers not merely as moral transformation but as the beginning of *theosis* — the process by which human nature is progressively permeated by divine grace until the human person participates in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). St. Irenaeus's theology of recapitulation — Christ undoing the damage of the Fall by uniting human nature to divine — is the fulfillment of what Jeremiah prophesied.

The promise that "they shall all know me, from the least of them to the greatest" (31:34) is central to Orthodox pneumatology. The direct knowledge of God promised in the new covenant is made possible by the indwelling of the Holy Spirit, whom the Orthodox tradition calls "the Lord, the Giver of Life." This knowledge is not abstract theological information but living, experiential communion — the kind of knowing that the hesychast tradition cultivates through the Jesus Prayer and the disciplines of watchfulness (*nepsis*). The new covenant is thus not merely a legal arrangement but a new mode of existence.

Rachel's weeping (31:15) occupies an important place in Orthodox hymnography, especially in the services of Great Week and in the commemoration of the Holy Innocents. The Orthodox tradition reads Rachel's tears as an icon of the Theotokos standing at the Cross — the mother who weeps for her child and, through him, for all the children of Israel. The promise that "they shall come back from the land of the enemy" (31:16) is read as a prophecy of the Resurrection, in which death itself — the ultimate enemy — is defeated and the children return from its domain.
