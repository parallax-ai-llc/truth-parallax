Jeremiah 36 recounts the writing, destruction, and rewriting of Jeremiah's scroll, a narrative that Orthodox theology reads as a paradigm for the indestructibility of divine revelation. The burning of the scroll by Jehoiakim is an act of archontic rage against the prophetic word — the attempt of temporal power to silence divine truth by destroying its material vehicle. But the word proves stronger than the fire: God commands the scroll rewritten, and "many similar words were added" (36:32). Orthodox theology's confidence in the Holy Tradition's survival through persecution and destruction is grounded in this prophetic precedent.

The role of Baruch — the faithful scribe who transcribes, reads, and preserves the prophetic word — is honored in Orthodox tradition, which recognizes the sacred function of those who transmit divine revelation across generations. The Orthodox monastic tradition of manuscript copying, icon-painting, and liturgical preservation continues Baruch's ministry. The scribe is not merely a copyist but a participant in the prophetic process, and the Orthodox veneration of sacred texts and objects reflects the conviction that material vehicles of divine revelation are themselves sanctified.

The contrast between Jehoiakim's response (burning the scroll) and the earlier response of Josiah (tearing his garments in repentance) illustrates the Orthodox theology of *synergeia* — the cooperation between divine grace and human will. The same divine word produces opposite responses depending on the disposition of the hearer. Orthodox spiritual theology teaches that the word of God is always efficacious — it always accomplishes God's purpose — but that purpose may be either salvation or judgment, depending on how it is received.
