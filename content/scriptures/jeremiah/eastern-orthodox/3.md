Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 3 centers on the theme of *metanoia* (repentance) — not merely a change of behavior but a fundamental reorientation of the nous (the spiritual intellect) toward God. The divine invitation "Return, faithless Israel" (3:12) is read in the liturgical context of Great Lent, when the Orthodox Church calls the faithful to the deep repentance that Jeremiah envisions. The prophet's insistence that repentance must be genuine, not superficial (3:10 — Judah "did not return to me with her whole heart, but in pretense"), aligns with the Orthodox understanding that the sacrament of confession requires sincere contrition, not mere formal participation.

The eschatological vision of 3:14-18 — when the Ark of the Covenant will no longer be needed because God's presence will be universal — is read by Orthodox interpreters as pointing toward the eschatological fulfillment when God will be "all in all" (1 Corinthians 15:28). The Ark, as a localized symbol of divine presence, gives way to the universal indwelling of the Holy Spirit that characterizes the age of the Church and will reach its fullness in the age to come. Orthodox ecclesiology understands the Church's liturgical life as the anticipation of this universal divine presence.

St. Ephrem the Syrian, in his poetic commentaries, drew attention to the tenderness of God's voice in this chapter — a father calling wayward children, a husband inviting back an unfaithful wife. Orthodox theology insists that divine judgment is never punitive for its own sake but always oriented toward restoration and healing. The patristic distinction between God's pedagogical wrath and vindictive wrath (which the Fathers unanimously reject as a divine attribute) is crucial for reading Jeremiah's alternation between judgment oracles and invitations to return.
