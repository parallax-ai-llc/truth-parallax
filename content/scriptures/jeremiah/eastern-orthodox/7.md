The Temple Sermon of Jeremiah 7 is read in Orthodox theology as a prophetic critique of ritualism divorced from ethical life — a theme that the Orthodox liturgical tradition addresses through the inseparable connection between worship and moral transformation. The Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom is structured so that the dismissal sends the faithful into the world to live the liturgy; Jeremiah's Temple Sermon stands as the prophetic warning against the separation of worship from daily life that the liturgy is designed to prevent.

The reference to Shiloh (7:12-14) — where God's dwelling was destroyed because of Israel's wickedness — demonstrates the Orthodox teaching that sacred spaces, while genuinely sanctified by divine presence, do not guarantee the holiness of those who worship in them. The iconography and architecture of Orthodox churches are understood as windows into the Kingdom of God, but Jeremiah's warning against treating the Temple as a talisman applies equally to any tendency to reduce the sacramental life to magical thinking.

Jesus's quotation of Jeremiah 7:11 during the cleansing of the Temple (Mark 11:17) establishes a direct Christological link that Orthodox interpretation emphasizes. Christ acts as the prophet greater than Jeremiah — not merely warning against the corruption of worship but inaugurating the true Temple (his own body, John 2:21) that can never be destroyed. The Orthodox understanding of the Church as the Body of Christ, continually constituted by the Eucharist, represents the fulfillment of what the Jerusalem Temple was always meant to be.
