Jeremiah 25's prophecy of seventy years (25:11-12) and the cup of wrath distributed to the nations (25:15-29) are read in Orthodox theology through the lens of divine providence and eschatological judgment. The seventy-year prophecy demonstrates God's sovereignty over historical timelines — a conviction that the Orthodox Church liturgically affirms through its understanding of the Church year as the sanctification of time itself. The prokeimenon and readings appointed for various occasions reflect the belief that God orders time according to His redemptive purposes.

The cup of wrath imagery carries profound significance in Orthodox liturgical theology. When Christ in Gethsemane prays "let this cup pass from me" (Matthew 26:39), the Orthodox tradition understands him to be accepting the cup that Jeremiah described — the full weight of divine judgment against human sin. The Eucharistic chalice is the transformation of this cup: what was once the vessel of wrath becomes, through Christ's self-offering, the cup of salvation. The Divine Liturgy's prayer over the chalice makes this connection explicit, recalling the Lord's words at the Last Supper in direct reference to the prophetic tradition.

The universal scope of judgment — all nations included, none exempt — reflects the Orthodox understanding of God as the universal Pantocrator (ruler of all), a title depicted in the central dome icon of every Orthodox church. The Pantocrator icon shows Christ as the righteous Judge who holds the book of life — the fulfillment of the prophetic vision in which all nations are accountable before the divine tribunal. This iconographic tradition makes visible what Jeremiah proclaimed verbally.
