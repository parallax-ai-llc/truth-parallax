Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 48 reads the oracle against Moab through the patristic theology of divine *paideia* (education) — God's corrective engagement with the nations as an expression of His universal salvific will. Moab's pride (48:29) is understood not merely as a political attitude but as the fundamental spiritual condition that the Orthodox ascetical tradition calls *hyperephania* (vainglory), the root of all other passions. St. John Climacus in *The Ladder of Divine Ascent* identifies pride as the first passion to enter and the last to leave the soul, and Moab's downfall illustrates its destructive power.

The divine lament over Moab (48:31-36) is particularly significant in Orthodox theology because it reveals God's compassion for those outside the covenant. The Orthodox liturgical tradition, especially the prayers for the departed that encompass all humanity, reflects this understanding that God's love is not bounded by covenant membership. St. Isaac the Syrian taught that God's heart grieves even over the demons, and the prophetic lament over Moab provides scriptural support for this expansive vision of divine compassion.

The promise of restoration "in the latter days" (48:47) is read in Orthodox eschatology as anticipating the *apokatastasis* — the final restoration of all things that some Fathers, notably St. Gregory of Nyssa, hoped would encompass all creation. While the Orthodox Church has not dogmatically affirmed universal salvation, the restoration promises within the prophetic oracles against the nations nourish the hope that God's redemptive purpose extends beyond the boundaries that human theology has drawn.
