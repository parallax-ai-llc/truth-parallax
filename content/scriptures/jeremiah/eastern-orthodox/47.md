Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 47 reads the oracle against the Philistines within the cosmic framework of divine judgment that the prophetic books present. The waters rising from the north (47:2), overwhelming the land and its inhabitants, evoke the primordial chaos that God ordered in creation — suggesting that divine judgment operates as a partial return to pre-creation disorder, a withdrawal of the sustaining grace that holds the cosmos in being. The Fathers understood this imagery as revealing the contingency of all created order upon God's sustaining will.

The anguish described in the chapter — the cries of the inhabitants, fathers abandoning their children in flight (47:3) — is read in the Orthodox tradition not with satisfaction but with the compassion that the liturgy expresses for all who suffer. The Orthodox funeral service prays for mercy on all the departed, and the Fathers taught that God takes no pleasure in the destruction of any creature. The judgment is real and necessary, but the Orthodox tradition insists that it proceeds from a God who "desires all to be saved" (1 Timothy 2:4).

The sword of the Lord that cannot rest (47:6-7) is interpreted in Orthodox theology as expressing the inescapable nature of divine justice within the moral cosmos. St. Gregory the Theologian taught that God's justice and mercy are not opposing attributes but complementary aspects of the divine love — justice that refuses to leave evil unchallenged is itself an expression of love for those whom evil oppresses. The Philistines' judgment, like all divine judgments, serves the ultimate purpose of restoring the created order to its intended harmony.
