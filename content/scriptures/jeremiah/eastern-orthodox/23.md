The messianic prophecy of the "righteous Branch" (23:5-6) is read in Orthodox Christology as a direct prophecy of Christ, the Son of David who is also "The Lord is our Righteousness." The title's attribution of the divine name (*Yahweh*) to the Davidic king supports the Orthodox doctrine of Christ's full divinity — the Messiah is not merely a righteous human ruler but God himself come to reign. The Councils of Nicaea (325) and Chalcedon (451) articulated in dogmatic form what Jeremiah expressed in prophetic poetry.

The critique of false prophets (23:9-40) provides the Orthodox tradition with criteria for authentic spiritual authority. The true prophet has "stood in the council of the Lord" (23:18) — an expression that Orthodox interpreters connect to the Church's conciliar tradition. The Ecumenical Councils are understood as instances of the Church collectively "standing in the council of the Lord," discerning His will through the guidance of the Holy Spirit. The false prophets who speak from their own hearts represent the danger of individual interpretation divorced from the communal discernment of the Church.

The declaration that God's word is "like fire" and "like a hammer" (23:29) resonates with the Orthodox experience of the word proclaimed in the Divine Liturgy. The Gospel reading, accompanied by candles, incense, and the deacon's exclamation "Wisdom! Let us attend!", is understood as a genuine encounter with the living Word — Christ himself speaking through the Scripture to His assembled people. The Orthodox liturgical setting for Scripture reading reflects the Jeremianic conviction that God's word is not inert text but active, transformative divine energy.
