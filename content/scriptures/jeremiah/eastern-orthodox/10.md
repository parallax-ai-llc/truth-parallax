Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 10 reads the polemic against idols in direct connection with the theology of icons. The key distinction, articulated definitively at the Seventh Ecumenical Council (787), is between the worship (*latreia*) due to God alone and the veneration (*proskynesis*) offered to icons. Jeremiah's ridicule is directed at idols — objects that are themselves treated as divine — not at images that serve as windows into the divine reality they depict. Orthodox iconography, rooted in the theology of the Incarnation (the invisible God has become visible in Christ), is the antithesis of the idolatry Jeremiah condemns.

The confession of God as "the living God and the everlasting King" (10:10) resonates with the Orthodox doxological tradition. Orthodox theology is fundamentally doxological — its deepest expressions are found not in systematic treatises but in liturgical hymns, prayers, and the divine services. The Jeremianic confession of the living God echoes through the Orthodox liturgy, from the Trisagion ("Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal") to the Great Doxology. The prophetic text is not merely studied but sung, entering the consciousness of the faithful through the rhythms of corporate worship.

Jeremiah's prayer for measured correction (10:24) — "not in your anger, lest you bring me to nothing" — reflects the Orthodox understanding of divine discipline as therapeutic rather than retributive. The Orthodox Fathers consistently taught that God's punishment is always aimed at healing and restoration, never at destruction for its own sake. St. Isaac the Syrian's teaching that even hell is an expression of God's love experienced as painful by those who resist it represents the most developed form of this Jeremianic insight in the Orthodox tradition.
