The oracle against Egypt in Jeremiah 46 is read in Orthodox tradition through the theology of the divine *oikonomia* (economy) — God's providential governance of all nations and all history toward their ultimate purpose. The Orthodox Fathers understood the rise and fall of empires not as random historical events but as movements within the divine plan, directed toward the preparation of the world for the Incarnation. Egypt's humiliation at Carchemish and the subsequent Babylonian invasion are episodes within this larger drama.

The poetic description of Egypt's military splendor and its collapse (46:3-12) is interpreted by St. John Chrysostom as illustrating the vanity of all earthly power that does not rest on divine foundation. The Orthodox liturgical tradition, which commemorates the fall of great empires in its hymnography, reads these oracles as perpetual warnings against the temptation of imperial Christianity itself — the identification of the Church's mission with any particular political order.

The promise to Israel at the chapter's conclusion (46:27-28) is understood in Orthodox theology as expressing God's covenant faithfulness, which remains operative even through judgment. The distinction between the destruction of the nations and the chastening of Israel reflects the Orthodox teaching on divine *paideia* (education through discipline) — God's corrective action toward those He loves, as distinct from the punitive judgment that falls on those who have no covenant relationship with Him. The Orthodox tradition reads this distinction christologically: in Christ, all humanity is offered the covenant relationship that transforms punishment into pedagogy.
