Jeremiah 11 returns to the covenant curses of Deuteronomy, and Orthodox interpreters read this within the broader patristic understanding of the relationship between the Old and New Covenants. The broken Sinai covenant demonstrates not the failure of God but the inadequacy of external law to transform the human heart — a theme that St. Paul develops in Romans 7-8 and that Orthodox theology systematizes in its distinction between the law as pedagogue and grace as transformative power. The brokenness of the old covenant points forward to the necessity of the new.

The plot against Jeremiah by his own kinsmen in Anathoth (11:18-23) and the "lamb led to the slaughter" imagery (11:19) are read in Orthodox Christological typology as direct prefigurations of Christ's Passion. The Orthodox Holy Week services draw extensively on the prophetic literature, and Jeremiah's suffering at the hands of his own people mirrors Christ's rejection by Jerusalem. The icon of Christ the Bridegroom (*Nymphios*), displayed during Holy Monday through Wednesday, depicts Christ in the posture of the suffering prophet — mocked, beaten, and yet silently bearing the sins of His people.

The prohibition against intercession (11:14) is understood in Orthodox theology within the framework of the distinction between God's antecedent will (that all be saved) and His consequent will (which responds to human choices). God's command to Jeremiah to stop praying does not indicate that God has ceased to love the people but that their persistent refusal of repentance has created a situation in which historical judgment is necessary for the ultimate purpose of restoration. Orthodox eschatology holds this tension between temporal judgment and eternal mercy.
