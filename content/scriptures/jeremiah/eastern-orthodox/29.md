Jeremiah 29 presents the prophet's letter to the Babylonian exiles, a passage that Orthodox theology reads in light of the Church's own experience of diaspora and minority existence. The instruction to build, plant, marry, and seek the welfare of the city (29:5-7) has guided Orthodox communities living under Ottoman, Soviet, and other non-Christian regimes. The theology of constructive engagement with a host culture — neither assimilation nor withdrawal but faithful presence — finds its prophetic charter in Jeremiah's letter.

The promise that God will be found by those who seek Him with their whole heart (29:13) is central to Orthodox spiritual theology. The Fathers consistently teach that the knowledge of God is not an intellectual achievement but the fruit of wholehearted seeking — a seeking that involves the entire person, body and soul, in the disciplines of prayer, fasting, and repentance. St. Gregory of Nyssa's theology of *epektasis* — the infinite progress of the soul toward God — is grounded in precisely this promise: God makes Himself findable to those who pursue Him with complete devotion.

The seventy-year period of exile (29:10) carries typological significance in Orthodox reading, echoing the sabbatical structure of sacred time. The Fathers see in this period not merely a chronological prediction but a theological statement about the purifying function of exile. Just as the land must rest to be restored, so the community must undergo the discipline of displacement to be renewed. Orthodox liturgical theology, which structures time around cycles of fasting and feasting, reflects this same conviction that periods of deprivation prepare the community for the fullness of divine encounter.
