## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah is rooted in the consensus of the Church Fathers (*consensus patrum*) and the liturgical life of the Church. Jeremiah is read extensively during Great Lent, where his calls to repentance and his prophecy of a New Covenant resonate with the penitential and paschal themes of the season. Orthodox exegesis reads Jeremiah through the lens of *theosis* — humanity's calling to participate in the divine nature — and sees in the prophet's suffering a pattern that anticipates Christ's kenotic self-emptying.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Exegesis
The Orthodox Church privileges the readings of the Greek Fathers:
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Emphasis on moral reform and the dangers of empty ritual
- **St. Cyril of Alexandria**: Christological typology in the prophets
- **Theodoret of Cyrrhus**: Historical-typological commentary on Jeremiah
- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Poetic-typological reading of prophetic lament

### Liturgical Context
Jeremiah is read in the *parimiae* (Old Testament readings) during Great Lent and on fast days. The Lamentations attributed to Jeremiah are central to Holy Week services. This liturgical setting shapes interpretation, always pointing toward Christ's Passion and Resurrection.

## Theological Emphases

- **Prophetic Kenosis**: Jeremiah's suffering as a model of self-emptying love that anticipates Christ
- **The New Covenant**: Read not as abrogation of the Old but as its fulfillment and interiorization through theosis
- **Repentance (*Metanoia*)**: Jeremiah's call to return as paradigmatic for the Orthodox understanding of conversion
- **True Worship**: The Temple Sermon as a call to worship in spirit and truth
