The potter and clay imagery of Jeremiah 18 is read in Orthodox theology through the lens of *synergia* — the cooperation of divine grace and human freedom. While the passage powerfully affirms God's sovereignty over His creation ("like clay in the potter's hand, so are you in my hand"), the conditional statements of 18:7-10 equally affirm that human response matters. Orthodox theology, following the Fathers, insists that God's sovereignty and human freedom are not competing forces but complementary dimensions of the single divine-human drama of salvation.

The image of the potter reworking a marred vessel rather than discarding it reflects the Orthodox understanding of God as the divine Artist whose creative work is oriented toward beauty (*kallos*). The patristic tradition, from St. Basil the Great's *Hexaemeron* through St. Maximus the Confessor's cosmic vision, understands creation as an artwork in progress, marred by sin but continually being restored by the divine Craftsman. The sacrament of confession in Orthodox practice is precisely this reworking — the penitent, deformed by sin, is reshaped by divine grace on the wheel of repentance.

The plot against Jeremiah (18:18-23) introduces the theme of the righteous sufferer that is central to Orthodox Christological typology. The pattern of innocent suffering at the hands of one's own community, followed by appeal to divine justice, anticipates the Passion of Christ. Orthodox Holy Week liturgy weaves together the sufferings of the prophets with the Passion narrative, creating a typological tapestry that reveals Christ as the ultimate righteous sufferer whose vindication encompasses and transcends all previous instances.
