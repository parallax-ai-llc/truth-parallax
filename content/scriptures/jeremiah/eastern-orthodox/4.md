Jeremiah 4 presents the terrifying vision of de-creation — "I looked at the earth, and it was without form and void" (4:23) — that Orthodox interpreters read as the cosmic consequence of human sin. In Orthodox theology, humanity is the microcosm whose vocation is to unite the created order with God; when humanity falls, the entire cosmos is affected. St. Maximus the Confessor's teaching on the five cosmic divisions that humanity was meant to overcome provides the systematic framework for understanding how Jeremiah's de-creation vision relates to the theology of the Fall and restoration.

The command to "circumcise yourselves to the Lord; remove the foreskin of your hearts" (4:4) is foundational to the Orthodox understanding of baptism as the true circumcision. The patristic tradition, drawing on Colossians 2:11-12, understands baptism as the spiritual circumcision of the heart that removes the hardness of sin and opens the person to divine grace. The catechumenate in the Orthodox Church, with its extended period of preparation, reflects the seriousness with which the Fathers took Jeremiah's demand for interior transformation rather than external conformity.

The prophet's personal anguish (4:19) — "My anguish, my anguish! I writhe in pain!" — is interpreted in Orthodox tradition as an instance of prophetic *sympatheia* (co-suffering), in which the prophet participates in God's own grief over human sin. This concept of divine suffering, carefully distinguished from the heresy of patripassianism (the Father suffering on the cross), is central to Orthodox spirituality. The prophetic grief of Jeremiah anticipates the kenotic self-emptying of Christ, who wept over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41) just as Jeremiah had before him.
