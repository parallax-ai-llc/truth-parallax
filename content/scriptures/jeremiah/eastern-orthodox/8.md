Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 8 attends particularly to the contrast between nature's faithfulness and humanity's rebellion (8:7). The migratory birds that follow their divinely ordained patterns put to shame a people who reject the ordinances of their Creator. Orthodox theology of creation, rooted in the patristic understanding of the *logoi* (divine principles embedded in all created things), teaches that the natural world bears witness to its Creator by faithfully fulfilling its purpose. Humanity alone, possessing free will, can choose to deviate from the divine purpose — and this deviation is the essence of sin.

The question "Is there no balm in Gilead? Is there no physician there?" (8:22) is read Christologically in the Orthodox tradition. Christ is the true Physician (*Christos Iatros*), a title found frequently in early Christian literature and iconography. The healing balm is the grace of the Holy Spirit, dispensed through the sacraments of the Church — particularly the Mystery of Holy Unction, in which the sick are anointed with oil and prayed over for healing of body and soul. Jeremiah's anguished question receives its answer in the incarnation, death, and resurrection of the divine Physician.

The indictment of the scribes (8:8) — "the lying pen of the scribes has made it into a lie" — is read in Orthodox tradition as a warning against the misuse of theological learning. The Orthodox emphasis on the unity of theology and prayer (captured in Evagrius of Pontus's dictum, "If you are a theologian, you truly pray; if you truly pray, you are a theologian") serves as a safeguard against the kind of intellectual manipulation that Jeremiah condemns. Knowledge of God's word without personal transformation is not theology but its counterfeit.
