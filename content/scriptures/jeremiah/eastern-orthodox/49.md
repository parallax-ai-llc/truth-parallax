Orthodox reading of Jeremiah 49 interprets the oracles against the five nations through the theological lens of God's universal *oikonomia* (economy). Each nation's judgment is understood not as arbitrary divine anger but as the necessary consequence of moral disorder within God's providential governance of creation. The Fathers taught that God's judgment on the nations serves the larger purpose of preparing the world for the revelation of Christ — the historical upheavals described in the prophets clear the ground for the planting of the gospel.

The oracle against Edom (49:7-22) receives extensive treatment in Orthodox patristic commentary due to Edom's symbolic significance. The Fathers frequently identified Edom with the "old man" of Pauline theology — the unredeemed human nature that relies on its own wisdom and strength. The question "Is wisdom no more in Teman?" (49:7) is read as an indictment not merely of Edomite philosophy but of all human wisdom that operates apart from divine illumination. The Orthodox tradition of *apophatic* theology — knowing God through what He is not — finds support in the prophetic critique of confident human knowing.

The restoration promises for Ammon (49:6) and Elam (49:39) are understood within the Orthodox theology of the *eschaton* as anticipating the gathering of all nations into the kingdom of God. The Orthodox liturgical tradition prays for the "peace of the whole world" and "the unity of all," and these prophetic promises of restoration beyond judgment nourish the liturgical hope that God's mercy will ultimately triumph over His judgment.
