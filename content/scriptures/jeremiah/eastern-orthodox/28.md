Jeremiah 28 presents the confrontation between Jeremiah and Hananiah, which Orthodox interpreters read as a paradigmatic illustration of the struggle between authentic and counterfeit spiritual authority. Hananiah possesses all the external markers of a true prophet — he speaks in God's name, operates within the Temple, and performs a symbolic act — yet his message is a lie. The Orthodox tradition's insistence on testing prophetic claims against the *consensus patrum* and the conciliar tradition reflects the hard lesson of this chapter: external credentials alone are insufficient to establish spiritual authenticity.

Jeremiah's restraint in the face of Hananiah's provocation is noteworthy from the perspective of Orthodox ascetical theology. When Hananiah breaks the yoke from his neck, Jeremiah does not retaliate or argue but walks away (28:11), waiting until God gives him a definitive word. This pattern of patient waiting — of refusing to respond to provocation with counter-provocation — reflects the hesychastic ideal of inner stillness (*hesychia*) that allows the monk to hear God's voice amid the noise of false teaching and personal attack. St. Isaac the Syrian taught that silence in the face of provocation is itself a prophetic act.

The escalation from wooden to iron yoke-bars (28:13) carries deep typological significance in Orthodox reading. The attempt to break God's decree through human effort results not in liberation but in a heavier bondage — a pattern that the Fathers see fulfilled in humanity's attempt to overcome sin through willpower alone, which only deepens enslavement. True liberation comes not through breaking the yoke but through the transformative power of divine grace, which the iron yoke foreshadows in its very severity.
