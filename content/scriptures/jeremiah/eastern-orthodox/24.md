The vision of the two baskets of figs reverses expectations — those in exile are the "good figs" while those in Jerusalem are the "bad figs." Orthodox interpreters read this as an instance of the gospel pattern in which the last become first and the mighty are brought low. The kenotic theology of the Orthodox tradition, rooted in Philippians 2:5-11, understands self-emptying and humiliation as the paradoxical path to exaltation. The exiles, stripped of temple, land, and political power, are precisely the ones whom God will restore.

The promise "I will give them a heart to know that I am the Lord" (24:7) is a key text for the Orthodox understanding of grace as the divine initiative in salvation. The Orthodox doctrine of prevenient grace — that God's action always precedes and enables the human response — finds strong support here. The "heart to know God" is not something the exiles generate from within themselves; it is a divine gift that creates the capacity for the relationship it makes possible. This aligns with the Orthodox understanding of faith as itself a grace, not merely a human decision.

The chapter's placement within the narrative of exile and return reflects the paschal pattern — death and resurrection — that the Orthodox tradition sees as the fundamental structure of salvation history. The exiles must pass through the death of all they have known (temple, city, homeland) in order to receive the new life God promises. Orthodox spirituality recognizes this pattern at every level of the spiritual life: the movement through loss, purification, and restoration that characterizes the journey toward *theosis*.
