The remnant's flight to Egypt (43:1-7) is interpreted in the Orthodox tradition as a reversal of the Exodus — the people of God returning to the house of bondage from which they were delivered. The Fathers saw in this movement a profound symbol of spiritual regression: when the soul refuses God's guidance, it inevitably returns to the slavery from which grace had liberated it. The typological reading connects this flight to every instance in which the baptized turn back from the freedom of Christ to the bondage of the passions.

Jeremiah's forced journey to Egypt — the innocent prophet dragged into exile by the disobedient community — is read through the Orthodox theology of co-suffering. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the righteous one who suffers with and for the sinful community participates in the redemptive pattern that finds its fullness in Christ. Jeremiah does not abandon the people even when they reject his counsel; his presence among them in Egypt becomes a continuing offer of divine mercy.

The prophetic sign-act at Tahpanhes (43:8-13) — stones buried before Pharaoh's palace as a foundation for Nebuchadnezzar's throne — demonstrates the Orthodox understanding that prophetic words are not merely predictive but performative. The liturgical tradition of the Orthodox Church, with its emphasis on the power of sacred words and actions to effect spiritual realities, finds an Old Testament precedent in the sign-acts of the prophets. Egypt's false security will be overthrown, confirming that no created power can substitute for the uncreated grace of God.
