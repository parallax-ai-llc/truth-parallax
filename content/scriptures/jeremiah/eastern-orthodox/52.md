Orthodox interpretation of Jeremiah 52 reads the historical narrative of Jerusalem's destruction through the paschal lens of death and resurrection. The meticulous account of the siege, the Temple's demolition, and the exile provides the "Great Friday" of the Old Testament — the moment when everything that sustained the covenant community's life was stripped away. The Orthodox liturgical tradition, which relives the passion narrative during Holy Week with intense dramatic detail, recognizes in Jeremiah 52 a foreshadowing of the desolation that precedes resurrection.

The detailed inventory of the Temple vessels (52:17-23) is read in Orthodox theology as a record of the sacred objects that mediated divine presence within the material cosmos. The Orthodox understanding of matter as capable of bearing divine grace — expressed in the theology of icons, relics, and holy objects — finds an Old Testament precedent in the reverence with which these Temple furnishings are catalogued even in the moment of their loss. The vessels taken to Babylon carry their sacredness with them, just as the divine presence accompanies the faithful into exile.

The release of Jehoiachin from prison after thirty-seven years (52:31-34) is read by the Orthodox Fathers as the paschal conclusion to the book of Jeremiah. The Davidic king, entombed in a Babylonian dungeon, is raised to new life and seated at the table of the king — an image that the Orthodox tradition reads as a type of Christ's resurrection and ascension. The book that began with a call to pluck up and tear down (1:10) ends with a quiet act of restoration, confirming the Orthodox conviction that God's last word is always mercy, not judgment, life, not death.
