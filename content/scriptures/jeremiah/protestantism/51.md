Jeremiah 51 continues and concludes the oracle against Babylon, the longest prophetic utterance against any single nation in Scripture. Protestant interpreters read the chapter as the definitive statement of God's sovereignty over the mightiest of earthly empires. The declaration that "the LORD is a God of recompense; he will surely repay" (51:56) grounds the Reformed understanding of divine justice in the specific historical example of Babylon's fall — what God decrees against the nations, He accomplishes, regardless of their military or economic power.

The chapter contains a remarkable theological poem about God as Creator and the vanity of idols (51:15-19), which functions as the theological foundation for the entire oracle. Protestant commentators note that the authority to judge Babylon rests on God's identity as the Creator of all things — the one who "made the earth by his power" and "established the world by his wisdom" (51:15). This creation theology provides the warrant for divine judgment: the Creator has the right to dispose of His creation according to His purposes.

The dramatic symbolic action that concludes the oracle — Seraiah's casting of the scroll into the Euphrates with the declaration "Thus shall Babylon sink, to rise no more" (51:63-64) — is read by Protestant interpreters as a prophetic sign-act of extraordinary power. The written word, weighted with a stone and cast into the river, enacts the very judgment it describes. Reformed theology sees in this act a demonstration of the performative power of God's word — a word that does not merely describe reality but creates it.
