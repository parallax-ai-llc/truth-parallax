Jeremiah 31 is arguably the single most important chapter in the Old Testament for Protestant theology, containing the foundational prophecy of the new covenant (31:31-34). The promise that God will make "a new covenant, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers" — a covenant in which the law is written on hearts rather than stone tablets, in which all shall know God directly, and in which sins are forgiven and remembered no more — is the text to which the entire New Testament refers when it speaks of the "new covenant" inaugurated by Christ. Protestant theology's central convictions — justification by faith, the priesthood of all believers, the finality of Christ's atoning work — find their prophetic foundation here.

The image of Rachel "weeping for her children" (31:15) is one of the most poignant in all Scripture, and Matthew 2:17-18 applies it to Herod's slaughter of the innocents. Protestant interpreters read this as a typological fulfillment — Rachel's weeping encompasses all the innocent suffering of God's people throughout history, reaching its climax in the events surrounding Christ's birth. The promise that follows — "there is hope for your future" (31:17) — transforms the weeping into anticipation, insisting that God's redemptive purpose will not be defeated by human cruelty.

The chapter's conclusion, with its cosmic imagery of God's covenant faithfulness being as certain as the fixed order of sun, moon, and stars (31:35-37), establishes the unconditional character of the new covenant. Protestant interpreters emphasize that unlike the Sinai covenant, which was conditional upon Israel's obedience and was broken, the new covenant rests entirely on God's initiative and faithfulness. This is the theological basis of the Reformation's doctrine of the perseverance of the saints — those whom God has brought into the new covenant are kept by His power, not their own.
