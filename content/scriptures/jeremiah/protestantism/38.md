Jeremiah 38 narrates the prophet's descent into the cistern — thrown there by officials who consider his message of surrender to be treasonous — and his rescue by the Ethiopian eunuch Ebed-melech. Protestant interpreters read this chapter as one of the most powerful illustrations of the cost and reward of prophetic faithfulness. Jeremiah sinks into the mud of the cistern, an image of death that anticipates the Psalmist's cry "I sink in deep mire, where there is no foothold" (Psalm 69:2), yet God provides deliverance through an unexpected agent.

Ebed-melech's rescue of Jeremiah is one of the most celebrated acts of moral courage in Scripture. A foreigner and a eunuch — doubly marginalized in Israelite society — he risks his position and his life to save the prophet. Protestant interpreters see in Ebed-melech a prefiguration of the gospel's reach beyond ethnic and social boundaries: God's agents of deliverance are not limited to the expected categories. The detail that Ebed-melech provides old rags and worn-out clothes to protect Jeremiah's armpits from the ropes (38:11-12) reveals a tenderness that Protestant devotional literature has long admired — moral courage combined with practical compassion.

Zedekiah's final secret meeting with Jeremiah (38:14-28) is the last direct encounter between prophet and king, and it is heartbreaking in its futility. Jeremiah offers clear terms: surrender and live, resist and die. Zedekiah believes the prophet but fears his own officials more than he fears God. Protestant interpreters read this as the tragic anatomy of a soul destroyed not by ignorance but by cowardice — the king who knows the truth but cannot act on it because he has surrendered his will to human opinion.
