Jeremiah 34 narrates the episode of the broken covenant of liberty — Zedekiah and the people of Jerusalem solemnly freed their Hebrew slaves in accordance with the Torah's sabbatical release law (Deuteronomy 15:12), only to re-enslave them when the Babylonian siege temporarily lifted. Protestant interpreters read this as a devastating illustration of covenant infidelity — the gap between profession and practice, between solemn vows and actual behavior. The Reformation's critique of formalism in religion finds prophetic support here: outward compliance with divine commands that is abandoned when circumstances change reveals a heart that has never truly submitted to God's authority.

The specific sin condemned is the violation of the sabbatical release — the law that Hebrew slaves must be freed after six years of service. Protestant social ethics has drawn on this passage to argue that economic relationships are subject to divine regulation and that the exploitation of vulnerable people is not merely a social problem but a covenant violation. The Puritan tradition in particular cited Jeremiah 34 in its arguments against various forms of economic oppression, insisting that the law of liberty applies to economic as well as spiritual relationships.

God's judgment is devastating in its ironic precision: "You have not obeyed me by proclaiming liberty to your neighbors; behold, I proclaim to you liberty — to the sword, to pestilence, and to famine" (34:17). Protestant interpreters note the theological principle embedded in this irony — those who refuse to extend liberty to others will find themselves subject to the ultimate "liberty" of divine judgment. The wordplay reveals that freedom from moral obligation is not freedom at all but exposure to the consequences that moral law was designed to prevent.
