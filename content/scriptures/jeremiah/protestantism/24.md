Jeremiah 24 presents the vision of two baskets of figs set before the Temple — one basket of very good figs and one of very bad figs that cannot be eaten. The good figs represent the exiles taken to Babylon with Jehoiachin in 597 BCE; the bad figs represent those who remained in Jerusalem under Zedekiah. This vision overturned conventional assumptions: those in exile, seemingly cursed, were actually the objects of divine favor, while those remaining in the holy city were destined for destruction.

Protestant interpreters find in this passage a powerful illustration of the reversal of human expectations. The visible community of faith (those in Jerusalem with access to the Temple) is not necessarily the true people of God. Calvin drew parallels to the Reformation situation: the institutional church in Rome, for all its external splendor, could be the "bad figs," while the scattered and persecuted Protestants could be the "good figs" whom God intended to restore. The passage challenges any equation of institutional presence with divine favor.

The promise attached to the good figs — "I will give them a heart to know that I am the Lord" (24:7) — anticipates the New Covenant promise of 31:33. Protestant interpreters note the divine initiative: it is God who gives the heart to know, not the people who generate this knowledge themselves. This aligns with the Reformed emphasis on regeneration as a sovereign work of God that precedes and enables human faith, providing an Old Testament foundation for the ordo salutis.
