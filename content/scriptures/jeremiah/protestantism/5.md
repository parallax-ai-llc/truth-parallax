Chapter 5 presents Jeremiah's search for a single righteous person in Jerusalem, echoing Abraham's intercession for Sodom in Genesis 18. The failure to find even one who "does justice and seeks truth" (5:1) underscores the totality of Judah's corruption. Protestant interpreters, particularly in the Reformed tradition, cite this passage as evidence of total depravity — that apart from divine grace, human society invariably tends toward comprehensive moral failure.

The people's theological complacency — "He will do nothing; no disaster will come upon us" (5:12) — represents what Protestant theology calls presumptuous grace. The false prophets who proclaim peace are the antithesis of faithful proclamation. Luther drew parallels between these false prophets and the indulgence sellers of his day, who assured people of God's favor without demanding repentance.

The chapter's closing question — "Shall I not punish them for these things? declares the Lord" (5:29) — frames judgment not as divine caprice but as moral necessity. Protestant theology emphasizes that God's justice is not in tension with His love but is an expression of His holiness. The rhetorical structure of the chapter moves from investigation (5:1-6) to indictment (5:7-19) to verdict (5:20-31), mirroring the covenant lawsuit pattern that Protestant covenant theology has extensively analyzed.
