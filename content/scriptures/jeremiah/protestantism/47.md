Jeremiah 47 contains a brief but intense oracle against the Philistines, foretelling their destruction by waters rising from the north (47:2) — a reference to the Babylonian invasion. Protestant interpreters read this oracle within the broader framework of God's sovereignty over the nations. The Philistines, ancient adversaries of Israel, are subject to the same divine judgment that has fallen on Judah and will fall on Egypt, demonstrating that God's justice is universal and impartial.

The imagery of the chapter is dominated by water and the sword. The "waters rising from the north" (47:2) that overwhelm the land like a flood recall the creation narrative's association of chaotic waters with divine judgment, a connection that Reformed commentators trace through the flood of Noah to the eschatological imagery of Revelation. The cry of the inhabitants — "every man without looking back" (47:3) — echoes the panic described in the Egyptian oracle, suggesting a common pattern of divine judgment across national boundaries.

The chapter's closing question — "How can it be quiet when the LORD has given it a charge?" (47:7) — is read by Protestant theologians as a rhetorical affirmation of divine sovereignty over the instruments of judgment. The sword of the Lord cannot rest because God has commissioned it for a purpose. This principle, that divine judgment is purposeful rather than arbitrary, is central to the Protestant understanding of God's governance of history.
