Jeremiah's prophetic ministry begins with a dramatic call narrative in which God declares that He knew and consecrated Jeremiah before his birth (1:5). This verse has become foundational in Protestant theology for discussions of divine foreknowledge, predestination, and the sanctity of life. Calvin saw in this passage evidence of God's sovereign election — that the prophetic office was not Jeremiah's choice but God's eternal decree.

The young prophet's objection — "I am only a youth" — and God's reassuring response ("Do not say, 'I am only a youth'") resonate deeply in the Protestant tradition of the "reluctant prophet." Like Moses before him, Jeremiah's inadequacy becomes the occasion for divine empowerment. Protestant interpreters emphasize that the authority of the prophetic word rests not in the messenger but in the God who sends him.

The two visions — the almond branch (*shaqed*, a wordplay on *shoqed*, "watching") and the boiling pot tilting from the north — establish the dual themes of the entire book: God is watching over His word to perform it, and judgment is coming from the north. Reformed commentators note that these visions ground Jeremiah's authority in direct divine revelation, consistent with the *sola scriptura* principle that God's word authenticates itself.
