Jeremiah 11 returns to the foundational theme of the Sinai covenant with an explicit reference to the curses of Deuteronomy: "Cursed be the man who does not hear the words of this covenant" (11:3). Protestant covenant theology reads this chapter as demonstrating the conditional nature of the Mosaic covenant — blessings for obedience, curses for disobedience — while simultaneously pointing forward to the need for a new and better covenant. The Deuteronomic framework is both affirmed and shown to be insufficient for Israel's salvation.

The revelation of a conspiracy against Jeremiah by the men of his own town, Anathoth (11:18-23), marks the beginning of the prophet's personal sufferings. Protestant interpreters have drawn extensive parallels between Jeremiah and Christ: both were rejected by their own people, both faced plots against their lives, and both entrusted their cause to God. The "lamb led to the slaughter" imagery (11:19) anticipates Isaiah 53 and is fulfilled in Christ.

The divine prohibition against intercession — God commanding Jeremiah not to pray for the people (11:14) — raises difficult theological questions that Protestant interpreters have engaged with seriously. It suggests a point of no return in divine judgment, a theme that the Reformed tradition connects to the doctrine of reprobation. Yet even here, the prohibition itself implies that intercession was Jeremiah's natural impulse, underscoring the tension between divine justice and prophetic compassion.
