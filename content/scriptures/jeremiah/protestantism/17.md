Jeremiah 17 opens with one of the most penetrating diagnoses of the human condition in all Scripture: "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" (17:9). This verse is foundational to the Protestant doctrine of total depravity. Calvin cited it extensively to argue that human nature is so thoroughly corrupted by sin that no one can trust their own moral instincts. The question "who can understand it?" implies that only God possesses the capacity to fully know the depths of human corruption.

The contrasting images of the cursed person who trusts in human strength (17:5-6) and the blessed person who trusts in the Lord (17:7-8) parallel Psalm 1 and establish a fundamental binary in Protestant piety: trust in self versus trust in God. The image of the tree planted by water, whose leaves remain green even in drought, has become a beloved metaphor in Protestant devotional literature for the life sustained by faith.

The sabbath oracle (17:19-27) — promising blessing for sabbath observance and destruction for its violation — has generated varied Protestant responses. Some traditions (particularly Puritan and Reformed) developed robust sabbatarian theology from this passage, reading the sabbath as a creation ordinance with continuing validity. Others, following Lutheran and dispensational readings, see the sabbath command as part of the Mosaic economy fulfilled in Christ, while retaining the principle of regular worship and rest.
