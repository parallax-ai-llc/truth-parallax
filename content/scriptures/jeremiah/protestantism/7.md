The Temple Sermon of Jeremiah 7 is one of the most significant passages in the entire prophetic corpus for Protestant theology. Jeremiah's command to "amend your ways and your deeds" (7:3) and his denunciation of the people's trust in "deceptive words" — "This is the temple of the Lord, the temple of the Lord, the temple of the Lord" (7:4) — became a foundational text for the Reformation critique of institutional religion. The thrice-repeated formula is understood as the epitome of false trust in external religious forms.

Protestant interpreters emphasize the radical nature of Jeremiah's claim: the Temple itself, the very dwelling place of God's name, offers no protection apart from genuine covenant faithfulness. The reference to Shiloh (7:12-14) — where God's tabernacle once stood before its destruction — demonstrates that God is not bound to any physical location. This argument was central to Protestant ecclesiology: no institution, however venerable, is exempt from God's judgment. Calvin applied this directly to Rome, arguing that the papacy's appeal to apostolic succession was analogous to Judah's appeal to the Temple.

The catalogue of sins in 7:5-11 — oppression of the vulnerable, idolatry, theft, murder — reveals that the prophetic critique is fundamentally ethical, not merely theological. The people's use of the Temple as a "den of robbers" (7:11, quoted by Jesus in Mark 11:17) exposes the perversion of worship into a covering for injustice. Protestant social ethics has consistently drawn on this passage to insist that authentic faith necessarily produces justice.
