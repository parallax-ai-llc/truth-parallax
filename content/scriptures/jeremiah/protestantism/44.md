Jeremiah 44 presents the final confrontation between the prophet and the Judean remnant in Egypt, centered on their worship of the "Queen of Heaven" (44:17-19). Protestant interpreters identify this deity with Ishtar or Astarte and see in the community's defiant return to her worship the ultimate expression of covenant unfaithfulness. The Reformers found in this chapter a powerful argument against the veneration of any figure other than God — Luther specifically cited the "Queen of Heaven" passage in his polemic against Marian devotion, though modern Protestant scholarship generally recognizes the distinction between ancient Mesopotamian worship and medieval Christian practice.

The community's remarkable theological argument — that they prospered when they worshipped the Queen of Heaven and suffered only after abandoning her cult (44:17-18) — illustrates what Protestant theologians call the "theology of glory" versus the "theology of the cross." The people interpret prosperity as divine approval and suffering as divine abandonment, exactly inverting the prophetic logic that reads catastrophe as the consequence of idolatry rather than of its cessation.

Jeremiah's response (44:20-30) insists that the calamity came precisely because of their idolatry, not because they stopped. This clash of interpretive frameworks — the prophetic reading versus the popular reading of historical events — remains central to Protestant hermeneutics. The Reformed tradition emphasizes that human reason, corrupted by sin, consistently misreads providence, and only the word of God rightly interpreted provides the lens through which history's meaning can be discerned.
