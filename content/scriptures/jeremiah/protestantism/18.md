Jeremiah 18, the parable of the potter and the clay, is one of the most theologically rich passages in the prophetic literature. God commands Jeremiah to observe a potter at work and draws the analogy: "Like the clay in the potter's hand, so are you in my hand, O house of Israel" (18:6). Protestant interpreters, particularly in the Reformed tradition, find here a foundational text for divine sovereignty. Paul explicitly draws on this imagery in Romans 9:19-24, arguing that the potter has the right to make vessels for honor or dishonor.

Yet the chapter's immediate context is not fatalistic but conditional. God declares that if a nation repents, He will relent of planned disaster; if a nation turns to evil, He will relent of promised good (18:7-10). This conditionality within sovereignty has generated extensive debate in Protestant theology. Arminian interpreters emphasize the passage's apparent affirmation of human responsiveness, while Calvinist interpreters argue that the conditionality operates within God's sovereign decree. The tension between divine sovereignty and human responsibility is nowhere more acute.

The chapter concludes with another plot against Jeremiah (18:18-23) and a prayer of imprecation that Protestant interpreters have found both troubling and instructive. Jeremiah's appeal for vengeance against his enemies stands in tension with the New Testament command to love one's enemies. Protestant commentators generally read these imprecations as expressions of the principle of divine justice entrusted to God rather than taken into human hands — a distinction that Calvin emphasized in his commentary.
