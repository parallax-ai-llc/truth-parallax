Jeremiah 15 contains some of the most intensely personal passages in all prophetic literature. The chapter opens with God's declaration that even Moses and Samuel could not intercede successfully for this people (15:1) — an extraordinary statement that Protestant interpreters read as establishing the absolute sovereignty of divine judgment. If the two greatest intercessors of Israel's history would be insufficient, the situation is truly beyond human remedy.

The prophet's personal lament (15:10-21) reaches a pitch of despair that has resonated with Protestant pastors and theologians across centuries. "Woe is me, my mother, that you bore me, a man of strife and contention to the whole land!" (15:10). Yet within this lament comes one of the most beautiful expressions of the prophet's relationship to God's word: "Your words were found, and I ate them, and your words became to me a joy and the delight of my heart" (15:16). Protestant theology of Scripture draws heavily on this verse — the word of God is simultaneously the source of suffering and the only source of sustenance.

God's response to Jeremiah's complaint contains both rebuke and promise: "If you return, I will restore you, and you shall stand before me. If you utter what is precious, and not what is worthless, you shall be as my mouth" (15:19). Protestant interpreters find here the principle that prophetic authority is contingent on prophetic faithfulness. The prophet is not a passive channel but must continually choose the precious over the worthless — a standard applied in Protestant homiletics to all who preach God's word.
