Jeremiah 29 contains the prophet's letter to the exiles in Babylon, one of the most frequently cited passages in Protestant devotional life. The famous promise — "For I know the plans I have for you, declares the Lord, plans for welfare and not for evil, to give you a future and a hope" (29:11) — has become a touchstone of Protestant piety, though responsible interpreters insist on reading it within its full context. The promise is addressed to a community in exile, not to individuals seeking personal comfort, and it is preceded by the sobering instruction to settle in Babylon for seventy years. Protestant hermeneutics emphasizes that divine promises cannot be detached from their narrative context without distortion.

The instructions to build houses, plant gardens, marry, and seek the welfare of the city (29:5-7) have profoundly shaped Protestant social ethics. The command to "seek the welfare of the city where I have sent you into exile" establishes the principle that God's people are called to be constructive participants in the societies where they find themselves, even when those societies are hostile or pagan. Reformed theology's concept of the "common grace" — God's provision of order, beauty, and justice in societies that do not acknowledge Him — finds prophetic grounding in this passage.

The chapter also addresses the problem of false prophets among the exiles (29:21-32), particularly Shemaiah, who wrote letters from Babylon demanding Jeremiah's arrest. Protestant interpreters note that false prophecy is not confined to the homeland; it travels with the community into exile. The church in every age and place must exercise discernment, and the criteria remain constant: true prophecy is consistent with God's revealed word and is confirmed by fulfillment.
