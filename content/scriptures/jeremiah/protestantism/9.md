Jeremiah 9 opens with one of the most poignant expressions of prophetic grief in all Scripture: "Oh that my head were waters, and my eyes a fountain of tears, that I might weep day and night for the slain of the daughter of my people!" (9:1). This verse gave Jeremiah his traditional title as the "weeping prophet" and has profoundly shaped the Protestant understanding of pastoral compassion. True prophetic ministry, in the Protestant view, combines unflinching truthfulness with genuine sorrow.

The chapter's central theological statement — "Let not the wise man boast in his wisdom, let not the mighty man boast in his might, let not the rich man boast in his riches, but let him who boasts boast in this, that he understands and knows me" (9:23-24) — is quoted by Paul in 1 Corinthians 1:31 and 2 Corinthians 10:17. This passage is foundational to the Protestant doctrine of *sola gratia*. All human achievement — intellectual, military, economic — is relativized before the singular value of knowing God. Calvin made this text central to his theology of the *sensus divinitatis* and the knowledge of God.

The reference to circumcision of the heart (9:25-26) — distinguishing between those circumcised in flesh and those "uncircumcised in heart" — reinforces the consistent Jeremianic theme that external religious identity without internal transformation is worthless. Protestant interpreters see this as anticipating Paul's argument in Romans 2-4 about the insufficiency of works of the law for justification.
