Jeremiah 28 narrates the dramatic confrontation between Jeremiah and the false prophet Hananiah, who breaks the yoke-bars from Jeremiah's neck and declares that within two years God will break Nebuchadnezzar's yoke and restore the Temple vessels and the exiles. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter a definitive case study in discerning true from false prophecy. Hananiah speaks in God's name, uses prophetic formulas, and delivers a message that is theologically attractive — yet he is utterly wrong. The lesson is stark: orthodox-sounding language and institutional credentials do not guarantee authentic divine speech.

Jeremiah's initial response is remarkable — he says "Amen! May the Lord do so!" (28:6), genuinely hoping that Hananiah's optimistic prophecy might be true. But Jeremiah then articulates a crucial hermeneutical principle: "The prophets who preceded you and me from ancient times prophesied war, famine, and pestilence... The prophet who prophesies peace will be recognized as one truly sent by the Lord only if the prophet's word comes to pass" (28:8-9). Protestant theology draws from this the principle that the burden of proof falls on the prophet of peace, not the prophet of judgment — a corrective to the human tendency to prefer comfortable words.

Hananiah's death within the year (28:17) vindicates Jeremiah and illustrates the deadly seriousness of speaking presumptuously in God's name. Protestant interpreters do not read this as a model for how all false prophets die, but rather as a specific divine judgment that confirms the Deuteronomic criterion: false prophecy is not a minor error but a fundamental violation of the prophetic office, and God Himself will ultimately vindicate His true word against every counterfeit.
