Chapter 3 develops the metaphor of marital unfaithfulness with a striking legal argument drawn from Deuteronomy 24:1-4: if a divorced woman remarries, her first husband may not take her back. Yet God, despite Israel's serial apostasy, extends the invitation to return. Protestant interpreters find here a powerful illustration of grace that transcends legal obligation — God's covenant love exceeds even the provisions of His own law.

The contrast between Israel (the northern kingdom, already exiled in 722 BCE) and Judah is significant for Protestant covenantal theology. Judah, having witnessed Israel's judgment, did not repent but only "pretended" to return (3:10). This theme of superficial repentance resonates with the Protestant critique of formalism — external religious performance without internal transformation. Luther's first thesis ("the entire life of the believer should be one of repentance") finds its prophetic antecedent here.

The chapter concludes with a vision of genuine return and restoration (3:14-18), including the remarkable prophecy that the Ark of the Covenant will no longer be remembered or missed. Protestant interpreters, especially those in the Reformed tradition, see this as anticipating the New Covenant's transcendence of old forms — the external symbols giving way to the internal reality of God's presence among His people.
