Jeremiah 22 pronounces judgment on three kings of Judah — Shallum (Jehoahaz), Jehoiakim, and Coniah (Jehoiachin) — providing a prophetic evaluation of political leadership that Protestant interpreters have found paradigmatic. The chapter opens with a conditional promise: if the king executes justice, the Davidic dynasty will endure; if not, "this house shall become a desolation" (22:5). Protestant covenant theology sees here the conditional dimension of the Davidic covenant operating alongside its unconditional promise.

The contrast between Josiah and Jehoiakim (22:13-19) is theologically significant. Josiah "judged the cause of the poor and needy" and "it was well with him" — then Jeremiah adds the striking interpretation: "Is not this to know me? declares the Lord" (22:16). Protestant interpreters have seized on this equation of knowing God with doing justice. Knowledge of God in the prophetic tradition is not abstract theology but concrete ethical practice. This verse has been central to Protestant social ethics and liberation theology alike.

The oracle against Coniah (Jehoiachin) — "Write this man down as childless" (22:30) — raises significant messianic questions. If no descendant of Coniah shall sit on David's throne, how can Jesus, traced through Jehoiachin's line in Matthew 1, be the Messiah? Protestant interpreters have proposed various solutions, including the distinction between Joseph's legal lineage and Mary's biological lineage, or the argument that the curse applied to Coniah's immediate offspring and was lifted by divine grace in subsequent generations.
