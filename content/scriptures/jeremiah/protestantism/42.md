The remnant's request for prophetic guidance (42:1-6) appears sincere — they promise to obey whatever God commands. Yet Protestant interpreters, following Calvin, detect the seeds of disobedience in the very framing of the request: the people approach Jeremiah rather than God directly, and their oath of compliance may already mask a predetermined decision to flee to Egypt. The Reformation principle of total depravity finds illustration in a community that seeks divine counsel while harboring contrary intentions.

After ten days, the word of the Lord comes with unmistakable clarity: remain in the land and God will build and plant the remnant; flee to Egypt and the sword, famine, and pestilence that they fear will follow them there (42:10-17). Protestant commentators emphasize the simplicity and directness of God's command — this is not an ambiguous oracle requiring interpretation but a clear imperative demanding obedience. The Reformed tradition sees here the sufficiency and clarity of Scripture: God's word provides all that is needed for faithful action.

The chapter's closing warning — "you have acted deceitfully against your own lives" (42:20) — reveals that Jeremiah perceives the people's hypocrisy before they act on it. Protestant theology reads this as an instance of prophetic discernment, where the Spirit enables the prophet to perceive the gap between professed faith and actual intention, a gap that characterizes unregenerate religion.
