Jeremiah 10 presents a powerful contrast between the living God and the futility of idols. The satirical description of idol-making — cutting a tree, overlaying it with silver and gold, fastening it with nails so it will not topple (10:3-5) — recalls Isaiah 44 and provides Protestant theology with one of its classic anti-idolatry texts. The Reformers applied this passage not only to literal idol worship but to any religious practice that substitutes created things for the Creator.

The chapter's doxological affirmation — "The Lord is the true God; he is the living God and the everlasting King" (10:10) — establishes the fundamental contrast between the God who acts in history and the gods who are merely human fabrications. Protestant theology's emphasis on the sovereignty and aseity of God finds strong prophetic grounding here. The passage moves from mockery of idols to worship of the true God, modeling the movement from critique to confession that characterizes Reformed preaching.

Jeremiah's prayer at the chapter's close — "Correct me, O Lord, but in justice; not in your anger, lest you bring me to nothing" (10:24) — introduces the pastoral dimension of prophetic theology. Even the prophet who pronounces judgment recognizes his own need for mercy. Protestant interpreters find in this prayer a model of the believer's stance before God: acknowledging the justice of divine correction while appealing to divine restraint. This tension between justice and mercy is at the heart of the Reformation's understanding of justification.
