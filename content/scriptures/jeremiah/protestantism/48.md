Jeremiah 48, the extensive oracle against Moab, is one of the longest prophecies against the nations in the book. Protestant interpreters read it as demonstrating God's sovereign judgment over Israel's eastern neighbor, whose pride is explicitly identified as the root cause of its downfall: "We have heard of the pride of Moab — how very proud he is" (48:29). The Reformed tradition connects this oracle to the biblical pattern of divine opposition to human arrogance, from Babel to Babylon, and sees in Moab's fall a universal warning against self-exaltation.

The remarkable expression of divine grief over Moab's destruction — "Therefore I wail for Moab; I cry out for all Moab" (48:31) — challenges simplistic readings of prophetic judgment. Protestant commentators note that God's lament over a pagan nation reveals the depth of the divine compassion that extends beyond the covenant people. Calvin interpreted this passage as demonstrating that God takes no pleasure in the destruction of any creature, a principle that Paul articulates in Romans 9:22 and that informs the Reformed understanding of divine judgment as a last resort rather than a first impulse.

The closing promise — "Yet I will restore the fortunes of Moab in the latter days" (48:47) — introduces an eschatological dimension that Protestant interpreters have debated extensively. Some read it as a historical prediction of Moab's partial recovery; others, particularly in the dispensationalist tradition, see it as pointing to Moab's inclusion in the millennial kingdom. The Reformed tradition generally reads such promises as anticipating the ingathering of the nations through the gospel, fulfilling the Abrahamic promise that all families of the earth will be blessed.
