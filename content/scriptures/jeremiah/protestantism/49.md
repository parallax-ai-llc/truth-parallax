Jeremiah 49 contains oracles against five nations — Ammon (49:1-6), Edom (49:7-22), Damascus (49:23-27), Kedar and Hazor (49:28-33), and Elam (49:34-39). Protestant interpreters read this collection as demonstrating the comprehensive scope of divine judgment: no nation, whether kindred to Israel (Ammon, Edom) or distant (Elam), stands outside the moral governance of God. The Reformed doctrine of God's universal sovereignty finds textual support in the breadth of these oracles.

The oracle against Edom (49:7-22) is particularly significant in Protestant interpretation because of Edom's identification with Esau and the theological tradition of divine election (Romans 9:13). The wisdom for which Edom was famous — "Is wisdom no more in Teman?" (49:7) — proves insufficient before divine judgment, illustrating the Protestant conviction that human wisdom, however sophisticated, cannot save apart from the grace of God. The imagery of Edom's desolation becoming like Sodom and Gomorrah (49:18) connects this oracle to the broader biblical narrative of judgment on the proud.

The concluding oracle against Elam (49:34-39), with its promise of future restoration, mirrors the pattern seen in the Moab oracle — judgment followed by the possibility of mercy. Protestant eschatology reads these restoration promises as foreshadowing the universal scope of the gospel, which extends God's saving purpose to all nations, fulfilling the prophetic vision of a world in which every people acknowledges the sovereignty of Israel's God.
