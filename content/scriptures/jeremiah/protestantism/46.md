Jeremiah 46 opens the collection of oracles against the nations (chapters 46-51) with a prophecy against Egypt, structured around the Battle of Carchemish in 605 BC (46:1-12) and a subsequent oracle about Nebuchadnezzar's invasion of Egypt itself (46:13-26). Protestant interpreters read these oracles as demonstrating God's sovereignty over all nations, not merely Israel — a principle foundational to the Reformed doctrine of divine providence. Calvin emphasized that these prophecies reveal God as the Lord of universal history, not merely the tribal deity of Israel.

The vivid poetic description of Egypt's military might and its subsequent humiliation (46:3-12) illustrates the prophetic theme of human pride brought low by divine judgment. The imagery of warriors fleeing without looking back, stumbling over one another in panic, powerfully conveys the collapse of imperial confidence. Protestant commentators connect this oracle to the biblical pattern of pride preceding destruction (Proverbs 16:18), applicable not only to Egypt but to every human power that exalts itself against God.

The chapter concludes with a word of comfort to Israel (46:27-28): "Do not fear, O Jacob my servant... I will discipline you in just measure, and I will by no means leave you unpunished." Protestant theology finds in this juxtaposition of judgment on the nations and chastening of Israel a distinction between punitive and corrective discipline. The nations face destruction; Israel faces correction. This distinction grounds the Reformed understanding of perseverance — God's covenant people may suffer, but they will not be destroyed.
