## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Jeremiah is shaped by the Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* and the conviction that the prophetic word stands in judgment over all human institutions — including the institutional church itself. The Reformers saw in Jeremiah a kindred spirit: a solitary voice confronting corrupt religious leadership, false prophets, and a people trusting in external forms of religion rather than genuine faith.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Covenant Theology
Reformed Protestants read Jeremiah within the framework of covenant theology. The New Covenant passage (31:31-34) is regarded as the climax of the book and a foundational text for understanding the relationship between the Old and New Testaments. The promise that God will "write the law on their hearts" is seen as fulfilled in Christ and the ministry of the Holy Spirit.

### Prophetic Preaching
Many Protestant interpreters emphasize Jeremiah as a model for prophetic preaching — the faithful proclamation of God's word regardless of consequences. Jeremiah's willingness to stand against kings, priests, and popular opinion resonates deeply with Protestant ecclesiology.

### Dispensationalism vs. Covenant Theology
Protestant interpreters diverge on whether the New Covenant promises apply primarily to the church (covenant theology) or to a future restored Israel (dispensationalism). This hermeneutical divide shapes the reading of chapters 30-33 significantly.

## Theological Emphases

- **True vs. False Religion**: Jeremiah's Temple Sermon (ch. 7) as a critique of empty ritualism
- **Individual Accountability**: The "sour grapes" passage (31:29-30) and personal faith
- **Justification and the New Covenant**: The internalization of the law as a precursor to justification by faith
- **Suffering for the Word**: Jeremiah's persecutions as paradigmatic for faithful ministry
