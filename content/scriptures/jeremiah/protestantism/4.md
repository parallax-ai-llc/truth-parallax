Jeremiah 4 unleashes the terrifying vision of invasion from the north — a poetic description of military catastrophe so vivid that scholars debate whether Jeremiah envisions a specific enemy (the Scythians, the Babylonians) or a cosmic undoing of creation itself. The language of 4:23-26 ("I looked at the earth, and it was without form and void") deliberately echoes Genesis 1:2, suggesting that judgment reverses creation. Protestant interpreters find in this passage a theology of divine judgment as de-creation.

The chapter's call to genuine repentance — "Circumcise yourselves to the Lord; remove the foreskin of your hearts" (4:4) — is a key text for the Protestant emphasis on interior transformation over external ceremony. Paul's argument in Romans 2:28-29 about circumcision of the heart draws directly on this Jeremianic tradition. For Reformed theologians, this verse demonstrates that even under the Old Covenant, God demanded the internal reality that external rites could only symbolize.

The prophet's personal anguish — "My anguish, my anguish! I writhe in pain!" (4:19) — presents the costly dimension of prophetic ministry that Protestant traditions have long honored. The true preacher of God's word does not deliver judgment with detachment but with a broken heart. This pattern of compassionate proclamation became a model for Protestant pastoral theology from the Puritans onward.
