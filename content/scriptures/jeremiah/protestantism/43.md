The remnant's rejection of Jeremiah's counsel and their flight to Egypt (43:1-7) represent the final act of national disobedience in the book. Protestant interpreters view this as the culmination of the hardened heart that Jeremiah diagnosed throughout his ministry. The accusation that Baruch has incited Jeremiah against them (43:3) reveals the human tendency to blame the messenger rather than heed the message — a pattern that the Reformers recognized in the institutional Church's resistance to prophetic correction.

The forced removal of Jeremiah to Egypt is deeply ironic: the prophet who warned against seeking refuge in Egypt is himself dragged there against his will. Reformed commentators see in this a type of the suffering servant whose identification with the people extends even to sharing their disobedience's consequences, though he did not participate in the sin. Jeremiah's solidarity with the remnant in exile prefigures Christ's solidarity with sinners.

Jeremiah's symbolic act of burying stones at the entrance of Pharaoh's palace in Tahpanhes (43:8-13), prophesying that Nebuchadnezzar will set his throne on that very spot, demonstrates that flight to Egypt provides no escape from Babylonian power. Protestant theology reads this as a powerful lesson in the futility of seeking worldly security apart from God — the arm of flesh will fail, and the nations in which the faithless trust will themselves become instruments of divine judgment.
