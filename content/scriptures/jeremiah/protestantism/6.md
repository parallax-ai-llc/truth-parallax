Jeremiah 6 brings the cycle of warnings about the northern invasion to its climax. The command to "flee from Jerusalem" (6:1) makes the threat concrete and urgent. Protestant interpreters note the imagery of the refiner's fire (6:27-30) — Jeremiah is appointed as a tester of metals, but the refining process fails because the people are "rejected silver." This passage informs the Protestant understanding of judgment as a revelatory process that exposes what is truly present in the human heart.

The verse "Stand at the crossroads and look; ask for the ancient paths, where the good way is, and walk in it" (6:16) has become one of the most quoted texts in Protestant preaching. Reformation traditions read this as an endorsement of return to scriptural foundations — the "ancient paths" being not human tradition but God's revealed word. The people's refusal ("We will not walk in it") embodies the tragedy of hearing God's word without obeying it, a theme central to Protestant homiletical theology.

The chapter's indictment of prophets and priests — "They have healed the wound of my people lightly, saying, 'Peace, peace,' when there is no peace" (6:14) — became a rallying text for the Protestant Reformation. Luther explicitly cited this verse against those who offered cheap assurance without true repentance. The false peace of institutional religion, for Protestant interpreters, is more dangerous than open hostility to God because it inoculates people against the genuine gospel.
