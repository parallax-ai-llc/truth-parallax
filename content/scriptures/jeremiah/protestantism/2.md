Chapter 2 presents God's covenant lawsuit (*rib*) against Israel, employing the powerful metaphor of a bride who has abandoned her husband. Protestant interpreters have long seen in this chapter a template for prophetic preaching — the exposition of sin as the necessary precursor to the proclamation of grace. The movement from honeymoon faithfulness (2:2-3) to wholesale apostasy (2:5-13) illustrates the Reformation understanding of total depravity.

The central image of the chapter — "My people have committed two evils: they have forsaken me, the fountain of living waters, and hewn out cisterns for themselves, broken cisterns that can hold no water" (2:13) — is one of the most frequently cited verses in Protestant homiletics. It captures the essential Protestant critique of human religion: the abandonment of the living God for self-made substitutes. Calvin used this verse to critique the Roman Catholic sacramental system; later Protestants applied it to any form of religious self-reliance.

Jeremiah's catalogue of Israel's sins — political alliances with Egypt and Assyria, Baal worship, social injustice — illustrates what Protestant theology calls the comprehensiveness of sin. The chapter's rhetorical questions ("Has a nation changed its gods, even though they are no gods?") emphasize the irrational and inexcusable nature of apostasy, reinforcing the Reformed doctrine that sin is not merely ignorance but willful rebellion against known truth.
