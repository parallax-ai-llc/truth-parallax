Jeremiah 20 records the prophet's arrest and public humiliation by Pashhur son of Immer, the chief officer of the Temple. Jeremiah's response — renaming Pashhur as "Terror on Every Side" (*Magor-Missabib*) — is both a personal rebuke and a prophetic oracle against all who oppose God's word. Protestant interpreters see in this confrontation the paradigmatic conflict between institutional religious authority and prophetic truth, a pattern that repeats throughout church history.

The chapter's most theologically significant passage is Jeremiah's lament in 20:7-18, which reaches depths of despair unmatched in the prophetic literature. "O Lord, you have deceived me, and I was deceived" (20:7) — the Hebrew *patah* suggests seduction or overpowering. Protestant interpreters have wrestled with this verse's implications: does the prophet accuse God of deception? Calvin understood it as an expression of the prophet's subjective experience, not an objective theological claim. The passage validates honest spiritual struggle within the life of faith.

The conclusion — "Cursed be the day on which I was born!" (20:14) — echoes Job 3 and represents the nadir of Jeremiah's personal experience. Yet Protestant interpreters note that this is not the book's last word. The lament's placement within the larger narrative of Jeremiah's continuing ministry suggests that despair, however real, does not have the final say. The prophet who curses his birth continues to speak God's word — a pattern that Protestant pastors facing burnout and despair have found both honest and sustaining.
