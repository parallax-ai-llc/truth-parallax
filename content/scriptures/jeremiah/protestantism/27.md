Jeremiah 27 introduces the dramatic sign-act of the yoke, in which God commands Jeremiah to fashion yoke-bars and wear them on his neck as a visual prophecy declaring that all nations must submit to Nebuchadnezzar, whom God calls "my servant" (27:6). Protestant interpreters find in this chapter a profound statement about divine sovereignty over all nations and rulers — even pagan kings serve God's purposes, whether they acknowledge Him or not. The Reformed tradition in particular draws on this passage to develop the doctrine of God's providential governance of human history, including His use of wicked rulers as instruments of judgment.

The chapter's warning against false prophets who promise deliverance (27:9-10, 14-18) resonates deeply with Protestant concerns about distinguishing true from false teaching. Jeremiah identifies the marks of false prophecy: it tells people what they want to hear, it contradicts the clear word of God, and it promises peace where there is no peace. The Protestant principle of *sola Scriptura* — testing all claims against the written word — finds its prophetic antecedent in Jeremiah's insistence that the true word of God may be deeply unwelcome and still be authoritative.

Jeremiah's instruction to the foreign ambassadors (27:3-11) demonstrates that God's sovereignty extends beyond the covenant community. Protestant missiology has drawn on passages like this to affirm that God's purposes encompass all peoples, and that submission to His sovereign will is required of every nation. The chapter's geopolitical scope — addressing Edom, Moab, Ammon, Tyre, and Sidon alongside Judah — prefigures the universal horizon of the gospel proclamation.
