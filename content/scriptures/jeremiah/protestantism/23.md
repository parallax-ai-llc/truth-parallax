Jeremiah 23 addresses two central themes — the promise of a righteous Davidic king and the exposure of false prophets — that are foundational to Protestant messianic theology and ecclesiology. The prophecy of the "righteous Branch" who will "execute justice and righteousness in the land" (23:5) and be called "The Lord is our Righteousness" (*Yahweh Tsidqenu*, 23:6) is one of the clearest messianic prophecies in the Old Testament. Protestant interpreters unanimously see its fulfillment in Jesus Christ, whose imputed righteousness is the basis of justification.

The extended critique of false prophets (23:9-40) provides Protestant theology with essential criteria for discerning true from false prophecy. The false prophets "strengthen the hands of evildoers" (23:14), prophesy from their own imagination (23:16), and steal God's words from one another (23:30). Against this, Jeremiah defines the true prophet as one who has "stood in the council of the Lord" (23:18) and whose message produces repentance, not complacency. This passage shaped the Reformation's insistence on prophetic preaching that confronts sin.

The chapter's rhetorical climax — "Is not my word like fire, declares the Lord, and like a hammer that breaks the rock in pieces?" (23:29) — became a touchstone for Protestant theology of preaching. The word of God is not merely informational but transformative, possessing inherent divine power. Luther's entire theology of the *viva vox evangelii* (living voice of the gospel) is grounded in this conviction that God's word accomplishes what it declares, breaking through human resistance and creating faith.
