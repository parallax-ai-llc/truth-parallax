Jeremiah 13 centers on the symbolic action of the linen belt (*ezor*), which Jeremiah is commanded to wear, then hide in a rock by the Euphrates, and finally retrieve in its ruined state. Protestant interpreters emphasize the didactic function of prophetic sign-acts: the belt represents Judah, once intimately bound to God ("as the belt clings to the waist of a man, so I made the whole house of Israel and the whole house of Judah cling to me," 13:11), now ruined by pride and disobedience.

The geographical detail of the Euphrates is significant — it points toward Babylon, the instrument of divine judgment. Some Protestant scholars argue that the Hebrew *Perath* may refer to a local site near Anathoth (Parah), but the majority reading sees a deliberate symbolic connection to the Mesopotamian river and the coming exile. The ruined belt thus becomes a visual prophecy of the exile, communicating through action what words alone could not convey.

The chapter's concluding oracle — "Can the Ethiopian change his skin or the leopard its spots? Then also you can do good who are accustomed to do evil" (13:23) — has been a key text in Protestant discussions of human depravity and the necessity of divine grace. The passage suggests that sin has become so ingrained in Judah's character that self-reformation is impossible, pointing to the need for the radical transformation promised in the New Covenant (31:31-34). Reformed theology cites this verse alongside Ephesians 2:1-5 to establish the doctrine of total inability.
