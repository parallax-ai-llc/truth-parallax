Jeremiah 45, the shortest chapter in the book, contains God's personal message to Baruch son of Neriah, Jeremiah's faithful scribe. Protestant interpreters read this brief oracle as a profound meditation on the cost of faithful service. Baruch's lament — "Woe is me! The LORD has added sorrow to my pain" (45:3) — expresses the exhaustion and discouragement that accompany prolonged obedience in a hostile environment. Reformed pastoral theology finds in Baruch's experience a pattern that recurs in every generation of faithful ministry.

God's response is both compassionate and sobering: "Should you then seek great things for yourself? Do not seek them" (45:5). Protestant interpreters from Calvin onward have read this as a definitive statement about the proper orientation of the servant of God. The command to relinquish personal ambition does not negate the value of Baruch's service but reframes it within God's larger purposes. The promise that Baruch will retain his life "as a prize of war" wherever he goes offers survival, not success — a distinction that Protestant theology considers essential to authentic discipleship.

The chapter's placement — between the narrative of the Egyptian exile and the oracles against the nations — is understood by Protestant commentators as deliberate. Baruch's personal story is set within the context of cosmic judgment, suggesting that individual faithfulness matters even when the larger structures of the world are collapsing. This juxtaposition speaks to the Protestant conviction that the individual before God, not the institution, is the fundamental unit of spiritual life.
