Jeremiah 36 narrates the writing, reading, and burning of Jeremiah's scroll — one of the most important passages in the Bible for Protestant theology of Scripture. At God's command, Jeremiah dictates his prophecies to his scribe Baruch, who reads them publicly in the Temple. When King Jehoiakim hears the scroll, he cuts it apart and burns it in a brazier — a dramatic act of royal contempt for the prophetic word. God then commands Jeremiah to write a second scroll, "and many similar words were added" (36:32). Protestant interpreters read this narrative as a foundational statement about the indestructibility of God's word: human rulers may burn, ban, or suppress Scripture, but God's word endures and increases.

The chapter's account of the scroll's production — dictation by the prophet, transcription by the scribe, public reading, and rewriting — provides crucial evidence for the Protestant understanding of biblical inspiration. The process involves genuine human agency (Baruch's scribal work, the "many similar words" added to the second scroll) without compromising divine authority. Protestant doctrines of inspiration recognize this partnership between divine initiative and human instrumentality, affirming that Scripture is simultaneously the word of God and the words of human authors.

Jehoiakim's contemptuous burning of the scroll stands in stark contrast to his father Josiah's response to the discovery of the Book of the Law (2 Kings 22), when Josiah tore his garments in repentance. Protestant interpreters draw from this contrast the principle that the word of God always provokes a response — either humble submission or defiant rejection — but never indifference. The Reformation's emphasis on the preached word as a means of grace that demands decision finds its prophetic antecedent here.
