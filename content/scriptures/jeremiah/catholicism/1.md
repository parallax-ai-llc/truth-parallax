Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah's call narrative (1:4-19) emphasizes the doctrine of divine vocation and its sacramental implications. God's declaration that He knew Jeremiah before he was formed in the womb (1:5) is a key text in Catholic teaching on the sanctity of human life from conception, cited in *Evangelium Vitae* (1995) and *Donum Vitae* (1987). The passage establishes that God's purposes for a person precede their existence, grounding the Catholic understanding of vocation as a divine initiative rather than a human choice.

Origen, in his *Homilies on Jeremiah*, read the prophet's call typologically: Jeremiah prefigures Christ, who was likewise consecrated before birth and sent to a people who would reject him. This Christological reading became standard in the Catholic interpretive tradition. The young prophet's protest of inadequacy and God's response — "I am with you to deliver you" — parallel the pattern of apostolic commissioning in the New Testament and inform the Catholic theology of ordained ministry.

The two visions — the almond branch and the boiling pot — are understood within the Catholic tradition as signs confirming the prophetic charism. The Church's teaching on private revelation and prophetic gifts, articulated in documents such as the *Catechism of the Catholic Church* (nos. 66-67), affirms that God continues to communicate through signs and visions, though public revelation closed with the apostolic age. Jeremiah's inaugural visions thus serve as the paradigmatic instance of prophetic confirmation in the Old Testament.
