Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 44 focuses on the theological confrontation between Jeremiah and the Egyptian community over the worship of the "Queen of Heaven" (44:17-19). Catholic scholars are careful to distinguish between this ancient Near Eastern fertility cult and the Catholic doctrine of Mary's queenship, which is grounded in Christological and ecclesiological principles rather than in pagan precedent. The Pontifical Biblical Commission's documents emphasize the importance of reading Old Testament passages within their original historical context before drawing theological conclusions.

The community's argument that prosperity attended their worship of the Queen of Heaven (44:17-18) represents what Catholic moral theology identifies as the error of consequentialism — judging the morality of an action solely by its perceived outcomes. The Church's consistent teaching, articulated in *Veritatis Splendor* (1993), holds that certain acts are intrinsically disordered regardless of their consequences. Jeremiah's counter-argument that the destruction came because of idolatry, not because it ceased, reasserts the primacy of objective moral truth over subjective interpretation of experience.

The chapter's final oracle against the remnant in Egypt (44:24-30) represents the definitive prophetic judgment on those who chose human wisdom over divine guidance. Catholic ecclesiology reads this as confirming the necessity of authoritative teaching — the community that interprets its own experience apart from prophetic (and later, magisterial) guidance inevitably falls into error. The promise that Pharaoh Hophra will be delivered to his enemies (44:30) serves as a sign that validates the prophetic word against popular opinion.
