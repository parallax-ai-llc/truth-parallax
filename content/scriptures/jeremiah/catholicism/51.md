Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 51 reads the culmination of the Babylon oracle through the lens of the Church's teaching on divine justice and the ultimate accountability of all temporal powers. The chapter's sweeping vision of Babylon's destruction — encompassing military defeat, cultural dissolution, and ecological devastation — is understood by the Fathers as anticipating the final judgment, when all earthly systems that have opposed God's purposes will be definitively overthrown. St. Augustine's *De Civitate Dei* draws on this prophetic tradition to contrast the City of God with the City of Man.

The creation hymn embedded in the oracle (51:15-19) is read in Catholic theology as establishing the metaphysical foundation for divine judgment. The God who created all things has the authority to judge all things — a principle that Catholic natural law theory extends to the moral governance of all human communities. The Thomistic understanding of divine providence, which holds that God governs all creatures according to their nature, finds support in this passage's integration of creation theology with the theology of judgment.

The symbolic sinking of the scroll in the Euphrates (51:63-64) is interpreted in Catholic sacramental theology as a prophetic sign-act that prefigures the efficacy of sacramental signs. Just as the sinking scroll effects what it signifies — Babylon's irrevocable fall — so the sacraments of the Church effect what they signify through the power of the divine word they embody. The Catholic tradition reads this act as evidence that God communicates through material signs invested with spiritual power, a principle foundational to the entire sacramental system.
