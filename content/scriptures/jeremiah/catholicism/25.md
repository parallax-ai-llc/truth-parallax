Jeremiah 25 introduces two powerful images — the seventy years of Babylonian captivity (25:11-12) and the cup of divine wrath distributed among the nations (25:15-29) — that have profoundly shaped Catholic eschatology and liturgical theology. The seventy-year prophecy demonstrates God's sovereignty over historical timelines, a conviction that Catholic theology affirms while resisting the temptation to calculate the date of Christ's return. The Catechism teaches that the Church lives in an eschatological tension between the "already" and the "not yet," a pattern first established in Jeremiah's temporal prophecies.

The cup of wrath imagery carries enormous theological weight in Catholic interpretation. When Jesus in Gethsemane prays "let this cup pass from me" (Matthew 26:39), he is taking upon himself the cup that Jeremiah described — the cup of divine judgment against all nations. Catholic atonement theology understands Christ's Passion as the drinking of this cup on behalf of humanity, and the Eucharistic cup as the transformation of the cup of wrath into the cup of salvation. The words of institution — "This is the cup of my blood, the blood of the new and eternal covenant" — directly link the Eucharist to Jeremiah's prophetic imagery.

The universal scope of judgment — encompassing all the nations of the earth, from Jerusalem to "all the kingdoms of the world" (25:26) — establishes the principle of God's universal sovereignty that Catholic theology affirms in its teaching on divine providence and the last judgment. The Nicene Creed's affirmation that Christ "will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead" echoes the Jeremianic vision of a God who holds all nations accountable. No human power is exempt from the divine tribunal.
