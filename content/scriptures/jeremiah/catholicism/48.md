Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 48 reads the oracle against Moab within the framework of God's universal justice articulated in Catholic social teaching. Moab's pride (48:29) — specifically its self-sufficiency and complacency ("Moab has been at ease from his youth," 48:11) — represents the spiritual condition that Catholic moral theology identifies as *acedia* combined with *superbia*: a lazy self-satisfaction that refuses the demands of the divine call. The metaphor of wine left undisturbed on its sediment (48:11) provides a vivid image of the spiritual stagnation that results from unchallenged comfort.

The divine lament over Moab's destruction (48:31-36) is read by the Church Fathers as revealing God's universal compassion. Origen argued that God's grief over Moab demonstrates that divine love extends to all peoples, not merely to Israel — a principle that the Second Vatican Council affirmed in *Nostra Aetate* and *Lumen Gentium*. The Catholic tradition holds that God's salvific will is universal (1 Timothy 2:4), and the prophetic lament over Moab provides Old Testament evidence for this teaching.

The promise of restoration "in the latter days" (48:47) is read in Catholic eschatology as part of the prophetic vision of universal redemption that finds its fulfillment in Christ. The Catholic understanding of *extra Ecclesiam nulla salus* has been significantly developed since Vatican II to encompass the possibility of salvation for those outside the visible boundaries of the Church, and the restoration promises embedded within the oracles against the nations provide scriptural support for this more expansive reading.
