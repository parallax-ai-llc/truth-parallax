Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 3 emphasizes the theme of divine mercy extended to the unfaithful spouse — a grace that exceeds the provisions of the Deuteronomic law (Deuteronomy 24:1-4). The Church Fathers, particularly St. Jerome, saw in this passage a prefiguration of the Sacrament of Reconciliation: God's willingness to receive back the adulterous partner demonstrates that no sin is beyond the reach of divine forgiveness when met with genuine repentance. The sacramental practice of confession finds its prophetic warrant here.

The distinction between Israel's open apostasy and Judah's "pretend" return (3:10) has been applied in Catholic moral theology to the difference between material and formal sin, and between genuine and superficial conversion. Pope Francis's emphasis on authentic encounter with Christ over mere observance echoes Jeremiah's critique of false repentance. The Catholic tradition insists that sacramental participation requires interior disposition — the outer form without inner conversion is precisely the kind of pretense Jeremiah condemns.

The eschatological vision of 3:14-18 — when the Ark of the Covenant will no longer be missed because God's presence will be universal — has been read by Catholic interpreters as pointing toward the Eucharistic presence of Christ. The Ark, which contained the manna, the tablets of the law, and Aaron's rod, is typologically fulfilled in the Eucharist and in Mary (the "Ark of the New Covenant" in Catholic Marian theology). Jeremiah's prophecy that the old Ark will pass away anticipates the greater reality to which it pointed.
