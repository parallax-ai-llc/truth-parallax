Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 10 reads the polemic against idols in light of the Church's nuanced theology of images and icons. While Jeremiah's ridicule of carved idols (10:3-5) — overlaid with gold and silver, fastened with nails, unable to speak or walk — clearly condemns the worship of false gods, Catholic theology distinguishes between idolatry (worship of creatures) and the veneration of sacred images (which honor the prototype they represent). The Second Council of Nicaea (787) drew this distinction precisely, and Catholic interpreters apply it when reading prophetic anti-idol polemic.

The doxological confession — "The Lord is the true God; he is the living God and the everlasting King" (10:10) — articulates what Catholic theology calls the divine attributes: truth, life, eternity, and sovereignty. The Catechism's treatment of God's attributes (CCC 198-231) draws on such prophetic confessions alongside philosophical theology. Jeremiah's language is not abstract metaphysics but doxological proclamation — theology as worship — which Catholic theology at its best has always sought to maintain.

Jeremiah's closing prayer — "Correct me, O Lord, but in justice; not in your anger" (10:24) — resonates with the Catholic understanding of divine discipline as medicinal rather than merely punitive. The theology of purgatory, the sacrament of reconciliation, and the penitential practices of the Church all reflect this conviction that God's correction aims at restoration, not destruction. The prayer models the Catholic disposition of humble submission to divine correction while trusting in divine mercy — the disposition that the liturgy cultivates through the Penitential Act at the beginning of every Mass.
