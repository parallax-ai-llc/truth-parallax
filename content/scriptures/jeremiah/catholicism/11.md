Jeremiah 11 returns to the covenant curses of Deuteronomy, and Catholic interpreters read this chapter within the context of salvation history's progressive unfolding. The broken covenant of Sinai (11:1-13) demonstrates the inadequacy of external legislation alone to secure obedience — a theme that Catholic theology develops in its understanding of the relationship between law and grace. The Council of Trent affirmed that the law reveals sin but cannot of itself confer the grace needed to fulfill it, a reality that Jeremiah 11 illustrates dramatically.

The plot against Jeremiah by the men of Anathoth (11:18-23) introduces the theme of the suffering prophet that Catholic Christological typology reads as prefiguring Christ's Passion. The image of the prophet as "a gentle lamb led to the slaughter" (11:19) directly anticipates Isaiah 53 and the Johannine identification of Christ as the Lamb of God (John 1:29). Catholic liturgy draws on this connection during Holy Week, when the readings trace the line from the suffering prophets through Christ to the paschal mystery.

God's command that Jeremiah cease interceding for the people (11:14) raises the question of the limits of intercessory prayer that Catholic theology has engaged with carefully. While the Catholic tradition strongly affirms the efficacy of intercessory prayer — through the saints, the Blessed Virgin, and the faithful on earth — Jeremiah 11 suggests that there are situations in which God's judgment is irrevocable within history, even as His mercy remains available to individuals who repent. This tension informs the Church's teaching on the relationship between divine justice and divine mercy.
