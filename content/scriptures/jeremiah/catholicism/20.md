Jeremiah 20 records the prophet's arrest by Pashhur, the chief Temple officer, and the devastating lament that follows. Catholic interpreters read this chapter through the lens of the Passion: Jeremiah, like Christ, is arrested by the religious establishment, publicly humiliated, and left to suffer. The typological connection is strengthened by the language of 20:7 — "O Lord, you have deceived me" — which Catholic exegetes translate more carefully as "you have persuaded me" or "you have been too strong for me," seeing in the prophet's protest not blasphemy but the honest cry of one overwhelmed by the demands of divine vocation.

The tension between compulsion and despair in 20:8-9 — "If I say, 'I will not mention him,' then within me there is something like a burning fire shut up in my bones" — has been a key text in Catholic theology of vocation. The prophet cannot suppress the word of God any more than he can escape his own nature. Catholic theology of ordained ministry and religious life draws on this passage to describe the irrevocable character of certain vocations: the charism, once received, cannot be set aside without violence to the soul.

The concluding lament — Jeremiah's cursing of the day of his birth (20:14-18) — is the most extreme expression of prophetic suffering in the Old Testament. Catholic spiritual theology, informed by the writings of St. John of the Cross on the "dark night of the soul," recognizes this as a genuine experience in the life of faith, not a failure of faith. The mystics understood that the deepest communion with God sometimes passes through an abyss of desolation, and Jeremiah's cry provides biblical warrant for this insight.
