Jeremiah 27 presents the sign-act of the yoke, in which God commands Jeremiah to wear yoke-bars on his neck and send them to neighboring kings, declaring that all nations must submit to Nebuchadnezzar, God's "servant" (27:6). Catholic social teaching finds in this chapter an important principle: temporal authority, even when exercised by pagan rulers, falls within God's providential ordering of history. The Catechism's teaching on the legitimacy of political authority (CCC 1897-1904) echoes Jeremiah's recognition that God establishes rulers for His purposes, while also insisting that all authority is ultimately accountable to divine law.

The chapter's sustained polemic against false prophets (27:9-10, 14-18) who promise deliverance without repentance addresses a perennial temptation within religious communities. Catholic magisterial teaching serves precisely the function that Jeremiah exercises here — distinguishing authentic prophetic speech from wishful thinking and self-serving prediction. The prophets, diviners, dreamers, and soothsayers whom Jeremiah condemns represent the full spectrum of unauthorized spiritual authority that the Church's teaching office is designed to evaluate and, when necessary, correct.

Jeremiah's diplomatic mission to the foreign ambassadors (27:3-11) reveals the universal scope of God's sovereignty — a theme central to Catholic ecclesiology's understanding of the Church as a sacrament of unity for all peoples. The prophet's willingness to deliver an unpopular message to both domestic and foreign audiences models the Church's prophetic vocation to speak truth to power regardless of political consequences, a principle affirmed in documents from *Rerum Novarum* to *Laudato Si'*.
