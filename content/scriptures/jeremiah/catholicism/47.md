Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 47, the oracle against the Philistines, situates the prophecy within the broader pattern of divine judgment on the nations that the prophetic books consistently present. The imagery of overwhelming waters from the north (47:2) evokes the biblical tradition of the flood as divine judgment, a connection that the Fathers of the Church developed extensively. St. Augustine read the judgment of the nations as demonstrating God's universal justice — no people, however powerful, stands outside the moral order that God has established.

The anguished imagery of the chapter — fathers not turning back for children, the sword of the Lord devouring without rest — raises the question of theodicy that Catholic theology addresses through the doctrine of divine justice tempered by mercy. The Catechism teaches that God's judgments, while severe, are always proportionate and ultimately ordered toward the restoration of justice. The Philistines' destruction, like the fall of other nations, serves the larger purpose of clearing the historical stage for God's redemptive work.

The personification of God's sword (47:6-7) and the rhetorical question "How can it rest when the LORD has given it a charge?" provide a striking image of divine purposefulness in judgment. Catholic theology interprets this not as divine cruelty but as the necessary consequence of a moral universe — God's justice cannot remain inactive in the face of persistent evil. This understanding informs the Church's teaching on the last judgment, where the same principle of purposeful divine justice will be applied universally.
