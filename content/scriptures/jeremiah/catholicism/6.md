Jeremiah 6 brings the cycle of early warnings to its conclusion with imagery of refinement and rejection. The metaphor of the refiner (6:27-30) — Jeremiah appointed to test the people like a metallurgist tests ore — resonates with the Catholic theology of purgation. The process of refining, which aims to separate precious metal from dross, parallels the Church's teaching on purgatory as the purification of souls that are destined for heaven but not yet fully purified. Jeremiah's verdict that the refining has failed ("rejected silver they are called") represents the tragic case where the refining process encounters material too corrupted to be saved.

The invitation to "stand at the crossroads and look; ask for the ancient paths" (6:16) has been a key text in Catholic traditionalism and the ressourcement movement. The Second Vatican Council's call to return to the sources (*ad fontes*) — patristic, liturgical, and biblical — finds its prophetic warrant in Jeremiah's invitation to seek the ancient way. Catholic interpreters read this not as mere conservatism but as authentic discernment: the ancient paths are not old for the sake of being old but represent the tested way of communion with God.

The prophetic denunciation of false peace — "Peace, peace, when there is no peace" (6:14) — has been applied in Catholic social teaching to situations of structural injustice masked by superficial order. Pope Paul VI's statement that "if you want peace, work for justice" echoes the Jeremianic insistence that true *shalom* requires righteousness. Catholic peace theology, rooted in such prophetic texts, refuses to identify the absence of conflict with genuine peace.
