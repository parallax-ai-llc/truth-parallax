Jeremiah 31 contains the prophecy of the new covenant (31:31-34), which Catholic theology reads as the prophetic foundation for the entire sacramental economy. When Jesus at the Last Supper declared "This cup is the new covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:20), He explicitly identified the Eucharist as the fulfillment of Jeremiah's oracle. Catholic eucharistic theology thus understands every celebration of the Mass as the actualization of the new covenant promised in Jeremiah 31 — not a repetition of Christ's sacrifice but its sacramental re-presentation, making the new covenant present to each generation.

The promise that God will write the law "on their hearts" (31:33) is understood in Catholic theology not as the abolition of external moral guidance but as the interior transformation effected by grace. The Catechism teaches that the natural law inscribed on the human heart (CCC 1954-1960) is perfected by the grace of the new covenant, which heals the will's inclination toward sin and empowers authentic obedience from within. The interplay between interior grace and external teaching authority — the written law of the heart and the teaching office of the Church — reflects the fullness of Jeremiah's vision rather than a reductive reading that eliminates either dimension.

Rachel's weeping for her children (31:15), applied by Matthew to the Holy Innocents, holds a special place in Catholic devotion. The feast of the Holy Innocents (December 28) commemorates the first martyrs of the Christian era, and the liturgical tradition reads Rachel's tears as encompassing all innocent suffering throughout salvation history. The Catholic tradition of offering suffering in union with Christ — the spirituality of redemptive suffering — finds its Old Testament foundation in this passage, where weeping is not the final word but is transformed by the promise of return and restoration.
