Jeremiah 46 opens the oracles against the nations, and Catholic interpretation reads these prophecies through the lens of God's universal sovereignty taught in *Gaudium et Spes* and the broader tradition of Catholic social thought. The oracle against Egypt, centered on the Battle of Carchemish (605 BC) and the subsequent Babylonian invasion, demonstrates that divine judgment extends beyond the covenant people to encompass all nations — a principle that the Catholic tradition applies to its understanding of natural law and universal moral accountability.

The Patristic tradition read the fall of Egypt typologically. Origen interpreted Egypt's humiliation as representing the defeat of worldly wisdom by divine power, connecting it to Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 1-2. St. Jerome, whose commentary on Jeremiah was among the most influential in the Latin West, saw in the oracle a prophecy of the ultimate triumph of the Church over the philosophical and political powers of the pagan world. This typological reading, while not exhausting the text's meaning, remains part of the Catholic interpretive heritage.

The concluding promise to Israel (46:27-28) is read in Catholic theology as evidence of God's irrevocable covenant faithfulness — a theme central to *Nostra Aetate* (1965) and subsequent Catholic teaching on the enduring validity of God's covenant with Israel. The distinction between the destruction of the nations and the discipline of Israel reveals a God who punishes in order to restore, not to annihilate — a principle that Catholic eschatology applies to its understanding of purgatorial purification.
