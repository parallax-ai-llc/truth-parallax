The flight to Egypt (43:1-7) represents in Catholic interpretation the definitive rejection of prophetic authority by the remnant community. The accusation against Baruch (43:3) — that the scribe has manipulated Jeremiah — echoes the perennial challenge to magisterial authority: the claim that the Church's teaching is merely human opinion rather than divinely guided truth. Catholic ecclesiology addresses this challenge through the doctrine of the prophetic office of the Church, which participates in the prophetic ministry of Christ.

Jeremiah's forced removal to Egypt, despite his innocence, is read by the Church Fathers as a type of Christ's passion: the righteous one suffers the consequences of others' sins, sharing in their exile without sharing in their guilt. St. Cyril of Alexandria drew parallels between Jeremiah's Egyptian exile and the Holy Family's flight to Egypt (Matthew 2:13-15), seeing in both episodes the pattern of divine providence working through apparent defeat.

The symbolic burial of stones at Pharaoh's palace in Tahpanhes (43:8-13) is interpreted in Catholic tradition as a prophetic sign-act (*oth*) that embodies the word it proclaims. The Catholic understanding of sacramental efficacy — that signs effect what they signify — finds an Old Testament antecedent in such prophetic actions. Nebuchadnezzar's future conquest of Egypt confirms that no earthly power can serve as a substitute for trust in God, a principle the Church applies to every form of misplaced reliance on temporal security.
