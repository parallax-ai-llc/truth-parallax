Catholic exegesis of Jeremiah 2 reads the covenant lawsuit against Israel through the lens of nuptial theology. The image of Israel as a bride who has abandoned her divine spouse resonates with the Catholic understanding of the Church as the Bride of Christ (Ephesians 5:25-32). The movement from bridal fidelity to harlotry becomes an allegory for the soul's departure from grace — a theme developed extensively by the Church Fathers, particularly Origen and St. Bernard of Clairvaux in their commentaries on the Song of Songs.

The metaphor of the broken cisterns (2:13) — forsaking the fountain of living waters for self-made containers that cannot hold water — is read in Catholic sacramental theology as an image of what happens when the faithful turn from the sacraments (the channels of living water) to merely human substitutes. The "living water" imagery connects to John 4 (the Samaritan woman) and John 7:37-39 (rivers of living water as the Holy Spirit), establishing a typological chain from Jeremiah through the Gospels to the Church's sacramental life.

The catalogue of Israel's infidelities — political alliances, Baal worship, social injustice — illustrates what Catholic moral theology calls the interconnectedness of sin. The Catechism teaches that sins are linked to one another (CCC 1849-1876); Jeremiah 2 provides the prophetic demonstration of how religious apostasy, political pragmatism, and social oppression form a single fabric of covenant violation. Catholic social teaching draws on such prophetic passages to insist that faith and justice are inseparable.
