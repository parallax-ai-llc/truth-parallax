Jeremiah 22 evaluates three kings of Judah — Shallum, Jehoiakim, and Coniah — providing Catholic political theology with prophetic criteria for assessing leadership. The contrast between Josiah, who "judged the cause of the poor and needy" and "it was well with him," and Jehoiakim, who built his palace with forced labor, embodies the Catholic principle of the preferential option for the poor. The declaration "Is not this to know me?" (22:16) equates knowledge of God with the practice of justice — a theme that Pope Francis has frequently invoked.

The condemnation of Jehoiakim's building projects (22:13-19) — "Woe to him who builds his house by unrighteousness, and his upper rooms by injustice, who makes his neighbor serve him for nothing" — reads as a prophetic anticipation of the Church's teaching on the rights of workers and the justice of wages. Catholic Social Teaching, beginning with *Rerum Novarum* and continuing through *Laborem Exercens* (1981), insists that labor has an inherent dignity and that the exploitation of workers is a sin crying out to heaven — language that echoes Jeremiah's prophetic indictment.

The oracle against Coniah (22:24-30) — "Write this man down as childless" — has generated significant discussion in Catholic biblical interpretation regarding the Davidic genealogy of Christ. The Church has traditionally resolved the apparent tension through the distinction between Joseph's legal lineage (through Coniah's line) and Mary's biological lineage (through Nathan's line, as in Luke 3). This solution preserves both the prophetic curse and the messianic promise, demonstrating the complexity of salvation history as it moves toward its fulfillment in Christ.
