Catholic exegesis of Jeremiah 5 reads the fruitless search for a righteous person (5:1) in light of the broader biblical theme of intercession and mediation. If a single just person could save the city — as Abraham hoped for Sodom — then the failure to find one demonstrates both the totality of corruption and the need for a perfect mediator. Catholic theology sees this need fulfilled in Christ, the one righteous man whose intercession avails for all, and extends the principle through the communion of saints and the intercessory role of the Blessed Virgin Mary.

The indictment of social injustice — "they judge not with justice the cause of the fatherless" and "they do not defend the rights of the needy" (5:28) — resonates deeply with Catholic Social Teaching. The encyclical tradition from *Rerum Novarum* (1891) through *Laudato Si'* (2015) consistently invokes the prophetic critique of economic exploitation. Jeremiah's description of the wealthy who "have grown fat and sleek" while ignoring the poor mirrors the Church's teaching on the preferential option for the poor and the universal destination of goods.

The chapter's critique of false prophets who "prophesy falsely" while "the priests rule at their direction" (5:31) addresses a perennial concern in Catholic ecclesiology: the relationship between prophetic truth and institutional authority. The Magisterium's role, in Catholic understanding, is precisely to prevent the kind of collusion between false prophecy and priestly power that Jeremiah condemns. The prophetic critique does not undermine institutional authority but demands its accountability to divine truth.
