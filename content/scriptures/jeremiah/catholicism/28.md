Jeremiah 28 recounts the confrontation between Jeremiah and the false prophet Hananiah in the Temple, a passage that Catholic theology reads as foundational for understanding the Church's responsibility to discern authentic from counterfeit prophecy. Hananiah's false prophecy is not the product of obvious heresy — he speaks in God's name, uses orthodox formulas, and claims the authority of the prophetic tradition. Catholic ecclesiology argues that precisely because false prophecy can so closely mimic true prophecy, the Church requires a teaching authority (the Magisterium) capable of making definitive judgments about the authenticity of prophetic claims.

Jeremiah's hermeneutical principle — that the prophet of peace bears the burden of proof (28:8-9) — informs Catholic moral theology's "hermeneutic of suspicion" toward claims that align too neatly with human desires. The Second Vatican Council's *Gaudium et Spes* warns against confusing the gospel with ideological programs, and Jeremiah's criterion serves this same function: the message that tells us exactly what we want to hear requires the most rigorous scrutiny.

The replacement of wooden yoke-bars with iron ones (28:13) illustrates a principle that Catholic moral theology recognizes: resistance to a divinely ordained correction often results in a heavier burden, not liberation. Hananiah's dramatic gesture of breaking the yoke was a prophetic counter-sign, but it proved to be empty theater. The Church's tradition of prophetic discernment insists that dramatic spiritual gestures must be evaluated by their fruits, not by their theatricality — a principle rooted in Christ's own teaching (Matthew 7:15-20).
