Jeremiah 4 presents the terrifying vision of invasion and cosmic undoing with language that Catholic interpreters read on multiple levels. The literal-historical sense describes the Babylonian threat; the allegorical sense sees in the de-creation imagery (4:23-26) a type of the eschatological judgment; the moral sense reads the call to "circumcise your hearts" (4:4) as an invitation to interior conversion; and the anagogical sense points toward the ultimate transformation of the cosmos at the end of time.

The command to circumcise the heart rather than the flesh (4:4) was central to the patristic understanding of the relationship between the Old and New Covenants. St. Augustine interpreted this as evidence that even under the Law, God desired the interior reality that sacramental signs pointed toward. Catholic theology draws on this to argue against any purely external understanding of the sacraments — baptism, like circumcision, requires the interior disposition of faith to be truly fruitful, even as the sacrament confers grace *ex opere operato*.

The prophet's personal anguish — "My anguish, my anguish! I writhe in pain!" (4:19) — is read in Catholic tradition as a type of Christ's agony and of the Church's compassion for a suffering world. Origen devoted particular attention to this passage, arguing that the true prophet (and by extension the true pastor) participates in God's own suffering over human sin. This "divine pathos," as Abraham Heschel would later call it, finds its fullest expression in the Passion of Christ, which the Catholic liturgy of Holy Week commemorates.
