Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 45 reads the brief oracle to Baruch as a model for the spirituality of vocation. Baruch's lament (45:3) and God's response (45:4-5) are understood within the Catholic tradition of the "dark night of the soul" articulated by St. John of the Cross — the experience of suffering and apparent abandonment that purifies the servant of God and strips away self-seeking motives. The Catechism teaches that God permits suffering in the lives of the faithful not as punishment but as a means of spiritual growth and deeper union with Christ.

The divine command "Do not seek great things for yourself" (45:5) resonates with the Catholic tradition of humility as the foundation of all virtue. St. Benedict's Rule places humility at the center of monastic spirituality, and Baruch's experience illustrates why: the servant of God must relinquish personal ambition in order to be fully available to the divine purpose. The promise that Baruch's life will be preserved "as a prize of war" offers not triumph but survival — a distinction that Catholic spirituality understands through the theology of the Cross.

The chapter's significance in Catholic tradition also extends to its implicit testimony about the role of the scribe in preserving sacred texts. Baruch's labor of transcription (referenced in Jeremiah 36) makes him a patron of the scribal tradition that would eventually produce and preserve the biblical manuscripts. The Catholic understanding of Sacred Tradition as a living transmission of divine truth finds an early exemplar in Baruch's faithful recording of the prophetic word.
