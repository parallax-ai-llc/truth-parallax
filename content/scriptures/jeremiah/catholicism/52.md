Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 52 reads the historical account of Jerusalem's fall as the fulfillment of the prophetic word and the vindication of the prophetic office. The detailed narrative — the siege, the breach, the king's capture, the Temple's destruction, the deportation — provides the factual framework upon which the entire theological structure of the book rests. The Catholic understanding of the relationship between prophecy and history, articulated in *Dei Verbum* (1965), insists that divine revelation operates through historical events, not apart from them.

The inventory of the Temple furnishings (52:17-23) is read in Catholic liturgical theology as a record of the sacred vessels whose loss prefigures the Catholic understanding of the Real Presence. The bronze pillars, the sea, and the utensils of worship embody the tangible, material dimension of the covenant relationship — the principle that the holy is mediated through physical objects. The Catholic tradition of reverence for sacred vessels and liturgical objects finds an Old Testament foundation in this careful cataloguing of what was taken from the House of God.

The release of Jehoiachin (52:31-34) is interpreted by the Church Fathers as a type of resurrection and exaltation. The Davidic king, buried alive in a Babylonian prison for thirty-seven years, is raised to a place of honor — an image that the Patristic tradition read christologically. Jehoiachin's liberation prefigures Christ's rising from the tomb, and his elevation to eat at the king's table anticipates the messianic banquet. The book of Jeremiah thus closes with a sign of hope that the Catholic tradition reads as the first foreshadowing of Easter within the narrative of the Babylonian exile.
