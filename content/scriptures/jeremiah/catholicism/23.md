Jeremiah 23 contains two of the most theologically significant passages in the prophetic literature: the messianic prophecy of the Righteous Branch (23:5-6) and the extended critique of false prophets (23:9-40). Catholic Christological interpretation identifies the "righteous Branch" who will reign as king and execute justice with Jesus Christ, the Son of David. The name "The Lord is our Righteousness" (*Yahweh Tsidqenu*) is understood as a divine title applied to the Messiah, supporting the Catholic affirmation of Christ's divine nature within the framework of Trinitarian theology.

The critique of false prophets provides Catholic ecclesiology with essential criteria for discerning authentic from inauthentic teaching. The false prophets are characterized by moral corruption (23:14), self-generated visions (23:16), failure to stand in the divine council (23:18), and the promotion of complacency rather than repentance (23:17). The Catholic response to this crisis of discernment is the Magisterium — the teaching authority of the Church, exercised by the Pope and bishops in communion with him, which functions as the institutional safeguard against the proliferation of competing and contradictory claims to speak for God.

The declaration that God's word is "like fire" and "like a hammer that breaks the rock in pieces" (23:29) grounds the Catholic theology of the word's sacramental efficacy. In Catholic understanding, the word proclaimed in the liturgy is not merely informational but performative — it accomplishes what it declares, much as the sacramental words of consecration effect the Eucharistic transformation. The Dogmatic Constitution *Dei Verbum* affirms that in the liturgy God speaks to His people and Christ proclaims His gospel, making the liturgical reading of Scripture a genuine encounter with the living word.
