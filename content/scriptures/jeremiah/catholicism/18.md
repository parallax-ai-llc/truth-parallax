The potter and clay imagery of Jeremiah 18 is one of the most theologically generative passages in the prophetic corpus. Catholic interpreters, while fully affirming divine sovereignty over creation ("like clay in the potter's hand, so are you in my hand"), emphasize the conditional nature of the passage: God's intention toward a nation changes in response to that nation's repentance or rebellion (18:7-10). This conditionality is central to the Catholic understanding of how divine providence and human freedom cooperate — what Thomistic theology calls the compatibility of divine sovereignty and secondary causation.

The potter's workshop, where the artisan reworks flawed vessels rather than discarding them, has been read by Catholic interpreters as an image of God's patient, restorative work in salvation history. The sacrament of reconciliation is understood as one instance of this divine reworking: the sinner who has been marred by sin is not destroyed but reshaped by divine grace through sacramental encounter. St. Irenaeus's famous dictum — "the glory of God is a human being fully alive" — reflects this vision of God as the divine craftsman who refuses to abandon His work.

The plot against Jeremiah (18:18-23) and his imprecatory prayer present a challenge that Catholic moral theology addresses through the distinction between the Old Testament dispensation of justice and the New Testament command of charity. While the Church does not endorse personal vengeance, it recognizes the legitimacy of appealing to God's justice — a practice preserved in the imprecatory psalms that remain in the Liturgy of the Hours, though with pastoral sensitivity to their christological and eschatological dimensions.
