The Temple Sermon of Jeremiah 7 is one of the most significant prophetic passages for Catholic ecclesiology and sacramental theology. Jeremiah's warning against trusting in "deceptive words" — the threefold invocation of "the temple of the Lord" — addresses the perennial temptation to treat sacred spaces and institutions as guarantees of divine favor independent of moral fidelity. Catholic theology affirms both the real presence of God in the Eucharist and the need for worthy reception, holding together what Jeremiah 7 insists cannot be separated: divine presence and human response.

The reference to Shiloh (7:12-14), where God's tabernacle once stood before its destruction, demonstrates that God is not bound even to His own sacred institutions. Catholic interpreters, following St. Augustine, distinguish between the *ecclesia* as the mystical Body of Christ (which can never fail) and particular institutional expressions of the Church (which can and have been subject to divine judgment). This distinction allows Catholic theology to maintain both the Church's indefectibility and the prophetic critique of ecclesial corruption.

Jesus's quotation of Jeremiah 7:11 — "Has this house, which is called by my name, become a den of robbers?" — during his cleansing of the Temple (Mark 11:17) establishes a direct continuity between Jeremiah's prophetic ministry and Christ's. Catholic Christology reads Jesus as the prophet like Moses promised in Deuteronomy 18:15, and his Temple action as the definitive fulfillment of Jeremiah's sermon. The Eucharistic sacrifice, celebrated in the Church, is thus understood as the true worship that the Temple was always meant to embody.
