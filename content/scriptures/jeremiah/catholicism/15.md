Jeremiah 15 contains some of the most painfully honest passages in prophetic literature, and Catholic spiritual theology has found in them a mirror for the experience of spiritual desolation. The declaration that even Moses and Samuel could not intercede for this people (15:1) establishes the severity of the situation while pointing forward to the need for a greater intercessor — Christ himself, whose priestly intercession (Hebrews 7:25) succeeds where even the greatest human mediators fail.

The prophet's confession — "Your words were found, and I ate them, and your words became to me a joy and the delight of my heart" (15:16) — is one of the most important texts for Catholic theology of the Word of God. The Second Vatican Council's *Dei Verbum* quotes this verse's spiritual reality when describing how the faithful receive God's word in Scripture and liturgy. The imagery of eating the word connects to Ezekiel 3:1-3, Revelation 10:9-10, and ultimately to the Eucharist — the Word made flesh who is consumed by the faithful in Holy Communion.

God's response to Jeremiah's complaint — "If you return, I will restore you" (15:19) — reveals that even the prophet is not exempt from the call to conversion. Catholic spiritual direction recognizes that ministers of the word themselves require ongoing repentance and renewal. The passage has been influential in the formation of priests and religious, reminding them that prophetic authority depends on personal holiness and continued faithfulness to the divine call.
