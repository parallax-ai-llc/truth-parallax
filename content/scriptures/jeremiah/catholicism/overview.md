## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah is guided by Sacred Tradition, the Magisterium, and the fourfold sense of Scripture. The book holds particular significance in Catholic theology because the New Covenant passage (31:31-34) is directly quoted in the institution narratives of the Eucharist and in the Letter to the Hebrews. Catholic exegesis reads Jeremiah not merely as a historical document but as a living text that speaks to the ongoing life of the Church.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Four Senses of Scripture
Catholic exegesis applies the traditional fourfold method:

1. **Literal**: Jeremiah's historical ministry during Judah's final decades
2. **Allegorical**: Jeremiah as a type of Christ, the suffering servant who weeps over Jerusalem
3. **Moral**: The call to interior conversion over external observance
4. **Anagogical**: The New Covenant as pointing toward the heavenly liturgy

### Patristic and Magisterial Guidance
The Church Fathers, especially St. Jerome (who translated Jeremiah in the Vulgate), St. Augustine, and Origen, provide the interpretive framework. Magisterial documents such as *Dei Verbum* (1965) affirm that the prophetic books must be read within the unity of Scripture and Tradition.

## Theological Emphases

- **New Covenant and Eucharist**: "This is the cup of the New Covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:20) directly fulfills Jeremiah 31:31-34
- **Prophetic Office of Christ**: Jeremiah as the supreme Old Testament type of Christ the Prophet
- **The Suffering Servant**: Jeremiah's personal anguish as prefiguring Christ's Passion
- **Interior Religion**: The critique of formalism as relevant to Catholic piety and sacramental life
