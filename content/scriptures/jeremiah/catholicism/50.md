Catholic interpretation of Jeremiah 50 reads the oracle against Babylon within the theological framework of divine justice applied to the instrument of divine judgment. The Church Fathers drew a crucial distinction: God used Babylon to punish Judah's sins, but Babylon itself sinned in exceeding its divine commission through cruelty and arrogance. St. Augustine developed this principle in *De Civitate Dei*, arguing that God's use of a nation as an instrument of judgment does not exempt that nation from moral accountability — a principle the Catholic tradition applies to all exercises of temporal power.

The interweaving of Babylon's judgment with Israel's restoration (50:4-7, 17-20) is read in Catholic theology as revealing the unity of God's salvific purpose. The Catholic understanding of salvation history (*heilsgeschichte*) sees divine judgment and divine mercy as complementary aspects of a single providential plan. The promise that Israel's guilt will be "sought but not found" (50:20) anticipates the New Testament proclamation of forgiveness through Christ, which Catholic theology understands as the definitive fulfillment of the prophetic vision.

The Catholic tradition's cautious engagement with typological readings of Babylon — particularly the Protestant identification of Rome with "Babylon the Great" — has produced a nuanced hermeneutic. Catholic exegetes acknowledge the legitimacy of typological reading within limits established by the Magisterium but reject interpretations that identify the Catholic Church with the oppressive Babylon of prophetic denunciation. The Pontifical Biblical Commission's guidelines on prophetic interpretation emphasize the historical specificity of the oracles while acknowledging their paradigmatic significance for all exercises of unjust power.
