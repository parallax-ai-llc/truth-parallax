## Overview

Islam does not include Jeremiah among its named prophets in the Quran, but Islamic tradition has long engaged with Jeremiah's story through *Isra'iliyyat* — narratives drawn from Jewish and Christian sources that entered Islamic historiography and tafsir. Several classical Muslim scholars identify Jeremiah (*Irmiya* in Arabic) as a prophet sent to the Israelites before the Babylonian destruction, and some associate him with the unnamed figure in Quran 2:259 who passes by a ruined city and is caused by God to die for a hundred years before being resurrected.

## Relationship to the Quran

While Jeremiah is not explicitly named in the Quran, several Quranic passages resonate with Jeremianic themes:

| Jeremiah Theme | Quranic Parallel | Key Passages |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Destruction of Jerusalem | Punishment of disobedient nations | Al-Isra 17:4-7 |
| A prophet rejected by his people | Pattern of prophetic rejection | Ya-Sin 36:13-30 |
| Call to repentance | Nations warned before destruction | Hud 11:117 |
| Death and resurrection of a prophet | The unnamed prophet of the ruined city | Al-Baqarah 2:259 |
| Covenant with God | God's covenant with the prophets | Al-Ahzab 33:7 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
Muslim scholars who accept Jeremiah as a prophet apply the doctrine of prophetic sinlessness. The personal laments and apparent despair found in the biblical Jeremiah are either reinterpreted as expressions of righteous grief or attributed to textual corruption (*tahrif*).

### Isra'iliyyat Tradition
Classical Islamic historians and commentators preserved extensive Jeremiah material:
- **Al-Tabari** narrates Jeremiah's story in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk*, describing his role before the Babylonian conquest
- **Al-Tha'labi** includes Jeremiah in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* (Stories of the Prophets)
- **Ibn Kathir** discusses the destruction of Jerusalem and its prophetic context in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah*

### Tawhid
All Jeremianic material is read through the lens of strict monotheism. The prophet's message is understood as fundamentally a call to *tawhid* — pure worship of God alone — consistent with the mission of all prophets in Islamic theology.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Punishment of Nations**: The Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem as an instance of God's judgment on disobedience
- **The Pattern of Prophetic Rejection**: Jeremiah's persecution fits the Quranic pattern of communities rejecting their prophets
- **Sovereignty of God**: God uses Nebuchadnezzar as an instrument of divine will
- **Resurrection**: The Quran 2:259 narrative as a sign of God's power over death
