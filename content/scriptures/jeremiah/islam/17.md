The diagnosis of the heart as "deceitful above all things" (17:9) resonates with the Quranic emphasis on the heart (*qalb*) as the locus of spiritual health and disease. The Quran describes hearts that are sealed (2:7), diseased (2:10), hardened (6:43), and sound (26:89). Islamic spiritual tradition (*tasawwuf*) has developed elaborate sciences of the heart aimed at diagnosing and treating spiritual diseases — envy, pride, hypocrisy, attachment to the world — that Jeremiah's diagnosis identifies in concentrated form.

The contrasting images of the cursed person trusting in human strength (17:5-6) and the blessed person trusting in God (17:7-8) directly parallel the Quranic concept of *tawakkul* (trust in God) versus reliance on worldly means. The Quran teaches: "And whoever puts their trust in Allah, He will be sufficient for them" (Quran 65:3). The tree by the water represents the believer whose roots are sunk in divine trust; the desert shrub represents the one who relies on human resources alone. This binary framework is fundamental to Islamic spirituality and ethics.

The sabbath oracle (17:19-27) is read by Islamic interpreters within the context of the Quranic treatment of the sabbath as a covenant obligation specific to the Bani Israil, not a universal commandment. The Quran mentions the sabbath as a test for the Children of Israel (Quran 4:154, 7:163-166) and records that those who violated it were punished. Islam replaced the sabbath with the Friday congregational prayer (*Jumu'ah*), which involves community worship but not the cessation of work that the sabbath requires. Jeremiah's sabbath warning is thus read as historically specific to the Israelite covenant.
