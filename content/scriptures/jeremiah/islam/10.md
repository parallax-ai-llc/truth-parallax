Jeremiah 10's polemic against idol-making finds its most direct Islamic parallel in the Quranic narrative of Ibrahim (Abraham) smashing the idols of his father's people (Quran 21:51-67). The satirical description of idols that must be carried because they cannot walk (10:5) echoes the Quranic ridicule of false gods: "Do they have legs by which they walk? Or do they have hands by which they strike? Or do they have eyes by which they see?" (Quran 7:195). Both prophetic traditions employ mockery to expose the absurdity of worshipping human-made objects.

The confession of God as "the living God and the everlasting King" (10:10) directly corresponds to the Quranic attributes *Al-Hayy* (the Ever-Living) and *Al-Malik* (the King/Sovereign). The Quran opens its greatest verse, Ayat al-Kursi, with precisely these attributes: "Allah — there is no deity except Him, the Ever-Living, the Sustainer of existence" (Quran 2:255). Islamic interpreters read Jeremiah's confession as an expression of the same *tawhid* that the Quran proclaims — a confirmation that the essential message of all prophets is the affirmation of God's absolute unity and sovereignty.

Jeremiah's prayer for measured correction (10:24) — asking for justice rather than anger — reflects the Islamic understanding of God's attributes of mercy and justice as complementary, not contradictory. The Islamic tradition teaches that God's mercy (*rahmah*) encompasses His wrath (*ghadab*), as stated in a hadith qudsi: "My mercy prevails over My wrath." The prophetic prayer for justice tempered by mercy models the Muslim's proper disposition before God, acknowledging both divine justice and divine compassion.
