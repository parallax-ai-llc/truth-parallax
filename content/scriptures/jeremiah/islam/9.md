Jeremiah's wish to weep ceaselessly for his people (9:1) is read in Islamic tradition as an expression of the *rahmah* (mercy) that characterizes all true prophets. The Quran describes the Prophet Muhammad as deeply grieved by the unbelief of his people: "Perhaps you would kill yourself through grief over them if they do not believe in this message" (Quran 18:6). The prophetic compassion that Jeremiah embodies — weeping for those he must condemn — is understood in Islam as a universal quality of authentic prophetic character.

The declaration that true boasting is found in knowing God (9:23-24) — not in wisdom, might, or riches — aligns with the Quranic teaching that the only criterion of value before God is *taqwa* (God-consciousness): "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (Quran 49:13). Islamic theology consistently relativizes all worldly distinctions — wealth, lineage, learning, military power — before the singular criterion of one's relationship with God. Jeremiah's pronouncement confirms this Quranic principle.

The distinction between circumcision of the flesh and circumcision of the heart (9:25-26) supports the Islamic understanding that external rites serve the internal reality of faith. While Islam practices physical circumcision as part of the *fitrah* (natural disposition), the Quran places far greater emphasis on the purification of the heart (*qalb*): "The Day when there will not benefit anyone wealth or children, except him who comes to Allah with a sound heart" (Quran 26:88-89). Jeremiah's prophetic prioritization of inner over outer religion is fully consonant with the Quranic message.
