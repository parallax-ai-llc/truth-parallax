The drought described in Jeremiah 14 is read in Islamic interpretation as an instance of God withdrawing His blessings from a disobedient community — a pattern the Quran describes with the term *istidraj* (gradual punishment). The Quran teaches that natural disasters and calamities are signs (*ayat*) that call people to repentance: "And We sent to no city a prophet except that We seized its people with poverty and hardship that they might humble themselves" (Quran 7:94). Islamic tradition includes specific prayers (*istisqa'*) for rain, reflecting the conviction that drought has spiritual as well as natural causes.

The exposure of false prophets (14:13-16) resonates with the Quranic condemnation of those who fabricate lies against God: "Who is more unjust than one who invents a lie about Allah or says, 'It has been inspired to me,' while nothing has been inspired to him?" (Quran 6:93). Islamic theology maintains strict criteria for authenticating prophetic claims, and the phenomenon of false prophecy that Jeremiah confronted is recognized as a perennial danger. The *hadith* literature records Muhammad's warnings about false prophets who will appear before the Day of Judgment.

Jeremiah's persistent intercession (14:19-22) models the Islamic practice of *istighfar* (seeking forgiveness) on behalf of the community. The Quran records multiple instances of prophets interceding for their peoples — Ibrahim for the people of Lut (Quran 11:74-76), Musa for the Bani Israil after the Golden Calf (Quran 7:155-156). While Islamic theology maintains that intercession (*shafa'ah*) is ultimately granted or withheld by God alone, the prophetic example of persistent pleading on behalf of others is honored as an expression of the *rahmah* that characterizes authentic prophetic character.
