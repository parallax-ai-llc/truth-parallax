Jeremiah 11's recitation of the covenant curses is read in Islamic interpretation as an instance of the divine pattern described in the Quran: God establishes covenants with communities through prophets, the communities violate these covenants, and divine punishment follows. The Quran specifically addresses the Bani Israil's covenant breaking: "And We took a covenant from the Children of Israel... Is it not that every time they took a covenant, a party of them threw it aside?" (Quran 2:83-100). Jeremiah 11 provides the prophetic evidence for this Quranic characterization.

The plot against Jeremiah by the men of Anathoth (11:18-23) fits the Quranic pattern of prophets being persecuted by their own communities. The Quran identifies this as a recurring feature of prophetic history: "Why is it that whenever a messenger came to you with something you did not desire, you were arrogant? A party of them you denied and another party you killed" (Quran 2:87). Islamic interpreters read Jeremiah's persecution as further evidence of this universal pattern, confirming the Quranic teaching that rejection of prophets is a characteristic failing of human communities.

The "lamb led to the slaughter" imagery (11:19) is read in Islamic interpretation not as a prefiguration of Jesus's crucifixion (which Islam denies; Quran 4:157) but as a description of the prophet's vulnerability and trust in God. The concept of *tawakkul* (trust in God) is demonstrated by Jeremiah's willingness to continue his mission despite mortal danger. The Quran teaches that trust in God does not guarantee physical safety but ensures spiritual victory: "If Allah should aid you, no one can overcome you" (Quran 3:160).
