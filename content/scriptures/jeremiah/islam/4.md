The terrifying vision of invasion and de-creation in Jeremiah 4 is read in Islamic interpretation as an instance of God's *sunnah* (established way) with disobedient nations. The Quran repeatedly describes how civilizations that rejected their prophets were destroyed: the people of Nuh (Noah), 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lut (Lot), and others (Quran 7:59-93, 11:25-95). Jeremiah's vision of Judah's destruction fits this Quranic pattern of divine judgment on communities that persistently refuse prophetic guidance.

The de-creation language of 4:23-26 — the earth returning to *tohu vavohu* (formlessness and void) — resonates with the Quranic descriptions of cosmic dissolution on the Day of Judgment: "When the sky is torn apart, when the stars are scattered, when the seas are made to burst forth" (Quran 82:1-3). Islamic interpreters note the parallel between Jeremiah's vision of historical judgment and the Quran's descriptions of eschatological judgment, understanding both as expressions of God's absolute power over creation.

The command to "circumcise your hearts" (4:4) aligns with the Islamic emphasis on inner purity (*tazkiyat al-nafs* — purification of the soul) over external ritual. While Islam maintains the practice of physical circumcision (*khitan*) as a sunnah, the Quran consistently prioritizes inner orientation: "Righteousness is not that you turn your faces toward the east or the west, but righteousness is in one who believes in Allah" (Quran 2:177). Jeremiah's demand for interior transformation over external observance is fully consonant with the Quranic message.
