Jeremiah 38 narrates the prophet's descent into the cistern and his rescue by Ebed-melech, and Islamic interpreters read this episode as illustrating the Quranic principle that God always provides protection for His prophets through unexpected agents. The Quran describes how God protected Ibrahim from the fire (Quran 21:69), Musa from Pharaoh (Quran 28:7), and Muhammad through the Hijra (Quran 9:40). Ebed-melech's intervention belongs to this pattern of divine rescue, demonstrating that God's agents of deliverance are not limited to the prophet's own community.

Ebed-melech's identity as an Ethiopian — a foreigner in the Judean court — resonates with the Islamic principle of the equality of all human beings before God regardless of race or ethnicity. The Prophet Muhammad declared in his farewell sermon: "No Arab has superiority over a non-Arab, and no non-Arab has superiority over an Arab... except by piety and good action." Ebed-melech's moral courage — his willingness to risk his position and his life to save a prophet — demonstrates that true nobility lies in righteousness, not in lineage or nationality.

Zedekiah's refusal to act on Jeremiah's counsel (38:14-28) illustrates the Quranic condemnation of those who hear the truth but refuse to follow it: "And who is more unjust than one who is reminded of the verses of his Lord but turns away from them?" (Quran 18:57). Islamic theology holds that knowledge without action is worse than ignorance, because the person who knows the right path and refuses to walk it has no excuse before God. Zedekiah's tragedy is the tragedy of *'ilm bila 'amal* — knowledge without deeds.
