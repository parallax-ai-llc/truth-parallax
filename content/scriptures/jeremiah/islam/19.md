The broken flask of Jeremiah 19 represents irreversible judgment — unlike the clay on the potter's wheel, the shattered vessel cannot be repaired. Islamic interpreters read this as illustrating the Quranic principle that divine patience, while vast, eventually reaches its limit within historical time. The Quran describes how nations were given respite (*imhal*) before judgment fell: "And for every nation is a specified term. So when their time has come, they will not remain behind an hour, nor will they precede it" (Quran 7:34).

The Valley of Ben-Hinnom (Ge-Hinnom), where children were sacrificed to Molech, represents the most extreme form of *shirk* (associating partners with God) and moral corruption. Islamic theology identifies child sacrifice as one of the worst practices of pre-Islamic Arabia (*jahiliyyah*), and the Quran explicitly condemns it: "Do not kill your children out of poverty" (Quran 6:151). The prophetic horror at the practices in Ben-Hinnom reflects the universal prophetic mission to protect the vulnerable and abolish practices that degrade human dignity.

The transformation of the valley's name into Gehenna — the eschatological place of punishment — connects to the Quranic concept of *Jahannam* (Hell), which derives from the same linguistic root. The Quran describes *Jahannam* in vivid detail as the destination of those who reject divine guidance and persist in sin (Quran 2:24, 4:56, 67:6-8). Islamic interpreters read the historical Valley of Ben-Hinnom as the earthly prototype of the eschatological reality that the Quran describes, linking prophetic history to eschatological destiny.
