Jeremiah 39 records the fall of Jerusalem, and Islamic interpreters read this catastrophe as an instance of the Quranic principle that God destroys nations that persist in rejecting their prophets. The Quran repeatedly cites the destruction of earlier civilizations as warnings: "Have they not traveled through the earth and observed how was the end of those before them? They were greater than them in power" (Quran 40:82). The fall of Jerusalem joins the destruction of the people of Nuh, 'Ad, Thamud, and Sodom as a historical demonstration that no nation is exempt from divine judgment.

The blinding and captivity of Zedekiah (39:6-7) illustrates the Quranic principle that those who refuse to see divine truth are ultimately deprived of sight entirely: "And whoever is blind in this world will be blind in the Hereafter and more astray in way" (Quran 17:72). Islamic theology reads Zedekiah's physical blinding as a manifestation of the spiritual blindness that characterized his entire reign — the refusal to act on prophetic counsel, the subordination of divine truth to political calculation, and the preference for comforting lies over difficult truths.

The promise to Ebed-melech (39:15-18) demonstrates the Islamic principle that God rewards individual righteousness even amid collective punishment. The Quran affirms: "Whoever does righteousness — it is for his own soul; and whoever does evil — it is against it. And your Lord is not ever unjust to His servants" (Quran 41:46). Ebed-melech's salvation is not a matter of ethnic identity or political alignment but of personal faith and moral courage — the qualities that Islamic theology identifies as the basis of divine favor in every age.
