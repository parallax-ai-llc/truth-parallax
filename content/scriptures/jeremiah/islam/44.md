Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 44 focuses on the community's worship of the "Queen of Heaven" as a paradigmatic instance of *shirk* (associating partners with God), the gravest sin in Islamic theology. The Quran consistently condemns the worship of celestial bodies and intermediary deities, and the Egyptian community's defiant devotion to the Queen of Heaven exemplifies the pattern that Surah Al-An'am (6:76-79) describes in Ibrahim's rejection of star worship. The community's theological sophistication — they offer a reasoned defense of their idolatry — makes their sin more, not less, grave in Islamic understanding, because it represents deliberate rebellion against known truth.

The community's argument that prosperity attended their worship (44:17-18) is countered in Islamic thought by the Quranic principle of *istidraj* — God's gradual leading of the sinner toward destruction through the granting of apparent blessings (Quran 7:182-183). What the community interprets as divine approval is actually divine patience, giving them opportunity for repentance before the final judgment falls. The distinction between *istidraj* and genuine divine blessing is one that Islamic scholars consider essential for the correct interpretation of historical events.

Jeremiah's final warning to the Egyptian community (44:24-30) embodies the Islamic understanding that prophetic responsibility extends to delivering the message clearly, but the prophet cannot compel acceptance. The Quran affirms: "You are only a warner" (35:23). Jeremiah's oracle discharges his prophetic duty; the community's rejection seals their fate. This narrative reinforces the Quranic teaching that on the Day of Judgment, the prophets will testify that they delivered God's message, and the disobedient will have no excuse.
