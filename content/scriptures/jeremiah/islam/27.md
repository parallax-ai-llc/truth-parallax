Jeremiah 27 presents the prophet's sign-act of the yoke, declaring that all nations must submit to Nebuchadnezzar by God's decree. Islamic interpreters recognize in this chapter the Quranic principle that God grants sovereignty to whom He wills and removes it from whom He wills (Quran 3:26). The designation of Nebuchadnezzar as God's "servant" does not sanctify his paganism but demonstrates that even unbelieving rulers operate within the framework of divine *qadr* (decree). This understanding underlies the Islamic political theology expressed in the concept of *tamkin* — God's establishment of authority on earth.

The chapter's condemnation of false prophets, diviners, and soothsayers (27:9-10) aligns precisely with the Quran's denunciation of *kahanah* (soothsaying) and unauthorized claims to knowledge of the unseen (*'ilm al-ghayb*). The Prophet Muhammad taught that "whoever goes to a fortune-teller and believes what he says has disbelieved in what was revealed to Muhammad" (Sahih Muslim). Jeremiah's distinction between authentic divine speech and human fabrication mirrors the Islamic distinction between *wahy* (revelation) and every counterfeit form of spiritual authority.

Jeremiah's call to submit to the yoke — to accept a divinely decreed hardship rather than rebel against it — resonates with the Islamic concept of *islam* itself, which means submission to God's will. The nations who refuse the yoke are not merely making a political miscalculation; they are refusing to submit to God's decree, which is the fundamental sin in Islamic theology. Acceptance of divine destiny, even when it involves suffering and subjugation, is a mark of authentic faith.
