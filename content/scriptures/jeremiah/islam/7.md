The Temple Sermon of Jeremiah 7 — the warning against trusting in "the temple of the Lord" as a guarantee of safety — resonates powerfully with the Quranic critique of ritualism without genuine faith. The Quran teaches that the mere possession of sacred sites or sacred lineage does not guarantee divine favor: "It is not your wealth or your children that will bring you closer to Us" (Quran 34:37). The people of Mecca similarly trusted in the Kaaba's presence as a guarantee of their security before Islam, and the Quran corrected this assumption.

The reference to Shiloh (7:12-14) — where God's dwelling was destroyed because of Israel's sin — demonstrates the Islamic principle that sacred spaces are instruments of God's will, not autonomous sources of blessing. The Quran's account of the Kaaba's history — built by Ibrahim and Ismail, then corrupted by centuries of idolatry before being purified by the Prophet Muhammad — follows a parallel logic. No sacred site is immune from the consequences of the worshippers' unfaithfulness, a principle that Islamic theology applies universally.

The catalogue of sins in 7:5-11 — oppression of orphans, widows, and foreigners; theft; murder; idolatry — mirrors the Quranic ethical framework, which consistently links worship of God with justice toward fellow human beings. The Quran describes those who pray but neglect the poor as denying the Day of Judgment itself (Quran 107:1-7). Jeremiah's Temple Sermon, in Islamic reading, confirms the prophetic principle that authentic worship of God is inseparable from just treatment of His creatures.
