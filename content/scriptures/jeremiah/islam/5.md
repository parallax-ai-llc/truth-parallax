The futile search for a single righteous person in Jerusalem (5:1) echoes the Quranic narrative of prophets searching for believers among their communities and finding almost none. Nuh (Noah) preached for 950 years and gained few followers (Quran 71:5-6); Lut (Lot) found only one household of believers (Quran 11:81). The pattern of minority righteousness amid majority corruption is one of the most consistent themes in the Quranic presentation of prophetic history, and Jeremiah's experience in Jerusalem exemplifies it.

The people's dismissal of prophetic warning — "He will do nothing; no disaster will come upon us" (5:12) — represents the attitude that the Quran describes as *ghurur* (delusion or self-deception). The Quran warns repeatedly against those who are deluded by worldly comfort into ignoring the reality of divine judgment: "Let not the worldly life delude you, and let not the Deceiver deceive you about Allah" (Quran 31:33). Islamic interpreters read Jeremiah's contemporaries as exemplifying this Quranic warning.

The rhetorical question "Shall I not punish them for these things?" (5:29) affirms the principle of divine justice that is central to Islamic theology. The Quran teaches that God never punishes a people unjustly and that every punishment is proportionate to the offense (Quran 4:40). The destruction of Jerusalem was not arbitrary but was the just consequence of persistent disobedience — a principle that Islamic theology applies universally, teaching that God's judgment, while sometimes delayed, is always certain and always just.
