Jeremiah's complaint about the prosperity of the wicked (12:1) engages with a question that Islamic theology addresses through the concepts of *hikmah* (divine wisdom) and *qadr* (divine decree). The Quran acknowledges that worldly prosperity does not indicate divine favor: "Do they think that because We have granted them abundance of wealth and children, We hasten to them with good things? Nay, they perceive not" (Quran 23:55-56). The apparent prosperity of the wicked is understood in Islamic theology as either a test (*ibtila'*) or a delay (*imhal*) before the ultimate reckoning.

God's response to Jeremiah — "If you have raced with men on foot and they have wearied you, how will you compete with horses?" (12:5) — resonates with the Quranic concept of *sabr* (patience and endurance), which is one of the most emphasized virtues in Islam. The Quran promises that "Allah is with the patient" (Quran 2:153) and describes the progressive nature of spiritual testing. Islamic spiritual tradition understands that God's servants are tested in proportion to their faith, with greater faith bringing greater trials — a principle that Jeremiah's experience illustrates.

The vision of the nations being restored (12:14-17) aligns with the Islamic understanding of God's *rahmah* (mercy) as encompassing all of creation. The Quran declares that the Prophet Muhammad was sent as "a mercy to all the worlds" (Quran 21:107), and Islamic theology affirms that God's mercy extends even to those who have been punished, offering the possibility of repentance and restoration. Jeremiah's vision of conquered nations learning the ways of God's people anticipates the Islamic concept of peoples entering Islam through contact with the Muslim community.
