Jeremiah 34 narrates the broken covenant of liberty, and Islamic interpreters read this episode as illustrating the Quranic condemnation of those who make promises to God and then break them. The Quran warns: "And fulfill the covenant of Allah when you have taken it, and do not break oaths after their confirmation while you have made Allah a witness over you" (Quran 16:91). The re-enslavement of freed slaves is not merely a social injustice but a violation of a sacred covenant — an act of *nifaq* (hypocrisy) that pretends devotion under pressure and abandons it when the pressure lifts.

The specific issue of slave release resonates deeply with Islamic jurisprudence, which consistently encourages manumission as an act of piety. The Quran identifies the freeing of slaves as one of the paths to righteousness (Quran 90:12-13), and numerous hadiths record the Prophet Muhammad encouraging and practicing manumission. The sin condemned in Jeremiah 34 is doubly grievous in Islamic terms: it involves both the violation of a covenant and the re-enslavement of people who had been granted their God-given right to liberty.

God's ironic judgment — proclaiming "liberty" to the sword, pestilence, and famine (34:17) — illustrates the Islamic principle of divine reciprocity (*jaza'*): those who withhold justice from others will find justice withheld from them. The Quran teaches: "Is the reward of good anything but good?" (Quran 55:60), and conversely, those who repay divine mercy with covenant-breaking will experience the consequences of their faithlessness. Islamic theology insists that divine judgment is never arbitrary but always corresponds precisely to the nature of the human offense.
