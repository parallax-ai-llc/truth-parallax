Jeremiah 21 records the prophet's response to Zedekiah's appeal during the Babylonian siege — God fights against His own people with "outstretched hand and strong arm." Islamic interpreters read this as a demonstration of the Quranic principle that God uses earthly powers as instruments of His will. The Quran describes how God employed various means to punish disobedient nations — wind against 'Ad (Quran 69:6), earthquake against Thamud (Quran 69:5), flood against the people of Nuh (Quran 71:25). Nebuchadnezzar and his army served as the instrument of God's decree against Jerusalem.

The counsel to surrender (21:8-10) is understood in Islamic political theology through the concept of submission to the divine decree (*qadr*). When God has decreed an outcome, resistance is futile and potentially sinful. The Quran teaches: "Say, 'Nothing will happen to us except what Allah has decreed for us'" (Quran 9:51). Jeremiah's counsel to surrender to Babylon is thus not treason but theological realism — the recognition that fighting against God's decree leads only to greater suffering.

The demand for justice directed at the royal house (21:11-12) reflects the Islamic principle that political authority (*wilayah*) is a trust (*amanah*) from God that must be exercised justly. The Quran commands rulers: "Indeed, Allah commands you to render trusts to whom they are due and when you judge between people to judge with justice" (Quran 4:58). Islamic political philosophy, from al-Mawardi through Ibn Khaldun, has consistently maintained that rulers are accountable to divine law and that unjust rule forfeits legitimacy.
