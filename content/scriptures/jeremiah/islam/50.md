Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 50 reads the oracle against Babylon through the Quranic theology of divine retribution on oppressive empires. The Quran repeatedly presents the destruction of arrogant civilizations as *sunnat Allah* (God's established way): "Such is the way of God with those who passed on before, and you will not find any change in the way of God" (Quran 33:62). Babylon's fall — the empire that destroyed the Temple and exiled God's people — exemplifies this unchanging divine pattern of holding the powerful accountable for their treatment of the vulnerable.

The interweaving of Babylon's judgment with Israel's restoration (50:4-7) illustrates the Quranic principle that God's justice and mercy operate simultaneously. The Quran affirms that God replaces unjust nations with new communities (Quran 47:38) and that the oppressed will inherit what the oppressors possessed. The prophetic vision of Babylon's fall and Israel's return is not merely a historical prediction but a demonstration of the divine *'adl* (justice) that governs the succession of civilizations.

Babylon's description as "the hammer of the whole earth" (50:23) that is itself broken resonates powerfully with the Quranic accounts of civilizations that considered themselves invincible. The people of 'Ad boasted "Who is mightier than us in power?" (Quran 41:15), and God responded by destroying them with a wind. Islamic scholars read Jeremiah 50 as confirming the Quranic principle that no human power, however overwhelming, can withstand divine decree — a principle that provides comfort to oppressed communities throughout Islamic history.
