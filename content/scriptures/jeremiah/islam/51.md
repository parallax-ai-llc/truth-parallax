Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 51 reads the climactic oracle against Babylon through the Quranic theology of divine retribution on oppressive civilizations. The declaration that Babylon "has been a golden cup in the LORD's hand, making all the earth drunken" (51:7) resonates with the Quranic description of civilizations that spread corruption (*fasad*) throughout the earth. Surah Ar-Rum (30:41) states: "Corruption has appeared on land and sea because of what people's hands have earned," and Babylon's role as an agent of global disruption exemplifies this corrupting influence.

The creation theology embedded in the oracle (51:15-19) aligns with the Quranic emphasis on God's creative power as the foundation of His authority to judge. The Quran repeatedly invokes creation as evidence of divine sovereignty: "Do they not look at the camels, how they are created? And at the sky, how it is raised? And at the mountains, how they are fixed? And at the earth, how it is spread out?" (Quran 88:17-20). The God who creates has the absolute right to destroy, and Babylon's fall demonstrates this right exercised in history.

The symbolic sinking of the scroll in the Euphrates (51:63-64) is understood in Islamic tradition as a prophetic act that embodies divine *qadar* (decree). Once the prophetic word is spoken and symbolically enacted, the outcome is sealed — no human power can alter what God has decreed. The finality of Seraiah's act — "Thus shall Babylon sink, to rise no more" — echoes the Quranic descriptions of destroyed civilizations that will never be rebuilt, their ruins serving as perpetual signs (*ayat*) for those who reflect.
