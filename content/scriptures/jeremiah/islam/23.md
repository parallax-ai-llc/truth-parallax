Jeremiah 23's prophecy of the "righteous Branch" is understood in Islamic interpretation not as pointing to Jesus (as Christians read it) but as describing a future righteous ruler from David's line whose coming would restore justice. Islamic theology does not affirm the Christian identification of this figure with a divine Messiah, since the Quran teaches that Jesus (*Isa*) was a prophet and servant of God, not divine (Quran 5:72-75). The messianic concept is acknowledged but reinterpreted within the framework of *tawhid* — no human figure, however righteous, shares in the divine nature.

The extended critique of false prophets (23:9-32) provides material that Islamic scholars have used to develop criteria for authenticating prophetic claims. The Quranic standard is clear: a true prophet's message must be consistent with previous revelation, confirmed by signs, and characterized by moral integrity. The false prophets of Jeremiah's time failed on all counts — they were morally corrupt, their visions were self-generated, and their message contradicted the Torah's warnings. Islamic heresiographical literature (*milal wa nihal*) applies similar criteria to distinguish authentic from fraudulent claims to prophecy.

The declaration that God's word is "like fire" and "like a hammer" (23:29) resonates with the Quranic description of divine revelation as weighty and overwhelming. The Quran describes itself as "a weighty word" (*qawlan thaqilan*, Quran 73:5), and the *hadith* literature records that the Prophet Muhammad experienced physical symptoms during revelation — sweating, heaviness, and hearing sounds. The divine word is not gentle information but a force that transforms whatever it touches, a reality that both Jeremiah and the Quranic tradition affirm.
