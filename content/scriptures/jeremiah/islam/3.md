Jeremiah 3's invitation to return despite gross unfaithfulness resonates with the Islamic theology of *tawbah* (repentance). The Quran emphatically declares that God is *Al-Tawwab* (the Accepter of Repentance) and *Al-Ghafur* (the Most Forgiving), and that no sin is beyond forgiveness for those who sincerely repent (Quran 39:53). Jeremiah's portrayal of God inviting back an adulterous people exceeds even the provisions of the Torah's own divorce law, illustrating a divine mercy that Islamic theology considers one of God's most fundamental attributes.

The distinction between Israel's open apostasy and Judah's pretended repentance (3:10) aligns with the Quranic concept of *nifaq* (hypocrisy) — outward conformity to God's commands without inner sincerity. The Quran devotes extensive attention to the *munafiqun* (hypocrites), describing them as more dangerous than open disbelievers because they corrupt the community from within (Quran 2:8-20, 63:1-8). Jeremiah's critique of Judah's "pretend" return represents the same prophetic discernment that the Quran exercises in exposing insincere faith.

The eschatological vision of 3:17 — "all nations shall gather to Jerusalem, to the presence of the Lord" — is read in Islamic interpretation through the lens of the Quranic teaching that all prophets called their peoples to the same fundamental message: *tawhid* (the oneness of God). The gathering of nations to acknowledge the true God represents the eschatological fulfillment of the prophetic mission that began with Adam and will culminate on the Day of Judgment, when all humanity will stand before the one God.
