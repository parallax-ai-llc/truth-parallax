The vision of the two baskets of figs in Jeremiah 24 reverses conventional expectations — those in exile are favored while those remaining in Jerusalem are condemned. Islamic interpreters read this reversal as consistent with the Quranic principle that God's favor operates according to His wisdom, not human assumptions. The Quran teaches: "Perhaps you dislike a thing and it is good for you; and perhaps you like a thing and it is bad for you. And Allah knows, while you know not" (Quran 2:216). The exiles' apparent misfortune was, in God's wisdom, the means of their preservation.

The promise "I will give them a heart to know that I am the Lord" (24:7) is understood in Islamic theology as an instance of divine guidance (*hidayah*) — God opening the heart to receive truth. The Quran teaches that guidance is ultimately from God: "Whoever Allah guides, he is the rightly guided; and whoever He sends astray, you will never find for him a guiding protector" (Quran 18:17). Islamic theology maintains that while God provides the capacity for faith, the individual must also exercise choice — a synergy between divine initiative and human response.

The exilic experience — removal from homeland, temple, and familiar structures — is read in Islamic interpretation through the lens of *hijrah* (migration for the sake of faith). The Prophet Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina is the paradigmatic *hijrah*, and Islamic theology understands forced displacement as potentially serving God's redemptive purposes. The Babylonian exile, like the *hijrah*, stripped away external supports and forced the community to depend on God alone, ultimately strengthening their faith and identity.
