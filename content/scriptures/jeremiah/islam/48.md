Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 48 reads the oracle against Moab through the Quranic framework of divine judgment on nations that succumb to pride and self-sufficiency. Moab's sin — "We have heard of the pride of Moab" (48:29) — corresponds to the Quranic concept of *istikbar* (arrogance), which the Quran identifies as the defining characteristic of Iblis (Satan) and the root cause of every community's downfall. Surah Al-A'raf (7:36) declares that "those who reject Our signs and treat them with arrogance — they are companions of the Fire," and Moab's fate illustrates this principle.

The divine lament over Moab's destruction (48:31-36) is read by Islamic scholars as consistent with the Quranic teaching that God is *al-Rahman* and *al-Rahim* (the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful) even in judgment. The Prophet Muhammad taught that God's mercy encompasses all things (Quran 7:156), and the prophetic grief over Moab demonstrates that divine judgment is not administered with indifference but with a compassion that human beings can barely comprehend. This understanding informs the Islamic approach to the destruction of any people: judgment is real, but it proceeds from a God who takes no pleasure in destruction.

The promise of restoration "in the latter days" (48:47) is interpreted in Islamic eschatology within the framework of *yawm al-qiyamah* (the Day of Resurrection), when all nations and individuals will face final judgment. Islamic tradition generally reads such prophetic promises as pointing toward the ultimate accountability of all peoples before God, rather than as predictions of specific historical restorations. The principle that God's mercy has the last word is central to Islamic eschatological hope.
