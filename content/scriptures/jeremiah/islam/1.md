The call narrative of Jeremiah — God's declaration that He knew and consecrated the prophet before his birth (1:5) — resonates with the Islamic understanding of prophetic election. In Islamic theology, all prophets (*anbiya*) are chosen by God before their birth and prepared for their mission through divine providence. Al-Tabari, in his *Tarikh*, identifies Jeremiah (*Irmiya*) as a prophet sent to the Bani Israil (Children of Israel) during the period before the Babylonian destruction, and presents his calling as consistent with the Quranic pattern of prophetic commissioning found throughout the stories of the prophets.

The young prophet's protest of inadequacy and God's reassurance parallel the experiences of other prophets in both biblical and Quranic tradition. Moses's reluctance at the burning bush (Quran 20:25-36) and Muhammad's initial fear upon receiving revelation in the Cave of Hira are understood within the same pattern: the divine call overwhelms human capacity, but God provides the strength necessary for the mission. Islamic interpreters read Jeremiah's call as further evidence of the unity of the prophetic experience across all dispensations.

The two visions — the almond branch and the boiling pot from the north — are understood in Islamic interpretation as instances of God communicating through signs and symbols, a mode of revelation recognized in the Quran. The Quranic concept of *ayat* (signs) encompasses both natural phenomena and supernatural visions, and Jeremiah's inaugural visions fall within this category. The boiling pot tilting from the north, prophesying the Babylonian invasion, demonstrates God's sovereignty over the nations — a theme that the Quran emphasizes repeatedly in its accounts of the rise and fall of civilizations.
