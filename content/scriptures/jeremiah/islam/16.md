The divine command that Jeremiah remain celibate (16:1-4) is understood in Islamic interpretation as an exceptional prophetic sign, not a normative model. Islam generally encourages marriage as a prophetic *sunnah* — the Prophet Muhammad said, "Marriage is half of faith" — and celibacy as a permanent state is not promoted. Jeremiah's celibacy is thus read as an extraordinary enacted prophecy specific to his historical situation: the coming destruction was so certain and so catastrophic that the normal blessings of family life were suspended as a sign of impending doom.

The prohibition against mourning and feasting (16:5-9) — severing the prophet from the social fabric of community life — is read in Islamic interpretation as an extreme form of *zuhd* (ascetic detachment from the world). While Islam does not prescribe monastic withdrawal (the Quran says "We did not prescribe monasticism for them," Quran 57:27), it recognizes that prophets sometimes exemplify a radical detachment that serves as a sign to their communities. Jeremiah's enforced isolation is understood as a warning that the social bonds his people take for granted are about to be destroyed.

The prophecy that nations will come from the ends of the earth to acknowledge the true God (16:19-21) aligns with the Islamic conviction that *tawhid* (pure monotheism) is the original and ultimate religion of all humanity. The Quran teaches that every nation received a prophet (Quran 10:47, 16:36) and that the final revelation through Muhammad was intended to confirm and complete all previous prophetic messages. Jeremiah's vision of universal acknowledgment of the one God is understood as an anticipation of Islam's global mission.
