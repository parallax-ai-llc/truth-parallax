Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 45 reads God's message to Baruch through the lens of prophetic companionship (*suhba*). Just as the Companions of the Prophet Muhammad (*Sahabah*) endured hardship and sacrifice for the sake of the divine message, Baruch's suffering in service to Jeremiah illustrates the cost of faithfulness to prophetic truth. The Islamic tradition holds the companions of all prophets in high regard, and Baruch's lament and God's response provide a window into the spiritual struggles that attend proximity to prophetic mission.

The divine command "Do not seek great things for yourself" (45:5) resonates with the Islamic virtue of *zuhd* (renunciation of worldly attachment). The Prophet Muhammad taught that the world is a prison for the believer and a paradise for the disbeliever (Sahih Muslim), and Baruch's instruction to relinquish personal ambition aligns with this teaching. Al-Hasan al-Basri and later Sufi masters emphasized that the servant of God must empty the heart of worldly desire in order to be filled with divine purpose.

The promise of life preserved "as a prize of war" speaks to the Islamic concept of *rizq* (divine provision). In times of universal calamity, survival itself is a gift from God, and the believer who receives it is entrusted with the responsibility of carrying the divine message forward. Baruch's role in preserving Jeremiah's words parallels the role of the *huffaz* (memorizers of the Quran) and the transmitters of *hadith*, who ensured that the prophetic word survived the upheavals of history.
