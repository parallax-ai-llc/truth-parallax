Jeremiah 31 contains the prophecy of the new covenant, which Islamic interpreters read in light of the Quranic concept of successive covenants (*mithaq*) between God and humanity. The Quran affirms that God has taken covenants from the prophets (Quran 3:81) and from humanity as a whole (Quran 7:172), and each prophetic dispensation renews and deepens the covenant relationship. The promise that God will write the law "on their hearts" (31:33) resonates with the Islamic concept of *fitra* — the innate disposition toward God that is inscribed in every human soul at creation (Quran 30:30).

The vision of universal knowledge of God — "they shall all know me, from the least of them to the greatest" (31:34) — aligns with Islam's understanding of its own universal mission. The Quran describes Muhammad as a messenger "to all of mankind" (Quran 34:28), and the Islamic *da'wa* (invitation) is directed to every human being without distinction of rank or status. The democratization of divine knowledge that Jeremiah prophesies finds its fulfillment, in the Islamic view, in the Quran's accessibility — a revelation in clear Arabic that requires no priestly mediator for its comprehension.

Rachel's weeping for her children (31:15) resonates with the Quranic attention to maternal suffering as a sign of divine compassion. The Quran commands kindness to parents and specifically highlights the suffering of mothers: "His mother carried him in weakness upon weakness" (Quran 31:14). The promise that Rachel's children "shall come back" (31:16-17) reflects the Quranic assurance that God does not waste the suffering of the faithful: "Indeed, Allah does not allow to be lost the reward of the doers of good" (Quran 12:90).
