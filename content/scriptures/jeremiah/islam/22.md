Jeremiah 22's evaluation of the kings of Judah aligns with the Islamic principle that rulers are judged by their fidelity to divine law and their treatment of the vulnerable. The Quranic concept of *'adl* (justice) is central to Islamic governance: "O you who believe, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah" (Quran 4:135). The contrast between Josiah, who practiced justice and "knew God," and Jehoiakim, who exploited workers, illustrates the Quranic teaching that knowledge of God and practice of justice are inseparable.

The condemnation of forced labor (22:13-17) resonates with Islamic labor law, which strictly protects workers' rights. A *hadith* records the Prophet Muhammad saying: "Give the worker his wages before his sweat dries." The exploitation described in Jeremiah 22 — making neighbors serve without payment — is categorically prohibited in Islamic law (*fiqh*), and rulers who engage in such practices are considered guilty of *zulm* (oppression), one of the gravest sins in Islamic ethics. Jeremiah's prophetic indictment provides the biblical parallel to these Islamic principles.

The prophecy against Coniah/Jehoiachin (22:24-30) illustrates the Islamic teaching that lineage alone does not guarantee divine favor. The Quran explicitly rejects the notion that biological descent from a prophet ensures salvation: "And when Abraham was tried by his Lord with commands... He said, 'And of my descendants?' Allah said, 'My covenant does not include the wrongdoers'" (Quran 2:124). Islamic theology insists that each person is judged by their own deeds, not by their ancestry — a principle that Jeremiah's oracle against the Davidic heir confirms.
