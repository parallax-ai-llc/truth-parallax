Jeremiah 36 narrates the writing, burning, and rewriting of Jeremiah's scroll, and Islamic interpreters read this episode in light of the Quranic emphasis on the preservation of divine revelation. The Quran affirms: "Indeed, it is We who sent down the message, and indeed, We will be its guardian" (Quran 15:9). Jehoiakim's burning of the scroll illustrates the human attempt to destroy revelation, while the rewriting demonstrates God's commitment to preserving His word. Islamic theology draws a contrast: whereas earlier scriptures were subject to *tahrif* (alteration and corruption), the Quran has been divinely protected from such interference.

The role of Baruch as Jeremiah's scribe parallels the Islamic tradition of the *kuttab al-wahy* — the scribes of revelation who recorded the Quranic revelations at the Prophet Muhammad's direction. Zayd ibn Thabit, the chief scribe, functioned in a role analogous to Baruch's, transcribing divine speech as dictated by the prophet. Islamic tradition honors the scribes of revelation as essential participants in the preservation of God's word, recognizing that divine speech requires faithful human transmission.

Jehoiakim's contemptuous destruction of the scroll — cutting it with a knife and burning it in the brazier — represents the ultimate act of *kufr* (rejection of faith): the deliberate destruction of divine communication. The Quran warns that those who reject God's signs (*ayat*) will face severe consequences: "Those who deny Our signs — We will drive them into a fire" (Quran 4:56). Jehoiakim's attempt to destroy the word of God by fire is itself answered by divine fire — a principle of reciprocal justice that Islamic theology recognizes as characteristic of God's dealings with the defiant.
