Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 46 reads the oracle against Egypt within the Quranic framework of God's sovereignty over all nations and civilizations. The Quran presents the rise and fall of empires as a recurring pattern (*sunnat Allah*) demonstrating divine power: "How many a generation have We destroyed before them!" (Quran 19:74). Egypt's defeat at Carchemish and its subsequent invasion by Nebuchadnezzar illustrate this pattern — no nation, however ancient or powerful, is exempt from God's judgment.

The military imagery of Jeremiah 46 — the marshaling of forces, the confident advance, and the catastrophic rout — recalls the Quranic accounts of the destruction of Pharaoh's army at the Sea (Quran 26:60-68). Islamic interpreters see a deliberate echo: Egypt, which once pursued God's people and was destroyed by water, now faces destruction by a northern army that advances like a flood (46:7-8). The repetition of Egypt's humiliation across centuries demonstrates the Quranic principle that God's *sunnah* (established way) does not change (Quran 33:62).

The concluding promise to Israel (46:27-28) is read in Islamic tradition as consistent with the Quranic affirmation that God fulfills His covenants. While Islamic theology maintains that the final covenant is the one established through Muhammad, it recognizes that God's earlier covenants with the Bani Israil were genuine and that His promises to them were fulfilled in their historical context. The distinction between the destruction of the nations and the discipline of Israel reflects God's differentiated dealings with communities according to their covenant relationship with Him.
