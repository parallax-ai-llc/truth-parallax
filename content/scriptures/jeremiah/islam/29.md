Jeremiah 29 presents the prophet's letter to the Babylonian exiles, and Islamic interpreters find in this passage profound resonances with the Quranic theme of patience (*sabr*) in the face of divine testing. The instruction to settle in Babylon, build houses, and plant gardens (29:5-7) parallels the Quranic command to remain steadfast during trials: "O you who have believed, seek help through patience and prayer. Indeed, Allah is with the patient" (Quran 2:153). The exiles are called not to fruitless resistance but to constructive patience — a posture that Islamic theology distinguishes from passive resignation.

The command to "seek the welfare of the city" (29:7) resonates with the Islamic concept of *maslaha* (public interest) and the prophetic hadith that "the best of people are those most beneficial to people." Islamic jurisprudence recognizes that Muslims living as minorities in non-Muslim societies have obligations to contribute to the common good, a principle that finds its scriptural antecedent in Jeremiah's letter. The concept of *muwatana* (citizenship) in modern Islamic political thought draws on this tradition of constructive engagement with the broader society.

The promise of restoration after seventy years (29:10) demonstrates the Islamic principle that God's plans unfold according to His timeline, not human impatience. The Quran repeatedly affirms that God's promise is true but that human beings are hasty: "Man is ever hasty" (Quran 17:11). The false prophets in exile who promise immediate return (29:21-32) represent precisely this human impatience — the refusal to submit to God's timetable, which is itself a failure of *tawakkul* (trust in God).
