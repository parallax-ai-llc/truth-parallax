The remnant's request for divine guidance and their oath of obedience (42:1-6) followed by their refusal to comply illustrate a pattern that the Quran identifies as characteristic of the Bani Israil's repeated interactions with their prophets. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:93) records a similar dynamic: "We hear and we disobey." Islamic scholars read Jeremiah 42 as further evidence that the prophetic message across all dispensations is essentially one — submission to God's will — and that the human resistance to that message is equally consistent.

The ten-day delay before God's response teaches the Islamic virtue of *sabr* (patience). The prophetic tradition (*hadith*) emphasizes that patience in awaiting God's guidance is itself an act of worship. Al-Ghazali, in the *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, identifies impatience with divine timing as a form of subtle *shirk* (associating partners with God), because it assumes that human urgency should override divine wisdom. The remnant's inability to wait faithfully reveals their spiritual condition.

The stark clarity of God's oracle — remain and be blessed, flee and perish — embodies the Quranic principle that God's guidance is always clear and sufficient. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:256) declares "the right path has become distinct from error," and the prophetic word in Jeremiah 42 leaves no room for misunderstanding. The people's subsequent disobedience cannot be attributed to ambiguity in the message but only to willful rejection of it, the very pattern for which the Quran holds previous communities accountable.
