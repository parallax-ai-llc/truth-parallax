Jeremiah 37 narrates the prophet's imprisonment, and Islamic interpreters read this chapter as another instance of the universal pattern of prophetic persecution described throughout the Quran. The Quran states: "And We have already sent messengers before you. Among them are those whose stories We have related to you, and among them are those whose stories We have not related to you" (Quran 40:78). Jeremiah's imprisonment is part of this larger narrative of prophetic suffering, a pattern that includes the persecution of Ibrahim, Musa, 'Isa, and Muhammad himself.

Zedekiah's secret consultation with Jeremiah (37:17) illustrates the Quranic category of those who recognize the truth privately but refuse to confess it publicly. The Quran describes the hypocrites (*munafiqun*) as those who "say with their mouths what is not in their hearts" (Quran 3:167), and while Zedekiah's case is the reverse — he believes in his heart what he will not say with his mouth — the underlying failure is the same: a disconnect between inner conviction and outward action. Islamic ethics insists on the unity of belief and practice (*iman* and *'amal*), and Zedekiah's failure illustrates the consequences of their separation.

The false accusation of desertion (37:13) resonates with the Prophet Muhammad's own experience of being accused of fabrication and madness by his opponents: "And they say, 'O you upon whom the message has been sent down, indeed you are mad'" (Quran 15:6). Islamic tradition teaches that false accusations against prophets are a tool of those who resist divine truth, and the believer's proper response is patience (*sabr*) and continued faithfulness to the prophetic mission, as Jeremiah demonstrates throughout his imprisonment.
