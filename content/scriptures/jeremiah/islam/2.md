Jeremiah 2's covenant lawsuit against Israel, depicting the people's abandonment of God for idols, is read in Islamic interpretation as a classic instance of the Quranic pattern of nations turning to *shirk* (associating partners with God) after receiving divine guidance. The Quran repeatedly describes how communities received prophets, initially followed their guidance, then gradually returned to idolatry — a pattern exemplified by the Bani Israil throughout their history (Quran 2:83-86, 5:70).

The metaphor of the broken cisterns (2:13) — forsaking the fountain of living water for containers that cannot hold water — resonates with the Quranic imagery of those who reject divine guidance in favor of false deities that can neither help nor harm (Quran 7:197, 35:13-14). Islamic interpreters read this as confirming the Quranic teaching that *shirk* is fundamentally irrational: the exchange of the infinite God for finite, powerless objects represents a catastrophic failure of reason as well as faith.

The catalogue of Israel's infidelities — seeking alliances with Egypt and Assyria rather than trusting in God — illustrates what the Quran calls *tawakkul* (trust in God) and its opposite. The Quranic command "Put your trust in Allah if you are truly believers" (Quran 5:23) presents the same demand that Jeremiah makes: political alliances and human strategies cannot substitute for genuine reliance on divine guidance. Islamic political theology, drawing on both Quranic and biblical-Isra'iliyyat material, consistently warns against placing trust in worldly powers rather than in God's decree.
