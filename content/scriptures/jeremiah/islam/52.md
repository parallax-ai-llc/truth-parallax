Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 52 reads the historical account of Jerusalem's fall as confirmation of the Quranic narrative about the Bani Israil's repeated transgressions and the divine punishments that followed. The Quran (17:4-8) specifically references the two corruptions of the Children of Israel and the two destructions of the Temple, and Jeremiah 52 provides the detailed historical record of the first destruction that the Quran summarizes. Islamic scholarship treats this chapter as historical corroboration of Quranic revelation.

The destruction of the Temple described in Jeremiah 52 is understood in Islamic theology not as God's rejection of the Temple concept — Islam honors the sanctity of all divinely ordained places of worship — but as the consequence of the community's failure to uphold the covenant that the Temple embodied. The Quran teaches that God does not change the condition of a people until they change what is within themselves (Quran 13:11), and the destruction of the Temple follows the internal moral collapse of the community that worshipped there.

The release of Jehoiachin from prison (52:31-34) is read in Islamic tradition as an instance of God's mercy (*rahmah*) operating even within the context of judgment. The Quran teaches that God's mercy encompasses all things (7:156), and the liberation of the exiled king demonstrates that divine compassion is never entirely withdrawn, even from a community under severe divine discipline. The book of Jeremiah closes with this sign of mercy, consistent with the Islamic understanding that God's essential nature is mercy and that judgment, however severe, serves ultimately merciful purposes.
