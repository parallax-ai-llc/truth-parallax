The potter and clay imagery of Jeremiah 18 is read in Islamic theology through the lens of *qadr* (divine decree) and *mashiah* (divine will). The image of God as the potter who shapes nations according to His purpose aligns with the Quranic affirmation of God's absolute sovereignty: "He creates what He wills" (Quran 42:49). At the same time, the conditional statements of 18:7-10 — that God's plan changes in response to human repentance or rebellion — reflect the Islamic understanding that *tawbah* (repentance) can alter the course of divine decree within the framework of God's ultimate will.

The potter's reworking of a flawed vessel (18:4) resonates with the Quranic teaching that God is always willing to accept repentance and begin anew with His servants. The Quran declares: "Allah wishes to accept your repentance" (Quran 4:27). The image of the divine artisan who does not discard flawed work but reshapes it reflects God's patience (*hilm*) and mercy (*rahmah*), two of the most emphasized divine attributes in Islamic theology. The potter's workshop thus becomes a parable of divine grace operating within human history.

The plot against Jeremiah (18:18-23) exemplifies the Quranic pattern of prophetic persecution. The Quran records that virtually every prophet faced opposition, conspiracy, and often violence from those they were sent to guide. The response that Jeremiah models — appealing to God rather than taking vengeance personally — is the response the Quran commends: "And be patient over what they say and avoid them with gracious avoidance" (Quran 73:10). The prophet entrusts his cause to the divine Judge, confident that God's justice will ultimately prevail.
