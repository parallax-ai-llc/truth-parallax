Jeremiah's arrest by Pashhur and subsequent lament are read in Islamic interpretation as illustrating the *sabr* (patience and steadfastness) required of prophets in the face of institutional persecution. The Quran describes the trials of prophets as part of God's testing: "Or do you think that you will enter Paradise while such trial has not yet come to you as came to those who passed on before you?" (Quran 2:214). Jeremiah's suffering at the hands of the Temple establishment parallels the experiences of Muhammad, who was persecuted by the Quraysh establishment in Mecca.

The prophet's internal struggle (20:7-9) — the tension between the desire to stop prophesying and the impossibility of suppressing the divine word — is understood in Islamic tradition through the concept of *amanah* (trust or responsibility). The Quran describes the prophetic burden as a trust that must be fulfilled regardless of personal cost: "We offered the trust to the heavens and the earth and the mountains, and they declined to bear it and feared it; but man bore it" (Quran 33:72). Jeremiah's inability to suppress the word within him reflects the weight of this prophetic *amanah*.

The cursing of the day of his birth (20:14-18) presents an interpretive challenge for Islamic theology, which holds the doctrine of prophetic *ismah* (infallibility or protection from major sin). Muslim scholars who accept Jeremiah as a prophet understand this outburst not as a sin but as an expression of extreme human anguish that does not constitute rejection of God or His decree. The prophets remain human (*bashar*) while fulfilling their divine mission, and their moments of anguish demonstrate the authenticity and cost of their prophetic experience.
