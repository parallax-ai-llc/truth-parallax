Islamic interpretation of Jeremiah 47 reads the oracle against the Philistines as another instance of God's universal judgment on nations that transgress the divine moral order. The Quran presents the destruction of ancient peoples — 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lut — as evidence that no civilization is immune to divine retribution, and the Philistines' destruction by Babylonian armies falls within this same pattern. The brevity of the oracle (seven verses) may itself reflect the swiftness of divine judgment when it finally falls.

The imagery of waters rising from the north (47:2) to overwhelm the land connects, in Islamic interpretation, to the Quranic theme of water as an instrument of both blessing and judgment. The Quran describes the flood of Nuh (71:25) and the drowning of Pharaoh's army (2:50) as acts of divine justice mediated through water. Jeremiah's vision of Philistia overwhelmed by a flood from the north extends this symbolic pattern to another nation whose time of judgment has arrived.

The rhetorical question at the chapter's close — "How can the sword of the LORD rest when He has given it a charge?" (47:7) — embodies the Islamic understanding of *qadar* (divine decree). Once God has decreed a matter, it cannot be averted by human effort or negotiation. The Quran affirms: "When God decrees a matter, He only says to it 'Be,' and it is" (2:117). The sword of the Lord, personified in Jeremiah's oracle, acts with the irresistible force of divine decree, and the Philistines' appeal for mercy comes too late.
