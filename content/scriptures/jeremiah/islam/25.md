Jeremiah 25's prophecy of seventy years of Babylonian domination (25:11-12) demonstrates God's sovereignty over historical timelines. Islamic theology affirms that God determines the rise and fall of nations according to His wisdom: "Say, 'O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will'" (Quran 3:26). The precise specification of seventy years illustrates the Quranic teaching that every nation has an appointed term (*ajal*) that cannot be delayed or advanced (Quran 10:49).

The cup of wrath imagery (25:15-29) — all nations compelled to drink from the cup of divine fury — resonates with the Quranic descriptions of the *yawm al-qiyamah* (Day of Resurrection) when all nations will face divine judgment. The Quran describes this day as one of universal reckoning: "And the earth will shine with the light of its Lord, and the record will be placed, and the prophets and the witnesses will be brought" (Quran 39:69). Jeremiah's vision of historical judgment on the nations prefigures the eschatological judgment that the Quran describes in its most vivid passages.

The universal scope of judgment — beginning with Jerusalem and extending to all nations, including Babylon itself — affirms the Islamic principle that no human power is exempt from divine accountability. The Quran teaches that the most powerful empires are subject to God's decree: "How many a city have We destroyed while it was committing wrong, and it is now fallen into ruin" (Quran 22:45). Islamic historiography, from al-Tabari through Ibn Khaldun, consistently interprets the rise and fall of civilizations through this prophetic lens of divine sovereignty and moral accountability.
