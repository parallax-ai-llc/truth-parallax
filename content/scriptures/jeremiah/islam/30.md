Jeremiah 30 opens the "Book of Consolation," and Islamic interpreters read these oracles of restoration through the Quranic lens of divine mercy (*rahma*). The Quran affirms that God's mercy "encompasses all things" (Quran 7:156), and Jeremiah's proclamation that God will heal Israel's incurable wounds (30:17) demonstrates this principle: divine mercy is not limited by the severity of human sin or the depth of human suffering. The transition from judgment to consolation in Jeremiah's prophecy mirrors the Quranic pattern in which warnings of punishment are consistently followed by offers of forgiveness.

The description of a time of unprecedented trouble — "a time of distress for Jacob" (30:7) — from which God promises deliverance resonates with the Islamic eschatological concept of the trials (*fitan*) that precede the Day of Judgment. Islamic tradition teaches that periods of intense suffering serve a purifying function, burning away the dross of sin and preparing the community for divine encounter. The Prophet Muhammad taught that "the greatest reward comes with the greatest trial" (Sunan Ibn Majah), a principle that Jeremiah 30 illustrates prophetically.

The promise that God will "break the yoke from their neck" and "burst their bonds" (30:8) echoes the Quranic description of the Prophet Muhammad's mission: "He relieves them of their burden and the shackles which were upon them" (Quran 7:157). Islamic interpreters see in Jeremiah's oracle of liberation a foreshadowing of the ultimate liberation that comes through submission to God — not political independence from Babylon alone, but spiritual freedom from the bondage of sin and ignorance.
