The invitation to "ask for the ancient paths" (6:16) resonates with the Islamic concept of the *Sirat al-Mustaqim* (Straight Path) — the path of righteousness that all prophets have taught and that the Quran calls believers to follow in every prayer: "Guide us to the straight path, the path of those upon whom You have bestowed favor" (Quran 1:6-7). Islamic interpreters understand the "ancient paths" as the original monotheistic way (*fitrah*) from which humanity repeatedly strays and to which the prophets repeatedly call.

The false cry of "Peace, peace, when there is no peace" (6:14) parallels the Quranic critique of those who falsely assure their communities that all is well when divine judgment approaches. The Quran describes how the people of Nuh, 'Ad, and Thamud were lulled into complacency by their leaders before destruction came upon them suddenly (Quran 7:97-99). The pattern of false assurance followed by sudden destruction is one of the clearest *sunan Allah* (divine patterns) that the Quran identifies in human history.

The refiner's fire imagery (6:27-30) — the testing of metals that reveals only dross — is read in Islamic interpretation as an instance of divine testing (*ibtila'*). The Quran teaches that God tests people and nations to reveal their true character: "Do people think that they will be left alone because they say, 'We believe,' and they will not be tested?" (Quran 29:2). Jeremiah's role as a tester of metals parallels the Quranic understanding that prophetic proclamation itself serves as a test that separates the sincere from the insincere.
