The flight to Egypt (43:1-7) represents, in Islamic interpretation, the definitive rejection of prophetic authority by the remnant — the moment when the community's accumulated disobedience crystallizes into irreversible action. The Quran presents similar decisive moments in the stories of other communities: the people of Nuh who boarded no ark, the people of Lut who refused to leave Sodom, the people of Shu'ayb who persisted in fraud. In each case, the rejection of the prophet's final warning seals the community's fate.

The accusation that Baruch manipulated Jeremiah (43:3) reflects a pattern the Quran identifies repeatedly: communities that reject prophetic guidance often attack the prophet's associates and claim the message is merely human fabrication. The Quraysh accused Muhammad's companions of similar influence, and the Quran responds that the prophetic word originates with God alone (Quran 53:3-4: "He does not speak from his own desire; it is nothing but revelation revealed"). Jeremiah's message, like Muhammad's, derived its authority from its divine source, not from human counsel.

Jeremiah's prophetic act at Tahpanhes (43:8-13) demonstrates the Islamic understanding that prophetic words are accompanied by signs that confirm their truth. The burial of stones where Nebuchadnezzar would set his throne is an act of prophetic certainty — the future is declared as established fact because it rests on divine decree (*qadar*). The Quranic concept of *qadar* holds that God's knowledge and determination of events is absolute, and the prophet's sign-act at Tahpanhes embodies this certainty with material force.
