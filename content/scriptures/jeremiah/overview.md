## Overview

Jeremiah is one of the Major Prophets of the Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament. Its Hebrew name, *Yirmeyahu* ("Yahweh will exalt" or "Yahweh establishes"), identifies both the book and its central figure — a priest from Anathoth called to prophetic ministry in the thirteenth year of King Josiah (approximately 626 BCE). Jeremiah's career spanned the final four decades of the Kingdom of Judah, from Josiah's reforms through the Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BCE.

## Structure

Jeremiah is traditionally divided into several major sections:

1. **Call and Early Oracles** (Chapters 1–6): Jeremiah's commission and warnings to Judah
2. **Temple Sermon and Conflict** (Chapters 7–20): Confrontation with religious complacency and personal laments
3. **Oracles Against Kings and Prophets** (Chapters 21–25): Judgment on Judah's leadership
4. **Narrative of Conflict** (Chapters 26–29): Jeremiah's clashes with institutional power
5. **Book of Consolation** (Chapters 30–33): The promise of restoration and the New Covenant
6. **Historical Narratives** (Chapters 34–45): The fall of Jerusalem and its aftermath
7. **Oracles Against the Nations** (Chapters 46–51): Prophecies concerning foreign powers
8. **Historical Appendix** (Chapter 52): The fall of Jerusalem paralleling 2 Kings 25

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Jeremiah holds a distinctive place across multiple religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: A central prophetic text read on fast days; the source of the concept of a "new covenant" (*brit hadashah*)
- **Christianity**: The New Covenant passage (31:31-34) is foundational to Christian theology of the Eucharist and supersessionism debates
- **Islam**: Jeremiah is associated with prophetic figures in Islamic tradition; some scholars identify him with the unnamed prophet of Surah 2:259
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding late Iron Age Judah, Babylonian imperialism, and the development of prophetic literature

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Covenant Faithfulness | God's steadfast commitment despite Judah's apostasy |
| Judgment and Exile | The inevitable consequences of persistent unfaithfulness |
| New Covenant | The promise of an internalized, renewed relationship with God (31:31-34) |
| True vs. False Prophecy | The conflict between authentic divine word and institutional religion |
| Suffering of the Prophet | Jeremiah's personal anguish as the "weeping prophet" |
| Repentance | The urgent call to return to God before judgment falls |
