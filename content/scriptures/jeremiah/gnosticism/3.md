Jeremiah 3's invitation to "return" (*shuv*) is read in Gnostic interpretation not as a call to return to the Demiurge but as an unconscious echo of the Pleroma's call to the divine sparks scattered in matter. The "return" that the Gnostic seeks is not repentance within the Demiurge's covenantal system but the return of the pneumatic self to the Pleroma — the divine fullness from which it fell. Jeremiah, as a prophet of the Demiurge, frames this return in terms of covenant faithfulness, but the Gnostic reader hears beneath the surface a deeper call.

The distinction between Israel's open sin and Judah's pretended repentance (3:10) takes on particular significance in Gnostic anthropology. The Valentinian tripartite division of humanity — *hylics* (material), *psychics* (soul-oriented), and *pneumatics* (spirit-bearers) — maps onto this passage. Israel's open apostasy represents the *hylic* condition of complete immersion in matter. Judah's pretended return represents the *psychic* condition — outward conformity to the Demiurge's demands without genuine transformation. True return — available only to the *pneumatic* — is the awakening to *gnosis* that Jeremiah glimpses but cannot fully articulate.

The prophecy that the Ark of the Covenant will no longer be remembered (3:16) is embraced by Gnostic interpreters as a prophetic admission that the entire apparatus of the Demiurge's cult — the Ark, the Temple, the sacrificial system — is ultimately dispensable. What the Ark symbolized (God's presence among His people) will be replaced by the direct, interior knowledge of the divine that requires no material mediation. This is precisely the Gnostic hope: that all external religious structures will be transcended in the direct encounter with the Pleroma.
