Jeremiah 33 continues the consolation oracles, and Gnostic interpreters find in the invitation to prayer (33:3) one of the most significant verses in the prophetic literature: "Call to me and I will answer you, and will tell you great and hidden things that you have not known." The "great and hidden things" are, for Gnostic readers, the mysteries of the Pleroma — the knowledge of divine origins, the cosmological drama of Sophia's fall, and the path of return that the Demiurge's system conceals. The promise that these things can be known through direct divine communication bypasses the Demiurge's institutional structures entirely.

The "righteous Branch" (33:15) is read in Gnostic tradition as a reference to the Redeemer figure who descends from the Pleroma to restore what has been lost. The Branch's function — to "execute justice and righteousness in the land" — is understood not as the establishment of political order (which is the Demiurge's concern) but as the restoration of spiritual order: the re-establishment of the Pleromatic harmony that was disrupted by Sophia's transgression. True justice, in the Gnostic view, is the restoration of each divine spark to its proper place in the fullness of the divine realm.

The cosmic imagery of the covenant with day and night (33:20-25) presents an interpretive challenge for Gnostic readers. The Demiurge's cosmic order — the fixed alternation of light and darkness — is presented as a guarantee of divine faithfulness, yet Gnostic theology regards this very order as the architecture of the prison-world. Some Gnostic interpreters resolve this by reading the day-night cycle as a degraded reflection of the Pleromatic pairs (*syzygies*) — the cosmic order dimly mirrors a higher order, and the faithfulness it represents points beyond itself to the true God's faithfulness, which will ultimately dissolve the Demiurge's cosmos entirely.
