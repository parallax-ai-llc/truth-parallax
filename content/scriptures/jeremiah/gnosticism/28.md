Jeremiah 28 recounts the confrontation between Jeremiah and the false prophet Hananiah, which Gnostic interpreters read as an allegory of the conflict between authentic *gnosis* and its counterfeit imitations. Hananiah represents the psychic teacher who operates within the Demiurge's system — using its language, occupying its sacred spaces, performing its rituals — and offers a false liberation that never transcends the material order. His promise that the Temple vessels will be restored is, in Gnostic terms, a promise of material restoration rather than spiritual awakening: rearranging the furniture of the Demiurge's house rather than escaping it.

The breaking of the wooden yoke and its replacement with iron (28:13) carries profound symbolic significance in Gnostic reading. The attempt to break the bonds of material existence through human effort — through political action, religious reform, or prophetic counter-signs — results not in liberation but in heavier bondage. This is the Gnostic critique of all materialist liberation movements: they address the symptoms of imprisonment without recognizing its fundamental cause, which is the entrapment of the divine spark in matter. Only *gnosis* — direct knowledge of one's true origin in the Pleroma — can dissolve the yoke that no human effort can break.

Hananiah's death (28:17) raises uncomfortable questions for Gnostic interpreters, as it appears to vindicate the Demiurge's authority. Some Gnostic readers interpret the death not as divine punishment but as the natural consequence of spiritual delusion — the psychic who invests entirely in the material order is destroyed when reality contradicts his vision. The pneumatic, by contrast, is not destroyed by the collapse of material expectations because his identity does not depend upon them.
