The declaration that even Moses and Samuel cannot intercede (15:1) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the Demiurge asserting his absolute sovereignty — no human mediator can modify his decrees. This implacability confirms the Gnostic critique: the Demiurge is not moved by compassion but by his own inscrutable will. The true God of the Pleroma, by contrast, is characterized by overflowing *agape* (love) that seeks restoration, not retribution. The failure of even the greatest intercessors within the Demiurge's system demonstrates that the system itself cannot provide genuine salvation.

The prophet's confession — "Your words were found, and I ate them" (15:16) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the pneumatic experience of receiving *gnosis*: the divine word enters the interior being and becomes inseparable from the self. The *Gospel of Truth* describes *gnosis* in similar terms: knowledge that is not external information but internal transformation. The joy that Jeremiah experiences is the joy of the divine spark recognizing its own origin — the momentary illumination that sustains the pneumatic soul through the darkness of the Demiurge's world.

The image of the divine word as "a burning fire shut up in my bones" (15:16, cf. 20:9) is one of the most powerful Gnostic metaphors in the prophetic corpus. The fire within — which cannot be extinguished by external persecution — is the pneumatic element itself, the divine light trapped in the materiality of flesh and bone. Gnostic soteriology understands salvation as the liberation of this fire, its release from the prison of the body and its return to the Pleroma of light. Jeremiah's experience of the fire within is thus a paradigm of the Gnostic condition: bearing divine fire in an alien world.
