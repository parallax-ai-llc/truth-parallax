Jeremiah 31 contains the new covenant prophecy, which Gnostic interpreters read as the single most important prophetic text in the Hebrew scriptures — the moment where the voice of the true God most clearly breaks through the Demiurge's textual apparatus to announce the coming of *gnosis*. The promise that God will write the law "on their hearts" (31:33) is understood not as the internalization of the Demiurge's moral code but as the awakening of the divine spark within — the recovery of the interior knowledge that the pneumatic always possessed but had forgotten through immersion in matter.

The declaration that "they shall all know me, from the least of them to the greatest" (31:34) is the most explicitly Gnostic promise in the prophetic literature. The abolition of mediated knowledge — "no longer shall each one teach his neighbor" — represents the end of the Demiurge's system of hierarchical authority, in which knowledge is controlled and dispensed by institutional gatekeepers. In the new covenant, knowledge of God is direct, interior, and universal — precisely the definition of *gnosis*. The psychic's dependence on external teaching gives way to the pneumatic's direct apprehension of divine reality.

The promise that God will "remember their sin no more" (31:34) is read by Gnostic interpreters not as forensic forgiveness but as ontological transformation — the dissolution of the very category of sin, which is itself a construct of the Demiurge's legal system. In the Gnostic view, sin is not a moral failure requiring punishment and pardon but a condition of ignorance requiring illumination. When *gnosis* comes, the entire framework of sin, guilt, and atonement dissolves as the pneumatic recognizes that the divine spark was never truly stained by its contact with matter.
