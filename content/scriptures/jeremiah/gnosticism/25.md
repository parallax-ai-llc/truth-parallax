The cup of wrath in Jeremiah 25 — distributed to all nations — reveals, in the Gnostic reading, the Demiurge's rage at the failure of his creation. The Demiurge's fury is not righteous indignation but the frustrated anger of a craftsman whose work has fallen apart, a ruler whose subjects have slipped beyond his control. The *Apocryphon of John* describes Yaldabaoth's rage in similar terms: when the divine spark within humanity proves resistant to his control, he responds with floods, fire, and destruction.

The seventy-year prophecy (25:11-12) is less significant for Gnostic interpreters than for other traditions, because Gnostic theology is not primarily concerned with historical timelines within the Demiurge's cosmos. The material world's temporal structure is itself part of the imprisonment — the archons use time as a mechanism of control, keeping the divine sparks trapped in an endless cycle of historical events. The Gnostic hope is not for a better historical moment but for liberation from history itself.

The universal scope of judgment (25:30-38) — all nations brought under divine wrath — confirms the Gnostic conviction that the entire material cosmos is under the Demiurge's wrathful governance. No nation, no political system, no geographical location offers refuge from the archontic powers. The only escape is upward — through *gnosis* to the Pleroma. Jeremiah's vision of universal judgment inadvertently demonstrates that the Demiurge's fury encompasses his entire creation, leaving nowhere to hide except beyond the cosmos itself.
