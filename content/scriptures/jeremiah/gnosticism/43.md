The flight to Egypt (43:1-7) is interpreted in Gnostic tradition as the soul's descent deeper into material bondage. Egypt, consistently symbolizing the realm of material enslavement in Gnostic hermeneutics, represents the lowest level of entrapment within the Demiurge's cosmos. The remnant's voluntary return to Egypt inverts the Exodus narrative and symbolizes the soul that, having been offered liberation through *gnosis*, chooses instead the familiar comforts of material existence — what the *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 28) describes as the intoxication of the world.

The accusation against Baruch (43:3) reflects the Gnostic understanding that the material world's inhabitants consistently misidentify the source of prophetic truth. The Archons, in Sethian mythology, attribute the divine voice to material causes precisely because they cannot perceive the Pleroma from which it originates. The community's insistence that Baruch, a mere scribe, is the true source of Jeremiah's message mirrors the Archons' inability to recognize the divine origin of the revelations that penetrate their realm.

Jeremiah's prophetic act at Tahpanhes (43:8-13) — burying stones that will serve as the foundation for a conqueror's throne — reads in Gnostic interpretation as a demonstration that the material cosmos contains within itself the seeds of its own dissolution. The stones hidden beneath the surface, invisible to ordinary sight but determinative of future events, symbolize the hidden workings of the Pleroma within the Demiurge's realm. The pneumatic prophet perceives these hidden foundations; the hylic community walks over them unknowing.
