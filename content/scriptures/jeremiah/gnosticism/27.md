Jeremiah 27 presents the sign-act of the yoke, and Gnostic interpreters read this chapter with deep ambivalence. On one level, the command to submit to Nebuchadnezzar's yoke represents the Demiurge's demand for submission to the archontic powers that govern the material world. The God who commands subjugation to a foreign tyrant and calls him "my servant" (27:6) displays precisely the characteristics that Gnostic theology attributes to the Demiurge — a deity who equates power with legitimacy and demands submission through threats of destruction.

Yet the chapter contains a deeper current that Gnostic readers find instructive. The false prophets who promise liberation without transformation (27:9-10, 14-18) represent the psychic teachers who offer easy answers and comfortable illusions rather than the difficult path of *gnosis*. In the Gnostic reading, both the false prophets and the Demiurge's decree are expressions of the same counterfeit reality — one promises escape within the material order, the other demands submission to it, but neither addresses the fundamental condition of the divine spark imprisoned in matter.

The yoke itself becomes a symbol of the pneumatic's condition in the material world. The Gnostic does not rebel against the archontic powers through political or military action — such rebellion merely reinforces engagement with the material order. Nor does the Gnostic passively accept the yoke as divinely ordained. Rather, the pneumatic wears the yoke with the inner knowledge that it is temporary, that the material structures it represents will ultimately dissolve, and that the divine spark within is never truly bound by any earthly power.
