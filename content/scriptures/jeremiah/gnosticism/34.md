Jeremiah 34 presents the broken covenant of liberty, and Gnostic interpreters read this episode as a parable of the Demiurge's false promises of liberation. The elite of Jerusalem freed their slaves as an act of apparent piety, only to re-enslave them when circumstances changed — a pattern that Gnostic theology sees as characteristic of the Demiurge's entire system. The material world offers the appearance of freedom (political liberation, religious reform, social progress) only to reimpose bondage in new forms. True liberation cannot be granted or revoked by earthly powers because true bondage is not political or economic but ontological — the imprisonment of the divine spark in matter.

The covenant ceremony of passing between the halves of a calf (34:18-19) is read by Gnostic interpreters as a dark ritual of the Demiurge's system — a blood covenant that binds through violence and the threat of violence. The Demiurge's covenants, in the Gnostic view, are fundamentally coercive: they demand obedience under threat of destruction, a pattern that contrasts sharply with the true God's relationship to the pneumatics, which is based on recognition and love rather than threat and obligation.

The ironic proclamation of "liberty" to sword, pestilence, and famine (34:17) reveals, in the Gnostic reading, the Demiurge's characteristic conflation of judgment with justice. The Demiurge responds to covenant violation not with healing or illumination but with destruction — confirming the Gnostic assessment that this deity operates through power rather than wisdom. The true God, by contrast, responds to ignorance not with punishment but with the gift of *gnosis*, which dissolves the chains of materiality from within rather than adding to the burden of suffering.
