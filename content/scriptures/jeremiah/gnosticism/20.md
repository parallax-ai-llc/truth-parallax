Jeremiah's confession "O Lord, you have deceived me" (20:7) is, for Gnostic interpreters, the single most revelatory verse in the entire book. Here, the prophet of the Demiurge accuses his own god of deception — and the Gnostic reader responds: of course. The Demiurge is a deceiver by nature, a being who declares "I am God and there is no other" while the Pleroma watches in sorrow. Jeremiah's accusation, wrung from his anguish, is the closest the prophet comes to *gnosis* — the recognition that the god he serves is not worthy of the trust he has placed in him.

The compulsion to speak (20:9) — "there is in my heart as it were a burning fire shut up in my bones" — is the pneumatic element asserting itself against the prophet's desire for silence and safety. The fire is the divine spark, and its refusal to be suppressed demonstrates the fundamental truth of Gnostic anthropology: the light within cannot be permanently extinguished by the Demiurge's world, no matter how much suffering it causes its bearer. The fire will speak, the spark will shine, because it participates in the nature of the Pleroma itself.

The cursing of the day of his birth (20:14-18) — Jeremiah's most extreme expression of despair — is read by Gnostic interpreters as the prophet's approach to the Gnostic insight that incarnation itself is the fundamental catastrophe. To be born into the material world is to be imprisoned in the Demiurge's creation; to be given prophetic sight within this prison is to suffer doubly — seeing the bars while being unable to escape. Jeremiah's curse on his birth is the cry of the pneumatic soul recognizing the horror of its situation, a cry that finds its resolution only in the liberating knowledge that the prison is not the whole of reality.
