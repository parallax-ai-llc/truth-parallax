The invitation to "ask for the ancient paths" (6:16) is reinterpreted by Gnostic readers as pointing not to the Mosaic covenant but to the primordial path of *gnosis* that existed before the Demiurge's creation. The truly "ancient paths" are not the traditions of Israel — which are themselves products of the Demiurge's system — but the eternal truths of the Pleroma that predate the material cosmos entirely. The Gnostic understanding of *anamnesis* (remembering one's divine origin) resonates with this call to seek the most ancient way.

The refiner's fire imagery (6:27-30) — the testing that reveals only dross — is read as further evidence that the Demiurge's creation contains no genuine gold. The material world, being a flawed imitation of the Pleroma, cannot produce the spiritual purity that the refining process seeks. The only genuine "gold" in the Demiurge's creation is the divine spark — the pneumatic element that came from beyond and does not belong to the material cosmos. Jeremiah's despair at finding only "rejected silver" is, in the Gnostic reading, the inevitable result of looking for divine substance in a world that, by definition, cannot contain it.

The false cry of "Peace, peace, when there is no peace" (6:14) is embraced by Gnostic interpreters as describing the fundamental condition of the Demiurge's world. The Demiurge and his archons promise order, security, and blessing through their religious system, but true peace — the stillness of the Pleroma — is impossible within a cosmos built on ignorance and error. The prophets and priests who proclaim peace are agents of the Demiurge's illusion, maintaining the fiction that the material world can satisfy the pneumatic soul's longing for its true home.
