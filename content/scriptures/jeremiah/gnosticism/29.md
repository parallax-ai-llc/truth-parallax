Jeremiah 29 contains the prophet's letter to the exiles, and Gnostic interpreters read the entire chapter as an allegory of the pneumatic's condition in the material world. The exiles in Babylon are the divine sparks trapped in matter, and the question of how to live in exile is the fundamental Gnostic question: how does the pneumatic exist within the Demiurge's cosmos while maintaining awareness of the Pleroma? The instructions to build, plant, and marry (29:5-7) represent the unavoidable engagement with materiality that even the most enlightened pneumatic cannot entirely escape.

The promise that "you will seek me and find me, when you seek me with all your heart" (29:13) is the most deeply Gnostic verse in the chapter. Gnostic interpreters read this as a call to *gnosis* — the wholehearted seeking that penetrates beyond the Demiurge's creation to the true God who transcends it. The "seeking with all your heart" is not the devotional piety of the psychic but the total commitment of the pneumatic to recovering the knowledge of the divine origin. The true God — not the Demiurge who imposed the exile — is found only through this radical inward turn.

Yet the letter also contains elements that Gnostic readers find troubling. The instruction to seek the welfare of Babylon — the city of exile, the archontic order — seems to counsel accommodation with the very system that imprisons the divine spark. Gnostic interpreters resolve this tension by distinguishing between external compliance and internal freedom: the pneumatic may participate in the structures of the material world without being defined by them, wearing the yoke of Babylon while knowing that Babylon is not home and never will be.
