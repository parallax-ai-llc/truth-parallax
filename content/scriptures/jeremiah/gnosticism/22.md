The evaluation of kings in Jeremiah 22 is read by Gnostic interpreters as exposing the archontic nature of earthly rule. The kings of Judah — some building palaces with forced labor, others neglecting justice — represent the range of archontic governance: from overtly tyrannical to merely incompetent. The one exception, Josiah, who "judged the cause of the poor and needy" (22:16), is read as an anomaly — a ruler who, by practicing justice, inadvertently reflected the Pleroma's values rather than the Demiurge's characteristic rule through exploitation.

The equation of knowing God with doing justice (22:16) presents a challenge for the Gnostic interpretive framework, which typically defines knowledge of God as interior, mystical illumination rather than ethical practice. Some Gnostic interpreters resolved this by distinguishing between the Demiurge's "justice" (external compliance with law) and the Pleroma's justice (the natural expression of the divine spark recognizing itself in the other). The pneumatic ruler who practices genuine justice does so not because the Demiurge commands it but because the divine spark within perceives the divine spark in others and acts accordingly.

The curse on Coniah — "Write this man down as childless" (22:30) — is read in Gnostic interpretation as the Demiurge's attempt to terminate a lineage that threatened his control. The Demiurge, in the Gnostic view, manipulates genealogies and dynastic successions to maintain his power; the curse on Coniah is an exercise of this control. Whether the curse was ultimately effective is less important, for Gnostic readers, than what it reveals about the Demiurge's mode of governance: ruling through punishment and exclusion rather than through illumination and inclusion.
