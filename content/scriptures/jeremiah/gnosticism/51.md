Gnostic interpretation of Jeremiah 51 reads the fall of Babylon as a vision of the Demiurge's chief city — the material cosmos's center of power — collapsing under the weight of its own contradictions. The declaration that Babylon was "a golden cup" that made the nations drunk (51:7) resonates with the Gnostic understanding of material existence as a form of intoxication — the *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 28) presents Jesus saying, "I found them all drunk; I found none of them thirsty." Babylon's golden cup is the Demiurge's seduction of the soul through material beauty that conceals spiritual emptiness.

The creation hymn within the oracle (51:15-19) presents a paradox for Gnostic interpreters: the God who claims credit for creation also claims the right to destroy. Gnostic hermeneutics reads this as the Demiurge's self-justification — the creator who made a flawed world now destroys portions of it, claiming justice as his motive. The true God of the Pleroma, by contrast, neither creates material worlds nor destroys them; the divine work is liberation through knowledge, not governance through power.

The sinking of the scroll in the Euphrates (51:63-64) is interpreted in Gnostic tradition as an image of the divine word descending into the depths of matter. The scroll, bearing the prophetic word, sinks beneath the waters — just as the pneumatic seed descends into the material cosmos, weighted by flesh and circumstance. Yet the Gnostic reader knows that what sinks will ultimately rise: the divine spark cannot be permanently submerged, and the word that descends into darkness will eventually illuminate it. The scroll's descent prefigures the return of all light to the Pleroma.
