The futile search for a righteous person in Jerusalem (5:1) is read by Gnostic interpreters as evidence that the Demiurge's covenant system cannot produce genuine righteousness. If the god of Israel's own people — chosen, covenanted, given the law and the Temple — cannot produce even a single righteous person, then the system itself is fundamentally flawed. The Gnostic conclusion is that righteousness within the Demiurge's framework is impossible because the framework itself is a distortion of the true divine order.

The people's complacent declaration — "He will do nothing" (5:12) — is reread by Gnostics not as false security but as an inadvertent truth: the Demiurge, despite his threats and posturing, is ultimately impotent before the Pleroma. The archons bluster and threaten, but the true God beyond them acts with quiet, subtle power — not through cosmic destruction but through the interior awakening of *gnosis* in individual pneumatic souls. The people's dismissal of the Demiurge's threats is wrong in its immediate historical context but contains a deeper spiritual truth.

The chapter's closing question about divine punishment (5:29) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the Demiurge justifying his own violence. The god of Israel, in the Gnostic view, is a deity who rules through fear and punishment rather than through love and knowledge. The *Testimony of Truth* (NHC IX,3) presents the Demiurge as a jealous, wrathful being whose "justice" is really vengeance. Jeremiah's prophetic endorsement of this punishing deity thus becomes, in the Gnostic reading, evidence of the prophet's incomplete understanding — he serves the Demiurge faithfully but does not yet see beyond him.
