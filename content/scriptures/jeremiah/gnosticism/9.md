Jeremiah's wish to weep ceaselessly (9:1) is interpreted in Gnostic reading as the expression of Sophia's grief — the divine Wisdom who mourns the scattering of her light-sparks throughout the material cosmos. The "weeping prophet" becomes, in Gnostic typology, a figure of Sophia herself, whose tears are both the cause and the consequence of the material world's existence. Some Gnostic cosmogonies describe the material elements as condensed from Sophia's tears, and Jeremiah's seemingly inexhaustible grief echoes this cosmic sorrow.

The declaration that true boasting is found only in knowing God (9:23-24) — not in wisdom, might, or riches — is one of the most naturally Gnostic statements in the prophetic corpus. *Gnosis* — knowledge of the true God — is the singular value that relativizes all worldly attainments. Valentinian interpreters would note that the "wisdom, might, and riches" that Jeremiah dismisses are attributes of the Demiurge's world: intellectual achievement within the material order, military power, and economic success. The knowledge of God that surpasses all these is precisely the pneumatic knowledge that Gnostic soteriology promises.

The distinction between circumcision of flesh and circumcision of heart (9:25-26) resonates with the Gnostic rejection of physical rituals as spiritually meaningless. The *Testimony of Truth* explicitly mocks physical circumcision as a practice of the Demiurge's covenant that has no bearing on the soul's relationship to the true God. Jeremiah's prophetic elevation of the heart over the flesh confirms, for Gnostic readers, that the authentic prophetic tradition always points beyond the material observances imposed by the god of Israel toward the interior transformation that only *gnosis* can accomplish.
