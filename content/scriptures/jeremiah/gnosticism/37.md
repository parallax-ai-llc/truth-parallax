Jeremiah 37 narrates the prophet's imprisonment, and Gnostic interpreters read this as a paradigm of the archontic system's response to pneumatic truth-telling. The prophet who reveals the hidden knowledge that contradicts the official narrative is inevitably persecuted by the powers that maintain the dominant order. The false accusation of desertion — claiming that Jeremiah was going over to the enemy — is a classic technique of archontic suppression: the pneumatic who speaks truth is reframed as a traitor, an enemy of the community, a threat to social order.

Zedekiah's secret consultation (37:17) reveals the peculiar fascination that the archontic powers have with the pneumatic's knowledge. The king imprisons the prophet but cannot resist seeking his counsel — he recognizes that Jeremiah possesses a knowledge that his own advisors lack, yet he cannot act on this knowledge without threatening the structures of power that sustain his rule. This dynamic — the powerful seeking secret counsel from the very people they persecute — is characteristic of the Demiurge's ambivalent relationship with *gnosis*: the archontic system simultaneously suppresses and depends upon the truth it cannot generate from within itself.

The physical imprisonment of Jeremiah — confined first in a dungeon, then in the court of the guard — is read by Gnostic interpreters as an image within an image: the pneumatic is imprisoned by the archontic authorities within the larger prison of the material world. Yet the truly imprisoned figure in this narrative is Zedekiah, who is bound by fear, political calculation, and the opinions of his officials. The prophet, though physically confined, retains the inner freedom that comes from *gnosis* — the knowledge of his true origin and destiny that no dungeon can contain.
