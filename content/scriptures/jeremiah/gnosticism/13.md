The symbolic action of the linen belt in Jeremiah 13 is read by Gnostic interpreters as a parable of the relationship between the divine spark and the material body. The belt clinging to the waist (13:11) represents the original intimacy between the pneumatic element and its divine source; the journey to the Euphrates and the belt's ruin represent the progressive degradation of the divine spark as it descends deeper into the material cosmos. The ruined belt is the human condition in the material world: once intimately connected to the divine, now corrupted by contact with the realm of the archons.

The geographical significance of the Euphrates — the river of Babylon, the great empire of exile — adds a cosmic dimension to the Gnostic reading. The Mandaean tradition, one of the surviving Gnostic communities, developed an elaborate mythology of rivers and running water as symbols of the divine life flowing from the Light-world into the material realm. The belt's ruin by the Euphrates thus represents the contamination of the divine element by the waters of the material world — a reversal of the purifying baptismal waters that Gnostic traditions understood as the means of restoration.

The question about the Ethiopian's skin and the leopard's spots (13:23) is read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the condition of the *hylic* (material) person, for whom transformation is indeed impossible because there is no divine spark to awaken. The tripartite Valentinian anthropology holds that *pneumatics* can be awakened, *psychics* can be partially enlightened, but *hylics* are as incapable of spiritual transformation as a leopard is of changing its spots. This passage thus becomes, in Gnostic hands, support for the predestinarian element in Gnostic soteriology.
