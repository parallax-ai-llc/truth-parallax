The broken flask of Jeremiah 19 — shattered beyond repair — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a revelation of the material world's ultimate destiny. The Demiurge's creation is not eternal; it will be destroyed, and the divine sparks trapped within it will either be liberated (for the pneumatics) or destroyed along with it (for the hylics). The irreversibility of the broken flask corresponds to the Gnostic teaching that the material cosmos is not being repaired or redeemed but is destined for dissolution — a necessary step in the liberation of the light imprisoned within it.

The Valley of Ben-Hinnom — the site of child sacrifice that becomes the image of Gehenna — represents, for Gnostic interpreters, the most extreme expression of the archontic system. The sacrifice of children to Molech is not an aberration within the Demiurge's world but its logical extreme: a system based on sacrifice, appeasement, and the exchange of life for favor inevitably escalates to the most horrific forms. The Gnostic critique of the sacrificial system — from the *Testimony of Truth*'s rejection of blood sacrifice to the *Gospel of Judas*'s denunciation of apostolic sacrificial theology — finds in Jeremiah 19 prophetic evidence for its position.

The public nature of the sign-act — performed before the elders and priests — is read as the prophet's unconscious exposure of the Demiurge's system to its own leaders. Jeremiah intends to warn them of the Demiurge's coming punishment, but the Gnostic reader sees a deeper message: the prophet is demonstrating the fragility and impermanence of everything the religious establishment values. The flask, the Temple, the city, the cosmos itself — all are destined for destruction. Only the divine spark, which is not of this world, will endure.
