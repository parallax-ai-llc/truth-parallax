The Temple Sermon of Jeremiah 7 is one of the most significant texts for Gnostic interpretation of the prophets, because the prophet himself attacks the institution most central to the Demiurge's cult. When Jeremiah declares "Do not trust in these deceptive words: 'The temple of the Lord'" (7:4), Gnostic readers hear the Demiurge's own prophet inadvertently exposing the emptiness of the Demiurge's worship. The Temple, in the Gnostic view, is the supreme idol — a material structure that concentrates and perpetuates the illusion that the god who dwells there is the true God.

The reference to Shiloh (7:12-14) — God's earlier dwelling, now destroyed — confirms for Gnostic readers that the Demiurge's sacred spaces are as impermanent as the rest of his creation. If the god of Israel destroyed his own sanctuary at Shiloh and threatens to destroy the Jerusalem Temple, then no material structure is truly sacred. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the true temple is the human body in which the divine spark resides, and the destruction of external temples is, from the Gnostic perspective, a preliminary step toward recognizing this interior sanctuary.

The catalogue of sins (7:5-11) — oppression, murder, idolatry — is read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the natural consequences of the Demiurge's rule. A world governed by an ignorant, jealous deity inevitably produces societies characterized by violence, exploitation, and religious confusion. The "den of robbers" (7:11) is not merely a metaphor for Temple corruption but a description of the material cosmos itself — a realm where the archons rob the divine sparks of their knowledge and freedom.
