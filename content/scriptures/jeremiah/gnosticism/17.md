The diagnosis of the heart as "deceitful above all things, and desperately sick" (17:9) is read by Gnostic interpreters as describing not human nature as corrupted by a primordial fall but the condition of consciousness within the Demiurge's world. The heart is deceitful because it has been shaped by the Demiurge and his archons, who have constructed a world of illusion in which the truth about the divine spark is systematically suppressed. The "sickness" of the heart is ignorance — *agnoia* — the fundamental Gnostic diagnosis of the human condition, which only *gnosis* can cure.

The contrasting images of the cursed shrub and the blessed tree (17:5-8) are given a Gnostic reading through the tripartite anthropology. The cursed shrub in the desert — trusting in human strength, unable to perceive goodness — represents the *hylic* person, completely immersed in the material world without access to the divine spark. The blessed tree by the water — its roots reaching hidden sources even in drought — represents the *pneumatic* person, whose connection to the Pleroma sustains life even in the arid wasteland of the Demiurge's creation. The "water" that feeds the tree's roots is *gnosis*, flowing from the Pleroma through hidden channels into the material world.

The sabbath oracle (17:19-27) is dismissed by Gnostic interpreters as a characteristic expression of the Demiurge's legalism — the reduction of the divine-human relationship to a system of rules, rewards, and punishments. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 27) provides the Gnostic alternative: "If you do not fast from the world, you will not find the kingdom." True rest is not the cessation of physical labor on a particular day but the liberation of the pneumatic soul from the Demiurge's entire system of material obligation.
