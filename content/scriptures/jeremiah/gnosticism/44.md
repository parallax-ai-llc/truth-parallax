Gnostic interpretation of Jeremiah 44 reads the confrontation over the "Queen of Heaven" through a distinctively different lens than orthodox traditions. Some Gnostic systems identified feminine divine figures — Sophia, Barbelo, or the Mother — as legitimate objects of devotion, representing aspects of the Pleroma that the Demiurge sought to suppress. The community's worship of the Queen of Heaven could be read, in certain Gnostic frameworks, as a distorted memory of authentic devotion to the divine feminine that the Demiurge's prophets sought to eradicate.

However, even within Gnostic hermeneutics, the Egyptian community's theological reasoning — equating material prosperity with divine approval — reveals a fundamentally *hylic* (material) consciousness. The Valentinian tradition distinguished sharply between material well-being, which the Demiurge distributes according to his own inscrutable logic, and spiritual knowledge, which comes only from the Pleroma. A community that measures divine favor by material prosperity has fully internalized the Demiurge's value system, regardless of which deity they worship.

The final oracle of judgment (44:24-30) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the Demiurge's characteristic response to disobedience: punishment and destruction rather than illumination and liberation. The true God of the Pleroma does not destroy those who err but sends revelatory light to awaken them. The severity of Jeremiah's oracle — total destruction of the Egyptian community — marks it, in Gnostic reading, as proceeding from the Demiurge's jealous rage rather than from the compassionate wisdom of the transcendent God.
