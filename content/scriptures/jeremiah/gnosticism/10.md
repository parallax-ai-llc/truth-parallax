Jeremiah 10's satire of idol-making presents an interesting challenge for Gnostic interpretation. While the prophet ridicules carved images as lifeless and powerless, the Gnostic reader applies the same critique to the Demiurge himself. The idols of the nations are carved from wood and covered with gold — human fabrications presented as divine. But is the Demiurge, in the Gnostic view, any different? The *Apocryphon of John* describes Yaldabaoth as a being who fashioned himself a cosmic dwelling and declared "I am God and there is no other" — a self-made deity as ignorant of his own origins as the carved idols are ignorant of theirs.

The confession "The Lord is the true God; he is the living God" (10:10) is read by Gnostic interpreters as accidentally truthful in a way the prophet does not intend. The "true God" and the "living God" are indeed real — but they are not the god of Israel. They are the transcendent, unknowable Father of the Pleroma, the Monad beyond all description, whom the Demiurge does not know and cannot represent. Jeremiah's confession thus points beyond its immediate referent (the god of Israel) to the genuine transcendent reality that the Demiurge unwittingly imitates.

Jeremiah's prayer for measured correction (10:24) reveals, in the Gnostic reading, the prophet's terror of the very god he serves. The Demiurge's "correction" is punishment — wrathful, potentially annihilating — and even his prophet must plead for restraint. The Gnostic contrast is stark: the true God of the Pleroma does not punish but illuminates; does not destroy but liberates. The need to beg the Demiurge for mercy exposes the fundamental difference between the Demiurge's rule through fear and the Pleroma's governance through love and knowledge.
