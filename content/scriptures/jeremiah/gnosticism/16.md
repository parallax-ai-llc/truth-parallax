The divine command that Jeremiah remain celibate (16:1-4) is embraced by Gnostic interpreters as prophetic validation of the ascetic rejection of procreation. Several Gnostic traditions, particularly the Encratites and certain Manichaean communities, taught that sexual reproduction perpetuates the imprisonment of divine sparks in material bodies. Each new birth scatters the light further into the realm of darkness. Jeremiah's enforced celibacy thus becomes, in the Gnostic reading, a prophetic anticipation of the Gnostic teaching that the liberation of the divine spark requires the cessation of its material propagation.

The prohibition against mourning and feasting (16:5-9) represents, for Gnostic interpreters, the prophet's progressive detachment from the social fabric of the Demiurge's world. The pneumatic soul, as it awakens to *gnosis*, naturally separates from the rituals and customs that bind the psychic and hylic masses to the material order. Mourning and feasting are both expressions of attachment to the material world — grief over material loss and celebration of material gain — and the Gnostic who has awakened transcends both.

The vision of the nations acknowledging the true God (16:19-21) is given a universalist Gnostic reading: the divine sparks are scattered among all nations, and the eschatological gathering is the Pleroma's recovery of all its light from wherever it has been dispersed. The Demiurge's concern with one particular nation (Israel) is another mark of his limited vision; the true God's redemptive purpose encompasses the entire cosmos and all its inhabitants. Jeremiah's vision of universal recognition inadvertently points beyond the Demiurge's parochial covenant to the Pleroma's universal embrace.
