Jeremiah 35 presents the Rechabites, and Gnostic interpreters find in this nomadic, ascetic clan a surprising point of identification. The Rechabites' refusal of wine, houses, and vineyards — their radical detachment from the settled, agricultural civilization of Canaan — mirrors the Gnostic impulse toward disengagement from the material order. The Rechabites live as strangers and sojourners in a world they do not fully inhabit, maintaining an alternative identity rooted not in land or property but in a commitment that transcends material circumstance. This is precisely the posture of the pneumatic in the Demiurge's cosmos.

The test in the Temple — offering wine to those who have vowed abstinence — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a trial of the pneumatic's resolve. The Demiurge's system constantly tempts the awakened soul to re-engage with materiality, to abandon its detachment and accept the comforts of the settled order. The Rechabites' refusal demonstrates the kind of spiritual discipline that the Gnostic tradition values: the ability to maintain one's commitment to transcendence even when the dominant culture offers attractive alternatives.

Yet the Gnostic reading also notes an irony: the Rechabites are praised by the Demiurge precisely because they obey — their virtue, as the text presents it, is obedience to authority. Gnostic theology is ambivalent about obedience as a virtue, since the archontic system depends on unquestioning compliance. The deeper Gnostic reading therefore distinguishes between the Rechabites' obedience to their ancestor (which is admirable as a form of spiritual discipline) and the Demiurge's use of their example to shame Judah into obedience (which is an exercise of archontic control). The pneumatic learns from the Rechabites' discipline while rejecting the Demiurge's conclusion.
