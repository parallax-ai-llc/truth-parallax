Gnostic interpreters read Jeremiah's call narrative through the lens of the divine spark imprisoned in matter. The declaration "Before I formed you in the womb I knew you" (1:5) is understood not as the Demiurge's claim of sovereignty over the prophet but as the voice of the true God from beyond the material cosmos, reaching through the Demiurge's creation to awaken one who carries the pneumatic seed. The "knowing" that precedes formation in the womb is the Pleroma's recognition of its own scattered light — the divine spark within Jeremiah that existed in the fullness before being trapped in flesh.

The prophet's reluctance — "I am only a youth" — is read as the confusion of a pneumatic soul awakening within a world it does not fully understand. The Gnostic revealer figures in texts like the *Apocryphon of John* similarly experience disorientation upon entering the material realm. God's reassurance — "I am with you" — is the Pleroma's promise that the divine spark will not be abandoned within the Demiurge's creation, however hostile that creation may prove to be.

The two visions — the almond branch and the boiling pot — are read as revealing the dual nature of the Demiurge's world: apparent order (the almond branch, symbol of watchfulness) concealing approaching chaos (the boiling pot of destruction). Gnostic interpreters see in these visions confirmation that the material cosmos is inherently unstable, governed by forces of conflict and destruction that the Demiurge cannot ultimately control. The prophet who perceives both the surface order and the underlying chaos has begun to see through the Demiurge's illusion.
