The vision of the two baskets of figs in Jeremiah 24 maps naturally onto the Gnostic tripartite anthropology. The "good figs" represent the pneumatic souls — those carrying the divine spark who are selected for preservation and eventual restoration. The "bad figs" represent the *hylic* or merely *psychic* souls who remain bound to the Demiurge's doomed system. The selection is not based on moral achievement within the Demiurge's framework but on the pneumatic nature of the individuals concerned — an election that precedes and transcends the historical circumstances of exile.

The promise "I will give them a heart to know that I am the Lord" (24:7) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the Pleroma's awakening of the divine spark within the pneumatic elect. The "heart to know" is not the Demiurge's gift (he cannot give what he does not possess) but the Pleroma's activation of the pneumatic seed that has lain dormant within the material body. The knowledge promised here is *gnosis* — the direct, experiential knowledge of the true God that transforms the knower and initiates the process of liberation.

The exile itself, in Gnostic reading, serves the Pleroma's purpose by stripping away the false securities of the Demiurge's system — Temple, land, monarchy — and forcing the pneumatic soul to rely on the inner knowledge that is its true sustenance. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 42) teaches, "Be passersby" — strangers in the material world who carry their true homeland within. The Babylonian exiles, stripped of every external support, are unwittingly placed in the ideal condition for the reception of *gnosis*.
