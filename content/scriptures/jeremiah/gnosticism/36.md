Jeremiah 36 narrates the writing, burning, and rewriting of Jeremiah's scroll, and Gnostic interpreters read this as a parable of the archontic suppression of *gnosis* and its ultimate indestructibility. Jehoiakim represents the archontic power that seeks to destroy divine knowledge by eliminating its material vehicles — burning texts, silencing teachers, persecuting communities. The history of Gnostic literature is itself a history of such suppression: the Nag Hammadi texts survived only because they were buried, hidden from the institutional powers that sought to destroy them.

The rewriting of the scroll with "many similar words added" (36:32) illustrates a principle that Gnostic tradition recognizes: *gnosis* cannot be destroyed because it originates beyond the material order. The archons can burn scrolls, but they cannot extinguish the divine knowledge that the scrolls imperfectly transmit. The true vehicle of *gnosis* is not the material text but the pneumatic consciousness that receives and retransmits it. Baruch's role as scribe — the faithful transmitter who preserves and augments the original revelation — mirrors the Gnostic teacher's function of receiving and passing on the tradition of hidden knowledge.

The contrast between the scroll's material destruction and its spiritual indestructibility reflects the fundamental Gnostic distinction between matter and spirit. The fire that consumes the scroll destroys only its material form; the divine content persists because it belongs to a dimension that fire cannot reach. This principle applies to the pneumatic self as well: the archons can destroy the body, but the divine spark — like the prophetic word — endures beyond every material catastrophe, returning always to its source in the Pleroma.
