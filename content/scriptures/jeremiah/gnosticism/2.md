Jeremiah 2's metaphor of the broken cisterns (2:13) — forsaking the fountain of living waters — is one of the most naturally Gnostic images in the prophetic corpus. In the Gnostic reading, the "fountain of living waters" is not the Demiurge (the god of Israel) but the true, transcendent God of the Pleroma, whose living water is *gnosis* — the salvific knowledge that liberates the divine spark from material imprisonment. The "broken cisterns" represent the Demiurge's own religious system — the Temple cult, the Mosaic law, the sacrificial system — which can never truly satisfy the pneumatic soul's thirst for the divine.

The irony that Gnostic interpreters find in this chapter is that Jeremiah, speaking on behalf of the Demiurge, inadvertently describes the Demiurge's own system as the broken cistern. The prophet attributes the metaphor to Israel's apostasy from the god of Israel, but the Gnostic reading inverts this: the original apostasy was the Demiurge's own rebellion against the Pleroma, and Israel's search for other gods — however misguided — reflects an unconscious intuition that the god of Israel is not the ultimate reality.

The catalogue of Israel's infidelities — running after foreign gods — is thus reread as a fumbling, distorted search for the true God beyond the Demiurge. The foreign gods are as false as the Demiurge himself, but the impulse to seek beyond what has been given reflects the divine spark's yearning for its source. Valentinian interpreters would say that the psychic person (bound to the Demiurge's system) mistakes this yearning for sin, while the pneumatic person recognizes it as the first stirring of *gnosis*.
