Jeremiah's complaint about the prosperity of the wicked (12:1) is, for Gnostic interpreters, not merely a personal grievance but an exposure of the fundamental injustice of the Demiurge's world. If the god who governs this cosmos allows the wicked to prosper and the righteous to suffer, then either this god is unjust or he is powerless. The Gnostic answer is that the Demiurge is both — ignorant of true justice (which belongs to the Pleroma) and unable to maintain even the semblance of justice in the world he has fashioned. The theodicy problem is not a problem to be solved but a symptom to be diagnosed: the world is unjust because its creator is flawed.

God's response — "If you have raced with men on foot and they have wearied you, how will you compete with horses?" (12:5) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as the Demiurge's characteristic response to complaint: not comfort or explanation but intensification of the challenge. The Demiurge does not answer the question of justice; he overwhelms it with greater demands. This pattern of escalating demands without genuine resolution is precisely what drives the pneumatic soul to seek liberation from the Demiurge's system altogether — to look beyond the god of this world for the true God who answers not with more suffering but with knowledge.

The vision of restoration for the nations (12:14-17) is given a surprising Gnostic reading: the gathering of the nations represents not the extension of the Demiurge's rule but the eventual liberation of divine sparks from every nation and people. The pneumatic seed is scattered across all humanity, not confined to Israel, and the eschatological gathering is the Pleroma's recovery of its own scattered light. Jeremiah's vision, despite its Demiurgic framing, inadvertently points toward the universal scope of the Gnostic redemption.
