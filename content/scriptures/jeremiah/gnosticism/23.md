The messianic prophecy of the "righteous Branch" (23:5-6) is given a complex Gnostic reading. The promise of a righteous Davidic ruler who will execute true justice is, on the surface, a promise within the Demiurge's system. But the name "The Lord is our Righteousness" introduces a dimension that transcends the Demiurge: if this coming figure embodies divine righteousness, he must be connected not to the Demiurge (who, in the Gnostic view, does not possess true righteousness) but to the Pleroma. Some Gnostic interpreters thus read the messianic prophecy as an inadvertent prophecy of the Gnostic Revealer — the figure who comes from beyond the Demiurge's system to bring *gnosis*.

The critique of false prophets (23:9-32) is embraced by Gnostic interpreters as applicable to the entire prophetic establishment of the Demiurge. The prophets who "speak visions of their own minds" (23:16) represent the *psychic* religious leaders who have no genuine connection to the Pleroma but mistake their own intuitions for divine revelation. The criterion that the true prophet must have "stood in the council of the Lord" (23:18) is reinterpreted: the true Gnostic teacher stands not in the council of the Demiurge but in the Pleroma itself, from which all authentic knowledge flows.

The declaration that God's word is "like fire" and "like a hammer" (23:29) describes the *gnosis* that shatters the rock of material ignorance. The divine word, in the Gnostic reading, is not the Torah or the prophetic oracle but the pneumatic truth that breaks through the Demiurge's system like fire through wood or a hammer through stone. The *Gospel of Truth* describes the revelation of *gnosis* in similar terms: a sudden, overwhelming disclosure that destroys the structures of ignorance and error that have kept the divine spark imprisoned.
