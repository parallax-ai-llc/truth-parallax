The drought of Jeremiah 14 is read by Gnostic interpreters as a metaphor for the spiritual aridity of the Demiurge's world — a cosmos deprived of the living water of *gnosis*. The physical drought mirrors the pneumatic drought: the divine sparks trapped in matter are parched, desperate for the sustenance that only the Pleroma can provide. The Demiurge's world, cut off from the true source of life, is inherently arid — a desert in which the pneumatic soul wanders in search of the oasis of knowledge.

The exposure of false prophets (14:13-16) — prophets who promise peace when judgment is coming — is read in Gnostic interpretation as a description of the archontic deception at its most insidious. The false prophets are agents of the Demiurge who perpetuate the illusion that all is well within his system. Their promises of peace function to keep the pneumatic souls pacified, preventing the awakening that would lead to liberation. The Gnostic teacher, by contrast, brings not peace but the disturbing truth of *gnosis* — knowledge that shatters comfortable illusions and initiates the painful process of spiritual awakening.

Jeremiah's persistent intercession (14:19-22), despite God's prohibition, reveals what Gnostic interpreters see as the prophet's pneumatic nature asserting itself against the Demiurge's commands. The impulse to intercede — to stand between judgment and the people — comes not from the Demiurge (who has forbidden it) but from the divine spark within Jeremiah that connects him to the merciful Pleroma. The prophet acts, in this moment, not as the Demiurge's servant but as the Pleroma's agent, even though he does not fully understand the source of his compassion.
