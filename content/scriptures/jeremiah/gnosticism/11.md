The broken covenant of Jeremiah 11 — the curse upon those who do not obey — is read by Gnostic interpreters as evidence of the Demiurge's coercive mode of governance. The Demiurge rules through threats and curses, demanding obedience under penalty of destruction. The *Testimony of Truth* contrasts this with the true God, who does not demand obedience but offers knowledge — *gnosis* that liberates rather than law that enslaves. The covenant curses of Deuteronomy, reactivated in Jeremiah 11, represent the Demiurge's characteristic instrument of control.

The plot against Jeremiah (11:18-23) and the "lamb led to the slaughter" imagery (11:19) are read in Gnostic tradition as illustrating the archons' hostility toward anyone who threatens their system. The archons — the Demiurge's servants — conspire against those who carry the divine spark with particular intensity, because pneumatic individuals pose a threat to the archontic order. Jeremiah's persecution by his own community is, in Gnostic reading, a paradigm of the pneumatic soul's experience in the material world: rejected, conspired against, and marked for destruction by the very powers that claim to serve God.

The prohibition against intercession (11:14) is read by Gnostic interpreters as revealing the Demiurge's implacability — his fundamental inability to forgive. The true God of the Pleroma is characterized by infinite mercy and compassion; the Demiurge, by contrast, reaches a point where even his own prophet's prayers are rejected. This contrast between the merciful Pleroma and the wrathful Demiurge is central to Gnostic theology and finds, in Jeremiah 11, a dramatic prophetic illustration.
