Jeremiah 39 records the fall of Jerusalem, and Gnostic interpreters read the destruction of the city and Temple as the collapse of the Demiurge's most elaborate material structure — the institution that claimed to house the divine presence within walls of stone. The destruction reveals what the pneumatic has always known: the Demiurge's sacred spaces are not truly sacred; the divine cannot be contained in material structures. The burning of the Temple is, in one sense, a liberation — the end of the illusion that the divine presence is localized in a building constructed by human hands.

The fate of Zedekiah — blinded and chained — is read by Gnostic interpreters as the inevitable destiny of the archontic ruler who serves the Demiurge's system. The king who exercises material power within the Demiurge's cosmos is himself subject to that cosmos's logic of violence and domination. Zedekiah's blinding is not merely a punishment but a revelation of his true condition: he was always blind, always chained to the material order, always subject to powers greater than himself. The fall simply makes visible what was always the case.

The liberation of Jeremiah (39:11-14) and the promise to Ebed-melech (39:15-18) represent the salvation of the pneumatics amid the collapse of the archontic order. The prophet and his rescuer survive the destruction precisely because their identity is not bound to the material structures that fall. The pneumatic endures the collapse of empires, temples, and civilizations because the divine spark within is not dependent on any material structure for its existence. The fall of Jerusalem is, for the pneumatic, not an ending but a revelation — the stripping away of material illusions that clears the way for the direct apprehension of the Pleroma.
