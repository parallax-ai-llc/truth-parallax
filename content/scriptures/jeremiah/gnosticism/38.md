Jeremiah 38 narrates the prophet's descent into the cistern, and Gnostic interpreters read this as a powerful myth of the pneumatic's condition in the material world. The cistern — dark, deep, filled with mud — is a perfect image of the hylic (material) realm into which the divine spark has fallen. Jeremiah, the bearer of *gnosis*, sinks into the depths of materiality, and his descent mirrors the primordial descent of Sophia into the lower regions of the cosmos. The mud that threatens to swallow the prophet represents the material substance that threatens to extinguish the divine spark entirely.

Ebed-melech's rescue of Jeremiah is read as the intervention of the Redeemer figure — the aeon who descends from above to rescue the trapped divine spark. That the rescuer is a foreigner and an outsider is theologically significant: the agent of salvation comes from outside the Demiurge's system, not from within it. The officials who threw Jeremiah into the cistern represent the archontic powers that suppress *gnosis*; Ebed-melech, the outsider who defies them, represents the Pleromatic intervention that breaks through the archontic order from beyond.

The rags and worn-out clothes placed under the ropes (38:11-12) carry symbolic weight in the Gnostic reading: the rescuer must use the materials of the material world — humble, degraded materials — to effect the rescue. The Redeemer who descends into matter must use material means to reach the trapped spark, just as the Gnostic teacher uses language, text, and ritual — all material vehicles — to communicate the immaterial truth of *gnosis*. The material order, though a prison, also provides the instruments of liberation.
