Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 3 centers on the halakhic question raised by the divorce analogy: Deuteronomy 24:1-4 prohibits a man from remarrying his divorced wife after she has been with another. Yet God invites Israel to return despite her "many lovers" (3:1). The Talmud (Yoma 86b) resolves this by distinguishing between human law and divine grace — God's capacity for forgiveness exceeds the categories of human jurisprudence. This becomes a foundational text for the rabbinic theology of *teshuvah* (repentance): God's mercy is not limited by the legal analogies the prophets employ.

The distinction between Israel (the northern kingdom) and Judah is significant for rabbinic interpretation. Judah's sin is worse than Israel's because Judah had the benefit of witnessing Israel's punishment and still "did not return with her whole heart, but in pretense" (3:10). The Midrash (Eichah Rabbah, Proem 3) elaborates: pretended repentance is worse than open rebellion because it corrupts the very mechanism of return. This insight informs the halakhic requirements for genuine confession (*vidui*) in the Yom Kippur liturgy, which demands sincerity of heart, not merely verbal formulas.

The prophecy that the Ark of the Covenant will no longer be remembered (3:16) generated significant discussion among the medieval commentators. Rashi interprets this as referring to the messianic era, when the divine presence will be so manifest that the Ark will be unnecessary. Abarbanel, writing after the destruction of the Second Temple (which never possessed the Ark), reads this as already partially fulfilled and pointing toward a future redemption in which the direct experience of God's presence will surpass anything mediated by sacred objects.
