Jewish exegesis of Jeremiah 5 reads the search for a righteous person (5:1) in light of the Abrahamic precedent of Genesis 18:23-32, where Abraham bargains with God over the fate of Sodom. The difference is striking: Abraham asks if fifty, then forty-five, then ten righteous could save the city, and God agrees. Here, God offers to spare Jerusalem for a single righteous person, and none can be found. Rashi connects this to the rabbinic principle that the merit of the righteous (*zekhut tsaddikim*) protects the community, and the absence of such merit leaves the city defenseless.

The people's complacent declaration — "He will do nothing" (5:12) — represents what the rabbis called *chutzpah klapei shemaya* (audacity toward heaven). The Talmud (Sotah 49b) identifies brazenness as one of the signs of the pre-messianic era, and Jeremiah's contemporaries embody this quality in their casual dismissal of divine warning. The Midrash (Eichah Rabbah 1:1) lists complacency among the factors that led to the destruction of Jerusalem, noting that the people trusted in the Temple's presence rather than in their own righteousness.

The closing rhetorical question — "Shall I not punish them for these things?" (5:29) — frames divine judgment as a response to moral necessity, not arbitrary caprice. Malbim, in his characteristic linguistic analysis, notes that the Hebrew construction implies inevitability: given the evidence, punishment is the only just response. This aligns with the rabbinic principle that God operates *middat hadin* (the attribute of justice) alongside *middat harachamim* (the attribute of mercy), and that persistent sin eventually exhausts even divine patience within the historical order.
