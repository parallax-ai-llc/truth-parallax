The vision of the two baskets of figs in Jeremiah 24 challenged the assumption that those who remained in Jerusalem were more blessed than those taken into exile. Rashi explains that the "good figs" represent the scholars and leaders (including Jehoiachin and Daniel) who were exiled in 597 BCE, while the "bad figs" represent those who remained behind under Zedekiah. The Talmud (Gittin 88a) preserves the tradition that the best of Judah's population was exiled first, suggesting that God preserved the most righteous by removing them from the doomed city.

The promise "I will give them a heart to know that I am the Lord" (24:7) is connected in Jewish theology to the concept of *daat Hashem* (knowledge of God) as a divine gift rather than a purely human achievement. While Maimonides emphasizes the intellectual dimension of this knowledge, the Hasidic tradition, particularly through the teachings of the Baal Shem Tov, understood *daat* as experiential intimacy with God — a heartfelt awareness that transcends mere intellectual comprehension. Jeremiah's promise of a "heart to know" supports this more holistic understanding.

The covenantal formula "they shall be my people, and I will be their God" (24:7) recurs throughout Jeremiah and represents the essence of the covenant relationship. The Midrash (Mekhilta de-Rabbi Ishmael, Shirata 3) explains this formula as mutual belonging — God belongs to Israel as Israel belongs to God. The exile, paradoxically, deepens this relationship by stripping away everything extraneous (land, Temple, monarchy) and revealing that the covenant bond itself is indestructible. This theology of exile as purification became central to Jewish self-understanding across subsequent centuries of diaspora.
