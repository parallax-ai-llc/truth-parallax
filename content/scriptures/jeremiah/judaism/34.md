Jeremiah 34 narrates the broken covenant of liberty — the release and subsequent re-enslavement of Hebrew slaves — which Jewish interpreters read as a critical moment in the halakhic and moral history of ancient Israel. The sabbatical release law (Deuteronomy 15:12-18) is not merely a recommendation but a binding mitzvah, and Jeremiah's oracle demonstrates that its violation carries catastrophic consequences. The Talmud (Gittin 65a) and Rashi's commentary discuss the legal mechanics of the release and re-enslavement, noting that the re-enslavement constituted not only a moral failing but a specific violation of Torah law.

The covenant ceremony described in 34:18-19 — passing between the halves of a calf — connects to the *brit bein ha-betarim* (the covenant between the pieces) of Genesis 15. Jewish commentators note the deliberate echoing: just as God made an unconditional covenant with Abraham through this ritual, the people of Jerusalem made a conditional covenant among themselves and then violated it. The contrast between divine faithfulness and human faithlessness is a central theme of Jewish covenant theology, and Jeremiah 34 provides one of its starkest illustrations.

The chapter's emphasis on the liberation of slaves has carried particular moral weight in Jewish ethical thought. The principle that human beings cannot be permanently enslaved — rooted in the Torah's sabbatical release laws and reinforced by Jeremiah's prophetic denunciation — influenced Jewish responses to slavery throughout history. Maimonides's laws of *avadim* (slaves) in the *Mishneh Torah* consistently emphasize the obligations of the master and the rights of the servant, reflecting the prophetic insistence that economic power must be exercised within covenantal limits.
