Jeremiah's arrest by Pashhur and subsequent lament (20:1-18) represents the nadir of prophetic experience, and Jewish interpreters have found in it both a model of honest prayer and a limit case for the theology of suffering. The Talmud (Megillah 14b) discusses the distinction between prophets who primarily served their own generation and those whose messages have permanent significance; Jeremiah's laments earned permanent status precisely because they articulate a universal experience of the cost of faithfulness.

The confession "O Lord, you have deceived me" (20:7) — or more precisely, "you have persuaded me and I was persuaded" — is read by Radak as expressing the prophet's realization that the prophetic calling, however glorious in principle, is devastating in practice. The Midrash (Shir Hashirim Rabbah 1:3) records that the prophets initially accepted their calling eagerly but later regretted it, except for Moses. Jeremiah's honesty in expressing this regret is valued in Jewish tradition as an example of *emet* (truth) in the divine-human relationship — faith that does not require the suppression of genuine feeling.

The cursing of the day of his birth (20:14-18), parallel to Job 3, represents the extreme limit of permissible religious protest in Jewish tradition. The rabbis note that Jeremiah does not curse God directly but curses his birth, his circumstances, and his messenger — directing his anguish at the conditions of his existence rather than at the divine nature itself. The distinction is halakhically significant: honest protest against one's situation is permitted and even valued, while blasphemy against God is prohibited. Jeremiah navigates this boundary with remarkable precision.
