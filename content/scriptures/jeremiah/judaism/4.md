Jeremiah 4 presents the terrifying vision of invasion and de-creation that Jewish interpreters read in the context of the coming Babylonian destruction. The call to "circumcise yourselves to the Lord; remove the foreskin of your hearts" (4:4) is central to Jewish theology of the inner meaning of circumcision. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 44a) discusses the concept of spiritual circumcision alongside physical circumcision, and Maimonides in the *Guide for the Perplexed* (III:49) argues that physical circumcision is a sign pointing toward the moral and spiritual transformation that God ultimately desires.

The de-creation imagery of 4:23-26 — "I looked at the earth, and it was without form and void" (*tohu vavohu*) — deliberately inverts the creation narrative of Genesis 1. Rashi notes the parallel and interprets it as meaning that the destruction of the land will be so thorough that it will appear as if creation itself has been undone. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 2:4) connects the *tohu vavohu* of Genesis 1:2 to the destructions of the four exiles, reading the primordial chaos as already containing the seeds of future catastrophe.

The prophet's personal grief (4:19-21) — "My anguish, my anguish!" — is understood in Jewish tradition as reflecting not merely human emotion but the prophetic experience of participating in God's own sorrow. The concept of God's suffering over Israel's sin (*kiyyavol*) appears throughout rabbinic literature and finds its prophetic archetype in Jeremiah. The Talmud (Hagigah 5b) quotes God as saying, "I weep in secret for your pride" — a divine grief that Jeremiah channels and embodies.
