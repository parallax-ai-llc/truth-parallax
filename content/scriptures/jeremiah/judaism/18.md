The potter and clay imagery of Jeremiah 18 raises fundamental questions about divine sovereignty and human freedom that Jewish philosophy has engaged with extensively. Maimonides (Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Teshuvah 5:1-5) insists that human beings possess genuine free will — God does not compel either obedience or disobedience. The conditional statements of 18:7-10 — that God's plans change in response to human choices — serve as a prooftext for this position. The potter metaphor illustrates God's power, but the conditional oracle demonstrates that this power operates in dialogue with human freedom.

Rashi interprets the potter's reworking of a marred vessel as a model of *teshuvah*: just as the potter does not discard the flawed clay but reshapes it, so God does not discard Israel but offers the opportunity for repentance and renewal. This image became foundational for the *teshuvah* theology of the High Holy Days. The liturgical poem *Ki Hinei KaChomer* ("Like Clay in the Hand of the Potter"), sung during Yom Kippur, is a direct elaboration of Jeremiah 18, applying the potter metaphor to the relationship between God and Israel in the context of the annual process of judgment and forgiveness.

The plot against Jeremiah (18:18) — "let us smite him with the tongue" — is interpreted in rabbinic tradition as an instance of *lashon hara* (evil speech), one of the gravest sins in Jewish ethics. The Talmud (Arakhin 15b) teaches that evil speech "kills three: the speaker, the listener, and the one spoken about." Jeremiah's experience of being targeted by malicious speech connects to the broader prophetic critique of the misuse of language, a theme that rabbinic Judaism developed into an elaborate ethical system governing speech.
