The remnant's approach to Jeremiah for divine guidance (42:1-6) is analyzed by the medieval commentators with characteristic psychological insight. Rashi notes the irony that the people swear "may the LORD be a true and faithful witness against us" (42:5) while already intending to disregard any answer that contradicts their plans. The Talmudic principle *mitoch she-lo lishmah, ba lishmah* (from insincere action, sincere action may come) does not apply here, because the insincerity is not a starting point for growth but a mask for predetermined rebellion.

The ten-day interval before God's response (42:7) is interpreted by Radak as a test of faith: a community genuinely seeking guidance would wait patiently, while those merely seeking validation for their own plans would grow impatient. Abarbanel suggests that the delay also served to give the people time to examine their hearts and align their will with God's before receiving the answer. The oracle itself — remain in the land and prosper, or flee to Egypt and perish — echoes the Deuteronomic covenant structure of blessing and curse that defines the theology of the entire Nevi'im.

Malbim observes that God's promise "I will build you up and not tear you down, I will plant you and not uproot you" (42:10) deliberately echoes the language of Jeremiah's original commission (1:10), suggesting that the remnant is being offered a fresh start — a new planting and building after the uprooting and destruction. The tragedy of chapter 43 is that this offer of renewal is refused, making the Egyptian exile a self-inflicted wound rather than a divine decree.
