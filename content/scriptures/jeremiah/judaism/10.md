Jeremiah 10's satire of idol-making (10:3-5) — the detailed description of cutting a tree, overlaying it with silver and gold, and nailing it in place — finds its natural Jewish context in the strict monotheism that defines the faith. The Talmud (Avodah Zarah) devotes an entire tractate to the laws of idolatry, reflecting the seriousness with which Judaism takes the prophetic critique. Rashi notes that Jeremiah's description is deliberately humorous — the gods that must be carried because they cannot walk are a parody of the true God who "rides the heavens" (Deuteronomy 33:26).

The confession "The Lord is the true God; he is the living God and the everlasting King" (10:10) resonates with the central Jewish confession of the *Shema* (Deuteronomy 6:4) and with the liturgical declaration "The Lord is God" (*Adonai hu ha-Elohim*) recited at the climax of the Yom Kippur service. Jewish liturgical theology weaves together these biblical confessions into a unified affirmation of God's reality, vitality, and sovereignty that stands in absolute contrast to the lifeless idols Jeremiah ridicules.

Jeremiah's prayer for measured correction (10:24) — "not in your anger, lest you bring me to nothing" — is echoed in the daily Jewish liturgy, where the worshiper asks God for correction that heals rather than destroys. The concept of *yissurim shel ahavah* (chastisements of love) in rabbinic theology (Berakhot 5a) understands divine discipline as an expression of love aimed at purification and growth. Jeremiah's prayer establishes the prophetic foundation for this theological concept that becomes central to rabbinic spirituality.
