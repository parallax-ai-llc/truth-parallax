Jeremiah 15 opens with the extraordinary declaration that even Moses and Samuel — Israel's two greatest intercessors — could not save this people. The Midrash (Pesikta de-Rav Kahana 15:6) elaborates: Moses interceded after the Golden Calf and prevailed; Samuel interceded at Mizpah and prevailed; but the accumulated sin of Jeremiah's generation has exceeded even their intercessory capacity. This text was invoked in rabbinic discussions about the limits of *zekhut avot* (merit of the ancestors) — the theological principle that the righteousness of the patriarchs and matriarchs protects their descendants, but not indefinitely.

The prophet's confession — "Your words were found, and I ate them" (15:16) — is one of the most important texts for the Jewish understanding of Torah study as spiritual nourishment. The metaphor of eating God's word connects to the rabbinic teaching that Torah study sustains the soul as food sustains the body (Avot 6:7). The daily rhythm of Jewish learning — morning, afternoon, and evening study — reflects the conviction that engagement with divine wisdom is not an occasional activity but a continuous sustenance necessary for spiritual life.

God's conditional restoration of the prophet — "If you return, I will restore you" (15:19) — applies the very principle of *teshuvah* (repentance) that Jeremiah has been preaching to the people. The rabbis noted the irony: even the prophet of repentance must himself repent. The Talmud (Berakhot 34b) teaches that "in the place where penitents stand, even the wholly righteous cannot stand" — suggesting that the experience of falling and returning creates a depth of spiritual knowledge that unbroken righteousness does not provide.
