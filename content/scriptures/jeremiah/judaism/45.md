Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 45 focuses on the personal relationship between the prophet and his scribe Baruch ben Neriah, and on the theological significance of Baruch's lament. Rashi notes that the chapter is dated to the fourth year of Jehoiakim (45:1), the same year Baruch wrote the first scroll (chapter 36), suggesting that Baruch's distress arose from the burden of transcribing prophecies of national destruction. The Talmud (Megillah 14b) includes Baruch among those who were worthy of prophecy, and this oracle confirms his special status.

The divine command "Do not seek great things for yourself" (45:5) is interpreted by Radak as addressing Baruch's aspiration to prophetic status equal to Jeremiah's. Abarbanel suggests a different reading: Baruch's "great things" were not spiritual ambitions but ordinary human desires — family, security, recognition — that the catastrophic times would deny him. The Midrash (Seder Olam Rabbah) preserves traditions about Baruch's later life, including his role in preserving and transmitting the prophetic writings, suggesting that his greatest contribution was not prophecy itself but the faithful stewardship of the prophetic word.

The promise of life preserved "as a prize of war" resonates with the rabbinic concept of *pikuach nefesh* — the supreme value of preserving life. In a time of universal destruction, mere survival becomes a divine gift. The Talmudic discussion of what constitutes adequate reward for the righteous finds in this chapter a sobering answer: in times of catastrophe, the righteous are promised not triumph but continuation — enough to carry the tradition forward into the next generation.
