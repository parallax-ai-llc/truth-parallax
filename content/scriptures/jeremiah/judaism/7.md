The Temple Sermon of Jeremiah 7 occupies a place of particular significance in Jewish theology because it addresses the central question of what the Temple truly means. Jeremiah's warning against trusting in "deceptive words" — "the temple of the Lord, the temple of the Lord, the temple of the Lord" (7:4) — challenged the popular assumption that the Temple's mere existence guaranteed divine protection. The Talmud (Yoma 9b) attributes the destruction of the First Temple to three sins: idolatry, sexual immorality, and bloodshed — the very sins Jeremiah catalogues in 7:5-11.

The reference to Shiloh (7:12-14), where the Tabernacle once stood before its destruction by the Philistines, was a devastating rhetorical move. Rashi explains that Jeremiah is arguing from precedent: if God destroyed His own dwelling at Shiloh because of Israel's sins, He will certainly not spare the Jerusalem Temple. This argument undermined the entire theology of Zion's inviolability that had developed since David's time, and it nearly cost Jeremiah his life (as narrated in chapter 26).

The prohibition against intercession (7:16) is discussed in rabbinic literature in the context of the limits of prayer. While Jewish theology affirms the power of prayer (*tefillah*) and the merit of the righteous in averting divine decrees, Jeremiah 7 establishes that there are moments when judgment is irrevocable. The Mishnah (Avot 2:10) teaches "Repent one day before your death" — since one never knows when that day will be, the urgency of repentance is always present. Jeremiah's generation discovered too late that the window of repentance had closed.
