Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 46, the oracle against Egypt, is situated within the broader prophetic tradition of *nevu'ot al ha-goyim* (prophecies concerning the nations) that appears in Isaiah, Ezekiel, and Amos as well. Rashi connects the Battle of Carchemish (605 BC) to the geopolitical context of Judah's final decades, noting that Egypt's defeat by Nebuchadnezzar eliminated the last serious rival to Babylonian power and sealed Judah's fate as a Babylonian vassal. The historical specificity of the oracle demonstrates, in rabbinic reading, that prophecy engages with concrete political reality rather than offering abstract spiritual truths.

Radak analyzes the vivid military imagery (46:3-12) with attention to its literary artistry, noting the progression from confident preparation for battle to panicked flight and total defeat. The Midrash uses Egypt's humiliation to illustrate the principle that God's providence governs the affairs of all nations, not Israel alone — a theme that Maimonides developed philosophically in the *Guide of the Perplexed*. The nations are judged by the same moral standards that apply to Israel, because the Noahide laws establish a universal framework of divine expectation.

The concluding oracle of comfort to Israel (46:27-28) — "Do not fear, O Jacob my servant" — is read in the Jewish liturgical tradition as an expression of covenant faithfulness that transcends historical catastrophe. The distinction between the total destruction of the nations and the measured discipline of Israel reflects the rabbinic teaching that God's relationship with Israel is fundamentally pedagogical: suffering is not punitive but corrective, aimed at *teshuvah* (repentance) and return. This passage is echoed in the liturgy of the High Holy Days, where divine judgment and divine mercy are held together.
