## Overview

In Judaism, Jeremiah (*Yirmeyahu*) is one of the three Major Prophets in the Nevi'im (Prophets) section of the Tanakh. The book occupies a central place in Jewish consciousness because Jeremiah witnessed and prophesied the destruction of the First Temple — an event that permanently shaped Jewish theology, liturgy, and identity. Jewish interpretation spans millennia, from the Targum Jonathan and Talmudic discussions through the medieval commentators (Rishonim) and into modern critical scholarship.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
The straightforward historical and literary reading. **Rashi** (1040-1105) provides the standard peshat commentary, drawing on Talmudic and midrashic traditions while emphasizing the contextual sense. **Radak** (Rabbi David Kimchi, 1160-1235) offers grammatical-historical analysis.

### Derash (Homiletical)
Midrashic elaboration on Jeremiah's prophecies:
- **Pesikta de-Rav Kahana**: Homilies linked to the haftarot from Jeremiah
- **Eichah Rabbah**: Midrash on Lamentations with extensive Jeremiah traditions
- **Talmud Bavli, Gittin 55b-58a**: Narratives of the destruction incorporating Jeremiah material

### Remez and Sod
Kabbalistic interpreters, especially the Zohar, read Jeremiah's prophecies of destruction and restoration as reflecting dynamics within the *sefirot* — the exile of the *Shekhinah* and its eventual reunion with the divine.

## Theological Emphases

- **Destruction and Memory**: Jeremiah as the prophet of the *Churban* (destruction), whose words are recited on Tisha B'Av
- **Teshuvah (Repentance)**: Jeremiah's insistent call to return to God as paradigmatic for Jewish penitential theology
- **Brit Hadashah (New Covenant)**: Understood in Judaism not as abrogation of the Torah but as its renewal and deeper internalization
- **Exile and Return**: The theological pattern of *galut* and *geulah* (exile and redemption) central to Jewish thought
- **False Prophecy**: The conflict between Jeremiah and Hananiah (ch. 28) as a defining case in Jewish law

## Liturgical Context

Jeremiah is read extensively in the haftarot:

| Haftarah | Chapters | Occasion |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Matot | 1:1-2:3 | Shabbat during Three Weeks |
| Masei | 2:4-28; 3:4; 4:1-2 | Shabbat during Three Weeks |
| Various fast days | Selected passages | Tisha B'Av, 17 Tammuz |

The three *haftarot of rebuke* (*tlat d'puranuta*) before Tisha B'Av are drawn from Jeremiah (chapters 1-2) and the Book of Jeremiah shapes the liturgical mood of the Three Weeks mourning period.
