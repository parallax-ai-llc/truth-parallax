Jeremiah 11 returns to the covenant framework of Deuteronomy, and Jewish interpreters read the chapter as a prophetic reiteration of the covenant curses that were operative from Sinai onward. Rashi connects "the words of this covenant" (11:2) to the specific curses of Deuteronomy 27-28, reading Jeremiah's oracle as a declaration that these curses are now being activated. The rabbinic understanding of covenant history as a continuous narrative — from Sinai through the prophets to the destruction and beyond — gives this chapter particular weight as the prophetic authentication of the Deuteronomic warnings.

The plot against Jeremiah by the men of Anathoth (11:18-23) has generated extensive midrashic elaboration. Pesikta de-Rav Kahana records that Jeremiah's own family conspired against him because his prophecies threatened their livelihood as priests — if the Temple were destroyed, their priestly functions would cease. This economic motive adds a dimension to the opposition that the rabbis recognized: the resistance to prophetic truth often has material as well as spiritual roots. The concept of *negiah* (personal bias) in halakhic decision-making reflects awareness of this reality.

The "lamb led to the slaughter" imagery (11:19) — describing Jeremiah's unknowing vulnerability to the conspiracy — is read in Jewish tradition as a metaphor for the innocent suffering of the righteous. While Christianity interprets this typologically as pointing to Jesus, Jewish exegesis maintains the focus on Jeremiah himself and, by extension, on the experience of the Jewish people throughout history. The image of innocent suffering became a powerful symbol in post-Holocaust Jewish theology, where Jeremiah's experience of betrayal by his own community resonates with painful modern parallels.
