Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 52 reads the historical appendix as the factual foundation of the *Churban* (destruction) that defines so much of subsequent Jewish life, liturgy, and theology. The chapter's content closely parallels 2 Kings 24-25 and provides the historical basis for the liturgical commemorations of Tisha B'Av, the Fast of Gedaliah, and the other fasts mentioned in Zechariah 8:19. Rashi and Radak treat the chapter as a historical record rather than a prophetic oracle, noting its function as the documentary conclusion to the prophetic book.

The inventory of the Temple vessels (52:17-23) is read in the Jewish tradition with profound grief and longing. The Talmud (Yoma 21b) discusses the fate of the sacred vessels, with some traditions holding that they were hidden before the destruction by Jeremiah or by angels and will be revealed in the messianic era. The Mishnah's detailed descriptions of the Temple's furnishings and rituals (*Middot*, *Tamid*) preserve the memory of what was lost, and Jeremiah 52's catalog participates in this literature of sacred remembrance.

The release of Jehoiachin (52:31-34) is interpreted in the Jewish tradition as the preservation of the Davidic line through exile — a slender thread of continuity that sustains the messianic hope. The Midrash (Ruth Rabbah 5:6) traces the genealogy of the Messiah through Jehoiachin's descendants, reading his liberation as a sign that the Davidic covenant, though eclipsed, remains unbroken. The book of Jeremiah ends not with destruction but with this quiet restoration, confirming the rabbinic teaching that the prophets always concluded their messages with words of comfort and hope (*Bava Batra* 14b-15a).
