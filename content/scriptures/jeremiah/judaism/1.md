Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah's call narrative begins with its liturgical context: Jeremiah 1:1-2:3 is read as the haftarah for Parashat Matot, the first of the three *haftarot of rebuke* (*tlat d'puranuta*) leading up to Tisha B'Av. This liturgical setting frames the call narrative not merely as biographical information about the prophet but as the opening act of the drama of destruction that culminates in the loss of the Temple. The rabbinic tradition reads Jeremiah's call as inseparable from his mission of witnessing the *Churban* (destruction).

Rashi, commenting on "Before I formed you in the womb I knew you" (1:5), explains that God's foreknowledge of Jeremiah reflects divine awareness of the prophet's particular suitability for the mission of warning and weeping. The Midrash (Pesikta de-Rav Kahana 13:4) elaborates that Jeremiah initially resisted his call by comparing himself unfavorably to Moses, but God responded that Jeremiah's mission was in some respects more difficult — Moses led a people out of slavery, while Jeremiah would witness a people's descent into exile.

The two visions — the almond branch (*shaqed*) and the boiling pot — are analyzed by Radak (Rabbi David Kimchi) with characteristic grammatical precision. The wordplay between *shaqed* (almond) and *shoqed* (watching) is understood as a prophetic pun that establishes God's vigilance over the prophetic word. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) places Jeremiah among the prophets who saw Jerusalem's destruction and wept, while Rabbi Akiva famously laughed — because the fulfillment of the prophecies of destruction guaranteed the future fulfillment of the prophecies of restoration.
