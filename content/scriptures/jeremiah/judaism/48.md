Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 48 reads the oracle against Moab within the complex biblical relationship between Israel and its eastern neighbor. Rashi notes that Moab's origins (Genesis 19:37) as the son of Lot, Abraham's nephew, establish a familial connection that makes Moab's hostility toward Israel a form of betrayal. The Torah's command "Do not harass Moab or contend with them in battle" (Deuteronomy 2:9) reflects this kinship, yet the prophetic oracle declares that Moab's pride and hostility have exhausted God's patience.

The divine lament over Moab's destruction (48:31-36) is analyzed by Radak as demonstrating God's compassion for all His creatures, even those under judgment. The Midrash extends this principle, teaching that God rebuked the angels for singing when the Egyptians drowned at the Sea of Reeds: "My creatures are drowning, and you sing?" This tradition contextualizes God's grief over Moab within the broader rabbinic understanding of divine compassion as universal, embracing even those who have acted as Israel's enemies.

The closing promise of restoration "in the latter days" (48:47) is debated in rabbinic tradition. Rashi interprets it as referring to the messianic era, when all nations will acknowledge God's sovereignty. The Talmudic concept of *gerim* (converts) who join the Jewish people from among the nations may be anticipated in such promises. Malbim sees the restoration promises as evidence that divine judgment, even against the nations, is ultimately corrective rather than purely punitive, reflecting the divine attribute of mercy (*rachamim*) that Jewish theology holds to be primary.
