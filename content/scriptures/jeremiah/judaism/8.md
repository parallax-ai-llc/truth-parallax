Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 8 focuses particularly on the contrast between nature's faithfulness and Israel's rebellion. The observation that migratory birds follow their divinely appointed patterns while God's people reject His ordinances (8:7) is glossed by Rashi as demonstrating that even creatures without rational choice fulfill their Creator's purpose, while human beings, endowed with reason and Torah, persistently deviate. The Midrash expands this into a broader argument about the natural world's testimony to its Creator — a theme developed extensively in Jewish philosophical literature.

The indictment of the scribes — "the lying pen of the scribes has made it into a lie" (8:8) — generated considerable rabbinic discussion. Rashi interprets this as referring to scribes who misinterpret Torah for their own purposes, while Radak understands it as describing those who write false prophecies and attribute them to God. The verse became significant in Jewish debates about the authority of oral tradition versus written text, with the rabbis insisting that the oral Torah (*Torah she-be'al peh*) provides the authoritative interpretation that prevents the kind of scribal manipulation Jeremiah condemns.

The question "Is there no balm in Gilead?" (8:22) is interpreted in rabbinic tradition as a rhetorical expression of divine grief. The Targum Jonathan renders it as a question about whether there is no merit in the patriarchs or good deeds in the community that could serve as healing medicine. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) connects the imagery of healing to the messianic hope — the ultimate balm for Israel's wounds will come with the arrival of the Messiah, who is described in Isaiah 53 as bearing the people's sicknesses.
