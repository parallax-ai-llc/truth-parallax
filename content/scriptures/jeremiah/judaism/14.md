Jeremiah 14 describes a drought that Jewish interpreters read within the framework of the covenant blessings and curses. Drought is among the explicit curses of Deuteronomy 28:23-24, and its occurrence confirms the prophetic warning that covenant violation has tangible consequences. The Mishnah (Ta'anit 1-3) prescribes a sequence of public fasts in response to drought, reflecting the conviction that natural disasters have spiritual causes and require spiritual remedies — a conviction grounded in passages like Jeremiah 14.

The exposure of false prophets (14:13-16) is central to the Jewish legal discussion of how to distinguish true from false prophecy. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 89a-90a) establishes criteria: a prophet who speaks in the name of an idol, who prophesies something he did not hear, or who suppresses prophecy is liable to death. Maimonides codifies these laws in Mishneh Torah (Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah 9-10), adding that a prophet who contradicts Torah is ipso facto false. Jeremiah's confrontation with the false prophets provides the narrative context for these halakhic rulings.

Jeremiah's intercessory prayer (14:19-22) models a form of prayer that became standard in Jewish liturgy: the combination of complaint, confession, and appeal to God's covenantal faithfulness. The structure of the prayer — acknowledging sin ("we have sinned"), appealing to God's honor ("do not spurn us, for your name's sake"), and confessing God's unique power ("you have done all these things") — mirrors the structure of the *Amidah* prayer and the *Selichot* (penitential prayers) recited during the High Holy Day season.
