The invitation to "ask for the ancient paths" (6:16) has resonated deeply in Jewish tradition as a validation of fidelity to Torah and ancestral wisdom. Rashi interprets the "ancient paths" as the ways of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — the patriarchal model of faith that the people have abandoned. The Talmud (Shabbat 31a) connects this to the teaching that a person will be asked on the day of judgment, "Did you engage in Torah study? Did you conduct business faithfully?" — questions that reflect the "ancient paths" of moral and intellectual integrity.

The refiner's metaphor (6:27-30) — Jeremiah appointed to test the people's metal, finding only dross — is analyzed by Radak as describing the prophetic ministry itself: the prophet's words are the fire that tests the people, and their response reveals their true nature. The concept of *tsiruf* (refining) appears throughout Jewish mystical literature, particularly in the Zohar, where the trials of the righteous are understood as a purifying process that separates the holy sparks from their material encasement.

The false cry of "Peace, peace, when there is no peace" (6:14) became one of the most frequently quoted prophetic phrases in rabbinic literature about the destruction of Jerusalem. The Talmud (Gittin 55b-56a) narrates how false optimism and factional conflict led to the destruction of the Second Temple, echoing Jeremiah's warning about the First. The phrase has become proverbial in Jewish discourse for any situation where reassuring words mask a dangerous reality, and it is frequently invoked in contemporary Jewish ethical discussion.
