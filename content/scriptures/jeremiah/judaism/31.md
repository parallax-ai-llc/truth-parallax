Jeremiah 31 is one of the most theologically significant and contested chapters in Jewish biblical interpretation, primarily because of the new covenant prophecy (31:31-34). Jewish interpreters insist that the "new covenant" does not replace Torah but rather promises its internalization — God will write the same Torah on Israel's hearts so that obedience becomes natural rather than coerced. Rashi and other medieval commentators emphasize that the content of the covenant remains unchanged; what changes is the mode of its apprehension. The *brit chadashah* is not a different Torah but the same Torah apprehended with new depth and immediacy.

Rachel's weeping for her children (31:15) is one of the most powerful images in Jewish liturgical and literary tradition. The Midrash (Lamentations Rabbah, Proem 24) develops an extraordinary narrative in which Rachel's intercession succeeds where the intercession of the patriarchs and Moses fails — God is moved by Rachel's tears because she herself endured the displacement of being replaced by her sister Leah, and so she can speak to God about Israel's displacement with unique authority. This midrashic tradition has sustained Jewish communities through centuries of persecution, affirming that the tears of the innocent are heard in heaven.

The cosmic imagery of the chapter's conclusion (31:35-37) — the fixed order of sun, moon, and stars as guarantees of Israel's permanence — has been read throughout Jewish history as a divine assurance that the people of Israel will endure despite every attempt at annihilation. The Talmud (Pesachim 68a) draws on this passage to affirm that God's covenant with Israel is as unbreakable as the laws of nature. Jewish theology has consistently cited this text in response to supersessionist claims, insisting that God's covenant with Israel is eternal and irrevocable.
