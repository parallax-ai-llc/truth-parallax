Jeremiah 21 records Zedekiah's appeal to the prophet during the Babylonian siege, and Jeremiah's shocking response that God fights against Jerusalem with the language of the Exodus — "with outstretched hand and strong arm" (21:5). Jewish interpreters note the devastating irony: the very phrases used to describe the liberation from Egypt (Deuteronomy 4:34, 5:15) now describe God's assault on His own city. The Midrash (Eichah Rabbah 1:41) elaborates this reversal, listing all the ways in which the Exodus imagery is inverted during the destruction.

Jeremiah's counsel to surrender (21:8-10) — presenting submission to Babylon as "the way of life" — was perceived as treason by his contemporaries. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 104b) discusses the ethical complexity of Jeremiah's position: was the prophet undermining national morale, or was he faithfully delivering God's word? The rabbinic consensus is that authentic prophecy always takes precedence over political considerations, even when the prophetic message appears to serve the enemy's interests. This principle was debated again during later Jewish historical crises, including the Bar Kokhba revolt.

The demand for justice directed at the Davidic house (21:11-12) reflects the Jewish understanding that kingship is a conditional institution, contingent on the king's fidelity to Torah. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 19b-20a) discusses the king's obligation to carry a Torah scroll and consult it constantly, and the Mishneh Torah (Hilkhot Melakhim) spells out the legal obligations of the Jewish king. Jeremiah's critique of the kings thus operates within a well-established halakhic framework that subordinates royal authority to divine law.
