The rejection of Jeremiah's oracle and the flight to Egypt (43:1-7) represent, in Jewish interpretation, the final failure of the generation of the *Churban*. Rashi notes that the accusation against Baruch (43:3) — that the scribe manipulated Jeremiah into forbidding the journey — reveals a community that has exhausted every excuse for disobedience. Having promised to obey God's word, they now deny that the word they received is genuinely divine, a strategy of evasion that the prophets consistently denounce.

The forced removal of Jeremiah and Baruch to Egypt (43:5-7) is read by Radak as the ultimate irony of the prophet's career: the man who spent forty years warning against reliance on Egypt is now compelled to live there. The Midrash (Seder Olam Rabbah 26) records a tradition that Jeremiah eventually returned to the Land of Israel, though the biblical text does not confirm this. Abarbanel suggests that Jeremiah's presence in Egypt served a continuing prophetic purpose — even in exile, the remnant was not abandoned by the prophetic word.

Jeremiah's sign-act at Tahpanhes (43:8-13) — burying stones where Nebuchadnezzar would set his throne — is understood in rabbinic tradition as demonstrating that *galut* offers no escape from divine judgment. The lesson applies not only to the historical flight to Egypt but to every subsequent Jewish community that has sought security through political alignment with powerful nations. The rabbinic tradition of placing ultimate trust in God rather than in the favor of empires finds one of its foundational texts in this passage.
