Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 47, the oracle against the Philistines, is read within the broader context of Israel's historical relationship with its western neighbors. Rashi identifies the approaching destruction with the Babylonian campaign that would sweep through the Philistine cities of Gaza, Ashkelon, and Ekron in the course of Nebuchadnezzar's western expansion. Radak notes the reference to Tyre and Sidon (47:4) as allies of the Philistines, suggesting a broader coalition that would be swept away by the Babylonian advance.

The imagery of overwhelming waters from the north (47:2) is analyzed by Malbim as a deliberate inversion of the Sea of Reeds narrative — whereas God once parted the waters to save Israel, now the waters are unleashed to destroy Israel's enemies. This typological reading connects the oracle to the broader pattern of divine salvation history (*heilsgeschichte*) that Jewish interpretation traces from the Exodus through the prophets to the eschatological redemption. The Midrash extends this pattern, reading the judgment of the Philistines as a partial fulfillment of the promise that God would ultimately remove all threats to Israel's security.

The chapter's brevity — only seven verses — is noted by Abarbanel as itself significant: the Philistines, despite their prominence in the books of Judges and Samuel, receive only a brief prophetic notice because their historical role in relation to Israel is drawing to a close. The Babylonian conquest would effectively end the Philistines as a distinct people, and the oracle's conciseness reflects the swiftness and finality of their judgment.
