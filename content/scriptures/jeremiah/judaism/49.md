Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 49 examines the oracles against the five nations with attention to each nation's particular historical relationship with Israel. The oracle against Ammon (49:1-6) is connected by Rashi to Ammon's seizure of Gadite territory after the Assyrian deportation of the northern tribes — a land-grab that violated the divine allocation of territory established in the conquest narratives. The prophetic question "Has Israel no sons? Has he no heir?" (49:1) implies that the land's rightful inhabitants will return, a principle that resonates deeply in Jewish thought about the Land of Israel.

The oracle against Edom (49:7-22) carries particular weight in rabbinic interpretation because of the identification of Edom with Esau and, in later rabbinic tradition, with Rome and its successor civilizations. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 65:21) elaborates the rivalry between Jacob and Esau into a cosmic principle: when one rises, the other falls. The question "Is wisdom no more in Teman?" (49:7) is read by the medieval commentators as addressing the philosophical tradition of the Greek and Roman world, which Edom represents in rabbinic typology.

The restoration promises for Ammon (49:6) and Elam (49:39) are interpreted by Abarbanel within the messianic framework: in the days of the Mashiach, the nations will recognize God's sovereignty and find their proper place within the divine order. The rabbinic tradition generally reads these promises as indicating that divine judgment on the nations is not God's last word — the ultimate vision is one of universal recognition of God's kingship, as expressed in the *Aleinu* prayer: "On that day the LORD will be one and His name one."
