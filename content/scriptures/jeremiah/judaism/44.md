Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 44 centers on the dramatic confrontation between the prophet and the Egyptian community over the worship of the *Melekhet ha-Shamayim* (Queen of Heaven, 44:17-19). Rashi identifies this deity with a celestial body — likely the planet Venus — whose cult involved the baking of special cakes (*kavvanim*) bearing the deity's image. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 63b) discusses the gravity of idolatrous worship and its communal dimensions, and Jeremiah 44 provides the most detailed biblical account of an entire community's conscious, articulate defense of idol worship.

The community's theological argument — that prosperity attended their worship and disaster followed its cessation — is addressed by Radak with characteristic logical precision. He argues that the community confused correlation with causation: the period of apparent prosperity coincided with God's patience, not His approval, while the destruction came as the long-deferred consequence of accumulated sin. Malbim adds that the community's reasoning reflects the fundamental error of empirical theology divorced from revelation — experience alone cannot interpret God's purposes.

The final oracle (44:24-30) and the sign of Pharaoh Hophra's downfall mark, in rabbinic tradition, the end of prophecy's engagement with the Egyptian diaspora. The Midrash records traditions about the ultimate fate of the Egyptian community, with some sources suggesting that Jeremiah's prophecy of their complete destruction (44:27) was eventually fulfilled, while others maintain that a remnant survived and was eventually absorbed into other Jewish communities. The chapter serves as a foundational text in rabbinic discussions of *galut* and the spiritual dangers of assimilation.
