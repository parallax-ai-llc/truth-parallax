The assassination of Gedaliah by Ishmael ben Netaniah (41:1-3) is one of the most consequential events in Jewish historical memory. The Fast of Gedaliah, established by the prophets and codified in the Shulchan Arukh (Orach Chaim 549), commemorates this murder as the extinguishing of the last ember of Jewish sovereignty in the land. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 18b) teaches that Gedaliah's death sealed the fate of the remnant and completed the destruction that Nebuchadnezzar had begun, which is why the fast is observed with the solemnity normally reserved for national catastrophes.

The massacre of the seventy pilgrims from Shechem, Shiloh, and Samaria (41:4-9) raises difficult questions in rabbinic commentary. These men, coming with grain offerings and incense to the ruined Temple site, represent the persistence of sacrificial devotion even after the destruction of the altar. Rashi observes that their shaved beards and torn garments mark them as mourners for the Temple, and their murder while engaged in an act of piety deepens the horror of Ishmael's treachery. The Midrash reads their fate as illustrating the vulnerability of the righteous in a lawless age.

Radak notes that Ishmael's royal lineage (41:1 — "of the royal family") suggests that his motive was not merely political ambition but resentment that governance had been entrusted to a non-Davidic figure. This reading connects the assassination to the broader biblical theme of contested succession and the dangers of dynastic pride, a theme that resonates throughout the books of Kings and Chronicles.
