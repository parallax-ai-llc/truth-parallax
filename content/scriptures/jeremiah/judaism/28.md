Jeremiah 28 records the confrontation between Jeremiah and Hananiah ben Azzur, which rabbinic tradition treats as the definitive case study in the halakhah of false prophecy. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 89a-b) discusses this episode extensively, noting that Hananiah's false prophecy was not a matter of obvious fabrication — he based his prediction on a reasonable extrapolation from God's own words about breaking Nebuchadnezzar's yoke. The rabbis conclude that false prophecy may arise from the unauthorized extension of authentic prophetic material, a principle that informs the halakhic discussion of who may teach Torah and under what authority.

Jeremiah's response — "Amen! May the Lord do so!" (28:6) — reveals the prophet's humanity in a way that deeply resonates with rabbinic sensibility. The Midrash notes that Jeremiah genuinely wished for Hananiah's prophecy to be true, demonstrating that a true prophet does not take pleasure in pronouncing judgment. The Talmudic principle that "the Holy One, blessed be He, does not desire the death of the wicked" (Ezekiel 33:11) is embodied in Jeremiah's generous response, even as he recognizes the falsity of Hananiah's message.

The criterion Jeremiah articulates (28:8-9) — that prophets of doom have historical precedent while prophets of peace must be verified by fulfillment — became a foundational principle in rabbinic discussions of prophetic authority. Maimonides codifies this distinction in the *Mishneh Torah* (Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah 10:4), ruling that a prophet who predicts good fortune must see his prediction fulfilled to be validated, while a prophet of calamity is not disproven if the calamity fails to materialize, because God may have relented in response to repentance.
