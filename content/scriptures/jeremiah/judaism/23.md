Jeremiah 23's prophecy of the "righteous Branch" (*Tsemach Tsaddiq*) who will reign wisely and execute justice (23:5-6) is one of the most important messianic texts in the Hebrew Bible. Jewish interpretation identifies this figure as the Messiah — the future Davidic king who will restore Israel's sovereignty and inaugurate an era of justice. The name "The Lord is our Righteousness" (*Adonai Tsidqenu*) is discussed in the Talmud (Bava Batra 75b), which lists it among the names of the Messiah alongside "Shiloh," "Menahem" (comforter), and others.

The extended critique of false prophets (23:9-32) established the criteria that rabbinic Judaism codified as halakhah. Maimonides (Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah 10) specifies: a true prophet must be of outstanding character, must be tested through prediction of future events, and must never contradict Torah. The false prophets of Jeremiah's time failed on all counts — they were morally corrupt (23:14), their visions were self-generated (23:16), and their message contradicted the covenantal warnings that God had given through Moses. The halakhic framework for evaluating prophecy is essentially the formalization of Jeremiah's prophetic criteria.

The metaphor of God's word as fire and as a hammer shattering rock (23:29) is expounded in the Talmud (Sanhedrin 34a) with the teaching: "Just as a hammer strikes the rock and scatters many sparks, so a single verse of Scripture yields many meanings." This hermeneutical principle — that the divine word is inexhaustible in its interpretive potential — became foundational for the rabbinic method of midrash. Jeremiah's metaphor thus provides the biblical warrant for the entire enterprise of rabbinic interpretation, which reads the Torah as containing infinite layers of meaning accessible to each generation.
