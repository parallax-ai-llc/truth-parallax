The symbolic action of the linen belt in Jeremiah 13 is interpreted by Rashi as representing the intimate relationship between God and Israel: "as the belt clings to the waist of a man, so I made the whole house of Israel cling to me" (13:11). The Hebrew word for "cling" (*davaq*) is the same used in Genesis 2:24 ("a man shall cling to his wife") and Deuteronomy 10:20 ("to Him you shall cling"), establishing a web of covenantal associations. The ruined belt thus represents a rupture in the most intimate possible relationship — a theme that resonates with the nuptial imagery of chapter 2.

Radak notes that the journey to the Euphrates (*Perat*) is symbolically significant regardless of whether it refers to the great river or to a local site, because the Euphrates represents Babylon — the destination of the coming exile. The belt's ruin by contact with foreign territory prophesies what will happen to Israel's identity in exile. Yet Jewish interpretive tradition also finds hope in the image: a belt can be woven anew, and the relationship it symbolizes can be restored. The post-exilic restoration is thus already implicit in the sign of judgment.

The rhetorical question about the Ethiopian's skin and the leopard's spots (13:23) — "Then also you can do good who are accustomed to do evil" — is discussed by Abarbanel in terms of the power of habit (*hergel*) to become quasi-natural. Jewish moral philosophy, from the Talmud through Maimonides's "Eight Chapters" (introduction to Avot), recognizes that habitual behavior creates character traits that are extremely difficult, though not impossible, to change. The Rambam's systematic program of ethical development (*tikkun ha-middot*) is designed precisely to address the kind of entrenched evil Jeremiah describes.
