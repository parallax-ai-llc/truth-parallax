Jewish interpretation of Jeremiah 51 reads the culmination of the Babylon oracle as the divine guarantee that the empire which destroyed the Temple will itself be destroyed. The Talmud (Berakhot 57b) associates Babylon with the most intense form of exile (*galut*), and the prophetic promise of its fall provides the theological foundation for the hope of return. Rashi's commentary emphasizes the measure-for-measure (*middah k'neged middah*) principle: Babylon shattered God's Temple; therefore Babylon itself will be shattered beyond recovery.

The creation hymn within the oracle (51:15-19) is connected by Radak to the theological argument of Second Isaiah: the God who created the cosmos has the power and authority to reshape history. The Jewish liturgy incorporates this creation theology into the daily prayers (*Yotzer Or*), affirming that the God who creates the world anew each morning is the same God who governs the rise and fall of empires. The oracle against Babylon thus participates in the liturgical rhythm of Jewish life, which continually affirms divine sovereignty over all creation.

The symbolic act of Seraiah casting the scroll into the Euphrates (51:59-64) is analyzed in rabbinic tradition as the definitive sealing of Babylon's fate. The Midrash notes that Seraiah was Baruch's brother (compare 32:12 with 51:59), connecting the preservation of the prophetic word (Baruch's task) with its enactment through symbolic destruction (Seraiah's task). The sinking scroll becomes, in rabbinic reading, a permanent witness — even at the bottom of the Euphrates, the divine word remains operative, waiting for the moment of its fulfillment.
