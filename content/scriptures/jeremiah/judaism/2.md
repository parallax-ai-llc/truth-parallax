Jeremiah 2 forms part of the haftarah readings during the Three Weeks of mourning before Tisha B'Av, and Jewish interpretation reads the chapter as God's covenant lawsuit (*rib*) against Israel. The Targum Jonathan renders the opening verses with characteristic interpretive expansion, translating "the devotion of your youth" as Israel's faithfulness during the wilderness period. Rashi notes that even Israel's wilderness obedience was imperfect, but God remembers it with affection because it represented genuine trust.

The metaphor of the broken cisterns (2:13) — forsaking the fountain of living waters for self-made containers — is interpreted in the Midrash (Shir Hashirim Rabbah 1:9) as referring to the Torah itself: "If they had forsaken Me but kept My Torah, the light within it would have brought them back." This remarkable midrashic statement suggests that even God's absence can be partially compensated by faithful engagement with Torah — a theology of Torah study as communion with God that is central to rabbinic Judaism.

Radak analyzes the catalogue of Israel's sins as a progression from subtle to overt apostasy: first the abandonment of gratitude (2:5-7), then the failure of leadership — priests, rulers, prophets (2:8) — and finally the overt worship of foreign gods (2:11-13). This structural analysis reveals the rabbinic understanding that communal apostasy does not happen overnight but follows a predictable pattern of spiritual deterioration. The Talmudic concept of *aveirah goreret aveirah* — "one transgression leads to another" (Avot 4:2) — is illustrated here.
