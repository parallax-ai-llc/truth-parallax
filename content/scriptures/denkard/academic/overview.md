Academic study of the Denkard is central to Zoroastrian studies, Sasanian intellectual history, and the comparative study of late antique religions. The text is one of the most important surviving examples of Pahlavi (Middle Persian) theological literature and provides invaluable information about the Sasanian Avesta — the 21-*nask* compilation of Zoroastrian scripture that is now mostly lost.

Scholars analyze the Denkard's philosophical arguments in relation to contemporary Islamic theology (*kalam*), Christian apologetics, and Manichaean thought, revealing the rich intellectual exchanges that characterized the late Sasanian and early Islamic periods. The text's compositional history — spanning at least two major redactions — provides evidence of the Zoroastrian community's evolving strategies for self-definition and cultural survival under Islamic rule.
