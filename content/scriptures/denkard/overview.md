## Overview

The Denkard (Dēnkard, "Acts of the Religion" or "Encyclopedia of the Mazdean Religion") is a monumental 9th-century Pahlavi text that serves as the most comprehensive encyclopedia of Zoroastrian theology, philosophy, and religious knowledge. Compiled during the late Sasanian and early Islamic periods (primarily by Adurbad Emedan and later by Adurfarnbag Farroxzadan), the Denkard represents a systematic attempt to preserve and defend Zoroastrian religious knowledge in the face of the Islamic conquest of Iran.

## Structure

The Denkard originally comprised nine books (*madan*), of which the first two are largely lost:

1. **Book 1** — Lost, with surviving fragments on creation and revelation
2. **Book 2** — Lost, with surviving fragments on cosmology
3. **Book 3** — The largest and most philosophical book: theological discourses on the nature of God, creation, good and evil, and the relationship between religion and reason
4. **Book 4** — An apologetic work defending the superiority of Zoroastrianism over other religions
5. **Book 5** — Eschatological teachings about the end times and the coming of the Saoshyant (Savior)
6. **Book 6** — A collection of wisdom sayings, ethical counsels, and practical guidance for righteous living
7. **Book 7** — A biography of Zoroaster, including accounts of his birth, revelation, and mission
8. **Book 8** — Summaries of the 21 *nasks* (volumes) of the Sasanian Avesta, most of which are now lost
9. **Book 9** — Interpretive commentary on select Avestan texts

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Zoroastrian**: The most important theological encyclopedia of the faith, preserving knowledge from lost Avestan texts
- **Academic**: An indispensable source for the study of Zoroastrian theology, Sasanian intellectual culture, and Pahlavi literature
- **Comparative**: Reveals the interconnections between Zoroastrian and Abrahamic theological concepts
- **Protestant**: Illuminates the ancient roots of concepts such as monotheism, dualism, eschatology, and moral choice
- **Jewish**: Provides context for understanding Persian influences on Second Temple Judaism
- **Islamic**: Documents the Zoroastrian intellectual tradition that influenced early Islamic theology and philosophy

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Apologetics | Defense of Zoroastrian religion against competing faiths and philosophies |
| Dualism | The cosmic struggle between Ahura Mazda (Good) and Angra Mainyu (Evil) |
| Eschatology | The final renovation (*frashokereti*) and triumph of good over evil |
| Wisdom | Practical and philosophical wisdom for righteous living |
| Preservation | The systematic preservation of Zoroastrian knowledge in the face of cultural destruction |
