The Jewish reading of the Denkard engages with Zoroastrian theology as a crucial context for understanding developments in Jewish thought during and after the Babylonian Exile. The Persian period (538–332 BCE) — when Jews lived under benevolent Zoroastrian rule — is recognized as a formative era for Jewish concepts of angelology, demonology, eschatology, resurrection, and the cosmic struggle between good and evil.

The Denkard, though composed centuries after the Persian period, preserves Zoroastrian theological traditions that may reflect earlier teachings that influenced Jewish thought. Jewish scholars examine the parallels between Zoroastrian and Jewish concepts — such as the similarity between the *Saoshyant* and the Messiah, between *Amesha Spentas* and archangels, and between *frashokereti* and *olam ha-ba* — to assess the nature and extent of Persian influence on Judaism.
