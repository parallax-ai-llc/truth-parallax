## Overview

2 John, like 1 John, is directed against those who deny that Jesus Christ came in the flesh — a denial characteristic of Gnostic Docetism. The letter's instruction to refuse hospitality to such teachers represents one of the earliest practical responses to the Gnostic challenge within Christian communities. For Gnostic interpreters, the letter's polemic was precisely the kind of materialist, incarnational Christianity that the path of gnosis sought to transcend.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Deceiver and the Antichrist
2 John 7 identifies those who deny Christ's coming in the flesh as "the deceiver and the antichrist." This language targets Gnostic Christologies — whether Cerinthian (the divine Christ descended on the human Jesus at baptism and departed before the crucifixion) or Docetic (Christ only appeared to have a body). The letter treats this denial as the most dangerous possible error.

### Hospitality and Gnostic Missionaries
The instruction not to welcome or greet those who do not "bring the teaching of Christ" (vv. 10–11) suggests that Gnostic-influenced traveling teachers were seeking hospitality in Johannine communities. The early Christian practice of itinerant teachers staying in believers' homes made the community vulnerable to the spread of false teaching through its own hospitality networks.

## Theological Emphases

- **Anti-Docetic Polemic**: The denial of the incarnation as the defining Gnostic error
- **Practical Resistance**: Refusing hospitality as a strategy against Gnostic missionaries
- **The Teaching of Christ**: Fidelity to the apostolic confession against Gnostic innovation
- **Community Protection**: The elder's responsibility to guard the community from false teaching
