## The Elder and Doctrinal Authority

2 John is written by "the elder" to "the elect lady and her children" — a house church under his pastoral care. Catholic interpreters read the elder's authority as a model of episcopal oversight: the bishop has the responsibility not only to teach but to guard the community from false doctrine. The elder's instruction to refuse hospitality to false teachers demonstrates the practical exercise of doctrinal authority.

## Walking in the Commandment

Verses 4–6 emphasize walking in truth and love according to the Father's commandment. Catholic moral theology reads this as an affirmation that the moral life is constituted by obedience to God's commands, animated by love. The commandments are not external impositions but expressions of God's loving will for human flourishing. Love and obedience are inseparable.

## The Incarnation and the Deceivers

Verse 7 identifies the fundamental error: denying that "Jesus Christ has come in the flesh." Catholic Christology, defined at the Councils of Nicaea and Chalcedon, is the full development of this confession. The instruction to refuse hospitality to those who deny the incarnation (vv. 10–11) supports the Catholic practice of excommunication and the restriction of communion to those who share the Church's faith.
