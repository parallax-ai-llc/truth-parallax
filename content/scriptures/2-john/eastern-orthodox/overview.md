## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 John reads the letter as a concise affirmation of the incarnation as the criterion of true faith and genuine love. The Orthodox tradition has always insisted that love without truth is meaningless, and 2 John provides scriptural support for the conciliar tradition of defining the boundaries of authentic Christian confession. The letter's brevity belies its doctrinal weight.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Incarnation as Criterion
2 John 7 — "those who do not confess the coming of Jesus Christ in the flesh" — is read as a definitive test of orthodoxy. The ecumenical councils, from Nicaea to Chalcedon, developed the full implications of this confession. The denial of the incarnation, in any form, is the fundamental heresy that the Church has always opposed.

### Truth and the Patristic Tradition
The elder's concern for "walking in truth" (v. 4) is understood within the Orthodox framework of Holy Tradition. Truth is not a private possession but the shared inheritance of the Church, preserved through the Scriptures, the Fathers, the councils, and the liturgy. The individual believer walks in truth by remaining within this living tradition.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Incarnation**: The confession of Christ's flesh as the boundary of authentic faith
- **Truth as Tradition**: Walking in truth as participation in the Church's living tradition
- **Love and Discernment**: Genuine love as inseparable from doctrinal clarity
- **Conciliar Authority**: The letter as a precedent for the Church's conciliar definitions
