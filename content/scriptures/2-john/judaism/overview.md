## Overview

Jewish scholars engage with 2 John as evidence of the tension between hospitality and doctrinal boundary-maintenance in early Christianity — a tension familiar to Jewish communities throughout history. The letter's instruction to refuse hospitality to certain traveling teachers raises questions about the limits of welcome and the relationship between communal identity and openness to outsiders.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Hospitality and Its Limits
The Jewish tradition places high value on hospitality (*hachnasat orchim*), considering it among the most important virtues. Abraham's welcome of the three strangers (Genesis 18) is the paradigmatic example. 2 John's instruction to refuse hospitality to certain teachers represents a significant boundary: some people are to be excluded from welcome based on their teaching. Jewish readers recognize this tension between openness and self-preservation.

### Sectarian Dynamics
The letter's sharp boundary between insiders who "walk in truth" and outsiders who are "deceivers" reflects the kind of sectarian dynamics found in the Dead Sea Scrolls and other Second Temple texts. Jewish scholars note that communities under threat often tighten their boundaries, and 2 John represents an early Christian example of this pattern.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hospitality Ethics**: The limits of welcome in the face of threatening teaching
- **Community Boundaries**: The definition of insider and outsider
- **Truth and Identity**: Doctrinal commitment as constitutive of communal identity
- **Sectarian Parallels**: Comparison with Jewish boundary-maintenance strategies
