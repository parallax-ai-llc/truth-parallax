## Hospitality and Its Boundaries

2 John's most distinctive feature for Jewish readers is its instruction to refuse hospitality to certain visitors (vv. 10–11). Jewish tradition holds hospitality in the highest regard — Abraham's welcome of strangers (Genesis 18) is the model for all subsequent Jewish practice. Yet Jewish communities have also had to navigate the tension between openness and self-preservation. 2 John illustrates how early Christians negotiated this same tension.

## Truth and Community

The letter's emphasis on "walking in truth" (v. 4) and the elder's joy at finding community members faithful resonates with the Jewish concept of a community defined by shared commitment to revealed truth. The Torah-observant community maintains its identity through adherence to shared practices and beliefs, and the exclusion of those who threaten these foundations is a recognized, if painful, necessity.

## The Love Command

2 John 5–6, with its reaffirmation of the love command, connects to Leviticus 19:18. Jewish readers note that the command to love is here directed inward — toward the community of faith — rather than outward toward all humanity. This particularist application of the love command parallels certain sectarian Jewish texts that restricted the obligation of love to members of the covenant community.
