## Overview

2 John is the shortest book in the New Testament, a brief letter from "the elder" to "the elect lady and her children" — likely a house church and its members. Written by the same author or school responsible for the Gospel of John and 1 John, the letter addresses two concerns: the command to love one another and the warning against welcoming false teachers who deny the incarnation. Its brevity belies its significance as a window into early Christian hospitality practices and doctrinal boundaries.

## Structure

The letter's thirteen verses divide into three sections:

1. **Greeting** (1–3): The elder's greeting in truth and love
2. **Walking in Truth and Love** (4–6): Rejoicing in faithfulness and reaffirming the love command
3. **Warning Against False Teachers** (7–11): Refusing hospitality to those who deny Christ's coming in the flesh
4. **Closing** (12–13): Anticipation of a personal visit

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Protestantism**: A text on doctrinal boundaries and the limits of Christian fellowship
- **Catholicism**: The relationship between truth and love, and the role of authoritative teaching
- **Eastern Orthodoxy**: The incarnation as the criterion of authentic faith
- **Judaism**: The letter's sectarian dynamics and hospitality ethics
- **Islam**: The insistence on truth alongside love resonates with Islamic moral teaching
- **Gnosticism**: The letter's explicit opposition to those who deny Christ's coming in the flesh
