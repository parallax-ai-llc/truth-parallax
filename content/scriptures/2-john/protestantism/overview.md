## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 2 John focuses on the letter's integration of truth and love and its practical guidance on maintaining doctrinal boundaries. The letter teaches that love is not an unlimited openness that tolerates all teaching; genuine love operates within the boundaries of truth. This has shaped Protestant approaches to church discipline, ecumenical dialogue, and the limits of Christian fellowship.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Truth and Love Together
2 John insists that truth and love are inseparable: the elder loves the recipients "in truth" (v. 1), and walking in love means walking "according to his commandments" (v. 6). Protestant interpreters have drawn on this to argue against a sentimentalized concept of love that avoids doctrinal clarity. Truth without love is harsh; love without truth is naively permissive.

### Doctrinal Boundaries
The letter's instruction not to welcome those who do not "bring the teaching of Christ" (v. 10) has been foundational for Protestant church discipline. The denial of the incarnation is treated as a boundary-marking issue that excludes one from Christian fellowship. This principle has been applied more broadly to fundamental doctrinal departures.

## Theological Emphases

- **Truth and Love**: The inseparability of doctrinal fidelity and genuine love
- **The Incarnation Test**: Confession of Christ's coming in the flesh as the boundary of orthodoxy
- **Hospitality Limits**: The ethical responsibility not to support false teaching through hospitality
- **Walking in Obedience**: Love defined by conformity to God's commands
