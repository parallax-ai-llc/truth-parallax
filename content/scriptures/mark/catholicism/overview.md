## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Mark draws on patristic tradition identifying the evangelist as the interpreter of Peter, a connection affirmed by Papias (c. 125 CE) and repeated by Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and Jerome. The Pontifical Biblical Commission and the documents of Vatican II (*Dei Verbum*, 1965) affirm the historical reliability of the Gospels while endorsing critical methods of study, allowing Catholic scholars to engage fully with modern Markan scholarship.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic and Medieval
The Church Fathers generally subordinated Mark to Matthew and John, but figures like St. Jerome and the Venerable Bede produced commentaries drawing moral and allegorical meaning from the text. Mark's account of the institution of the Eucharist (14:22-25) received particular attention in sacramental theology.

### Modern Catholic Scholarship
Following *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943) and *Dei Verbum* (1965), Catholic scholars like Rudolf Schnackenburg and Morna Hooker have engaged with historical-critical approaches while maintaining the text's ecclesial significance. The *New Jerome Biblical Commentary* represents a landmark of Catholic Markan scholarship.

## Theological Emphases

- **Petrine Authority**: Mark as Peter's interpreter reinforces the Petrine foundation of ecclesial authority
- **Sacramental Reading**: The feeding miracles, the Last Supper, and baptismal imagery are read through the lens of Catholic sacramental theology
- **The Suffering Church**: Mark's community under persecution prefigures the Church's ongoing mission through suffering
- **Mary in Mark**: Though Mary's role is limited (3:31-35; 6:3), Catholic interpretation situates these passages within the broader Marian theology of the tradition
