Mark 8 presents Peter's confession of Jesus as the Messiah, an episode that Jewish scholars examine in light of the diversity of messianic expectations in Second Temple Judaism. The Dead Sea Scrolls attest to expectations of both a royal Messiah (of David) and a priestly Messiah (of Aaron). The Psalms of Solomon (first century BCE) envision a conquering Davidic king. Jesus' redefinition of messiahship through suffering (8:31) would have been largely unprecedented — the concept of a suffering Messiah is debated in Jewish sources, with Isaiah 53 interpreted variously.

The 'Son of Man' title (8:31, 38) is analyzed within its Jewish context. In Daniel 7:13-14 it refers to a heavenly figure receiving dominion from God. In Ezekiel it is simply a term for a human being. The Similitudes of Enoch develop the Danielic figure into a pre-existent judge. Jesus' use of the term participates in this rich tradition while giving it a distinctive shape. Jewish scholars note that the combination of exaltation and suffering in Mark's use of the title is without clear parallel in pre-Christian Jewish literature.
