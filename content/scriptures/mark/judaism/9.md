Mark 9 contains the Transfiguration, the appearance of Moses and Elijah, and teaching on humility and radical commitment. Jewish scholars note that the Transfiguration narrative draws on biblical and Second Temple traditions of divine glory and heavenly transformation. Moses' shining face (Exodus 34:29-35) and Elijah's ascension (2 Kings 2:11) provide the scriptural background. The apocalyptic motif of a human figure being transfigured into heavenly glory appears in texts like 2 Enoch and the Dead Sea Scrolls.

The discussion about Elijah's return (9:11-13) reflects the widespread Jewish expectation, based on Malachi 4:5, that Elijah would return before the Day of the Lord. This expectation is attested in the Mishnah (m. Eduyot 8:7) and remains part of Jewish liturgical practice — the cup of Elijah at the Passover Seder and the invocation of Elijah at the close of Shabbat. Jesus' identification of John the Baptist with Elijah represents one interpretation of this tradition.
