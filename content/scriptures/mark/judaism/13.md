Mark 13's apocalyptic discourse is analyzed by Jewish scholars within the context of Second Temple Jewish apocalypticism. The prediction of the temple's destruction (13:1-2) was not unique to Jesus — Josephus records other prophetic figures who warned of Jerusalem's fall. The language of cosmic upheaval (13:24-25) draws on prophetic texts (Isaiah 13:10, 34:4, Joel 2:10) and reflects the apocalyptic genre well attested in Jewish literature from Daniel through 4 Ezra and 2 Baruch.

The 'abomination of desolation' (13:14) echoes Daniel 9:27, 11:31, and 12:11, where it originally referred to Antiochus IV's desecration of the temple in 167 BCE. Jewish scholars note that this phrase was reapplied to new historical situations — a hermeneutical practice common in Jewish interpretation. The discourse's emphasis on watchfulness and endurance resonates with Jewish attitudes during periods of persecution and exile, when faithfulness in suffering was understood as the appropriate response to God's mysterious providential plan.
