Mark 10's teachings on divorce, children, and wealth engage directly with halakhic discussions familiar from rabbinic literature. The divorce debate (10:2-12) reflects the well-known dispute between the schools of Shammai (divorce only for sexual immorality) and Hillel (divorce for any cause). Jesus' position is more restrictive than either school, appealing to the creation narrative (Genesis 1:27, 2:24) rather than the Deuteronomic legislation (Deuteronomy 24:1-4).

The encounter with the rich man (10:17-31) and his question about inheriting eternal life reflect genuine Jewish theological concerns. The concept of 'eternal life' (*olam ha-ba*, the world to come) was a matter of debate between Pharisees (who affirmed resurrection) and Sadducees (who denied it). Jewish scholars note that Jesus' listing of commandments (10:19) draws from the second table of the Decalogue — the commandments governing human relationships — and adds 'do not defraud,' possibly reflecting concern for economic justice.
