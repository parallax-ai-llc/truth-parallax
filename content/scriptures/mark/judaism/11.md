Mark 11's account of Jesus' entry into Jerusalem and the temple action are examined by Jewish scholars for their historical and political implications. The entry riding on a colt (11:1-10) alludes to Zechariah 9:9, a passage with messianic overtones. The acclamation 'Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord' (Psalm 118:26) was used in Jewish liturgy, particularly during the festival of Sukkot. Jewish historians note the political volatility of Jerusalem during Passover, when Roman garrisons were reinforced to manage the pilgrim crowds.

The temple action (11:15-19) is one of the most debated episodes in historical Jesus research. Jewish scholars offer various interpretations: a prophetic protest against commercial exploitation, a symbolic enactment of the temple's coming destruction, or a challenge to the priestly aristocracy's control. E.P. Sanders argued it was a symbolic destruction rather than a cleansing, signaling the temple's replacement in the coming kingdom. The fig tree episode (11:12-14, 20-25) draws on the prophetic tradition of using agricultural imagery for judgment on Israel (Jeremiah 8:13, Hosea 9:10).
