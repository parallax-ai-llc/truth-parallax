## Overview

Jewish engagement with the Gospel of Mark operates on multiple levels. Historically, rabbinic Judaism did not produce commentaries on the New Testament, and Mark was read — when read at all — as a text belonging to an opposing tradition. However, modern Jewish scholarship has approached Mark with great interest as a window into first-century Jewish life, messianic expectation, and the intra-Jewish disputes that shaped the eventual parting of the ways between Judaism and Christianity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historical-Critical
Jewish historians like Geza Vermes, David Flusser, and Amy-Jill Levine have used Mark to reconstruct the Jewish context of Jesus' ministry. They emphasize that Jesus' teaching, healing, and conflicts with Pharisees and scribes are best understood within the spectrum of Second Temple Judaism, not as a rejection of it.

### Anti-Judaism Critique
Jewish scholars have drawn attention to passages in Mark that have fueled Christian anti-Judaism, particularly the trial before the Sanhedrin (14:53-65) and the crowd's call for crucifixion (15:6-15). Scholars argue these reflect later polemics more than historical events.

## Theological Emphases

- **Jesus as Jew**: Mark's Jesus observes Sabbath, attends synagogue, debates Torah, and quotes the Shema (12:29) — he operates entirely within a Jewish framework
- **Halakhic Disputes**: The controversies over Sabbath, purity, and fasting (chapters 2-3, 7) reflect intra-Jewish debates attested in rabbinic literature
- **Messianic Expectation**: Mark's messianic secrecy resonates with the diversity of messianic ideas in Second Temple texts (Dead Sea Scrolls, Psalms of Solomon)
- **The Passion Narrative**: Jewish scholars scrutinize the historicity of the Sanhedrin trial, noting contradictions with known Jewish legal procedures as recorded in the Mishnah
