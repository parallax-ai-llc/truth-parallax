Mark 4's parables are recognized by Jewish scholars as fitting squarely within the tradition of the *mashal* — the Hebrew parable or wisdom saying. The agricultural imagery of sowing, growing, and harvesting is pervasive in rabbinic literature, and Jesus' use of parables to teach about the kingdom of God reflects a method well attested in later rabbinic sources, though the literary forms differ.

The Parable of the Sower (4:1-20) addresses the varied reception of teaching, a concern shared by rabbinic educators. The statement that parables are given to conceal truth from outsiders (4:11-12) echoes Isaiah 6:9-10 and raises the question of esoteric teaching in Judaism. The Dead Sea Scrolls reveal that some Jewish groups distinguished between public and secret knowledge — the *razim* (mysteries) of God were not for everyone. Jesus' use of parables participates in this broader Jewish discourse about the hiddenness and revelation of divine truth.
