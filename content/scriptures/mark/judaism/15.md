Mark 15's crucifixion narrative is the most sensitive passage in the Gospel for Jewish-Christian relations. Jewish scholars emphasize that crucifixion was a Roman punishment, not a Jewish one — Jewish law prescribed stoning for blasphemy (Leviticus 24:16). The role of Pilate as the authority who actually condemned and executed Jesus is historically certain, and Jewish scholars argue that Mark's narrative tendency to shift blame from Rome to the Jewish crowd reflects the political needs of early Christians living under Roman rule.

The cry of dereliction (15:34) — 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?' — quotes Psalm 22:1 in Aramaic. Jewish scholars note that this psalm, while beginning in despair, ends in vindication and praise, and that Jesus' quotation should be read within the full context of the psalm. The burial by Joseph of Arimathea (15:42-47) follows Jewish burial customs, including the urgency of burial before nightfall, especially before Sabbath. The presence of women witnesses (15:40-41, 47) is historically significant, as women's testimony carried less legal weight in Jewish law.
