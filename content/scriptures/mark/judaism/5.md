Mark 5 raises questions about purity, death, and the boundaries of Jewish identity that are central to Jewish scholarly analysis. The Gerasene demoniac episode (5:1-20) takes place in Gentile territory — the presence of pigs confirms this. Jewish readers note the cultural and halakhic implications: Jesus enters a region of impurity and engages with a man who dwells among tombs, both sources of ritual contamination.

The hemorrhaging woman (5:25-34) suffered from a condition that rendered her ritually impure under Levitical law (Leviticus 15:25-30). Her touch of Jesus' garment would normally transmit impurity, yet in Mark's narrative the flow of power moves in the opposite direction — from Jesus to her. Jewish scholars note that this reversal challenges certain purity assumptions while remaining within the broad framework of Jewish healing traditions. The raising of Jairus' daughter (5:35-43) involves contact with a corpse, the most severe source of impurity in Jewish law, yet Jesus crosses this boundary to restore life.
