Mark 2 presents a series of disputes that Jewish scholars read as intra-Jewish halakhic debates, not as a rejection of Judaism itself. The Sabbath controversies (2:23-28) reflect genuine disagreements about Sabbath observance that existed among Jewish groups. The Pharisees' position on plucking grain was not universal — different schools (Shammai and Hillel) had varying rulings on permissible Sabbath activity.

The claim to forgive sins (2:5-7) and the title 'Son of Man' (2:10, 28) are analyzed by Jewish scholars within the context of Second Temple Judaism. Daniel 7:13-14 and the Similitudes of Enoch (1 Enoch 37-71) attest to diverse understandings of a heavenly figure who exercises divine prerogatives. The controversies in Mark 2, rather than proving a clean break between Jesus and Judaism, reveal the internal diversity and vitality of Jewish thought in this period.
