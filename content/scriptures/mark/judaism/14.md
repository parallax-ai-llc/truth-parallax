Mark 14's passion narrative raises some of the most difficult questions in Jewish-Christian relations. Jewish scholars have subjected the trial before the Sanhedrin (14:53-65) to intense scrutiny, noting multiple inconsistencies with known Jewish legal procedures as recorded in the Mishnah: trials were not held at night, not during festivals, and not in the high priest's house. These discrepancies suggest either that the Sanhedrin violated its own rules or — more likely, in the view of many Jewish scholars — that Mark's account reflects later Christian polemical interests rather than strict historical reporting.

The Passover setting of the Last Supper (14:12-25) is significant for Jewish-Christian understanding. Whether the supper was a Seder meal (as the Synoptics suggest) or occurred before Passover (as John implies) remains debated. Jewish scholars note that the Passover context situates Jesus' final meal within the central Jewish narrative of liberation from bondage. The arrest in Gethsemane (14:43-52) involves the temple police, not Roman soldiers (in Mark's account), suggesting an intra-Jewish conflict rather than a purely Roman affair.
