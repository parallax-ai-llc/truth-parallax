Jewish scholarly engagement with Mark 16 focuses on the historical and literary questions surrounding the resurrection narrative. The empty tomb tradition is examined within the context of first-century Jewish beliefs about resurrection. The Pharisaic tradition affirmed bodily resurrection at the end of days, but the claim that a single individual had been raised in the middle of history was unprecedented. Jewish scholars note that the belief in Jesus' resurrection cannot be explained by simple reference to prior Jewish expectations — something happened that the earliest followers interpreted through but also transformed their existing categories.

The women's role as first witnesses is historically noteworthy. Jewish scholars observe that the inclusion of women as primary witnesses — despite the cultural devaluation of women's testimony — suggests the tradition's antiquity and authenticity; a fabricated account would more likely have featured male witnesses. The abrupt ending at 16:8, with the women fleeing in fear and telling no one, is read by some Jewish scholars as Mark's honest acknowledgment of the trauma and confusion surrounding the events, rather than a triumphal narrative imposed after the fact.
