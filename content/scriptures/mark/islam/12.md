Mark 12's controversies about taxation, resurrection, and the love commandment engage with themes that have parallels in Islamic theology. The question about paying taxes to Caesar (12:13-17) is read by Muslim scholars in light of Islamic political theology, which ideally envisions a unified governance under divine law (*shari'ah*) but practically accommodates living under non-Muslim rule when necessary.

The Sadducees' question about resurrection (12:18-27) is of particular interest because Islam firmly affirms bodily resurrection (*ba'th*) and the Day of Judgment (*yawm al-qiyamah*). The Quran argues extensively against those who deny resurrection (Quran 17:49-51, 36:78-83, 75:3-4). Jesus' affirmation of resurrection in this passage aligns with Islamic doctrine. The Great Commandment — loving God with all one's heart, soul, mind, and strength (12:29-30) — echoes the Islamic concept of complete submission (*islam*) to God. The citation of the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4) — 'The Lord our God, the Lord is one' — is taken by Muslim scholars as Jesus affirming strict monotheism consistent with tawhid.
