Islamic engagement with Mark 1 begins with the figure of John the Baptist, known in the Quran as Yahya ibn Zakariyya. The Quran confirms Yahya as a righteous prophet (Quran 3:39, 19:12-15), and his role as a forerunner is broadly consistent with Mark's portrayal. However, the Islamic understanding differs on the nature of baptism — Islam has no sacrament of baptism, and ritual purification (*wudu* and *ghusl*) serves a different theological function.

Jesus' miracles of healing and exorcism in Mark 1 are understood in Islamic theology as *mu'jizat* — miraculous signs granted by God to authenticate a prophet's mission. The Quran affirms that Isa healed the blind, cured lepers, and raised the dead 'by God's permission' (Quran 3:49, 5:110). The crucial qualifier 'by God's permission' (*bi-idhni Allah*) distinguishes the Islamic reading from the Christian claim that Jesus performed miracles by his own divine power. The title 'Son of God' in Mark 1:1 is rejected as incompatible with tawhid.
