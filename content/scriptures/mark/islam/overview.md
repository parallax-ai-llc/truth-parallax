## Overview

Islam reveres Jesus (Isa ibn Maryam) as one of the greatest prophets and affirms that he received a divine revelation called the *Injil* (Gospel). However, Islamic theology holds that the extant Gospels, including Mark, do not faithfully preserve the original *Injil* but have undergone *tahrif* (alteration). Mark is therefore read by Muslim scholars with a critical filter: passages consistent with Quranic teaching are accepted as potentially preserving authentic prophetic tradition, while those contradicting Islamic theology are attributed to human corruption of the text.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Quranic Corrective
Muslim scholars evaluate Mark's content against the Quran. Jesus' miracles (healing, exorcism) are affirmed as consistent with Quranic accounts, while claims of divinity or the Son of God title are rejected as later theological additions.

### Comparative Prophetic Studies
Modern Muslim scholars like Ahmed Deedat and Zakir Naik have engaged directly with Mark's text in apologetic contexts, highlighting passages that seem to support Islamic claims about Jesus — his prayer and submission to God, his denial of goodness apart from God (10:18), and his cry of abandonment on the cross (15:34).

## Theological Emphases

- **Rejection of Crucifixion**: The Quran (4:157-158) states that Jesus was not crucified but was raised to God. Mark's passion narrative is therefore considered a distortion of what actually occurred
- **Jesus' Humanity**: Mark's depiction of Jesus praying, experiencing distress, and expressing limited knowledge (13:32) resonates with the Islamic view of Jesus as a human prophet, not divine
- **Tawhid**: Any suggestion that Jesus is the "Son of God" (1:1, 15:39) contradicts the absolute oneness of God; Muslim interpreters attribute these titles to Pauline or Hellenistic influence
- **Miracles as Signs**: Jesus' miracles in Mark are understood as *mu'jizat* (prophetic signs granted by God), not evidence of divinity
