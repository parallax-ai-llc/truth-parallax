Mark 15's crucifixion narrative is the passage where Islamic and Christian readings diverge most sharply. The Quran states categorically: 'They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them' (Quran 4:157). This verse has generated extensive tafsir. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir record multiple theories about what occurred: a common view is that God cast Jesus' likeness upon another person (sometimes identified as Judas, Simon of Cyrene, or an unnamed volunteer) who was crucified in his place, while Jesus was raised alive to heaven.

The cry of dereliction — 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?' (15:34) — is sometimes cited by Muslim apologists as the cry of the substitute, not of Jesus himself, since a true prophet would not experience divine abandonment. The centurion's confession 'Truly this man was the Son of God' (15:39) is rejected as a theological interpolation. Muslim scholars maintain that the earliest followers of Jesus, the true believers, knew that he had not been crucified, but later generations corrupted the account under Pauline influence.
