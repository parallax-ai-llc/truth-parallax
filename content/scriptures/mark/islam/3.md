Mark 3's appointment of the Twelve is understood in Islamic perspective as the selection of disciples (*hawariyyun*), a concept affirmed in the Quran (3:52, 5:111-112, 61:14). The Quran presents the disciples as helpers of Isa who submitted to God — 'We are the helpers of Allah' — emphasizing their role as supporters of monotheistic faith rather than founders of a new religion.

The Beelzebul controversy (3:22-30) reflects a pattern familiar in Islamic prophetology: prophets are regularly accused of sorcery, madness, or demonic influence by those who reject their message. The Quran records similar accusations against Muhammad (Quran 15:6, 68:2) and other prophets. The rejection by Jesus' own family (3:21, 31-35) fits the prophetic paradigm of a messenger facing opposition even from his closest kin — a theme resonant with Muhammad's early rejection by the Quraysh.
