Mark 14's passion narrative presents the most fundamental theological divergence between Christianity and Islam. The Last Supper, the agony in Gethsemane, the arrest, and the trial before the Sanhedrin are all elements that Muslim scholars approach with the conviction that the ultimate outcome — crucifixion and death — did not actually occur as described.

The institution of the Eucharist (14:22-25) is rejected as a sacramental innovation without basis in the original teaching of Jesus. The agony in Gethsemane (14:32-42), however, presents an interesting case: Jesus' prayer to be delivered from the cup of suffering is read by some Muslim apologists as evidence that God answered this prayer by rescuing Jesus from crucifixion. The arrest and trial are considered historically plausible — the enemies of Jesus did seek to kill him — but God intervened to save his prophet. Various Islamic theories propose that a substitute was crucified in Jesus' place, or that Jesus was taken up to heaven before the crucifixion could occur.
