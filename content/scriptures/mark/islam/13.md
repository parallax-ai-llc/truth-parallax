Mark 13's eschatological discourse is of great interest to Islamic scholars because Islam has a developed eschatological tradition that includes the return of Jesus (*nuzul Isa*). The Quran states that Jesus is 'a sign of the Hour' (Quran 43:61), and hadith literature elaborates extensively on his return — he will descend near a white minaret in Damascus, break the cross, kill the swine, and establish justice on earth.

The signs described in Mark 13 — wars, earthquakes, persecution, cosmic upheaval — find parallels in Islamic eschatological literature, which describes similar tribulations (*fitan*) preceding the Day of Judgment. The 'abomination of desolation' (13:14) is sometimes compared to the Islamic figure of the Dajjal (Antichrist), a false messiah who will deceive many before being defeated by Jesus upon his return. The exhortation to watchfulness (13:33-37) resonates with the Islamic emphasis on being prepared for death and judgment at all times.
