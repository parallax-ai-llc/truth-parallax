The Transfiguration in Mark 9:2-8 presents Jesus appearing in glory alongside Moses and Elijah. Islamic interpretation acknowledges the prophetic continuity implied — Moses (*Musa*) and Elijah (*Ilyas*) are both recognized prophets in the Quran (Quran 6:85, 37:123-132). The gathering of three great prophets is consistent with the Islamic understanding of prophethood as a continuous chain, each prophet confirming those who came before.

However, the divine voice declaring 'This is my beloved Son' (9:7) is rejected as incompatible with tawhid. Muslim scholars interpret this either as a later theological addition or, if original, as an honorific title that does not imply biological or ontological sonship — similar to how the Hebrew Bible calls Israel 'my firstborn son' (Exodus 4:22) without implying literal divine paternity. The healing of the epileptic boy (9:14-29) is understood as a prophetic miracle emphasizing the necessity of trust in God and prayer.
