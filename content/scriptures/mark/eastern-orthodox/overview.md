## Overview

In the Eastern Orthodox tradition, Mark is read primarily through the lens of the Church Fathers and the liturgical cycle. St. Mark is venerated as the founder of the Church of Alexandria, and his Gospel occupies a distinctive place in the Egyptian and broader Orthodox liturgical tradition. Orthodox interpretation resists reducing the Gospel to a historical document, insisting that it remains a living text proclaimed in the Divine Liturgy and read within the consensus of the Fathers (*consensus patrum*).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Commentary
Although fewer patristic commentaries were devoted exclusively to Mark (compared to Matthew or John), the Fathers frequently drew on Markan passages in homilies and catechetical works. Theophylact of Ohrid (11th century) produced the most widely used Orthodox commentary on Mark, synthesizing Chrysostom and other Fathers.

### Liturgical Context
Mark's Gospel is read in the liturgical cycle, particularly during weekday readings and the Lenten season. The passion narrative is central to Holy Week services. Orthodox interpretation insists that the text's meaning is fully disclosed only within the worshipping community.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Divine Economy**: Mark's narrative unfolds God's plan of salvation (*oikonomia*) through Christ's life, suffering, death, and resurrection
- **Theosis**: The healing miracles and exorcisms reveal Christ's power to restore humanity to communion with God
- **The Transfiguration**: Mark 9:2-8 is central to Orthodox theology of divine light and the uncreated energies
- **Kenosis and Glory**: Christ's self-emptying on the cross is understood as paradoxically revealing divine glory
- **The Cosmic Dimension**: The rending of the heavens at baptism (1:10) and the tearing of the temple veil (15:38) signal the restoration of communion between heaven and earth
