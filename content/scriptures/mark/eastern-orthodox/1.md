Orthodox interpretation of Mark 1 begins with the Theophany — the baptism of Jesus in the Jordan. This event is one of the great feasts of the Orthodox liturgical calendar (January 6), and its theological significance is immense. The descent of the Spirit as a dove and the Father's voice constitute the fullest revelation of the Holy Trinity in the Gospels. St. John Chrysostom teaches that Christ did not need baptism but sanctified the waters for all who would be baptized after him. The Great Blessing of Waters performed on Theophany reenacts this moment.

The calling of the first disciples (1:16-20) is read within the framework of the divine economy — God's plan of salvation unfolding through particular people and events. The exorcisms and healings that follow reveal Christ's authority over the demonic realm. Orthodox theology takes the reality of demonic powers seriously, and the Church continues the ministry of exorcism through prayers and the sacrament of baptism, which includes renunciation of Satan.
