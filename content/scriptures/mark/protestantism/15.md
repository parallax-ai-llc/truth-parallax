Mark 15 narrates the trial before Pilate, the crucifixion, and the burial. Protestant interpreters focus on the theological significance of the passion: Jesus dies as the substitute for sinners, bearing the wrath of God. The exchange of Barabbas for Jesus (15:6-15) is read as an enacted parable of substitutionary atonement — the guilty goes free, the innocent dies. The darkness from the sixth to the ninth hour (15:33) signals cosmic significance.

The cry of dereliction — 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?' (15:34) — is one of the most theologically charged verses in the New Testament. Luther interpreted it as the moment when Christ bore the full weight of divine judgment, experiencing the God-forsakenness that sinners deserve. Calvin similarly saw it as Christ enduring the penalty of sin. The centurion's confession, 'Truly this man was the Son of God' (15:39), completes the Gospel's christological arc — the title announced in 1:1 is finally spoken by a human, and it is a Gentile at the cross who speaks it.
