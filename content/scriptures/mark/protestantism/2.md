Mark 2 introduces a series of conflict stories that Protestant interpreters see as programmatic for understanding Jesus' relationship to Jewish authority. The healing of the paralytic (2:1-12) is significant because Jesus first declares the man's sins forgiven — a claim the scribes recognize as divine prerogative. Protestant theology reads this as an early assertion of Jesus' divine authority, grounding the Reformation conviction that forgiveness comes directly from God through Christ.

The call of Levi and the eating with tax collectors and sinners (2:13-17) illustrates the Protestant emphasis on grace extended to the undeserving. The Sabbath controversies (2:23-28) culminate in Jesus' declaration that 'the Son of Man is lord even of the Sabbath' (2:28). Luther saw in these conflicts the distinction between the gospel of grace and the legalism of human religious systems — a distinction central to Reformation theology.
