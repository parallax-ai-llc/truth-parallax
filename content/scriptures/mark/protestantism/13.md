Mark 13, the Olivet Discourse, is Mark's longest sustained teaching passage. Protestant interpreters are deeply divided over its interpretation. Preterists argue that the entire discourse was fulfilled in the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. Futurists read it as a prophecy of end-time events still to come. Many Protestant scholars adopt a blended approach: the destruction of Jerusalem is both a historical event and a type of the final judgment.

The 'abomination of desolation' (13:14) is particularly contested. Dispensationalist Protestants identify this with a future Antichrist figure, while others see it fulfilled in the Roman desecration of the temple. Jesus' statement that 'concerning that day or that hour, no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father' (13:32) has been important for Protestant discussions of Christ's kenosis — how can the divine Son not know? The chapter's concluding exhortation to watchfulness (13:33-37) shapes Protestant eschatological ethics: live faithfully in the present, alert to the Lord's return.
