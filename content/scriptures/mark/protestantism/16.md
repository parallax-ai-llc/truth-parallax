Mark 16 is textually the most contested chapter in the New Testament. The earliest and most reliable manuscripts end at 16:8, with the women fleeing the empty tomb in fear and silence. The 'Longer Ending' (16:9-20) is widely regarded by Protestant text critics as a later addition, though it is printed in most Bibles with a note. This textual issue has significant theological implications: if Mark ended at 16:8, the Gospel concludes with trembling, astonishment, and fear — not with triumphant resurrection appearances.

Protestant interpreters have offered various explanations: the original ending was lost, Mark was interrupted before finishing, or the abrupt ending is intentional — forcing the reader to complete the story through their own response to the Gospel. The Longer Ending's inclusion of snake-handling and poison-drinking (16:17-18) has influenced some Pentecostal and Holiness traditions, while mainstream Protestantism treats these verses with caution. Whether Mark 16:8 or 16:20 is the ending, the empty tomb proclamation — 'He has risen; he is not here' (16:6) — remains the Gospel's climactic announcement.
