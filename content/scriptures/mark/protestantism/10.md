Mark 10 addresses marriage, children, wealth, and servanthood. Jesus' teaching on divorce (10:2-12) has been foundational for Protestant marital ethics, though interpretations vary — some traditions allow divorce and remarriage on the basis of the Matthean exception clause (not present in Mark), while others follow Mark's stricter formulation. The blessing of children (10:13-16) supports the Protestant affirmation of infant baptism in Reformed traditions, though Baptists read it differently.

The encounter with the rich young man (10:17-31) is central to Protestant soteriology. Jesus' statement that 'it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God' (10:25) has been read literally by most Protestant interpreters, rejecting the popular but unfounded suggestion of a small gate called 'the Needle's Eye.' The healing of Bartimaeus (10:46-52) closes the section with a model of persistent, vocal faith — he cries out despite being silenced, and his faith makes him well.
