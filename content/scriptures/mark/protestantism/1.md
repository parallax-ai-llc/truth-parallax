Mark opens with a declaration rather than a genealogy: 'The beginning of the gospel of Jesus Christ, the Son of God' (1:1). Protestant interpreters note the programmatic force of this title, which the entire Gospel will unpack. John the Baptist appears as the forerunner prophesied in Isaiah, preparing the way in the wilderness. Jesus' baptism by John has generated significant Protestant discussion — why would the sinless one submit to a baptism of repentance? Calvin argued it was an act of solidarity with sinners, anticipating the cross.

The rapid sequence of events — baptism, temptation, calling of disciples, exorcisms, and healings — establishes the pattern that will characterize Mark's narrative. The word 'immediately' (*euthys*) appears repeatedly, conveying urgency. Jesus' command to the healed leper to 'say nothing to anyone' (1:44) introduces the messianic secret, a theme that Protestant scholar William Wrede identified as central to Mark's theological program. Protestant interpreters debate whether this secrecy reflects historical reality or Mark's editorial theology, but most agree it underscores that Jesus' identity cannot be understood apart from the cross.
