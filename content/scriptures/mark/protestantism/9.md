Mark 9 opens with the Transfiguration (9:2-8), where Jesus is revealed in glory alongside Moses and Elijah. Protestant interpreters read this as a temporary unveiling of the divine glory normally hidden beneath Jesus' humanity. The divine voice — 'This is my beloved Son; listen to him' — echoes the baptism (1:11) and reinforces the Protestant principle that Christ's word is the ultimate authority.

The healing of the epileptic boy (9:14-29) highlights the disciples' failure and the father's poignant cry: 'I believe; help my unbelief!' (9:24). This verse has become one of the most beloved in Protestant devotional life, expressing the honest struggle of faith. The chapter closes with teaching on humility, exclusivism, and the radical seriousness of sin — 'it is better for you to enter life maimed' (9:43-47). Protestant interpreters stress that this hyperbolic language underscores the seriousness of moral choice without endorsing literal self-mutilation.
