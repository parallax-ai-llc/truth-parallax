Mark 4 contains Jesus' parable discourse, centered on the Parable of the Sower (4:1-20). Protestant interpretation has emphasized the sower as a model for gospel proclamation — the preacher casts the seed broadly, and different soils represent different responses. The allegorical explanation (4:13-20) has been debated: some scholars consider it a later addition, while evangelical interpreters defend its authenticity as Jesus' own hermeneutical key.

The statement that the mystery of the kingdom is given to the disciples but outsiders receive everything in parables (4:11-12) raises difficult questions about election and predestination. Calvin saw here confirmation of the doctrine of unconditional election, while Arminian Protestants interpret the passage as describing a consequence of hardened hearts rather than a divine decree. The calming of the storm (4:35-41) reveals Jesus' authority over nature — Protestant interpreters see this as evidence of his divine identity, echoing Old Testament passages where God alone controls the sea.
