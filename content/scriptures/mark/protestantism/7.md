Mark 7 addresses the question of purity and the boundary between Jew and Gentile. The dispute over handwashing (7:1-13) is programmatic for Protestant hermeneutics: Jesus distinguishes between the commandment of God and the tradition of the elders, a distinction that resonated powerfully with the Reformers' critique of Catholic tradition. Luther and Calvin both cited this passage in arguing that human traditions cannot bind the conscience where Scripture is silent.

The declaration that all foods are clean (7:19) is read by Protestant interpreters as a decisive break with the ceremonial law — a hermeneutical move that grounds the Protestant distinction between the moral law (still binding) and the ceremonial law (fulfilled in Christ). The healing of the Syrophoenician woman's daughter (7:24-30) raises challenging questions about the scope of Jesus' mission. Her bold faith, crossing ethnic and gender boundaries, prefigures the Gentile mission that will define the early church.
