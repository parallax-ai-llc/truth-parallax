Mark 12 presents a series of controversies and teachings in the temple. The Parable of the Wicked Tenants (12:1-12) is read by Protestant interpreters as a salvation-historical allegory: God sends prophets to Israel, they are rejected, and finally the Son is killed. The stone rejected by the builders becoming the cornerstone (12:10-11) is a key Protestant proof text for Christ as the foundation of the church.

The question about paying taxes to Caesar (12:13-17) has shaped Protestant political theology. Luther's two-kingdoms doctrine finds support here — the Christian has obligations to both the spiritual and temporal realms. The Great Commandment dialogue (12:28-34) is notable in Mark for the scribe's agreement with Jesus and Jesus' affirmation that the scribe is 'not far from the kingdom' — a passage that complicates any simplistic reading of Jewish-Christian opposition. The widow's mite (12:41-44) has become a paradigm of generous, sacrificial giving in Protestant stewardship teaching.
