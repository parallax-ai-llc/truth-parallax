Mark 14 begins the passion narrative proper, which many scholars consider the earliest continuous narrative tradition about Jesus. The anointing at Bethany (14:3-9) is read by Protestant interpreters as extravagant devotion that anticipates Jesus' burial. The institution of the Lord's Supper (14:22-25) is central to Protestant sacramental theology, though traditions diverge sharply: Lutherans affirm the real presence ('This is my body'), Reformed traditions emphasize spiritual presence, and Baptists understand the elements as memorial symbols.

Gethsemane (14:32-42) reveals Jesus in profound anguish — 'greatly distressed and troubled' (14:33). Protestant interpreters emphasize the full humanity of Christ here, struggling with the cup of suffering yet submitting to the Father's will. Peter's denial (14:66-72) is read as both a warning and a source of hope: even the chief apostle failed utterly, yet the Gospel's promise extends to the fallen. The cock crows, Peter weeps, and grace remains available.
