Mark 11 narrates Jesus' entry into Jerusalem, the cursing of the fig tree, and the cleansing of the temple — all read by Protestant interpreters as prophetic symbolic actions announcing judgment on the temple establishment. The triumphal entry (11:1-11) fulfills Zechariah 9:9, though Mark's account is more restrained than Matthew's. Protestant commentators note the deliberate messianic symbolism while observing that Jesus comes in humility, not military triumph.

The fig tree episode (11:12-14, 20-25), sandwiching the temple cleansing, is a classic Markan intercalation. Protestant interpreters read the barren fig tree as a symbol of Israel's failure to produce the fruit of genuine worship. The temple cleansing (11:15-19) has been foundational for Protestant critiques of religious corruption — Luther explicitly connected this passage to his protest against indulgences. The question about authority (11:27-33) illustrates Jesus' rhetorical skill and the religious leaders' bad faith.
