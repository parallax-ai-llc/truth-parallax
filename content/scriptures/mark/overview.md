## Overview

The Gospel of Mark is widely regarded as the earliest of the four canonical Gospels, likely composed between 65 and 75 CE. Its author, traditionally identified as John Mark — a companion of the apostle Peter — crafted a fast-paced, urgent narrative centered on the suffering, death, and resurrection of Jesus of Nazareth. The frequent use of the Greek word *euthys* ("immediately") gives the text a breathless quality, driving the reader from one episode to the next.

## Structure

Mark's Gospel can be divided into two major halves, pivoting around Peter's confession at Caesarea Philippi (8:27-30):

1. **The Galilean Ministry** (Chapters 1-8): Jesus' baptism, calling of disciples, miracles, parables, and growing conflict with religious authorities
2. **The Journey to Jerusalem and the Passion** (Chapters 9-16): The transfiguration, teaching on discipleship, the triumphal entry, the passion narrative, crucifixion, and the empty tomb

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Mark holds a distinctive place across multiple religious and scholarly traditions:

- **Christianity**: The foundational narrative Gospel, source for Matthew and Luke in most scholarly reconstructions
- **Judaism**: A primary source for understanding first-century Jewish life, synagogue practice, and messianic expectation
- **Islam**: Jesus (Isa) is a revered prophet; Mark's portrayal of his miracles and teaching resonates with Quranic themes
- **Gnosticism**: The messianic secret and hidden knowledge motifs attracted Gnostic interpreters
- **Academic**: Central to the Synoptic Problem and historical Jesus research

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Messianic Secret | Jesus repeatedly commands silence about his identity and miracles |
| Suffering Servant | Jesus redefines messiahship through suffering, not political triumph |
| Discipleship | The Twelve repeatedly fail to understand, modeling the cost of following Jesus |
| Kingdom of God | Proclaimed as "at hand" from the opening chapter |
| Son of God | The framing title (1:1), confirmed at baptism, transfiguration, and the cross |
| Passion | The extended passion narrative (chapters 14-16) dominates the Gospel's climax |
