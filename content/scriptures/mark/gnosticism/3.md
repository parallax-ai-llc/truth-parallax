Mark 3's division between insiders and outsiders resonates powerfully with Gnostic anthropology. The appointment of the Twelve (3:13-19) establishes an inner circle who will receive private instruction — the esoteric teaching that the masses cannot comprehend. Gnostic interpreters distinguished between the public teaching of parables and the private revelation of mysteries, seeing in the Twelve the prototype of the Gnostic elect.

The Beelzebul controversy (3:22-30) is significant because the scribes' accusation that Jesus works by demonic power mirrors the Gnostic claim that the religious authorities serve the Demiurge without knowing it. The 'strong man' whose house is plundered (3:27) is the Demiurge himself, and the plundering represents the liberation of divine sparks trapped in the material world. Jesus' redefinition of family (3:31-35) — those who do the will of God — is read as defining the pneumatic community bound by spiritual kinship rather than fleshly ties.
