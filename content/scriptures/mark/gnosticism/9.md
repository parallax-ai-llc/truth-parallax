The Transfiguration (9:2-8) is one of the most important passages for Gnostic Christology. The luminous transformation of Jesus on the mountain reveals his true nature as a being of light from the Pleroma. The garments 'intensely white' (9:3) represent the light-body of the heavenly Christ, temporarily visible through the material shell. Moses and Elijah represent earlier bearers of divine knowledge who now confirm the supreme revelation brought by Christ.

Peter's desire to build three tents (9:5) represents the psychic impulse to domesticate the transcendent — to contain the Pleroma within material structures. The cloud and divine voice reveal the true Father, the God above the Demiurge, who speaks from beyond the visible cosmos. The command to 'listen to him' directs the disciples away from the Demiurge's law (Moses) and prophets (Elijah) toward the direct revelation of the heavenly Christ. The command to secrecy (9:9) — 'tell no one what they had seen' — confirms that this revelation is esoteric, reserved for the pneumatic few.
