Mark 8 presents the pivotal moment of recognition — Peter's confession (8:29) — followed immediately by the teaching on suffering (8:31-33). Gnostic interpreters read this passage through the lens of docetism: Jesus rebukes Peter not for rejecting suffering per se but for clinging to a materialist understanding of the Messiah. The statement 'Get behind me, Satan' (8:33) reveals Peter's entrapment in the Demiurge's way of thinking — 'you are setting your mind on human things, not the things of God.'

The call to 'deny yourself and take up your cross' (8:34) is reinterpreted as the call to deny the material self — the false identity imposed by the Demiurge — and embrace the path of gnosis, which leads through apparent death to true life. 'Whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel's will save it' (8:35) describes the pneumatic's release from material existence. The warning about being ashamed of Jesus 'in this adulterous and sinful generation' (8:38) refers to the present cosmic age under archonic rule.
