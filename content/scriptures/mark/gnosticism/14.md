Mark 14's passion narrative is the most theologically charged section for Gnostic interpretation. The anointing at Bethany (14:3-9) is read as a ritual preparation — not for burial but for the separation of the heavenly Christ from the material body of Jesus. The Last Supper (14:22-25) is reinterpreted: 'This is my body' refers not to the physical bread but to the spiritual body of gnosis that the pneumatics must consume and internalize.

Gethsemane (14:32-42) presents the moment of crisis: the heavenly Christ contemplates departure from the material plane. The 'cup' is the cup of material existence that the earthly Jesus must drink but that the heavenly Christ will escape. Some Gnostic traditions taught that the divine element withdrew from Jesus before the passion — the Basilidean view, recorded by Irenaeus, held that Simon of Cyrene (who carries the cross in Mark 15:21) was actually the one crucified, while Jesus stood by laughing in Simon's form. Peter's denial (14:66-72) illustrates the psychic's inability to maintain spiritual commitment under pressure.
