Mark 2's controversies are read by Gnostic interpreters as demonstrations of the heavenly Christ's superiority over the religious structures established by the Demiurge. The forgiveness of sins (2:5-7) represents an authority that transcends the Demiurge's legal system — the true God operates outside the framework of law and punishment. The scribes' objection that only God can forgive sins is ironically correct: the heavenly Christ is acting on behalf of the true God, not the god of this world.

The Sabbath controversies (2:23-28) are particularly appealing to Gnostic readers, who saw the Sabbath as an institution of the Demiurge, established to bind humanity in servitude. The declaration that 'the Son of Man is lord even of the Sabbath' (2:28) asserts the superiority of the pneumatic Christ over the Demiurge's creational ordinances. The metaphor of new wine in old wineskins (2:22) is read as the incompatibility of gnosis with the structures of conventional religion.
