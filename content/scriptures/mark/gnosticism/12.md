Mark 12's controversies reveal the ignorance of the Demiurge's servants. The Parable of the Wicked Tenants (12:1-12) is reread: the 'vineyard' is the Pleroma, the 'tenants' are the archons who have usurped control, the 'servants' are earlier messengers of gnosis, and the 'son' is the heavenly Christ. The archons kill the son, but the stone they rejected becomes the cornerstone of a new spiritual edifice beyond their control.

The question about resurrection (12:18-27) is answered by Jesus with the statement that the resurrected 'are like angels in heaven' (12:25). Gnostic interpreters took this as confirmation that the ultimate destiny of the pneumatic is not bodily resurrection but angelic, spiritual existence in the Pleroma. The Great Commandment dialogue (12:28-34) — loving God with all heart, soul, mind, and strength — is read as directing love not to the Demiurge but to the true God beyond the Demiurge, the unknown Father revealed by Christ.
