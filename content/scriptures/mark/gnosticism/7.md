Mark 7's teaching on purity is central to the Gnostic rejection of the material world's religious systems. Jesus' declaration that 'there is nothing outside a person that by going in can defile' (7:15) is read as a radical critique of the Demiurge's purity legislation. The entire system of clean and unclean, established through the Torah, is exposed as the Demiurge's mechanism of control — keeping humanity focused on external observance rather than inner spiritual knowledge.

The healing of the Syrophoenician woman's daughter (7:24-30) is significant because this Gentile woman demonstrates a spiritual insight that surpasses the religious insiders. In Gnostic anthropology, gnosis is not limited by ethnic or religious boundaries — the divine spark can be found in anyone, regardless of their position within the Demiurge's religious systems. The healing of the deaf and mute man (7:31-37) with the word 'Ephphatha' ('Be opened') is read as a metaphor for the opening of spiritual perception that gnosis effects — ears opened to hear the secret teaching, tongue loosed to speak the hidden truth.
