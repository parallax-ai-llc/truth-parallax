Gnostic readers found the opening of Mark deeply significant. The rending of the heavens at Jesus' baptism (1:10) is interpreted as the breaking open of the cosmic barrier between the Pleroma (the divine fullness) and the material world ruled by the Demiurge and his archons. The Spirit descending 'like a dove' represents the divine spark entering the material realm — the heavenly Christ uniting with the man Jesus for the first time. This adoptionist Christology, in which the divine being enters Jesus at baptism, was characteristic of several Gnostic schools.

The wilderness temptation (1:12-13) is read as the confrontation between the heavenly Christ and the archonic powers who rule the material world. The exorcisms and commands to silence that follow (1:23-25, 34, 44) are not merely narrative devices but revelations of the esoteric structure of reality: the demons recognize the true identity of the heavenly visitor, but this knowledge must remain hidden from the archons and from the uninitiated masses. The messianic secret is, for Gnostic interpreters, the secret of gnosis itself.
