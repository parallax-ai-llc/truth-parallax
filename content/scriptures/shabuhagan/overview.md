## Overview

The Shabuhagan (Shābuhragān) is a unique text in the Manichaean canon, being the only major work that Mani composed in Middle Persian rather than his usual Syriac-Aramaic. Written specifically for the Sasanian king Shapur I (r. 240-270 CE), the text presents a systematic summary of Manichaean cosmology and eschatology designed to win royal patronage for Mani's universal religion.

The Shabuhagan is organized around the Manichaean doctrine of the "Two Principles and Three Times" — the eternal existence of Light and Darkness (the two principles), and the three cosmic moments of their separation, mixture, and final re-separation. The text gives particular attention to eschatological themes, describing the cosmic conflagration and final judgment in vivid detail. Substantial fragments of the original Middle Persian text were discovered among the Turfan manuscripts, making the Shabuhagan one of the better-preserved Manichaean scriptures and an invaluable source for understanding Mani's thought in his own cultural context.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Manichaean**: A key canonical text presenting Mani's cosmology for a Zoroastrian audience
- **Academic**: Among the better-preserved Manichaean texts thanks to the Turfan discoveries
- **Comparative**: Reveals Mani's engagement with Zoroastrian eschatology and cosmology
- **Gnostic**: Demonstrates the gnostic framework adapted to Iranian religious categories
- **Christian**: Shows how Manichaean theology related to and diverged from Christian eschatology
- **Interfaith**: Illustrates Mani's strategy of adapting his message to different cultural contexts

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Two Principles | The eternal co-existence of Light and Darkness as foundational reality |
| Three Times | The cosmic periodization: prior separation, present mixture, future re-separation |
| Royal Patronage | Mani's mission to win Sasanian royal support for his universal religion |
| Eschatology | The cosmic conflagration and final judgment described in vivid detail |
| Cultural Adaptation | Mani's strategy of presenting his teaching in Zoroastrian terms |
| Cosmic Mechanism | The sun, moon, and Column of Glory as instruments of light-liberation |
