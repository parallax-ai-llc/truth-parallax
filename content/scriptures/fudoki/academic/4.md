Academic study of the Hizen Province (肥前国) Fudoki applies the methodologies of historical geography, philology, archaeology, and religious studies to the records from modern Saga and Nagasaki Prefectures. Scholars examine this provincial gazetteer as a product of the ritsuryō state's administrative apparatus, as a record of oral traditions captured at a specific historical moment in the eighth century CE, and as a source for understanding the social, economic, and religious conditions of Nara-period provincial Japan.

The Hizen Province (肥前国) records present records from northern Kyushu reflecting continental cultural influences and distinctive local traditions. Textual scholars analyze the relationship between the surviving text and the original provincial submission, the degree to which local traditions were filtered through the perspectives of the literati who compiled them, and the identification of different chronological layers within the text. Archaeological evidence from the region provides material correlates for the textual record, enabling a more complete reconstruction of eighth-century provincial life.
