## Overview

The religious Shinto tradition values the Fudoki as an irreplaceable record of local kami worship and shrine traditions that the centralized mythology of the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki did not fully preserve. These provincial records reveal the rich diversity of Japanese religious practice at the ground level — the local deities, sacred sites, ritual customs, and mythological traditions that constituted the living religious experience of ordinary people in Nara-period Japan.

## Local Kami Traditions

The Fudoki preserve accounts of regional kami who do not appear in the imperial mythological compilations. These local deities — mountain gods, river spirits, land-clearing heroes, and agricultural benefactors — represent the grassroots level of Shinto spirituality, rooted in the direct relationship between human communities and the sacred landscape they inhabit.

## Sacred Geography

For the Shinto tradition, the Fudoki demonstrate that every region of Japan possesses its own sacred geography — a network of shrines, sacred mountains, divine rivers, and hallowed groves that together constitute a spiritual landscape as real and important as the physical terrain.
