The Religious Shinto tradition values the Izumo Province (出雲国) Fudoki as an irreplaceable record of local kami worship in modern Shimane Prefecture. These records preserve accounts of regional deities, shrine traditions, and sacred sites that the centralized mythology of the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki did not fully capture, revealing the grassroots level of Shinto spirituality rooted in the direct relationship between human communities and their sacred landscape.

The Izumo Province (出雲国) records present the most complete surviving Fudoki, from the region most sacred to indigenous Shinto worship. For Shinto practitioners, these accounts testify to the pervasive presence of kami in the natural world — in mountains, rivers, forests, and fields — and to the ancient roots of shrine traditions that continue to be observed in the region today. The Fudoki remind the faithful that every locality possesses its own sacred geography, its own network of divine presences that sustain the life of the community.
