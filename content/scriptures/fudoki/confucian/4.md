Confucian interpretation of the Hizen Province (肥前国) Fudoki reads this provincial gazetteer within the framework of proper governance and administrative virtue. The imperial edict commanding its compilation reflects Confucian ideals of rational administration: a virtuous ruler must know the conditions, resources, and customs of every province in order to govern wisely. The records from modern Saga and Nagasaki Prefectures demonstrate the application of Chinese-influenced bureaucratic rationality to the task of understanding and ordering the Japanese realm.

The Hizen Province (肥前国) records present records from northern Kyushu reflecting continental cultural influences and distinctive local traditions. Confucian readers note the administrative dimensions of the text: its systematic recording of agricultural production, natural resources, and population — information essential for effective governance. At the same time, the text's extensive mythological content reveals the limits of bureaucratic rationality in a culture where divine narrative and administrative record coexist as complementary ways of knowing the land.
