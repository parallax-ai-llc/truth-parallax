## Overview

Confucian interpretation of the Fudoki reads these provincial gazetteers within the framework of proper governance and administrative virtue. The imperial edict commanding their compilation reflects Confucian ideals of rational administration: a virtuous ruler must know the conditions, resources, and customs of every province in order to govern wisely. The Fudoki thus represent the application of Chinese-influenced bureaucratic rationality to the task of understanding and ordering the Japanese realm.

## Administrative Virtue

From a Confucian perspective, the Fudoki embody the principle that good governance requires comprehensive knowledge of the governed territory. The systematic recording of geography, agriculture, natural resources, and local customs reflects the Confucian conviction that a ruler's moral authority depends on his practical understanding of his domain.

## Civilizing Narrative

Confucian readers note that several Fudoki narratives follow a pattern in which the arrival of imperial authority transforms wild or chaotic local conditions into ordered, productive communities — a narrative structure that resonates with the Confucian concept of the civilizing influence (jiaohua/教化) of virtuous governance.
