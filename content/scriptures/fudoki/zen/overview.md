## Overview

Zen interpretation of the Fudoki discovers in these provincial records a sensibility that resonates with Zen's own emphasis on the sacredness of the immediate, the concrete, and the particular. The Fudoki's attentiveness to specific mountains, rivers, plants, and animals — its insistence that meaning is found not in abstract principles but in the particular features of actual landscapes — mirrors the Zen tradition's famous declaration that "mountains are mountains, rivers are rivers."

## Mountains and Waters

The Zen tradition finds in the Fudoki a precursor to Dōgen's Sansuikyō ("Mountains and Waters Sutra"), which teaches that mountains and waters are themselves the body of the Buddha. The Fudoki's accounts of mountains where kami dwell and rivers where divine events occurred express a similar intuition: the natural world is not separate from the sacred but is itself the primary locus of divine presence.

## Ordinary Sacredness

Zen readers are drawn to the Fudoki's democratic vision of the sacred — its insistence that every province, every mountain, every river possesses its own sacred history. This vision accords with the Zen teaching that awakening is not found in extraordinary experiences but in the ordinary, everyday reality of the world as it presents itself.
