Zen interpretation of the Harima Province (播磨国) Fudoki discovers in these records from modern Hyogo Prefecture a sensibility that resonates with Zen's own emphasis on the sacredness of the particular, the immediate, and the concrete. The Fudoki's attentiveness to specific mountains, rivers, and natural features — its insistence that meaning is found not in abstract principles but in the particular features of actual landscapes — mirrors the Zen teaching that 'mountains are mountains, rivers are rivers.'

The Harima Province (播磨国) records present geographical and mythological records reflecting the cultural traditions of the Inland Sea region. Zen readers find in these local accounts an expression of what Dōgen would later articulate as the teaching that mountains and waters are themselves the body of the Buddha — that the natural world is not separate from the sacred but is itself the primary locus of divine presence. The Fudoki's democratic vision of the sacred, in which every province possesses its own sacred history, accords with the Zen conviction that awakening is found in the ordinary reality of the world as it presents itself.
