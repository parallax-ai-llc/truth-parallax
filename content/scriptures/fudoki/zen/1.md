Zen interpretation of the Hitachi Province (常陸国) Fudoki discovers in these records from modern Ibaraki Prefecture a sensibility that resonates with Zen's own emphasis on the sacredness of the particular, the immediate, and the concrete. The Fudoki's attentiveness to specific mountains, rivers, and natural features — its insistence that meaning is found not in abstract principles but in the particular features of actual landscapes — mirrors the Zen teaching that 'mountains are mountains, rivers are rivers.'

The Hitachi Province (常陸国) records present local kami narratives and place-name etymologies that reveal the religious landscape of eastern Japan. Zen readers find in these local accounts an expression of what Dōgen would later articulate as the teaching that mountains and waters are themselves the body of the Buddha — that the natural world is not separate from the sacred but is itself the primary locus of divine presence. The Fudoki's democratic vision of the sacred, in which every province possesses its own sacred history, accords with the Zen conviction that awakening is found in the ordinary reality of the world as it presents itself.
