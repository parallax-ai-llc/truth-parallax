## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Fudoki situates these Japanese provincial records within the broader Asian tradition of geographical writing and the global phenomenon of sacred geography. Chinese difangzhi (local gazetteers) and Korean eupji (county records) provide the most direct comparisons, but the Fudoki's integration of mythology, place-name etymology, and sacred landscape description also invites comparison with indigenous geographical traditions worldwide.

## Asian Gazetteer Traditions

The Fudoki were compiled in conscious imitation of Chinese models, yet they differ significantly from their Chinese counterparts in their extensive inclusion of mythological and supernatural narratives. While Chinese gazetteers generally subordinated such material to administrative and economic information, the Fudoki give equal or greater weight to local legends and divine histories, reflecting the particular character of Japanese religious culture.

## Sacred Landscape

Comparative scholars examine the Fudoki's treatment of landscape alongside parallel traditions: the Aboriginal Australian Dreaming tracks that map sacred geography through narrative, the European tradition of local hagiography that sanctifies landscape through saints' legends, and the Native American place-name traditions that encode ecological and spiritual knowledge in geographical nomenclature.
