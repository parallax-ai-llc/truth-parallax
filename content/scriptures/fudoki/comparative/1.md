Comparative interpretation of the Hitachi Province (常陸国) Fudoki situates these records from modern Ibaraki Prefecture within the broader Asian tradition of geographical writing and the global phenomenon of sacred geography. The Chinese difangzhi (local gazetteers) and Korean eupji (county records) provide the most direct comparisons, but the Fudoki's integration of mythology, place-name etymology, and sacred landscape description also invites comparison with indigenous geographical traditions worldwide.

The Hitachi Province (常陸国) records present local kami narratives and place-name etymologies that reveal the religious landscape of eastern Japan. Comparative scholars examine these local traditions alongside parallel phenomena: the Aboriginal Australian Dreaming tracks, the European tradition of local hagiography, and the Native American place-name traditions that encode ecological and spiritual knowledge in geographical nomenclature. Each tradition demonstrates the universal human impulse to invest landscape with narrative meaning and sacred significance.
