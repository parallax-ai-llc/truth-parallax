scripture: "fudoki"

The Harima Fudoki describes the province lying west of the capital along the Inland Sea, a region of strategic importance and rich agricultural productivity. The text is notable for its extensive mythological narratives, which link nearly every geographic feature to the actions of deities and legendary rulers.

The deity Onamuchi, identified with Okuninushi of the Izumo tradition, features prominently in the Harima myths. Stories recount his journeys through the province, creating hot springs by planting his staff in the ground, naming mountains after events that occurred during his travels, and competing with other deities for control of the land. These narratives reveal the interaction between different regional mythological traditions as they were woven into the fabric of imperial ideology.

The Harima text contains an unusually large number of place-name etymologies, each embedded in a narrative that makes the landscape legible in mythological terms. A mountain where a deity rested becomes "Resting Mountain." A river where a sacred deer was seen becomes "Deer River." These etymologies transform the physical landscape into a text that can be read for its sacred and historical meanings.

The agricultural sections of the Harima Fudoki describe the iron-rich soils that support rice cultivation, the salt production along the coast, and the fishing grounds of the Inland Sea. The text notes the province's role in maritime trade and its strategic position controlling access between the capital and the western provinces. The people of Harima are described as industrious farmers and skilled fishermen, and their customs and festivals are recorded with ethnographic precision.

The Harima Fudoki also preserves fragments of local poetry and song, including work songs associated with agricultural labor and ritual songs performed at seasonal festivals. These literary elements distinguish the Harima text from more purely administrative provincial records and suggest the participation of local literati in its compilation.
