title: "Fudoki Section 5: Bungo Province"
scripture: "fudoki"
chapter: 5

The Bungo Fudoki describes the province on the eastern coast of Kyushu, facing the Inland Sea and serving as a gateway between Kyushu and the Japanese heartland. Though only fragments survive, these preserves contain significant mythological narratives and geographic descriptions that illuminate the culture of ancient Kyushu.

The most celebrated passage describes the hot springs of Beppu, among the most volcanically active areas in Japan. The text records the wonder of local people at the variety of springs, some producing clear hot water, others emitting sulphurous steam, and still others bubbling with mineral-rich mud. These natural phenomena are attributed to the activity of underground deities whose power manifests through the heated waters.

The mythological traditions of Bungo connect the province to the broader narrative of the imperial line's origins in Kyushu. The text references the legends of Emperor Jimmu's ancestor Ninigi, whose descent from heaven is placed in nearby Hyuga, and describes local sites associated with the mythological events preceding the eastern expedition that established imperial rule in Yamato.

The provincial descriptions note the abundance of timber in Bungo's mountainous interior, the rich fishing grounds along its coast, and the agricultural productivity of its river valleys. The text describes the customs of the local people, including their maritime traditions, their religious observances at local shrines, and their distinctive dialect and place-naming conventions.

The fragmentary nature of the surviving text is itself historically significant, illustrating the hazards that ancient manuscripts faced over the centuries. What remains of the Bungo Fudoki provides tantalizing glimpses of a rich provincial culture that existed at the intersection of Kyushu's continental connections and the emerging imperial civilization of the Japanese heartland. The text serves as a reminder that early Japan was a mosaic of regional cultures united by the administrative and ideological framework of the imperial state.
