The Izumo Fudoki stands as the most complete surviving provincial gazetteer and one of the most important documents of early Japanese religion and mythology. Izumo Province, located on the Japan Sea coast, was the center of an independent mythological tradition that rivaled and eventually became integrated with the imperial mythology centered on the sun goddess Amaterasu.

The text opens with a mythological account of land-pulling, in which the deity Yatsukamizu Omitsunu pulls pieces of land from Korea and other distant places, attaching them to Izumo with great ropes to enlarge the province. This extraordinary creation narrative, unique to Izumo, suggests ancient connections between the region and the Asian continent and reflects Izumo's self-understanding as a place of cosmic significance.

The great shrine of Izumo, dedicated to Okuninushi, the deity who agreed to cede the earthly realm to the descendants of the sun goddess, is described in detail that has been invaluable for understanding ancient shrine architecture. The text records the shrine's enormous dimensions, which later archaeological evidence has partially confirmed, and describes the rituals performed there, including the annual gathering of all Japan's deities in the tenth month.

Each district of Izumo is described with attention to its shrines, myths, and natural features. The text records 399 shrines, providing the most comprehensive picture of the religious landscape of any ancient Japanese province. The deities enshrined range from cosmic figures of national significance to local spirits of springs, mountains, and crossroads, revealing the layered nature of early Japanese religious practice.

The natural history sections describe Izumo's lakes, rivers, mountains, and coastline with a specificity that has allowed modern scholars to trace geographic changes over the past thirteen centuries. The province's distinctive culture, shaped by its Japan Sea orientation and its strong mythological traditions, emerges clearly from the text, making the Izumo Fudoki an irreplaceable source for understanding regional diversity in ancient Japan.
