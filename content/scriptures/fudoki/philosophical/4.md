Philosophical interpretation of the Hizen Province (肥前国) Fudoki examines the records of modern Saga and Nagasaki Prefectures for their implicit philosophy of place, landscape, and the relationship between human dwelling and natural meaning. The place-name etymologies preserved in this provincial gazetteer reveal a mode of thinking in which landscape is not mere terrain but a meaning-laden reality in which the sacred manifests through specific geographical features.

The Hizen Province (肥前国) records present records from northern Kyushu reflecting continental cultural influences and distinctive local traditions. Philosophical readers find in these accounts a vision of the world in which naming is not arbitrary but revelatory — each place name encodes a story of divine action, human encounter, or natural transformation that discloses the deeper significance of the landscape. This philosophy of naming anticipates modern phenomenological approaches to the relationship between language, place, and meaning.
