## Overview

The Fudoki (風土記, "Records of Wind and Earth") are a collection of provincial gazetteers compiled in the Nara period following an imperial edict of 713 CE issued by Empress Gemmei. Each province was ordered to submit records of its geography, place-name etymologies, local myths, legends, natural resources, and agricultural conditions. Of the original submissions from all provinces, only five survive in substantial form, preserving an invaluable record of local Japanese mythology, religious practice, and cultural identity that complements the centralized narratives of the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki.

## Structure

The surviving Fudoki represent five provinces:

1. **Hitachi Province** (常陸国風土記): Rich in local mythology and place-name legends from modern Ibaraki Prefecture
2. **Harima Province** (播磨国風土記): Geographic and mythological records from modern Hyogo Prefecture
3. **Izumo Province** (出雲国風土記): The most complete surviving Fudoki, from the region most sacred to indigenous Shinto
4. **Hizen Province** (肥前国風土記): Records from northern Kyushu, reflecting continental cultural influences
5. **Bungo Province** (豊後国風土記): Accounts from eastern Kyushu with distinctive local traditions

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Fudoki have been interpreted across multiple traditions:

- **Shinto**: Invaluable for understanding local kami worship and shrine traditions not recorded in the central myths
- **Confucian**: Read as models of administrative record-keeping and governance during the ritsuryō state
- **Academic**: Critical sources for historical geography, linguistics, and the study of local religious traditions
- **Comparative**: Examined alongside regional gazetteers from China and Korea
