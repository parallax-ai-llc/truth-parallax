---
title: "Varaha Purana Section 3"
scripture: "varaha-purana"
chapter: 3
---

**1** The Varaha Purana describes the sacred Vratas, the holy observances, and the stories that illustrate their power.

**2** The Lord said: "Vratas are the means by which the householder, while living in the world, can accumulate spiritual merit. They discipline the body, purify the mind, and strengthen devotion."

**3** The Vrata of the Shivaratri is described: "On the fourteenth night of the dark fortnight of Phalguna, Shiva is worshipped with vigil, fasting, and the offering of bilva leaves. This Vrata destroys the sins of a hundred lifetimes."

**4** The story of the Shivaratri hunter is told. A hunter, stranded in a tree at night above a Shiva Linga, unknowingly dropped bilva leaves upon it while keeping vigil through the night. At dawn, the messengers of Shiva came to take him to Kailash.

**5** The Vrata of Ananta Chaturdashi is described: "On the fourteenth day of the bright fortnight of Bhadrapada, the serpent Ananta, a form of Vishnu, is worshipped. He who ties a thread with fourteen knots and worships Ananta obtains unlimited prosperity."

**6** The Vrata of Karva Chauth, the Vrata of Vata Savitri, and the Vrata of Haritalika Teej are described as Vratas observed by women for the welfare and longevity of their husbands.

**7** The Purana teaches: "A Vrata performed without faith is fruitless. A Vrata performed with faith but without proper procedure yields partial results. A Vrata performed with both faith and correct procedure yields complete results."

**8** The Purana describes the Chaturmas Vrata: "During the four months of the rainy season, from Ashadha to Kartika, the Lord Vishnu sleeps on the ocean of milk. During this period, the devout should observe special austerities, increase their worship, and abstain from certain foods."

**9** The Purana narrates the origin of the Satyanarayan Vrata: "This Vrata, dedicated to Vishnu in his form as the Lord of Truth, can be performed by anyone on any auspicious day. It requires the preparation of sacred food, the recitation of the Satyanarayan Katha, and the distribution of prasad."

**10** The story is told of a poor Brahmin who performed the Satyanarayan Vrata with faith and was blessed with infinite wealth, or of a merchant who neglected to distribute the prasad and lost everything until he performed the Vrata again with proper devotion.

**11** The Purana teaches: "Every Vrata is a small sacrifice. In every sacrifice, the devotee offers something — food, sleep, comfort, desire — and receives in return spiritual merit. The greatest sacrifice is the offering of the ego itself."

**12** The Lord concluded: "The Vratas I have described are like ladders that lead from earth to heaven, and from heaven to liberation. He who climbs them step by step, with patience and devotion, shall surely reach the summit."
