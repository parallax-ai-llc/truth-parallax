## Overview

Shaiva interpreters approach the Varaha Purana with a perspective that seeks to reconcile its Vaishnava theological content with Shaiva metaphysics and devotional practice. Rather than rejecting the text outright, Shaiva scholars have historically engaged constructively, demonstrating the complementarity or ultimate unity of Shiva and Vishnu.

The Shaiva reading often invokes the Harihara concept — the inseparability of Hari (Vishnu) and Hara (Shiva) — to argue that the text's theological affirmations implicitly include or point to Shiva as the ultimate ground of reality.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Harihara Unity
Shaiva readers invoke the inseparability of Shiva and Vishnu, arguing that theological affirmations about one deity implicitly include the other. The ultimate reality transcends sectarian divisions.

### Shiva as Parameshvara
The cosmic functions described in the text — creation, preservation, dissolution — are understood as ultimately governed by Shiva in his aspect as the supreme lord of the universe.

### Panchakritya
Shiva's five cosmic acts (creation, preservation, dissolution, concealment, and grace) provide the framework through which Shaiva interpreters read Puranic narratives.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shiva as Ultimate Ground**: All divine manifestations ultimately derive from and return to Shiva
- **Trimurthi Theology**: Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva as co-equal aspects of the one supreme reality
- **Inclusive Monotheism**: A theological position that includes all deities within the Shaiva framework
- **Lila and Maya**: Cosmic play and illusion as functions of Shiva's creative power
