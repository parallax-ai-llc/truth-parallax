## Overview

The Classic of Poetry (Shijing) is the oldest surviving collection of Chinese poetry, comprising 305 poems dating from the early Western Zhou dynasty (c. 1046 BCE) through the middle of the Spring and Autumn period (c. 600 BCE). Traditionally attributed to the editorial hand of Confucius himself, the anthology gathers folk songs, courtly odes, and sacrificial hymns that together form a vivid portrait of early Chinese life, values, and ritual practice. As one of the Five Classics (Wujing), the Shijing has occupied a central place in Confucian education and literary culture for over two millennia.

## Structure

The 305 poems are organized into four major sections:

1. **Guofeng (Airs of the States)** — 160 folk songs drawn from fifteen feudal states, expressing themes of love, labor, longing, and social complaint
2. **Xiaoya (Minor Odes)** — 74 poems associated with court gatherings, banquets, and the concerns of the lesser nobility
3. **Daya (Major Odes)** — 31 poems celebrating dynastic founders, narrating cosmic and political origins, and offering praise of royal virtue
4. **Song (Hymns)** — 40 poems used in ancestral sacrifices and temple ceremonies of the Zhou, Shang, and Lu courts

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Shijing has been read and interpreted across multiple intellectual traditions:

- **Confucian**: The definitive anthology for moral education, teaching the reader to express emotion with propriety
- **Neo-Confucian**: Studied for its illustrations of cosmic pattern (li) manifest in human feeling and natural imagery
- **Daoist**: Valued for its depictions of rural simplicity, natural rhythms, and spontaneous human emotion
- **Buddhist-Chinese**: Read comparatively for its portrayal of impermanence, longing, and the conditioned nature of worldly attachments
- **Academic**: Subject of extensive philological, archaeological, and comparative literary scholarship
