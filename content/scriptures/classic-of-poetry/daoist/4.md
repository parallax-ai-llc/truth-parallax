Daoist interpretation of the Song (Hymns) presents the most pointed contrast between Daoist and Confucian readings of the Shijing. Where the Confucian tradition venerates the sacrificial hymns as the apex of ritual civilization, Daoist readers see in their elaborate ceremonial context the furthest development of the artificial structures that separate human beings from the spontaneous order of the Dao. The formalized language, prescribed musical settings, and choreographed ritual performances represent the kind of imposed order that the Zhuangzi satirizes.

Yet Daoist interpretation is not merely dismissive. Some Daoist readers recognize in the hymns' invocation of ancestral spirits and cosmic forces a dim echo of the shamanic traditions that preceded and inform both Daoist and Confucian practice. The Song's attempt to mediate between the human and the numinous reflects a genuine human need to connect with powers beyond ordinary comprehension — a need that Daoism addresses through meditation, inner cultivation, and direct attunement to the Dao rather than through external ceremony.

The antiquity of the Song also carries significance for Daoist readers. The Shang dynasty hymns, with their more archaic and less systematized ritual language, are sometimes valued as preserving traces of a religious sensibility closer to the Daoist ideal than the highly formalized Zhou ceremonies. The very evolution from Shang spontaneity to Zhou formality documented within the Song section mirrors the Daoist narrative of progressive alienation from original simplicity.
