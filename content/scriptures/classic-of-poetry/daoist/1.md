Daoist interpretation of the Guofeng finds in these folk songs the most authentic and unmediated portion of the entire Shijing. Where Confucian commentators overlay the poems with political allegory, Daoist readers strip away these interpretive layers to recover the spontaneous voices of ordinary people expressing love, longing, frustration, and joy in their natural forms. The Guofeng's folk origins connect it to the Daoist ideal of simplicity and the uncarved block (pu) — human nature before it is shaped by the conventions of civilization.

The love poems of the Guofeng are valued precisely for the directness and intensity of their emotional expression. Daoist readers see in a poem like "Guanju" not a moral lesson about marital propriety but the natural stirring of desire that mirrors the creative impulse of the Dao itself. The seasonal imagery — plum blossoms falling, rivers flowing, birds calling — is read as the Dao's spontaneous self-expression through the natural world, and the human emotions that arise in response are understood as continuous with this cosmic creativity rather than as something to be regulated and controlled.

The songs of hardship and political complaint within the Guofeng also resonate with Daoist sensibilities. From a Daoist perspective, the suffering expressed in these poems results from the imposition of artificial political and social structures on naturally fluid human communities. The folk voice becomes a protest not just against bad governance but against the very project of civilization that separates human beings from their original nature.
