Daoist interpretation of the Xiaoya approaches the minor court odes with a critical eye toward the institutional life they represent, while finding unexpected moments of naturalness and authenticity within the courtly setting. The banquet poems, with their descriptions of wine drinking and musical entertainment, contain elements that resonate with the Daoist appreciation of spontaneous fellowship, even as the formal protocols of court life embody the kind of rigid hierarchy that Daoist thought critiques.

The Xiaoya's poems of political complaint and moral frustration are read by Daoist interpreters as evidence of the inherent contradictions within the Confucian institutional project. Officials who lament the decline of virtuous governance are seen, from a Daoist perspective, as recognizing — perhaps without fully understanding — that the elaborate structures of bureaucratic governance inevitably generate the very disorders they are designed to prevent. The more earnestly one attempts to impose moral order through institutional means, the more conspicuously that order breaks down.

Yet Daoist readers also find in certain Xiaoya poems moments of genuine insight. Poems that describe the pleasures of withdrawing from court life, enjoying natural scenery, or finding solace in simplicity resonate with the Daoist tradition of reclusion. The minor odes that express weariness with political obligation speak to the Daoist conviction that authentic human flourishing requires freedom from the artificial demands of institutional life, a theme that later inspired the great tradition of Chinese recluse poetry.
