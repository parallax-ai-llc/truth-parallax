Daoist interpretation of the Daya engages critically with the major odes' celebration of dynastic founding and political authority. Where Confucian readers find in these poems the supreme expression of virtuous governance, Daoist readers detect the origins of the hierarchical civilization that the Daodejing identifies as a departure from the original simplicity of the Dao. The very grandeur of the dynastic narratives marks them, from a Daoist perspective, as symptoms of the human drive toward power and self-glorification that produces suffering.

However, Daoist readers do not dismiss the Daya entirely. The poems' vivid descriptions of landscape — mountains, rivers, plains, and the movement of heavenly bodies — are appreciated as moments when the poetic voice transcends political ideology and connects with the natural world. The origin narratives, particularly the story of Hou Ji's miraculous birth, contain mythological elements that resonate with Daoist cosmogony and the understanding of the sage as one who emerges from the creative power of the Dao rather than from human institutional design.

The Daya's warning poems about the decline of the Zhou dynasty hold particular interest for Daoist readers. The cycle of founding virtue giving way to corruption and excess confirms the Daoist teaching articulated in the Daodejing: when the great Way falls into disuse, benevolence and righteousness appear; when intelligence and wit arise, great hypocrisy follows. The Daya inadvertently documents the very process that Daoist philosophy diagnoses as the root of civilizational malaise.
