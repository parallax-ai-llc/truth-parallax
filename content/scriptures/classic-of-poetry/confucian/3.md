Confucian interpretation of the Daya (Major Odes) treats these 31 poems as the literary heart of Zhou dynastic ideology and the supreme expression of the Confucian understanding of virtuous rulership. The Daya poems narrate the founding of the Zhou dynasty, celebrate the virtue of its sage-kings, and articulate the cosmic and moral principles that legitimize royal authority. For Confucian readers, these are not merely historical poems but normative texts that establish the standard against which all governance should be measured.

The foundational narratives of the Daya — poems recounting the deeds of Hou Ji, King Wen, and King Wu — are read as embodying the Confucian ideal of the sage-king who governs through moral example rather than coercion. King Wen's virtue is portrayed as so powerful that it attracted the allegiance of the feudal lords without military force, illustrating the Confucian conviction that true authority radiates from moral character. The Mandate of Heaven (Tianming) is presented not as arbitrary divine favor but as a response to demonstrable virtue.

Confucian commentators also attend carefully to the Daya poems that warn against complacency and moral decline. Poems lamenting the failings of later Zhou kings serve as cautionary tales, reinforcing the teaching that the Mandate of Heaven is conditional and must be constantly renewed through virtuous conduct. This dialectic of praise and warning makes the Daya a comprehensive manual of political ethics.
