Confucian interpretation of the Xiaoya (Minor Odes) reads these 74 poems as expressions of the concerns, aspirations, and moral sentiments of the lesser aristocracy and court officials. The Xiaoya poems occupy a middle register between the folk directness of the Guofeng and the solemn grandeur of the Daya, reflecting the experiences of those who served in the Zhou court and the feudal administrations. Confucian commentators treat them as valuable records of the moral atmosphere of governance at the level of daily administration.

The banquet poems of the Xiaoya receive special emphasis in Confucian reading. Poems describing feasts and gatherings are understood as illustrations of ritual propriety (li) in social interaction. The proper conduct of a banquet — the ordering of guests, the offering and receiving of toasts, the performance of music — embodies the Confucian conviction that harmonious relationships require formal structures of mutual respect. When the odes lament the deterioration of banquet decorum, Confucian readers detect the broader decline of the ritual order upon which political stability depends.

The Xiaoya also contains poems of political complaint and moral protest, which Confucian interpreters regard as exemplifying the tradition of loyal remonstrance. Officials who express grief at governmental failings are not seen as seditious but as fulfilling their moral duty to voice criticism. The Confucian tradition holds that such poems demonstrate the proper channel through which dissatisfaction should be expressed: through artful, indirect speech rather than violent action.
