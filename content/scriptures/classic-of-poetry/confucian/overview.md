## Overview

The Classic of Poetry is regarded by the Confucian tradition as Confucius's own curated anthology for moral education. According to the Analerta, the Master declared that the three hundred poems could be summed up in a single phrase: "thoughts without depravity." Confucian interpreters read the Shijing as a manual for cultivating proper emotions and expressing them in accord with ritual propriety, making poetry an essential tool of self-cultivation and social harmony.

## Interpretive Approach

Traditional Confucian commentary on the Shijing, exemplified by the Mao Commentary (Maoshi) and the Great Preface (Daxu), emphasizes the allegorical and political dimensions of each poem. Folk songs are read not merely as expressions of personal feeling but as reflections of the moral condition of their respective states. Love poems become allegories of the ruler-minister relationship, and laments over abandonment express the loyal minister's grief at being dismissed. The Confucian reading prioritizes the civilizing function of poetry over its aesthetic qualities alone.

## Contemporary Relevance

Modern Confucian scholars continue to draw on the Shijing to articulate a vision of emotional education that balances authentic expression with social responsibility. The text's integration of personal feeling with communal values speaks to ongoing conversations about character formation, the role of the arts in moral development, and the relationship between cultural literacy and civic virtue.
