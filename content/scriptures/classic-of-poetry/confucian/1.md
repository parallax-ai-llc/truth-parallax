Confucian interpretation of the Guofeng (Airs of the States) treats these 160 folk songs as mirrors of the moral condition of their respective feudal states. Following the Mao Commentary and the Great Preface, traditional Confucian readers understand each poem as reflecting the customs and governance quality of the state from which it originates. A state with virtuous rulers produces songs of contentment and harmonious relationships, while a state in moral decline produces laments, complaints, and expressions of frustrated longing.

The love poems of the Guofeng receive particular attention in Confucian interpretation. Rather than reading them as simple expressions of romantic desire, Confucian commentators typically allegorize them as portrayals of the ruler-minister relationship. The opening poem "Guanju" (Crying Ospreys), for example, is read not merely as a courtship song but as a celebration of the queen's virtue and the proper relationship between husband and wife as the foundation of social order. Confucius himself stated that "Guanju" expresses "joy without licentiousness, grief without self-injury," establishing the Confucian principle that authentic emotion must be harmonized with propriety.

The labor songs, songs of military service, and seasonal songs of the Guofeng are similarly read as evidence of the people's moral condition under good or poor governance. The Confucian tradition sees in these poems the voice of the common people responding to the quality of leadership they receive, making the Guofeng an indirect manual of political assessment.
