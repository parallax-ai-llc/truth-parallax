Confucian interpretation of the Song (Hymns) regards these 40 poems as the most sacred portion of the Shijing, composed for performance during ancestral sacrifices and temple ceremonies. The Song section comprises hymns of the Zhou court, the Shang dynasty descendants in the state of Song, and the state of Lu, where Confucius himself resided. Confucian readers treat these hymns as the highest expression of the unity between poetry, music, and ritual (shi, yue, li) that constitutes the foundation of civilized order.

The Zhou hymns celebrate the dynasty's founding and the virtue of its ancestors, while simultaneously enacting the ritual relationship between the living and the dead that sustains the moral order across generations. Confucian commentators emphasize that the sacrificial context of the Song means these poems were not merely recited but performed with full musical accompaniment and choreographed movement, embodying the total integration of aesthetic and moral practice that Confucius identified as the apex of culture.

The inclusion of Shang hymns preserved by the state of Song demonstrates, for Confucian readers, the Zhou dynasty's magnanimity in honoring the ritual traditions of the dynasty it had conquered. The Lu hymns hold special significance because they connect the ritual heritage of the Shijing directly to Confucius's own state. Confucian tradition holds that the Master's deep knowledge of these hymns informed his entire understanding of ritual propriety and its civilizing power.
