Academic study of the Xiaoya analyzes these 74 poems as evidence for the political culture, ritual practices, and social structures of the Zhou aristocracy. Scholars examine the minor odes for insights into court ceremonial, the conduct of diplomatic relations, the organization of military campaigns, and the intellectual life of the Zhou elite. The Xiaoya provides a window into a social world intermediate between the folk communities of the Guofeng and the royal court of the Daya.

Linguistic analysis of the Xiaoya reveals a more elevated and formal register than the Guofeng, reflecting the poems' origins in aristocratic rather than folk settings. Scholars have identified characteristic formulaic phrases, structural patterns, and vocabulary choices that distinguish the minor odes as a literary register. The relationship between oral and written composition in the Xiaoya is debated, with some scholars arguing that these poems show greater evidence of literary craft and deliberate composition than the more spontaneous-seeming folk songs.

Historical analysis of the Xiaoya has proven particularly productive for understanding the political dynamics of the late Western Zhou and early Eastern Zhou periods. Poems referencing specific historical events, military campaigns, and political crises have been correlated with bronze inscriptions and archaeological evidence to construct a more detailed picture of Zhou political history. The Xiaoya's poems of political complaint have been studied as early examples of the Chinese tradition of political protest literature, providing evidence for the development of conventions of indirect criticism that would become central to Chinese literary culture.
