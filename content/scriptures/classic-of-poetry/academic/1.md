Academic study of the Guofeng examines these 160 poems as the richest source of evidence for the social life, material culture, and oral traditions of early Zhou-era China. Scholars approach the folk songs through philological analysis, archaeological contextualization, anthropological comparison, and literary criticism, seeking to understand both the original contexts of composition and performance and the processes through which the poems were collected, edited, and transmitted.

Philological research has reconstructed the Old Chinese phonology of the Guofeng, revealing rhyme patterns and sound symbolism that are invisible in the modern Chinese pronunciation. These reconstructions demonstrate the sophisticated oral artistry of the original performers and provide evidence for dialectal variation across the fifteen states represented in the collection. The archaic vocabulary of the folk songs preserves terms for plants, animals, tools, textiles, and social customs that illuminate the daily life of Zhou-era communities in ways that other sources do not.

Anthropological approaches to the Guofeng have drawn on comparative ethnography to understand the poems' functions in courtship rituals, agricultural festivals, and communal celebrations. Marcel Granet's pioneering Festivals and Songs of Ancient China (1919) interpreted the Guofeng as evidence for seasonal fertility festivals, while later scholars have refined and critiqued his approach. Gender studies have highlighted the prominence of women's voices in the Guofeng, analyzing how the poems represent female experience and agency within a patriarchal social order.
