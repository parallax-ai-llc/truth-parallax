Academic study of the Song (Hymns) examines these 40 sacrificial poems as primary evidence for the ritual practices, religious beliefs, and liturgical traditions of the Zhou and Shang courts. The Song section is of particular scholarly interest because it preserves the most archaic linguistic features in the entire Shijing and provides direct evidence for the ceremonial contexts in which poetry, music, and ritual were integrated in early Chinese civilization.

The Shang dynasty hymns preserved in the Song section have attracted intense scholarly attention because they provide rare poetic evidence from the perspective of Shang cultural descendants. Comparison between these hymns and Shang oracle bone inscriptions reveals continuities and transformations in Shang religious vocabulary and ritual practice. The Zhou hymns are studied for their evidence of how the conquering dynasty adapted and transformed inherited Shang ritual traditions while developing its own distinctive sacrificial ideology.

The Lu hymns within the Song section raise important questions about the relationship between ritual privilege and political status, since the state of Lu was granted exceptional permission to use royal-level ritual music. Scholars debate whether this privilege reflects Lu's status as the fiefdom of the Duke of Zhou or represents a later claim to enhanced ritual standing. Musicological research has attempted to reconstruct the performance contexts of the Song, examining evidence for instrumentation, choreography, and the integration of sung text with ritual action to understand how these poems functioned as components of a total ceremonial experience.
