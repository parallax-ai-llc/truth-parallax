Academic study of the Daya examines the 31 major odes as crucial sources for understanding Zhou dynastic ideology, origin mythology, and the development of Chinese political thought. These poems represent some of the earliest articulations of concepts — such as the Mandate of Heaven and the moral basis of political legitimacy — that would shape Chinese civilization for millennia. Scholars analyze them through the combined lenses of literary criticism, political history, religious studies, and intellectual history.

The Daya's origin narratives, particularly the accounts of Hou Ji's miraculous birth and the migration and settlement of the Zhou people, have been studied extensively as examples of founding mythology comparable to origin stories in other ancient civilizations. Scholars debate the historical kernel within these mythologized accounts, using archaeological evidence from sites in the Wei River valley to correlate poetic descriptions with material culture. The relationship between the Daya's narratives and other sources, including the Book of Documents and bronze inscriptions, illuminates the processes through which the Zhou ruling house constructed its legitimating ideology.

Scholars of ancient Chinese religion find in the Daya important evidence for the transition from the Shang dynasty's conception of a supreme deity (Di) to the Zhou concept of Heaven (Tian) as the source of moral and political authority. The theological vocabulary of the major odes documents this crucial shift in Chinese religious thought, marking the emergence of a more abstract and moralized understanding of divine authority that distinguished Chinese political theology from the personal-deity models prevalent in other ancient civilizations.
