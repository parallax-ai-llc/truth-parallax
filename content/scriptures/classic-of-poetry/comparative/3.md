Comparative study of the Daya places the Zhou dynastic odes alongside founding narratives and royal praise poetry from other ancient civilizations. Scholars compare the Daya's accounts of the Zhou dynasty's origins and divine mandate with Mesopotamian royal hymns, Egyptian pharaonic literature, Homeric accounts of heroic ancestry, the Aeneid's founding mythology, and Vedic praise hymns to Indra and other deities. These comparisons illuminate the diverse strategies through which ancient civilizations legitimated political authority through narrative and poetic art.

The Daya's articulation of the Mandate of Heaven provides a fascinating point of comparison with divine kingship ideologies elsewhere. Unlike Egyptian pharaonic theology, which identified the king as a living god, or Mesopotamian concepts of kingship as divinely ordained office, the Chinese model made legitimacy conditional on the ruler's moral conduct. This comparative perspective reveals the distinctive Chinese contribution to political thought: the idea that divine sanction is earned through virtue and can be lost through moral failure, creating an early framework for the accountability of rulers.

The origin mythology of the Daya also invites comparison with founding narratives in other traditions. Hou Ji's miraculous birth, abandonment, and survival parallel motifs found in the stories of Moses, Romulus and Remus, Sargon of Akkad, and Krishna. Comparative mythology scholars identify these as instances of a widespread narrative pattern associated with the births of culture heroes and dynasty founders, suggesting common psychological or social functions served by founding myths across diverse civilizations.
