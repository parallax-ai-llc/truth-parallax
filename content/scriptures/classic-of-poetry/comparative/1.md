Comparative study of the Guofeng places these folk songs in dialogue with oral poetry traditions from around the ancient world. Scholars have drawn illuminating parallels between the Guofeng's love songs, labor songs, and seasonal poems and comparable material from traditions including Sappho's lyric poetry, the Song of Songs in the Hebrew Bible, Tamil Sangam poetry, and Japanese folk songs preserved in the Man'yoshu. These comparisons reveal both universal features of folk poetry and the distinctive characteristics of the early Chinese tradition.

The Guofeng's courtship and love poetry invites comparison with erotic verse in other ancient traditions. Like the Song of Songs, the Guofeng uses elaborate nature imagery to express desire and longing; like Sappho's fragments, many Guofeng poems adopt a female speaking voice that articulates women's experience of love, absence, and social constraint. Comparative analysis reveals that the Chinese tradition is distinctive in the degree to which love poetry was subsequently allegorized for political purposes — a hermeneutic move with few parallels in other traditions.

The Guofeng's labor songs and songs of military service find parallels in the work songs, sailor's chanteys, and soldier's songs preserved in various cultures. The distinctive feature of the Chinese collection is its integration of these folk materials into a canonical anthology that served elite educational purposes — a process of canonization that transformed the social function of the songs from communal performance to moral instruction. Comparative study illuminates how different civilizations have negotiated the relationship between folk culture and elite literary tradition.
