## Overview

Comparative study of the Classic of Poetry situates the Shijing within a global context of ancient anthologies, oral poetry traditions, and sacred song collections. Scholars have drawn illuminating parallels between the Shijing and works such as the Psalms of the Hebrew Bible, the Rig Veda's hymns, Homeric poetry, and the Japanese Man'yoshu. These comparisons reveal both shared features of early literary cultures — including the integration of poetry with ritual, the prominence of nature imagery, and the interplay between individual voice and communal identity — and the distinctive characteristics of the Chinese tradition.

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative interpretation examines the Shijing across several dimensions. Structurally, scholars compare the organization of the collection into folk, courtly, and sacred divisions with similar categorizations in other traditions. Functionally, the roles of the Shijing in education, diplomacy, and ritual are compared with the uses of poetry in other civilizations. Thematically, cross-cultural analysis of love poetry, lament, praise, and seasonal song reveals both universal human concerns and culturally specific modes of expression. The comparative lens also illuminates how different civilizations have used canonical poetry collections to construct cultural identity and moral education.

## Contemporary Relevance

In an era of global literary studies, the Shijing serves as a crucial reference point for understanding how poetry functions across cultures as a medium for emotional expression, social commentary, and spiritual practice. Comparative approaches help readers appreciate the Shijing's distinctive contributions while recognizing its place within the broader human tradition of poetic creation.
