Comparative study of the Xiaoya examines the minor odes alongside courtly poetry traditions from other ancient civilizations to understand how aristocratic cultures used poetry to negotiate social relationships, express political concerns, and maintain communal identity. The Xiaoya's banquet poems, political complaints, and ceremonial odes find parallels in Greek symposium poetry, the courtly verse of medieval Arabic and Persian traditions, and the praise poetry of various African royal courts.

The banquet poems of the Xiaoya invite comparison with Greek symposium culture, where drinking gatherings served as venues for poetic performance and the negotiation of aristocratic social bonds. Both traditions used the regulated consumption of alcohol and the formal exchange of toasts as frameworks for expressing friendship, loyalty, and shared values. However, the Chinese tradition placed greater emphasis on the hierarchical ordering of the gathering as a microcosm of the political order, while the Greek symposium allowed for more egalitarian interaction among participants.

The Xiaoya's poems of political complaint represent an early development in a tradition of indirect political criticism through literary art. Comparative analysis with the Hebrew prophetic tradition, Greek political poetry by figures such as Solon and Theognis, and later Roman satirical tradition reveals different strategies for expressing dissent within hierarchical societies. The Chinese convention of indirect criticism through poetic imagery and allusion stands in contrast to the more direct prophetic denunciation found in the Hebrew Bible, reflecting different assumptions about the relationship between the critic and political authority.
