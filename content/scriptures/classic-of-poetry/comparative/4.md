Comparative study of the Song (Hymns) examines the Shijing's sacrificial poetry alongside liturgical and temple hymn traditions from other ancient civilizations. The Song's integration of poetic text, musical performance, and ritual action finds striking parallels in the Rig Veda's sacrificial hymns, the Psalms of the Hebrew Bible, Egyptian temple liturgies, and Mesopotamian cultic poetry. These comparisons illuminate both the universal human impulse toward sacred song and the distinctive features of the Chinese ritual tradition.

The Song's hymns to ancestral spirits invite comparison with ancestor veneration practices in other cultures, including Roman domestic religion, African ancestral traditions, and Japanese Shinto practices. The Chinese tradition is distinctive in the degree to which ancestral sacrifice was integrated into the political order, with the right to perform certain levels of sacrifice directly tied to political rank. This politicization of ritual distinguishes the Chinese Song from more purely devotional traditions of sacred hymns elsewhere.

The relationship between the Song's different subcollections — Zhou, Shang, and Lu hymns — illuminates the cross-cultural phenomenon of how conquering civilizations appropriate, adapt, and transform the ritual traditions of their predecessors. Comparative examples include the Roman adoption of Greek religious practices, the Islamic incorporation of pre-Islamic Arabian sacred sites, and the Christian transformation of pagan ceremonial traditions. The preservation of Shang hymns within a Zhou-era collection represents a particularly sophisticated example of cultural appropriation through ritual inclusion rather than suppression.
