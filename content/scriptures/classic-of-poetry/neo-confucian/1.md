Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Guofeng marks a revolutionary departure from the allegorical tradition of the Mao Commentary. Zhu Xi's Collected Commentary on the Poetry insists that many Guofeng poems should be read in their plain sense as expressions of authentic human emotion, including improper desire. This approach reflects the core Neo-Confucian distinction between the heavenly principle (tianli) and selfish human desire (renyu), making the Guofeng a practical textbook for moral discernment.

Zhu Xi controversially identified numerous Guofeng love poems as expressions of licentious desire (yinshi) rather than as political allegories. Far from dismissing these poems, he argued that they serve an essential pedagogical function: by presenting both virtuous and licentious emotions in their natural form, the anthology trains the reader to recognize and distinguish between feelings aligned with cosmic principle and those driven by selfish desire. This transformed the Guofeng from a collection of coded political commentary into a laboratory for moral self-examination.

The Neo-Confucian approach also emphasizes the investigation of things (gewu) through careful attention to the natural imagery of the Guofeng. Birds, insects, plants, and seasonal changes are understood not merely as poetic devices but as manifestations of cosmic pattern (li) that the attentive reader can apprehend. Cheng Yi taught that even a single poem could serve as a starting point for the investigation of principle, making the Guofeng an accessible entry into Neo-Confucian metaphysics.
