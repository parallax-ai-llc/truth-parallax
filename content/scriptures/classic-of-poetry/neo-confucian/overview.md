## Overview

Neo-Confucian interpreters approached the Classic of Poetry through the metaphysical framework of principle (li) and material force (qi), reading the poems as expressions of cosmic pattern manifest in human emotion. Zhu Xi's influential Collected Commentary on the Poetry (Shijing jizhuan) broke decisively with the allegorical tradition of the Mao Commentary, arguing that many poems should be understood as direct expressions of feeling — both virtuous and licentious — rather than as coded political allegories.

## Interpretive Approach

Zhu Xi's revolution in Shijing interpretation insisted on reading poems in their plain sense, acknowledging that some expressed improper desire and served as negative moral examples. This approach reflected the Neo-Confucian conviction that understanding human nature requires confronting both its heavenly endowment (tianli) and its selfish desires (renyu). The poems thus became a laboratory for moral discernment, training the reader to distinguish between feelings aligned with principle and those driven by selfish desire. Cheng Yi and other Song thinkers further connected poetic imagery to the investigation of things (gewu).

## Contemporary Relevance

The Neo-Confucian approach to the Shijing continues to influence discussions about the relationship between literature and moral philosophy. Its insistence on honest engagement with human emotion, rather than forced allegorization, resonates with modern hermeneutic approaches that seek to balance textual integrity with ethical reading.
