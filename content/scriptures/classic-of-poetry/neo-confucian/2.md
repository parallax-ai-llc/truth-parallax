Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Xiaoya applies the philosophical framework of principle (li) and material force (qi) to the court and banquet poems of the lesser aristocracy. Zhu Xi reads the Xiaoya as documenting the moral atmosphere of the Zhou court at a level of specificity that reveals how cosmic principle operates through — and is sometimes obscured by — the complexities of institutional life. The minor odes become a case study in how political virtue waxes and wanes within established structures.

The banquet poems of the Xiaoya are read through the Neo-Confucian lens of self-cultivation and communal harmony. Zhu Xi notes that the proper conduct of court gatherings reflects the alignment of human relationships with principle, while the deterioration of banquet decorum signals the intrusion of selfish desire into communal life. The poems that describe the pleasures of fellowship among virtuous officials illustrate what Neo-Confucians call the "joy of the Way" — a happiness rooted in moral cultivation rather than sensory gratification.

The political complaints within the Xiaoya receive careful Neo-Confucian attention as examples of the tension between the mind of the Dao (daoxin) and the human mind (renxin). Officials who lament governmental failings are seen as expressing the moral clarity of the Dao-mind struggling against the corruptions of institutional life. Neo-Confucian readers find in these poems confirmation that political engagement and moral cultivation are inseparable enterprises.
