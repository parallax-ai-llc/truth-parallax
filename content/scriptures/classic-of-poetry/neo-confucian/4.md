Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Song (Hymns) understands these sacrificial poems as the most direct connection between human ritual practice and the cosmic order that undergirds all of reality. The hymns, performed in the context of ancestral sacrifice, embody the Neo-Confucian understanding of ritual as the point where principle (li) and material force (qi) converge in structured human activity. The solemnity and formality of the Song reflect the gravity of aligning human action with heavenly pattern.

Zhu Xi read the Zhou hymns as expressing the founders' awareness that their political authority was grounded in moral and cosmic principle rather than in mere military conquest. The sacrificial context of these poems demonstrates that the sage-kings understood governance as a fundamentally spiritual undertaking, requiring continual renewal of the relationship between the human and the cosmic. For Neo-Confucians, the ancestral sacrifices celebrated in the Song are not superstitious rituals but refined practices for cultivating reverence (jing) — the attitude that keeps the mind attentive to principle.

The Neo-Confucian reading of the Song also engages with the metaphysics of sacrifice itself. Zhu Xi's treatment of ghosts and spirits (guishen) as the expansion and contraction of material force connects the Song's ritual world to the broader Neo-Confucian cosmology. The hymns become evidence that the ancient sages possessed an intuitive understanding of the relationship between the seen and unseen dimensions of reality that Neo-Confucian philosophy articulates systematically.
