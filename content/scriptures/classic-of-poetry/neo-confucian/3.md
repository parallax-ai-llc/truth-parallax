Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Daya engages deeply with the dynastic narratives as demonstrations of how cosmic principle (tianli) manifests in the founding and governance of political communities. The founding narratives of the Zhou dynasty are read not simply as historical accounts but as illustrations of the Neo-Confucian thesis that legitimate political order arises when rulers bring their governance into alignment with the principle inherent in all things. King Wen's virtue is understood as his complete realization of the principle within his own nature.

Zhu Xi and the Cheng brothers treated the Daya's articulation of the Mandate of Heaven with particular philosophical seriousness. The Mandate is understood through Neo-Confucian metaphysics as the expression of Heaven's principle (tianli) in the political realm. When a ruler's virtue aligns with principle, Heaven's mandate naturally confers authority; when a ruler's conduct departs from principle, the mandate is withdrawn. This reading integrates the Daya's political theology into the broader Neo-Confucian framework of moral cosmology, making political history a manifestation of metaphysical truth.

The Daya's warning poems about the decline of later Zhou kings serve the Neo-Confucian pedagogical purpose of illustrating how selfish desire (renyu) gradually obscures the heavenly principle even within outwardly magnificent institutions. Neo-Confucian readers find in these cautionary odes confirmation that constant moral vigilance — the effort to preserve and clarify principle — is the essential work of governance.
