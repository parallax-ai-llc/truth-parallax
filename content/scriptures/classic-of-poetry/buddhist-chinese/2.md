Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Xiaoya approaches the court and banquet poems as portraits of worldly existence within the realm of institutional power. The minor odes' depictions of court life — its pleasures, anxieties, rivalries, and disappointments — are read as illustrations of the Second Noble Truth: that suffering arises from craving and attachment, including the craving for status, recognition, and political influence that permeates the world of the Zhou aristocracy.

The banquet poems receive particular attention as depictions of the pleasures of samsara. Buddhist readers note that the convivial gatherings described in these poems — the wine, music, camaraderie, and formal exchanges of courtesy — represent precisely the kind of agreeable worldly experience that creates attachment and obscures the mind's capacity for deeper insight. The poems that describe the breakdown of banquet decorum and the failure of fellowship serve as reminders that even the most refined worldly pleasures are inherently unstable and subject to dissolution.

The Xiaoya's poems of political complaint and personal frustration are read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as expressions of the suffering that inevitably accompanies engagement with worldly affairs. The officials who lament governmental failures are seen as experiencing the particular suffering of those who invest their hopes and identities in institutions that are, by their nature, impermanent. Buddhist readers find in these laments confirmation that political life, however necessary, cannot provide the lasting peace that comes only from spiritual awakening.
