Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Daya reads the major odes' dynastic narratives through the lens of impermanence and the rise and fall of kingdoms as manifestations of collective karma. The founding of the Zhou dynasty, celebrated in these poems as the triumph of virtue over corruption, is understood by Buddhist readers as one moment in the endless cycle of political formation and dissolution that characterizes samsaric existence. Even the most glorious dynasty, founded by the most virtuous rulers, is subject to the universal law of impermanence.

The Daya's portraits of sage-kings are read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as depicting individuals who, while not possessing Buddhist awakening, exhibited qualities resonant with the bodhisattva ideal. King Wen's compassion for his people, his restraint in the use of force, and his cultivation of inner virtue parallel aspects of the bodhisattva path. Buddhist readers note, however, that the Confucian sage-king remains oriented toward worldly governance rather than liberation, placing this form of virtue within the realm of merit-making rather than wisdom-realization.

The Daya's warning poems about dynastic decline hold particular significance for Buddhist-Chinese readers as parables of the inevitable deterioration of all conditioned achievements. The poetic voice that mourns the fall from founding virtue to later corruption expresses, in Buddhist terms, the fundamental unsatisfactoriness (dukkha) of relying on worldly accomplishments for lasting fulfillment. These poems point, perhaps inadvertently, toward the need for a form of cultivation that transcends the political realm.
