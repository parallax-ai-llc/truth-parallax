## Overview

Chinese Buddhist engagement with the Classic of Poetry reflects the broader project of harmonizing imported Buddhist teachings with indigenous Chinese literary culture. Buddhist monks and scholars who were immersed in classical education naturally read the Shijing through the lens of Buddhist concepts such as impermanence (wuchang), attachment (zhizhuo), and the conditioned nature of emotional experience. The poems' pervasive themes of longing, separation, and the passage of seasons offered rich material for Buddhist reflection on the nature of suffering and desire.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist-Chinese readers of the Shijing draw attention to the poems' portrayal of emotional attachment and its consequences. Love poems that Confucian commentators read as allegories of political loyalty are understood by Buddhist readers as illustrations of how desire binds sentient beings to the cycle of suffering. The seasonal imagery is read as a meditation on impermanence, while the folk songs' depictions of hardship and loss become occasions for reflecting on the First Noble Truth. At the same time, Buddhist interpreters appreciate the Shijing's use of poetic language as a form of skillful means (upaya), recognizing the power of literary art to awaken insight.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Buddhist-Chinese reading of the Shijing continues to enrich the dialogue between contemplative traditions and literary studies. Its attention to the emotional textures of poetic language and its sensitivity to themes of impermanence and longing offer distinctive perspectives on the intersection of aesthetics and spiritual practice.
