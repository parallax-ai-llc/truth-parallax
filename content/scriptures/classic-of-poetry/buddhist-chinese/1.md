Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Guofeng reads these folk songs through the lens of Buddhist psychology, finding in their expressions of love, longing, and loss vivid illustrations of how attachment (upadana) generates suffering (dukkha). The love poems that dominate the Guofeng become case studies in the mechanics of desire: the yearning for an absent beloved, the pain of separation, the anxiety of uncertainty — all are understood as manifestations of the fundamental craving (tanha) that binds sentient beings to the cycle of samsara.

The seasonal imagery of the Guofeng carries special resonance for Buddhist-Chinese readers. The falling leaves, flowing rivers, and changing weather that pervade these poems serve as natural meditations on impermanence (anicca). Where Confucian readers find in seasonal imagery metaphors for moral qualities, and Daoist readers perceive the spontaneous creativity of the Dao, Buddhist readers encounter the ceaseless arising and passing away of all conditioned phenomena. Each poem becomes an occasion for reflecting on the transient nature of worldly experience.

Buddhist interpreters also appreciate the Guofeng as a form of skillful means (upaya). The poems' emotional power can be redirected from the reinforcement of attachment to the cultivation of insight. By engaging deeply with the expressed emotions and then recognizing their impermanent and conditioned nature, the reader practices the Buddhist discipline of observing mental states without being enslaved by them. The folk songs thus become unexpected instruments of contemplative training.
