Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Song (Hymns) engages thoughtfully with the sacrificial poetry as an expression of the universal human impulse to connect with dimensions of reality beyond the mundane. While Buddhist doctrine rejects the efficacy of animal sacrifice and the Brahmanical assumptions underlying much sacrificial practice, Chinese Buddhist readers approach the Zhou temple hymns with a degree of sympathetic understanding, recognizing in them a genuine aspiration toward transcendence that Buddhism redirects toward awakening.

The hymns' invocation of ancestral spirits provides a point of contact with Buddhist teachings on the realms of existence. Buddhist-Chinese readers relate the Shijing's ancestral world to the Buddhist cosmology of the six realms, understanding the continued influence of ancestors as consistent with the Buddhist teaching that beings in various realms of existence interact and influence one another. The merit generated through sacrificial practice is understood through the Buddhist framework of merit transfer, connecting the Song's ritual world to Buddhist practices of dedicating merit to deceased relatives.

The aesthetic dimension of the Song — the solemn beauty of the hymnic language, the integration of word, music, and movement — is appreciated by Buddhist-Chinese readers as a form of mindful practice. The concentrated attention required to perform or witness these ceremonies parallels the meditative focus cultivated in Buddhist practice. The hymns thus represent, in Buddhist perspective, a culturally specific form of contemplative discipline that, while not leading to full awakening, cultivates qualities of reverence, attention, and moral seriousness that support spiritual development.
