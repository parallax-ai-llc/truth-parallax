## Overview

Islamic engagement with 1 Thessalonians focuses on areas of convergence and divergence between Pauline teaching and Qur'anic revelation. The letter's eschatological themes — the return of the Lord, resurrection of the dead, and divine judgment — find significant parallels in Islamic theology, though the Christological framework differs fundamentally.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Second Coming of Isa
Islam affirms the return of Isa (Jesus) at the end of days, a belief with interesting parallels to 1 Thessalonians 4:16. However, Islamic theology holds that Isa was not crucified or killed (Qur'an 4:157) and therefore did not rise from the dead, creating a fundamental divergence from Paul's teaching.

### Resurrection and Judgment
The themes of bodily resurrection and divine judgment in 1 Thessalonians (4:13-5:11) resonate strongly with Qur'anic teaching on *yawm al-qiyamah* (the Day of Resurrection). Islamic scholars note these shared eschatological convictions while rejecting the Pauline framework of salvation through Christ's death.

### Ethical Monotheism
The call to turn from idols to serve the living God (1:9) aligns with the Islamic principle of *tawhid* (the absolute oneness of God) and the rejection of *shirk* (associating partners with God). Muslim scholars, however, view the later Christian doctrine of the Trinity as itself a form of *shirk*.
