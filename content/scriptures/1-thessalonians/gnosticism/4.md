## Living to Please God and the Coming of the Lord

Paul's call to sanctification (4:3-5) is read by Gnostic interpreters not as mere moral regulation but as the discipline of separating the pneumatic self from entanglement with the hylic body. The "lustful passion" of the Gentiles "who do not know God" (4:5) describes the condition of hylic humanity, those so deeply embedded in material existence that they have no awareness of the divine spark within them. Sanctification in the Gnostic sense is the progressive purification of consciousness, stripping away the layers of archontic conditioning that keep the soul identified with flesh rather than with its true luminous nature.

The instruction to "aspire to live quietly" (4:11) reflects the Gnostic ideal of withdrawal from the turbulence of the material world. The pneumatic does not seek worldly acclaim or power, which belong to the domain of the archons, but cultivates inner stillness where gnosis can deepen. Working with one's hands is not glorification of labor but a strategy of inconspicuousness, avoiding the attention of both human authorities (agents of the Demiurge's social order) and the cosmic powers that target those who shine too brightly with the inner light.

The dramatic vision of the Lord descending with "the voice of an archangel and the trumpet of God" (4:16) and believers "caught up in the clouds" (4:17) is understood not as a literal future event but as the moment of pneumatic liberation. The "dead in Christ" who rise first are those who have already shed their material bodies and await the final dissolution of the cosmic prison. Being "caught up" signifies the ascent of the liberated spark through the archontic spheres, passing each checkpoint of the planetary rulers, until it reaches the Pleroma. The promise that "we will always be with the Lord" (4:17) is the assurance of permanent reunion with the divine fullness, beyond the cycle of incarnation imposed by the Demiurge.
