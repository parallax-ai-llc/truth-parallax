## Overview

Jewish scholarship on 1 Thessalonians focuses on the letter's place in the earliest history of the parting of the ways between Judaism and Christianity. The controversial passage in 2:14-16, which speaks of Jews who "killed the Lord Jesus" and "displease God and oppose all mankind," has been a focal point for discussions of anti-Judaism in the New Testament.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Anti-Judaism Question
Jewish scholars critically engage with 2:14-16, which has been used throughout history to justify antisemitism. Some scholars argue the passage is a later interpolation; others see it as reflecting intra-Jewish polemic similar to that found in the Dead Sea Scrolls and prophetic literature.

### Eschatological Parallels
The eschatological expectations in 1 Thessalonians — resurrection of the dead, divine judgment, the coming of the Messiah — have clear roots in Second Temple Jewish apocalypticism. Jewish scholars trace these concepts to Daniel, 1 Enoch, and other Jewish apocalyptic texts.

### Paul's Jewish Identity
Jewish readers note that Paul's language, metaphors, and theological categories remain deeply Jewish even as he articulates a distinctive messianic faith. The letter offers a window into the diversity of first-century Judaism.
