## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads this section of the Matsya Purana through the non-dual lens, understanding its theological content as a provisional (vyavaharika) description of reality that ultimately points to the attributeless Brahman (nirguna Brahman). Principles of temple construction, site selection, and architectural design — these themes are understood as descriptions of the phenomenal world that, while valid at the conventional level, are transcended in the ultimate realization of non-duality.

For the Advaitin, the narratives and doctrines presented in this section serve a pedagogical function — they prepare the aspirant for the higher knowledge (para-vidya) by cultivating devotion, ethical conduct, and intellectual discrimination. The cosmological and theological content is read through the method of adhyaropa-apavada: attributes are first superimposed on Brahman for the sake of instruction, then negated to reveal the truth beyond all characterization.

The Advaita reading ultimately points the reader beyond the text's sectarian framework to the direct realization that atman (the individual self) is identical with Brahman (the ultimate reality).

## Key Themes

- **Vivarta**: The phenomenal world described here as apparent, not real, transformation of Brahman
- **Maya**: The cosmic illusion that creates the appearance of multiplicity and divine personality
- **Adhyaropa-Apavada**: Superimposition and negation as the hermeneutical method for reading theological claims
- **Saguna Brahman**: The personal deity described in this section as Brahman with attributes
- **Jivanmukti**: The possibility of liberation while still engaging with the phenomenal world described in the text

## Key Verses and Passages

### Non-Dual Reading

Advaitins read this section as describing the phenomenal (vyavaharika) level of reality. The theological claims, while valid at their own level, are understood as stepping stones toward the realization that there is only one non-dual reality.

### Pedagogical Value

The devotional and theological content serves as upasana (meditation practice) that purifies the mind and prepares it for the direct knowledge of Brahman that constitutes liberation.

## Commentary Tradition

Sridhara Swami's commentarial approach provides the most influential Advaita reading of Puranic material like this section. His method maintains genuine devotional engagement while interpreting the theological content through Shankara's non-dual framework, demonstrating that bhakti and jnana are ultimately complementary rather than contradictory paths.
