## Pride as the Root Passion

Sirach 10's declaration that "pride is the beginning of sin" (10:13) aligns with the Orthodox patristic analysis of the passions. Evagrius listed pride (*hyperēphania*) as the last and most dangerous of the eight principal thoughts, while John Climacus treated it as the crown of all vices. The entire ascetical struggle aims at uprooting this fundamental distortion of the human relationship with God.

## God Overthrows the Proud

The theme of divine reversal — God casting down the mighty and exalting the humble (10:14-18) — is sung daily in the Orthodox liturgy through the Magnificat and the Song of Hannah. The Orthodox vision of history is shaped by this conviction: earthly power is transient, and God's purposes ultimately prevail through the humble and the faithful.

## True Honor Before God

The chapter's conclusion that the person who fears the Lord, regardless of social status, deserves true honor (10:19-31) reflects the Orthodox theology of personhood. Every human being bears the image of God, and genuine dignity derives not from wealth or rank but from the relationship with God — a conviction that grounds Orthodox social ethics.
