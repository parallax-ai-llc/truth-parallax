## The Inescapability of Divine Judgment

Sirach 16 warns that no one is hidden from God's sight (16:17). Orthodox theology affirms divine judgment while holding it in tension with divine mercy. The Orthodox funeral service prays for mercy precisely because judgment is real; the two are not contradictory but complementary aspects of God's love.

## Lessons from Sacred History

The chapter's survey of God's judgments — the flood, Sodom, the Canaanites (16:7-10) — parallels the Orthodox liturgical practice of commemorating sacred history throughout the year. The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete, chanted during Great Lent, similarly reviews salvation history as a mirror for repentance.

## Creation's Order as Revelation

The closing meditation on creation's order (16:24-30) supports the Orthodox understanding of the natural world as a theophany — a revelation of God's wisdom. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the *logoi* of creation reflect the divine Logos, so that contemplation of nature (*theōria physikē*) becomes a path to knowledge of God.
