## The Incomprehensibility of God

Sirach 18's doxology to divine transcendence (18:1-7) resonates with the Orthodox apophatic tradition. St. Gregory of Nyssa taught that God's infinity means the soul's journey into God is never completed — even in eternity, there is always more of God to discover. Sirach's acknowledgment that God's works are beyond human comprehension is the starting point of Orthodox mystical theology.

## The Philanthropy of God

Despite His transcendence, God is moved to compassion by human frailty (18:11-14). The Orthodox liturgy constantly addresses God as *philanthrōpos* (lover of humankind), holding together transcendence and intimacy. The Incarnation is the supreme expression of this divine condescension — God becoming human so that humans might become divine.

## The Discipline of Preparation

The practical counsel on self-examination and preparation (18:19-33) aligns with the Orthodox practice of preparing for Holy Communion through fasting, prayer, confession, and the reading of preparatory prayers. The spiritual life requires attentiveness and planning, not merely spontaneous impulse.
