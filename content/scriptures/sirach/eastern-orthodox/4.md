## Compassion as Christlikeness

Sirach 4:1-10 commands care for the poor with prophetic urgency. Orthodox theology reads these imperatives through the lens of Matthew 25:31-46 — what is done for the least is done for Christ. The Orthodox tradition of philanthropic institutions — hospitals, orphanages, hospices — flowing from the early Byzantine period embodies Sirach's social ethic.

## Wisdom as Divine Pedagogy

In 4:11-19, Wisdom tests and disciplines her children before exalting them. Orthodox spiritual theology calls this divine pedagogy (*paideia*) — the process by which God educates the soul through trials, purification, and illumination. The three stages of the spiritual life articulated by Evagrius Ponticus and Maximus the Confessor — purification, illumination, and union — elaborate this fundamental pattern.

## Shame and Repentance

The distinction between destructive and constructive shame (4:20-31) informs the Orthodox practice of confession. The shame that leads to repentance (*metanoia*) is healing; the shame that leads to hiding from God (as in Eden) is destructive. The sacrament of confession provides a space where shame is transformed into healing through the mercy of God.
