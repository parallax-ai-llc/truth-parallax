## Divine Providence

Sirach 11's affirmation that both prosperity and adversity come from the Lord (11:14) expresses the Orthodox trust in divine providence. The Orthodox prayer tradition — epitomized in the Jesus Prayer — cultivates a disposition of surrender to God's will in all circumstances, whether favorable or adverse.

## Patience and the Long View

The counsel against hasty judgment (11:7-13) supports the Orthodox virtue of patience (*makrothymia*). The Orthodox liturgical year, with its cycles of fasting and feasting, trains the faithful in the long view — the capacity to endure present difficulty in light of the eschatological hope.

## Detachment from Wealth

The warning against trusting in riches (11:14-28) resonates with the Orthodox tradition of *aktēmosynē* (non-possessiveness). The monastic vow of poverty embodies this detachment in its most radical form, but even lay Orthodox are called to hold material goods lightly, understanding them as gifts entrusted for stewardship rather than ultimate goods.
