## The Social Effects of Wealth

Sirach 13's unflinching portrayal of how wealth distorts relationships resonates with the Orthodox patristic tradition of social criticism. St. Basil the Great thundered against the hoarding of wealth: "The bread in your cupboard belongs to the hungry; the cloak in your wardrobe belongs to the naked." The Fathers read Sirach's social observations as prophetic diagnosis of a society distorted by greed.

## The Dignity of the Poor

The chapter's observation that the poor person's wise speech is ignored while the rich person's folly is applauded (13:23) challenges the Orthodox community to practice genuine equality before God. The liturgical assembly, where rich and poor stand side by side, is meant to embody the eschatological reversal that Sirach anticipates and the gospel proclaims.

## Solidarity in Christ

Orthodox social theology insists that in Christ there is "neither slave nor free" (Galatians 3:28). Sirach 13's realistic portrait of economic inequality serves as a prophetic call to the Church to be a community where the dynamics of wealth and power are transformed by the love of Christ.
