## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church includes Sirach among the canonical books of the Old Testament, following the Septuagint tradition. The Fathers of the Church quoted Sirach extensively, and it remains a significant text in Orthodox liturgical life, moral instruction, and theological reflection — particularly in the development of Sophia theology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Foundation
The Greek Fathers — Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Basil the Great, and John Chrysostom — cited Sirach frequently, treating it as authoritative Scripture. Clement's *Paedagogus* draws extensively on Sirach's ethical teachings for Christian formation.

### Sophia and the Logos
Sirach's personification of Wisdom (especially chapter 24) contributed to the Orthodox theological tradition of identifying Wisdom with the Logos — the pre-incarnate Christ. This connection is embodied in the great churches dedicated to Hagia Sophia (Holy Wisdom).

### Liturgical Integration
Sirach's texts appear in Orthodox liturgical readings, particularly during feast days celebrating wisdom, creation, and the saints. The book's praise of the ancestors (chs. 44-50) resonates with the Orthodox veneration of the saints.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: The pursuit of wisdom as participation in divine life, leading to deification
- **Ascetic Life**: Sirach's call to self-discipline and mastery of the passions supports the hesychastic tradition
- **Fear and Love**: The progression from fear of the Lord to love of God, as articulated by the Philokalic fathers
- **Creation Theology**: God's glory revealed through the natural order (chs. 42-43)
