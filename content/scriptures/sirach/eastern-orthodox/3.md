## The Commandment to Honor Parents

Sirach 3:1-16 extends the fifth commandment with practical detail and spiritual depth. Orthodox moral theology emphasizes the obligation to honor parents as foundational to all social ethics. The promise that honoring parents brings forgiveness (3:3) is understood within the Orthodox framework of synergy — human effort cooperating with divine grace to bring about healing and restoration.

## Humility and the Limits of Knowledge

The call to humility (3:17-24) — "Seek not what is too difficult for you" — resonates with the Orthodox apophatic tradition: the recognition that God ultimately transcends all human understanding. The Cappadocian Fathers and Pseudo-Dionysius developed this insight into a systematic theology of divine unknowability that preserves both intellectual humility and genuine knowledge of God through His energies.

## Almsgiving as Spiritual Medicine

The teaching that almsgiving atones for sin (3:30) is read in Orthodox theology as part of the broader practice of mercy (*eleēmosynē*) that heals the soul. Almsgiving is not a transaction but a medicine — a practice that both relieves the suffering of others and cures the giver's attachment to material possessions.
