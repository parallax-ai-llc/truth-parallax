## Wisdom as Divine Attribute

Sirach 1 declares that all wisdom originates with the Lord and abides with Him forever (1:1). The Orthodox Church reads this as a statement about the divine Logos, through whom all things were made and in whom all wisdom subsists. St. Clement of Alexandria, in his *Stromateis*, drew on this tradition to argue that all genuine wisdom — whether found among Greeks or barbarians — ultimately derives from the one divine source.

## The Fear of the Lord and Theosis

The fourfold description of the fear of the Lord as beginning, fullness, crown, and root of wisdom (1:11-20) maps onto the Orthodox understanding of the spiritual life as a progressive journey toward *theosis* (deification). Fear is the beginning — the awakening of the soul to God's holiness — but the goal is union with God, where fear is perfected in love.

## Patience as Spiritual Discipline

The chapter's closing counsel on patience and self-control (1:22-30) resonates with the Orthodox ascetical tradition. The Philokalic fathers taught that patience (*hypomonē*) is not passive resignation but active endurance — the soul's participation in Christ's own patient suffering, leading to transformation.
