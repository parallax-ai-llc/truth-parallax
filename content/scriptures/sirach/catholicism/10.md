## The Responsibilities of Authority

Sirach 10 opens with the principle that a wise ruler educates his people (10:1-3). Catholic social teaching affirms the positive role of government in promoting the common good and educating citizens in virtue. The chapter's vision of governance as a form of service rather than domination aligns with the Church's teaching on authority as stewardship.

## Pride as the Root of Sin

The declaration that "pride is the beginning of sin" (10:13) echoes the Augustinian tradition that identifies *superbia* (pride) as the root of all sin — the original temptation in Eden. Catholic moral theology treats pride as the first and most dangerous of the seven capital sins, from which all others flow.

## The Exaltation of the Humble

Verses 14-18 describe God overthrowing the proud and exalting the humble — the same dynamic celebrated in the Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55), which the Church prays daily in the Liturgy of the Hours at Vespers. Catholic devotion to Mary is inseparable from this theme of humble exaltation that Sirach proclaims.
