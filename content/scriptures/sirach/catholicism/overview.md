## Overview

The Catholic Church regards Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) as fully canonical, affirmed at the Councils of Hippo (393), Carthage (397), and definitively at the Council of Trent (1546). The book's title "Ecclesiasticus" — "the Church's book" — reflects its extensive use in catechesis, liturgy, and moral formation throughout Catholic history.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Deuterocanonical Authority
Catholic theology treats Sirach on equal footing with the protocanonical books. Its teachings on almsgiving, honoring parents, humility, and the fear of the Lord carry full doctrinal weight and appear frequently in the Catechism.

### Moral Theology and Virtue
Sirach's comprehensive ethical instruction maps naturally onto Catholic moral theology. The book's treatment of prudence, temperance, justice, and fortitude provides a biblical foundation for the cardinal virtues.

### Liturgical Tradition
Sirach is read extensively in the Roman Lectionary, especially in the weekday readings of Ordinary Time. Its passages on wisdom, creation, and the praise of ancestors enrich the liturgical year.

## Theological Emphases

- **Wisdom and Torah**: The identification of Wisdom with the law (24:23) prefigures the Catholic understanding of Scripture and Tradition
- **Merit and Almsgiving**: Sirach 3:30 ("almsgiving atones for sins") supports Catholic teaching on meritorious works
- **Honoring Parents**: Extensive teaching on filial piety (3:1-16) informs Catholic family ethics
- **The Praise of Ancestors**: The heroes of faith (chs. 44-50) model the communion of saints
