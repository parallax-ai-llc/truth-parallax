## The Fourth Commandment Expanded

Sirach 3:1-16 offers the most detailed meditation on honoring parents found in all of Scripture. Catholic moral theology cites these verses extensively in its treatment of the fourth commandment. The promise that honoring one's father atones for sins (3:3) and caring for parents "stores up treasure" (3:4) supports the Catholic understanding of meritorious works performed in the state of grace.

## The Virtue of Humility

Verses 17-24 present humility as essential to wisdom: "The greater you are, the more you must humble yourself" (3:18). Catholic spiritual theology connects this to the Benedictine and Franciscan traditions of humility as the foundation of the spiritual life. St. Bernard's twelve degrees of humility draw on the same tradition that Sirach represents.

## Almsgiving Atones for Sin

The declaration that "almsgiving atones for sins" (3:30) carries special weight in Catholic theology. The Council of Trent cited this and similar passages in affirming the meritorious character of good works performed in grace. The Catechism teaches that almsgiving is one of the chief forms of penance, alongside prayer and fasting.
