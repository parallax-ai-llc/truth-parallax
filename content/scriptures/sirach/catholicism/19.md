## Temperance and Self-Control

Sirach 19 opens with warnings about wine and lust leading to ruin (19:1-3). Catholic moral theology treats these under the virtue of temperance — the cardinal virtue that moderates the attraction of pleasures and ensures balance in the use of created goods. The Catechism identifies temperance as ensuring "the will's mastery over instincts."

## The Sin of Detraction

The chapter's teaching on gossip — "Have you heard something? Let it die with you" (19:10) — informs the Catholic understanding of detraction and calumny as sins against the eighth commandment. The obligation to protect another's reputation, even when the information is true (the sin of detraction as distinct from calumny), finds scriptural support in Sirach's rigorous ethic of speech.

## True Wisdom and the Fear of the Lord

The concluding distinction between cleverness in evil and true wisdom (19:20-30) reinforces the Catholic conviction that moral virtue and intellectual excellence must go together. Knowledge without virtue is not wisdom but cunning — an insight that shapes the Catholic intellectual tradition from Augustine through the modern Catholic university.
