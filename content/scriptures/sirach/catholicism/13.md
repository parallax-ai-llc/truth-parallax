## The Social Dynamics of Wealth

Sirach 13 observes with penetrating realism how wealth distorts human relationships: "What peace between a hyena and a dog? And what peace between the rich and the poor?" (13:18). Catholic social teaching draws on this tradition of social analysis in its critique of economic inequality. The social encyclicals — from Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* to Francis's *Fratelli Tutti* — continue Sirach's concern for the exploitation of the poor by the powerful.

## Structural Sin

The chapter's description of how the rich exploit the poor (13:3-7, 18-23) anticipates the Catholic theological category of "structural sin" — social arrangements that systematically disadvantage the vulnerable. Liberation theologians have drawn on this biblical tradition to analyze contemporary economic injustice.

## Solidarity with the Poor

Catholic teaching insists on solidarity (*solidaritas*) with the poor as a moral imperative. Sirach 13's unflinching portrait of economic exploitation serves as a prophetic call that the Church continues to amplify, most recently in Pope Francis's emphasis on the "peripheries" and the victims of a "throwaway culture."
