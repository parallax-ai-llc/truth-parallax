## Hokhmah and Yirat Hashem

Sirach 1 opens with the declaration that all wisdom (*hokhmah*) comes from the Lord (1:1), echoing Proverbs 2:6. For Jewish readers, this chapter establishes the central axiom of the entire book: wisdom is inseparable from *yirat Hashem* (the fear of the Lord). The fourfold description of the fear of God as beginning, fullness, crown, and root (1:11-20) goes beyond the canonical formulations in Proverbs, offering a systematic theology of reverence that later rabbinic thought would develop.

## A Pre-Rabbinic Voice

Ben Sira wrote around 180 BCE — after the close of the canonical period but before the full flowering of rabbinic Judaism. His voice represents the world of Second Temple wisdom, where scribal piety, Temple worship, and Torah study coexisted in a unified vision. Jewish scholars value Sirach as a window into this formative period.

## Patience and Mussar

The chapter's closing counsel on patience and self-control (1:22-30) anticipates the Jewish *mussar* (ethical self-improvement) tradition. The 19th-century Mussar movement, founded by Rabbi Israel Salanter, systematized precisely the kind of character formation that Ben Sira commends — the disciplined cultivation of virtues rooted in the fear of God.
