## Overview

Though Sirach was ultimately excluded from the Jewish canon, it occupied a remarkably important place in Jewish intellectual history. The book was composed in Hebrew by a Jerusalem sage around 180 BCE, making it one of the last examples of Second Temple Jewish wisdom literature. Fragments were discovered in the Cairo Genizah (1896) and at Masada and Qumran, confirming that the Hebrew text circulated widely in Jewish communities for centuries.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Non-Canonical but Respected
The Talmud (Sanhedrin 100b) records a debate about whether Sirach may be read; while it was excluded from the canon, the rabbis quoted its maxims with approval. Several of its sayings entered rabbinic discourse without attribution.

### Torah and Wisdom
Ben Sira's identification of Wisdom with Torah (chapter 24) represents a pivotal moment in Jewish intellectual history: the fusion of the international wisdom tradition with the particular revelation of the Mosaic covenant.

### Hebrew Text Recovery
The discovery of approximately two-thirds of the Hebrew text in the Cairo Genizah by Solomon Schechter (1896) was a landmark event. These manuscripts confirmed that later rabbinic citations reflected genuine knowledge of the original Hebrew.

## Theological Emphases

- **Yirat Hashem**: The fear of the Lord as the foundation and crown of wisdom
- **Torah Study**: Wisdom as inseparable from the study and observance of Torah
- **Practical Ethics**: Detailed guidance on friendship, speech, wealth, and family life
- **Priestly Piety**: The climactic praise of the High Priest Simon II (ch. 50) reflects pre-Maccabean Temple devotion
