## Gaavah: The Sin of Pride

Sirach 10's denunciation of pride as "the beginning of sin" (10:13) aligns with the Jewish tradition's deep suspicion of *gaavah* (arrogance). The Talmud (Sotah 5a) teaches that God says of the arrogant person: "He and I cannot dwell in the same world." Maimonides, departing from his usual advocacy of the golden mean, counseled extreme humility as the only appropriate response to God's greatness.

## Divine Sovereignty over Nations

The chapter's theme of God overthrowing the proud and exalting the humble (10:14-18) echoes the Jewish theology of divine sovereignty over history. The liturgical prayer *Alenu le-Shabeach* proclaims God's ultimate kingship over all nations — the same conviction that grounds Ben Sira's observations about the rise and fall of rulers.

## True Kavod

The conclusion that the one who fears the Lord deserves true honor (*kavod*), regardless of social status (10:19-31), reflects the rabbinic principle that Torah knowledge, not wealth or lineage, confers genuine dignity. Pirkei Avot 4:1 asks: "Who is honored? The one who honors others."
