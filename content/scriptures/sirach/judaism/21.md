## Fleeing from Sin

The command to "flee from sin as from a snake" (21:1-2) echoes the rabbinic counsel to distance oneself from transgression. The Mishnah (Avot 2:4) teaches: "Do not trust in yourself until the day of your death" — a vigilance against sin that Ben Sira's vivid imagery reinforces.

## The Hakham and the Kesil

The contrast between the wise (*hakham*) and the fool (*kesil*) in 21:11-28 continues the canonical tradition of Proverbs and Ecclesiastes. The rabbinic tradition elaborates: the wise person speaks little and does much, while the fool speaks much and does little (Avot 1:15). Ben Sira's images — the spring versus the broken jar — give vivid expression to this fundamental distinction.

## Torah as the Straight Path

The image of the path of sinners as smooth but ending in Sheol (21:10) connects to the rabbinic *mashal* (parable) of the two paths. Jewish tradition consistently teaches that the path of Torah, though demanding, leads to life, while the path of ease leads to destruction — the same moral geography that Ben Sira maps.
