## Hashgachah Pratit: Divine Providence

Sirach 11's affirmation that both good and evil come from God (11:14) connects to the Jewish concept of *hashgachah pratit* (individual divine providence). The Talmud (Berakhot 60b) teaches: "A person is obligated to bless God for the bad just as for the good." This radical trust in providence — even when circumstances are adverse — is a cornerstone of Jewish piety.

## Patience and Restraint

The counsel against hasty judgment and rash intervention (11:7-13) reflects the rabbinic virtue of patience. Pirkei Avot 2:4 teaches: "Do not judge your fellow until you have reached his place." Ben Sira's practical wisdom about waiting and listening before acting anticipates this rabbinic principle of restraint.

## The Uncertainty of Wealth

The warning that fortune can reverse in a single day (11:14-28) resonates with the Jewish awareness of life's fragility, expressed in the Talmudic teaching that one should repent one day before death — and since one does not know when death will come, one should repent every day (Shabbat 153a).
