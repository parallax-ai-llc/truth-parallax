## Tzelem Elohim: The Divine Image

Sirach 17's account of humanity's creation echoes Genesis 1-2 and develops the concept of *tzelem Elohim* (the image of God). Jewish theology derives from this teaching the infinite value of every human life — "Whoever saves a single life is as if he saved an entire world" (Mishnah Sanhedrin 4:5). Ben Sira's emphasis on human dignity, reason, and moral capacity reflects this foundational anthropology.

## Matan Torah: The Giving of the Torah

The description of Sinai (17:11-14) connects to the central Jewish narrative of *matan Torah* — the giving of the Torah at Sinai, which the tradition regards as the defining event of Jewish history. Ben Sira emphasizes both the glory of the revelation and the commandment to "beware of all evil" — the ethical content of the covenant.

## Teshuvah: The Call to Return

The urgent call to repentance (17:25-32) resonates with the prophetic summons that structures Jewish liturgical life, especially during the Days of Awe (Rosh Hashanah through Yom Kippur). The reminder that the dead cannot praise God reinforces the urgency of repentance in this life — the same urgency that drives the Yom Kippur liturgy.
