## Jewish Engagement with Ben Sira Chapter 47

Chapter 47 of Ben Sira (Sefer Ben Sira), "David, Solomon, and Rehoboam," contains 25 verses. Although Ben Sira was excluded from the Hebrew canon — the Talmud (Sanhedrin 100b) records debate about whether it may be read — the book occupies a unique position in Jewish literary and intellectual history. Fragments discovered in the Cairo Genizah (1896) and at Masada (1964) demonstrate that the Hebrew text continued to be copied and read well into late antiquity.

## Rabbinic and Medieval Reception

The treatment of David as liturgist and Solomon as wise king resonates with Talmudic tradition that attributes Psalms to David and debates Solomon's sin (Shabbat 56b). Ben Sira's assessment represents an important early witness.

## Significance in Jewish Thought

Ben Sira stands at a critical juncture in Jewish intellectual history, bridging the biblical wisdom tradition and rabbinic literature. This chapter's teachings on Israel's heroes and covenant faithfulness anticipate themes developed extensively in Pirkei Avot and the broader Talmudic literature. Modern Jewish scholars such as Moshe Tzvi Segal and the discoveries of additional Hebrew manuscripts have renewed scholarly and communal interest in this text as a witness to Second Temple Jewish piety and thought.
