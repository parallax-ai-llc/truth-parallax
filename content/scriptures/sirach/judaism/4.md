## Justice for the Poor

Sirach 4:1-10 commands care for the poor with prophetic urgency. Jewish law codifies the obligations of tzedakah extensively: Maimonides' eight levels of charity (*Mishneh Torah*, Hilkhot Matanot Aniyim 10:7-14) systematize the kind of compassionate giving that Ben Sira demands. The highest level — enabling the poor person to become self-sufficient — reflects the same concern for dignity that Sirach expresses.

## Hokhmah as Teacher

In 4:11-19, Wisdom disciplines and tests her students before revealing her secrets. This pedagogy resonates with the rabbinic model of Torah study: the student submits to rigorous discipline under a master, endures difficulty, and is gradually initiated into deeper understanding. The Talmud (Berakhot 63b) teaches that Torah is acquired only through hardship.

## Bushah: Constructive and Destructive Shame

The chapter's treatment of shame (4:20-31) connects to the Jewish ethical concept of *bushah*. The Talmud (Berakhot 12b) distinguishes between the shame that leads to repentance (*teshuvah*) and the shame that destroys — a distinction Ben Sira makes with characteristic precision.
