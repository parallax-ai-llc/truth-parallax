## Jewish Engagement with Ben Sira Chapter 35

Chapter 35 of Ben Sira (Sefer Ben Sira), "Sacrifices and Divine Justice," contains 24 verses. Although Ben Sira was excluded from the Hebrew canon — the Talmud (Sanhedrin 100b) records debate about whether it may be read — the book occupies a unique position in Jewish literary and intellectual history. Fragments discovered in the Cairo Genizah (1896) and at Masada (1964) demonstrate that the Hebrew text continued to be copied and read well into late antiquity.

## Rabbinic and Medieval Reception

The insistence that sacrifice without justice is worthless echoes the prophetic tradition (Isaiah 1:11-17, Amos 5:21-24) and anticipates the rabbinic teaching that acts of lovingkindness replace sacrifices (Avot de-Rabbi Natan 4:5).

## Significance in Jewish Thought

Ben Sira stands at a critical juncture in Jewish intellectual history, bridging the biblical wisdom tradition and rabbinic literature. This chapter's teachings on ethics, social conduct, and the fear of God anticipate themes developed extensively in Pirkei Avot and the broader Talmudic literature. Modern Jewish scholars such as Moshe Tzvi Segal and the discoveries of additional Hebrew manuscripts have renewed scholarly and communal interest in this text as a witness to Second Temple Jewish piety and thought.
