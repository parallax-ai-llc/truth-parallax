## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 31

Sirach chapter 31, "Temperance and Table Manners," with 31 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The temperance teachings operate at the psychic level — moral discipline necessary for those not yet awakened to gnosis. The Valentinian distinction between the pneumatic (who transcends rules), the psychic (who needs them), and the hylic (who cannot follow them) provides the framework for reading such practical counsel.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 31 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
