## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 27

Sirach chapter 27, "Sin, Foolishness, and Retribution," with 30 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The testing of character through speech is reinterpreted through the Gnostic lens of the three natures — pneumatic (spiritual), psychic (soulish), and hylic (material). The tongue reveals one's essential nature, and retribution is not divine justice but the natural consequence of remaining trapped in material ignorance.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 27 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
