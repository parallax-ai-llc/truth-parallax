## Prayer to the True Father

Sirach 23's opening prayer — "O Lord, Father and Master of my life" (23:1) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as an address to the true Father beyond the Demiurge. The intimate address "Father" connects to the Gnostic practice of invoking the unknown God through names and formulas revealed in the secret tradition. The *Apocryphon of John* preserves elaborate invocations of the transcendent Father.

## The Power of Names

The chapter's treatment of oaths (23:7-15) connects to the Gnostic theology of sacred names. In Gnostic practice, knowledge of the true names of the aeons and archons grants power over them. The misuse of divine names is dangerous not merely because it offends God but because names carry actual spiritual power.

## The Body and Its Desires

The sexual ethics passage (23:16-27) is engaged diversely in Gnostic traditions. Ascetic Gnostics read it as confirming the need to transcend bodily desire entirely — the body is a prison, and its desires are chains. Libertine Gnostics argued the opposite: since the body is irrelevant to the pneumatic self, physical acts carry no spiritual weight. Both readings subvert Ben Sira's conventional moral framework.
