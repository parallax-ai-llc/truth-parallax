## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 28

Sirach chapter 28, "Forgiveness and the Power of the Tongue," with 26 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The command to forgive takes on a different valence in Gnostic thought. Rather than a moral obligation to a just God, forgiveness represents the pneumatic's release of attachment to the material drama of offense and injury. The tongue's destructive power (28:13-26) reflects the archons' use of language and law to bind souls to the material world.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 28 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
