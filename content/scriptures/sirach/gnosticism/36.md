## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 36

Sirach chapter 36, "Prayer for Israel's Deliverance," with 27 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The nationalistic prayer reveals the Demiurge's particularism — his claim to be the god of one nation, ignorant of the universal Pleroma. Gnostic universalism, which sees the divine spark in all humanity regardless of ethnic identity, stands in tension with this prayer.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 36 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
