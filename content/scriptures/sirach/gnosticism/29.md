## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 29

Sirach chapter 29, "Lending, Almsgiving, and Surety," with 28 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The practical economic teachings represent precisely the kind of worldly entanglement that Gnostic practitioners sought to transcend. The *Gospel of Thomas* (logion 95) says, 'If you have money, do not lend it at interest, but give it to one from whom you will not get it back' — a radical detachment that contrasts with Ben Sira's pragmatism.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 29 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
