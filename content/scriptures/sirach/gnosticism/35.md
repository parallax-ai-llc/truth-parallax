## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 35

Sirach chapter 35, "Sacrifices and Divine Justice," with 24 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The teaching that sacrifice requires justice is read as an unwitting revelation of the Demiurge's legalistic character. The true God requires neither sacrifice nor the justice defined by the Demiurge's law; the *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the true offering is the recognition of one's divine origin.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 35 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
