## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 32

Sirach chapter 32, "Conduct at Banquets and the Law," with 24 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The emphasis on seeking the Law is deeply problematic for Gnostic readers, who identified the Torah with the Demiurge's commandments — rules designed to keep humanity in subjection. The *Testimony of Truth* (NHC IX,3) explicitly critiques Law-observance as bondage to the creator god.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 32 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
