## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 51

Sirach chapter 51, "Ben Sira's Prayer and Hymn of Thanksgiving," with 30 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

Ben Sira's concluding wisdom search becomes, in Gnostic reading, both a testimony and a tragedy. The sage sought wisdom his entire life and found much — but not the ultimate gnosis. The invitation to the unlearned (51:23) is generous but limited; true wisdom requires not the academy but the Gnostic revelation that shatters all conventional categories.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 51 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates the heroes of Israel's covenant, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
