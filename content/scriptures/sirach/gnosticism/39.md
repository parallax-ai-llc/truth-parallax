## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 39

Sirach chapter 39, "The Scribe and Praise of God," with 35 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The ideal scribe who studies the Law and praises the creator-god is the epitome of the psychic — devout, moral, wise by worldly standards, but fundamentally unaware of the Pleroma. The Gnostic teacher surpasses the scribe by possessing *gnosis* — direct knowledge of transcendent reality.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 39 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
