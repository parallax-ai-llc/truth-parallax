## Gnostic Reading of Sirach 26

Sirach chapter 26, "The Good and Bad Wife," with 29 verses, presents a complex case for Gnostic interpretation. Ben Sira's celebration of conventional wisdom, Torah observance, and the goodness of the created order stands in tension with core Gnostic convictions about the material world's deficiency. Nevertheless, Gnostic readers found subversive possibilities within the wisdom tradition — Sophia (Wisdom) is central to both Sirach's theology and Gnostic mythology, creating an unexpected bridge between the two.

## Counter-Reading and Appropriation

The chapter's domestic wisdom is read within the framework of the Sophia myth. The 'good wife' becomes an allegory for Sophia restored to her proper place in the Pleroma, while the 'bad wife' represents Sophia in her fallen state — Achamoth, the lower Wisdom whose error produced the Demiurge. The Valentinian *Gospel of Philip* (NHC II,3) similarly uses marital imagery to convey pneumatic truths.

## The Gnostic Hermeneutic of Suspicion

Gnostic interpreters approached texts like Sirach 26 with a "hermeneutic of suspicion," reading against the grain to discover hidden knowledge beneath conventional piety. Where Ben Sira celebrates social order and ethical conduct, the Gnostic reader asks what deeper reality lies concealed. The Valentinian tradition in particular, as described by Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria, was adept at finding pneumatic (spiritual) meanings in texts that appeared to teach only psychic (soulish) or hylic (material) wisdom.
