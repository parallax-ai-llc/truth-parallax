## The Goods of the Material World

Sirach 25's celebration of harmony, friendship, and marital love (25:1-2) represents the highest goods available within the Demiurge's world. Gnostic readers do not necessarily dismiss these goods — even the pneumatic lives in the material world and benefits from social harmony — but they recognize them as penultimate, not ultimate. The highest good is *gnosis*, which transcends all earthly satisfactions.

## Wisdom Beyond Age

The praise of wisdom in old age (25:3-6) is selectively embraced: the Gnostic honors wisdom wherever it appears but insists that true spiritual knowledge is not a function of age or experience. The *Gospel of Thomas* (logion 4) teaches that "a small child seven days old" can instruct an old man — *gnosis* transcends the categories of time and maturity.

## Gender and the Divine

The chapter's patriarchal treatment of domestic strife (25:13-26) is subverted by Gnostic traditions that honor the divine feminine. Where Ben Sira warns about "wicked wives," Gnostic texts celebrate the syzygy — the divine male-female pairing that constitutes the fullness of the Pleroma. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the separation of male and female is itself the original wound, and salvation involves their reunion.
