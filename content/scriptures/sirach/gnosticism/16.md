## The Demiurge's Justice

Sirach 16's survey of God's judgments in history — the flood, Sodom, the Canaanites — is read by Gnostic interpreters as evidence of the Demiurge's wrathful, punitive character. The true God does not destroy cities or drown humanity; these acts reveal the flawed, jealous nature of the creator god who confuses justice with vengeance.

## Hidden from the Demiurge

The warning "Do not say, 'I am hidden from the Lord'" (16:17) is ironically inverted: the Gnostic actually is hidden from the Demiurge in one sense, possessing a divine spark that the creator cannot comprehend. The archons patrol the material world, but the pneumatic self belongs to a realm beyond their reach.

## Creation's Flawed Order

The closing meditation on creation's order (16:24-30) is read as describing the Demiurge's imperfect cosmos. While the created order displays a kind of regularity, it is ultimately a prison — beautiful on the surface but designed to keep the divine sparks trapped. True order belongs to the Pleroma, the fullness of divine life beyond the material world.
