## Wisdom as Sophia's Welcome

Sirach 15:1-6 describes Wisdom welcoming the faithful like a bride and a mother. Gnostic readers identify this Wisdom not with Torah but with Sophia herself — the divine aeon who descends to welcome the pneumatic souls who recognize her. The nuptial imagery connects to the *Gospel of Philip*'s teaching on the "bridal chamber" as the sacrament of spiritual reunion.

## The Question of Free Will

Verses 11-20 present a defense of free will that Gnostic interpreters engage with ambivalence. The Valentinian system taught a form of spiritual determinism: pneumatics are saved by nature, hylics are lost by nature, and only psychics exercise genuine moral choice. Ben Sira's universal claim that all humans can choose life or death is thus qualified — the pneumatic's "choice" is really the recognition of what they already are.

## Beyond Good and Evil

The Demiurge's moral categories — "He has not commanded anyone to be wicked" (15:20) — are the rules of a limited system. Some Gnostic libertines argued that the pneumatic person, being of divine nature, transcends conventional morality entirely. Others maintained that while freedom from the law is real, it expresses itself in love rather than in license.
