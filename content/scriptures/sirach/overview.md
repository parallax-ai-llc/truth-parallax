## Overview

Sirach, also known as *Ecclesiasticus* or the *Wisdom of Ben Sira*, is a wisdom book composed around 180 BCE by the Jerusalem sage Yeshua ben Eleazar ben Sira (Jesus son of Sirach). Originally written in Hebrew, it was translated into Greek by the author's grandson around 132 BCE, who provided a prologue explaining the circumstances of translation. The book is one of the longest and most comprehensive wisdom texts of the Second Temple period.

## Structure

The book can be organized into several major sections:

1. **Chapters 1–23**: Instructions on wisdom, piety, and ethical conduct — covering topics from the fear of the Lord to proper speech, friendship, and self-discipline
2. **Chapter 24**: The great hymn to Wisdom, who identifies herself with the Torah
3. **Chapters 25–43**: Further ethical teachings on domestic life, social relations, wealth, and creation
4. **Chapters 44–50**: The "Praise of the Ancestors" (*Laus Patrum*) — a panoramic survey of Israelite heroes from Enoch to the High Priest Simon II
5. **Chapter 51**: A concluding prayer and an alphabetic acrostic poem on the search for wisdom

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Sirach occupies a unique and contested position across religious traditions:

- **Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy**: Fully canonical as part of the deuterocanonical books; read liturgically and cited authoritatively in doctrinal matters
- **Protestantism**: Classified as apocryphal; historically valued for edification but excluded from the canon since the Reformation
- **Judaism**: Though not included in the Tanakh, Sirach was widely read in rabbinic circles; fragments were found at Qumran and in the Cairo Genizah, and the Talmud quotes it occasionally
- **Islam**: Solomon and wisdom traditions are honored; the ethical teachings resonate with Quranic and hadith literature
- **Gnosticism**: The personification of Wisdom in chapter 24 provided material for Gnostic speculation on Sophia

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Fear of the Lord | The crown and root of wisdom (1:11-20) |
| Torah and Wisdom | Wisdom is identified with the Mosaic law (24:23) |
| Ethics of Speech | Extensive instruction on the tongue, gossip, and oaths |
| Friendship | Discerning true friends from false ones (6:5-17) |
| Praise of Ancestors | The faithful heroes of Israel as models of wisdom |
| Creation Theology | The glory of God revealed in the natural order |
