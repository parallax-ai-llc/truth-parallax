## Testing as Divine Wisdom

Sirach 2's counsel to prepare for testing resonates with the Quranic teaching that God tests the faithful: "Do you think you will be left to say, 'We believe,' and not be tested?" (Quran 29:2). Islam teaches that trials (*ibtila'*) are instruments of divine pedagogy — refining faith rather than punishing it.

## Tawakkul: Trust in God

The appeal to trust in God's faithfulness (2:6-10) corresponds to the Islamic virtue of *tawakkul* (reliance on God). The Quran declares: "Whoever puts his trust in Allah, He will be sufficient for him" (65:3). Both Sirach and the Quran teach that genuine trust is demonstrated not in times of ease but in times of adversity.

## Divine Mercy

The chapter's closing affirmation that God's mercy matches His majesty (2:18) echoes a foundational hadith qudsi: "My mercy prevails over My wrath" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Islamic theology affirms that God's *rahmah* (mercy) encompasses all things (Quran 7:156), and Sirach's confidence in divine compassion resonates with this central Islamic conviction.
