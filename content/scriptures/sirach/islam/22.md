## Against Laziness

Sirach 22's condemnation of laziness resonates with Islam's emphasis on productive activity. The Prophet sought refuge from laziness (*kasal*) in his daily supplications: "O Allah, I seek refuge in You from incapacity and laziness" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Islam teaches that work is a form of worship when performed with right intention.

## Grief for the Spiritually Lost

The comparison between mourning the dead and mourning the fool (22:7-18) connects to the Islamic concern for those who are spiritually heedless (*ghafilin*). The Quran describes such people: "They have hearts with which they do not understand, eyes with which they do not see, and ears with which they do not hear. Those are like livestock; rather, they are more astray" (7:179). Islam's call to *da'wah* (invitation) aims to awaken the spiritually dead.

## Preserving Brotherhood

The meditation on broken friendship (22:19-26) resonates with Islam's strong emphasis on maintaining the bonds of brotherhood (*ukhuwwah*). The Prophet taught: "It is not lawful for a Muslim to forsake his brother for more than three days" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Islam actively promotes reconciliation and the healing of broken relationships.
