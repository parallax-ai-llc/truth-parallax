## Hikmah and Taqwa

Sirach 1's declaration that all wisdom originates with God (1:1) resonates with the Quranic concept of *hikmah* (wisdom) as a divine gift: "He gives wisdom to whom He wills" (Quran 2:269). The chapter's insistence that the fear of the Lord is the beginning and crown of wisdom parallels the Islamic concept of *taqwa* (God-consciousness), which the Quran identifies as the foundation of all righteousness.

## Parallels with Quranic Teaching

The fourfold description of the fear of God (1:11-20) finds echoes throughout the Quran: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous" (49:13). Both Sirach and the Quran teach that true wisdom is inseparable from reverential awe before the Creator — not mere intellectual achievement but a comprehensive orientation of the soul toward God.

## Patience as Spiritual Virtue

The chapter's counsel on patience and self-control (1:22-30) corresponds to the Islamic virtue of *sabr* (patience), praised extensively in the Quran: "Indeed, Allah is with the patient" (2:153). Patience in adversity, self-restraint in temptation, and perseverance in worship are central to both Sirach's wisdom and Islamic ethics.
