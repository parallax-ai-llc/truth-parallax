## Divine Transcendence

Sirach 18's doxology to God's incomprehensibility (18:1-7) resonates with the Islamic emphasis on divine transcendence (*tanzih*). The Quran declares: "There is nothing like unto Him" (42:11). God's nature is beyond human comprehension, and the appropriate response is awe, humility, and worship.

## Rahmah: Divine Mercy

Despite God's transcendence, He is compassionate toward human frailty (18:11-14). Islam names God *al-Rahman* and *al-Rahim* (the Most Merciful, the Especially Merciful) — the two names that open every surah of the Quran except one. The hadith teaches that God divided mercy into one hundred parts, sent one to earth, and reserved ninety-nine for the Day of Judgment (Sahih al-Bukhari).

## Self-Discipline and Preparation

The practical counsel on self-discipline (18:15-33) corresponds to the Islamic emphasis on *muhasabah* (self-examination). Umar ibn al-Khattab counseled: "Take account of yourselves before you are taken to account." The daily prayers, Ramadan fasting, and regular self-examination in Islam institutionalize the discipline that Sirach commends.
