## Economic Justice

Sirach 13's portrayal of how wealth distorts relationships resonates with the Quran's persistent concern for economic justice. The Quran condemns those who "hoard gold and silver and do not spend them in the way of Allah" (9:34) and warns that wealth can become a source of *fitnah* (trial and temptation). Ben Sira's observation that the rich exploit the poor echoes the Quranic critique of economic oppression.

## The Equality of Believers

Islam's teaching on the fundamental equality of all believers before God challenges the social dynamics that Sirach describes. The Prophet's farewell sermon declared: "An Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab, nor does a non-Arab have superiority over an Arab... except by piety and good action." Sirach's portrait of unequal relationships serves as a diagnosis of the social illness that Islam aims to cure.

## Social Realism

The practical observation that the rich person's folly is praised while the poor person's wisdom is ignored (13:23) reflects a social realism that Islamic reformers have long recognized. The *maqasid al-shari'ah* (objectives of Islamic law) include the protection of wealth and the welfare of the community — goals that require confronting the kind of social distortions Sirach describes.
