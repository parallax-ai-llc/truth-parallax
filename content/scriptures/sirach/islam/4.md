## Care for the Poor

Sirach 4:1-10 commands compassion for the needy with prophetic urgency. Islam places care for the poor at the center of religious obligation: *zakat* (obligatory almsgiving) is one of the five pillars, and the Quran warns that those who neglect the hungry and orphaned deny the very faith they profess (107:1-7). Ben Sira's social ethic aligns closely with Quranic economic justice.

## Wisdom as Divine Gift

In 4:11-19, Wisdom tests and disciplines her students. Islam similarly teaches that *hikmah* (wisdom) is a divine gift that must be sought with patience and discipline. The Quran praises those who "listen to speech and follow the best of it" (39:18) — a disposition of teachability that Sirach's Wisdom demands of her students.

## Constructive Shame

The distinction between destructive and constructive shame (4:20-31) finds resonance in the Islamic concept of *haya'* (modesty, shame). The Prophet taught: "Modesty brings nothing but good" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Islam distinguishes between the healthy shame that restrains wrongdoing and the paralyzing shame that prevents repentance.
