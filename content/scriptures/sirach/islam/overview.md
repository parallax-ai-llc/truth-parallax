## Overview

Islam does not include Sirach in its scriptural canon, and the book is viewed through the broader Islamic lens of *tahrif* (textual alteration of earlier revelations). However, Sirach's ethical and wisdom teachings demonstrate striking parallels with Quranic and hadith literature, reflecting a shared Abrahamic heritage of moral instruction rooted in the fear of God and the pursuit of wisdom.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Hikmah (Wisdom) Tradition
The Quran repeatedly emphasizes *hikmah* (wisdom) as a divine gift: "He gives wisdom to whom He wills, and whoever has been given wisdom has certainly been given much good" (Quran 2:269). Sirach's central concern with wisdom resonates deeply with this Quranic theme.

### Ethical Convergence
Many of Sirach's practical teachings — on honoring parents, controlling the tongue, caring for the poor, and cultivating humility — find direct parallels in the Quran and the Sunnah of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him).

### Prophetic Wisdom Legacy
Islam honors Solomon (*Sulayman*) as a prophet endowed with extraordinary wisdom. Sirach's wisdom tradition is understood as part of the broader legacy of prophetic teaching that culminated in the Quran.

## Theological Emphases

- **Taqwa**: Sirach's "fear of the Lord" corresponds to the Quranic concept of God-consciousness
- **Adab**: Proper conduct and manners, a central concern in both Sirach and Islamic ethics
- **Luqman Parallel**: Surah 31 (Luqman) presents wisdom instruction from father to son, closely paralleling Sirach's didactic form
- **Social Justice**: Concern for the poor and critique of wealth mirror Quranic economic ethics
