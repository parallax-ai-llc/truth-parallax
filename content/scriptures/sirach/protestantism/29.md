## Protestant Perspective on Sirach 29

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) chapter 29, titled "Lending, Almsgiving, and Surety," contains 28 verses of practical wisdom attributed to Jesus ben Sira (c. 180 BCE). While Protestant churches classify Sirach as part of the Apocrypha rather than canonical Scripture, the book has historically been valued for edification and instruction. The 1647 Westminster Confession acknowledges that apocryphal books may be read "for example of life and instruction of manners," though not used to establish doctrine.

## Historical and Theological Context

This chapter treats lending to neighbors, giving alms, and the risks of going surety for another's debts. Proverbs 6:1-5 and 22:26-27 address similar concerns about surety, and Protestant interpreters connect the almsgiving passages to 2 Corinthians 9:6-7. The practical financial wisdom here demonstrates the continuity of biblical wisdom across the canonical and apocryphal traditions.

## Significance for Protestant Readers

Although Sirach lacks canonical authority in Protestant traditions, this chapter's emphasis on practical godly living resonates with themes found throughout the canonical wisdom literature. Reformers like Luther included Sirach in his Bible translation, noting its usefulness for moral guidance despite its non-canonical status. The chapter's teachings on wisdom and ethics can be profitably compared with Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the canonical Psalms.
