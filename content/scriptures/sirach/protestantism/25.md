## Numerical Wisdom Sayings

Sirach 25 opens with a series of numerical sayings — "Three things I delight in... and three things I hate" (25:1-2) — a literary form familiar from Proverbs 30:15-31. Protestant interpreters appreciate the rhetorical craftsmanship while noting the content: agreement among kindred, friendship among neighbors, and a married couple living in harmony represent the highest social goods.

## The Blessedness of Old Age

Verses 3-6 praise the wisdom that comes with age, culminating in the declaration that the fear of the Lord surpasses everything (25:11). Protestant commentators connect this to the canonical theme of wisdom maturing over a lifetime (Job 12:12) and the Reformation honor for the calling of the elder.

## Domestic Discord

The chapter's second half (25:13-26) addresses domestic strife, particularly the pain of living with a contentious spouse. The language is notably one-sided and reflects the patriarchal assumptions of the ancient world. Protestant interpreters read these verses with historical awareness, distinguishing between the culturally conditioned expression and the underlying wisdom about the centrality of marital harmony — a theme developed more equitably in Ephesians 5:21-33.
