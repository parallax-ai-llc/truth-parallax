## The Disgrace of Laziness

Sirach 22 opens with striking images of the lazy person: like a filthy stone, like a lump of dung — everyone who picks it up shakes it off (22:1-2). Protestant interpreters connect this to the strong Reformation emphasis on vocation and diligent labor, rooted in Proverbs' condemnation of the sluggard (Proverbs 6:6-11) and Paul's injunction that those who will not work should not eat (2 Thessalonians 3:10).

## Grief for the Foolish

Verses 7-18 compare mourning for the dead with mourning for the fool: the dead are at rest, but the fool's life is worse than death. The passage is sharply realistic in its assessment of incorrigible folly. Protestant commentators note this sober realism while affirming the gospel's power to transform even the most hardened fool.

## The Preservation of Friendship

The chapter concludes with a meditation on friendship (22:19-26): once trust is broken by insults or betrayal, reconciliation is possible but difficult — like a bird released from the hand, a friend driven away is hard to recapture. Protestant readers find here wisdom for the ministry of reconciliation, noting both the fragility and the value of human relationships.
