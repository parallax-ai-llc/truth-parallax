## Fleeing from Sin

Sirach 21 opens with a vivid image: "Have you sinned, my child? Do so no more, but ask forgiveness for your past sins. Flee from sin as from a snake" (21:1-2). Protestant interpreters appreciate the urgency of the call to repentance, connecting it to the apostolic command to "flee from sin" (1 Corinthians 6:18, 2 Timothy 2:22).

## The Contrast of Wise and Foolish

The chapter presents an extended comparison between the wise and the foolish (21:11-28): the wise person's mind is like a spring of water, while the fool's is like a broken jar. The wise person's speech builds up; the fool's tears down. Protestant commentators note the affinity with the Proverbs tradition of contrasting the wise and the foolish, and the Pauline distinction between edifying and destructive speech (Ephesians 4:29).

## The Path of Righteousness

Sin is described as a two-edged sword (21:3) and the path of sinners as smooth but ending in Hades (21:10). Protestant readers find here the familiar biblical pattern of the two ways — the broad road and the narrow road (Matthew 7:13-14) — expressed in wisdom categories.
