## Protestant Perspective on Sirach 26

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) chapter 26, titled "The Good and Bad Wife," contains 29 verses of practical wisdom attributed to Jesus ben Sira (c. 180 BCE). While Protestant churches classify Sirach as part of the Apocrypha rather than canonical Scripture, the book has historically been valued for edification and instruction. The 1647 Westminster Confession acknowledges that apocryphal books may be read "for example of life and instruction of manners," though not used to establish doctrine.

## Historical and Theological Context

This chapter addresses the virtuous and the wicked wife, themes that parallel Proverbs 31. Protestant commentators note the patriarchal framing while extracting principles about marital faithfulness and character. The passage contrasts the blessings of a good spouse with the miseries of a bad one, offering wisdom that resonates with the broader biblical emphasis on godly relationships.

## Significance for Protestant Readers

Although Sirach lacks canonical authority in Protestant traditions, this chapter's emphasis on practical godly living resonates with themes found throughout the canonical wisdom literature. Reformers like Luther included Sirach in his Bible translation, noting its usefulness for moral guidance despite its non-canonical status. The chapter's teachings on wisdom and ethics can be profitably compared with Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the canonical Psalms.
