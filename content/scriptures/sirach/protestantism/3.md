## The Commandment to Honor Parents

Sirach 3:1-16 offers one of the most extensive meditations on the fifth commandment in all biblical literature. Protestant interpreters appreciate the practical detail: caring for aging parents, bearing with them patiently, and the promise that honoring parents atones for sins (3:3). This last claim, however, raises Protestant caution — the language of atonement through filial piety sits uneasily with the Reformation doctrine of justification by faith alone.

## The Virtue of Humility

Verses 17-24 counsel humility and warn against intellectual presumption: "Seek not what is too difficult for you" (3:21). Protestant commentators note the resonance with Romans 12:3 and the Reformation critique of speculative theology. The call to attend to what has been commanded rather than what is hidden aligns with Deuteronomy 29:29.

## Almsgiving and Its Limits

The chapter's closing claim that "almsgiving atones for sin" (3:30) is precisely the kind of passage that led Protestants to question Sirach's canonical status. While charitable giving is praised throughout Scripture, the suggestion that it effects atonement appears to conflict with the sufficiency of Christ's sacrifice.
