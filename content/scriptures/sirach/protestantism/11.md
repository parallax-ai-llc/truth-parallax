## Divine Reversals

Sirach 11 opens with the theme of divine reversal: God lifts the lowly from the dust and seats them with princes (11:1-6). Protestant interpreters find this consistent with the biblical pattern from Hannah's song (1 Samuel 2:1-10) through the Magnificat, affirming God's sovereign freedom to overturn human expectations.

## Prudence and Patience

Verses 7-13 counsel against hasty judgment and rash speech: do not answer before you listen, do not intervene in disputes that are not yours, and do not spread yourself too thin. Protestant commentators appreciate this practical wisdom, connecting it to James 1:19 and the Reformed emphasis on calling — attending faithfully to one's own vocation rather than chasing every opportunity.

## The Uncertainty of Wealth

The chapter warns against trusting in riches (11:14-28), since both prosperity and adversity come from the Lord. One can be rich in the evening and destitute by morning. Protestant readers connect this to Jesus' parable of the rich fool (Luke 12:13-21) and the Reformation conviction that earthly goods are stewardship, not possession.
