## Protestant Perspective on Sirach 39

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) chapter 39, titled "The Scribe and Praise of God," contains 35 verses of practical wisdom attributed to Jesus ben Sira (c. 180 BCE). While Protestant churches classify Sirach as part of the Apocrypha rather than canonical Scripture, the book has historically been valued for edification and instruction. The 1647 Westminster Confession acknowledges that apocryphal books may be read "for example of life and instruction of manners," though not used to establish doctrine.

## Historical and Theological Context

This chapter presents the ideal scribe who devotes himself to the study of the Law, travels widely, and serves among rulers. Protestant scholars compare this portrait with Ezra the scribe and note that Ben Sira's ideal anticipates the Reformation emphasis on educated ministry.

## Significance for Protestant Readers

Although Sirach lacks canonical authority in Protestant traditions, this chapter's emphasis on practical godly living resonates with themes found throughout the canonical wisdom literature. Reformers like Luther included Sirach in his Bible translation, noting its usefulness for moral guidance despite its non-canonical status. The chapter's teachings on wisdom and ethics can be profitably compared with Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the canonical Psalms.
