## The Certainty of Divine Justice

Sirach 16 affirms that God's justice is inescapable: "Do not say, 'I am hidden from the Lord; who from on high has me in mind?'" (16:17). Protestant interpreters connect this to Psalm 139 and Hebrews 4:13 — nothing is hidden from God's sight. The chapter's survey of God's judgments in history (the generation of the flood, Sodom, the Canaanites) echoes the canonical pattern of divine retribution.

## Against Ungodly Children

The chapter opens with the surprising claim that it is better to have no children than to have ungodly ones (16:1-4). Protestant commentators read this within the broader context of covenant theology, where faithfulness matters more than mere biological multiplication.

## Creation and Order

Verses 24-30 transition to creation theology: God established the works of creation in an enduring order. Protestant readers note the natural theology implicit here — creation reveals God's wisdom and governance — while affirming with the Reformers that creation's testimony, though real, is insufficient for salvation apart from special revelation.
