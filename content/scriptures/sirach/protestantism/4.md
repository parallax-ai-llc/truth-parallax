## Compassion for the Needy

Sirach 4 opens with urgent commands to care for the poor: do not grieve the hungry, do not anger the desperate, do not reject the suppliant (4:1-6). Protestant readers find these imperatives fully consistent with the canonical prophetic tradition (Amos 2:6-7, Isaiah 58:6-7) and with James 2:15-16. The warning that the cry of the oppressed reaches God's ears echoes Exodus 22:23.

## Wisdom as Mother and Teacher

In 4:11-19, Wisdom is personified as a mother who disciplines and tests her children before revealing her secrets. Protestant interpreters note the parallel with Proverbs 1-9 while observing that Ben Sira develops Wisdom's maternal role more explicitly. The passage anticipates the New Testament theme that God disciplines those He loves (Hebrews 12:5-11).

## Godly and Ungodly Shame

The chapter concludes with a nuanced discussion of shame (4:20-31): there is a shame that leads to sin and a shame that brings honor. Protestant commentators connect this to the Pauline distinction between godly sorrow and worldly sorrow (2 Corinthians 7:10), finding in Sirach a practical wisdom applicable to pastoral care.
