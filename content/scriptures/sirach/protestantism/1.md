## Wisdom's Divine Origin

Sirach opens with a magnificent declaration that all wisdom comes from the Lord and remains with Him forever (1:1). Protestant interpreters, while classifying Sirach as apocryphal, acknowledge the theological consistency of this claim with Proverbs 2:6 and James 1:5. The chapter establishes the fear of the Lord as both the beginning and the fullness of wisdom — a theme that pervades the entire book.

## The Fear of the Lord as Wisdom's Foundation

Verses 11-20 present a fourfold description of the fear of the Lord: it is the beginning, fullness, crown, and root of wisdom. Protestant commentators note the striking parallel with Proverbs 1:7 and 9:10, though they observe that Ben Sira develops the theme more systematically than the canonical wisdom books. The emphasis on patience and self-control (1:22-24) as expressions of the fear of God resonates with the Pauline fruit of the Spirit.

## Protestant Assessment

While appreciating the chapter's theological depth, Protestant scholars note that it represents a Second Temple development of canonical wisdom themes. The chapter's value lies in its witness to how faithful Jews before Christ understood wisdom and piety — a bridge between Proverbs and the New Testament.
