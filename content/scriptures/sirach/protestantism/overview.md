## Overview

Protestantism has historically classified Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) as apocryphal rather than canonical. The Reformers — Luther, Calvin, and the Westminster divines — excluded it from the canon of inspired Scripture while acknowledging its value for moral instruction and historical insight. Luther included it in his Bible translation between the Old and New Testaments with the note that these books are "useful and good to read."

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Apocryphal but Edifying
The Protestant tradition distinguishes between canonical authority and devotional usefulness. Sirach's ethical teachings — on friendship, speech, humility, and the fear of the Lord — are appreciated as consistent with canonical wisdom literature, even if they do not carry doctrinal authority.

### Continuity with Proverbs
Protestant scholars note Sirach's deep dependence on Proverbs, Job, and Ecclesiastes. It is read as an example of how Second Temple Judaism extended and systematized the older canonical wisdom traditions.

### Doctrinal Caution
Protestant interpreters flag certain passages — such as the apparent endorsement of meritorious almsgiving (3:30) and prayers for the dead — as theologically problematic and as evidence for the book's sub-canonical status.

## Theological Emphases

- **Fear of the Lord**: Affirmed as consistent with Proverbs 1:7 and the canonical witness
- **Wisdom and Torah**: The identification of wisdom with Torah (ch. 24) is noted as a significant development
- **Historical Value**: The book illuminates the intellectual world of Second Temple Judaism
- **Canon Formation**: Sirach serves as a case study in Protestant discussions of canonicity
