## Protestant Perspective on Sirach 31

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) chapter 31, titled "Temperance and Table Manners," contains 31 verses of practical wisdom attributed to Jesus ben Sira (c. 180 BCE). While Protestant churches classify Sirach as part of the Apocrypha rather than canonical Scripture, the book has historically been valued for edification and instruction. The 1647 Westminster Confession acknowledges that apocryphal books may be read "for example of life and instruction of manners," though not used to establish doctrine.

## Historical and Theological Context

This chapter teaches moderation in eating, drinking, and conduct at feasts. The temperance themes connect with Protestant emphasis on self-control as a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:23). Ben Sira's counsel against excess at the table reflects wisdom that Protestant moral theology has historically affirmed.

## Significance for Protestant Readers

Although Sirach lacks canonical authority in Protestant traditions, this chapter's emphasis on practical godly living resonates with themes found throughout the canonical wisdom literature. Reformers like Luther included Sirach in his Bible translation, noting its usefulness for moral guidance despite its non-canonical status. The chapter's teachings on wisdom and ethics can be profitably compared with Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the canonical Psalms.
