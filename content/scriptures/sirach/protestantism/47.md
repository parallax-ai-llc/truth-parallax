## Protestant Perspective on Sirach 47

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) chapter 47, titled "David, Solomon, and Rehoboam," contains 25 verses of practical wisdom attributed to Jesus ben Sira (c. 180 BCE). While Protestant churches classify Sirach as part of the Apocrypha rather than canonical Scripture, the book has historically been valued for edification and instruction. The 1647 Westminster Confession acknowledges that apocryphal books may be read "for example of life and instruction of manners," though not used to establish doctrine.

## Historical and Theological Context

This chapter treats David's establishment of worship and Solomon's wisdom and folly. Protestant interpreters note the balanced assessment — celebrating David's musical contributions while acknowledging Solomon's downfall through foreign wives (1 Kings 11).

## Significance for Protestant Readers

Although Sirach lacks canonical authority in Protestant traditions, this chapter's emphasis on Israel's covenant history resonates with themes found throughout the canonical wisdom literature. Reformers like Luther included Sirach in his Bible translation, noting its usefulness for moral guidance despite its non-canonical status. The chapter's teachings on faithfulness and remembrance can be profitably compared with Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the canonical Psalms.
