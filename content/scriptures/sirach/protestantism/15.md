## Wisdom and Torah Obedience

Sirach 15 opens by declaring that the one who fears the Lord and holds to the Torah will find Wisdom waiting like a mother and a bride (15:1-6). Protestant interpreters note the continued identification of wisdom with Torah observance — a theme that distinguishes Sirach from the more universally oriented Proverbs.

## The Defense of Free Will

Verses 11-20 contain one of the most theologically significant passages in Sirach: a vigorous defense of human free will. "Do not say, 'It was the Lord's doing that I fell away'" (15:11). God has set before humanity fire and water, life and death — "stretch out your hand for whichever you choose" (15:16-17). Protestant interpreters engage this passage seriously, noting both its consistency with Deuteronomy 30:19 and its tension with Augustinian and Reformed doctrines of predestination and total depravity.

## The Sovereignty-Responsibility Tension

This chapter crystallizes a central theological debate. While Protestant theology generally emphasizes divine sovereignty more strongly than Ben Sira does here, the passage serves as an important witness to Second Temple Jewish theology and provides a foil for understanding the later Pauline and Augustinian developments.
