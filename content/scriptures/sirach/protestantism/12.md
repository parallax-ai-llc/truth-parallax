## Giving to the Righteous

Sirach 12 opens with a controversial claim: "If you do good, know to whom you do it" (12:1), and advises giving to the godly rather than to sinners (12:4-7). Protestant interpreters note the tension between this counsel and Jesus' command to love one's enemies (Matthew 5:44). The passage reflects the retribution theology common in wisdom literature, which the gospel both fulfills and transcends.

## The Unreliability of Enemies

Verses 8-18 warn that enemies cannot be trusted even when they appear friendly: "An enemy speaks sweetly with his lips, but in his heart he plans to throw you into a pit" (12:16). Protestant commentators find this consistent with the realism of Proverbs (26:24-26) while noting that the New Testament calls believers to a higher ethic of forgiveness — not naivete, but redemptive love.

## Wisdom and Discernment

The chapter ultimately teaches discernment rather than misanthropy. Protestant readers appreciate the call to wisdom in relationships while insisting that such discernment must be tempered by the grace revealed in Christ.
