## Overview

The Taiping Jing (太平經, "Scripture of Great Peace") is one of the earliest and most historically significant texts of religious Taoism, associated with the millenarian movements that shaped the late Han dynasty (second century CE). The text envisions a utopian era of Great Peace (taiping) in which heaven, earth, and humanity exist in perfect harmony, and provides a comprehensive program — cosmological, political, ethical, and ritual — for bringing this ideal age into being. Its influence on the Yellow Turban Rebellion (184 CE) and subsequent Taoist movements makes it one of the most consequential religious texts in Chinese history.

## Structure

The Taiping Jing is organized into ten major sections:

1. **The Dao of Heaven and Great Peace** (天道太平): The cosmic foundations of the Great Peace ideal
2. **Yin-Yang and the Five Phases** (陰陽五行): The cosmological framework of correlative thinking
3. **The Duty of the Ruler** (君道): The ruler's responsibility to align governance with heaven
4. **Inherited Burden and Merit** (承負與功德): The transmission of karmic debt across generations
5. **The Mandate of Heaven's Instructions** (天命教令): Divine revelations and instructions
6. **Guarding the One** (守一): Meditation and spiritual cultivation practices
7. **Communication Between Heaven and Earth** (天人感應): The reciprocal relationship between cosmic and human realms
8. **The Ideal Community** (理想社會): The social and economic vision of the Great Peace
9. **Healing and Exorcism** (治病驅邪): Medical and ritual healing practices
10. **The Coming of Great Peace** (太平將至): The eschatological vision of the age to come

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Taiping Jing has been interpreted across multiple traditions:

- **Religious Taoism**: A foundational scripture of communal Taoism with lasting influence on liturgy and social vision
- **Philosophical Taoism**: Examined for its development of cosmological and ethical thought
- **Confucian**: Engaged for its incorporation of Confucian governance ideals and its theory of inherited moral responsibility
- **Academic**: Studied as a primary source for late Han religious history and the origins of organized Taoism
- **Comparative**: Analyzed alongside millenarian movements and utopian texts worldwide

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Great Peace | The utopian ideal of cosmic harmony between heaven, earth, and humanity |
| Inherited Burden | The transmission of moral debt (chengfu) from ancestors to descendants |
| Cosmic Correspondence | The mutual influence between heaven, earth, and human action |
| Social Justice | The equitable distribution of resources as a cosmic imperative |
| Millenarianism | The expectation of a coming age of perfect harmony |
| Guarding the One | Meditative practice of maintaining unity with the Tao |
