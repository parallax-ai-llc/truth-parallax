Comparative interpretation of Section 9, 'Healing and Exorcism,' places this portion of the Taiping Jing within the global context of millenarian, utopian, and apocalyptic religious movements. The themes addressed in this section — the expectation of cosmic transformation, the critique of existing social arrangements, the vision of an ideal future order — find parallels across religious traditions worldwide, from Jewish messianism and Christian apocalypticism to Buddhist Maitreya expectations and the cargo cults of Melanesia.

Comparative scholars examine the specific features of this section alongside analogous elements in millenarian traditions elsewhere, identifying both universal patterns in millenarian thought — the sense of crisis, the expectation of divine intervention, the hope for radical transformation — and the distinctive forms these patterns take in the Chinese cultural context.
