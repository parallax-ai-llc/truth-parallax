## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Taiping Jing places it within the global phenomenon of millenarian and utopian religious movements. The text's vision of a coming age of perfect harmony, its critique of existing social arrangements as fallen and corrupt, and its program for cosmic renewal find parallels in Jewish messianism, Christian apocalypticism, Islamic Mahdism, Buddhist Maitreya expectations, and numerous indigenous millenarian movements worldwide.

## Millenarian Patterns

Comparative scholars identify in the Taiping Jing the classic features of millenarian thought as described by Norman Cohn and others: the conviction that the present age is corrupt and nearing its end, the expectation of a radical transformation that will establish a perfect new order, the belief in divine revelation as the source of knowledge about this coming transformation, and the call for human action to prepare for or hasten the new age.

## Social Justice

The Taiping Jing's insistence that the hoarding of wealth produces cosmic disharmony and that the equitable distribution of resources is a precondition for Great Peace resonates with social justice traditions worldwide — from the Hebrew prophets' denunciation of economic exploitation to liberation theology's preferential option for the poor.
