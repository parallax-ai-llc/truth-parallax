Academic analysis of Section 4, 'Inherited Burden and Merit,' examines this portion of the Taiping Jing within the context of late Han dynasty religious and social history. Scholars attend to the text's complex compositional history, distinguishing earlier layers that may reflect second-century millenarian movements from later redactions and additions. The pioneering work of scholars such as Barbara Hendrischke and Max Kaltenmark has established the framework for critical study of this challenging text.

Academic interpreters situate this section's teachings within the broader context of late Han society: the political instability, economic inequality, and social dislocation that created the conditions for the millenarian movements with which the Taiping Jing is associated. Understanding the social context illuminates both the text's utopian vision and its practical prescriptions for communal transformation.
