## Overview

Zen interpretation of the Taiping Jing focuses on the text's contemplative dimension, particularly its teaching on "guarding the one" (shouyi), a practice of sustained meditative attention to the fundamental unity underlying all apparent multiplicity. While the Taiping Jing's millenarian and political dimensions may seem remote from Zen's emphasis on individual awakening, its contemplative core reveals a shared concern with the cultivation of a still, unified awareness that transcends the discriminating mind.

## Guarding the One

The practice of shouyi described in the Taiping Jing — maintaining continuous awareness of the One (yi), the undivided reality from which all multiplicity emerges — resonates with Zen's own emphasis on single-pointed attention and the cultivation of a mind that rests in its original, undivided nature. Both traditions teach that this unified awareness is not something to be achieved but something to be uncovered by ceasing the mental activities that obscure it.

## Inner Peace as Foundation

Zen readers find wisdom in the Taiping Jing's insight that outer peace — in society, in nature, in the cosmos — depends upon inner peace. The text's teaching that Great Peace begins with the individual's cultivation of inner harmony, and radiates outward to transform family, community, and ultimately the cosmos, mirrors the Zen conviction that awakening transforms not only the individual but the entire field of relations in which the individual participates.
