## Overview

Comparative philosophers have found in the Mozi one of the most productive texts for cross-cultural ethical dialogue. Mozi's consequentialism — evaluating actions by their consequences for human welfare — anticipates the utilitarian tradition of Bentham and Mill by over two thousand years. His doctrine of universal love invites comparison with cosmopolitan ethics, while his political theory of identifying with the superior anticipates elements of social contract theory.

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative readers place Mozi in dialogue with Western ethical traditions, exploring both structural parallels and illuminating differences. The comparison with utilitarianism is particularly rich: like Bentham, Mozi evaluates practices by their tendency to promote or diminish welfare; unlike Bentham, Mozi grounds his ethics in the will of heaven rather than in sentient experience. This difference reveals how similar ethical conclusions can arise from radically different metaphysical foundations.

## Key Comparisons

Mozi's universal love has been compared with Peter Singer's expanding circle of moral concern, with Kantian cosmopolitanism, and with Christian agape. His anti-war arguments anticipate modern just war theory. His critique of luxury and waste resonates with contemporary debates about global distributive justice and effective altruism.
