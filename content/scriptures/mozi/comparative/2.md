Comparative reading of Mozi section 2, "Universal Love II," places the Mohist arguments in dialogue with global philosophical traditions. The reasoning presented here invites comparison with Western ethical frameworks including utilitarianism, cosmopolitanism, and rights-based theories. Mozi's method of evaluating practices by their consequences for human welfare anticipates key insights of the utilitarian tradition while differing in its theological grounding and its emphasis on social harmony.

This section also illuminates productive comparisons with contemporary philosophical debates. The arguments resonate with discussions in global justice theory, effective altruism, and the ethics of war and peace. Mozi's willingness to challenge established customs on consequentialist grounds represents a form of philosophical radicalism that finds parallels across cultures and historical periods.
