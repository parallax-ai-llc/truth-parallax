## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Mozi is characterized by ambivalence. On one hand, Daoists share Mozi's opposition to Confucian ritualism and extravagance. On the other, Zhuangzi criticized Mozi for replacing one form of purposive striving with another — substituting utilitarian calculation for ritual propriety while remaining equally trapped in the prison of deliberate effort.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist readers appreciate Mozi's critique of wasteful Confucian ceremonies but find his alternative — a relentlessly purposive program of social benefit — equally contrary to the Way. The Zhuangzi's famous critique accuses Mozi of making life joyless through excessive austerity, arguing that his followers "sing but may not enjoy singing, mourn but may not express grief." From the Daoist perspective, both Confucius and Mozi err by trying to impose artificial order on the natural flow of life.

## Points of Convergence

Despite these criticisms, Daoists recognize in Mozi a kindred spirit of sorts — a thinker willing to challenge conventional wisdom and question established authority. His critique of fatalism also resonates with the Daoist emphasis on responsive adaptation to circumstances rather than passive acceptance of fate.
