## Overview

Confucian engagement with the Mozi has been defined by vigorous opposition since Mencius famously declared that Mozi's doctrine of universal love "denies the father" by abolishing the special obligations of filial piety. For the Confucian tradition, the Mozi represents the most dangerous challenge to the principle that moral life begins with particular attachments — to parents, siblings, and community — and extends outward in graded degrees of affection.

## Interpretive Approach

Confucian readers approach the Mozi as a worthy but ultimately misguided opponent. They acknowledge Mozi's genuine concern for human welfare but argue that his program of impartial love is psychologically impossible and socially destructive. The Confucian conviction is that attempting to love all people equally results in loving no one deeply — that the warmth of particular relationships is the soil from which broader social virtue grows.

## Key Tensions

The deepest Confucian objection concerns Mozi's consequentialism. By evaluating practices solely by their material benefit, Mozi strips moral life of its intrinsic dignity and reduces it to a calculation of utility. The Confucian defense of ritual, music, and elaborate funerals rests on the conviction that these practices express and cultivate moral sentiments that cannot be measured in utilitarian terms.
