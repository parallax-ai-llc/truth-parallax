Confucian interpretation of Mozi section 15, "Against Fatalism," engages critically with Mozi's arguments from the standpoint of graded affection and ritual propriety. The Confucian tradition recognizes Mozi's sincere concern for human welfare but maintains that his approach fundamentally misunderstands the nature of moral motivation. Genuine benevolence, Confucians argue, must be rooted in particular relationships and extended outward through cultivated virtue, not imposed as an abstract universal principle.

Mencius's critique remains the touchstone for Confucian readings: by denying the special claims of family relationships, Mozi undermines the very foundation upon which broader social harmony is built. The Confucian reader finds in this section both a challenge to be taken seriously and a confirmation that the Confucian Way of graded love represents a more psychologically realistic and morally profound approach to human flourishing.
