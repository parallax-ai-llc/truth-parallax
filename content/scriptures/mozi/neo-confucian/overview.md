## Overview

Neo-Confucian treatment of the Mozi largely follows Mencius's condemnation, classifying Mohism as a heterodox teaching that threatens the moral foundations of civilization. Zhu Xi and other Song dynasty thinkers saw Mozi's universal love as a forerunner of Buddhist compassion — and rejected both as incompatible with the Confucian understanding that moral obligations are naturally differentiated by relationship and social role.

## Interpretive Approach

Neo-Confucian readers approach the Mozi through the lens of the li (principle) metaphysics established by Cheng Yi and Zhu Xi. Since the principle of heaven is expressed differently in different relationships — the love between parent and child differs in kind from the concern one owes to strangers — Mozi's demand for impartiality violates the very structure of moral reality.

## Qualified Appreciation

Some Neo-Confucian thinkers acknowledged Mozi's contributions to logic and argumentation, and his sincere commitment to self-sacrifice for the public good. Wang Yangming's emphasis on the unity of knowledge and action occasionally echoed Mohist insistence on practical benefit, though Wang would have rejected the utilitarian framework.
