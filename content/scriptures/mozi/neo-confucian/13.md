Neo-Confucian reading of Mozi section 13, "On Ghosts and Spirits," evaluates Mozi's arguments against the metaphysical framework of principle (li) and material force (qi). The Neo-Confucian tradition holds that moral distinctions arise from the heavenly principle inherent in all things, not from calculations of utility. Mozi's consequentialism, from this perspective, mistakes the effects of virtue for its essence.

While acknowledging the logical rigor of Mozi's argumentation in this section, Neo-Confucian interpreters maintain that his fundamental error lies in treating morality as a matter of social engineering rather than the cultivation of innate moral nature. The Mencian conviction that human beings possess innate moral sprouts (si duan) renders Mozi's external program of reform unnecessary at best and harmful at worst.
