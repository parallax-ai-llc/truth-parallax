## Overview

The *Mozi* is the foundational text of the Mohist school, attributed to Mo Di (c. 470–391 BCE), known as Mozi ("Master Mo"). Writing in the early Warring States period, Mozi developed a radical philosophical program centered on universal love (jian ai), consequentialist ethics, opposition to aggressive warfare, and a utilitarian critique of Confucian ritual extravagance. The Mohist movement was organized as a tightly disciplined community with a quasi-military structure, making it unique among the Hundred Schools of Chinese philosophy.

The *Mozi* is notable for its systematic argumentation and its willingness to challenge the Confucian establishment on fundamental questions of ethics and social policy. Mozi argued that partial love — the Confucian emphasis on graded affection beginning with the family — was the root cause of social conflict, and that only impartial concern for all people could produce a harmonious society. His consequentialist method of evaluating practices by their benefit to the people anticipated utilitarian reasoning by two millennia.

## Structure

The fifteen sections of the Mozi address the following themes:

1. **Universal Love I** (Section 1): The principle of impartial concern for all people
2. **Universal Love II** (Section 2): Arguments and evidence for universal love
3. **Universal Love III** (Section 3): Responses to objections against universal love
4. **Against Offensive Warfare I** (Section 4): Moral and practical arguments against aggressive war
5. **Against Offensive Warfare II** (Section 5): Historical examples of war's destructiveness
6. **Against Offensive Warfare III** (Section 6): Defensive strategies and the duty of protection
7. **On Economy of Expenditure** (Section 7): Critique of wasteful spending and luxury
8. **Against Elaborate Funerals** (Section 8): Opposition to extravagant burial practices
9. **Against Music** (Section 9): Critique of music and entertainment as wasteful
10. **The Will of Heaven I** (Section 10): Heaven's desire for universal love and righteousness
11. **The Will of Heaven II** (Section 11): Heaven as moral judge and source of standards
12. **The Will of Heaven III** (Section 12): Heaven's rewards and punishments
13. **On Ghosts and Spirits** (Section 13): Arguments for the existence and moral role of spirits
14. **Identifying with the Superior** (Section 14): Political theory of unified moral authority
15. **Against Fatalism** (Section 15): Refutation of determinism and defense of human agency

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Confucian**: The primary philosophical rival, critiqued by Mencius as denying the special obligations of family yet acknowledged as sharing a commitment to benevolent governance
- **Neo-Confucian**: Largely dismissed as heterodox, though Mozi's logical rigor was occasionally admired
- **Daoist**: Criticized by Zhuangzi for excessive purposiveness, yet sharing opposition to Confucian ritualism
- **Buddhist-Chinese**: Mozi's universal compassion invites comparison with Buddhist karuna, though his utilitarian framework differs from Buddhist soteriology
- **Academic**: Studied for contributions to logic, epistemology, ethics, and the history of Chinese science and technology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Jian Ai (兼愛) | Universal, impartial love for all people without distinction |
| Consequentialism | Evaluating actions and practices by their benefit to the people |
| Anti-War | Systematic opposition to offensive warfare as the greatest social evil |
| Tian Zhi (天志) | The will of heaven as the objective standard of morality |
| Jie Yong (節用) | Economy of expenditure — opposing waste and luxury |
| Shang Tong (尚同) | Political unity through identification with moral authority |
