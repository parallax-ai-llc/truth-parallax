## Overview

Chinese Buddhist engagement with the Mozi has been shaped by the striking parallel between Mozi's universal love (jian ai) and the Buddhist ideal of universal compassion (karuna). Both traditions insist that moral concern should extend to all sentient beings without partiality, and both critique the Confucian emphasis on graded affection as a form of attachment. Yet the philosophical foundations and ultimate aims of the two traditions differ fundamentally.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist readers appreciate Mozi's insistence on impartial concern but note that his motivation remains utilitarian — he seeks social benefit and material welfare — while Buddhist compassion arises from insight into the interdependence of all beings and aims at liberation from suffering itself. Mozi's heaven-centered morality also differs from the Buddhist understanding of karma as an impersonal moral law.

## Key Comparisons

The most productive comparison concerns the question of motivation. Mozi argues that people should practice universal love because it produces social benefit; Buddhism teaches that compassion arises naturally from the recognition that all beings share the same fundamental nature. The Buddhist critique would be that Mozi's approach, however admirable, remains trapped in instrumental reasoning rather than arising from genuine insight into the nature of reality.
