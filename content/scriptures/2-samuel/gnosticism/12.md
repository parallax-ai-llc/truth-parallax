## Summary

2 Samuel 12 — Nathan's Rebuke. Gnostic hermeneutics provide a radically alternative framework for reading this chapter, viewing the events of David's reign through the lens of Demiurgic power, archontic control, and the imprisonment of the divine spark in material existence.

## Gnostic Reading

### Nathan's Parable

The parable of the rich man and the poor man's lamb is a masterpiece of indirect revelation — the prophet leads the king to condemn himself before recognizing his own guilt. In Gnostic terms, Nathan functions as a bearer of *gnosis* who uses narrative to penetrate the archontic ruler's self-deception. The moment of recognition — "You are the man!" — is a moment when the archontic illusion cracks and the truth becomes unavoidable.

### The Death of the Child

The death of David and Bathsheba's first child as divine punishment raises the sharpest Gnostic critique of the Demiurge's character. An innocent child dies for the father's sin — a deity who inflicts such punishment is, in Gnostic terms, not the true God but a jealous, retributive archon. The *Testimony of Truth* asks: "What kind of God is this?" The innocent suffer while the powerful receive forgiveness.

### David's Response to Death

David's behavior — fasting while the child lives, then rising and eating after the child dies — represents an unusual moment in the Davidic narrative. His acceptance of irreversible loss, his refusal to continue pleading with the Demiurge, hints at a wisdom that transcends the transactional relationship. "I will go to him, but he will not return to me" acknowledges the finality of the material world's losses.

### The Birth of Solomon

Solomon's birth from the union of David and Bathsheba — a relationship born of violence and adultery — illustrates the Gnostic observation that the Demiurge's purposes are served through morally compromised means. The future builder of the Demiurge's Temple emerges from the wreckage of sin and punishment.
