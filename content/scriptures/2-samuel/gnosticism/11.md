## David and Bathsheba

For Gnostic interpreters, the Bathsheba narrative strips away the illusion of righteous kingship more effectively than any prophetic critique. Here the "man after God's own heart" is revealed as an agent of the Demiurge exercising raw material power: lust, deception, and murder. The chapter is not a tale of exceptional sin but a revelation of what earthly power always is.

## Gnostic Reading

### Kingship Unmasked

Orthodox traditions treat 2 Samuel 11 as an anomaly — the righteous king's tragic fall. Gnostic interpretation sees it as a revelation of the norm. Earthly power, established by the Demiurge, inevitably corrupts because it is rooted in the material realm where desire and violence rule. David's sin is not an exception but the essence of archontic governance.

### The Abuse of Power as Archontic Pattern

The archons in Gnostic mythology exercise power through coercion, deception, and violence — precisely the pattern David follows. He sees, desires, takes, deceives, and kills. This is not merely human sin but the replication of the Demiurge's own pattern: creating without consent, demanding obedience, and destroying what resists.

### Bathsheba as the Captive Spark

In one possible Gnostic reading, Bathsheba — "taken" by the king, having no voice in the narrative — represents the divine spark imprisoned by archontic power. She is acted upon, not acting; the material power structure uses her without regard for her own being.

### The Silence of the Text

What is most striking to Gnostic readers is the narrator's restraint: "the thing that David had done displeased the LORD" (v. 27). The "LORD" who is displeased is the same deity who established David's power. The Demiurge is displeased with his own agent — a contradiction that Gnostic thought resolves by recognizing the inherent instability of the archontic order.
