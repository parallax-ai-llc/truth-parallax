## Summary

2 Samuel 13 — Amnon and Tamar. Gnostic hermeneutics provide a radically alternative framework for reading this chapter, viewing the events of David's reign through the lens of Demiurgic power, archontic control, and the imprisonment of the divine spark in material existence.

## Gnostic Reading

### Amnon's Desire

Amnon's lust for Tamar represents the hylic condition at its most destructive — desire that reduces another person to an object of consumption. In Gnostic anthropology, the soul trapped in matter is driven by passions that are not its own but are implanted by the archons. Amnon's "love" that turns instantly to hatred after satisfaction reveals desire as an archontic mechanism, not a genuine expression of the divine spark.

### The Rape of Tamar

Tamar's violation is one of the most disturbing passages in Scripture. Gnostic reading sees in it the material world's fundamental violence against the feminine — against *Sophia*, the wisdom principle. Tamar's wisdom ("this kind of thing should not be done in Israel"), her pleading, and her devastation represent the Sophia figure's suffering within the Demiurge's creation. Her torn robe of many colors symbolizes the destruction of her identity within the material order.

### David's Failed Response

David's anger without action (13:21) epitomizes the failure of the archontic system to address its own violence. The king — the Demiurge's agent — has authority but does not exercise it to protect the vulnerable. The system that produces violence cannot correct violence because it would have to dismantle itself.

### Absalom's Cold Vengeance

Absalom's calculated murder of Amnon two years later represents not justice but another iteration of the cycle of violence. In Gnostic terms, material retribution perpetuates rather than resolves the archontic pattern. Each act of vengeance generates the next crisis.
