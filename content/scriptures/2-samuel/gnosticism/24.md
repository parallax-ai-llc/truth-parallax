## The Census and the Plague

The final chapter of 2 Samuel provides Gnostic interpreters with a concentrated illustration of the Demiurge's character: a deity who incites sin, punishes the innocent for the guilt of another, and demands sacrifice to appease his wrath. For Gnostic thought, this chapter is not the conclusion of a story of divine faithfulness but a final revelation of the nature of the god who rules the material world.

## Gnostic Reading

### God Inciting Sin

"The anger of the LORD was kindled against Israel, and he incited David to number them" — this verse is devastating for orthodox theology and illuminating for Gnostic interpreters. The Demiurge provokes the sin and then punishes it. In the *Testimony of Truth*, exactly this kind of divine behavior is cited as evidence that the biblical God is not the true, transcendent deity.

### Collective Punishment

Seventy thousand die for David's census — a punishment that falls on the people, not on the king. Gnostic cosmology explains this as the nature of archontic power: the archons do not operate according to justice but according to the arbitrary exercise of force. The material world is not a realm of moral order but of capricious power.

### The Demand for Sacrifice

The plague stops only when David builds an altar and offers sacrifice. In Gnostic reading, this confirms the Demiurge's essential character: he feeds on worship and sacrifice. The *Hypostasis of the Archons* describes how the archons require offerings to sustain their power. David's altar is not an act of genuine communion with the divine but a transaction with the Demiurge.

### "I Will Not Offer What Costs Me Nothing"

David's insistence on paying for the threshing floor takes on an ironic meaning: the Demiurge's economy requires payment. Nothing is free in the material realm. True divinity, by contrast, gives without demanding payment — the gift of *gnosis* is freely available to those who awaken.

## Theological Significance

The book of 2 Samuel ends as it began — with death, power, and sacrifice. For Gnostic interpreters, the entire arc confirms that the material world under the Demiurge's governance is a cycle of violence and appeasement from which only *gnosis* offers genuine escape.
