## Nathan's Rebuke and David's Repentance

Catholic tradition holds this chapter as the paradigmatic narrative of sin, prophetic confrontation, repentance, and the mercy of God. Nathan's parable, David's self-condemnation, the prophetic word of judgment, and David's immediate confession form the theological foundation for the Sacrament of Reconciliation.

## Catholic Interpretive Tradition

### The Prophetic Office

Nathan's courage in confronting the king illustrates the prophetic office that the Church inherits. Catholic teaching holds that bishops and the Pope exercise a prophetic role in speaking moral truth to political power — a role modeled here by Nathan.

### The Sacrament of Reconciliation

David's confession — "I have sinned against the LORD" — and Nathan's response — "The LORD also has put away your sin" — are foundational to Catholic sacramental theology. The elements of the sacrament are present: contrition (David's confession), confession (verbal acknowledgment), and absolution (Nathan's declaration of forgiveness). Psalm 51, traditionally connected to this moment, is the Church's great penitential prayer.

### Temporal Punishment

Catholic theology distinguishes between the forgiveness of sin and the remission of temporal punishment. David is forgiven ("you shall not die") but still suffers consequences (the child's death, the sword not departing from his house). This distinction undergirds the Catholic doctrine of purgatory and indulgences.

### Solomon's Birth (12:24-25)

The birth of Solomon from the union of David and Bathsheba — now married — demonstrates God's capacity to bring good from evil. Catholic theology sees here a foreshadowing of the paschal mystery: from death comes new life.

## Liturgical Context

Psalm 51, born from this moment of repentance, is prayed throughout the liturgical year in the Liturgy of the Hours and during penitential seasons, especially Lent. It remains the Church's most beloved expression of contrition.
