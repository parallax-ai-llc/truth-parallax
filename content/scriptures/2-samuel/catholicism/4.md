## Summary

2 Samuel 4 — Murder of Ish-bosheth. Catholic interpretation of this chapter draws on Sacred Scripture, Sacred Tradition, and the teaching authority of the Magisterium to unfold its theological meaning within the larger narrative of salvation history.

## Catholic Interpretive Tradition

### Patristic Reading

The Church Fathers approached this passage within the fourfold sense of Scripture. The literal sense establishes the historical events of David's reign, while the allegorical sense reveals how these events prefigure Christ and the Church. The moral sense draws lessons for Christian living, and the anagogical sense points toward the eschatological fulfillment of God's promises.

**St. Augustine** in *City of God* situated David's story within the two cities — the city of God and the earthly city — reading the political events as illustrations of the tension between divine purpose and human ambition.

### Typological Significance

Catholic typology reads the events of David's reign as shadows of the greater realities fulfilled in Christ. David's kingship prefigures Christ's; his failures point to the need for a perfect King; his covenant anticipates the New Covenant sealed in Christ's blood.

### Moral Theology

This chapter offers material for Catholic moral reflection on the virtues and vices, the use and abuse of power, the nature of justice, and the role of divine providence in human affairs. The Catechism's teaching on conscience, sin, and grace finds illustration in the Davidic narrative.

## Liturgical Context

Texts from 2 Samuel appear in the Roman Lectionary and the Liturgy of the Hours, connecting the Old Testament narrative to the Church's ongoing prayer and worship.
