## David and Bathsheba

Catholic moral theology has engaged extensively with 2 Samuel 11 as a case study in the anatomy of sin. The chapter traces the progression from idleness to lust, from lust to adultery, from adultery to deception, and from deception to murder — illustrating the traditional Catholic teaching on the escalation of sin.

## Catholic Interpretive Tradition

### The Anatomy of Sin

Catholic commentators since Augustine have analyzed David's fall through the lens of concupiscence and the disordered will. The Catechism identifies the root sins: lust of the eyes (seeing Bathsheba), lust of the flesh (the act of adultery), and pride of life (the abuse of royal power to cover sin).

**St. Thomas Aquinas** discussed David's sin in the context of moral theology, noting how venial sins of negligence (remaining in Jerusalem) opened the door to mortal sin.

### Royal Power and Moral Responsibility

Catholic social teaching draws from this narrative the principle that authority carries proportional moral responsibility. David's sin is compounded by his position: he is not merely a private individual but the shepherd of God's people.

### Bathsheba's Agency

Catholic feminist theologians have raised important questions about Bathsheba's role. The text emphasizes David's initiative — "he sent messengers and took her" — suggesting the coercive dimension of royal power over a subject.

## Moral Theology

The chapter illustrates several principles of Catholic moral theology:

- **Occasions of Sin**: David's idleness created the occasion
- **Concupiscence**: Disordered desire leading to action
- **Mortal Sin**: Grave matter, full knowledge, deliberate consent
- **Cover-up as Compounding Sin**: Deception and murder follow inevitably
- **The Necessity of Confession**: Only prophetic confrontation (ch. 12) breaks the cycle
