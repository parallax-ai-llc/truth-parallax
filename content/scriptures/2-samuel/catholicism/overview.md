## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 2 Samuel draws on Sacred Scripture, Sacred Tradition, and the Magisterium. David's reign is read through a typological lens in which the Davidic kingdom prefigures the Kingdom of God and the Church. The Davidic covenant (ch. 7) is understood as a crucial stage in salvation history, pointing toward Christ's eternal kingship.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Typological Reading
The Church Fathers and medieval exegetes read David as a type of Christ. His anointing, his conquest of Jerusalem, his bringing the Ark to Zion, and his covenant all prefigure aspects of Christ's work and the Church's life.

### Moral Theology
David's sin with Bathsheba and its consequences serve as a foundational case study in Catholic moral theology. The tradition emphasizes the gravity of sin, the necessity of repentance, the role of prophetic rebuke (Nathan), and the reality of temporal punishment even after forgiveness.

### Liturgical Context
Texts from 2 Samuel appear in the Liturgy of the Hours and the Lectionary. David's canticle (ch. 22) and the Davidic covenant narrative are proclaimed in liturgical settings.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Davidic Covenant as Ecclesial**: The promise to David's house is fulfilled in Christ's Church
- **Mary as the Ark**: The Ark narrative (ch. 6) is read typologically as prefiguring Mary bearing Christ
- **Penance and Reconciliation**: David's repentance as a model for the Sacrament of Reconciliation
- **Just War**: David's military campaigns examined through Catholic just war tradition
