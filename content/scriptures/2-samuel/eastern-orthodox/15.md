## Summary

2 Samuel 15 — Absalom's Conspiracy. Eastern Orthodox interpretation of this chapter is rooted in the patristic consensus and the liturgical life of the Church, reading the historical narrative as part of the divine *oikonomia* — God's plan for the salvation and deification of humanity.

## Patristic Reading

### The Fathers' Approach

The Greek and Syriac Fathers read the Davidic narrative on multiple levels simultaneously. **St. John Chrysostom** attended to the moral and pastoral dimensions, drawing practical lessons for the Christian life. **Theodoret of Cyrus** provided historical commentary while noting typological connections to Christ and the Church.

The events described in this chapter are understood not merely as ancient history but as part of the ongoing divine pedagogy — God teaching his people through the successes and failures of their leaders.

### Typological Significance

Orthodox typology reads David's experiences as anticipations of Christ's own journey. The sufferings, victories, and relationships of David point forward to their fulfillment in the life, death, and resurrection of the incarnate Word.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine Providence**: God's *pronoia* (providential care) guides events toward their appointed end
- **Theosis**: The narrative illustrates the human capacity for both communion with and alienation from God
- **Repentance and Restoration**: The pattern of fall and return that characterizes the Christian life
- **Cosmic Significance**: Human events participate in the larger drama of creation's journey toward transfiguration

## Liturgical Connection

Passages from 2 Samuel appear in the *parimiae* (Old Testament readings) of the Orthodox liturgical cycle, connecting the Davidic narrative to the Church's worship and the rhythm of the liturgical year.
