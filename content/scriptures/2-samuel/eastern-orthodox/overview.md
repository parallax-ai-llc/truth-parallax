## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Samuel is rooted in the consensus of the Church Fathers and the liturgical life of the Church. David is understood not primarily through juridical categories of guilt and punishment, but through the lens of *theosis* and the divine economy. His rise, fall, and repentance illustrate the human journey toward union with God and the consequences of turning away from that path.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Exegesis
The Greek and Syriac Fathers provide the primary interpretive framework:
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Homilies emphasizing David's repentance as a model for all Christians
- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Poetic-typological reading of David's life
- **Theodoret of Cyrus**: Historical-literal commentary with theological application
- **St. Ambrose of Milan**: *Apologia David* — defense of David as penitent

### Liturgical Context
2 Samuel texts appear in the *parimiae* (Old Testament readings) during Great Lent and on feast days. David's penitential psalm (Psalm 50/51, connected to 2 Samuel 12) is central to Orthodox worship, recited at nearly every Matins service.

## Theological Emphases

- **Repentance as Renewal**: David's repentance (ch. 12) is paradigmatic — not merely juridical forgiveness but ontological restoration
- **Ancestral Sin**: The consequences flowing through David's family illustrate how sin distorts human nature across generations
- **The Ark and Divine Presence**: The Ark narrative (ch. 6) prefigures the Incarnation and the Theotokos
- **Kingship as Service**: David's anointing points to Christ the true King who serves
