## David and Bathsheba

The biblical narrative of David's adultery with Bathsheba and murder of Uriah is categorically rejected by Islamic theology. Prophets in Islam are protected from major sins (*ismah*), and the biblical account is considered an example of *tahrif* (textual corruption). The Quran provides an alternative account in Surah Sad (38:21-25) involving two disputants who test Dawud.

## Islamic Interpretation

### The Quranic Account (Sad 38:21-25)

The Quran describes two disputants who climbed over the wall of Dawud's private chamber. One claimed the other had ninety-nine ewes while he had only one, and asked Dawud to judge between them. Dawud ruled in favor of the plaintiff, then realized he had been tested: "And David became certain that We had tried him, and he asked forgiveness of his Lord and fell down bowing and turned in repentance" (38:24).

### Rejection of the Biblical Account

Muslim scholars uniformly reject the biblical narrative of 2 Samuel 11:

- **Ibn Kathir** explicitly states that the story of David committing adultery and ordering murder contradicts the prophetic character. He attributes it to fabrications by the People of the Book.
- **Al-Qurtubi** in his tafsir of Surah Sad warns against accepting Isra'iliyyat that contradict the principle of prophetic infallibility.
- **Al-Razi** argues that the Quranic test was a test of judicial wisdom, not a cover for moral failure.

### The Nature of Dawud's "Sin"

Even in the Quranic account, Dawud seeks forgiveness. Islamic scholars debate what exactly he needed forgiveness for:
- Judging hastily without hearing the other party
- Being alarmed by the unexpected visitors (lack of trust in God's protection)
- Some scholars see a minor lapse in judicial procedure, not a moral transgression

## Theological Themes

- **Prophetic Infallibility**: Prophets are protected from adultery and murder
- **Textual Corruption**: The biblical account demonstrates *tahrif*
- **Just Judgment**: The episode is about judicial fairness, not sexual sin
- **Immediate Repentance**: Dawud's swift turning to God is exemplary
