## Overview

Islam does not regard the books of Samuel in their current form as fully preserved divine revelation, but recognizes David (Dawud) as one of the great prophets and kings to whom God gave the Zabur (Psalms). The Quran and Hadith contain narratives that parallel and often diverge from 2 Samuel, and these serve as the corrective standard for Islamic interpretation. Dawud is presented as a model of just kingship, devotion, and repentance.

## Relationship to the Quran

Key narratives from 2 Samuel that appear in the Quran:

| 2 Samuel Narrative | Quranic Parallel | Key Surahs |
|-------------------|-----------------|------------|
| David as king | Dawud given dominion and wisdom | Sad 38:17-26, Al-Baqarah 2:251 |
| David and Bathsheba | Two disputants test Dawud | Sad 38:21-25 |
| David's psalms | Zabur given to Dawud | An-Nisa 4:163, Al-Isra 17:55 |
| David's kingdom | Mountains and birds praise with Dawud | Saba 34:10, Al-Anbiya 21:79 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
Islamic theology holds that prophets are protected from major sin. The Bathsheba narrative is reinterpreted: the Quran (Sad 38:21-25) presents two disputants who come to Dawud, and he realizes he has been tested. Muslim scholars do not accept the biblical account of adultery and murder as historical.

### *Tahrif* (Textual Alteration)
Passages showing prophets in morally compromising situations are attributed to textual corruption of the original scripture.

### Just Kingship
Dawud is presented as the ideal ruler who judges with justice and equity: "O Dawud, indeed We have made you a successor upon the earth, so judge between the people in truth" (Quran 38:26).

## Theological Emphases

- **Dawud as Prophet-King**: Combines prophetic authority with political rule
- **The Zabur**: David's psalms as genuine divine revelation
- **Repentance**: Even in the Quranic account, Dawud's immediate repentance is exemplary
- **Praise and Worship**: Mountains and birds joining Dawud in praise of God
