## The Census and the Plague

The account of David's census and the resulting plague is approached cautiously in Islamic tradition. The idea that God "incited" David to sin and then punished the people contradicts Islamic theology on multiple points: God does not incite to sin, prophets are protected from major sins, and the innocent are not punished for another's transgression.

## Islamic Interpretation

### Theological Objections

Muslim scholars raise several concerns with the biblical account:

- **God inciting sin**: Islamic theology holds that God tests but does not incite to sin. The Quran states: "God does not wrong people at all, but it is the people who wrong themselves" (10:44)
- **Collective punishment for individual sin**: The plague killing 70,000 for David's census contradicts the Quranic principle: "No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another" (6:164)
- **Prophetic fallibility**: The degree of sin attributed to David exceeds what Islamic *ismah* allows

### Isra'iliyyat Assessment

**Ibn Kathir** classifies this narrative among the Isra'iliyyat that contradicts Quranic principles and should therefore be rejected. **Al-Tabari**, who is more permissive in recording such traditions, includes the narrative but without endorsement.

### The Temple Site

Islamic tradition does acknowledge the significance of the sacred site in Jerusalem. The Noble Sanctuary (al-Haram al-Sharif) is the third holiest site in Islam, and the Night Journey (*Isra*) brought Muhammad to "al-Masjid al-Aqsa." However, Islamic tradition connects the site's sanctity to broader prophetic history rather than specifically to David's purchase.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine Justice**: God is perfectly just and does not cause sin
- **Prophetic Protection**: Dawud is shielded from the degree of sin described
- **Sacred Sites**: Jerusalem's holiness in Islamic tradition
- **Textual Criticism**: The narrative illustrates why Muslims consider the biblical text altered
