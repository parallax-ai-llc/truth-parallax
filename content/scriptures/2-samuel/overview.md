## Overview

2 Samuel is the tenth book of the Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament, continuing the narrative begun in 1 Samuel. In the Hebrew tradition, 1 and 2 Samuel were originally a single work known as *Shmuel*. The book chronicles the reign of King David from his accession after Saul's death to the final years of his rule, encompassing both his greatest triumphs and his most devastating failures.

## Structure

2 Samuel is generally divided into four major sections:

1. **David's Rise to Power** (Chapters 1-5): From Saul's death to David's coronation over all Israel and conquest of Jerusalem
2. **David's Reign at Its Height** (Chapters 6-10): The Ark's arrival in Jerusalem, the Davidic covenant, and military victories
3. **David's Sin and Its Consequences** (Chapters 11-20): The Bathsheba affair, Nathan's rebuke, Amnon and Tamar, Absalom's rebellion
4. **Appendices** (Chapters 21-24): Miscellaneous narratives, psalms, and lists

## Cross-Traditional Significance

2 Samuel holds profound significance across multiple religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: Central to understanding the Davidic monarchy, messianic expectation, and the theology of the Tanakh
- **Christianity**: The Davidic covenant (ch. 7) is foundational to Christology and messianic theology
- **Islam**: David (Dawud) is recognized as a prophet and king given the Zabur (Psalms)
- **Gnosticism**: The tensions between earthly kingship and divine authority resonate with Gnostic themes of power and knowledge

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Covenant | God's unconditional promise to David's dynasty (ch. 7) |
| Kingship | The nature, responsibilities, and dangers of royal power |
| Sin and Consequence | David's adultery and murder, and the unraveling that follows |
| Succession | The question of who will succeed David on the throne |
| Hesed | Covenant loyalty and faithfulness, exemplified in David's treatment of Mephibosheth |
