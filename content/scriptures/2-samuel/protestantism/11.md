## David and Bathsheba

2 Samuel 11 narrates David's most devastating moral failure. While his army besieges Rabbah, David remains in Jerusalem, sees Bathsheba bathing, commits adultery with her, and when she becomes pregnant, orchestrates the death of her husband Uriah the Hittite. The chapter is a masterpiece of understated narrative art, allowing the events to speak for themselves without explicit moral commentary — that comes in chapter 12.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "In the spring, when kings go out to battle" (11:1)

The opening verse is loaded with irony. David should be with his army, but he has stayed behind. **Davis** notes that the Hebrew can also be read "at the return of the year," but either way, David is where a king should not be.

### The Sequence of Sin (11:2-5)

"He saw... he sent... he inquired... he took" — the verbs accelerate, each step drawing David deeper. Protestant commentators trace the progression from temptation to action, noting the abuse of royal power. **Brueggemann** emphasizes that this is not merely personal sin but the exploitation of power.

### The Cover-Up and Uriah's Murder (11:6-27)

David's increasingly desperate schemes to cover his sin culminate in ordering Uriah placed where the fighting is heaviest. The narrative's restraint is devastating: "But the thing that David had done displeased the LORD" (v. 27).

## Theological Significance

- **The Abuse of Power**: David's sin is enabled by unchecked royal authority
- **Total Depravity**: Even a man after God's own heart is capable of grievous sin
- **Providence**: God sees what is hidden; no sin remains concealed
- **Consequences**: The sword will never depart from David's house (ch. 12)
- **Typological Contrast**: Where David fails, Christ the true King is faithful
