## The Ark Brought to Jerusalem

David's transfer of the Ark of the Covenant to Jerusalem is a pivotal moment in biblical theology. The narrative includes the death of Uzzah, David's fear, the Ark's stay at Obed-edom's house, and David's uninhibited dancing before the LORD as the Ark enters Jerusalem. Michal's contempt for David's dancing concludes the chapter with a note of domestic discord and divine judgment.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Death of Uzzah (6:1-11)

When the oxen stumble and Uzzah reaches out to steady the Ark, God strikes him dead. Protestant interpreters wrestle with this passage. **Luther** saw it as a reminder of God's holiness that cannot be approached casually. **Davis** notes that the proper method of transport (carried by Levites, not on a cart) had been prescribed in the Torah.

### David's Dancing (6:12-19)

David's ecstatic worship — dancing before the LORD "with all his might" wearing only a linen ephod — presents a king who abandons royal dignity for uninhibited praise. Reformed commentators emphasize that true worship transcends social convention.

### Michal's Contempt (6:20-23)

Michal's scorn and David's sharp reply create one of Scripture's most poignant domestic scenes. Her barrenness is noted as divine judgment. Protestant interpreters see a contrast between external dignity (Michal) and genuine devotion (David).

## Theological Significance

- **God's Holiness**: The Uzzah incident underscores that God is not to be approached on human terms
- **Worship**: David's dancing becomes a paradigm for wholehearted, embodied worship
- **The Ark and Christ's Presence**: Typologically, the Ark prefigures Christ dwelling among his people
- **Cost of Discipleship**: True devotion may cost social standing and relationships
