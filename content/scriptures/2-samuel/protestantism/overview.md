## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 2 Samuel is shaped by the Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* and the conviction that the Old Testament narratives, while historically situated, point forward to their fulfillment in Christ. David is read as both a historical king and a type of Christ, with his reign, failures, and covenant forming a crucial link in the chain of redemptive history.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Redemptive-Historical
Reformed interpreters read 2 Samuel within the arc of salvation history. The Davidic covenant (ch. 7) is the pivotal text, establishing the dynasty that will ultimately produce the Messiah. David's failures (chs. 11-12) demonstrate the need for a greater King.

### Moral-Exemplary
Many Protestant preachers and teachers draw moral lessons from David's life: his faith, his repentance (Psalm 51), his failures, and the consequences of sin within families and nations.

### Literary-Theological
Modern Protestant scholars attend to the sophisticated narrative artistry of 2 Samuel, recognizing how the author uses irony, wordplay, and structural patterns to convey theological meaning.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Davidic Covenant**: An unconditional, eternal promise that grounds messianic expectation
- **Sin and Grace**: David's adultery and murder met by Nathan's rebuke and God's discipline
- **Typology**: David as a type of Christ; Jerusalem as a type of the heavenly city
- **Sovereignty of God**: God's purposes advance even through human failure
