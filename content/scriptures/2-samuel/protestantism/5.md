## David Becomes King over All Israel

After seven and a half years as king of Judah alone, all the tribes of Israel come to David at Hebron, acknowledging him as their rightful king. David then conquers Jerusalem from the Jebusites, making it his capital — a stroke of political genius that united north and south under a neutral city.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Tribal Covenant (5:1-5)

The elders cite three reasons for accepting David: shared kinship ("we are your bone and flesh"), his proven military leadership, and the LORD's promise. **Calvin** emphasized that David's kingship rested on divine appointment, not merely popular will.

### The Conquest of Jerusalem (5:6-10)

The Jebusite taunt — "You will not come in here; even the blind and the lame will turn you away" — and David's response have generated extensive scholarly discussion. Protestant interpreters see in Jerusalem's capture the establishment of the city that will become central to God's purposes in both testaments.

### Davidic Expansion (5:11-25)

David's recognition by Hiram of Tyre and his victories over the Philistines confirm God's blessing on his reign. The "sound of marching in the tops of the balsam trees" (v. 24) signals divine intervention in battle.

## Theological Significance

- **Unified Kingdom**: David's united monarchy as a type of the unity Christ brings
- **Jerusalem/Zion**: The establishment of God's chosen city, foundational for biblical theology
- **Theocratic Kingship**: The king rules under God, not independently
- **Providence**: The long journey from shepherd boy to king demonstrates God's faithfulness
