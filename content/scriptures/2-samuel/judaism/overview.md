## Overview

In Judaism, 2 Samuel (*Shmuel Bet*) belongs to the Nevi'im Rishonim (Former Prophets) section of the Tanakh. The book is not merely historical narrative but prophetic literature, conveying divine instruction through the story of David's reign. Jewish interpretation spans from the Talmud and Midrash through the medieval commentators (Rishonim) to modern scholarship.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
**Rashi** (1040-1105) provides close textual commentary, often resolving ambiguities by reference to Talmudic discussions. **Radak** (David Kimchi, 1160-1235) offers more grammatically focused interpretation.

### Derash (Homiletical)
Midrashic traditions elaborate on the narrative extensively. The Talmud (Shabbat 56a) famously debates David's culpability in the Bathsheba episode, with some sages arguing that Uriah had given Bathsheba a conditional divorce before going to war.

### Mystical
Kabbalistic tradition associates David with the sefirah of Malkhut (Sovereignty), the final sefirah that receives from all the others. David's life becomes a map of the dynamics within the divine realm.

## Theological Emphases

- **Messianic Expectation**: The Davidic covenant (ch. 7) is the foundation of Jewish messianic hope — the Mashiach will be a descendant of David
- **Teshuvah (Repentance)**: David's repentance in chapter 12 is paradigmatic for the Jewish understanding of return to God
- **Kingship in Halakhah**: The laws of the king (*Hilkhot Melakhim*) are developed partly from David's example
- **Historical Memory**: The narrative preserves Israel's political and spiritual history

| Radak (David Kimchi) | 12th-13th century | Grammatical-contextual |
| Ralbag (Gersonides) | 14th century | Philosophical-ethical lessons (*to'alot*) |
