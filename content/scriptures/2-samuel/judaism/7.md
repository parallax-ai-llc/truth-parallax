## The Davidic Covenant

2 Samuel 7 contains the divine promise to David's house — the foundation of Jewish messianic expectation. God's promise that David's throne will be established forever (*ad olam*) becomes the basis for the belief in a future Mashiach (Messiah) who will be a descendant of David.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Nathan's Oracle

**Rashi** explains the wordplay on "house": David proposes to build a house (temple) for God, but God will build a house (dynasty) for David. Rashi connects this to the principle that those who seek to honor God are honored by God in return.

**Radak** provides detailed analysis of the covenant's terms, distinguishing between the conditional elements (individual kings may be punished) and the unconditional element (the dynasty itself will never be permanently removed). This distinction is crucial for understanding how the covenant survived the Babylonian exile and the end of the Davidic monarchy.

### Messianic Interpretation

The promise "your throne shall be established forever" (7:16) is the primary biblical basis for the Jewish doctrine of the Mashiach. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 97a-99a) discusses the messianic age extensively, and the Davidic covenant is always the starting point. **Rambam** (Maimonides) in *Hilkhot Melakhim* (Laws of Kings) 11:1 codifies the belief: the Mashiach will be a king from the house of David, from the seed of Solomon.

### The Prayer of David (7:18-29)

**Abarbanel** noted that David's prayer is remarkable for its humility. Despite being promised an eternal dynasty, David does not become arrogant but asks "Who am I?" This becomes a model for the proper response to divine election in Jewish thought.

## Haftarah Connection

2 Samuel 7:1-17 serves as the Haftarah reading for certain Shabbatot, linking the Davidic covenant to the weekly Torah reading.
