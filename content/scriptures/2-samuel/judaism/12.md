## Nathan's Rebuke

The prophetic confrontation between Nathan and David is central to Jewish theology of repentance. Nathan's parable of the poor man's lamb, David's self-condemning verdict, and the devastating "You are the man!" constitute the definitive biblical model of prophetic rebuke (*tokhachah*).

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Art of Rebuke

The Talmud (Arakhin 16b) discusses the mitzvah of *tokhachah* (reproof) and Nathan's approach is considered the exemplary model. By using a parable rather than direct accusation, Nathan enabled David to see his own sin from outside — a technique that disarms defensiveness and activates the conscience. **Abarbanel** noted that Nathan's genius was in engaging David's sense of justice before revealing that David himself was the object of judgment.

### "I Have Sinned Against the LORD" (12:13)

David's immediate, unqualified confession — only four words in Hebrew (*chatati la-Adonai*) — is held as the model of true *teshuvah*. The Midrash contrasts David's response with Saul's self-justifying response when confronted by Samuel (1 Samuel 15:20-21). David does not make excuses; Saul does. This is why David's dynasty endures while Saul's does not.

### The Death of the Child (12:14-23)

David's behavior — fasting while the child lives, then rising to eat after the child dies — puzzled his servants. **Rashi** explains David's logic: while the child lived, prayer and fasting might move God to mercy; once the child died, mourning practices were appropriate but fasting was pointless. **Radak** sees in this a model of accepting divine judgment while maintaining faith.

### Solomon — Jedidiah (12:24-25)

The birth of Solomon and his divine name Jedidiah ("beloved of the LORD") demonstrates the rabbinic principle that God's love persists through judgment. The Davidic line continues precisely through the circumstances of David's sin and repentance.

## Theological Themes

- **Tokhachah**: The obligation and art of righteous rebuke
- **Teshuvah**: The power of immediate, unqualified repentance
- **Divine Justice**: Consequences persist even after forgiveness
- **Messianic Lineage**: Solomon's birth from the context of sin and grace
