## David and Bathsheba

The narrative of David and Bathsheba is among the most extensively discussed passages in rabbinic literature. The Talmud's treatment (Shabbat 56a) is particularly notable for its attempt to mitigate David's culpability while acknowledging his sin.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Talmudic Defense (Shabbat 56a)

The Gemara famously states: "Whoever says David sinned is mistaken" — a provocative assertion followed by the explanation that soldiers going to war would give their wives a conditional bill of divorce (*get*), meaning Bathsheba was technically unmarried. However, the Talmud immediately qualifies this by acknowledging that David still sinned in other respects: taking another man's wife (even if technically divorced) and orchestrating Uriah's death.

**Rashi** follows the Talmudic line, noting the conditional divorce but emphasizing that David's actions were still gravely wrong. **Radak** takes a more straightforward approach, accepting the plain meaning of the text: David committed adultery and murder.

### The Moral Lessons

**Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws multiple *to'alot* (moral lessons):
- A person should not remain idle when they should be engaged in their responsibilities
- One should not place oneself in situations of temptation
- Sin leads to greater sin as one attempts to cover the first transgression
- Even the greatest person is susceptible to the evil inclination (*yetzer hara*)

### David as Model of Teshuvah

Despite the gravity of the sin, Jewish tradition ultimately holds David as the paradigm of *teshuvah* (repentance). The Midrash Tehillim states that God specifically allowed David to sin so that individuals who sin might say: "David sinned and repented — how much more so should I repent."

## Theological Themes

- **The Yetzer Hara**: Even the righteous struggle with the evil inclination
- **Teshuvah**: Repentance is always possible, no matter how grave the sin
- **Kingship and Accountability**: The king is not above the Torah's moral demands
