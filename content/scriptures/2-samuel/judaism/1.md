## David Learns of Saul's Death

The opening chapter of Shmuel Bet presents David receiving the report of Saul's death from an Amalekite who claims to have administered the final blow. David responds with grief and the great lament known as *Kinah* (elegy) for Saul and Jonathan.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Amalekite's Testimony

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 95b) and midrashic sources discuss the discrepancy between the Amalekite's account (claiming he killed Saul) and the narrative of 1 Samuel 31 (Saul falling on his own sword). **Rashi** explains that the Amalekite fabricated his story, hoping to gain David's favor by claiming credit for Saul's death. **Radak** (David Kimchi) agrees, noting that the Amalekite's lie led to his own execution — a case of divine irony.

### The Lament (*Kinah*)

The elegy "How the Mighty Have Fallen" (*Eikh Naflu Gibborim*) is studied both as literature and as halakhic model. **Ralbag** (Gersonides) draws the moral lesson (*to'elet*) that a righteous leader publicly honors even those who wronged him, establishing peace and unity.

The instruction to "teach the sons of Judah the bow" (1:18) has generated extensive discussion. **Rashi** interprets "the bow" as the name of the song itself. **Radak** suggests it refers to teaching archery as a practical response to the Philistine military advantage.

### David and Jonathan

The friendship between David and Jonathan — described as love surpassing the love of women — is treated in Jewish tradition as the paradigmatic example of *ahavah she-einah teluyah ba-davar* (love not dependent on an external cause), a concept from Pirkei Avot 5:16.

## Theological Themes

- **Respect for Authority**: David's refusal to celebrate the death of the anointed king
- **The Power of Lament**: Jewish tradition values the expression of grief as a spiritual practice
- **Truth and Falsehood**: The Amalekite's lie leads to his own destruction
