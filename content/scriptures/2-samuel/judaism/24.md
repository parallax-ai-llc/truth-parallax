## The Census and the Plague

The final chapter of Shmuel Bet raises profound theological questions about divine anger, human sin, and the establishment of the temple site. Jewish tradition identifies the threshing floor of Araunah (Aravnah/Ornan) with the site of the future Beit HaMikdash (Holy Temple).

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Why Was the Census Sinful?

The Talmud and commentators offer several explanations:
- **Rashi**: David's sin was counting the people directly rather than using the half-shekel method prescribed in Exodus 30:12, which would have avoided a plague
- **Radak**: The census reflected David's pride and reliance on military power rather than trust in God
- **Abarbanel**: The census was unnecessary and burdensome to the people, an act of royal overreach
- **Ramban** (on the Torah): Direct counting invites the "evil eye" (*ayin hara*) — a superstitious concern that reflects deep theological intuitions about the limits of human knowledge and control

### "Fall Into the Hand of the LORD" (24:14)

David's choice of plague over famine or military defeat is explained by **Rashi** as reflecting David's knowledge that God's mercy (*rachamim*) is greater than human mercy. Even God's punishment is tempered by compassion.

### The Threshing Floor and the Temple

The Talmud (Zevachim 62a) identifies the threshing floor of Araunah with Mount Moriah — the site of the Akedah (binding of Isaac) and the future Holy Temple. This identification links three great moments of sacrifice and divine encounter across Genesis, 2 Samuel, and the temple tradition.

David's insistence on paying full price establishes the legal acquisition of the site, a detail that matters for both halakhic and theological reasons: the holiest site in Judaism must be acquired through legitimate means.

## Theological Themes

- **The Temple Site**: The chain from Moriah to the threshing floor to the Temple
- **Divine Justice and Mercy**: Even in punishment, God's compassion prevails
- **The Census and Humility**: The danger of counting and categorizing what belongs to God
