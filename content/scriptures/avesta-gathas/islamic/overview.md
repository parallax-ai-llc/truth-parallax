## Overview

Islamic engagement with the Gathas and Zoroastrian tradition is framed by the Quran's reference to the *Majus* (Zoroastrians) in Surah 22:17, which lists them alongside Jews, Christians, and Sabians as communities that will be judged by God on the Day of Resurrection. This Quranic mention has prompted centuries of Islamic scholarly discussion about the status of Zoroastrians — whether they constitute *Ahl al-Kitab* (People of the Book) possessing a divinely revealed scripture, or a distinct category of religious community.

## Theological Assessment

Muslim scholars have noted both convergences and divergences between Gathic theology and Islamic monotheism (*tawhid*). The Gathic emphasis on a single supreme creator deity (Ahura Mazda), moral accountability, and a final judgment resonates with Quranic teachings. However, the Gathic doctrine of cosmic dualism — the existence of an independent destructive spirit (*Angra Mainyu*) opposing God — is incompatible with the Islamic insistence on God's absolute sovereignty over all creation. Some Muslim scholars have suggested that Zarathustra may have been a prophet whose original monotheistic message was later corrupted by dualistic innovations.

## Historical Context

The Islamic conquest of Sasanian Iran (632–654 CE) brought Muslims into direct contact with Zoroastrian communities, their scriptures, and their religious practices. Islamic historians such as al-Shahrastani and al-Biruni provided detailed accounts of Zoroastrian beliefs, demonstrating scholarly engagement with the tradition. The Gathas, as the oldest stratum of Zoroastrian scripture, hold particular interest for Muslim scholars exploring whether Zarathustra's original message aligns with the prophetic pattern described in the Quran.
