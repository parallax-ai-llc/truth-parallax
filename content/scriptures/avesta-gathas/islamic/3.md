## Islamic Reading

Islamic reading of Yasna 30 — The Two Spirits engages its 11 verses through the lens of Quranic theology and the Islamic understanding of prophethood. Muslim scholars observe that the hymn's emphasis on the oneness and supremacy of Ahura Mazda echoes the Quranic principle of *tawhid* (divine unity), while noting that the dualistic elements — the independent existence of a destructive spirit — depart from the Islamic insistence on God's absolute sovereignty over all creation, including evil.

The prophetic voice of Zarathustra as expressed in this hymn invites comparison with the Quranic portrayal of earlier prophets who called their communities to worship one God, uphold righteousness, and prepare for divine judgment. Some Muslim scholars have considered whether Zarathustra may be counted among the prophets mentioned generally in the Quran (4:164: "messengers We have told you about and messengers We have not told you about"), though this remains a matter of scholarly discussion rather than doctrinal consensus.

The ethical teachings of this hymn — truthfulness, justice, care for the weak, and resistance to oppression — align broadly with Islamic moral principles. Islamic engagement with the Gathas thus balances recognition of genuine theological affinity with clear identification of doctrinal differences, reflecting the Quran's nuanced approach to earlier religious communities.
