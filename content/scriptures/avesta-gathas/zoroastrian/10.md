## Zoroastrian Reading

The Zoroastrian tradition receives Yasna 45 — Zarathustra's Proclamation as a sacred hymn of Zarathustra, recited within the Yasna liturgy before the sacred fire. Each of its 11 verses carries liturgical weight, chanted by the *mobed* (priest) during the daily Yasna ceremony as part of the unbroken chain of oral transmission that connects the contemporary Zoroastrian community to its prophet. The ritual context is essential — the Gathas are not merely read but performed, with precise pronunciation believed to activate the spiritual power (*manthric* force) of the words.

Within Zoroastrian theological interpretation, this hymn reveals aspects of Ahura Mazda's nature and Zarathustra's relationship with the divine. The themes expressed here — the supremacy of asha (truth/righteousness), the moral imperative of choosing good over evil, and the promise of ultimate renovation — constitute the living theological core of Zoroastrian faith. Parsi and Irani communities continue to draw spiritual sustenance from these verses in daily prayer, seasonal festivals, and life-cycle rituals.

The priestly exegetical tradition, preserved in the Pahlavi commentaries (the *Zand*), provides a Middle Persian translation and interpretation of the Old Avestan text, bridging the linguistic gap of millennia while maintaining continuity with the original prophetic message.
