## Overview

The Gathas occupy the supreme position within Zoroastrian sacred literature as the direct utterances of the prophet Zarathustra, spoken in communion with Ahura Mazda. Within the living Zoroastrian tradition — encompassing both the Parsi communities of India and the Iranis of Iran and the diaspora — the Gathas are recited daily as part of the Yasna ceremony, the central liturgical act of the faith. Every practicing Zoroastrian priest (*mobed*) memorizes these hymns, preserving an unbroken oral tradition stretching back millennia.

## Liturgical Centrality

The Gathas are embedded within the Yasna, the primary ritual of the Zoroastrian liturgy. The Yasna ceremony, performed before a sacred fire, involves the preparation and offering of *haoma* (a sacred plant drink) and the recitation of all 72 chapters of the Yasna text, with the Gathas forming the spiritual core. The metrical precision and archaic language of the Gathas are preserved with extraordinary fidelity, as any mispronunciation is believed to compromise the ritual's efficacy.

## Theological Foundations

For the Zoroastrian community, the Gathas establish the fundamental theological principles of the faith: the supremacy of Ahura Mazda, the reality of asha (truth/righteousness), the moral choice facing every human being, and the ultimate renovation of the world (*frashokereti*). Modern Zoroastrian reformists, particularly in the diaspora, often emphasize the Gathas over later Avestan texts as the authentic core of Zarathustra's message.
