## Academic Reading

Academic analysis of Ahunavaiti Gatha — Yasna 28 engages with significant challenges of translation and interpretation inherent to Old Avestan poetry. The 11 verses of this hymn present characteristic difficulties: archaic grammar, hapax legomena (words occurring only once), and allusive references to a social and religious context that must be reconstructed through comparative philology. Scholars such as Insler, Humbach, and Kellens have offered substantially different renderings of key passages, reflecting the interpretive complexity of the text.

From a form-critical perspective, this hymn exhibits the metrical patterns and rhetorical structures typical of Gathic composition. The use of paired oppositions, rhetorical questions addressed to Ahura Mazda, and first-person declarations by Zarathustra place the text within the broader tradition of Indo-Iranian liturgical poetry while demonstrating distinctive innovations. The linguistic evidence consistently supports a dating to the late second millennium BCE.

Historical-critical scholarship examines this hymn for evidence of Zarathustra's social milieu, his conflicts with rival religious practitioners, and the pastoral economy of the proto-Zoroastrian community. The references to cattle, pasture, and the settled versus nomadic life illuminate the material conditions underlying the spiritual message.
