## Jewish Reading

Jewish scholarly reading of Yasna 48 — Asha and the Kingdom examines its 12 verses for light they may shed on the religious environment of the Persian Empire during the formative centuries of Second Temple Judaism. The theological concepts expressed in this hymn — divine righteousness, moral choice, eschatological judgment, and the struggle between truth and falsehood — correspond to themes that gained prominence in Jewish literature during and after the Babylonian Exile, prompting careful scholarly investigation of possible channels of influence.

The hymn's treatment of cosmic order (asha) invites comparison with the Jewish concept of divine justice (*mishpat*) and righteousness (*tsedaqah*). Its vision of a final transformation of the world resonates with the prophetic eschatology of Isaiah, Daniel, and the later apocalyptic tradition. Jewish scholars approach these parallels critically, distinguishing between genuine historical influence, shared ancient Near Eastern heritage, and parallel but independent theological development.

Within contemporary Jewish scholarship, this hymn contributes to the broader understanding of the multicultural matrix in which rabbinic Judaism emerged. The Persian period (539–332 BCE) remains one of the most consequential yet least documented eras in Jewish history, and the Gathas provide an essential window into the religious thought of Israel's Persian overlords and neighbors.
