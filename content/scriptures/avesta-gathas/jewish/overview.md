## Overview

Jewish scholarly engagement with the Gathas centers on one of the most significant questions in the history of religion: the extent to which Zoroastrian ideas influenced Jewish theology during and after the Babylonian Exile (586–539 BCE). When Cyrus the Great, a Zoroastrian monarch, liberated the Jews from Babylonian captivity, he initiated a period of sustained Persian-Jewish contact that lasted for centuries. Several theological concepts that appear in post-exilic Jewish literature — but are absent from pre-exilic texts — bear striking resemblance to Gathic teachings.

## Areas of Potential Influence

The most frequently cited parallels include the development of angelology and demonology (compare the Amesha Spentas with the archangels), the concept of bodily resurrection (Daniel 12:2), the idea of a final cosmic judgment, and an intensified ethical dualism contrasting the righteous and the wicked. The Gathic figure of Saoshyant (a future savior) has been compared to the Jewish concept of Mashiach. Jewish scholars approach these parallels with nuance, recognizing both genuine points of contact and the internal dynamics of Jewish theological development.

## Contemporary Jewish Perspectives

Modern Jewish scholars such as Shaul Shaked and Geo Widengren have contributed significantly to the academic study of Persian-Jewish religious interaction. Within the Jewish intellectual tradition, the Gathas are typically approached with historical interest rather than religious authority, appreciated as testimony to the broader Near Eastern context in which Second Temple Judaism took shape. The Talmud itself references Zoroastrian practices and beliefs, suggesting an awareness of this neighboring tradition.
