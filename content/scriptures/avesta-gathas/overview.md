## Overview

The Gathas (Avestan: *Gāθās*, "songs" or "hymns") are the oldest and most sacred texts of Zoroastrianism, attributed directly to the prophet Zarathustra (Zoroaster). Composed in Old Avestan, a language closely related to Vedic Sanskrit, they are believed to date from approximately 1700–1300 BCE, making them among the oldest religious poetry in existence. The Gathas are embedded within the larger liturgical text known as the Yasna, occupying Yasna 28–34, 43–51, and 53.

## Structure

The Gathas consist of seventeen hymns organized into five groups, each named after its opening words or metrical pattern:

1. **Ahunavaiti Gatha** (Yasna 28–34): Seven hymns in the *ahunavaiti* metre
2. **Ushtavaiti Gatha** (Yasna 43–46): Four hymns beginning with "happiness" (*ushta*)
3. **Spenta Mainyu Gatha** (Yasna 47–50): Four hymns on the Holy Spirit
4. **Vohu Khshathra Gatha** (Yasna 51): A single hymn on the Good Dominion
5. **Vahishtoishti Gatha** (Yasna 53): A single hymn, possibly a wedding song

The collection concludes with the Airyaman Ishya Prayer, a brief invocation closely associated with the Gathas.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Gathas hold distinct significance across multiple interpretive traditions:

- **Zoroastrian**: Revered as the direct words of Zarathustra, recited daily in liturgical practice
- **Academic**: Studied as primary sources for reconstructing proto-Indo-Iranian religion and Zarathustra's historical milieu
- **Comparative**: Examined alongside Vedic hymns, biblical prophetic literature, and Greek philosophical texts
- **Protestant Christian**: Read for parallels with monotheistic theology, eschatology, and the cosmic struggle between good and evil
- **Jewish**: Explored for connections to post-exilic Jewish theology, angelology, and dualistic motifs
- **Islamic**: Considered in relation to pre-Islamic Iranian religion and Quranic references to the Majus (Zoroastrians)

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Asha | Truth, righteousness, and cosmic order — the central ethical principle |
| Ahura Mazda | The Wise Lord, supreme deity and creator of all good |
| Cosmic Dualism | The opposition between Spenta Mainyu (Holy Spirit) and Angra Mainyu (Destructive Spirit) |
| Free Will | Human choice between truth (asha) and falsehood (druj) |
| Fire | Sacred fire as the visible symbol of asha and divine presence |
| Eschatology | Final renovation (frashokereti) and the triumph of good over evil |
