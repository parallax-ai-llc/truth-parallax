## Overview

Protestant Christian engagement with the Gathas has been shaped by a longstanding recognition of theological affinities between Zarathustra's vision and biblical monotheism. Since the Enlightenment, Protestant scholars and theologians have noted the parallels between the Gathic concept of Ahura Mazda as the one supreme creator God and the Judeo-Christian understanding of God as revealed in Scripture. These parallels have prompted both cautious comparisons and vigorous debates about the nature and extent of Zoroastrian influence on biblical religion.

## Theological Resonances

Protestant readers find several striking resonances in the Gathas. Zarathustra's emphasis on moral choice between truth and falsehood echoes the biblical call to choose between good and evil (Deuteronomy 30:19). The Gathic concept of a cosmic struggle between the Holy Spirit (*Spenta Mainyu*) and the Destructive Spirit (*Angra Mainyu*) invites comparison with the New Testament's portrayal of the conflict between Christ and Satan. The eschatological vision of a final renovation, a judgment of souls, and the triumph of righteousness resonates deeply with Christian apocalyptic hope.

## Missiological and Apologetic Perspectives

Some Protestant thinkers have viewed Zarathustra as a figure of *praeparatio evangelica* — a pre-Christian prophet whose insights into the nature of God, while incomplete, pointed toward the fuller revelation in Christ. Others have emphasized the fundamental differences between Gathic dualism and Christian trinitarianism, cautioning against superficial harmonization. The Gathas continue to feature in Protestant discussions of general revelation, natural theology, and interfaith dialogue.
