## Overview

The Gathas of Zarathustra hold a pivotal position in comparative religious studies as one of the earliest expressions of ethical monotheism and cosmic dualism. Their relationship to the Vedic hymns of India — composed in a closely related language and reflecting a shared Indo-Iranian cultural heritage — provides scholars with an unparalleled opportunity to trace the divergence of two major religious traditions from a common source. The Gathas thus serve as a critical reference point for understanding the development of monotheistic theology across cultures.

## Indo-Iranian Connections

The linguistic and conceptual parallels between the Gathas and the Rig Veda are striking. The Avestan *ahura* corresponds to the Vedic *asura*, and the Avestan *daeva* to the Vedic *deva* — yet these terms undergo a remarkable reversal: Zarathustra's supreme god is an Ahura, while the daevas are demons; in the Vedic tradition, the devas are gods. This inversion points to a deliberate theological break, making the Gathas a foundational document in the history of religious reform.

## Influence on Western Religion

Comparative scholars have long debated the extent of Zoroastrian influence on Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, particularly regarding concepts of cosmic dualism, angelology, a final judgment, resurrection of the body, and the eschatological triumph of good over evil. The Gathas provide the earliest evidence for many of these ideas, which appear in Jewish texts only after the Babylonian Exile, when the Judeans came into sustained contact with the Persian Empire.
