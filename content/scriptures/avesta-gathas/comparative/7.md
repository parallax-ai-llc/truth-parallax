## Comparative Reading

Comparative analysis of Yasna 34 — Devotion to Ahura situates its 15 verses within the broader context of ancient Indo-Iranian religious expression and the development of ethical monotheism. The hymn invites comparison with Vedic hymns of similar structure and theme — particularly the philosophical hymns of the Rig Veda (such as the Nasadiya Sukta and the Purusha Sukta) — while revealing the decisive theological break that distinguishes Zarathustra's vision from the Vedic worldview.

The themes expressed in this hymn resonate across religious traditions. The emphasis on truth versus falsehood finds parallels in Egyptian Ma'at, Vedic *rta*, biblical *emet*, and Greek *aletheia*. The concept of divine judgment and eschatological transformation anticipates themes central to later Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. Comparative scholars debate whether these parallels reflect direct historical influence, shared Indo-European heritage, or independent convergent development.

This hymn also contributes to broader comparative discussions about the nature of prophetic experience, the relationship between ritual and ethics, and the tension between cosmic dualism and monotheistic absolutism — questions that recur across the world's religious traditions.
