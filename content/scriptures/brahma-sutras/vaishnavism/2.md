In Ramanuja's reading of Avirodha Adhyaya, the Brahma Sutras defend the Vedantic position against objections from rival schools — Samkhya, Vaisheshika, Buddhism, and Jainism — while establishing that the Vaishnava understanding of Brahman is internally consistent. The chapter addresses the apparent problem of how a perfect God can be the material cause of an imperfect world, which Ramanuja resolves through his doctrine of real transformation (*parinama*): the world is a genuine modification of Brahman's attributes, not an illusion.

Ramanuja's refutation of Advaita is particularly vigorous in this chapter. He argues that Shankara's concept of *maya* (cosmic illusion) is itself an inexplicable addition that the Upanishads do not teach. If Brahman is the sole reality, the very ignorance that supposedly produces the world of appearances cannot be explained — it can belong neither to Brahman (who is pure knowledge) nor to the individual soul (which is itself a product of ignorance). This critique became a cornerstone of Vaishnava philosophical polemic.
