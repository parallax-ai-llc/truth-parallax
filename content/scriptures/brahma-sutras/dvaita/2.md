Madhva's reading of Avirodha Adhyaya mounts a comprehensive defense of Dvaita metaphysics against both external rivals and internal Vedantic opponents. Against Advaita, Madhva argues that the concept of *maya* is self-refuting: if the world is illusory, the very scriptures that teach this doctrine are also illusory, undermining their authority. Against Samkhya, he rejects the notion of an unconscious first cause. Against Buddhism, he insists on the reality of permanent substances.

The chapter's discussion of causation is pivotal for Dvaita. Madhva argues that Brahman is both the efficient and material cause of the world, but this does not mean the world is identical with Brahman (as Advaita would have it) or that Brahman undergoes change (as Ramanuja's *parinama* suggests). Rather, Brahman creates through His will, producing a genuinely real world that is distinct from Him yet entirely dependent on Him. Matter (*prakriti*) is a real, eternal substance that God shapes and governs.
