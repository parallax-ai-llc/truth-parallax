Phala Adhyaya's descriptions of the liberated state and the soul's post-mortem journey invite comparison with eschatological visions across traditions. The Vedantic "path of light" parallels the Zoroastrian Chinvat Bridge, the Egyptian passage through the Duat, the Tibetan Buddhist *bardo* states, and the Christian judgment and entry into heavenly beatitude. Each tradition envisions a transitional process between earthly life and the ultimate state.

The nature of the liberated state itself reveals fundamental differences. The Advaita dissolution of individuality into non-dual Brahman contrasts with the Christian beatific vision, where the soul retains its identity while beholding God face to face. The Vaishnava vision of eternal service in Vaikuntha more closely parallels the Islamic *janna* (paradise) and Christian heaven as states of eternal, conscious relationship with God. The Buddhist *nirvana*, which refuses to characterize the liberated state in positive metaphysical terms, stands apart from all of these. These differences illuminate how each tradition's understanding of ultimate reality shapes its vision of ultimate human destiny.
