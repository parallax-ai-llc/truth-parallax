Avirodha Adhyaya's defense of Vedantic metaphysics against rival systems provides rich material for comparative philosophical analysis. The refutation of Samkhya's unconscious first cause parallels the Aristotelian and Thomistic argument that only an intelligent being can be the ultimate cause of an intelligible cosmos. The critique of Buddhist momentariness resonates with Western debates about substance and identity over time (Leibniz, Hume, personal identity theory).

The chapter's treatment of the God-world relationship — how can an unchanging absolute produce a changing world? — is a perennial problem across philosophical traditions. The Vedantic solutions (Shankara's *vivarta*, Ramanuja's *parinama*, Madhva's creation through divine will) parallel the Neoplatonic emanation theory, Islamic *kalam* arguments for continuous creation, and the Christian debate between creatio ex nihilo and panentheism. Each tradition must explain how the infinite and the finite, the eternal and the temporal, can be related without compromising the nature of either.
