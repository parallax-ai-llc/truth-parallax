## Overview

Shaiva interpreters read the Brahma Sutras as demonstrating that the supreme Brahman taught in the Upanishads is Shiva — the absolute consciousness, cause of creation, and grantor of liberation. Srikantha's *Brahma Sutra Bhashya* (13th century) is the principal Shaiva commentary, articulating a Shaiva Vedanta that interprets every reference to Brahman's attributes as pointing to Shiva's transcendent and immanent nature.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Srikantha's Shaiva Vishishtadvaita
Srikantha adapts a framework similar to Ramanuja's qualified non-dualism but identifies the Supreme with Shiva rather than Vishnu. Brahman possesses real attributes, and the world is His genuine transformation, animated by Shakti.

### Appaya Dikshita
The 16th-century scholar Appaya Dikshita wrote the *Shivarka Mani Dipika*, a sub-commentary on Srikantha, defending the Shaiva reading against both Vaishnava and Advaita challenges and arguing for a comprehensive Shaiva Vedanta.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shiva as Para Brahman**: The sutras' descriptions of Brahman's omniscience and creative power apply to Shiva
- **Shakti**: Brahman's creative power is understood as Shiva's Shakti, the dynamic principle of manifestation
- **Pashupata Grace**: Liberation comes through Shiva's grace to the bound soul (*pashu*)
- **Integration of Agama and Vedanta**: Shaiva commentators harmonize Vedic and Agamic authorities
