The Shaiva reading of Avirodha Adhyaya addresses the sutras that appear to reject the Pashupata and other Shaiva systems (2.2.35-38). Srikantha argues that these sutras criticize only certain specific Shaiva sub-schools whose doctrines deviate from Vedic teaching, not Shaivism as a whole. The true Shaiva Vedanta, grounded in both Veda and Agama, is what the sutras themselves teach.

On the question of how Brahman can be both the material and efficient cause of the world, Shaiva Vedanta employs the concept of Shakti. Shiva creates through His inherent power (Shakti), which is not separate from Him but is His own dynamic nature. This resolves the problem of how an unchanging reality can produce change — it is Shakti that transforms while Shiva remains transcendent. The refutation of rival systems (Samkhya, Buddhism) in this chapter is accepted, with the Shaiva commentator adding that these systems fail precisely because they do not recognize Shiva as the supreme conscious principle.
