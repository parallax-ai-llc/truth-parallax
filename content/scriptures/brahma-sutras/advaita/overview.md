## Overview

Adi Shankara's *Brahma Sutra Bhashya* (8th century) is historically the most influential commentary on the Brahma Sutras and the cornerstone of the Advaita Vedanta tradition. Shankara reads the sutras as demonstrating that the Upanishads consistently teach one non-dual reality (*advaita*) — Brahman without qualities (*nirguna*), which is identical with the individual self (*Atman*). The world of multiplicity is a product of cosmic ignorance (*avidya/maya*) superimposed upon the changeless Brahman.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Shankara's Bhashya
Shankara's commentary employs rigorous dialectical reasoning to demonstrate that all Upanishadic passages, even those apparently describing a qualified Brahman, ultimately point to the attributeless absolute. Apparent contradictions between Upanishadic texts are resolved through the distinction between higher (*para*) and lower (*apara*) Brahman.

### Post-Shankara Tradition
Commentators like Vacaspati Mishra (*Bhamati*) and Prakashatman (*Panchapadika Vivarana*) developed two sub-schools within Advaita, debating the precise nature and locus of *avidya*.

## Theological Emphases

- **Nirguna Brahman**: Brahman in its ultimate nature is beyond all attributes and distinctions
- **Adhyasa**: The fundamental error of superimposing the self on the not-self and vice versa
- **Mahavakyas**: The great sayings ("Tat tvam asi," "Aham brahmasmi") reveal the identity of Atman and Brahman
- **Jnana as Liberation**: Only knowledge, not action or devotion, directly produces liberation
