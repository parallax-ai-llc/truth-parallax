Shankara's interpretation of Phala Adhyaya presents liberation (*moksha*) as the removal of the beginningless ignorance (*avidya*) that conceals the Self's true nature as Brahman. Unlike the Vaishnava reading, liberation is not a journey to another realm or the attainment of something new. It is the recognition of what was always the case — that the individual self was never separate from Brahman. There is, in the deepest sense, no one who is liberated and no state of liberation, because bondage was never real.

Shankara interprets the chapter's discussion of the soul's post-mortem journey through the path of light as applying only to those who practiced *saguna upasana* (qualified Brahman meditation) and attain *krama mukti* — gradual liberation through the world of Brahma. The *jnani* (knower of the non-dual Brahman), by contrast, is liberated here and now (*sadyo mukti*), without any journey, because the realization of identity with Brahman is immediate and complete. Death merely drops the body; the Self, being Brahman, was never born and never dies.
