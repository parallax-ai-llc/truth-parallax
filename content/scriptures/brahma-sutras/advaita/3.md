Shankara's commentary on Sadhana Adhyaya distinguishes sharply between meditations (*upasanas*) that produce improved worldly or celestial results and the direct knowledge of Brahman (*brahma jnana*) that alone liberates. The various Upanishadic *vidyas* discussed in this chapter — meditation on *prana*, on the *dahara* (space in the heart), on the *vaishvanara* (cosmic fire) — are classified as *saguna upasanas* (meditations on the qualified Brahman) that purify the mind but do not directly produce liberation.

The chapter's discussion of the prerequisites for Vedantic inquiry is critical for Advaita. The fourfold qualification (*sadhana chatushtaya*) — discrimination between the real and the unreal, dispassion, the six virtues (tranquility, restraint, etc.), and the desire for liberation — establishes that only the properly prepared mind can receive the *mahavakya* teaching. Liberation is not produced by action, meditation, or devotion but by the immediate, direct knowledge that arises when a qualified student hears "Tat tvam asi" from a qualified teacher.
