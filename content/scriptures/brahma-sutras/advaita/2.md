Shankara's reading of Avirodha Adhyaya addresses the central philosophical challenge to Advaita: how can the one, unchanging Brahman appear as the manifold world? His answer employs the concept of *vivarta* (apparent transformation) as opposed to *parinama* (real transformation). Just as a rope appears as a snake in dim light without actually changing, Brahman appears as the world without undergoing any real modification. The world is neither wholly real nor wholly unreal but *mithya* — an appearance with practical but not ultimate reality.

The refutation of rival systems in this chapter is central to Shankara's project. He argues that Samkhya's unconscious *pradhana* cannot be the intelligent cause of the world, that the Buddhist *shunyata* (emptiness) is philosophically incoherent, and that the Vaisheshika atomism fails to explain how inert atoms can combine without a conscious agent. Each refutation serves to eliminate alternatives and establish Brahman as the sole possible cause of the universe, one that creates without undergoing change.
