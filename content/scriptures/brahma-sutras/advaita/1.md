Shankara's commentary on Samanvaya Adhyaya establishes the foundational Advaita claim that all Upanishadic texts harmoniously teach one truth: Brahman alone is real, and the individual self (*Atman*) is identical with that Brahman. The opening sutra's call for "inquiry into Brahman" presupposes the distinction between the eternal and the temporal, the self and the not-self — a distinction that Shankara's *adhyasa bhashya* (introduction on superimposition) establishes as the fundamental human error.

Shankara reads the Upanishadic passages discussed in this chapter — the *Anandamaya*, the cosmic source, the *akasha* (space), the light — as referring to the *nirguna* (qualityless) Brahman. Where the texts appear to describe Brahman with attributes (creator, lord, inner controller), Shankara distinguishes between the higher Brahman (*para Brahman*) that transcends all description and the lower Brahman (*apara Brahman*) that serves as an object of meditation for aspirants not yet ready for non-dual knowledge. This hermeneutical strategy pervades his entire commentary.
