Scholars approach Samanvaya Adhyaya as an early attempt at systematic Upanishadic theology. The chapter's primary method — demonstrating that various Upanishadic passages consistently refer to Brahman rather than to individual souls, elements, or other entities — provides important evidence for how the Upanishads were being read in Badarayana's era. Scholars note that the need for such harmonization implies that by Badarayana's time, the Upanishads were already recognized as a corpus requiring systematic interpretation.

The chapter's discussions reveal which Upanishadic passages were considered most important and most contested in the centuries surrounding the common era. Academic analysis examines how Badarayana's sutras relate to the *mimamsa* (exegetical) tradition that developed methods for interpreting Vedic texts. Some scholars (e.g., Hajime Nakamura) argue that Badarayana's positions can be partially reconstructed independently of later commentaries, representing a pre-Shankara Vedanta that may have been more theistic than Shankara's non-dual reading suggests.
