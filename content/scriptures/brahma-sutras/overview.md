## Overview

The Brahma Sutras (also known as the Vedanta Sutras), attributed to Badarayana (c. 5th-2nd century BCE), consist of 555 terse aphorisms that systematize the philosophical teachings of the Upanishads. As one of the three pillars of the *prasthanatraya* (triple canon of Vedanta), alongside the Upanishads and the Bhagavad Gita, the Brahma Sutras have been the subject of extensive commentary by every major Vedantic school. Their primary purpose is to demonstrate the coherence of Upanishadic teaching regarding Brahman, the ultimate reality.

## Structure

The Brahma Sutras are organized into four chapters (*adhyayas*), each with four sections (*padas*):

1. **Samanvaya (Harmony)**: Establishes that all Upanishadic texts consistently teach Brahman as the ultimate reality
2. **Avirodha (Non-conflict)**: Defends Vedantic teaching against objections from rival philosophies
3. **Sadhana (Means)**: Discusses the spiritual practices and qualifications for attaining knowledge of Brahman
4. **Phala (Fruit/Liberation)**: Describes the nature of liberation and the fate of the soul after death

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Brahma Sutras are pivotal across Hindu philosophical traditions:

- **Vaishnavism**: Ramanuja and Madhva wrote major commentaries establishing theistic Vedanta
- **Shaivism**: Srikantha's *Brahma Sutra Bhashya* interprets the sutras through Shaiva theology
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Shankara's commentary is the most influential, establishing non-dual interpretation
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Madhva's commentary rigorously argues for eternal difference between God and souls
- **Academic**: Central to understanding the development of Indian systematic philosophy
- **Comparative**: Compared with Aristotle's Metaphysics and Aquinas's Summa Theologica as systematic theology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Brahman | The nature and attributes of the ultimate reality |
| Jiva | The individual self, its nature and relation to Brahman |
| Jagat | The origin, sustenance, and dissolution of the world |
| Moksha | Liberation from the cycle of rebirth |
| Sadhana | The means of attaining liberating knowledge |
