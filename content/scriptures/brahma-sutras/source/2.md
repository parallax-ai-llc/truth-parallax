---
title: "Brahma Sutras - Chapter 2"
scripture: "brahma-sutras"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Brahman is not to be known through mere reasoning, for reasoning has no independent authority in matters beyond the senses.

**2** But Brahman, being the cause of the universe, is not contradicted by reasoning. The Vedantic texts and reasoning together establish Brahman as the cause.

**3** If it be asked how an intelligent being can be the cause of an unconscious world — we reply that even in ordinary experience, intelligent beings produce unconscious things, as a spider produces its web.

**4** The creation of the world is not purposeless, for the Lord creates out of sport, as kings engage in play not from any need but from their own inherent nature.

**5** No partiality or cruelty can be ascribed to the Lord, for He creates in accordance with the karma of the individual souls. The inequality of creation is due to the merits and demerits of the beings themselves.

**6** The individual soul is not entirely different from Brahman, nor entirely identical. It is a part of Brahman, as a spark is part of the fire.

**7** The individual soul, though a part of Brahman, is subject to ignorance and thereby becomes bound. When ignorance is destroyed by knowledge, the soul realizes its identity with Brahman.

**8** Prakriti, the material nature, cannot be the independent cause of the universe, for an unintelligent principle cannot create an ordered and purposeful universe without the direction of an intelligent being.

**9** The atoms of the Vaisheshikas cannot be the ultimate cause of the universe, for atoms being inert cannot by themselves combine to form the world.

**10** Thus Brahman alone is the material and efficient cause of the universe. He creates it out of Himself, by Himself, and for His own delight.

**11** The world is not an illusion, nor is it absolutely real. It is a transformation of Brahman, having Brahman as its ground and substance.

**12** He who knows Brahman as the cause of all, as the Self of all, as that from which all proceeds and into which all returns — he attains the supreme goal.
