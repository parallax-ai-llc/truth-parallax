---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 3"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 3
---

**1** This is the commentary on the Citta Vagga, the Chapter on Thought. The verse "As a fletcher makes straight his arrow, a wise man makes straight his trembling and unsteady thought" was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to a certain monk named Meghiya.

**2** The Elder Meghiya was the personal attendant of the Buddha. One day, while on alms round with the Blessed One near the town of Jantu, he saw a beautiful mango grove and desired to go there to meditate. Three times he asked leave of the Buddha, and three times the Buddha asked him to wait, for there was no other monk present to attend upon him.

**3** But Meghiya, overcome by his desire, went nonetheless. In the mango grove, despite the pleasant surroundings, he was assailed by evil and unwholesome thoughts — thoughts of sensuality, thoughts of ill-will, and thoughts of cruelty. He was unable to concentrate and returned to the Buddha, confessing his failure.

**4** The Buddha said: "Meghiya, when the liberation of the heart is not yet ripe, five things conduce to its ripening: a good friend, virtuous conduct, profitable conversation, earnest effort, and the development of insight." Then he spoke the verse on straightening the mind as a fletcher straightens an arrow.

**5** The verse "As a fish taken from his watery home" was spoken with reference to a monk who had been a goldsmith in his former life. Even after ordination, his mind would dart back to memories of fine ornaments and craftsmanship, as a fish gasps when removed from water. The Buddha, perceiving this, taught that the mind, like a fish out of water, trembles when removed from its accustomed objects.

**6** The verses on guarding the mind were spoken with reference to a group of monks who, practising meditation in a forest, were distracted by the sounds and sights around them. The Buddha admonished them: "Monks, the mind travels far, wanders alone, is bodiless, and dwells in the cave of the heart. Those who subdue it are freed from the bonds of Mara."

**7** The verse "Knowing that this body is fragile like a jar" was spoken with reference to the Elder Tissa, whose body was covered with sores. The other monks avoided him, but the Buddha himself came to attend to him, washing his sores and changing his robes. Then the Buddha taught that the body is fragile and impermanent, and that one should fortify the mind against Mara.

**8** The verses on the wrongly-directed mind and the well-directed mind were spoken with reference to Nanda, the half-brother of the Buddha, who was ordained reluctantly and could not stop thinking of his beautiful betrothed, Janapada-Kalyani. The Buddha took Nanda to the heaven of the Thirty-Three, where he saw celestial nymphs far surpassing his betrothed in beauty, and promised him these nymphs as a reward for practice. Nanda then practised with great earnestness and eventually attained Arahatship, releasing the Buddha from his promise. Thus was it shown that a well-directed mind surpasses all other helpers.
