---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 1"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Honour to that Blessed One, the Arahat, the all-knowing Buddha. This is the commentary on the Yamaka Vagga, the Chapter of Twin Verses.

**2** The first verse, "All that we are is the result of what we have thought," was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to Cakkhupala, a blind elder. The story is as follows.

**3** In times past, during the dispensation of the Buddha Kassapa, there lived a physician who, through greed, gave false medicine to a woman and caused her to lose her sight. As a result of this evil deed, he was reborn in states of suffering for many lifetimes, and even when reborn as a human being, was blind from birth in five hundred successive existences.

**4** In his final existence, he was born into a good family in the city of Savatthi and was named Cakkhupala. When he came of age, he heard the Teacher preach the Dhamma and, being deeply moved, entered the Order and devoted himself to the practice of meditation with great earnestness.

**5** During the rainy season, while practising walking meditation with intense striving, he developed an affliction of the eyes. A physician offered him medicine, but he refused to lie down for treatment, lest it interfere with his meditation. As a result, he lost his sight — but attained Arahatship.

**6** One night, while doing walking meditation, he inadvertently crushed insects beneath his feet. Other monks, seeing the dead insects in the morning, accused him of killing living beings. They reported the matter to the Buddha.

**7** The Blessed One asked: "Did you see him killing the insects?" The monks replied: "No, Lord." The Buddha said: "Just as you did not see him killing them, so also he did not see those insects. Moreover, he is an Arahat, free from all intention to kill. There is no offence for him."

**8** Then the Buddha, perceiving the occasion for a profound teaching, spoke this verse: "All that we are is the result of what we have thought: it is founded on our thoughts, it is made up of our thoughts. If a man speaks or acts with an evil thought, pain follows him, as the wheel follows the foot of the ox that draws the carriage."

**9** The second verse was spoken with reference to Matthakundali, a young brahmin. His father, Adinnapubbaka, was a miser who never gave anything to anyone. When Matthakundali fell gravely ill, his father would not pay for a physician, and the boy lay dying.

**10** The father carried the boy outside so that visitors would not see his possessions within the house. The Buddha, surveying the world at dawn with his divine eye, saw Matthakundali and went to his door on his alms round. The boy, seeing the golden radiance of the Buddha, placed his faith in him with a gladdened heart, and died, and was reborn in the Tavatimsa heaven.

**11** Appearing before his grieving father in divine form, Matthakundali declared that he had been reborn in heaven through his faith in the Buddha. The father, astonished, went to the Buddha, who preached the Dhamma to the assembled people.

**12** Then the Teacher spoke the verse: "All that we are is the result of what we have thought: it is founded on our thoughts, it is made up of our thoughts. If a man speaks or acts with a pure thought, happiness follows him, like a shadow that never leaves him."

**13** The verses on hatred — "He abused me, he beat me" — were spoken with reference to the Elder Tissa, who harboured resentment towards another monk who had offended him. The Buddha, seeing his state of mind, taught that hatred is overcome not by hatred but by love alone.

**14** Thus the Buddha taught the Twin Verses, showing that the mind is the forerunner of all actions, that good and evil spring from the mind, and that the consequences of one's deeds follow one as surely as the shadow follows the body and the wheel follows the foot of the ox.
