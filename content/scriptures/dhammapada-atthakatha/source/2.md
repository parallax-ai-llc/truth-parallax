---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 2"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 2
---

**1** This is the commentary on the Appamada Vagga, the Chapter on Earnestness. The verse "Earnestness is the path of immortality" was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to Samavati, the queen consort of King Udena of Kosambi.

**2** Queen Samavati was a woman of great virtue and devotion. She had come to the palace of King Udena after many tribulations. Her parents had died during a famine, and she was raised by Ghosaka, a treasurer of Kosambi, who adopted her as his own daughter.

**3** When she became queen, she was instructed in the Dhamma by her maidservant Khujjuttara, who would go to hear the Buddha preach and return to teach the queen and her attendants. In this way, Samavati and her five hundred attendants became devoted followers of the Buddha.

**4** But Magandiya, another consort of King Udena, was filled with hatred towards the Buddha because he had once rejected her when her father offered her in marriage. She plotted to destroy Samavati and her attendants.

**5** Magandiya first attempted to turn the king against Samavati by placing a snake in his lute-case and accusing the queen of trying to murder him. But the king, upon investigation, discovered the plot and Samavati was cleared.

**6** Not deterred, Magandiya bribed Samavati's uncle to set fire to the queen's quarters. Samavati and her five hundred attendants perished in the flames. But because they had practised earnestness in meditation and had established themselves in the fruits of the path, they were reborn in the celestial realms.

**7** When the matter was reported to the Buddha, he declared that Samavati and her attendants had attained various stages of enlightenment. Then, perceiving the occasion for a profound teaching, he spoke: "Earnestness is the path of immortality, thoughtlessness the path of death."

**8** The verse "By earnestness did Maghavan rise to the lordship of the gods" was spoken with reference to Sakka, lord of the gods. In a former birth, Sakka was a young man named Magha who lived in a village in the kingdom of Magadha. He organized a group of thirty-three friends who worked together performing meritorious deeds — repairing roads, building rest houses, and planting trees.

**9** Through the power of their earnest and sustained good works, all thirty-three were reborn as gods in the Tavatimsa heaven, with Magha as their chief, Sakka the king of the gods. Thus earnestness led to the highest station among the devas.

**10** The final verse, on the Bhikshu who delights in earnestness and "cannot fall away from his perfect state," was spoken to encourage the monks to be ever vigilant, for earnestness is the one quality that encompasses all others, as the footprint of the elephant encompasses all other footprints.
