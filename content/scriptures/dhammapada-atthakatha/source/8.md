---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 8"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 8
---

**1** This is the commentary on the Sahassa Vagga, the Chapter on the Thousands. The verse "Even though a speech be a thousand words" was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to a certain brahmin woman named Tambadathika.

**2** This brahmin woman had been in the habit of reciting many mantras and performing elaborate rituals, believing that the sheer number of sacred syllables uttered would bring her merit and salvation. When she heard the Buddha preach a single verse of the Dhamma, understanding arose in her, and she attained Stream-entry. The Buddha then declared: "Even though a speech be a thousand words, but made up of senseless words, one word of sense is better."

**3** The verse "If one man conquer in battle a thousand times thousand men, and if another conquer himself, he is the greatest of conquerors" was spoken with reference to a monk who had been a warrior in his former life. This monk, even after ordination, took pride in his past martial prowess. The Buddha said: "Monk, what value is there in conquering others? True victory is victory over oneself."

**4** The verses on honouring a man of true knowledge were spoken with reference to the Elder Sariputta. A brahmin who had performed fire-sacrifices for many years came to the Buddha, boasting of his rituals. The Buddha taught that a single moment of reverence towards one who possesses true knowledge surpasses a hundred years of sacrificial offerings, for wisdom is the highest offering.

**5** The verse "He who always greets and constantly reveres the aged" was spoken with reference to the Elder Ayupala, who was known for his unfailing respect toward senior monks. The Buddha taught that four blessings — long life, beauty, happiness, and strength — increase for one who consistently honours the worthy.

**6** The verses comparing a hundred years of vicious life to a single day of virtuous life were spoken on various occasions. The verse on seeing "beginning and end" was spoken with reference to a monk named Patacara, who had suffered the loss of her entire family — husband, children, parents, and brother — all in a single day. Coming nearly mad with grief to the Buddha, she was restored to her senses by his teaching. When the Buddha spoke of seeing the arising and passing away of all conditioned things, Patacara attained Arahatship.

**7** The verse on seeing the immortal place was spoken with reference to Kisagotami, who came to the Buddha carrying her dead child, begging for medicine to restore its life. The Buddha told her to bring a mustard seed from any house where no one had ever died. Going from door to door, she found no such house, and understanding dawned upon her. She laid down the child's body and returned to the Buddha, who taught her the verse on the immortal, and she attained Stream-entry.

**8** Thus the Chapter on the Thousands teaches that quality surpasses quantity in all things — one word of truth surpasses a thousand senseless words, one day of wisdom surpasses a hundred years of ignorance, and one moment of self-conquest surpasses the defeat of a million foes.
