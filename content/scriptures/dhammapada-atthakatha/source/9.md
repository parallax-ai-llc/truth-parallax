---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 9"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 9
---

**1** This is the commentary on the Papa Vagga, the Chapter on Evil. The verse "If a man would hasten towards the good, he should keep his thought away from evil" was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to a certain brahmin who had resolved to perform a great act of generosity.

**2** This brahmin had decided to offer a meal to the Buddha and the community of monks. But on the appointed day, he began to have second thoughts, regretting the expense. The mind that begins a good deed slothfully allows evil to creep in. Three times the impulse to cancel arose, and three times he suppressed it. He completed the offering, and the Buddha, perceiving his inner struggle, spoke the verse: "If a man would hasten towards the good, he should keep his thought away from evil; if a man does what is good slothfully, his mind delights in evil."

**3** The verse "If a man commits a sin, let him not do it again" was spoken with reference to a certain merchant who had taken to cheating his customers with false weights and measures. After hearing the Dhamma, he resolved to stop but found the habit difficult to break. The Buddha taught that just as a wound heals when one stops scratching it, so the mind is purified when one ceases to repeat unwholesome actions.

**4** The verse "Even an evil-doer sees happiness as long as his evil deed has not ripened" was spoken with reference to the robber Nangala, who prospered for a time through his crimes. The other monks wondered why the wicked seemed to flourish while the virtuous suffered. The Buddha explained that the fruit of evil, like a seed beneath the soil, is hidden for a time but inevitably breaks forth.

**5** The verse on filling the water-pot drop by drop was spoken with reference to a group of laypeople who thought small acts of merit were negligible. They asked: "What good is a single offering, a single act of kindness?" The Buddha replied: "Drop by drop the water-pot is filled. Little by little the wise man, gathering merit moment by moment, fills himself with good." And he spoke the corresponding verse about evil as well.

**6** The verse "He who has no wound on his hand may touch poison with his hand" was spoken with reference to a monk who handled poisonous herbs without harm while preparing medicine for a sick brother. The Buddha taught that just as poison cannot enter a hand that has no wound, so evil has no power over one who commits no evil.

**7** The verse "If a man offend a harmless, pure, and innocent person" was spoken with reference to Kokalika, a monk who slandered the Elders Sariputta and Moggallana, accusing them of having evil desires. The earth opened and swallowed Kokalika alive, and he was reborn in the Paduma hell. The Buddha declared that evil directed at the innocent returns to the sender, like fine dust thrown against the wind.

**8** The verses "Not in the sky, not in the midst of the sea" were spoken with reference to three groups of persons: those who sought to escape death by hiding in the sky, beneath the sea, and in mountain caves. The Buddha taught that no place in the entire world can serve as refuge from death or from the consequences of evil deeds. Only the cessation of evil, through the practice of the Dhamma, brings true safety.
