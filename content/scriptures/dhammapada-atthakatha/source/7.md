---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 7"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 7
---

**1** This is the commentary on the Arahanta Vagga, the Chapter on the Venerable. The verse "There is no suffering for him who has finished his journey" was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to a group of Arahats.

**2** A certain group of monks, having attained Arahatship, were dwelling in a forest. The other monks, observing their perfect serenity, asked the Buddha about their condition. The Buddha explained: "These monks have completed their journey. They have abandoned grief and freed themselves on all sides. For them, there is no more suffering."

**3** The verse "They depart with their thoughts well-collected" was spoken with reference to the Elder Mahakassapa. When Mahakassapa would leave one dwelling for another, he went with perfect mindfulness, his thoughts gathered together like a swan rising from a lake. The Buddha praised this quality, saying that the Arahats, like swans leaving their lake, leave their worldly abode without looking back.

**4** The verses on the one "whose appetites are stilled" and whose "path is difficult to understand, like that of birds in the air" were spoken with reference to the Elder Anuruddha. Anuruddha, though born a prince, had attained Arahatship and lived without any attachment. The gods themselves came to attend upon him, but he remained unmoved. The Buddha declared that his path was as untraceable as the path of birds in the sky.

**5** The verse "The gods even envy him whose senses have been subdued" was spoken with reference to the Elder Mahakaccayana, whose mastery over his senses was so complete that even the devas regarded him with reverence and wonder.

**6** The verse "Such a one who does his duty is tolerant like the earth" was spoken with reference to the Elder Sariputta. Once a brahmin, wishing to test Sariputta's patience, struck him from behind. Sariputta did not even turn around. The brahmin, filled with remorse, begged forgiveness. Sariputta forgave him instantly, saying: "I bear no ill-will." The Buddha declared that Sariputta was indeed like the earth, bearing all things without complaint.

**7** The verse "In a hamlet or in a forest, wherever venerable persons dwell, that place is delightful" was spoken with reference to the Elder Revata, who dwelt in a remote forest. Though the forest was wild and fearsome, wherever Revata sat in meditation, the place became peaceful and pleasant. The Buddha declared that any place where Arahats dwell becomes a place of delight, for the passionless find joy where others find only wilderness.
