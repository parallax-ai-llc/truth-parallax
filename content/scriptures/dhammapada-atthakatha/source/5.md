---
title: "Dhammapada Atthakatha - Chapter 5"
scripture: "dhammapada-atthakatha"
chapter: 5
---

**1** This is the commentary on the Bala Vagga, the Chapter on the Fool. The verse "Long is the night to him who is awake" was spoken by the Buddha while dwelling at Savatthi, with reference to a company of monks who had committed a trifling offence and lay awake the whole night through, tormented by remorse and unable to sleep.

**2** The Buddha, perceiving their distress, sent for them and said: "Monks, to one who is watchful and whose mind is stirred by remorse, the night is indeed long. To one who is weary with wandering, the journey is indeed long. Long is the round of rebirth to the fool who does not know the true Dhamma." Then he spoke the verse, and many attained Stream-entry.

**3** The verse "If a traveller does not meet with one who is his better" was spoken with reference to the Elder Mahakassapa, who for seven days practised walking meditation alone in a forest grove. The Buddha praised his solitary practice, teaching that companionship with a fool is worse than solitude.

**4** The verse "These sons belong to me, and this wealth belongs to me" was spoken with reference to the treasurer Ananda, who had amassed great wealth but died without ever having enjoyed it or shared it with others. He had buried five pots of gold, telling no one of their location. After death, he was reborn as a hungry ghost near his former home. The Buddha took the monks to see the wretched spirit hovering near its buried treasure, unable to enjoy what it once clung to so fiercely.

**5** The verse "The fool who knows his foolishness is wise at least so far" was spoken with reference to two monks — one who was learned but proud of his learning, and another who was unlettered but humble and sincere in practice. The proud scholar looked down upon the humble monk, but the Buddha declared that the one who knows his own limitations is wiser than the one who is deluded about his own attainments.

**6** The verse on the fool and the spoon was spoken with reference to the Elder Udayin, who though he lived long in the company of the Buddha, failed to grasp the deep meaning of the teaching. Like a spoon that passes through soup countless times without tasting it, Udayin heard the Dhamma daily but did not penetrate its meaning.

**7** The verses on evil deeds and their ripening were spoken with reference to a certain monk who, having committed an evil deed in a former life, experienced its consequences only after a long delay. The Buddha taught that evil, like milk fresh from the cow, does not curdle immediately; it follows the fool, smouldering like fire beneath ashes, until at last it blazes forth.

**8** The final verse, "One is the road that leads to wealth, another the road that leads to Nirvana," was spoken to a monk who was torn between the desire for worldly honour and the desire for liberation. The Buddha made clear that these two paths are utterly different, and that the true disciple abandons the pursuit of honour for the pursuit of freedom.
