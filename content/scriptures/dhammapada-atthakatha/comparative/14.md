Comparative study of the commentary on the The Buddha (Buddhavagga) chapter places its treatment of the rare appearance of a Buddha and the supreme value of the Buddha's teaching alongside similar themes in other religious and ethical traditions. The commentary's narrative approach — providing stories that illustrate moral principles — finds parallels in the Christian parable tradition, the Jewish midrash, the Islamic hadith and qisas al-anbiya literature, and the Hindu itihasa-purana tradition. Each of these traditions recognizes the power of narrative to communicate ethical truths more effectively than abstract argumentation.

The commentary's specific treatment of the rare appearance of a Buddha and the supreme value of the Buddha's teaching invites comparison with how other traditions address the same moral concerns. The Buddhist analysis of mental causation, the emphasis on personal responsibility for one's moral development, and the vision of liberation through ethical transformation all represent distinctive contributions to the world's moral literature while addressing concerns shared across cultures and traditions.
