## Celestial Cartography: Mapping the Afterlife Across Traditions

The First Book of Jeu's detailed mapping of sixty heavenly emanations — each with its own seal, number, and diagram — places it within a cross-cultural tradition of celestial cartography: the systematic mapping of the afterlife or the divine realm as a navigable space. This impulse to chart the invisible world appears across widely separated cultures.

The Egyptian *Amduat* ("That Which Is in the Underworld") provides an hour-by-hour map of the sun god's nighttime journey through the underworld, complete with illustrations of the beings encountered at each stage. The Tibetan Buddhist tradition of the *bardo* describes distinct stages of the afterlife, each with characteristic visions and dangers. The Islamic *mi'raj* narratives map Muhammad's ascent through seven heavens, each governed by a different prophet.

In each tradition, the map serves a dual function: it describes the objective structure of the cosmos, and it provides the individual with a guide for navigating that structure. The Books of Jeu's combination of text and diagram — verbal instruction supplemented by visual representation — anticipates the multimedia approach to spiritual cartography found in Tibetan thangka paintings, Kabbalistic diagrams of the sefirot, and Dante's architectonic rendering of Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise.
