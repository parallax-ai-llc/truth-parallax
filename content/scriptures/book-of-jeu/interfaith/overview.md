## Overview

The Books of Jeu address one of the most universal of human concerns: what happens after death, and how can the living prepare for the transition? Across cultures and centuries, religious traditions have developed elaborate systems for guiding the soul through the postmortem journey — from Egyptian funerary texts to Tibetan *bardo* teachings to Christian ars moriendi. The Books of Jeu belong to this global tradition of death preparation and afterlife cartography.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Universal Journey

Nearly every religious tradition posits some form of postmortem journey — whether through judgment halls (Egyptian, Islamic), heavenly palaces (Jewish hekhalot), purgatorial stages (Catholic), bardos (Tibetan Buddhist), or celestial realms (Gnostic). The Books of Jeu's detailed instructions for navigating the afterlife reflect a human need that transcends any single tradition.

### Knowledge as Preparation

The Books of Jeu insist that preparation for death involves acquiring specific knowledge — seals, passwords, invocations. This emphasis on knowledge resonates with traditions that teach the dying specific prayers or mantras: the Buddhist practice of *nianfo* (reciting the Buddha's name), the Christian commendation of the dying, the Hindu practice of hearing sacred texts at the deathbed.

### The Guide Figure

Jesus functions in the Books of Jeu as a guide for the postmortem journey — a role paralleled by Virgil in Dante's *Inferno*, the dakinis in Tibetan death traditions, and Hermes Psychopompos in Greek religion. The presence of a guide figure across traditions reflects the conviction that the journey after death is too dangerous to undertake alone.
