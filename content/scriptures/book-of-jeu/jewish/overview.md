## Overview

From a Jewish perspective, the Books of Jeu are significant for their extensive use of divine names, angelic hierarchies, and ascent motifs that have deep roots in Jewish mystical tradition. The texts illustrate the complex interchange between Jewish and Gnostic religious thought in late antiquity — a relationship of borrowing, transformation, and mutual influence that shaped both traditions.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Hekhalot Parallels

The structure of the Books of Jeu — ascent through multiple heavenly levels, each guarded by beings who demand specific passwords — closely parallels the *hekhalot* literature's description of the mystic's journey through the seven heavenly palaces. Scholars such as Gershom Scholem and Peter Schäfer have explored these parallels extensively, debating whether they reflect direct literary dependence or shared cultural milieu.

### Divine Names

The Books of Jeu make extensive use of divine names and *voces mysticae* (mystical utterances) as instruments of power. This practice resonates with the Jewish tradition of the power of the divine Name (*Shem ha-Meforash*), the theurgic use of names in *Sefer ha-Razim*, and the broader ancient Near Eastern conviction that knowledge of a being's true name confers power over it.

### Emanation and Sefirot

The sixty emanations of Jeu, each a distinct expression of divine light, invite comparison with the later kabbalistic concept of the ten *sefirot* as emanations of the divine *Ein Sof*. While direct historical connection is debated, the structural parallel — divine self-disclosure through a series of differentiated emanations — is striking.
