## Divine Names, Angelic Hierarchies, and the Jewish Mystical Tradition

The First Book of Jeu's system of sixty emanations, each with its own seal and name, draws extensively on Jewish traditions of divine naming and angelic classification. The use of *voces mysticae* — mystical utterances that combine Hebrew, Aramaic, and invented syllables — reflects a practice rooted in Jewish magical and mystical traditions, attested in texts such as *Sefer ha-Razim*, the Greek Magical Papyri, and the *Sar Torah* traditions.

For Jewish readers, the Books of Jeu illustrate the complex relationship between Jewish mystical practice and its Gnostic appropriation. The conviction that knowledge of the divine Name confers power is deeply rooted in Jewish tradition — from the Tetragrammaton's prohibition in post-exilic Judaism to the rabbinic legends about the seventy-two-letter Name and its theurgic applications. The Books of Jeu transform this tradition into a Gnostic system in which divine names function as passwords for cosmic ascent rather than as instruments of prayer or theurgic practice within the covenant.

The sixty emanations of Jeu also invite comparison with the angelic hierarchies of Jewish apocalyptic literature. The books of 1 Enoch, 2 Enoch, and 3 Enoch describe elaborate angelic orders, each with specific names, functions, and domains. The Books of Jeu's emanative system participates in this tradition while transforming its function: the angelic beings are not servants of the God of Israel but manifestations of a divine figure (Jeu) who mediates between the transcendent God and the material cosmos.
