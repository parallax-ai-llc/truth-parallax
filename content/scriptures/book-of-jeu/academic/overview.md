## Overview

The Books of Jeu, preserved in the Bruce Codex (Bodleian Library, MS Bruce 96), present significant challenges for textual scholarship. The codex was acquired by James Bruce in Upper Egypt circa 1769, making it one of the earliest Gnostic manuscripts to reach European collections. Carl Schmidt published the first critical edition in 1892, with Violet MacDermot providing an updated translation in 1978.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Manuscript and Codicological Issues

The Bruce Codex's physical condition and the arrangement of its leaves have generated scholarly debate. The codex contains the two Books of Jeu along with an untitled text (sometimes called the "Bruce Untitled Treatise"), and the relationship between these works — whether they form a coherent collection or were bound together incidentally — remains unresolved.

### Diagrammatic Tradition

The Books of Jeu are remarkable for their inclusion of diagrams depicting the emanations and heavenly realms. These visual representations — among the earliest in Christian esoteric literature — raise questions about the relationship between text and image in Gnostic ritual practice and about the transmission of knowledge through non-verbal means.

### Relationship to Pistis Sophia

The Pistis Sophia (Askew Codex) refers to the "Books of Jeu" by name, placing them within a broader literary and ritual context. Scholars debate whether the texts in the Bruce Codex are identical to those referenced in Pistis Sophia or whether the title designates a broader genre of Jeu-related literature.
