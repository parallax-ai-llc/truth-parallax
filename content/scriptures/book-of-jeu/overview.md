## Overview

The Books of Jeu (First and Second Book of Jeu) are Gnostic instructional texts preserved in the Bruce Codex, a Coptic manuscript acquired by the Scottish traveler James Bruce in Upper Egypt around 1769 and now housed in the Bodleian Library, Oxford. The texts are generally dated to the third century CE and represent a practical, ritual-oriented strand of Gnosticism concerned with the soul's postmortem ascent through the heavenly realms.

The central figure is Jeu, a divine emanation whom Jesus identifies as "the true God" and the overseer of the Treasury of Light. Jesus instructs his disciples — with Mary Magdalene and the Virgin Mary prominent among them — in the secret names, seals, numbers, and passwords required to pass through each level of the cosmic hierarchy. The texts are notable for their inclusion of diagrams depicting the heavenly realms, making them among the earliest illustrated Christian esoteric manuscripts.

## Structure

The work is divided into two books:

| Book | Title | Content |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The First Book of Jeu | Jesus reveals the sixty emanations of Jeu, each with its own seal, number, and character; the disciples learn the invocations needed to ascend through the Treasury of Light |
| 2 | The Second Book of Jeu | Jesus describes the three baptisms (water, fire, Holy Spirit), the mystery of forgiveness of sins, and the final mysteries that allow passage beyond the archons into the Treasury of Light |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Read as a ritual manual for initiates, providing the practical knowledge needed for the soul's ascent through hostile archontic realms
- **Academic**: Studied for its relationship to Pistis Sophia, its unique diagrammatic tradition, and its evidence of Gnostic liturgical practice
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to ascent traditions in Merkabah mysticism, Egyptian funerary texts, and Mandaean masiqta rituals
- **Christian**: Raises questions about the boundaries of early Christian sacramental theology, particularly baptism and the remission of sins
- **Jewish**: Examined for its use of Hebrew divine names, its parallels to hekhalot literature, and its transformation of Jewish angelic hierarchies
- **Interfaith**: Illustrates the cross-cultural phenomenon of guided postmortem journeys and the universal concern with what happens to the soul after death

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Cosmic Cartography | Detailed maps of the heavenly realms, each governed by specific beings with specific seals |
| Ritual Knowledge | Salvation depends on knowing the correct names, passwords, and gestures for each heavenly gate |
| Emanation | Jeu produces sixty emanations, each a distinct expression of divine light |
| Baptismal Mysteries | Three distinct baptisms (water, fire, Spirit) are required for full initiation |
| Treasury of Light | The ultimate destination of the saved soul, a realm of pure light beyond the material cosmos |
| Diagrams and Seals | Visual representations of heavenly spaces accompany the textual instructions |
