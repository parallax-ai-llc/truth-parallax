## Gnostic Baptism and Orthodox Sacramentology

The Second Book of Jeu's three baptisms present a striking parallel to the sacramental theology of the canonical New Testament and early church. Jesus's instruction to his disciples to receive baptisms of water, fire, and the Holy Spirit echoes the Baptist's prophecy (Matthew 3:11) and the Pentecost narrative (Acts 2:3-4). The "mystery of forgiveness of sins" corresponds to a core baptismal promise in canonical Christianity (Acts 2:38).

The fundamental divergence lies in the role of knowledge versus faith. In canonical Christian baptism, the recipient is asked to profess faith in the Triune God and to renounce sin. The sacrament is effective through the grace of the Holy Spirit, received in faith. In the Books of Jeu, baptism requires not only faith but the possession of specific esoteric knowledge — seals, passwords, invocations — without which the sacrament is incomplete. This subordination of grace to knowledge was precisely what the church fathers identified as the core error of Gnosticism.

The prominent role of Mary Magdalene and the Virgin Mary in the ritual instructions of the Books of Jeu is noteworthy for Christian readers. Both women actively participate in the discourse, ask questions, and receive baptism alongside the male disciples. This egalitarian representation contrasts with the increasingly restrictive attitudes toward women's liturgical roles that developed in mainstream Christianity from the third century onward.
