## The Emanations of Jeu and the Doctrine of God

The First Book of Jeu's description of sixty emanations proceeding from a divine figure raises fundamental questions about the Christian doctrine of God. Christian theology, as crystallized in the Nicene and Chalcedonian creeds, affirms a trinitarian God — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — in whom there is no hierarchy of being. The Son is "begotten, not made, of one substance with the Father." The Gnostic system of the Books of Jeu, by contrast, posits a cascading series of emanations in which each successive level is further removed from the divine source.

For Christian readers, this emanative theology is both recognizable and alien. The Johannine prologue ("In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God") and the Pauline hymn of Colossians 1:15-20 describe the Son as the agent of creation and the image of the invisible God — language that shares structural similarities with emanation theology. But the Christian tradition insisted on the full divinity of the Son, rejecting any system that made the Son (or any divine being) a diminished emanation of the Father. The Arian controversy of the fourth century turned precisely on this question.

The Books of Jeu's diagrammatic tradition also challenges Christian iconographic theology. While the Eastern Orthodox tradition eventually affirmed the theological legitimacy of icons (Second Council of Nicaea, 787), it did so within strict christological parameters. The Books of Jeu's diagrams represent a fundamentally different approach to visual theology — schematic and esoteric rather than representational and liturgical.
