The divine decision to destroy creation raises a question addressed by every tradition that includes a flood narrative: is divine destruction an act of justice, power, or something else entirely?

- **Judaism**: The flood is just — God responds to human wickedness (Genesis 6:5-7), but also merciful — God preserves Noah
- **Christianity**: The flood is read typologically as a figure of baptism and new creation
- **Islam**: Nuh's people are destroyed for refusing to heed the prophet's warning (Quran 11:25-48)
- **Hinduism**: Vishnu warns Manu of the flood as part of cosmic dissolution (*pralaya*) — destruction as natural cycle rather than punishment

The Eridu Genesis, by leaving the motivation unclear (or attributing it to divine annoyance), contributes a provocative perspective: perhaps divine decisions do not always conform to human expectations of justice.
