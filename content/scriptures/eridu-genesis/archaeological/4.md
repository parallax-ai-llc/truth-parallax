The concept of the divine assembly is attested in Mesopotamian texts from the Early Dynastic period onward. Temple architecture — particularly the design of large courtyard complexes — has been interpreted as reflecting the earthly analogue of the divine council: the gods assembled in their temple, as elders assembled in the courtyard.

Seal impressions from the third millennium BCE depict scenes of divine assembly, with a seated deity presiding over standing or approaching figures. These images provide visual evidence for the concept that the Eridu Genesis describes: a deliberative body of gods making collective decisions about cosmic governance.
