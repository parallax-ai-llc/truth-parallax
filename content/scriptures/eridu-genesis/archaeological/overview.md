## Overview

The Eridu Genesis is preserved on a single major cuneiform tablet, CBS 10673, excavated at Nippur by the University of Pennsylvania expedition in the 1890s. The archaeological context of both the tablet itself and the sites mentioned in the text — particularly Eridu, the city the text identifies as the first human settlement — provides essential material evidence for understanding this foundational composition.

## The Tablet

### CBS 10673
The tablet is a six-column Sumerian text, approximately 18 x 13 cm, now housed at the University of Pennsylvania Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology. It was discovered in the scribal quarter at Nippur, among thousands of other literary and administrative tablets. The tablet dates to the Old Babylonian period (c. 1600 BCE) based on paleographic analysis.

### Condition
Approximately two-thirds of the tablet's surface is damaged or illegible. The upper portions of all six columns are missing, and significant gaps exist throughout. No duplicate copies have been identified, making this a uniquely important artifact.

## Archaeological Correlations

### Eridu (Tell Abu Shahrain)
Excavations at Eridu by Fuad Safar (1946–1949) revealed:
- A sequence of eighteen superimposed temples, the earliest dating to the Ubaid period (c. 5400 BCE), supporting the text's claim that Eridu was the first city
- Evidence of the transition from village to urban settlement
- The cult of Enki/Ea, consistent with the text's theological framework

### Flood Deposits
Woolley's discovery of a "flood deposit" at Ur (a layer of water-laid silt between occupation levels) generated debate about archaeological evidence for a historical flood. Similar deposits at Shuruppak (Tell Fara) and Kish have been identified, though scholars now regard these as evidence of local flooding rather than a single catastrophic event.
