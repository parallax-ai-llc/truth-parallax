The opening section of the Eridu Genesis describes the creation of humanity, though the text is heavily damaged at this point. Surviving fragments indicate that the gods created humans to serve their needs — a theological motif consistent across Mesopotamian creation traditions (compare Atrahasis I, Enuma Elish VI). Thorkild Jacobsen's reconstruction suggests that the text described the conditions of the world before human creation — a 'when not yet' formula characteristic of Sumerian cosmogonic literature.

The fragmentary nature of this section has led to significant scholarly disagreement about the precise sequence of events. Miguel Civil's philological analysis identified key verbal forms suggesting purposeful divine action rather than spontaneous generation, aligning the Eridu Genesis with the 'creation through labor' model found in other Sumerian texts rather than the 'creation through word' model more familiar from Genesis 1.
