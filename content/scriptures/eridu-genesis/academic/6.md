The final section of the Eridu Genesis describes the aftermath of the flood: Ziusudra performs sacrifices, prostrates himself before Anu and Enlil, and is granted eternal life in Dilmun — the Sumerian paradise, identified by most scholars with the island of Bahrain or the adjacent Arabian coast. The text's concluding lines are fragmentary, but the essential narrative is clear: the flood hero is rewarded with deification, removed from the human world to a paradisiacal realm.

Scholarly attention has focused on the concept of Dilmun as a paradise — a land of purity, sunrise, and eternal life. Dilmun appears in other Sumerian literary texts (notably the myth of Enki and Ninhursag) as a primordial paradise, and the archaeological record confirms Dilmun (Bahrain) as a major trade center associated with purity and the freshwater springs that rise in the Gulf.
