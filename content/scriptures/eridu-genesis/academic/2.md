The second section of the Eridu Genesis describes the founding of the first five cities: Eridu, Bad-tibira, Larak, Sippar, and Shuruppak. Each city is assigned a patron deity, and kingship descends from heaven to be established among humans. This passage closely parallels the Sumerian King List, which names the same five antediluvian cities and assigns supernatural reign lengths to their kings.

Scholarship has debated the historical kernel behind this tradition. The five cities are real sites, all located in southern Mesopotamia, and their listing may preserve genuine memory of the earliest urban centers. Jacobsen argued that the tradition reflects the historical process of urbanization in the Uruk period (c. 4000–3100 BCE), while others have treated it as a purely literary and theological construction.
