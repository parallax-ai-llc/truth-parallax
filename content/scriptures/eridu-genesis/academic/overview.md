## Overview

Academic study of the Eridu Genesis centers on the fragmentary cuneiform tablet CBS 10673, a six-column Sumerian text discovered at Nippur and first published by Arno Poebel in 1914. Thorkild Jacobsen's landmark 1981 study coined the term "Eridu Genesis" and provided the interpretive framework that has shaped subsequent scholarship. The text's significance lies in its position as the earliest known creation-and-flood narrative, predating the Akkadian Atrahasis by several centuries.

## Key Scholarly Issues

### Textual Condition
The tablet is heavily damaged, with significant lacunae in all six columns. Only approximately one-third of the original text is legible. This fragmentary state means that scholarly reconstructions involve considerable inference, and the relationship between preserved passages remains debated.

### Dating and Composition
The tablet itself dates to the Old Babylonian period (c. 1600 BCE), but the traditions it preserves are considerably older. Miguel Civil and other scholars have argued that elements of the narrative can be traced to Early Dynastic and Ur III period compositions.

### Relationship to Later Traditions
The Eridu Genesis stands at the head of a literary tradition that includes:
- The Akkadian *Atrahasis* (c. 1700 BCE)
- The flood narrative in Tablet XI of the *Epic of Gilgamesh*
- The biblical flood narrative (Genesis 6–9)

Scholars debate whether these represent independent retellings of a shared oral tradition or direct literary dependence.
