## Overview

The Eridu Genesis is the earliest known creation and flood narrative, composed in Sumerian and dating to approximately 1600 BCE, though drawing on traditions reaching back to the third millennium BCE. Preserved on a fragmentary tablet discovered at Nippur (CBS 10673), the text recounts the creation of humanity, the founding of the first cities, the institution of kingship, and the great flood survived by the pious king Ziusudra. The name "Eridu Genesis" was coined by the Assyriologist Thorkild Jacobsen, reflecting the text's focus on Eridu as the first city.

## Structure

The six sections of the text trace a cosmological arc from creation to post-flood restoration:

1. **Section 1**: Creation of humanity and the establishment of civilization
2. **Section 2**: The founding of the first five cities and the descent of kingship from heaven
3. **Section 3**: The antediluvian period and the role of early kings
4. **Section 4**: The divine council's decision to send the flood
5. **Section 5**: Ziusudra receives warning and survives the deluge
6. **Section 6**: The restoration of life and Ziusudra's deification

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Eridu Genesis occupies a pivotal position in the study of ancient literature and religion:

- **Academic**: The foundational text for understanding Mesopotamian cosmogony and flood traditions
- **Comparative**: Directly ancestral to the Akkadian Atrahasis and the flood narrative in Genesis 6–9
- **Theological**: Addresses the relationship between divine purpose and human civilization
- **Literary**: Establishes the antediluvian/postdiluvian narrative framework adopted by later traditions
- **Archaeological**: The tablet's provenance at Nippur illuminates the city's role as a scribal and religious center
- **Interfaith**: The shared flood narrative connects Sumerian, Babylonian, biblical, and Quranic traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Creation | The gods create humanity to serve and worship them |
| Kingship | Divine institution of royal authority as cosmic order |
| The Flood | Divine destruction and the survival of a righteous figure |
| Civilization | The founding of cities as an act of divine planning |
| Deification | Ziusudra's elevation to immortality as reward for piety |
