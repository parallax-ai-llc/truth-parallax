The creation of humanity in the Eridu Genesis invites comparison with creation narratives across the ancient Near East and beyond. The Mesopotamian model — humanity created to serve the gods — contrasts sharply with the biblical account (Genesis 1:26-28), where humans are created in God's image and given dominion. The Atrahasis epic elaborates the Sumerian tradition: humans are created from clay mixed with the blood of a slain god, combining earthly material with divine essence.

Broader comparative parallels include Greek traditions of Prometheus fashioning humans from clay, the Egyptian account of Khnum shaping humans on a potter's wheel, and the Quran's description of Adam created from clay and divine breath (15:28-29). Each tradition addresses the same fundamental question — what are humans made of, and why do they exist? — with remarkably different answers.
