The divine council's decree to destroy humanity raises the most acute theological question in the text: is divine governance fundamentally just? The Atrahasis tradition, which elaborates the same story, suggests the gods' motivation is annoyance rather than moral judgment — humans are too noisy. If this was also the Eridu Genesis's rationale, then the flood represents arbitrary divine power rather than righteous judgment.

This theology of divine arbitrariness stands in stark contrast to the biblical flood narrative, where the flood is explicitly a response to human wickedness. The Mesopotamian tradition presents a darker theology: the gods can destroy what they created for reasons having nothing to do with human moral behavior.
