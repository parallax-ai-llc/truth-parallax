The deification of Ziusudra represents the text's most radical theological claim: a human being can become divine. This possibility — unthinkable in the later monotheistic traditions — is presented as the natural reward for extraordinary piety and survival of cosmic catastrophe. The boundary between human and divine, which the Descent of Inanna showed the gods cannot cross downward without consequence, can be crossed upward by an exceptional mortal.

The location of Ziusudra's paradise in Dilmun — associated with sunrise, fresh water, and purity — suggests a theology of reward that is spatial rather than moral: the deified hero is moved to a place of eternal blessing rather than transformed into a different kind of being.
