The Eridu Genesis presents a utilitarian theology of human creation: humanity exists to serve the gods. This purpose-driven creation theology raises the question of human dignity — if humans are created as servants, what is their inherent worth? The text offers no equivalent to the biblical *imago Dei* (image of God) or the Quranic *khalifah* (vicegerent).

Yet even within this functional framework, the act of creation itself implies divine intention and care. The gods do not merely produce servants; they create beings capable of building cities, establishing temples, and maintaining the cult — activities that require intelligence, skill, and devotion. The theology of the Eridu Genesis thus contains an implicit dignity: humans are the beings through whom divine worship becomes possible in the material world.
