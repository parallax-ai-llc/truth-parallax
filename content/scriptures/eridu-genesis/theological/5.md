Ziusudra's survival introduces the theme of divine mercy within divine judgment. The flood is decreed by the assembly, but one god (presumably Enki) subverts the decree to preserve a righteous man. This tension between collective divine judgment and individual divine mercy is theologically productive: it suggests that the divine is not monolithic but contains internal disagreement and compassion.

The characterization of Ziusudra as pious — he is described as a devout king who fears the gods — establishes the theological principle that righteousness can be recognized and rewarded even within cosmic catastrophe. The gods do not simply destroy; they discriminate between the wicked and the righteous, even when the formal decree makes no such distinction.
