---
title: "Digha Nikaya - Chapter 9: Potthapada Sutta (About Potthapada)"
scripture: "digha-nikaya"
chapter: 9
---

**1** Thus have I heard. On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling at Savatthi, in Jeta's Grove, Anathapindika's Park. At that time the wanderer Potthapada was at the Mallika debating hall with a large group of wanderers.

**2** The Blessed One went to visit them. Potthapada said: "Lord, there are some recluses and brahmans who say that a person's perceptions arise and cease without cause. Others say that perception is a person's self, which comes and goes. Others say that there are ascetics with great powers who can draw out a person's perception and insert it again. What does the Lord say?"

**3** The Blessed One said: "Potthapada, those who say that perceptions arise and cease without cause are wrong from the very start. For perceptions arise because of a cause and cease because of a cause. Through training, certain perceptions arise and certain perceptions cease."

**4** "And what is that training? Here, a monk dwells practising morality, then enters the first jhana, with applied and sustained thought, with rapture and happiness born of seclusion. His former perception of sensuality ceases, and the subtle, real perception of rapture and happiness born of seclusion arises. Through training, certain perceptions arise and certain perceptions cease."

**5** "Then, with the subsiding of applied and sustained thought, he enters the second jhana. His former perception ceases, and a new perception arises."

**6** "Then, with the fading of rapture, he enters the third jhana. Then, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, he enters the fourth jhana."

**7** "Then, by completely surmounting perceptions of material form, he enters the base of infinite space. Then the base of infinite consciousness. Then the base of nothingness. Then the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception."

**8** "Then, he enters the cessation of perception and feeling. His former perception ceases, and no new perception arises. This is the gradual cessation of perception through training."

**9** Potthapada asked: "Lord, is perception a person's self, or is perception one thing and the self another?" The Blessed One said: "What self do you posit, Potthapada?"

**10** "I posit a gross self, composed of the four elements, feeding on material food." The Blessed One said: "If that were so, then your perception would be one thing and your self another. For, through training, certain perceptions arise and cease while the gross self remains."

**11** "Or I posit a mind-made self," said Potthapada. "Or I posit a formless self, made of perception." The Blessed One said: "In each case, the same analysis applies. The perception is not the self. The self, as you conceive it, changes while remaining, and perception changes with training."

**12** "Potthapada, whether or not there is a self—this much is certain: the practice of morality, concentration, and wisdom leads to the abandoning of unwholesome states and the arising of wholesome states, to the increase of wisdom, to the realization of the full perfection of wisdom in this very life. This is what is meaningful. This is what leads to peace."
