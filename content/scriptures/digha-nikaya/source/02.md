---
title: "Digha Nikaya - Chapter 2: Samannaphala Sutta (The Fruits of the Contemplative Life)"
scripture: "digha-nikaya"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Thus have I heard. On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling at Rajagaha, in Jivaka's Mango Grove, with a large company of monks—twelve hundred and fifty monks.

**2** Now at that time King Ajatasattu Vedehiputta of Magadha, on the Uposatha day, the fifteenth, on the night of the full moon, was seated on the upper terrace of his palace surrounded by his ministers.

**3** And King Ajatasattu exclaimed: "What a beautiful moonlit night! To what ascetic or brahman could I pay a visit tonight, visiting whom my mind might find peace?"

**4** One minister suggested Purana Kassapa, another suggested Makkhali Gosala, another suggested Ajita Kesakambali, another suggested Pakudha Kaccayana, another suggested Sanjaya Belatthaputta, another suggested Nigantha Nataputta. But the king remained silent.

**5** Then Jivaka Komarabhacca, who had remained silent, said: "Lord, the Blessed One, the Arahant, the Perfectly Enlightened One, is dwelling in my mango grove. Let the king visit him." And the king agreed.

**6** Having arrived and exchanged courtesies, the king sat to one side and asked: "Lord, there are various crafts and professions, and those who practise them can point to the fruits of their work. Can the Lord show a similar fruit of the contemplative life, visible here and now?"

**7** "Great king, I shall ask you a counter-question. Suppose you had a slave who rose early and retired late, doing whatever you commanded. And suppose he thought, 'The king enjoys the fruits of his merit; I, too, am a human being. Why should I not do the same?' And he shaved off his hair and beard, put on ochre robes, and went forth from home to homelessness. Would you then say, 'Come back, slave, be my slave again?'"

**8** "No indeed, Lord. Rather, we would rise to greet him, invite him to sit, and provide him with robes, food, lodging, and medicine."

**9** "So there, great king, is a fruit of the contemplative life, visible here and now."

**10** "But, Lord, can you show another fruit, more excellent and more sublime?"

**11** "Here, great king, a Tathagata arises in the world. He teaches the Dhamma. A householder hears the Dhamma and gains faith. He goes forth, trains in the higher moral discipline, the higher mind, and the higher wisdom. He attains the four jhanas. He attains the three knowledges."

**12** "He understands: 'Birth is destroyed, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further beyond this.' This, great king, is a fruit of the contemplative life, visible here and now, higher and more sublime than the previous ones. And there is no fruit of the contemplative life higher and more sublime than this."
