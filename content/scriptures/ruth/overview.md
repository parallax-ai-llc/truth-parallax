## Overview

Ruth is a short narrative masterpiece set "in the days when the judges ruled" (1:1). It tells the story of Ruth, a Moabite woman who pledges loyalty to her Israelite mother-in-law Naomi and to Naomi's God. Through Ruth's faithfulness and Boaz's generosity as kinsman-redeemer (*go'el*), the family line is restored — and Ruth becomes the great-grandmother of King David.

## Structure

1. **Chapter 1**: Naomi's loss and Ruth's loyalty — "Where you go I will go" (1:16)
2. **Chapter 2**: Ruth gleans in Boaz's field — providence and *hesed*
3. **Chapter 3**: The threshing floor — Ruth's bold initiative and Boaz's integrity
4. **Chapter 4**: Redemption at the gate — marriage, birth of Obed, genealogy to David

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: One of the Five Megillot, read at Shavuot; Ruth as the paradigmatic convert; the genealogy connects to David and messianic hope
- **Christianity**: Ruth in the genealogy of Christ (Matthew 1:5); Boaz as type of Christ the Redeemer; the inclusion of a Gentile in Israel's story
- **Islam**: The themes of loyalty, providence, and divine care resonate with Quranic values
- **Gnosticism**: A rare text that some Gnostics could read positively — divine providence working outside the Demiurge's rigid law

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Hesed | Covenant loyalty and lovingkindness — the book's central virtue |
| Redemption | The *go'el* (kinsman-redeemer) as legal and theological concept |
| Providence | God works through ordinary human faithfulness |
| Inclusion | A Moabite woman becomes an ancestor of David and the Messiah |
| Loyalty | Ruth's devotion to Naomi transcends ethnic and religious boundaries |
