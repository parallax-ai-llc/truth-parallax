## Descent and Return

Jewish interpretation of Ruth 1 situates the narrative within the turbulent period of the Judges and reads it through the double lens of peshat (plain meaning) and midrash (homiletical expansion). The chapter's movement — from Bethlehem to Moab and back — is understood as a descent from holiness and a return to it.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "In the days when the judges ruled, there was a famine in the land" (1:1)

**Rashi** comments that the famine was a divine punishment for Israel's sins during the period of the Judges. Elimelech's departure to Moab is viewed critically: as a wealthy and prominent man, he abandoned his community in its time of need. The Midrash Ruth Rabbah (1:4) states that he was punished with death for leaving the Land of Israel.

### The Names as Narrative Theology

Rabbinic tradition reads the characters' names as prophetic:

| Name | Meaning | Significance |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Elimelech | "My God is King" | Ironic — he fled from God's domain |
| Naomi | "Pleasant" | She becomes Mara — "Bitter" |
| Mahlon | "Sickness" | Foreshadows early death |
| Chilion | "Annihilation" | Foreshadows early death |
| Orpah | "Back of the neck" | She turns away |
| Ruth | "Friend" or "Satiated" | She clings to Naomi |

### Ruth's Declaration as Model of Conversion (1:16-17)

The Talmud (Yevamot 47b) derives the laws of conversion from Naomi's three-fold attempt to dissuade Ruth. Each of Naomi's warnings corresponds to a halakhic requirement:

- "Your people shall be my people" — acceptance of the Jewish community and its obligations
- "Your God my God" — acceptance of monotheism and the yoke of the commandments
- "Where you die I will die, and there I will be buried" — commitment to Jewish burial and afterlife beliefs

Ruth's declaration is recited in some conversion ceremonies to this day.

### Orpah's Departure (1:14)

The Midrash contrasts Ruth and Orpah sharply. Orpah kissed Naomi goodbye and returned to Moab; Ruth clung to her. Rabbinic tradition (Ruth Rabbah 2:20) states that Orpah became the ancestress of Goliath — the enemy that Ruth's descendant David would defeat.

## Theological Themes

- **The Convert's Journey**: Ruth as the archetypal righteous convert (*ger tzedek*)
- **Hesed**: Lovingkindness that exceeds legal obligation — the chapter's governing virtue
- **Return and Teshuvah**: Naomi's physical return mirrors the spiritual concept of repentance
- **Reward and Consequence**: Elimelech's punishment and Ruth's reward illustrate divine justice

## Reflection

Jewish reading of this chapter finds in Ruth the embodiment of sincere conversion — a Moabite who, against all cultural and familial pressure, chooses the God of Israel. The rabbis celebrate Ruth precisely because her choice was costly: she had every reason to return to Moab, yet she clung to Naomi and to Naomi's God. In this, she becomes the model for all who enter the covenant by choice.
