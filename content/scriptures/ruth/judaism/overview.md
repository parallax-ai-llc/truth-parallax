## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Ruth (*Megillat Rut*) occupies a cherished place as one of the Five Megillot (scrolls) and is read liturgically on the festival of Shavuot. Ruth is regarded as the paradigmatic righteous convert (*giyoret*), and her declaration to Naomi — "Your people shall be my people, and your God my God" (1:16) — is understood as a model of sincere conversion. The book's genealogy, linking Ruth to King David, carries profound messianic significance.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Midrashic

The Midrash Ruth Rabbah and other rabbinic texts expand the narrative with rich homiletical interpretation. The rabbis explore Ruth's inner motivations, Naomi's three-fold discouragement (paralleling the halakhic requirement to dissuade converts), and the providential timing of Ruth's arrival at Boaz's field.

### Halakhic

Jewish legal commentators mine the text for precedents: the laws of gleaning (*leket, shikhhah, pe'ah*), levirate marriage (*yibum*), and the redemption of land. The book illustrates how Torah law functions in practice to protect the vulnerable.

### Kabbalistic and Mystical

Kabbalistic readings see Ruth's journey as a *tikkun* (repair) — the rectification of the spiritual damage caused by Lot's incestuous relationship with his daughters (the ancestral origin of Moab). Ruth's conversion transforms the impurity of Moab into the sanctity of David's line.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hesed**: The central virtue of the book — lovingkindness that goes beyond legal obligation
- **Conversion**: Ruth as the model convert; the Talmud derives laws of conversion from her words to Naomi
- **Shavuot Connection**: Ruth accepted Torah just as Israel did at Sinai; both harvest and revelation
- **Davidic Messiah**: The genealogy establishes that the Messiah descends from a righteous convert
- **Social Ethics**: The gleaning laws embody the Torah's concern for the poor, the stranger, and the widow

## Notable Jewish Commentators

- **Rashi**: Provided concise peshat (plain sense) commentary with midrashic elements
- **Ibn Ezra**: Offered grammatical and contextual analysis
- **The Zohar**: Read Ruth mystically as the soul's journey toward divine union
- **Yalkut Shimoni**: Collected midrashic traditions on Ruth
