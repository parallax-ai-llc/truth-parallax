## The Threshing Floor: Law and Love

Jewish interpretation of Ruth 3 reads the threshing-floor encounter through the lenses of halakhah (law), middot (character traits), and the concept of *tikkun* (cosmic repair). The rabbis carefully defended the modesty and righteousness of both Ruth and Boaz, while also mining the scene for legal and theological significance.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Naomi's Plan (3:1-4)

**Rashi** explains that Naomi sought Ruth's *manoah* ("rest" or "security") — a settled home and family. Her instructions are read as strategic rather than manipulative: Naomi understands the legal mechanism of the *go'el* (kinsman-redeemer) and guides Ruth toward activating it.

The Midrash (Ruth Rabbah 5:12) adds that Naomi waited until the harvest was finished because Boaz would be in a good mood after the celebration.

### "Uncover his feet and lie down" (3:4)

Rabbinic tradition vigorously defends the chastity of this encounter. **Rashi** interprets "feet" (*margelot*) literally — the place at his feet. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 19b) emphasizes that Boaz overcame his inclination (*yetzer ha-ra*) and behaved with exemplary restraint, comparing him to Joseph in Potiphar's house.

### "Spread your wing over your servant, for you are a redeemer" (3:9)

Ruth invokes the legal institution of the *go'el*. Jewish commentators note that she is not merely requesting charity but asserting a legal claim. The Targum renders *go'el* in terms of marriage obligation, connecting the scene to the laws of levirate marriage (*yibum*).

### The Nearer Kinsman (3:12)

Boaz's revelation that a closer redeemer exists demonstrates his commitment to legal process over personal desire. The Midrash praises his honesty: many men in his position would have remained silent.

## Theological Themes

- **Tikkun**: Ruth's union with Boaz repairs the damage of Lot's incestuous relationship with his daughter (the origin of Moab)
- **Yetzer Ha-Ra**: Boaz's mastery of his inclination as a model of righteous character
- **Halakhic Process**: Both Ruth and Boaz operate within legal channels, demonstrating respect for Torah
- **Modesty (Tzniut)**: The scene models modesty and restraint within a charged situation

## Reflection

The rabbis read this chapter as a triumph of character over circumstance. In the darkness of the threshing floor, where no one would have known what happened, both Ruth and Boaz chose righteousness. Jewish tradition sees in this the highest expression of piety — not the piety performed in public, but the integrity maintained in secret. The threshing floor becomes, paradoxically, a place of holiness.
