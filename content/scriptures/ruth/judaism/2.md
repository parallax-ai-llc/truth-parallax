## The Laws of the Harvest in Practice

Jewish interpretation of Ruth 2 finds in this chapter a living illustration of the Torah's agricultural laws in practice. The gleaning laws — *leket* (dropped sheaves), *shikhhah* (forgotten sheaves), and *pe'ah* (corners of the field) — are not abstract legal categories but the means by which Ruth survives and through which God provides.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Let me go to the field and glean" (2:2)

Ruth's initiative demonstrates her character. **Rashi** notes that she asked Naomi's permission, showing respect for her mother-in-law. The Midrash adds that Ruth chose to glean only where she was welcome, avoiding fields where she might face hostility as a Moabite foreigner.

### "She happened to come to the part of the field belonging to Boaz" (2:3)

**Ibn Ezra** reads this as simple narrative, but the Midrash sees divine orchestration. Ruth Rabbah (4:1) comments: "Rabbi Yohanan said, 'She came by her own effort, but the Holy One, blessed be He, arranged her steps.'" This reflects the rabbinic balance between human initiative and divine guidance.

### Boaz's Character (2:4-8)

Boaz is identified in rabbinic tradition as a judge and leader of his generation — some identify him with Ibzan of Bethlehem (Judges 12:8). His greeting to his workers, "The LORD be with you," reveals a man who integrates piety with daily life. The Talmud (Berakhot 54a) cites this verse as evidence that one may use God's name in ordinary greetings.

### The Gleaning Laws in Detail

| Law | Source | Application in Ruth 2 |
|-----|--------|-----------------------|
| Leket | Lev. 19:9 | Dropped stalks left for the poor |
| Shikhhah | Deut. 24:19 | Forgotten sheaves left in the field |
| Pe'ah | Lev. 19:9 | Corners of the field unharvested |

Boaz goes beyond these requirements, instructing his workers to pull stalks from the bundles and leave them for Ruth (2:16) — an act of *hesed* that exceeds the demands of *din* (strict justice).

## Theological Themes

- **Torah in Practice**: The gleaning laws as a living system of social welfare
- **Hesed Beyond Din**: Boaz's generosity exceeds legal requirements, modeling the rabbinic ideal
- **Divine Providence**: God works through human faithfulness and social structures
- **The Dignity of the Poor**: Ruth is treated with respect, not condescension

## Reflection

Jewish reading of this chapter celebrates the Torah's vision of a just society — one where the poor have legal rights to sustenance, where landowners are obligated to leave the margins for the marginal, and where generosity extends beyond obligation. Boaz is the model of the righteous person (*tzaddik*) who gives more than the law requires and does so with grace.
