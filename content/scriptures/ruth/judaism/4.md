## Redemption at the Gate and the Davidic Promise

Jewish interpretation of Ruth 4 reads the legal proceedings at the gate, the marriage, and the genealogy as the culmination of God's providential plan — a plan that transforms a Moabite convert into the great-grandmother of King David and an ancestor of the Mashiach (Messiah).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Legal Proceeding at the Gate (4:1-6)

The gate (*sha'ar*) is the place of justice in ancient Israel, where elders sat and cases were adjudicated. **Rashi** notes that Boaz assembled ten elders (4:2), which the Talmud (Ketubot 7a) takes as the source for the minyan (quorum of ten) required for the sheva berakhot (seven wedding blessings).

The unnamed kinsman (*ploni almoni* — "so-and-so") is deliberately left anonymous. The Midrash explains that because he refused to fulfill the mitzvah of redemption, he was denied the honor of being named in Scripture.

### The Sandal Exchange (4:7-8)

The removal of the sandal (*na'al*) ratifies the transaction. Jewish commentators connect this custom to the *halitzah* ceremony (Deuteronomy 25:9-10) — the formal release from levirate marriage obligation. The act transfers the right and duty of redemption from the unnamed kinsman to Boaz.

### The Genealogy and Messianic Hope (4:18-22)

The genealogy from Perez to David is the theological climax of the book:

- **Perez** was born from Tamar and Judah — another irregular union that served God's purposes
- **David** — Israel's greatest king — descends from a Moabite convert
- Rabbinic tradition extends this line to the Mashiach: the Messiah will descend from Ruth

The Zohar (1:188a) sees the genealogy as a chain of *tikkunim* (repairs), each generation rectifying the spiritual damage of the previous: Lot's incest is repaired by Ruth's conversion, and from this repair emerges the Davidic dynasty.

### "A restorer of life and a nourisher of your old age" (4:15)

The women's blessing to Naomi identifies Ruth as more precious than seven sons — the highest praise imaginable. **Ibn Ezra** notes that this inverts the cultural assumption of male superiority, elevating Ruth's *hesed* above all conventional measures of worth.

## Theological Themes

- **Go'el**: The kinsman-redeemer institution as both legal mechanism and theological symbol
- **Messianic Genealogy**: The Davidic line, and ultimately the Messianic hope, flows through Ruth
- **Tikkun**: The repair of Moab's origin through Ruth's righteous conversion
- **The Value of Hesed**: Ruth's lovingkindness is worth more than seven sons
- **Divine Justice**: The anonymous kinsman is forgotten; Ruth and Boaz are eternized

## Reflection

Jewish tradition reads the closing genealogy of Ruth not as a mere appendix but as the revelation of God's purpose in every preceding event. The famine, the exile, the deaths, the gleaning, the threshing floor — all led to this: the birth of Obed, the grandfather of David, the ancestor of the Mashiach. The Book of Ruth, read on Shavuot alongside the giving of the Torah, teaches that the Torah's deepest purpose is *hesed* — and that the future redemption of Israel grows from the faithfulness of a single Moabite woman who chose to cling to God.
