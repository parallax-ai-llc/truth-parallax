## Divine Provision and Social Responsibility

Islamic reading of Ruth 2 centers on the themes of divine provision (*rizq*), charitable giving, and social responsibility — values deeply embedded in the Quran and the practice of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The chapter's portrayal of gleaning laws and Boaz's generosity resonates with Islamic institutions of *zakat* (obligatory charity) and *sadaqah* (voluntary charity).

## Key Themes and Islamic Parallels

### Divine Provision (Rizq)

Ruth's arrival at Boaz's field illustrates the Quranic principle that God is *al-Razzaq* (the Provider). "And in the heaven is your provision and whatever you are promised" (Surah 51:22). Ruth went out seeking sustenance through honest labor, and God directed her to the source of provision. Islamic theology affirms that *rizq* is decreed by God but requires human effort — a principle exemplified in Ruth's industriousness.

### The Gleaning Laws and Zakat

The Israelite gleaning laws find a functional parallel in the Islamic institution of *zakat*:

| Israelite Practice | Islamic Parallel |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Leket (dropped sheaves) | Zakat (obligatory 2.5% of wealth) |
| Pe'ah (corners of field) | Sadaqah (voluntary charity) |
| Shikhhah (forgotten sheaves) | Waqf (charitable endowments) |

Both systems recognize that wealth carries social obligation and that the poor have a right (*haqq*) to a portion of the harvest.

### Boaz's Generosity as Ihsan

Boaz exceeds legal requirements in his treatment of Ruth — offering food, water, protection, and extra grain. This embodies the Islamic concept of *ihsan* (excellence in conduct), which the Prophet defined as "worshipping God as if you see Him" (Sahih Muslim). Generosity beyond obligation is a mark of true faith.

### Protection of the Vulnerable

Boaz instructs his workers not to harass Ruth (2:9) — protecting a foreign woman in a vulnerable position. The Quran repeatedly commands the protection of widows, orphans, and the vulnerable: "And do not approach the property of an orphan except in the way that is best" (Surah 6:152).

## Theological Themes

- **Rizq**: God provides sustenance through both natural means and human generosity
- **Social Justice**: Wealth carries obligation; the poor have rights to provision
- **Ihsan**: Excellence in character, going beyond the minimum requirement
- **Tawakkul and Effort**: Trust in God combined with diligent work

## Reflection

Islamic reading of Ruth 2 sees in the gleaning field a microcosm of the just society the Quran envisions — where wealth is shared, the vulnerable are protected, and generosity flows from awareness of God. Boaz is not merely a kind man; he is a model of the believer who recognizes that all provision comes from God and must be shared accordingly.
