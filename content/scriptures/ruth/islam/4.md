## Marriage, Lineage, and the Prophetic Chain

Islamic reading of Ruth 4 focuses on the themes of righteous marriage, community witness, and the prophetic lineage leading to Dawud (David) — recognized in Islam as both king and prophet. The chapter's resolution demonstrates how God's plan unfolds through faithful human action within established social institutions.

## Key Themes and Islamic Parallels

### Marriage as Sacred Institution

The public, witnessed marriage of Boaz and Ruth accords with Islamic principles of *nikah* (marriage contract):

- **Public declaration**: The marriage is announced before witnesses at the gate, as Islamic marriage requires witnesses (*shuhud*)
- **Mahr (dower)**: Boaz's assumption of the financial obligations parallels the Islamic requirement of *mahr*
- **Community blessing**: The elders' blessing (4:11-12) reflects the Islamic encouragement of communal celebration (*walimah*)

The Quran describes marriage as a "solemn covenant" (*mithaqan ghaliza*, Surah 4:21), and the marriage of Boaz and Ruth exemplifies this solemnity.

### The Prophetic Lineage to Dawud

The genealogy from Perez to David (4:18-22) has special significance for Muslim readers because Dawud is a prophet (*nabi*) and messenger in Islam. The Quran mentions Dawud multiple times:

- "And We gave him wisdom and discernment in speech" (Surah 38:20)
- He received the Zabur (Psalms) as revealed scripture
- He is a model of repentance and justice

Ruth's place in this lineage demonstrates that God chooses the instruments of his purpose according to his wisdom, not according to human expectations of lineage or ethnicity.

### Community Witness and Social Order

The legal proceedings at the gate — with named witnesses and formal procedures — reflect the Islamic emphasis on proper social order (*nizam*). The Quran commands: "And bring to witness two just men from among you" (Surah 65:2). The public nature of the transaction ensures justice and protects the rights of all parties.

### Divine Sovereignty (Qadar)

"The LORD gave her conception" (4:13) — the acknowledgment that children are a gift from God aligns with the Quranic teaching: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth. He creates what He wills. He gives to whom He wills female children, and He gives to whom He wills males" (Surah 42:49).

## Theological Themes

- **Marriage as Covenant**: The sacred, public, witnessed character of righteous marriage
- **Prophetic Lineage**: God's sovereign choice in establishing the line of prophets
- **Community and Justice**: Proper legal process protects the vulnerable and honors God's law
- **Divine Sovereignty**: God alone grants children, provision, and purpose

## Reflection

Islamic reading of Ruth 4 sees in the resolution of this narrative a confirmation of God's sovereignty over human history. Through the faithful actions of ordinary people — a loyal daughter-in-law, a generous landowner, a wise elder — God establishes the lineage that will produce Dawud, the prophet-king. The book of Ruth, read through Islamic eyes, is a testament to the truth that God's plan encompasses all nations and all peoples, and that faithfulness to God's moral law is the truest form of worship.
