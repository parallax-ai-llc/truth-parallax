## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Ruth draws on Scripture, Tradition, and the Magisterium to read this narrative as a rich tapestry of divine providence, sacramental theology, and moral virtue. The book has been valued in Catholic tradition for its portrayal of faithful womanhood, the sanctity of marriage, and the mysterious workings of grace through human cooperation.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic and Medieval Allegorical Reading

The Church Fathers and medieval commentators frequently read Ruth allegorically. Ruth the Moabite represents the Gentile Church, leaving behind pagan origins to cling to the God of Israel. Boaz represents Christ, the divine Bridegroom who redeems and weds his Church.

### Marian Typology

Catholic tradition sometimes sees in Ruth a prefigurement of Mary: a woman of humble station whose faithful "yes" cooperates with God's salvific plan. Just as Ruth's obedience placed her in the lineage of David, Mary's fiat brought forth the Messiah.

### Moral-Theological

Catholic moral theology highlights the virtues displayed in Ruth — loyalty, humility, chastity, and industriousness — as models for the faithful life. The Catechism's emphasis on the dignity of human work resonates with Ruth's gleaning.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sacramental Marriage**: The marriage of Ruth and Boaz prefigures the sacramental bond, reflecting Christ's union with the Church (Ephesians 5)
- **Cooperation with Grace**: Ruth's initiative on the threshing floor illustrates how human action cooperates with divine providence
- **The Communion of Saints**: The genealogy linking Ruth to David and ultimately to Christ demonstrates the interconnectedness of the faithful across generations
- **Social Justice**: The gleaning laws reflect Catholic social teaching on the universal destination of goods and the preferential option for the poor

## Notable Catholic Commentators

- **St. Ambrose**: Drew moral lessons from Ruth's loyalty and chastity
- **The Venerable Bede**: Provided allegorical commentary linking Ruth to the Church
- **St. Thomas Aquinas**: Referenced Ruth in discussions of natural law and virtue
- **Pope Benedict XVI**: Cited Ruth's faithfulness as an example of the "logic of love"
