## Redemption and the Sacramental Vision

Ruth 4 presents the legal redemption at the gate, the marriage of Boaz and Ruth, the birth of Obed, and the genealogy leading to David. Catholic interpretation reads this chapter through a sacramental and ecclesiological lens — the marriage as a sign of Christ and the Church, and the genealogy as the unfolding of God's salvific plan.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Nearer Kinsman's Refusal (4:6)

Catholic commentators contrast the unnamed kinsman's refusal with Boaz's willing self-offering. The parallel to Christ is drawn: the Law (represented by the nearer kinsman) identifies the need for redemption but cannot accomplish it at personal cost. Grace, embodied in Boaz, redeems at the price of self-giving love.

### The Public Transaction (4:9-12)

The witnesses at the gate who bless Boaz and Ruth invoke Rachel, Leah, and Tamar — mothers of Israel. Catholic liturgical theology notes the communal, public character of the event. Marriage in Catholic teaching is not a private contract but a sacrament witnessed and blessed by the community of faith.

### "The LORD gave her conception" (4:13)

Catholic theology reads this not as a formulaic statement but as an affirmation of divine cooperation in human procreation. Every child is a gift of God. The birth of Obed is an act of providence that connects Ruth's faithfulness to the Davidic and ultimately Messianic line.

### The Genealogy (4:18-22)

The ten-generation genealogy from Perez to David places Ruth within salvation history:

- Catholic Christology traces this line through Matthew 1:5 to Jesus
- Ruth's inclusion as a Gentile prefigures the universal Church, drawn from all nations
- The genealogy demonstrates that God's redemptive plan embraces the unexpected and the marginal

## Theological Themes

- **Sacramental Marriage**: The union of Boaz and Ruth as a sign of Christ's love for the Church (Ephesians 5:25-32)
- **The Universal Church**: Ruth the Moabite in the Messianic genealogy points to the catholicity of the Church
- **Redemption as Community Event**: The public transaction models the communal character of salvation
- **The Communion of Saints**: The genealogy links the faithful across generations in one salvific history

## Reflection

Catholic reading of this final chapter celebrates the fullness of God's redemptive work. What began with death in Moab ends with life in Bethlehem. The marriage of Boaz and Ruth is a sacramental sign; the birth of Obed is a gift of grace; the genealogy is a testament to God's faithfulness across generations. Ruth's story is the Church's story — the stranger welcomed, the broken redeemed, the barren made fruitful.
