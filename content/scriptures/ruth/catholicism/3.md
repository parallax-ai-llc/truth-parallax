## The Threshing Floor: Virtue and Trust

Catholic interpretation of Ruth 3 navigates the threshing-floor episode with attention to both its narrative boldness and its moral integrity. The Church Fathers consistently defended the chastity of Ruth and Boaz in this scene, reading it as a demonstration of virtue under pressure rather than as moral compromise.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Naomi's Instructions (3:1-4)

Naomi's detailed instructions — wash, anoint, dress, go to the threshing floor — have been read by Catholic commentators as a preparation for a covenantal encounter. **St. Ambrose** interpreted Ruth's anointing as a type of baptismal anointing, a spiritual preparation for union with the Redeemer.

### "Spread your wings over your servant" (3:9)

Ruth's request is both a marriage proposal and a theological statement. The "wings" (*kanaph*) evoke divine protection (Psalm 91:4) and the covenant relationship. Catholic bridal mysticism — from the Song of Songs through St. John of the Cross — sees in this moment an image of the soul's desire for union with God.

### Chastity and Restraint (3:10-14)

Boaz praises Ruth's *hesed* and promises to act as redeemer. Catholic moral theology notes:

- Both Ruth and Boaz exercise the cardinal virtue of temperance
- Their restraint demonstrates that passion ordered by virtue leads to authentic love
- The scene models the Catholic teaching that chastity is not the absence of desire but its proper ordering

**The Venerable Bede** praised Boaz's self-control as evidence of his worthiness to be an ancestor of Christ.

## Theological Themes

- **Virtue Ethics**: The chapter models prudence, temperance, and justice in the context of human desire
- **Bridal Mysticism**: The threshing floor as a place of intimate encounter between the soul and God
- **Obedience**: Ruth's obedience to Naomi illustrates the Catholic value of filial obedience within the order of charity
- **Cooperation with Grace**: Ruth's bold action is not presumption but cooperation with God's unfolding plan

## Reflection

Catholic readers find in this chapter a model of how human love, purified by virtue, becomes an instrument of God's saving purpose. The threshing floor is not a place of moral compromise but of covenantal encounter — where grain is separated from chaff, and where the faithful are tested and found worthy.
