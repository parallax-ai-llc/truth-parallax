## Overview

Gnostic engagement with the Book of Ruth is largely reconstructive, as no surviving Gnostic text offers a systematic commentary on this narrative. However, the themes of Ruth — exile, alienation, return, and redemption — resonate powerfully with core Gnostic myths. Modern scholars of Gnosticism can trace how Gnostic hermeneutical principles would likely have been applied to this text: reading it as an allegory of the divine spark's journey through the material world.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Allegorical-Pneumatic Reading

Gnostic interpreters characteristically read Hebrew Bible narratives as allegories of the soul's entrapment in matter and its liberation through *gnosis*. Ruth's journey from Moab (the realm of foreign gods / the material world) to Bethlehem (the place of divine bread / spiritual nourishment) maps onto the Gnostic myth of the soul's return to the Pleroma.

### Sophia Typology

Ruth can be read as a Sophia figure — the divine feminine principle exiled from her true home, wandering in a foreign land, until she is recognized and redeemed by a higher power (Boaz as a figure of the Redeemer-Aeon).

### Subversive Reading of Torah Law

Gnostics often read the Torah's legal framework as the work of the Demiurge. In Ruth, the *go'el* institution and levirate law could be read as material-world structures that are ultimately transcended by the deeper spiritual reality of Ruth's transformation.

## Theological Emphases

- **Exile and Return**: Ruth's journey from Moab to Bethlehem as the soul's journey from ignorance to gnosis
- **The Divine Spark**: Ruth carries within her a pneumatic seed that the material world cannot extinguish
- **Sophia Parallels**: Ruth as the exiled feminine divine, longing for restoration
- **Transcendence of Law**: The legal mechanisms of redemption point beyond themselves to spiritual liberation
- **Bethlehem as Pleroma**: The "House of Bread" as a symbol of spiritual fullness and divine nourishment

## Notable Gnostic and Scholarly Sources

- **The Gospel of Philip**: Contains analogies of bridal-chamber mysticism relevant to Ruth's threshing-floor scene
- **Valentinian theology**: The Sophia myth provides a framework for reading Ruth's exile and restoration
- **Elaine Pagels**: Modern scholarship on Gnostic hermeneutics applicable to Old Testament narratives
- **Karen King**: Reconstructive work on Gnostic reading practices
