## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Ruth is shaped by the patristic consensus, liturgical tradition, and the theology of *theosis* (deification). The book is read within the divine economy (*oikonomia*) — God's providential ordering of all things toward salvation. Ruth's journey from Moab to Bethlehem is understood as a movement from death to life, from idolatry to true worship, mirroring the soul's journey toward God.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Typology

The Greek and Syriac Fathers read Ruth through a typological lens. Ruth is a type of the soul turning from the darkness of paganism to the light of the true God. Boaz is a type of Christ, the true Kinsman-Redeemer who restores fallen humanity through his incarnation and sacrificial love.

### Liturgical Context

In Orthodox tradition, the narrative of Ruth is encountered within the broader liturgical reading of the Old Testament. The themes of harvest, bread, and redemption connect to the Eucharistic celebration — Bethlehem itself means "House of Bread."

### The Divine Economy

Orthodox theology reads Ruth as a revelation of God's *oikonomia*: his providential arrangement of human history toward the Incarnation. Every detail — the famine, the journey, the gleaning, the marriage — serves God's purpose of bringing forth the Messiah.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: Ruth's transformation from Moabite widow to ancestress of Christ illustrates the soul's journey toward deification through grace and synergy
- **Kenotic Love**: Ruth's self-emptying loyalty to Naomi mirrors the kenosis of Christ (Philippians 2:7)
- **Bethlehem as House of Bread**: The Eucharistic overtones of the setting are deeply significant in Orthodox reading
- **Synergy**: The cooperation of divine grace and human will, visible in Ruth's free choice to follow Naomi's God

## Notable Orthodox Commentators

- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Offered poetic meditations on Ruth's faithfulness
- **Theodoret of Cyrrhus**: Provided patristic commentary on Ruth's typological significance
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Referenced Ruth in homilies on virtue and divine providence
