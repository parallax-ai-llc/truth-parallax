## The Threshing Floor as Sacred Encounter

Orthodox interpretation of Ruth 3 reads the threshing-floor scene with patristic sobriety and mystical depth. The threshing floor is a place of separation — grain from chaff — and thus a place of purification and judgment. In Orthodox typology, the threshing floor is also a place of theophany: David will later build the Temple on the threshing floor of Araunah (2 Samuel 24:18-25).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Ruth's Preparation (3:3)

"Wash and anoint yourself, and put on your best clothes." The Greek Fathers read these instructions as a spiritual preparation:

- **Washing**: Baptismal purification — cleansing from the old life
- **Anointing**: Chrismation — the seal of the Holy Spirit
- **New garments**: Putting on the "new self" (Colossians 3:10)

**St. Ephrem the Syrian** saw in Ruth's preparation an image of the soul adorning itself for the encounter with the divine Bridegroom.

### "Spread your wings over your servant" (3:9)

Orthodox bridal theology reads this as an icon of the soul's desire for union with God. The "wings" (*kanaph*) connect to the liturgical imagery of the cherubim covering the mercy seat — the place where God meets his people. Ruth's request is both earthly (marriage) and heavenly (divine union).

### The Night Vigil (3:7-14)

Ruth lies at Boaz's feet through the night — a scene Orthodox interpreters read as a vigil of faith. The night setting evokes the Paschal vigil and the monastic tradition of nighttime prayer. Both Ruth and Boaz demonstrate the ascetic virtue of restraint — desire purified by patience and trust.

## Theological Themes

- **Purification**: The threshing floor as a place where the soul is refined
- **Bridal Mysticism**: The encounter between Ruth and Boaz as an icon of the soul's union with Christ
- **Ascetic Restraint**: Both characters demonstrate the Orthodox virtue of self-mastery (*enkrateia*)
- **Liturgical Typology**: The threshing floor as the future site of the Temple, connecting Ruth's story to liturgical worship

## Reflection

Orthodox readers approach this chapter with reverence for its mystery. The threshing floor is liminal space — between night and day, between law and grace, between the human and the divine. Ruth's boldness is not recklessness but the daring of faith; Boaz's restraint is not coldness but the patience of love. Together they enact a drama that prefigures the great bridal mystery: Christ and his Church.
