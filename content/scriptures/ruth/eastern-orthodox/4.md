## The Fulfillment of the Divine Economy

Ruth 4 brings the narrative to its providential conclusion. Orthodox interpretation reads this chapter as the culmination of God's *oikonomia* — his plan for the salvation of the world worked out through the faithfulness of ordinary people. The redemption at the gate, the marriage, and the genealogy all point forward to the Incarnation.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Nearer Kinsman's Refusal (4:6)

Orthodox Fathers saw the unnamed kinsman's refusal as symbolic of the Old Covenant's inability to fully redeem humanity. The Law reveals the need for redemption but cannot accomplish it. Boaz, who willingly takes on the cost of redemption, prefigures Christ, who "emptied himself" (Philippians 2:7) to redeem what the Law could not.

### The Witnesses and Blessing (4:11-12)

The community's blessing at the gate — invoking Rachel, Leah, and Tamar — places Ruth within the great narrative of Israel's mothers. Orthodox liturgical theology values the communal, public character of this moment: redemption is not a private transaction but a cosmic event witnessed by the assembled people of God.

### "The LORD gave her conception" (4:13)

Orthodox theology reads this as an affirmation of God's direct involvement in the gift of life. The birth of Obed is an act of divine grace that restores Naomi's emptiness to fullness — an icon of the resurrection, where death is swallowed by life.

### The Genealogy (4:18-22)

The ten-generation genealogy from Perez to David is read in Orthodox tradition as a "genealogy of grace":

- Each generation is a link in the chain of God's providential plan
- Ruth the Moabite demonstrates that the divine economy embraces all nations
- The genealogy points beyond David to Christ — the ultimate fulfillment of the promise
- **Theodoret of Cyrrhus** noted that God chose to include a Gentile woman in the Messianic line precisely to demonstrate the universality of salvation

## Theological Themes

- **The Divine Economy Fulfilled**: The entire narrative reveals God's providential ordering of history toward the Incarnation
- **Cosmic Redemption**: Boaz's redemption of Ruth prefigures Christ's redemption of all creation
- **The Genealogy of Grace**: The Messianic line includes the unexpected, demonstrating God's boundless mercy
- **Resurrection Typology**: Naomi's restoration from emptiness to fullness images the movement from death to life

## Reflection

Orthodox reading of Ruth 4 sees in the genealogy not merely a list of names but a hymn to God's faithfulness. From Perez — born of Tamar's desperate act — to David — the king after God's own heart — every generation is sustained by grace. And at the center of this genealogy stands Ruth, the Moabite woman whose love carried her from the land of death to the House of Bread, where the Bread of Life would one day be born. The entire book of Ruth is, in the Orthodox vision, a preparation for the Incarnation.
