## Naomi's Tragedy and Ruth's Declaration

Ruth 1 opens with a devastating cascade of loss. Elimelech takes his family to Moab during a famine — a move that Protestant commentators have often viewed as a lapse of faith, leaving the Promised Land for pagan territory. The deaths of Elimelech, Mahlon, and Chilion leave Naomi bereft, setting the stage for the book's central drama of redemption.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "In the days when the judges ruled" (1:1)

Protestant interpreters emphasize the historical setting as a time of spiritual and moral decline (cf. Judges 21:25, "everyone did what was right in his own eyes"). Ruth's faithfulness stands in sharp contrast to the unfaithfulness that characterizes the era.

### Naomi's Bitter Return (1:19-21)

Naomi's request to be called *Mara* ("bitter") rather than *Naomi* ("pleasant") expresses a raw honesty before God that Protestant pastoral theology values. She does not hide her grief; she names it. Yet her return to Bethlehem — whose name means "House of Bread" — at the beginning of the barley harvest signals that God's provision is already underway.

### "Where you go I will go" (1:16-17)

Ruth's declaration is one of the most celebrated passages in Protestant devotional literature. It represents:

- **Covenant commitment**: Ruth binds herself to Naomi and to Naomi's God with language that echoes covenant formulae
- **Faith over circumstance**: Ruth chooses loyalty despite having no guarantee of provision or security
- **Grace foreshadowed**: A Moabite — from a people excluded from the assembly (Deuteronomy 23:3) — is drawn into Israel by faith, not by birth

**Matthew Henry** observed that Ruth's words reveal a heart already transformed by grace: she chose not merely Naomi, but Naomi's God.

## Theological Themes

- **Providence in Suffering**: God's hand is not visible in the chapter's tragedies, but the reader knows the outcome — a pattern Protestants identify as ordinary providence
- **The Sovereignty of God**: Even the famine and the deaths serve God's redemptive purpose
- **Faith and Works**: Ruth's loyalty is not mere sentiment but costly action — she leaves everything to follow
- **Gentile Inclusion**: From the very first chapter, the inclusion of a foreigner in God's plan is established

## Reflection

Protestant preachers have long drawn from this chapter the theme that God's greatest works often begin in the darkest circumstances. The road from Moab to Bethlehem is a journey from despair to hope, from emptiness to fullness — a pattern that mirrors the gospel itself.
