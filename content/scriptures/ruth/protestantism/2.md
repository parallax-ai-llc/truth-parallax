## Providence at Work

Ruth 2 introduces Boaz and narrates Ruth's "chance" arrival at his field — a moment Protestant interpreters regard as a masterclass in divine providence. The narrator's comment that Ruth "happened to come to the part of the field belonging to Boaz" (2:3) is understood as deliberate irony: nothing in God's economy happens by chance.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Boaz's Greeting (2:4)

"The LORD be with you!" — Boaz greets his workers with a blessing that reveals his character. Protestant commentators note that his piety is not merely private but shapes his public and economic life.

### "Under whose wings you have come to take refuge" (2:12)

Boaz's blessing of Ruth employs the image of divine wings — an image echoed in Psalm 91:4 and Matthew 23:37. Protestant typology sees Boaz extending the very wings of God's protection to Ruth, foreshadowing Christ's welcome of sinners.

### Extraordinary Generosity (2:14-16)

Boaz goes far beyond the legal minimum required by the gleaning laws:

- He invites Ruth to eat with the reapers
- He commands his men to leave extra grain deliberately
- He ensures her safety and dignity

**Spurgeon** preached that Boaz's generosity illustrates the superabundance of God's grace — God does not merely permit sinners to glean at the edges; he invites them to the table.

## The Gleaning Laws and Grace

Protestant interpreters see the gleaning laws (Leviticus 19:9-10; 23:22) as embedded in the Torah to provide for the vulnerable. Yet Boaz exceeds the law's requirements, illustrating the Reformation principle that grace surpasses law. The law provides a safety net; grace provides a feast.

## Theological Themes

- **Ordinary Providence**: God works through human decisions, agricultural seasons, and social institutions
- **Christ as Boaz**: The kinsman who notices the outcast and provides abundantly
- **The Dignity of Work**: Ruth's willingness to labor in the fields is honored, not demeaned
- **Hesed in Action**: Boaz's kindness is not abstract but concrete — food, protection, and honor

## Reflection

This chapter teaches that providence often wears the garments of the ordinary. Ruth went out to work; God directed her steps. Boaz followed the law; God multiplied his generosity into redemption. Protestant theology finds in this the assurance that God is always at work, even when his hand is hidden.
