## Ruth's Bold Initiative

Ruth 3 presents the dramatic threshing-floor encounter between Ruth and Boaz. Naomi instructs Ruth to approach Boaz at night, uncover his feet, and lie down — a scene that has generated extensive interpretive discussion among Protestant commentators. The majority view holds that while the scene carries unmistakable romantic and even sexual overtones, both Ruth and Boaz act with integrity.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Wash and anoint yourself" (3:3)

Naomi's instructions to Ruth echo bridal preparation. Protestant commentators note the transformation: Ruth sets aside her widow's garments, signaling a transition from mourning to hope. Some Reformed interpreters see here a picture of the believer preparing to meet Christ.

### "Spread your wings over your servant" (3:9)

Ruth's request to Boaz deliberately echoes his earlier blessing — that she would find refuge under the "wings" of the God of Israel (2:12). She is, in effect, asking Boaz to become the answer to his own prayer. Protestant preachers have drawn from this the principle that God often uses human agents to fulfill his promises.

The word *kanaph* ("wing" or "corner of garment") connects divine protection with the concrete act of marriage — a union of theology and everyday life that Protestant theology values.

### The Nearer Kinsman (3:12-13)

Boaz reveals that a closer kinsman-redeemer exists who has the prior right of redemption. His honesty and patience in the face of desire demonstrate integrity. Protestant commentators emphasize that Boaz submits to the legal process rather than seizing what he wants — a model of godly self-restraint.

## Theological Themes

- **Human Initiative and Divine Sovereignty**: Ruth acts boldly; God directs the outcome
- **The Kinsman-Redeemer**: The *go'el* concept as a foreshadowing of Christ's redemptive work
- **Integrity Under Pressure**: Both Ruth and Boaz resist the temptation of expediency
- **Faith in Action**: Ruth's approach to Boaz is an act of trust, not manipulation

## Reflection

Protestant interpretation of this chapter resists both prudish avoidance and sensationalism. The threshing floor is a place of vulnerability, honesty, and covenant-making. Ruth's boldness is an expression of faith; Boaz's restraint is an expression of righteousness. Together, they model how human love can serve divine purposes.
