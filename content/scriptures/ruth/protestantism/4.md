## Redemption at the Gate

Ruth 4 brings the narrative to its resolution at the city gate — the place of legal transaction and public witness. Boaz navigates the kinsman-redeemer process with skill and integrity, securing the right to redeem Elimelech's land and to marry Ruth. The unnamed nearer kinsman declines, and Boaz steps forward.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Nearer Kinsman's Refusal (4:6)

"I cannot redeem it for myself, lest I impair my own inheritance." Protestant commentators see here a contrast between self-interested calculation and self-giving love. The nearer kinsman represents the law, which identifies the need for redemption but cannot accomplish it without cost. Boaz represents grace, which pays the price willingly.

### The Sandal Ceremony (4:7-8)

The exchange of a sandal as legal confirmation draws from an ancient custom. Protestant scholars note the concrete, public nature of the transaction — redemption is not a private spiritual event but a visible, communal act.

### "So Boaz took Ruth" (4:13)

The marriage of Boaz and Ruth fulfills the promise of the entire narrative. Protestant interpreters highlight the language of "taking" — Boaz receives Ruth as Yahweh had received Israel, and as Christ receives the Church.

### The Genealogy (4:18-22)

The book concludes with a ten-generation genealogy from Perez to David. This genealogy is theologically explosive for Protestant readers:

- It establishes Ruth — a Moabite — in the ancestry of Israel's greatest king
- Matthew 1:5 places Ruth in the genealogy of Jesus Christ
- The inclusion of a Gentile woman in the Messianic line demonstrates that salvation was never intended for Israel alone

## Theological Themes

- **Redemption Accomplished**: The legal process at the gate mirrors the finality of Christ's atoning work
- **Law and Grace**: The nearer kinsman (law) cannot redeem; Boaz (grace) willingly does
- **The Genealogy of Grace**: God's redemptive plan includes the unexpected, the outsider, and the broken
- **Public Witness**: Redemption happens before witnesses — it is a community event

## Reflection

Protestant theology reads this chapter as the climax of a story that began in death and ends in life, began in emptiness and ends in fullness. Naomi holds a child in her arms; the women of Bethlehem declare that God has not left her without a redeemer. The genealogy stretching to David — and beyond David to Christ — places this small story within the vast sweep of salvation history. The book of Ruth is, finally, a gospel in miniature.
