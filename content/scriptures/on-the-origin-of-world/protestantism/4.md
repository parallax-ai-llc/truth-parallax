The eschatological vision in chapter 4 shares with biblical eschatology the conviction that the present world order will come to an end. However, the nature of that end differs fundamentally. Protestant eschatology, grounded in texts like Revelation 21-22, Romans 8:19-23, and 2 Peter 3:13, anticipates the renewal and transformation of creation — "a new heaven and a new earth." On the Origin of the World envisions the annihilation of the material cosmos and the destruction of the archons, with no promise of a renewed creation.

This difference reflects the underlying theological divergence between the two systems. Protestant theology affirms the essential goodness of creation and therefore hopes for its redemption; the Gnostic text treats creation as inherently flawed and therefore hopes for its elimination. Protestant interpreters see in this contrast a fundamental choice between two visions of the material world — one that sees matter as God's good gift, capable of being renewed, and one that sees it as a prison from which escape is the only salvation.
