The figure of Pistis Sophia in chapter 2 draws on the biblical Wisdom tradition — particularly Proverbs 8 and the Wisdom of Solomon — but deploys these traditions in a way that Protestant interpreters find deeply problematic. In Proverbs 8, Wisdom (Hokhmah) is present with God at creation and delights in the inhabited world; she is a joyful collaborator in God's good creative work. In On the Origin of the World, Sophia's interaction with chaos produces the ignorant Demiurge — a catastrophic event, not a celebration of creation's goodness.

Protestant theology sees in this transformation of the Wisdom tradition a cautionary example of theological speculation untethered from the canonical witness. The biblical Wisdom figure affirms the goodness and order of creation; the Gnostic Sophia explains how creation went wrong. These are not merely different readings of the same tradition but fundamentally opposed theological visions. Protestant interpreters insist on reading the Wisdom texts within their canonical context, where they function as affirmations of God's sovereign creative wisdom rather than explanations for cosmic tragedy.
