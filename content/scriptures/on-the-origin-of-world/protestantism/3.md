Chapter 3's account of humanity's creation and the Garden carries forward the inversions found in other Gnostic Eden narratives. Protestant theology maintains that humanity is created in the image and likeness of God (imago Dei) as an act of purposeful love, that the Garden of Eden was a place of genuine blessing, and that God's commands to Adam were expressions of fatherly care. On the Origin of the World reverses each of these: the body is an archontic creation, paradise is a trap, and divine commands are instruments of control.

The text's eclectic blending of traditions — incorporating Greek mythological elements alongside its rereading of Genesis — further distances it from Protestant hermeneutical commitments. Protestant interpreters prioritize the plain sense of Scripture and are wary of readings that impose external philosophical or mythological frameworks on the biblical text. On the Origin of the World's synthesis of Gnostic, Platonic, and biblical elements represents exactly the kind of theological syncretism that the Reformation sought to counter with its return to Scripture alone as the source and norm of Christian teaching.
