Protestant creation theology begins with the affirmation that God created all things from nothing (ex nihilo) by his sovereign word. Chapter 1 of On the Origin of the World presents a fundamentally different picture: matter emerges from primordial chaos through a process that does not involve the true God's intentional creative act. This cosmogony removes divine sovereignty from the origin of the material world, treating matter as an autonomous emergence from shadow and deficiency rather than as the product of God's purposeful will.

The text's philosophical engagement with the concept of chaos invites comparison with Genesis 1:2 ("the earth was without form and void"), but the similarities mask a profound theological difference. In Protestant readings of Genesis, the initial formlessness is the raw material that God immediately shapes by his word; it does not exist independently of God's creative will. On the Origin of the World, by contrast, treats chaos as a pre-existing condition that gives rise to matter apart from and prior to the Demiurge's activity, effectively removing the origin of the physical world from divine control.
