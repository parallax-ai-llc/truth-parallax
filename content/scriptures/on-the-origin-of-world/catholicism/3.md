Catholic anthropology teaches that humanity is created in the image and likeness of God, endowed with reason, free will, and an immortal soul. The body is not a prison but an integral part of the human person, destined for resurrection. On the Origin of the World's presentation of human creation — in which the archons fashion bodies to trap divine light — contradicts each of these affirmations. The human body, in Catholic teaching, is a temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19), not an archontic trap.

The text's account of the Garden also conflicts with Catholic teaching on original sin and original justice. Catholic doctrine holds that Adam and Eve were created in a state of original justice and holiness, which they lost through their free choice to disobey God's command. On the Origin of the World presents the Garden as a mechanism of control and the acquisition of knowledge as liberation. These are irreconcilable frameworks: one treats the original state as blessed and the fall as catastrophic; the other treats the original state as imprisonment and the fall as awakening.
