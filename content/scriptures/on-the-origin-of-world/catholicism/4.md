Catholic eschatology anticipates not the annihilation of creation but its renewal and perfection. The Catechism teaches that "at the end of time, the Kingdom of God will come in its fullness" and that "the visible universe itself is destined to be transformed" (CCC 1042-1047). On the Origin of the World's vision of cosmic conflagration and the destruction of the archons presents a starkly different hope — one centered on escape from and destruction of the material order rather than its redemption.

The Catholic hope for the resurrection of the body further distinguishes its eschatology from the vision presented in chapter 4. Catholic teaching affirms that the bodies of the dead will be raised and transformed, participating in the glory of the risen Christ. On the Origin of the World, which views the body as an archontic creation, can offer no such hope for material existence. The text's eschatological vision is one of liberation from matter, while Catholic eschatology envisions the glorification of matter — a difference that reflects the fundamental divergence between the two theological systems on the question of creation's value.
