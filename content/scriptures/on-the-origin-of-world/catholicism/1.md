Catholic creation theology, as defined by the Fourth Lateran Council and the First Vatican Council, teaches that God created all things from nothing (ex nihilo) at the beginning of time. Chapter 1 of On the Origin of the World presents a cosmogony incompatible with this teaching: matter emerges from primordial chaos without the involvement of the supreme God. The very concept of pre-existing chaos — a substrate from which the material world arises independently of divine will — undermines the Catholic doctrine that nothing exists apart from God's creative act.

The philosophical sophistication of the chapter's argument does not, from the Catholic perspective, mitigate its theological errors. The Church Fathers engaged with similar philosophical cosmogonies — particularly Platonic accounts of the Demiurge working with pre-existing matter — and consistently affirmed the distinctively biblical teaching that God alone is the source of all being. On the Origin of the World's synthesis of Gnostic and philosophical concepts produces a cosmogony that is intellectually interesting but doctrinally unacceptable.
