The Pistis Sophia figure of chapter 2 represents a dramatic transformation of the biblical Wisdom tradition that Catholic theology cannot accept. In the Catholic canon, Wisdom literature (Proverbs, Sirach, Wisdom of Solomon) presents personified Wisdom as God's companion in creation, a figure the Church Fathers associated with the eternal Logos (John 1:1-3). On the Origin of the World's Sophia, by contrast, is a tragic figure whose interaction with chaos produces the ignorant Demiurge — a narrative of cosmic error rather than divine collaboration.

Catholic Mariology occasionally draws on Wisdom traditions, identifying aspects of the personified Sophia with Mary, the Mother of God. The Gnostic transformation of Sophia into a fallen divine being whose error generates the material world stands in stark contrast to the Catholic vision of Mary as the New Eve whose obedience ("Let it be done to me according to your word") participates in the redemption of the world. The Gnostic Sophia and the Catholic Mary occupy structurally similar positions — divine feminine figures at a pivotal moment in cosmic history — but their theological significance is entirely opposed.
