The concept of primordial chaos in chapter 1 invites comparison with the Hebrew Bible's tohu wa-bohu — the "formless and void" of Genesis 1:2. Jewish interpreters across the centuries have debated the nature of this primordial state, with some (such as the medieval commentator Nahmanides) positing a formless primal matter that God subsequently shaped, and others insisting on creation ex nihilo in the strictest sense. On the Origin of the World's treatment of chaos as an independent, pre-divine substrate goes well beyond anything found in the Jewish interpretive tradition.

Jewish scholars studying this text note its appropriation and transformation of Jewish cosmogonic concepts. The text takes the brief and enigmatic reference to pre-creation chaos in Genesis and expands it into an elaborate cosmogonic narrative in which chaos gives rise to matter independently of God's creative will. This represents a significant departure from the Jewish understanding that even the primordial state described in Genesis 1:2 exists within the framework of God's sovereignty and purpose.
