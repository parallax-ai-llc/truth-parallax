## Overview

On the Origin of the World is not part of the Jewish canon and represents a theological tradition fundamentally at odds with Jewish monotheism and creation theology. The text reinterprets Jewish scriptural concepts — including the primordial chaos (tohu wa-bohu) of Genesis 1:2, the figure of Wisdom (Hokhmah), and the Garden of Eden — within a Gnostic framework that severs these elements from their original theological context. Jewish scholarly engagement with the text focuses on tracing these appropriations and understanding their significance for Jewish-Gnostic relations.

## Jewish Traditions Redeployed

The text's account of primordial chaos bears a superficial resemblance to Jewish traditions about the state of the world before creation, but the resemblance masks a fundamental difference. In Jewish theology, chaos is the raw material that God shapes into an ordered cosmos; in On the Origin of the World, chaos is the independent ground from which the flawed material world emerges without the involvement of the true God. Similarly, the text's Pistis Sophia figure draws on Jewish Wisdom traditions but deploys them to explain the origin of evil rather than to celebrate God's creative wisdom.

## Scholarly Significance

For scholars of Judaism, the text provides evidence for how Jewish cosmogonic traditions were received and transformed in the syncretic religious environment of late antique Egypt. The interplay between Jewish, Gnostic, and Greek philosophical ideas visible in the text illuminates the broader process by which Second Temple Jewish concepts were appropriated by emerging religious movements.
