The text's retelling of the creation of humanity and the Garden narrative continues the pattern of appropriating and inverting Jewish scriptural traditions. Jewish tradition, expressed in the Talmud and Midrash, presents the Garden of Eden (Gan Eden) as a place of blessing and the creation of Adam as the crowning act of divine generosity. The rabbinic tradition celebrates the human being as the pinnacle of creation, endowed with the divine image and entrusted with stewardship of the earth.

On the Origin of the World reverses these valuations. The Garden becomes a site of archontic deception, the Tree of Knowledge becomes the instrument of liberation from archontic control, and human bodies are prisons designed to trap divine light. Jewish scholars note that even the most creative and provocative midrashim never approach this kind of wholesale reversal of the Garden narrative's theological meaning. The text's treatment of Genesis represents not an alternative Jewish reading but a fundamentally non-Jewish — indeed anti-Jewish — rereading of Israel's foundational scriptures.
