Jewish eschatological hope centers on the coming of the Messiah, the restoration of Israel, the resurrection of the dead, and the establishment of God's kingdom on earth. These hopes are fundamentally affirmative of the material world — the messianic age (olam ha-ba) is envisioned as a perfected version of this world, not an escape from it. On the Origin of the World's eschatology, which envisions the annihilation of the material cosmos and the destruction of its rulers, represents a profoundly different vision.

From a Jewish perspective, the text's eschatological promise of liberation from the material world reflects the anti-cosmic stance that pervades Gnostic theology. The Jewish tradition's commitment to this-worldly engagement — through Torah observance, acts of lovingkindness (gemilut hasadim), and the pursuit of justice (tzedek) — rests on the conviction that the material world is the arena of God's purposes, not a prison to be escaped. On the Origin of the World's eschatology, in which the destruction of matter is the ultimate good, is incompatible with Judaism's affirmation of the goodness and redeemability of creation.
