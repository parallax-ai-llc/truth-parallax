Orthodox anthropology, grounded in the Fathers' reading of Genesis and perfected in the doctrine of theosis, teaches that humanity is created in the image of God and called to grow into the likeness of God through participation in the divine life. The body is not a prison but the locus of spiritual transformation — sanctified through the sacraments, transfigured by grace, and destined for resurrection. On the Origin of the World's portrayal of human creation as archontic entrapment contradicts this vision at every point.

The Orthodox understanding of paradise draws on patristic sources that present the Garden as a place of genuine communion with God — a foretaste of the eschatological kingdom. The text's reinterpretation of paradise as a mechanism of control inverts this understanding. For Orthodox theology, the incarnation definitively demonstrates the goodness and potential of material existence: God became human so that humans might become God (Athanasius). A text that treats the body and the material world as inherently hostile to the spirit cannot accommodate this central Orthodox conviction.
