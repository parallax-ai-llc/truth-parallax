Orthodox creation theology, articulated by the Cappadocian Fathers and especially Basil the Great in his Hexaemeron, affirms that God created the world through his Word (Logos) and that the initial formlessness of Genesis 1:2 represents the first stage of a purposeful creative process, not an independent chaotic substrate. On the Origin of the World's account of pre-demiurgic chaos — matter emerging from shadow independently of divine will — contradicts this teaching at its foundation.

The Orthodox liturgy celebrates creation as a theophany — a manifestation of God's wisdom, beauty, and love. The troparion "O Heavenly King" invokes the Holy Spirit as "the Treasury of blessings and Giver of life" who fills all things. This vision of a cosmos permeated by divine presence stands in direct opposition to the text's portrayal of matter as emerging from deficiency and shadow. For Orthodox theology, chaos is not the ground of creation but the absence of the Logos's ordering presence — and the entire biblical narrative is the story of the Logos bringing order, meaning, and life to all things.
