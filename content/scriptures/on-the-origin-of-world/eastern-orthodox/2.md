The Orthodox tradition has a deep relationship with the figure of Holy Wisdom (Hagia Sophia), expressed architecturally in the great cathedral of Constantinople and theologically in the identification of Christ as the Wisdom of God (1 Corinthians 1:24). The Gnostic transformation of Sophia into a tragic figure whose interaction with chaos produces the ignorant Demiurge stands in direct opposition to the Orthodox celebration of Wisdom as the joyful architect of creation (Proverbs 8:30-31).

In Orthodox theology, the Logos (Word/Wisdom) of God creates the world in a single, undivided creative act in which the Father wills, the Son executes, and the Spirit perfects. On the Origin of the World's multi-stage cosmogonic process — involving Pistis Sophia, primordial chaos, and the accidental birth of the Demiurge — fragments this unified creative act into a sequence of events marked by error and ignorance. The Orthodox understanding of creation as Trinitarian cooperation stands as the most direct theological counter to the text's narrative of cosmic mishap.
