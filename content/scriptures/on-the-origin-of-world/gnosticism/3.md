Chapter 3 presents a distinctive account of humanity's creation and the true purpose of paradise. The Garden, in this reading, is not a gift from a benevolent God but a trap set by the archons to keep humanity in ignorance. The Tree of Knowledge, correspondingly, is the instrument of liberation — its fruit conveys the gnosis that the archons wish to withhold. The Gnostic reader finds here a more elaborate version of the reversed Eden narrative found in the Hypostasis of the Archons.

The text's treatment of Eros — desire, love, and creative energy — as a cosmogonic force is unusual among Gnostic writings and may reflect the influence of Greek philosophical and mythological traditions, particularly Hesiod's Theogony, where Eros is one of the primordial forces. For the Gnostic reader, this blending of traditions enriches the cosmological framework without compromising the fundamental anti-cosmic message.

The creation of humanity is presented as a complex event with multiple causes and purposes. The archons create human bodies to serve as containers for divine light, but the introduction of the spiritual element exceeds their intentions and creates beings capable of recognizing their true origin. The Garden narrative thus contains within it the seeds of the archons' undoing — by creating humanity, they have created the very beings who will eventually comprehend and overthrow their rule.
