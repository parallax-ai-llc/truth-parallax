For the Gnostic reader, chapter 1 addresses the most fundamental metaphysical question: what existed before the Demiurge? The text's answer — primordial chaos, shadow, and matter emerging from the interaction of light and darkness — provides a cosmogonic framework deeper than that of most other Gnostic texts, which typically begin with the Pleroma and Sophia's fall. On the Origin of the World goes further back, explaining how the raw material of the cosmos came into being.

This pre-demiurgic cosmogony reinforces the Gnostic conviction that matter itself is a degraded, secondary form of existence. Chaos is not a neutral substrate that a good God shapes into an ordered world (as in some readings of Genesis 1:2) but the primordial expression of deficiency — the shadow cast by the absence of light. The material world is thus flawed from its very foundation, not merely because of the Demiurge's ignorance but because the substance from which it is built is inherently defective.

The chapter's systematic, almost philosophical approach to cosmogony distinguishes it from the more mythological Gnostic texts. The Gnostic reader finds here a reasoned argument for the anti-cosmic position, one that engages with Greek philosophical concepts of chaos and matter while deploying them in the service of the Gnostic worldview.
