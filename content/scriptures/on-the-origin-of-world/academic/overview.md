## Overview

On the Origin of the World (NHC II,5; also XIII,2) is recognized by scholars as one of the most eclectic and synthetic texts in the Nag Hammadi library. Unlike many Gnostic works that can be assigned to specific schools (Sethian, Valentinian), this text draws on a remarkably wide range of sources — Gnostic mythological traditions, Greek philosophy, Manichaean motifs, Jewish scriptural interpretation, and possibly Egyptian religious ideas. Scholars including Hans-Gebhard Bethge and Bentley Layton have produced critical editions and analyses of the text.

## Genre and Composition

The text is unusual among Nag Hammadi writings in adopting an expository, almost philosophical tone rather than the dialogical or apocalyptic forms common to Gnostic literature. This has led scholars to classify it as a cosmogonic treatise or essay. Source-critical analysis has identified multiple traditions woven together by the author-compiler, and the text's relationship to other Nag Hammadi tractates — particularly the Hypostasis of the Archons, with which it shares considerable material — remains a subject of active scholarly debate.

## Contribution to the Field

On the Origin of the World is particularly valuable for studies of religious syncretism in late antiquity. Its confident blending of diverse traditions suggests an intellectual milieu in which the boundaries between religious systems were fluid and permeable. The text has contributed to scholarly discussions about the nature of "Gnosticism" as a category, the mechanisms of religious borrowing and transformation, and the social settings in which syncretic cosmological speculation flourished.
