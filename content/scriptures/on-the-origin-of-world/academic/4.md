The eschatological conclusion of On the Origin of the World has drawn scholarly attention for its possible relationship to Stoic ekpyrosis (cosmic conflagration) and Manichaean eschatological traditions. The vision of a final fire that destroys the material world and liberates trapped light particles bears structural similarities to the Manichaean eschatological scenario, leading some scholars to explore the possibility of Manichaean influence on or connection to the text. However, the dating and provenance of the text remain sufficiently uncertain that no scholarly consensus has been reached.

The text's eschatology also raises important questions about the social function of Gnostic apocalypticism. Scholars including Karen King and Michael Williams have debated whether Gnostic eschatological texts functioned primarily as expressions of genuine cosmic expectation, as symbolic narratives of interior transformation, or as community-building rhetoric that reinforced group identity through the promise of vindication. On the Origin of the World's systematic and comprehensive eschatological scenario — covering the fate of the archons, the material world, and the elect — provides rich material for these scholarly debates.
