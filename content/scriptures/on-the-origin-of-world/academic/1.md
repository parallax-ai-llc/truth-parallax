The opening section of On the Origin of the World has attracted scholarly attention for its engagement with Greek cosmogonic thought. The text's treatment of primordial chaos as the substrate from which matter emerges invites comparison with Hesiod's Theogony, pre-Socratic cosmological speculation (particularly Anaximander's apeiron), and Platonic accounts of the receptacle (chora) in the Timaeus. Hans-Gebhard Bethge and other scholars have argued that the author possessed genuine familiarity with Greek philosophical traditions and deliberately wove them into the Gnostic cosmological framework.

The literary character of chapter 1 is also noteworthy. Unlike the mythological narratives common in Gnostic literature, the opening section adopts an argumentative, almost discursive tone — presenting reasons and evidence for the Gnostic cosmogonic account. This essay-like quality has led scholars to classify the text differently from revelation dialogues or apocalypses. The genre question has implications for understanding the text's intended audience and social setting: the philosophical tone suggests an educated readership comfortable with rational argumentation as well as mythological narrative.
