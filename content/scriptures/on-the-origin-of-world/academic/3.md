Chapter 3's account of human creation and the Garden represents one of the most syncretic passages in the entire Nag Hammadi library. The text weaves together elements from Genesis, Greek mythology (particularly the figure of Eros as a cosmogonic force), Gnostic archon mythology, and possibly Egyptian religious traditions into a complex narrative that defies simple classification. Scholars have debated whether this syncretism reflects the work of a single, highly educated author or the accumulated layering of traditions over time.

The introduction of Eros into the cosmogonic narrative has attracted particular scholarly attention. The figure of Eros as a primordial creative force derives from Hesiod's Theogony and was subsequently developed in Platonic and Orphic traditions. Its appearance in a text otherwise dominated by Jewish and Christian source material illustrates the remarkable fluidity of religious ideas in late antique Egypt. Gerard Luttikhuizen and others have analyzed this passage as evidence for the cultural environment in which the text was composed — one in which Jewish, Christian, Greek, and Egyptian traditions were readily available and freely combined.
