## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri places it within the global context of creation mythology. The narrative's core elements — the emergence of order from primordial chaos, the separation of heaven and earth, the contest between twin brothers for cosmic sovereignty, and the imperfect ordering of the world — find parallels in creation myths from cultures worldwide. Yet the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri's distinctive features, rooted in Korean shamanic practice, offer unique perspectives on these universal themes.

## Twin Myths

The contest between Daebyeol-wang (Great Star King) and Sobyeol-wang (Small Star King) resonates with twin myths found across the globe — from the Popol Vuh's Hero Twins to the Greek Castor and Pollux, from the Zoroastrian Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu to the Japanese Amaterasu and Susanoo. The Korean version is distinctive in its emphasis on the trickery by which the inferior twin wins rulership of the human world, providing an etiological explanation for the presence of evil and injustice.

## Cosmogonic Patterns

Comparative scholars identify in the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri the classic cosmogonic pattern described by Charles Long: the movement from chaos to cosmos through acts of separation, differentiation, and ordering. The Korean myth's emphasis on the imperfection of this ordering — the world is ruled by the less worthy twin — represents a sophisticated theological reflection on the problem of evil that parallels similar reflections in traditions worldwide.
