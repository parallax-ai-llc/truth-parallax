Comparative interpretation of Section 3, 'Cheonjiwang's Descent,' places this portion of the Korean creation myth alongside parallel narratives from cultures worldwide. The cosmogonic themes addressed in this section — the processes by which an ordered cosmos emerges and takes shape — appear in creation myths across human cultures, from the ancient Near Eastern Enuma Elish to the Polynesian narratives of Rangi and Papa to the Norse account of Ginnungagap. Each tradition articulates these universal themes through culturally distinctive imagery and narrative structures.

The Korean narrative's distinctive treatment of these cosmogonic themes reflects its shamanic performance context: unlike literary creation myths that exist as fixed texts, the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri lives in oral performance, adapting to each recitation while maintaining its essential narrative architecture. This performance dimension connects the Korean creation myth to other oral cosmogonic traditions worldwide.
