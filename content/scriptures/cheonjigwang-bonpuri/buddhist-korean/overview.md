## Overview

Buddhist-Korean interpretation of the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri examines the creation myth through the lens of Buddhist cosmology and soteriology. While Buddhism does not traditionally emphasize creation myths — the Buddha famously declined to speculate on the origin of the universe — Korean Buddhism has engaged extensively with indigenous cosmogonic narratives, finding in them expressions of Buddhist truths articulated through Korean cultural forms.

## Cosmological Parallels

Buddhist readers note structural parallels between the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri's cosmogony and Buddhist cosmological schemes: the emergence of the world from an undifferentiated state recalls the Buddhist concept of world-cycles (kalpa), the separation of heaven and earth mirrors the Buddhist enumeration of multiple realms of existence, and the imperfect ordering of the human world resonates with the Buddhist teaching that samsara is inherently characterized by suffering (dukkha).

## Karmic Interpretation

The contest between the twin brothers, in which the less worthy wins through deception, can be read through a Buddhist lens as illustrating the workings of karma: the present state of the world reflects the accumulated actions of past beings, and the prevalence of injustice is not a cosmic accident but the fruit of collective karmic patterns.
