## Overview

Academic study of the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri examines it as Korea's most significant indigenous creation myth, applying the methodologies of comparative mythology, folkloristics, oral literature studies, and religious studies. Scholars analyze its narrative structure, its regional variants (particularly between Jeju Island and mainland versions), its relationship to broader East Asian cosmogonic traditions, and its function within the living context of shamanic ritual performance.

## Textual History

The academic tradition has documented multiple versions of the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri collected from different mudang across Korea's regions. The Jeju Island versions, recorded by scholars such as Hyeon Yong-jun and Mun Mu-byeong, are generally considered the most complete and archaic. Mainland versions tend to be shorter and show greater Buddhist and Confucian influence. This textual plurality reflects the oral tradition's inherent variability.

## Comparative Context

Scholars situate the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri within several comparative frameworks: the pan-Asian motif of cosmic egg or primordial chaos, the widespread twin/sibling rivalry theme found in creation myths worldwide, and the distinctively Korean elaboration of these universal motifs through the lens of shamanic practice and performance.
