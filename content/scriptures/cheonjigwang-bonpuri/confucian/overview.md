## Overview

Confucian interpretation of the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri focuses on the myth's implicit theories of governance, legitimacy, and moral order. The contest between the twin brothers for rulership of the human world raises questions central to Confucian political philosophy: What constitutes legitimate authority? How should rulers be selected? What is the relationship between cosmic order and human governance? While Confucian scholars historically regarded shamanic narratives with suspicion, the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri's engagement with these questions reveals unexpected common ground.

## The Problem of Governance

The myth's most provocative element from a Confucian perspective is its conclusion: the less worthy twin (Sobyeol-wang) wins rulership of the human world through trickery, while the more worthy twin (Daebyeol-wang) is relegated to the realm of the dead. This narrative directly challenges the Confucian ideal of virtuous governance and the Mandate of Heaven, suggesting that the human world is inherently subject to imperfect rule.

## Moral Cosmology

Despite these tensions, Confucian readers find in the Cheonjigwang Bonpuri a moral cosmology that shares fundamental assumptions with Confucian thought: the universe possesses an inherent moral order, human institutions should reflect cosmic patterns, and the relationship between heaven and earth provides a model for human governance.
