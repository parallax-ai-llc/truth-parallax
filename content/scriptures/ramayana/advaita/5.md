The Advaita reading of Sundara Kanda treats Hanuman's ocean crossing as a metaphor for transcending the ocean of *samsara*. The obstacles Hanuman encounters — the demoness Surasa, the shadow-grasping Simhika, the mountain Mainaka offering rest — represent the temptations and challenges the seeker faces on the path to self-knowledge. Hanuman's refusal to rest on Mainaka parallels the Advaita insistence that the seeker must not settle for partial attainments but press on to complete realization.

Hanuman's search for Sita in Lanka — systematically examining every palace and garden — symbolizes the discriminative inquiry (*viveka*) through which the intellect searches for the true Self among the countless appearances of the phenomenal world. When he finally discovers Sita in the Ashoka grove, it represents the moment of initial recognition — the first glimpse of the Self's true nature, still apparently bound but inherently free. The message Hanuman carries from Rama is the *mahavakya* itself: the assurance that liberation is coming, that the Self was never truly imprisoned.
