The Advaita tradition handles Uttara Kanda's troubling narrative by emphasizing its pointer toward the ultimate nature of reality beyond all appearances. Sita's return to the earth is not a tragedy but a revelation: the earth (matter, *prakriti*) receives back what was always its own, while the true Sita — the Self — was never born and never dies. The episode illustrates the Advaita teaching that all phenomenal identities, however beloved, are ultimately resolved back into their source.

Rama's own departure via the Sarayu River symbolizes the dissolution of the avatar's apparent form back into the formless Brahman from which it emerged. For Advaita, this is not a loss but a completion: the narrative itself, like the world it describes, is a temporary manifestation that serves its purpose (teaching, purification, inspiration) and then resolves into the silence from which it arose. The Advaita message of the entire Ramayana is that the Self — present as Rama throughout — is never actually born, never actually exiled, never actually separated from anything, and never actually returns, because it is eternally and changelessly the one Brahman.
