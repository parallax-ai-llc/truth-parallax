Advaita interpreters read Kishkindha Kanda as the stage of spiritual seeking (*mumukshutva*) where the aspirant gathers the inner resources needed for liberation. The *vanaras* represent the mental faculties — restless, powerful, capable of great leaps but needing direction. Rama's alliance with Sugriva symbolizes the seeker's engagement with the disciplined mind, which must first be purified of its internal conflicts (the Vali-Sugriva rivalry) before it can serve the quest for self-knowledge.

The killing of Vali receives a characteristically Advaita interpretation: Vali represents the deeply entrenched pattern of ego-identification that cannot be overcome by direct confrontation (since Vali absorbs half the strength of any face-to-face opponent). Only an unexpected insight — the arrow from hiding — can shatter this identification. The Advaita tradition sees here a reflection of how self-knowledge operates: it does not engage the ego on its own terms but dissolves it through a sudden recognition (*pratyabhijna*) that comes from a direction the ego cannot anticipate or defend against.
