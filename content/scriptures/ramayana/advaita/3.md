The Advaita allegory reaches its crisis point in Aranya Kanda with Sita's abduction. In the non-dual reading, Sita represents the individual mind (*jiva*) or *buddhi* (intellect), and her capture by Ravana (ego/ignorance) symbolizes the fundamental error of *adhyasa* — superimposition — whereby consciousness appears to be bound by the limitations of individual experience. Rama's grief over Sita's loss represents the apparent effect of *maya* on Brahman, though the Advaita tradition insists this grief is appearance only.

The golden deer (*maricha*) is a powerful Advaita symbol: it represents the alluring world of names and forms that draws attention away from the Self. Rama's pursuit of the illusory deer mirrors the individual's pursuit of sense objects, which creates the conditions for the apparent bondage of the mind. The Adhyatma Ramayana's version makes explicit what the Valmiki narrative implies — that Sita leaves behind a shadow-image of herself while her true form remains with Rama, just as the true Self is never actually touched by ignorance, however convincing the appearance of bondage may be.
