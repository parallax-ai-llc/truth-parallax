The Advaita reading of Ayodhya Kanda treats Rama's exile as an allegory of the Self's apparent departure from its natural state of royal sovereignty (self-knowledge) into the forest of worldly experience. Rama's equanimity in accepting exile demonstrates the nature of the *jivanmukta* — one who remains undisturbed by the changes of fortune because he is established in the knowledge of the Self as Brahman.

The Yoga Vasishtha's teachings, set in the period of Rama's youth, are particularly relevant here. Vasishtha teaches Rama that the world is a projection of consciousness, that bondage and liberation are both mental constructs, and that the Self is eternally free. The Advaita tradition reads Rama's calm acceptance of exile as the fruit of this teaching — he does not resist because he knows that the Self cannot be exiled from itself. Ayodhya (literally "unconquerable") represents the Self that cannot be diminished by any circumstance, and Rama's departure from the physical city points to the deeper truth that he never leaves his true nature.
