The Advaita reading of Bala Kanda, drawing heavily on the Adhyatma Ramayana, presents Rama as always conscious of his identity as Brahman. Unlike the Valmiki original where Rama often appears genuinely human, the Advaita retelling emphasizes that Rama's apparent ignorance of his divinity is itself a divine play (*lila*) that does not affect his ever-present self-knowledge. The incarnation demonstrates that Brahman can appear in human form without ceasing to be the infinite, attributeless absolute.

The young Rama's education under the sage Vishwamitra is read allegorically as the Atman's apparent engagement with the world of knowledge and action. The breaking of Shiva's bow symbolizes the destruction of the ego's false identification with limitation — only when the bow of ahamkara (ego) is broken can the marriage of Atman (Rama) and the purified mind (Sita) take place. The Advaita tradition values these narrative symbols as entry points for contemplation on the nature of the Self.
