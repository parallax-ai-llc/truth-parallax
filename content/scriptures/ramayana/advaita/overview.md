## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the Ramayana as a profound allegory for the soul's journey from ignorance to self-knowledge. The Adhyatma Ramayana, which heavily influenced the Advaita reception of the epic, presents Rama as always conscious of his identity as Brahman, and the apparent sufferings of the narrative — exile, loss of Sita, the war — as the play of *maya* that does not affect the ever-free Self. The epic becomes a map of the spiritual journey from *avidya* to *jnana*.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Allegorical Reading
The Advaita tradition interprets the Ramayana's characters and events symbolically. Rama represents the Atman; Sita represents the individual mind; her abduction by Ravana (ego) is the soul's entrapment in ignorance; the war is the inner battle against the *kleshas*; and the reunion of Rama and Sita is the realization of non-dual truth.

### Yoga Vasishtha
The Yoga Vasishtha, a text highly esteemed in Advaita, presents the teachings of the sage Vasishtha to the young Rama, offering a comprehensive exposition of non-dual philosophy within the Ramayana framework.

## Theological Emphases

- **Rama as Brahman**: The hero's external adventures symbolize Brahman's apparent engagement with the phenomenal world
- **Maya**: The sufferings and conflicts of the epic illustrate the nature of cosmic illusion
- **Self-Knowledge**: True liberation comes not from defeating external enemies but from recognizing the Self
- **Jivanmukti**: Rama models the *jivanmukta* — one who is liberated while still embodied
