Yuddha Kanda's great battle is read by the Advaita tradition as the final confrontation between knowledge (*vidya*) and ignorance (*avidya*). Ravana, the ten-headed demon king, represents the ego in its fullest elaboration — powerful, learned (he is a great scholar and devotee), but fundamentally confused by the identification of the Self with the body-mind complex. His ten heads symbolize the ten sense organs and their ceaseless outward activity.

The war's progression mirrors the stages of Vedantic inquiry. The building of the bridge represents the construction of the path from the known to the unknown. The battles with Ravana's generals represent the overcoming of subsidiary ignorances. Indrajit (Ravana's son), who fights invisibly, represents the most subtle form of ignorance — the unconscious assumptions that operate below awareness. The final battle between Rama and Ravana is the decisive confrontation between Atman and ahamkara, ending only when the arrow of knowledge pierces the ego's vital center. Sita's reunion with Rama represents the restoration of the mind to its natural state of transparency to the Self.
