Ayodhya Kanda's exile narrative resonates with one of the world's most widespread mythological patterns: the rightful ruler driven from his kingdom by treachery. Moses leading the Israelites from Egypt, the young David fleeing Saul, Odysseus's long absence from Ithaca, and the exile of the Pandavas in the Mahabharata all share the structure of separation, trial, and eventual return. Each culture uses this pattern to explore themes of legitimacy, endurance, and the testing of virtue.

Rama's acceptance of exile out of filial obedience invites comparison with the Confucian concept of *xiao* (filial piety) as the foundation of all virtue. Both traditions elevate obedience to parents to a cosmic principle — in the Ramayana, Rama's obedience preserves his father's truthfulness and thus sustains dharma itself. The contrast with Western traditions is instructive: Antigone defies her uncle-king to honor a higher law, while Rama obeys his father precisely because obedience *is* the higher law. These different resolutions reveal fundamentally different assumptions about the relationship between individual conscience and social obligation.
