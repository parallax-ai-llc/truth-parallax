Yuddha Kanda's great battle invites comparison with war epics across world literature: the Iliad's siege of Troy, the Shahnameh's battles between Iran and Turan, the battle of Kurukshetra in the Mahabharata, and the Book of Joshua's conquest narratives. Each tradition uses the great battle to dramatize the cosmic significance of military conflict — war as the arena where divine justice, human courage, and the fate of civilizations converge.

The figure of Vibhishana — the righteous brother who defects from the enemy's side — has parallels in the biblical Rahab (who shelters Israelite spies), in Hector's moral conflict in the Iliad, and in Arjuna's reluctance in the Bhagavad Gita. Each represents the possibility of moral choice transcending ethnic or familial loyalty. The post-war fire ordeal of Sita raises questions about female purity that resonate with the trial of suspected adultery in Numbers 5 (the *sotah* ritual) and the medieval European "trial by ordeal," revealing cross-cultural anxiety about female sexuality and the patriarchal need to publicly verify female virtue.
