The abduction of Sita resonates with one of the oldest narrative patterns in world mythology: the seizure of the beautiful woman that precipitates a great war. The parallel with the abduction of Helen by Paris, which launches the Trojan War, is the most frequently cited comparison. Both epics feature a wrongful seizure that mobilizes vast armies, a long siege, and the eventual recovery of the abducted woman. Scholars debate whether these parallels reflect shared Indo-European mythological roots or independent developments.

The forest as a space of transformation and danger is a cross-cultural constant. The Arthurian romances' enchanted forests, the Brothers Grimm's dark woods, Dante's *selva oscura* (dark wood), and the Buddhist *aranya* (forest) tradition all assign to the wilderness the dual role of place of danger and place of spiritual awakening. Rama's forest life, combining the roles of warrior, protector, and ascetic, parallels the knight-errant of medieval romance who proves his virtue through encounters with monsters and marvels in wild spaces.
