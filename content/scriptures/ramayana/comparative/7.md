Uttara Kanda's troubled conclusion — the banishment of the faithful wife, the splitting of the kingdom between the hero's sons, and the hero's final departure — resonates with a cross-cultural pattern in which the hero's later years are marked by sorrow and loss. King Arthur's betrayal by Lancelot and Guinevere, David's troubled succession, and Odysseus's restless post-return life (as told in later traditions) all follow this pattern of the hero who achieves the great deed but cannot sustain the ideal in ordinary life.

Sita's return to the earth — her mother, the goddess Earth, receiving her back — is one of the most striking images in world mythology. It parallels Persephone's annual return to the underworld, the various Earth Mother traditions where the feminine divine is associated with the ground itself, and the Chinese myth of Nu Wa. The Ramayana's ending, with its refusal of simple closure and its acknowledgment that even divine rule cannot eliminate suffering and injustice, gives the epic a tragic dimension that allies it more closely with Greek tragedy than with the romantic resolution of fairy tale. This open ending has made the Ramayana endlessly adaptable across cultures and centuries.
