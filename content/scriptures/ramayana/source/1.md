---
title: "Ramayana Bala Kanda (Book 1)"
scripture: "ramayana"
chapter: 1
---

**1** There was a great city called Ayodhya, in the kingdom of Kosala, on the banks of the river Sarayu. It was prosperous and well-governed, founded by Manu himself, the progenitor of mankind.

**2** In that city ruled King Dasharatha of the Ikshvaku dynasty, mighty as Indra, truthful as Dharma, a protector of his people, and a performer of great sacrifices. He had all the virtues of a noble king, yet he had no son to continue his line.

**3** On the advice of his ministers, Dasharatha performed the Putrakameshti sacrifice, the fire-ritual for obtaining sons. From the sacrificial fire arose a divine being bearing a golden vessel filled with celestial food.

**4** The divine being said: "O king, the gods are pleased with your sacrifice. Give this sacred food to your queens, and you shall obtain the sons you desire." Dasharatha distributed the food among his three queens: Kausalya, Kaikeyi, and Sumitra.

**5** In due time, Kausalya bore Rama, Kaikeyi bore Bharata, and Sumitra bore the twins Lakshmana and Shatrughna. Of these, Rama was the eldest and most virtuous. He was an incarnation of Lord Vishnu, born on earth to destroy the demon king Ravana.

**6** Rama grew to manhood, excelling in all arts and sciences. He was devoted to his parents, kind to all beings, skilled in warfare, and learned in the Vedas. The people of Ayodhya loved him as the moon loves the night.

**7** The sage Vishvamitra came to King Dasharatha and asked that Rama and Lakshmana accompany him to protect his sacrifice from the demons Maricha and Subahu. Though Dasharatha was reluctant, he granted the sage's request.

**8** Rama slew the demoness Tataka, who terrorized the forests. Vishvamitra, pleased, bestowed upon Rama many celestial weapons and mantras. Then Rama and Lakshmana protected the sacrifice, driving away the demons with divine arrows.

**9** Vishvamitra led the princes to the kingdom of Mithila, where King Janaka had proclaimed that whoever could string the great bow of Lord Shiva would win the hand of his daughter Sita, who had been born from a furrow in the earth.

**10** Many mighty kings had tried and failed to even lift the bow. Rama, at Vishvamitra's bidding, lifted it with one hand, strung it, and drew it until it broke with a sound like thunder that shook the heavens. Thus did Rama win Sita, the daughter of the earth, who was an incarnation of the goddess Lakshmi.

**11** The wedding of Rama and Sita was celebrated with great splendor. Bharata married Mandavi, Lakshmana married Urmila, and Shatrughna married Shrutakirti — all daughters of Janaka's family. The four princes returned to Ayodhya with their brides.

**12** Rama and Sita lived in perfect happiness in Ayodhya. Their love was the joy of the city, and the people said: "As the moon cannot be separated from its light, so Rama and Sita cannot be parted from each other."
