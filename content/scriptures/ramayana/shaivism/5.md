The Shaiva reading of Sundara Kanda emphasizes the extraordinary expansion of consciousness that Hanuman's ocean leap represents. In assuming a form vast enough to cross the sea, Hanuman demonstrates the Shaiva principle that individual consciousness, when freed from self-imposed limitations (*anava mala*), can expand to cosmic proportions. Hanuman's feat is not merely physical but a manifestation of liberated awareness acting without constraint.

Hanuman's discovery of Sita in the Ashoka grove carries deep Shaiva significance. The grove's name — *ashoka*, "without sorrow" — ironically contrasts with Sita's grief, but the Shaiva reading sees a deeper truth: Shakti (Sita) is inherently *ashoka* (beyond sorrow), even when apparently trapped by ego (Ravana). Hanuman's role as the messenger who reconnects consciousness (Rama) with its Shakti (Sita) parallels the guru's role in the Shaiva tradition — the one who facilitates the recognition (*pratyabhijna*) that consciousness and its power were never truly separated.
