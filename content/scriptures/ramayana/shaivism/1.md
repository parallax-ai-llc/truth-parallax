Shaiva interpreters find rich significance in Bala Kanda's episodes involving Shiva. Rama's breaking of Shiva's bow (*Pinaka*) at Sita's svayamvara is the chapter's theological center for the Shaiva reading. Rather than seeing this as Vishnu's triumph over Shiva, Shaiva commentators read it as Shiva's gracious enabling of the divine plan — the bow breaks because Shiva wills it, facilitating the union of Rama and Sita that the cosmic drama requires.

The encounter with Parashurama after the bow-breaking is equally significant. Parashurama, carrying Vishnu's bow, confronts Rama, but the tension resolves peacefully. Shaiva interpreters read this as demonstrating the harmony between Shaiva and Vaishnava principles: Shiva's bow is broken (material limitation is transcended), and Vishnu's bow is received (divine purpose is accepted). The young Rama's education under the sages reflects the Shaiva value of knowledge transmission through the guru-disciple lineage, and his protection of Vishwamitra's sacrifice demonstrates the warrior's sacred duty to preserve the conditions for spiritual practice.
