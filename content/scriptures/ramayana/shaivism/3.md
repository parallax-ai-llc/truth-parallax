Aranya Kanda's forest setting resonates deeply with Shaiva themes. Rama's life among the ascetic communities of the Dandaka forest mirrors the tapas-centered existence of Shaiva yogis. His protection of the forest-dwelling sages from demonic forces reflects Shiva's role as the supreme protector of those engaged in spiritual practice. The encounters with various demons represent the inner obstacles (*vighneshvaras*) that the practitioner must overcome.

The Shaiva reading of Sita's abduction introduces a distinctly philosophical dimension. Sita, understood as Shakti — the dynamic creative principle — is separated from Rama (pure consciousness) by Ravana (the ego driven by desire). This separation represents the fundamental alienation that Shaiva philosophy seeks to overcome: the apparent split between consciousness and its creative power. The golden deer (*maricha*) that lures Rama away symbolizes the power of *maya* to create alluring illusions that distract consciousness from its inseparable unity with Shakti.
