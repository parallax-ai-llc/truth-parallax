The Shaiva reading of Uttara Kanda finds meaning in the narrative's difficult conclusion by reading it through the lens of *lila* — divine play that transcends human moral categories. Sita's banishment and eventual return to the earth are understood as the withdrawal of Shakti from manifest form back into the ground of being. This is not a tragedy but a cosmic process: Shakti periodically withdraws and re-manifests, and the apparent suffering is part of the divine drama.

Rama's own departure — walking into the Sarayu River — mirrors Shiva's association with water and dissolution. In the Shaiva reading, this represents the return of individuated consciousness to the ocean of universal consciousness. The narrative cycle of manifestation, play, and withdrawal reflects the Shaiva cosmological process of *srishti* (creation), *sthiti* (sustenance), *samhara* (dissolution), *tirodhana* (concealment), and *anugraha* (grace). The Ramayana, in this reading, is itself a form of *anugraha* — the grace through which Shiva reveals the nature of consciousness to those who engage with the story.
