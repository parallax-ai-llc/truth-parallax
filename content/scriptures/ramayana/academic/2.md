Scholars consider Ayodhya Kanda part of the core Ramayana and one of its most literarily accomplished sections. The court intrigue through which Kaikeyi secures Rama's exile reflects a pattern familiar in ancient literatures: the succession crisis that drives the hero into exile. Scholars compare this with Absalom's rebellion against David, Telemachus's crisis with the suitors, and Arjuna's exile in the Mahabharata.

The chapter provides rich material for the study of ancient Indian political and social ideals. The concept of *raja dharma* (royal duty) as articulated through Rama's dilemma — personal rights versus paternal obligation versus public expectation — has been analyzed as reflecting historical debates about kingship and law. Sheldon Pollock's influential reading argues that the Ramayana constructs a "theology of politics" in which divine kingship legitimates political authority. The extensive depictions of Ayodhya's grief at Rama's departure reveal conventions of emotional expression (*rasa*) that became normative for later Sanskrit literature.
