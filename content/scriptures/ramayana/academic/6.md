Yuddha Kanda is the longest book of the Ramayana and has attracted scholarly attention for its elaborate battle descriptions, which follow conventions later codified in Sanskrit poetics. The bridge to Lanka (*setu*) has generated debate about whether it preserves a memory of actual maritime connections between India and Sri Lanka, a question complicated by the geological formation known as Adam's Bridge (Rama Setu) in the Palk Strait.

Scholars analyze the war narrative for its political theology. Vibhishana's defection to Rama has been read as reflecting ancient Indian norms about the legitimacy of rebellion against an unjust ruler. The fire ordeal (*agni pariksha*) of Sita after the war — in which she proves her chastity by entering fire and emerging unscathed — has been extensively debated by feminist scholars, who see it as reflecting patriarchal anxieties about female sexuality and purity. Some scholars argue that the fire ordeal was a later addition designed to address audience discomfort with Sita's prolonged captivity in Ravana's palace.
