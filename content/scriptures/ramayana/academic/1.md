Scholars have long identified Bala Kanda as a later addition to the original Ramayana, distinct in style and content from the core narrative (books 2-6). The book's mythological framework — the gods' petition, the divine incarnation, the elaborate genealogies — serves to elevate Rama from a human hero of the Ikshvaku dynasty to an incarnation of Vishnu, reflecting the development of avatar theology in the centuries surrounding the common era.

The narrative of Vishwamitra and the young Rama's adventures contains material that appears to be drawn from independent mythological cycles subsequently woven into the Ramayana framework. Scholars note that the conflict between Vishwamitra and Vasishtha reflects historical tensions between *kshatriya* and *brahmana* authority. The svayamvara episode, where Rama wins Sita by breaking Shiva's bow, follows a widespread Indo-European motif of the hero proving himself through a test of strength, with parallels in the Odyssey's bow-stringing scene.
