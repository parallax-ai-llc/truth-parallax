## Overview

Academic scholarship approaches the Ramayana as a literary and cultural monument whose composition, transmission, and reception illuminate the development of South and Southeast Asian civilizations. Scholars generally regard the core narrative (books 2-6) as the earliest layer, with books 1 (Bala Kanda) and 7 (Uttara Kanda) as later additions that frame the heroic narrative with theological and mythological material. Dating proposals range from the 7th century BCE to the 3rd century CE for the various layers.

## Key Scholarly Approaches

### Textual Criticism
Robert Goldman's critical edition project at Princeton (the Baroda Critical Edition is a parallel effort) has established a reliable text by comparing manuscripts from different regional traditions (Northern, Southern, Northwestern), revealing significant variation in the transmitted text.

### Cultural Diffusion
The Ramayana's spread across Southeast Asia — Thailand's Ramakien, Indonesia's Kakawin Ramayana, Cambodia's Reamker — provides a major case study in cultural transmission and adaptation. Scholars study how the narrative was transformed to reflect local religious and political contexts.

## Research Themes

- **Composition Layers**: Internal evidence suggests the text grew over centuries through accretion
- **Gender and Power**: Sita's trials and Uttara Kanda's treatment of her have been central to feminist scholarship
- **Political Uses**: The Ramayana's deployment in modern Indian politics raises questions about the relationship between scripture and nationalism
- **Oral and Written**: The interplay between oral performance traditions and written text
