Academic analysis of Aranya Kanda focuses on its representation of the forest as a contested space in ancient Indian culture. The Dandaka forest, inhabited by sages and threatened by demons, represents the frontier between Brahmanical civilization and the forces that challenge it. Scholars (particularly Sheldon Pollock) have read Rama's demon-slaying in the forest as reflecting the historical expansion of Brahmanical culture into forested and tribal regions.

The abduction of Sita follows a narrative pattern — the seizure of the hero's wife — found across Indo-European mythology (Helen of Troy, the Sabine women). Scholars debate whether this reflects a shared mythological archetype or independent developments. The golden deer episode has attracted attention as a sophisticated literary device that compresses multiple themes: *maya* (illusion), desire (*kama*) as the root of catastrophe, and the transgression of protective boundaries. The character of Jatayu, the aged vulture who fights Ravana, may preserve an older stratum of the narrative connected to totemic or animal clan traditions.
