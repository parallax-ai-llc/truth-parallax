Scholarly consensus holds that Uttara Kanda is a later addition to the Ramayana, composed to address questions left unresolved by the core narrative and to integrate the epic more fully into the Puranic mythological framework. The book's distinct style, its inconsistencies with the earlier narrative, and its elaborate mythological digressions (including extensive backstories for Ravana and Hanuman) all support the conclusion of separate authorship.

Sita's banishment and the earth's swallowing of her have been the most intensely studied episodes in modern Ramayana scholarship. A.K. Ramanujan's influential essay "Three Hundred Ramayanas" demonstrated how different traditions handle this episode in radically different ways — from Jain versions where Sita is Ravana's daughter to Southeast Asian versions where the episode is omitted entirely. Feminist scholars (e.g., Madhu Kishwar, Nabaneeta Dev Sen) have analyzed Sita's treatment as reflecting and reinforcing patriarchal norms, while others argue that Sita's final act of returning to the earth constitutes a powerful assertion of female autonomy and agency.
