Scholars regard Sundara Kanda as one of the Ramayana's literary high points, notable for its narrative sophistication and emotional depth. Hanuman's ocean crossing is a set piece of Sanskrit epic poetry, deploying elaborate similes and descriptions that became models for later *mahakavya* (court epic) literature. The chapter's name — "Beautiful" — is itself debated: it may refer to Hanuman (*sundara* as an epithet), to Lanka, or to the literary quality of the kanda itself.

The depiction of Lanka under Ravana's rule provides valuable evidence for how ancient Indian authors imagined an ideal (if demonic) city. Scholars have compared the description with archaeological and textual evidence for ancient urban planning in South and Southeast Asia. Hanuman's encounter with Sita in the Ashoka grove is one of the most psychologically nuanced scenes in the epic, with Sita's oscillation between hope, despair, and suspicion rendered with remarkable dramatic skill. The burning of Lanka raises questions about narrative consistency that scholars have used as evidence for compositional layering.
