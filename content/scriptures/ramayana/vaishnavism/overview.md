## Overview

For Vaishnavism, the Ramayana is supremely the story of God's descent into the world. Rama is not merely a heroic prince but an *avatar* (incarnation) of Lord Vishnu who assumes human form to destroy the demon Ravana and re-establish dharma. Every event in the epic — from Rama's exile to Sita's abduction to the great war — is a manifestation of divine *lila* (play), orchestrated by the Lord for the protection of the righteous.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Tulsidas and the Ramcharitmanas
Tulsidas's Hindi retelling (16th century) is the most beloved Vaishnava Ramayana, presenting Rama as the supreme object of devotion. For Tulsidas, hearing and reciting the Ramayana is itself an act of worship that purifies the heart and leads to liberation.

### Sri Vaishnava Reading
The Sri Vaishnava tradition, following Ramanuja, reads Rama's story through the theological categories of *sharira-shariri* (body-soul relationship) and *prapatti* (surrender). Sita's unwavering devotion to Rama models the ideal relationship between soul and God.

## Theological Emphases

- **Rama as Vishnu**: Every action of Rama reveals a divine attribute — His exile shows renunciation, His war shows protection of the righteous
- **Bhakti through Narrative**: The Ramayana is a vehicle of devotion; hearing it with faith is itself salvific
- **Hanuman as Ideal Devotee**: Hanuman's selfless service (*dasyam*) is the model for all Vaishnava devotional life
- **Dharma Exemplified**: Rama's adherence to truth and duty, even at personal cost, defines righteous conduct
