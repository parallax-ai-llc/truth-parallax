Ayodhya Kanda presents the supreme test of dharma, and Vaishnavas read Rama's acceptance of exile as the definitive illustration of God's commitment to righteousness over personal comfort. When Kaikeyi demands Rama's banishment and Bharata's coronation, Rama does not resist, argue, or use his divine power to override the situation. He submits to his father's word because truthfulness (*satya*) is the foundation of all dharma.

Vaishnavas draw profound theological lessons from this chapter. Rama's willing exile demonstrates that God Himself is subject to dharma — not out of compulsion but as a model for all beings. Sita's insistence on accompanying Rama reveals the inseparability of Lakshmi and Vishnu; divine Shakti never abandons the Lord. Lakshmana's devoted service foreshadows the selfless devotion (*dasyam*) that becomes the hallmark of Vaishnava spirituality. And Bharata's grief and refusal of the throne — ruling only as Rama's regent by placing Rama's sandals on the throne — is celebrated as one of the highest expressions of *bhakti* in all of Hindu literature.
