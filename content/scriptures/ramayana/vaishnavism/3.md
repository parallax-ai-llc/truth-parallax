Aranya Kanda, with its culminating tragedy of Sita's abduction, is read by Vaishnavas as the divine catalyst for the events that will fulfill Rama's incarnation purpose — the destruction of Ravana. Rama's forest life among the sages demonstrates the harmony between the warrior (*kshatriya*) and renunciant ideals. He protects the sages from demons while living with the simplicity of an ascetic, embodying the Gita's teaching of action without attachment.

Sita's abduction by Ravana is the pivotal event that sets the stage for the great war. Vaishnavas understand this as divinely ordained: Rama's apparent vulnerability (being drawn away by the golden deer) and Sita's crossing of Lakshmana's protective line are elements of *lila* that allow the cosmic drama to unfold. The devoted vulture Jatayu, who fights Ravana to protect Sita and dies in Rama's arms receiving liberation, exemplifies the Vaishnava principle that even death in the Lord's service is the highest blessing.
