Kishkindha Kanda introduces the *vanara* (monkey) allies, and Vaishnavas read the friendship between Rama and Sugriva as a model of divine grace extended to all beings, regardless of species or station. Rama's willingness to form an alliance with the monkey king and to intervene in Sugriva's conflict with his brother Vali demonstrates that God's protection extends universally to whoever takes refuge in Him.

Hanuman's first appearance in this chapter establishes the supreme paradigm of Vaishnava devotion. Hanuman's recognition of Rama as the Lord, his complete self-offering, and his tireless service become the template for *dasya bhakti* — the devotion of a servant to the master. The Vaishnava tradition treasures Hanuman not merely as a heroic figure but as the embodiment of perfect devotion: knowledgeable yet humble, powerful yet entirely surrendered. His devotion is not diminished by his extraordinary abilities but expressed through them.
