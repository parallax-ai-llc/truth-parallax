The Dvaita tradition approaches Uttara Kanda's difficult narrative with characteristic realism. Rama's banishment of Sita, while painful, demonstrates the Dvaita principle that God's actions, even when they cause apparent suffering, are always guided by a higher justice that finite minds may not fully comprehend. Rama acts as the ideal king whose duty to the public good supersedes personal attachment. The Dvaita reading does not soften this episode but presents it as an expression of God's inscrutable (*achintya*) will.

Sita's life in Valmiki's hermitage and the birth of Lava and Kusha demonstrate that divine purpose operates even in apparent exile. The twins' recitation of the Ramayana to Rama himself creates a remarkable narrative loop that the Dvaita tradition reads as demonstrating the eternality of scripture — the Ramayana, like the Vedas, is not created but merely revealed through human agency. Rama's final departure confirms the Dvaita teaching that God's incarnation is a real but voluntary descent. The Lord returns to Vaikuntha, where He eternally resides, leaving behind the legacy of dharma and the promise of divine accessibility through devotion.
