Kishkindha Kanda holds special significance for the Dvaita tradition because of Hanuman's centrality. In Madhva's theology, Hanuman is an incarnation of Vayu (the wind god), and Madhva himself is considered a later incarnation of the same deity. Hanuman thus occupies the highest position in the hierarchy of devotees, second only to Lakshmi among those who serve Vishnu. His first meeting with Rama in this chapter establishes the paradigm of the perfectly qualified devotee recognizing and serving the Supreme.

The Dvaita reading of Vali's killing addresses the ethical controversy directly. Madhva's tradition argues that Rama, as the Supreme Lord, is not bound by conventional warrior ethics. His killing of Vali from concealment is an exercise of divine justice — Vali had committed the sin of taking his brother's wife and had sided with Ravana. God's justice operates according to the soul's karma and innate nature, not according to human social conventions. This episode demonstrates the Dvaita principle that God's ways, though always just, may transcend human understanding.
