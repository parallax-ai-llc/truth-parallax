The Dvaita reading of Bala Kanda emphasizes the literal reality of Rama's divine incarnation. When Vishnu responds to the gods' petition and chooses to be born as Dasharatha's son, this is a genuine act of the independently real Supreme Being (*svatantra*), not a metaphorical descent or an appearance (*vivarta*). The divine fire-sacrifice, the celestial food, and the miraculous birth are all real events demonstrating God's sovereign power to enter His own creation while remaining transcendent.

Madhva's tradition reads the young Rama's extraordinary feats — the killing of Tataka, the protection of Vishwamitra's sacrifice, the breaking of Shiva's bow — as genuine demonstrations of Vishnu's omnipotence operating through His incarnate form. Unlike the Advaita allegorical reading, the Dvaita interpretation insists that these are historical events with theological significance precisely because they are real. The marriage of Rama and Sita is the union of Vishnu and Lakshmi in human form, a real event that blesses the entire cosmos.
