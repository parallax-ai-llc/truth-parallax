The Dvaita tradition reveres Sundara Kanda as a supreme demonstration of what devotion empowered by divine grace can accomplish. Hanuman's leap across the ocean is not merely physical strength but the power of Vayu — the second-highest deity in the Dvaita hierarchy — acting in perfect alignment with Vishnu's will. The obstacles he faces (Surasa, Simhika, the mountain Mainaka) test and reveal his fitness for the divine mission, and his success confirms his exalted rank in the hierarchy of souls.

Hanuman's discovery of Sita and his reassurance of her is read as a model of the spiritual teacher (*acharya*) who brings God's message to suffering souls. In the Dvaita framework, the guru is not merely a human teacher but a soul of inherently elevated nature who is uniquely qualified to transmit divine knowledge. Hanuman's burning of Lanka demonstrates the righteous use of divinely granted power — not for personal glory but for the service of God and the punishment of the wicked. Every action Hanuman takes in this chapter confirms the Dvaita teaching that the highest devotion combines supreme capability with complete surrender.
