## Overview

The sixth Ansha is of great interest to Advaita Vedanta because its teachings on dissolution and liberation most directly engage with the philosophical questions that are central to Advaitic inquiry. The dissolution of the cosmos back into its source provides a powerful illustration of the Advaitic teaching that the manifold universe is ultimately resolved into the one non-dual Brahman.

For Advaitins, the description of the four types of pralaya corresponds to different levels of the Advaitic teaching. The ultimate dissolution (prakritika pralaya) points to the philosophical dissolution of all duality in the realization of Brahman. And the atyantika pralaya — the personal liberation of the individual soul — is the practical goal of Advaitic sadhana.

Sridhara Swami's commentary on this book is particularly valuable for understanding the Advaita reading. He interprets the moksha described here as the realization of the identity of atman and Brahman, despite the text's more personal Vaishnava framing.

## Key Themes

- **Dissolution as Philosophical Model**: Pralaya as illustrating the resolution of multiplicity into unity
- **Atyantika Pralaya**: Personal liberation as the dissolution of ignorance
- **Return to Source**: The cosmos returning to Brahman as the soul returns to its true nature
- **Beyond Personal and Impersonal**: The ultimate reality transcending both saguna and nirguna descriptions
- **Jivanmukti**: Liberation as achievable in this life through knowledge

## Key Verses and Passages

### Prakritika Pralaya

The description of the elemental dissolution — where earth dissolves into water, water into fire, fire into air, air into space, and space into the unmanifest — perfectly mirrors the Advaitic teaching of the resolution of effects back into their causes, ultimately reaching the causeless cause, Brahman.

### The Nature of the Liberated State

Advaitins read the Purana's description of the liberated state as pointing beyond even the personal relationship with Vishnu to the non-dual realization: 'I am Brahman.'

## Commentary Tradition

Sridhara Swami's commentary on this final book is widely regarded as one of the most philosophically sophisticated treatments in the Advaita Puranic tradition. His ability to read a Vaishnava text through an Advaitic lens while maintaining devotional warmth made his commentary authoritative across sectarian lines.
