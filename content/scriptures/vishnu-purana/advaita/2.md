## Overview

Advaita Vedanta approaches the cosmographic content of the second Ansha as part of the lower knowledge (apara vidya) — useful for worship and meditation but not describing ultimate reality. The elaborate cosmic structure, with its continents, oceans, and celestial spheres, belongs to the realm of vyavaharika (conventional reality), which is transcended in the realization of non-dual Brahman.

For Advaitins, the detailed geography serves a pedagogical purpose: by demonstrating the vastness and complexity of the created order, it points the aspirant toward the question of what reality underlies all these appearances. The answer, for Advaita, is that the single, non-dual Brahman alone is real, and the entire cosmic structure described here is its apparent projection through maya.

Sridhara Swami's commentary on this section balances respect for the Puranic cosmography with gentle reminders that all spatial and temporal categories are ultimately products of ignorance (avidya) and are dissolved in the knowledge of Brahman.

## Key Themes

- **Apara Vidya**: Cosmographic knowledge as lower, conventional truth
- **Maya's Projection**: The universe as an apparent manifestation of Brahman
- **Vyavaharika Reality**: The conventional status of cosmic descriptions
- **Pedagogical Function**: How cosmography leads the mind toward the Absolute
- **Space as Brahman**: Akasha (space) as a symbol of the infinite Brahman

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Cosmic Structure as Brahman's Body

Advaitins read descriptions of the universe as elaborations on the Upanishadic teaching that everything is Brahman. The seven continents, seven oceans, and multiple worlds are all modifications (or apparent modifications) of the one reality.

### Dissolution and Re-creation

The cyclical description of cosmic dissolution at the end of each kalpa is read as demonstrating the impermanence of all phenomenal existence — pointing toward the unchanging Brahman.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on cosmographic material is typically brief, treating it as conventional description useful for meditation but not philosophically ultimate. Sridhara Swami acknowledges the traditional cosmography while consistently pointing the reader beyond it to the non-dual ground.
