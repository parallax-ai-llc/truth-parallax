## Overview

The Advaita Vedanta tradition reads the first Ansha of the Vishnu Purana as a cosmogonic account that, properly understood, points beyond the personal deity Vishnu to the attributeless Brahman (nirguna Brahman). For Advaitins, the creation narrative is not a description of a real event but an apparent (vivarta) projection of the one non-dual reality through the power of maya.

Shankaracharya himself cited Puranic passages to support his philosophical positions, and later Advaitins like Sridhara Swami provided commentaries on the Vishnu Purana that read its theistic framework through a non-dual lens. In this reading, Vishnu is saguna Brahman — Brahman as apprehended through the limiting adjuncts (upadhis) of maya.

The stories of Prahlada and Dhruva are interpreted in Advaita as illustrations of the stages of spiritual development. Prahlada's unshakeable conviction in Vishnu's omnipresence prefigures the Advaitic realization that Brahman is all — the very insight that dissolves the illusion of duality.

## Key Themes

- **Vivarta**: Creation as apparent transformation, not real modification of Brahman
- **Maya and Avidya**: Cosmic illusion as the mechanism of apparent creation
- **Saguna and Nirguna**: Vishnu as the personal face of the impersonal Absolute
- **Adhyaropa-Apavada**: The method of superimposition and negation applied to cosmogonic narratives
- **Brahma-vidya**: The knowledge of Brahman as the true import of Puranic teaching

## Key Verses and Passages

### "He is Brahman, supreme, the lord" (1.2)

Advaitins read this verse as pointing to Vishnu's identity with Brahman. The attributes described are understood as provisional characterizations of what is ultimately beyond all characterization.

### The Nature of Vishnu (1.1-2)

The opening description of Vishnu as the cause, substance, and end of all existence is read as consistent with the Upanishadic teaching that Brahman is the material and efficient cause of the universe — though in Advaita, this causation is apparent, not real.

## Commentary Tradition

Sridhara Swami's commentary on the Vishnu Purana represents the most influential Advaita reading of this text. Writing in the 14th century, Sridhara interpreted the Purana's theology through Shankaracharya's non-dual framework while maintaining genuine devotion to Vishnu as saguna Brahman. His commentary was so respected that even later Vaishnava commentators felt compelled to engage with it.
