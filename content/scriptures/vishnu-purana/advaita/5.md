## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the Krishna narrative of the fifth Ansha as an extended illustration of Brahman's manifestation within the phenomenal world. For Advaitins, Krishna is saguna Brahman — the Absolute appearing in personal form for the benefit of devotees — but the ultimate truth transcends even this exalted personal manifestation.

The Krishna lila is understood as a demonstration of the principle that the Absolute, though formless, can assume form without being limited by it. Krishna's ability to perform superhuman feats while appearing fully human illustrates the Advaitic concept of maya — the divine power that projects apparent limitations without actually constraining the unlimited Brahman.

Madhusudana Sarasvati, the great Advaitic devotee-philosopher, represents a tradition within Advaita that gives genuine theological value to the Krishna narrative and bhakti while maintaining the ultimate primacy of nirguna Brahman. For this school, devotion to Krishna is not merely a stepping stone but a valid and even preferred path to the non-dual realization.

## Key Themes

- **Saguna Brahman in Action**: Krishna as the personal Absolute manifesting in the world
- **Maya Demonstrated**: Krishna's lila as illustrating the nature of divine projection
- **Bhakti and Jnana**: The relationship between devotion to Krishna and knowledge of Brahman
- **Lila as Pedagogy**: Krishna's actions as teaching tools pointing to non-dual truth
- **Transcendence through Immanence**: The Absolute revealed through personal divine activity

## Key Verses and Passages

### Krishna's Cosmic Form

Advaitin commentators note passages where Krishna's universal nature is revealed, reading these as moments where the saguna form points beyond itself to the nirguna Brahman that underlies all appearances.

### Krishna's Departure

Krishna's departure from the world and the subsequent dissolution of the Yadava clan are read by Advaitins as powerful illustrations of the impermanence of all phenomenal existence, including divine manifestation in form.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhusudana Sarasvati's Gudhartha Dipika on the Bhagavad Gita represents the most sophisticated Advaitic engagement with Krishna theology. While the Vishnu Purana narrative is simpler, the same interpretive principles apply: the personal deity points beyond himself to the impersonal Absolute.
