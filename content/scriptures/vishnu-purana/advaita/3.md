## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the third Ansha's discussion of dharma and Vedic knowledge as belonging to the preparatory stage (sadhana-chatushtaya) of spiritual development. The duties of varnashrama are valid and binding at the conventional (vyavaharika) level but are ultimately transcended in the realization of non-dual Brahman.

Shankaracharya's distinction between karma-kanda (the ritualistic portion of the Vedas) and jnana-kanda (the knowledge portion) is applied to this book. The detailed prescriptions for rituals, duties, and social obligations belong to karma-kanda and serve to purify the mind (chitta-shuddhi), preparing the aspirant for the higher pursuit of Brahma-vidya.

The discussion of the Vedic tradition and its transmission through Vyasa is significant for Advaitins because it establishes the authority of the Vedas, including the Upanishads, which are the primary scriptural basis for Advaita philosophy.

## Key Themes

- **Chitta-shuddhi**: Dharmic duties as means of mental purification
- **Karma-kanda and Jnana-kanda**: The distinction between ritual and knowledge
- **Vyavaharika Dharma**: Social duties as conventionally valid but ultimately transcended
- **Vedic Authority**: The Vedas as the source of knowledge about Brahman
- **Sannyasa**: Renunciation as the stage most conducive to Self-realization

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Division of the Vedas

Advaitins read the account of Vyasa dividing the Vedas as establishing the authority of the entire Vedic corpus, including the Upanishads. The division into Rig, Yajur, Sama, and Atharva does not affect their essential unity as revealing Brahman.

### Stages of Life

The discussion of the four ashramas is particularly significant for Advaita, which privileges the fourth stage (sannyasa) as the most suitable for the pursuit of liberation through knowledge.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaita commentators treat this book's dharmic content as useful for spiritual preparation but insist that true liberation comes only from the knowledge 'I am Brahman' (aham brahmasmi), which transcends all social roles and ritual obligations.
