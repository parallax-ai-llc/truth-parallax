## Overview

The Krishna narrative of the fifth Ansha offers abundant material for comparative study. The story of the divine child threatened by a tyrant who orders the slaughter of infants parallels the biblical narratives of Moses (Pharaoh's decree) and Jesus (Herod's massacre of the innocents), as well as similar birth narratives in Zoroastrian and Greek mythology.

Krishna's dual nature as divine being and human participant in worldly affairs invites comparison with other figures who embody the divine in human form — Jesus in Christianity, certain bodhisattvas in Mahayana Buddhism, and the incarnate lamas of Tibetan Buddhism. The theological questions raised are remarkably parallel: How can the infinite be contained in the finite? How does the divine relate to suffering?

The pastoral setting of the Vrindavan narratives, with Krishna as the divine cowherd, connects to broader comparative themes of pastoral deity figures, from the Greek Pan and Apollo as herdsman to the biblical image of God as shepherd.

## Key Themes

- **Threatened Divine Child**: Krishna, Moses, and Jesus — parallel birth narratives
- **Incarnation Theology**: Krishna compared with Christ, bodhisattvas, and other incarnate deities
- **Divine-Human Relationship**: The gopi-Krishna relationship compared with mystical traditions elsewhere
- **Pastoral Divinity**: The divine cowherd compared with shepherd figures in other traditions
- **Tyrannicide**: The slaying of Kamsa compared with liberation narratives in other cultures

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Threatened Infant

The parallel between Kamsa's attempt to kill the divine child Krishna and Herod's massacre of the innocents (Matthew 2) has been noted since the earliest Western encounters with the Krishna narrative. Both reflect a widespread mythic pattern of the threatened divine child who escapes to fulfill his cosmic destiny.

### The Pastoral Deity

Krishna as the divine cowherd in Vrindavan parallels Greek narratives of gods in pastoral settings (Apollo with the flocks of Admetus) and the biblical imagery of God as shepherd (Psalm 23, John 10).

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative studies of the Krishna narrative have generated important methodological discussions about the relationship between parallel myths. Some scholars (like Weber and Garbe in the 19th century) proposed direct borrowing between Christian and Krishna traditions, but contemporary scholars generally favor independent development from shared mythic patterns.
