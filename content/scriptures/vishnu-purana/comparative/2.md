## Overview

The cosmographic content of the second Ansha provides rich material for comparative study. The Vishnu Purana's vision of a multi-layered, structured cosmos centered on a sacred mountain finds remarkable parallels across world mythological and religious traditions.

The concept of a world axis (axis mundi) represented by Mount Meru parallels the Greek Mount Olympus, the Norse Yggdrasil, the Mesopotamian ziggurat, the Chinese Kunlun, and the Mesoamerican concept of the world tree. The idea of concentric rings of land and water surrounding a central point appears in Buddhist cosmology (the Abhidharmakosa), Jain geography, and echoes of similar models in early Greek (Anaximander) and Norse cosmography.

The division of the cosmos into upper, middle, and lower worlds — with gods above, humans in the middle, and demons below — represents one of the most widespread cosmological schemas in human religious history, found in traditions as diverse as Sumerian, Egyptian, Greek, Norse, and Mesoamerican religion.

## Key Themes

- **Axis Mundi**: Meru compared with Olympus, Yggdrasil, and other world-center mountains
- **Concentric Cosmography**: Ringed world-models across cultures
- **Three-tiered Cosmos**: Upper, middle, and lower worlds in comparative perspective
- **Sacred Geography**: How cosmographic models shape religious practice
- **Cosmological Imagination**: The human tendency to construct ordered world-models

## Key Verses and Passages

### Mount Meru and the Axis Mundi

Meru as the cosmic axis invites comparison with similar structures worldwide. Eliade's concept of the axis mundi as a universal religious symbol finds one of its clearest expressions in the Puranic cosmography.

### The Seven Continents and Oceans

The seven-dvipa model parallels the Buddhist seven mountain rings and seven seas of the Abhidharmakosa, as well as echoes in Greek geographical speculation about concentric world-zones.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholars note that the Puranic cosmographic model, while sharing structural features with many other traditions, is distinguished by its enormous scale, its integration of time and space through the yuga-kalpa system, and its explicitly theological function as a demonstration of divine sovereignty.
