## Overview

The sixth Ansha's treatment of cosmic dissolution and liberation provides rich material for comparative eschatology. The Vishnu Purana's cyclical model — in which the universe is periodically dissolved and re-created — stands in contrast to the linear eschatologies of the Abrahamic traditions but finds parallels in Stoic cosmology, Buddhist cosmic cycles, and Mesoamerican world ages.

The concept of multiple types of dissolution invites comparison with the layered eschatological expectations found in other traditions. Christianity, for example, distinguishes between individual death, historical judgment events, and the final cosmic consummation — a typology not unlike the Vishnu Purana's four-fold classification of pralaya.

The moksha teaching — liberation from the cycle of rebirth — can be compared with Christian salvation, Islamic paradise, Buddhist nirvana, and the Daoist concept of returning to the Tao. Each tradition offers a distinctive vision of the ultimate human destiny, and comparison reveals both structural parallels and irreducible differences.

## Key Themes

- **Cyclical vs. Linear Eschatology**: Hindu cosmic cycles compared with Abrahamic linear history
- **Multiple Eschatological Levels**: The pralaya typology compared with layered eschatologies elsewhere
- **Liberation and Salvation**: Moksha compared with Christian salvation, Buddhist nirvana, Islamic paradise
- **Cosmic Dissolution**: Hindu pralaya compared with Norse Ragnarok, Stoic ekpyrosis, and other end-of-world myths
- **Hope Beyond Ending**: The promise of renewal or transcendence in the face of cosmic dissolution

## Key Verses and Passages

### Pralaya and Ragnarok

The Vishnu Purana's account of cosmic dissolution finds a striking parallel in the Norse Ragnarok — both describe the destruction of the cosmic order, the death of divine beings, and the eventual renewal of the world. The crucial difference: in Hindu cosmology, the cycle has no beginning or end.

### Moksha and Salvation

Comparison between Hindu moksha and Christian salvation reveals different understandings of the human problem (ignorance vs. sin), the means of liberation (knowledge/devotion vs. faith/grace), and the final state (release from rebirth vs. eternal life).

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative eschatology has been a productive field of study, with scholars like Mircea Eliade, S.G.F. Brandon, and John Hick examining how different traditions imagine the end of all things and the ultimate destiny of the soul.
