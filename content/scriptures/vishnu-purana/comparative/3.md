## Overview

The third Ansha's treatment of dharma, social order, and sacred knowledge transmission invites comparison with legal and ethical systems across world religions. The concept of a divinely ordained social and moral order finds parallels in many traditions, from the Jewish Torah's legal codes to the Islamic shariah, from Confucian li (ritual propriety) to Plato's vision of cosmic justice.

The Vishnu Purana's account of the four varnas and four ashramas can be compared with other traditional societies' systems of social stratification and life-stage organization. Medieval European society's tripartite division (oratores, bellatores, laboratores — those who pray, fight, and work) offers a structural parallel, as does Plato's ideal city in the Republic with its three classes corresponding to three parts of the soul.

The concept of periodic revelation through the Vyasa figures across different ages parallels Islamic ideas about prophets being sent to every nation, the Baha'i concept of progressive revelation, and the Buddhist notion of multiple Buddhas appearing across vast cosmic cycles.

## Key Themes

- **Sacred Law Comparison**: Dharma alongside Torah, shariah, and li
- **Social Stratification**: Varna compared with caste and class systems elsewhere
- **Life Stages**: Ashrama system compared with life-cycle concepts in other cultures
- **Progressive Revelation**: Vyasa across ages compared with prophetic traditions
- **Ethical Universals**: Common moral principles across dharmic and non-dharmic traditions

## Key Verses and Passages

### Dharma and Natural Law

The concept of dharma as the inherent law governing all existence invites comparison with the Stoic concept of natural law, Thomas Aquinas's lex naturalis, and the Chinese concept of Tao (the Way).

### The Guru-Disciple Tradition

The transmission of knowledge through authorized teacher-student lineages parallels the Islamic isnad system, the Jewish concept of mesorah, and the Buddhist emphasis on unbroken lineage transmission.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholars note that while many traditions share the concept of divinely ordained social order, the Hindu system is distinctive in its explicit linking of social duty, cosmic law, and individual spiritual progress within a framework of multiple lifetimes.
