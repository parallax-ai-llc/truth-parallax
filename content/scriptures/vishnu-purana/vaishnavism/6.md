## Overview

The sixth and final Ansha describes the various forms of cosmic dissolution (pralaya) and the nature of liberation (moksha). For Vaishnavas, this book completes the theological arc of the entire Purana: having established Vishnu as the source of creation, the sustainer of the cosmos, and the active presence in human history, the text now reveals him as the ultimate destination to which all things return.

The description of the four types of dissolution — naimittika (periodic), prakritika (elemental), atyantika (absolute, i.e., liberation), and nitya (continuous, i.e., daily death) — demonstrates the comprehensive scope of Vishnu's sovereignty. He presides over every form of ending, from the daily rhythm of sleep and waking to the final dissolution of the entire cosmos.

For Vaishnava devotees, the teaching on moksha in this book is particularly significant. Liberation is described not as absorption into an impersonal Absolute but as the soul's attainment of Vishnu's supreme abode (Vaikuntha), where it exists in eternal conscious relationship with the Lord.

## Key Themes

- **Cosmic Dissolution**: Vishnu's role in the periodic destruction and re-creation of the universe
- **Moksha as Vaikuntha**: Liberation as eternal personal relationship with Vishnu
- **Kali Yuga Decline**: The present age as a time requiring intensified devotion
- **Prapatti**: Complete surrender to Vishnu as the means of liberation
- **Eschatological Hope**: Vishnu's future intervention through the Kalki avatar

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Four Pralayas

Vaishnava commentators read the four types of dissolution as demonstrating Vishnu's complete control over all endings. Nothing ceases to exist except by Vishnu's will, and no dissolution is final for the devoted soul.

### The Nature of Liberation

The text's description of moksha as the soul's release from material bondage and attainment of Vishnu's nature (though not identity with Vishnu) is a crucial proof-text for Vaishnava theology.

## Commentary Tradition

Vaishnava commentators give extensive attention to the moksha teachings of this final book, as they bear directly on the goal of Vaishnava spiritual practice. The description of liberation as personal, conscious, and relational rather than impersonal and absorptive is foundational.
