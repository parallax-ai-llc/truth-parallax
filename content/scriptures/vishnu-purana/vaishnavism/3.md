## Overview

The third Ansha discusses the Vedic traditions, the institution of varnashrama dharma, and the duties incumbent on different classes and stages of life. For Vaishnavas, this book demonstrates that dharma in all its forms is ultimately grounded in Vishnu's cosmic order and his will for humanity.

Vaishnava interpreters emphasize that the elaborate system of duties described here is not an end in itself but a means of pleasing Vishnu and progressing toward liberation through devotion. The duties of the various varnas and ashramas are understood as divinely ordained pathways that, when performed as offerings to Vishnu, lead to spiritual advancement.

The book's discussion of the Vedic tradition and the dissemination of sacred knowledge through the Vyasa figures across different manvantaras reinforces the Vaishnava understanding that all authentic knowledge originates from and leads back to Vishnu.

## Key Themes

- **Dharma as Vishnu's Order**: All righteous conduct as an expression of Vishnu's will
- **Varnashrama as Spiritual Path**: Social duties as means of devotional service
- **Vedic Authority**: The Vedas as Vishnu's revelation
- **Manvantara Cycles**: The periodic re-establishment of dharma by Vishnu
- **Nishkama Karma**: Selfless action offered to Vishnu as the ideal

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Origin of the Vedas

Vaishnavas read the account of Vyasa dividing the Vedas as demonstrating Vishnu's continuous care for humanity — Vishnu himself incarnates as Vyasa in each age to preserve and transmit sacred knowledge.

### Duties of the Householder

The detailed prescriptions for householder duties are interpreted by Vaishnavas as opportunities for devotional service, with daily worship of Vishnu at their center.

## Commentary Tradition

Vaishnava commentators on this section emphasize the connection between dharmic conduct and devotion to Vishnu. The performance of prescribed duties without attachment to results (nishkama karma) is seen as preparatory to the higher stages of bhakti.
