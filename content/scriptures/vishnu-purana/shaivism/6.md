## Overview

Shaiva interpreters read the sixth Ansha's dissolution material with particular interest, since dissolution (samhara or pralaya) is one of the five cosmic functions (panchakritya) that Shaiva theology attributes primarily to Shiva. The very theme of this book — the end of all things — resonates deeply with Shiva's identity as the Destroyer within the Trimurti.

Shaiva readers note that even within this Vaishnava text, the process of dissolution involves the withdrawal of creation into its source through a series of elemental re-absorptions that mirror the Shaiva account of cosmic dissolution. The description of the dissolution of the elements back into prakriti and ultimately into the Absolute echoes Shaiva accounts where this process is governed by Shiva's will.

The Shaiva perspective on moksha differs from the Vaishnava reading of this book. For Shaivas, liberation is not entry into a divine abode but the soul's recognition of its identity with Shiva — or, in some Shaiva schools, the removal of the impurities (malas) that prevent the soul from realizing its inherent divine nature.

## Key Themes

- **Shiva as Destroyer**: Dissolution as Shiva's primary cosmic function
- **Panchakritya**: The five acts (creation, preservation, dissolution, concealment, grace) as Shiva's
- **Shaiva Moksha**: Liberation as identity with or proximity to Shiva
- **Cosmic Dance**: The rhythm of creation and dissolution as Shiva's Tandava
- **Mala Removal**: Liberation through removal of spiritual impurities

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Great Dissolution

Shaiva interpreters read the description of the great dissolution (mahapralaya) as an account that, regardless of its Vaishnava framing, describes an activity that properly belongs to Shiva. The reabsorption of all into the Absolute mirrors Shiva's samhara function.

### Beyond Liberation

Some Shaiva interpreters note that the Vishnu Purana's moksha description, while framed in Vaishnava terms, points to a reality beyond sectarian categories — the soul's return to its source.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva engagement with dissolution literature is extensive, as this is their theological home territory. Comparative analysis between Vaishnava and Shaiva Puranic accounts of pralaya reveals substantial structural similarity despite differing theological attributions.
