## Overview

Shaiva readers of the second Ansha find in its cosmographic descriptions a largely non-sectarian account of cosmic structure that can be assimilated into Shaiva cosmology. The description of the seven dvipas, the mountain systems, and the planetary spheres follows a common Puranic template shared across both Vaishnava and Shaiva texts.

Shaiva interpreters note that the cosmographic framework described here is essentially identical to that found in Shaiva Puranas like the Linga Purana and Shiva Purana, suggesting that these descriptions belong to a shared cosmological tradition rather than being uniquely Vaishnava.

Where the text attributes the ordering of the cosmos to Vishnu, Shaiva readers understand this as describing one aspect of the divine function that ultimately belongs to Shiva as Maheshvara, the Great Lord who encompasses all cosmic activities including the creation, maintenance, and dissolution described across different Puranas.

## Key Themes

- **Shared Cosmological Framework**: The dvipa-loka system as common Puranic heritage
- **Shiva's Cosmic Presence**: The universe as the body or expression of Shiva
- **Kailasa**: Mount Kailasa's significance within the cosmic geography
- **Rudra-lokas**: The presence of Shaiva sacred spaces within the cosmography
- **Transcendent Geography**: The cosmos as a map of spiritual realization

## Key Verses and Passages

### Mount Kailasa

Shaiva interpreters note with interest that even within the Vishnu Purana's cosmography, Mount Kailasa — Shiva's abode — is mentioned, reflecting the inescapable presence of Shiva within any comprehensive cosmic description.

### The Planetary System

The description of the celestial bodies and their orbits is read by Shaivas as reflecting the cosmic dance of Shiva Nataraja, whose movements govern the celestial spheres.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva engagement with this book tends to be less polemical than with the explicitly theological first book, as the cosmographic material is largely shared tradition. Shaiva scholars use these parallel accounts to argue for the fundamental unity of the Puranic worldview.
