## Overview

Shaiva interpreters approach the Vishnu Purana with a nuanced perspective that acknowledges its Vaishnava orientation while reinterpreting key passages within a Shaiva theological framework. Rather than rejecting the text outright, Shaiva scholars have historically engaged with it to demonstrate the complementarity or ultimate unity of Shiva and Vishnu.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Harihara Unity
Shaiva readers often invoke the Harihara concept — the inseparability of Hari (Vishnu) and Hara (Shiva) — to argue that the Vishnu Purana's praise of Vishnu implicitly includes Shiva. When the text describes Vishnu as the supreme pervading reality, Shaiva interpreters may understand this as a description applicable to Shiva as well.

### Sectarian Context
Shaiva scholars recognize the Vishnu Purana as a product of sectarian devotional literature and read it alongside Shaiva Puranas to construct a more comprehensive understanding. The text's polemical passages against Shaivism are understood as rhetorical strategies rather than absolute theological claims.

### Complementary Functions
The cosmic roles attributed to Vishnu in the text — creation, preservation, dissolution — are reinterpreted as functions that ultimately point to Shiva, particularly in his aspects as Sadashiva or Maheshvara.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shiva as the Ultimate Ground**: Even Vishnu's supremacy derives from or points to Shiva
- **Trimurthi Theology**: Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva as co-equal aspects of the one supreme reality
- **Inclusive Monotheism**: A theological position that includes all deities within the Shaiva framework
- **Lila and Maya**: Cosmic play and illusion as functions of Shiva manifesting through Vishnu
