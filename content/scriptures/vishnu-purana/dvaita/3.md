## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the third Ansha's treatment of dharma as demonstrating the binding nature of Vishnu's cosmic law on all beings. For Dvaitins, dharma is not a mere social convention or a preliminary stage to be transcended but an eternal expression of Vishnu's will that structures reality at every level.

Madhvacharya insisted on the reality and permanent validity of dharmic duties, arguing against the Advaita position that they are transcended in liberation. Even liberated souls, in the Dvaita view, maintain their essential nature and their eternal relationship of service to Vishnu. The varnashrama system, as described in this book, reflects the real differences among souls (jiva-bheda) that are part of the permanent structure of reality.

The book's emphasis on the Vedic tradition and its preservation through Vyasa supports the Dvaita insistence on scriptural authority (shastra-pramana) as the primary means of knowing the nature of Vishnu and the soul's relationship to him.

## Key Themes

- **Eternal Dharma**: Dharmic obligations as permanently binding, not provisionally valid
- **Jiva-bheda**: Real differences among souls reflected in social duties
- **Vishnu's Will**: All dharma as an expression of Vishnu's sovereign command
- **Scriptural Authority**: The Vedas as the primary and infallible source of knowledge
- **Service in Liberation**: Eternal dharmic relationship with Vishnu even after moksha

## Key Verses and Passages

### Vyasa as Vishnu's Incarnation

Madhvacharya identifies Vyasa as a direct incarnation of Vishnu, making the Vedic tradition itself a product of Vishnu's salvific activity. This identification is crucial for establishing the authority of scripture in Dvaita theology.

### The Permanence of Dharma

Dvaita interpreters emphasize passages that describe dharma as eternal and unchanging, reading them as evidence against the Advaita claim that dharma is provisional.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's treatment of dharma in his various works draws heavily on Puranic accounts like those in this book. His insistence on the reality of dharmic distinctions and their grounding in Vishnu's will sets the Dvaita approach apart from both Advaita and Vishishtadvaita readings.
