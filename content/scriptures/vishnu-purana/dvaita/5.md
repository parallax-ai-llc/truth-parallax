## Overview

For Dvaita Vedanta, the fifth Ansha's Krishna narrative presents the earthly activities of the Supreme Person in all his power, beauty, and grace. Madhvacharya identifies Krishna as the complete (purna) avatara of Vishnu — not a partial descent or a divinely empowered human but Vishnu himself in human form, possessing the fullness of divine attributes.

The Dvaita tradition reads every episode in the Krishna narrative as revealing real divine attributes. Krishna's childhood pranks in Vrindavan display his accessibility and love; his defeat of demons demonstrates his omnipotence; his relationships with the gopis reveal the nature of the soul's highest aspiration — complete, unqualified devotion to the Supreme Person.

Madhvacharya's Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya provides an extensive treatment of Krishna's life that draws on the Vishnu Purana alongside other sources, offering a characteristically Dvaita reading that emphasizes the reality of every event and the permanent, personal nature of the divine-human relationship.

## Key Themes

- **Purna Avatara**: Krishna as the complete and perfect manifestation of Vishnu
- **Real Divine Activity**: Every event in Krishna's life as historically and metaphysically real
- **Supreme Personhood**: Krishna's life as revealing the personal nature of ultimate reality
- **Devotion as Eternal Relationship**: The gopis' devotion as a model for the soul's permanent bond with Vishnu
- **Anti-Docetism**: Against any interpretation that denies the reality of Krishna's human activities

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Butter-stealing Lila

Madhva and his commentators read Krishna's childhood pranks not merely as charming stories but as revelations of divine attributes: his omniscience (knowing where butter is hidden), his sovereignty (taking what belongs to him as Lord of all), and his accessibility (allowing himself to be caught and scolded).

### Krishna and the Gopis

The Dvaita tradition reads Krishna's relationship with the gopis as illustrating the highest form of devotion — complete self-surrender to the Supreme Person. Unlike the Advaita reading, this devotion is eternally maintained, never dissolved into identity.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya provides the most systematic Dvaita treatment of the Krishna narrative, correlating the Vishnu Purana's account with other sources. His student Jayatirtha and later scholars like Raghavendra Swami further developed the devotional and theological implications.
