## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the sixth Ansha's teachings on dissolution and liberation as confirming core Dvaita positions: the reality of cosmic dissolution (against Advaita's denial of real change), the permanence of individual souls (against absorption into an impersonal Absolute), and the eternal distinction between the liberated soul and Vishnu.

For Dvaitins, the detailed description of the various types of pralaya demonstrates that dissolution is a real process involving real entities, not the collapse of an illusion. The cosmos genuinely comes into being, genuinely persists, and genuinely dissolves — all under Vishnu's sovereign control.

The moksha teaching is critically important for Dvaita. Liberation in the Dvaita view is not identity with Brahman but the soul's attainment of its natural state of bliss in the presence of Vishnu, eternally distinct from him yet eternally devoted to him. The Vishnu Purana's descriptions of the liberated state are read as supporting this personal, relational understanding of moksha.

## Key Themes

- **Real Dissolution**: Pralaya as a genuine cosmic event, not the fading of an illusion
- **Eternal Individuality**: The soul maintains its distinct identity in liberation
- **Moksha as Proximity**: Liberation as eternal existence in Vishnu's presence, not identity
- **Vishnu's Sovereign Control**: All cosmic events as expressions of Vishnu's independent will
- **Graded Liberation**: Different levels of liberation corresponding to the hierarchy of souls

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Dissolution Process

Dvaita interpreters emphasize the reality of each stage of dissolution, reading the text as describing actual cosmic events rather than metaphorical or apparent processes. This supports the Dvaita insistence on the reality of the material world.

### Liberation and Eternal Service

The text's description of the liberated soul attaining Vishnu's nature is read by Dvaitins not as identity but as similarity — the soul attains qualities like Vishnu's (bliss, knowledge) while remaining forever distinct from him.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's treatment of moksha draws on this Purana's final book to establish the Dvaita position that liberation is eternal, personal, and hierarchical. His commentary tradition emphasizes that the five-fold difference (pancha-bheda) persists even in the liberated state.
