## Overview

For Dvaita Vedanta, the first Ansha of the Vishnu Purana provides some of the strongest Puranic evidence for their central theological claims: the absolute supremacy and independence (svatantrya) of Vishnu, the reality of creation, and the eternal distinction between God and the individual soul.

Madhvacharya and his commentators read this book as directly supporting the Dvaita position against both Advaita (non-dualism) and Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism). The text's unambiguous declarations of Vishnu's supremacy, its detailed cosmogony, and its emphasis on devotion as the means of liberation all align naturally with Dvaita theology.

The narrative of Prahlada holds special significance in the Dvaita tradition. Madhvacharya identified Prahlada as one of the foremost devotees in the hierarchy of souls (taratamya), and the Dvaita tradition sees in Prahlada's story a demonstration that true devotion arises from the inherent nature (svarupa-yogyata) of the soul and Vishnu's special grace.

## Key Themes

- **Svatantrya**: Vishnu's absolute independence as the only self-sufficient reality
- **Real Creation**: The universe as a genuine product of Vishnu's creative will
- **Bheda**: The eternal, real distinction between Vishnu, souls, and matter
- **Taratamya**: The inherent hierarchy among souls, illustrated by Prahlada's exalted status
- **Sakshi**: Vishnu as the inner witness and controller of all souls

## Key Verses and Passages

### Vishnu as the Supreme Cause (1.1)

Madhvacharya reads this passage as an unequivocal statement of Vishnu's causal supremacy. Unlike the Advaita interpretation, which sees this as a description of saguna Brahman, Madhva insists that the personal, qualified Vishnu is the highest reality — there is no higher nirguna Brahman behind the personal God.

### Prahlada's Devotion (1.17-20)

In Dvaita theology, Prahlada's unwavering devotion is not merely an example of piety but evidence of the soul's innate orientation toward Vishnu. Prahlada belongs to the highest class of souls (uttama-jivas) who are destined for liberation by their very nature.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's Vishnu Purana citations appear throughout his major works, including the Anuvyakhyana and Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya. His student Jayatirtha systematized these citations, while later scholars like Vyasatirtha and Raghavendra Swami elaborated the Dvaita reading of this Purana in their own commentaries and independent works.
