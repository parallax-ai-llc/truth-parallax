Madhva reads the three births as describing the real journey of the individual soul through Vishnu's creation. Unlike Advaita, which treats these births as illusory, Dvaita affirms their reality while insisting that the soul remains eternally distinct from God throughout all three births. The father-son relationship illustrates the soul's dependence on God: just as the child depends on the father, every soul depends on Vishnu.

The third birth — the soul's continuation after death — is particularly significant for Dvaita theology, which teaches that souls proceed after death either to liberation (for those who have attained God's grace) or to rebirth (for those still bound by karma). The soul's journey is real, and liberation requires the real grace of a real God.
