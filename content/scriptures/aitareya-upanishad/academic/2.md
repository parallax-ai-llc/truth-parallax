Chapter 2's three births doctrine provides valuable evidence for early Vedic attitudes toward reproduction, family, and death. Scholars note the text's remarkably detailed description of conception and gestation, suggesting an interest in embryology unusual for philosophical texts of this period. The notion of the father as the true agent of the child's conception reflects patriarchal assumptions common in ancient Indian society.

The third birth — after death — connects the Aitareya to the broader Upanishadic discourse on transmigration (*samsara*), which was becoming a central concern in the middle Vedic period. Olivelle suggests that the three births doctrine may represent an attempt to integrate the older Vedic emphasis on continuation through progeny with the newer concept of individual rebirth.
