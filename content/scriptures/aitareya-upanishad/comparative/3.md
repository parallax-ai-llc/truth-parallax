The Aitareya's identification of consciousness as the ultimate reality invites comparison with philosophical idealism across traditions. "Consciousness is Brahman" parallels Berkeley's *esse est percipi* (to be is to be perceived), Hegel's identification of the Absolute with Spirit, and the Yogacara Buddhist reduction of reality to mind-only (*vijnapti-matra*).

The enumeration of cognitive functions in a single unifying category anticipates modern cognitive science's search for a unified theory of consciousness. The hard problem of consciousness — how subjective experience arises from objective processes — receives the Aitareya's radical answer: consciousness does not arise from anything; it is the ground of all arising. This compares with the panpsychism of Alfred North Whitehead and the integrated information theory of contemporary consciousness studies.
