## Overview

The Letter of Aristeas is a Jewish pseudepigraphical work composed in the second century BCE, purporting to be a letter from Aristeas, a courtier of Ptolemy II Philadelphus of Egypt (285-247 BCE), to his brother Philocrates. The text narrates the story of how the Hebrew Torah came to be translated into Greek — the origin legend of the Septuagint. According to the narrative, Ptolemy, at the suggestion of his librarian Demetrius of Phalerum, invited seventy-two Jewish elders from Jerusalem to Alexandria to produce the translation.

While ostensibly a historical account, the Letter of Aristeas is widely recognized as a work of Jewish apologetics written to enhance the authority of the Septuagint and to present Judaism favorably to a Hellenistic audience. The text includes an elaborate description of Jerusalem, the Temple, and the Jewish high priest, followed by a philosophical banquet at which the Jewish elders demonstrate wisdom superior to Greek philosophy by answering the king's questions on governance and virtue.

The Letter of Aristeas played a crucial role in establishing the divine authority of the Septuagint translation, which became the Bible of early Christianity and remains the Old Testament text of the Eastern Orthodox Church. Beyond its translation narrative, the text provides invaluable evidence for Hellenistic Jewish self-understanding, the interaction between Jewish and Greek culture in Ptolemaic Alexandria, and the strategies of cultural apologetics employed by diaspora Jews.

## Structure

Letter of Aristeas is structured as follows:

1. **Section 1**: The Royal Initiative: Ptolemy's desire to include the Jewish law in the Library of Alexandria
2. **Section 2**: The Embassy to Jerusalem: Description of the holy city, the Temple, and the high priest Eleazar
3. **Section 3**: Eleazar's Apology: The high priest's philosophical defense of Jewish law and its rational basis
4. **Section 4**: The Royal Banquet (Part 1): The Jewish elders' wisdom on kingship and governance
5. **Section 5**: The Royal Banquet (Part 2): Further questions and the elders' responses on virtue and justice
6. **Section 6**: The Translation: The miraculous completion and authentication of the Septuagint

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: Essential for understanding Hellenistic Jewish apologetics, the origins of the Septuagint, and Jewish-Greek cultural interaction
- **Comparative**: Provides parallels with other sacred translation narratives and the broader question of translating divine texts across cultures
- **Protestant**: Studied for understanding the Septuagint's origins and its relationship to the Hebrew Masoretic text used by Protestants
- **Catholic**: Valued as a witness to the authority of the Septuagint tradition that influenced the Vulgate and Catholic Old Testament canon
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Foundational for the Orthodox Church's use of the Septuagint as its authoritative Old Testament text
- **Interfaith**: A model for reflecting on the translation of sacred texts, cultural apologetics, and interfaith philosophical dialogue

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Sacred Translation | The divinely guided process of translating the Torah into Greek, legitimizing the Septuagint |
| Jewish Apologetics | The presentation of Judaism as a philosophically sophisticated and morally superior tradition |
| Hellenistic Judaism | The creative encounter between Jewish tradition and Greek culture in Ptolemaic Alexandria |
| Wisdom and Governance | The Jewish elders' demonstration that Torah-based wisdom exceeds Greek philosophical counsel |
| Temple and Cult | An idealized description of the Jerusalem Temple, its priesthood, and its ritual practices |
| Cultural Bridge | The Septuagint as a bridge between Jewish revelation and the wider Greek-speaking world |
