The trecenas — twenty periods of thirteen days each — structure the 260-day *tonalpohualli* into manageable ceremonial units, each governed by a presiding deity who sets the character of the entire thirteen-day period. The Codex Borgia depicts each trecena with its patron deity, associated imagery, and the sequence of day-signs that fall within it. The first trecena, beginning with 1 Cipactli (Crocodile), is governed by Ometecuhtli/Ometeotl, the primordial dual deity, establishing the entire calendar cycle as an expression of the fundamental creative duality at the heart of Mesoamerican theology.

Each trecena creates a narrative arc within the larger calendar. The early days of a trecena are often characterized by the gathering of the governing deity's energy, the middle days by its full expression, and the final days by its dispersal or transformation into the next trecena's quality. Indigenous calendar practitioners experience this rhythm as a kind of breathing: each thirteen-day period inhales a specific cosmic quality, holds it, and then exhales it to make room for the next. Planning major activities — ceremonies, business ventures, marriages, journeys — requires understanding where one stands within the current trecena's arc.

The presiding deities of the trecenas include major and minor figures from the Mesoamerican pantheon. Some trecenas are governed by fearsome powers — Mictlantecuhtli, Tezcatlipoca, Itzpapalotl (Obsidian Butterfly) — and their periods carry heightened spiritual risk. Others are governed by beneficent figures — Xochiquetzal (Flower Quetzal), Tlazolteotl in her aspect as purifier, Tonatiuh (Sun) — and their days are favorable for celebration and creation. This alternation of dangerous and auspicious periods teaches that life cannot be lived in perpetual safety or perpetual celebration; both risk and joy are necessary components of a complete human existence.

The trecena system also provides a framework for understanding individual destiny within a communal context. A person born during a particular trecena inherits that period's governing qualities, but these qualities unfold differently depending on the specific day-sign and night lord of their birth. The *tonalpouhqui* (calendar reader) interprets these layered influences to counsel parents about a child's character and potential, recommend naming ceremonies, and suggest which activities or professions the child's *tonalli* best supports. This practice, maintained and revitalized in contemporary indigenous communities, represents a sophisticated form of spiritual counseling rooted in cosmological knowledge.
