The Venus pages of the Codex Borgia constitute one of the most astronomically sophisticated sections of any Mesoamerican manuscript. Venus's 584-day synodic cycle — its period of visibility as morning star (236 days), disappearance at superior conjunction (90 days), reappearance as evening star (250 days), and disappearance at inferior conjunction (8 days) — is tracked with remarkable precision and correlated with the *tonalpohualli* and solar calendar to produce long-range astronomical predictions.

In Mesoamerican tradition, Venus is identified with Quetzalcoatl in his celestial aspect: the morning star is Tlahuizcalpantecuhtli (Lord of the Dawn), the warrior aspect of the Plumed Serpent who shoots darts of light at the earth upon rising. The first appearance of Venus as morning star after its period of invisibility was considered a moment of cosmic danger, when the star's piercing rays could bring drought, disease, or warfare. The Codex Borgia identifies which categories of beings are vulnerable to Venus's rays during each of its five apparition cycles within the Venus-calendar round: rulers, warriors, the elderly, maize crops, or rain itself.

Indigenous astronomical knowledge preserved in the Venus pages reflects centuries of systematic naked-eye observation. The Borgia group codices demonstrate that Mesoamerican astronomers had calculated Venus's synodic period with an accuracy comparable to modern values and had discovered the correction factors needed to keep their Venus tables aligned with actual planetary positions over long periods. Contemporary indigenous scholars like those associated with the Mixtec and Nahua cultural revitalization movements point to these achievements as evidence of a rigorous intellectual tradition that Western scholarship has been slow to recognize as science.

For communities that maintain awareness of Venus's movements, the morning star and evening star continue to serve as temporal markers and spiritual indicators. The appearance of Venus as evening star in the western sky marks a period associated with feminine power, introspection, and the descent into the underworld. Its reappearance as morning star signals renewal, aggressive creative energy, and the reassertion of light over darkness. These associations inform the timing of agricultural activities, healing ceremonies, and community gatherings in ways that integrate celestial observation with lived spiritual practice.
