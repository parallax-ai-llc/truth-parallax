The directional associations of the Codex Borgia map the cosmos onto a four-quartered (plus center) spatial framework in which each cardinal direction possesses its own color, deity, tree, bird, and quality. East is associated with red, fertility, and the rising sun. North is associated with black, cold, death, and the flint knife. West belongs to white (or sometimes other colors depending on regional tradition), the setting sun, and the feminine powers. South is associated with blue-green, rain, and abundance. The center, where the four directions converge, belongs to Xiuhtecuhtli, the fire god, who unites and balances the cosmic quarters.

This directional cosmology is not abstract geometry but a lived spatial practice. In indigenous communities throughout central Mexico, houses are oriented with reference to the cardinal directions, ceremonial altars are constructed with offerings placed at each quarter, and the human body itself is understood as a microcosmic expression of the four-quartered cosmos. When a *curandero* performs a healing ceremony, they typically address each direction in turn, invoking its specific powers and requesting their aid. The directional system thus provides the spatial grammar of indigenous ceremonial life.

The Codex Borgia's directional pages assign specific day-signs to each quarter, dividing the twenty signs into four groups of five. This directional assignment determines which cosmic quadrant governs a given day, adding another layer to the calendar's complexity. Days assigned to the east carry solar, fertile, masculine energy; days assigned to the north carry the energy of sacrifice and austerity; western days invoke the feminine, twilight, and introspection; southern days invoke rain, growth, and the generative powers of the earth. The intersection of these directional qualities with day-signs, night lords, and trecena rulers creates the dense web of temporal meaning that indigenous calendar tradition seeks to interpret.

The four world-trees depicted in the directional pages — each associated with its cardinal point and supporting the sky at its respective corner — reflect the Mesoamerican understanding that the cosmos is an architectural structure that requires active maintenance. These trees were established at the beginning of the current age and must be sustained through ceremony. When communities perform the offerings prescribed by the directional system, they understand themselves as participating in the structural support of the cosmos itself, a responsibility shared between humans and the gods.
