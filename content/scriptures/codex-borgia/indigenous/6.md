The cosmic journey through the underworld depicted in the central pages of the Codex Borgia (pages 29-46) is widely regarded as the most visually powerful and spiritually profound section of the manuscript. These pages narrate the nocturnal journey of the sun through Mictlan, the realm of the dead, from sunset to sunrise — the nightly death and resurrection that sustains the cycle of day and night. The imagery is dense, violent, and transformative: deities are dismembered and reconstituted, sacrificial blood flows between cosmic levels, and the boundaries between life and death dissolve and reform.

For indigenous practitioners, this section maps the territory of spiritual transformation. The sun's journey through the underworld is the archetype for all passages through darkness — illness, grief, initiation, death itself. The stages of the nocturnal journey correspond to phases of healing and spiritual growth: the descent into darkness, the confrontation with death's agents, the dismemberment of the old self, and the reassembly and emergence into renewed life. Curanderos who guide patients through serious illness understand their work as facilitating a passage analogous to the sun's nightly journey.

The central image of this section — often identified as the death and resurrection of Quetzalcoatl or the transformation of Venus — shows a skeletal figure emerging from the jaws of the earth monster to be reborn as the morning star. The red and black body paint, the sacrificial implements, and the flowing blood depicted in these pages encode ritual instructions for ceremonies of transformation. Indigenous communities in the Puebla-Mixteca region have maintained ceremonial traditions that enact elements of this cosmic journey, using cave sites and mountain shrines as physical analogues of the underworld through which initiates pass.

The underworld journey also establishes the theological foundation for the Mesoamerican understanding of sacrifice. The sun must die each night to be reborn each morning, and this cosmic sacrifice requires reciprocal human participation. The offerings depicted in the Codex Borgia's underworld pages — blood, incense (*copal*), food, flowers — represent the human contribution to the cosmic economy of energy exchange. Contemporary indigenous communities that maintain offering practices at sacred sites understand themselves as participants in this ongoing cycle, sustaining the sun's journey through their ceremonial labor.
