The closing pages of the Codex Borgia return to themes of cosmic renewal, completing the cycle that began with the twenty day-signs. The imagery depicts the regeneration of the world after passage through the underworld — the sun's emergence from darkness, the renewal of vegetation, the restoration of cosmic order after the trials depicted in the central pages. This circular structure mirrors the calendar itself: the 260-day *tonalpohualli* has no beginning or end, only the continuous turning of interlocked cycles.

The closing rituals emphasize completion and transition rather than finality. In Mesoamerican thought, every ending is simultaneously a beginning. The completion of a calendar cycle initiates the next; the death of the old sun at dusk initiates the nocturnal journey that produces dawn. The Codex Borgia's closing pages thus function as both conclusion and prelude, instructing practitioners in the ceremonies required to properly close one cycle and open the next. Failure to perform these transitional rituals leaves the cosmos in a liminal state — neither fully ended nor fully begun — which is spiritually dangerous.

For contemporary indigenous communities, the closing rituals of the Codex Borgia resonate with the ceremonial practices that mark transitions in community and individual life: the rituals that close the agricultural year and open the next planting season, the ceremonies that mark the completion of a cargo (community service obligation) and the assumption of a new one, the healing rites that close a period of illness and inaugurate recovery. Each of these transitions follows the same cosmic logic encoded in the Codex: proper closure requires ceremony, gratitude, and the orderly transfer of energy from one state to the next.

The Codex Borgia itself, as a physical object — a screenfold manuscript painted on prepared deerskin, currently housed in the Vatican Library — embodies the tensions between indigenous sacred knowledge and colonial appropriation. That one of the most important documents of Mesoamerican spiritual tradition is named for an Italian cardinal and held in a European collection is a source of ongoing concern for indigenous communities who regard it as a stolen sacred text. Efforts to repatriate Mesoamerican codices, or at minimum to ensure indigenous access and interpretive authority over them, are part of broader movements for cultural sovereignty that draw their moral force from the very tradition the Codex encodes.
