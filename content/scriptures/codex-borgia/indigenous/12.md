The offering and sacrificial rites depicted in the Codex Borgia encode the Mesoamerican theology of reciprocity (*nextlahualli*) — the understanding that the cosmos is sustained through ongoing exchange between humans and the divine powers. The offerings depicted range from the simple (copal incense, flowers, food, tobacco) to the elaborate (autosacrificial bloodletting, quail decapitation, and other forms of ritual offering), reflecting a graduated system of ceremonial obligation appropriate to different occasions and different levels of spiritual need.

The fundamental principle underlying all offerings is that the gods gave of themselves to create the world, and humans must return energy to the cosmos to prevent its depletion. The sun requires sustenance to make its daily journey; the rain needs encouragement to fall; the maize must be thanked and nourished to grow again. This is not a transaction but a relationship, analogous to the mutual obligations between family members or between a community and its land. Contemporary indigenous communities that maintain offering practices at mountain shrines, springs, and crossroads understand their ceremonies as fulfilling this relational obligation.

Copal incense (*copalli*) is the most ubiquitous offering depicted in the Codex Borgia and remains central to indigenous ceremonial practice throughout Mesoamerica. The rising smoke of copal is understood as the vehicle through which prayers ascend to the gods and through which divine presence descends to the ceremonial space. The specific resins used, the manner of burning, and the timing of the offering all carry significance. Flower offerings (*xochitl*), particularly marigolds (*cempoalxochitl*), serve a similar mediating function, their color and fragrance creating a sensory bridge between human and divine realms. These same flowers remain essential to Day of the Dead observances and other indigenous ceremonial contexts.

The Codex Borgia's depiction of autosacrificial bloodletting — drawing blood from ears, tongue, or limbs with maguey spines or obsidian blades — represents a personal offering of life-force (*tonalli*) that was practiced across social classes in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica. While this practice is largely discontinued, its theological logic persists: genuine spiritual engagement requires personal cost, the offering of something truly valued. Contemporary ceremonial participants who endure physical discomfort during extended ceremonies, vigils, or pilgrimages understand their effort as a form of sacrifice continuous with the tradition the Codex Borgia depicts.
