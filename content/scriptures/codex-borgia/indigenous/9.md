The agricultural and fertility rituals depicted in the Codex Borgia connect the sacred calendar to the most fundamental human activity: the cultivation of food. Maize (*centli*), the staff of Mesoamerican life, occupies the center of these ritual pages. The maize god Centeotl and the maize goddess Chicomecoatl ("Seven Serpent") appear in scenes of planting, growth, harvest, and offering, establishing the ceremonial protocols that ensure agricultural success.

In indigenous understanding, agriculture is never merely a technical activity. The earth is alive and relational; it gives food in exchange for ceremony, respect, and reciprocal care. The Codex Borgia's agricultural pages specify which day-signs are favorable for planting, which deities must be invoked at each stage of the growing cycle, and what offerings are required to secure rain, protect crops from pests, and ensure a sufficient harvest. Contemporary milpa farmers in central Mexico who maintain traditional practices still time their planting and harvesting to calendar positions, understanding that the alignment between human labor and cosmic rhythm determines agricultural outcomes as much as soil quality or rainfall.

The fertility rituals extend beyond agriculture to encompass human reproduction, the fertility of animals, and the general productive capacity of the earth. Tlazolteotl, the earth goddess associated with sexuality, childbirth, confession, and purification, appears prominently in these pages. Her role as the "eater of filth" (*tlaelquani*) — the deity who absorbs sin and impurity — connects fertility to the transformative processing of waste into new life, a principle that the milpa system embodies practically in its cycling of organic matter through compost back into productive soil.

The rain rituals depicted here show offerings made at mountain tops and springs — the sources of water in Mesoamerican geography. The *tlaloque* (rain spirits subordinate to Tlaloc) dwell within mountains and emerge through cave openings and spring mouths to deliver rain. Communities in highland Mexico continue to make offerings at these sites, particularly at the beginning of the rainy season (May-June), performing ceremonies that have been documented at the same locations for over five centuries. The Codex Borgia's ritual instructions thus describe a living practice rather than an extinct religion.
