The auguries and divinatory tables of the Codex Borgia represent the practical application of the calendar system to everyday decision-making. These pages provide systematic guidance on the qualities of specific days and day-combinations, enabling the *tonalpouhqui* (calendar reader) to advise individuals and communities on the optimal timing for marriages, journeys, business ventures, medical treatments, and ceremonial activities. The system is not fatalistic prediction but probabilistic guidance: certain days carry energies favorable to certain activities, and wisdom consists in aligning human action with cosmic rhythm.

The divinatory tables operate through a combinatorial logic that layers multiple calendar cycles. A day's augury depends not only on its day-sign but also on its number coefficient (1-13), its night lord, its trecena patron, and its directional association. The resulting interpretations have the nuance and contextual sensitivity of a sophisticated counseling system. A day of 7 Coatl (Serpent) during the trecena of Xochiquetzal carries very different implications than the same day-sign during the trecena of Mictlantecuhtli, just as the same weather pattern means different things in different seasons.

Contemporary indigenous diviners in Mexico and Central America maintain versions of this divinatory practice, sometimes using maize kernels, crystals, or seed-casting rather than (or in addition to) the calendar system. The Codex Borgia's tables provide a reference framework for these practitioners, though all emphasize that the tables are guides for interpretation rather than rigid deterministic charts. The diviner's intuition, developed through years of training and ceremonial practice, is essential for reading the tables accurately — they are instruments that require a skilled practitioner to produce meaningful results.

The augury tradition also serves a community governance function. Major collective decisions — when to plant, when to hold market, when to undertake construction or warfare — were traditionally informed by calendar consultation. This practice democratized access to temporal knowledge: while specialists performed the readings, the results were communal property, shared openly to coordinate collective action. Contemporary indigenous governance bodies that consult the calendar before major decisions are maintaining a tradition of spiritually informed collective decision-making that predates colonial-era political structures by millennia.
