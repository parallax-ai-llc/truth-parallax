The twenty day-signs (*tonalli*) of the Codex Borgia constitute the foundational structure of the Mesoamerican sacred calendar, the *tonalpohualli*. Each day-sign — Cipactli (Crocodile), Ehecatl (Wind), Calli (House), Cuetzpalin (Lizard), Coatl (Serpent), and so on through Xochitl (Flower) — represents a distinct quality of time, a specific relationship between cosmic forces and earthly life. In the Codex Borgia's vivid pictorial system, each sign appears alongside its patron deity, the divine power that governs and animates that day's particular energy.

For contemporary Nahua and Mixtec communities in Puebla, Oaxaca, and Guerrero — the regions most closely associated with the Codex Borgia's Puebla-Tlaxcala tradition — the day-signs remain a living framework for understanding personality, fate, and the proper timing of human activities. A child's *tonalli* (day-sign at birth) shapes their character and destiny, much as it did before the Spanish conquest. Although the continuous count was disrupted in many communities by colonial suppression, revitalization movements have restored calendar knowledge through consultation with communities that maintained the count and through careful study of codices like the Borgia itself.

Each patron deity depicted alongside its day-sign reveals a web of associations connecting time to landscape, animals, plants, and human activities. Cipactli's patron, Tonacatecuhtli (Lord of Sustenance), connects the first day-sign to agricultural abundance. Miquiztli (Death) is patroned by Tecciztecatl, the moon god, linking mortality to lunar cycles. These associations are not arbitrary but encode millennia of observed correspondences between celestial patterns, ecological cycles, and human experience. Indigenous calendar practitioners (*tonalpouhque*) read these associations as a practical guide: specific days are favorable for planting, healing, travel, or ceremony, and others demand caution or rest.

The Codex Borgia's visual presentation of the day-signs — rendered in brilliant polychrome on prepared deerskin — represents an indigenous intellectual technology distinct from alphabetic writing. The pictorial system communicates simultaneously through color symbolism, spatial arrangement, and iconographic detail in ways that linear text cannot replicate. Indigenous scholars argue that reading the Codex Borgia requires not just iconographic knowledge but participation in the ceremonial tradition that gives its images their full meaning. The codex is not a book to be read in silence but a guide to be activated through ritual practice.
