## Overview

The Codex Borgia is one of the most important pre-Columbian Mesoamerican manuscripts, a masterwork of Aztec-Puebla religious art and ritual knowledge. Named after Cardinal Stefano Borgia, who acquired it in the eighteenth century, the codex is a screenfold manuscript painted on animal hide, consisting of 39 leaves (76 pages) of elaborate iconographic imagery. It is believed to have been produced in the Puebla-Tlaxcala region of central Mexico before the Spanish conquest, probably in the fifteenth century.

The Codex Borgia is primarily a divinatory and ritual calendar (tonalamatl), organized around the 260-day ritual cycle known as the tonalpohualli. Its pages depict elaborate sequences of deities, cosmic symbols, ritual implements, and calendrical signs used by priests (tonalpouhque) for divination, the timing of ceremonies, and the interpretation of cosmic forces. The central section of the codex (pages 29-46) contains a remarkable narrative sequence depicting a cosmic journey — possibly the passage of Venus through the underworld — that transcends simple calendrical function and achieves the status of sacred narrative.

## Structure

The Codex Borgia can be divided into the following sections:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| 1 | The twenty day-signs (tonalli) with their patron deities and associated symbolism |
| 2 | The Lords of the Night (Yoalteuctin) — nine deities governing the night hours |
| 3 | The trecenas — twenty groups of thirteen days with their presiding deities |
| 4 | Directional pages — associations between day-signs and the four cardinal directions |
| 5 | Venus pages — the cycles of the planet Venus and their ritual significance |
| 6 | The cosmic journey — a narrative sequence depicting passage through the underworld |
| 7 | Ritual scenes involving Quetzalcoatl and Tezcatlipoca |
| 8 | Deity pairs and their cosmic functions |
| 9 | Agricultural and fertility rituals tied to the calendar |
| 10 | The 52-year calendar round and New Fire ceremony |
| 11 | Auguries and divinatory tables for specific activities |
| 12 | Offerings and sacrificial rites associated with calendrical periods |
| 13 | Closing ritual sequences and cosmic renewal |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: A sacred document of Nahua-Puebla spiritual knowledge, preserving pre-conquest ritual practice
- **Academic**: One of the most studied pre-Columbian manuscripts, essential for Mesoamerican iconography and calendrics
- **Comparative**: Provides rich material for comparison with divinatory and calendrical systems worldwide
- **Theological**: Embodies a sophisticated theology of time, cosmic forces, and divine-human interaction
- **Archaeological**: Correlated with archaeological evidence from central Mexican ceremonial centers
- **Interfaith**: Offers a non-Western sacred calendar tradition of extraordinary complexity and beauty
