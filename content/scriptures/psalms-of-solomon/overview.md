## Overview

The Psalms of Solomon is a collection of eighteen psalms composed in the first century BCE, most likely in response to the Roman general Pompey's conquest of Jerusalem in 63 BCE. Written in the voice of Solomon but reflecting Pharisaic or proto-Pharisaic theology, these psalms address the crisis of foreign domination, the corruption of the Hasmonean priesthood, and the hope for a righteous Davidic Messiah who will purify Jerusalem and establish God's kingdom.

Preserved in Greek and Syriac, the Psalms of Solomon were originally composed in Hebrew. Though never included in any biblical canon, they circulated widely in early Christianity and were listed in some early canonical catalogs. The psalms provide an invaluable window into the theological convictions of pious Jews who opposed both Hasmonean corruption and Roman oppression, trusting in God's covenantal faithfulness to restore righteous rule.

The messianic expectations expressed in Psalms of Solomon 17-18 are among the most detailed pre-Christian descriptions of the expected Davidic Messiah, making them essential for understanding the political and theological context of early Christian claims about Jesus. The collection also reflects sophisticated reflection on the distinction between the righteous and the sinners, divine discipline versus divine punishment, and the nature of God's covenant loyalty.

## Structure

Psalms of Solomon is structured as follows:

1. **Psalms 1-2**: Response to Pompey's conquest: Jerusalem's defilement and the invader's fate
2. **Psalms 3-6**: Reflections on the righteous and the wicked: divine discipline and judgment
3. **Psalms 7-11**: National lament and hope: exile, return, and covenant faithfulness
4. **Psalms 12-16**: Individual piety: tongue, temptation, and trust in divine mercy
5. **Psalms 17-18**: The Messianic Psalms: the Son of David and the new age

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: Crucial for reconstructing first-century BCE Jewish messianism, Pharisaic theology, and reactions to Pompey's conquest
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels with royal psalms, messianic expectations across traditions, and the theology of righteous suffering
- **Protestant**: Essential background for understanding NT messianic claims and Pharisaic theology encountered in the Gospels
- **Catholic**: Valued for illuminating pre-Christian messianic expectation and the development of Jewish prayer tradition
- **Eastern Orthodox**: A witness to the rich hymnographic tradition of Second Temple Judaism that influenced Orthodox liturgical theology
- **Interfaith**: Provides common ground for exploring messianic hope, righteous governance, and divine justice across traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Davidic Messianism | The hope for a righteous king from David's line who will purge Jerusalem and rule with divine wisdom |
| Pompey's Invasion | The trauma of Roman conquest interpreted as divine judgment on the Hasmonean rulers' sins |
| Righteous vs. Sinners | A sharp distinction between the pious who accept divine discipline and the wicked who face judgment |
| Divine Discipline | God's correction of the righteous through suffering, distinguished from the punishment of the ungodly |
| Covenant Faithfulness | God's enduring loyalty to Israel despite the nation's failures and foreign domination |
| Anti-Hasmonean Polemic | Criticism of the Hasmonean priest-kings who usurped the Davidic throne and defiled the sanctuary |
