## The Lord as Warrior Against His Own People

Protestant reading of Lamentations 2 confronts one of the most disturbing portraits in Scripture: God as the active destroyer of his own people. The Lord has become "like an enemy" (2:5), tearing down the walls, destroying the Temple, and abolishing the appointed feasts. Protestant theology does not soften this portrait but reads it as a revelation of God's holiness and the seriousness of sin.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The Lord has become like an enemy" (2:5)

Reformed commentators stress that this language does not mean God has ceased to be Israel's God — but that his covenant faithfulness demands judgment on covenant-breaking. God's wrath is not capricious but righteous.

### "He has laid waste his booth like a garden" (2:6)

The destruction of the Temple is the climax of judgment. Protestant interpreters see here a foreshadowing of Jesus' words: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" (John 2:19). The old sanctuary must fall so that the true temple — Christ's body — can be raised.

### The Failure of the Prophets (2:14)

"Your prophets have seen for you false and deceptive visions." Protestant interpreters, shaped by *sola scriptura*, read this as a warning against any teaching that does not conform to God's revealed Word.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine Holiness**: God's destruction of Jerusalem reveals the absolute seriousness of sin
- **The End of the Old Order**: Temple, priesthood, and festival are abolished — pointing forward to Christ
- **False Prophecy**: The failure of Israel's prophets underscores the need for the true Word of God
- **The Suffering of the Innocent**: Children and infants suffer in the siege — a theme that challenges easy theodicy
