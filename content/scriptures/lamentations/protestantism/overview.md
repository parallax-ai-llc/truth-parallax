## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Lamentations reads the book as a searing witness to the consequences of covenant unfaithfulness and the sovereignty of God in judgment. The Reformers valued Lamentations for its unflinching honesty before God — its willingness to name sin, accept divine discipline, and yet cling to the hope that God's mercies are "new every morning" (3:23). The book exemplifies what Protestant theology calls the proper response to divine judgment: repentance, confession, and trust in grace alone.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Covenant Theology

Reformed Protestants read Lamentations within the framework of covenant: Israel broke the covenant, and the curses of Deuteronomy 28 have been fulfilled. The destruction is not arbitrary but covenantal — God is faithful even in judgment.

### Christological Reading

Protestant commentators connect the suffering of Jerusalem to the suffering of Christ. Just as Jerusalem bore God's wrath for the sins of the people, so Christ bore the wrath of God on behalf of sinners. Lamentations 1:12 — "Is any sorrow like my sorrow?" — has been read devotionally as a voice of Christ on the cross.

### Pastoral-Devotional

Evangelical and pastoral interpreters value Lamentations as a biblical model for lament — giving permission to grieve, question, and cry out to God while remaining within the framework of faith.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Sovereignty in Judgment**: God is the active agent of Jerusalem's destruction, not merely a passive observer
- **Sin and Consequence**: The poet acknowledges that Jerusalem's suffering is the result of her rebellion
- **Hope in Grace**: Lamentations 3:22-23 is among the most beloved verses in Protestant devotion
- **The Sufficiency of Scripture**: The raw honesty of the text validates lament as a legitimate mode of faith

## Notable Protestant Commentators

- **John Calvin**: Preached on Lamentations, emphasizing God's justice and the call to repentance
- **Matthew Henry**: Drew moral and devotional lessons from every chapter
- **Walter Kaiser**: Provided evangelical exegesis connecting Lamentations to covenant theology
- **Kathleen O'Connor**: Offered a theological reading emphasizing trauma and survival
