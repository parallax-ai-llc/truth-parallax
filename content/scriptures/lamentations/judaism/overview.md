## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Lamentations (*Eikhah*) holds a central and solemn place as one of the Five Megillot. It is read on the ninth of Av (Tisha B'Av), the fast day commemorating the destruction of both the First Temple (586 BCE) and the Second Temple (70 CE), as well as other catastrophes in Jewish history. The opening word *Eikhah* ("How!") gives the book its Hebrew name and sets the tone: an anguished cry over the destruction (*churban*) of Jerusalem. Jewish interpretation engages with the text through halakhah, midrash, liturgy, and theology, asking the hardest questions about divine justice and covenant faithfulness.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Midrashic

Midrash Eikhah Rabbah extensively expands the text, providing homiletical interpretations of every verse. The midrash explores the sins that led to the destruction, the grief of God himself over the Temple's ruin, and the ultimate hope for restoration.

### Liturgical

Lamentations is not merely read but chanted in a distinctive mournful melody (*trop*) on the eve of Tisha B'Av, with congregants seated on the floor or on low stools, in a darkened synagogue. The liturgical context transforms the text into a communal act of mourning.

### Kabbalistic

Kabbalistic readings see the destruction of the Temple as a rupture in the divine realm — the Shekhinah (divine presence) going into exile with Israel. The restoration of the Temple is understood as a cosmic repair (*tikkun*).

## Theological Emphases

- **Churban**: The destruction as a defining event in Jewish history and theology
- **Theodicy**: The agonizing question of why God permitted — or caused — the destruction
- **Teshuvah**: The call to repentance as the path toward restoration
- **The Suffering of God**: Midrashic traditions that depict God himself weeping over the Temple
- **Communal Memory**: Lamentations ensures that the catastrophe is never forgotten

## Notable Jewish Commentators

- **Rashi**: Provided peshat and midrashic commentary on every verse
- **Ibn Ezra**: Offered grammatical and contextual analysis
- **The Zohar**: Read the destruction as a kabbalistic event affecting the sefirot
- **Rabbi Joseph B. Soloveitchik**: Explored the theology of mourning and *eikhah* as existential question
