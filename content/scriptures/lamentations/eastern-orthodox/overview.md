## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Lamentations is shaped by the patristic tradition, liturgical practice, and the theology of *kenosis* — God's self-emptying love that enters into the depths of human suffering. The book is encountered primarily within the services of Holy Week, where the destruction of Jerusalem becomes a lens through which to contemplate the passion and death of Christ. Orthodox theology does not shy away from the darkness of Lamentations but sees in it a necessary passage through death to resurrection.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Typology

The Greek and Syriac Fathers read the destruction of Jerusalem as a type of the cosmic consequences of sin — and ultimately of the death of Christ, through which the old creation is destroyed so that the new creation may arise. Jerusalem's suffering prefigures the suffering of Christ, who takes upon himself the grief of the whole world.

### Liturgical Theology

In Orthodox worship, Lamentations is experienced through hymnography and the Holy Week services. The *Epitaphios* — the liturgical burial of Christ on Great Friday — echoes the themes of Lamentations: the city mourns, the Temple is destroyed, and yet the faithful watch for the dawning of Pascha.

### The Theology of Holy Tears

Orthodox spiritual tradition values the "gift of tears" (*penthos*) as a sign of genuine repentance and spiritual sensitivity. Lamentations is a biblical foundation for this tradition — tears as prayer, grief as a path to God.

## Theological Emphases

- **Kenosis**: God empties himself into human suffering; Christ's passion is the ultimate lament
- **Theosis through Suffering**: The path to deification passes through the cross; Lamentations maps this passage
- **The Paschal Mystery**: The darkness of Lamentations is read in light of the Resurrection — death is not the final word
- **Penthos**: The gift of tears as a spiritual discipline rooted in biblical precedent

## Notable Orthodox Commentators

- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Composed poetic meditations on suffering and divine mercy
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Referenced Jerusalem's fall in homilies on repentance
- **Theodoret of Cyrrhus**: Provided patristic commentary on the prophetic books including Lamentations
