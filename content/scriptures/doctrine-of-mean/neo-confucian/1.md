Neo-Confucian reading of the Doctrine of the Mean treats it as the deepest philosophical text in the Confucian canon. Zhu Xi's commentary on the opening passage identifies the Heaven-bestowed nature with heavenly principle (tianli), establishing the foundational Neo-Confucian equation of nature and principle (xing ji li). The Way, as accordance with this nature, is the natural expression of principle in human conduct, and instruction is the deliberate effort to restore this expression when it has been disrupted by selfish desire.

The distinction between the state before the emotions are aroused (wei fa) and after arousal (yi fa) became one of the most intensely debated topics in Neo-Confucian philosophy. The Cheng brothers and Zhu Xi understood this as the relationship between substance (ti) and function (yong)—the original nature as principle constitutes the substance, while the expressed emotions constitute its function. The cultivation of equilibrium before arousal requires the practice of moral seriousness (jing), maintaining reverent attention to the original nature even when the mind is not actively engaged with external affairs. Sincerity (cheng) is identified with principle itself—the real, the true, the unfailing—and the text's teaching that sincerity enables the sage to participate in the work of Heaven and Earth is read as describing the full realization of heavenly principle in human life.
