Chinese Buddhist reading of the Doctrine of the Mean discovers deep structural parallels between the Confucian concept of the mean (zhong) and the Buddhist middle way (madhyama pratipad). Both traditions teach that the path to wisdom lies in avoiding extremes and maintaining a centered, balanced awareness. The Zhongyong's teaching on the state of equilibrium before the emotions are aroused (wei fa) parallels the Chan Buddhist emphasis on the original mind prior to the arising of discriminative thought—the "mind before thinking" that is the foundation of all Chan practice.

The text's concept of sincerity (cheng) invites comparison with the Buddhist cultivation of samadhi (meditative concentration) and the clear awareness that characterizes the awakened mind. Chinese Buddhist interpreters such as Zongmi argued that the Confucian concept of sincerity and the Buddhist concept of awakening both point to the same fundamental quality of the mind—its inherent clarity, authenticity, and self-transparency. The Zhongyong's vision of the sage who participates in the transformative work of Heaven and Earth resonates with the Mahayana bodhisattva ideal, in which the awakened being works ceaselessly for the transformation and liberation of all sentient beings, fully integrated with the dynamic processes of reality.
