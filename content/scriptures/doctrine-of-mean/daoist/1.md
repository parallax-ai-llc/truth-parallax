Daoist reading of the Doctrine of the Mean finds this text more congenial than most Confucian works, owing to its emphasis on naturalness, harmony, and the cosmic grounding of human nature. The opening statement that human nature is mandated by Heaven resonates with the Daoist conviction that authentic human life flows from the natural Dao rather than from social construction. The concept of the Way as "accordance with the nature" echoes the Daodejing's teaching that the sage follows the natural (ziran) rather than imposing artificial norms.

The text's description of sincerity (cheng) as effortless moral perfection—the sage who "hits the mark without thinking and embodies the Way without effort"—closely parallels the Daoist ideal of wu-wei (non-deliberate action). Daoist readers recognize in this description the same state of spontaneous rightness that the Zhuangzi attributes to the skilled craftsman, the butcher Cook Ding, and the genuine person (zhen ren). However, the Confucian text embeds this spontaneity within a program of deliberate moral cultivation and institutional governance that reintroduces the very effortfulness that sincerity was supposed to transcend. A Daoist reading would suggest that genuine sincerity cannot be the product of a cultivation program—it arises only when one abandons the desire to cultivate altogether and returns to the natural simplicity of the unconditioned mind.
