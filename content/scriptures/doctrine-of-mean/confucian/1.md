Confucian interpretation of the Doctrine of the Mean reads the text as the most profound expression of the relationship between human moral effort and the cosmic order. The opening declaration—"What Heaven mandates is called the nature; accordance with the nature is called the Way; cultivating the Way is called instruction"—establishes that moral cultivation is not an arbitrary human project but the fulfillment of a nature rooted in the structure of reality. Confucian readers take this as the ultimate grounding of ethical life: morality is not mere convention but participation in the Way of Heaven.

The text's teaching on centrality (zhong) and harmony (he) describes two dimensions of moral life. Before the emotions are aroused, the mind exists in a state of perfect equilibrium—this is the "great root of the world." When the emotions are aroused and each attains its due measure, this is harmony—the "universal Way of the world." Confucian interpreters emphasize that both states require cultivation: equilibrium through inner composure and self-watchfulness, harmony through the measured expression of feeling in accordance with ritual propriety. The concept of sincerity (cheng) unifies these dimensions, describing the person whose moral integrity is so complete that virtue flows naturally and effortlessly, enabling the sage to "form a triad with Heaven and Earth" and participate in the cosmic work of transformation and nourishment.
