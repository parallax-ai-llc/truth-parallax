Academic analysis of the Doctrine of the Mean addresses questions of composition, authorship, and philosophical structure. The traditional attribution to Zisi, Confucius's grandson, has been partially supported by the discovery of the Guodian bamboo manuscripts (ca. 300 BCE), which include texts with strong thematic and linguistic affinities to the Zhongyong. Mark Csikszentmihalyi and others have argued that these manuscripts confirm the existence of a "Zisi school" that developed the metaphysical dimensions of Confucian thought before Mencius.

The text's philosophical structure has been analyzed by scholars including Tu Weiming, whose influential study *Centrality and Commonality* argues that the Zhongyong presents a distinctive "anthropocosmic" vision in which the human being serves as the mediating link between Heaven and Earth. Andrew Plaks examines the text's use of correlative cosmology and its relationship to other Han-era metaphysical systems. Scholarly debate continues over whether the text represents a unified philosophical argument or a compilation of disparate materials. The concept of sincerity (cheng) has received particular attention from scholars such as Kidder Smith, who traces its evolution from a simple ethical notion of honesty to a full-fledged metaphysical concept bridging the gap between human effort and cosmic process.
