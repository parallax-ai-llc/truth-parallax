Comparative reading of the Doctrine of the Mean places its concept of the mean alongside Aristotle's doctrine of the golden mean and the Buddhist middle way. Aristotle teaches that virtue lies in the mean between excess and deficiency—courage between cowardice and rashness, generosity between miserliness and prodigality. The Zhongyong's concept of centrality (zhong) similarly identifies the optimal moral state as a balanced center, though it adds a cosmological dimension absent from Aristotle: the state of equilibrium is the "great root of the world," not merely a personal virtue but a cosmic principle.

The text's concept of sincerity (cheng) as both a human virtue and a cosmic quality invites comparison with the Platonic concept of truth as participation in the really real, the Christian understanding of integrity as alignment with divine truth, and the Hindu concept of satya (truth/being) as the fundamental nature of reality. The Zhongyong's vision of the sage who "forms a triad with Heaven and Earth" resonates with the Christian concept of theosis (divinization), the Hindu concept of the jivanmukta (one liberated while living), and the Sufi concept of the perfect human being (al-insan al-kamil)—all describing a state in which the fully realized person participates in the creative and sustaining activity of ultimate reality.
