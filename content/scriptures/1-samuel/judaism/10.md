## The First Mashiach

Jewish interpretation of 1 Samuel 10 reads Saul's anointing as the foundational event of Israelite kingship and the origin of the *mashiach* concept. The anointing with oil establishes the sacred character of the king and inaugurates the messianic lineage that will pass to David.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Anointing (10:1)

Rashi notes that Samuel anoints Saul with oil from a *pakh* (flask), not a *keren* (horn). The Talmud (Megillah 14a) observes that kings anointed from a flask have temporary dynasties, while those anointed from a horn have enduring ones — David will be anointed from a horn.

### "God gave him another heart" (10:9)

Radak reads this as a temporary endowment for the purpose of kingship — not a permanent spiritual transformation. The rabbis debate whether Saul's change of heart involved genuine spiritual growth or merely the external capacities needed for governance.

### Saul Among the Prophets (10:10-13)

The proverb "Is Saul also among the prophets?" becomes a way of expressing surprise at unexpected behavior. The Talmud uses it to illustrate the principle that God can use anyone for prophetic purposes, regardless of their background.

### Hiding Among the Baggage (10:21-23)

The Midrash reads Saul's hiding as a sign of genuine humility — a quality the rabbis value highly but which Saul will later lose. Alternatively, some commentators see it as a sign of the ambivalence that will characterize his reign.

## Theological Themes

- **The Mashiach Concept**: Anointing as the foundation of messianic theology
- **Flask vs. Horn**: The sign of temporary vs. enduring kingship
- **The Spirit's Versatility**: God can bestow prophetic gifts on anyone
- **Humility and Kingship**: The tension between royal authority and personal modesty

## Reflection

Jewish messianic theology begins here — with the anointing of an imperfect king whose dynasty will not endure. Yet the concept of the *mashiach* (anointed one) established in this chapter will evolve through David, through the prophets, into the rabbinic hope for the ultimate Messiah who will restore Israel and bring justice to the world.
