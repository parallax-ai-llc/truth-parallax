## The Deep Sleep of God

Jewish interpretation of 1 Samuel 26 notes the divine origin of the "deep sleep" (*tardemah*) that falls on Saul's camp — the same word used for Adam's sleep in Genesis 2:21. God orchestrates the situation to test David's character a second time.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Deep Sleep (26:12)

Rashi connects the *tardemah* to divine intervention: this is not ordinary sleep but a God-sent condition that prevents the guards from waking. The Talmud notes the parallel to Adam's *tardemah* in Genesis — in both cases, God acts while humans sleep, creating something new.

### David's Second Refusal (26:9-11)

Radak emphasizes that David's reasoning has deepened: he now explicitly articulates three possibilities for Saul's end — God will strike him, his day will come naturally, or he will die in battle. In no scenario does David take matters into his own hands. This represents mature theological reasoning.

## Theological Themes

- **Tardemah**: The divine sleep that enables God to work without human interference
- **Maturation of Faith**: David's theological reasoning deepens with each test
- **The Threefold Trust**: God will act in his own way and time
- **The Merit of Patience**: Sustained restraint as evidence of genuine righteousness

## Reflection

Jewish tradition reads the double test at En-gedi and in Saul's camp as evidence that David's mercy is not accidental but essential — rooted in his understanding of God's sovereignty and the sanctity of the anointed. The deep sleep (*tardemah*) is God's signature: just as he put Adam to sleep to create Eve, he puts Saul's army to sleep to reveal David's heart.
