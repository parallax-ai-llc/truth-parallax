## The End of Saul's Dynasty

Jewish interpretation of 1 Samuel 15 treats Samuel's oracle — "To obey is better than sacrifice" — as a foundational principle of Jewish ethics. The chapter also raises the difficult question of the *herem* (devotion to destruction) and Saul's failure to execute it fully.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Command Against Amalek (15:1-3)

The commandment to destroy Amalek (Deuteronomy 25:17-19) is one of the most discussed in rabbinic literature. Rambam includes it among the 613 commandments. The rabbis debated whether this commandment applies only to the historical Amalek or to all who embody Amalekite hatred of Israel.

### "To obey is better than sacrifice" (15:22)

Rashi reads this as the definitive statement on the relationship between ritual and obedience. The sacrificial system, central as it is, is subordinate to faithfulness to God's commands. Malbim draws a distinction between *shome'a* (obedience — listening to God's specific command) and *zevach* (sacrifice — a generalized ritual act).

### Saul's Repentance (15:24-30)

The rabbis compare Saul's repentance unfavorably with David's later repentance. Saul says "I have sinned" but immediately adds a request to be honored before the people (15:30). The Talmud reads this as evidence that Saul's repentance was incomplete — concerned with public image rather than genuine return to God.

## Theological Themes

- **Obedience over Sacrifice**: The hierarchy of values in Torah observance
- **The Amalek Problem**: The ongoing challenge of a commandment that mandates destruction
- **Incomplete Repentance**: The difference between genuine and performative *teshuvah*
- **Prophetic Authority**: Samuel's role as the arbiter of divine will

## Reflection

Jewish tradition reads this chapter as a study in the anatomy of incomplete repentance. Saul confesses his sin but cannot relinquish his concern for public honor. The rabbis teach that genuine *teshuvah* requires complete honesty — not the managed confession of a politician but the broken heart of a penitent.
