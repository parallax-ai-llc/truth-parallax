## Overview

In Jewish tradition, 1 Samuel (*Shmuel Aleph*) is part of the Nevi'im Rishonim (Former Prophets) and narrates the critical transition from the period of the judges to the monarchy. The book raises fundamental questions about the nature of political authority under God's sovereignty, the role of the prophet, and the meaning of divine election. Samuel is revered as a prophet equal to Moses and Aaron (Psalm 99:6), and the anointing of David establishes the Davidic line from which the Messiah is expected to come.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Midrashic

The Midrash and Talmud expand the narrative with rich homiletical and legal interpretations. Hannah's prayer is analyzed for laws of silent prayer (Berakhot 31a–b). David's character is explored in detail, with the rabbis defending his righteousness while acknowledging his complexity.

### Halakhic

Jewish legal commentators derive important halakhic principles from 1 Samuel: the laws of monarchy (Deuteronomy 17 applied through Saul's reign), the authority of the prophet, and the precedence of obedience over sacrifice.

### Kabbalistic and Mystical

Kabbalistic readings see the transition from Saul to David as reflecting the movement from the sefirah of *Gevurah* (strict judgment) to *Malkhut* (sovereignty in its rectified form). David is associated with the Shekhinah and the restoration of divine presence.

## Theological Emphases

- **Prophecy and Kingship**: The tension between prophetic authority and royal power is a central theme
- **The Anointed One**: The concept of *mashiach* — anointed king — originates here and develops into messianic theology
- **Teshuvah**: Saul's incomplete repentance versus David's future wholehearted teshuvah
- **Divine Election**: God's choice of the unlikely — the barren woman, the youngest son — as a pattern of redemption
- **Prayer**: Hannah's prayer as the model for the Amidah and personal devotion

## Notable Jewish Commentators

- **Rashi**: Provided concise commentary integrating peshat and midrash
- **Radak (David Kimchi)**: Offered detailed grammatical and contextual analysis
- **Abarbanel**: Examined the political theology of monarchy in depth
- **Malbim**: Distinguished between synonymous terms to reveal deeper meaning
