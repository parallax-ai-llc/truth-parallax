## The Crime of Doeg

Jewish interpretation of 1 Samuel 22 reads the massacre at Nob as one of the most heinous crimes in biblical history. The Talmud devotes extensive discussion to Doeg as the paradigm of the evil tongue — the scholar whose *lashon hara* (evil speech) leads to mass murder.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### David's Band (22:1-2)

The Midrash reads David's gathering of the distressed and indebted as a sign of his future kingship: the true king is the one who gathers the outcasts and gives them purpose. Rashi notes that David transformed these desperate men into a disciplined force — a model of redemptive leadership.

### Doeg's Slander (22:9-10)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 106b) devotes extended discussion to Doeg's sin. His report to Saul is technically accurate — Ahimelech did give David bread and a sword — but its intent is murderous. The rabbis derive from Doeg the principle that *lashon hara* (evil speech) can kill three: the speaker, the listener, and the subject.

### The Massacre (22:18-19)

The killing of eighty-five priests is read as the destruction of a "small Sanhedrin" — an act that strikes at the heart of Torah learning and religious life. The rabbis regard this as one of the worst crimes recorded in Scripture.

## Theological Themes

- **Lashon Hara**: The devastating power of evil speech — truth weaponized for destructive purposes
- **The Destruction of Torah**: The killing of priests as an assault on learning and religious life
- **David's Responsibility**: David's deception in chapter 21 as a contributing cause
- **Redemptive Leadership**: Gathering the broken and giving them purpose

## Reflection

Jewish ethical tradition places the sin of *lashon hara* among the most serious transgressions — and Doeg is its paradigmatic perpetrator. His speech was technically true, which makes it more dangerous, not less. The massacre at Nob demonstrates that truth weaponized by malice is more destructive than any lie.
