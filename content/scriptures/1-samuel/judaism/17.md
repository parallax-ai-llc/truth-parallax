## The Sanctification of God's Name

Jewish interpretation of 1 Samuel 17 reads David's victory over Goliath as a supreme act of *kiddush Hashem* — the sanctification of God's name before the nations. David fights not for personal glory but for the honor of the God of Israel.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he should defy the armies of the living God?" (17:26)

Radak notes that David's outrage is theological, not military. The issue is not Israel's honor but God's honor. Goliath's defiance constitutes a *chilul Hashem* (desecration of God's name), and David's response is an act of *kiddush Hashem* (sanctification of God's name).

### The Five Stones (17:40)

The Midrash offers various interpretations of the five stones. One tradition reads them as representing the merits of five righteous figures: Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, and Aaron. Another reads them as the five books of Torah — the word of God as David's true weapon.

### "The LORD saves not with sword and spear" (17:47)

Rashi comments that David's declaration echoes the principle throughout Jewish tradition: military victory comes from God, not from human technology. The Psalms attributed to David consistently make this point: "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD our God" (Psalm 20:8).

## Theological Themes

- **Kiddush Hashem**: Sanctifying God's name through courageous faithfulness
- **Trust in God**: Military victory as divine gift, not human achievement
- **The Power of Torah**: The five stones as symbols of the five books of Moses
- **The Shepherd King**: David's pastoral experience as preparation for kingship

## Reflection

Jewish tradition reads the David and Goliath narrative as the ultimate *kiddush Hashem*: a young man, armed only with faith and the name of God, defends the honor of the Almighty before the nations. Every Jew who sanctifies God's name in the face of danger — from the Maccabees to the martyrs of the Shoah — walks in David's footsteps.
