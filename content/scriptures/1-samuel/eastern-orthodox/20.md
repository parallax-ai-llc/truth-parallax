## The Sacrifice of Love

Orthodox interpretation of 1 Samuel 20 reads the farewell of David and Jonathan as a supreme expression of *kenotic* love — the self-emptying that mirrors Christ's own self-giving. Jonathan sacrifices his royal future for the sake of God's purposes and his friend's life.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Jonathan's Kenosis (20:30-34)

Jonathan endures his father's rage and even a spear-throw rather than betray David. Orthodox theology sees in this an act of kenotic love — the willingness to suffer rather than violate the bond of love. This mirrors the kenosis of Christ described in Philippians 2:5-8.

### "The LORD shall be between me and you forever" (20:42)

The covenant grounded in God anticipates the Orthodox understanding of every sacred bond: marriage, friendship, and community are sustained not by human effort alone but by the divine presence that indwells them.

## Theological Themes

- **Kenotic Love**: Self-emptying love that gives without limit
- **Covenant in God**: Human bonds sustained by divine presence
- **The Cost of Faithfulness**: Loyalty to God's purposes may require the sacrifice of one's own ambitions
- **Eschatological Hope**: The covenant extends "forever" — beyond death itself

## Reflection

Orthodox theology sees in Jonathan's farewell one of the most beautiful acts of love in the Old Testament. He gives up everything — his father's approval, his claim to the throne, his very safety — for the sake of love. This is the kenosis that the Church celebrates in Christ and calls every believer to imitate.
