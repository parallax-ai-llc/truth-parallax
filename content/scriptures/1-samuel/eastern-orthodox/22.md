## The Blood of the Innocent

Orthodox interpretation of 1 Samuel 22 reads the massacre of the priests of Nob as an act of sacrilegious persecution — the slaughter of God's consecrated ministers by a tyrant consumed by paranoia. The Orthodox Church, which has endured centuries of persecution, reads this chapter with the memory of its own martyrs.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Massacre of the Priests (22:18-19)

Eighty-five priests die because they offered bread to God's anointed. Orthodox martyrology records countless parallel instances: clergy who suffered for offering sanctuary, administering sacraments, or simply being faithful to their calling.

### David Gathers the Outcasts (22:1-2)

David's community of the distressed and indebted anticipates the monastic communities that formed in the deserts of Egypt and Syria — gatherings of those who fled the world's corruption to find God in the wilderness.

## Theological Themes

- **Martyrdom**: The priests of Nob as proto-martyrs of the faith
- **Monastic Anticipation**: David's wilderness community as a type of desert monasticism
- **Tyranny and the Church**: The perennial conflict between worldly power and sacred ministry
- **The Protection of the Innocent**: The moral obligation to provide sanctuary

## Reflection

The Orthodox Church, which has known the persecution of Ottoman rule, Soviet atheism, and countless other trials, reads this chapter as a mirror of its own experience. The blood of the priests of Nob mingles with the blood of every martyr who died for faithfulness to Christ and his anointed.
