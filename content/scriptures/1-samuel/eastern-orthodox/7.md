## Repentance, Sacrifice, and Deliverance

Orthodox interpretation of 1 Samuel 7 reads Samuel's ministry at Mizpah as a liturgical event: repentance, sacrifice, intercessory prayer, and divine deliverance form a pattern that the Church's worship continues to enact. The Ebenezer stone becomes a type of the Church's commemorative traditions.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Call to Repentance (7:3-4)

Samuel demands the removal of foreign gods before approaching God. Orthodox Lenten practice follows this pattern: the Great Fast is a period of stripping away attachments before the encounter with the Risen Christ at Pascha.

### Samuel's Sacrifice and Intercession (7:5-11)

Samuel's offering of a lamb and his intercessory cry foreshadow the Eucharistic liturgy: the priest offers the Lamb and intercedes for the people. Orthodox theology sees in every Divine Liturgy a continuation of this pattern.

### Ebenezer (7:12)

The memorial stone finds its Orthodox parallel in the Church's calendar of feasts, the veneration of holy places, and the construction of churches on sites of divine intervention. Memory is a liturgical act.

## Theological Themes

- **Liturgical Pattern**: Repentance, sacrifice, intercession, and deliverance as the structure of worship
- **Lenten Preparation**: The removal of idols as preparation for divine encounter
- **Eucharistic Foreshadowing**: Samuel's sacrifice as a type of the Divine Liturgy
- **Sacred Memory**: The Ebenezer stone as a precedent for the Church's commemorative tradition

## Reflection

Orthodox theology reads this chapter as a liturgical blueprint: the people confess, the priest offers, God delivers, and the community remembers. This is the pattern of every Divine Liturgy, every feast day, every act of worship that the Church performs. The Ebenezer stone stands where the altar will stand.
