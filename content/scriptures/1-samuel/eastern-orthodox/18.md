## The Bond of Souls

Orthodox interpretation of 1 Samuel 18 reads the friendship of David and Jonathan as a model of *agape* — the self-sacrificing love that the Church celebrates in the lives of paired saints. The contrast with Saul's jealousy illustrates the Orthodox distinction between the passions and the virtues.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The soul of Jonathan was knit to the soul of David" (18:1-4)

Orthodox hagiography knows many paired saints — Sts. Sergius and Bacchus, Sts. Cosmas and Damian — whose bonds of friendship are celebrated as expressions of divine love. Jonathan's love for David exemplifies *agape*: love that gives without counting the cost.

### Saul's Jealousy (18:6-9)

Orthodox ascetic theology identifies jealousy as one of the passions — disordered movements of the soul that must be healed through *praxis* (ascetic discipline) and the grace of the Holy Spirit. Saul's jealousy is a textbook case of a passion unchecked, destroying its host.

## Theological Themes

- **Agape**: Self-sacrificing love as the highest virtue
- **The Passions**: Jealousy as a disordered passion that must be healed through asceticism
- **Kenotic Friendship**: Jonathan's self-emptying love as a model of kenosis
- **The Transfer of Grace**: Jonathan recognizes God's anointing on David and yields to it

## Reflection

Orthodox theology sees in Jonathan and Saul two responses to the other's success: Jonathan responds with love, giving away his own robe and weapons; Saul responds with envy, hurling his spear. The spiritual life consists largely in cultivating the first response and healing the second through prayer, fasting, and repentance.
