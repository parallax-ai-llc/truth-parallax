## Seeking God's Direction

1 Samuel 23 presents David as a leader who consistently seeks divine guidance before acting. Through the ephod carried by Abiathar, David inquires of the LORD before rescuing Keilah and before every major decision. Protestant interpretation highlights this as the model of Spirit-led decision-making.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### David Inquires of the LORD (23:2, 4, 10-12)

David's repeated consultation of God through the priestly ephod stands in sharp contrast to Saul's increasingly erratic decision-making. Protestant theology values this pattern: genuine leadership begins with seeking God's will, not asserting one's own.

### Keilah's Potential Betrayal (23:12)

God reveals that the people of Keilah, whom David just rescued, would hand him over to Saul. Protestant commentators note the painful realism: gratitude does not guarantee loyalty. David must move on, trusting God rather than human reciprocity.

### Jonathan's Visit in the Wilderness (23:15-18)

Jonathan finds David at Horesh and "strengthened his hand in God" (23:16). This final meeting between the two friends is deeply moving. Jonathan affirms David's destiny: "You shall be king over Israel, and I shall be next to you." Protestant preaching treasures this scene as the ministry of encouragement at its finest.

## Theological Themes

- **Seeking God's Will**: David models dependence on divine guidance in every decision
- **The Unreliability of Human Gratitude**: Even those we serve may betray us
- **Encouragement in the Wilderness**: Jonathan's ministry to David as a model of spiritual friendship
- **God's Faithfulness in Flight**: Even in constant danger, God directs and protects David

## Reflection

Protestant reading of this chapter holds up David's prayer-dependent leadership as the model for all believers. In the chaos of flight and danger, David's instinct is not to plan but to pray — not to calculate but to consult. This is the heart that God chose.
