## A Song of Reversal

Hannah's song in 1 Samuel 2:1-10 is one of the great theological poems of the Old Testament. Protestant interpreters recognize it as a precursor to Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55), celebrating God's pattern of reversing human expectations: lifting the humble and bringing down the proud.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Hannah's Song (2:1-10)

The song moves from personal thanksgiving to cosmic theology. Its themes of reversal — the barren bearing seven, the mighty broken, the poor raised from the dust — express the core of Protestant soteriology: salvation belongs to the Lord, not to human strength or merit.

### The Corruption of Eli's Sons (2:12-17)

Hophni and Phinehas represent institutional religion at its worst: exploiting their office for personal gain, treating God's offerings with contempt. Protestant commentators see here a template for the critique of clerical corruption that fueled the Reformation.

### The Man of God's Warning (2:27-36)

The prophetic oracle against Eli's house establishes a key Protestant principle: no priestly lineage is exempt from judgment. God's faithfulness is to his covenant, not to human dynasties.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine Reversal**: God exalts the humble and humbles the exalted — the gospel in miniature
- **Judgment on Corrupt Leadership**: Religious authority that serves itself rather than God faces divine reckoning
- **The Sovereignty of God**: "The LORD kills and brings to life" (2:6) — all events are under God's sovereign hand
- **Messianic Foreshadowing**: "He will give strength to his king and exalt the horn of his anointed" (2:10) — the first reference to the anointed king

## Reflection

Hannah's song is a theological manifesto: the God of Israel is not a tribal deity but the Lord of history who overturns every human calculation. For Protestant theology, this is the logic of grace itself — God works not through the powerful but through the broken, not through merit but through mercy.
