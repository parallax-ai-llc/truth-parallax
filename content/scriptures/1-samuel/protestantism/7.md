## Ebenezer — The Stone of Help

1 Samuel 7 presents Samuel at the height of his authority as judge and intercessor. After twenty years of Philistine oppression, Samuel calls Israel to repentance at Mizpah, and God responds with a thunderous deliverance. The chapter is a model of national revival in Protestant preaching.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Samuel's Call to Repentance (7:3-4)

Samuel demands that Israel put away foreign gods and serve the LORD only. Protestant interpretation emphasizes that genuine repentance requires action — not merely sorrow but the removal of idols. This pattern of repentance and reformation resonates with the Reformation's own call to purify the church.

### The Sacrifice and Intercession at Mizpah (7:5-11)

Samuel offers a suckling lamb as a burnt offering and cries out to the LORD on Israel's behalf. God responds with thunder that routs the Philistines. Protestant theology highlights the centrality of intercessory prayer and the sufficiency of God's power over military strategy.

### Ebenezer — "Thus far the LORD has helped us" (7:12)

Samuel's memorial stone is one of the most beloved images in Protestant hymnody and devotion. The hymn "Come, Thou Fount of Every Blessing" echoes this verse: "Here I raise mine Ebenezer." The stone marks not human achievement but divine faithfulness.

## Theological Themes

- **Repentance and Revival**: National turning from idolatry as the precondition for deliverance
- **Intercessory Prayer**: Samuel as mediator between God and the people
- **Divine Deliverance**: God fights for Israel — salvation is the LORD's work
- **Memorial and Gratitude**: Ebenezer as a reminder of God's faithfulness through history

## Reflection

Protestant preaching has drawn from this chapter a template for spiritual renewal: honest confession, radical removal of idols, fervent prayer, and grateful remembrance of God's past faithfulness. The Ebenezer stone stands as a permanent testimony that God helps those who return to him.
