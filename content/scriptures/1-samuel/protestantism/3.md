## The Lord Calls Samuel

1 Samuel 3 recounts the dramatic nighttime calling of the boy Samuel — one of the most beloved narratives in Protestant preaching. The story of a child learning to hear God's voice has been central to Protestant teaching on vocation, discernment, and the authority of God's word.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The word of the LORD was rare in those days" (3:1)

This verse frames the entire chapter. Protestant commentators read it as a description of spiritual famine — when God's word is suppressed or ignored, the people perish (Proverbs 29:18). The Reformation itself was understood as a restoration of the word after centuries of scarcity.

### "Speak, LORD, for your servant is listening" (3:9-10)

Samuel's response, coached by Eli, becomes the paradigmatic posture of the believer before God's word. Protestant spirituality emphasizes receptivity to Scripture as the primary means of hearing God.

### The Judgment on Eli's House (3:11-14)

God's devastating message to Samuel — that Eli's house will be judged irrevocably — tests the young prophet's obedience. Samuel must speak truth to power, even when the truth is painful. Protestant preaching values this prophetic courage.

## Theological Themes

- **Vocation**: God calls individuals by name for specific purposes — a foundational Protestant doctrine
- **The Word of God**: The rarity and preciousness of God's word underscores its centrality
- **Prophetic Authority**: Samuel is established as a trustworthy prophet "from Dan to Beersheba" (3:20)
- **Faithful Obedience**: Samuel's willingness to deliver a hard message models prophetic integrity

## Reflection

Protestant tradition treasures this narrative as a reminder that God still speaks — through Scripture, through the Spirit, through the community of faith. Samuel's posture of listening remains the model for every believer who opens the Bible expecting to hear from God.
