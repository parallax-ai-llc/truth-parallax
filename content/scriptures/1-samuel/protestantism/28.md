## The Dead Prophet Speaks

1 Samuel 28 is one of the most debated chapters in the Hebrew Bible: Saul, desperate and abandoned by God, consults a medium at Endor and encounters the spirit of the dead Samuel. Protestant interpreters have vigorously debated whether this was a genuine appearance of Samuel or a demonic deception, but all agree on the chapter's devastating theological message.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The LORD did not answer him" (28:6)

Saul seeks God through dreams, Urim, and prophets — but receives only silence. Protestant theology reads this as the ultimate consequence of persistent disobedience: God withdraws his voice from those who have repeatedly rejected his word.

### The Medium at Endor (28:7-14)

Saul, having previously banned necromancy, now resorts to it — a final act of hypocrisy. Protestant commentators note the tragic irony: the king who outlawed mediums now depends on one. When we abandon God's approved means of guidance, we turn to forbidden ones.

### Samuel's Appearance and Message (28:15-19)

The apparition delivers a devastating prophecy: "The LORD has torn the kingdom out of your hand and given it to David... Tomorrow you and your sons shall be with me." Most Protestant commentators, following Calvin, believe God permitted Samuel's actual spirit to appear — not because the medium had power over the dead, but because God sovereignly chose to deliver one final judgment.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine Silence**: The most terrifying judgment is God's refusal to speak
- **Forbidden Knowledge**: The desperation that drives people to seek guidance from prohibited sources
- **The Finality of Rejection**: Saul's story reaches its irreversible conclusion
- **Sovereignty over Death**: Even the appearance of the dead is under God's control, not the medium's

## Reflection

Protestant theology reads this chapter as the final act of Saul's tragedy. Having rejected God's word through Samuel in life, Saul now hears that same word from Samuel in death — and it brings no comfort, only judgment. The chapter stands as a permanent warning: there are no shortcuts to the God who has been abandoned.
