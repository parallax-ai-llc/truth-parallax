## The Canticle of Reversal

Catholic tradition treasures Hannah's song (2:1-10) as the Old Testament precursor to Mary's Magnificat. The Liturgy of the Hours includes this canticle, and its themes of divine reversal — the mighty humbled, the lowly exalted — run through Catholic social teaching and Marian theology.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Hannah's Song and the Magnificat (2:1-10)

The structural and thematic parallels between Hannah's song and Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55) are central to Catholic exegesis. Both celebrate God's preferential care for the poor and lowly. Catholic social teaching draws from both texts the principle of God's "preferential option for the poor."

### The Corruption of Eli's Sons (2:12-17)

Hophni and Phinehas abuse their priestly office, treating sacrifices with contempt. Catholic moral theology reads this as a grave violation of sacred trust. The Council of Trent's reforms of clerical life were motivated in part by recognition that priestly corruption has ancient roots and devastating consequences.

### The Faithful Priest (2:35)

The prophetic promise of a "faithful priest" who will serve before God's anointed forever is read christologically: Christ is the ultimate faithful priest, whose sacrifice replaces all others.

## Theological Themes

- **The Magnificat Tradition**: Hannah's song as the foundation of Marian praise and liberation theology
- **Priestly Corruption and Reform**: The Church's ongoing need for internal purification
- **Divine Reversal**: God's consistent pattern of exalting the humble and humbling the proud
- **The Faithful Priest**: Christological fulfillment of the prophetic promise

## Reflection

Catholic theology finds in this chapter the foundational grammar of the Magnificat tradition. Hannah sings what Mary will sing, what the Church continues to sing: that God's power operates not through the mighty but through the humble, not through human strength but through divine grace.
