## Overview

Academic study of the Ihya Ulum al-Din has been central to the fields of Islamic intellectual history, comparative ethics, and the history of moral psychology. Scholars have analyzed the work's sources, its innovative synthesis of diverse Islamic traditions, and its lasting influence on Muslim religious life.

## Interpretive Approach

Scholars employ multiple methods: source criticism traces Ghazali's debts to earlier authors including al-Muhasibi, al-Makki, and al-Qushayri; intellectual history situates the Ihya within Ghazali's broader philosophical and theological project; comparative analysis explores parallels with Western moral philosophy and spiritual literature; and reception history traces the work's enormous influence across the Islamic world.

## Key Contributions

The Ihya is recognized as making major contributions to moral psychology (its analysis of the soul's faculties, virtues, and vices rivals Aristotle), the phenomenology of religious experience (its descriptions of spiritual states are remarkably precise), and the sociology of religion (its analysis of how institutional religion can lose its spiritual core remains relevant). Recent scholarship has also examined Ghazali's pedagogical method and his sophisticated understanding of how written texts can function as spiritual guides.
