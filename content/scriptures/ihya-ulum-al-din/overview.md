## Overview

The *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences) is the magnum opus of Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (1058–1111 CE), widely regarded as the most influential work of Islamic spirituality ever written. Composed after Ghazali's dramatic spiritual crisis and departure from his prestigious teaching position in Baghdad, the Ihya represents his attempt to reunite the external practice of Islamic law with the inner spiritual life that he believed the scholarly establishment had neglected.

The work is divided into four "quarters" of ten books each, totaling forty books that systematically address every dimension of Muslim religious life — from the foundations of worship and social conduct to the most advanced stages of the mystical path. Ghazali's genius lay in his ability to integrate the legal, theological, ethical, and mystical dimensions of Islam into a single coherent framework, making Sufi spirituality accessible and respectable within mainstream Sunni Islam.

## Structure

| Quarter | Books | Theme |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Acts of Worship | Books 1–10 | The external practices of Islam: knowledge, prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, Quran recitation |
| Social Customs | Books 11–20 | Conduct in daily life: eating, marriage, earning a livelihood, friendship, travel |
| Destructive Vices | Books 21–30 | The diseases of the heart: appetite, anger, envy, pride, worldliness, ostentation |
| Saving Virtues | Books 31–40 | The stations of the spiritual path: repentance, patience, love, sincerity, death |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Sufi**: The work that made Sufi spirituality the heart of mainstream Islamic practice, establishing the inward journey as essential to religious life
- **Sunni**: Embraced as the most comprehensive guide to Islamic piety, bridging the gap between law and spirituality
- **Shia**: Studied for its spiritual psychology and ethical teachings, though its Sunni legal framework is adapted
- **Academic**: Central to the study of Islamic intellectual history, moral psychology, and the integration of mystical and legal traditions
- **Interfaith**: Offers a vision of spiritual life centered on sincerity, self-knowledge, and love of God that resonates across traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Revival | Restoring the inner spiritual dimension to external religious practice |
| The Heart | The spiritual heart (qalb) as the center of the human being and the seat of knowledge of God |
| Sincerity | Ikhlas — purifying intention from all motives other than God |
| Balance | Integrating law, theology, ethics, and mysticism into a unified spiritual life |
| Self-Knowledge | Understanding the diseases and virtues of the soul as the prerequisite for knowledge of God |
| Remembrance of Death | Constant awareness of mortality as the foundation of spiritual seriousness |
