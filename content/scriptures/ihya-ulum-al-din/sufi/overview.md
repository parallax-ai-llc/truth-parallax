## Overview

For the Sufi tradition, the Ihya Ulum al-Din represents Ghazali's greatest achievement: the integration of Sufi spiritual practice into the mainstream of Islamic religious life. Before Ghazali, Sufism existed in tension with the scholarly establishment; after the Ihya, Sufi concepts of the heart, spiritual stations, and the inner dimensions of worship became central to normative Islamic piety.

## Interpretive Approach

Sufi readers emphasize Ghazali's teaching that every external religious act has an inner reality that must be cultivated for the act to be spiritually effective. Prayer without presence of heart is mere physical motion; fasting without disciplining the soul's desires is mere hunger. The Ihya maps the entire Sufi path — from the purification of the soul (nafs) through the cultivation of virtues to the ultimate goal of love of God (mahabba) — within the framework of Islamic law and practice.

## Significance

The Ihya's fourth quarter, which treats the saving virtues of the spiritual path — repentance, patience, trust in God, love, sincerity, and the remembrance of death — is considered the heart of the work from a Sufi perspective. Here Ghazali draws on the teachings of earlier Sufi masters including al-Muhasibi, al-Makki, and al-Qushayri to present a systematic guide to the stations and states of the mystical journey.
