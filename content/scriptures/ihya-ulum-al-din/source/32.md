## On Fear and Hope

**1.** Fear and hope are the two reins by which the heart is guided on the straight path. Fear without hope leads to despair, and hope without fear leads to complacency.

**2.** The believer's fear is not the fear of the coward—it is the fear of the reverent. It is the awe of the creature before the Creator, the trembling of the small before the Infinite.

**3.** The Prophet said: "If the believer knew what God has of punishment, no one would hope for His Paradise. And if the disbeliever knew what God has of mercy, no one would despair of His Paradise."

**4.** Fear should be dominant when the servant is in the midst of sin, to pull him back. Hope should be dominant when the servant is in the midst of worship, to encourage him.

**5.** At the time of death, hope should prevail, for the servant is about to meet his Lord, and it is more fitting to meet Him with hope than with despair.

**6.** The Prophet said: "Let none of you die except while thinking well of God." This is the hope of the dying—that God's mercy is greater than their sins.

**7.** The fear of God is the fear that refines, not the fear that destroys. It is the fire that purifies gold, not the fire that burns wood.

**8.** The one who fears God alone fears nothing else. The one who does not fear God fears everything else.

**9.** Hope in God's mercy is not the hope of the lazy—it is the hope of the worker who has planted his field and trusts that the rain will come.

**10.** The hopeful man acts. The despairing man does not. Hope is not passivity—it is the engine of action.

**11.** Fear narrows the path so that you do not stray. Hope widens it so that you do not stop. Between narrowing and widening, the traveler arrives.

**12.** The saints lived in a state of constant oscillation between fear and hope—never resting in either, always moving between the two.

**13.** The perfection of this station is to arrive at a place where fear and hope merge into a single state: the state of awe, which is both fear and hope and neither.

**14.** In this state, the servant neither despairs of God's mercy nor presumes upon it. He stands in the mystery of God's will, not knowing his fate but trusting in God's wisdom.

**15.** This is the station of the knowers: to live between fear and hope, in a state of trembling trust, and to find in that trembling the stillness of absolute faith.
