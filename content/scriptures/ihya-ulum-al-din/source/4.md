## The Mysteries of Prayer

**1.** Prayer is the pillar of religion and the meeting between the servant and his Lord. It is the ascension of the believer, the most excellent act after the declaration of faith.

**2.** The Prophet said: "The coolness of my eyes is in prayer." This means that prayer was not a burden upon him but a delight—the place where his soul found rest.

**3.** There are three degrees of prayer: the prayer of the body, the prayer of the heart, and the prayer of the spirit.

**4.** The prayer of the body is the standing, bowing, and prostrating. The prayer of the heart is the presence and attention. The prayer of the spirit is the witnessing of God.

**5.** Many pray with their bodies while their hearts are in the marketplace. This prayer is like a body without a soul—it has a form but no life.

**6.** The Prophet said: "Many a person who prays gains nothing from his prayer but fatigue." This is the one who moves his body but leaves his heart behind.

**7.** The mystery of the standing position is that you stand before God as a servant stands before his Master. The mystery of the bowing is that you humble yourself. The mystery of the prostration is that you place the highest part of yourself—your face—upon the lowest place—the ground.

**8.** In prostration, the servant is nearest to God. This is because in prostration, the self is at its lowest, and when the self is low, God is near.

**9.** The prayer should be performed as if it were your last prayer. For indeed you do not know whether you will live to perform another.

**10.** The opening words of prayer—"God is Greatest"—mean that God is greater than anything that occupies your heart. When you say these words, you are declaring that nothing in the world is more important than the One before whom you stand.

**11.** If your heart does not affirm what your tongue declares, then your tongue is a liar, even in prayer.

**12.** The perfection of prayer is to be so absorbed in the presence of God that you forget yourself, forget the world, and forget even the prayer itself—until nothing remains but the One to whom you pray.

**13.** This is the prayer of the saints, and it is rare. But even to aspire to it is itself a form of prayer.

**14.** The Prophet stood in prayer until his feet swelled, and when asked why, he said: "Shall I not be a grateful servant?" His prayer was an expression of gratitude, not obligation.

**15.** Make your prayer a garden, not a prison. Enter it with joy, not with resentment. And leave it refreshed, not relieved. This is the sign that your prayer is alive.
