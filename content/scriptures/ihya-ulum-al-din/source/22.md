## On the Discipline of the Soul

**1.** The discipline of the soul is the foundation of all spiritual progress. Without it, the seeker is like a rider on an untamed horse—he may travel far, but not in the direction he intends.

**2.** The soul has three stations: the soul that commands evil, the soul that blames itself, and the soul that is at peace.

**3.** The soul that commands evil is the raw soul, dominated by appetite and desire. It drags its owner toward destruction as a wild horse drags its rider.

**4.** The soul that blames itself is the soul that has begun to awaken. It sins, but it recognizes its sin and reproaches itself. This self-reproach is the beginning of transformation.

**5.** The soul that is at peace is the soul that has been tamed by discipline and illuminated by grace. It desires only what God desires, and its rest is in God alone.

**6.** The path from the first station to the third is the path of discipline. And discipline means opposing the desires of the lower soul at every turn.

**7.** The lower soul is like a child: if you indulge it, it grows wild; if you discipline it, it grows wise. Do not be its slave; be its teacher.

**8.** The tools of discipline are four: hunger, to weaken the appetite; silence, to still the tongue; wakefulness, to sharpen the awareness; and solitude, to escape the distractions of the crowd.

**9.** But discipline without love is barren. The soul that is disciplined by fear alone becomes rigid, not refined. True discipline is motivated by love for God, not fear of punishment.

**10.** Know yourself, for self-knowledge is the beginning of discipline. The one who does not know his own faults cannot correct them.

**11.** The Prophet said: "Your worst enemy is the soul that lies between your two sides." This soul is more dangerous than any external enemy, for it knows your weaknesses from within.

**12.** Examine your soul daily as a merchant examines his ledger. Note the profits and the losses, and adjust your conduct accordingly.

**13.** Do not despair if the soul resists discipline, for resistance is natural. The horse resists the bridle at first but eventually submits, and then the bridle becomes unnecessary.

**14.** The goal of discipline is not the suppression of the soul but its transformation. The wild horse, once tamed, is the swiftest and most noble of mounts.

**15.** And the soul, once disciplined, is the greatest ally of the spirit—a servant of God that runs eagerly toward its Lord without being driven.
