## Overview

Shia engagement with the Ihya Ulum al-Din has been selective but appreciative. While the work's Sunni legal framework (Shafi'i jurisprudence) and certain theological positions are adapted or set aside, Ghazali's spiritual psychology, ethical analysis, and mystical teachings have been embraced as valuable contributions to the Islamic spiritual heritage. The Shia tradition of irfan (gnosis) shares many of the Ihya's concerns with inner purification and knowledge of God.

## Interpretive Approach

Shia readers focus on the Ihya's treatment of the heart, the diseases of the soul, and the stations of the spiritual path — dimensions that transcend sectarian boundaries. Ghazali's analysis of sincerity (ikhlas), self-accounting (muhasaba), and the remembrance of death resonates with Shia ethical teachings attributed to the Imams, particularly Imam Ali's Nahj al-Balagha and Imam Sajjad's Sahifa Sajjadiyya.

## Significance

The Ihya's influence on Shia thought is mediated through later thinkers such as Fayd Kashani (d. 1680), whose al-Mahajja al-Bayda adapted the Ihya's structure and much of its content for a Shia audience, replacing Sunni hadith with narrations from the Ahl al-Bayt and adjusting legal positions to conform with Ja'fari jurisprudence.
