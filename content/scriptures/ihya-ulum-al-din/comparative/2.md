Comparative reading of Ihya Ulum al-Din book 2, "The Foundations of Belief," places Ghazali's teaching in dialogue with parallel traditions of moral psychology and spiritual practice. The themes addressed in this book resonate across cultures: the analysis of virtues and vices, the discipline of desire, the cultivation of inner states, and the relationship between knowledge and practice are concerns shared by Aristotelian ethics, Buddhist psychology, and Christian spiritual theology.

Specific parallels illuminate both convergences and differences. Ghazali's method of analyzing the soul's diseases and prescribing remedies mirrors the therapeutic approach found in Stoic philosophy, Buddhist Abhidharma, and the Christian tradition of spiritual direction. Yet his insistence on grounding moral psychology in Islamic theology — particularly the doctrines of divine sovereignty and the afterlife — distinguishes his approach from purely philosophical or therapeutic frameworks.
