## Overview

Comparative scholars have found in the Ihya Ulum al-Din one of the most productive texts for cross-cultural dialogue on moral psychology, virtue ethics, and spiritual practice. Ghazali's systematic treatment of virtues and vices, his analysis of the soul's faculties, and his practical program of spiritual transformation invite comparison with Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics, Buddhist Abhidharma psychology, and the Christian spiritual tradition from the Desert Fathers to Ignatius of Loyola.

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative readers explore structural parallels between Ghazali's moral psychology and other traditions' accounts of the soul and its transformation. The comparison with Aristotle is particularly rich: both thinkers analyze virtues as stable dispositions acquired through practice, both identify a mean between extremes, and both see the goal of moral life as a form of happiness rooted in knowledge and virtue.

## Key Comparisons

Ghazali's analysis of the "diseases of the heart" parallels the Desert Fathers' catalogues of the eight deadly thoughts (logismoi) and Buddhist analyses of the mental afflictions (klesha). His teaching on sincerity (ikhlas) resonates with Kierkegaard's concept of "purity of heart." His comprehensive program of self-examination anticipates the Ignatian Examen. These parallels suggest deep structural similarities in how different traditions have understood the human soul and its transformation.
