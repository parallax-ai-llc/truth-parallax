## Overview

Sunni engagement with the Ihya Ulum al-Din has been overwhelmingly positive, establishing the work as the most authoritative guide to Islamic piety in the Sunni tradition. Ghazali's integration of legal obligation with spiritual aspiration resolved a tension that had plagued Sunni Islam since the early centuries — the perceived gap between the external focus of the jurists and the inner focus of the mystics.

## Interpretive Approach

Sunni readers value the Ihya for its comprehensive treatment of religious life within the framework of Shafi'i jurisprudence and Ash'ari theology. Ghazali presents Sufi concepts not as esoteric alternatives to normative practice but as the inner dimension of what every Muslim is already obligated to do. This approach made spiritual aspiration accessible to ordinary believers while preserving the authority of the scholarly establishment.

## Reception

The Ihya has been called "a book that, were all other books of Islam lost, would suffice." While some scholars — notably Ibn al-Jawzi — criticized Ghazali for including weak hadiths and for excessive Sufi influence, the overwhelming Sunni consensus has embraced the work as indispensable. It has been taught, studied, and lived by Sunni Muslims for nine centuries.
