The Surangama Sutra's investigation of where the mind resides finds resonance in the Theravada Abhidhamma's analysis of consciousness (citta) and its relationship to objects. Ananda proposes seven locations for the mind — inside the body, outside, in the sense organs, in darkness, at the point of contact, between, and nowhere — each of which the Buddha refutes. While not canonical for Theravada, this systematic dismantling of naive realism about consciousness parallels the Abhidhamma's teaching that consciousness arises dependently and cannot be located in any single place. The Theravada practitioner recognizes in this dialogue the same insight that underpins vipassana: the mind is not a thing residing somewhere but a process arising and passing away moment by moment.
