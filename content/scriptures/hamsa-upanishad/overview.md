## Overview

The Hamsa Upanishad belongs to the Shukla Yajur Veda and takes its name from the *hamsa* (swan), one of the most potent symbols in Hindu spirituality. The swan represents the supreme self (*paramahamsa*) that can discriminate between the real and the unreal, just as the mythical swan separates milk from water. The Upanishad teaches meditation on the *hamsa mantra* — the natural sound of breathing ("ham" on inhalation, "sa" on exhalation) — as a path to realizing the identity of the individual self with Brahman.

## Structure

The Hamsa Upanishad consists of three sections:

1. **Section 1 — The Hamsa Teaching**: The nature of the hamsa as the supreme self
2. **Section 2 — Meditation on the Hamsa Mantra**: Practical instructions for hamsa meditation
3. **Section 3 — The Paramahamsa**: The characteristics of the fully realized sage

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The hamsa as Vishnu's vehicle and symbol of divine discrimination
- **Shaivism**: The hamsa mantra as Shiva's breath, 'So'ham' — 'I am That'
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The paramahamsa as the liberated sage who knows Brahman
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: The hamsa as the soul journeying toward God through devotion
- **Academic**: Important source for the history of mantra meditation in India
- **Comparative**: Breath-based meditation parallels across contemplative traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Hamsa Symbol | The swan as the supreme self discriminating between real and unreal |
| Hamsa Mantra | The natural breath-mantra 'hamsa/so'ham' as meditation practice |
| The Paramahamsa | The supreme sage who has realized the identity of self and Brahman |
| Breath and Consciousness | The connection between breathing and the flow of awareness |
| Discrimination | The ability to discern the eternal from the transient |
