Section 2 preserves one of the earliest descriptions of hamsa mantra meditation, a practice that became widespread across Indian contemplative traditions. The technique of attending to the natural sound of breathing — without artificially imposing a mantra — represents a distinctive approach to meditation that differs from both the Vedic repetition of ritual formulas and the Buddhist practice of bare attention.

Scholars note the ingenuity of the hamsa/so'ham reversal: the natural breathing sound (hamsa) automatically generates the philosophical statement (so'ham, "I am That"), embedding a metaphysical teaching in a physiological process. This may represent an early example of the tantric principle that the body itself is a vehicle for liberation.
