---
title: "Brahmavaivarta Purana Sri Krishna Janma Khanda (Book 4)"
scripture: "brahmavaivarta-purana"
chapter: 4
---

**1** The Sri Krishna Janma Khanda, the longest section of the Brahmavaivarta Purana, narrates the divine birth and childhood sports of Lord Krishna.

**2** The sage said: "When the earth was burdened by the tyranny of wicked kings and demons, she assumed the form of a cow and went to Brahma, weeping. Brahma took her to Vishnu, who promised to incarnate as Krishna."

**3** Krishna was born at midnight in the prison of Kamsa, to Vasudeva and Devaki. The guards fell asleep, the chains fell away, and the prison doors opened. Vasudeva carried the divine infant through the stormy night to Vrindavan.

**4** In Vrindavan, Krishna was raised by Nanda and Yashoda. His childhood sports are described with loving detail: he stole butter from the homes of the Gopis, he played pranks on the milkmaids, he danced on the hood of the serpent Kaliya.

**5** When Yashoda scolded Krishna for eating dirt and asked him to open his mouth, she saw within it the entire universe — the sun, the moon, the stars, the mountains, the oceans, and all the worlds. She was overcome with wonder and then, by Krishna's power, forgot what she had seen.

**6** The Purana describes the Rasa Lila in elaborate detail. On the full moon nights of autumn, Krishna played his flute in the forest of Vrindavan. The Gopis, hearing the divine music, left their homes, their families, and all worldly bonds to dance with Krishna.

**7** Krishna multiplied himself so that each Gopi danced with him alone. The dance continued through the night, and the gods gathered in the sky to watch. Brahma said: "This is the highest form of worship — the complete surrender of the soul to the divine beloved."

**8** The love of the Gopis is described as the model of supreme devotion: "The Gopis did not know the Vedas. They did not practice yoga. They did not perform sacrifices. They simply loved Krishna with every fiber of their being. This love was their path, their practice, and their liberation."

**9** The Purana narrates Krishna's departure from Vrindavan. Called by destiny to destroy Kamsa and fulfill his divine mission, he left the land of his childhood. The Gopis wept inconsolably, and Krishna promised: "I am never absent from Vrindavan. Wherever you think of me, there I am."

**10** The Purana teaches the theology of separation: "The pain of separation from the Lord is itself a form of union, for in the agony of longing, the devotee is closest to the divine. The Gopis' grief was their highest joy, for it proved the depth of their love."

**11** The Purana describes the ultimate teaching of Krishna: "I am the beginning, the middle, and the end of all beings. I am the Self in the heart of every creature. There is nothing that exists apart from me. He who sees me in all things and all things in me — he never loses sight of me, and I never lose sight of him."

**12** The sage concluded: "Thus ends the Brahmavaivarta Purana. Its central teaching is that the Supreme Being is a person — Krishna — who loves, who plays, who dances, who steals butter, and who calls the soul to himself with the music of his flute. This personal God is the final truth behind all impersonal philosophies."
