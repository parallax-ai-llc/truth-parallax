---
title: "Brahmavaivarta Purana Ganesha Khanda (Book 3)"
scripture: "brahmavaivarta-purana"
chapter: 3
---

**1** The Ganesha Khanda narrates the birth, exploits, and worship of Lord Ganesha, the elephant-headed god, the remover of obstacles and the lord of beginnings.

**2** The Purana narrates: Parvati, desiring a son, was blessed by Krishna. She performed severe austerities and was granted a son of surpassing beauty, endowed with the splendor of Krishna himself.

**3** The gods and sages came to see the child. But when Shani (Saturn) looked upon the infant, the child's head was severed by the power of Saturn's gaze, for Shani had been cursed to destroy whatever he looked upon directly.

**4** Parvati was inconsolable. Vishnu, to console her, flew northward on Garuda and found an elephant sleeping with its head to the north. He took the elephant's head and placed it upon the child's body, restoring him to life.

**5** The child, now with the head of an elephant, was blessed by all the gods. Brahma named him Ganesha, the lord of the Ganas, Shiva's attendants. He was also called Vighnaharta, the remover of obstacles, and Ganapati, the chief of the celestial hosts.

**6** Shiva declared: "Before any worship, before any ceremony, before any undertaking, Ganesha shall be worshipped first. He who neglects Ganesha at the beginning shall meet with failure. He who honors Ganesha shall succeed in all things."

**7** The Purana describes the forms of Ganesha: "He has an elephant's head, a pot belly, and four arms. He holds the axe, the noose, the modaka (sweet), and bestows blessings with one hand. His vehicle is the mouse, symbolizing the conquest of desire."

**8** The story of how Ganesha became the scribe of the Mahabharata is narrated. Vyasa needed a scribe who could keep pace with his recitation. Ganesha agreed, on the condition that Vyasa not pause. Vyasa agreed, on the condition that Ganesha understand every verse before writing it.

**9** The Purana describes Ganesha's marriage to Siddhi (attainment) and Buddhi (wisdom). From Siddhi was born Kshema (prosperity), and from Buddhi was born Labha (profit). Thus the family of Ganesha represents the completeness of worldly and spiritual success.

**10** The worship of Ganesha is prescribed: "On the fourth day of each fortnight, Ganesha should be worshipped with red flowers, durva grass, and modakas. The Ganesha Chaturthi in the month of Bhadrapada is the greatest festival in his honor."

**11** The Purana teaches: "Ganesha is not merely a deity to be worshipped for material success. He is the lord of wisdom, the guardian of the threshold between the material and the spiritual. He who worships Ganesha with understanding attains both worldly success and spiritual liberation."

**12** The sage concluded: "The Ganesha Khanda teaches that every beginning requires divine grace. Ganesha stands at the doorway of every endeavor, every scripture, and every path. Without his blessing, no journey can be completed. With his blessing, no obstacle can endure."
