## Overview

The Baal Cycle tablets were discovered at Ras Shamra (ancient Ugarit), a major port city on the Syrian coast, during French archaeological excavations that began in 1929 under the direction of Claude Schaeffer. The tablets were found in the house of the high priest, located between the two main temples of Ugarit (the temples of Baal and Dagan), establishing a clear cultic context for the mythological texts.

## Archaeological Context

### The Discovery
A farmer's chance discovery of a tomb at the nearby port of Minet el-Beida in 1928 led to systematic excavation at Ras Shamra beginning the following year. The cuneiform tablets were recognized almost immediately as a previously unknown Semitic language written in a new alphabetic cuneiform script — a discovery that transformed the study of the ancient Near East.

### The House of the High Priest
The Baal Cycle tablets were found in a building located between the temples of Baal (on the acropolis) and Dagan (slightly lower), interpreted as the residence and library of the chief priest. This architectural context connects the mythological texts directly to the city's cultic establishment.

### The Script
Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform is one of the earliest alphabetic writing systems, consisting of 30 signs (27 consonants plus three aleph variants). The invention of this script at Ugarit in the 14th century BCE represents a crucial step in the development of the alphabet.

### The Temples
The temple of Baal on the acropolis has been partially excavated, revealing a large courtyard and main sanctuary. Votive objects, offering installations, and inscriptions confirm the importance of the Baal cult at Ugarit.

## Key Archaeological Features

| Feature | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Temple of Baal | Confirms Baal's prominence in Ugaritic religion |
| Alphabetic cuneiform | Earliest known alphabetic script for literary composition |
| Priestly library | Mythological texts in a cultic institutional context |
| International archive | Letters, treaties, and texts in multiple languages |
| Destruction layer | The city was destroyed c. 1185 BCE, preserving the archive |
