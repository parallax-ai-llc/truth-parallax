## Overview

The Baal Cycle (KTU 1.1–1.6) is the most extensive mythological composition from ancient Ugarit, comprising approximately 2,350 preserved lines of poetry inscribed on six large clay tablets in the Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform script. The tablets were discovered in the house of the high priest at Ras Shamra (ancient Ugarit) during French excavations directed by Claude Schaeffer, beginning in 1929. The text is dated to approximately the 14th–13th centuries BCE based on stratigraphic and paleographic evidence.

## Key Scholarly Issues

### Tablet Order and Unity
The sequence of the six tablets has been debated since their discovery. The current consensus (established by Mark S. Smith and others) places them in the order KTU 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4, 1.5, 1.6, but the relationship between the Yamm and Mot episodes — whether they represent a single continuous narrative or originally independent compositions — remains discussed.

### The Scribe Ilimilku
Colophons on several tablets identify the scribe as Ilimilku (also read Ilimalku), a priest in the service of King Niqmaddu (II or III). This attribution makes the Baal Cycle one of the few ancient Near Eastern literary texts whose scribe is known by name.

### Relationship to Biblical Studies
The Baal Cycle has transformed the study of the Hebrew Bible. The mythological language of YHWH's conflict with Sea (Psalm 74, Isaiah 27, Job 26), the storm god imagery applied to YHWH, and the polemics against Baal worship are now understood against the background of Ugaritic mythology.
