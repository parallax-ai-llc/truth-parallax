The sixth tablet brings the cycle to its conclusion through a masterful inversion: the god of Death is treated like grain — cut, threshed, winnowed, and sown. The agricultural imagery transforms the cosmic battle into a harvest scene, collapsing the distinction between mythology and agronomy. This literary strategy is among the most original in ancient literature: the instruments of death (sword, fire, millstones) are simultaneously the instruments of agriculture, suggesting that death and life are two aspects of a single process.

The cycle's conclusion — with Shapsh mediating between Baal and Mot — avoids the simplicity of total victory. The literary structure is open-ended: the conflict will recur, the cycle will repeat, and the poem can be performed again at the next turning of the seasons.
