The third tablet presents a theology of divine violence that is difficult for modern readers. Anat's joy in slaughter — 'her liver swells with laughter, her heart fills with joy' — depicts the divine as finding pleasure in destruction. This theology of the warrior goddess challenges traditions that associate the divine exclusively with peace, mercy, and compassion.

Yet the violence serves a theological purpose: Anat's ferocity is directed against enemies of the divine order, and her warrior nature will be essential when she later battles Mot to rescue Baal. The text suggests that divine love and divine violence are not opposites but complementary aspects of divine power.
