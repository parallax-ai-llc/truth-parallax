## Overview

The Thought of Norea (NHC IX,2) is one of the shortest texts in the Nag Hammadi library — a brief Sethian hymnic composition of only about fifty lines. The text celebrates Norea, a figure identified in various Gnostic texts as the daughter of Eve (or sometimes the wife of Noah), who functions as a representative of the divine feminine and the spiritual seed of Seth. In this text, Norea cries out to the highest God — the Father of the All — for deliverance from the archons, and receives assurance through four "holy helpers" (the four luminaries of Sethian mythology: Harmozel, Oroiael, Daveithai, and Eleleth) that she belongs to the imperishable realm above. The text is notable for its positive portrayal of a female figure as the protagonist of a salvific narrative and for its concise summary of key Sethian theological themes.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Celebrated as a hymn to the divine feminine, affirming the spiritual authority and salvific role of Norea
- **Academic**: Studied for its concise summary of Sethian theology and its place within the broader Norea/Noria tradition
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to feminine divine figures in Hindu (Shakti), Buddhist (Tara), and Kabbalistic (Shekhinah) traditions
- **Christian**: Challenges the marginalization of feminine imagery in orthodox theology and provides evidence of early Christian engagement with divine femininity
- **Jewish**: Transforms the minor figure of Naamah/Norea from Genesis traditions into a powerful protagonist of salvation
- **Interfaith**: Addresses the universal recognition of the feminine dimension of the divine

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Norea as Divine Feminine | Norea embodies the feminine aspect of the divine, capable of direct communication with the highest God |
| The Cry for Help | The spiritual person's cry to the transcendent God is heard and answered, despite the archons' opposition |
| The Four Luminaries | The Sethian four lights serve as mediators of assurance and salvation |
| Imperishable Belonging | Norea's true identity is not in the material world but in the imperishable aeon above |
