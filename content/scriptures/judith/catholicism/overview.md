## Overview

The Catholic Church affirms Judith as canonical scripture, definitively confirmed at the Council of Trent (1546). Catholic interpretation reads the book as inspired narrative that demonstrates God's power to deliver through unexpected agents and the efficacy of prayer combined with courageous action.

## Marian Typology

One of the most distinctive features of Catholic interpretation is the reading of Judith as a type of the Blessed Virgin Mary. Just as Judith crushed the head of Holofernes to save Israel, Mary is seen as the woman who crushes the serpent's head (Genesis 3:15). The praise given to Judith, "You are the glory of Jerusalem, the great pride of Israel, the great boast of our nation" (15:9), is echoed in Marian hymns and antiphons. This typological connection is reflected in Catholic art, liturgy, and the writings of Church Fathers including Clement of Rome and Ambrose.

## Theological Significance

Catholic teaching draws from Judith the principle that God works through human cooperation with grace. Judith's fasting, prayer, and decisive action exemplify the Catholic understanding of faith and works operating together. Her trust in God combined with strategic planning demonstrates that divine providence does not eliminate human agency but elevates it.
