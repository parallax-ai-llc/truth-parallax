Catholic interpretation of Judith 1 reads the chapter as inspired narrative that establishes the cosmic scale of the conflict between human pride and divine sovereignty. The identification of Nebuchadnezzar as 'king of the Assyrians' is understood not as a historical error but as a deliberate literary device, perhaps combining features of multiple historical tyrants to create a composite figure of imperial hubris. The Church does not require the book to be read as strict history.

The chapter's description of Nebuchadnezzar's military ambitions and his rage at nations that refused to join his war establishes the theological context for the entire narrative: the pride of worldly power set against the faithfulness of God's people. Catholic exegesis reads this within the broader scriptural theme of God humbling the proud (Luke 1:51-52), a theme that the Magnificat, Mary's hymn, will later echo in language reminiscent of Judith's victory.
