Catholic exegesis of Judith 10 reads Judith's adorning of herself as the preparation of a divine instrument. Her beauty is understood not as a worldly weapon but as a gift of God employed in service to God's purposes. Catholic typological interpretation connects Judith's beauty to the beauty of the Blessed Virgin, whose grace and favor with God made her the instrument of the Incarnation.

The chapter's description of Judith leaving the city gates and descending to the enemy camp mirrors the spiritual courage that Catholic hagiography celebrates in the lives of the saints. The guards' astonishment at her beauty serves the narrative's theological purpose of demonstrating that God's instruments often confound human expectations. Catholic moral theology reads Judith's action as an exercise of the cardinal virtue of fortitude, supported by prudence and faith.
