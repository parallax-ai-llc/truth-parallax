Catholic interpretation of Judith 16 reads the hymn of praise as an inspired canticle that celebrates God's deliverance and warns against future enemies. The hymn is used in the Catholic Liturgy of the Hours and is recognized as standing in the canonical tradition of victory songs. Its declaration that God is greater than all sacrifices and burnt offerings (16:16) anticipates the prophetic critique found in the canonical prophets.

The chapter's epilogue, recording Judith's refusal to remarry, her long life, and her generous death, presents her as a model of the consecrated life. Catholic tradition has read Judith's widowhood and ascetic discipline as a prefiguration of religious life, particularly the vocation of consecrated widows and virgins. Her death at 105, honored by all Israel, illustrates the Catholic ideal of a life completed in holiness and recognized by the community of faith.
