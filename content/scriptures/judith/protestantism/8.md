Judith 8 introduces the heroine and contains her rebuke of the elders for their decision to surrender if God does not act within five days. Judith argues that they have no right to set conditions on God or to 'test' the Lord. Her speech is theologically sophisticated, drawing on themes of divine sovereignty and the inscrutability of God's purposes. Protestant readers find this speech deeply resonant with Reformed theology.

Judith's insistence that God cannot be subjected to human timetables echoes Job's discovery that God's ways transcend human understanding. Her argument that suffering may be a test of faith rather than a sign of divine absence parallels Protestant discussions of theodicy. The chapter also establishes Judith's character: a wealthy, beautiful, pious widow who has lived an ascetic life since her husband's death, a portrait of female piety that Protestant interpreters find admirable.
