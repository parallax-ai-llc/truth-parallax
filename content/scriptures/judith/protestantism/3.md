Judith 3 describes the rapid capitulation of the coastal nations before Holofernes' advance. City after city surrenders, and Holofernes destroys their sacred groves and temples, demanding that Nebuchadnezzar alone be worshipped as a god. Protestant readers note the blasphemous claim of divine status for a human ruler, a theme that connects to the canonical book of Daniel's portrayal of Nebuchadnezzar's hubris.

The chapter establishes a contrast between the nations who submit out of fear and Israel, which will resist out of faith. This contrast between worldly capitulation and faithful resistance is a theme that Protestant theology has historically valued, particularly in the context of the Reformation's resistance to spiritual tyranny. The chapter serves as the backdrop against which Israel's unique response will stand out.
