Protestant readers of Judith 1 immediately encounter one of the primary reasons for the book's exclusion from the canon: the identification of Nebuchadnezzar as 'king of the Assyrians who reigned in Nineveh.' Historically, Nebuchadnezzar was the king of Babylon, not Assyria, and Nineveh had fallen before his reign. Protestant scholars have long noted this apparent anachronism as evidence that the book is historical fiction rather than historical record.

Nevertheless, the chapter's portrait of imperial hubris and military aggression establishes a powerful theological contrast that the rest of the narrative will exploit. The description of an all-powerful emperor whose will brooks no resistance sets up the dramatic reversal to come, when a single woman of faith will accomplish what no army could. Protestant readers can appreciate this literary structure as an illustration of the biblical theme that God resists the proud and exalts the humble.
