## Overview

Protestantism does not include Judith in the biblical canon. The book was excluded by the Reformers on the grounds that it was absent from the Hebrew Bible and contained apparent historical inaccuracies, most notably, the attribution of the campaign to "Nebuchadnezzar, king of the Assyrians," a conflation that does not match known history. Luther nonetheless included it in his Bible as an apocryphal book, calling it "a fine, good, holy, useful book."

## Reasons for Exclusion

Beyond the canonical criterion of the Hebrew Bible, Protestant scholars have noted the book's literary character as a probable work of historical fiction, a theological narrative using historical names symbolically. The book's theology of deception (Judith deceives Holofernes) also raised ethical questions for some Protestant moralists, though others defended her actions as justified in the context of holy war and divine mandate.

## Engagement with the Content

Despite exclusion from the canon, Protestant interpreters have long valued Judith as a narrative of extraordinary faith and courage. The book has been read as a typological precursor: Judith as a type of the Church militant, defeating the enemies of God through faith rather than military power. Her prayer in chapter 9 and hymn in chapter 16 are recognized as powerful expressions of trust in divine deliverance.
