Judith 6 narrates Holofernes' angry rejection of Achior's warning and his delivery of Achior to the Israelites at Bethulia, intending this as both punishment and mockery. The people of Bethulia receive Achior with hospitality. Protestant commentators note the irony: the pagan commander rejects the truth about Israel's God, while the pagan Achior embraces it.

The chapter illustrates the biblical principle that divine truth can be recognized even by those outside the covenant community, a theme that Paul develops in Romans 1-2. Achior's eventual circumcision and incorporation into Israel (14:10) completes this trajectory. Protestant readers see in this subplot a reminder that faith, not ethnicity, determines one's relationship to God.
