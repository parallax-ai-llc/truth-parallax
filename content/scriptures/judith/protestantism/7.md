Judith 7 describes the Assyrian siege of Bethulia and the city's growing desperation as water supplies run out. The elders propose to surrender if God does not deliver them within five days. This sets the stage for Judith's intervention in the next chapter. Protestant interpreters note the parallel with the Israelites' complaints in the wilderness (Exodus 17), where lack of water led to a crisis of faith.

The five-day ultimatum given to God raises significant theological questions about testing God, a practice condemned in Deuteronomy 6:16 and cited by Jesus in his temptation (Matthew 4:7). This becomes the theological pivot of the narrative, as Judith will rebuke the elders precisely for presuming to put God to the test. Protestant readers find this theological tension engaging and well-crafted.
