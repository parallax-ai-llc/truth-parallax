Judith 2 describes Holofernes receiving his commission from Nebuchadnezzar to punish the western nations that refused to join his war. The chapter catalogs the vast military resources at Holofernes' disposal, emphasizing the overwhelming nature of the threat. Protestant interpreters read this military buildup as the narrative's way of establishing the impossibility of human resistance, which will make God's deliverance through Judith all the more striking.

The theological pattern here resonates with canonical narratives such as Gideon's reduction of his army (Judges 7) and David's confrontation with Goliath (1 Samuel 17). In each case, the odds are deliberately stacked against Israel to demonstrate that victory belongs to God, not to military might. While Protestants do not regard Judith as canonical, they recognize this theological pattern as thoroughly biblical.
