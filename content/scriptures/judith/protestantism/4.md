Judith 4 describes Israel's response to the Assyrian threat: rather than surrendering like the surrounding nations, the Israelites fast, pray, and prepare their defenses under the direction of the high priest Joakim. Protestant readers note the combination of spiritual preparation (prayer and fasting) with practical action (fortifying the mountain passes), a balance that resonates with Protestant theology of divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

The chapter's portrayal of communal prayer and fasting as the primary response to military threat echoes canonical passages such as 2 Chronicles 20, where Jehoshaphat responds to invasion with prayer and worship. While the specifics of Judith 4 are not granted canonical authority, Protestant interpreters recognize the biblical pattern of faith expressed through both prayer and prudent action.
