Judith 11 contains Judith's speech to Holofernes, in which she uses carefully crafted ambiguity to deceive him while speaking words that are technically true from her own perspective. She tells him that God will deliver her people's enemies into his hands, and that she will lead him to the heart of Israel. Protestant commentators note the masterful use of dramatic irony throughout.

The ethics of Judith's deception have been debated across traditions. Some Protestant moralists have found the deception troubling, while others compare it favorably to the Hebrew midwives' deception of Pharaoh (Exodus 1:19-20) and Rahab's deception of the soldiers (Joshua 2:4-5), both of which are commended in canonical scripture. The chapter demonstrates literary artistry in its use of double meaning and dramatic irony.
