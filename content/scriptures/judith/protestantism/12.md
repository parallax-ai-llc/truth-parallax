Judith 12 narrates the banquet at which Holofernes, infatuated with Judith, drinks himself into a stupor. Judith maintains her ritual purity throughout her stay in the camp, eating only her own food and going out nightly to pray. The contrast between Holofernes' indulgence and Judith's disciplined piety is stark.

Protestant interpreters read this chapter as illustrating the biblical contrast between worldly excess and spiritual discipline. Holofernes' drunkenness, like Nabal's in 1 Samuel 25 and Belshazzar's in Daniel 5, leads to his downfall. Judith's restraint and prayerfulness, maintained even in the enemy camp, demonstrate a faith that operates under the most extreme pressure. The chapter builds inexorably toward the climactic act of deliverance.
