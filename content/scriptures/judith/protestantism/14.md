Judith 14 describes the aftermath of the beheading. Achior identifies the head of Holofernes, is overwhelmed, and converts to the God of Israel through circumcision. The Israelites mount a counterattack as the Assyrians discover their general's body and fall into panic. Protestant commentators note Achior's conversion as the fulfillment of the trajectory begun in chapter 5.

The chapter illustrates the biblical theme that God's mighty acts lead to the conversion of outsiders, a pattern found in Joshua (Rahab), 1 Kings (the widow of Zarephath), and 2 Kings (Naaman). Achior's circumcision represents full incorporation into Israel, anticipating the theological discussions about Gentile inclusion that would become central in the New Testament. Protestant interpreters find this subplot theologically rich.
