A Gnostic reading of Judith 16's hymn would interpret the song as Sophia's proclamation of triumph over the archonic forces. The hymn's declaration that God is greater than all earthly powers and that even the mountains and waters tremble before the divine would be read as a celebration of the transcendent God's absolute superiority over the Demiurge's material creation.

The chapter's conclusion, with Judith living out her remaining years in ascetic widowhood, presents the pneumatic ideal: having accomplished the liberating act, the Gnostic sage withdraws from worldly engagement and lives in contemplative awareness of the transcendent. Judith's refusal to remarry represents the pneumatic's detachment from the material world's entanglements. Her death and the period of peace that follows symbolize the hope that archonic power, once broken, will not reassert itself over the awakened community.
