Orthodox exegesis of Judith 16 reads the hymn of praise as an inspired canticle that takes its place within the Orthodox liturgical tradition. The hymn's celebration of God as Creator and divine warrior resonates with Orthodox hymnography, particularly the victory hymns of Pascha and the troparia of the Theotokos feasts. The song's warning to the enemies of God's people carries eschatological weight.

The chapter's conclusion, with Judith's long life of ascetic widowhood and honored death, presents her as a model of the consecrated life. Orthodox monasticism, which honors voluntary celibacy, poverty, and prayer as the highest calling, finds a precedent in Judith's chosen widowhood. Her death at a great age, mourned by all Israel, illustrates the Orthodox ideal of a life completed in holiness, its fragrance spreading through the community as the lives of the saints do.
