Orthodox interpretation of Judith 3 emphasizes the chapter's portrayal of the nations' surrender and their acceptance of Nebuchadnezzar as a deity. The destruction of sacred sites and the demand for worship of a human ruler represents the ultimate form of idolatry, which the Orthodox Church condemns as the fundamental spiritual error. The contrast between these nations and Israel's refusal to submit frames the narrative's theological argument.

Orthodox ecclesiology draws from this chapter the teaching that the Church must never surrender its worship to any earthly power. The Orthodox experience of resisting imperial interference in worship, from Byzantine iconoclasm to Soviet persecution, resonates with Israel's stance in the narrative. The chapter teaches that fidelity to true worship may require standing alone against the entire world.
