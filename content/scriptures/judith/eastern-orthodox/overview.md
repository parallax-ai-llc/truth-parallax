## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church receives Judith as canonical scripture within the broader Septuagint tradition. Orthodox interpretation emphasizes the book's teaching on spiritual warfare, the power of fasting and prayer, and Judith as a model of holy courage that prefigures the Theotokos (Mother of God).

## Typological Reading

Orthodox tradition reads Judith typologically as an image of the Theotokos. As Judith defeated Holofernes through humility and divine assistance, Mary's fiat brought about the defeat of the spiritual enemy through the Incarnation. Orthodox hymnography draws on this parallel, and Judith appears in iconographic programs alongside other Old Testament women who prefigure Mary.

## Ascetic and Liturgical Dimensions

The Orthodox reading highlights Judith's ascetical life, her fasting, prayer, and widowhood, as a model for monastic spirituality. Her three-year period of mourning and ascetic discipline before her decisive action illustrates the Orthodox principle that spiritual preparation precedes effective action. The book is read in Orthodox lectionary cycles, and its themes of fasting and deliverance connect it to Great Lent and the Paschal narrative of liberation.
