## Overview

Judith is a deuterocanonical book that tells the story of a pious and beautiful Jewish widow who saves her people from the Assyrian army by infiltrating the enemy camp and beheading the general Holofernes. The book is a dramatic narrative of faith, courage, and divine deliverance against overwhelming odds.

## Structure

The book divides into two halves that contrast human military power with divine deliverance through an unlikely agent:

1. **The Assyrian Threat** (Chapters 1–7): Nebuchadnezzar's campaign, Holofernes' siege of Bethulia, and Israel's desperation
2. **Judith's Triumph** (Chapters 8–16): Judith's rebuke of the elders, her plan, the banquet and beheading, Israel's victory, and her hymn of praise

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Judith holds varied status across religious communities:

- **Catholicism / Eastern Orthodoxy**: Canonical scripture with liturgical use
- **Protestantism**: Apocryphal; admired as literature but excluded from the canon
- **Judaism**: Not in the Tanakh, but celebrated in connection with Hanukkah traditions
- **Art and Culture**: One of the most depicted biblical women in Western art (Artemisia Gentileschi, Caravaggio, Klimt)

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Courage | Judith acts when male leaders falter, trusting God rather than armies |
| Faith | Victory comes through prayer and trust in God, not military strength |
| Reversal | A widow defeats the mightiest general; the weak overcome the strong |
| Prayer | Judith's prayer in chapter 9 is a model of petition before action |
| Holy War | God fights for Israel through unexpected means |
