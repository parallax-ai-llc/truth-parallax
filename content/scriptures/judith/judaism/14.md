Jewish scholarly interest in Judith 14 focuses on Achior's circumcision and conversion, which provides evidence for Second Temple Jewish attitudes toward proselytes. The conversion of a righteous Gentile who has witnessed God's power reflects the theological position that God's mighty acts should lead to universal recognition of divine sovereignty. This universalistic strain coexists in Jewish thought with the particularistic emphasis on Israel's special covenant.

The chapter's account of the Assyrian rout provides a satisfying narrative resolution that affirms the book's theology of divine deliverance. Jewish readers note the classic pattern: the enemy's strength is turned to weakness when God intervenes, a pattern that defines Jewish historical consciousness from the Exodus through the Maccabean revolt and beyond.
