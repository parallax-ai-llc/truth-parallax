Jewish scholars recognize that Judith 1's identification of Nebuchadnezzar as 'king of the Assyrians who reigned in Nineveh' is a deliberate anachronism that signals the book's genre as theological fiction rather than historical chronicle. This literary device, combining elements from different historical periods, was a recognized technique in Second Temple Jewish literature for creating narratives that addressed contemporary concerns through historical dress.

The chapter's portrait of an all-powerful emperor demanding universal submission reflects the political anxieties of Jews living under Hellenistic and later Roman domination. Jewish scholars read the book as resistance literature, composed to inspire courage in communities facing persecution. The theological message, that no human power can withstand God's purposes, resonates with the experience of Jewish communities throughout history.
