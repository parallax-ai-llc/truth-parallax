Jewish readers of Judith 3 note the chapter's contrast between nations that surrender their identity and worship and Israel, which maintains both. The destruction of sacred sites and the demand for worship of a human ruler touches on anxieties central to Second Temple Judaism, particularly during the Hellenistic period when Antiochus IV Epiphanes attempted to impose Greek worship on Jews.

The chapter's theology of resistance to forced assimilation resonates with the Hanukkah narrative and the broader Jewish tradition of maintaining distinctiveness under pressure. Jewish scholars read the nations' capitulation as a cautionary tale about the loss of cultural and religious identity that comes from yielding to imperial demands. Israel's unique refusal to submit, described in the next chapter, represents the Jewish commitment to covenantal fidelity that has defined Jewish identity across millennia.
