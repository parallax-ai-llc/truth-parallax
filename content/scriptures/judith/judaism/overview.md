## Overview

Judith is not part of the Jewish biblical canon, and the original Hebrew text has been lost. The book survives primarily in Greek. However, the story of Judith has had a lasting presence in Jewish tradition, particularly in connection with Hanukkah celebrations, where the theme of miraculous deliverance against a superior military force resonates strongly.

## Historical and Literary Significance

Jewish scholars have long recognized the book as a work of historical fiction, composed during the Second Temple period to inspire courage and faithfulness during times of persecution. The deliberate historical anachronisms (Nebuchadnezzar as king of the Assyrians) signal that the author was writing theology through narrative rather than strict history. The book reflects the concerns of Jews living under foreign domination and the belief that God delivers the faithful through unlikely agents.

## Judith in Jewish Culture

The figure of Judith became associated with Hanukkah through medieval midrashic traditions, particularly the story of Judith feeding cheese to Holofernes to make him thirsty and then drunk. This connection gave rise to the custom of eating dairy foods on Hanukkah in some communities. Judith also appears in medieval Hebrew retellings and piyyutim (liturgical poems), celebrated as a model of Jewish female courage alongside Deborah, Jael, and Esther.
