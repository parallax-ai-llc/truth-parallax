Jewish scholars read Judith 9 as a significant example of women's prayer in Second Temple Judaism. The prayer's theology, appealing to God as the defender of the weak and the opponent of arrogant power, draws on canonical traditions of divine justice while expressing them through a distinctly female voice. The prayer provides evidence for the spiritual lives of Jewish women in a period from which few direct testimonies survive.

The prayer's reference to the rape of Dinah (Genesis 34) and God's response to it is noteworthy for its interpretation of that difficult narrative as an act of divine justice. This reading reflects a Second Temple Jewish perspective on Genesis 34 that differs from some modern interpretations. The prayer demonstrates the creative engagement with Torah that characterized Second Temple Jewish piety.
