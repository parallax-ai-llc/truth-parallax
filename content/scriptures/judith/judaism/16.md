Jewish scholars read Judith 16's hymn within the tradition of Israelite victory poetry that includes the Song of Moses (Exodus 15), the Song of Deborah (Judges 5), and Hannah's song (1 Samuel 2). Judith's hymn shares structural and thematic features with all of these canonical precedents, demonstrating the continuity of Jewish literary and theological traditions.

The epilogue's description of Judith's long, honored life, her refusal to remarry, and the period of peace that followed her victory reflect the Jewish ideal of the righteous person whose influence extends beyond their individual actions to bless the entire community. Jewish readers note the parallel with the epilogue of Job, where the righteous sufferer is vindicated and blessed. The freeing of Judith's maid mentioned in the epilogue connects to the Torah's concern for the manumission of slaves and the practice of chesed (lovingkindness) toward dependents.
