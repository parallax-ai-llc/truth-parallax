Jewish scholars read Achior's speech in Judith 5 as a compact retelling of salvation history that reflects the Deuteronomistic theology dominant in Second Temple Judaism. The outsider's recognition that Israel's God protects the faithful echoes the canonical pattern of foreign witnesses to God's power, from Jethro (Exodus 18) to Naaman (2 Kings 5).

The speech's theological framework, linking faithfulness to protection and sin to vulnerability, represents a standard interpretation of Jewish history that the rabbis would both affirm and complicate. The book of Job and Ecclesiastes challenge this theology from within the canon, while Judith affirms it through narrative. Jewish readers appreciate the complex theological conversation that emerges from reading these diverse perspectives together.
