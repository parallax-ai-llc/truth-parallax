Islamic engagement with Judith 16 would affirm the hymn's central declaration of God's sovereignty over all nations and creation. The theological content of the song, praising God as incomparably great and warning against opposing divine will, is fully consistent with Quranic theology. The Quran's own hymnic passages, particularly the Ayat al-Kursi (2:255) and the closing surahs, express similar themes of divine majesty and power.

The epilogue's description of Judith's pious, disciplined life and honored death resonates with the Islamic ideal of the righteous person who lives and dies in service to God. The period of peace that followed her faithfulness illustrates the Quranic principle that communities that maintain their worship and obedience are granted stability and blessing. Muslim readers would recognize in Judith's character many qualities valued in Islamic tradition: faith, courage, discipline, and trust in God.
