Islamic engagement with Judith 3 would focus on the chapter's depiction of nations submitting to worship a human ruler as their god. This represents the gravest sin in Islam: shirk, associating partners with God or attributing divine status to a created being. The Quran's sustained polemic against idol worship and the deification of human rulers finds a narrative illustration in this chapter's description of universal capitulation.

The contrast between the nations that accept Nebuchadnezzar's claim to divinity and Israel's refusal mirrors the Quranic contrast between those who practice shirk and the monotheistic believers (mu'minun) who worship God alone. Muslim readers would affirm Israel's resistance as consistent with the fundamental Islamic principle of tawhid and reject the nations' submission as the epitome of spiritual corruption.
