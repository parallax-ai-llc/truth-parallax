## Overview

The Apocryphon of John is one of the most extensively studied texts from the Nag Hammadi library. Its survival in four copies — two in the longer recension (Codex II and Codex IV) and two in the shorter (Codex III and Berlin Codex 8502) — makes it uniquely valuable for textual criticism. Scholars including Michael Waldstein, Frederik Wisse, and Karen King have produced critical editions and detailed analyses of the text's literary history, theological framework, and cultural context.

## Classification and Origins

The text is classified as a foundational document of Sethian Gnosticism, a category developed by scholars such as Hans-Martin Schenke and John Turner. Its cosmological system draws on Middle Platonic philosophy (particularly the triad of the One, Mind, and Soul), Jewish wisdom traditions, and early Christian revelation genres. The dating of the text remains debated, with most scholars placing the earliest stratum in the second century CE, though some elements may reflect first-century traditions.

## Significance for the Field

The Apocryphon of John has been central to academic debates about the origins and nature of Gnosticism. It provides the most complete surviving example of the Sethian cosmological myth, allowing scholars to reconstruct the theological system that Irenaeus described and refuted. The text also raises important questions about the relationship between "orthodox" and "heterodox" Christianity, the role of Jewish traditions in the formation of Gnostic thought, and the influence of Platonic philosophy on early Christian theology.
