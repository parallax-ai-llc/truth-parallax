The Monad theology of chapter 1 has been extensively analyzed for its relationship to Middle Platonic and Neoplatonic thought. The extended negative description of the supreme principle closely parallels Alcinous's description of the First God in the Didaskalikos and anticipates Plotinus's account of the One in the Enneads. Scholars debate whether the Apocryphon drew directly on Platonic philosophical texts, mediated Platonism through Jewish wisdom traditions, or developed its negative theology independently from common intellectual roots.

Textual comparison of the four manuscript versions reveals significant variation in chapter 1, particularly between the longer and shorter recensions. The longer versions expand the negative descriptions of the Monad with additional epithets and qualifications, suggesting a process of theological elaboration over time. John Turner and other scholars have used these variations to reconstruct the text's literary history, arguing that the negative theology was progressively intensified as the text was transmitted and revised within Sethian communities.
