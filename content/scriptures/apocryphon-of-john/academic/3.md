Chapter 3's account of Sophia's fall and Yaldabaoth's creation has generated extensive scholarly discussion. The Sophia myth — in which a divine being's unauthorized creative act produces the material world — is recognized as one of the most distinctive and consequential innovations of Gnostic thought. Scholars debate whether the myth originated as a reinterpretation of Jewish wisdom traditions (particularly Proverbs 8 and Sirach 24), as a response to the problem of theodicy, or as a philosophical meditation on the Platonic distinction between the intelligible and sensible worlds.

The archon lists in chapter 3 vary significantly between the four manuscripts, providing important evidence for the text's redactional history. The names combine Jewish angelic names, Hebrew divine epithets, and what appear to be magical or liturgical terms. Michael Williams, Karen King, and others have analyzed these lists for evidence of the Apocryphon's relationship to Jewish magical traditions, early Christian demonology, and other Nag Hammadi texts. Comparison with Irenaeus's summary in Against Heresies 1.29 reveals both close correspondences and significant divergences, illuminating the text's transmission history.
