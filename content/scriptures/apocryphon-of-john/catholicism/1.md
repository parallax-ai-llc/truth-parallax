Catholic theology recognizes a legitimate tradition of apophatic discourse about God — the Pseudo-Dionysian tradition of via negativa has deep roots in Catholic mystical theology. However, the Apocryphon's use of negative theology serves a fundamentally different purpose. Where Catholic apophaticism affirms that God transcends human language while remaining knowable through revelation and analogy, the Apocryphon uses the Monad's transcendence to sever the supreme God from the created order entirely, creating space for the Demiurge.

Irenaeus, in his refutation of the system presented in this chapter, argued that the multiplication of divine beings between God and creation was both philosophically unnecessary and theologically dangerous. Catholic theology affirms that God creates directly, without intermediaries, and that his transcendence does not prevent him from being intimately present to his creation. The Apocryphon's Monad, by contrast, is so removed from the material world that it bears no responsibility for creation — a position the Catholic tradition firmly rejects.
