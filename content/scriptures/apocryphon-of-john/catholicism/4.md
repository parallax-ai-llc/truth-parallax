The Catholic understanding of the human person differs profoundly from the anthropology presented in chapter 4. Where the Apocryphon presents the body as a prison constructed by archons to entrap divine light, Catholic theology teaches that the body is an integral part of the human person, created by God and destined for resurrection. The doctrine of the incarnation — that God became flesh in Jesus Christ — represents the ultimate affirmation of the body's dignity and goodness.

Catholic sacramental theology further underscores this difference. The sacraments employ material elements — water, bread, wine, oil — as vehicles of divine grace, affirming that matter can be sanctified and made a means of encounter with God. The Apocryphon's view that matter is inherently a mechanism of entrapment is incompatible with a sacramental worldview in which the material and spiritual interpenetrate. The chapter's "counterfeit spirit" also contrasts with the Catholic teaching on the Holy Spirit's genuine indwelling in believers through baptism and confirmation.
