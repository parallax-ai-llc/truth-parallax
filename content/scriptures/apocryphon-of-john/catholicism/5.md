Catholic Christology stands in direct opposition to the Savior figure presented in chapter 5. The Catholic Church confesses, following the Council of Chalcedon (451), that Christ is truly God and truly human, possessing two complete natures united in one person. The Apocryphon's Savior, by contrast, descends as a revealer without truly becoming human — a position the Church classifies as docetism, one of the earliest and most persistent christological heresies.

The chapter's soteriology — salvation through gnosis rather than through faith, sacraments, and grace — also conflicts with Catholic teaching. The Catholic Church teaches that salvation comes through Christ's sacrificial death and resurrection, applied to believers through the sacraments and lived out in the community of the Church. The Apocryphon replaces this with an intellectualist model in which knowledge alone saves. The text's division of humanity into categories based on their capacity to receive gnosis also conflicts with the Catholic affirmation of the universal salvific will of God — that God desires the salvation of all people, not merely a pneumatic elite.
