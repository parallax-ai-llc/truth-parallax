The emanation system described in chapter 2 was a primary target of Irenaeus's *Against Heresies*. Irenaeus devoted extensive sections to refuting the Barbeloite system, arguing that the multiplication of divine beings through emanation undermined the unity and simplicity of God. Catholic theology, following the Councils and the Fathers, affirms that God is absolutely simple — not composed of parts or layers — and that the three divine persons of the Trinity are co-equal and co-eternal, not stages in a chain of emanation.

The Apocryphon's Pleroma, with its hierarchy of aeons arranged in syzygies (male-female pairs), also conflicts with the Catholic understanding of the heavenly realm. While Catholic theology acknowledges hierarchies of angels, it does not posit divine beings between God and the angelic orders. The Pleroma's structure served, in Irenaeus's view, to make salvation unnecessarily complex — requiring knowledge of elaborate divine genealogies rather than simple faith in the one God revealed in Christ and transmitted through the Church.
