Catholic doctrine explicitly contradicts the central claim of chapter 3. The Fourth Lateran Council, the First Vatican Council, and the Catechism all affirm that God — the one, true, good God — created all things visible and invisible. The Apocryphon's claim that the material world is the product of an ignorant, arrogant Demiurge born from Sophia's error is irreconcilable with this teaching. For Catholic theology, creation is not a cosmic accident but a deliberate act of divine love and wisdom.

The chapter's portrayal of Yaldabaoth declaring "I am God; there is none besides me" — echoing Isaiah 45:5 — represents a hermeneutical move that the Fathers found particularly objectionable. By placing the words of YHWH in the mouth of a monstrous Demiurge, the Apocryphon effectively characterizes the God of the Old Testament as a blind pretender. Catholic theology insists on the unity of the Old and New Testaments and the continuity between the God of Israel and the Father of Jesus Christ, a position articulated against Marcion and the Gnostics in the second century.
