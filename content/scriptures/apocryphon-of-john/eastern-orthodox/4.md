Orthodox anthropology, shaped by the Fathers' reading of Genesis and by the doctrine of the incarnation, affirms that the human person — body and soul together — is created in the image and likeness of God and destined for theosis, participation in the divine nature. The Apocryphon's view of the body as an archontic prison designed to trap divine light is irreconcilable with this vision. The incarnation of the Logos — God taking on human flesh — represents Orthodoxy's definitive affirmation that the body is not a prison but a temple.

The Orthodox understanding of the image of God (Imago Dei) also differs fundamentally from the Apocryphon's concept of the divine spark. In Orthodox thought, the image of God pertains to the whole person and is a gift of the Creator, not a fragment of stolen divine substance. The goal of human life is theosis — growing in likeness to God through grace, prayer, and the sacramental life — rather than the Gnostic goal of liberating a pre-existing divine particle from material entrapment. The Apocryphon's anthropology thus distorts both the origin and the destiny of the human person.
