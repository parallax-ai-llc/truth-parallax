The Orthodox Church confesses, with the Council of Chalcedon, that Christ is perfect in divinity and perfect in humanity, truly God and truly human. The Apocryphon's Savior figure, who descends as a revealer without genuine incarnation, represents the docetic heresy that the Fathers repeatedly condemned. Orthodox soteriology insists that "what is not assumed is not healed" (Gregory of Nazianzus) — if Christ did not truly become human, humanity cannot truly be saved. The Apocryphon's docetic Christology thus undermines the very possibility of salvation.

Orthodox soteriology also differs from the Apocryphon's in its mechanism: salvation is not primarily through knowledge but through participation in the divine life, made possible by Christ's incarnation, death, and resurrection. The sacraments — baptism, chrismation, and above all the Eucharist — are the means by which believers are united to Christ and begin the process of theosis. The Apocryphon's intellectualist soteriology, in which knowledge alone liberates, bypasses the incarnational and sacramental framework that Orthodox Christianity considers essential.
