The emanation scheme described in chapter 2 conflicts with the Orthodox doctrine of the Trinity on multiple levels. Orthodox theology confesses that the Son is "begotten, not made, of one substance with the Father" — a formulation specifically designed to exclude emanationist models in which divine beings proceed from the source with diminishing degrees of divinity. Barbelo, as the first emanation, occupies a position structurally analogous to the Son, but the parallel breaks down entirely: Barbelo is one of many emanations, each progressively further from the source.

The Orthodox tradition also rejects the Pleroma's elaborate structure of paired aeons as an unnecessary multiplication of divine beings. The celestial hierarchies described by Dionysius the Areopagite — seraphim, cherubim, thrones, and so forth — are understood as created angelic beings, not divine emanations. The distinction between uncreated God and created beings is absolute in Orthodox thought, whereas the Apocryphon's Pleroma occupies an ambiguous middle ground between the Monad and the material creation.
