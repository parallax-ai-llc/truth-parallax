Orthodox theology possesses a rich apophatic tradition — the Cappadocian Fathers and later Dionysius the Areopagite insisted that God's essence is ultimately unknowable. In this narrow sense, the Apocryphon's negative descriptions of the Monad resemble Orthodox theological language. However, the resemblance is superficial. Orthodox apophatic theology, especially as developed by Gregory Palamas, distinguishes between God's unknowable essence and his knowable energies — his real, active self-communication to creation. The Apocryphon makes no such distinction; its Monad is wholly inaccessible.

The Orthodox tradition also maintains that the unknowable God is nevertheless the Creator of all things. The Nicene Creed confesses one God, "the Father Almighty, Maker of heaven and earth." The Apocryphon's separation of the transcendent Monad from the act of creation — introducing Barbelo, aeons, and ultimately the Demiurge as intermediaries — contradicts this fundamental Orthodox confession. Divine transcendence, in Orthodox thought, does not entail divine absence from creation but rather grounds God's free and loving act of bringing all things into being.
