Chapter 5's salvation narrative is distinctly Christian in its framing, centering on a Savior figure who descends from the Pleroma. This places the text firmly outside the boundaries of Jewish tradition, which does not recognize a salvific role for Jesus or any descending redeemer figure of this type. Jewish soteriology, to the extent the concept applies, centers on covenant faithfulness, Torah observance, repentance (teshuvah), and the hope for messianic redemption — not on the liberation of divine sparks through esoteric knowledge.

For Jewish scholars, chapter 5 is primarily significant as evidence of how early Christian movements developed soteriological frameworks that drew on and transformed Jewish concepts. The text's references to biblical figures and events — reinterpreted within the Gnostic framework — illustrate the process by which Jewish traditions were appropriated into increasingly foreign theological contexts. The chapter also demonstrates the widening gap between Jewish and Christian thought in the second century, as Christian Gnostic movements developed salvation narratives that were unintelligible within the framework of Jewish theology.
