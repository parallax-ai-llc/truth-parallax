The Apocryphon's account of Adam's creation inverts the Jewish reading of Genesis 2:7 — "the LORD God formed man of dust from the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life." In rabbinic tradition, this breath (neshamah) is a divine gift that endows humanity with consciousness, moral capacity, and the potential for relationship with God. The Apocryphon reinterprets this act as Sophia's light being inadvertently transferred to an archontic creation — a fundamentally different theological framework.

Jewish anthropology affirms the unity and goodness of the human person as created by God. The rabbinic tradition, while acknowledging the tension between the yetzer ha-tov (good inclination) and yetzer ha-ra (evil inclination), does not view the body itself as a prison or the material world as inherently hostile to the soul. The Apocryphon's dualistic anthropology — in which the body is an archontic trap and the soul a fragment of stolen divinity — departs entirely from the Jewish affirmation that "God saw everything that he had made, and indeed, it was very good" (Genesis 1:31).
