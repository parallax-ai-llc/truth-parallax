Jewish scholars note that the Apocryphon's description of the Monad bears a structural resemblance to later Kabbalistic descriptions of Ein Sof — the infinite, unknowable aspect of God that transcends all attributes. Both traditions employ negative theology to describe the divine source, and both posit a process by which the unknowable God gives rise to knowable emanations (the Pleroma in the Apocryphon, the Sefirot in Kabbalah). Whether these similarities reflect direct historical influence or parallel developments from common Jewish mystical roots remains a subject of scholarly debate.

However, the functional role of divine transcendence differs critically. In Jewish theology, even the most radically transcendent descriptions of God do not sever God from creation. The same God who is beyond all comprehension is also the Creator who sustains all things, gives the Torah, and enters into covenant with Israel. The Apocryphon uses the Monad's transcendence to introduce a lower, ignorant creator — a move that is fundamentally incompatible with Jewish monotheism in any of its forms.
