## Overview

The Apocryphon of John is not a Jewish text and holds no place in the Jewish canon or tradition. However, it is deeply dependent on Jewish scripture, particularly Genesis 1-6, and extensively reinterprets Jewish theological concepts — the creation narrative, the divine name, angelology, and the figure of Wisdom (Hokhmah/Sophia). Jewish scholars engage with the text primarily to understand how Jewish traditions were appropriated and transformed by Gnostic movements in the Second Temple and early rabbinic periods.

## Jewish Sources Transformed

The text takes the God of Genesis — YHWH, the Creator — and reinterprets this figure as Yaldabaoth, a blind and arrogant lesser deity. This radical inversion of Jewish theology represents one of the most extreme examples of anti-Jewish hermeneutics in antiquity. The Apocryphon draws on Jewish angelological traditions, particularly those found in 1 Enoch and Merkabah literature, but turns them toward a fundamentally different theological purpose: the angels become archons, and the heavenly throne room becomes a prison.

## Scholarly Engagement

Jewish scholars such as Gedaliahu Stroumsa and Guy Stroumsa have studied the Apocryphon of John as evidence for the complex relationship between Judaism and its sectarian offshoots in late antiquity. The text is seen as illustrating how Jewish mythological traditions — particularly those surrounding Wisdom, the divine name, and the angelic hierarchy — could be radically redeployed in ways that ultimately subverted the theological framework from which they originated.
