From a Protestant perspective, chapter 1's description of the Monad through extended negation raises important theological questions. While the Bible affirms that God transcends human comprehension (Isaiah 55:8-9), it also insists that God has made himself known through creation, through Scripture, and supremely through Jesus Christ. The Apocryphon's apophatic theology pushes divine transcendence to an extreme that effectively severs God from any relationship with the created order.

Protestant theologians note that the text's insistence on the Monad's utter unknowability undermines the biblical witness to God's self-revelation. The God of Scripture is not merely a philosophical abstraction defined by negation but a personal being who speaks, acts, loves, and enters into covenant relationship with his people. The Apocryphon's Monad, by contrast, is so radically transcendent that it cannot be the God who walks with Adam in the Garden, speaks to Moses from the burning bush, or becomes incarnate in Jesus Christ.
