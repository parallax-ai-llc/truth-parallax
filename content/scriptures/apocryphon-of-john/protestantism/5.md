Chapter 5's portrayal of the Savior as a revealer who descends to impart knowledge contrasts fundamentally with the Protestant understanding of Christ's saving work. Protestant soteriology centers on the cross and resurrection — Christ's substitutionary atonement for sin and his bodily victory over death. The Apocryphon's Savior does not suffer, die, or rise; he simply teaches. Salvation comes through receiving gnosis rather than through faith in Christ's atoning sacrifice.

Protestant theologians identify this as a docetic Christology — one that denies the reality of Christ's human nature and physical suffering. The New Testament insists that Christ came "in the flesh" (1 John 4:2) and that his death was a real, historical event with cosmic saving significance. The Apocryphon's framework, in which the Savior's descent is purely revelatory, empties the cross of its meaning and replaces the gospel of grace with a gospel of knowledge. This represents, from the Protestant perspective, the very error Paul warned against: "another gospel" (Galatians 1:6-9).
