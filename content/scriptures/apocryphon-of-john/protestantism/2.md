Protestant theology finds the emanation scheme of chapter 2 fundamentally at odds with the biblical doctrine of the Trinity. Where the Apocryphon presents a complex hierarchy of divine beings emanating from the Monad in a descending chain, Protestant doctrine affirms three co-equal, co-eternal persons in one God. The emanationist model implies degrees of divinity — Barbelo is less than the Monad, the aeons less than Barbelo — which contradicts the Nicene affirmation that the Son is "of one substance with the Father."

The Pleroma's elaborate structure of named aeons and luminaries also contrasts sharply with the biblical presentation of the heavenly realm. While Scripture acknowledges angelic beings, it nowhere presents the kind of speculative divine genealogy found here. Protestant interpreters see in this chapter an example of the theological speculation that Paul warned against in Colossians 2:18 and 1 Timothy 1:4 — "endless genealogies" and the worship of intermediate spiritual beings that distract from the simplicity and sufficiency of Christ.
