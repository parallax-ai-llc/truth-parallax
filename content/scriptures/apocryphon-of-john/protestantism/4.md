The Apocryphon's account of Adam's creation by the archons stands in sharp contrast to the Protestant reading of Genesis 2, in which God personally forms Adam and breathes life into him as an act of love and purpose. In the Apocryphon, Adam's body is a prison designed by malevolent beings to trap divine light. Protestant anthropology, by contrast, affirms that the human body is part of God's good creation, that the whole person — body and soul — bears the image of God, and that the body will be resurrected and glorified.

The text's concept of the "counterfeit spirit" — an archontic imitation of true spiritual life designed to keep humanity in ignorance — has no parallel in Protestant theology. While Protestantism acknowledges the reality of spiritual deception (2 Corinthians 11:14, "Satan disguises himself as an angel of light"), it does not share the Gnostic framework in which the entire material order is a deliberate mechanism of entrapment. Protestant theology affirms that creation, though fallen, continues to reveal God's glory and that the Holy Spirit — not a counterfeit — genuinely dwells in believers.
