## Overview

Protestantism does not accept the Apocryphon of John as canonical or authoritative scripture. The text's cosmology — positing an ignorant creator god distinct from the true supreme deity — stands in fundamental opposition to Protestant theology, which affirms the goodness of creation and the identity of the Creator with the God revealed in Jesus Christ. Protestant engagement with the text is primarily historical and apologetic, seeking to understand the diversity of early Christianity and the theological errors the early church confronted.

## Historical Interest

Protestant scholars have studied the Apocryphon of John extensively since its discovery at Nag Hammadi in 1945. The text is recognized as an invaluable primary source for understanding the Gnostic movements that the church fathers, particularly Irenaeus, wrote against. Its elaborate reinterpretation of Genesis serves as a cautionary example of how scripture can be radically reread when detached from apostolic tradition and the rule of faith.

## Theological Critique

From a Protestant perspective, the Apocryphon of John represents a fundamental distortion of the biblical message. Its radical dualism between spirit and matter contradicts the biblical affirmation that creation is good (Genesis 1:31). Its docetic Christology — presenting a Savior who descends as a revealer but does not truly suffer — undermines the centrality of the cross. Protestant theologians view the text as illustrating the importance of scriptural boundaries and sound hermeneutics.
