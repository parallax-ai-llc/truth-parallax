Chapter 3 presents the most direct challenge to Protestant creation theology. The identification of the biblical Creator with an ignorant, arrogant Demiurge named Yaldabaoth inverts the central affirmation of Genesis 1 — that God created all things and declared them good. Protestant theology insists on the identity of the Creator and Redeemer: the God who made the world is the same God who saves it. The Apocryphon's splitting of these functions between the unknowable Monad and the ignorant Yaldabaoth is seen as a fundamental theological error.

Protestant commentators also reject the text's explanation of evil. Where the Apocryphon locates the origin of evil in Sophia's unauthorized emanation and Yaldabaoth's ignorance, Protestant theology maintains that evil entered a good creation through the free choice of created beings to rebel against God. The Gnostic explanation removes moral responsibility from the equation, replacing it with a metaphysical drama of divine accidents — an approach that Protestant theologians find incompatible with the biblical emphasis on human accountability before a just and sovereign God.
