The thirteenth chapter teaches that the offering of the Dharma surpasses all material offerings. This is a teaching broadly shared with the Theravada tradition, where the gift of Dhamma (dhamma-dana) is described as the highest gift. The Dhammapada states: "The gift of Dhamma surpasses all gifts."

The chapter elaborates on what constitutes a genuine Dharma offering: following the sutras, practicing in accord with reality, taking up the burden of all beings' suffering, and maintaining the continuity of the Three Jewels. The Theravada tradition similarly values the preservation and practice of the teaching as the supreme offering.

Where the traditions diverge is in the scope of the offering. The Mahayana framework presented here extends the offering of Dharma to encompass the bodhisattva's dedication of all merit to the liberation of every sentient being, a universalist aspiration that goes beyond the Theravada emphasis on individual liberation and the sharing of merit with specific recipients.
