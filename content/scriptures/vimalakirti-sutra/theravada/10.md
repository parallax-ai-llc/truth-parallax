The tenth chapter continues the theme of the fragrant meal, with bodhisattvas from the Fragrant buddha land marveling at the conditions in Shakyamuni's world. The contrast between pure and impure buddha lands raises questions about why a buddha would choose to manifest in a world of suffering.

The Theravada tradition addresses a related question through the concept of the Buddha's perfections (paramis) developed over countless lifetimes specifically to bring awakening to beings in difficult circumstances. The Buddhavamsa describes how each Buddha arises in a specific world-system according to conditions and the maturation of beings' merit.

The bodhisattvas from the Fragrant land learn that practice in a defiled world produces faster spiritual progress than in a pure land. This echoes the Theravada recognition that the human realm, with its mixture of pleasure and pain, is the optimal condition for developing insight, as opposed to the heavenly realms where comfort can obstruct diligent practice.
