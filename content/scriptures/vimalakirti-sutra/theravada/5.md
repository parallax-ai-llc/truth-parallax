Manjushri, the bodhisattva of wisdom, finally agrees to visit the ailing Vimalakirti, initiating a profound dialogue on the nature of illness, the body, and emptiness. From a Theravada perspective, the meditation on the body's impermanence and insubstantiality resonates with the Satipatthana Sutta's instructions on contemplation of the body (kayanupassana).

Vimalakirti's teaching that the bodhisattva's illness arises from great compassion for all beings introduces a dimension absent from Theravada practice: the voluntary assumption of suffering out of solidarity with sentient beings. The Theravada tradition emphasizes liberation from suffering rather than its deliberate embrace.

The dialogue on how a bodhisattva should console another bodhisattva who is ill touches on the relationship between conventional care and ultimate truth. This mirrors the Theravada practice of visiting the sick (gilana-paccaya) as one of the four requisites of monastic support, though the metaphysical framework differs significantly.
