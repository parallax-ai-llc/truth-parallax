This chapter introduces Vimalakirti's skillful means, particularly his feigning of illness as a teaching device. The Theravada tradition recognizes upaya (skillful means) primarily in the Buddha's own pedagogical adaptations, such as his use of similes, graduated instruction, and his accommodation to the capacities of different audiences as described in the Pali Nikayas.

Vimalakirti's pretense of illness to draw visitors represents a more dramatic form of skillful means than is typically found in the Pali canon. The Theravada commentarial tradition discusses the Buddha's knowledge of the appropriate teaching for each individual (puggala-paropariyatti-nana), which serves a similar function in adapting the dharma to specific circumstances.

The chapter raises the question of how far a teacher may go in using unconventional methods. Theravada ethics generally emphasize straightforwardness (ujukata) as a virtue, creating an interesting tension with the Mahayana celebration of deceptive means employed for compassionate ends.
