The third chapter narrates how the Buddha's foremost disciples, including Shariputra, Maudgalyayana, Mahakashyapa, and Subhuti, each decline to visit the ailing Vimalakirti, recounting past encounters in which the layman bested them in doctrinal debate. For the Theravada tradition, these figures are revered as the Buddha's greatest disciples.

The portrayals here reflect a characteristically Mahayana literary strategy of showing the limitations of the Sravaka path by depicting its greatest representatives as spiritually incomplete. Theravada readers may note that the Pali canon presents these same figures as fully liberated arahants with profound wisdom and attainments.

This tension between the two portrayals reveals different soteriological frameworks. The Theravada tradition holds that the arahant's liberation is complete and genuine, while the Mahayana perspective presented here suggests that even accomplished arahants may lack the fullness of the bodhisattva's non-dual wisdom and universal compassion.
