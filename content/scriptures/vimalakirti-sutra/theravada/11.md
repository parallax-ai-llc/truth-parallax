This chapter addresses the proper conduct of bodhisattvas in the world, teaching how awakened beings should engage with society while maintaining their inner realization. The Theravada tradition has its own extensive framework for ethical conduct through the Vinaya, the Sigalovada Sutta's teachings for householders, and the graduated path of sila, samadhi, and panna.

The bodhisattva ideal presented here emphasizes active engagement with worldly affairs, including entering places of gambling, drinking, and political power, in order to guide beings toward liberation. This contrasts with the Theravada monastic emphasis on withdrawal from worldly entanglements, though the tradition does recognize the role of lay practice and social engagement.

The chapter's teaching on the "inexhaustible lamp," where one candle lights many without being diminished, parallels the Theravada concept of the teacher-student transmission of the Dhamma, where sharing the teaching diminishes nothing. Both traditions affirm that the Dharma is enriched rather than depleted through generous sharing.
