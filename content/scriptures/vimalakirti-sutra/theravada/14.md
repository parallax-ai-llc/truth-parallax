The final chapter records the Buddha's entrustment of the sutra's teaching to Maitreya and the assembled bodhisattvas, ensuring its preservation and transmission for the benefit of future generations. The Theravada tradition has its own account of entrustment in the Mahaparinibbana Sutta, where the dying Buddha instructs his disciples to let the Dhamma and Vinaya be their teacher.

The emphasis on preserving and propagating the teaching resonates across both traditions, though their canons and methods of transmission differ. The Theravada tradition places great value on memorization and oral recitation, codified through three major councils, while the Mahayana traditions developed new literary forms and commentarial methods.

The sutra concludes with a vision of dharma preservation that is characteristically Mahayana in its scope, involving cosmic bodhisattvas and protective deities. The Theravada tradition maintains its own framework for protecting the sasana (dispensation), centered on the threefold division of learning, practice, and realization.
