The sixth chapter presents Vimalakirti's most dramatic demonstrations of inconceivable liberation: fitting an enormous throne into his tiny room, placing the universe on a mustard seed, and similar feats that defy conventional physics. The Theravada tradition recognizes supernatural powers (iddhi) as attainments available to advanced meditators, described extensively in the Patisambhidamagga.

However, the scale and metaphysical implications differ. In the Pali canon, psychic powers operate within a relatively conventional framework, while the Vimalakirti Sutra uses these demonstrations to illustrate the Mahayana teaching that emptiness and form are mutually interpenetrating. When large fits within small without either changing size, the conventional categories of spatial reasoning collapse.

Theravada tradition also cautions against attachment to supernatural displays, with the Buddha discouraging public demonstrations of psychic powers. The Vimalakirti Sutra repurposes such displays as pedagogical tools for teaching the nature of reality.
