The opening chapter presents the Buddha teaching about the nature of purified buddha lands. From a Theravada perspective, the concept of purifying a buddha field can be compared with the Pali canon's descriptions of the Buddha's sphere of influence and the conditions that make a world-system conducive to the arising of a Buddha. The Theravada tradition does not emphasize the bodhisattva's role in purifying lands in the same way, but it recognizes that the moral quality of beings shapes their environment.

The chapter introduces the theme that the purity of a buddha land reflects the purity of a bodhisattva's mind. This internal-external correspondence resonates with the Theravada teaching in the Anguttara Nikaya that when the mind is purified, one's experience of the world is transformed. The emphasis on mental purification as the foundation of all spiritual progress is common ground between the traditions.

The teaching that the land appears pure to those with purified minds and impure to others parallels the Theravada understanding that perception is conditioned by one's mental state and level of realization.
