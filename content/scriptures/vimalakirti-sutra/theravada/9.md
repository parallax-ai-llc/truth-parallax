In this chapter, Vimalakirti sends an emanation to a distant buddha land called Sarvagandhasugandha (All Fragrances) to obtain fragrant rice for the assembled company. The emanation bodhisattva travels across countless buddha fields and returns with food that nourishes all present and produces extraordinary effects.

The Theravada tradition includes accounts of miraculous provisions, such as the stories of divine food (dibba-ahara) in the Vimana Vatthu and the celestial offerings described in the Jataka tales. However, the cosmological scale of the Vimalakirti Sutra, with its vision of innumerable buddha lands across infinite space, extends far beyond the Theravada cosmology of a single world-system with its Tusita heaven.

The teaching that food from a pure land can transform those who eat it has no direct Theravada parallel, though the tradition does recognize that the quality of one's nourishment, both physical and spiritual, affects one's mental state and capacity for practice.
