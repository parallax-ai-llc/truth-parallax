Vimalakirti's silence as the ultimate expression of non-duality finds profound parallels across the world's contemplative traditions. The apophatic theology of Pseudo-Dionysius teaches that God is beyond all speech and all knowing, accessible only through unknowing. The Daoist teaching that "the Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao" (Laozi, chapter 1) makes the same point about the inadequacy of language before ultimate reality.

The Quaker tradition of silent worship, where the community sits in shared silence waiting for the Spirit to speak, provides an interesting structural parallel. In both cases, silence is understood not as the absence of communication but as a higher form of it, one that transcends the limitations of conceptual thought.

The Jewish mystical tradition similarly recognizes the limits of language before the divine. The Tetragrammaton (YHWH), the unpronounceable name of God, points to a reality that exceeds all naming. The Sufi concept of fana (annihilation in the divine) describes a state where the mystic's individual voice falls silent in the presence of ultimate reality. Vimalakirti's silence belongs to this universal family of sacred wordlessness.
