Vimalakirti's use of feigned illness as a teaching device resonates with the cross-traditional archetype of the wounded healer: the spiritual figure whose suffering becomes a source of wisdom and healing for others. In Greek mythology, Chiron the centaur teaches medicine despite bearing an incurable wound. In Christianity, Jesus's suffering on the cross becomes the instrument of salvation.

The concept of skillful means, where a teacher employs unconventional methods for the student's benefit, appears across traditions. The Sufi master who assigns bizarre tasks, the Hasidic rebbe who tells stories, the Christian spiritual director who gives unexpected counsel: each adapts the teaching to the student's needs rather than applying a uniform method.

The question of whether deception can serve spiritual purposes is debated across traditions. Plato's "noble lie" in the Republic, the Hindu tradition of divine lila (play), and the Mahayana concept of upaya all address the tension between absolute truthfulness and the pragmatic adaptation of teaching to the capacities of different audiences.
