The teaching that the gift of wisdom surpasses material gifts finds echoes across traditions. The Jewish tradition elevates Torah study above all other commandments. The Islamic hadith tradition records Muhammad saying, "The superiority of knowledge over worship is like my superiority over the least of you." The Hindu tradition of jnana-yoga (the path of knowledge) is often considered the highest of the four yogas.

The emphasis on preserving sacred teachings resonates with the anxiety about transmission that appears across traditions. The Jewish concept of masorah (tradition), the Islamic science of hadith authentication (isnad), and the Christian concern with apostolic succession all reflect the conviction that authentic teaching must be carefully preserved against corruption and loss.

The chapter's vision of dharma offering as the supreme spiritual practice connects with the broader cross-traditional theme of knowledge as sacred gift. The Greek concept of paideia (education as formation of the soul), the Confucian emphasis on learning and teaching as the highest human activities, and the Enlightenment ideal of universal education all, in different ways, echo the Mahayana conviction that sharing wisdom is the greatest possible act of generosity.
