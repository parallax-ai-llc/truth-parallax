The goddess's transformation of Shariputra raises questions about gender and spiritual identity that resonate across religious traditions. Many indigenous and ancient traditions recognize gender fluidity as a sign of spiritual power. The Navajo nadleehi, the Indian hijra tradition, and the Siberian shamanic practice of cross-gender transformation all associate gender crossing with enhanced spiritual capacity.

The exchange challenges gender essentialism in a way that parallels contemporary feminist theology across traditions. Christian feminists such as Rosemary Radford Ruether have questioned whether maleness is essential to the image of God. Islamic feminists have distinguished between the Quran's egalitarian message and later patriarchal interpretations. The Vimalakirti Sutra's teaching that spiritual qualities have no gender anticipates these modern critiques.

The goddess's demonstration also resonates with the Hindu concept of Ardhanarishvara, the half-male, half-female form of Shiva that represents the integration of masculine and feminine principles in the divine. The Daoist concept of the integration of yin and yang within the realized sage offers another parallel. Each tradition, in its own way, recognizes that wholeness transcends gender categories.
