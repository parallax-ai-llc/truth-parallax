The teaching that spiritual practice in a difficult world produces faster progress than in an ideal environment finds parallels across traditions. The Christian concept of "the way of the cross," where suffering becomes a path to sanctification, reflects a similar revaluation of adversity. The Jewish tradition of tikkun olam (repair of the world) teaches that the broken world is precisely the arena where spiritual work must be done.

The Sufi teaching that the nafs (ego-self) is refined through friction with the world, much as gold is purified through fire, expresses the same insight through different imagery. The Hindu concept of tapas (spiritual heat generated through ascetic practice) similarly recognizes that difficulty and resistance can be transformed into spiritual fuel.

The contrast between the Fragrant Land bodhisattvas' comfortable practice and the intense engagement of Shakyamuni's world mirrors the theological debate in Christianity between the contemplative and active lives. Thomas Merton's integration of monastic contemplation with social engagement, and the liberation theology movement's insistence on praxis amid injustice, reflect the same conviction that the impure world is the proper arena for spiritual transformation.
