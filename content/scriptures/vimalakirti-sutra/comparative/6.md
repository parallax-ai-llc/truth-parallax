The demonstrations of inconceivable liberation, where large fits within small and multiple dimensions coexist without obstruction, find parallels in the mystical literature of many traditions. The Christian mystic Nicholas of Cusa described God as the "coincidence of opposites" (coincidentia oppositorum), where all contradictions are reconciled. The Kabbalistic concept of tzimtzum, where the infinite God contracts to make room for creation, addresses a similar paradox of the infinite dwelling within the finite.

The Hindu tradition offers the image of Vishnu as the cosmic dwarf Vamana who, given three steps of land by the demon king Bali, expands to encompass the entire universe. The Jain concept of the soul (jiva) existing as a point yet pervading the body also explores the paradox of the dimensionless containing the dimensional.

These cross-traditional paradoxes of scale and containment point to a common mystical intuition: that the categories of ordinary spatial reasoning do not apply to ultimate reality. The inconceivable liberation of Vimalakirti, the infinite God dwelling in a mustard seed (Hildegard of Bingen), and the Daoist sage who contains heaven and earth all express the insight that reality transcends conventional measurement.
