The journey to a distant sacred realm to obtain spiritual nourishment resonates with mythological and religious narratives across cultures. The Christian Eucharist, in which bread and wine become the body and blood of Christ, similarly transforms ordinary food into a vehicle of divine grace. The Jewish tradition of manna from heaven provided supernatural sustenance to the Israelites in the wilderness.

The concept of a realm where teaching occurs through fragrance rather than words finds parallels in traditions that associate the sacred with specific sensory experiences. The use of incense in Christian, Hindu, Buddhist, and indigenous ceremonies reflects an intuition that spiritual reality can be communicated through scent. The Islamic tradition describes paradise as a place of extraordinary fragrances.

The emanation bodhisattva's ability to travel across cosmic distances instantaneously parallels the shamanic journey to other worlds found in indigenous traditions globally. The Australian Aboriginal Dreamtime, the Celtic Otherworld, and the Norse concept of traveling between the nine worlds all describe the ability of spiritual adepts to traverse dimensions of reality inaccessible to ordinary consciousness.
