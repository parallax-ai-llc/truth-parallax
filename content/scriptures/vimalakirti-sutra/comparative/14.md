The entrustment of sacred teaching to future custodians is a theme that appears at the conclusion of many sacred texts and traditions. Moses entrusts the Torah to Joshua before his death. Jesus commissions his disciples to "go and teach all nations" (Matthew 28:19). Muhammad's farewell sermon entrusts the Quran and Sunnah to the Muslim community. Each scene establishes both the text's authority and the community's responsibility.

The concern about the corruption or loss of authentic teaching parallels anxieties across traditions. The Jewish concept of the Oral Torah being gradually forgotten, the Christian fear of heresy, the Islamic concern with bid'ah (innovation in religion), and the Hindu concept of cosmic decline through the four yugas all reflect the perception that sacred knowledge is vulnerable to degradation over time.

The sutra's concluding emphasis on the rarity and preciousness of those who can understand deep teachings resonates with the esoteric traditions within many religions: the Kabbalistic concept of hidden wisdom accessible to few, the Sufi distinction between exoteric and esoteric knowledge, the Christian tradition of the "disciplina arcani," and the Hindu concept of adhikara (qualification for receiving teaching). Each tradition recognizes that not all teachings are equally accessible to all hearers.
