The inexhaustible lamp of bodhisattva conduct, where one flame kindles others without diminishing, finds parallels across traditions. The Jewish Hanukkah tradition of lighting candles, the Christian symbol of the paschal candle from which all other candles are lit, and the Zoroastrian sacred fire that is never extinguished all employ the image of inextinguishable light as a symbol of spiritual transmission.

The bodhisattva's ability to enter all social environments without being corrupted parallels the Christian concept of being "in the world but not of it" (John 17:14-16). The Sufi tradition's ideal of the mystic who operates within society while maintaining inner detachment (zuhd) expresses the same aspiration. The Bhagavad Gita's teaching on nishkama karma (action without attachment to results) offers yet another framework for engaged spiritual practice without contamination.

The question of how spiritual practitioners should relate to ethical norms when guided by higher insight is debated across traditions. Antinomian movements in Christianity, the concept of jivanmukta (liberated while living) in Hinduism, and the Zen teaching of "no-mind" all address the tension between conventional morality and the spontaneous ethical expression of the realized person.
