The teaching that the bodhisattva's illness arises from compassion parallels the Christian concept of sympathetic suffering. Saint Paul wrote, "Who is weak, and I am not weak?" (2 Corinthians 11:29). The Jewish concept of tzaar baalei chayim (the pain of all living creatures) and the Sufi teaching on the perfect human (al-insan al-kamil) who bears the suffering of creation all describe a realized being whose consciousness encompasses the pain of others.

The dialogue on the nature of the body as empty parallels the contemplative traditions' teachings on the illusory nature of the physical self. The Hindu teaching on the five sheaths (pancha kosha) progressively strips away layers of identification to reveal the atman. The Christian mystic Teresa of Avila described the body as the outer walls of the Interior Castle, necessary but not to be confused with the soul within.

The question of how to console a suffering spiritual practitioner is addressed differently across traditions. The Christian tradition emphasizes the value of redemptive suffering. The Buddhist tradition emphasized here points to the recognition of emptiness. The Stoic tradition counsels recognition that suffering arises from judgment rather than from external events. Each offers a distinctive path from suffering to peace.
