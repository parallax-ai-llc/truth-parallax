The Buddha Land chapter's teaching that the purity of the world depends on the purity of the perceiver's mind finds parallels across religious traditions. The Christian mystic Meister Eckhart taught that the eye with which one sees God is the same eye with which God sees oneself. The Sufi poet Rumi wrote that the world is a mirror reflecting the observer's inner state. Hindu Advaita Vedanta teaches that the world of multiplicity is maya projected by the mind's ignorance of its true nature as Brahman.

The Buddhist concept of a purified buddha land can be compared with the Christian concept of the Kingdom of God as described by Jesus: "The kingdom of God is within you" (Luke 17:21). Both teachings locate the sacred realm not in a distant future or place but in a transformation of consciousness that reveals the holiness already present in the ordinary world.

The Daoist tradition offers another resonance: Zhuangzi's teaching that the sage sees the world as it is, without imposing human categories of pure and impure, beautiful and ugly. The sage's perception is pure not because the world has been cleaned up but because the mind has stopped dividing reality into preferred and rejected categories.
