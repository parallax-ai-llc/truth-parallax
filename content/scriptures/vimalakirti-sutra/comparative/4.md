The teaching that even advanced spiritual practitioners may harbor subtle delusions finds expression across traditions. The Christian mystical tradition speaks of the "dark night of the soul" (John of the Cross), where even advanced contemplatives must pass through a period of radical unknowing. The Sufi concept of maqamat (stations) recognizes that each spiritual stage brings its own characteristic temptations and attachments.

Vimalakirti's challenge to Maitreya's concept of future buddhahood resonates with the mystical critique of spiritual ambition found across traditions. Meister Eckhart taught that one must let go even of God in order to find the Godhead beyond God. The Hindu sage Ramana Maharshi warned against the subtle ego of the spiritual seeker who reifies the goal of enlightenment.

The chapter's dismantling of temporal categories in relation to spiritual attainment parallels the mystical teaching, found in multiple traditions, that ultimate reality transcends time. The Christian concept of God's eternal now (nunc stans), the Islamic understanding of divine timelessness, and the Hindu concept of the eternal Atman all challenge the assumption that liberation is an event occurring at a particular time.
