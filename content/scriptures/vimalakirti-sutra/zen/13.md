The offering of the Dharma is the offering of one's entire being. In Zen, this is expressed through the totality of practice: every bow, every step in kinhin, every strike of the bell is a complete offering. The monk who sweeps the monastery courtyard with full attention is making the supreme dharma offering.

The sutra distinguishes between those who delight in elaborate dharma and those who can penetrate profound meaning. Zen has always favored the latter, stripping away elaboration to expose the essential. Bodhidharma's "vast emptiness, nothing holy" is a dharma offering that surpasses volumes of commentary. Joshu's "Mu" is a dharma offering that has nourished practitioners for a thousand years.

The chapter teaches that preserving the dharma is the highest gift. In Zen, the dharma is preserved not in texts but in realization, transmitted from warm hand to warm hand across generations. Each authentic encounter between teacher and student is the offering of dharma, the lamp lighting another lamp, the tradition continuing through living practice.
