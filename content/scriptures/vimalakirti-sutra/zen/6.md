Fitting enormous thrones into a tiny room without the room expanding or the thrones shrinking. Pouring oceans into pores without disturbing the creatures within. From a Zen perspective, these are not miracles but descriptions of ordinary reality seen with awakened eyes. The small contains the large, and the large contains the small. This is the meaning of Zen Master Yunmen's teaching: "Every day is a good day."

The inconceivable liberation is inconceivable precisely because the conceptualizing mind cannot grasp it. When thinking stops, what remains? A mustard seed contains Mount Sumeru. This very breath contains the entire universe. Zen practice is the direct entry into this inconceivable reality, not through understanding but through the cessation of the effort to understand.

Hakuin's Song of Zazen says: "This very place is the Lotus Land, this very body the Buddha." The inconceivable liberation described in this chapter is available right here, right now, to anyone who stops trying to conceive it. The gateless gate is standing wide open.
