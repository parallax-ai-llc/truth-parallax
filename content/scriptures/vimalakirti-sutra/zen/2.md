Vimalakirti's skillful means are the activity of a Zen master operating in the marketplace rather than the monastery. His feigned illness is a brilliant trap, a dharma snare set with compassion and sprung with wisdom. Every great Zen master has employed such tactics: Linji's shouts, Deshan's blows, Gutei's single raised finger. Each disrupts ordinary thinking and opens a gap through which realization can enter.

The chapter shows that true teaching is not limited to formal dharma talks or meditation instruction. Vimalakirti teaches through his very way of being in the world: visiting wine shops, engaging in business, moving through all levels of society. This prefigures the Zen ideal that chopping wood and carrying water are themselves the path.

Zen practitioners reading this chapter should ask: what is my illness? What skillful means am I employing? If Vimalakirti could turn illness into teaching, what prevents any moment of life from becoming a turning word?
