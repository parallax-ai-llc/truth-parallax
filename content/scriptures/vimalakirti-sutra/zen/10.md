The bodhisattvas from the Fragrant Land are astonished by the difficulty of conditions in Shakyamuni's world. From a Zen perspective, their astonishment reveals their limitation. A pure land bodhisattva who has never faced the fire of defilements is like a sword that has never been tempered. Shakyamuni's teaching that practice in a defiled world produces rapid progress is the Zen understanding of practice itself.

Zen does not seek ideal conditions. The heat of the kitchen, the pain in the knees, the confusion of the busy mind: these are the fuel of awakening. Linji said, "If you meet the Buddha on the road, kill him." This is the teaching of the defiled world as the supreme practice ground: all obstacles are teachings, all enemies are benefactors.

The ten dharmas available only in the impure world include patience amid hostility and wisdom amid confusion. These are the very conditions of zazen, where the practitioner sits with whatever arises without preference. The sitting cushion in a noisy city is a more powerful teaching seat than a throne in a perfect paradise.
