The entrustment of the teaching to Maitreya and the assembly is, from a Zen perspective, both necessary and paradoxical. Necessary because the dharma must be transmitted. Paradoxical because the dharma that can be entrusted is not the true dharma. The finger pointing at the moon is not the moon.

Zen's own entrustment happens in the moment of transmission between master and student: Shakyamuni's flower and Mahakashyapa's smile, Bodhidharma's marrow and Huike's bow, Hongren's robe and Huineng's midnight departure. Each entrustment is unique, unrepeatable, and yet the same thing passes through each one.

The sutra ends, but practice does not end. The reader who puts down the text and sits in zazen continues the entrustment. The practitioner who meets daily life with open eyes and an open heart fulfills Vimalakirti's teaching more completely than any amount of study. The thundering silence continues to thunder. The lamp continues to burn. What has been entrusted is this very moment, just as it is.
