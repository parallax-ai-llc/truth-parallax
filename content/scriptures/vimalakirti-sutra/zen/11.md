The inexhaustible lamp of bodhisattva conduct is the transmission of dharma from master to student, from practitioner to practitioner. In Zen, this transmission does not require formal ceremony. It happens when a gesture, a word, or a silence sparks recognition in a ripe mind. One lamp lights another, endlessly.

Vimalakirti teaches that the bodhisattva enters every sphere of activity without being corrupted. This is the Zen ideal of the person of no rank, Linji's famous teaching of one who moves through all situations with complete freedom. Neither grasping nor avoiding, neither monk nor layperson, neither holy nor profane: this is the conduct of one who has seen through all categories.

The chapter's teaching on the bodhisattva's relationship to precepts reflects the Zen understanding that true morality arises spontaneously from realization. When the mind is clear, appropriate action follows naturally. The precepts are not abandoned but fulfilled in their deepest sense, as expressions of awakened nature rather than external rules imposed on a wayward self.
