The opening chapter establishes the fundamental Mahayana teaching that the purity of a buddha land is determined by the purity of a bodhisattva's mind. When Shariputra questions why Shakyamuni's world appears impure, the Buddha touches the earth with his toe and the entire world-system is revealed in its true splendor, demonstrating that impurity is a function of perception, not reality.

This teaching is central to the Mahayana understanding of the relationship between mind and world. The bodhisattva's path of purification is simultaneously a purification of how reality is perceived and a transformation of the conditions beings encounter. The doctrine has profound implications for Pure Land Buddhism, which understands Amitabha's pure land as the expression of infinite compassion and wisdom.

The chapter introduces the key Mahayana themes that will unfold throughout the sutra: the interpenetration of samsara and nirvana, the centrality of the bodhisattva vow, and the teaching that all distinctions between pure and impure ultimately dissolve in the light of prajna.
