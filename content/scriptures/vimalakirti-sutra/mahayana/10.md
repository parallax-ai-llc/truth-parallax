The bodhisattvas from the Fragrant Land express amazement at the difficult conditions in Shakyamuni's world, where beings suffer from ignorance, aggression, and craving. Shakyamuni explains that bodhisattvas in his world accomplish in one lifetime what takes eons of practice in a pure land, because the intensity of suffering produces correspondingly powerful compassion and wisdom.

This teaching is significant for the Mahayana understanding of the relationship between samsara and nirvana. Rather than being an obstacle to awakening, the suffering world becomes the optimal field of practice. The bodhisattva who chooses to remain in samsara for the benefit of beings is not making a sacrifice but is engaging with the most powerful conditions for spiritual development.

The chapter also teaches that the bodhisattvas of Shakyamuni's world practice ten dharmas unavailable in pure lands, including generosity amid poverty, ethical conduct amid temptation, and patience amid hostility. These practices of the impure world are presented as the most rapid path to supreme awakening.
