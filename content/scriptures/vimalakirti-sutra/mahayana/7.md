The exchange between the goddess and Shariputra provides one of the most radical teachings on emptiness in Mahayana literature. When Shariputra asks why she does not transform her female body, the goddess transforms Shariputra into a woman and herself into a man, then asks Shariputra to transform back. Unable to do so, Shariputra must acknowledge that gender is neither fixed nor essential.

This episode applies the Prajnaparamita teaching on emptiness to the question of gender and identity. If all dharmas are empty, then the categories "male" and "female" are conventional designations without ultimate reality. The goddess demonstrates this not through philosophical argument but through direct transformation, forcing experiential recognition of what conceptual teaching alone cannot convey.

The chapter has been widely discussed in contemporary Mahayana scholarship as an early articulation of gender fluidity within Buddhist thought. It challenges the assumption, present in some Buddhist texts, that a female body is a lesser birth, insisting that awakening has nothing to do with bodily form.
