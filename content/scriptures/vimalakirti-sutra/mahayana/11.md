The chapter on bodhisattva conduct describes how awakened beings navigate the world without being corrupted by it. The teaching of the "inexhaustible lamp" compares the bodhisattva's activity to a lamp that kindles others without being diminished. This image captures the Mahayana understanding that sharing the dharma and generating merit are inexhaustible activities.

The bodhisattva enters all situations, including those that would seem to compromise spiritual purity, because genuine realization cannot be defiled by contact with the world. Just as a lotus grows from mud but is not stained by it, the bodhisattva moves through samsara with complete freedom. This teaching has been central to the Mahayana justification of engaged Buddhist practice.

The chapter addresses the practical concern of how bodhisattvas should relate to precepts. The bodhisattva observes all precepts perfectly not through rigid adherence but through the spontaneous expression of wisdom and compassion. When the bodhisattva appears to transgress conventional morality, it is always in service of a deeper ethical commitment to the liberation of all beings.
