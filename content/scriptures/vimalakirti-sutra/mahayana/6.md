The sixth chapter's demonstrations of inconceivable liberation are among the most celebrated passages in Mahayana literature. Vimalakirti places thirty-two thousand lion thrones, each enormously tall, into his tiny room without the room expanding or the thrones shrinking. He pours the waters of an ocean into a single pore without disturbing the ocean's creatures.

These demonstrations illustrate the Mahayana teaching of the mutual non-obstruction of all phenomena (dharmadhatu). When one fully realizes emptiness, the conventional categories of large and small, inside and outside, one and many lose their binding power. This is not magic but the natural expression of a mind freed from conceptual impositions.

The chapter's imagery profoundly influenced later Huayan philosophy, particularly Fazang's teaching on the interpenetration of all phenomena. The inconceivable liberation described here is the lived reality of one who has realized that all distinctions are conventional designations without ultimate ground. Liberation is inconceivable precisely because it cannot be captured within any conceptual framework.
