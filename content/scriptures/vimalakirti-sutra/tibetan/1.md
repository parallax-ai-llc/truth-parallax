The opening chapter's teaching on the purification of buddha lands resonates with the Tibetan Buddhist understanding of pure perception (dag snang), a practice central to Vajrayana Buddhism. The Tibetan tradition teaches that through proper view and meditation, the practitioner can transform ordinary perception to recognize the world as a mandala, the pure display of enlightened qualities.

The Buddha's demonstration of touching the earth and revealing the hidden purity of the world parallels the Dzogchen teaching on the primordial purity (ka dag) of all phenomena. In both cases, impurity is understood as an adventitious obscuration rather than the fundamental nature of reality. The ground is already pure; only the recognition is lacking.

Tibetan commentators have connected this chapter to the practice of guru devotion, where seeing the teacher as a buddha and the environment as a pure land is not mere pretense but a method for accessing the deeper reality that ordinary perception obscures. The purification of the buddha land begins with the purification of how the student sees the teacher and the world.
