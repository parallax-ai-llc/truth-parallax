The Fragrant Land bodhisattvas' astonishment at conditions in Shakyamuni's world provides a teaching that Tibetan Buddhism has incorporated into its understanding of the precious human rebirth (dal 'byor). The Tibetan lam rim (stages of the path) tradition emphasizes that human birth in a world where suffering is present offers unique opportunities for spiritual development unavailable in higher realms.

Shakyamuni's explanation that practice amid adversity produces rapid progress parallels the Tibetan teaching on transforming adverse conditions into the path (rkyen ngan lam 'khyer). Lojong (mind training) practice specifically cultivates the ability to use difficulties, enemies, and suffering as catalysts for developing bodhicitta and deepening realization.

The ten dharmas unique to impure worlds connect with the Tibetan understanding of the six paramitas practiced under difficult conditions. Generosity practiced amid poverty, patience practiced amid hostility, and wisdom cultivated amid confusion are understood to generate far more merit and deeper realization than the same practices performed under ideal conditions.
