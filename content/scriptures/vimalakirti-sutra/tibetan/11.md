The conduct of bodhisattvas described in this chapter is understood in Tibetan Buddhism through the framework of the three vows (sdom pa gsum): the pratimoksha vow of individual liberation, the bodhisattva vow of universal compassion, and the tantric samaya of sacred commitment. The bodhisattva's ability to enter all situations without being corrupted reflects the integration of all three levels of vow.

The inexhaustible lamp metaphor resonates with the Tibetan understanding of the guru-disciple relationship, where the teacher's realization ignites the student's potential without being diminished. This image is central to the Tibetan lineage system, where the transmission of empowerment, instruction, and realization from teacher to student maintains the vitality of the teaching across generations.

The chapter's teaching on the bodhisattva's spontaneous morality connects with the Tibetan concept of natural ethical conduct (rang bzhin gyi tshul khrims), which arises from realization rather than rule-following. In the Dzogchen tradition, the practitioner who has recognized the nature of mind acts appropriately in all situations through the natural expression of awareness.
