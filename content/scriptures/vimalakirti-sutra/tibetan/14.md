The entrustment of the teaching to Maitreya and the assembly is understood in Tibetan Buddhism through the framework of dharma preservation (bstan pa srung ba). The Tibetan tradition has developed elaborate institutional structures for preserving and transmitting the dharma, including the tulku (incarnate lama) system, monastic universities, and the tradition of recognized lineage holders.

The Buddha's concern that the teaching may be distorted resonates with the Tibetan emphasis on authentic lineage (brgyud pa) as the guarantee of genuine dharma. The unbroken chain of transmission from teacher to student, validated through experience and realization, is understood as the primary safeguard against corruption of the teaching.

The final chapter's instruction to preserve the sutra for future beings connects with the Tibetan terma (hidden treasure) tradition, where teachings are concealed by Padmasambhava and later masters for discovery at the appropriate time by predestined treasure revealers (gter ston). This practice of strategic preservation ensures that teachings appear when beings are ready to receive them.
