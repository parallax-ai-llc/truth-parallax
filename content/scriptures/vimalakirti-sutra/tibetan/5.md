The dialogue between Manjushri and Vimalakirti on illness is read in Tibetan Buddhism through the practice of tonglen (gtong len), the meditation of taking on others' suffering and sending them happiness. Vimalakirti's teaching that his illness arises from compassionate identification with all beings describes the lived experience of a realized tonglen practitioner.

The Tibetan medical tradition (Sowa Rigpa) also provides context for this chapter, as it understands illness as arising from the three root poisons of ignorance, attachment, and aversion. Vimalakirti's teaching that the bodhisattva's illness is different from ordinary illness, arising from compassion rather than from the poisons, introduces a category that Tibetan medicine recognizes in the form of the enlightened master's voluntary assumption of disciples' suffering.

Manjushri's question about how to console a sick bodhisattva receives a teaching that connects directly to Tibetan Buddhist practices of visualization and dedication. The bodhisattva consoles the sick by teaching them to transform illness into the path through recognizing the empty nature of the body, the illness, and the sufferer.
