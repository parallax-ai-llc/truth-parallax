The goddess's transformation of Shariputra is read in Tibetan Buddhism within the context of tantric practice, where the practitioner visualizes themselves as the deity, transcending ordinary concepts of gender, age, and physical form. In highest yoga tantra, the practitioner may visualize themselves as either male or female deities, recognizing that the awakened body transcends ordinary gender categories.

The Tibetan tradition includes important female masters such as Yeshe Tsogyal, Machig Labdron, and numerous recognized female tulkus (incarnate lamas). The goddess's teaching that "all qualities and forms are neither male nor female" directly supports the authority of female teachers and practitioners within Tibetan Buddhist lineages.

The philosophical basis for the goddess's demonstration lies in the Madhyamaka analysis of inherent existence. If gender were inherently real, it could not be transformed. The very fact of transformation proves emptiness. This argument mirrors the Tibetan philosophical method of using the analysis of change to establish the lack of inherent nature in all phenomena.
