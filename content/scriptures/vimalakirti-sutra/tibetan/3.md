The refusal of the great Sravaka disciples to visit Vimalakirti is read in Tibetan Buddhism within the context of the three vehicles (theg pa gsum). The Tibetan doxographical tradition classifies the Sravaka path as valid but limited, a stepping stone rather than the ultimate goal. The disciples' inability to match Vimalakirti's understanding illustrates this hierarchical framework.

Each disciple's encounter with Vimalakirti can be understood as a specific teaching on the limitations of a particular practice when it is not informed by the Mahayana view. Shariputra's meditation practice is genuine but lacks the understanding that samadhi pervades all activities. Subhuti's practice of giving is sincere but maintains the dualistic distinction between donor and recipient.

Tibetan commentators note that the disciples' reluctance shows proper spiritual humility rather than weakness. Recognizing the limits of one's realization is itself a sign of wisdom. In the Tibetan tradition, a student who acknowledges what they do not know is better positioned for progress than one who overestimates their attainment.
