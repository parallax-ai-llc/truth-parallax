The third chapter's systematic humiliation of the Sravaka disciples has been analyzed by scholars as reflecting historical tensions between emergent Mahayana communities and established monastic institutions. Sasaki Shizuka and others have argued that such polemical passages reveal the Mahayana movement's need to establish its legitimacy against better-established Buddhist traditions.

Each encounter between Vimalakirti and a disciple follows a consistent literary pattern: the disciple is engaged in a characteristic practice, Vimalakirti challenges the assumptions underlying that practice, and the disciple is left speechless. Etienne Lamotte's commentary identifies these encounters as structured around key doctrinal debates within Indian Buddhist scholasticism.

The portrayal of Shariputra in this chapter contrasts sharply with his depiction in the Pali canon, where he is the "general of the dharma" (dhammasenāpati) renowned for his wisdom. This discrepancy has led scholars to examine how different Buddhist communities constructed competing narratives about the same historical figures to support their soteriological and institutional claims.
