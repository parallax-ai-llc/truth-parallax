The chapter on dharma offering has been analyzed by scholars as reflecting the economic and social dynamics of Buddhist textual production. Gregory Schopen's research on inscriptions and manuscripts has shown that the production, copying, and distribution of Buddhist texts was a major form of religious merit-making in ancient India, and this chapter provides the doctrinal justification for that practice.

The distinction between material and dharma offering has been compared by scholars with similar hierarchies in other religious traditions. The Jewish concept of Torah study as supreme worship, the Islamic emphasis on knowledge (ilm) over wealth, and the Christian tradition of contemplative prayer as superior to active works all establish parallel valuations of intellectual or spiritual gifts over material ones.

The chapter's emphasis on preserving the dharma has been read in the context of the Buddhist awareness of impermanence as applied to the tradition itself. Scholars have examined how the concept of dharma decline (saddharma-vipralopa) motivated the production of texts, the building of permanent monuments, and the establishment of institutional structures designed to ensure the teaching's survival.
