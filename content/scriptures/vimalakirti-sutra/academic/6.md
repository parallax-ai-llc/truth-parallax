The demonstrations of inconceivable liberation in this chapter have been analyzed by scholars as literary expressions of the philosophical principle of non-obstruction (apratighata). Imre Hamar and others have traced the influence of these images on later Chinese Buddhist philosophy, particularly the Huayan school's doctrine of the mutual interpenetration of all phenomena (shi shi wu ai).

The chapter's imagery of fitting enormous objects into tiny spaces without alteration has been compared with similar paradoxes in Indian philosophical literature, including Jain teachings on the relationship between the soul (jiva) and the body. Douglas Duckworth has analyzed how such paradoxes function as pedagogical devices that force the reader beyond conventional logical categories.

From a text-critical perspective, the chapter shows evidence of accretion, with some demonstrations appearing to be later additions to the original narrative. The Sanskrit manuscript, the Chinese translations, and the Tibetan version show variations in the number and order of the demonstrations, suggesting that this material was particularly subject to expansion over time.
