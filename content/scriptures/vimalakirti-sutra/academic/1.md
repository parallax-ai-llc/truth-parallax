The Buddha Land chapter introduces the pure land concept that scholars have traced through the development of Mahayana Buddhism. Jan Nattier and others have argued that early Mahayana pure land ideas were connected to visualization practices rather than to the devotional rebirth aspirations that developed later. The Vimalakirti Sutra's presentation, where purity depends on the practitioner's mind, supports this interpretation.

The chapter's climactic moment, where the Buddha reveals the hidden purity of the world through his toe-touch, has been analyzed as a literary device common in Mahayana sutras that dramatizes the gap between conventional and ultimate perception. Paul Harrison has noted that such revelatory moments serve to validate the sutra's doctrinal claims through narrative authority.

The Sanskrit manuscript discovered at the Potala Palace in 1999 provides important evidence for reconstructing the original form of this chapter. Comparison with Kumarajiva's Chinese translation reveals that the translator sometimes condensed or rearranged material to produce a more elegant literary text in Chinese.
