The goddess episode in chapter seven has attracted extensive academic attention as one of the most significant discussions of gender in Buddhist literature. Scholars including Miriam Levering, Liz Wilson, and Bernard Faure have examined the passage for what it reveals about attitudes toward gender in Indian and Chinese Buddhism. The episode's reception history across different Buddhist cultures provides a lens for understanding evolving gender norms within the tradition.

The transformation of Shariputra's body challenges the assumption, common in many Buddhist texts, that a female body is an obstacle to enlightenment. However, scholars debate whether the passage genuinely undermines gender hierarchy or reinforces it by treating gender as something that can be set aside rather than as a meaningful category of social experience.

The Sanskrit text uses the term stri-bhava (female state) rather than a term for biological sex, suggesting that the original discourse was focused on the construction of gendered identity rather than on anatomy. This distinction is significant for contemporary readings that seek to understand the sutra's relevance to modern discussions of gender identity and social construction.
