The dialogue between Manjushri and Vimalakirti has been extensively studied as a literary and philosophical composition. Robert Thurman's translation and commentary situates it within the context of Indian philosophical debate (vadasthana), where interlocutors of equal standing engage in systematic examination of a topic. The chapter's structure follows conventions of Indian dialectical literature.

The teaching on the bodhisattva's illness arising from compassion has been analyzed by scholars of Buddhist ethics as an early formulation of the relationship between empathy and suffering. Paul Williams has compared this with the Christian concept of compassionate co-suffering, noting both structural similarities and significant philosophical differences in the underlying metaphysics of selfhood.

The Sanskrit manuscript provides important textual evidence for this chapter, revealing passages that Kumarajiva omitted or condensed in his Chinese translation. These variations suggest that Kumarajiva exercised significant editorial judgment, prioritizing doctrinal clarity and literary elegance over literal fidelity to his source text.
