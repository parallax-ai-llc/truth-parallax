The chapter on bodhisattva conduct has been studied by scholars of Buddhist ethics as a key text for understanding the Mahayana approach to moral theory. Charles Goodman and others have analyzed the tension between the bodhisattva's commitment to universal liberation and the conventional ethical norms that govern ordinary behavior, noting that the sutra resolves this tension through the concept of skill-in-means informed by wisdom.

The inexhaustible lamp metaphor has been traced through its reception in Chinese Buddhism, where it became a frequently cited image in Chan/Zen literature. Scholars have noted its functional similarity to the concept of dharma transmission that became central to Chan lineage claims, suggesting a possible channel of influence.

The chapter's vision of bodhisattvas entering all social environments without being corrupted has been examined by scholars of Buddhist social theory. Gregory Schopen and others have questioned whether such texts reflect actual practice or represent idealized visions that served rhetorical purposes within Buddhist communities, legitimizing engagement with patrons, merchants, and political figures.
