The concluding entrustment chapter follows a pattern common in Mahayana sutras, where the Buddha formally charges specific beings with the responsibility of preserving and transmitting the text. Scholars have analyzed this literary convention as serving both narrative and institutional functions, closing the sutra's frame while establishing the text's canonical authority and ensuring its circulation.

The designation of both Maitreya and Ananda as custodians of the teaching reflects the text's dual audience: bodhisattvas who will carry the teaching forward cosmically and monastic communities who will preserve it textually. Etienne Lamotte's exhaustive commentary traces the entrustment formula through dozens of Mahayana sutras, showing how it became a standard literary convention.

The chapter's concern with the possibility of dharma corruption has been studied in the context of the Buddhist concept of the decline of the true dharma. Jan Nattier's research on the "three periods" schema (true dharma, counterfeit dharma, dharma ending) has shown how this anxiety about decline motivated both textual preservation efforts and the production of new texts claiming to offer teachings appropriate for degenerate times.
