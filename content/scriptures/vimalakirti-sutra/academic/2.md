The second chapter introduces Vimalakirti as a lay householder of extraordinary spiritual accomplishment. Academic scholars have examined this figure as a literary type: the ideal layperson whose realization challenges monastic authority. Gregory Schopen's research on Indian Buddhist inscriptions suggests that lay donors wielded significant social and economic power within Buddhist communities, providing a social context for the sutra's pro-lay rhetoric.

The concept of upaya (skillful means) as presented here has been analyzed by Michael Pye, John Schroeder, and others as a distinctive Mahayana hermeneutical strategy. By establishing the principle that the Buddha and advanced practitioners may employ any method appropriate to the circumstances, the Mahayana tradition created a flexible framework for incorporating new teachings and practices.

The description of Vimalakirti frequenting wine shops, gambling houses, and brothels has been compared with similar accounts of transgressive holy figures in Indian literature, including certain Shaiva ascetics and later Vajrayana mahasiddhas. This literary motif serves to dramatize the teaching that awakening transcends conventional moral categories.
