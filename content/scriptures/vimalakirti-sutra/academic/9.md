The emanation narrative in chapter nine reflects the developed Mahayana cosmology of multiple buddha lands, which scholars have traced through the evolution of Mahayana thought from the earliest Prajnaparamita texts through the mature sutra literature. Jan Nattier's work on the origins of Mahayana has examined how the concept of multiple buddha fields emerged and developed within Indian Buddhist communities.

The Fragrant Land (Sarvagandhasugandha) and its teaching through fragrance rather than words represents a creative variation on the theme of diverse skillful means across buddha lands. Scholars have noted that the multiplication of buddha lands with distinctive characteristics reflects the Mahayana principle that awakening manifests in infinite forms appropriate to different contexts.

The sharing of fragrant rice across cosmic distances has been compared by some scholars with the exchange of relics and sacred objects between Buddhist communities along the Silk Road. The narrative may reflect the historical reality of Buddhist networks that connected distant communities through the circulation of material and spiritual goods.
