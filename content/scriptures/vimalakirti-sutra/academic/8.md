The Dharma Gate of Non-duality chapter has been the subject of extensive philosophical analysis. The progressive sequence of thirty-two bodhisattvas' responses has been studied as a systematic deconstruction of dualistic categories, with each pair reflecting specific debates within Indian Buddhist philosophy. Edward Conze and others have compared this structure with the dialectical method of the Prajnaparamita literature.

The three levels of response, the bodhisattvas' paired-concept transcendence, Manjushri's meta-level transcendence of language, and Vimalakirti's silence, have been analyzed as representing different epistemological positions within Buddhist philosophy. Thomas Kasulis and others have compared Vimalakirti's silence with apophatic traditions in Western philosophy and theology.

The rhetorical function of silence in this context has been examined by literary scholars, who note that the text must use words to describe and praise the silence, creating a productive paradox. The narrative evaluates Vimalakirti's silence as superior to Manjushri's verbal response, yet this evaluation is itself verbal. Scholars have debated whether this paradox is a limitation of the text or a deliberate literary strategy.
