The extension of critique from Sravaka disciples to bodhisattvas in the fourth chapter has been interpreted by scholars as an intra-Mahayana polemic, suggesting that the sutra's authors were challenging not only the Sravaka ideal but also certain Mahayana formulations that they considered insufficiently radical. Luis Gomez has analyzed this as evidence of internal debate within the Mahayana movement during the early centuries of the Common Era.

Maitreya's encounter with Vimalakirti has attracted particular scholarly attention because it challenges the concept of vyakarana (prediction of future buddhahood), one of the most important Mahayana ritual and doctrinal categories. The suggestion that Maitreya's prediction is based on a misunderstanding of time and identity undermines a key mechanism of Mahayana legitimation.

Academic analysis reveals that the chapter pushes the logic of emptiness to its extreme: if all dharmas are empty, then even the bodhisattva path, the stages of practice, and the concept of future buddhahood are conventional designations without ultimate validity. This self-reflexive critique is characteristic of the Prajnaparamita literature's radical epistemology.
