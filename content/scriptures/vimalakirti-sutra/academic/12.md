The vision of Akshobhya's realm has been analyzed by scholars as reflecting the development of pure land imagery in Mahayana Buddhism. Akshobhya's Abhirati pure land represents one of the earliest buddha land conceptions, predating Amitabha's Sukhavati in some scholarly reconstructions. The Vimalakirti Sutra's use of Akshobhya rather than Amitabha has been interpreted as evidence of the text's relatively early composition.

The philosophical implications of displaying an entire buddha realm within the assembly have been examined through the lens of Huayan philosophy. Scholars including Thomas Cleary and Francis Cook have traced the influence of such Vimalakirti passages on Fazang's development of the concept of the mutual interpenetration of phenomena.

Text-critical analysis reveals interesting variations between the Sanskrit, Chinese, and Tibetan versions of this chapter. The Sanskrit manuscript includes details about Akshobhya's realm that are absent from Kumarajiva's Chinese translation, suggesting either that Kumarajiva's source text differed from the surviving Sanskrit or that the translator chose to abbreviate certain descriptive passages.
