## Comparative Reading

The comparative study of the Religious Constitution examines how new religious movements create institutional structures by drawing on existing organizational models. The Cao Dai constitution's combination of Catholic hierarchy, Buddhist monasticism, and Confucian bureaucracy illustrates the broader phenomenon of institutional syncretism — the creative adaptation of established organizational forms for new religious purposes.

Comparative scholars note parallels with other syncretic movements that adopted hierarchical structures from established religions: the Unification Church's adaptation of Christian ecclesiastical forms, the Baha'i Administrative Order's combination of democratic and hierarchical elements, and the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints' elaborate priesthood structure. The Cao Dai constitution is distinctive in explicitly drawing from multiple religious traditions simultaneously.
