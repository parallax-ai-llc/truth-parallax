## Overview

From a comparative perspective, the Cao Dai Canon represents one of the most ambitious attempts in religious history to create a deliberately syncretic scripture. While many scriptures contain syncretic elements — the Guru Granth Sahib incorporates Hindu and Muslim compositions, the Quran references Biblical narratives — the Cao Dai Canon is unique in its explicit programmatic synthesis of multiple named religious traditions into a unified theological system.

Comparative scholars examine the canon's methods of synthesis: how Buddhist concepts of karma and reincarnation are integrated with Confucian social ethics, Taoist cosmology, Christian soteriology, and Vietnamese folk religion. The canon's theory of progressive revelation — that God has spoken through all religions but now offers a final, unified teaching — parallels similar claims in Baha'i, Unification Church, and other modern syncretic movements, raising comparative questions about the dynamics of religious innovation.
