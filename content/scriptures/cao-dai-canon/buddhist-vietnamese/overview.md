## Overview

The Buddhist-Vietnamese reading of the Cao Dai Canon examines how the text integrates Mahayana Buddhist concepts into its syncretic framework. Vietnamese Buddhism — a complex tradition blending Chan (Thiền), Pure Land, and Tantric elements — provides much of the philosophical vocabulary and soteriological structure of the Cao Dai teaching. The canon's concepts of karma, reincarnation, spiritual cultivation, and ultimate liberation all draw heavily on Vietnamese Buddhist thought.

Buddhist-Vietnamese scholars note both the borrowings and the transformations: the Cao Dai Canon adopts the Buddhist concept of karma but places it within a theistic framework absent from classical Buddhism; it affirms reincarnation but teaches that the Supreme Being can intervene to alter karmic trajectories; it honors the Buddha as a divine teacher but subordinates Buddhist teaching to the overarching unity of the Great Way. These adaptations reflect the creative religious environment of southern Vietnam, where Buddhist, Taoist, and folk traditions had long interpenetrated.
