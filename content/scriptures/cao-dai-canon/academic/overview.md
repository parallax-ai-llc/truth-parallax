## Overview

Academic study of the Cao Dai Canon examines it as a product of the remarkable religious creativity of early twentieth-century Vietnam. Scholars situate the text within the context of French colonial rule, the encounter between Asian and European religious traditions, and the broader phenomenon of syncretic new religions that emerged across Asia during the colonial period.

The canon's composition through *cơ bút* (spirit writing) is analyzed alongside similar practices in Chinese *fuji* divination, European Spiritualism, and other traditions of mediumistic revelation. Scholars note the sophisticated theological architecture of the canon — its systematic integration of Buddhist, Taoist, Confucian, Christian, and indigenous Vietnamese elements — as evidence of deliberate intellectual construction rather than haphazard eclecticism. The canon's organizational prescriptions, modeled in part on the Catholic Church's hierarchical structure, reflect the colonial encounter and the creative appropriation of Western institutional forms.
