## Overview

The Buddhist-Korean tradition examines the Mugyeong through the lens of the deep syncretism that has characterized Korean religious life for over a millennium. Since Buddhism's arrival on the Korean peninsula in the fourth century CE, shamanic and Buddhist practices have interpenetrated extensively. The Mugyeong incorporates Buddhist deities (particularly Gwaneum/Avalokitesvara and Jijang/Ksitigarbha), cosmological frameworks (the realms of rebirth), and soteriological concepts (karma, merit transfer) into its fundamentally shamanic ritual structure.

## Syncretic Integration

Buddhist-Korean interpreters observe that the Mugyeong does not simply borrow Buddhist elements but transforms them through shamanic practice. The bodhisattva Gwaneum appears not as an object of contemplation but as a spirit who possesses the mudang and speaks directly to the living. The Buddhist concept of merit transfer is enacted not through sutra recitation but through the dramatic ritual of guiding the dead through the spirit world.

## Compassionate Practice

Both traditions share a fundamental orientation toward the relief of suffering. Buddhist readers of the Mugyeong recognize in the mudang's healing work a form of compassionate action (karuna) that, while operating outside monastic Buddhism's institutional frameworks, addresses the same human needs that motivate Buddhist pastoral care.
