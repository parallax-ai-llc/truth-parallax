Academic analysis of Section 10, 'Divination and Prophecy,' employs the methodologies of religious studies, anthropology, and performance studies to examine this portion of the Mugyeong within its cultural, historical, and comparative contexts. Scholars note that the practices described in this section reflect layers of historical development, incorporating pre-Buddhist indigenous elements alongside later accretions from Buddhist, Confucian, and even Christian-influenced modern adaptations.

Ethnographic fieldwork by scholars such as Kim Tae-gon, Cho Hung-yun, and Laurel Kendall has documented regional variations in how these rituals are performed across Korea's provinces. The Seoul tradition, the Jeju tradition, the Hwanghae-do tradition, and the East Coast tradition each preserve distinctive approaches to the practices described in this section, reflecting the rich diversity of Korean shamanic practice beneath its apparent unity.
