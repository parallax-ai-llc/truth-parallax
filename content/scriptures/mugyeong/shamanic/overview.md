## Overview

The shamanic tradition receives the Mugyeong as the living script of Korean Musok practice, inseparable from the gut (굿) rituals in which it is performed. For practicing mudang (shamans), these texts are not literature to be read but invocations to be sung, danced, and embodied. Each section represents a dimension of the mudang's sacred work: summoning spirits, diagnosing afflictions, healing the sick, guiding the dead, and maintaining cosmic harmony between the human and spirit worlds.

## Ritual Context

Within shamanic practice, the Mugyeong functions as the structural backbone of the gut ceremony. The mudang does not simply recite these texts but enters altered states of consciousness in which the spirits themselves speak through her body. The transition from human voice to spirit voice marks the most sacred moment of the ritual — the point at which the boundary between the visible and invisible worlds dissolves and direct communication becomes possible.

## Living Tradition

The shamanic tradition emphasizes that the Mugyeong cannot be fully understood through textual study alone. Each mudang receives her particular versions through her initiatory spirit sickness (sinbyeong/신병), her apprenticeship with a spirit mother (sin eomeoni/신어머니), and her ongoing relationship with her personal spirits (momju/몸주). The written text is a skeleton; the living tradition is the flesh.
