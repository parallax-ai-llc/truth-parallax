## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Mugyeong situates Korean shamanism within the global phenomenon of shamanic practice. Drawing on the foundational work of Mircea Eliade and subsequent scholars, this tradition examines the structural parallels between Korean gut ceremonies and shamanic rituals found across Siberia, Central Asia, the Americas, and Southeast Asia. The Mugyeong reveals universal patterns — the shaman's initiatory crisis, the journey to other worlds, the negotiation with spirits on behalf of the community — while also displaying distinctive features shaped by Korean culture and history.

## Universal Patterns

Comparative scholars identify in the Mugyeong the classic shamanic complex: the experience of a calling through illness or vision, the acquisition of helping spirits, the mastery of ecstatic techniques, and the performance of healing and divination for the community. The Korean mudang's sinbyeong (spirit sickness) closely parallels the initiatory ordeals described in Siberian, Amazonian, and African shamanic traditions.

## Distinctive Features

At the same time, the comparative tradition highlights what is uniquely Korean about the Mugyeong: the prominence of female practitioners, the elaborate theatrical and musical dimensions of the gut, the deep integration of ancestor veneration, and the historical negotiation with Buddhist and Confucian traditions that has shaped Korean shamanism into its present form.
