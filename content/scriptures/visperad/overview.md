## Overview

The Visperad (Avestan: *vīspe ratavō*, "all the lords/judges") is a Zoroastrian liturgical text consisting of 24 chapters (*kardas*) that serve as supplements to the Yasna ceremony during the seasonal festivals known as the Gahambars. Rather than an independent text, the Visperad's chapters are intercalated into the Yasna at specific points, expanding and solemnizing the liturgy for these special occasions. The text invokes the spiritual lords (*ratus*) who preside over the various domains of creation.

## Structure

The Visperad's 24 kardas are interpolated into the Yasna liturgy at designated points:

1. **Opening Invocations** (Kardas 1-5): Summons to the spiritual lords and communal assembly
2. **Festival Liturgy** (Kardas 6-15): Extensions of the Yasna for the six Gahambar festivals, praising seasonal renewal and communal obligation
3. **Expanded Dedications** (Kardas 16-20): Additional offerings and invocations to the Amesha Spentas and creation
4. **Closing Additions** (Kardas 21-24): Supplementary prayers, communal benedictions, and closing formulas

The text is composed entirely in Younger Avestan and presupposes the Yasna framework.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Zoroastrian**: Essential for the proper celebration of the Gahambars, the six seasonal festivals that structure the liturgical year
- **Academic**: Evidence for the development of Zoroastrian liturgical practice and the evolution of the priestly ritual calendar
- **Comparative**: Parallels with seasonal festival cycles in Vedic, Mesopotamian, and agrarian religious traditions
- **Protestant**: Of interest for understanding liturgical calendar development and communal worship patterns
- **Jewish**: Parallels with Jewish festival cycles and the priestly calendar traditions of the Second Temple period
- **Islamic**: Context for understanding pre-Islamic Persian religious observance and seasonal piety
