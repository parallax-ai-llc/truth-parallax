## Overview

Islamic engagement with the Visperad and the Gahambar festivals it accompanies is shaped by the historical encounter between Islam and Persian Zoroastrian culture following the Arab conquest. The Gahambars — seasonal festivals deeply embedded in Persian agricultural and religious life — persisted in various forms even after Islamization, and Muslim scholars documented and debated these observances.

## Key Interpretive Themes

### Nowruz and the Persian Calendar
The Gahambar cycle is part of the Zoroastrian sacred calendar that also includes Nowruz (New Year). Muslim rulers and scholars engaged with these festivals: some banned them, others tolerated or adopted them. Al-Biruni's *al-Athar al-Baqiyah* provides detailed accounts of Zoroastrian festivals.

### Communal Worship and Social Ethics
The Gahambars' emphasis on communal feasting and charity (*hamazor* — the sharing of food with all, regardless of class) has been compared to Islamic concepts of communal solidarity and *zakat* (charitable giving).

### The Question of Bid'ah
Muslim jurists debated whether Persian Muslims could participate in Gahambar-derived celebrations. This intersects with broader *fiqh* discussions about *bid'ah* (innovation) and the permissibility of pre-Islamic cultural practices.
