## Jewish Reading

Jewish scholars find in the Right Ginza a complex engagement with traditions shared with ancient Judaism. The cosmogony employs Aramaic terminology and narrative structures that recall the Genesis creation accounts, yet transforms them in distinctive ways. The figure of Adam plays a central role, but as a spiritual archetype — *Adam Kasia* (Hidden Adam) — whose true nature is luminous and divine, contrasting with the physical Adam of rabbinic interpretation.

The Right Ginza's references to Abel (*Hibil*), Seth (*Šitil*), and other figures familiar from the Hebrew Bible suggest a shared matrix of Second Temple Jewish traditions, even as the Mandaean text develops these figures in radically different theological directions. The ethical teachings of the Right Ginza — honesty, charity, sexual purity — overlap significantly with rabbinic moral instruction, though grounded in a dualistic cosmology foreign to mainstream Jewish theology.
