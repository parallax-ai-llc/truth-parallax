## Overview

Jewish scholars approach the Ginza Rabba with particular interest because of the Mandaeans' complex relationship to Judaism. The Mandaeans employ Aramaic — the lingua franca of the Jewish world in the Second Temple and Talmudic periods — and their traditions reference figures from the Hebrew Bible, including Adam, Abel (*Hibil*), Seth (*Šitil*), and Noah. The Ginza Rabba's baptismal theology may preserve practices related to the Jewish baptismal sects described by ancient authors.

At the same time, the Mandaean tradition is notably critical of mainstream Judaism, and the Ginza Rabba contains polemical passages against Moses and the Mosaic law. Jewish scholars find in this tension valuable evidence for the diversity of religious life in ancient Mesopotamia and the Levant, where multiple communities drawing on shared Semitic and Aramaic traditions developed in strikingly different theological directions.
