## Overview

The interfaith reading of the Ginza Rabba highlights the Mandaean tradition as a living bridge between the ancient and modern religious worlds. As one of the oldest continuously practiced religions, Mandaeism and its central scripture offer interfaith dialogue partners a tradition that is neither Abrahamic nor Eastern in the conventional sense, but occupies a unique position at the intersection of Semitic monotheism, Gnostic dualism, and ritual sacramentalism.

Interfaith practitioners find in the Ginza Rabba themes of universal resonance: the primacy of the sacred in water, the longing of the soul for its divine home, the ethical imperative to live in light and truth, and the importance of community in sustaining spiritual practice. The survival of the Mandaean community — often as a persecuted minority — also raises important interfaith questions about religious freedom, minority rights, and the preservation of endangered religious traditions.
