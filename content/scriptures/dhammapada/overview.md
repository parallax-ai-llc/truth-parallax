## Overview

The Dhammapada, meaning "Path of the Dhamma" or "Verses of Truth," is one of the best-known and most widely translated texts in the Buddhist canon. Comprising 423 verses arranged in 26 chapters, it belongs to the Khuddaka Nikaya of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali canon. The verses are attributed to the historical Buddha, Siddhattha Gotama, and address fundamental themes of Buddhist ethics, psychology, and soteriology in accessible, memorable poetry.

## Structure

The 26 chapters (vaggas) are organized thematically, progressing from foundational teachings on the nature of mind through ethical conduct, mental cultivation, and the qualities of the liberated person:

| Chapters | Theme |
|----------|-------|
| 1-3 | Mind, heedfulness, and mental discipline |
| 4-9 | Ethics, wisdom, and the consequences of action |
| 10-13 | Violence, aging, self, and the world |
| 14-17 | The Buddha, happiness, attachment, and anger |
| 18-20 | Purity, justice, and the path |
| 21-26 | Miscellaneous, hell, endurance, craving, the monastic life, and true nobility |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Dhammapada is treasured across all Buddhist traditions. The Pali version is central to Theravada Buddhism, while related collections such as the Gandhari Dharmapada and the Udanavarga are studied in Mahayana and Tibetan contexts. Its ethical teachings on non-violence, mental discipline, and compassion have also given it wide appeal beyond the Buddhist world.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mind | All experience is shaped by the quality of one's mental states |
| Karma | Intentional actions bear consequences across lifetimes |
| Impermanence | All conditioned phenomena are transient |
| Non-self | No permanent, unchanging self underlies experience |
| The Path | The Noble Eightfold Path as the way to liberation |
| Liberation | Nibbana as the cessation of suffering and the unconditioned |
