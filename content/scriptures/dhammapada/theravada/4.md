The Pupphavagga uses the metaphor of flowers across 16 verses to convey teachings on impermanence, moral beauty, and the transience of life. The Theravada tradition reads the flower imagery as pointing toward the practice of contemplating impermanence (anicca), one of the three characteristics of existence.

Theravada commentators note the chapter's instruction to gather the "flowers of virtue" while one can, before death arrives "like a great flood sweeping away a sleeping village." The chapter's comparison of the practitioner to a bee that gathers nectar without harming the flower illustrates the ideal of living harmlessly while accumulating merit.
