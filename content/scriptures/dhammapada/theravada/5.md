The Balavagga's 16 verses offer a Theravada analysis of ignorance and folly. The fool (bala) in Theravada understanding is not merely intellectually deficient but spiritually blind, unable to recognize the consequences of unwholesome action. The chapter warns against the company of fools and the suffering that arises from moral and spiritual ignorance.

Theravada interpretation emphasizes that folly is not a permanent condition but a quality of mind that can be overcome through wise attention (yoniso manasikara) and association with spiritual friends (kalyana mitta). The chapter's teaching that "long is the night for the sleepless, long is the road for the weary" captures the existential weight of unenlightened existence.
