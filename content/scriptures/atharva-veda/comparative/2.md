The Atharva Veda's counter-sorcery hymns compare with anti-witchcraft traditions across cultures. The Mesopotamian *maqlû* texts, the biblical injunction against witchcraft (Exodus 22:18), the medieval European *Malleus Maleficarum*, and West African anti-sorcery practices all reflect the widespread human experience of perceiving hostile spiritual forces and developing ritual countermeasures. The Atharvan material is among the oldest and most systematic of these traditions.

The use of protective amulets (*mani*) in the Atharva Veda compares with amulet traditions worldwide: Jewish *mezuzot* and *tefillin*, Christian scapulars and medals, Islamic *ta'wiz*, and Buddhist protective charms all embody the principle that material objects can be charged with spiritual power for protection. The comparative study of these practices illuminates the universal human desire for tangible, wearable forms of divine protection.
