## Overview

For Advaita Vedanta, the Atharva Veda's greatest significance lies in its Upanishads, which include three of the most important texts in the Advaitic canon: the Mundaka, Mandukya, and Prashna Upanishads. The Mundaka Upanishad's distinction between *para vidya* (higher knowledge of Brahman) and *apara vidya* (lower knowledge of ritual and worldly matters) provides a foundational framework for the Advaitic hierarchy of knowledge that places self-realization above all ritual action.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Mundaka Upanishad
The Mundaka's teaching that Brahman is "that by knowing which everything is known" establishes the Advaitic principle that non-dual knowledge is the ultimate goal of all Vedic study. Its critique of ritual — "Frail indeed are those rafts of sacrifice" (1.2.7) — is read by Advaitins as directing the aspirant beyond the Atharva Veda's own practical content toward pure knowledge.

### Mandukya Upanishad
The Mandukya, though only twelve verses long, is considered by Gaudapada and Shankara to contain the essence of all Vedantic teaching. Its analysis of the four states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and *turiya*) through the syllable Om is a cornerstone of Advaita philosophy.

## Theological Emphases

- **Para and Apara Vidya**: The Atharva Veda itself teaches the superiority of knowledge over its own practical content
- **Turiya**: The Mandukya's fourth state of consciousness is pure non-dual awareness, identical with Brahman
- **Self-Transcendence**: The Atharva Veda's Upanishads direct the seeker from worldly concerns to absolute knowledge
- **Gaudapada's Karika**: The commentary on the Mandukya by Gaudapada, Shankara's grand-teacher, is the earliest systematic Advaita text
