The philosophical hymns of the Atharva Veda are studied by scholars as evidence for the development of abstract thought in the late Vedic period. The Skambha Sukta's systematic questioning about the cosmic principle that underlies all phenomena represents a mode of philosophical inquiry that anticipates the Upanishadic quest for *Brahman*. Scholars trace a clear intellectual trajectory from these Atharvan speculations to the more developed philosophical systems of the Upanishads.

The Kala Sukta's personification of Time and the Prana Sukta's identification of breath as the supreme principle are studied as examples of 'speculative hymns' (*brahmodya*) — a genre of competitive philosophical questioning that emerged in the late Vedic period. Scholars including Brian K. Smith and Joanna Jurewicz have analyzed these hymns as products of a ritual context in which priestly knowledge-contests drove the development of increasingly abstract theological and philosophical thought.
