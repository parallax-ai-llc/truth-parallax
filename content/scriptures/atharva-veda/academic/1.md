The healing hymns of the Atharva Veda are a primary source for the history of medicine in ancient India. Scholars have identified references to specific diseases — including fever (possibly malaria), jaundice, leprosy, and parasitic infections — and to medicinal plants that can in some cases be identified with species still used in Ayurvedic practice. Kenneth Zysk's *Religious Medicine* traces the evolution from the Atharvan healer-priest to the secular physician of the classical Ayurvedic texts.

The combination of empirical observation and magical thinking in these hymns has been central to scholarly debates about the relationship between 'rational' medicine and 'magical' healing in pre-modern societies. Scholars note that the Atharvan healing tradition does not distinguish between these categories as modern observers do — the herbal prescription and the incantation are parts of a single therapeutic system in which the physical and spiritual dimensions of illness are inseparable.
