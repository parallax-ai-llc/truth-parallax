## Overview

The Atharva Veda ("Veda of the Atharvan Priests") is the fourth and most distinctive of the Vedas, compiled around 1000 BCE. Unlike the other three Vedas, which focus on the solemn *shrauta* sacrifice, the Atharva Veda addresses the concerns of everyday life: healing, protection, love, prosperity, statecraft, and philosophical speculation. It preserves a layer of popular religion and folk practice alongside some of the most sophisticated cosmological hymns in Vedic literature, including the Prithivi Sukta (hymn to Earth) and profound speculations on time, breath, and the unity of existence.

## Structure

The Atharva Veda's approximately 730 hymns and 6,000 verses are organized into five thematic groupings:

1. **Healing Hymns and Medical Charms**: Incantations against disease, fever, and bodily afflictions
2. **Protection and Counter-Sorcery**: Defensive hymns against witchcraft, enemies, and malevolent forces
3. **Marriage, Love, and Domestic Rites**: Hymns for household ceremonies, conjugal harmony, and fertility
4. **Royal Power and Statecraft**: Formulas for the king's consecration, political authority, and warfare
5. **Philosophical Hymns and Cosmology**: Speculations on Brahman, time (*kala*), breath (*prana*), and cosmic unity

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Traditional**: The Atharva Veda was the last to be accepted into the Vedic canon, sometimes called the "Veda of the people"
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The Mundaka, Mandukya, and Prashna Upanishads belong to the Atharva Veda tradition
- **Vaishnavism**: Hymns on cosmic unity and the supreme being resonate with Vaishnava theology
- **Shaivism**: Hymns to Rudra-Shiva and references to *vratas* (vows) connect to Shaiva ascetic traditions
- **Academic**: A primary source for the history of Indian medicine (Ayurveda), magic, and popular religion
- **Comparative**: Studied alongside Greek magical papyri, Mesopotamian incantations, and European folk charms
