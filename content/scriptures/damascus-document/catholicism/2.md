## The Admonition: Sacred History as Moral Instruction

The second chapter's use of biblical history as moral instruction resonates with Catholic homiletical and catechetical tradition. The Church Fathers regularly employed the same method, drawing moral and typological lessons from the narratives of the Watchers, the Flood, and Israel's repeated failures. This approach presupposes the unity of salvation history — a principle central to Catholic biblical interpretation.

The "three nets of Belial" — fornication, wealth, and Temple defilement — correspond to enduring concerns of Catholic moral theology. The Church's teaching on marriage and sexuality, the proper use of wealth (developed through papal encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* to *Laudato Si'*), and the integrity of worship and sacramental practice reflect a continuous tradition of moral discernment that the Damascus Document shows was already active in the Second Temple period.

Catholic interpreters also note the admonition's emphasis on the danger of following one's own interpretation rather than the community's authoritative teaching. The warning against those who "walk in the stubbornness of their hearts" anticipates the Catholic concern with private judgment that would become particularly acute during the Reformation era. The Damascus community's insistence on communal authority over individual interpretation provides a striking ancient parallel to Catholic ecclesial principles.
