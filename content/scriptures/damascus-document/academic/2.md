## The Admonition: Scholarly Perspectives

The admonition section of the Damascus Document employs a technique scholars term *rewritten Bible* or *parabiblical interpretation*, in which biblical narratives are retold and reinterpreted for the community's purposes. This method, also found in texts like Jubilees and the Genesis Apocryphon, represents a distinctive Second Temple hermeneutical approach that stands between straightforward citation and full literary recomposition.

The "three nets of Belial" passage (CD IV:14-V:11) has generated significant scholarly attention as evidence of specific halakhic disputes between the Damascus community and other Jewish groups. The charge of fornication appears to target polygamy and possibly uncle-niece marriage, which Pharisaic halakhah permitted but which the community rejected on the basis of a distinctive reading of the Torah's marriage laws. Lawrence Schiffman and others have used this material to argue that the community's halakhic positions share more with Sadducean than Pharisaic jurisprudence.

The identity of the "builders of the wall" and the figure called "Zaw" (literally "precept" or "filth," depending on vocalization) remains contested. The Groningen hypothesis, developed by Florentino García Martínez, distinguishes between a broader Essene movement and the specific Qumran community, suggesting that the polemics in the Damascus Document may target internal rivals as well as external opponents.
