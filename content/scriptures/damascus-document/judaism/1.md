## The Historical Prologue: A Jewish Reading

The opening chapter of the Damascus Document presents a sectarian account of Jewish history that diverges sharply from the narrative that would become normative in rabbinic tradition. The reference to 390 years after the destruction by Nebuchadnezzar, echoing Ezekiel 4:5, places the community's origins in a schematic chronological framework that may reflect theological rather than historical calculation.

From a Jewish perspective, this chapter reveals the intensity of sectarian identity in the Second Temple period. The claim that God raised a "Teacher of Righteousness" to lead a small remnant while the rest of Israel had gone astray represents one of several competing claims to authentic Torah interpretation. The Pharisees, Sadducees, and other groups made analogous (if differently framed) claims to represent the true tradition.

The chapter's rhetoric of remnant and apostasy — the "root of planting" emerging from a corrupted Israel — finds parallels in prophetic literature but takes on a sharper sectarian edge. Rabbinic Judaism would eventually absorb prophetic remnant theology into a more inclusive framework, but the Damascus Document preserves the raw urgency of a community convinced that it alone had remained faithful to God's covenant.
