## The Admonition: Lessons from History

Chapter 2 employs biblical history as a warning against covenant infidelity, using the examples of the Watchers, the generation of the Flood, and other biblical rebels. This technique of drawing moral lessons from scriptural narrative (*ma'aseh avot siman le-banim* — the deeds of the ancestors are a sign for the children) became a standard feature of rabbinic homiletics, though the conclusions drawn here differ markedly from later rabbinic teaching.

The "three nets of Belial" — fornication, wealth, and defilement of the Temple — represent specific halakhic accusations against the Jerusalem establishment. Jewish scholars have noted that the charge of fornication likely refers to disputed marriage laws (particularly regarding polygamy and uncle-niece marriage), while Temple defilement concerns purity regulations that the community considered insufficiently strict.

The admonition's polemical tone reveals the fractured landscape of Second Temple Judaism. The "builders of the wall" who followed a false teacher are probably Pharisees or another rival group whose legal interpretations the Damascus community rejected. This internal Jewish debate over proper Torah observance provides essential context for understanding the diversity that characterized Judaism before the rabbinic consolidation after 70 CE.
