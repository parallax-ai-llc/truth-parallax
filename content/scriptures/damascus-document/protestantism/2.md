## The Admonition: Warning from Sacred History

The second chapter's use of biblical history as a source of moral warning resonates strongly with Protestant homiletical tradition. Paul employed the same method in 1 Corinthians 10:1-11, where the wilderness generation's failures serve as types (*typoi*) warning believers against idolatry and presumption. Hebrews 3-4 similarly uses Israel's history of disobedience as a cautionary framework.

The "three nets of Belial" — fornication, wealth, and Temple defilement — reflect concerns that the New Testament addresses in transformed ways. Jesus's teachings on marriage and divorce (Matthew 19), warnings about the deceitfulness of riches (Mark 10:23-25), and the cleansing of the Temple (John 2:13-22) address analogous themes, though from a different theological foundation.

Protestant interpreters observe that the Damascus community's response to corruption was separation and intensified legal observance, while the early Christian response — particularly as articulated by Paul — was to proclaim a righteousness apart from law, received through faith (Romans 3:21-22). Both movements diagnosed the same disease; they prescribed radically different remedies.
