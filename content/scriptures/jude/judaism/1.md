## Jewish Apocalyptic Traditions

Jude is a treasure trove for scholars of Second Temple Judaism. The direct quotation of 1 Enoch 1:9 in verses 14–15, the allusion to the Assumption of Moses in verse 9, and the use of the Watchers tradition in verse 6 demonstrate the author's deep immersion in Jewish apocalyptic literature. Jewish scholars value Jude as evidence of the widespread influence of these traditions in the first century and of the continuity between Jewish and early Christian thought.

## Cain, Balaam, and Korah

Jude 11 invokes three paradigmatic wicked figures from the Hebrew Bible: Cain (the first murderer), Balaam (the prophet corrupted by greed), and Korah (the rebel against divinely appointed authority). Rabbinic literature also uses these figures as moral exempla, and the overlap between Jude's usage and rabbinic tradition demonstrates the shared heritage of Jewish and Christian ethical discourse. Pirkei Avot 5:19 distinguishes between the students of Abraham (generous) and the students of Balaam (greedy).

## Michael and the Devil

Jude 9, describing the archangel Michael's dispute with the devil over Moses's body, draws on a tradition known from Jewish sources. Michael is a prominent figure in Jewish angelology (Daniel 10:13; 12:1), and the tradition of a contest over Moses's body reflects Jewish reverence for Moses and speculation about his death and burial. Jewish readers recognize in Jude's use of this tradition the author's rootedness in the Jewish literary and theological world.
