## Overview

Jude is one of the most Jewish documents in the New Testament, drawing extensively on traditions from 1 Enoch, the Assumption of Moses, and other Second Temple Jewish texts. Jewish scholars find in Jude a valuable window into the Jewish apocalyptic worldview that shaped early Christianity. The letter's vivid use of Jewish judgment traditions — the Exodus generation, the fallen angels, Sodom and Gomorrah, Cain, Balaam, and Korah — demonstrates the deep rootedness of early Christian moral discourse in Jewish narrative and tradition.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### 1 Enoch and Jewish Apocalyptic
Jude 14–15 directly quotes 1 Enoch 1:9, and the reference to angels who abandoned their position (v. 6) draws on the Enochic Watchers tradition. Jewish scholars note that 1 Enoch was widely influential in Second Temple Judaism and that Jude's use of this text demonstrates the fluidity of the canon in the first century. The Enochic traditions about fallen angels, divine judgment, and cosmic eschatology form the conceptual background of Jude's entire argument.

### The Assumption of Moses
Jude 9, describing Michael's dispute with the devil over Moses's body, apparently draws on the lost Assumption of Moses. This tradition, known from references in patristic literature, illustrates the rich world of Jewish parabiblical literature that informed early Christian authors. Jewish readers appreciate the evidence that early Christians were deeply embedded in the Jewish literary and theological world.

### Judgment Typology
Jude's use of Cain, Balaam, and Korah (v. 11) as types of false teachers draws on a well-established Jewish tradition of moral typology. Rabbinic literature also uses these figures as paradigmatic examples of wickedness, and the overlap between Jude's usage and rabbinic moral instruction demonstrates the shared heritage of Jewish and Christian ethical discourse.

## Theological Emphases

- **Jewish Apocalyptic Heritage**: Jude as a repository of Jewish Second Temple traditions
- **1 Enoch**: The direct citation of Enochic prophecy within the New Testament
- **Moral Typology**: The shared use of biblical figures as ethical paradigms
- **Canonical Fluidity**: The first-century understanding of sacred text beyond the strict canon
