## Anti-Gnostic Polemic

Jude's false teachers, who "pervert the grace of our God into sensuality" (v. 4), may represent proto-Gnostic libertines who claimed that spiritual knowledge freed them from moral constraints. The Carpocratians and similar groups taught that the pneumatic could engage in any behavior without spiritual consequence. Jude's vigorous condemnation of this position represents one of the earliest Christian responses to the Gnostic challenge of moral antinomianism.

## The Watchers and Gnostic Cosmology

Jude 6, describing angels who "did not stay within their own position of authority," draws on the Enochic Watchers tradition that would be developed in different directions by Gnostic cosmology. In Gnostic systems, the archons — cosmic rulers analogous to the Watchers — are the creators and jailers of the material world. Jude uses the tradition to warn against transgression, while Gnostics would use similar traditions to explain the origin of the hostile material cosmos.

## Contending Against Gnosis

Jude 3 — "contend for the faith once for all delivered to the saints" — represents an appeal to fixed, authoritative tradition that the Gnostic claim to ongoing secret revelation directly challenged. The Gnostic response was that the public apostolic tradition was incomplete without the esoteric knowledge transmitted through secret channels. Jude's insistence on a faith "once for all delivered" — complete, final, and public — is a direct counter to the Gnostic claim of progressive, hidden revelation.
