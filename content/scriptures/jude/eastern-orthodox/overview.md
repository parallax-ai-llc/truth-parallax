## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Jude values the letter for its defense of apostolic tradition, its use of Jewish apocalyptic traditions, and its magnificent doxology. The Orthodox tradition, which has always maintained a broader view of sacred tradition than Protestantism, is comfortable with Jude's citation of 1 Enoch and the Assumption of Moses, reading these as evidence that truth can be found in traditions beyond the narrow biblical canon. The great doxology (vv. 24–25) is cherished in Orthodox worship.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Sacred Tradition
Jude's use of extra-canonical sources exemplifies the Orthodox understanding that the Church's tradition is broader than the biblical text. The Orthodox approach to Scripture is never *sola Scriptura* but always Scripture within Tradition — the living memory and worship of the Church. Jude's citation of 1 Enoch and the Assumption of Moses demonstrates that the apostolic authors drew on a rich tradition beyond the strict canonical boundaries.

### The Angelic Fall
Jude 6, describing angels who "did not stay within their own position of authority" and are "kept in eternal chains," draws on the 1 Enoch tradition of the Watchers. Orthodox angelology and demonology, developed in the patristic period, incorporates these traditions into a coherent account of the cosmic struggle between good and evil that shapes the Christian spiritual life.

### The Doxology
Jude 24–25 is one of the great doxologies of the Orthodox liturgical tradition. The affirmation that God is able "to keep you from stumbling and to present you blameless before the presence of his glory with great joy" captures the Orthodox confidence in God's preserving power — a power experienced in the sacramental and liturgical life of the Church.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tradition**: The broader tradition of the Church as the context for Scripture
- **Angelic Beings**: The reality of fallen angels and the cosmic spiritual struggle
- **Contending for the Faith**: The defense of apostolic teaching as the Church's perennial task
- **Doxological Confidence**: The closing doxology as an expression of Paschal joy
