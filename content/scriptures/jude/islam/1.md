## False Prophets and Divine Punishment

Jude's denunciation of false teachers who corrupt the community resonates with the Quranic concern about those who distort divine revelation. The Quran warns: "And who is more unjust than one who invents about Allah a lie or denies His verses?" (6:21). Jude's examples of divine punishment — Israel in the wilderness, the angels, Sodom — parallel the Quran's narratives of destroyed peoples who rejected their prophets. Both texts serve as warnings that persistent rejection of divine guidance leads to judgment.

## The Prophetic Tradition of Warning

Jude functions as a prophetic warning letter, calling the community back to faithfulness and away from corruption. This resonates with the Islamic understanding of prophetic mission: every prophet was sent to warn his people against deviation and to call them back to the straight path. The Quran describes Muhammad himself as "a warner to you before a severe punishment" (34:46). Muslim readers recognize in Jude's urgency a voice consistent with the prophetic tradition.

## The Doxology and Divine Sovereignty

Jude 24–25 praises God's power "to keep you from stumbling" and ascribes to God "glory, majesty, dominion, and authority." This doxological affirmation of divine sovereignty resonates with the Islamic understanding of God's absolute power and authority. The Quran declares: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth" (3:189). Muslim readers appreciate the doxology's unambiguous affirmation of God's supreme authority over all creation.
