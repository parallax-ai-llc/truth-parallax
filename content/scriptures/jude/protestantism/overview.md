## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Jude centers on the letter's urgent call to "contend for the faith that was once for all delivered to the saints" (v. 3). This verse has become a rallying cry for Protestant defenders of orthodox doctrine across the centuries, from the Reformation's defense of biblical authority to modern evangelical apologetics. The letter's vivid warnings against false teachers and its magnificent closing doxology have made it a valued text despite its brevity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Contending for the Faith
Jude 3 is one of the most cited verses in Protestant apologetics. The "faith" is understood as the objective body of apostolic teaching — the doctrinal content of the gospel — not merely subjective belief. The call to "contend" (*epagōnizesthai*) implies vigorous defense, and Protestant interpreters have applied this to every challenge to biblical orthodoxy.

### False Teachers and Moral License
Jude's false teachers "pervert the grace of our God into sensuality" (v. 4). Protestant interpreters identify this as antinomianism — the claim that grace eliminates moral obligation. The letter serves as a warning that doctrinal error and moral license are inseparably connected, and that the church must be vigilant against both.

### The Great Doxology
Jude 24–25 — "Now to him who is able to keep you from stumbling and to present you blameless before the presence of his glory with great joy" — is one of the most beloved doxologies in Protestant worship. It affirms God's sovereign power to preserve the faithful and brings the letter's urgent warnings to a conclusion of confident praise.

## Theological Emphases

- **Contending for the Faith**: The defense of apostolic doctrine as a Christian duty
- **Antinomianism**: The perversion of grace into moral license
- **Divine Judgment**: The certainty of judgment on false teachers
- **Preservation**: God's power to keep believers from falling
