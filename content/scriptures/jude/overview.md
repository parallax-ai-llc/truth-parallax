## Overview

Jude is a brief but passionate letter attributed to "Jude, a servant of Jesus Christ and brother of James" — traditionally identified as a brother of Jesus. Written to warn against false teachers who have infiltrated the community, the letter draws on a remarkable range of Jewish traditions, including the pseudepigraphal works of 1 Enoch and the Assumption of Moses. Its vivid imagery, urgent tone, and magnificent closing doxology have made it a distinctive voice in the New Testament canon.

## Structure

Jude's twenty-five verses divide into several sections:

1. **Greeting** (1–2): Address to the beloved called by God
2. **Occasion: Contend for the Faith** (3–4): The urgent need to defend the faith against intruders
3. **Three Examples of Judgment** (5–7): Israel in the wilderness, fallen angels, Sodom and Gomorrah
4. **Denunciation of the False Teachers** (8–16): Their character, conduct, and doom
5. **Exhortation to the Faithful** (17–23): Remember the apostolic warning, build up, pray, show mercy
6. **Doxology** (24–25): The magnificent closing praise to God

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Protestantism**: A call to contend for the faith once delivered to the saints
- **Catholicism**: The defense of apostolic tradition and the reality of divine judgment
- **Eastern Orthodoxy**: The use of extra-canonical traditions and the great doxology
- **Judaism**: The letter's extensive use of Jewish apocalyptic and pseudepigraphal traditions
- **Islam**: The themes of false prophets and divine judgment resonate with Quranic teaching
- **Gnosticism**: The letter's polemic may target proto-Gnostic opponents
