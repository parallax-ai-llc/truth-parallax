Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Announcement of the Duke of Zhou reads the Duke's speeches as articulating the metaphysical foundations of political legitimacy in terms that anticipate Neo-Confucian philosophy. The Mandate of Heaven is understood not as an arbitrary divine decision but as the expression of cosmic principle (tianli) in the political sphere. When a ruler aligns his governance with principle, Heaven's mandate naturally follows; when he departs from principle, the mandate is withdrawn according to the same inexorable logic that governs all phenomena.

The Duke of Zhou's warnings to the Zhou people against complacency receive particular emphasis in Neo-Confucian reading. The insistence that the Mandate must be constantly renewed through moral effort mirrors the Neo-Confucian teaching that the mind requires continual cultivation to maintain its alignment with principle. Just as the individual must practice "seriousness" (jing) to keep selfish desires from obscuring the heavenly principle within, so must the political community practice moral vigilance to preserve Heaven's favor.

Neo-Confucian readers also value the Announcement for its demonstration of the daotong (orthodox transmission of the Way). The Duke of Zhou's role as regent and moral teacher places him in the lineage of sages extending from Yao and Shun through the founders of the Zhou. His combination of philosophical insight and practical governance embodies the Neo-Confucian ideal that the sage is simultaneously a thinker and a statesman, one who realizes principle in both personal cultivation and public service.
