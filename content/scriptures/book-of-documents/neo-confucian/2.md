Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Canon of Shun and the Documents of Shang reads these texts through the philosophical framework of principle (li) and material force (qi), finding in Shun's governance and the Shang dynasty's political culture illustrations of how cosmic principle operates in the realm of human affairs. Shun's legendary filial piety is understood not merely as moral exemplarity but as the natural expression of principle fully realized in the sphere of family relationships.

The Neo-Confucian reading of the Shang documents focuses on the moral-metaphysical dimensions of the dynastic transitions they describe. Tang's overthrow of the corrupt Xia ruler Jie is understood as the reassertion of heavenly principle (tianli) against the accumulated distortions of human desire (renyu). The Neo-Confucian tradition emphasizes that revolution is justified not by the ambition of the challenger but by the degree to which the existing ruler has departed from principle. This reading elevates the political narratives to the level of metaphysical drama.

The ministerial dialogues preserved in these documents receive careful Neo-Confucian attention as models of the relationship between the investigation of principle (qiongli) and practical governance. Ministers such as Yi Yin are understood as sages who had penetrated to the principle underlying all affairs and could therefore offer counsel that aligned political action with cosmic moral order. Their example demonstrates the Neo-Confucian conviction that philosophical understanding and political engagement are not separate activities but complementary aspects of the sage's vocation.
