Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Great Plan (Hong Fan) treats this text as the most important bridge between cosmology and governance in the classical canon. The nine categories outlined in the Hong Fan are read by Neo-Confucian philosophers as a practical demonstration of how principle (li) manifests across all domains of reality — from the five phases and natural phenomena to human affairs and political institutions. The Hong Fan thus provides a systematic framework for the Neo-Confucian project of investigating things (gewu) to arrive at comprehensive understanding.

Zhu Xi gave the Hong Fan's treatment of the five phases (wuxing) careful metaphysical analysis, interpreting them not as material elements but as dynamic patterns of principle operating through material force (qi). This reading integrates the Hong Fan's cosmology into the broader Neo-Confucian framework, showing that the ancient sages understood, in practical terms, the same metaphysical truths that the Song philosophers articulated theoretically. The correspondence between natural signs and political conduct described in the Hong Fan confirms the Neo-Confucian conviction that the moral and natural orders are fundamentally unified.

The Hong Fan's teaching on the royal standard (huangji) resonated deeply with the Neo-Confucian emphasis on the mean (zhong). The ruler who establishes the grand standard embodies the perfectly balanced expression of principle, serving as the center point around which the political cosmos organizes itself. This Neo-Confucian reading connects the political theory of the Hong Fan to the metaphysics of the Doctrine of the Mean, creating a unified vision of moral cosmology.
