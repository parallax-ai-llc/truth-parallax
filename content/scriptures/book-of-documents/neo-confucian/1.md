Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Canon of Yao centers on the famous sixteen-character transmission formula that Yao supposedly communicated to Shun: "The human mind is precarious, the mind of the Dao is subtle; be refined, be single-minded; hold fast the mean." This passage became the cornerstone of the Neo-Confucian theory of the orthodox transmission of the Way (daotong), establishing an unbroken chain of sage-to-sage transmission from Yao and Shun through Confucius and Mencius to the Song dynasty Neo-Confucians themselves.

Zhu Xi treated the sixteen-character formula as the distillation of all sage learning, articulating the fundamental Neo-Confucian distinction between the human mind (renxin), which is vulnerable to selfish desire, and the mind of the Dao (daoxin), which perceives heavenly principle directly. The practice of being "refined and single-minded" describes the work of self-cultivation through which the scholar-sage purifies the human mind until it aligns with the Dao-mind. This reading transforms the Canon of Yao from a political document into a treatise on inner cultivation.

The abdication from Yao to Shun is understood by Neo-Confucian readers as demonstrating that the sage-kings had fully realized principle (li) in their own natures and could therefore recognize it in others. The meritocratic ideal of the abdication system reflects, in Neo-Confucian terms, the capacity of the illuminated mind to perceive moral reality without distortion — the same capacity that Neo-Confucian self-cultivation seeks to restore in every individual.
