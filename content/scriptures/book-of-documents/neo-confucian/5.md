Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Testaments and Charges reads these royal speeches as expressions of the sages' effort to transmit their understanding of principle (li) to successive generations of rulers and officials. The testamentary genre is valued for its distillation of moral insight at moments of ultimate seriousness, when the departing ruler is most fully concentrated on essential truths. Neo-Confucian readers find in these final words confirmation that the sages understood governance as fundamentally an expression of inner moral realization.

The charges to officials are interpreted through the Neo-Confucian framework as demonstrations of the sage-king's capacity to perceive principle in others and to match individuals with appropriate responsibilities. The process of commissioning officials becomes an exercise in the investigation of things (gewu) applied to human character — the ruler discerns the specific principle realized in each individual and assigns duties accordingly. This reading elevates the administrative act of appointment to a philosophical practice continuous with the sage's broader project of comprehending principle in all its manifestations.

Neo-Confucian textual criticism played a crucial role in the study of these documents. Scholars such as Yan Ruoqu and Hui Dong subjected the Testaments and Charges to rigorous philological analysis, demonstrating that several documents in this section of the received text were later fabrications. This critical work exemplifies the Neo-Confucian commitment to intellectual honesty and the investigation of things, even when the results challenged received tradition.
