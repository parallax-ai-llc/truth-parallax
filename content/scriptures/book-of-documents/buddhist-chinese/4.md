Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Announcement of the Duke of Zhou engages with the Mandate of Heaven doctrine as a Chinese expression of the universal principle of moral causation that Buddhism articulates through the concept of karma. The transfer of the mandate from Shang to Zhou is understood as demonstrating that collective unwholesome conduct inevitably produces political collapse, while collective virtue generates the conditions for stable and just governance. This parallel allows Buddhist interpreters to affirm the essential moral logic of the Confucian political tradition.

The Duke of Zhou's warnings against complacency resonate deeply with Buddhist teachings on the impermanence of favorable conditions. Buddhist readers note that the Duke's insistence on constant moral vigilance mirrors the Buddhist emphasis on heedfulness (appamada) — the teaching that favorable circumstances, including political power, must not be taken for granted because all conditioned states are inherently impermanent. The Duke's awareness that the Zhou could follow the path of the Shang reflects a political wisdom consistent with Buddhist insight into the transient nature of worldly achievements.

Buddhist-Chinese interpreters also find in the Duke of Zhou's compassionate concern for the conquered Shang people an attitude consistent with the bodhisattva's universal compassion. The Duke's efforts to reassure and integrate the defeated population, rather than simply subjugating them, suggest a form of political compassion that Buddhist readers connect to the Mahayana ideal of acting for the benefit of all sentient beings, regardless of affiliation or status.
