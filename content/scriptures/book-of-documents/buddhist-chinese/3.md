Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Great Plan (Hong Fan) finds in its cosmological framework significant points of contact with Buddhist philosophical systems. The Hong Fan's integration of natural phenomena, human conduct, and political governance into a unified system resonates with the Buddhist understanding of interdependent origination (pratityasamutpada), in which all phenomena arise in dependence upon conditions and are interconnected within a vast web of causation.

The five phases (wuxing) described in the Hong Fan are read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters through the lens of conditioned arising. Rather than treating them as fixed elemental categories, Buddhist readers understand them as illustrating the ceaseless transformation and mutual conditioning of phenomena — a dynamic that parallels the Buddhist analysis of how mental and physical events co-arise in dependence upon one another. The Hong Fan's observation that misgovernance disrupts the natural order reflects the Buddhist understanding that human actions have consequences reaching far beyond the human realm.

The Hong Fan's royal standard (huangji) is compared by Buddhist readers to the Middle Way (madhyamaka) of Buddhist philosophy. Both traditions locate the ideal in a balanced position that avoids extremes, though they understand the nature and purpose of this balance differently. The Confucian middle way serves the harmonious ordering of society, while the Buddhist Middle Way points toward liberation from conditioned existence altogether. Buddhist-Chinese commentators note this parallel while acknowledging the divergence in ultimate orientation.
