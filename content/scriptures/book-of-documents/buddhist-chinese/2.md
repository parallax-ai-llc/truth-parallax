Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Canon of Shun and the Documents of Shang focuses on the moral psychology of governance and the karmic dimensions of political action. Shun's legendary filial piety, maintained even toward family members who sought to harm him, is understood through the Buddhist framework of patience (kshanti) and the transformation of adversity into spiritual opportunity. Buddhist readers see in Shun's conduct an example of how the cultivation of virtue in difficult circumstances generates merit that naturally brings about worldly success.

The Shang dynasty documents are read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as illustrating the principle of karmic causation in the political realm. The dynasty's rise through Tang's virtue and its eventual fall through later rulers' corruption mirror the Buddhist teaching that wholesome actions produce favorable results while unwholesome actions lead inevitably to suffering. The dynastic cycle described in these documents is understood as a collective manifestation of the same karmic principles that govern individual lives, demonstrating the interpenetration of personal and communal karma.

The ministerial counsel preserved in these documents resonates with the Buddhist tradition of the kalyanamitra — the spiritual friend who guides others on the path. Buddhist readers draw parallels between the loyal minister who speaks truth to the ruler and the dharma teacher who confronts the student with uncomfortable truths for the sake of spiritual progress. Both relationships require courage, compassion, and a commitment to truth that transcends personal comfort or self-interest.
