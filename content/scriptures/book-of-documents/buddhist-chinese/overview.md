## Overview

Chinese Buddhist engagement with the Book of Documents reflects the broader Buddhist project of demonstrating compatibility between Buddhist teachings and Chinese political culture. Buddhist monks and scholars who participated in imperial courts needed to show that Buddhist values of compassion, merit, and moral causation complemented rather than contradicted the political ideals articulated in the Confucian classics. The Shujing's emphasis on the moral foundations of governance provided natural points of convergence with Buddhist political thought.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist-Chinese readers of the Shujing draw parallels between the Mandate of Heaven and the Buddhist concept of karmic causation in the political realm. The principle that Heaven withdraws its mandate from immoral rulers is understood through the lens of collective karma and the merit of rulers. The sage-kings' compassion for their people resonates with the bodhisattva ideal of selfless service. Buddhist commentators also find in the Shujing's emphasis on ministerial counsel a parallel to the sangha's role in advising secular rulers. The text's vision of governance as fundamentally moral rather than merely administrative aligns with Buddhist teachings on the cakravartin, the wheel-turning righteous king.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Buddhist-Chinese reading of the Shujing contributes to ongoing discussions about the role of religious ethics in public life and the compatibility of contemplative traditions with active engagement in governance and social justice.
