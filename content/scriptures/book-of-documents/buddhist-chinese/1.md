Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Canon of Yao finds in the sage-king narratives compelling parallels with Buddhist ideals of selfless leadership and non-attachment to power. Yao's abdication in favor of Shun resonates with the bodhisattva ideal of renouncing personal privilege for the benefit of all beings. Buddhist readers note that the sage-king model, in which authority flows to the most virtuous rather than the most powerful, mirrors the Buddhist understanding that genuine leadership arises from spiritual cultivation rather than worldly ambition.

The Canon of Yao's portrait of ideal governance is read through the Buddhist lens of compassion (karuna) and wisdom (prajna). Yao's attentiveness to the welfare of his people, his concern with harmonizing human activity with natural rhythms, and his humility in recognizing a worthier successor all reflect qualities that Buddhism attributes to the enlightened ruler. Chinese Buddhist commentators drew on these parallels to argue that Confucian political virtues and Buddhist spiritual qualities are complementary rather than contradictory, supporting the project of cultural harmonization.

The Documents of Yu and Xia, particularly Yu's legendary flood control, are read by Buddhist interpreters as an illustration of selfless action (analogous to the bodhisattva's activity) within the worldly realm. Yu's willingness to sacrifice his own comfort for the welfare of his people parallels the bodhisattva's vow to remain in samsara until all beings are liberated. Buddhist readers understand Yu's labors as an expression of worldly compassion that, while not constituting Buddhist awakening, represents a high form of ethical accomplishment.
