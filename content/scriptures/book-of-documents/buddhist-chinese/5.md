Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Testaments and Charges approaches these deathbed speeches and official commissions as moments that illuminate the relationship between mortality, wisdom, and ethical responsibility. The testamentary speeches, delivered at the threshold of death, carry particular significance for Buddhist readers, who understand the moment of death as a crucial juncture in the karmic continuum. The dying ruler's effort to transmit moral wisdom reflects a form of merit-making that benefits both the departing individual and those who receive the teaching.

Buddhist interpreters read the testaments' emphasis on moral legacy through the Buddhist concept of "merit transfer" — the practice of dedicating accumulated merit to the benefit of others. The sage-king who, in his final moments, concentrates on transmitting ethical guidance rather than securing personal comfort demonstrates a form of selfless concern that parallels the Buddhist practice of dedicating one's spiritual accomplishments to the liberation of all beings. The testaments thus become occasions for reflecting on the Buddhist teaching that the value of a life is measured by the benefit it brings to others.

The charges to officials are read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as establishing a framework of moral accountability that resonates with the Buddhist concept of right action (samma kammanta). The formal articulation of ethical expectations in these commissions reflects a recognition that political power entails moral responsibility — a principle fully consistent with the Buddhist teaching that those who exercise authority bear a heavier karmic burden and must therefore practice greater mindfulness in their conduct.
