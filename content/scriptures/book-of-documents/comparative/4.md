Comparative study of the Announcement of the Duke of Zhou places the Chinese Mandate of Heaven doctrine in dialogue with other ancient traditions of legitimating political authority through divine sanction. The Hebrew Bible's concept of divine covenant, in which God establishes a conditional relationship with kings and peoples, provides the closest structural parallel to the Mandate of Heaven. Both traditions make divine favor contingent upon moral conduct, creating a framework for holding rulers accountable to transcendent standards. However, the Chinese concept is more impersonal and moralized than the Hebrew covenantal model.

The Duke of Zhou's rhetorical justification for conquest invites comparison with political apologetics in other ancient civilizations. The Assyrian annals' claims of divine commission, the Persian inscriptions at Behistun justifying Darius's seizure of power, and the Roman tradition of bellum iustum (just war) all represent attempts to legitimize military conquest through moral or religious argument. The Chinese tradition is distinctive in the degree to which it foregrounds the moral failure of the displaced dynasty rather than the military prowess or divine election of the conqueror.

The Duke of Zhou's role as regent also invites cross-cultural comparison. The tension between the regent's power and the young king's nominal authority parallels situations in other ancient monarchies, from the regencies of the Egyptian queen Hatshepsut to the Norse tradition of trusted kinsmen governing for young kings. The Duke of Zhou's eventual peaceful transfer of power to King Cheng became a paradigmatic example in Chinese political culture, comparable in its normative influence to Cincinnatus's return to private life in the Roman tradition.
