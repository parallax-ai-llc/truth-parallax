Comparative study of the Great Plan (Hong Fan) situates its cosmological governance theory alongside attempts by other ancient civilizations to integrate their understanding of the natural world with principles of political administration. The Hong Fan's system of correspondences between the five phases, human affairs, and natural phenomena finds parallels in the Platonic-Aristotelian tradition of governance modeled on cosmic order, Indian varnashrama dharma connecting social organization to cosmic principles, and the Egyptian concept of ma'at as a cosmological foundation for political and moral order.

The Hong Fan's five-phase theory invites comparison with the Greek theory of four elements, the Indian concept of five great elements (mahabhuta), and other ancient systems of elemental classification. While all these traditions sought to identify the fundamental constituents of nature and their relationships, the Chinese system is distinctive in its emphasis on dynamic transformation and mutual interaction among the phases rather than on static composition. Comparative analysis reveals fundamentally different metaphysical assumptions underlying superficially similar cosmological projects.

The concept of the royal standard (huangji) as a cosmological center point invites comparison with other traditions that locate the ruler at the axis of the political cosmos. The Hindu concept of the chakravartin as the universal monarch, the Roman idea of the emperor as the center of the orbis terrarum, and the Japanese concept of the emperor as the living axis connecting heaven and earth all share with the Hong Fan the notion that legitimate political authority requires cosmic positioning, though each tradition constructs this relationship differently.
