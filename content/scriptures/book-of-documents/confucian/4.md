Confucian interpretation of the Announcement of the Duke of Zhou treats these speeches as the definitive articulation of the Mandate of Heaven (Tianming) doctrine and the moral justification for the Zhou dynasty's conquest of the Shang. The Duke of Zhou, serving as regent for the young King Cheng, addresses the conquered Shang people, the Zhou nobility, and the royal court in a series of proclamations that became the template for all subsequent Confucian political rhetoric.

The Duke of Zhou's central argument — that the Shang lost Heaven's mandate through moral corruption and that the Zhou received it through superior virtue — establishes the fundamental Confucian teaching that political legitimacy is conditional upon moral conduct. The Duke's repeated warnings to the Zhou people not to follow the path of Shang decadence demonstrate that the Mandate is not a permanent possession but a trust that must be continually renewed through righteous governance. Confucian readers find in this teaching the essential framework for holding rulers accountable.

The Confucian tradition reveres the Duke of Zhou as second only to Confucius himself in the lineage of sages. His willingness to serve as regent without seizing the throne, his tireless efforts to consolidate Zhou rule through moral governance rather than mere force, and his establishment of ritual and institutional structures for the new dynasty embody the Confucian ideal of the scholar-statesman. Confucius himself expressed the wish to dream of the Duke of Zhou, indicating the depth of his reverence for this figure.
