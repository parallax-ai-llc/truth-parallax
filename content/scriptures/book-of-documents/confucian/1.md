Confucian interpretation of the Canon of Yao and the associated Documents of Yu and Xia treats these texts as the most authoritative record of ideal governance in human history. The sage-king Yao is portrayed as the archetype of the virtuous ruler: one who governs through moral example, harmonizes the calendar with the natural order, and — most remarkably — yields the throne not to his own son but to the most worthy candidate, Shun. This practice of abdication (shanrang) establishes the Confucian principle that political authority properly flows to moral merit rather than hereditary privilege.

The Canon of Yao's description of Yao's governance sets the standard against which all subsequent rulers are measured. His regulation of the calendar, appointment of capable officials, and attentiveness to the welfare of the people embody the Confucian conviction that the ruler's primary responsibility is to serve as a moral exemplar who brings order to human society by aligning it with the patterns of Heaven and Earth. The text's emphasis on Yao's humility and accessibility models the Confucian ideal of leadership without domination.

The transmission from Yao to Shun to Yu represents, for Confucian readers, the golden age of governance when the Way (Dao) prevailed in the world. Yu's founding of the Xia dynasty by accepting the throne from Shun, and his legendary labors in controlling the floods, further demonstrate that legitimate authority is earned through selfless service to the people.
