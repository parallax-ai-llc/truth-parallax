Confucian interpretation of the Canon of Shun and the Documents of Shang reads these texts as demonstrating the continuity of sage governance from the legendary era into the historical Shang dynasty. Shun's exemplary conduct — his filial piety toward an abusive father, his wise appointment of officials, and his establishment of the five relationships as the foundation of social order — provides the Confucian tradition with its most detailed portrait of the sage-ruler's character before assuming power.

The Documents of Shang preserve speeches and proclamations attributed to the dynasty's rulers and ministers, most notably King Tang's justification for overthrowing the corrupt last ruler of the Xia dynasty. Confucian readers find in Tang's speech the foundational logic of the Mandate of Heaven: when a ruler's corruption becomes intolerable, Heaven withdraws its mandate and transfers authority to a more virtuous leader. This passage establishes the Confucian teaching that revolution against tyranny can be morally justified, a principle that Mencius would later develop more fully.

The dialogues between rulers and ministers in these documents model the Confucian ideal of political counsel. Ministers such as Yi Yin serve as examples of the loyal advisor who speaks truth to power, offering guidance grounded in moral principle rather than flattery. The Confucian tradition finds in these exchanges the template for the relationship between the scholar-official and the sovereign that would define Chinese political culture for centuries.
