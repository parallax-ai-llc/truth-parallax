Confucian interpretation of the Testaments and Charges reads these royal speeches and mandates as the most intimate expressions of the sage-kings' moral vision for governance. The testaments (gu) represent the final words of departing rulers, charged with the weight of a lifetime's experience and the urgency of transmitting wisdom to successors. The charges (ming) are formal investitures and commissions given to officials and heirs, establishing the moral expectations that accompany political authority.

The testamentary speeches are valued by Confucian readers as moments of supreme moral clarity, when the dying ruler distills the essential principles of governance for the benefit of those who will carry the burden of authority after him. These final words carry a particular moral authority in the Confucian tradition, where the approach of death is understood to concentrate the mind on what is truly important. The teachings conveyed in these testaments emphasize vigilance against complacency, attention to the welfare of the common people, and the preservation of ritual propriety.

The charges to officials model the Confucian understanding of the reciprocal obligations between rulers and ministers. In commissioning officials, the ruler articulates the moral standards expected of those who exercise delegated authority, while simultaneously acknowledging his dependence on their competence and integrity. The Confucian tradition finds in these charges a vision of governance as a collaborative moral enterprise, where authority flows from virtue rather than position and where the ruler's greatest power lies in his ability to identify and empower worthy servants.
