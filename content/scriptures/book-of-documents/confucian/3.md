Confucian interpretation of the Great Plan (Hong Fan) treats this text as the most systematic articulation of cosmological governance in the classical canon. Attributed to the Viscount of Ji, who transmitted it to King Wu of Zhou after the conquest of Shang, the Hong Fan outlines nine categories (jiuchou) that together constitute a comprehensive theory of statecraft integrating cosmology, ethics, and practical administration. Confucian readers regard it as demonstrating that good governance requires the ruler to align human institutions with the cosmic order.

The nine categories of the Hong Fan encompass the five phases (wuxing), the five affairs of governance, the eight instruments of administration, the five markers of time, the royal standard, the three virtues of rulership, the examination of doubts through divination, the verification through natural signs, and the five sources of happiness. Confucian commentators emphasize that this integrated system reflects the conviction that political, natural, and moral orders are fundamentally interconnected. A ruler who neglects any category disrupts the harmony of the whole.

The Hong Fan's teaching on the royal standard (huangji) receives particular Confucian attention. The ruler must embody the "grand mean" — a standard of conduct that is neither excessive nor deficient — serving as the pivot around which the entire social and political order revolves. This concept connects the Hong Fan to the broader Confucian tradition of the mean (zhongyong), establishing that governance at its highest level is an expression of moral balance and cosmic attunement.
