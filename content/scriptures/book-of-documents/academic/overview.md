## Overview

Academic study of the Book of Documents has been dominated by questions of textual authenticity that have occupied Chinese scholars since the Song dynasty and intensified in the Qing. The received text includes both "New Text" chapters, transmitted through oral tradition after the Qin burning of books, and "Old Text" chapters, supposedly recovered from the walls of Confucius's former home. Yan Ruoqu's seventeenth-century demonstration that the Old Text chapters were later forgeries ranks among the most celebrated achievements of Chinese textual criticism.

## Interpretive Approach

Modern academic approaches to the Shujing employ multiple methodologies. Textual critics continue to refine the distinction between authentic and forged chapters using linguistic analysis, stylistic comparison, and newly discovered manuscript evidence from sites such as the Tsinghua bamboo slips. Historians mine the authentic chapters for evidence about early Chinese political institutions, religious practices, and social organization. Linguists study the archaic prose style as evidence for the development of literary Chinese. Comparative political theorists examine the Documents' conceptions of sovereignty, legitimacy, and the limits of royal authority in relation to other ancient traditions of political thought. Archaeological discoveries continue to reshape understanding of the historical contexts described in the text.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Shujing remains central to academic debates about the origins of Chinese political thought, the reliability of transmitted texts, and the methods of historical reconstruction. The Tsinghua bamboo slips, acquired in 2008, have opened exciting new avenues for understanding the text's earliest forms.
