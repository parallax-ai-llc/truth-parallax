Academic study of the Canon of Shun and the Documents of Shang situates these texts at the intersection of mythology, political ideology, and historical record. Scholars analyze the Shun narratives using comparative mythology to identify widespread folk motifs — the persecuted youngest son, the tests imposed by a hostile father, the rise of the virtuous outsider — while also investigating whether these narratives preserve genuine memories of pre-dynastic political arrangements, including possible traditions of non-hereditary succession.

The Shang dynasty documents present particular challenges and opportunities for academic study. The discovery of Shang oracle bone inscriptions in the late nineteenth century provided an independent source for evaluating the historical claims made in the Shangshu's Shang documents. Scholars have compared the political vocabulary, divine names, and institutional references in the documents with evidence from oracle bones and bronze inscriptions, finding both correspondences and discrepancies that illuminate the processes through which historical memory was preserved and transformed.

The institution of abdication (shanrang) described in these chapters has generated extensive scholarly debate. Some scholars view the abdication narratives as reflecting genuine pre-dynastic political practices in which leadership circulated among competing lineages; others interpret them as later ideological constructions projecting Confucian meritocratic ideals onto a mythologized past. Archaeological and anthropological evidence from contemporary and comparative sources has been marshaled on both sides of this debate, which remains one of the most consequential questions in early Chinese studies.
