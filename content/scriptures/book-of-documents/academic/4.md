Academic study of the Announcement of the Duke of Zhou regards these speeches as among the most historically significant texts in the entire Shangshu, providing primary evidence for the political ideology of the early Zhou dynasty. Scholars analyze the rhetorical strategies employed by the Duke of Zhou to legitimize the Zhou conquest, noting the innovative articulation of the Mandate of Heaven doctrine as a response to the specific political challenge of justifying the overthrow of an established dynasty.

The Announcement documents have been subjected to rigorous linguistic analysis to determine their dating and authenticity. Several of the speeches attributed to the Duke of Zhou exhibit archaic linguistic features consistent with early Western Zhou composition, including characteristic verb constructions, particle usage, and vocabulary that differ from later classical Chinese. Bronze inscription evidence has been used to corroborate specific linguistic features, strengthening the case for authenticity of key passages. However, scholars continue to debate the extent to which the transmitted text preserves the original speeches versus later editorial revision.

Intellectual historians study the Announcement for its role in the development of the Mandate of Heaven concept, examining how the Duke of Zhou's formulation differs from and extends Shang conceptions of divine authority. The transition from the Shang concept of a supreme deity (Di) who personally supported the ruling house to the Zhou concept of a moralized Heaven (Tian) that responds to the virtue of rulers represents one of the most consequential intellectual transformations in Chinese history, and the Duke of Zhou's speeches provide the primary textual evidence for this shift.
