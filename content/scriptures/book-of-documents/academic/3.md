Academic study of the Great Plan (Hong Fan) focuses on questions of dating, composition, and intellectual history. The text purports to record the Viscount of Ji's transmission of cosmological knowledge to King Wu of Zhou after the conquest of Shang, but scholars have debated whether the Hong Fan reflects genuine early Zhou political thought or represents a later systematization of ideas that developed over centuries. Linguistic analysis suggests that the text in its current form dates to no earlier than the Warring States period, though it may preserve older conceptual elements.

The Hong Fan's system of correspondences between the five phases, human affairs, and natural phenomena has been studied as a key document in the history of Chinese correlative cosmology. Scholars trace the development of five-phase theory from its earliest appearances in texts like the Hong Fan through its elaboration by Zou Yan and its eventual integration into Han dynasty imperial ideology. The Hong Fan represents a relatively early stage in this intellectual development, before the system of correspondences became fully systematized.

The concept of the royal standard (huangji) has received extensive scholarly attention as a document in the history of Chinese political thought. Scholars debate whether huangji originally referred to the ruler's cosmic position as the center of a moral-political order or whether this interpretation represents a later recontextualization of a term with more specific administrative meaning. Comparative study of political cosmology in other ancient civilizations provides a broader context for understanding the Hong Fan's attempt to integrate cosmology, ethics, and governance into a unified theoretical framework.
