Academic study of the Testaments and Charges has been at the center of the most important textual criticism debate in Chinese literary history. Several documents in this section belong to the "Old Text" (guwen) portion of the Shangshu, which Yan Ruoqu demonstrated in his 1745 work Evidence of the Forged Old Text Documents to be later fabrications rather than authentic ancient texts. This groundbreaking work of philological criticism, which analyzed vocabulary, grammar, allusions, and conceptual frameworks to identify anachronisms, established methods of textual criticism that influenced Chinese scholarship for centuries.

The authentic "New Text" (jinwen) documents in this section provide valuable evidence for understanding the political rhetoric and institutional practices of the early Zhou dynasty. Scholars analyze the formal structure of testamentary speeches and official charges to reconstruct the protocols of Zhou court communication, the ideological framework of political authority, and the mechanisms through which rulers transmitted power and delegated responsibility. These institutional practices are studied in comparison with court protocols documented in bronze inscriptions and archaeological evidence from Western Zhou sites.

The genre of the testament (gu) as a form of political literature has attracted scholarly attention in its own right. Comparative literary scholars examine the deathbed speech as a recurrent genre across ancient civilizations, noting parallels with Jacob's blessings in Genesis, the testamentary traditions of Greco-Roman literature, and the final teachings attributed to the Buddha. These comparisons illuminate the universal human impulse to invest dying words with special authority while revealing the culturally specific conventions that shape each tradition's treatment of this liminal moment.
