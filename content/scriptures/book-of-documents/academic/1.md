Academic study of the Canon of Yao and the Documents of Yu and Xia addresses fundamental questions about the historicity, dating, and composition of these texts. Modern scholars debate whether the sage-kings Yao, Shun, and Yu represent historical individuals, mythologized cultural heroes, or composite literary creations. Archaeological evidence from Neolithic sites such as Taosi and Erlitou has been invoked in attempts to correlate the legendary accounts with material culture, though consensus remains elusive.

The textual history of the Canon of Yao is particularly complex. The sixteen-character transmission formula that became central to Neo-Confucian philosophy appears only in the "Old Text" version of the document, which Yan Ruoqu and subsequent scholars have identified as a later fabrication. This finding has profound implications for the history of Chinese philosophy, since one of the most important doctrines in the Neo-Confucian tradition rests on a text that may not have existed before the fourth century CE. The Tsinghua bamboo slips have provided new evidence for reconstructing the earliest forms of these documents.

Linguistic analysis of the authentic portions of these documents reveals archaic features consistent with very early composition, including grammatical structures and vocabulary that differ significantly from later classical Chinese. Scholars debate whether these features reflect genuine oral traditions dating to the late third or early second millennium BCE or represent deliberate archaization by later compilers seeking to create an impression of antiquity.
