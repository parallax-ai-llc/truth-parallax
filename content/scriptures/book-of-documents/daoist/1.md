Daoist interpretation of the Canon of Yao engages selectively with the sage-king narratives, finding in the abdication tradition an unexpected resonance with Daoist ideals of non-attachment and selfless leadership. Yao's willingness to surrender the throne to Shun, and Shun's subsequent yielding to Yu, embody a principle of rulership through self-effacement that echoes the Daodejing's teaching that the best leader is one whose subjects barely know he exists. The Daoist reading recovers from these narratives a model of power exercised through letting go rather than grasping.

However, Daoist readers maintain a critical perspective on the institutional framework that the Canon of Yao describes. Yao's regulation of the calendar, appointment of officials, and organization of administrative functions represent, from a Daoist standpoint, the beginning of the artificial ordering that progressively separates human society from the spontaneous order of the Dao. The Zhuangzi's own treatment of Yao and Shun is deeply ambivalent, sometimes praising their humility while elsewhere suggesting that even their benevolent governance represents a departure from the pristine simplicity of an earlier age.

The Documents of Yu and Xia, particularly Yu's legendary labors in controlling the floods, receive a mixed Daoist reception. While Yu's selfless service is admirable, his monumental engineering project can be read as the archetype of the human attempt to impose order on nature — an attempt that Daoist philosophy views as ultimately futile and counterproductive.
