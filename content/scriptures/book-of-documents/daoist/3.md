Daoist interpretation of the Great Plan (Hong Fan) presents a pointed critique of the systematic attempt to reduce the governance of human society and the natural world to a set of fixed categories. The nine categories of the Hong Fan, which Confucian readers celebrate as a comprehensive cosmological framework for statecraft, are viewed by Daoist readers as the epitome of the classifying, systematizing impulse that the Daodejing warns against: "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao."

The Hong Fan's treatment of the five phases (wuxing) and their correspondences with human affairs is critiqued by Daoist interpreters as an attempt to impose rigid conceptual categories on the fluid and indeterminate processes of nature. While Daoism developed its own sophisticated cosmological theories incorporating the five phases, the Daoist approach emphasizes their dynamic interaction and mutual transformation rather than their use as a fixed framework for administrative control. The Hong Fan's application of these categories to governance represents, from a Daoist perspective, the confusion of the map with the territory.

Yet Daoist readers find in the Hong Fan's acknowledgment of natural signs and portents a grudging recognition that the natural world operates according to its own logic, which human governance must heed rather than override. The passages describing how misgovernance produces natural disasters suggest an awareness — consistent with Daoist teaching — that human political action has consequences in the natural order that cannot be controlled through administrative means alone.
