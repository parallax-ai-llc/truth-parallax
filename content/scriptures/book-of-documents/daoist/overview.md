## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Book of Documents is largely critical, reading the text as an exemplar of the over-governed civilization that Daoist thinkers sought to transcend. The Zhuangzi and other Daoist works pointedly contrast the elaborate institutional arrangements described in the Shujing with the ideal of governing through non-action (wu-wei). Yet Daoist readers also find within certain passages — particularly those describing the sage-kings' deference and abdication — echoes of the Daoist principle that true leadership involves self-effacement rather than self-assertion.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist interpreters approach the Shujing by distinguishing between two layers within the text. The first layer, which they critique, presents governance as a complex system of rules, offices, and ritual prescriptions — the very apparatus of civilization that Laozi and Zhuangzi identified as the source of artificiality and social discord. The second layer, which they selectively appropriate, shows sage-kings like Yao and Shun governing with humility and willingness to yield power, resonating with the Daodejing's teaching that the best ruler is one whose people barely know he exists. The abdication narratives become parables of non-attachment to power.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Daoist critique of the Shujing's institutional vision remains relevant in discussions about governance, bureaucracy, and the relationship between natural order and human law. Its emphasis on leadership through self-effacement and simplicity offers a counterpoint to technocratic models of governance.
