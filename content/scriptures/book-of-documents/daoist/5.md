Daoist interpretation of the Testaments and Charges finds in these final speeches and official commissions both the most human and the most problematic aspects of the Confucian political tradition. The testaments, with their deathbed urgency and concentration on essential truths, approach the kind of existential clarity that Daoist practice cultivates — the direct confrontation with mortality that strips away pretense and reveals what matters. In this sense, Daoist readers acknowledge that the dying sage-kings may have come closest to genuine insight at precisely the moment they let go of their power.

However, the content of these testaments — their emphasis on maintaining institutional structures, preserving ritual protocols, and continuing the project of civilized governance — represents, for Daoist readers, a failure to follow this existential clarity to its logical conclusion. From a Daoist perspective, the approaching death of a ruler should reveal the ultimate insignificance of political institutions rather than intensify the desire to perpetuate them. The sage-kings' final words reveal their attachment to the very structures that a fully awakened individual would release.

The charges to officials are read by Daoist interpreters as the most explicitly bureaucratic and therefore most problematic texts in the Shujing. The formalization of authority through official commissions embodies the Confucian belief that social order requires explicit institutional structures — a conviction directly opposed to the Daoist insight that the most effective governance arises spontaneously from the natural order of things rather than from human design.
