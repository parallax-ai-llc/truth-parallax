Daoist interpretation of the Canon of Shun and the Documents of Shang reads these texts as documenting the progressive institutionalization of governance that Daoist thought identifies as the source of social disorder. While Shun's personal virtue — particularly his extraordinary filial piety and humility — may resonate with Daoist ideals of the unconditioned response, his establishment of the five relationships and elaborate system of official appointments represents exactly the kind of imposed social structure that the Daodejing critiques.

The Shang dynasty documents, with their accounts of dynastic overthrow and political legitimation, are approached by Daoist readers as evidence of the cycle of rise and fall that inevitably accompanies the attempt to construct permanent political institutions. Tang's overthrow of Jie, while justified within the Confucian framework of the Mandate of Heaven, is read by Daoist interpreters as merely one turn in an endless cycle of ambition, consolidation, corruption, and collapse. The Daoist perspective suggests that this cycle is inherent in the very nature of institutional governance rather than the result of particular rulers' moral failings.

The ministerial counsel preserved in these documents receives a characteristically ambivalent Daoist reception. While the sincerity of advisors like Yi Yin is acknowledged, Daoist readers note that the elaborate system of political counsel itself perpetuates the assumption that governance requires constant intervention — an assumption fundamentally at odds with the Daoist ideal of wu-wei, governing through minimal interference and trust in the natural order.
