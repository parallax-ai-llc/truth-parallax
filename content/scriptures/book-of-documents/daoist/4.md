Daoist interpretation of the Announcement of the Duke of Zhou approaches the Duke's speeches with characteristic skepticism toward the ideological project they serve. From a Daoist perspective, the Duke of Zhou's elaborate justification for the Zhou conquest of Shang — however sincerely motivated — represents the kind of moral rhetoric that powerful actors deploy to legitimize the use of force. The Daodejing's observation that "when weapons are sharpened, the state falls into confusion" applies to the Zhou conquest regardless of the moral framework in which it is presented.

The Mandate of Heaven doctrine articulated in these announcements is critiqued by Daoist readers as a human projection onto the cosmic order. Where Confucian interpreters find in the Mandate a genuine expression of Heaven's moral will, Daoist readers see a political theology constructed to serve the interests of the conquering dynasty. The Zhuangzi's skepticism about human claims to understand Heaven's purposes extends to the Duke of Zhou's confident assertions about why Heaven transferred the mandate from Shang to Zhou.

Nevertheless, Daoist readers find in certain aspects of the Duke of Zhou's rhetoric an unconscious alignment with Daoist principles. His warnings against complacency and his acknowledgment that power is inherently unstable echo the Daoist teaching that those who grasp at power inevitably lose it. The Duke's recognition that the Zhou must constantly prove their worthiness suggests an awareness, however dimly perceived, that political authority is fundamentally precarious — a truth that the Daoist tradition articulates more radically.
