The Buddha's first sermon at Deer Park can be compared with founding discourses across world religions. Like Jesus' Sermon on the Mount, it establishes the fundamental ethical and spiritual framework of the tradition. Like Muhammad's first recitation of the Quran, it marks the moment when private realization becomes public teaching. The Four Noble Truths' diagnostic structure — identifying a problem, diagnosing its cause, affirming a cure, and prescribing treatment — has been compared to the medical model found across ancient Indian thought, and it resonates with therapeutic approaches in other traditions. The Middle Way's rejection of extremes parallels Aristotle's doctrine of the mean, Confucius' zhongyong (Doctrine of the Mean), and the Jewish concept of the 'golden path.' These convergences among Axial Age traditions suggest a shared recognition that wisdom lies in balance. Yet the Buddhist analysis is distinctive in its identification of craving as the root cause of suffering and its prescription of a specific eightfold path as the remedy.
