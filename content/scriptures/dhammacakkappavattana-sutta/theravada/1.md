The single discourse of the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta is the doctrinal bedrock of the entire Theravada tradition. The Buddha's opening declaration of the Middle Way establishes the fundamental orientation of Buddhist practice — avoiding the extremes of sensual indulgence and self-mortification that characterized the religious landscape of his time. The Four Noble Truths then provide the complete diagnostic framework: suffering (dukkha) is identified in its full range, from gross pain to the subtle unsatisfactoriness of conditioned existence; its origin in craving (tanha) is diagnosed; its cessation (nirodha) is affirmed as a real possibility; and the Noble Eightfold Path (magga) is prescribed as the way to realize that cessation. The three turnings of each truth — knowledge, task, and accomplishment — ensure that the teaching is not merely intellectual but experiential, requiring practice and realization. Kondanna's awakening at the conclusion demonstrates that the Dhamma works: when properly heard and understood, it leads to direct realization.
