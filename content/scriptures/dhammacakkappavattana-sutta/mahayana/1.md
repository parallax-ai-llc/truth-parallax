Mahayana Buddhism honors this discourse as the First Turning of the Dharma Wheel while situating it within a progressive revelation. The Four Noble Truths taught at Deer Park are understood as the foundational teaching appropriate for practitioners focused on individual liberation from suffering. The Second Turning, represented by the Prajnaparamita literature, deepens this teaching by revealing that the truths themselves are empty of inherent existence — suffering, its cause, its cessation, and the path are all empty, yet function conventionally. The Third Turning, represented by the Yogacara and tathagatagarbha texts, reveals the luminous nature of mind that underlies all experience. In this framework, the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta is not superseded but progressively deepened. The Middle Way itself is reinterpreted: in Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka, the Middle Way is not merely between asceticism and indulgence but between all extreme views, including existence and non-existence.
