## Overview

Tibetan Buddhism places the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta at the foundation of its graduated path (lam rim) structure. The Four Noble Truths taught at Varanasi constitute the First Turning of the Dharma Wheel, which establishes the basis for all subsequent Buddhist teaching and practice in the Tibetan system.

## The Graduated Path

In the lam rim framework systematized by Atisha and elaborated by Tsongkhapa, the Four Noble Truths form the teaching appropriate for practitioners of "middling capacity" — those motivated by the desire for personal liberation from samsara. This placement situates the sutta's teaching within a progressive curriculum that begins with precious human rebirth and culminates in the Vajrayana practices of the "great capacity" practitioner.

## Sixteen Aspects of the Four Truths

Tibetan Abhidharma elaborates each of the Four Noble Truths into four aspects, yielding sixteen aspects in total. This systematic analysis — impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and selflessness for the truth of suffering, for example — represents a characteristically Tibetan approach of detailed analytical meditation on the sutta's foundational teaching.
