## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Ezra emphasizes the book's witness to God's covenant faithfulness in restoring Israel from exile. Reformed and evangelical scholars read the narrative as a demonstration of God's sovereign use of pagan rulers to accomplish His redemptive purposes, while also highlighting the centrality of Scripture in communal renewal.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historical-Grammatical
Conservative Protestants read Ezra as reliable historical narrative, confirmed by Persian-period archaeology and the Cyrus Cylinder. The book's Aramaic sections (4:8–6:18; 7:12–26) are taken as authentic court documents.

### Redemptive-Historical
Reformed interpreters place Ezra within the broader arc of salvation history: the return from exile as a partial fulfillment of prophetic promises (Jeremiah 29:10), pointing toward the greater restoration in Christ.

### Typological
Ezra the scribe is seen as a type of the faithful pastor-teacher who calls God's people back to Scripture. The temple rebuilding prefigures Christ as the true temple (John 2:19-21).

## Theological Emphases

- **Sovereignty of God**: God moves the heart of Cyrus (Ezra 1:1) — even pagan kings serve God's purposes
- **Authority of Scripture**: Ezra's reading and teaching of the Torah models Protestant devotion to the Word
- **Church Discipline**: The intermarriage crisis parallels Protestant concerns about maintaining doctrinal and moral purity
- **Providence**: God's hidden hand guiding history through political events
