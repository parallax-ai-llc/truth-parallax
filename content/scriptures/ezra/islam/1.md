## The Return from Exile in Islamic Perspective

Islamic engagement with Ezra 1 is shaped by the Quran's affirmation of God's absolute sovereignty (*tawhid*) over all creation, including the rise and fall of empires. The decree of Cyrus illustrates a principle affirmed throughout the Quran: God raises up and brings down nations according to His will (Quran 3:26).

## Key Themes

### God's Sovereignty Over Kings

The text's declaration that "the LORD stirred up the spirit of Cyrus" resonates with the Quranic teaching that God controls the hearts of rulers. Al-Tabari notes that the Persian Empire, like all kingdoms, served divine purposes without necessarily understanding them. The concept of God directing pagan rulers parallels the Quranic accounts of Pharaoh and other rulers who served as instruments of God's plan.

### The Question of Tahrif

Muslim scholars approach the biblical text of Ezra with the understanding that it may reflect *tahrif* (alteration) from the original revelation. The narrative's core claim — that God restores His people after exile — is consistent with Quranic theology. However, specific details are evaluated against Quranic standards.

### Temple Worship

Islamic theology does not affirm the sacrificial cult of the Jerusalem temple as normative. However, the principle of sacred space is recognized: the Quran affirms the sanctity of al-Masjid al-Aqsa (the Farthest Mosque, identified with the Temple Mount) and recognizes Jerusalem's sacred significance.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: God alone controls the destiny of nations and peoples
- **Sacred geography**: Jerusalem holds significance in Islamic tradition
- **Divine restoration**: God can restore communities after judgment
- **Prophetic fulfillment**: God keeps His promises through history
