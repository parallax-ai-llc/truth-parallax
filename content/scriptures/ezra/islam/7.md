## Ezra/Uzayr and the Torah

Ezra 7 introduces the figure whom most Muslim commentators identify with Uzayr, mentioned in Quran 9:30. This chapter's description of Ezra as an expert in the Torah of Moses raises the critical Islamic question of whether the Torah was faithfully preserved or corrupted during and after the exile.

## Key Themes

### Uzayr in the Quran

Quran 9:30 states: "The Jews say, 'Uzayr is the son of Allah.'" Muslim commentators have debated this verse extensively:

| Commentator | View |
|-------------|------|
| Al-Tabari | Some Jewish groups may have held this belief |
| Ibn Kathir | Addresses a specific sect, not all Jews |
| Al-Zamakhshari | A polemical overstatement by some Jews expressing extreme veneration |
| Modern scholars | Possibly referring to a Yemeni Jewish sect or an exaggerated claim of Ezra's semi-divine status |

### The Tahrif Question

Ezra's role as Torah restorer is central to Islamic discussions of *tahrif*. If Ezra reconstructed the Torah after the exile, did he reproduce it faithfully or introduce alterations? Some Muslim scholars argue that Ezra's version introduced distortions, while others hold that *tahrif* is primarily interpretive rather than textual.

### Ezra's Scholarly Authority

The chapter's description of Ezra as "a scribe skilled in the Torah of Moses" (7:6) is consistent with the Quranic recognition that learned people among the Ahl al-Kitab (People of the Book) possessed genuine knowledge, even if their communities later strayed from the original message.

## Theological Significance

- **Uzayr debate**: The identity and veneration of Ezra in Islamic theology
- **Tahrif**: The question of Torah corruption or preservation
- **Ahl al-Kitab**: The People of the Book possess real but potentially corrupted knowledge
- **Scholarly authority**: Learning alone does not guarantee preservation of truth
