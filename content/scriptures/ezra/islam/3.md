## The Restoration of Worship

Islamic perspective on Ezra 3 engages with the theme of restored worship while noting significant theological differences. Islam does not affirm animal sacrifice as a permanent mode of worship (though the practice of *udhiyah/qurbani* during Eid al-Adha continues), and the Islamic understanding of sacred space differs from the biblical temple theology.

## Key Themes

### Sacred Space in Islam

While Islam recognizes the sanctity of Jerusalem and the Temple Mount (*al-Haram al-Sharif*), the theological framework differs. The Quran teaches that the entire earth is a place of worship (hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari), and the mosque replaces the temple as the primary locus of communal prayer. The rebuilding of the altar in Ezra 3 reflects a mode of worship that Islam considers superseded by the final revelation.

### Sacrifice in Islamic Theology

The Quran (22:37) teaches that "It is not their meat nor their blood that reaches Allah: it is your piety that reaches Him." Islamic theology thus interprets the elaborate sacrificial system of Ezra 3 as pointing toward the inner reality of submission (*islam*) to God, which is the essence of true worship.

### Joy and Grief (3:10-13)

The mingling of joy and grief at the foundation's laying resonates with the Islamic understanding that worldly restoration is always incomplete. True fulfillment comes only in the afterlife (*akhirah*), and earthly achievements, however significant, remain provisional.

## Theological Significance

- **Worship's essence**: True worship is submission to God, not ritual alone
- **Sacred space**: Islam universalizes the concept of holy ground
- **Sacrifice reinterpreted**: Outward sacrifice points to inner piety
- **Provisional restoration**: Earthly achievements anticipate eschatological fulfillment
