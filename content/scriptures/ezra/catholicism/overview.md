## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Ezra is shaped by the Church's broader canonical perspective, reading the book within the unity of Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition. The Vulgate tradition originally combined Ezra and Nehemiah as "1 Esdras" and "2 Esdras," while the deuterocanonical 1 Esdras (3 Esdras in Vulgate numbering) provides additional material. Catholic exegesis emphasizes the ecclesial dimensions of the restoration: temple worship, priestly authority, and communal identity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic
The Church Fathers read Ezra typologically: the return from Babylon prefigures baptismal liberation from sin, and the temple rebuilding anticipates the building of the Church. St. Bede the Venerable wrote an allegorical commentary on Ezra identifying the temple with the Church and its construction with the work of evangelization.

### Liturgical
The restoration of temple worship in Ezra 3 and 6 is seen as affirming the centrality of liturgical worship in communal life — a theme central to Catholic ecclesiology.

### Historical-Critical
Modern Catholic scholarship, following *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943), engages freely with historical-critical methods while maintaining the text's inspired character.

## Theological Emphases

- **Ecclesiology**: The restored community as a model of the Church gathered around worship and Torah
- **Sacramental Worship**: The rebuilt temple and restored sacrifices prefigure the Eucharistic liturgy
- **Authority and Tradition**: Ezra's role as priest-scribe models the teaching authority of the Magisterium
- **Communal Holiness**: The intermarriage crisis reflects the Church's call to holiness and separation from sin
