## The Resolution of the Crisis

Ezra 10 records the community's painful decision to dissolve marriages with foreign wives. Catholic interpretation approaches this with both respect for canonical authority and sensitivity to moral complexity.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Shecaniah's Proposal (10:2-4)

The proposal comes from within the community. Catholic moral theology values the sensus fidelium, the faithful's collective discernment, in recognizing what faithfulness requires. However, the proposal requires authoritative leadership to be enacted properly.

### The Three-Day Assembly (10:7-8)

The entire community gathers under threat of exclusion for non-attendance. Catholic canon law similarly holds that membership in the communion carries obligations. Excommunication exists as a medicinal penalty designed to provoke repentance.

### The Process of Reform (10:16-17)

The investigation takes three months, examining each case individually. Catholic matrimonial tribunals follow a similar principle: each marriage case is evaluated on its own merits.

### The Moral Difficulty

Catholic interpreters acknowledge the tension with the Church's teaching on the indissolubility of marriage:

- **Historical context**: These marriages violated explicit Torah prohibitions and were canonically irregular from the start
- **Old Testament dispensation**: The full theology of marriage was not yet revealed
- **Typological reading**: The separation prefigures the Church's separation from sin, not a model for dissolving valid marriages

## Theological Significance

- **Communal discernment**: The faithful can recognize what holiness requires
- **Authoritative leadership**: Reform requires legitimate pastoral authority
- **Case-by-case justice**: Individual situations demand individual attention
- **Moral complexity**: Faithfulness sometimes involves painful choices
