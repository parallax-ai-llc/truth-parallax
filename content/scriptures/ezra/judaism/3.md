## Restoring the Sacrificial Service

Ezra 3 describes the rebuilding of the altar and the laying of the Second Temple's foundation. Jewish interpretation focuses on the restoration of the sacrificial system (*avodah*) and the emotional complexity of rebuilding after catastrophic loss.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Altar Before the Temple (3:1-3)

The priority of the altar reflects the halakhic principle that sacrificial worship could be conducted even without a standing temple (Megillah 10a; Zevachim 62a). The Talmud discusses how the exact location of the altar was determined — tradition holds that the altar's location on Mount Moriah was identified by prophetic guidance.

### The Feast of Tabernacles (3:4-6)

The immediate celebration of Sukkot connects the returnees to the Israelites who first entered the land under Joshua. Midrashic tradition emphasizes that Sukkot commemorates both the wilderness tabernacles and the divine protection (*ananei ha-kavod*) that accompanied Israel.

### The Foundation Ceremony (3:10-13)

The mingling of joy and weeping is frequently cited in Jewish homiletics. The Talmud (Bava Batra 3a) records that the Second Temple, though eventually expanded by Herod, never achieved the full glory of Solomon's temple. The elders' tears anticipate this diminishment.

### What Was Missing

Rabbinic tradition (Yoma 21b) lists five things present in the First Temple but absent from the Second: the Ark of the Covenant, the holy fire from heaven, the Shekhinah, the Holy Spirit, and the Urim and Thummim.

## Theological Significance

- **Avodah**: The sacrificial service is the heart of temple worship
- **Continuity and loss**: Restoration is real but incomplete
- **Sacred geography**: The altar's location is divinely determined
- **Emotional complexity**: Joy and grief coexist in legitimate ways
