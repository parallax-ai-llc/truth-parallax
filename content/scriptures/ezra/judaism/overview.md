## Overview

In the Jewish canon, Ezra-Nehemiah forms a single book in the Ketuvim (Writings). Ezra holds an extraordinary status in rabbinic tradition: the Talmud (Sanhedrin 21b) states that Ezra was worthy of receiving the Torah had Moses not preceded him. He is credited with establishing the Great Assembly, fixing the Hebrew script (from paleo-Hebrew to Assyrian/Aramaic square script), and instituting public Torah reading — innovations that shaped Judaism permanently.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Talmudic
The Talmud treats Ezra as a pivotal figure in the chain of tradition (Avot 1:1). His reforms — public Torah reading on Mondays, Thursdays, and Shabbat afternoons, communal courts, and ritual purity regulations — are considered foundational to rabbinic Judaism.

### Medieval Commentators
Rashi, Ibn Ezra, and other rishonim comment on Ezra primarily in historical terms, identifying the genealogies and clarifying the chronology of the Persian period. The intermarriage crisis receives extensive legal discussion.

### Modern Critical
Contemporary Jewish scholars examine Ezra within the context of Persian imperial policy and the sociology of Second Temple Judaism, while also engaging with the text's enduring religious significance.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shivat Tzion (Return to Zion)**: The paradigmatic return that shapes Jewish messianic hope
- **Torah as Constitution**: Ezra establishes the Torah as the governing document of the Jewish community
- **Ezra as Second Moses**: The rabbinic elevation of Ezra as restorer of the Torah
- **Intermarriage**: A defining issue in Jewish identity and halakhic discussion to this day
