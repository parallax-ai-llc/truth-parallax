## The Register of the Returning Community

Jewish interpretation of Ezra 2 focuses on the concept of *yichus* (lineage) and communal identity. The detailed genealogical list establishes who belongs to the restored community — a question of enduring importance in Jewish law.

## Key Interpretive Themes

### The Importance of Yichus

In rabbinic Judaism, genealogical purity (*yichus*) determines eligibility for priestly service and certain marriage permissions. The Mishnah (Kiddushin 4:1) identifies ten categories of lineage among those who returned from Babylon, establishing a hierarchy of genealogical status that has implications for marriage law.

### Discrepancies with Nehemiah 7

The parallel list in Nehemiah 7 contains minor numerical differences. Medieval commentators (Rashi, Ibn Ezra) address these discrepancies variously — some attributing them to different counting methods, others to changes in family composition between the time of departure and arrival.

### Those of Uncertain Lineage (2:59-63)

The exclusion of those who could not prove their priestly ancestry raises important halakhic questions. The phrase "until a priest should stand with Urim and Thummim" (2:63) indicates that the Second Temple lacked the oracular capacity of the First — a significant theological diminishment.

### The Total Number (2:64-67)

The count of 42,360 does not match the sum of the individual numbers listed. The Talmud (Kiddushin 70b) explains that the difference represents members of the other ten tribes who returned with Judah and Benjamin but are not listed separately.

## Theological Significance

- **Yichus**: Lineage establishes communal membership and priestly eligibility
- **Halakhic precision**: Every person's status has legal implications
- **Second Temple diminishment**: The restoration is real but incomplete
- **Pan-Israelite identity**: The total includes members of all twelve tribes
