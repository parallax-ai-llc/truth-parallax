## Ezra the Scribe: A Second Moses

Jewish tradition elevates Ezra to extraordinary status. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 21b) declares: "Ezra was worthy of having the Torah given through him to Israel, had not Moses preceded him." This chapter introduces the figure who would reshape Judaism for all subsequent generations.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Ezra's Credentials (7:1-5)

Ezra's priestly lineage extends to Aaron, establishing his authority in both cultic and scholarly domains. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 21b-22a) credits Ezra with changing the Hebrew script from paleo-Hebrew (*ktav ivri*) to the Assyrian square script (*ktav ashuri*) used to this day — a reform that literally changed how Torah is read and written.

### "He was a ready scribe in the Torah of Moses" (7:6)

The title *sofer mahir* (ready/skilled scribe) establishes Ezra as the prototype of the rabbinic sage. The Talmud attributes to him the institution of public Torah reading on Mondays, Thursdays, and Shabbat afternoons (Bava Kamma 82a) — a practice that continues in every synagogue worldwide.

### The Three-Fold Commitment (7:10)

"Ezra had set his heart to study the Torah of the LORD, to do it, and to teach statutes and judgments in Israel." This verse is a cornerstone of Jewish pedagogical philosophy: learning leads to practice, which leads to teaching. The Mishnah (Avot 1:15) expresses a similar principle.

### Artaxerxes' Letter (7:11-26)

The royal authorization gives Ezra civil and religious authority. Jewish legal tradition sees this as a precedent for the community's self-governance under gentile rule — the model of internal autonomy that characterized Jewish communities throughout the diaspora.

## Theological Significance

- **Ezra as second Moses**: The restorer who re-established the Torah
- **Scribal tradition**: Ezra founds the rabbinic tradition of Torah study
- **Script reform**: The very letters of Torah bear Ezra's mark
- **Communal autonomy**: Self-governance within the framework of imperial rule
