## The Prophets Restart the Work

Jewish interpretation of Ezra 5 highlights the essential role of prophecy in sustaining communal life. Haggai and Zechariah represent the last generation of classical prophecy before its cessation — a transition of immense significance in Jewish thought.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Haggai and Zechariah (5:1-2)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 11a) lists Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi as the last of the prophets. After them, prophecy departed from Israel and was replaced by the *bat kol* (heavenly voice). Their ministry during the temple rebuilding thus represents prophecy's final major act in biblical history.

### "The eye of their God was on the elders" (5:5)

Rashi interprets this phrase as indicating divine providence protecting the Jewish leaders from Persian interference. The "eye of God" is a biblical metaphor for attentive care, reflecting the teaching that God watches over Israel even when they are subject to gentile rule.

### The Appeal to Historical Record (5:13-16)

The Jewish leaders' appeal to Cyrus's original decree demonstrates the importance of documentary evidence in Jewish legal thought. The preservation of official records and their retrieval when needed illustrates the rabbinic value of careful record-keeping.

### Tattenai's Fairness

The governor's honest report, rather than suppressing the Jewish claim, is seen as an act of divine providence. God arranges for fair-minded administrators at critical moments — a pattern recognized throughout Jewish historical experience.

## Theological Significance

- **End of prophecy**: Haggai and Zechariah belong to prophecy's final generation
- **Divine watchfulness**: God's "eye" protects the vulnerable community
- **Documentary evidence**: Records serve as instruments of justice
- **Providential governance**: God places fair administrators in positions of power
