## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church reads Ezra within the framework of the divine economy — God's providential plan for the salvation of the world. The Septuagint tradition, which is the Orthodox Church's Old Testament, presents Ezra-Nehemiah as 2 Esdras (with 1 Esdras being the additional Greek text). Orthodox interpretation is shaped by patristic commentary, liturgical use, and the theology of restoration and repentance.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic-Liturgical
The Fathers read the return from Babylon as a type of spiritual liberation. The rebuilding of the temple is understood as the restoration of the human soul through repentance and divine grace. Readings from Ezra appear in the liturgical calendar during periods of penitence.

### Typological
Orthodox exegesis sees the entire narrative as prefiguring the Church: the community gathered around worship, led by priestly authority, and committed to holiness. The Persian kings who enable the return are instruments of divine providence, illustrating God's sovereignty over all earthly powers.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Economy**: God's providential ordering of history for salvation
- **Repentance and Renewal**: The exile-return pattern as a model of spiritual life
- **Temple as Theophany**: The rebuilt temple as a locus of divine presence
- **Communal Asceticism**: The community's separation from foreign practices as spiritual discipline
