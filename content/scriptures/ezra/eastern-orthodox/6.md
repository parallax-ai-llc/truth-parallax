## The Temple Completed and the Paschal Feast

Ezra 6 brings the first half of the book to its joyful conclusion: the temple is completed, dedicated, and the Passover is celebrated. Orthodox interpretation sees this as a restoration of the liturgical center of the cosmos.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Darius's Decree (6:1-12)

The pagan king's support for God's temple illustrates the Orthodox understanding that all earthly authority derives from God. The Byzantine concept of *symphonia* — the harmonious cooperation of Church and state — finds a prototype in Darius's relationship with the temple builders.

### The Temple Completed (6:14-15)

The attribution of the completion to both divine command and royal decree reflects the Orthodox understanding that divine and human action cooperate (*synergeia*) in God's work. Grace does not replace human effort but elevates and perfects it.

### The Dedication (6:16-18)

The twelve male goats offered for all Israel express the unity of God's people transcending physical separation. Orthodox liturgical theology similarly understands the Divine Liturgy as offered for the whole world, not merely for those physically present.

### The Passover (6:19-22)

The Paschal feast crowns the restoration. In Orthodox theology, every celebration of the Eucharist is a participation in the one Pascha of Christ — the ultimate deliverance from the ultimate exile (death and sin).

## Theological Significance

- **Symphonia**: Church and state cooperate in service of God's purposes
- **Synergeia**: Divine grace and human effort work together
- **Universal liturgy**: Worship encompasses the whole people of God
- **Paschal fulfillment**: All restoration points to Christ's Pascha
