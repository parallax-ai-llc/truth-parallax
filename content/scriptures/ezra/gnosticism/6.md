## The Demiurge's House Completed

Gnostic interpretation reads the completion and dedication of the Second Temple as the successful re-establishment of the Demiurge's worship center. The sacrifices, the Passover, and the communal joy all serve to bind the worshippers more firmly to the material realm.

## Key Themes

### The Temple as Prison

The completed temple is, in Gnostic perspective, a perfected prison. It provides a beautiful, ordered space that makes material worship seem natural and good — precisely the kind of deception the Demiurge excels at. The *Gospel of Philip* warns against mistaking the holy places of this world for the true sanctuaries above.

### Sacrifice as Feeding the Demiurge

The dedication sacrifices — burnt offerings and sin offerings — represent the transfer of life energy to the Demiurge. In the *Apocryphon of John*, the archons are described as parasitic beings who feed on humanity's spiritual energy, and the sacrificial cult is the formalized version of this parasitism.

### The Passover Reinterpreted

While orthodoxy celebrates the Passover as liberation from Egypt, Gnostic thought sees it as exchange of one form of bondage for another. The Children of Israel left Pharaoh's Egypt only to enter the Demiurge's cult system. True Passover is the soul's passage beyond all archontic powers into the Pleroma.

## Theological Significance

- **Temple as deception**: Beautiful worship spaces mask spiritual imprisonment
- **Sacrificial parasitism**: The Demiurge feeds on sacrificial worship
- **True Passover**: Liberation is from the material realm itself, not just Egypt
- **Cosmic irony**: Celebration of restoration is celebration of renewed bondage
