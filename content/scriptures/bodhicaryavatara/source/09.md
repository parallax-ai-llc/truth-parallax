---
title: "Bodhicaryavatara - Chapter 9: Wisdom"
scripture: "bodhicaryavatara"
chapter: 9
---

**1** All these branches of the teaching were taught by the Sage for the sake of wisdom. Therefore, those who wish to pacify suffering should generate wisdom.

**2** Conventional truth and ultimate truth—these are accepted as the two truths. Ultimate truth is beyond the reach of the intellect. The intellect is called conventional truth.

**3** The world is seen in two ways: the yogic and the commonplace. The commonplace world is challenged by the yogic world.

**4** And yogins themselves, according to the degree of their understanding, are refuted by those who are more advanced, by means of analogies accepted by both parties, and regardless of what they seek to prove.

**5** Phenomena are perceived by the senses and are considered to be real. But even this perception is established by convention, not by valid cognition in the ultimate sense.

**6** If the things one perceives directly were ultimately real, then since even fools perceive them, what would be the use of the saintly path? What purpose would the wise serve?

**7** "But if there are no beings," you may say, "for whom should we feel compassion?" For those who are projected by the delusion that we have embraced for the sake of the task to be accomplished.

**8** "If there are no beings, whose is the task?" True. The effort, too, is like a dream. But still it serves to pacify suffering. Why should it not be done?

**9** "But suffering itself is not ultimately real." True. But neither is it utterly nonexistent. For those whose minds cling to the idea of a self, suffering arises dependently. So we must put an end to it.

**10** That which is dependently arisen is empty of self-nature. This is the meaning of emptiness. That which is empty of self-nature is dependently arisen. This is the meaning of the Middle Way.

**11** Since there is nothing that is not dependently arisen, there is nothing that is not empty. If things possessed self-nature, they could not arise dependently. Therefore, all things are empty.

**12** If all things were not empty, there could be no arising and no ceasing. The Four Noble Truths would then be impossible. Without the truths, attainment and practice would be impossible.

**13** In emptiness, all is possible. In non-emptiness, nothing is possible. Therefore, one should meditate on emptiness—not to grasp emptiness as a thing, but to let go of all grasping.

**14** Just as a plantain tree, when its layers are peeled off, has no core, so when the self is analyzed, it is found to have no essence whatsoever.

**15** "If beings do not exist," you may ask, "for whom is the compassion?" For those who are imagined through the delusion that is embraced for the sake of the goal. And what is the goal? The peace of suffering. There is no fault in this.
