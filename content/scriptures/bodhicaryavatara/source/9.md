---
title: "Bodhicaryavatara Chapter 9"
scripture: "bodhicaryavatara"
chapter: 9
---

**1.** All these branches of the Doctrine the Able One has taught for the sake of wisdom. Therefore they must generate this wisdom who wish to have an end of suffering.

**2.** The veiling of the truth and the ultimate — these two are held to be the truths. The ultimate is not within the reach of intellect; intellect is said to be the veiling truth.

**3.** In light of this, the world is seen to be divided into two: the yogis and the common people. The common people are refuted by the yogis.

**4.** And even yogis are refuted by successively more adept yogis. Both accept the same example, and if no analysis is made — that suffices for the common view.

**5.** Things are seen and then, from this, are understood to be existent. But if they had inherent being, what would be the need of perception to establish them?

**6.** If perception were the test of truth, what would be the use of holy teaching? The path of others' practice is not ours to follow.

**7.** "But worldly people see the world and do not see it as illusory. This is a point where yogis differ from the common folk."

**8.** Form and other things that seem so clear and evident are established by the common mind, not by the valid reasoning. What the common mind regards as true is false, like the idea of purity applied to what is unclean.

**9.** The Protector spoke of things to train the world. These things do not in truth exist. It is like speaking of a "self" — the Buddhas spoke this way to guide the world.

**10.** "If things have no inherent being, then even the absence of inherent being has no existence. How can the absence of a thing exist when the thing itself does not?"

**11.** When neither entity nor nonentity remains before the mind, then since there is no other option, the mind, without a referent, is at peace.

**12.** Just as a wishing-jewel and a tree of miracles fulfil all wishes, likewise, through the vow to train disciples, the Buddha's form appears.

**13.** For when a man sets up a healing shrine and, subsequently, long after his death, the sick are cured who come before it — the shrine and healer both are gone, and yet the cures continue.

**14.** Likewise, deeds performed according to the Bodhisattva way, even after he himself has passed beyond all sorrow, continue to accomplish every purpose.

**15.** If through wisdom the mind perceives reality as it is, free from the two obscurations, then this is liberation — the cessation of all suffering. To what else would the wise aspire?
