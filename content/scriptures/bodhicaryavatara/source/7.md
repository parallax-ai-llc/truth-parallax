---
title: "Bodhicaryavatara Chapter 7"
scripture: "bodhicaryavatara"
chapter: 7
---

**1.** Patience is practised thus. And now with vigour I should strive. For Buddhahood depends on vigour. Without effort there is no merit, just as there is no movement without wind.

**2.** What is vigour? It is finding joy in what is wholesome. Its opposite, the source of laziness, is termed the delight in evil, or in doing nothing, and self-contempt and self-discouragement.

**3.** From attachment to the pleasant taste of idleness, from craving for the comfort of one's rest, from lack of sense regarding all the sorrows of Samsara — such indolence arises.

**4.** Snared by the hunter of afflictions, enmeshed within the web of birth — how is it that you still do not perceive that you have fallen in the jaws of death?

**5.** You fail to see that one by one the Lord of Death is slaying all your kind? How can you lie about like buffaloes beside their herdsman, sleeping?

**6.** When all the exits have been blocked and Death is watching over all, how can you take delight in eating? How can you enjoy your sleep?

**7.** Death will quickly come for you; so gather merit until that time! For even if you then renounce your laziness, what will you do when there is no more time?

**8.** "This I have not done, and this I'm only starting. And this — I'm only halfway through..." Then suddenly the Lord of Death will come, and the thought will rise "Oh no, I'm finished!"

**9.** You'll look upon the tear-stained faces of those around you, overwhelmed with grief, and see the messengers of Yama waiting. You'll remember all your sins and hear the cries of hell.

**10.** Terrified and smeared with excrement, what will you do? For you will be like a live fish writhing on the ground. So since you know the terrors yet to come, why do you act like this now?

**11.** This human life, so hard to find, has now been won. The chance to practise virtue has been gained. If I now fail to put it to good use, when will it come again? This I must reflect upon.

**12.** Just as on a dark night black with clouds, the sudden lightning glares and all is clearly shown, likewise rarely, through the Buddhas' power, wholesome thoughts rise, brief and fleeting, in the world.

**13.** Goodness thus is feeble always, while the power of evil is vast and terrible. By what other virtue can it be overcome, if not by the supreme awakening mind?

**14.** Therefore taking up the awakening mind, I should always strive with vigour. I should apply the remedies in ways prescribed, just as an ill person follows a physician's orders.

**15.** If those who work for happiness destroy the very cause of happiness, treating it like an enemy, what happiness can they expect? Therefore I shall never turn away from vigour but shall always wear the armour of exertion, like a soldier marching into battle.
