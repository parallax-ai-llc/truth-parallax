---
title: "Bodhicaryavatara - Chapter 6: Patience"
scripture: "bodhicaryavatara"
chapter: 6
---

**1** All the good works gathered in a thousand ages, such as deeds of generosity and offerings to the Blissful Ones—a single flash of anger shatters them.

**2** There is no evil like hatred, and no austerity like patience. Therefore, one should earnestly cultivate patience, in various ways.

**3** The mind finds no peace, enjoys no pleasure or delight, finds no sleep, no stability, when the thorn of hatred lodges in the heart.

**4** Even dependants who are sustained by gifts of honour and of wealth will seek the overthrow of a master who is filled with hate.

**5** In brief, it is not possible for an angry person to be happy. Therefore, the enemy that is anger creates sufferings of every kind.

**6** But the one who firmly overcomes anger is happy in this world and in others.

**7** Finding its fuel in the discontent that comes from getting what I do not want and from the frustration of my wishes, anger grows and then destroys me.

**8** Therefore, I shall utterly destroy the fuel of this enemy, for this enemy has no function other than to harm me.

**9** Whatever happens, I shall not allow my cheerfulness to be disturbed. Being unhappy never accomplishes what I wish, and it robs me of all my virtues.

**10** If there is a remedy, then what is the use of frustration? If there is no remedy, then what is the use of frustration?

**11** Pain, humiliation, insults, and blame—these are unwanted for ourselves and for those we love; for those we dislike it is the opposite.

**12** The causes of happiness sometimes occur, but the causes of suffering are very many. Without suffering, there is no determination to be free; therefore, mind, you should stand firm!

**13** If even the devotees of Durga and the people of Karnata endure the meaningless pain of burning and cutting, then why am I so cowardly in pursuing liberation?

**14** There is nothing whatsoever that is not made easier through acquaintance. So through acquaintance with small hurts, I should learn to patiently accept greater harms.

**15** Moreover, the aggressor is impelled by conditions; he does not independently decide to be angry. And the anger itself, once arisen, has no freedom to think, "I shall arise." Therefore, whom should I be angry at?
