---
title: "Bodhicaryavatara Chapter 8"
scripture: "bodhicaryavatara"
chapter: 8
---

**1.** Having thus developed diligence, I'll place my mind in concentration. For the man whose mind is distracted lives within the fangs of the afflictions.

**2.** Through solitude of body and of mind, no distractions will occur. Therefore I shall leave the worldly life and wholly cast away all mental wandering.

**3.** First of all, I should reflect with care that the world, with all its tumult and distraction, has nothing of importance to detain me.

**4.** Attachment to one's kindred and associates is something that the wise have put aside. By thinking in such ways, distraction is abandoned.

**5.** In solitary woods, the trees ask nothing in return. They are the best of friends, these peaceful trees. When shall I go to dwell among them?

**6.** When shall I take up my abode in caves, or in deserted shrines, or underneath the trees, with a free, unclinging heart that never turns to cast a backward glance?

**7.** When shall I abide in such a place — unclaimed, by nature free and wide — a place where I may move without obstruction, acting as I wish?

**8.** A begging bowl, a few old garments, clothes that no one wants, a body I need not protect — when shall I attain such freedom?

**9.** When shall I, comparing self with others, be free from grasping? When shall all my longing and attachment be exhausted? How happy I will be!

**10.** And this I should reflect upon: all beings share the same desire for happiness and pain. So I should guard and tend them all, as I do myself.

**11.** Since I and other beings both, in wanting happiness, are equal and alike, what difference is there to distinguish us, that I should strive to have my bliss alone?

**12.** The hand and other limbs are many and distinct, but all are one — the body to be kept and guarded. Likewise, different beings, in their joys and sorrows, are, like me, all wanting happiness. This I should accept.

**13.** The pain I feel does not afflict or cause discomfort to another's body. Through the bonds of habit, I identify with it and feel it as my own.

**14.** And so, the pain of others — I should make it mine. Their happiness as well should be a cause of joy to me. I feel discomfort because I cherish self; I feel the pain of others because I cherish them.

**15.** All the joy the world contains has come through wishing happiness for others. All the misery the world contains has come through wanting pleasure for oneself. What need is there to say much more? The childish work for their own benefit; the Buddhas work for the benefit of others. Just look at the difference between them!
