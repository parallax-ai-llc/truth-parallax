## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Bodhicaryavatara places its teachings on compassion, patience, and self-sacrifice in dialogue with similar themes across world traditions. The text's radical ethic of universal compassion and its willingness to sacrifice personal welfare for others invites comparison with the Christian agape tradition, the Jewish concept of chesed, the Islamic ideal of rahmah, and the Hindu devotional tradition of seva.

## Cross-Traditional Parallels

The Bodhicaryavatara's teaching on the exchange of self and other (Chapter 8) has drawn comparisons with the Christian mystical tradition of kenosis (self-emptying), the Sufi concept of fana (annihilation of the ego), and Emmanuel Levinas's philosophy of radical responsibility for the Other. The text's treatment of patience (Chapter 6) invites comparison with Stoic teachings on equanimity and the Christian virtue of long-suffering.

## Global Significance

The Bodhicaryavatara has become one of the most widely translated and studied Buddhist texts in the contemporary world, valued not only within Buddhist communities but also by philosophers, ethicists, and spiritual seekers across traditions. Its vision of compassion as the highest human aspiration resonates with emerging global ethical frameworks.
