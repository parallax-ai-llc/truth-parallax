Dvaita Vedanta affirms the opening chapter's definition of supreme devotion as its most natural philosophical reading. Madhva's theology of eternal difference provides the metaphysical foundation for Narada's vision of bhakti as an everlasting love relationship between the soul and God. The teaching that bhakti is supreme love (parama prema) describes, for Dvaita interpreters, the highest possible spiritual attainment: the soul's perfected love for Vishnu, which is eternal, real, and never dissolved.

The chapter's description of the devotee's transformed consciousness — marked by joy, wonder, and self-forgetfulness — is understood literally as describing the soul's natural state when freed from the obscurations of ignorance and directed wholly toward its proper object, the Supreme Person.
