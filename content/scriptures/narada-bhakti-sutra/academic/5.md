Academic analysis of the concluding chapter on the fruits of devotion examines the text's soteriology — its theory of liberation through devotion. Scholars study how the chapter's vision of liberation compares with alternative soteriologies in Indian philosophy, including the Advaita concept of moksha as non-dual realization, the Samkhya-Yoga concept of kaivalya as the isolation of pure consciousness, and the Buddhist concept of nirvana as the cessation of suffering.

The chapter's description of the transformed life of the perfected devotee has been studied from perspectives including the sociology of religion, the psychology of mystical experience, and the philosophy of emotion. Comparative scholars have noted parallels with descriptions of sanctity in Christian, Sufi, and Jewish mystical traditions.
