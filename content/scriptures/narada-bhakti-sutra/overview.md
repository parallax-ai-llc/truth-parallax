## Overview

The Narada Bhakti Sutra is a foundational text of Hindu devotional literature, attributed to the divine sage Narada. Composed of 84 aphorisms (sutras) traditionally divided into five chapters, it presents a comprehensive philosophy of bhakti (devotion) as the supreme spiritual path. The text defines bhakti as supreme love for God (parama prema rupa), distinguishes it from lesser forms of attachment, describes its characteristics and stages, and explains the means of cultivating it. The Narada Bhakti Sutra, along with the Shandilya Bhakti Sutra, forms the foundational scriptural authority for the theology of devotion in Hinduism.

## Structure

The five chapters present a progressive unfolding of the bhakti path:

| Chapter | Theme |
|---------|-------|
| 1 | The nature of supreme devotion |
| 2 | The superiority of devotion over other paths |
| 3 | The means of cultivating devotion |
| 4 | The forms and expressions of divine love |
| 5 | The fruits and consummation of devotion |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Narada Bhakti Sutra has influenced devotional practice across Hindu traditions:

- **Vaishnavism**: A primary scriptural authority for the theology and practice of bhakti
- **Shaivism**: Engaged as a parallel to Shaiva devotional texts, with adaptations for Shiva worship
- **Advaita**: Interpreted through the lens of non-dual knowledge, with bhakti as a means to jnana
- **Dvaita**: Affirmed as expressing the eternal devotional relationship between soul and God
- **Academic**: Studied for its philosophical contributions to the theory of religious emotion

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Para Bhakti | Supreme devotion defined as the highest love for God |
| Prema | Divine love that transcends desire and fear |
| Sadhana | The practical means of cultivating devotion |
| Vairagya | Dispassion toward worldly attachments as a prerequisite for devotion |
| Sat-sanga | The company of holy persons as essential for spiritual growth |
| Surrender | Complete self-offering to God as the culmination of bhakti |
