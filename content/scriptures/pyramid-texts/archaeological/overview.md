## Overview

Archaeological study of the Pyramid Texts is inseparable from the study of the Old Kingdom pyramids in which they were inscribed. The texts exist not as portable manuscripts but as integral parts of monumental architecture, and their archaeological context is essential to understanding their meaning and function.

## Key Archaeological Approaches

### Architectural Context
The spatial arrangement of utterances within the pyramid chambers — burial chamber, antechamber, corridor, and vestibule — follows a deliberate pattern that scholars have linked to the ritual sequence of the royal funeral.

### Epigraphy
The carved and painted hieroglyphic texts on the limestone walls require careful epigraphic recording, as many inscriptions have suffered damage from humidity, salt crystallization, and the collapse of structural elements.

### Excavation History
The discovery of the Pyramid Texts by Gaston Maspero in 1881 in the pyramid of Unas at Saqqara remains one of the landmark moments in Egyptological archaeology.

## Key Archaeological Sites

| Pyramid | Dynasty | Significance |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Unas | 5th Dynasty | Oldest Pyramid Texts; best preserved |
| Teti | 6th Dynasty | Extensive corpus with unique utterances |
| Pepi I | 6th Dynasty | Large corpus; recent conservation work |
| Merenre | 6th Dynasty | Important additions to the corpus |
| Pepi II | 6th Dynasty | Largest corpus; longest reign |
