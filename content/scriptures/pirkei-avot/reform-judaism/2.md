## Ethics as the Heart of Torah

Reform reading of Chapter 2 highlights Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi's opening teaching about choosing "the right path" — one that is honorable both in one's own eyes and in the eyes of others (2:1). This dual criterion resonates with Reform's emphasis on individual conscience guided by communal standards. The ethical life is not imposed externally but chosen through informed, thoughtful engagement.

## The Value of Diverse Perspectives

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's five students each offer a different answer to the question of the "good path," and all are valued. Reform interpretation sees here a model for its own congregational life: different individuals bring different spiritual gifts, and the community is enriched by welcoming this diversity rather than imposing uniformity.

## Responsibility to the World

The chapter's teaching "Do not separate yourself from the community" (2:4) is read by Reform as extending beyond the Jewish community to embrace responsibility for the broader world. Combined with Hillel's "If I am only for myself, what am I?" this chapter grounds Reform's commitment to social justice and tikkun olam.
