## Torah Study as Spiritual Practice

Reform Judaism reads Chapter 6's praise of Torah study as affirming that learning is a lifelong spiritual discipline accessible to all Jews. The 48 qualities through which Torah is acquired (6:6) are understood not as prerequisites for an elite scholarly class but as virtues cultivated through any sincere engagement with Jewish texts, ideas, and values.

## Torah for Its Own Sake

The concept of Torah lishmah — study for its own sake — resonates with Reform's emphasis on learning as intrinsically valuable, not merely instrumental. Torah study is not undertaken to earn divine reward but because the encounter with sacred text is itself transformative, shaping character and deepening understanding.

## The Democratization of Learning

Reform interpretation of this chapter emphasizes the accessibility of Torah. The teaching that Torah was given in the wilderness — a place belonging to no one — is read as affirming that Torah belongs to all who seek it, regardless of gender, status, or background. This undergirds Reform's commitment to universal Jewish education and egalitarian access to sacred learning.
