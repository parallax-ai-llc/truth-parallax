## Overview

Reform Judaism finds in Pirkei Avot a quintessential expression of Judaism's ethical core. Because the tractate focuses on moral instruction rather than ritual law, it resonates strongly with Reform's emphasis on the prophetic tradition and ethical monotheism. Reform interpreters value Pirkei Avot as evidence that the rabbinic tradition itself prioritized ethics and character alongside, and sometimes above, legal observance.

## Interpretive Approach

Reform scholars approach Pirkei Avot historically, situating its teachings within the social and intellectual context of the Tannaitic period, while also drawing out their enduring relevance. The emphasis falls on the universal applicability of its ethical maxims — teachings like Hillel's "If I am not for myself, who will be for me?" (1:14) are read as speaking to all humanity, not only to Jews. Reform commentary often highlights the tractate's pluralism, noting how multiple, sometimes contradictory, teachings are preserved side by side.

## Theological Emphases

Reform reading foregrounds the humanistic dimensions of Pirkei Avot. The sages' emphasis on derekh eretz (decent conduct), justice, and compassion aligns with Reform's vision of Judaism as a force for social good. The tractate's relative silence on ritual matters is seen not as an omission but as a statement of priority — that the ethical life is the true foundation of religious life.
