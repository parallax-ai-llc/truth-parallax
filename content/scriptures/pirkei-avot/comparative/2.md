## Master-Disciple Relationships

Chapter 2's portrait of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai and his five students parallels the master-disciple paradigm found across world religions. The Buddha's chief disciples (Sariputta and Moggallana), Confucius's inner circle, Jesus's twelve apostles, and Socrates's students each represent a tradition where the master identifies and cultivates the distinct talents of individual followers.

## The Good Path

The question "What is the good path?" parallels fundamental ethical inquiries across traditions. Aristotle's eudaimonia, the Buddhist Noble Eightfold Path, the Confucian zhongyong (Doctrine of the Mean), and the Islamic sirat al-mustaqim (straight path) all seek to define the ideal way of life. The diversity of the five students' answers mirrors the diversity of responses to this question across civilizations.

## Community and Solitude

The chapter's tension between communal belonging and individual responsibility finds expression in every major tradition. The Buddhist sangha, the Christian ecclesia, the Islamic ummah, and the Confucian family-state all grapple with balancing individual conscience against communal norms.
