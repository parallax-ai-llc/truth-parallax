## The Sacralizing of Learning

Chapter 6's elevation of Torah study to the highest religious act parallels similar valorizations of learning in other traditions. The Confucian Analerta opens with "Is it not a pleasure to learn?" Islamic civilization exalted 'ilm (knowledge) as a religious duty: "Seek knowledge even unto China." The Buddhist emphasis on right understanding (samma ditthi) as the first element of the Eightfold Path places knowledge at the foundation of spiritual progress.

## Qualities of the Ideal Student

The 48 qualities for Torah acquisition parallel descriptions of the ideal student or seeker in other traditions. The Bhagavad Gita's description of the wise person (sthitaprajna), the Buddhist paramitas (perfections), and the Sufi stations (maqamat) all enumerate the virtues required for spiritual advancement, combining intellectual, moral, and social qualities in an integrated ideal.

## Scripture as Cosmic Principle

The chapter's teaching that Torah preceded creation and serves as its blueprint parallels the Logos theology of Hellenistic Judaism and early Christianity, the Hindu concept of Veda as eternal and uncreated (apaurusheya), and the Islamic understanding of the Quran as the uncreated word of God existing eternally with God. Each tradition attributes cosmic significance to its sacred text, placing it at the foundation of reality itself.
