## Redefinition of Virtues

Ben Zoma's redefinition of strength, wisdom, wealth, and honor (4:1) parallels the transvaluation of conventional values found in many traditions. The Stoic insistence that virtue is the only true good, the Buddhist teaching that attachment to worldly success produces suffering, the Daoist reversal of strength and weakness (Dao De Jing 76), and Jesus's Beatitudes all challenge conventional hierarchies of value.

## Contentment as Wealth

The teaching "Who is rich? One who is happy with their lot" resonates with the Buddhist concept of santosa (contentment), the Stoic apatheia, the Hindu teaching of santosha (one of the niyamas), and the Islamic concept of qana'ah. This cross-traditional convergence on contentment as a spiritual virtue suggests a widely shared insight about the relationship between desire and suffering.

## Humility Across Traditions

The chapter's repeated emphasis on humility parallels teachings across world religions. Confucian ren (benevolence) requires putting others first, Buddhist teachings on anatta (non-self) dissolve ego-attachment, Christian kenosis (self-emptying) follows the pattern of Christ, and Islamic submission (the literal meaning of "islam") demands surrender of ego before God.
