## Numerical Wisdom Traditions

Chapter 5's use of numbered lists to organize ethical teaching is a widespread literary device across world traditions. The Buddhist enumeration of the Eightfold Path, Four Noble Truths, and Five Precepts; the Confucian Five Relationships; the Islamic Five Pillars; and the Christian Seven Deadly Sins all employ numerical structures as mnemonic and organizational tools.

## Typologies of Character

The chapter's classification of people into four types parallels character typologies in other traditions. The Hindu system of gunas (sattva, rajas, tamas) classifies temperaments; the Greek four humors (sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic) categorize personality; Buddhist psychology identifies mental factors (cetasikas) that shape behavior. Each system attempts to map the varieties of human character for the purpose of moral development.

## Sacred Dispute

The distinction between disputes for the sake of heaven and those motivated by self-interest resonates with traditions that sanctify principled disagreement. The Buddhist tradition preserves debates between different schools, the Islamic tradition of ikhtilaf (scholarly disagreement) recognizes legitimate diversity of legal opinion, and the Hindu tradition's astika-nastika debates model constructive philosophical contestation.
