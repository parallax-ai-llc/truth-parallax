## Overview

In Orthodox Judaism, Pirkei Avot occupies a uniquely cherished place as the tractate of the Mishnah devoted to ethical instruction and the transmission of Torah values. It is customarily studied on Shabbat afternoons between Pesach and Rosh Hashanah, cycling through its six chapters repeatedly. Orthodox interpretation reads Pirkei Avot as authoritative guidance for character development (mussar) rooted in the divine origin of the Oral Torah.

## Interpretive Approach

Orthodox commentators treat each mishnah as carefully worded teaching from sages who received and transmitted an unbroken chain of tradition from Sinai. Major commentaries include Rabbeinu Yonah (13th century), whose ethical analysis became foundational, and the Bartenura (Ovadiah of Bertinoro), whose clear explanations accompany the text in standard editions. The Maharal of Prague offered philosophical depth, while later mussar masters such as Rabbi Yisrael Salanter drew extensively on Pirkei Avot for moral instruction.

## Theological Emphases

Orthodox reading emphasizes that the ethical teachings of Pirkei Avot are not mere human wisdom but carry the weight of divine revelation. The opening mishnah — "Moses received the Torah from Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua" — is understood as establishing that ethical conduct and character refinement are integral to the Torah itself, not additions to it. The virtues commended by the sages are seen as prerequisites for proper Torah study and halakhic observance.
