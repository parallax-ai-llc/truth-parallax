## The Good Path and the Sefirot

Kabbalistic reading maps the five students' answers about the "good path" onto the sefirot. A good eye (Rabbi Eliezer) corresponds to chokhmah (wisdom, the visionary faculty); a good friend (Rabbi Yehoshua) to chesed (loving-kindness in relationship); a good neighbor (Rabbi Yose) to tiferet (harmonious community); foresight (Rabbi Shimon) to binah (understanding that anticipates consequences); and a good heart (Rabbi Elazar) to malkhut, which receives and integrates all the higher sefirot.

## Teshuvah as Tikkun

The chapter's call to "repent one day before your death" (2:10) is read mystically as teaching that teshuvah (return) is a cosmic process of tikkun (repair). Since one does not know the day of death, one must engage in continuous teshuvah — a perpetual realignment of the soul with its divine source. The kabbalists taught that each act of teshuvah elevates fallen sparks (nitzotzot) and mends the broken vessels (shevirat ha-kelim).

## The Fire of Torah

The teaching "warm yourself by the fire of the sages, but be wary of their coals lest you be burned" (2:10) is understood mystically as referring to the divine fire (esh) that animates Torah teaching. The tzaddik radiates spiritual light and heat; proximity brings illumination but also demands readiness, for the unprepared soul can be overwhelmed by direct contact with holiness.
