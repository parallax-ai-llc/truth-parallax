## Overview

Kabbalistic interpretation reads Pirkei Avot as a guide to the rectification of the soul (tikkun ha-nefesh) through the framework of the sefirot, the ten divine emanations described in Jewish mystical tradition. Each ethical teaching is understood as addressing not merely external behavior but the inner spiritual dynamics that connect the human soul to the divine.

## Interpretive Approach

Kabbalists such as Rabbi Chaim Vital (in his *Sha'arei Kedushah*) and the Ramchal (Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto, in *Mesillat Yesharim*) drew extensively on Pirkei Avot's ethical teachings, mapping them onto the sefirotic tree. The middot (character traits) commended by the sages correspond to specific sefirot: kindness (chesed), strength and restraint (gevurah), beauty and balance (tiferet), and so on. The refinement of character is thus understood as a process of aligning the soul with the divine structure of reality.

## Theological Emphases

The Hasidic masters added further layers, reading Pirkei Avot's teachings on humility, joy, and attachment to one's teacher as pathways to devekut — cleaving to God. The Baal Shem Tov and his successors taught that the simple ethical instructions of the Mishnah conceal profound mystical truths about the nature of the soul and its relationship to the Ein Sof, the Infinite. Chapter 6's praise of Torah study is understood as describing the soul's ultimate reunion with its divine source.
