## Overview

Jewish scholarship views the War Scroll as a significant witness to the apocalyptic strand within Second Temple Judaism. While mainstream rabbinic Judaism would later temper apocalyptic expectations, the War Scroll demonstrates that eschatological hope for a final divine battle was a powerful force in Jewish thought during the centuries surrounding the turn of the era.

## Interpretive Approach

Jewish scholars read the War Scroll as historical evidence for the diversity of eschatological belief in the Second Temple period. The text is placed alongside other apocalyptic works — Daniel, 1 Enoch, 4 Ezra — to reconstruct the full range of Jewish hopes for divine intervention and cosmic redemption. The scroll's military imagery is analyzed in light of both biblical holy war traditions and the political realities of Roman occupation.

## Key Emphases

Jewish interpretation notes the War Scroll's deep rootedness in biblical tradition, particularly the holy war narratives of Deuteronomy and Joshua and the apocalyptic visions of Daniel. The scroll's elaborate military organization may reflect actual Hellenistic and Roman military practices, suggesting the community was not entirely isolated from its cultural environment. The emphasis on divine sovereignty — God ultimately decides the outcome — resonates with broader Jewish theology. The scroll's dualism, however, is recognized as standing at the margins of Jewish thought.

## Significance

The War Scroll enriches the Jewish understanding of the Second Temple period as an era of intense eschatological expectation. It demonstrates that the yearning for divine redemption and the overthrow of evil, which would continue to animate Jewish thought through the Bar Kokhba revolt and beyond, had deep roots in sectarian communities devoted to interpreting biblical prophecy.
