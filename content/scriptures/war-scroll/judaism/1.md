## Apocalyptic Warfare in Jewish Tradition

Jewish scholars read Chapter 1's description of the final war within the broader context of Jewish apocalyptic literature. The chapter draws on biblical holy war traditions from Deuteronomy, Joshua, and Judges, transforming them into an eschatological framework. The identification of specific enemy nations — Edom, Moab, Ammon — roots the cosmic conflict in biblical geography and prophetic tradition, demonstrating the scroll's deep engagement with scripture.

## The Sons of Light and Israel

The chapter's division of humanity into Sons of Light and Sons of Darkness raises important questions about Jewish identity in the Second Temple period. Jewish scholars note that the Sons of Darkness include not only gentile nations but also Jews who do not belong to the community, revealing the sectarian character of the text. This intra-Jewish polemic illuminates the intense debates about who constituted the true Israel during the centuries preceding the destruction of the Temple.

## Messianic Expectation

The chapter's eschatological scenario reflects a form of messianic expectation in which divine intervention, rather than a single messianic figure, brings about the redemption of Israel. Jewish scholars place this within the spectrum of Second Temple messianic beliefs, which ranged from expectations of a Davidic king to priestly messiahs to direct divine action. The War Scroll's emphasis on God as the ultimate warrior resonates with biblical traditions of divine kingship.
