## Composition and Dating

Academic analysis of Chapter 1 focuses on questions of composition, dating, and historical context. Scholars have proposed dates ranging from the mid-second century BCE to the first century CE, with most favoring a date in the late first century BCE. The identification of the Kittim with Rome (rather than the earlier identification with the Greeks) provides an important terminus post quem. The chapter's literary style, vocabulary, and theological concepts are analyzed in comparison with other Qumran texts to establish its place within the community's literary production.

## The Kittim and Historical Identification

The identity of the Kittim — the primary enemy of the Sons of Light — has been extensively debated. While earlier scholars (following Yadin) identified them with the Romans, others have proposed the Seleucids or have argued that the term is used generically for any foreign oppressor. The specific military characteristics attributed to the Kittim are compared with known Roman and Hellenistic military practices to determine the most likely historical referent.

## Genre Analysis

The War Scroll's genre has been variously classified as apocalyptic literature, military manual, liturgical text, and utopian fiction. Academic scholars increasingly recognize that the text combines elements of all these genres, suggesting that conventional genre categories may be inadequate for classifying sectarian compositions that deliberately merge military planning, theological speculation, and liturgical prescription into a unified vision of eschatological warfare.
