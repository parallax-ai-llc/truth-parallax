## Holy Orders and Military Metaphor

Catholic scholars read Chapter 2's organizational structure in light of the Church's own hierarchical ordering. The chapter's assignment of specific roles based on status, age, and fitness resonates with the Catholic theology of Holy Orders, in which different ministries (bishop, priest, deacon) serve the unity and mission of the whole body. The military metaphor of the Church as an organized force under divine command has deep roots in Catholic spiritual literature, from Ignatius of Loyola's military imagery to the modern concept of Catholic Action.

## Universal Call to Holiness

The chapter's inclusion of all tribal divisions — not merely an elite force — in the eschatological army resonates with the Second Vatican Council's teaching on the universal call to holiness. Every member of the community has a role to play, even if the specific service differs according to capacity. Catholic scholars see in this comprehensive mobilization an anticipation of the conciliar vision of the entire People of God called to participate in the Church's mission.

## The Body of Christ

The chapter's careful coordination of different units into a unified fighting force invites comparison with Paul's image of the Body of Christ, in which "the eye cannot say to the hand, 'I don't need you'" (1 Corinthians 12:21). Catholic ecclesiology emphasizes that the Church's effectiveness depends on the proper functioning of every member, just as the War Scroll's army requires each division to fulfill its assigned role.
