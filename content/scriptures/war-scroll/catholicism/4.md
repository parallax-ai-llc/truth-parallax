## Priestly Mediation

Catholic scholars read Chapter 4's depiction of priestly leadership in battle as confirmation of the essential role of the ordained ministry in mediating between God and his people. The priests of the War Scroll do not merely support the warriors; they direct the battle through liturgical actions that invoke divine power. Catholic sacramental theology similarly understands the priest as one who acts *in persona Christi*, channeling divine grace through the sacramental signs.

## The Communion of Saints and Angels

The chapter's vision of angels fighting alongside the human army resonates with the Catholic doctrine of the Communion of Saints, in which the faithful on earth, the souls in purgatory, and the saints in heaven — together with the angelic hosts — form a single community united in worship and mission. Catholic theology affirms the reality of angelic assistance in the spiritual life and the intercession of the saints on behalf of the living.

## The Mass as Spiritual Combat

Catholic interpreters draw a connection between the War Scroll's liturgical warfare and the Catholic understanding of the Mass as a participation in Christ's victory over sin and death. Just as the priests of the War Scroll fight through prayer and blessing, the Catholic priest at the altar engages in the supreme act of spiritual warfare — the re-presentation of Christ's sacrifice that conquered the powers of darkness.
