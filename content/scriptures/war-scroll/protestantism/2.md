## The Church as Army

Protestant scholars read Chapter 2's military organization in light of New Testament imagery of the Church as a disciplined body. Paul's metaphor of the Body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12) and his military language of "putting on the armor of God" (Ephesians 6:10-18) draw on the same tradition of organized spiritual warfare that the War Scroll expresses in literal military terms. The chapter's emphasis on each member fulfilling their assigned role resonates with Paul's teaching that every member of the body has a specific function.

## Age, Service, and Calling

The chapter's assignment of different roles based on age and capacity invites comparison with the New Testament's recognition of diverse gifts and callings within the community of faith. Protestant theology has developed this principle into the doctrine of vocation — the conviction that every believer is called to serve God in their particular station. The War Scroll's differentiated service, while military in context, reflects a similar understanding that the community's effectiveness depends on each member fulfilling their designated role.

## The Restoration of Israel

The chapter's vision of the twelve tribes reassembled for the final battle connects to New Testament themes of the reconstitution of Israel, particularly Jesus' appointment of twelve apostles as representatives of a renewed Israel. Protestant scholars see in both texts an eschatological hope for the restoration of God's people, though the New Testament extends this hope beyond ethnic boundaries to encompass all nations.
