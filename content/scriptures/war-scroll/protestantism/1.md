## Apocalyptic Background of the New Testament

Protestant scholars read Chapter 1 as essential background for understanding the apocalyptic worldview of the New Testament. The chapter's division of humanity into Sons of Light and Sons of Darkness, its expectation of a final cosmic conflict, and its identification of present political powers with eschatological enemies all find parallels in the Book of Revelation, where Rome is identified with Babylon and the final battle of Armageddon brings God's triumph over evil.

## Spiritual Warfare in Pauline Thought

The chapter's framework of cosmic conflict illuminates Paul's teaching that "our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the powers of this dark world" (Ephesians 6:12). Protestant scholars note that while Paul spiritualizes the warfare imagery, the underlying conviction that believers are caught in a cosmic struggle between divine and demonic forces reflects the same apocalyptic worldview expressed in the War Scroll.

## The Already and Not Yet

Protestant interpretation emphasizes the crucial difference between the War Scroll's eschatology and the Christian understanding: whereas the War Scroll places the decisive battle entirely in the future, the New Testament proclaims that Christ has already won the decisive victory through his death and resurrection. The "already but not yet" tension of Christian eschatology — the kingdom inaugurated but not yet consummated — transforms the War Scroll's linear expectation into a dialectical reality.
