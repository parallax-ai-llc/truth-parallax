## Overview

Protestant scholars have found the War Scroll illuminating for understanding New Testament apocalyptic thought, particularly the Book of Revelation, Paul's imagery of spiritual warfare in Ephesians 6, and the broader eschatological framework of early Christianity. The scroll demonstrates that the earliest Christians inherited a rich Jewish vocabulary for describing the cosmic conflict between good and evil.

## Interpretive Approach

Protestant interpretation reads the War Scroll comparatively alongside New Testament eschatological texts. While maintaining the distinctiveness of the Christian message, Protestant scholars recognize that the War Scroll and the New Testament share a common apocalyptic worldview in which the present age is characterized by a struggle between divine and demonic forces that will culminate in God's definitive victory.

## Key Emphases

The War Scroll's division of humanity into Sons of Light and Sons of Darkness invites comparison with the Johannine writings, while its detailed battle imagery illuminates the apocalyptic symbolism of Revelation. Paul's description of the "armor of God" in Ephesians 6 gains new depth when read alongside the War Scroll's meticulous descriptions of military equipment with inscribed divine names. Protestant scholars also note the significant difference: where the War Scroll envisions a literal military conflict, the New Testament spiritualizes the warfare, placing the decisive battle at the cross and resurrection.

## Significance

For Protestantism, the War Scroll provides crucial context for understanding the apocalyptic dimensions of the New Testament that are sometimes neglected in Western theology. It demonstrates that early Christianity emerged from a world saturated with eschatological expectation and adapted that expectation in light of the Christ event.
