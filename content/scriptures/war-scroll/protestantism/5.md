## The Decisive Divine Act

Protestant scholars read Chapter 5's climactic seventh battle — in which God intervenes to secure victory after human forces have reached a stalemate — as a dramatic illustration of the theological principle that salvation is ultimately God's work, not humanity's achievement. This pattern resonates with the Reformation doctrine of *sola gratia*: human effort, however faithful, cannot achieve final redemption; only God's sovereign act can defeat the powers of evil.

## Suffering Before Glory

The three defeats suffered by the Sons of Light before God's decisive intervention resonate with the New Testament pattern of suffering before glory. Protestant theology, following Paul (Romans 8:17), teaches that believers share in Christ's sufferings before sharing in his glory. The War Scroll's acknowledgment that the righteous must endure temporary defeat anticipates this cruciform pattern of redemption.

## The Final Victory

The chapter's vision of total and permanent victory over evil finds its most powerful echo in Revelation 19-21, where Christ returns as a warrior king, defeats the forces of evil, and establishes the new heaven and new earth. Protestant eschatology, while varying in its specific interpretive schemes, shares the War Scroll's fundamental conviction that history moves toward a divine conclusion in which evil is finally and completely overthrown and God's righteousness prevails forever.
