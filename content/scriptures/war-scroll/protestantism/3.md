## The Armor of God

Protestant scholars find Chapter 3's detailed descriptions of military equipment particularly illuminating for understanding Paul's "armor of God" passage in Ephesians 6:13-17. Paul's references to the belt of truth, breastplate of righteousness, shield of faith, helmet of salvation, and sword of the Spirit gain new depth when read alongside the War Scroll's inscribed weapons and armor, where each piece of equipment bears theological inscriptions declaring God's sovereignty and the warrior's identity.

## Banners and Identity

The chapter's elaborate system of inscribed banners — each bearing the name of a tribal division, a theological declaration, and a phase of battle — reflects a theology of identity that Protestant scholars compare with the New Testament's language of believers being "named" and "known" by God. The banners proclaimed who the warriors were and whose cause they served, just as Christian identity is defined by belonging to Christ.

## Worship in Battle

The integration of trumpet signals with tactical movements demonstrates that the War Scroll's battle is simultaneously a military operation and a liturgical event. Protestant scholars note this fusion of worship and warfare as background for the New Testament's understanding of prayer as spiritual combat and the Church's worship as a participation in Christ's victory over the powers of darkness.
