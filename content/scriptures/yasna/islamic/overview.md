## Overview

Islamic engagement with the Yasna and Zoroastrian scripture broadly is shaped by the Quran's references to the Majus (Zoroastrians) in Surah 22:17, where they are listed alongside Jews, Christians, and Sabians. The historical encounter between Islam and Zoroastrianism following the Arab conquest of Persia (7th century CE) produced complex theological debates about the status of Zoroastrians as potential *ahl al-kitab* (People of the Book) and the nature of their scriptures. The Yasna, as the central Zoroastrian liturgical text, has been examined within this framework.

## Key Interpretive Themes

### The Ahl al-Kitab Question
Whether Zoroastrians qualify as People of the Book — and whether the Avesta (including the Yasna) represents a divinely revealed scripture — has been debated by Muslim jurists and theologians since the early Islamic period. Some scholars, including al-Shahrastani, classified Zarathustra as a prophet.

### Monotheism and Dualism
Muslim theologians have engaged critically with Zoroastrian theology as presented in the Yasna. The monotheistic dimension — the exaltation of Ahura Mazda — is acknowledged, but the dualistic elements (the Two Spirits of Yasna 30) have been critiqued as incompatible with *tawhid* (divine oneness).

### Fire Worship Critique
The Yasna's centrality of fire in worship has been the subject of Islamic critique, with Muslim polemicists characterizing Zoroastrians as fire-worshippers (*atashparast*). Zoroastrian apologists have responded that fire is a *qiblah* (direction of prayer), not an object of worship — a response that engages Islamic theological categories.

### Persian Cultural Heritage
Muslim Persian scholars, including al-Biruni and Ferdowsi, preserved knowledge of Zoroastrian traditions including elements reflected in the Yasna, integrating this heritage into Islamic Persian civilization.
