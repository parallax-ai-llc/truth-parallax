## Overview

Within the living Zoroastrian tradition, the Yasna is not merely a text to be read but a ritual to be performed. The 72 chapters constitute the script for the Yasna ceremony, the central act of Zoroastrian worship conducted by a team of two priests (the *zaotar* and the *raspi*) before the sacred fire. The ceremony typically lasts several hours and involves the preparation and offering of haoma (the sacred plant juice mixed with milk), the recitation of the Gathas, and the consecration of bread (*drōn*) and water (*zōhr*).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Priestly Ritual Practice
The Yasna is understood primarily through its ritual enactment. Each *ha* corresponds to specific ritual actions — the pounding of haoma, the tending of the fire, the offering of libations. The text cannot be separated from its performative context. In Parsi and Irani Zoroastrian practice, the Yasna ceremony remains the most solemn form of worship.

### Pahlavi Commentary Tradition
The 9th-century Pahlavi translations and commentaries (*zand*) provide the traditional interpretive framework. Works such as the *Dēnkard* and the *Zand ī Wahman Yasn* offer theological glosses on the Avestan text, though the Pahlavi commentators sometimes struggled with the archaic Old Avestan of the Gathas.

### The Gathas as Zarathustra's Words
The Gathas (Yasna 28-34, 43-51, 53) are revered as the direct words of the prophet Zarathustra, preserved through millennia of oral transmission. They hold supreme authority in Zoroastrian theology and are treated with particular reverence during recitation.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Significance |
|-------|-------------|
| Ritual Efficacy | The Yasna ceremony maintains cosmic order and strengthens the forces of good |
| Fire as Sacred Symbol | Atar (fire) is the visible presence of asha, to be maintained and honored |
| Haoma | The sacred drink connects the worshipper to divine power and immortality |
| Priestly Authority | The proper performance of ritual requires trained hereditary priests |
| Gathic Primacy | Zarathustra's own words carry the highest scriptural authority |
