## Overview

Jewish scholarly engagement with the Yasna is shaped by the profound historical encounter between Judaism and Zoroastrianism during and after the Babylonian Exile (6th-5th centuries BCE). The Persian Empire under Cyrus the Great liberated the Jews from Babylon, and the subsequent centuries of Persian rule left deep marks on Jewish theology, angelology, eschatology, and apocalypticism. The Yasna, particularly the Gathas, provides primary evidence for the Zoroastrian teachings that many scholars believe influenced Second Temple Judaism.

## Key Interpretive Themes

### Persian Period Influence
The question of Zoroastrian influence on Judaism is central to Jewish engagement with the Yasna. Concepts that emerged or developed significantly during the Persian period — angels and demons as organized hierarchies, bodily resurrection, final judgment, Satan as cosmic adversary — have been traced by scholars to Zoroastrian antecedents found in the Gathas and the broader Yasna.

### Angelology and Demonology
The Amesha Spentas of the Yasna parallel the archangelic figures that emerge in Jewish literature during the Persian and Hellenistic periods. The Gathic opposition between Spenta Mainyu and Angra Mainyu has been compared to the developing Jewish concept of Satan.

### Eschatological Parallels
The frashokereti teachings implicit in the Yasna — resurrection, final judgment, cosmic renovation — parallel developments in Daniel, 1 Enoch, and other Second Temple texts that shaped Pharisaic and rabbinic Judaism.

### Purity and Ritual
While the Yasna's ritual framework differs from the Temple cult, structural parallels exist in the emphasis on priestly mediation, sacred fire, and ritual purity as cosmic maintenance.
