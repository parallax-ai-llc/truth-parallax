## Overview

From a Jewish scholarly perspective, Yasna Ha 31 (Gatha Ahunavaiti — Ahura Mazda the Creator) is examined in light of the historical encounter between Judaism and Zoroastrianism during the Persian period and beyond. This chapter, part of the Gathas, the oldest and most sacred Zoroastrian hymns attributed directly to Zarathustra, addresses themes that intersect with Jewish theological development — particularly the concepts that emerged or evolved significantly during the centuries of Persian imperial rule over the Jewish community.

The question of influence — what Judaism may have absorbed, adapted, or independently developed in parallel with Zoroastrian ideas — remains central to Jewish scholarly engagement with this text. The themes of Gathas and creation in this chapter find echoes in Second Temple literature.

## Key Themes

- **Persian period context**: The historical setting of Jewish-Zoroastrian interaction and potential influence
- **Theological parallels**: Shared concepts in angelology, eschatology, purity, and cosmic dualism
- **Distinctive Jewish responses**: How Judaism adapted or independently developed similar themes within its own covenantal framework

## Key Verses

The chapter's approximately 22 verses contain material relevant to the Jewish study of Persian-period religious exchange, particularly passages addressing Gathas and the relationship between the divine and created order.

## Commentary Tradition

Jewish scholarly engagement with Zoroastrian texts draws on the work of **Shaul Shaked**, **Jacob Neusner**, and **James Barr**, among others. The Babylonian Talmud itself contains references to Zoroastrian practices and beliefs, providing evidence for ongoing Jewish-Zoroastrian dialogue in the Sasanian period.
