## Overview

Comparative study of the Yasna examines this central Zoroastrian liturgical text alongside parallel traditions in Vedic Hinduism, Abrahamic religions, and other world faiths. The Yasna offers uniquely rich material for comparison: its shared Indo-Iranian heritage with the Vedas, its possible historical influence on Jewish, Christian, and Islamic eschatology, and its universal themes of worship, ethical choice, and cosmic struggle between good and evil.

## Key Comparative Themes

### Indo-Iranian Ritual Parallels
The Yasna (*yasna*) and the Vedic sacrifice (*yajña*) descend from a common Proto-Indo-Iranian ritual. Cognate elements include the sacred drink (haoma/soma), the fire cult (Atar/Agni), priestly roles, and ritual formulas. Comparing the two illuminates the shared heritage and points of divergence.

### Monotheism and the Abrahamic Traditions
The Yasna's exaltation of Ahura Mazda as supreme creator bears comparison with biblical monotheism. The Gathas' ethical dualism (Yasna 30) — the choice between the two spirits — parallels and may have influenced Jewish and Christian concepts of good and evil.

### Eschatological Influence
The Yasna's references to frashokereti (cosmic renovation), individual judgment, resurrection, and a final battle have been widely studied as possible sources for Second Temple Jewish eschatology, which in turn shaped Christian and Islamic end-times theology.

### Liturgical Patterns
The structure of the Yasna ceremony — invocation, praise, offering, petition, benediction — follows patterns found in Jewish temple worship, Christian eucharistic liturgy, and Vedic ritual.
