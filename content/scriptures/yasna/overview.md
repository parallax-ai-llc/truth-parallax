## Overview

The Yasna (Avestan: *yasna*, "worship, sacrifice") is the principal liturgical text of Zoroastrianism and the core of the Avestan canon. It consists of 72 chapters (*has*) that form the script for the central Zoroastrian act of worship — the Yasna ceremony, a ritual offering of prayers and libations centered on the sacred fire and the preparation of the haoma drink. The Yasna contains the Gathas, the oldest and most sacred hymns attributed to the prophet Zarathustra himself, embedded within a later liturgical framework.

## Structure

The Yasna's 72 chapters are organized as follows:

1. **Introductory Liturgy** (Has 1-27): Invocations, offerings, and the preparation of haoma, building toward the recitation of the Gathas
2. **The Gathas** (Has 28-34, 43-51, 53): The five groups of hymns attributed to Zarathustra — Ahunavaiti, Ushtavaiti, Spenta Mainyu, Vohu Khshathra, and Vahishtoishti
3. **Yasna Haptanghaiti** (Has 35-41): The "Liturgy of the Seven Chapters," an early prose liturgy praising creation
4. **Later Liturgy** (Has 52, 54-72): Supplementary prayers, hymns to specific divinities, and the fire liturgy

The Gathas (17 hymns across the five groups) constitute the oldest stratum of the text, composed in Old Avestan, while the surrounding liturgy is in Younger Avestan.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Yasna holds distinct significance across multiple interpretive traditions:

- **Zoroastrian**: The heart of daily priestly ritual, preserving Zarathustra's own words and the living liturgical tradition
- **Academic**: A critical primary source for Indo-Iranian linguistics, comparative mythology, and the historical study of Zarathustra
- **Comparative**: Rich in parallels with Vedic ritual (the cognate *yajna*), Abrahamic liturgy, and universal worship patterns
- **Protestant**: Of interest for its monotheistic theology, ethical dualism, and possible influence on Jewish-Christian eschatology
- **Jewish**: Important for understanding Persian-period influences on Second Temple Judaism, angelology, and resurrection beliefs
- **Islamic**: Examined as part of the Majusi heritage referenced in the Quran, with implications for the *ahl al-kitab* debate

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Worship of Ahura Mazda | The supreme deity as sole creator and source of asha (truth/righteousness) |
| The Gathas | Zarathustra's original prophetic hymns — prayers, questions, and ethical exhortations |
| Haoma Ritual | The preparation and offering of the sacred plant drink as cosmic renewal |
| Fire (Atar) | The sacred fire as the visible symbol of asha and the focal point of worship |
| Asha vs. Druj | The cosmic struggle between truth/righteousness and falsehood/deceit |
| Ethical Choice | Human freedom to choose between good and evil — a central Gathic teaching |
| Frashokereti | The eschatological renovation of the world and the triumph of good |
