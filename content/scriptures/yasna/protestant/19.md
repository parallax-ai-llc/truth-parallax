## Overview

From a Protestant theological perspective, Yasna Ha 19 (The Zand of Creation) invites reflection on the parallels and contrasts between Zoroastrian and biblical faith. This chapter, part of the Yasna liturgy, providing the ritual framework for Zoroastrian worship, engages themes that resonate with Protestant concerns — including the nature of God, the reality of evil, the prophetic vocation, and the hope of ultimate redemption.

Protestant scholars have long been interested in the Zoroastrian tradition as a possible influence on biblical theology, particularly regarding eschatology, angelology, and the concept of cosmic struggle between good and evil. This chapter contributes to that discussion through its treatment of creation.

## Key Themes

- **Theological parallels**: Points of convergence with biblical monotheism, prophetic religion, and ethical teaching
- **Contrast and critique**: Where Zoroastrian and Protestant theological frameworks diverge — on revelation, salvation, grace, and the nature of scripture
- **Historical influence**: The possible transmission of ideas between Zoroastrian and Israelite/early Christian traditions

## Key Verses

Among the chapter's approximately 12 verses, passages addressing creation and cosmogony are of particular interest for Protestant engagement, inviting comparison with biblical texts from the prophets, psalms, and apostolic writings.

## Commentary Tradition

Protestant engagement with Zoroastrian texts has a long history, from **James Hope Moulton's** *Early Zoroastrianism* (1913) to contemporary comparative theology. The tradition navigates between appreciation for Zoroastrian monotheism and ethical teaching, and the Protestant insistence on the unique and final revelation in Christ.
