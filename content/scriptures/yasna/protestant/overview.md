## Overview

Protestant engagement with the Yasna draws on a long tradition of comparative theology, beginning with the Reformation-era interest in "natural theology" and intensifying with 19th-century comparative religion. Protestant scholars and theologians have been particularly drawn to the Gathas within the Yasna for their striking parallels with biblical monotheism, ethical prophecy, and eschatological hope — while also noting fundamental differences regarding salvation, grace, and the nature of revelation.

## Key Interpretive Themes

### Monotheistic Parallels
The Gathas' exaltation of Ahura Mazda as the sole creator and source of asha (righteousness) resonates with the prophetic monotheism of Isaiah and Deuteronomy. Protestant theologians have debated whether Zarathustra's monotheism represents independent revelation, natural theology, or historical contact with Israelite tradition.

### Ethical Dualism and Spiritual Warfare
Yasna 30's presentation of the Two Spirits choosing between truth and falsehood parallels Pauline and Johannine language about light and darkness, the Spirit and the flesh. Protestant systematic theology has engaged with the question of whether biblical dualism was influenced by Zoroastrian thought.

### Prophetic Vocation
Zarathustra's role as depicted in the Gathas — a lonely prophet called by God, rejected by the powerful, persevering in faith — resonates with the biblical prophetic tradition from Moses to Jeremiah.

### Eschatology
The Yasna's references to final judgment, bodily resurrection, and the renovation of the world (frashokereti) have been compared to Revelation, 1 Thessalonians, and Daniel, raising the question of Zoroastrian influence on biblical eschatology.
