## Overview

Haggai is one of the shortest books in the Hebrew Bible, consisting of just two chapters containing four precisely dated oracles delivered between August and December of 520 BCE. The prophet addresses the returned exiles in Jerusalem who have rebuilt their own houses but left the Temple of the LORD in ruins. His message is direct and practical: rebuild the Temple, get your priorities right, and trust that God's glory will fill this house even more abundantly than Solomon's original.

## Structure

The book contains four distinct oracles, each precisely dated:

1. **First Oracle** (1:1-11): A call to rebuild the Temple, linking the community's economic hardship to their neglect of God's house
2. **The People's Response** (1:12-15): Zerubbabel, Joshua, and the remnant obey and begin work
3. **Second Oracle** (2:1-9): Encouragement that the glory of the latter house will surpass the former
4. **Third and Fourth Oracles** (2:10-23): A priestly ruling on holiness and defilement, followed by a messianic promise to Zerubbabel

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Haggai's brief but pointed message resonates across traditions:

- **Judaism**: Central to the history of the Second Temple and the theology of return from exile
- **Christianity**: The promise of a greater glory (2:9) is read as pointing to Christ's presence in the Temple and in the Church
- **Islam**: The rebuilding of a house of worship after exile resonates with the Quranic narrative of sacred spaces
- **Academic**: A key primary source for the early post-exilic period and Persian-period Yehud
