Haggai 2 contains three distinct oracles that move from encouragement to eschatological promise. The first (2:1-9) addresses the disappointment of those who remembered Solomon's Temple and found the rebuilt structure pathetically modest by comparison. The prophet's response is not to deny the disparity but to transcend it: "The latter glory of this house shall be greater than the former" (2:9). Protestant Christological interpretation reads this as fulfilled when Christ — the glory of God incarnate — entered the Second Temple, filling it with a presence that far exceeded the Shekinah of Solomon's day.

The priestly ruling in verses 10-19, where Haggai asks the priests about the transmissibility of holiness and defilement, establishes a principle that Protestant interpreters apply broadly: uncleanness is more contagious than holiness. Contact with something holy does not automatically make a person holy, but contact with something unclean does defile. Reformed commentators see here a refutation of works-righteousness — merely being near sacred things or performing religious rituals cannot sanctify a person whose heart remains unchanged. The application to the rebuilt Temple is that the building alone cannot save the community; genuine repentance and obedience are required.

The final oracle (2:20-23), addressed personally to Zerubbabel, elevates the Davidic governor to cosmic significance: "I will make you like a signet ring, for I have chosen you." This reverses the judgment pronounced on Zerubbabel's grandfather Jehoiachin (Jeremiah 22:24), restoring the Davidic line to divine favor. Protestant interpreters read this as a messianic promise pointing to Christ, the ultimate son of David, through whom God's purposes for His people are sealed and guaranteed.
