## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Haggai emphasizes the prophet's call to covenant faithfulness and right priorities as paradigmatic for the Christian life. The book's central challenge — that the returned exiles had built their own paneled houses while the LORD's house lay in ruins — is read as a perennial warning against misplaced priorities. Reformed commentators particularly appreciate Haggai's direct, unadorned style and his insistence that obedience to God's commands must take precedence over personal comfort.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Typological Reading
The promise that "the glory of this latter house shall be greater than of the former" (2:9) is widely read as a Christological prophecy. Christ's physical presence in the Second Temple fulfills this promise in a way that transcends architectural splendor. The "desire of all nations" (2:7, KJV) has been interpreted messianically since at least the time of the Church Fathers.

### Application to Church Life
Protestant preachers frequently apply Haggai's message to contemporary church priorities: investing in comfortable personal lives while neglecting corporate worship, mission, and the maintenance of church communities. The principle is not about architecture per se but about the ordering of commitments.
