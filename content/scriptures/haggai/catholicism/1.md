Catholic interpretation of Haggai 1 underscores the theological significance of sacred architecture and liturgical space. The prophet's rebuke — "this house lies in ruins" while the people dwell in "paneled houses" — is read not merely as a matter of practical priorities but as a statement about the ordering of human life around the worship of God. Catholic social teaching, following the principle that the liturgy is the "source and summit" of Christian life (Sacrosanctum Concilium 10), finds in Haggai a biblical warrant for investing in beautiful churches, proper liturgical furnishings, and the material elements of worship.

The economic consequences of neglecting the Temple (1:6-11) are interpreted in light of Catholic teaching on stewardship and the universal destination of goods. When a community fails to center its life on God, even its material prosperity becomes hollow — "you eat, but you never have enough." The Catechism's teaching on the first commandment (CCC 2084-2094) echoes Haggai's logic: when God is not given first place, everything else falls into disorder.

The community's obedient response (1:12-15) models the Catholic understanding of the proper relationship between the hierarchy (Zerubbabel and Joshua as governor and priest) and the laity (the "remnant of the people"). Together, moved by the Spirit of God, they undertake the sacred work. Catholic ecclesiology sees in this passage a precedent for the collaboration of clergy and laity in building up the Church, each contributing according to their vocation and gifts.
