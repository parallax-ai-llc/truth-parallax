The promise that "the latter glory of this house shall be greater than the former" (2:9) is one of the most important Old Testament texts for Catholic sacramental theology. The glory of the Second Temple, modest as its architecture was, exceeded Solomon's Temple because Christ himself — the fullness of divine glory — walked its courts. By extension, every Catholic church building, however humble, is filled with a glory that surpasses Solomon's Temple through the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist. This conviction undergirds the Catholic tradition of Eucharistic adoration, tabernacle lamps, and the reverential treatment of sacred space.

The priestly consultation on holiness and defilement (2:10-19) resonates with Catholic sacramental theology's careful attention to the conditions for valid and fruitful reception of the sacraments. Holiness is not automatically transferred by external contact; the disposition of the recipient matters. This principle informs the Catholic practice of requiring proper preparation for the Eucharist, including the state of grace and the Eucharistic fast. The passage also supports the distinction between *ex opere operato* (the sacrament's objective efficacy) and *ex opere operantis* (the subjective disposition of the recipient).

The signet ring promise to Zerubbabel (2:23) is read in Catholic tradition as a messianic prophecy fulfilled in Jesus Christ, descended from David through Zerubbabel's line (Matthew 1:12-13, Luke 3:27). The signet ring — an instrument of royal authority used to seal official documents — signifies that Christ bears the full authority of God and authenticates the new covenant with His own person. This reading connects Haggai's promise to the theology of Christ as the seal and guarantee of God's faithfulness.
