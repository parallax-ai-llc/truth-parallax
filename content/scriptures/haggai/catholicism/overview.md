## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Haggai highlights the theological significance of sacred space and liturgical worship. The prophet's insistence on rebuilding the Temple is read not merely as a practical directive but as an affirmation that God wills to dwell among His people in a particular, localized way — a principle that finds its fulfillment in the Incarnation and, sacramentally, in the Eucharistic presence. Catholic tradition sees in Haggai a biblical foundation for the Church's commitment to building, adorning, and maintaining houses of worship.

## Theological Emphases

The promise that the glory of the latter house will exceed the former (2:9) is interpreted in Catholic tradition as fulfilled in the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist. The Second Temple's physical modesty compared to Solomon's Temple is irrelevant because the glory that fills it is the person of Christ himself. This reading undergirds the Catholic theology of sacred architecture: churches are not merely gathering spaces but dwelling places of God, deserving of beauty and reverence.

## Liturgical Connection

Haggai's challenge to examine one's priorities (1:5-7) resonates with the Catholic practice of examination of conscience. The prophet's call to "consider your ways" anticipates the Lenten discipline of self-examination and the sacrament of Reconciliation. The emphasis on the community's collective responsibility for worship mirrors Catholic social teaching on the communal dimension of faith.
