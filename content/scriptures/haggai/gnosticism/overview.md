## Overview

Gnostic traditions generally viewed the Jerusalem Temple with suspicion, seeing it as a center of worship directed toward the Demiurge rather than the true, transcendent God. Haggai's passionate advocacy for rebuilding the physical Temple thus stands in tension with the Gnostic impulse to move beyond material structures toward interior, spiritual knowledge. Nevertheless, Gnostic interpreters could appropriate the book's language by distinguishing between the outward temple of stone and the inward temple of *gnosis*.

## Key Interpretive Themes

The contrast between the people's "paneled houses" and the ruined Temple (1:4) is reread as a critique not of neglecting a physical building but of investing in the material world while ignoring the spiritual self. The true "temple" that needs rebuilding, in Gnostic thought, is the pneumatic self — the divine spark imprisoned in matter that must be awakened through knowledge. The Gospel of Philip (NHC II,3) teaches that the true holy of holies is the "bridal chamber" of spiritual union, not any physical structure.

## Theological Tensions

Haggai's thoroughly material concerns — economic hardship, agricultural failure, the physical dimensions of the Temple — sit uneasily with Gnostic metaphysics. Gnostic readers would likely view the prophet's economic theology (obey and prosper, disobey and suffer) as characteristic of the Demiurge's system of rewards and punishments, which keeps the *hylic* and *psychic* classes in line through material incentives rather than liberating them through knowledge.
