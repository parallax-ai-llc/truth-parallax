The promise that "the latter glory of this house shall be greater than the former" (2:9) is given a characteristically inverted reading by Gnostic interpreters. If the First Temple housed the Shekinah — the visible glory of the Demiurge — then the "greater glory" of the Second Temple must refer to something that transcends the Demiurge entirely. Gnostic Christians could read this as an unwitting prophecy of Christ's presence in the Temple, where Christ represents not the fulfillment of the Demiurge's purposes but the intrusion of the true divine light into the Demiurge's domain, subverting it from within.

The cosmic shaking (2:6-7) — "I will shake the heavens and the earth and the sea and the dry land" — aligns with the Gnostic expectation of the material cosmos's ultimate dissolution. The Demiurge's creation is unstable because it was fashioned in ignorance and from deficient substance. The shaking of all nations and the redistribution of their treasures (2:7-8) anticipates the eschatological moment when the material order collapses and the divine sparks trapped within it are released to return to the Pleroma.

The signet ring promise to Zerubbabel (2:23) could be read in Valentinian terms as a reference to the sealing of the pneumatic elect. The signet ring (*sphragis*) was an important symbol in early Christian and Gnostic initiation rites, representing the seal of divine identity that protects the soul on its ascent through the archonic spheres. Zerubbabel, as the chosen vessel, becomes a type of the pneumatic individual who bears the authentic imprint of the true God — not the Demiurge's mark but the hidden seal of the Father of All.
