## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Haggai (Chaggai) is one of the last three prophets — together with Zechariah and Malachi — marking the twilight of biblical prophecy. The Talmud (Yoma 9b) teaches that with the death of these three prophets, the Holy Spirit departed from Israel. Haggai's ministry is inseparable from the historical drama of *shivat tzion* (the return to Zion), when the exiles who returned from Babylon faced the daunting task of rebuilding both their community and the Temple.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The Talmud preserves several traditions about Haggai's prophetic authority. Bava Batra 15a attributes the compilation of several biblical books to the "Men of the Great Assembly," which tradition associates with the post-exilic prophets including Haggai. The Mishnah (Yadayim 4:3) records halakhic disputes settled by prophetic authority, and Haggai is credited with transmitting certain oral traditions that bridged the pre-exilic and post-exilic periods.

## Theological Emphases

The centrality of the *Beit HaMikdash* (Temple) in Jewish life cannot be overstated, and Haggai's insistence on rebuilding it reflects the conviction that the Temple is not optional but essential for the proper worship of God and the fullness of Jewish communal life. The prophet's challenge — "Is it time for you yourselves to dwell in your paneled houses while this house lies in ruins?" (1:4) — remains a powerful text for Jewish reflection on communal priorities and the relationship between personal prosperity and sacred obligation.
