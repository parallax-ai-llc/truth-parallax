The promise of greater glory for the latter house (2:9) is central to Orthodox theology of the Incarnation and the Eucharist. The Fathers taught that the Second Temple's true glory was not architectural but personal — the bodily presence of the Logos incarnate. This principle extends to the Orthodox understanding of the church building as a microcosm of the transfigured cosmos: every liturgical celebration makes present the glory of the age to come. The iconostasis, the altar, and the Eucharistic gifts are not mere symbols but participations in the heavenly reality.

The shaking of heavens and earth (2:6-7) is read eschatologically in Orthodox tradition. The Fathers connected this to the cosmic transformation that accompanies the Incarnation, the Crucifixion (when the earth shook and rocks split), and the final consummation. Gregory of Nyssa and Maximus the Confessor spoke of the Incarnation as a cosmic event that shook the foundations of the fallen order and initiated the process of recapitulation — the restoration of all things in Christ.

The designation of Zerubbabel as God's "signet ring" (2:23) carries particular weight in Orthodox Christology. The signet ring bears the image of the king and impresses that image on everything it touches. Christ, the ultimate fulfillment of this promise, bears the image of the Father (Hebrews 1:3) and impresses the divine likeness on all who are united to Him through baptism, chrismation, and the Eucharist. The theology of *theosis* — humanity's participation in the divine nature — finds a prophetic anticipation in this Haggai text.
