## Overview

Haggai does not appear by name in the Quran, and the book holds no canonical status in Islam. However, the themes of Haggai — particularly the rebuilding of a sacred house of worship, the prophetic call to right priorities, and the divine promise of blessing for faithful obedience — resonate with Islamic theological concerns. Islamic engagement with Haggai occurs primarily through *Isra'iliyyat* traditions and through thematic parallels with Quranic narratives about sacred architecture and prophetic leadership.

## Quranic Parallels

The rebuilding of the Temple recalls the Quranic account of Ibrahim (Abraham) and Isma'il (Ishmael) building the Kaaba (Quran 2:127). Both narratives center on the construction of a sacred house of worship as an act of obedience to divine command, and both emphasize that the building itself matters less than the devotion it represents. Haggai's rebuke of misplaced priorities similarly echoes the Quranic warning against those who "build a mosque to cause harm" (9:107) rather than for sincere worship.

## Theological Emphases

The principle that material hardship can result from neglecting divine commands (Haggai 1:6-11) aligns with the Quranic teaching that blessings follow obedience and trials follow heedlessness. The prophetic role of Haggai as a motivator and encourager of the community parallels the Quranic description of prophets who guide their peoples through periods of difficulty and reconstruction.
