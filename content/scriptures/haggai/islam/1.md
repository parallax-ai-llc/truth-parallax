Islamic engagement with Haggai 1 centers on the universal prophetic principle that communities must prioritize worship of God above material comfort. The prophet's rebuke of the returned exiles — dwelling in comfortable houses while God's house lies desolate — parallels the Quranic admonition against those who prioritize worldly life (*dunya*) over devotion to God. Surah At-Takathur (102:1-2) warns: "Competition for worldly increase diverts you, until you visit the graves." Haggai's message fits within this broader prophetic tradition of calling communities back from materialism to spiritual purpose.

The economic consequences described in verses 6-11 — planting much but harvesting little, earning wages that vanish — resonate with the Quranic principle that God controls provision (*rizq*) and distributes it according to His wisdom. Surah Ar-Ra'd (13:26) states: "Allah extends provision for whom He wills and restricts it." Islamic theology interprets material hardship not as random misfortune but as a potential sign of divine displeasure or a test of faith, consistent with Haggai's prophetic diagnosis.

The community's prompt obedience (1:12-15) models the Islamic ideal of *sam'ina wa ata'na* — "we hear and we obey" (Quran 24:51). The immediate, collective response to prophetic instruction, without argument or delay, represents the paradigm of faithful submission. The stirring of the people's spirits by God (1:14) is consistent with the Islamic understanding that genuine faith and motivation come ultimately from God's guidance (*hidayah*), even as humans are responsible for their response.
