The promise that God will "shake the heavens and the earth" (2:6) and that the glory of the latter house will surpass the former (2:9) resonates with Islamic eschatological imagery. The Quran describes cosmic upheaval on the Day of Judgment: "When the earth is shaken with its earthquake" (Surah Az-Zalzalah 99:1). While Haggai's context is the rebuilt Temple, Islamic readers recognize the prophetic pattern of using cosmic language to signal divine intervention in history, a pattern that finds its ultimate fulfillment in the events of the Last Day.

The priestly ruling on holiness and defilement (2:10-14) connects to Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) regarding *taharah* (ritual purity). Islamic law, like the priestly system Haggai invokes, maintains careful distinctions between states of purity and impurity, with detailed rules about what transmits contamination and what restores cleanliness. The principle that impurity spreads more readily than purity finds echoes in the hadith literature and in the legal schools' treatment of *najasah* (ritual impurity).

The elevation of Zerubbabel as God's "signet ring" (2:23) is of limited direct interest in Islamic theology, as the specifically Davidic messianic expectations are not part of the Islamic framework. However, the broader principle — that God raises and empowers chosen leaders to accomplish His purposes — is entirely consistent with the Quranic theology of divine appointment. The Quran affirms that God grants authority (*mulk*) to whom He wills (2:247) and raises prophets and leaders according to His plan.
