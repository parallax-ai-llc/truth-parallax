Islamic engagement with Zephaniah 1 centers on the thematic parallels between the Day of the LORD and the Quranic Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*). While Zephaniah is not a recognized prophet in the Quran, the vivid imagery of cosmic destruction in this chapter echoes Quranic descriptions with remarkable similarity. The "day of wrath, distress, and anguish" (1:15) parallels the Quranic descriptions in Surah Al-Haqqah (69:1-3) and Surah Al-Qari'ah (101:1-5), where the coming catastrophe is so immense that human language can barely contain it.

The condemnation of idolatry and syncretism (1:4-6) aligns directly with the Quranic emphasis on *tawhid* (divine unity) and the categorical rejection of *shirk* (associating partners with God). The prophet's rebuke of those who bow to the "host of heaven" on their rooftops resonates with the Quranic denunciation of astral worship and the pre-Islamic Arabian practice of worshipping celestial bodies. Muslim commentators who encounter this passage through *Isra'iliyyat* traditions recognize it as consistent with the universal prophetic message: worship God alone.

The declaration that wealth will be useless on the day of reckoning (1:18) finds a direct parallel in Surah Al-Shu'ara (26:88-89): "The Day when neither wealth nor children will be of any benefit, except for one who comes to God with a sound heart." This convergence of prophetic themes across biblical and Quranic traditions is understood in Islamic thought not as literary borrowing but as evidence of a unified divine message delivered through successive messengers.
