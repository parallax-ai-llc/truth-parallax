The call to repentance in Zephaniah 2:1-3 resonates with the Quranic concept of *tawbah* (turning back to God). The urgency of Zephaniah's summons — "before the day of the LORD's anger comes upon you" — parallels the repeated Quranic warnings to repent before it is too late, as in Surah Az-Zumar (39:54): "Turn to your Lord and submit to Him before the punishment comes upon you, for then you will not be helped." Islamic theology emphasizes that the door of repentance remains open until the moment of death or until the cosmic signs of the Last Day appear.

The oracles against surrounding nations (2:4-15) demonstrate the principle, affirmed in Islam, that God sends warners to every people. The Quran states: "There is no community to which a warner has not come" (35:24). The judgment of Philistia, Moab, Ammon, Cush, and Assyria illustrates that divine justice is universal, not limited to a single covenant people. This universalism is central to the Islamic worldview, which holds that God's prophets have appeared among all nations throughout history.

The destruction of Nineveh prophesied in 2:13-15 creates an interesting intertextual connection with the Quranic account of Yunus (Jonah) and the people of Nineveh. In Surah Yunus (10:98), Nineveh is uniquely praised as "the only community that believed and its faith benefited it." Zephaniah's later prophecy of Nineveh's destruction suggests that this repentance was temporary, a reading consistent with the Islamic understanding that communities, like individuals, must persevere in faith to remain under divine protection.
