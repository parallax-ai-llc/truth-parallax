The indictment of Jerusalem's corrupt leaders in Zephaniah 3:1-4 aligns with the Quranic theme of religious leaders who distort divine guidance for personal gain. The Quran warns against scholars who "sell the revelations of God for a small price" (2:41) and leaders who "consume people's wealth unjustly" (4:29). The prophetic critique of institutional corruption is a consistent theme across the biblical and Quranic prophetic traditions, reinforcing the Islamic conviction that all prophets delivered essentially the same message of justice and monotheism.

The vision of universal worship in 3:9-10 — all peoples calling on the name of the LORD with one accord, bringing offerings from beyond the rivers of Cush — resonates with the Islamic vision of *ummah*, a universal community of believers transcending ethnic and national boundaries. The Quran presents Islam not as a new religion but as the primordial faith (*din al-fitrah*) to which all peoples are called to return. Zephaniah's vision of purified speech and unified worship anticipates this ideal.

The closing promise of restoration and gathering (3:19-20) parallels eschatological themes in Islamic tradition. The gathering of the dispersed faithful, the vindication of the oppressed, and the establishment of divine justice are central features of Islamic eschatology. The beautiful image of God rejoicing over His people (3:17) echoes hadith traditions that describe God's joy at the repentance of a single servant, likened to a traveler in the desert who finds his lost camel laden with provisions.
