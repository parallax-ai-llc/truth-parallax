The opening of Zephaniah 2 — "Gather together, gather, O shameless nation" (2:1) — is interpreted in rabbinic literature as a call to collective repentance before the window of opportunity closes. The phrase "before the decree takes effect" (2:2) establishes a theology of conditional judgment that is central to Jewish understanding of prophecy: prophetic warnings of doom are invitations to repentance, not irrevocable decrees. The Talmud (Berakhot 7a) teaches that even when a sharp sword rests upon a person's neck, one should not despair of divine mercy.

The instruction to "seek the LORD, seek righteousness, seek humility" (2:3) introduces the key concept of the *anawim* — the humble poor who constitute the faithful core of Israel. This concept, developed extensively in the Psalms (particularly Psalms 9, 10, 22, 25, 37), became foundational for Jewish piety. The *anawim* are not merely economically poor but spiritually humble, wholly dependent on God rather than on their own power or wealth. Hasidic tradition later developed this theology of spiritual poverty into a central spiritual practice.

The oracles against neighboring nations (2:4-15) are read in Jewish tradition as evidence of God's universal sovereignty. Radak (Rabbi David Kimchi) emphasizes that these judgments demonstrate that the LORD is not merely a national deity but the Judge of all the earth. The promise that "the remnant of the house of Judah shall pasture on the coastlands" (2:7) feeds directly into messianic hope for territorial restoration, a theme that remained alive throughout the centuries of exile.
