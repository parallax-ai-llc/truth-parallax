Jewish interpretation of Zephaniah 1 situates the prophecy within the historical context of King Josiah's reign and the broader prophetic tradition of Yom YHWH (the Day of the LORD). The Talmud (Megillah 15a) identifies Zephaniah as a contemporary of Jeremiah, Huldah, and possibly Habakkuk. Rabbinic tradition holds that his preaching helped prepare the ground for Josiah's reforms, which are described in 2 Kings 22-23. The prophet's genealogy extending four generations to Hezekiah (1:1) is unusual and has led many commentators, including Rashi and Radak, to identify this ancestor as King Hezekiah, giving the prophet both royal and prophetic authority.

The judgment against those who worship both the LORD and Milcom (1:5) targets the sin of religious syncretism — serving God while simultaneously hedging with other powers. The Midrash draws a parallel to the generation of Elijah, who challenged Israel on Mount Carmel: "How long will you go limping between two opinions?" (1 Kings 18:21). Ibn Ezra notes that the specificity of Zephaniah's targets — priests of Baal, those who bow on rooftops, those who swear by Milcom — indicates an intimate knowledge of Jerusalem's religious landscape, consistent with his royal connections.

The terrifying description of the Day of the LORD in verses 14-18 has influenced Jewish liturgy, particularly the High Holiday prayers. The themes of judgment, reckoning, and the inadequacy of material wealth to provide shelter from divine scrutiny echo throughout the Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur liturgy. The declaration that "neither their silver nor their gold shall be able to deliver them" (1:18) reinforces the Yom Kippur emphasis on repentance, prayer, and charity — not material offerings — as the response to divine judgment.
