## Overview

In the Jewish tradition, Zephaniah (Tsefanyah) is part of the Trei Asar (Twelve Minor Prophets), counted as a single book in the Tanakh. The prophet's name, meaning "the LORD has hidden" or "the LORD has treasured," is itself laden with theological significance — God conceals and preserves the righteous through judgment. Rabbinic tradition connects Zephaniah's ministry to the era of Josiah's reforms and sees his message as a call to *teshuvah* (repentance).

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The Talmud (Megillah 15a) lists Zephaniah among the prophets who prophesied during the same period as Jeremiah. Midrashic sources emphasize his royal lineage as lending authority to his prophetic rebuke. The concept of the *anawim* — the humble, lowly poor who constitute the faithful remnant — became central to Jewish piety, particularly in the Psalms and later mystical traditions.

## Theological Emphases

- **Yom YHWH**: The Day of the LORD as a concrete historical and eschatological reality, not mere metaphor
- **Teshuvah**: The urgent call to repentance before the day of judgment (2:1-3) resonates with High Holiday liturgy
- **Anawim**: The theology of the humble remnant as the true Israel, influential in Hasidic spirituality
- **Ingathering of exiles**: The promise of restoration (3:19-20) is central to Jewish messianic hope and liturgical prayer
