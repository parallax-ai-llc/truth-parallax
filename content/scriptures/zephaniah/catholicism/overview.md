## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Zephaniah draws on both the literal and spiritual senses of Scripture as articulated in the Church's exegetical tradition. The book is read within the broader canon and in light of Sacred Tradition, with particular attention to its liturgical use and its resonance with Catholic doctrines of divine justice, purification, and eschatological hope.

## Liturgical and Devotional Use

Zephaniah 3:14-18 is used in the liturgy of Advent, where the joyful summons to "Sing aloud, O daughter Zion" anticipates the joy of the Incarnation. The *Dies Irae*, the medieval Latin hymn used in the Requiem Mass for centuries, draws its opening words directly from the Vulgate translation of Zephaniah 1:15-16, linking the prophet's vision of judgment to Catholic eschatology and the theology of purgatory.

## Theological Emphases

- **Purgation and purification**: The fires of judgment (1:18, 3:8) are read in connection with the doctrine of purgatory as a refining process
- **The faithful remnant as ecclesial type**: The humble remnant (3:12-13) prefigures the Church as the faithful community preserved through judgment
- **Universal scope of salvation**: The vision of nations worshiping God (3:9-10) supports the Catholic understanding of the universal call to salvation
- **Marian echoes**: "Daughter Zion" (3:14) is read typologically as a figure of Mary, the ark of the new covenant
