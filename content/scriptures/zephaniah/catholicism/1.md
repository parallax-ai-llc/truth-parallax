Catholic interpretation of Zephaniah 1 reads the Day of the LORD through the dual lens of historical judgment and eschatological consummation. The Vulgate rendering of verses 15-16 — *Dies irae, dies illa* — gave Western Christianity its most enduring liturgical expression of divine judgment. For centuries, the *Dies Irae* sequence was sung at every Requiem Mass, embedding Zephaniah's imagery deep in Catholic consciousness. Even after its removal from the ordinary form of the Mass in 1970, it remains in the Extraordinary Form and in the Liturgy of the Hours for the last week of the Church year.

The prophet's indictment of syncretistic worship (1:4-6) resonates with Catholic teaching on the First Commandment and the danger of divided allegiance. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 2110-2117) addresses superstition, idolatry, and divination — modern parallels to the astral worship and religious syncretism Zephaniah condemns. The Church reads this not as ancient history but as a perennial warning against substituting created things for the Creator.

The comprehensive scope of judgment — "I will utterly sweep away everything" (1:2) — is read in light of Catholic eschatology as pointing toward the universal judgment (CCC 1038-1041). Catholic teaching distinguishes between the particular judgment at death and the general judgment at the end of time; Zephaniah's cosmic imagery speaks primarily to the latter, when all things will be brought before God's tribunal.
