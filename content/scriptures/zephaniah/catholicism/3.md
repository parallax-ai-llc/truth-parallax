Catholic reading of Zephaniah 3 finds in the transition from judgment to restoration a powerful expression of the *felix culpa* — the "happy fault" that occasions greater grace. The indictment of Jerusalem's corrupt leaders (3:1-4) is read as an honest reckoning with institutional failure that the Church applies to itself. Catholic tradition does not exempt its own leadership from prophetic critique; the reform movements that culminated in Vatican II drew on precisely this prophetic tradition.

The promise of "pure speech" (3:9) resonates with the Catholic theology of liturgical language. The restoration of a common language for worship anticipates the Latin liturgical tradition and, more profoundly, the Eucharistic unity that transcends linguistic boundaries. The vision of peoples bringing offerings "from beyond the rivers of Cush" (3:10) is read as a prophecy of the Church's global mission.

The Marian dimension of Zephaniah 3:14-17 is significant in Catholic interpretation. The address to "Daughter Zion" is read typologically as addressed to Mary, who embodies the faithful remnant of Israel and in whom the LORD truly "is in your midst" through the Incarnation. The Advent liturgy's use of this passage connects Zephaniah's promise of divine indwelling to the mystery of God becoming present in Mary's womb and, sacramentally, in the Eucharist.
