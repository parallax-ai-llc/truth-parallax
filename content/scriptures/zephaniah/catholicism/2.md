The call to "seek the LORD, seek righteousness, seek humility" (2:3) is central to Catholic moral theology's understanding of the virtues. The threefold imperative corresponds to the theological and cardinal virtues: seeking the LORD (faith), seeking righteousness (justice), and seeking humility (temperance and humility as the foundation of all virtue). Catholic spiritual writers from Bernard of Clairvaux to Therese of Lisieux have emphasized humility as the essential disposition for receiving divine grace.

The oracles against the nations demonstrate the universal scope of God's sovereignty, a theme that Catholic social teaching has developed in its doctrine of the common good and the universal destination of goods. The judgment of Moab and Ammon for their "taunts" and "boasts" against God's people (2:8-10) is read as a warning against the pride of nations, applicable to modern geopolitics as much as to ancient Near Eastern rivalries.

The promise that the remnant will possess the coastland (2:7) has been read typologically as the Church's inheritance of the promises made to Israel. Catholic ecclesiology sees the Church as the continuation and fulfillment of Israel's vocation, inheriting both its privileges and its responsibilities. The vision of nations worshiping the LORD "each in its place" (2:11) anticipates the catholicity (universality) that defines the Church's mission.
