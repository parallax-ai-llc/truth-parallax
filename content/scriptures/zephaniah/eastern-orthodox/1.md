Orthodox interpretation of Zephaniah 1 approaches the Day of the LORD through the patristic understanding of divine judgment as revelation rather than mere retribution. The Fathers taught that God's wrath is not an emotion opposed to love but the experience of God's uncompromising holiness by those who have turned away from it. Zephaniah's fire (1:18) is the same fire that warms the righteous and burns the wicked — the difference lies not in the fire but in the disposition of those who encounter it.

The sweeping judgment announced in verses 2-3, which threatens to reverse creation itself, is read in Orthodox theology as a necessary prelude to the new creation. Just as the Flood prepared the way for a renewed world, the Day of the LORD prepares for the eschatological transfiguration of all things. The liturgical use of prophetic judgment texts during Great Lent reflects this understanding: the faithful must pass through the purgative experience of repentance before arriving at the joy of Pascha.

The condemnation of those who are "thickening on their dregs" and who say "the LORD will not do good, nor will he do ill" (1:12) targets spiritual complacency — what the Desert Fathers called *akedia* (listlessness). Orthodox spiritual theology identifies this torpor as among the most dangerous of the passions, a settled indifference to divine reality that effectively makes one's own comfort into a god.
