Zephaniah 3 holds a special place in Orthodox theology for its vision of eschatological joy. The indictment of Jerusalem's leaders (3:1-4) is read as a warning to the hierarchy of the Church itself: bishops, priests, and teachers bear particular responsibility and face particular judgment. The Orthodox tradition of *parrhesia* — the freedom to speak truth to ecclesial authority — finds prophetic warrant in passages like this.

The promise that God will "change the speech of the peoples to a pure speech" (3:9) is connected in Orthodox thought to the Pentecost event and to the theology of divine liturgy. In the Eucharistic celebration, the assembled faithful are united in a common language of worship that transcends national and ethnic boundaries. The Slavic and Greek traditions of translating the liturgy into vernacular languages reflect this vision of diverse peoples united in praising God.

The climactic verse — "The LORD your God is in your midst, a mighty one who will save; he will rejoice over you with gladness; he will quiet you by his love; he will exult over you with loud singing" (3:17) — is one of the most beloved verses in Orthodox devotion. It captures the essence of *theosis*: God not merely tolerating or forgiving His people but actively delighting in them. The image of God singing over His people reverses the expected direction of worship and reveals the reciprocal joy at the heart of the divine-human relationship. This verse is frequently cited in Orthodox wedding services and in the Paschal celebration.
