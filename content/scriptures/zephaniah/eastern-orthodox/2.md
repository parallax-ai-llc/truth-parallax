The call to seek humility in Zephaniah 2:3 resonates deeply with the Orthodox spiritual tradition, where *tapeinophrosyne* (humility) is considered the crown of all virtues. The Philokalia and the writings of the Desert Fathers consistently identify humility as the prerequisite for *theosis* (deification) — the transformative union with God that is the goal of the Christian life. Zephaniah's *anawim*, the humble of the land, become models for the monastic ideal of kenotic self-emptying.

The oracles against the nations (2:4-15) are read in Orthodox tradition as demonstrating God's providential governance of all peoples, not merely Israel. The fall of Nineveh (2:13-15), once the world's greatest city, serves as a patristic commonplace about the transience of earthly power. John Chrysostom frequently invoked the rise and fall of empires to remind his congregations that only the Kingdom of God endures. The Orthodox liturgical calendar commemorates the repentance of Nineveh in the book of Jonah, creating an intertextual reading where Zephaniah's prophecy of Nineveh's destruction represents the consequence of returning to sin after repentance.

The promise of restoration for the remnant (2:7, 9) is read eschatologically as God's faithfulness to His Church through persecution. The Orthodox experience of centuries under Ottoman rule, communist oppression, and ongoing marginalization gives particular poignancy to these promises of vindication for the faithful few who persevere.
