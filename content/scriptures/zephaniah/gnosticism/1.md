Gnostic interpretation of Zephaniah 1 inverts the orthodox reading by identifying the wrathful deity who threatens cosmic destruction with the Demiurge rather than the true, transcendent God. The sweeping annihilation promised in verses 2-3 — "I will utterly sweep away everything from the face of the earth" — is read as the Demiurge's characteristic rage against a creation he cannot fully control. This destructive impulse, in the Gnostic view, reveals not divine justice but the Demiurge's fundamental instability and the impermanence of the material cosmos he has fashioned.

The *Apocalypse of Zephaniah*, a pseudepigraphical text surviving in Coptic fragments, reimagines the prophet as a visionary who ascends through multiple heavens. In this text, Zephaniah witnesses angelic beings recording the deeds of souls, encounters terrifying cosmic powers, and ultimately receives reassurance of divine mercy. The canonical chapter's imagery of darkness, gloom, and trumpet blasts (1:15-16) is recontextualized as the fearsome landscape of the archonic realms through which the soul must pass after death.

The condemnation of those who are "thickening on their dregs" and claim "the LORD will not do good, nor will he do ill" (1:12) is given a particular twist in Gnostic reading: these are the *hylic* (material) humans who, lacking the divine spark, cannot perceive spiritual reality at all. Their complacency is not merely moral failure but ontological limitation — they are creatures of the Demiurge who have no capacity for *gnosis*.
