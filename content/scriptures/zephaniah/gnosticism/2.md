The call to "seek the LORD, all you humble of the land" (2:3) is reinterpreted in Gnostic thought as an appeal to the pneumatic elect — those who carry the divine spark and are capable of receiving *gnosis*. The "humble of the land" (*anawim*) become the hidden community of knowers who live inconspicuously within the Demiurge's cosmos, awaiting liberation. The admonition to "perhaps you may be hidden on the day of the LORD's anger" takes on literal significance: the pneumatics must remain concealed from the archons and their earthly agents.

The oracles against the nations (2:4-15) are read as descriptions of the Demiurge's arbitrary and destructive governance of the material world. The rise and fall of empires — Philistia, Moab, Assyria — demonstrates not divine justice but the chaotic, purposeless violence that characterizes the realm of the archons. Nineveh's boast, "I am, and there is no one else" (2:15), directly echoes the Demiurge's own blasphemous declaration in the *Apocryphon of John* and other Gnostic texts: "I am God and there is no other." Its destruction, then, is the Demiurge turning his destructive power against his own creation.

The promise that the remnant will inherit the coastlands (2:7) is allegorized as the pneumatics' eventual inheritance of the Pleroma — the true, transcendent realm of fullness from which the divine sparks originally descended. The material territory is of no ultimate interest; what matters is the spiritual reality it dimly reflects.
