## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Zephaniah emphasizes the prophet's proclamation of the Day of the LORD as both a historical event and a typological foreshadowing of final judgment. The Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* leads Protestant readers to attend closely to the text's own theological logic, tracing themes of divine sovereignty, radical judgment, and gracious preservation of a remnant.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Covenant Theology
Reformed interpreters read Zephaniah within the framework of covenant, seeing Judah's judgment as the consequence of covenant unfaithfulness and the remnant promise as an expression of God's unbreakable commitment to His people. The call to "seek the LORD" (2:3) is understood as a gracious invitation within the covenant relationship.

### Eschatological Reading
Many Protestants see Zephaniah's Day of the LORD as having both near (the fall of Jerusalem in 586 BCE) and far (final judgment) horizons. The cosmic language of chapter 1 is taken as pointing beyond any single historical event to the ultimate consummation of God's justice.

## Theological Emphases

- **Total depravity**: Zephaniah's sweeping indictment of Judah illustrates the pervasiveness of human sin
- **Sovereign grace**: The preservation of a remnant (3:12-13) demonstrates God's initiative in salvation
- **Christological typology**: The gathering and restoration in chapter 3 foreshadows the work of Christ
- **Soli Deo gloria**: The final hymn of joy (3:14-20) centers on God's presence among His people
