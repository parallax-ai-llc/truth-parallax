Zephaniah 3 divides sharply between indictment (vv. 1-8) and restoration (vv. 9-20), and Protestant interpretation attends carefully to the theological logic connecting them. The "woe" pronounced against Jerusalem (3:1-4) is particularly devastating because it targets the city's leadership: officials who are "roaring lions," judges who are "evening wolves," prophets who are "reckless, faithless," and priests who "profane what is holy." The institutional corruption of every pillar of society demonstrates the need for divine intervention rather than human reform.

The pivotal verse 9 — "At that time I will change the speech of the peoples to a pure speech, that all of them may call upon the name of the LORD" — is read by many Protestants as a reversal of Babel (Genesis 11). Where human pride fragmented language and community, God's eschatological action will restore unity in worship. Reformed interpreters see here a foreshadowing of Pentecost, where the Holy Spirit enabled people of every tongue to hear the gospel.

The closing verses (3:14-20) constitute one of the most exuberant passages in the prophetic literature. "The LORD your God is in your midst, a mighty one who will save; he will rejoice over you with gladness; he will quiet you by his love; he will exult over you with loud singing" (3:17). Protestant preachers from Luther to Spurgeon have celebrated this verse as a supreme expression of God's delight in His redeemed people, a joy that originates not in human achievement but in divine grace.
