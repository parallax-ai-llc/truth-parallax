Zephaniah 2 opens with an urgent call to repentance — "Seek the LORD, all you humble of the land" (2:3) — before transitioning into a series of oracles against surrounding nations. Protestant interpreters emphasize the conditional structure of verse 3: "perhaps you may be hidden on the day of the LORD's anger." This "perhaps" is not divine uncertainty but a sober acknowledgment that repentance offers no mechanical guarantee; salvation remains God's sovereign prerogative.

The oracles against the nations — Philistia (2:4-7), Moab and Ammon (2:8-11), Cush (2:12), and Assyria (2:13-15) — demonstrate that the LORD's sovereignty extends beyond Israel. Protestant covenant theology reads these oracles as evidence that God holds all nations accountable, not only those in formal covenant relationship. The taunt against Nineveh — "Is this the exultant city that said, 'I am, and there is no one else'?" (2:15) — is read as a warning against the pride that precedes destruction, applicable to nations, churches, and individuals alike.

The promise that "the coast shall belong to the remnant of the house of Judah" (2:7) introduces the remnant theme that will culminate in chapter 3. Protestant interpreters see here the pattern of God preserving a faithful minority through judgment — a pattern that runs from Noah through Elijah's seven thousand to the New Testament church.
