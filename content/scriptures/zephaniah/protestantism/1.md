The opening chapter of Zephaniah delivers one of the most sweeping judgment oracles in the Hebrew Bible. Protestant interpreters read this passage as a definitive statement of God's sovereign right to judge all creation. The prophet announces that the LORD will "utterly sweep away everything from the face of the earth" (1:2), using language that deliberately echoes the Flood narrative of Genesis 6-9, signaling a judgment of cosmic proportions.

The specific indictments against Judah target religious syncretism — those who worship Baal alongside the LORD, who bow on rooftops to the host of heaven, and who swear by both the LORD and Milcom (1:4-6). Reformed commentators note that this is not atheism but the more insidious sin of divided loyalty, attempting to serve God while hedging one's bets with other powers. Calvin's commentary emphasizes that half-hearted devotion is itself a form of rebellion against God's exclusive claim.

The chapter's climax in verses 14-18, describing the Day of the LORD as a day of "wrath, distress, anguish, devastation, darkness, gloom, trumpet blast, and battle cry," became the basis for the medieval hymn *Dies Irae* and has profoundly shaped Protestant eschatology. The passage insists that neither silver nor gold can deliver on that day (1:18), a theme that resonated deeply with Reformation critiques of indulgences and works-righteousness.
