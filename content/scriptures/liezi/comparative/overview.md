## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Liezi finds in the text rich points of connection with philosophical traditions worldwide. The dream parables of chapter 3 invite comparison with Descartes' dream argument and the Indian concept of maya; the fatalism of chapter 6 resonates with Stoic and Islamic perspectives on destiny; and the Yang Zhu chapter's advocacy of self-preservation parallels Epicurean and Cyrenaic philosophies in the Greek tradition.

## Dreams and Skepticism

The Liezi's extended treatment of dreams — a man who lives as a servant by day dreams of being a king by night, and cannot determine which life is real — anticipates the skeptical arguments of Descartes by more than a millennium. Comparative philosophers examine these parallels not to establish historical influence but to illuminate the universal philosophical puzzles that arise when human beings reflect on the relationship between experience and reality.

## Fatalism East and West

The Liezi's philosophy of fate invites comparison with deterministic traditions worldwide: Stoic acceptance of logos, Islamic submission to divine will, Calvinist predestination, and the Indian concept of karma. Each tradition grapples with the same fundamental tension between human agency and cosmic determination, arriving at distinctive solutions that reflect their particular cultural and philosophical commitments.
