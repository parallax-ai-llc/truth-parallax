Comparative interpretation of Chapter 5, 'The Questions of Tang,' identifies themes and narrative strategies that resonate with philosophical and literary traditions worldwide. The Liezi's use of parables to convey philosophical insight invites comparison with the teaching methods of Jesus, the Buddha, and Sufi masters; its exploration of dreams and illusion parallels the Indian concept of maya and Western philosophical skepticism; its treatment of fate echoes Stoic, Islamic, and Calvinist reflections on determinism and human agency.

Comparative scholars examine these parallels not to establish historical influence but to illuminate the universal philosophical questions that diverse traditions address through their particular cultural resources. The Liezi's distinctive contribution — its combination of philosophical depth with literary playfulness, its refusal to resolve the tensions it raises — offers a mode of philosophical engagement that enriches comparative understanding.
