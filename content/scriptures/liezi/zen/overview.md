## Overview

Zen interpretation of the Liezi discovers a kindred spirit in a text that relentlessly questions the boundaries between dreaming and waking, reality and illusion, self and other. The Liezi's dream parables — which render uncertain the distinction between the dreamer and the dreamed — resonate deeply with the Zen understanding of the illusory nature of the self and the awakened nature of all experience.

## Emptiness

The Liezi's teaching on emptiness (xu) aligns naturally with the Zen (and broader Mahayana Buddhist) concept of sunyata. For Zen readers, the Liezi's insistence that emptiness is not mere nothingness but the pregnant source from which all phenomena arise mirrors the Heart Sutra's teaching that "form is emptiness, emptiness is form."

## Spontaneous Action

Zen practitioners find in the Liezi's accounts of effortless skill — archery without aiming, walking on air, riding the wind — descriptions of the spontaneous, ego-free action that Zen calls mushin (no-mind). These stories function as Taoist equivalents of Zen's own accounts of masters who demonstrate awakened consciousness through perfectly natural, uncontrived activity.
