Confucian interpretation of Chapter 1, 'Heaven's Gifts,' confronts a text that both engages and challenges fundamental Confucian commitments. The Liezi frequently invokes Confucius as a character, but presents him in ways that subvert orthodox Confucian expectations — as a figure who recognizes the limits of moral activism and acknowledges the wisdom of those who have transcended conventional social engagement.

Confucian readers grapple with this chapter's implications for the relationship between human effort and cosmic destiny, moral cultivation and natural spontaneity, social engagement and spiritual withdrawal. The Liezi's provocations are not easily dismissed, and the most thoughtful Confucian interpreters recognize in them genuine philosophical challenges that demand serious engagement rather than simple refutation.
