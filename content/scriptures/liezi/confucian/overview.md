## Overview

Confucian interpretation of the Liezi confronts a text that both honors and subverts the Confucian tradition. Chapter 4, titled "Zhongni" (Confucius), presents the sage as a figure who recognizes the limits of his own activism and acknowledges the superior wisdom of those who have achieved Taoist emptiness. This reimagining of Confucius as a proto-Taoist directly challenges Confucian self-understanding while simultaneously paying tribute to the sage's spiritual depth.

## The Challenge of Fate

The Liezi's extended treatment of fate in chapter 6 poses a fundamental challenge to Confucian moral activism. If the outcomes of human effort are determined by forces beyond individual control, what becomes of the Confucian commitment to self-cultivation, social reform, and moral engagement? Confucian readers have grappled with this question, finding in it either a dangerous quietism or a legitimate corrective to excessive human self-importance.

## The Yang Zhu Problem

The notorious chapter 7, attributed to the hedonist philosopher Yang Zhu, presents a philosophy of radical self-interest that directly contradicts Confucian altruism. Confucian interpreters have variously condemned this chapter as immoral, read it as satire, or recognized in it a legitimate philosophical position that Confucian ethics must address rather than simply dismiss.
