Philosophical Taoism reads Chapter 3, 'King Mu of Zhou,' as a distinctive contribution to the classical Taoist exploration of the fundamental nature of reality, knowledge, and human existence. The Liezi's approach in this chapter is characteristically oblique: rather than arguing directly for philosophical positions, it presents parables, dialogues, and thought experiments that unsettle conventional assumptions and invite the reader to a mode of understanding that transcends discursive reasoning.

Philosophical interpreters examine how this chapter develops the Liezi's central themes — emptiness, fate, relativism, and the limits of knowledge — through its distinctive literary strategies. The text's use of narrative rather than argument as its primary philosophical vehicle represents a methodological choice with deep implications: truth, the Liezi suggests, is better shown than stated, better enacted than formulated.
