Though the Heart Sutra is not part of the Theravada Pali canon, these 16 verses encapsulate a teaching on emptiness that connects to the Theravada doctrine of anatta. The sutra's declaration that form is emptiness can be understood as a Mahayana intensification of the insight that all five aggregates are impermanent and devoid of a permanent self. Avalokiteshvara's teaching to Shariputra, revered in Theravada as the foremost disciple in wisdom, bridges the two traditions.

Theravada scholars note that while the Pali canon does not use sunyata in the same systematic way, the Sunyata Sutta and other texts do employ the concept. The Heart Sutra's negation of the skandhas, sense bases, and even the Four Noble Truths challenges practitioners to examine whether analytical categories are means to liberation rather than ends in themselves.

The concluding mantra, while not part of Theravada liturgical practice, expresses an aspiration to transcendence that resonates with the goal of nibbana as the unconditioned, beyond all conceptual formulation.
