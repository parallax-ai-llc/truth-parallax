Academic scholarship on these 16 verses has produced consequential debates in Buddhist Studies. Jan Nattier's 1992 hypothesis that the Heart Sutra was composed in Chinese, based on evidence that its core passage was extracted from Kumarajiva's translation of the Large Prajnaparamita, challenged centuries of tradition.

Textual analysis reveals two recensions: a shorter version (East Asia) lacking the narrative frame, and a longer version (Tibet and Nepal) including the Vulture Peak setting and Buddha's approval. The manuscript history from Dunhuang to Gilgit illuminates complex processes of Buddhist textual transmission.

Philosophically, scholars examine how the Heart Sutra's treatment of emptiness relates to Nagarjuna and Candrakirti's Madhyamaka philosophy. The negation of the Four Noble Truths raises questions about the Prajnaparamita literature's consistency with other Mahayana teachings.
