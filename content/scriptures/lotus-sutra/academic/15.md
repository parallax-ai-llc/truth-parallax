## Academic Analysis

Chapter 15 of the Lotus Sutra presents the emergence of countless bodhisattvas from beneath the earth, revealing that the Buddha has been teaching for an inconceivably long time. Academic scholarship situates this chapter within the broader compositional history of the sutra, examining its literary structure, doctrinal content, and relationship to other Buddhist and non-Buddhist literature.

Textual scholars have analyzed this chapter's position within the sutra's probable compositional layers. The consensus holds that the core doctrinal chapters (2–9) represent the earliest stratum, while the later chapters (10–28) were added in successive stages, expanding the sutra's devotional scope and introducing new bodhisattva figures. The chapter's language, imagery, and rhetorical strategies provide evidence for dating and locating its composition within the broader history of Indian Mahayana literature.

The chapter's cultural afterlife — in East Asian art, liturgy, literature, and politics — has also been the subject of significant scholarly attention. The Lotus Sutra's chapters have been individually illustrated, chanted, copied as acts of devotion, and invoked in political contexts from the Nara period to the present day.
