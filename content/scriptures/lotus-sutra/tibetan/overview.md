## Overview

In Tibetan Buddhism, the Lotus Sutra (*mDo sde pad ma dkar po*) is included in the Kangyur and studied as an important Mahayana sutra, though it does not hold the supreme position it occupies in East Asian Buddhism. Tibetan interpreters read the sutra through the lenses of Madhyamaka philosophy and the tantric *trikaya* (three-body) doctrine, finding in it support for the universality of Buddha nature and the graduated path.

## The One Vehicle in Tibetan Context

Tibetan scholastic traditions generally interpret the Lotus Sutra's One Vehicle (*theg pa gcig*) teaching in terms of the Madhyamaka-Yogacara synthesis. The sutra's assertion that all beings will attain Buddhahood is read alongside the *tathagatagarbha* (Buddha-nature) teaching, which Tibetan masters integrate with the Madhyamaka understanding of emptiness. The provisional/definitive (*neyartha/nitartha*) hermeneutic is used to classify the sutra's various claims.

## Tantric Connections

The Lotus Sutra's portrayal of the Buddha as an eternal, cosmic presence resonates with the Tibetan understanding of the *dharmakaya*. The sutra's emphasis on skillful means anticipates the tantric principle that any experience can be transformed into a vehicle for awakening. Tibetan commentators have noted parallels between the Lotus Sutra's *upaya* and the tantric methods of transforming afflictive emotions (*klesha*) into wisdom.

## Study and Practice

While the Lotus Sutra does not serve as the basis for a specific Tibetan lineage or school, it is studied in the monastic curriculum, particularly in relation to the bodhisattva path and the classification of Buddhist teachings (*drang don* and *nges don*). Its parables and prophecies are cited by Tibetan masters across all four major schools.
