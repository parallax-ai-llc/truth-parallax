## Tibetan Buddhist Reading

Chapter 3 presents the parable of the burning house, in which a father lures his children out of a blazing house with promises of different carts, only to give them all the same magnificent ox-cart. Tibetan interpreters engage with this chapter through the hermeneutical frameworks of Madhyamaka philosophy and the provisional/definitive (*drang don/nges don*) distinction, while also drawing connections to tantric practice.

In the Tibetan scholastic tradition, the Lotus Sutra's teachings are classified according to the three turnings of the wheel of Dharma. The sutra's claim to be the final and supreme teaching is assessed in light of the Madhyamaka analysis of emptiness and the *tathagatagarbha* doctrine. Tibetan commentators generally respect the Lotus Sutra's soteriological vision while situating it within a broader framework that includes the Perfection of Wisdom sutras and the tantras.

The chapter's themes resonate with Tibetan practice traditions, particularly the emphasis on the bodhisattva's compassionate activity and the transformation of all experience into the path. Tibetan masters cite the Lotus Sutra alongside tantric texts to illustrate the principle that skillful means and wisdom (*upaya* and *prajna*) must be united on the path to Buddhahood.
