## Overview

The Lotus Sutra offers remarkably fertile ground for comparative religious study. Its themes of universal salvation, divine pedagogy through parable, the tension between provisional and ultimate truth, and the cosmic scope of its narrative find parallels across the world's religious traditions.

## Universal Salvation and Inclusivism

The Lotus Sutra's teaching that all beings will eventually attain Buddhahood invites comparison with universalist strands in Christianity (Origen, Gregory of Nyssa, and modern universalism), the Quranic teaching that God's mercy encompasses all things, and the Hindu understanding of all paths leading to the same ultimate reality. The sutra's insistence that earlier, apparently exclusivist teachings were merely provisional raises profound questions about religious pluralism and the nature of salvific truth.

## Parable and Allegory

The Lotus Sutra's great parables — the Burning House, the Prodigal Son, the Hidden Jewel — share structural and thematic elements with the parables of Jesus, the allegories of Plato, and the stories of the Sufi masters. In each tradition, narrative becomes a vehicle for truths that resist direct conceptual expression, and the hearer's capacity determines what can be communicated.

## The Eternal Teacher

The Lotus Sutra's revelation that the Buddha has been present since the beginningless past, only appearing to be born and die, parallels the Christian doctrine of the pre-existent Christ, the Hindu concept of divine avatara, and the Sufi teaching of the *nur Muhammadi* (the pre-eternal light of Muhammad). These structural parallels illuminate the shared human intuition that ultimate wisdom or divinity is not confined to a single historical moment.
