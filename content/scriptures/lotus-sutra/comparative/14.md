## Comparative Perspective

Chapter 14 of the Lotus Sutra presents the four peaceful practices (*anrakugyo*) that bodhisattvas should cultivate when teaching the Lotus Sutra — conduct, speech, thought, and vow. Viewed through a comparative lens, this chapter's themes resonate with motifs found across the world's religious and philosophical traditions.

The chapter's soteriological claims — the universality of the path, the cosmic scope of the Buddha's compassion, the use of provisional teachings to guide beings toward ultimate truth — find parallels in the Christian concept of divine accommodation (the idea that God adjusts revelation to human capacity), the Quranic teaching that God sends messengers to every nation, and the Hindu understanding of *avatara* (divine descent adapted to the needs of the age).

The literary and rhetorical strategies employed in this chapter — parable, prophecy, cosmic spectacle, and devotional exhortation — are shared features of scriptural traditions worldwide. Comparative analysis illuminates both the universal patterns of religious literature and the distinctive features of the Lotus Sutra's vision within the broader landscape of world scripture.
