The second tablet contains the tale's most dramatic scene: the confrontation between Anat and Aqhat. The literary technique is dialogue — offer, refusal, escalation — and the scene derives its power from the asymmetry between the speakers: a goddess with unlimited power and a mortal with only his words. Aqhat's speech is remarkable for its tonal complexity: it combines philosophical gravity ('What does a mortal finally get?') with calculated insult ('Do not lie to me, O Maiden').

The murder scene shifts register from dialogue to action narrative. Anat's deployment of Yatpan in eagle form introduces an element of deception — the hero is not killed in open combat but by treachery from above. This literary choice emphasizes the injustice of Aqhat's death: he is not defeated but assassinated.
