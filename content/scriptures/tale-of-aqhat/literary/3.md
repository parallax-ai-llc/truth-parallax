The third tablet contains some of the finest poetry in the Ugaritic corpus. Danel's eagle hunt is described with vivid, cinematic detail: he scans the skies, identifies the birds, breaks them open one by one until he finds his son's remains. The image of a father searching through the bowels of eagles for his child's body is one of the most harrowing in ancient literature.

Paghat's transformation — from mourning daughter to disguised avenger — introduces a narrative of female agency that is remarkable for its psychological depth. She 'washes and rouges herself,' puts on a warrior's garment beneath a woman's cloak, arms herself with sword and dagger, and sets out. The literary construction of Paghat as a figure who uses the appearance of femininity to conceal the intention of violence is sophisticated and compelling.

The text's abrupt ending — breaking off as Paghat is about to act — is one of the most frustrating lacunae in ancient literature, leaving the tale's resolution to the imagination.
