The first tablet introduces Danel as a model of piety: he sits at the gate judging cases for seven days, performing rituals of incubation until El grants him a son. The birth of Aqhat fulfills the divine promise. Subsequently, the divine craftsman Kothar-wa-Khasis visits Danel and presents a magnificent bow — intended (in some scholarly reconstructions) for Danel but given to his son Aqhat. The bow's beauty and craftsmanship will attract the fatal attention of the goddess Anat.

Margalit's commentary analyzes the incubation ritual described in the text as reflecting actual Ugaritic cultic practice: the petitioner sleeps in a sacred precinct awaiting divine communication.
