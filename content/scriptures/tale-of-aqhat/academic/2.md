The second tablet narrates the central dramatic confrontation of the tale: Anat covets Aqhat's bow and offers him first silver and gold, then immortality, in exchange. Aqhat refuses both offers, delivering a famous speech: 'Do not lie to me, O Maiden — to a hero, your lies are trash. What does a mortal finally get? What does a mortal finally get? Glaze will be poured on my head, plaster on my skull. The death of all I shall die; even I shall surely die.' Anat, enraged by the refusal and the insult, arranges Aqhat's murder through her agent Yatpan, who takes the form of an eagle to strike.

Margalit and Greenstein have debated the precise meaning of Aqhat's speech: is it a philosophical acceptance of mortality or a rude dismissal of a goddess? Most scholars read it as both — wisdom expressed with offensive bluntness.
