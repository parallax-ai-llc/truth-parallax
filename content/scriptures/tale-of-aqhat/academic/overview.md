## Overview

The Tale of Aqhat (KTU 1.17–1.19) is the third of the three major narrative compositions from Ugarit, preserved on three tablets written by the scribe Ilimilku. The text has attracted particular scholarly attention for its treatment of human mortality — Aqhat's refusal of Anat's offer of immortality is one of the most discussed passages in Ugaritic literature — and for the prominent roles of both a female deity (Anat) and a human woman (Paghat) in driving the narrative.

## Key Scholarly Issues

### The Missing Ending
The third tablet breaks off before the narrative's conclusion, and no additional tablets have been identified. Scholars have proposed various endings: Aqhat's resurrection, Paghat's successful vengeance, or a tragic conclusion without restoration. The question remains unresolved.

### Aqhat's Refusal of Immortality
Aqhat's speech rejecting Anat's offer ("Do heroes not die? / Am I not destined to die like every mortal?") has been compared to the Gilgamesh tradition's treatment of mortality. Scholars debate whether the speech represents wisdom, hubris, or both.

### Danel's Identity
The name Danel (or Daniel) has attracted attention because of its appearance in Ezekiel 14:14, 20 and 28:3, where Daniel is listed alongside Noah and Job as a righteous figure. Most scholars accept a connection between the Ugaritic Danel and the biblical reference.
