The third tablet of Aqhat addresses themes that resonate across every religious tradition: the grief of a parent for a lost child, the obligation to honor the dead, and the demand for justice in the face of injustice.

- **Judaism**: The obligation of *kevura* (burial) and *shiva* (mourning) as sacred duties; the concept of *go'el ha-dam* (blood avenger)
- **Christianity**: The Pieta — Mary holding the body of her dead son; the promise that God will 'wipe every tear' (Revelation 21:4)
- **Islam**: The proper burial of the dead as a *fard kifaya* (communal obligation); the promise of divine justice on the Day of Judgment
- **Buddhism**: The Kisagotami story — a mother's grief as the gateway to understanding impermanence
- **Hinduism**: The Mahabharata's extended mourning rituals after the great battle

The Tale of Aqhat's third tablet — a father's grief, a daughter's courage, and the insistence that justice matters — speaks across four thousand years to the enduring human demand that wrong not go unanswered.
