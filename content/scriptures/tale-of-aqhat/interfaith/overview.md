## Overview

The Tale of Aqhat speaks to universal human questions that transcend any single religious tradition: What does it mean to be mortal? Can divine gifts become curses? Is human dignity preserved in the face of overwhelming divine power? Aqhat's insistence that mortality is the honest human condition resonates with philosophical and theological traditions across the world, from Ecclesiastes to the Buddha's teaching on impermanence.

## Interfaith Resonances

### The Acceptance of Mortality
Aqhat's refusal of immortality resonates with:
- **Judaism**: Ecclesiastes 3:19-20 — "humans and animals have the same breath; all go to the same place"
- **Buddhism**: The acceptance of impermanence (*anicca*) as fundamental to wisdom
- **Islam**: The Quranic emphasis on mortality — "Every soul shall taste death" (3:185)
- **Existentialism**: Heidegger's concept of *Sein-zum-Tode* (being-toward-death) as authentic existence

### Divine Justice and Human Suffering
Anat's unjust killing of Aqhat and El's inability to prevent it resonate with:
- **Judaism and Christianity**: The Book of Job's challenge to divine justice
- **Hinduism**: The problem of *karma* and unmerited suffering
- **Buddhism**: The recognition that craving (*tanha*) — even divine craving — causes suffering

### The Grieving Parent
Danel's mourning for Aqhat is one of literature's earliest portraits of parental grief, a theme that resonates with:
- **Christianity**: Mary at the foot of the cross
- **Judaism**: David's lament for Absalom (2 Samuel 18:33)
- **Islam**: Jacob's grief for Joseph (Quran 12:84)
- **Buddhism**: Kisagotami's grief for her dead child, which leads to her enlightenment

## Shared Themes

| Theme | Cross-Traditional Expression |
|-------|----------------------------|
| Mortality | The honest acceptance of death as the human condition |
| Divine caprice | The gods do not always act justly |
| Parental grief | The universality of mourning a child |
| Justice sought | Paghat's quest parallels the cry for justice across traditions |
