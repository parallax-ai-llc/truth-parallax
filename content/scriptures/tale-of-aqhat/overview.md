## Overview

The Tale of Aqhat (KTU 1.17–1.19) is one of the three major narrative compositions from ancient Ugarit, preserved on three clay tablets in Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform and dating to approximately 1400–1200 BCE. The poem tells the story of the righteous judge Danel, who receives a son (Aqhat) through divine intervention, only to lose him when the goddess Anat covets Aqhat's magnificent bow, crafted by the divine artisan Kothar-wa-Khasis. When Aqhat refuses to surrender the bow — and refuses Anat's offer of immortality — Anat arranges his murder. The final tablet narrates Danel's mourning and his daughter Paghat's quest for vengeance.

## Structure

The three tablets trace an arc from divine gift through tragic loss to the quest for justice:

1. **Tablet 1 (KTU 1.17)**: Danel's piety and childlessness, El's granting of a son, Aqhat's birth and the gift of the divine bow
2. **Tablet 2 (KTU 1.18)**: Anat's desire for the bow, her offer of immortality to Aqhat, his refusal, and his murder by Anat's agent Yatpan
3. **Tablet 3 (KTU 1.19)**: Danel's discovery of Aqhat's death, the recovery of his remains from the eagles, seven years of mourning, and Paghat's mission of vengeance

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Tale of Aqhat resonates deeply across scholarly and religious domains:

- **Academic**: A key text for Ugaritic literary analysis and understanding of Canaanite concepts of mortality
- **Comparative**: Parallels with the Greek myths of Orion and Actaeon, the biblical Elisha narratives, and the motif of divine jealousy
- **Theological**: A profound meditation on mortality, the limits of divine gifts, and human dignity in the face of divine power
- **Literary**: Notable for its psychological complexity, especially in Aqhat's defiant speech and Paghat's determined quest
- **Archaeological**: Provides evidence for Ugaritic social structure, including the roles of judge-king and avenging daughter
- **Interfaith**: The tension between human mortality and divine immortality is a universal religious theme

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mortality | Aqhat's insistence that death is the universal lot of humanity |
| Divine Gift and Loss | The bow as symbol of blessing that becomes the cause of destruction |
| Piety and Reward | Danel's righteousness and the granting (and taking) of a son |
| Female Agency | Both Anat and Paghat exercise decisive power in the narrative |
| Justice and Vengeance | Paghat's quest to avenge her brother's murder |
