Aqhat's refusal of immortality is the tale's theological center. His speech to Anat constitutes one of the most remarkable theological statements in ancient literature: mortality is not a problem to be solved but a fact to be acknowledged. 'Do not lie to me' — Aqhat accuses the goddess of offering what she cannot deliver. The theology here is anti-mythological in its realism: Aqhat refuses to participate in the divine pretense that death can be overcome.

Anat's response — arranging Aqhat's murder — raises the theological problem of divine ethics in its starkest form. A goddess kills a mortal for speaking the truth about mortality. The divine is revealed as capable of petty vengeance, and the mortal emerges as the more admirable figure. This theological reversal — the human being more honest than the god — is radical.
