The third tablet addresses the theology of death's aftermath: what obligations do the living owe the dead? Danel's recovery of Aqhat's remains from the eagles, his proper burial of his son, and his seven-year mourning period all reflect a theology of filial obligation that transcends death. The dead must be properly mourned, properly buried, and properly avenged.

Paghat's quest for vengeance introduces the theology of justice: if the gods will not punish Aqhat's unjust death, then a human must. Paghat's action is not personal revenge but the pursuit of cosmic justice in the absence of divine intervention. This theology — human responsibility for justice when the gods are silent — is both radical and resonant.
