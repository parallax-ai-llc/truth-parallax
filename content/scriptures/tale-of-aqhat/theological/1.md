The first tablet presents a theology of piety rewarded: Danel's faithful performance of ritual and his just governance are recognized by El with the gift of a son. This theology of reciprocity — human faithfulness met by divine generosity — establishes the narrative's theological baseline, against which the tragedy of Aqhat's death will be measured.

The divine bow introduces a theological paradox: the gift of the gods becomes the occasion of catastrophe. The bow, crafted by divine skill and given in divine favor, will attract divine jealousy and lead to Aqhat's murder. This paradox — that blessing can become curse — is a profound theological theme shared with the Tree of Knowledge in Genesis and the ring of the Nibelungs.
