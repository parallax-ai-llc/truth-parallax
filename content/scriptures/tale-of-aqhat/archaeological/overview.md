## Overview

The Tale of Aqhat, like the other major Ugaritic narratives, was discovered in the house of the high priest at Ras Shamra (ancient Ugarit) during the French excavations that began in 1929. The three tablets (KTU 1.17, 1.18, 1.19) share the same scribal hand (Ilimilku) and archival context as the Baal Cycle and the Legend of Keret, confirming the existence of a literary corpus within Ugarit's priestly establishment.

## Archaeological Context

### The Tablets
The three Aqhat tablets are inscribed in the standard Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform script on large multi-column tablets. The third tablet is notably damaged at the end, leaving the story's conclusion unknown. No additional tablets belonging to the composition have been identified despite decades of continued excavation.

### Material Culture and the Text
The Tale of Aqhat provides unusually specific descriptions of material objects that can be correlated with archaeological finds:
- **The Bow**: Composite bows are well attested in Late Bronze Age archaeology; the text's description of a bow crafted by the divine artisan may reflect the high value placed on specialized weapon manufacture
- **Danel's Gate**: The description of Danel sitting at the city gate to judge cases corresponds to archaeological evidence of gate complexes serving as judicial venues at Ugarit and other Levantine cities
- **Eagle Recovery**: Danel's breaking open of eagles to find Aqhat's remains reflects knowledge of raptor behavior and perhaps falconry practices

### Ugaritic Social Structure
The text illuminates Ugaritic social institutions:
- The role of the *mt rpi* (man of the Rephaim) as a designation for Danel
- Filial obligations including the duty to care for a father in old age
- The avenging role of the female family member (Paghat)

## Key Material Evidence

| Evidence | Connection to Text |
|----------|-------------------|
| Gate complexes | Danel's judicial seat at the city gate |
| Composite bows | The divine bow as a weapon of exceptional quality |
| Mortuary practices | Danel's mourning rituals and the *marzihu* feast |
| Ivory panels | Depictions of goddess figures paralleling Anat's description |
| Seal impressions | Royal and priestly authority attested in administrative texts |
