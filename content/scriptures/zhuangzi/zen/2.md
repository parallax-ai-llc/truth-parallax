The 'Discussion on Making All Things Equal' is perhaps the chapter of the Zhuangzi most closely aligned with Zen sensibility. The butterfly dream has been celebrated in Zen literature as a perfect expression of the non-dual awareness that is the fruit of meditation practice. The question 'Am I Zhuangzi dreaming I am a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming I am Zhuangzi?' dissolves the boundary between subject and object, dreaming and waking, in a manner that directly parallels the Zen experience of the collapse of the self-other distinction in deep samadhi.

Zen practitioners find in the chapter's equalization of all perspectives an expression of the insight that Zen calls 'one taste' (ichimi) — the recognition that all phenomena, without exception, are manifestations of the same empty, luminous awareness. The chapter's refusal to privilege any particular standpoint over any other mirrors the Zen master's refusal to affirm or deny any proposition, a pedagogical strategy designed to exhaust the student's reliance on conceptual thinking and open a space for direct, non-mediated perception. In both traditions, the goal is not the acquisition of a correct view but the liberation from the tyranny of views altogether.
