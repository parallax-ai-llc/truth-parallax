Zen readers of 'The Sign of Virtue Complete' find in the chapter's deformed sages a powerful expression of the Zen insight that awakening has nothing to do with external appearances or conventional qualifications. The Zen tradition is full of stories of unlikely enlightened beings — the illiterate kitchen monk, the wandering beggar, the seemingly mad hermit — whose inner realization is hidden from those who judge by surface appearances. The Zhuangzi's physically deformed sages are the precursors of these Zen figures, embodying the truth that the treasure of awakening is often found in the most unexpected places.

The chapter's teaching that genuine virtue attracts others without any effort or intention mirrors the Zen concept of 'virtue power' (kudoku), the natural radiance of an awakened being that draws seekers without advertisement or persuasion. In both the Zhuangzi and the Zen tradition, the realized person does not need to announce their attainment or actively seek followers; the quality of their being speaks for itself, creating a field of influence that transforms all who enter it. This understanding shapes the Zen tradition's characteristic suspicion of those who advertise their spiritual achievements and its reverence for the hidden sage who lives simply and without pretension.
