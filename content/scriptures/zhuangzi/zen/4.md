Zen practitioners receive 'In the World of Men' as a teaching on the practice of emptiness in daily life. The concept of 'fasting the mind' (xin zhai) directly parallels the Zen practice of shikantaza (just sitting), in which the meditator sits without goal, expectation, or object of concentration, allowing the mind to empty itself naturally. Both practices point toward a mode of awareness that is fully present yet completely unattached — responsive to whatever arises without grasping or rejection.

The parable of the useless tree resonates with the Zen tradition's characteristic reversal of conventional values. In Zen, the 'useless' person — the one who has abandoned ambition, competition, and the desire for recognition — is the one most likely to achieve genuine awakening. The useless tree, standing alone in the village square with its gnarled trunk and inedible fruit, becomes a gathering place for the community precisely because it has nothing to offer. Zen readers find in this image a perfect expression of the paradox at the heart of their practice: it is by relinquishing all desire for attainment that attainment becomes possible.
