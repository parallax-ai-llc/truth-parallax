'The Great and Venerable Teacher' speaks to Zen practitioners with particular directness because of its treatment of death, transformation, and the nature of the awakened person. The chapter's description of the True Person who faces death without anxiety parallels the Zen tradition's extensive teachings on dying well, from the practice of 'death poems' (jisei) composed by Zen masters in their final moments to the tradition's insistence that the quality of one's death reveals the quality of one's practice. In both traditions, the ability to face death with equanimity and even joy is understood as the ultimate test and confirmation of spiritual realization.

Zen readers connect the chapter's metaphysics of ceaseless transformation to the Heart Sutra's teaching that 'form is emptiness, emptiness is form.' Just as the Zhuangzi teaches that all beings are temporary configurations of the Tao's transformative energy, Zen teaches that all phenomena are empty of inherent self-nature and arise through the interdependent co-origination of causes and conditions. Both traditions find in the impermanence of all things not a cause for grief but a source of liberation: it is precisely because nothing is permanent that everything is possible, and the acceptance of impermanence opens the door to a mode of being that is infinitely responsive and creative.
