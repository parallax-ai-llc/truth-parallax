The Cook Ding passage is one of the most beloved texts in the Zen tradition, read as a perfect description of the state of mushin (no-mind) that is the goal of Zen practice. The cook's effortless mastery, his ability to carve an entire ox without dulling his blade, embodies the Zen ideal of action that flows from a mind empty of self-conscious deliberation. Like the Zen archer who hits the target without aiming, Cook Ding succeeds not by trying but by forgetting himself in the activity, allowing the body's wisdom to operate without interference from the calculating mind.

Zen readers connect the Cook Ding story to their own practice traditions, finding in the cook's mastery a model for every form of engagement with the world. The tea ceremony, calligraphy, flower arrangement, martial arts — all the 'Ways' (do) of Japanese Zen culture aim at the same state of effortless perfection that Cook Ding exemplifies. The key insight, shared by both the Zhuangzi and the Zen tradition, is that this state cannot be achieved through effort alone but requires the paradoxical combination of rigorous training and the complete abandonment of striving. Cook Ding practiced for nineteen years before transcending technique; the Zen practitioner sits for years before the mind's chatter subsides.
