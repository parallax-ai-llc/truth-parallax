Zen practitioners read 'Free and Easy Wandering' as a direct expression of the liberated consciousness that Zen calls satori or kensho. The Peng bird's vast flight is not a metaphor to be analyzed but an image to be entered, a doorway into the boundless awareness that underlies the apparently limited self. In Zen terms, the chapter describes the transition from the 'small self' (the quail) to the 'big self' (the Peng bird) and ultimately to 'no-self' (the sage who 'has no self'), a progression that mirrors the stages of Zen awakening.

The chapter's humor and absurdist imagery resonate deeply with the Zen tradition's own use of the ridiculous and the shocking to dislodge habitual patterns of thought. The idea that a fish can transform into a bird that darkens the sky — an image that defies all common sense — functions in a manner similar to the Zen koan: it presents the rational mind with something it cannot process, creating an opening for a mode of awareness that transcends logical categories. Zen readers receive chapter 1 not as an argument to be understood but as a provocation to be experienced.
