Zen practitioners read 'Fit for Emperors and Kings' as a teaching on the paradox of spiritual authority. The Zen roshi, like the Zhuangzi's ideal ruler, leads not by imposing a doctrine but by embodying the practice, creating conditions for awakening without prescribing the form it must take. The best Zen teaching, like the best governance in the Zhuangzi's vision, is invisible: the student awakens and feels that the insight arose from within rather than being imposed from without.

The Hundun parable holds special significance for Zen practitioners as a warning against the tendency to over-analyze, over-systematize, and over-regulate spiritual practice. The boring of holes in Hundun's face — giving it the capacity to see, hear, and eat — parallels the Zen critique of the discriminating consciousness (vijnana) that carves reality into separate categories and loses sight of the undivided wholeness that precedes all distinction. Zen practice aims to recover this primordial awareness, not by destroying the senses but by seeing through the illusion that the differentiated world of sense experience is all there is. In the Zen reading, Hundun's death is not the end of the story but a call to practice: to recover, through meditation and right living, the wholeness that was lost when consciousness first divided the world into self and other.
