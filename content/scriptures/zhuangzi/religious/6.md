'The Great and Venerable Teacher' holds profound significance for religious Taoism as a text about the relationship between the practitioner and the Tao as the ultimate source of spiritual authority and transformation. The chapter's description of the 'True Person' (zhen ren) became a foundational concept in religious Taoist anthropology, describing the fully realized human being who has transcended the ordinary limitations of embodied existence. The True Person breathes from the heels, does not dream, and faces death without anxiety — attributes understood as the fruits of sustained spiritual cultivation.

The chapter's teaching on death as transformation rather than extinction is central to religious Taoism's understanding of immortality. The realized practitioner does not 'die' in the ordinary sense but undergoes a transformation (hua) in which the spirit, having been refined through inner alchemical practice, separates from the physical body and continues its existence in a purified form. The chapter's dialogues in which characters greet the approach of death with equanimity and even celebration are read as descriptions of the state achieved by the accomplished adept who has fully prepared for this final and supreme transformation.
