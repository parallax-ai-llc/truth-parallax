The story of Cook Ding is revered in religious Taoism as a parable of the spiritual adept who has achieved perfect mastery through years of disciplined cultivation. The cook's ability to carve an ox without dulling his blade for nineteen years is understood as a metaphor for the practitioner who navigates the challenges of life without depleting his vital energy (qi), preserving the essence (jing) and spirit (shen) that are the raw materials of inner alchemical transformation. The 'spaces between the joints' through which the knife passes correspond to the subtle channels (meridians) through which qi flows in the human body.

Religious Taoist communities draw practical guidance from chapter 3's teaching on 'caring for life' (yang sheng), understanding it as an endorsement of the longevity practices — dietary regimen, breath cultivation, movement arts, and sexual hygiene — that constitute an essential dimension of Taoist religious life. The chapter's emphasis on finding the natural way through apparent obstacles is applied to the practitioner's encounter with physical illness, emotional turbulence, and spiritual crisis, teaching that these challenges, like the ox's joints, can be navigated without resistance if one has cultivated sufficient sensitivity and skill.
