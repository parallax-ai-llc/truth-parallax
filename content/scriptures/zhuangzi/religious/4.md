Religious Taoists find in 'In the World of Men' practical wisdom for the spiritual practitioner who must live in a world that does not share or understand their spiritual commitments. The chapter's teaching on the 'fasting of the mind' (xin zhai) is received as a foundational instruction in Taoist meditation: the emptying of the mind of all conceptual content to create a space in which the Tao's presence can be directly experienced. This practice of mental fasting is incorporated into the preparatory disciplines observed before major religious ceremonies and retreats.

The parable of the useless tree, which survives by producing no valuable timber, is read in religious Taoism as counsel for the practitioner to cultivate a kind of spiritual invisibility, pursuing inner cultivation without seeking worldly recognition or power. This teaching shapes the monastic ideal of the Quanzhen tradition, in which monks and nuns withdraw from worldly engagement to devote themselves entirely to spiritual practice. The 'useless' practitioner, like the useless tree, achieves a fullness of life that those consumed by worldly ambition can never know.
