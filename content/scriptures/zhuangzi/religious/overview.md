## Overview

Religious Taoism receives the Zhuangzi as a sacred text within the Taoist canon (Daozang), reading its fantastic narratives and philosophical arguments as teachings about spiritual cultivation, transcendence, and the attainment of immortality. The text's vivid descriptions of spirit journeys, bodily transformations, and encounters with divine teachers are understood not as literary inventions but as accounts of genuine spiritual experiences accessible to the dedicated practitioner.

## Spiritual Cultivation

Religious Taoist commentators interpret the Zhuangzi's parables as coded instructions for meditation and inner alchemical practice. The famous story of Cook Ding (chapter 3), who carves an ox with supernatural skill, is read as a description of the practitioner who has achieved perfect alignment between intention and action through years of disciplined cultivation. The spirit journeys described in chapter 1 are understood as accounts of the adept's ability to travel beyond the physical body through advanced meditation techniques.

## Transformation and Immortality

The Zhuangzi's central theme of transformation (hua) is read by religious Taoists as pointing toward the ultimate transformation: the transcendence of death through spiritual realization. Zhuangzi's equanimity in the face of his wife's death and his own approaching mortality is understood not as philosophical resignation but as the confidence of one who has realized the deathless nature of the spirit through direct spiritual experience.
