Religious Taoists receive 'Free and Easy Wandering' as a revelation of the possibilities that await the accomplished spiritual practitioner. The Peng bird's cosmic flight is understood not merely as a metaphor for philosophical freedom but as a description of the spirit journey (shenyou) that advanced meditators undertake, traveling beyond the confines of the physical body to explore the celestial realms. The 'listless immortal' of Mount Guye, who 'rides the clouds and mounts a flying dragon,' is venerated as a model of the Taoist transcendent (xian) who has achieved spiritual liberation.

In the ritual and devotional context of religious Taoism, chapter 1's imagery of vast distances, cosmic beings, and transcendent flight shapes visualization practices in which the practitioner mentally ascends through the heavenly bureaucracy to petition the celestial officials on behalf of the community. The chapter's teaching that ordinary creatures cannot comprehend the Peng bird's perspective is read as an affirmation that spiritual realization opens dimensions of experience invisible to the uninitiated, validating the practitioner's commitment to the demanding disciplines of Taoist cultivation.
