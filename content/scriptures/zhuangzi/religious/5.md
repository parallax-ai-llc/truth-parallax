Religious Taoists read 'The Sign of Virtue Complete' as a teaching about the transformative power of genuine spiritual attainment. The physically deformed characters whose inner virtue draws others to them are understood as models of the realized Taoist, whose spiritual radiance transcends external appearances and circumstances. In the hagiographical tradition of religious Taoism, many saints and immortals are depicted as having unusual or even repulsive physical features, a convention that reflects the Zhuangzi's teaching that true virtue is an inner quality independent of bodily form.

The chapter's emphasis on the independence of virtue from physical condition has practical significance for religious Taoist communities, informing attitudes toward illness, aging, and disability. While religious Taoism values health and longevity as conditions favorable to spiritual cultivation, the Zhuangzi's teaching prevents the tradition from reducing spiritual worth to physical vitality. The practitioner who maintains inner equanimity and spiritual radiance in the face of physical decline embodies the chapter's teaching that 'the sign of virtue complete' is a quality of the spirit, not the body.
