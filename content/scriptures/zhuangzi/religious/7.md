'Fit for Emperors and Kings' is read in religious Taoism as a teaching about the ideal relationship between spiritual authority and worldly governance. Religious Taoism has a long history of engagement with political power, from the Celestial Masters' theocratic communities to the imperial patronage of the Tang and Song dynasties. The chapter's insistence that the best ruler governs without imposing his will shapes the Taoist priesthood's self-understanding as mediators between the cosmic order and the human community, channeling the Tao's transformative power rather than exercising personal authority.

The concluding parable of Hundun, whose death results from the well-intentioned imposition of sensory openings, carries special weight in religious Taoism as a warning against the destructive effects of excessive analysis and categorization on spiritual life. Religious Taoist practice aims to reverse the 'boring of holes' — the fragmentation of consciousness into separate sensory and cognitive channels — and return to the primordial unity of Hundun. This return is understood not as a regression to unconsciousness but as an ascent to a higher mode of awareness in which the distinctions that structure ordinary experience are transcended without being destroyed.
