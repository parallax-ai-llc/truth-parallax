Comparative scholars find in 'Free and Easy Wandering' rich material for cross-cultural analysis of concepts of freedom, transcendence, and the nature of the ideal life. The chapter's vision of the sage who 'rides the wind' has been compared to the Neoplatonic concept of the soul's ascent, the Sufi tradition of fana (annihilation of the self in God), and the Romantic ideal of the unfettered imagination. In each case, the emphasis falls on liberation from the constraints of ordinary, limited experience through a transformation of perspective or consciousness.

The chapter's relativistic framework — in which the perspectives of the Peng bird and the quail are presented as equally valid within their own terms — anticipates developments in Western philosophy from Nietzsche's perspectivism to Thomas Nagel's 'view from nowhere.' Comparative scholars note, however, that the Zhuangzi's response to the problem of perspectivism differs significantly from its Western counterparts: rather than seeking a view from nowhere or embracing perspectivism as a form of will to power, the Zhuangzi advocates a radical openness that embraces all perspectives without being captured by any. This distinctive response represents the Zhuangzi's original contribution to the global philosophical conversation about the nature of knowledge and freedom.
