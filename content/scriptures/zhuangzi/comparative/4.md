Comparative scholars examine 'In the World of Men' in relation to global philosophical discussions about the ethics of survival, resistance, and accommodation in the face of political oppression. The chapter's strategy of 'uselessness' as a mode of self-preservation has been compared to the Stoic concept of withdrawal (anachōrēsis), the Epicurean retreat from public life, and the strategies of covert resistance documented by James Scott in his studies of peasant communities. Each tradition develops tactics for maintaining integrity and autonomy under conditions of domination.

The concept of 'fasting the mind' (xin zhai) has attracted comparative analysis from scholars working at the intersection of Chinese and Western phenomenology. The practice of emptying the mind of all conceptual content to achieve a state of pure receptivity parallels Husserl's phenomenological reduction, Heidegger's Gelassenheit (letting-be), and certain contemplative practices within the Christian mystical tradition. Comparative scholars note that these independently developed practices converge on a similar insight: that the ordinary, concept-laden mode of awareness obscures rather than reveals the nature of experience, and that a radical transformation of attention is required to encounter reality as it truly is.
