'The Sign of Virtue Complete' offers comparative scholars material for examining cross-cultural understandings of the relationship between body and soul, appearance and reality, disability and spiritual worth. The chapter's celebration of physically deformed sages challenges assumptions about the body that are deeply embedded in many cultural traditions, from the Greek ideal of kalokagathia (the unity of beauty and goodness) to the Hindu concept of karma as a determinant of physical form. The Zhuangzi's radical disjunction of inner virtue from outer appearance represents a distinctive philosophical position with relevance to contemporary discussions of disability, embodiment, and social inclusion.

Comparative analysis also connects the chapter's themes to contemporary philosophical discussions about the nature of personhood and the criteria for moral status. The Zhuangzi's insistence that virtue is independent of physical form raises questions that resonate with debates in bioethics about the moral status of individuals with severe cognitive or physical disabilities. While the Zhuangzi does not address these modern issues directly, its radical commitment to finding value where conventional judgment sees only deficiency provides a philosophical resource for challenging discriminatory assumptions about the relationship between bodily form and human worth.
