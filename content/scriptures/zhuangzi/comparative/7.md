Comparative analysis of 'Fit for Emperors and Kings' examines the Hundun parable in relation to global creation and destruction myths. Scholars have compared Hundun to the Greek concept of Chaos (in Hesiod's Theogony), the Babylonian Tiamat, the Hindu concept of prakriti (undifferentiated nature), and the Kabbalistic Ein Sof (the infinite without attributes). In each tradition, the undifferentiated ground of being is paradoxically both the source of all order and that which is destroyed by the imposition of order. The Hundun parable uniquely frames this destruction as the result of benevolence rather than malice, adding a distinctive moral dimension to the cosmogonic pattern.

The chapter's political philosophy of non-interference has been compared to Western libertarian and anarchist traditions, to the Gandhian concept of swaraj (self-rule), and to the principle of subsidiarity in Catholic social teaching. Comparative scholars note that while these traditions share a skepticism about centralized power and a preference for organic over imposed order, they differ significantly in their metaphysical foundations and practical prescriptions. The Zhuangzi's distinctive contribution is to ground political non-interference not in individual rights or democratic principles but in a cosmological vision of natural order that precedes and exceeds all human contrivance.
