The 'Discussion on Making All Things Equal' has become a central text in comparative philosophy, generating dialogues with Western traditions ranging from Pyrrhonian skepticism to Wittgensteinian philosophy of language. The butterfly dream has been compared to Descartes's dream argument, Nozick's experience machine, and the contemporary simulation hypothesis, with scholars noting that the Zhuangzi's response to the challenge of radical uncertainty differs profoundly from the Cartesian quest for indubitability. Where Descartes seeks a foundation beyond doubt, the Zhuangzi embraces the groundlessness of experience as itself a form of liberation.

Comparative interpreters have also explored parallels between chapter 2's equalization of all perspectives and the Madhyamaka Buddhist concept of the two truths (conventional and ultimate). Both traditions argue that ordinary discriminations between right and wrong, true and false, are valid at the conventional level but dissolve at the ultimate level. The question of whether the Zhuangzi's 'hinge of the Tao' corresponds to the Madhyamaka's ultimate truth or represents a fundamentally different philosophical move remains a productive site of cross-cultural philosophical investigation.
