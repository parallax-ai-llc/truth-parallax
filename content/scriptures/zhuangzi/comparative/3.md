Comparative analysis of 'The Secret of Caring for Life' examines Cook Ding's mastery in light of global philosophical discussions about the nature of skill, embodied knowledge, and the relationship between theory and practice. The passage has been compared to Aristotle's concept of phronesis (practical wisdom), Polanyi's theory of tacit knowledge, and Dreyfus's phenomenological account of expertise. In each case, the emphasis falls on a form of knowing that exceeds what can be stated in explicit rules and operates through an intuitive grasp of the particular situation.

The Cook Ding passage also invites comparison with the martial arts traditions of East Asia, the craft traditions of pre-industrial Europe, and the indigenous knowledge systems of various cultures worldwide. In each case, mastery is achieved not through the mechanical application of abstract principles but through extended apprenticeship, bodily engagement, and the gradual internalization of a practice's tacit norms. Comparative scholars use the Zhuangzi's account of skill as a lens for examining assumptions about the relationship between knowing and doing that are deeply embedded in Western philosophical and educational traditions.
