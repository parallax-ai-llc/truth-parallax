'The Sign of Virtue Complete' presents a gallery of physically deformed or mutilated characters whose inner virtue (de) is so powerful that it attracts followers and transforms those who encounter them. Philosophical interpreters read these stories as a radical challenge to the assumption that external appearance reflects inner reality, arguing that genuine virtue is a quality of the whole person that transcends physical form. The chapter's cripples and amputees embody a wholeness deeper than bodily integrity.

The philosophical implications of chapter 5 extend to the nature of identity itself. If virtue is independent of the body's condition, then what constitutes the essential self? The chapter suggests that personal identity is not located in any particular physical or psychological attribute but in the quality of one's relationship to the Tao. This position anticipates debates in contemporary philosophy of mind about the nature of personal identity and the relationship between consciousness and embodiment, though the Zhuangzi's approach is characteristically more playful and less systematic than its Western counterparts.
