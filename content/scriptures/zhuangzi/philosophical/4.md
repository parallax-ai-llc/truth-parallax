'In the World of Men' addresses the practical problem of how to survive and maintain one's integrity in a dangerous political environment. The chapter's central metaphor is the 'useless tree' that survives because it produces no valuable timber, while the useful trees are felled. Philosophical interpreters read this as a serious engagement with the question of how the sage can live authentically in a world that punishes authenticity, finding in the concept of 'uselessness' a paradoxical strategy for preserving one's life and freedom.

The chapter's most philosophically significant passage is the dialogue on the 'fasting of the mind' (xin zhai), in which Confucius (appearing as a character in the Zhuangzi) advises Yan Hui to empty his mind of all preconceptions before entering the court of a dangerous ruler. Philosophical commentators interpret this as a description of the state of radical receptivity that precedes effective action: by emptying oneself of fixed intentions and expectations, one becomes responsive to the situation's actual demands rather than imposing a preconceived agenda. This teaching connects the chapter's practical concerns with the text's deeper epistemological and metaphysical themes.
