'The Secret of Caring for Life' centers on the famous parable of Cook Ding, who carves an ox with supernatural skill, his knife gliding through the joints and spaces without meeting any resistance. Philosophical interpreters read this story as a paradigm for the Taoist ideal of effortless action (wu wei): the skilled practitioner who has internalized the structure of reality so thoroughly that action becomes spontaneous, responsive, and seemingly effortless. Cook Ding's skill is not the result of mere technical training but of a deep attunement to the natural patterns of things.

The philosophical significance of the Cook Ding story extends beyond the individual to the political sphere. The chapter's title, 'The Secret of Caring for Life,' suggests that the skill of navigating life's complexities is analogous to the cook's skill with the ox: both require finding the spaces and yielding points in an apparently solid and resistant reality. Philosophical commentators read the chapter as an argument that genuine skill — whether in cooking, carpentry, or statecraft — transcends the mechanical application of rules and operates from an intuitive grasp of the particular situation's unique demands.
