'Fit for Emperors and Kings' brings the Inner Chapters to a close with a meditation on the paradox of ruling without ruling. The chapter argues that the best governance arises not from the exercise of political will but from the ruler's alignment with the Tao's effortless spontaneity. The ideal ruler, like the Tao itself, accomplishes everything by doing nothing — not through passive inaction but through an active receptivity that allows natural order to emerge without coercive interference.

The chapter concludes with the parable of Hundun (Chaos), in whose undifferentiated wholeness the emperors of the North and South Sea bore seven holes — the openings of the sensory organs — so that Hundun could see, hear, and eat. On the seventh day, Hundun died. Philosophical interpreters read this as a warning against the well-intentioned but destructive imposition of order on what is better left undifferentiated. The parable suggests that the analytical, categorizing mind, for all its usefulness, can destroy the primordial wholeness from which all genuine life and creativity emerge.
