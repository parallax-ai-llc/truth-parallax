---
title: "Zhuangzi Chapter 5 — The Sign of Virtue Complete"
scripture: "zhuangzi"
chapter: 5
---

**1** In Lu there was a man named Wang Tai who had had his foot cut off. He had as many followers gathered around him as Confucius. Chang Chi asked Confucius, "This Wang Tai who has had his foot cut off — how does he get to divide up the state of Lu with you, Master, in the matter of followers?"

**2** "He does not teach with words and he does not influence with his conduct. Yet people go to him empty and come home full. Is there really such a thing as teaching without words, a way of influencing the mind without any outward form? What sort of man is this?"

**3** Confucius said, "This man is a sage. I have merely been tardy and have not yet gone to see him. And if I find him to be my teacher, how much more should those who are lesser than I. Why only the state of Lu? I shall bring the whole world to his feet."

**4** Chang Chi said, "He is a man who has had his foot chopped off, and yet people call him Master. He must be very different from the ordinary person. What is it about the way he uses his mind?"

**5** Confucius said, "Death and life are great affairs, and yet they are no change to him. Though heaven and earth flop over and fall down, it is no loss to him. He sees clearly into what has no falsehood and does not shift with things. He takes it as fate that things should change, and he holds fast to the source."

**6** "What do you mean by that?" asked Chang Chi. Confucius said, "If you look at them from the point of view of their differences, then there is liver and gall, the state of Chu and the state of Yueh. But if you look at them from the point of view of their sameness, then the ten thousand things are all one."

**7** "A man like this does not know what his ears or eyes should approve — he lets his mind play in the harmony of virtue. As for things, he sees them as one and does not see their loss. He regards the loss of a foot as a lump of earth thrown away."

**8** Duke Ai of Lu said to Confucius, "In Wei there was an ugly man named Ai Tai To. But when men were around him, they thought only of him and could not break away. When women saw him, they ran begging to their fathers and mothers, saying, 'I would rather be this gentleman's concubine than another man's wife.'"

**9** "I called him to court and saw that he was indeed ugly enough to astound the world. But before he had been with me a full month, I began to realize what sort of man he was. Before he had been with me a full year, I really trusted him. There was no prime minister in the state, so I offered him the post."

**10** "He was vague about giving me an answer, evasive, as though he would rather have declined. I was embarrassed, but in the end I turned the state over to him. In a little while he left me and went away. I felt so depressed, as though I had suffered a loss and had no one left to enjoy my state with. What kind of man is this?"

**11** Confucius said, "Virtue that is complete is something that cannot be added to. Now Ai Tai To says nothing and is trusted, accomplishes nothing and yet is sought after. He makes a man appoint him prime minister and be afraid only that he will not accept. He must be a man whose virtue is complete, though his form may not match it."
