Confucian readers of 'The Sign of Virtue Complete' confront the Zhuangzi's challenge to the assumption that external bearing reflects internal worth. The Confucian tradition places great emphasis on deportment, ritual propriety, and the cultivation of a harmonious physical presence as expressions of inner moral refinement. The chapter's parade of physically deformed sages whose virtue attracts followers despite — or because of — their appearance challenges this assumption, suggesting that genuine virtue may be invisible to the conventional gaze.

The Confucian response to this challenge has been nuanced. On one hand, Confucian thinkers recognize that the Zhuangzi is correcting a genuine tendency toward superficiality in the evaluation of moral character. Confucius himself taught that 'a man of virtue will certainly also possess eloquence, but a man of eloquence will not necessarily possess virtue,' a principle that chapter 5 extends to the domain of physical appearance. On the other hand, Confucians maintain that moral cultivation does tend to produce a kind of inner harmony that manifests outwardly, and that the Zhuangzi's extreme cases of deformed sages are deliberately provocative rather than representative.
