Confucian readers approach 'Free and Easy Wandering' with a mixture of admiration for its literary brilliance and skepticism toward its philosophical conclusions. The chapter's celebration of transcendent freedom, embodied in figures who ride the wind and subsist on dew, seems to Confucian eyes to evade the unavoidable responsibilities of social life. Freedom for the Confucian tradition is not escape from human relationships but the capacity to fulfill them with grace, sincerity, and moral imagination.

Yet thoughtful Confucian readers recognize that the chapter's critique of limited perspectives applies to Confucianism itself. The quail who mocks the Peng bird's aspirations is a figure for anyone who dismisses what lies beyond their own experience — including the Confucian who dismisses Taoist spiritual aspiration as impractical. The chapter challenges Confucian readers to expand their horizons without abandoning their commitments, a challenge that the best Confucian thinkers have accepted as a stimulus to deeper reflection on the nature and limits of moral cultivation.
