Confucian readers find 'In the World of Men' uncomfortably relevant to the political challenges they themselves face. The chapter's portrayal of intellectuals navigating the courts of dangerous rulers mirrors the experience of Confucian scholars throughout Chinese history, many of whom served tyrannical emperors at great personal risk. The Zhuangzi's advice to practice emptiness and uselessness challenges the Confucian commitment to active engagement in governance, raising the painful question of whether such engagement is always possible or desirable.

The chapter's use of Confucius as a character who advises the 'fasting of the mind' has been interpreted by Confucian readers as evidence that even the Zhuangzi acknowledges the wisdom of the Confucian tradition. In this reading, Confucius's advice to empty the mind before entering a dangerous situation is not a rejection of Confucian moral commitment but a deepening of it: the sage who has fasted the mind acts not from rigid principle but from responsive attentiveness to the unique demands of each situation. This interpretation allows Confucian readers to appropriate the chapter's insights without abandoning their own tradition's commitment to moral engagement.
