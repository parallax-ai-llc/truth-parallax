Confucian engagement with 'The Secret of Caring for Life' focuses on the Cook Ding passage, which resonates deeply with the Confucian ideal of cultivated skill and effortless moral action. The cook's mastery, achieved through years of devoted practice, parallels the Confucian understanding of how moral virtue is internalized through sustained effort until it becomes second nature. Confucius himself spoke of reaching the age of seventy, when he could 'follow his heart's desire without overstepping the line' — a state of spontaneous moral action strikingly similar to Cook Ding's effortless skill.

Confucian readers note, however, that the Zhuangzi's celebration of skill is deliberately disconnected from moral categories. Cook Ding's excellence lies in his mastery of a technical craft, not in his moral character, and the chapter makes no effort to connect skillful action with ethical goodness. For the Confucian tradition, this disconnection is problematic: genuine skill in living cannot be separated from moral cultivation, and the highest form of skill is the ability to act rightly in complex social situations. The Confucian reading of chapter 3 thus appropriates the text's insights into spontaneity and skill while insisting that these must be grounded in moral purpose.
