'The Great and Venerable Teacher' elicits complex responses from Confucian readers. The chapter's description of the 'True Person' who transcends ordinary human limitations and faces death with equanimity represents an ideal that is at once attractive and troubling from a Confucian perspective. The Confucian tradition values courage in the face of death, but it insists that such courage must be grounded in moral commitment rather than in metaphysical detachment. Confucius taught that 'the resolute scholar and the person of humaneness will not seek to live at the expense of humaneness; they will even sacrifice their lives to preserve humaneness.'

The chapter's treatment of death as a natural transformation challenges the Confucian emphasis on mourning rituals and filial grief as appropriate responses to loss. Confucian readers have argued that the Zhuangzi's equanimity in the face of death, while philosophically impressive, risks undermining the bonds of affection and obligation that constitute the fabric of human community. The Confucian tradition maintains that grief is not a failure of wisdom but an expression of love, and that the rituals of mourning serve essential social and psychological functions. Yet some Neo-Confucian thinkers drew on the Zhuangzi's teaching to develop a more serene attitude toward death within the Confucian framework.
