Confucian readers of 'Fit for Emperors and Kings' engage the chapter's political philosophy as both a challenge and a complement to their own tradition's understanding of governance. The Zhuangzi's vision of the ruler who governs without governing shares with Confucianism the conviction that political legitimacy rests on moral authority rather than coercive power. However, where Confucianism advocates the active cultivation of virtue through education, ritual, and institutional reform, the Zhuangzi suggests that the best governance is the least governance.

The parable of Hundun resonates with Confucian concerns about the unintended consequences of well-intentioned reform. Confucian history includes numerous examples of rulers whose aggressive pursuit of moral improvement produced results opposite to those intended, destroying the organic social bonds that sustained community life. The Zhuangzi's warning that imposing order on what is better left undifferentiated serves as a valuable check on the Confucian reformist impulse, reminding Confucian statesmen that the art of governance requires knowing when to act and when to refrain from action.
