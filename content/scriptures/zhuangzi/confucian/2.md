The 'Discussion on Making All Things Equal' presents perhaps the most direct philosophical challenge to Confucian thought in the entire Zhuangzi. The chapter's argument that all judgments of right and wrong are perspectival and cannot claim objective validity strikes at the foundation of the Confucian moral project, which depends on the possibility of distinguishing between genuine virtue and its counterfeits, between righteous and unrighteous conduct. Confucian readers have responded by arguing that the Zhuangzi's relativism is self-defeating: if all perspectives are equal, then the perspective that they are equal cannot claim privileged status.

Despite this critique, Confucian thinkers have found in chapter 2 valuable resources for self-reflection. The chapter's insistence that judgments are shaped by the judge's particular standpoint serves as a corrective against the danger of moral absolutism and self-righteousness. The Confucian tradition's own emphasis on self-examination (zixing) and the rectification of the mind requires precisely the kind of perspectival awareness that the Zhuangzi cultivates. The most generous Confucian reading of the chapter treats it not as a refutation of morality but as a reminder that genuine moral insight requires humility, flexibility, and openness to perspectives other than one's own.
