Academic interpreters analyze 'The Sign of Virtue Complete' for its philosophical treatment of embodiment, identity, and the nature of virtue. Scholars note the chapter's systematic use of physical deformity as a device for separating inner virtue from external appearance, a technique that raises important questions about the text's understanding of the relationship between body and spirit. Some scholars read the chapter as advocating a dualistic separation of inner worth from outer form, while others argue that the point is precisely to undermine the distinction between inner and outer.

Comparative analysis connects chapter 5's themes to broader Warring States debates about the nature of virtue and the criteria for political leadership. The chapter's physically deformed sages challenge the Confucian assumption that moral cultivation produces a harmonious physical bearing, suggesting instead that genuine virtue may be invisible to conventional observation. Academic scholars have read this as a critique of the meritocratic ideology that was becoming increasingly important in Warring States political thought, arguing that the Zhuangzi questions the very possibility of reliably identifying and rewarding moral worth.
