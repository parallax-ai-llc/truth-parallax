Academic scholarship on 'In the World of Men' examines the chapter's engagement with the political realities of the Warring States period. Scholars read the chapter's parables of dangerous missions to tyrannical rulers as reflecting the genuine existential challenges faced by intellectuals in an era of political violence and arbitrary power. The chapter's advice — to practice 'uselessness,' to fast the mind, to empty oneself of fixed intentions — is analyzed as a survival strategy that is simultaneously a philosophical practice.

Textual analysis reveals that chapter 4 incorporates material from multiple sources, including dialogues that may have circulated independently before being incorporated into the Zhuangzi. The chapter's extended use of Confucius as a character raises questions about the text's relationship to the Confucian tradition: is Confucius being satirized, rehabilitated, or used as a convenient mouthpiece for Taoist ideas? Academic scholars have argued all three positions, and the ambiguity itself is taken as evidence of the text's deliberate resistance to univocal interpretation.
