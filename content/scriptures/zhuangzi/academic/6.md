'The Great and Venerable Teacher' has been extensively analyzed by academic scholars for its treatment of death, transformation, and the nature of the ideal person. The chapter's description of the 'True Person' (zhen ren) provides important evidence for understanding how the Zhuangzi's religious and philosophical dimensions interact: the True Person's extraordinary attributes straddle the boundary between philosophical ideal and spiritual attainment, between metaphor and literal description. Scholars debate where to draw this line.

The chapter's extended dialogue on the acceptance of death — in which characters respond to their own impending mortality with equanimity and even humor — has attracted attention from scholars working in philosophical thanatology and existential philosophy. Academic interpreters compare the Zhuangzi's treatment of death with that of Epicurus, the Stoics, and Heidegger, noting both parallels and significant differences. The Zhuangzi's approach, rooted in the metaphysics of ceaseless transformation, offers a distinctive alternative to both the Western philosophical tradition's emphasis on the individual's confrontation with mortality and the religious traditions' promises of personal survival.
