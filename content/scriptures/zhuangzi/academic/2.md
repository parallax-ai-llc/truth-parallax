The 'Discussion on Making All Things Equal' has generated more academic commentary than any other chapter of the Zhuangzi, attracting the attention of sinologists, philosophers, and literary theorists worldwide. A.C. Graham's pioneering work rearranged sections of the chapter on the grounds that textual corruption had disrupted the original argument, a move that remains controversial but illustrates the challenges of interpreting a text transmitted over more than two millennia. Chad Hansen's linguistic interpretation reads the chapter as an argument about the indeterminacy of word-world relations.

Recent academic work has focused on the chapter's sophisticated use of meta-level argumentation: the text not only makes claims about the relativity of judgments but enacts this relativity through its own shifting perspectives, unreliable narrators, and self-undermining conclusions. Scholars have compared this technique to the literary strategies of Borges, Calvino, and other postmodern writers, while noting that the Zhuangzi anticipates these developments by more than two thousand years. The butterfly dream passage has been subjected to formal logical analysis, phenomenological interpretation, and deconstructive reading, each approach revealing different dimensions of the text's meaning.
