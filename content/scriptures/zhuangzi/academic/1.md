Academic scholarship on 'Free and Easy Wandering' examines the chapter's literary structure, philosophical arguments, and historical context. Scholars note the chapter's careful construction, progressing from the cosmic scale of the Peng bird through increasingly modest examples to the figure of the sage who 'has no self.' This rhetorical structure is analyzed as a deliberate strategy for leading the reader from wonder to philosophical insight, a technique that distinguishes the Zhuangzi from the more aphoristic style of the Tao Te Ching.

Textual scholars have identified possible interpolations and editorial additions within the chapter, noting shifts in style and vocabulary that may indicate the work of later hands. The relationship between chapter 1 and parallel passages in the Outer Chapters (particularly chapter 17, 'Autumn Floods') provides evidence for the process by which the Zhuangzi school elaborated and extended the master's teachings. Comparative analysis with other Warring States texts reveals shared concerns with the nature of freedom and the critique of conventional values, situating the chapter within a broader intellectual milieu.
