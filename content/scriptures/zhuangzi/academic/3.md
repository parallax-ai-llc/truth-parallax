Academic analysis of 'The Secret of Caring for Life' focuses on the Cook Ding passage as a key text for understanding the Zhuangzi's philosophy of skill and its relationship to the concept of wu wei. Scholars debate whether the passage advocates a specific method of self-cultivation or presents an ideal that is, by its very nature, incapable of being taught through explicit instruction. The tension between the communicability and the incommunicability of skill connects the chapter to contemporary debates in philosophy of mind about tacit knowledge and the limits of propositional representation.

Historical analysis situates the chapter's concern with 'caring for life' (yang sheng) within the broader context of Warring States interest in health, longevity, and self-preservation. Scholars have connected the text's teachings to excavated medical and self-cultivation manuscripts from Mawangdui and Zhangjiashan, revealing a shared vocabulary and conceptual framework that links philosophical Taoism to practical traditions of bodily cultivation. This connection complicates the traditional distinction between philosophical and religious Taoism, suggesting that the two dimensions were intertwined from the earliest period.
