## Balaam's First Two Oracles

Numbers 23 records the first two of Balaam's oracles — both of which, to Balak's fury, bless Israel instead of cursing them. Balaam ascends to high places, offers sacrifices, and receives the word of the LORD, which he faithfully transmits. Catholic tradition reads these oracles as powerful testimonies to God's irrevocable election of His people and to the impossibility of overturning divine blessing.

### The First Oracle (vv. 7–10)

From Bamoth-baal, Balaam surveys Israel's camp and declares: "How can I curse whom God has not cursed? How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?" (v. 8). He describes Israel as "a people dwelling alone, and not counting itself among the nations" (v. 9). Catholic ecclesiology hears in these words a description of the Church's distinctive identity — a people set apart, holy, not reducible to any earthly category. The Second Vatican Council teaches that the Church is "a kind of sacrament or instrumental sign of intimate union with God and of the unity of all humanity" (*Lumen Gentium* 1), and Balaam's first oracle witnesses to this separateness.

### The Death of the Righteous

Balaam's first oracle concludes with the wish: "Let me die the death of the righteous, and let my end be like his!" (v. 10). Catholic eschatology places great emphasis on the manner of one's death. The Church prays for the grace of a holy death and teaches that the final moments of life are decisive for eternal destiny (CCC 1014). The irony of Balaam's prayer is that he desires the reward of the righteous without embracing the life of righteousness — a warning against presumption that Catholic moral theology takes seriously.

### The Second Oracle (vv. 18–24)

From the top of Pisgah, Balaam delivers his second oracle, containing the theologically crucial declaration: "God is not man, that he should lie, or a son of man, that he should change his mind. Has he said, and will he not do it? Or has he spoken, and will he not fulfill it?" (v. 19). Catholic theology of divine immutability and faithfulness draws heavily on this verse. The Catechism teaches that God is "the faithful God who keeps covenant" (CCC 207), and Balaam's oracle articulates this attribute with unsurpassed clarity.

### No Misfortune in Israel

Verse 21 declares: "He has not beheld misfortune in Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in Israel. The LORD their God is with them, and the shout of a king is among them." Catholic theology interprets this not as a denial of Israel's sins (which are amply documented in Numbers itself) but as a declaration that God's elective grace sees beyond human failure. The Catholic doctrine of justification holds that God imputes righteousness to those who are in covenant relationship with Him, not because they are sinless but because His grace covers their sin (CCC 1987–1995).

### Balak's Frustration

After each oracle, Balak reproaches Balaam and moves him to a different location, hoping a change of vantage point will yield a different result (vv. 13, 27). Catholic commentators observe the futility of this strategy: God's word is not contingent on geography or circumstance. The truth of divine revelation cannot be manipulated by changing the conditions under which it is received. The Church's teaching authority (*Magisterium*) exists precisely to preserve this objectivity — to ensure that the faith once delivered is not distorted by shifting human perspectives (CCC 890).

### The Sacrifices

Before each oracle, Balaam instructs Balak to build seven altars and offer seven bulls and seven rams (vv. 1–2, 14–15). The number seven, signifying completeness, appears frequently in Catholic liturgy and theology. But the chapter makes clear that the sacrifices do not compel God to speak favorably. Catholic teaching on prayer and sacrifice insists that worship does not manipulate God but disposes the worshiper to receive what God freely gives (CCC 2735).

### Theological Significance

Numbers 23 proclaims that God's blessing is irrevocable, that His character is utterly reliable, and that no human scheme can overturn His purposes. Catholic theology of grace — especially the doctrine of perseverance and the indefectibility of the Church — rests on precisely these convictions. The Balaam oracles, spoken by a pagan diviner against his own interests, demonstrate that God's truth breaks through every human barrier, using even the most unlikely voices to proclaim His faithfulness.
