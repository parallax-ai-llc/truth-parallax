## Zelophehad's Daughters and Joshua's Commission

Numbers 27 contains two pivotal episodes: the successful petition of Zelophehad's five daughters for a share in the land inheritance (vv. 1–11), and the commissioning of Joshua as Moses' successor (vv. 12–23). Catholic tradition reads both events as anticipations of fundamental Gospel principles — the inclusion of the marginalized and the transmission of authority through divine appointment.

### The Daughters of Zelophehad

The five daughters — Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah — approach Moses with an unprecedented legal petition. Their father died in the wilderness without sons, and they argue that his name should not be lost simply because he had no male heirs (vv. 3–4). Moses brings the case before the LORD, who rules in the daughters' favor: "The daughters of Zelophehad are right. You shall give them possession of an inheritance among their father's brothers" (v. 7). Catholic social teaching emphasizes the equal dignity of women and men, both created in the image of God (CCC 2334). This episode, extraordinary in its ancient Near Eastern context, demonstrates that God's justice transcends the limitations of human custom.

### A Precedent for Justice

The LORD establishes the daughters' case as legal precedent, extending the principle to cover future situations of inheritance (vv. 8–11). Catholic jurisprudence, rooted in natural law, affirms that positive law must conform to the demands of justice. The Catechism teaches that "every institution is inspired, at least implicitly, by a vision of man and his destiny, from which it derives the point of reference for its judgment" (CCC 2244). The divine ruling in favor of Zelophehad's daughters exemplifies the principle that law must serve persons, not the reverse.

### Moses on the Mountain

The LORD tells Moses to ascend Mount Abarim to view the Promised Land, and then to prepare for his death (vv. 12–14). The sight of the land without entry into it is both a mercy and a judgment. Catholic spiritual writers have meditated on this scene as an image of the Christian life: the pilgrim is given glimpses of the heavenly homeland — in prayer, in the sacraments, in the beauty of creation — while still awaiting the definitive entry. The Catechism teaches that "in hope we are saved" (Rom 8:24; CCC 1817), and Moses' viewing of the land embodies this eschatological hope.

### Moses' Pastoral Concern

Rather than pleading for himself, Moses asks God to appoint a successor "that the congregation of the LORD may not be as sheep that have no shepherd" (v. 17). Catholic ecclesiology draws on this image extensively. Christ is the Good Shepherd (Jn 10:11), and the bishops and priests who serve the Church participate in His pastoral ministry. The Catechism teaches that "the ordained ministry is a service to the People of God" (CCC 876), and Moses' selfless concern for the flock's future care is a model for every pastor.

### The Commissioning of Joshua

God commands Moses to take Joshua, "a man in whom is the Spirit," and to lay his hands on him before the entire assembly (vv. 18–20). Some of Moses' authority is transferred to Joshua, and Eleazar the priest is to discern the LORD's will on Joshua's behalf through the Urim and Thummim (v. 21). Catholic theology of apostolic succession is deeply rooted in this passage. The laying on of hands as the essential rite of ordination (CCC 1573), the public character of the commissioning, and the complementary roles of priestly and governing authority — all are visible in this scene.

### The Shared Nature of Authority

Moses is told to invest Joshua with "some of your authority" (v. 20) — not all. The complete fullness of Moses' role passes to no single successor. Catholic ecclesiology recognizes a similar principle: no individual bishop possesses the fullness of apostolic authority in isolation. It is the college of bishops, in communion with the successor of Peter, that exercises the fullness of pastoral governance (CCC 880–883).

### Theological Significance

Numbers 27 teaches that God's justice reaches beyond human conventions, that leadership must be transmitted in an ordered and public manner, and that the welfare of the community takes precedence over the prestige of the individual leader. Catholic ecclesiology, social teaching, and sacramental theology all draw sustenance from this chapter's vision of justice, succession, and pastoral care.
