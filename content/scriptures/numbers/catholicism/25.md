## Sin at Peor

Numbers 25 records one of Israel's gravest moral and spiritual failures: while camped at Shittim, the men of Israel engage in sexual immorality with Moabite women and are drawn into the worship of Baal of Peor. A plague kills 24,000 before the priest Phinehas, Aaron's grandson, halts the disaster through an act of decisive zeal. Catholic tradition reads this chapter as a solemn warning about the connection between sexual sin and idolatry, and about the priestly vocation to defend the holiness of God's people.

### The Sin of Idolatry and Immorality

The chapter links sexual immorality and idol worship inseparably: the people "began to whore with the daughters of Moab," and these women "invited the people to the sacrifices of their gods" (vv. 1–2). Catholic moral theology has always recognized the intimate connection between sins against the sixth commandment and sins against the first. The Catechism teaches that lust "darkens the mind" and can lead to the abandonment of the true God (cf. CCC 2351–2359). The episode at Peor dramatizes how quickly sexual compromise leads to spiritual apostasy.

### The Plague and Divine Anger

God commands Moses to execute the ringleaders (v. 4), and a plague breaks out among the people (v. 9). Catholic theology affirms that divine punishment is not arbitrary but proportionate to the gravity of the sin. The worship of false gods constitutes a direct violation of the first commandment, and the Catechism identifies idolatry as "a perversion of man's innate religious sense" (CCC 2114). The severity of God's response at Peor reflects the seriousness with which the covenant treats infidelity.

### Phinehas' Zeal

In the midst of the crisis, an Israelite man brazenly brings a Midianite woman into the camp. Phinehas, seizing a spear, follows them into the tent and pierces them both (vv. 6–8). Catholic tradition regards Phinehas as a type of priestly zeal for the holiness of God. Psalm 106:30–31 declares that Phinehas' act "was counted to him as righteousness." While Catholic moral theology does not endorse vigilante violence, it does affirm that pastoral leadership sometimes requires courageous and decisive action to protect the community from spiritual danger. The Catechism speaks of the duty of pastors to "watch over and correct" those entrusted to their care (cf. CCC 2032).

### The Covenant of Peace

God rewards Phinehas with a "covenant of peace" and "a covenant of a perpetual priesthood" (vv. 12–13). Catholic theology sees in this covenant a confirmation of the priestly office as rooted in zeal for God's honor. The perpetual priesthood promised to Phinehas prefigures the eternal priesthood of Christ, who is "a priest forever, after the order of Melchizedek" (Heb 7:17). The priestly vocation, Catholic teaching affirms, is sustained not by human merit but by divine covenant.

### Balaam's Counsel

Later texts (Num 31:16; Rev 2:14) reveal that Balaam counseled the Moabites to use sexual seduction as a weapon against Israel. In the letter to the church at Pergamum, Christ warns against those "who hold the teaching of Balaam, who taught Balak to put a stumbling block before the sons of Israel" (Rev 2:14). Catholic pastoral theology draws from this the warning that the greatest threats to the Church often come not from external attack but from internal corruption and accommodation to the surrounding culture.

### Theological Significance

Numbers 25 presents the inseparability of worship and morality, the devastating consequences of syncretism, and the priestly responsibility to defend the covenant at all costs. Catholic theology holds that the Church must maintain both doctrinal purity and moral integrity, that compromise on either front leads to spiritual death. The Catechism's treatment of the first and sixth commandments together reflects the same conviction that animated the narrative at Peor: fidelity to God and fidelity in the body are two aspects of a single covenantal commitment.
