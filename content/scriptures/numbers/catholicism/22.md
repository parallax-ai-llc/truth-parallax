## Balaam and the Donkey

Numbers 22 introduces one of the most extraordinary narratives in the Pentateuch: the story of Balaam, a non-Israelite diviner summoned by King Balak of Moab to curse Israel. The chapter recounts Balak's repeated embassies, God's instructions to Balaam, and the famous episode of Balaam's donkey, who sees the angel of the LORD blocking the road when Balaam himself cannot. Catholic tradition reads this chapter as a powerful demonstration of divine sovereignty over human schemes and a lesson in the priority of obedience to God's word.

### Balak's Fear and Balaam's Reputation

Balak, terrified by Israel's victories over Sihon and Og, sends messengers to Balaam at Pethor, requesting that he curse Israel (vv. 1–6). Balaam is evidently a famous diviner whose words are believed to be efficacious. Catholic theology acknowledges that the biblical world took seriously the power of blessing and curse, and that God chose to work through — and ultimately override — Balaam's prophetic gifts. The Catechism warns against recourse to divination and sorcery (CCC 2115–2117) while affirming that God can use any instrument, however unlikely, for His purposes.

### God's Initial Command

When Balaam inquires of God, the answer is unambiguous: "You shall not go with them. You shall not curse the people, for they are blessed" (v. 12). The divine protection over Israel is absolute. Catholic theology of grace emphasizes that God's blessing, once bestowed, cannot be revoked by human or demonic agency. The Catechism teaches that "nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus" (Rom 8:39; cf. CCC 603), and Balak's attempt to hire a curse against God's people is doomed from the start.

### Balaam's Wavering

Despite God's clear prohibition, Balaam entertains a second embassy from Balak, and God permits him to go — with the stipulation that he may speak only what God commands (vv. 20–21). Catholic moral theology observes the danger of Balaam's position: he obeys the letter of God's command while his heart is drawn by the promise of reward. The Catechism teaches that moral acts must be evaluated not only by their external conformity to the law but by the intention of the agent (CCC 1750–1756). Balaam's mixed motives will ultimately lead to his downfall.

### The Donkey and the Angel

In one of Scripture's most vivid episodes, Balaam's donkey sees the angel of the LORD standing with drawn sword and repeatedly turns aside, despite being beaten by Balaam (vv. 22–30). Finally, God opens the donkey's mouth, and she speaks: "What have I done to you, that you have struck me these three times?" (v. 28). Catholic commentators have drawn two lessons from this remarkable scene. First, God can use the humblest of creatures to reveal His will — a principle that undergirds the Catholic sacramental imagination, in which ordinary elements (water, bread, wine, oil) become vehicles of divine grace. Second, the episode warns against spiritual blindness: the professional seer cannot see what a beast of burden perceives.

### The Angel's Rebuke

When God opens Balaam's eyes, he sees the angel and falls on his face (v. 31). The angel declares that the donkey saved Balaam's life by turning aside. Catholic angelology affirms that angels are "servants and messengers of God" (CCC 329) who act as guardians and guides. The angel's intervention on the road to Moab illustrates the Catholic teaching on guardian angels, who protect those in their charge even when those persons are unaware of the danger.

### Theological Significance

Numbers 22 demonstrates that God's purposes cannot be thwarted by human opposition. Catholic theology of providence holds that God "in his almighty power can always bring good even out of evil" (CCC 311). Balak's plan to curse Israel will be transformed into a series of blessings. The chapter also warns against the spiritual danger of divided loyalty — seeking to serve God while yearning for worldly reward. The Catechism's teaching that "no one can serve two masters" (Mt 6:24; CCC 2424) finds a vivid illustration in the figure of Balaam.
