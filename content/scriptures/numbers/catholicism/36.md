## Inheritance and the Daughters of Zelophehad

Numbers concludes with a brief chapter that revisits the case of Zelophehad's daughters (first addressed in chapter 27). The tribal leaders of Manasseh raise a concern: if the daughters marry men from other tribes, their inherited land will transfer permanently to those tribes, diminishing Manasseh's allotment. The LORD rules that while the daughters may marry whom they wish, they must marry within their father's tribe to preserve the tribal inheritance. Catholic tradition reads this final chapter as a meditation on the balance between individual rights and communal obligations, and as a concluding affirmation that divine law governs all aspects of the covenant community's life.

### The Problem of Inheritance Transfer

The concern raised by Manasseh's leaders is practical and legal: the Jubilee law, which returns land to its original tribal owners every fifty years, would not apply to land transferred through marriage to another tribe (v. 4). The potential erosion of tribal territory threatens the equitable distribution established by divine command. Catholic social teaching recognizes the legitimacy of concerns about the common good that may limit individual freedom of action. The Catechism teaches that "the right to private property does not abolish the universal destination of goods" (CCC 2403) and that property rights must be balanced against the needs of the community.

### The Ruling

God's solution preserves both the daughters' right to inherit and the tribe's territorial integrity: the daughters must marry within the clan of their father's tribe (vv. 6–9). Catholic moral theology appreciates the balance struck here between competing legitimate interests. The principle of the common good — defined by the Catechism as "the sum total of social conditions which allow people, either as groups or as individuals, to reach their fulfillment more fully and more easily" (CCC 1906) — governs the ruling. Individual freedom is not absolute but exists within the framework of communal responsibility.

### The Obedience of the Daughters

The chapter records that Zelophehad's daughters obey the ruling, marrying men from the clans of Manasseh (vv. 10–12). Catholic tradition values this obedience as a model of faithful response to divine instruction. The daughters' earlier boldness in petitioning for their rights and their present obedience to the Lord's ruling together illustrate the Catholic understanding of freedom: true freedom is not the absence of constraint but the capacity to choose what is good (CCC 1731).

### The Integrity of the Tribal System

The concern for tribal territorial integrity reflects a conviction that the ordered structure of the covenant community is itself a divine gift. Catholic ecclesiology affirms a similar principle: the Church's visible structure — its dioceses, parishes, religious orders, and canonical arrangements — serves the spiritual welfare of the faithful and must be maintained with care. The Code of Canon Law exists precisely to protect the ordered life of the Church, just as the ruling of Numbers 36 protects the ordered distribution of Israel's inheritance.

### Marriage within the Covenant

The requirement that the daughters marry within their tribe anticipates the Catholic concern that marriage be contracted within the community of faith. While the Church does not prohibit marriages between Catholics and non-Catholics, it strongly encourages marriages between those who share the same faith and provides special pastoral provisions (canonical form, dispensations) for mixed marriages (CCC 1633–1637). The underlying principle is the same as in Numbers 36: the covenant community's integrity is best served when its most intimate bonds are formed within its own fellowship.

### The Book's Conclusion

Numbers ends with the formulaic statement: "These are the commandments and the rules that the LORD commanded through Moses to the people of Israel in the plains of Moab by the Jordan at Jericho" (v. 13). The book that began at Sinai concludes at the threshold of the Promised Land. Catholic liturgy and theology see in this journey from Sinai to Moab a type of the Church's own journey from Pentecost to the Parousia — from the reception of the law of the Spirit to the entrance into the eternal inheritance.

### Theological Significance

Numbers 36 teaches that justice requires attending to both individual rights and communal welfare, that obedience and initiative are not contradictory but complementary, and that the ordered structure of the covenant community is a divine gift worthy of protection. As the final chapter of Numbers, it also provides a fitting conclusion to the book's overarching narrative: a people formed by law, tested by wilderness, chastened by judgment, and sustained by grace now stand ready — in the next generation — to enter the land of promise. Catholic theology recognizes in this narrative the permanent pattern of the Church's own life: always journeying, always being formed, always being led by divine providence toward the fulfillment of God's promises.
