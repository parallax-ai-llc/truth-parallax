## The Twelve Spies Sent to Canaan

Numbers 13 narrates the sending of twelve spies — one from each tribe — to reconnoiter the land of Canaan. After forty days, they return with abundant fruit but a fearful report: the land's inhabitants are giants, and the cities are fortified. Only Caleb (and Joshua, as confirmed in chapter 14) urge the people to trust God and advance. The majority report overwhelms the community with fear.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

This chapter represents one of the most direct points of contact between Numbers and the Quran. Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:20-26) provides the Quranic version: Musa tells his people, "O my people, enter the Holy Land which Allah has assigned to you and do not turn back, for then you will become losers." The people respond, "O Musa, indeed within it is a people of tyrannical strength, and indeed, we will never enter it until they leave it."

The Quran identifies two unnamed God-fearing men who urge the people to enter — traditionally identified with Caleb and Joshua (*Yusha' ibn Nun*). These men say, "Enter upon them through the gate, for when you enter it, you will be predominant. And upon Allah rely, if you should be believers" (Al-Ma'idah 5:23). This Quranic account aligns with the basic narrative of Numbers 13-14 while condensing and focusing the theological lesson.

The ten fearful spies represent the Quranic concept of *dhann* — negative assumption about God's power and intentions. Surah Al-Hujurat (49:12) warns against excessive suspicion, and the spies' report embodies exactly this spiritual failure: they saw the obstacles but not the God who had already delivered them from Egypt.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The obligation to obey prophetic commands connects to the Islamic principle of *wujub al-ta'ah* (obligatory obedience) to prophetic authority. The spies' fearful report effectively constituted a *fatwa* against obedience — they used their observation to justify disobeying God's command. Islamic jurisprudence would classify such counsel as *fasid* (corrupt), since no human assessment can override a clear divine command.

The principle of *tawakkul* (reliance on God) — exemplified by Caleb and the unnamed men in the Quran — is not passive resignation but active trust combined with effort. The two faithful spies did not deny the military challenges; they acknowledged them while affirming that God's power supersedes all human calculations.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the Quranic account of the spies as the authoritative version of this event, noting that it preserves the essential narrative while eliminating details that may have been added or modified in the Torah's transmission. The Quran does not name the spies or describe the specific fruits they brought back, focusing instead on the theological confrontation between faith and fear.

Al-Tabari harmonizes the Quranic and biblical accounts in his *Tarikh*, using *isra'iliyyat* traditions to flesh out the Quranic narrative while maintaining the Quran's theological framework as authoritative.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir provides extensive commentary on the spy narrative, identifying Yusha' ibn Nun (Joshua) as one of the two faithful men and emphasizing that this episode set the stage for the forty-year punishment. He notes that the majority's refusal to trust God despite witnessing the miracles of the Exodus represents one of the most egregious failures of faith in prophetic history.

Sayyid Qutb, in *Fi Zilal al-Quran*, uses this narrative to warn the Muslim ummah against the same spiritual failure: allowing worldly calculations to override divine commands. He argues that the spies' fear of the Canaanites reflected a materialistic worldview incompatible with true faith — a lesson directly applicable to contemporary Muslim communities facing daunting challenges.
