## Daily Offerings and Festival Calendar

Numbers 28 prescribes the regular system of sacrificial offerings: daily morning and evening burnt offerings, additional Sabbath offerings, monthly new moon offerings, and the offerings for the spring festivals of Passover and the Feast of Weeks (Shavuot). Each occasion requires specific animals and grain offerings.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The daily morning and evening sacrifices parallel the Islamic daily prayer schedule. The five daily prayers (*salawat*) structure the Muslim's day around worship, just as the *tamid* (daily offering) structured the Israelite day. The Quran establishes prayer times: "Establish prayer at the decline of the sun until the darkness of the night and the Quran of dawn" (Al-Isra 17:78). Both systems ensure that no part of the day passes without formal acknowledgment of God.

However, the mode of worship differs fundamentally. The Israelite system centers on animal sacrifice administered by priests at a central sanctuary, while Islamic worship centers on individual and congregational prayer accessible to all believers in any clean place. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "The entire earth has been made a mosque for me" (Bukhari), democratizing worship in a way that contrasts with the centralized, priestly model.

The festival calendar in Numbers 28 invites comparison with the Islamic liturgical calendar. While Islam has fewer prescribed festivals — the two Eids (Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha) being the primary celebrations — the Islamic calendar also structures the year around commemorative worship. The month of Ramadan, Hajj season, and the day of Arafat create a rhythm of intensified devotion.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The detailed specification of offerings for each occasion connects to the Islamic *fiqh* of worship (*'ibadat*), which precisely defines the form, timing, and conditions for each act of worship. Just as Numbers 28 specifies exact numbers and types of animals for each occasion, Islamic jurisprudence specifies the exact number of *rak'at* (prayer units), their components, and their timing. Both legal systems reflect the principle that worship must follow divine prescription rather than human preference.

The concept of additional offerings for special occasions parallels the Islamic distinction between obligatory (*fard*) and supererogatory (*nafl*) worship. The daily prayers are obligatory; additional prayers — such as *tahajjud* (night prayer) and *duha* (mid-morning prayer) — are voluntary acts of devotion that bring additional reward, analogous to the Sabbath and festival supplements in Numbers 28.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the elaborate sacrificial system in Numbers 28 as representing a phase of revelation that was superseded by the Islamic model. The Quran explicitly states that "It is not their meat nor their blood that reaches Allah, but it is your piety (*taqwa*) that reaches Him" (Al-Hajj 22:37), reorienting worship from external sacrifice to internal devotion. The spirit of the offerings — regular, disciplined, sacrificial worship — is preserved in Islam, while the specific animal-sacrifice framework was replaced.

Al-Ghazali argued that all legitimate forms of worship across prophetic dispensations share common inner dimensions: sincerity, humility, gratitude, and submission. The external forms change, but these internal realities remain constant from Musa to Muhammad.

## Scholarly Reflections

Al-Tabari records that the Israelite sacrificial calendar was one of the most distinctive features of their covenant with God. Its elaborate requirements both distinguished them from surrounding nations and imposed significant communal obligations that tested their commitment.

Mohammad Hashim Kamali has compared the legal methodologies of biblical and Islamic ritual law, noting that both operate on the principle that worship details are matters of divine prescription (*tawqifi*), not human innovation. This shared legal principle — that the form of worship is fixed by revelation — underlies both Numbers 28 and the Islamic *fiqh* of *salah*.
