## Balaam's Final Oracles and Departure

Numbers 24 records Balaam's third and fourth oracles. In the third, Balaam blesses Israel with the famous words, "How lovely are your tents, O Jacob!" In the fourth, he delivers a messianic oracle: "A star shall come out of Jacob, and a scepter shall rise out of Israel." Additional short oracles against Amalek, the Kenites, and other nations follow. Balak dismisses Balaam in anger, and they part ways.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Balaam's "star" prophecy (24:17) has been interpreted across traditions as a messianic prediction. While Jewish tradition identifies the star with various figures (including Bar Kokhba) and Christians identify it with Jesus, Islamic tradition offers a distinctive perspective. Some Muslim commentators, particularly in the medieval period, suggested that prophetic oracles in the Torah anticipated the coming of Muhammad, referencing Surah Al-A'raf (7:157): "Those who follow the Messenger, the unlettered prophet, whom they find written in what they have of the Torah and the Gospel."

The portrait of Israel's blessed state in the third oracle — prosperous, numerous, and divinely favored — is consistent with the Quran's acknowledgment that Allah favored the Children of Israel: "O Children of Israel, remember My favor which I have bestowed upon you and that I preferred you over the worlds" (Al-Baqarah 2:47). However, the Quran consistently adds that this favor was conditional on obedience.

Balaam's compelled prophecy concludes in this chapter with his permanent departure. The Quranic parallel in Al-A'raf (7:175-176) — the man who "detached himself" from God's signs — suggests that Balaam's story does not end with these beautiful oracles but with spiritual decline. The contrast between inspired speech and personal corruption is the Quran's central concern.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The oracles against various nations (Amalek, Kenites, Asshur) introduce the topic of divine judgment upon nations, which connects to the Quranic concept of *'adhab al-umam* (punishment of nations). The Quran extensively describes the destruction of peoples who rejected prophetic guidance — Ad, Thamud, the people of Lut — establishing a universal principle that national persistence depends on moral conduct. Surah Al-Isra (17:16) states: "When We intend to destroy a city, We command its affluent ones, but they defiantly disobey therein; so the word comes into effect upon it, and We destroy it."

The relationship between prophecy and political power — Balaam speaking to and against kings and nations — connects to the Islamic discourse on the role of religious authority vis-a-vis political power. The prophetic voice must speak truth to power regardless of consequences, a principle embodied in the Islamic concept of *nasiha* (sincere counsel) to rulers.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars approach Balaam's oracles with particular interest because they are presented as genuine divine speech within the Torah itself — words God placed in the mouth of a non-Israelite figure. This raises the question of whether the oracles preserve authentic prophetic material, including possible allusions to future prophets. The Islamic interpretive tradition has sometimes read references to a coming figure in these oracles as pointing toward Muhammad rather than (or in addition to) an Israelite messiah.

Al-Qurtubi discusses how certain biblical passages may contain traces of the original predictions about Muhammad's coming that were subsequently reinterpreted or obscured in the transmitted text. Balaam's oracles, as prophecies attributed to a non-Israelite seer, may have been more resistant to this process of reinterpretation.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir situates Balaam's story within the larger narrative of the final stages of the wilderness period. The oracles confirm that despite forty years of wandering and the death of the rebellious generation, God's purposes for Israel remain intact — the blessings spoken through Balaam will be fulfilled through the generation that enters the land.

Abdal Hakim Murad has reflected on the paradox of Balaam: a man who speaks the most beautiful prophecies about divine blessing while personally embodying the tragedy of spiritual corruption. This paradox serves as a warning for all religious communities: eloquent theology is no guarantee of authentic faith.
