## Korah's Rebellion Against Moses and Aaron

Numbers 16 records the rebellion of Korah, Dathan, Abiram, and 250 community leaders against the authority of Moses and Aaron. Korah, a Levite, challenges the exclusive priestly authority of Aaron's family, while Dathan and Abiram challenge Moses' leadership. God vindicates Moses and Aaron: the earth opens and swallows Korah, Dathan, and Abiram alive, and fire consumes the 250 incense-bearers.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

This chapter has one of the most direct Quranic parallels: Qarun (Korah) in Surah Al-Qasas (28:76-82). The Quran identifies Qarun as a man from Musa's people who was given immense wealth: "We gave him such treasures that their keys would have been a burden to a body of strong men." When counseled to use his wealth for the Hereafter, Qarun arrogantly replied, "I was only given it because of knowledge I have" (28:78).

The Quranic account emphasizes Qarun's pride and materialism: "So he went out before his people in his adornment. Those who desired the worldly life said, 'Oh, would that we had like what was given to Qarun!'" (28:79). The punishment is identical in both accounts: "We caused the earth to swallow him and his home" (28:81). This shared narrative element — the earth opening to consume the arrogant rebel — is one of the strongest points of convergence between Numbers and the Quran.

However, the Quran shifts the emphasis from a challenge to priestly authority (the Numbers account) to a broader lesson about the arrogance of wealth. The Quranic Qarun is primarily a symbol of *istikbar* (arrogance) and *tuqhyan* (tyranny) — spiritual diseases that wealth can produce. This reframing universalizes the lesson beyond the specific Israelite context.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Korah's challenge to established religious authority connects to the Islamic principle of respecting scholarly consensus (*ijma'*) and the authority of qualified scholars. While Islam does not have a hereditary priesthood, it does recognize the authority of qualified scholars (*ulama*) to interpret divine law. Challenging such authority without legitimate grounds is viewed as *fitnah* (sedition) — a grave sin in Islamic jurisprudence.

The Quranic Qarun narrative supports the Islamic legal principle that wealth carries obligations. The *fiqh* of *zakat* (obligatory charity) and the prohibition of *israf* (extravagance) both address the danger that Qarun represents: wealth used for self-aggrandizement rather than divine service.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars note both the convergence and divergence between the Numbers and Quranic accounts of Korah/Qarun. The convergence — particularly the earth swallowing him — is viewed as confirmation that this event actually occurred. The divergence in emphasis is understood as the Quran providing the theologically correct interpretation: the lesson is not primarily about priestly authority (a concern of the later Israelite text) but about the universal danger of arrogance and ingratitude before God.

Al-Zamakhshari (d. 1144 CE) argues in *Al-Kashshaf* that the Numbers account has been shaped by priestly editorial concerns — legitimizing Aaronic authority — while the Quran restores the original prophetic lesson about humility and accountability before God.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir devotes extensive attention to Qarun in *Qisas al-Anbiya*, describing him as one of the most learned of the Israelites who was corrupted by wealth. He serves as a warning that knowledge and wealth without *taqwa* (God-consciousness) lead to destruction. This interpretation enriches the Numbers narrative by adding the dimension of corrupted knowledge.

Muhammad Abduh used the Qarun narrative to critique the wealthy elites of his own time who neglected social responsibility. The destruction of Qarun demonstrates that no amount of wealth or influence can protect one from divine judgment — a message as relevant to modern Muslim societies as it was to ancient Israel.
