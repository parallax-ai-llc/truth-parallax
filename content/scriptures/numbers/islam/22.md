## Balaam, His Donkey, and Balak's Summons

Numbers 22 introduces Balaam son of Beor, a renowned diviner summoned by King Balak of Moab to curse Israel. God initially forbids Balaam to go, then permits him to go but only to speak what God commands. On the journey, Balaam's donkey sees an angel blocking the path and refuses to proceed; Balaam beats the donkey until God opens its mouth to speak. Finally, the angel reveals himself to Balaam and reiterates that he must speak only God's words.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The Balaam narrative is widely connected to Surah Al-A'raf (7:175-176): "And recite to them the news of him to whom We gave Our verses, but he detached himself from them; so Satan pursued him, and he became of the deviators. And if We had willed, We could have elevated him thereby, but he adhered to the earth and followed his own desire. So his example is like that of the dog: if you chase him, he pants, or if you leave him, he still pants."

Although the Quran does not name this figure, classical commentators — including Ibn Abbas, Mujahid, and Ibn Kathir — widely identify him with Bal'am ibn Ba'ura (Balaam son of Beor). The Quranic description of someone who received divine knowledge but abandoned it for worldly gain matches the Numbers portrayal precisely.

The talking donkey is not mentioned in the Quran, but miraculous animal speech appears elsewhere in Islamic tradition. Surah An-Naml (27:18-19) records the speech of ants warning each other about Sulayman's army, and Sulayman's ability to understand the language of birds. The Hadith also records instances of animals speaking. The principle that God can cause any creature to speak is well-established in Islamic theology.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Balaam's role as a diviner (*kahin*) connects to the Islamic prohibition of *kahanah* (soothsaying) and *sihr* (sorcery). The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "Whoever goes to a fortune-teller and asks him about something, his prayer will not be accepted for forty nights" (Muslim). Islamic jurisprudence categorically prohibits the practices associated with Balaam's profession, viewing them as a form of *shirk* (associating partners with God).

The principle that one who possesses knowledge of God's signs but turns away is morally culpable connects to the Islamic concept of *hujjah* (proof being established). Once divine knowledge has reached a person, the standard of accountability rises. This principle informs Islamic jurisprudence regarding *riddah* (apostasy) and the gravity of abandoning known truth.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the Quranic treatment of Balaam as providing the essential moral lesson without the narrative elaboration found in Numbers. The focus on spiritual declension — from possessing divine knowledge to following desire — is the core teaching. The specific details of the donkey, the angel, and the interaction with Balak may contain historical elements, but the Quranic condensation identifies what matters: the tragedy of squandered spiritual knowledge.

Al-Tabari records various *isra'iliyyat* traditions about Balaam, including the donkey episode, treating them as historical background for the Quranic verse. He presents these traditions as illustrative rather than authoritative, maintaining the distinction between Quranic certainty and supplementary reports.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir provides one of the most detailed Islamic accounts of Balaam, emphasizing that he was among the most knowledgeable of his time — some traditions say he possessed the "Greatest Name" of God (*ism Allah al-a'zam*) — yet his knowledge could not save him from the corruption of desire. This paradox — knowledge without salvation — is a persistent concern in Islamic spirituality.

Hamza Yusuf has used the Balaam/Bal'am narrative to warn against the danger of religious knowledge divorced from practice. The Quranic comparison to a panting dog — the same whether chased or left alone, unable to control its own state — vividly illustrates how desire can reduce even the learned to a state of spiritual helplessness.
