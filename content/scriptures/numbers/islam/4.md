## Duties of the Kohathite, Gershonite, and Merarite Clans

Numbers 4 specifies the duties of the three Levitical clans in transporting the Tabernacle. The Kohathites carry the most sacred objects (the Ark, table, lampstand, and altars), but only after Aaron and his sons have covered them. The Gershonites handle the curtains and coverings, and the Merarites carry the structural frames and posts. A second census counts Levite men aged 30-50 for service.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The reverence shown toward sacred objects in this chapter resonates with Islamic traditions regarding the Quran and the Ka'bah. Just as the Kohathites could not look upon or touch the holy objects directly, Muslims handle the Quran with ritual purity (*wudu*) and treat the Ka'bah's covering (*kiswah*) with great reverence. Surah Al-Waqi'ah (56:79) states, "None touch it except the purified."

The covering of sacred objects before transport parallels the Islamic practice of veiling the Ka'bah with the *kiswah*. Both traditions express the theological insight that the most holy things require mediation and protection, reflecting human finitude before divine holiness.

The age range of 30-50 for Levitical service invites comparison with Islamic tradition. While Islam has no equivalent age restriction for religious service, the Hadith literature emphasizes that maturity and experience qualify one for religious leadership. The Prophet (peace be upon him) appointed experienced companions to positions of authority.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The principle that sacred objects require special handling connects to the Islamic jurisprudence of *adab* (proper conduct) regarding sacred texts and spaces. The *fiqh* literature extensively discusses the proper handling of the Quran, including rules about ritual purity, respectful placement, and appropriate disposal of worn copies. These rulings reflect the same theological impulse seen in the Kohathite regulations.

The warning that improper handling of sacred objects brings death (4:15, 4:20) resonates with the Islamic concept of *hurmah* (sanctity/inviolability). Violating sacred boundaries carries severe consequences in both traditions, reflecting the seriousness with which God regards the distinction between the holy and the common.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars would note that the elaborate ritual system described in Numbers 4 reflects the Israelite priesthood's development of increasingly complex ceremonial requirements. While the original revelation to Musa likely included guidance on proper worship and reverence for sacred space, the specific details of covering and carrying procedures may represent later priestly elaboration rather than direct divine command.

Al-Biruni observed that religious communities tend to develop increasingly elaborate ritual systems over time, and that one purpose of new prophetic revelations is to restore simplicity and directness in worship. Islam's comparatively streamlined approach to sacred objects reflects this prophetic correction.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir notes that the Children of Israel were entrusted with great responsibilities regarding the Tabernacle, and that their later failures to maintain proper reverence contributed to their spiritual decline. This observation connects the Levitical regulations to the broader Quranic theme of *amanah* (trust) — the sacred responsibilities that God entrusts to communities of believers.

Fazlur Rahman, in his analysis of Quranic ethics, noted that the Quran consistently emphasizes the spirit of worship over its external forms. While Numbers 4 focuses on precise ritual procedures, the Quranic approach stresses the inner attitude of reverence and submission that should accompany all acts of worship.
