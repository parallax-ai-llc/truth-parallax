## Water from the Rock and the Death of Aaron

Numbers 20 begins with Miriam's death, followed by the community's complaint about water at Meribah. God commands Moses to speak to a rock to produce water, but Moses strikes the rock instead. For this act of disobedience, God tells Moses and Aaron they will not enter the Promised Land. The chapter also records Edom's refusal to grant passage and concludes with Aaron's death on Mount Hor, where Eleazar succeeds him.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The Quran references the miracle of water from the rock in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:60): "And when Musa prayed for water for his people, We said, 'Strike with your staff the stone.' And there gushed forth from it twelve springs." Notably, the Quranic version commands striking rather than speaking to the rock, and presents twelve springs — one for each tribe. This is one instance where the Quranic and biblical accounts differ in specific detail while sharing the core miraculous event.

The tradition that Moses was denied entry to the Promised Land because of a specific act of disobedience is present in Islamic commentary, though the Quran itself does not explain why Musa did not enter the land. Various *isra'iliyyat* traditions in Islamic sources offer different explanations, and scholars like Al-Tabari record multiple accounts without definitively endorsing one.

Harun's (Aaron's) death and the transfer of authority to his son resonates with the Islamic concept of the transition of religious leadership. While Islam does not endorse hereditary religious authority as a general principle, the Quran acknowledges that Allah chooses certain families for special roles: "Allah chose Adam and Nuh and the family of Ibrahim and the family of 'Imran over the worlds" (Al Imran 3:33).

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Moses' striking the rock rather than speaking to it raises the jurisprudential question of whether modifying the method of obedience while achieving the intended result constitutes disobedience. In Islamic *fiqh*, the concept of *kayfiyyah* (manner of performance) is significant: certain acts of worship must be performed in their prescribed manner, and deviation — even if the general purpose is achieved — may invalidate the act. This principle applies to the specific forms of *salah*, *wudu*, and Hajj rituals.

The succession from Aaron to Eleazar through investiture connects to the Islamic concept of *bay'ah* (pledge of allegiance) in leadership transition. While the mechanisms differ, both traditions recognize that legitimate authority requires formal transfer and communal acknowledgment.

## The Tahrif Perspective

The discrepancy between the Quranic command to "strike" the rock and the Numbers narrative (where striking rather than speaking constitutes the sin) is noted by Muslim scholars as an example of how the Torah text may have been altered. The Quran's version, in which striking the rock is the divinely commanded act, is taken as authoritative. The idea that Moses sinned at Meribah is viewed cautiously, as Islamic theology upholds the *'ismah* (infallibility/protection) of prophets from major sins.

Al-Razi addresses this issue by noting that even if Moses made an error (*ijtihadi*) rather than a sin, the Quranic emphasis is on the miracle itself — God's provision for His people — rather than on prophetic fallibility.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir discusses both the water miracle and Aaron's death, situating them within the broader narrative of the wilderness period. He notes that these events occurred near the end of the forty years, as the old generation was passing away and the new generation was preparing for a new chapter.

Abdulaziz Sachedina has examined how the concept of prophetic infallibility differs between Jewish and Islamic traditions, with the Meribah incident serving as a key test case. Islam's insistence on *'ismah* leads to different interpretive strategies when encountering Torah narratives that seem to attribute sin to prophets.
