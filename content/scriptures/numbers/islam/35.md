## Levitical Cities and Cities of Refuge

Numbers 35 establishes forty-eight cities for the Levites throughout Israel's territory, including six "cities of refuge" — three east and three west of the Jordan — where a person who commits accidental homicide can flee for protection from the "blood avenger" (*go'el ha-dam*). The chapter carefully distinguishes between murder (intentional killing) and manslaughter (unintentional killing), establishing criteria for each and specifying that the manslayer must remain in the city of refuge until the death of the high priest.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The cities of refuge address the fundamental tension between the right of retaliation (*qisas*) and the need to protect those who kill unintentionally. Islamic law addresses this same tension through its detailed jurisprudence of homicide. The Quran distinguishes between intentional murder — for which *qisas* (retaliatory punishment) is prescribed (Al-Baqarah 2:178) — and accidental killing — for which *diyah* (blood money) and expiation are required (An-Nisa 4:92).

The figure of the "blood avenger" in Numbers 35 parallels the Islamic concept of *wali al-dam* (the victim's legal guardian/next of kin). In both systems, the victim's family has a recognized role in the justice process. However, the Quran introduces a significant modification: "But whoever overlooks from his brother anything, then there should be a suitable follow-up and payment to him with good conduct" (Al-Baqarah 2:178), encouraging forgiveness alongside the right to retaliation.

The distinction between intentional and unintentional killing — central to Numbers 35 — is equally fundamental in Islamic criminal jurisprudence. The Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools all develop sophisticated classifications of homicide: *qatl 'amd* (intentional), *qatl shibh al-'amd* (quasi-intentional), and *qatl khata'* (accidental). These categories determine whether *qisas*, *diyah*, or *kaffarah* applies.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The cities of refuge function as an institutional protection against precipitous revenge — ensuring that the accused has access to a fair hearing before punishment is exacted. This resonates with the Islamic emphasis on due process (*qada'*). The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) established courts and appointed judges, institutionalizing the principle that justice requires deliberation, evidence, and proper authority.

The provision that the manslayer must remain in the city of refuge until the high priest's death introduces a temporal element to justice. Islamic jurisprudence does not have an exact parallel, but the concept of *'afw* (pardon) by the victim's family can resolve the situation at any time. The Islamic system's emphasis on reconciliation (*sulh*) and pardon provides an alternative mechanism for resolving the ongoing tension between the manslayer and the victim's family.

The prohibition against accepting ransom for a murderer (35:31) connects to the Islamic principle that *qisas* for intentional murder cannot be commuted to *diyah* without the victim's family's consent. Both systems recognize that certain crimes are too grave for monetary compensation alone.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the cities of refuge as an institution that addresses a genuine need — protecting the accidentally accused from mob justice — through a mechanism specific to the Israelite context. Islamic law achieves the same goal through its comprehensive criminal justice framework, which may represent a more systematized version of the underlying principle.

The linking of the manslayer's release to the high priest's death is seen as reflecting the priestly-centered theology of the Torah, which Islam replaces with a framework centered on divine law and communal adjudication rather than priestly mediation.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah extensively analyzes the jurisprudence of homicide in *I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in*, noting that the distinction between intentional and unintentional killing is one of the foundations of justice across all legal systems. The cities of refuge represent an early institutional attempt to implement this distinction — an attempt that Islamic law refined and systematized.

Wael Hallaq has compared the criminal justice frameworks of biblical and Islamic law, noting that while both start from the same basic categories (intentional versus unintentional), they develop different institutional solutions. The Islamic system's integration of retaliation, compensation, and forgiveness into a single framework represents a distinctive legal achievement.
