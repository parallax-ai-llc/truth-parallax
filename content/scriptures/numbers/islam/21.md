## The Bronze Serpent and Military Victories

Numbers 21 records several events: defeat of the Canaanite king of Arad, a plague of venomous serpents punishing further complaints (with healing through a bronze serpent on a pole), Israel's journey around Edom, and decisive victories over the Amorite kings Sihon and Og. These victories establish Israel's foothold east of the Jordan.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The bronze serpent episode — where looking at an image brings healing — is particularly interesting from an Islamic perspective because of Islam's strong prohibition against image worship. While the bronze serpent served a divinely commanded purpose in its original context, the Quran's emphatic rejection of idolatry (*shirk*) as the unforgivable sin (An-Nisa 4:48) means that Islamic commentary approaches this narrative with caution.

The biblical text itself acknowledges the danger: 2 Kings 18:4 records that the bronze serpent was eventually destroyed because people had begun worshipping it. Islamic scholars cite this as a classic example of how legitimate religious objects become idols — the very process that the Quran warns against and that Islam's iconoclastic stance is designed to prevent.

The people's complaints and the resulting serpent plague continue the Quranic theme of Israelite ingratitude. The cycle of sin, punishment, repentance, and deliverance mirrors the pattern described in multiple Quranic passages about the Bani Isra'il. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:55-56) records another instance: "You said, 'O Musa, we will never believe you until we see Allah outright,' so the thunderbolt struck you while you were looking on. Then We revived you after your death so perhaps you would be grateful."

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The military victories over Sihon and Og raise the jurisprudential topic of *jihad* (struggle in God's cause) and the Islamic laws of warfare. The Quran establishes principles for just warfare, including the prohibition of aggression (Al-Baqarah 2:190), protection of non-combatants, and the offering of peace before combat. While the Numbers account of herem (total destruction) warfare differs from Islamic rules of engagement, both traditions recognize that military action can be divinely sanctioned when undertaken in obedience to God's command.

The concept of healing through divinely appointed means connects to the Islamic *fiqh* of medicine (*fiqh al-tibb*). The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "For every disease, Allah has sent a cure" (Bukhari). Islamic jurisprudence encourages seeking medical treatment as consistent with trust in God — combining divine reliance with practical means.

## The Tahrif Perspective

The bronze serpent narrative is viewed by Muslim scholars as a cautionary tale about the trajectory from prophetic instruction to idolatrous practice. The original command may have been genuinely from God, but the Israelites' later worship of the object demonstrates the human tendency to substitute the sign for the Signified. Islam's strict prohibition of religious images represents a preventive measure against this recurring pattern.

Al-Shahrastani discusses how the Israelites' use of material objects in worship — the golden calf, the bronze serpent — reflects a persistent tendency toward *tajsim* (anthropomorphism and materialism) in their religious practice, which the Quran diagnoses and corrects.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir mentions the Israelites' military campaigns east of the Jordan as part of the narrative preparing for their eventual entry into the Promised Land under Yusha' ibn Nun. These victories demonstrate that when the new generation obeyed divine commands — unlike their parents who refused to enter the land — God granted them success.

Khaled Abou El Fadl has analyzed the comparative ethics of warfare across Abrahamic traditions, noting that while the Torah's herem concept and Islamic jihad doctrine both recognize divinely sanctioned warfare, they differ significantly in scope, limitations, and rules of engagement. The Islamic framework consistently emphasizes restraint and proportionality.
