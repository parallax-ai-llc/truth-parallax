## War Against Midian

Numbers 31 describes Israel's war of vengeance against Midian for the Peor incident. Twelve thousand Israelite soldiers defeat Midian, killing five kings and Balaam son of Beor. When Moses finds that the soldiers have spared the Midianite women — those responsible for the seduction at Peor — he commands the execution of all women who are not virgins and all male children. The chapter details the elaborate distribution of spoils.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

Numbers 31 presents one of the most challenging narratives in the Torah from an Islamic perspective. While Islam recognizes the concept of divinely sanctioned warfare (*jihad fi sabil Allah*), the Quran establishes clear rules of engagement that differ significantly from the *herem* (total destruction) warfare described here. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:190) commands, "Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress. Indeed, Allah does not like transgressors."

Islamic law of warfare (*ahkam al-jihad*) prohibits the killing of women, children, the elderly, and non-combatants. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) explicitly forbade killing women and children in battle (Bukhari and Muslim). The commands in Numbers 31 regarding the execution of women and male children stand in stark contrast to these Islamic prohibitions.

Balaam's death in battle confirms the Quranic narrative of his spiritual decline. The man described in Al-A'raf (7:175-176) as having "detached himself" from God's signs meets his end among the enemies of God's people. Ibn Kathir notes the tragic irony: Bal'am died among the very people he had counseled against Israel, having fully aligned himself with those opposed to divine purpose.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The distribution of spoils in Numbers 31 — divided between combatants and the community, with portions for the priests and Levites — parallels the Quranic legislation of *ghanimah* (spoils of war) in Surah Al-Anfal (8:41). The Quran specifies that one-fifth goes to God, the Messenger, near relatives, orphans, the needy, and travelers, with the remainder distributed among the fighters. Both systems establish divinely mandated distribution rules rather than leaving allocation to arbitrary decision.

The purification of soldiers and captured goods after battle (31:19-24) connects to the Islamic requirement that returning warriors purify themselves. The *fiqh* of *jihad* addresses the spiritual dimensions of warfare, including the need for ritual purification and the psychological care of those who have been through combat.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view Numbers 31 as a particularly clear case where the Torah narrative may reflect later editorial elaboration rather than faithful transmission of prophetic commands. The scale and nature of the violence described — particularly the targeting of women and children — is difficult to reconcile with the Quranic portrait of Musa as a mercy-oriented prophet and with the Quran's own rules of warfare. Some scholars suggest that this chapter may reflect the military traditions of a later period projected back onto the Mosaic era.

Al-Jawziyyah noted that one function of Muhammad's prophethood was to correct the excesses that had crept into earlier religious traditions, including the justification of extreme violence in divine name. The Islamic rules of warfare represent a deliberate moderation of such tendencies.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir discusses the end of Balaam alongside the fate of the Midianites, treating both as consequences of opposing divine purpose. The Midianite strategy of moral corruption — more effective than military confrontation — represents a form of warfare that both traditions condemn: the use of moral seduction to undermine a community of faith.

Khaled Abou El Fadl has analyzed the comparative ethics of warfare across Abrahamic traditions, arguing that while all three traditions contain resources for both the justification and limitation of violence, Islam's explicit rules of engagement represent a systematic attempt to constrain the violence that warfare inevitably involves.
