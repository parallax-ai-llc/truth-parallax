## The Daughters of Zelophehad and Joshua's Appointment

Numbers 27 records two significant events: the five daughters of Zelophehad petition for their father's inheritance since he died without sons, and God rules in their favor, establishing a new precedent for female inheritance. The chapter then narrates God's command that Moses commission Joshua (*Yusha' ibn Nun*) as his successor, since Moses will not enter the Promised Land.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The daughters of Zelophehad's successful petition for inheritance rights is particularly significant when compared with Islamic inheritance law. The Quran revolutionized Arabian practice by establishing specific inheritance shares for women: "For men is a share of what the parents and close relatives leave, and for women is a share of what the parents and close relatives leave" (An-Nisa 4:7). While the specific proportions differ between the two systems, both traditions recognize women's right to inherit — a progressive stance in their respective ancient contexts.

The Quranic inheritance system (An-Nisa 4:11-12) is more comprehensive than the Numbers provision, establishing fixed shares for daughters, wives, mothers, and sisters rather than treating female inheritance as an exceptional case. Muslim scholars view this as an advancement: what was an ad hoc ruling in the Mosaic system became a systematic, universal principle in Islamic law.

The appointment of Joshua (*Yusha' ibn Nun*) as Moses' successor is significant in Islamic tradition. While the Quran does not name Joshua directly in connection with the succession, Islamic tradition identifies him as the prophetic leader who completed the entry into the Promised Land. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir provide extensive accounts of Yusha's leadership after Musa's death.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Zelophehad ruling established inheritance rights through judicial precedent — a method with parallels in Islamic jurisprudence. While Islamic inheritance law is primarily derived from Quranic text and Hadith, the method of *qiyas* (analogical reasoning) allows jurists to extend principles to new situations, functioning similarly to the precedent-setting ruling in Numbers 27.

The succession from Moses to Joshua through public commissioning parallels the Islamic concept of *bay'ah* (pledge of allegiance). When Abu Bakr was appointed the first Caliph after the Prophet's death, the process involved public acknowledgment and formal acceptance — echoing the public commissioning of Joshua before the entire community.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the Zelophehad ruling as evidence that the Torah was developing inheritance law through specific cases rather than through comprehensive revelation. The Quranic inheritance system, revealed as a complete framework in Surah An-Nisa, is seen as the definitive divine resolution of inheritance questions that the Torah addressed only incrementally.

The theological significance of Moses not entering the Promised Land is treated with sensitivity in Islamic tradition. While the Torah attributes this to Moses' sin at Meribah, Islamic scholars, maintaining prophetic *'ismah*, tend to view it as a divine decree serving broader purposes rather than a punishment for sin.

## Scholarly Reflections

Ibn Kathir discusses Yusha' ibn Nun as one of the great prophets of the Israelites, divinely chosen to complete the mission that Musa began. His appointment represents the principle of continuity in divine guidance — when one prophet's mission ends, another carries it forward.

Amina Wadud has analyzed the Zelophehad narrative alongside Quranic inheritance provisions, noting that both traditions contain resources for women's economic rights. She argues that the progressive trajectory visible in the movement from the Zelophehad exception to the Quranic system suggests that the divine intention consistently moves toward greater equity.
