## Balaam's First and Second Oracles

Numbers 23 records Balaam's first two attempts to curse Israel at Balak's behest, both of which result in blessings instead. In the first oracle, Balaam declares, "How can I curse whom God has not cursed?" In the second, he proclaims Israel's special status: "God is not a man, that He should lie." Balak, frustrated, moves Balaam to another location hoping for a different result.

## Islamic Parallels and Contrasts

The irony of Balaam blessing instead of cursing resonates with the Quranic principle that human schemes cannot thwart divine will. Surah Al-Anfal (8:30) states: "They plan, and Allah plans. And Allah is the best of planners." Balak's repeated attempts to manipulate the outcome by changing locations reflect the futile human belief that circumstances can alter divine decree.

Balaam's declaration that "God is not a man, that He should lie" (23:19) aligns perfectly with the Quranic insistence on divine truthfulness and transcendence. Surah Al-Ikhlas (112:1-4) declares God's absolute uniqueness and incomparability. The Islamic concept of *tanzih* (divine transcendence and incomparability) finds a clear affirmation in this verse.

The motif of compelled prophecy — Balaam speaking God's words despite his own inclinations — illustrates the Islamic understanding that prophecy (*nubuwwah*) is a divine gift that operates according to God's will, not the prophet's preferences. The Quran describes the Prophet Muhammad's reception of revelation as something he could not control: "It is not but a revelation revealed" (An-Najm 53:4).

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Balak's attempts to use sacrificial offerings to manipulate the divine outcome connect to the Islamic condemnation of transactional worship. The Quran states, "It is not their meat nor their blood that reaches Allah, but it is your piety that reaches Him" (Al-Hajj 22:37). Balak's seven altars and fourteen sacrificed animals represent the futility of attempting to bribe God — an attitude Islamic jurisprudence categorizes as *riya'* (showing off) or worse.

The principle that one cannot be compelled to act against conscience, illustrated by Balaam's refusal to say what God has not authorized, connects to the Islamic concept of *ikrah* (compulsion). Islamic jurisprudence holds that statements and actions performed under compulsion lack legal validity, though Balaam's case is inverted: he is compelled by God to speak truth despite external pressure to lie.

## The Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars note that Balaam's oracles, while containing authentic prophetic content (such as the declaration of God's truthfulness), are embedded within a narrative framework that may reflect later Israelite literary composition. The oracles serve to affirm Israel's chosen status — a theological claim that the Quran both acknowledges (Bani Isra'il were indeed favored, Al-Baqarah 2:47) and qualifies (favor depends on obedience, not ethnic identity).

The tension between Balaam as an authentic prophet forced to speak truth and Balaam as a corrupt diviner who later leads Israel astray (see chapter 31) reflects what Islamic scholars view as the complexity of the *isra'iliyyat* traditions surrounding this figure.

## Scholarly Reflections

Al-Razi discusses the principle that divine blessings and curses operate according to God's sovereign will, not human manipulation. Balaam's inability to curse Israel when God has blessed them illustrates the fundamental Islamic conviction that *qadr* (divine decree) cannot be altered by human machination.

Ismail al-Faruqi used the Balaam narrative to illustrate the Islamic concept that truth is objective and cannot be suppressed: even a compromised figure like Balaam, when confronted with divine reality, must speak what is true. This principle underlies the Islamic confidence in the ultimate triumph of truth over falsehood.
