## Arei Miklat: Cities of Refuge and the Sanctity of Life

Numbers 35 establishes two interconnected institutions: the forty-eight cities allocated to the Levites throughout the Land of Israel, and the six cities of refuge (*arei miklat*) designated to protect the inadvertent killer (*rotzeach be-shogeg*) from the blood avenger (*go'el ha-dam*). The chapter also presents the Torah's fundamental principles distinguishing murder from manslaughter, establishing evidentiary standards for capital cases.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Rotzeach 8:1) explains the system's dual purpose: the cities of refuge protect the inadvertent killer from revenge while simultaneously serving as a form of atonement through exile (*galut*). The inadvertent killer remains in the city of refuge until the death of the *Kohen Gadol* (High Priest), after which he may return home. The Talmud (Makkot 11a) explains that the Kohen Gadol's death serves as atonement because he should have prayed more effectively for his generation to prevent such tragedies.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The cities shall be for you a refuge from the avenger" (35:12)

The Mishnah (Makkot 2:4–7) provides extensive details about life in the cities of refuge. A student exiled there was accompanied by his teacher (so Torah study would not be interrupted), and a teacher by his students. The roads leading to the cities had to be well-maintained with clear signage — the Talmud (Makkot 10b) states that at every crossroads, signs reading *Miklat* ("Refuge") directed the fleeing killer. The Rambam (Hilkhot Rotzeach 8:5) codifies these requirements.

### Distinguishing Murder from Manslaughter (35:16–25)

The chapter defines murder as killing with intent, using an iron implement, stone, or wooden weapon, or through hatred and ambush. Manslaughter is unintentional killing without enmity. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 37b) derives from these verses the requirement for careful judicial investigation: the court must establish intent, means, and motive. The famous principle *hatzalat nefashot* (saving lives) demands that courts err on the side of acquittal in capital cases.

### "You shall not pollute the land" — *Lo tachanif et ha-aretz* (35:33)

The chapter concludes with a theological declaration: bloodshed pollutes the Land, and the only atonement is the blood of the one who shed it. The Ramban interprets this as establishing a metaphysical connection between moral behavior and the Land's capacity to sustain its inhabitants. The Sifrei derives from this verse that the Shekhinah dwells in the Land of Israel only when it is free from the pollution of innocent blood.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of the cities of refuge are codified by the Rambam in Hilkhot Rotzeach u'Shemirat Nefesh, chapters 5–8. Although the institution ceased to function after the destruction of the Temple and the exile, the Talmud (Makkot 13a) discusses its theoretical restoration. The requirement of two witnesses for capital conviction (35:30) and the prohibition of ransom for a murderer's life (35:31) are foundational principles of Jewish criminal law.

The Mishnah (Makkot 2:6) records that the mothers of the *Kohanim Gedolim* would supply food and clothing to the exiles, hoping the exiles would not pray for their sons' deaths (since the Kohen Gadol's death would free them). This poignant detail illustrates the human complexity embedded in the legal system.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 35 continues Parashat Masei. The Netziv connects the Levitical cities to the cities of refuge: since the Levites were scattered throughout the Land to teach Torah, their cities naturally became places where an exiled killer could encounter spiritual guidance and rehabilitation. The juxtaposition with the Land's boundaries (chapter 34) teaches that the Land's sanctity depends not only on geographic definition but on the moral order maintained within it.
