## Ever HaYarden: The Transjordanian Request

Numbers 32 records the request of the tribes of Reuven and Gad (later joined by half of Menasheh) to settle in the Transjordanian territories conquered from Sichon and Og, rather than crossing the Jordan into the Promised Land proper. Moshe's initial reaction is fierce — he compares them to the spies who discouraged the nation from entering the Land. A negotiation follows, resulting in the agreement that these tribes will serve as the vanguard in the conquest of Canaan before returning to their Transjordanian inheritance.

Rashi observes a revealing detail in the tribes' initial request (32:16): they said "pens for our livestock... and cities for our children" — placing their property before their children. Moshe, in his response (32:24), reverses the order: "Build cities for your children and pens for your flocks" — prioritizing children over wealth. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 22:9) uses this as a lasting lesson about distorted priorities.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Shall your brothers go to war while you sit here?" (32:6)

Moshe's rebuke echoes the spy narrative: he fears that the eastern tribes' reluctance to cross the Jordan will demoralize the nation, just as the spies' report did. The Ramban notes that Moshe's comparison was both warning and test — would these tribes prove as faithless as the previous generation? Their response demonstrates that they have learned from history, accepting the military obligation willingly.

### The Conditional Agreement (32:20–24)

Moshe establishes a formal condition (*tenai*): if the tribes cross the Jordan and fight, they receive the Transjordanian lands; if not, they must settle within Canaan proper. The Talmud (Kiddushin 61a) derives from this passage the legal principles governing conditional agreements (*tenai kaful*) — a valid condition must be stated in both positive and negative terms, the positive must precede the negative, and the condition must precede the action. These technical rules, derived from Moshe's precise formulation, govern all conditional transactions in Jewish law.

### "Your sin will find you" — *U'da'u chatatkem* (32:23)

Moshe warns that if they fail to fulfill their commitment, "be sure your sin will find you out." This phrase entered the broader Jewish ethical vocabulary. The Talmud (Bamidbar Rabbah 22:7) interprets it not merely as a threat of punishment but as a statement about the nature of sin itself — concealed transgressions inevitably surface.

## Halakhic Significance

The conditional agreement (*tenai*) structure derived from this chapter is one of the most important legal mechanisms in Jewish law. The Rambam (Hilkhot Ishut 6:1–17) applies it to marriage and divorce — a *get* (divorce document) can be given conditionally, and the condition must meet the formal requirements derived from Numbers 32. The Shulchan Aruch (Even HaEzer 38) extensively codifies these principles.

The question of whether Transjordan possesses the full sanctity of the Land of Israel generates ongoing halakhic discussion. The Rambam (Hilkhot Terumot 1:6) distinguishes between the sanctity of territories conquered by the entire nation (full obligation) and those settled individually.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 32 concludes Parashat Matot. The Sforno observes that the chapter's negotiation demonstrates a mature relationship between leader and people — unlike the spy crisis, where panic replaced dialogue, here reasoned discussion produces a workable compromise. The arrangement foreshadows challenges that would recur throughout Jewish history: how to balance national solidarity with legitimate individual or tribal interests.
