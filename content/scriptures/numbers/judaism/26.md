## The Second Census: A New Generation

Numbers 26 records the second census of Israel, conducted after the plague of Baal Peor and the death of the wilderness generation. The count reveals a new nation — the same tribes but different individuals, prepared to enter the Promised Land. The census also establishes the basis for the territorial division of the Land, with each tribe receiving an inheritance (*nachalah*) proportional to its numbers.

Rashi notes that the census serves a dual purpose: military preparation for conquest and proportional land allocation. The total — 601,730 — is remarkably close to the first census total of 603,550, demonstrating that despite the plague and the forty-year decree, God maintained Israel at virtually the same strength. The Ramban sees this as evidence of divine providence sustaining the nation through its darkest period.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The sons of Korach did not die" (26:11)

This brief parenthetical is one of the most dramatic statements in the Torah. Despite Korach's catastrophic rebellion and the earth swallowing his household, his sons survived. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 110a) explains that at the last moment, they repented in their hearts, and a ledge was created for them within the abyss. They later composed some of the most beautiful psalms in Tehillim (Psalms 42, 44–49, 84–85, 87–88). The Midrash derives from this the principle that the gates of repentance are never fully closed.

### Land Allocation by Lot and Population (26:52–56)

The Land was divided by both *goral* (lot) and population size — a seemingly contradictory method. The Talmud (Bava Batra 122a) explains that the lot was cast with divine guidance (*ruach ha-kodesh*): the lot itself announced which tribe received which territory, and the allocation invariably matched the population-based entitlement. The Ramban explains that the lot served to confirm God's will publicly, preventing inter-tribal disputes.

### The Daughters of Tzelofchad Are Previewed (26:33)

The chapter mentions that Tzelofchad had no sons, only five daughters. This sets the stage for the landmark case in chapter 27, where the daughters petition for inheritance rights. The Sifrei notes that the Torah's mention of their father's name here — identifying him through his lineage — signals the importance of the upcoming legal discussion about women's inheritance.

## Halakhic Significance

The census-based land allocation establishes foundational principles of Jewish inheritance law (*dinei yerushah*). The Rambam (Hilkhot Nachalot 1:1) derives from this chapter the basic rules of inheritance: property passes to sons, then to daughters, then to the father, then to brothers, following the Torah's prescribed order. The concept that the Land belongs to specific families and tribes (*nachalah*) informs ongoing halakhic discussions about the inalienability of ancestral property.

The survival of Korach's sons provides halakhic support for the principle that children do not bear punishment for their parents' sins when they themselves are righteous (Deuteronomy 24:16). The Rambam (Hilkhot Teshuvah 6:1) emphasizes that each person's fate is determined by his own choices.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 26 continues Parashat Pinchas. The second census mirrors the first (chapter 1), creating a literary frame around the wilderness narrative. The Netziv observes that the detailed genealogies serve to connect the second generation to the patriarchal promises — each family traced to its tribal ancestor and ultimately to the patriarchs who received the covenantal promise of the Land.
