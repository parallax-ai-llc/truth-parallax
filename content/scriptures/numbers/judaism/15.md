## Laws of Offerings, Inadvertent Sin, and Fringes

Numbers 15 presents a collection of laws that the Midrash connects thematically to the spy episode. After the devastating decree of chapter 14, God immediately gives laws concerning offerings to be performed "when you come into the Land" (15:2) — a reassurance that despite the current generation's failure, Israel will ultimately enter the Promised Land. The chapter covers meal and drink offerings (*menachot u'nesachim*), the *challah* offering, inadvertent communal sin, the Sabbath-breaker, and the commandment of *tzitzit*.

Rashi explains the juxtaposition: just as the spies denied the Land, God reaffirms its promise. The Ramban adds that these laws were given specifically to comfort the younger generation, assuring them that they would indeed perform these commandments in their future homeland.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Challah Offering (15:17–21)

The Torah commands separating a portion of dough (*challah*) as a gift to the kohen. The Mishnah (Challah 1:1) specifies that this applies to dough made from the five species of grain (wheat, barley, spelt, rye, oats) in a minimum quantity. The Rambam (Hilkhot Bikkurim 5:1) codifies the ongoing obligation of *challah*, which remains in effect today as one of the few agricultural laws observed even outside the Land of Israel (by rabbinic decree).

### The Sabbath-Breaker — *Mekoshesh Eitzim* (15:32–36)

A man is found gathering wood on Shabbat and is brought before Moshe. The Torah states he was placed in custody "because it had not been clarified what should be done to him." The Talmud (Sanhedrin 78b) debates whether the uncertainty concerned the applicable death penalty (not whether he deserved death) or some other procedural matter. Rashi identifies the wood-gatherer as Tzelofchad, based on Rabbi Akiva's opinion (Shabbat 96b), though Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira criticized this identification as potentially defamatory.

### The Commandment of Tzitzit (15:37–41)

The chapter concludes with the mitzvah of *tzitzit* — fringes on the corners of garments, including a thread of *tekhelet* (blue dye). The Talmud (Menachot 43b) teaches that the *tekhelet* resembles the sea, which resembles the sky, which resembles God's Throne of Glory. The Mishnah (Menachot 4:1) discusses whether the white and blue threads are interdependent. This passage forms the third paragraph of the *Shema*, recited twice daily.

## Halakhic Significance

The *tzitzit* commandment is among the most widely observed mitzvot. The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chaim 8–24) devotes extensive chapters to its details: the garment must have four corners, the fringes must be made of the same material as the garment, and the tying follows specific patterns. The rediscovery of *tekhelet* from the *chilazon* (a marine creature) has generated significant contemporary halakhic discussion.

The distinction between inadvertent (*shogeg*) and intentional (*meizid*) sin (15:22–31) is foundational to the entire Jewish legal system. The Torah prescribes sacrificial atonement for unintentional communal and individual sins, while declaring that the intentional sinner who acts defiantly (*beyad ramah*) is "cut off" (*karet*). The Rambam (Hilkhot Shegagot) codifies the full range of offerings for inadvertent transgressions.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 15 concludes Parashat Shelach. The Sforno observes that the progression from offerings (hope for the Land) to the Sabbath-breaker (violation of covenant) to tzitzit (a protective reminder) creates a pastoral arc: God provides both warning and practical tools to prevent future failures like the spy episode.
