## Shelach Lecha: The Mission of the Meraglim

Numbers 13 narrates one of the most consequential episodes in the Torah — the sending of twelve spies (*meraglim*) to scout the Land of Canaan. Their mission and subsequent report would determine the fate of the entire wilderness generation. The chapter opens with God's command *Shelach lecha* ("Send for yourself"), which Rashi interprets as permissive rather than imperative: "I am not commanding you; if you wish, send." The initiative came from the people, and God merely acquiesced.

The Ramban disagrees, arguing that the mission was legitimate reconnaissance — standard military practice before entering a land. The sin lay not in the sending but in the spies' interpretation and presentation of their findings. This disagreement between Rashi and Ramban reflects a deeper question: was the fundamental error a lack of faith in sending spies at all, or a failure of courage in evaluating what they found?

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Selection of Leaders (13:2–16)

Each spy was a *nasi* (tribal prince), a respected leader. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 16:5) states that at the time of their appointment, they were all righteous men. Their subsequent failure teaches that even great individuals can falter when confronted with fear. Rashi notes that Moshe changed Hoshea's name to Yehoshua (adding the letter *yud*) as a prayer: "May God save you from the counsel of the spies."

### "A land flowing with milk and honey" (13:27)

The spies acknowledged the land's bounty, bringing back enormous clusters of grapes, pomegranates, and figs from the valley of Eshkol. The Talmud (Sotah 35a) describes the grape cluster as so large that it required eight men to carry it. Yet this truthful beginning was followed by the devastating word *efes* ("however") — "However, the people who dwell in the land are fierce" (13:28). The Zohar teaches that the spies' error was introducing *efes*, a word implying negation and impossibility, into their report about God's promised land.

### Calev's Dissent (13:30)

Calev silenced the people and declared: "We shall surely go up and possess it, for we are well able to overcome it." The Talmud (Sotah 35a) describes how Calev used a rhetorical strategy — initially appearing to side with the other spies to gain the crowd's attention before revealing his true position. The Midrash credits Calev's courage to his visit to the Cave of Machpelah in Hebron, where he prayed at the graves of the patriarchs for strength to resist the spies' counsel.

## Halakhic Significance

The sin of the spies is linked to the establishment of Tisha B'Av. The Talmud (Ta'anit 29a) records that the people wept on the night the spies returned — the ninth of Av — and God declared: "You wept without cause; I will establish this as a night of weeping for generations." This connects the destruction of both Temples and subsequent Jewish tragedies to the original failure of faith regarding the Land of Israel.

The Rambam (Hilkhot Teshuvah 3:6) includes denial of the Torah's promises regarding the Land among beliefs that can disqualify one from the World to Come, reflecting the theological severity of the spies' sin.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 13 opens Parashat Shelach. The juxtaposition with Miriam's story (chapter 12) is deliberate: the Midrash Tanchuma states that the spies should have learned from Miriam's punishment for speaking against Moshe that evil speech carries severe consequences. Their failure to internalize this lesson compounded their sin — they spoke *lashon hara* not merely about a person but about the land God had promised.
