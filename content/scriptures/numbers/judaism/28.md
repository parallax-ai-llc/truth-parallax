## Korban Tamid: The Perpetual Daily Offering

Numbers 28 presents the comprehensive calendar of communal offerings (*korbanot tzibbur*), beginning with the daily *tamid* offering and proceeding through the additional (*musaf*) offerings for Shabbat, Rosh Chodesh (New Moon), and the spring festivals — Pesach and Shavuot. This systematic catalog serves as the liturgical blueprint for Jewish worship both in the Temple era and, by extension, in the prayer services that replaced sacrifices.

Rashi explains the chapter's placement: now that the new generation has been counted (chapter 26) and a successor appointed (chapter 27), God instructs them in the sacrificial system they will observe upon entering the Land. The Ramban emphasizes that the *tamid* offering — two lambs daily, one in the morning and one in the afternoon — is the foundation of all communal worship, representing Israel's continuous relationship with God.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "My offering, My bread, My fire-offerings" (28:2)

The anthropomorphic language — God calling the offerings "My bread" (*lachmi*) — raised philosophical concerns. The Rambam (Moreh Nevukhim 3:32) interprets this figuratively: God has no need for food, but the language accommodates human understanding. Rashi simply translates *lachmi* as "My food-offering," referring to the portions consumed on the altar. The Sifrei derives from this verse that communal offerings take precedence over individual ones.

### The Tamid: Morning and Afternoon (28:3–8)

The morning lamb (*tamid shel shachar*) and afternoon lamb (*tamid shel bein ha-arbayim*) frame each day's Temple service. The Talmud (Berakhot 26b) records the tradition that the daily prayer services correspond to these offerings: *Shacharit* to the morning tamid, *Minchah* to the afternoon tamid. The Mishnah (Tamid) devotes an entire tractate to the daily procedure. The Rambam (Hilkhot Temidin U'Musafin 1:1) calls the tamid "the most important of all communal offerings."

### The Musaf Offerings for Shabbat and Rosh Chodesh (28:9–15)

The Shabbat musaf added two lambs beyond the daily tamid, and Rosh Chodesh required two bulls, one ram, and seven lambs. The Talmud (Chullin 60b) explains that the he-goat of Rosh Chodesh serves as "atonement for God" — a startling phrase the Midrash interprets as God's own expression of regret for diminishing the moon, an act of divine acknowledgment woven into the calendar itself.

## Halakhic Significance

The entire structure of Jewish liturgy derives from this chapter. The *Musaf* prayer recited on Shabbat, Rosh Chodesh, and festivals corresponds directly to the additional offerings listed here. The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chaim 591) specifies that the Musaf Amidah includes a recitation of the relevant sacrificial passage. The Talmud (Berakhot 26b) records the debate about whether prayer was instituted by the patriarchs or as a replacement for the sacrifices — the conclusion is that both traditions are valid.

The tamid offering also establishes the principle that no other sacrifice may precede the morning tamid or follow the afternoon tamid (Pesachim 58a), creating the temporal boundaries of daily worship.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 28 continues Parashat Pinchas. The Sforno observes that after the disruptions of rebellion, plague, and generational transition, God returns to the orderly rhythm of sacred time. The sacrificial calendar reasserts that Jewish life is structured by fixed encounters with the divine — daily, weekly, monthly, and annually — regardless of historical circumstances.
