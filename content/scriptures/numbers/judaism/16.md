## Korach: The Archetypal Dispute Not for the Sake of Heaven

Numbers 16 recounts the rebellion of Korach, a Levite of the clan of Kehat, along with Datan, Aviram, and 250 prominent men, against the leadership of Moshe and Aharon. This chapter is the Torah's definitive statement on destructive dispute (*machloket*) and illegitimate challenges to divinely ordained authority.

Rashi explains Korach's grievance: as a grandson of Kehat through Yitzhar (the second son), Korach expected the leadership of the Kehatite clan, but it was given to Elitzafan ben Uziel, son of the youngest brother. Korach combined personal ambition with populist rhetoric — "The entire congregation is holy, and God is among them; why do you raise yourselves above God's assembly?" (16:3). The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 18:2) notes that Korach used specious halakhic arguments to mock Moshe's authority: "Does a house full of Torah scrolls still need a mezuzah?"

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Korach took" — *Vayikach Korach* (16:1)

The opening verb *vayikach* ("he took") is unusual because it has no direct object. Rashi explains: "He took himself to one side" — meaning he separated himself from the community. The Targum Onkelos translates it as "he divided," emphasizing that Korach's action was fundamentally divisive. The Ramban suggests he "took" people — persuading them through rhetoric to join his faction.

### The Test of the Incense (16:16–18)

Moshe proposes a trial: let Korach and his followers offer incense, and God will choose whom He accepts. The Ramban notes the brilliance of this test — the rebels knew that unauthorized incense had killed Nadav and Avihu, yet their arrogance overrode their caution. The offering of incense, the most intimate Temple service, became the instrument of their judgment.

### The Earth Swallows Korach (16:31–33)

God creates an unprecedented miracle: the earth opens its mouth and swallows Korach, Datan, Aviram, and their households alive. The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 10:3) states that "Korach and his company have no share in the World to Come," citing this verse. However, the Talmud (Sanhedrin 110a) records that "the sons of Korach did not die" (as stated in Numbers 26:11) — they repented at the last moment and were given a ledge within the abyss, where they composed psalms (Psalms 42, 44–49, 84–85, 87–88).

## Halakhic Significance

The Mishnah (Avot 5:17) uses Korach as the paradigm of a dispute "not for the sake of Heaven" (*she'einah le-shem shamayim*), contrasting it with the constructive debates of Hillel and Shammai. The defining characteristic: a dispute for the sake of Heaven will endure (its ideas remain valuable), while one not for the sake of Heaven will not endure. This principle governs rabbinic attitudes toward disagreement — legitimate halakhic debate is sacred, but personal power struggles are corrosive.

The Rambam (Hilkhot De'ot 6:8) derives from Korach's fate the gravity of maintaining *machloket* (communal discord), which he ranks among the most destructive social sins.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 16 opens Parashat Korach. The Netziv observes that the rebellion occurs in the narrative gap between the decree of wandering (chapter 14) and the eventual entry into the Land — a period of purposelessness that bred resentment and ambition. Korach exploited the demoralization following the spy debacle to advance his own agenda.
