## Blessings from the Mouth of an Enemy

Numbers 23 records Bilaam's first two prophetic oracles (*mashal*), both of which, contrary to Balak's intentions, bless Israel rather than curse them. Brought to three different vantage points overlooking the Israelite camp, Bilaam finds himself compelled by God to speak words of praise and blessing. The chapter demonstrates that divine protection overrides even the most powerful human sorcery.

The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 20:14) explains that Bilaam attempted to use the precise moment of divine wrath — which occurs briefly each day — to insert his curse. But during the days of Bilaam's prophecy, God suppressed His anger entirely, as alluded to in Micah 6:5: "My people, remember what Balak king of Moav plotted and what Bilaam son of Beor answered him."

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "How shall I curse whom God has not cursed?" (23:8)

Bilaam's first oracle declares the impossibility of cursing Israel when God has not cursed them. The Ramban interprets this as Bilaam's genuine prophetic insight: he perceived that Israel's covenant with God rendered external curses powerless. The Talmud (Berakhot 7a) elaborates that Bilaam knew how to identify the moment of divine anger and exploit it — but God denied him that opportunity.

### "A people that dwells alone" — *Hen am levadad yishkon* (23:9)

This phrase has become one of the most quoted descriptions of Jewish destiny. Rashi interprets *levadad* ("alone") positively: Israel will not be reckoned among the nations — they are set apart for a unique covenantal destiny. The Ramban reads it as a prophecy of survival: when other nations fall, Israel will endure because they "dwell alone" under divine protection. The phrase resonates through Jewish history as a description of both isolation and chosenness.

### "God does not look upon iniquity in Yaakov" (23:21)

Bilaam declares that God does not perceive wrongdoing in Israel. The Talmud (Berakhot 7a) explains this does not mean Israel is sinless but that God, in His love, chooses not to scrutinize Israel through the lens of strict judgment (*middat ha-din*). The Sforno interprets it more literally: at this particular moment, Israel was in a state of collective righteousness, having just emerged from military victories achieved through faith.

## Halakhic Significance

Several of Bilaam's statements entered the liturgy. The phrase *mah tovu ohalekha Yaakov* ("How goodly are your tents, O Jacob") from the next chapter (24:5) is recited upon entering the synagogue. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 105b) states that all of Bilaam's blessings eventually reverted to curses — except for *mah tovu*, which remained a permanent blessing. This liturgical incorporation transforms the words of an enemy into daily Jewish prayer.

The principle that God can redirect speech against the speaker's intention has halakhic implications for the power and limits of verbal declarations (*nedarim* and *shevu'ot*) — words have force, but God retains ultimate sovereignty over their effect.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 23 continues Parashat Balak. The Ohr HaChaim notes that each successive vantage point from which Balak positioned Bilaam represented a different spiritual strategy for finding Israel's vulnerability — yet each attempt only revealed further dimensions of Israel's merit. The repetitive structure (altar-building, sacrifice, oracle) emphasizes the futility of opposing divine will through ritual manipulation.
