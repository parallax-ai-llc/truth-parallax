## Balak and Bilaam: The Pagan Prophet and Israel's Enemies

Numbers 22 opens the remarkable Bilaam narrative — a story told entirely from the perspective of Israel's enemies. Balak king of Moav, terrified by Israel's victories over Sichon and Og, sends messengers to Bilaam son of Beor, a renowned pagan prophet, to curse Israel. God initially forbids Bilaam from going, then permits him to go but only to speak what God commands. On the journey, Bilaam's donkey sees an angel blocking the path and speaks, rebuking its master.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 105a) ranks Bilaam among the greatest prophets of the nations, comparing his prophetic level to that of Moshe. Yet the same passage emphasizes the vast moral gulf between them: Moshe was humble, Bilaam arrogant; Moshe was holy, Bilaam licentious. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 20:1) explains that God gave the nations a prophet equal to Moshe to prevent them from claiming they would have been righteous if only they had proper prophetic guidance.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "God came to Bilaam" (22:9, 22:20)

Rashi notes that God's communication with Bilaam occurred at night, a lesser form of prophecy reserved for gentile prophets. The Ramban explains that Bilaam possessed genuine prophetic ability but corrupted it through sorcery (*keshafim*) and divination (*nachash*). His gift was real but his moral framework was not, making him the paradigm of spiritual talent without ethical grounding.

### The Talking Donkey — *Ha-Aton* (22:28–30)

The Mishnah (Avot 5:6) lists the mouth of Bilaam's donkey among the ten miraculous things created at twilight on the sixth day of creation — extraordinary phenomena prepared in advance within the natural order. The donkey saw the angel while Bilaam did not, prompting the Midrash's observation that the lowest animal perceived what the greatest prophet could not — a devastating commentary on how sin blinds even the spiritually gifted.

### The Angel with the Drawn Sword (22:31)

When God "uncovered Bilaam's eyes," he saw the angel standing with a drawn sword. The Ramban explains that the angel had been visible to the donkey because animals, lacking free will and moral corruption, are more attuned to spiritual reality. Bilaam's inability to perceive what his donkey saw illustrated the darkening effect of his malicious intent.

## Halakhic Significance

The Bilaam narrative raises fundamental questions about free will and divine control of speech. The Talmud (Makkot 10b) derives from Bilaam's story the principle that "in the way a person wishes to go, he is led" — God allowed Bilaam to go because he insisted, even though destruction awaited him. The Rambam (Hilkhot Teshuvah 6:5) uses this as an example of how God sometimes removes the opportunity for repentance from persistent sinners.

The Mishnah (Avot 5:19) contrasts the students of Avraham with the students of Bilaam: the former possess a good eye, a humble spirit, and a modest soul, while the latter possess an evil eye, an arrogant spirit, and a greedy soul. This has become a foundational ethical teaching in Jewish education.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 22 opens Parashat Balak. The Netziv observes that the entire Bilaam narrative is unique in the Torah — an extended episode in which Moshe and Israel are completely absent as actors. The story unfolds among gentile characters, demonstrating that God's providence extends to all nations and that Israel's protection operates even when Israel is unaware of the threats against it.
