## Baal Peor: Seduction, Idolatry, and the Zeal of Pinchas

Numbers 25 records the catastrophic sin at Baal Peor — Israel's seduction by the Moabite and Midianite women into idolatry and sexual immorality — and Pinchas's dramatic act of zealotry that stopped a plague killing 24,000. The chapter reveals Bilaam's ultimate strategy: having failed to curse Israel directly, he advised Balak to use the Moabite women to entice Israel into sin, thereby causing God to punish His own people (as revealed in Numbers 31:16).

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 106a) attributes the plan to Bilaam: "Their God hates sexual immorality — entice them with your daughters." The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 20:23) describes an elaborate campaign of commercial seduction that gradually drew Israelite men into Moabite tents, where they were plied with wine and then invited to worship Baal Peor.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Israel attached itself to Baal Peor" — *Vayitzamed Yisrael* (25:3)

The verb *vayitzamed* ("attached") implies a deep, clinging connection to the idol. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 64a) describes the worship of Baal Peor in shockingly degrading terms — its worship involved excretory acts before the idol. Rashi explains that the Israelites became "attached" like a bracelet (*tzamid*) clinging to an arm, suggesting the sin penetrated deeply into their identity, not merely their behavior.

### Pinchas's Act — Killing Zimri and Kozbi (25:7–8)

When the Simeonite prince Zimri brazenly brought a Midianite woman Kozbi before Moshe and the congregation, Pinchas took a spear and killed them both. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 82a) records the halakhic principle invoked: *ha-bo'el aramit, kana'in pog'in bo* — "one who cohabits with a gentile woman, zealots may strike him." However, the same passage emphasizes that this is not a *pesak din* (formal ruling) — if a zealot asks a court for permission, they may not grant it. Zealotry, by definition, must be spontaneous and purely motivated.

### The Plague and Its Cessation (25:8–9)

The plague that killed 24,000 ceased only when Pinchas acted. The Ramban explains that Moshe and the elders were immobilized — Moshe because the halakhah had escaped him in his distress, and the elders because they wept in helpless grief. Pinchas alone remembered the law and had the courage to act. The Yerushalmi (Sanhedrin 9:7) adds that the angels tried to push Pinchas away but God intervened.

## Halakhic Significance

The case of Pinchas generates complex halakhic analysis about the limits of zealotry (*kana'ut*). The Rambam (Hilkhot Issurei Biah 12:4–5) codifies the law permitting a zealot to act against public desecration but cautions that if the zealot is killed by the transgressor in self-defense, the transgressor bears no liability. The act must be purely for the sake of Heaven — any personal motivation disqualifies it. Later authorities virtually never invoke this principle as practical halakhah, treating it as an exceptional historical precedent.

The sin of Baal Peor also informs the stringent halakhic prohibitions against interfaith relationships, codified by the Rambam (Hilkhot Issurei Biah 12:1–3) and the Shulchan Aruch (Even HaEzer 16).

## Parashah Context

Numbers 25 bridges Parashat Balak and Parashat Pinchas (the parashah break falls at 25:10). The narrative connection between Bilaam's blessings and the Baal Peor disaster illustrates the Talmudic principle that an enemy's true strategy is often indirect. The Keli Yakar observes that the juxtaposition teaches a painful lesson: the same nation declared invulnerable to external curses in chapter 24 is devastated by its own moral failure in chapter 25.
