## The Nazir: Sanctity Through Abstinence

Numbers 6 presents two of the most significant institutions in Jewish law: the Nazirite vow (*nezirut*) and the Priestly Blessing (*Birkat Kohanim*). The *nazir* voluntarily accepts upon himself three prohibitions — wine and grape products, cutting hair, and contact with the dead — for a specified period, thereby attaining a level of holiness compared to that of the High Priest.

Rashi, following the Talmud (Sotah 2a), explains the juxtaposition with the sotah passage: "Why is the section of the nazir placed next to the section of the sotah? To tell you that anyone who sees a sotah in her disgrace should abstain from wine, for wine leads to adultery." This linkage illustrates the rabbinic principle that the Torah's arrangement itself constitutes a form of commentary (*semuchim*).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Holy to God" — *Kadosh la-Shem* (6:8)

The nazir is described as "holy to God" throughout the days of his vow. The Ramban sees the nazir as an ideal of piety — a person who elevates himself above ordinary pleasures to draw closer to the Divine. In contrast, the Rambam (Hilkhot De'ot 3:1) takes a more moderate view, classifying the nazir as someone who has gone to an extreme. He writes that a nazir must bring a sin-offering at the conclusion of his vow because he sinned by depriving himself of permitted pleasures.

### The Tension: Saint or Sinner? (6:11)

The Talmud (Nazir 19a, Ta'anit 11a) records a famous debate: Rabbi Elazar HaKappar calls the nazir a sinner (*chotei*) because he denied himself wine, while Rabbi Elazar considers him holy (*kadosh*). This debate reflects a fundamental tension in Jewish ethics between asceticism and engagement with the world.

### Birkat Kohanim — The Priestly Blessing (6:24–26)

The three-fold blessing — "May God bless you and guard you; may God shine His face upon you and be gracious to you; may God lift His face toward you and grant you peace" — is among the most recited texts in Jewish liturgy. The Mishnah (Tamid 7:2) describes the daily recitation by the kohanim in the Temple. The Talmud (Sotah 39b) details the precise ritual: the kohanim raise their hands, spread their fingers, and face the congregation.

## Halakhic Significance

*Birkat Kohanim* remains a living halakhic institution. In Israel, kohanim recite the blessing daily during the repetition of the *Amidah*; in the Diaspora (Ashkenazi custom), it is recited only on festivals. The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chaim 128) provides extensive regulations: the kohen must wash his hands before blessing, face the congregation, and recite each word after the *chazan* (cantor). A kohen who does not ascend when called violates three positive commandments (Talmud Sotah 38b).

The laws of the nazir, though practically dormant without the Temple (since the concluding sacrifices cannot be offered), remain subject to extensive Talmudic study in Tractate Nazir. The Rambam (Hilkhot Nezirut) codifies all the details, anticipating the Temple's restoration.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 6 continues Parashat Naso. The Sforno connects the Nazirite vow to the broader theme of the parashah — the elevation of individuals within the national framework. While the Levites serve by birth, the nazir demonstrates that any Israelite can voluntarily achieve heightened holiness. The chapter's conclusion with Birkat Kohanim seals the entire organizational section (chapters 1–6) with divine blessing.
