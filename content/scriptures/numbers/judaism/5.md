## Purity of the Camp and the Sotah Ritual

Numbers 5 addresses three distinct topics: the expulsion of ritually impure persons from the camp, the law of restitution for wrongs (*me'ilah*), and the elaborate ritual of the *sotah* — the woman suspected of adultery. Together these laws protect the sanctity of the camp and the integrity of relationships within the covenantal community.

Rashi explains the juxtaposition: the chapter begins with sending the impure out of the camp because the Shekhinah (Divine Presence) has just been established in the midst of Israel (chapters 1–4), and impurity cannot coexist with holiness. The Sifrei adds that each type of impurity — *tzara'at* (skin disease), *zav* (bodily discharge), and *tamei met* (corpse contamination) — required removal to a different zone of the camp.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Confession for Wrongs (5:6–7)

The Torah requires that a person who wrongs another must confess (*vehitvadu*) and make full restitution plus an additional fifth. Rashi identifies this case as one involving a false oath denying a monetary obligation. The Rambam (Hilkhot Teshuvah 1:1) uses this passage as the primary biblical source for the mitzvah of verbal confession (*vidui*), which he considers essential to the repentance process.

### The Sotah Procedure (5:12–31)

The *sotah* ritual is one of the most discussed passages in rabbinic literature, occupying an entire tractate of the Mishnah and Talmud (Sotah). When a husband suspects his wife of infidelity and has warned her (*kinui*) not to seclude herself with a particular man, and she is then witnessed in seclusion (*setirah*), she is brought to the Temple. The kohen writes curses on a scroll, dissolves the ink in bitter waters (*mei ha-marim*), and the woman drinks. If guilty, her body suffers; if innocent, she is vindicated and blessed with children.

### "God's name is erased for the sake of peace" (5:23)

The Talmud (Shabbat 116a) derives from the sotah ritual a profound principle: God permitted His own holy Name, written on the scroll, to be dissolved in water in order to restore peace between husband and wife. This teaches that *shalom bayit* (domestic peace) is of such supreme importance that even the sanctity of God's Name may be subordinated to it.

## Halakhic Significance

The Mishnah (Sotah 9:9) records that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai abolished the sotah ritual, because when adulterers became numerous, the bitter waters ceased to be effective. The Talmud (Sotah 47b) confirms that the ritual could only function in a society where the husband himself was free of sin. Thus the sotah institution carries within it a built-in moral check on the accuser.

The restitution laws in 5:6–8 are codified by the Rambam (Hilkhot Gezelah 8:1) and inform the halakhic structure of *teshuvah* (repentance) for monetary sins. Full restitution, the additional fifth, and a guilt offering (*asham*) are all required.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 5 opens Parashat Naso, the longest single Torah reading. The placement of the sotah laws after the tribal and Levitical organization underscores that communal holiness depends on both institutional structure and personal moral integrity. The Ohr HaChaim notes that sending the impure out of the camp and investigating the suspected adulteress both serve the same goal: preserving the camp as a worthy dwelling place for the Shekhinah.
