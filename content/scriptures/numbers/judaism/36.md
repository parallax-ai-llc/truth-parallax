## The Final Resolution: Inheritance, Marriage, and Tribal Integrity

Numbers 36, the final chapter of Bamidbar, resolves a question raised by the inheritance ruling for Tzelofchad's daughters (chapter 27). The leaders of the tribe of Menasheh approach Moshe with a concern: if Tzelofchad's daughters marry men from other tribes, their inherited land will transfer permanently to those tribes, diminishing Menasheh's ancestral territory. God rules that daughters who inherit land must marry within their father's tribe, preserving the tribal land allocation. The five daughters comply, marrying their cousins.

Rashi notes that the Torah concludes Bamidbar with this chapter to create a literary frame: the book opened with a census organized by tribes and closes with a law preserving tribal boundaries. The Ramban observes that both the daughters' original petition (chapter 27) and the tribal leaders' concern (chapter 36) were motivated by love of the Land of Israel — a positive bookend to the spy narrative, where love of the Land was tragically absent.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Let them marry whom they see fit, but only within the tribe" (36:6)

God's ruling balances two values: the daughters' right to choose their husbands and the tribal obligation to maintain territorial integrity. The Talmud (Bava Batra 120a) clarifies that this restriction applied only to the generation that entered the Land — future generations of inheriting daughters could marry freely. The Ramban explains that the initial distribution required strict tribal boundaries, but once the Land was fully settled and the allotments permanently established, the restriction was no longer necessary.

### "They married their uncles' sons" (36:11)

The Talmud (Bava Batra 120a) praises the daughters for their righteousness in marrying men who were both suitable and within the required tribe. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 21:11) notes that they are listed in different orders in chapters 27 and 36 — in chapter 27 by wisdom (when they argued their legal case) and in chapter 36 by age (when they married). This detail teaches that wisdom and age do not always correspond.

### "These are the commandments... in the plains of Moav" (36:13)

The book's final verse states that these are the commandments God gave through Moshe in the plains of Moav by the Jordan opposite Jericho. The Sifrei derives from this verse that no prophet after Moshe may introduce new commandments — the 613 mitzvot are complete. The Talmud (Megillah 2b) uses this verse in discussions about the authority of later prophetic institutions (such as the reading of Megillat Esther), concluding that prophets cannot innovate Torah law but can institute protective measures and commemorations.

## Halakhic Significance

The inheritance laws finalized in this chapter are codified by the Rambam (Hilkhot Nachalot 1:8) and the Shulchan Aruch (Choshen Mishpat 276). The principle that a daughter inherits in the absence of sons remains binding law. The temporary marriage restriction for inheriting daughters is no longer in effect, as the Talmud explicitly limits it to the conquest generation.

The chapter also contributes to the broader halakhic discussion of tribal identity (*shevet*). The Talmud (Kiddushin 70a) discusses the extent to which tribal affiliations can still be determined after the Babylonian exile mixed the populations. The Rambam (Hilkhot Melakhim 12:3) states that in the messianic era, tribal identities will be restored through prophetic revelation.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 36 concludes both Parashat Masei and the entire Book of Bamidbar. Upon completing the book's public reading, the congregation declares *Chazak, chazak, ve-nitchazek!* ("Be strong, be strong, and let us be strengthened!"). The Vilna Gaon notes that Bamidbar, which began with Israel organized and poised at Sinai, ends with Israel organized and poised at the Jordan — the journey has come full circle, and the new generation stands ready to fulfill what the old generation could not.

The Sforno observes that ending with the daughters' faithful obedience — marrying as instructed, preserving the Land's allocation — provides a hopeful conclusion. The people who will enter the Land are those who love it, respect its laws, and balance individual rights with communal responsibility.
