## Chatzotzrot: The Silver Trumpets and the Great Departure

Numbers 10 describes the fashioning of two silver trumpets (*chatzotzrot*) for signaling the congregation and initiating journeys, then narrates Israel's momentous departure from Mount Sinai. The chapter marks a turning point in the Book of Numbers — from organizational preparation to actual movement through the wilderness.

Rashi distinguishes the *chatzotzrot* (straight silver trumpets) from the *shofar* (curved ram's horn), noting that each served distinct functions. The trumpets were used for assembling the community, signaling military campaigns, and accompanying festival offerings. The Sifrei emphasizes that only Moshe was authorized to use the trumpets during his lifetime — they were not transferable to the next leader.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Make for yourself two trumpets of silver" (10:2)

The Talmud (Menachot 28b) specifies that these had to be fashioned from a single piece of beaten silver, similar to the construction of the Menorah from a single ingot of gold. The Rambam (Hilkhot Klei HaMikdash 3:5) codifies the requirement for trumpets in the Temple service. Ramban notes that the number two is significant — one trumpet for partial assemblies, both for the entire congregation — teaching that different occasions require different levels of communal mobilization.

### The Departure from Sinai (10:11–12)

On the twentieth day of the second month of the second year, the cloud lifted from the Mishkan and Israel began their journey. The Ramban describes this moment with great pathos — Israel was leaving the place of divine revelation, never to return. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 15:15) compares Israel's departure to a child leaving school, running away joyfully, which the rabbis interpret critically as suggesting Israel left Sinai too eagerly.

### The Inverted *Nun* Verses (10:35–36)

Two verses — "When the Ark traveled, Moshe would say: 'Arise, God, and let Your enemies be scattered...'" and "When it rested, he would say: 'Return, God, to the myriads of thousands of Israel'" — are bracketed by inverted letter *nun* in the Torah scroll. The Talmud (Shabbat 115b–116a) records that these two verses constitute a separate book of the Torah, effectively dividing Numbers into three books and bringing the total count of Torah books to seven. This opinion carries profound implications for how Jews understand the structure of Scripture.

## Halakhic Significance

The trumpets established the precedent for sounding instruments during public fasts and in times of national distress. The Rambam (Hilkhot Ta'aniyot 1:1–2) rules that blowing trumpets during communal crises is a positive Torah commandment, based on 10:9: "When you go to war... you shall sound the trumpets." This obligation later transferred in part to the shofar blasts of Rosh Hashanah and fast days.

The inverted *nun* passages are recited in the synagogue when the Torah scroll is removed from and returned to the Ark, forming a central element of the Torah reading liturgy.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 10 continues Parashat Beha'alotcha. Moshe's invitation to his father-in-law Yitro (called Chovav here) to join the journey (10:29–32) generates discussion about the identity and ultimate fate of Yitro. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 104a) states that Yitro's descendants sat in the Sanhedrin, while Rashi identifies Chovav as Yitro himself. The chapter's shift from organization to movement signals the beginning of the narrative crises that will dominate the remainder of Numbers.
