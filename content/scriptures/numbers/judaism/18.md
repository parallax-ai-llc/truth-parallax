## Matnot Kehunah: The Priestly and Levitical Entitlements

Numbers 18 establishes the comprehensive system of gifts and tithes that sustain the kohanim and Levites. Since these tribes received no territorial inheritance in the Land of Israel, God Himself is declared their inheritance (*nachalah*), and the people's agricultural and sacrificial gifts provide their material support. This chapter is the primary source for the elaborate tithing system that became central to Jewish agricultural law.

Rashi explains the chapter's placement after Korach's rebellion: the people's fear of approaching the Mishkan (17:27–28) required clarification of who bears responsibility for the sanctuary. God assigns the kohanim and Levites as guardians, absorbing the risk of improper approach and thereby protecting the general population.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "I am your portion and your inheritance" (18:20)

God tells Aharon that the kohanim will have no land inheritance — God Himself is their portion. The Rambam (Hilkhot Shemitah V'Yovel 13:12) extends this concept beyond the literal priesthood, declaring that any person who dedicates himself entirely to divine service and Torah study, walking uprightly, is sanctified as "holy of holies" and God provides for him. This remarkable passage universalizes the priestly ideal.

### The Twenty-Four Priestly Gifts (18:8–19)

The Talmud (Bava Kamma 110b) enumerates twenty-four distinct gifts (*matnot kehunah*) given to the kohanim, derived largely from this chapter. These include portions of sacrifices, the *terumah* (heave offering) of grain, wine, and oil, the firstborn of kosher animals, the redemption money for firstborn sons, and the *challah* offering. The Mishnah (Challah, Terumot) provides extensive details on the quantities and procedures for each.

### The Levitical Tithe and the Tithe of the Tithe (18:21–32)

The Levites receive the *ma'aser rishon* (first tithe) — one-tenth of all agricultural produce. They must then separate one-tenth of what they receive as *terumat ma'aser* and give it to the kohanim. The Rambam (Hilkhot Ma'aser 1:1) codifies this as a Torah-level obligation applicable to produce of the Land of Israel. The Talmud (Yevamot 86a) records a dispute about whether Ezra penalized the Levites by redirecting their tithe to the kohanim because the Levites did not ascend from Babylon in sufficient numbers.

## Halakhic Significance

The tithing system described here forms the backbone of *Hilkhot Zeraim* (agricultural laws) in the Talmud and Rambam's Mishneh Torah. In practical halakhah today, *terumot* and *ma'asrot* (tithes) must be separated from produce grown in the Land of Israel, though the actual portions are not given to kohanim or Levites due to issues of ritual purity. The Shulchan Aruch (Yoreh De'ah 331) codifies the contemporary procedures.

The principle that the tribe dedicated to spiritual service is supported by the community also influenced the concept of *kolel* (communal support for Torah scholars), though this application is debated among authorities.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 18 concludes Parashat Korach. The chapter transforms Korach's challenge — "the entire congregation is holy" — into a constructive framework. Yes, all Israel is holy, but holiness operates through distinct roles and mutual obligation. The kohanim serve, the Levites assist, and the Israelites support — creating a covenantal economy where spiritual labor is materially sustained.
