## The Serpent, the Song, and the Sword

Numbers 21 is a pivotal chapter marking Israel's transition from passive wandering to active conquest. It encompasses the attack by the Canaanite king of Arad, the plague of venomous serpents and the bronze serpent (*nachash ha-nechoshet*) that healed the afflicted, the Song of the Well, and the military victories over Sichon king of the Amorites and Og king of Bashan. The chapter signals that the second generation is fundamentally different from the first.

Rashi connects the serpent plague to the people's sin of speaking against God and Moshe (21:5). The punishment was measure for measure (*middah keneged middah*): the serpent, the original purveyor of evil speech in Eden, was sent against those who spoke evil. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 19:22) elaborates that the serpent, which finds all food tasteless, punished those who complained about the manna's taste.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Bronze Serpent — *Nachash Nechoshet* (21:8–9)

God instructs Moshe to fashion a serpent and place it on a pole; anyone bitten who looked at it would live. The Mishnah (Rosh Hashanah 3:8) asks: "Does a serpent kill or heal? Rather, when Israel looked upward and directed their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they were healed; when they did not, they perished." This interpretation transforms the bronze serpent from a magical object into a catalyst for repentance — the healing came from turning one's heart to God, not from the object itself.

### The Song of the Well — *Shirat Ha-Be'er* (21:17–18)

"Then Israel sang this song: Rise up, O well, sing to it!" This is one of the ten canonical songs (*shirot*) in Jewish tradition (Mekhilta, Beshalach). The Talmud (Ta'anit 9a) identifies this well as Miriam's well, which returned in the merit of the community after a temporary absence. The Ramban notes that the well "rose up" miraculously through the desert, following Israel in their journeys.

### The Defeat of Sichon and Og (21:21–35)

Israel's victories over Sichon and Og were of enormous halakhic and historical significance. The Talmud (Berakhot 54b) teaches that one who sees the crossing of the Arnon must recite a blessing of thanksgiving. The Mishnah (Yadayim 4:4) records that the conquered Transjordanian territories acquired the status of the Land of Israel for certain halakhic purposes. God specifically encouraged Moshe not to fear Og (21:34), as Og was a giant descended from the *Rephaim* whose merit might have protected him.

## Halakhic Significance

The bronze serpent was eventually destroyed by King Chizkiyahu (2 Kings 18:4) because the people had begun worshipping it. The Rambam (Hilkhot Avodah Zarah 3:2) praises Chizkiyahu for this act and uses it to derive the principle that even objects associated with miracles must be destroyed if they become objects of idolatrous devotion.

The conquests of Sichon and Og established the halakhic basis for Israelite settlement in Transjordan. The Rambam (Hilkhot Terumot 1:2) discusses the implications for agricultural laws in these territories.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 21 concludes Parashat Chukat. The chapter's rapid pace — serpents, song, and two major military victories — contrasts sharply with the lethargy of the wandering years. The Sforno observes that the new generation demonstrates both the capacity for sin (complaining) and the capacity for repentance and military courage that their parents lacked. The Song of the Well, celebrating God's provision, stands in sharp contrast to the earlier generation's complaints about water.
