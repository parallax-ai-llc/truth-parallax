## Bnot Tzelofchad: A Landmark of Torah Justice

Numbers 27 contains two pivotal events: the petition of Tzelofchad's five daughters for inheritance rights and the appointment of Yehoshua bin Nun as Moshe's successor. The daughters' case represents one of the Torah's most celebrated instances of law developing in response to a righteous claim, while Yehoshua's appointment ensures the continuity of leadership for the entry into the Land.

The Talmud (Bava Batra 119b) praises the daughters — Machlah, No'ah, Choglah, Milkah, and Tirtzah — as wise women (*chakmaniyot*), Torah scholars (*darshaniyot*), and righteous (*tzidkaniyot*). They were wise because they raised their petition at the precisely opportune moment; scholars because they argued from legal principle; and righteous because they married only men worthy of them.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Why should our father's name be lost?" (27:4)

The daughters argued that their father died for his own sin (not Korach's rebellion) and left no sons. Without a provision for daughters to inherit, his name and portion would vanish from his clan. The Sifrei records that their argument was so compelling that Moshe brought the case before God, as the halakhah had not been previously revealed. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 8a) derives from this that a judge should not be embarrassed to say "I have not heard the answer to this question."

### "The daughters of Tzelofchad speak correctly" — *Ken bnot Tzelofchad dovrot* (27:7)

God affirms their claim: "You shall surely give them a hereditary possession among their father's brothers." The Ramban notes that the Torah uses the emphatic *ken* ("correctly" or "rightly"), indicating that their argument was not merely legally valid but morally compelling. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 21:11) states that this section of inheritance law was "written" in heaven before it was revealed through the daughters' petition — God honored righteous women by allowing the law to be promulgated through their initiative.

### Moshe's Request and Yehoshua's Appointment (27:15–23)

Moshe asks God to appoint a leader "who will go out before them and come in before them" — a military and spiritual leader. God chooses Yehoshua, "a man in whom there is spirit." Rashi explains that Yehoshua could adapt his communication to the temperament of each person. The *semicha* (laying of hands) that Moshe performed on Yehoshua (27:23) became the paradigm for rabbinic ordination, a chain of authority extending from Moshe to subsequent generations.

## Halakhic Significance

The inheritance laws revealed through the daughters' petition are codified in Hilkhot Nachalot (Rambam, Hilkhot Nachalot 1:1–3). The basic order of inheritance: sons, then daughters, then the father, then brothers, then other relatives. The Shulchan Aruch (Choshen Mishpat 276) details the full application. The ruling in favor of the daughters did not create gender parity in inheritance but established daughters' rights in the absence of sons — a significant legal development.

The appointment of Yehoshua through *semicha* established the concept of formal rabbinic authority (*reshut*). The Rambam (Introduction to Mishneh Torah) traces the chain of tradition (*shalshelet ha-kabbalah*) from Moshe through Yehoshua to the elders and eventually to the Talmudic sages.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 27 continues Parashat Pinchas. The Ohr HaChaim connects the daughters' petition to Pinchas's zealotry — both demonstrate individual initiative in defense of Torah values. The chapter also prepares the narrative for the transition from Moshe's leadership, introducing the bittersweet reality that Moshe may see the Land from afar but will not enter it (27:12–14).
