## Milchemet Midian: The Holy War and Its Laws

Numbers 31 narrates the war against Midian — God's final command to Moshe before his death — and its aftermath, which introduces important laws regarding war spoils, purification of captured vessels, and the division of booty. The campaign avenges the Midianite role in the Baal Peor catastrophe (chapter 25). Bilaam is killed during this war (31:8), confirming that he was the architect of the strategy that led Israel to sin.

Rashi notes that although Moshe knew this war would be his final mission ("Avenge... and then you shall be gathered to your people," 31:2), he did not delay. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 22:2) praises Moshe's readiness to fulfill God's command even when it hastened his own death, contrasting this with ordinary human nature that seeks to postpone its mortality.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### A Thousand from Each Tribe (31:4–6)

Moshe sends 12,000 soldiers — one thousand per tribe. Pinchas leads the campaign, carrying the holy vessels and the trumpets. The Talmud (Sotah 43a) discusses the role of the *mashuach milchamah* (the kohen anointed for war), who addressed the troops before battle. The Ramban notes that the modest force size demonstrated reliance on divine intervention rather than military superiority.

### The Death of Bilaam (31:8)

Bilaam is killed by the sword alongside the five kings of Midian. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 106b) provides a detailed account of Bilaam's fate, noting that he had come to collect his "payment" for the counsel that led to the deaths of 24,000 Israelites. The Midrash states that Bilaam attempted to use his prophetic powers to escape by flying, but Pinchas pursued him. His death by the sword was *middah keneged middah* — he who lived by manipulating spiritual forces died by physical force.

### Purification of Captured Vessels — *Hag'alat Kelim* (31:21–23)

Elazar HaKohen teaches the soldiers the laws of purifying metal utensils captured from the Midianites: "Whatever comes into fire, you shall pass through fire; and whatever does not come into fire, you shall pass through water." The Talmud (Avodah Zarah 75b) derives the principle *ke-vol'o kach polto* ("as it absorbs, so it purges") — a vessel must be purified using the same process by which it absorbed the prohibited substance.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *hag'alat kelim* (purging vessels) from this chapter form the basis for the extensive halakhic framework governing the kashering of utensils. The Shulchan Aruch (Yoreh De'ah 121) applies the principles from Numbers 31 to everyday situations: vessels used with fire (such as grills) must be purged with fire (*libun*); vessels used with hot liquids must be purged with boiling water (*hag'alah*); and vessels used only with cold food need only rinsing.

The division of spoils — half to the soldiers, half to the community (31:27) — establishes a halakhic precedent for equitable distribution of war gains. The Rambam (Hilkhot Melakhim 4:9) codifies this system, noting that the king receives a portion before the division.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 31 continues Parashat Matot. The chapter's juxtaposition with the laws of vows (chapter 30) is significant: the war fulfills God's vow of vengeance against Midian, while the laws of speech and commitment ensure that Israel's internal moral structure remains intact. The Netziv observes that the Midian war is classified as a *milchemet mitzvah* (obligatory war), distinguishing it from optional wars of territorial expansion.
