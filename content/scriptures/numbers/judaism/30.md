## Nedarim: The Binding Power of Speech

Numbers 30 establishes the comprehensive legal framework for vows (*nedarim*) and oaths (*shevu'ot*), and introduces the mechanism by which a father or husband may annul a woman's vow. The chapter opens with the foundational principle: "He shall not profane his word; according to all that comes from his mouth, he shall do" (30:3). In Jewish law, a verbal commitment creates binding obligation — speech is not cheap but constitutionally powerful.

Rashi notes that this section was addressed to the *rashei ha-matot* (heads of the tribes) rather than to the general assembly, teaching that the annulment of vows (*hatarah* or *hafarah*) is a specialized procedure requiring qualified authority. The Ramban adds that the tribal leaders were specifically addressed because they would serve as judges in vow-related disputes.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "He shall not profane his word" — *Lo yachel devaro* (30:3)

The verb *yachel* is related to *chullin* ("profane" or "desecrate"). The Talmud (Nedarim 77a) derives from this that one must not make one's words *chullin* — ordinary or empty. A person's speech should carry the weight of commitment. The Rambam (Hilkhot Nedarim 1:4) codifies the general prohibition against making vows, while acknowledging that valid vows are fully binding. The Talmud (Nedarim 22a) advises: "Accustom yourself not to vow."

### Annulment by Father and Husband (30:4–16)

The chapter distinguishes three categories of women whose vows may be annulled: an unmarried woman living in her father's house, a married woman, and a betrothed woman. The father or husband must act on the day he hears the vow (*be-yom shom'o*); silence implies consent and ratification. The Talmud (Nedarim 66b–68a) extensively discusses what constitutes "hearing" and "the same day," establishing that the window extends from the moment of hearing until nightfall.

### The Principle of *Hafarat Nedarim* (30:13–16)

The husband's power to annul (*hafer*) applies only to vows that involve self-affliction (*inui nefesh*) or that affect the marital relationship. He cannot annul vows unrelated to these categories. The Ramban explains the rationale: since the husband bears responsibility for the household and the marriage, he has legitimate standing to prevent vows that would disrupt domestic harmony (*shalom bayit*).

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of vows occupy two full Talmudic tractates — *Nedarim* and *Nazir* — reflecting their complexity and importance. The Rambam (Hilkhot Nedarim, Hilkhot Shevu'ot) devotes extensive chapters to the subject. In practical halakhah, the annual *Kol Nidrei* prayer on Yom Kippur evening is a preemptive annulment of future inadvertent vows, reflecting the seriousness with which Jewish law treats verbal commitments.

The procedure of *hatarat nedarim* (release from vows by a qualified authority) is available to both men and women. The Shulchan Aruch (Yoreh De'ah 228) details the requirements: three laypeople or one expert may release a person from a vow by finding an "opening" (*petach*) — a circumstance the person did not anticipate when making the vow. Many observe the custom of *hatarat nedarim* on the eve of Rosh Hashanah.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 30 opens Parashat Matot. The Sforno connects the laws of vows to the preceding festival calendar: the festivals involve numerous obligations, and the Torah now clarifies how voluntary commitments interact with mandatory ones. The Keli Yakar sees the juxtaposition as teaching that the same power of speech that creates prayer and blessing also creates binding obligation — speech is the instrument of both worship and law.
