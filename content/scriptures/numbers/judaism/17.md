## The Aftermath of Korach: Plague and the Blossoming Staff

Numbers 17 continues the resolution of Korach's rebellion through two dramatic episodes. First, the people blame Moshe and Aharon for the deaths of "God's people," provoking a plague that kills 14,700 before Aharon stops it with incense. Second, God commands a test of twelve staffs — one per tribe — to settle the question of priestly authority permanently. Aharon's staff alone blossoms, buds, and produces almonds overnight, confirming God's choice of the tribe of Levi and the house of Aharon.

Rashi notes the remarkable irony: the same incense (*ketoret*) that killed the 250 rebels now saves the people from the plague. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 18:8) explains that the Angel of Death himself revealed to Moshe that incense can arrest plague — a secret from the time of the giving of the Torah.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Aharon Stands Between the Living and the Dead (17:13)

The image of Aharon standing with the incense pan between those already dead and those still living is one of the most powerful in the Torah. The Talmud (Shabbat 89a) teaches that this knowledge — that incense atones and stops death — was given to Moshe by the Angel of Death when Moshe ascended to receive the Torah. Aharon's willingness to run into the midst of the plague embodies the priestly ideal of self-sacrifice for the community.

### The Twelve Staffs and Aharon's Almond Branch (17:16–24)

Each tribal prince inscribed his name on a staff, and Aharon's name was written on the staff of Levi. Overnight, Aharon's staff alone sprouted, blossomed, and bore ripe almonds. The Ramban explains that the three stages — sprouting, blossoming, and bearing fruit — demonstrated that the priesthood was not a temporary arrangement but a permanent, living institution. The almond (*shaked*) symbolizes diligence and watchfulness, as God declared to Jeremiah (1:12): "I am watching (*shoked*) over My word to perform it."

### "We are perishing! We are all perishing!" (17:27–28)

The chapter concludes with the people's terrified cry. The Sforno interprets this as genuine repentance — the people finally acknowledged the danger of approaching the sacred without authorization. The Ramban sees it as the necessary prelude to the next chapter, which assigns the Levites as protective intermediaries between the people and the Mishkan.

## Halakhic Significance

The power of *ketoret* (incense) to arrest plague generated both halakhic and mystical traditions. The Talmud (Berakhot 43b) discusses the spiritual properties of incense, and the Zohar extensively elaborates on its kabbalistic significance. The custom of reciting the passage of the *ketoret* daily in the morning prayers derives in part from this chapter's demonstration of its protective power.

Aharon's staff was preserved within the Mishkan as a permanent sign (17:25). The Rambam (Hilkhot Klei HaMikdash 1:7) discusses the items stored alongside the Ark, including Aharon's staff, as evidence of God's designation of the priestly line.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 17 continues Parashat Korach. The chapter resolves the crisis of chapters 16–17 by providing an irrefutable, visible sign of Aharon's divine selection. The Ohr HaChaim observes that after argument, punishment, and plague all failed to convince the people, God chose a gentle, organic miracle — a blossoming branch — to settle the matter. Sometimes quiet evidence persuades where dramatic intervention does not.
