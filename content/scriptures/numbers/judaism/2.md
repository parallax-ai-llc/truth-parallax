## The Encampment of the Degalim

Numbers 2 establishes the precise arrangement of the twelve tribes around the *Mishkan* (Tabernacle) using *degalim* (banners or standards). Each tribe encamped under its own banner in a specific direction — east, south, west, or north — forming a sacred square around the dwelling place of God. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 2:3) teaches that when God revealed Himself at Sinai, He was accompanied by 22,000 angelic chariots arranged in banners, and Israel longed for a similar arrangement.

Rashi explains that the *degalim* were distinguished by colored cloths matching the colors of the tribal stones on the High Priest's breastplate (*choshen*). Ramban emphasizes that the encampment pattern mirrors the heavenly host and reflects the cosmic order of creation.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Each person by his banner" (2:2)

The Midrash Tanchuma records that when God commanded the tribal formations, Moshe was distressed, fearing the tribes would quarrel over positions. God reassured him: the tribes already knew their positions from the instructions Yaakov gave his sons regarding carrying his coffin. The formation of the *degalim* thus stretches back to the patriarchal era.

### The Eastern Camp — Yehudah, Yissachar, Zevulun (2:3–9)

Yehudah's camp, positioned to the east (*mizrach*), held the place of honor and led the march. The total count of 186,400 made this the largest division. Rashi notes that Yehudah's preeminence fulfills Yaakov's blessing in Genesis 49. The Talmud (Pesachim 4a) connects the eastern position to the concept of spiritual primacy.

### The Central Position of the Mishkan (2:17)

The Levites and the Mishkan occupied the center of the camp. The Ibn Ezra explains this reflects the principle that holiness radiates outward from the center. The Keli Yakar notes that this spatial arrangement teaches that God must be at the center of communal life — not peripheral but the organizing principle of the entire nation.

## Halakhic Significance

The arrangement of the camps informs later halakhic categories of spatial holiness (*kedushat makom*). The Mishnah (Kelim 1:6–9) delineates concentric circles of holiness: from the camp of Israel, to the Levite camp, to the camp of the *Shekhinah* (Divine Presence). These distinctions determine laws of ritual purity — who may enter which zone in states of impurity (*tumah*). The Rambam (Hilkhot Beit HaBechirah 7:11) maps these three camps onto the Temple Mount, the Temple courtyard, and the Temple itself.

The directional arrangement also influenced synagogue design. The custom of placing the *aron kodesh* (holy ark) on the eastern wall in Ashkenazi communities echoes the primacy of the eastern camp.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 2 continues within Parashat Bamidbar. The Sforno observes that the detailed tribal arrangement demonstrates God's care for communal order — the wilderness journey required military discipline and spiritual cohesion simultaneously. The Netziv (Ha'amek Davar) notes that the arrangement also facilitated distinct tribal identities within national unity, a balance Judaism considers ideal.
