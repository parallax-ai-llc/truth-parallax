## Mah Tovu and the Star of Yaakov

Numbers 24 contains Bilaam's third and fourth oracles, culminating in a messianic prophecy that became one of the most important eschatological texts in Jewish tradition. Bilaam, now abandoning sorcery and speaking under pure prophetic inspiration, utters the famous *Mah tovu ohalekha Yaakov* ("How goodly are your tents, O Jacob") and prophesies the rise of a future king from Israel — "A star shall step forth from Yaakov, and a scepter shall rise from Israel."

Rashi explains that when Bilaam saw Israel encamped tribe by tribe, he observed that their tent openings were positioned so that no family could see into another's dwelling — a manifestation of modesty (*tzeniut*). This inspired his praise of their "tents." The Talmud (Bava Batra 60a) derives from this the halakhic requirement that windows should not face a neighbor's property, protecting privacy as a legal right.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "How goodly are your tents, O Yaakov" — *Mah Tovu* (24:5)

This verse opens the daily morning liturgy in most traditions. The Midrash (Bamidbar Rabbah 20:21) interprets "tents" (*ohalekha*) as synagogues and study halls, and "dwellings" (*mishkenotekha*) as the Tabernacle and Temple. The blessing thus encompasses both the communal prayer life and the sacred architecture of Judaism. Despite originating from Bilaam, the words are recited with full devotion — the Talmud (Sanhedrin 105b) teaches that this blessing alone was never reversed.

### "A star from Yaakov, a scepter from Israel" (24:17)

The Rambam (Hilkhot Melakhim 11:1) cites this verse as a primary biblical source for the belief in the Messiah (*Mashiach*). He identifies the "star" with King David initially and with the future Messiah ultimately. Notably, Rabbi Akiva applied this verse to Bar Kokhba ("Son of a Star"), the leader of the revolt against Rome (Talmud, Yerushalmi Ta'anit 4:8). After Bar Kokhba's failure, Rabbi Akiva's colleagues challenged his identification, but the verse's messianic significance was never disputed — only its application to that particular figure.

### Bilaam's Prophecy Against the Nations (24:20–24)

Bilaam concludes with oracles against Amalek ("the first of nations, but its end is destruction"), the Kenites, Assyria, and others. The Ramban interprets these as prophecies extending to the end of days, covering the rise and fall of empires that would oppress Israel. The Ibn Ezra sees in them a compressed history of ancient Near Eastern power shifts.

## Halakhic Significance

The verse "How goodly are your tents" generates the halakhic principle of *hezek re'iyah* — visual intrusion as a legally recognized form of damage. The Talmud (Bava Batra 2b–3a) builds an entire body of property law on this concept: a neighbor has the right to demand that windows, doors, and rooftops not overlook his property. Privacy is not merely a courtesy but a legal entitlement rooted in Torah.

The messianic prophecy of 24:17 is one of the Rambam's proof texts for the obligation to believe in the coming of the Messiah, which he enshrines as the twelfth of the Thirteen Principles of Faith.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 24 continues Parashat Balak. The Netziv observes that Bilaam's final oracle (24:14–24) is introduced with the phrase "in the end of days" (*acharit ha-yamim*), placing it among the Torah's small number of explicitly eschatological passages. The chapter concludes with Bilaam and Balak parting ways — Bilaam to his eventual death at Israel's hands (Numbers 31:8) and Balak to historical obscurity.
