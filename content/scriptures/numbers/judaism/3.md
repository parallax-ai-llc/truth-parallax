## The Tribe Set Apart: Shevet Levi

Numbers 3 introduces the Levites as the tribe consecrated for divine service in place of the firstborn (*bekhorot*). After the sin of the Golden Calf, the firstborn of all tribes forfeited their priestly role, and the Levites — who did not participate in the sin — were chosen as replacements. The chapter catalogs the Levite clans (Gershon, Kehat, and Merari) and assigns each specific duties regarding the Mishkan.

Rashi explains that the opening verse — "These are the generations of Aharon and Moshe" — proceeds to list only Aharon's sons because Moshe taught them Torah, and one who teaches Torah to another's child is considered as though he fathered them (Talmud Sanhedrin 19b). This principle elevates Torah education to the level of biological parenthood.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The Levites shall be Mine" (3:12)

God declares the Levites His own (*li yihyu*), a status of special sanctity. The Ramban explains this as a form of sacred substitution (*pidyon*): just as God acquired the firstborn through the Exodus, He now transfers that claim to the Levites. The Sifrei notes that the Levites' dedication began with their zealous response at the Golden Calf: "Who is for God, come to me!" (Exodus 32:26).

### The Death of Nadav and Avihu (3:4)

The chapter briefly recalls that Aharon's elder sons Nadav and Avihu died "when they offered strange fire" (*eish zarah*). Rashi enumerates multiple rabbinic explanations for their death: entering the Sanctuary intoxicated, issuing halakhic rulings in the presence of their teacher Moshe, or failing to marry and have children. The Talmud (Eruvin 63a) derives from their fate the prohibition against a student rendering legal decisions in the presence of his master.

### The Redemption of the Firstborn (3:46–51)

Since the Levites numbered 22,000 and the firstborn 22,273, the surplus 273 firstborn had to be redeemed with five *shekalim* each. This redemption (*pidyon*) is the basis for the ongoing mitzvah of *Pidyon HaBen* — the redemption of the firstborn son on his thirty-first day. The Rambam (Hilkhot Bikkurim 11:1) codifies this as a positive commandment binding upon Jewish fathers throughout all generations.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *Pidyon HaBen* trace directly to this chapter. The Shulchan Aruch (Yoreh De'ah 305) details the requirements: the father gives five silver coins to a *kohen* on the thirty-first day after birth. This ceremony reenacts the original redemption of the 273 surplus firstborn. If the mother is a daughter of a kohen or Levi, no pidyon is required, as the child already belongs to the consecrated lineage.

The Levitical service assignments also inform the later Temple divisions. The Talmud (Arakhin 11a) discusses the Levitical singing and gatekeeping duties that evolved from the Mishkan-era responsibilities described here.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 3 remains within Parashat Bamidbar. The juxtaposition of the tribal census (chapters 1–2) with the Levite census (chapter 3) highlights the distinction between the general population and those set apart for sacred service. The Netziv observes that the Levites were counted from the age of one month — not twenty years — because their consecration was inherent from birth, not dependent on military capacity.
