## The Complaints Begin: Manna, Meat, and Murmuring

Numbers 11 marks the beginning of the crisis narratives that define the middle section of Bamidbar. The people complain (*mitonenim*), first about unspecified hardships and then about the monotony of the manna, longing for the foods of Egypt. God responds with anger and ultimately provides quail — but with devastating consequences. Moshe himself reaches a point of despair, and God appoints seventy elders to share the burden of leadership.

Rashi explains that *mitonenim* means the people were seeking a pretext (*to'anah*) to distance themselves from God. The Midrash (Sifrei) identifies the initial complainers as the *erev rav* (mixed multitude) who had joined Israel from Egypt. Their dissatisfaction was contagious, spreading to the broader community.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "We remember the fish we ate in Egypt for free" (11:5)

The Talmud (Yoma 75a) asks: did Egypt truly provide free food to slaves? Rather, "free" means free from the obligations of Torah and mitzvot. The longing for Egypt represents not mere culinary nostalgia but a desire to return to a life without spiritual responsibility. The Sifrei adds that the complaint about "fish" was a veiled protest against the sexual restrictions the Torah imposed.

### Moshe's Despair (11:11–15)

Moshe's cry to God — "Did I conceive this people? Did I give birth to them?" — is one of the rawest expressions of leadership crisis in Scripture. He concludes: "If this is how You treat me, kill me now." The Ramban notes that Moshe's distress was not selfishness but the agony of a leader who cannot satisfy both God and the people. The Rambam (Shemoneh Perakim, ch. 7) uses this passage to discuss how even prophets can experience moments of human weakness.

### The Seventy Elders and Eldad and Medad (11:24–29)

God takes from the spirit resting on Moshe and distributes it to seventy elders, who then prophesy. Two men, Eldad and Medad, remain in the camp yet also prophesy. Yehoshua urges Moshe to stop them, but Moshe responds: "Would that all God's people were prophets!" The Talmud (Sanhedrin 17a) identifies the seventy elders as the origin of the Sanhedrin, the supreme Jewish court. The Midrash debates what Eldad and Medad prophesied — one opinion says they foretold that Moshe would die and Yehoshua would lead Israel into the Land.

## Halakhic Significance

The institution of the seventy elders establishes the paradigm for shared governance in Jewish law. The Rambam (Hilkhot Sanhedrin 1:3) traces the Great Sanhedrin of seventy-one members directly to this chapter (seventy elders plus Moshe). The principle that leadership must be distributed rather than concentrated in a single individual remains foundational to Jewish communal governance.

The manna narratives also generate halakhic discussion. The Talmud (Yoma 75a–76a) extensively analyzes the manna's properties, concluding that it could taste like whatever the eater desired — making the complaint particularly ungrateful.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 11 continues Parashat Beha'alotcha. The Ohr HaChaim observes that the sequence of complaints — after the departure from Sinai — illustrates how spiritual decline begins with small grievances and escalates. The chapter's placement immediately after the triumphant departure signals that the wilderness generation's failure was not inevitable but resulted from choices made at critical moments.
