## Mei Merivah: The Waters of Strife

Numbers 20 is one of the Torah's most poignant chapters, framed by two deaths — Miriam at its opening and Aharon at its close — with the fateful incident at *Mei Merivah* (the Waters of Strife) at its center. Moshe's striking of the rock instead of speaking to it results in God's decree that neither Moshe nor Aharon will enter the Promised Land. The chapter also recounts Edom's refusal to allow Israel passage through its territory.

Rashi connects Miriam's death to the water crisis that immediately follows: the miraculous well that had accompanied Israel through the wilderness existed in Miriam's merit. When she died, the well disappeared, and the people thirsted. This teaches that the righteous sustain the world even in ways invisible during their lifetimes.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Speak to the rock" vs. "He struck the rock" (20:8, 20:11)

God commanded Moshe to *speak* to the rock, but Moshe *struck* it — twice. This deviation resulted in the most debated punishment in the Torah: Moshe and Aharon were barred from entering the Land. The commentators offer strikingly diverse explanations for the sin. Rashi says the sin was striking instead of speaking — speaking would have demonstrated God's power more profoundly. The Rambam (Shemoneh Perakim, ch. 4) identifies the sin as anger: Moshe said "Listen, you rebels!" — displaying inappropriate fury. The Ramban argues the sin was saying "shall *we* bring forth water" — implying that Moshe and Aharon, rather than God, performed the miracle.

### "Because you did not believe in Me to sanctify Me" (20:12)

The phrase *lo he'emantem bi* ("you did not believe in Me") is extraordinarily harsh when applied to Moshe. The Or HaChaim lists no fewer than ten different interpretations of this verse. The Abarbanel catalogues the major opinions and concludes that the multiplicity of explanations itself reveals the Torah's intent: Moshe's sin was subtle, befitting the exacting standard God applies to the righteous (*tzaddikim*).

### Aharon's Death on Mount Hor (20:22–29)

Aharon dies on the first of Av, and all Israel mourns him for thirty days. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 3a) records the date. Rashi notes that the mourning for Aharon was greater than for Moshe because Aharon was beloved as a pursuer of peace (*rodef shalom*) who reconciled quarreling individuals and estranged spouses. The Mishnah (Avot 1:12) immortalizes Aharon's peacemaking: "Be of the disciples of Aharon, loving peace and pursuing peace, loving people and bringing them close to Torah."

## Halakhic Significance

The incident at Mei Merivah informs the halakhic principle that God is exacting with the righteous (*medakdek im ha-tzaddikim ke-chut ha-sa'arah*) — holding them to the finest standard. The Rambam (Hilkhot De'ot 1:7) uses Moshe's punishment as evidence that anger is an especially destructive trait, forbidden even in moderation for Torah scholars.

The transfer of Aharon's priestly garments to his son Elazar (20:26–28) establishes the protocol for priestly succession and the sanctity of the priestly vestments (*bigdei kehunah*).

## Parashah Context

Numbers 20 continues Parashat Chukat. The chapter marks the transition from the first generation (whose leaders now die) to the second generation that will enter the Land. The Netziv notes that the thirty-eight-year gap between chapters 19 and 20 is passed over in silence — the Torah has nothing to say about the wandering years when no progress toward the Promised Land occurred.
