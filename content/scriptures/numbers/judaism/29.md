## The Autumn Festivals: Rosh Hashanah Through Shemini Atzeret

Numbers 29 completes the sacrificial calendar begun in chapter 28, detailing the *musaf* offerings for the autumn festivals: Rosh Hashanah (the first of Tishrei), Yom Kippur (the tenth of Tishrei), Sukkot (the fifteenth through twenty-first), and Shemini Atzeret (the twenty-second). The chapter is notable for its elaborate Sukkot offerings — a total of seventy bulls over seven days — which the rabbis interpreted as offerings on behalf of all the nations of the world.

Rashi, following the Talmud (Sukkah 55b), explains that the seventy bulls of Sukkot correspond to the seventy nations. Israel offered them as an intercession for universal welfare and peace. The declining sequence — thirteen bulls on the first day, twelve on the second, decreasing by one each day — symbolizes the diminishing power of the nations and the increasing prominence of Israel's spiritual mission.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Rosh Hashanah: "A day of blowing" — *Yom Teruah* (29:1)

The Torah identifies the first of Tishrei as a *yom teruah* — a day of blasting. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 33b–34a) derives the obligation of shofar blowing from this verse, debating the precise nature of the *teruah* sound (broken sobs, staccato blasts, or a combination). The Rambam (Hilkhot Shofar 1:1) codifies the requirement of hearing at least nine blasts — three sets of *tekiah-teruah-tekiah*. The Musaf offering for Rosh Hashanah included one bull, one ram, and seven lambs — a modest offering for such a momentous day, reflecting the inward character of judgment.

### Yom Kippur: "You shall afflict your souls" (29:7)

The chapter prescribes Yom Kippur's musaf offering alongside the command of self-affliction (*ve-initem et nafshoteikhem*). The Mishnah (Yoma 8:1) enumerates five forms of affliction: no eating, drinking, washing, anointing, wearing leather shoes, or marital relations. The Rambam (Hilkhot Shevitat Asor 1:4–5) codifies these as Torah-level prohibitions. The combination of elaborate sacrifices and personal deprivation encapsulates Yom Kippur's dual nature: the kohen gadol's Temple service and the individual's self-examination.

### The Seventy Bulls of Sukkot (29:12–34)

The declining sequence of bulls (13+12+11+10+9+8+7 = 70) is one of the Torah's most symbolically rich passages. The Talmud (Sukkah 55b) laments: "If the nations of the world knew how much the Temple benefited them, they would have surrounded it with fortresses to protect it." The Midrash Tanchuma adds that the seventy bulls correspond to the seventy angelic princes appointed over the nations in the heavenly court.

## Halakhic Significance

The Musaf prayers for Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and Sukkot include recitation of the sacrificial passages from this chapter. The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chaim 591) specifies the precise Torah readings for each day, drawing from Numbers 29. The distinct offerings for each day of Sukkot require a different Torah reading daily — a unique feature that generates detailed rules about the *aliyot* and their distribution.

Shemini Atzeret (29:35–38), with its single bull, stands in stark contrast to Sukkot's multitude. The Talmud (Sukkah 55b) compares this to a king who hosted a grand banquet for many guests, and on the final day asked his closest friend to stay for "a small meal, just the two of us." Shemini Atzeret represents God's intimate request that Israel linger after the universal celebration.

## Parashah Context

Numbers 29 concludes the sacrificial calendar within Parashat Pinchas. The Vilna Gaon observes that the festivals move from the universal (Sukkot's seventy nations) to the particular (Shemini Atzeret's intimacy with Israel), tracing a spiritual trajectory from cosmic scope to covenantal closeness. The chapter's placement before the laws of vows (chapter 30) links the public obligations of the festival calendar to private commitments of personal piety.
