## The Law of Vows

Numbers 30 regulates the making and keeping of vows, with particular attention to the circumstances under which a woman's vow may be annulled by her father (if unmarried) or her husband. The chapter begins with the absolute principle that vows must be kept and then addresses the complexities of household authority.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "If a man vows a vow to the LORD... he shall not break his word. He shall do according to all that proceeds out of his mouth" (30:2)

The chapter opens with an unqualified demand: a man who makes a vow must fulfill it. **Calvin** saw here a fundamental principle of integrity — one's word, especially when directed to God, is sacred and binding.

**Luther** connected this to the Reformation's emphasis on the sanctity of speech: the Christian's word should be reliable because it reflects the character of the God who always keeps His word.

### A Woman's Vow and Male Authority (30:3-15)

The chapter specifies that a father may annul his unmarried daughter's vow on the day he hears of it, and a husband may similarly annul his wife's vow. If neither speaks against it on the day they learn of it, the vow stands. If a man annuls a vow improperly (after initially allowing it), he bears the guilt.

**Matthew Henry** interpreted this not as a statement about women's inherent capacity but as a regulation of household authority in the ancient Near Eastern context. He emphasized the notable provision that widows and divorced women are fully responsible for their own vows (30:9) — they have the same obligation as men.

### Silence as Consent (30:14-15)

If the father or husband says nothing, the vow is confirmed by his silence. This principle — that authority must be actively exercised to be effective — has implications for Protestant ecclesiology regarding pastoral oversight.

## Theological Significance

### The Sanctity of Speech

Numbers 30 establishes that words matter. Vows are not casual statements but binding commitments before God. Protestant theology, following Jesus' teaching in Matthew 5:33-37 ("Let what you say be simply 'Yes' or 'No'"), has generally counseled caution in making vows while insisting on absolute fidelity to those made.

### Authority and Accountability

The vow regulations assume a structured household with clear authority relationships. Reformed interpreters have noted two important features: authority is not unlimited (the father or husband can only annul on the day he hears, and if he annuls improperly, he bears the guilt), and independent women (widows, divorcees) have full autonomy.

**Gordon Wenham** argues that the chapter actually constrains male authority as much as it establishes it: the man is accountable for how he exercises his veto, and delay in exercising it forfeits the right.

### Christ and Vow-Keeping

Evangelical interpreters note that Christ perfectly fulfills the principle of Numbers 30: He keeps every promise, fulfills every covenant obligation, and His word is absolutely reliable. His faithfulness is the foundation of the believer's confidence (2 Corinthians 1:20: "All the promises of God find their Yes in him").

### Practical Wisdom

The Reformers, particularly in the Reformed tradition, applied Numbers 30 to the question of monastic vows. If a young woman's vow could be annulled by her father, then rash religious vows — made under pressure or without mature reflection — should not be considered permanently binding. **Calvin** used this principle in arguing against the irrevocability of monastic commitments.

### Integrity in the Christian Life

Evangelical preachers have consistently drawn from Numbers 30 the practical application that Christians must be people of their word. In a culture of broken promises, the church should stand out as a community where commitments are honored and speech is trustworthy.
