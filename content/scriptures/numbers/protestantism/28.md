## Daily and Festival Offerings

Numbers 28 provides a comprehensive calendar of required offerings: the daily (tamid) offering, the Sabbath offering, the monthly new moon offering, and the festivals of Passover/Unleavened Bread and Weeks (Pentecost). These instructions prepare the new generation for worship in the promised land.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "My offering, my food for my food offerings" (28:2)

God calls the offerings "my food" — language that might suggest God needs sustenance. **Calvin** was careful to note that this is anthropomorphic language: God does not eat, but the offerings are described in terms of a host providing for a guest, underscoring the personal nature of worship. The offerings are acts of communion, not provision.

### The Daily Offering (28:3-8)

Two lambs each day — one in the morning, one in the evening — offered with grain and drink offerings. This continual sacrifice framed each day in worship. **Matthew Henry** saw the daily offering as teaching that every day belongs to God and that the believer's life should begin and end in devotion.

Protestant interpreters connect the daily sacrifice to the sufficiency of Christ's offering. Hebrews 7:27 declares that Christ does not need to offer sacrifices daily, "since he did this once for all when he offered up himself."

### The Sabbath Offering (28:9-10)

On the Sabbath, the daily offering is doubled — four lambs instead of two. **Luther** noted that the intensification of worship on the Sabbath confirms the special character of the day, a principle he applied to the Christian Lord's Day as a time of intensified devotion.

### The New Moon Offering (28:11-15)

Monthly new moon offerings include two bulls, a ram, and seven lambs with grain offerings, plus a male goat for a sin offering. This monthly rhythm of worship punctuated Israel's calendar with regular acts of consecration.

### Passover and Unleavened Bread (28:16-25)

The Passover on the fourteenth of the first month is followed by seven days of Unleavened Bread. The offerings during this period are substantial, reflecting the centrality of the Exodus deliverance in Israel's worship life.

### The Feast of Weeks (28:26-31)

On the day of firstfruits (Pentecost/Weeks), fresh grain offerings are presented, and the required animal sacrifices are offered. Protestant interpreters note the connection to Acts 2, where the Spirit is poured out on this very festival.

## Theological Significance

### Christ the Fulfillment of the Sacrificial Calendar

Protestant theology reads Numbers 28 through the lens of Hebrews: "When Christ appeared as a high priest of the good things that have come... he entered once for all into the holy places" (Hebrews 9:11-12). Every daily, weekly, monthly, and annual sacrifice pointed to the one sacrifice that would render them all obsolete.

### The Rhythm of Worship

The sacrificial calendar established daily, weekly, monthly, and annual rhythms of worship. Evangelical interpreters draw from this the principle that Christian devotion should be similarly structured — daily prayer, weekly corporate worship, and regular seasons of special observance.

### The Costliness of Worship

The sheer volume of animals and grain required is staggering when calculated over a year. **Gordon Wenham** estimates the annual cost was enormous. Protestant interpreters find here the principle that worship is costly — not because God needs our resources but because sacrifice shapes our hearts.

### Continuity and Completion

These offerings are not new commands but a restatement and expansion of earlier regulations (Exodus 29; Leviticus 23) for the new generation. The continuity underscores that God's worship requirements do not change with changing generations. **R.K. Harrison** notes that what changes is the generation of worshippers, not the character of the God they worship.
