## Balaam's First Two Oracles

Numbers 23 records Balaam's first two oracles, both of which bless Israel instead of cursing them. Despite Balak's frustration and repeated attempts to change the outcome by changing the location, Balaam can only speak what God gives him.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The First Oracle (23:7-10)

"How can I curse whom God has not cursed? How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?" Balaam's oracle acknowledges that Israel's standing is determined by God, not by human pronouncement. He concludes: "Let me die the death of the upright, and let my end be like his!"

**Calvin** noted the tragedy of Balaam's final wish: he desires the righteous person's death without being willing to live the righteous person's life. This is the condition of many who admire godliness from afar without embracing it.

### "God is not man, that he should lie" (23:19)

This verse has become one of the most quoted statements of divine immutability in Protestant theology. God does not change His mind in the way humans do. What He has spoken, He will accomplish.

**Luther** preached frequently on this verse, emphasizing the absolute reliability of God's promises. If God has declared Israel blessed, no human stratagem can alter that declaration. This verse grounds the assurance of salvation: if God has justified believers in Christ, that verdict cannot be reversed.

### The Second Oracle (23:18-24)

"He has not beheld misfortune in Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in Israel. The LORD their God is with them, and the shout of a king is among them." Balaam declares that God sees no iniquity in His people — not because they are sinless but because He has chosen to cover their sin.

**Matthew Henry** saw here the doctrine of justification in seed form: God looks upon His people not as they are in themselves but as they are in His grace. The "shout of a king" anticipates the reality of God's royal presence among His people.

### Balak's Frustration (23:25-26)

After the second oracle, Balak pleads: "Neither curse them at all, nor bless them at all." He would settle for neutrality. But Balaam replies: "All that the LORD speaks, that I must do." The prophet has no power to alter God's word even by omission.

## Theological Significance

### The Irrevocability of God's Blessing

The central theological message of Numbers 23 is that God's blessing on His people cannot be revoked by external forces. Reformed theology draws from this the doctrine of the perseverance of the saints: what God has blessed, no power in heaven or earth can curse (Romans 8:31-39).

### God's View of His People

The declaration that God "has not beheld misfortune in Jacob" raises the question of imputed righteousness. Protestant interpreters see here a preview of the gospel: God views His people through the lens of grace rather than strict merit. **Gordon Wenham** notes that this does not mean Israel is sinless (as the book of Numbers amply demonstrates) but that God's covenantal commitment overrides their failures.

### The Limitations of the Prophet

Balaam's inability to deviate from God's word demonstrates the sovereignty of revelation. The prophet is a vessel, not a source. Evangelical interpreters draw from this the principle of the sufficiency and authority of Scripture: what God has spoken stands, regardless of the character of the human instrument.

### True Versus False Prophecy

Balaam's oracles are true because they come from God, despite Balaam's corrupt motivations. **R.K. Harrison** observes that this raises an important theological point: the validity of a message depends on its divine source, not on the moral quality of the messenger. This principle is echoed in Paul's willingness to affirm that Christ is proclaimed even from impure motives (Philippians 1:15-18).
