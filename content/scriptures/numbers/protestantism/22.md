## Balaam and the Talking Donkey

Numbers 22 introduces one of the most colorful narratives in the Pentateuch: the story of Balaam, a non-Israelite diviner hired by King Balak of Moab to curse Israel. The chapter is famous for the episode of Balaam's donkey, which sees the angel of the LORD and speaks.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Balak's Fear (22:1-6)

After Israel's victories over Sihon and Og, Balak of Moab is terrified. He sends for Balaam, a renowned diviner from Pethor in Mesopotamia, saying: "Come now, curse this people for me... for I know that he whom you bless is blessed, and he whom you curse is cursed."

**Calvin** noted the irony: Balak recognizes supernatural power at work in Israel but tries to combat it with sorcery rather than submission. This represents the world's characteristic response to God's power — opposition rather than faith.

### God's Initial Prohibition (22:12)

"You shall not go with them. You shall not curse the people, for they are blessed." God's statement is absolute. **Matthew Henry** observed that what God has blessed, no human power can reverse — a principle that underlies the entire Balaam narrative.

### Balaam's Greed (22:15-21)

When more prestigious messengers arrive with greater incentives, Balaam asks God again. God permits him to go but warns he may only speak what God tells him. **Luther** saw in Balaam's repeated asking a warning about seeking permission for what God has already forbidden — using prayer to negotiate with God rather than to submit.

### The Donkey Episode (22:22-35)

The angel of the LORD blocks the road three times. The donkey sees the angel and turns aside; Balaam, blind to the divine presence, beats the donkey. God opens the donkey's mouth, and she speaks. Then God opens Balaam's eyes, and he sees the angel.

**Gordon Wenham** notes the devastating irony: the professional seer is blind while the dumb animal sees. The donkey demonstrates more spiritual perception than the prophet.

### "If it is evil in your sight, I will turn back" (22:34)

Even after seeing the angel, Balaam's offer to turn back is conditional — "if it is evil in your sight." Protestant commentators detect lingering reluctance. True repentance does not negotiate; it obeys unconditionally.

## Theological Significance

### God's Sovereignty over Blessing and Curse

The foundational lesson of the Balaam narrative is that no one can curse whom God has blessed. Reformed theology draws from this a powerful doctrine of divine sovereignty: God's purposes cannot be thwarted by human machination, whether political (Balak) or spiritual (Balaam).

### The Danger of Mercenary Religion

Balaam becomes a paradigmatic figure for corrupt religion. Jude 11 warns against "Balaam's error" — using spiritual gifts for profit. Peter calls Balaam one "who loved gain from wrongdoing" (2 Peter 2:15-16). Protestant critique of indulgences, simony, and prosperity theology draws on this tradition.

### Spiritual Blindness

The donkey-and-prophet contrast illustrates a recurring biblical theme: those who should see are often blind, while the unlikely perceive clearly. **R.K. Harrison** connects this to Jesus' warnings to the Pharisees and to the principle that religious expertise does not guarantee spiritual sight.

### The Angel of the LORD

Protestant interpreters, especially in the Reformed tradition, have often identified the angel of the LORD as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (a Christophany). The angel stands with drawn sword, ready to execute judgment — a picture of Christ as both savior and judge.

### God Uses All Things

God speaks through a donkey. This is not magical but sovereign — God can use any instrument to accomplish His purposes. Lutheran interpreters connect this to the principle that God's Word is effective regardless of the vessel through which it comes.
