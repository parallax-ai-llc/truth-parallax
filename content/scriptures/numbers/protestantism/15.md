## Various Laws: Offerings, Sin, and Tassels

Numbers 15 follows the devastating judgment of chapter 14 with a collection of laws — grain and drink offerings that accompany sacrifices, provisions for unintentional sin, the case of the Sabbath breaker, and the command to wear tassels. The chapter's placement after the rebellion narrative is theologically significant.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "When you come into the land" (15:2)

The chapter opens with a striking phrase of hope: "When you come into the land you are to inhabit." Despite the forty-year sentence, God reaffirms that the people *will* reach the promised land — the next generation will inherit what this generation forfeited.

**Calvin** found profound pastoral comfort here: even in the midst of judgment, God speaks of future fulfillment. The promise is delayed but not revoked.

### Supplementary Offerings (15:1-16)

Grain offerings and drink offerings are prescribed to accompany animal sacrifices. These regulations envision settled agricultural life in Canaan. **Matthew Henry** saw these as teaching that worship involves the whole of one's resources — not only livestock but also the produce of field and vineyard.

### One Law for Native and Sojourner (15:15-16)

"There shall be one statute for you and for the stranger who sojourns with you." Protestant interpreters find here an important principle of equality. **Gordon Wenham** notes that the inclusion of foreigners in Israel's worship anticipates the universal scope of the gospel. There is one law because there is one God.

### Unintentional Versus Deliberate Sin (15:22-31)

The chapter distinguishes sharply between sins committed unintentionally (for which sacrifice atones) and sins committed "with a high hand" (deliberately), for which there is no sacrificial remedy — the person is "cut off." Reformed theology sees this distinction as clarifying the limits of the sacrificial system and pointing to the need for a greater sacrifice.

**Luther** argued that this passage exposes the insufficiency of the law: it can address accidental transgression but has no remedy for willful rebellion. Only Christ's sacrifice covers the full range of human sin.

### The Sabbath Breaker (15:32-36)

A man caught gathering wood on the Sabbath is brought to Moses. God commands that he be stoned. This incident has troubled many readers, but Protestant commentators place it in context: immediately after the distinction between unintentional and deliberate sin, this man's public Sabbath-breaking is a "high-handed" violation.

### Tassels on Garments (15:37-41)

Israel is commanded to wear tassels (*tzitzit*) with a blue cord on their garments as a reminder to obey God's commandments and "not follow after your own heart and your own eyes."

## Theological Significance

### Grace After Judgment

The placement of these laws — looking forward to life in the land — immediately after the forty-year sentence communicates grace. God's discipline is not His final word. Evangelical interpreters emphasize that divine correction always serves the purpose of restoration, not destruction.

### The Limits of Law

The distinction between unintentional and deliberate sin reveals what the Reformers called the *usus theologicus* (theological use) of the law — its function in showing humanity's need for grace. The law provides for some sins but not all, pointing forward to the need for a savior.

### Physical Reminders of Spiritual Reality

The tassels serve as tangible aids to obedience. Protestant interpreters, while not binding this specific command on Christians, recognize the principle: physical reminders of spiritual commitments have legitimate value. The Lord's Supper and baptism serve an analogous function in the church.
