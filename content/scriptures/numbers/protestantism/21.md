## The Bronze Serpent and Wilderness Victories

Numbers 21 contains one of the most important typological passages in the Old Testament: the account of the bronze serpent. The chapter also records military victories over Arad, Sihon, and Og, marking a turning point as the new generation begins to experience God's power in battle.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Victory over Arad (21:1-3)

The Canaanite king of Arad attacks Israel. The people vow to devote the Canaanite cities to destruction if God grants victory. God responds, and the Canaanites are defeated. **Matthew Henry** saw this as the first sign that the new generation would succeed where their parents failed — they responded to threat with faith rather than fear.

### The Bronze Serpent (21:4-9)

The people again complain about food and water. God sends fiery (venomous) serpents, and many die. When the people repent, God instructs Moses to make a bronze serpent and set it on a pole. Anyone bitten who looks at the bronze serpent lives.

**Calvin** emphasized the paradox: the image of the very thing that kills them becomes the instrument of their healing. The cure resembles the disease. This is not sympathetic magic but a divinely appointed sign that requires faith.

### Jesus' Interpretation (John 3:14-15)

"As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in him may have eternal life." This is Jesus' own typological interpretation, making the bronze serpent one of the most important Christological types in the Old Testament.

**Luther** preached extensively on this connection: Christ, "made sin for us" (2 Corinthians 5:21), is lifted up on the cross bearing the likeness of the very thing that kills us — sin. Those who look to Him in faith are healed.

### The Song of the Well (21:17-18)

"Spring up, O well! — Sing to it!" This joyful song contrasts sharply with the earlier complaining. **Gordon Wenham** notes the shift in tone: the generation of complaint is giving way to a generation of praise.

### Victories over Sihon and Og (21:21-35)

Israel defeats Sihon king of the Amorites and Og king of Bashan, taking possession of their territories east of the Jordan. These victories provide tangible evidence that God fights for Israel when they trust Him.

## Theological Significance

### The Bronze Serpent and the Gospel

Protestant theology finds in the bronze serpent a near-perfect illustration of justification by faith:

- The remedy is **provided by God**, not earned by the people
- It requires only **looking** — the simplest act of faith, not elaborate works
- It is **available to all** who have been bitten — the offer is universal
- The healing is **immediate** — there is no probationary period
- The instrument of healing **bears the likeness of the curse** — Christ made sin for us

**R.C. Sproul** called the bronze serpent "the Old Testament gospel in miniature."

### Sin, Judgment, and Grace

The sequence — sin, judgment, repentance, divine provision — is the pattern of the entire Bible in microcosm. Evangelical interpreters note that God does not remove the serpents but provides a remedy within the situation. Grace does not always eliminate suffering but always provides a way through it.

### The New Generation's Faith

The military victories at the end of the chapter demonstrate that faith produces different outcomes than unbelief. Where the parents failed at Kadesh, the children succeed against Sihon and Og. Reformed interpreters see this as illustrating the principle that each generation must exercise its own faith.

### Looking and Living

The simplicity of the required response — looking at the serpent — has been central to evangelical preaching. Charles Spurgeon preached his first sermon on this text, and it became one of the most famous conversion sermons in Protestant history. The message is: salvation requires not great effort but simple trust directed toward God's provision.
