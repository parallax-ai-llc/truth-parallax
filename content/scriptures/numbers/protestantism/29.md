## Festival Offerings: Trumpets, Atonement, and Tabernacles

Numbers 29 continues the festival calendar from chapter 28, detailing the offerings for the three major observances of the seventh month: the Feast of Trumpets, the Day of Atonement, and the Feast of Tabernacles (Booths). The seventh month is the climax of Israel's liturgical year.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Feast of Trumpets (29:1-6)

On the first day of the seventh month, a sacred assembly is convened with trumpet blasts. **Calvin** saw the trumpet as a call to spiritual alertness — the beginning of a solemn month that demands repentance and reflection.

Protestant interpreters connect the Feast of Trumpets to eschatological themes: the trumpet of God that will announce Christ's return (1 Thessalonians 4:16). The feast inaugurates a season of judgment and redemption that finds its ultimate expression in the last days.

### The Day of Atonement (29:7-11)

On the tenth day of the seventh month, Israel fasts and afflicts their souls. The offerings are prescribed in addition to the regular sin offering of atonement described in Leviticus 16. This is the most solemn day in Israel's calendar.

**Luther** emphasized that the Day of Atonement reveals the heart of the human predicament: sin requires atonement, and atonement requires sacrifice. The Reformation's central insight — that justification comes through faith in Christ's atoning work — is anchored in passages like this.

### The Feast of Tabernacles (29:12-38)

The Feast of Tabernacles is a seven-day celebration beginning on the fifteenth of the seventh month, followed by an eighth-day assembly. The offerings are extraordinary in their abundance — thirteen bulls on the first day, decreasing by one each day to seven on the seventh day, totaling seventy bulls over the week.

**Matthew Henry** noted the descending number of bulls as perhaps symbolizing the fading of the old covenant's sacrificial system, moving toward the single, final sacrifice of Christ.

**Gordon Wenham** points out that the total of seventy bulls may represent the seventy nations of Genesis 10, suggesting that Israel's worship has implications for the entire world.

### The Eighth Day Assembly (29:35-38)

The eighth day requires only one bull, one ram, and seven lambs — a stark simplification after the abundance of the preceding week. Protestant interpreters have seen in the eighth day a symbol of the new creation, the day beyond the seven-day week, pointing to the eschatological fulfillment of all festivals.

## Theological Significance

### The Seventh Month and Consummation

The concentration of three major festivals in a single month creates a sense of climax and consummation. Reformed eschatology draws from this structure the pattern of end-times events: the trumpet call (resurrection), the day of judgment (atonement), and the age of rejoicing (tabernacles). **R.K. Harrison** notes that Jewish tradition itself interprets these feasts in eschatological terms.

### Christ as All the Festivals

Protestant theology reads every festival as pointing to Christ:

| Festival | Fulfillment in Christ |
|----------|----------------------|
| Trumpets | Christ's return, the trumpet call |
| Day of Atonement | Christ's once-for-all sacrifice (Hebrews 9:12) |
| Tabernacles | God dwelling with His people (John 1:14; Revelation 21:3) |

### Joy in Worship

The Feast of Tabernacles is preeminently a feast of joy. Deuteronomy 16:14 commands Israel to "rejoice in your feast." Evangelical interpreters stress that the liturgical calendar includes not only solemn repentance but exuberant celebration. The Christian life is not all gravity — there is an appointed time for joy.

### The Abundance of Sacrifice

The sheer quantity of offerings during Tabernacles — 199 animal sacrifices over eight days — demonstrates the extravagance of worship in the old covenant. Protestant interpreters use this abundance to magnify, by contrast, the singular sufficiency of Christ's sacrifice. What required hundreds of offerings is accomplished in one act on the cross.
