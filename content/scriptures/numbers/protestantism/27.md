## The Daughters of Zelophehad and the Commissioning of Joshua

Numbers 27 contains two pivotal episodes: the legal appeal of Zelophehad's five daughters for inheritance rights, and God's appointment of Joshua as Moses' successor. Both narratives address the question of succession — of property and of leadership.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Daughters' Appeal (27:1-4)

Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah stand before Moses, Eleazar, and the entire congregation to argue their case: their father died in the wilderness without sons, and they ask for a land inheritance to preserve his name. Their father, they note, was not among Korah's rebels — he died for his own sin, like the rest of his generation.

**Calvin** praised the daughters' boldness and intelligence, noting that they reasoned from theological principle (the injustice of their father's name being lost) and presented their case through proper channels.

### God's Verdict (27:5-11)

God rules in the daughters' favor: "The daughters of Zelophehad are right. You shall give them possession of an inheritance." He then establishes a broader law of inheritance succession: daughters, brothers, uncles, and nearest kinsmen, in that order.

**Matthew Henry** saw this as evidence that God's law, while given in a patriarchal context, contains principles of justice that transcend cultural norms. When confronted with a legitimate claim, God adjusts the legal framework.

### Moses Views the Land (27:12-14)

God tells Moses to ascend Mount Abarim and view the promised land, then he will die. The reason is restated: "You rebelled against my command... at the waters of Meribah." Moses' response is not protest but concern for the people.

### Joshua Commissioned (27:15-23)

Moses asks God to appoint a successor "that the congregation of the LORD may not be as sheep that have no shepherd." God designates Joshua, "a man in whom is the Spirit," and Moses lays hands on him before the congregation.

## Theological Significance

### Justice and Gender

The case of Zelophehad's daughters has been significant in Protestant discussions of women's roles and dignity. **Gordon Wenham** notes that the narrative portrays the women as legally competent, theologically articulate, and divinely vindicated. While Protestant traditions differ on the implications for church office, the text undeniably affirms women's standing before God and their right to justice.

### God's Law Is Not Static

God's response to a new situation — daughters without brothers seeking inheritance — demonstrates that divine law has principled flexibility. Reformed interpreters draw from this the understanding that God's moral principles are unchanging, but their application may develop as new circumstances arise. This is the basis for the Reformed principle that "the general equity" of the law remains binding even when specific applications change.

### Leadership Succession

The commissioning of Joshua establishes principles of leadership transition that Protestant ecclesiology has drawn on extensively:

- The outgoing leader should actively prepare the successor
- Succession is public, not private
- Authority is conferred through the laying on of hands
- The new leader stands in continuity with the old but is not identical

### Moses' Selflessness

Moses' first response to learning of his own death is concern for the people, not grief for himself. **R.K. Harrison** sees this as the capstone of Moses' character development: the man who began by making excuses (Exodus 3-4) ends by thinking only of others. Evangelical interpreters hold this up as the mark of mature spiritual leadership.

### The Shepherd Metaphor

Moses' request for a leader so that the people will not be "as sheep without a shepherd" introduces a metaphor that runs through Scripture to Jesus' compassion for the shepherdless crowds (Matthew 9:36) and His self-identification as the Good Shepherd (John 10:11).
