## Priestly Duties and the Tithe System

Numbers 18 responds to the people's cry of terror at the end of chapter 17 by detailing the priestly system that mediates between a holy God and a sinful people. It specifies the duties of priests and Levites, the portions they receive from offerings, and the tithe system that supports them.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Bearing Iniquity (18:1)

"You and your sons and your father's house with you shall bear iniquity connected with the sanctuary." The priests take upon themselves the responsibility for any violation of the sanctuary's holiness. **Calvin** saw this as a profound burden: the priests stand in the gap, absorbing the risk of the people's approach to God.

This concept of bearing iniquity points directly to Christ, who "bore the sin of many" (Isaiah 53:12). Reformed interpreters consistently identify this as typological foreshadowing.

### Priestly Portions (18:8-19)

Priests receive portions of the grain offerings, sin offerings, guilt offerings, wave offerings, the best of oil, wine, and grain (firstfruits), and every devoted thing. These are called "a covenant of salt forever."

**Matthew Henry** emphasized that God provides for those who serve Him. The priests receive no land inheritance but are sustained by the offerings. This establishes a principle for ministerial support that Paul invokes in 1 Corinthians 9:13-14.

### "I am your portion and your inheritance" (18:20)

This is one of the most striking declarations in Numbers. The Levites receive no territorial inheritance because God Himself is their inheritance. **Luther** found this verse deeply moving, calling it "the highest dignity and the greatest treasure" — to have God Himself as one's possession.

### The Levitical Tithe (18:21-24)

The Levites receive a tenth of all Israel's produce as compensation for their tabernacle service. They in turn tithe from what they receive, giving "a tithe of the tithe" to the priests (18:26-28).

## Theological Significance

### The Priesthood as Mediation

Numbers 18 answers the terror of 17:12-13. Yes, approaching God is dangerous — but God has provided mediators who bear the risk on behalf of the people. Protestant theology sees this entire system as pointing to Christ, the one mediator who bears the full weight of human sin in His approach to God.

### God as Inheritance

The declaration that God is the Levites' inheritance has resonated throughout Protestant devotional literature. Psalm 73:25-26 echoes this theme: "Whom have I in heaven but you?" The Reformers applied this principle to all believers, arguing that earthly possessions are secondary to the treasure of knowing God.

### Support for Ministry

Protestant debates about pastoral compensation have frequently referenced Numbers 18. **Gordon Wenham** notes that the tithe system establishes a clear principle: those who devote themselves to sacred service are to be supported by the community they serve. Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 9 relies on this precedent.

### The Tithe of the Tithe

Even the Levites tithe — from what they receive, they give a tenth to the priests. No one is exempt from giving. Evangelical interpreters draw from this the principle that generosity is required at every level, not just from those with the most resources.

### Service as Privilege

God describes Levitical service as a "gift" (18:7). The privilege of serving God is itself a form of divine grace. This reframes ministry not as a burden imposed but as a gift bestowed — a perspective that has shaped Protestant pastoral theology from the Reformation onward.
