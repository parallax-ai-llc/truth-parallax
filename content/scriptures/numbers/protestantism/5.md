## Purity of the Camp and the Jealousy Ritual

Numbers 5 addresses three matters of communal holiness: the removal of unclean persons from the camp, restitution for wrongs committed, and the elaborate ritual for a wife suspected of adultery (the *sotah* ordeal).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Putting the Unclean Outside the Camp (5:1-4)

Persons with skin diseases, bodily discharges, or corpse contamination are to be sent outside the camp so as not to defile the place where God dwells. Protestant interpreters read this both literally (as ancient hygiene) and typologically (as illustrating sin's incompatibility with God's presence).

**Calvin** argued that these regulations taught Israel to understand spiritual realities through physical pictures: just as uncleanness cannot coexist with God's dwelling, so sin cannot coexist with divine holiness.

### Confession and Restitution (5:5-10)

When a person wrongs another, they must confess the sin and make full restitution plus twenty percent. If no heir of the wronged party can be found, restitution goes to the priest. Protestant commentators highlight the link between confession and tangible restoration.

**Luther** valued this passage for its insistence that repentance must bear fruit in action. Forgiveness is not merely internal; it demands the repair of broken relationships and restoration of what was taken.

### The Sotah Ordeal (5:11-31)

The jealousy ritual involves a woman suspected of adultery drinking "bitter water" mixed with tabernacle dust and dissolved ink from a written curse. If guilty, she suffers physical consequences; if innocent, she is vindicated.

**Matthew Henry** acknowledged the strangeness of this ritual to modern readers but argued it served multiple purposes: protecting innocent wives from false accusation, deterring actual adultery, and placing the judgment in God's hands rather than the husband's.

## Theological Significance

### Holiness of the Community

The chapter's three sections share a common theme: the holiness of the covenant community matters. Protestant theology, especially in its Reformed expressions, has emphasized that the church is called to maintain purity and discipline — not through Old Testament rituals, but through the means Christ has appointed.

### Sin Requires More Than Apology

The restitution law (5:5-10) has been influential in Protestant ethics. **Dietrich Bonhoeffer** echoed its principle in his concept of "costly grace" — genuine repentance involves real cost and real change, not merely verbal acknowledgment.

### God as Judge

The sotah ritual places the verdict in God's hands through a divinely ordained process. Protestant interpreters note that this removes private vengeance from the equation. **Gordon Wenham** argues the ritual actually served to protect women in a patriarchal society by providing a formal mechanism for establishing innocence.

### Typological Readings

Some evangelical interpreters see the sotah ritual as pointing to the relationship between God and Israel, where Israel is the "wife" suspected of spiritual adultery (idolatry). This reading connects to the prophetic tradition of Hosea and Ezekiel, where Israel's unfaithfulness is depicted in marital terms.

### The Church and Discipline

Reformed Protestantism has drawn from Numbers 5 principles for church discipline: sin within the community must be addressed, the process must be orderly and fair, and the goal is restoration and purity rather than punishment for its own sake.
