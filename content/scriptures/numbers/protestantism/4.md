## The Duties of the Levitical Clans

Numbers 4 specifies the duties of the three Levitical clans — Kohathites, Gershonites, and Merarites — in transporting the tabernacle during Israel's wilderness journeys. Each clan is counted (men aged 30 to 50) and assigned specific responsibilities.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Kohathites and the Most Holy Things (4:4-20)

The Kohathites are responsible for carrying the most sacred objects — the ark, the table of showbread, the lampstand, the golden altar, and the vessels of ministry. However, Aaron and his sons must first cover these items before the Kohathites may approach them.

**Calvin** noted that even those closest to the holy things could not look upon them uncovered: "God taught by visible signs what no mortal could see with the naked eye — His unapproachable majesty."

### "They shall not go in to look on the holy things even for a moment, lest they die" (4:20)

This verse has been significant in Protestant discussions of God's holiness. **Luther** used such passages to argue that human beings cannot stand before a holy God by their own merit. The covering of the sacred objects is, for Lutheran interpreters, a picture of the gospel: God's grace covers what would otherwise destroy us.

### The Service Census (4:34-49)

The total number of Levites eligible for tabernacle service was 8,580. Protestant commentators note the specificity: God has a precise role for each servant, and the work is bounded by age and ability. **Matthew Henry** saw this as teaching that God calls workers to specific tasks for specific seasons.

## Theological Significance

### The Danger of Casual Holiness

Numbers 4 makes unmistakably clear that God's holiness is not to be treated casually. The elaborate covering procedures for the holy objects before the Kohathites handle them illustrate what Reformed theologians call the "otherness" of God. **R.C. Sproul** frequently cited passages like this in teaching the holiness of God, arguing that modern Christianity has lost the sense of reverence these texts demand.

### Mediated Access

Aaron and his sons serve as mediators between the holy objects and the Levites. Protestant theology sees this as pointing forward to Christ, the one mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5). The covering of holy things anticipates the veil of Christ's flesh through which believers now approach God.

### Vocation and Calling

Each Levitical clan has a distinct assignment — the Kohathites carry the sacred objects, the Gershonites the curtains and coverings, the Merarites the structural frames. No role is unimportant. Evangelical interpreters draw from this the dignity of every calling within the church. The person who carries a frame is as essential as the one who carries the lampstand.

### The Weight of Ministry

The physical labor of dismantling, carrying, and reassembling the tabernacle was demanding work. **Gordon Wenham** notes that this chapter corrects any romanticized view of priestly or ministerial service. Ministry is real labor, and God assigns it according to capacity and calling.

### Order in Worship

The precise instructions for covering, carrying, and transporting the tabernacle establish a principle that carries through Protestant worship theology: worship should be conducted "decently and in order" (1 Corinthians 14:40). The Regulative Principle of Worship, central to Reformed practice, finds some of its roots in passages like this.
