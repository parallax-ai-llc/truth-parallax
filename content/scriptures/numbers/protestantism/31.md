## The War Against Midian

Numbers 31 recounts Israel's war of vengeance against Midian for their role in the Baal Peor apostasy. The chapter includes the death of Balaam, instructions for purifying warriors and spoils, and the division of plunder. It is one of the most challenging chapters in Numbers for modern readers.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Avenge the people of Israel on the Midianites" (31:2)

God commands this war as His final directive to Moses before his death. It is explicitly framed as divine vengeance for Midian's role in seducing Israel at Peor. **Calvin** was careful to distinguish this divinely commanded warfare from ordinary human aggression, arguing that Israel acted as God's instrument of judgment, not on their own initiative.

### The Death of Balaam (31:8)

Balaam, who could not curse Israel through oracles but succeeded in corrupting them through counsel (31:16), is killed in the battle. **Matthew Henry** noted the fitting end: Balaam died among the very people whose strategy of seduction he had devised. His story is a warning against using spiritual knowledge for corrupt purposes.

### Purification of Warriors and Spoil (31:19-24)

Warriors who killed or touched corpses must undergo seven-day purification outside the camp. Metal objects taken as spoil must be purified by fire; other items by water. **Gordon Wenham** notes that even a divinely commanded war produces defilement — an important theological statement about the nature of violence.

### The Division of Spoils (31:25-47)

The plunder is divided equally between the warriors and the rest of the congregation. From the warriors' share, one-five-hundredth goes to the priest; from the congregation's share, one-fiftieth goes to the Levites. This distribution system ensures that all share in both the costs and benefits of the conflict.

### The Officers' Offering (31:48-54)

The military officers discover that not a single Israelite soldier was killed in the battle. In gratitude, they offer the gold jewelry captured from the Midianites as a memorial offering. The total is 16,750 shekels of gold.

## Theological Significance

### Holy War and Its Limitations

Numbers 31 is one of the most difficult passages in the Old Testament for modern readers. Protestant interpreters have addressed this text with various approaches:

- **Calvin** insisted that this was a unique, unrepeatable divine command, not a model for Christian warfare
- **Luther** distinguished between God's sovereign right to judge nations and any human claim to wage holy war
- **R.K. Harrison** placed the chapter in its ancient Near Eastern context while affirming God's sovereign right to execute judgment through human agents

The Reformation consistently rejected any claim by the church to wage holy war, insisting that these commands were specific to Israel's unique historical situation.

### The Consequences of False Teaching

Balaam's death underscores the gravity of leading others astray. His story, referenced in Jude 11, 2 Peter 2:15, and Revelation 2:14, became in Protestant tradition a paradigmatic warning against false teachers who corrupt God's people for personal gain.

### Purification After Violence

The requirement for warriors to purify themselves after battle conveys a profound theological truth: violence, even when divinely sanctioned, produces defilement. Evangelical interpreters find here a recognition that warfare, however necessary, is never morally neutral.

### Gratitude for Preservation

The officers' spontaneous offering of gratitude for zero casualties models the appropriate response to divine protection. Protestant worship has always included thanksgiving for God's preservation as a central element.

### Justice in Distribution

The detailed system for dividing spoils demonstrates that even in wartime, principles of justice and equity apply. No individual or group may enrich themselves at others' expense. This has been applied in Protestant social ethics to questions of economic fairness.
