## The Red Heifer and Purification from Death

Numbers 19 prescribes a unique purification ritual using the ashes of a red heifer for cleansing from corpse contamination. This enigmatic chapter has fascinated Protestant interpreters for centuries, both for its unusual ritual details and its rich typological potential.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Red Heifer (19:1-10)

A red heifer without blemish, on which no yoke has been placed, is slaughtered outside the camp. It is burned entirely — skin, flesh, blood, and dung — along with cedar wood, hyssop, and scarlet yarn. The resulting ashes are stored for use in the "water of purification."

**Calvin** noted the uniqueness of this sacrifice: the entire animal is consumed, nothing is eaten by the priest, and the ashes serve a continuing purpose rather than being discarded. He saw this as pointing beyond the ordinary sacrificial system to something greater.

### Application of the Purification Water (19:11-13)

Anyone who touches a corpse becomes unclean for seven days and must be sprinkled with the purification water on the third and seventh days. Failure to undergo purification results in being "cut off from Israel."

**Matthew Henry** observed that death — the consequence of sin — renders everything it touches unclean. The pervasiveness of death contamination illustrates the pervasiveness of sin's effects.

### "Whoever touches the dead body of any person... and does not cleanse himself, defiles the tabernacle of the LORD" (19:13)

Individual impurity affects the entire community and defiles the sanctuary itself. Protestant commentators draw from this the interconnected nature of sin within the covenant community — no sin is purely private.

## Theological Significance

### Christ as the Red Heifer

The writer of Hebrews explicitly connects the red heifer to Christ: "If the blood of goats and bulls, and the sprinkling of defiled persons with the ashes of a heifer, sanctify for the purification of the flesh, how much more will the blood of Christ... purify our conscience from dead works" (Hebrews 9:13-14).

Protestant interpreters have elaborated the typological connections:

| Red Heifer | Christ |
|-----------|--------|
| Without blemish | Sinless |
| No yoke | Never in bondage to sin |
| Slaughtered outside the camp | Crucified outside Jerusalem |
| Entirely consumed | Gave Himself completely |
| Ashes for ongoing purification | His blood cleanses continually |

### Death as the Great Defilement

The chapter's focus on corpse contamination reflects the theological reality that death is the ultimate consequence of sin and the great enemy of God's creation. **Gordon Wenham** argues that the purification ritual teaches Israel to take death seriously as an alien intrusion into God's good world — a perspective that aligns with the New Testament's treatment of death as "the last enemy" (1 Corinthians 15:26).

### The Paradox of the Ritual

Those who prepare the purification water themselves become temporarily unclean (19:7-8, 21). **R.K. Harrison** notes this paradox: the means of cleansing renders its handlers unclean. Reformed interpreters see here a picture of Christ "who knew no sin" being "made sin for us" (2 Corinthians 5:21) — the pure one bearing impurity so that the impure may be cleansed.

### Ongoing Efficacy

The ashes are stored and used repeatedly over time. Protestant interpreters find in this a picture of the ongoing efficacy of Christ's single sacrifice — offered once, effective continually (Hebrews 10:12-14).
