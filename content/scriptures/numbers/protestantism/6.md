## The Nazirite Vow and the Aaronic Blessing

Numbers 6 contains two distinct but theologically rich sections: the regulations for the Nazirite vow (6:1-21) and the priestly blessing that Aaron and his sons are to pronounce over Israel (6:22-27).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Nazirite Vow (6:1-21)

A Nazirite voluntarily consecrates himself or herself to God for a specific period, abstaining from wine and grape products, avoiding contact with the dead, and leaving the hair uncut. Notable Nazirites in Scripture include Samson, Samuel, and John the Baptist.

**Calvin** noted the remarkable feature that this vow was open to both men and women, seeing in it a principle of spiritual equality before God. The vow represents an intensification of the holiness that all Israel was called to embody.

**Matthew Henry** emphasized that the Nazirite vow was voluntary — it was an act of devotion beyond what was required. This anticipates the New Testament teaching that believers may make special commitments of service to God beyond ordinary duty.

### The Three Prohibitions

| Prohibition | Symbolic Meaning |
|------------|-----------------|
| No wine or grape products | Self-denial, sobriety, separation from worldly pleasure |
| No contact with the dead | Complete ritual purity, life-orientation |
| No cutting of hair | Visible mark of consecration, strength in God |

Lutheran interpreters have been cautious about drawing too direct an application from Nazirite restrictions, warning against legalism while affirming the principle of voluntary consecration.

### The Aaronic Blessing (6:24-26)

"The LORD bless you and keep you; the LORD make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you; the LORD lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace."

This is one of the most beloved passages in all of Scripture. Protestant worship traditions across denominations have used this benediction as a closing blessing. **Luther** called it "the most sublime and most comprehensive blessing in the entire Old Testament."

## Theological Significance

### Voluntary Consecration

The Nazirite vow demonstrates that within the covenant community, individuals may freely offer themselves to God in extraordinary ways. Reformed interpreters connect this to the doctrine of Christian liberty — believers are free to take on additional spiritual disciplines as acts of devotion, so long as they do not impose them as requirements on others.

### Christ as the True Nazirite

Many Protestant commentators see typological significance in the Nazirite. Jesus, who was "set apart" from birth, perfectly consecrated to God's service, and whose life was entirely devoted to the Father's will, fulfills the Nazirite ideal. His title "Jesus of Nazareth" — while geographically derived — has been connected by some interpreters to the Nazirite concept.

### The Threefold Blessing and the Trinity

Protestant interpreters from the Reformation onward have noted the threefold structure of the Aaronic blessing. While not a proof of the Trinity, it has been read as consistent with Trinitarian theology:

- "The LORD bless you and keep you" — the Father's providential care
- "The LORD make his face shine upon you" — the Son as the light of the world
- "The LORD lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace" — the Spirit's gift of peace

### Grace Precedes Law

**Gordon Wenham** observes that the blessing is placed after the laws of chapters 1-6, reminding Israel that God's ultimate posture toward His people is one of blessing, not condemnation. This resonates deeply with the Protestant emphasis on grace as the foundation of the believer's relationship with God.

### "Put My Name upon the People" (6:27)

The chapter concludes with God's declaration that through this blessing, the priests "shall put my name upon the people of Israel, and I will bless them." Evangelical interpreters stress that the power of the blessing lies not in the priest but in the divine Name — it is God Himself who blesses.
