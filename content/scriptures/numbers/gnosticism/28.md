## The Daily and Festival Offerings

Numbers 28 prescribes the regular sacrificial calendar: the daily burnt offering (tamid), Sabbath offerings, new moon offerings, Passover offerings, and the feast of Weeks. For Gnostic interpreters, this systematic schedule of animal sacrifice reveals the Demiurge's insatiable appetite for material offerings and his reduction of time itself to a sacrificial grid.

## Gnostic Critique

### Valentinian Reading

The Valentinian teacher Heracleon, as reported by Origen, argued that the Demiurge's sacrificial system represents the psychic level of worship — sincere but ultimately inadequate. The daily tamid offering, requiring two lambs per day without ceasing, reveals a god who must be constantly fed. The Valentinian reading contrasts this with the Pleroma, where the Father is utterly self-sufficient, needing nothing from his emanations. The Demiurge's daily requirement for blood and fire exposes his dependency on his creation — a reversed relationship that betrays his derivative, insufficient nature.

### Sethian Perspective

The *Testimony of Truth* explicitly condemns animal sacrifice as the practice of a bloodthirsty deity. Numbers 28's detailed quantification of animals — two lambs daily, plus additional bulls, rams, and lambs for each Sabbath, new moon, and festival — allows the Sethian reader to calculate the enormous toll of animal life demanded by the Demiurge's system. This systematic killing, performed in the name of holiness, is in the Sethian view precisely what distinguishes the Demiurge from the true God: the true God gives life; the Demiurge takes it.

## The Demiurge's Role

The Demiurge describes these offerings as "my food" (*lehem*), "my offerings made by fire," and "a pleasing aroma" (28:2). The language is strikingly physical: a deity who eats, who smells, who finds pleasure in the scent of burning animal flesh. This is not the language of transcendence but of material appetite. Jonas argued that the Gnostic rejection of sacrifice was rooted in this anthropomorphic theology: a god who requires food is a god of the material realm, not the spiritual one.

The calendrical structure of the offerings — daily, weekly (Sabbath), monthly (new moon), annually (festivals) — imposes the Demiurge's temporal grid on every aspect of life. Time itself becomes a medium of archontic control, with each temporal unit marked by its required sacrifice. The *Apocryphon of John* describes how the archons created time (*chronos*) to bind souls to cycles of birth, death, and obligation. The sacrificial calendar of Numbers 28 is the liturgical expression of this temporal bondage.

## Contrast with Pneumatic Gnosis

The pneumatic offers no animal sacrifice because the true God requires none. The *Gospel of Thomas* (Saying 14) records Jesus declaring: "If you fast, you will beget sin for yourselves; if you pray, you will be condemned; if you give alms, you will harm your spirits." This radical rejection of conventional religious practice points toward a worship that transcends all material transactions — the pure contemplation of the divine, the interior recognition of the Pleroma, the awakening of the spark.

Pearson notes that the absence of sacrificial practice in Gnostic communities was one of their most distinctive features, rooted in the theological conviction that the god who demands sacrifice is not the God who deserves worship. The elaborate sacrificial calendar of Numbers 28 represents, in Gnostic perspective, the fullest expression of the Demiurge's material religion — and therefore the fullest expression of what gnosis transcends.
