## Victories, the Bronze Serpent, and Songs of the Journey

Numbers 21 covers Israel's victory over the Canaanite king of Arad, the incident of the fiery serpents and the bronze serpent, the continued journey with stops at various wilderness locations (including the song of the well), and victories over the Amorite kings Sihon and Og. The chapter marks a turning point: the new generation begins to achieve victories, and the horizon of the Promised Land draws near.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### The Fiery Serpents (21:4-6)

The people complain again about the food and water, speaking against God and Moses. God sends "fiery serpents" (*seraphim*) whose bites are lethal. The name *seraphim* — "burning ones" — connects serpents with the angelic beings of Isaiah 6. Origen interprets the serpents as the consequences of sin itself: when we turn from God, the very passions that seemed harmless reveal their deadly nature. Orthodox ascetical theology teaches that sin always carries within it the seeds of its own punishment.

### "Make a fiery serpent and set it on a pole" (21:8-9)

God commands Moses to fashion a bronze serpent and lift it on a pole; anyone bitten who looks upon it will live. Christ Himself provides the definitive interpretation: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in Him should not perish but have eternal life" (John 3:14-15). This is arguably the single most important christological type in Numbers.

The bronze serpent on the pole is one of the most frequently depicted Old Testament types in Orthodox iconography. The cruciform shape of the pole, the act of looking upward in faith, and the healing that follows — all point to the Cross. St. John Chrysostom elaborates: just as the bronze serpent bore the likeness of the deadly serpent but carried no venom, so Christ took on the likeness of sinful flesh (Rom. 8:3) but was Himself without sin.

### The Song of the Well (21:17-18)

"Spring up, O well! Sing to it!" This brief song celebrates God's provision of water. Origen reads the well as a type of Scripture itself — a source of living water that must be "dug" through spiritual labor and study. The Orthodox practice of lectio divina, nourished by the patristic commentaries, is this digging: the surface text yields deeper meaning to those who labor in prayer and attention.

## Theological Significance

### The Cross as the Cure for Sin

The bronze serpent is the Old Testament's clearest image of the paradox of the Cross. The instrument of death (the serpent) becomes the instrument of healing when lifted up by God's command. The Cross, the instrument of shameful execution, becomes the instrument of cosmic salvation when Christ is lifted upon it. Orthodox theology of the Cross celebrates this reversal: "By the Cross, joy has come to all the world."

### Looking and Living

The act of looking at the bronze serpent is an act of faith — trust in God's word against appearances. The serpent on the pole does not look like a cure; it looks like the problem. Similarly, the Crucified Christ does not look like a savior; He looks like a defeated criminal. Faith sees beyond appearances to the reality of God's saving work. The Orthodox practice of venerating the Cross — kissing it, bowing before it, carrying it in procession — enacts this faith.

### Victory Through God's Power

The military victories over Arad, Sihon, and Og demonstrate that the new generation is learning what the old generation refused to believe: God fights for His people. Orthodox prayers before battle and the blessing of military standards appeal to this biblical precedent, always with the caveat that victory belongs to God, not to human prowess.
