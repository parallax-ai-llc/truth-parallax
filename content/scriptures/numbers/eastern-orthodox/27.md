## The Daughters' Petition and the Commissioning of Joshua

Numbers 27 contains two significant episodes. First, the five daughters of Zelophehad — Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah — petition Moses for their father's inheritance, since he died without sons. God affirms their claim and establishes the precedent that daughters may inherit when there are no sons. Second, God tells Moses to ascend Mount Abarim to view the Promised Land he will not enter, and commands him to commission Joshua as his successor by laying hands on him before the priest and the congregation.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "Our father died in the wilderness... Why should the name of our father be removed?" (27:3-4)

The daughters' appeal is grounded in justice: their father's name and inheritance should not perish because he had no sons. God validates their claim: "The daughters of Zelophehad speak what is right." Origen sees this as evidence that God's law, while given in specific historical circumstances, contains principles of justice that can be extended and applied to new situations. The Orthodox canonical tradition operates similarly — the canons provide foundational principles, and the bishops apply them with *oikonomia* (pastoral wisdom) to new circumstances.

### "Give us a possession among our father's brothers" (27:4)

The boldness of these women in approaching Moses and the tribal leaders is remarkable in its ancient context. Orthodox tradition honors bold women of faith — from the Theotokos at the Annunciation ("Let it be to me according to your word") to the Myrrh-bearing Women who went to the tomb when the male disciples hid. The daughters of Zelophehad stand in this lineage of courageous female faithfulness.

### The Commissioning of Joshua (27:18-23)

God instructs Moses to lay hands on Joshua, who "has the Spirit in him," before Eleazar the priest and the whole congregation. This is the definitive Old Testament ordination. The laying on of hands (*semikha*) transfers authority publicly and sacramentally. Orthodox ordination preserves this form exactly: the bishop lays hands on the candidate in the presence of the clergy and the assembled faithful, invoking the Holy Spirit.

### "Set him before Eleazar the priest and before all the congregation" (27:19)

The commissioning is public, not private. Ordination in the Orthodox Church must take place during the Divine Liturgy, in the presence of the people, because ministry is exercised within and for the community. The congregation's "Axios!" ratifies what God has initiated.

## Theological Significance

### Justice and the Development of Law

The case of Zelophehad's daughters shows that God's law is not static but responsive to genuine claims of justice. Orthodox canon law develops through conciliar decisions, patriarchal rulings, and episcopal application — always in continuity with the foundational principles of Scripture and Tradition, but attentive to new circumstances and genuine needs.

### The Transition from Moses to Joshua

Moses represents the Law; Joshua represents salvation (his name means "the Lord saves"). The transition from Moses to Joshua prefigures the transition from the Old Covenant to the New, from Law to Grace. Orthodox theology holds that the Law is not abolished but fulfilled — Moses sees the land, and Joshua enters it, but both serve God's purpose.

### Leadership as Shepherding

God describes Joshua's role in pastoral language: the people should not be "like sheep which have no shepherd" (27:17). This image of the leader as shepherd is foundational to Orthodox ecclesiology. The bishop carries a pastoral staff; the priest is called a "shepherd of souls." Christ, the Good Shepherd, is the archetype, and all pastoral ministry is participation in His shepherding.
