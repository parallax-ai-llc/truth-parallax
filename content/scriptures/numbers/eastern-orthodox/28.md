## The Calendar of Regular Offerings

Numbers 28 prescribes the regular sacrificial offerings that structure Israel's worship life: the daily (*tamid*) offering of two lambs (morning and evening), the additional Sabbath offering, the monthly offering at the new moon, and the offerings for the Feast of Passover and Unleavened Bread. Together with chapter 29, this section establishes the complete liturgical calendar of Israel.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "My offering, My food for My offerings made by fire" (28:2)

God speaks of the offerings as "My food" — not because God literally eats, but because the sacrificial language establishes a covenant meal between God and His people. St. John Chrysostom notes that God accommodates His language to human understanding (*synkatabasis*). The Orthodox Eucharist fulfills this pattern: the bread and wine are offered to God and returned to the faithful as the Body and Blood of Christ — the ultimate covenant meal.

### The Daily Offering: Morning and Evening (28:3-8)

Two lambs each day — one in the morning, one at evening — frame the day with sacrifice. This daily rhythm is the origin of the canonical hours of prayer. The Orthodox cycle of daily services — Orthros (Matins) and Vespers as the primary poles, with the other hours filling the day — preserves this biblical pattern of consecrating time through regular worship. The morning and evening offerings ensure that no day passes without explicit acknowledgment of God's sovereignty.

### The Sabbath Offering (28:9-10)

On the Sabbath, the daily offering is doubled. The day of rest receives additional worship, not less. Orthodox Sunday worship — the Divine Liturgy as the center of the week — follows this logic. The Lord's Day is not merely a day of inactivity but a day of intensified worship, the weekly Pascha celebrating Christ's Resurrection.

### The New Moon Offering (28:11-15)

The monthly offering at the new moon marks the passage of liturgical time. While the Orthodox Church does not observe new moon festivals, the principle of marking time's passage with worship is preserved in the monthly commemorations of saints, the monthly *paraklesis* (supplicatory canon to the Theotokos), and the liturgical cycle that ensures no month passes without focused devotion.

## Theological Significance

### The Sanctification of Time

Numbers 28 establishes that time itself is not neutral but is to be consecrated through regular worship. The Orthodox understanding of time is deeply liturgical: each day, each week, each month, each season has its particular spiritual character. The liturgical calendar transforms chronological time (*chronos*) into sacred time (*kairos*).

### The Lamb of God

The daily offering of lambs points directly to Christ, "the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29). The Orthodox Proskomedia (Preparation of the Gifts) explicitly invokes this connection: the priest cuts the Lamb (the central portion of the altar bread) with a lance, reciting the words of Isaiah 53 and John 19. The daily tamid finds its eternal fulfillment in the once-for-all sacrifice of Christ, made present in every Eucharist.

### The Discipline of Regularity

The tamid offering is offered regardless of circumstances — in peace and war, in prosperity and famine. The Orthodox insistence on the daily cycle of prayer, particularly in monastic communities where the full cycle is observed, reflects this biblical discipline. Regularity in prayer is not mere routine but the training ground for constant communion with God.

### Community Worship as Primary

These offerings are communal, not individual. They are offered on behalf of the entire people. The Divine Liturgy is similarly a corporate act: even when celebrated with a small congregation, it is offered "on behalf of all and for all." Private devotion is valuable, but the communal sacrifice is the heart of the Church's worship.
