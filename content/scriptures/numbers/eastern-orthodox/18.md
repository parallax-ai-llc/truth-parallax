## The Support of the Priesthood

Numbers 18 addresses the practical economics of sacred service. Aaron and his sons bear responsibility for any transgressions related to the sanctuary, and the Levites assist them. In return, the priests receive portions of the offerings — grain offerings, sin offerings, guilt offerings, and firstfruits. The Levites receive the tithes of Israel as their inheritance, and they in turn tithe their tithe to the priests. The chapter emphasizes that the Lord Himself is the inheritance of the priestly tribe.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "I am your portion and your inheritance among the children of Israel" (18:20)

This extraordinary declaration — that God Himself is the Levites' inheritance — is the foundation of the theology of ordained ministry. The priest possesses no land, no tribal territory; his portion is God alone. Origen reads this as the highest possible calling: to have God as one's inheritance is to possess everything that matters. Orthodox monasticism takes this principle to its fullest expression: the monk renounces property, marriage, and self-will to possess God alone.

### The Heave Offering and the Wave Offering (18:8-19)

The portions given to the priests from the people's sacrifices establish the principle that those who serve the altar live from the altar (cf. 1 Cor. 9:13). St. Paul explicitly cites this Old Testament pattern to justify the support of Christian ministers. The Orthodox Church continues this practice: the faithful support their clergy and their parishes through offerings, and the clergy receive their sustenance from the community they serve.

### "A covenant of salt forever" (18:19)

The provisions for the priests are described as a "covenant of salt" — an irrevocable, perpetual agreement. Salt preserves, purifies, and flavors. The Orthodox use of blessed salt in various rites (blessing of water, consecration of a church) connects to this biblical symbolism. The priestly covenant, sealed with salt, is a covenant of enduring faithfulness between God and those who serve Him.

### The Tithe of the Tithe (18:25-32)

The Levites receive tithes from the people but must themselves tithe what they receive, giving the best portion to the priests. This principle of graduated giving — even those who receive must give — prevents the creation of a privileged clerical class that only takes and never offers. Orthodox clergy are expected to be generous in their own giving, modeling the stewardship they teach.

## Theological Significance

### The Economics of the Kingdom

Numbers 18 demonstrates that God's community has a material dimension. Spiritual service does not exempt one from economic realities, and economic provision for ministers is a sacred obligation, not an optional kindness. The Orthodox tradition of supporting monasteries, seminaries, and parishes reflects this biblical mandate.

### God as Inheritance

The radical claim that God is the Levites' portion transforms the concept of inheritance. While the other tribes measure wealth in land and livestock, the priestly tribe measures wealth in proximity to God. This inversion of values — the last shall be first, the poor in spirit inherit the Kingdom — runs through the entire biblical narrative and reaches its fullest expression in the Beatitudes.

### The Priesthood and the Eucharist

The priests eating the offerings in a holy place (18:10) prefigures the clergy's reception of the Eucharist at the altar. In the Orthodox Liturgy, the clergy commune first, within the sanctuary, before distributing the gifts to the laity. This practice preserves the biblical pattern of Numbers 18 while pointing beyond it to the eschatological banquet.
