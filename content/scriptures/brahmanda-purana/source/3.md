---
title: "Brahmanda Purana Upodghata Pada (Book 3)"
scripture: "brahmanda-purana"
chapter: 3
---

**1** The Upodghata Pada describes the paths to liberation, the nature of the soul, and the means by which the individual attains union with the Supreme.

**2** The Purana teaches: "The soul is a spark of the divine fire, temporarily enclosed in the body. It is eternal, unchanging, and blissful by nature. Its bondage is caused by ignorance, and its liberation is achieved through knowledge."

**3** Three paths to liberation are described. Jnana Yoga, the path of knowledge: through the discrimination of the real from the unreal, the aspirant realizes that the Self alone is real and the world is an appearance.

**4** Bhakti Yoga, the path of devotion: through love for the Lord, surrender to his will, and constant remembrance of his name, the devotee attains union with the divine. This path is accessible to all, regardless of caste, learning, or status.

**5** Karma Yoga, the path of selfless action: through the performance of duty without attachment to results, the aspirant purifies the mind and prepares it for the reception of knowledge. Action performed as an offering to the Lord leads to liberation.

**6** The Purana describes the practice of meditation: "The aspirant should sit in a secluded place, upon a seat neither too high nor too low. He should fix his gaze upon the tip of his nose, control his breath, and concentrate his mind upon the form of the Lord within the heart."

**7** The obstacles to meditation are enumerated: desire, anger, greed, delusion, pride, and jealousy. These six enemies of the soul must be conquered through constant practice and dispassion. The sage who conquers these attains the peace of Brahman.

**8** The Purana teaches the Kundalini Yoga. The serpent power, Kundalini, lies coiled at the base of the spine. Through the practice of Pranayama, mantra, and meditation, it rises through the six chakras — Muladhara, Svadhisthana, Manipura, Anahata, Vishuddha, and Ajna — to the Sahasrara at the crown of the head, where it unites with Shiva.

**9** The Purana describes the state of Samadhi: "When the mind is completely absorbed in the divine, when all distinctions between the knower, the known, and the act of knowing disappear, when the individual consciousness merges with the cosmic consciousness — that is Samadhi."

**10** The sage teaches: "Liberation is not something to be achieved in the future. It is the natural state of the soul, obscured by ignorance. When ignorance is removed through knowledge, devotion, or practice, the soul shines in its own light, like the sun emerging from behind the clouds."

**11** The Purana describes the liberated soul: "The liberated one sees the Self in all beings and all beings in the Self. He is free from desire, fear, and anger. He is beyond pleasure and pain, honor and disgrace. He dwells in the eternal present, at peace with all."

**12** The sage concluded: "The three paths — knowledge, devotion, and action — are not separate but complementary. They are like three rivers that flow into the same ocean. The aspirant should practice all three according to his nature and capacity."
