## Overview

The Gospel of Philip is not a Jewish text, and it holds no place in the Jewish canon or liturgical tradition. Nevertheless, it is of considerable interest to Jewish scholars because of its extensive engagement with the Hebrew Bible — particularly Genesis — and its radical reinterpretation of concepts drawn from Jewish theology. The text appropriates Jewish ideas about names, the Temple, the bridal canopy (*chuppah*), and the fall of Adam, reworking them within a Valentinian Gnostic framework that often inverts their original meaning.

## Jewish Source Material in Philip

The Gospel of Philip draws heavily on Jewish traditions while subverting them. The text discusses the power of names — a theme deeply rooted in Jewish mysticism and the theology of the divine Name (*Shem*) — but applies it to a Gnostic theory in which ordinary names deceive and only secret names reveal truth. Philip's reinterpretation of Adam, Eve, and the Garden of Eden stands in sharp contrast to rabbinic readings: where the Midrash sees the fall as a moral failure redeemable through Torah and repentance, Philip treats it as a cosmic fragmentation requiring mystical reunion in the bridal chamber.

## Scholarly Significance

Jewish scholars have contributed to the academic study of the Gospel of Philip by tracing its use and transformation of Second Temple Jewish traditions, including merkabah mysticism, wisdom theology, and priestly imagery. The text's anti-demiurgic stance — its characterization of the Creator God as ignorant or malevolent — represents a direct challenge to the Jewish affirmation of God's goodness and the goodness of creation. This tension makes the Gospel of Philip a valuable case study in the complex relationship between Judaism and early Gnostic movements.
