## The Perfect Human

The final chapter of the Gospel of Philip describes the "perfect human" as one who has achieved complete spiritual unity. Jewish theology has its own understanding of human perfection, exemplified in the figure of the *tzaddik* (righteous one) — the person who lives in complete alignment with God's will as expressed in the Torah. The kabbalistic tradition speaks of *tikkun* (repair) — the restoration of cosmic harmony through righteous action and the observance of the commandments.

However, the Jewish understanding of perfection differs fundamentally from the Valentinian vision. The *tzaddik* achieves perfection through concrete action in the material world — deeds of lovingkindness (*gemilut chasadim*), study of Torah, prayer, and the fulfillment of the 613 commandments. This perfection is communal and this-worldly: it sanctifies the material order rather than transcending it. The Valentinian "perfect human" who has become "a Christ" through esoteric sacramental initiation reflects a model of perfection that is individual, otherworldly, and detached from the covenantal framework that defines Jewish spiritual life.
