## Knowledge and Ignorance

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Philip meditates on the relationship between names and truth, arguing that names deceive and conceal rather than reveal reality. Jewish scholars recognize in this discussion a transformed version of Jewish traditions about the power and sanctity of names — particularly the divine Name (*Shem ha-Meforash*), which in rabbinic and mystical tradition possesses creative and revelatory power. The Talmud discusses the restrictions on pronouncing the Tetragrammaton, and the kabbalistic tradition assigns profound significance to the letters of the divine Name.

Philip's treatment diverges from these Jewish sources in a crucial respect: where Jewish tradition affirms that the divine Name genuinely reveals something true about God's nature and relationship to Israel, Philip's Valentinian framework treats all names as fundamentally deceptive — even the names "God" and "Father." This radical nominalism reflects the Gnostic conviction that the material world, including its linguistic structures, is the product of an ignorant creator. Jewish theology, by contrast, affirms the goodness of creation and the capacity of language — especially sacred language — to mediate genuine encounter with the divine.
