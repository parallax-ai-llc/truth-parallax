## The Sacraments

Chapter 2 describes five Valentinian sacraments: baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, and the bridal chamber. Jewish scholars note that several of these rites have antecedents in Jewish practice. Baptism echoes the *mikveh* (ritual immersion bath) used for purification in Second Temple Judaism. Anointing with oil recalls the consecration of priests and kings in the Hebrew Bible. The notion of a sacred meal resonates with the Passover *seder* and other sacrificial meals of the Temple period.

However, the Valentinian transformation of these practices removes them from their Jewish context and purpose. The *mikveh* purifies the body for participation in the community's sacred life; Valentinian baptism initiates the soul into esoteric knowledge. Biblical anointing consecrates individuals for service to God and the community; Philip's chrism confers a secret spiritual nature. The Jewish ritual system is oriented toward holiness within the created order, affirming the goodness of the body and the material world. The Valentinian sacramental system, by contrast, is oriented toward escape from the material order — a fundamental inversion of Jewish values.
