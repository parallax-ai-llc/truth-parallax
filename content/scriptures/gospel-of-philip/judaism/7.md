## Resurrection and the Living

Chapter 7 teaches that resurrection must be experienced during one's lifetime rather than after death. Jewish teaching on resurrection varies across periods and movements, but the Pharisaic and rabbinic traditions affirm the future bodily resurrection of the dead (*techiyat ha-metim*) as a fundamental article of faith. Maimonides listed it as the thirteenth of his Thirteen Principles of Faith, and the *Amidah* prayer praises God as the one who "revives the dead" (*mechayeh ha-metim*).

The Gospel of Philip's purely spiritual, present-tense understanding of resurrection has no parallel in mainstream Jewish theology. The rabbis debated many details of the resurrection — when, how, for whom — but they consistently affirmed its future, bodily character. The Sadducees, who denied resurrection, were opposed by the Pharisees precisely on this point (Acts 23:8). Jewish hope for the world to come (*olam ha-ba*) includes the restoration of the body and the renewal of creation, not escape from materiality into a disembodied spiritual state. The Valentinian redefinition of resurrection as present gnosis represents a departure from the Jewish eschatological vision.
