## Adam and the Fall

Chapter 6 reinterprets the Genesis narrative of Adam and Eve, presenting their separation as a cosmic catastrophe orchestrated by archonic forces. Jewish interpretation of Genesis 2-3 diverges sharply from this Valentinian reading. In rabbinic tradition, the creation of Eve is a divine act of compassion — "It is not good for the man to be alone" (Genesis 2:18) — and the relationship between Adam and Eve is the foundation of human community and the institution of marriage.

Judaism does not share the Christian (particularly Augustinian) doctrine of original sin, and it diverges even further from the Valentinian reading. The rabbinic tradition teaches that every person possesses both a *yetzer ha-tov* (good inclination) and a *yetzer ha-ra* (evil inclination), and that the task of the moral life is to channel both inclinations in the service of God through Torah observance. The fall is not a cosmic tragedy from which humanity cannot recover but a moral failure that each person can address through repentance (*teshuvah*) and righteous living. The Valentinian reinterpretation, which locates the problem in cosmic fragmentation rather than moral choice, is alien to the Jewish understanding.
