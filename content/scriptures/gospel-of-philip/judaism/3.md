## Christ and Mary Magdalene

Chapter 3 presents Mary Magdalene as the "companion" of Christ. While the figures of Jesus and Mary Magdalene are not subjects of Jewish theology per se, Jewish scholars have contributed to the historical and sociological analysis of this passage by situating it within the context of Second Temple and early rabbinic Judaism. The role of women in Jewish religious life during this period was complex: women participated in synagogue worship, patronized religious movements, and in some cases exercised teaching authority, though formal rabbinic leadership remained largely male.

The Valentinian portrayal of Mary Magdalene as the bearer of secret knowledge and the feminine complement to a male revealer draws on traditions that have partial analogues in Jewish wisdom literature, where Sophia (Wisdom) is personified as a feminine divine figure (Proverbs 8, Wisdom of Solomon 7). Jewish scholars note that the Valentinian appropriation transforms the Jewish Wisdom tradition into something that would be unrecognizable to its original authors — a cosmic feminine principle paired with a divine male in a Gnostic syzygy rather than a personification of Torah and divine creativity.
