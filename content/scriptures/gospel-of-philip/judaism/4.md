## The Bridal Chamber Mystery

Chapter 4 describes the bridal chamber as the supreme Valentinian sacrament, identifying it with the Holy of Holies in the Jerusalem Temple. Jewish scholars find this identification significant because it appropriates two deeply Jewish institutions — the Temple and the marriage canopy (*chuppah*) — and fuses them within a Gnostic framework. In rabbinic tradition, the Holy of Holies was the place where God's *Shekhinah* (divine presence) dwelt among Israel, accessible only to the High Priest on Yom Kippur. The *chuppah* symbolizes the home that the bride and groom will build together, blessed by God's presence.

The Valentinian bridal chamber transforms both of these into a rite of individual spiritual reunion with an angelic counterpart — a concept foreign to Jewish theology. Judaism understands marriage as a concrete, embodied covenant between two persons, sanctified by God and oriented toward the building of family and community. The mystical dimension of marriage in Jewish thought — expressed in the Song of Songs and in kabbalistic teaching about the union of the *Kadosh Baruch Hu* (Holy One) and the *Shekhinah* — remains rooted in the material world and the covenantal relationship between God and Israel, not in escape from materiality.
