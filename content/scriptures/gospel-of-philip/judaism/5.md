## Light and Darkness

Chapter 5 explores Valentinian dualism between light and darkness, treating the material world as a realm of inferior darkness trapping sparks of divine light. Jewish theology affirms the fundamental goodness of creation: "God saw all that He had made, and behold, it was very good" (Genesis 1:31). Light and darkness in the Jewish framework are both creations of God — "I form light and create darkness, I make peace and create evil; I, the Lord, do all these things" (Isaiah 45:7) — not independent cosmic principles.

The rabbinic tradition develops a nuanced understanding of the interplay between light and darkness. The Midrash teaches that the primordial light of creation (*or haganuz*) was hidden away for the righteous in the world to come. The kabbalistic tradition associates light with the *sefirot* and the emanation of divine energy into the world. But in all cases, darkness is not an independent evil force or evidence of a flawed creation — it is part of God's sovereign creative act. The Valentinian devaluation of the material world as a realm of darkness produced by an ignorant Demiurge represents a fundamental rejection of the Jewish affirmation of creation.
