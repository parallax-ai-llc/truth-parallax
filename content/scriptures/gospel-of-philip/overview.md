## Overview

The Gospel of Philip is a Valentinian Gnostic text discovered in 1945 among the Nag Hammadi codices (NHC II,3) in Upper Egypt. Unlike the canonical Gospels, it is not a narrative of Jesus' life but a collection of theological reflections, parables, and aphorisms that explore the nature of sacraments, the relationship between Christ and the soul, and the mystery of the "bridal chamber." The text is attributed to Philip the Apostle, though its actual authorship and date remain uncertain — most scholars place its composition in the late second or third century CE, likely in Syria.

## Structure

The Gospel of Philip is organized thematically rather than narratively, and its structure has been variously divided by scholars. This edition follows an eight-chapter division:

1. **Knowledge and Ignorance** — Names, truth, and the limits of language
2. **The Sacraments** — Baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, and bridal chamber
3. **Christ and Mary Magdalene** — The companion and sacred partnership
4. **The Bridal Chamber Mystery** — The central sacramental mystery
5. **Light and Darkness** — Dualism and spiritual transformation
6. **Adam and the Fall** — Gnostic reinterpretation of Genesis
7. **Resurrection and the Living** — Spiritual resurrection before death
8. **The Perfect Human** — Unity, restoration, and the fullness of being

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Gospel of Philip occupies a distinctive place across religious traditions:

- **Gnosticism**: A primary liturgical and theological text of the Valentinian school
- **Christianity**: Rejected from the canon but valued as evidence of early Christian diversity
- **Judaism**: Engages deeply with Genesis themes through a radically different hermeneutic
- **Academic**: A key source for understanding early Christian sacramental practice and Gnostic theology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Sacraments | Five sacraments: baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, bridal chamber |
| Bridal Chamber | The supreme mystery uniting the soul with its angelic counterpart |
| Names and Truth | Language conceals and reveals — names can deceive or illuminate |
| Mary Magdalene | Presented as the companion (*koinonos*) of Christ |
| Resurrection | Spiritual resurrection must occur during life, not after death |
