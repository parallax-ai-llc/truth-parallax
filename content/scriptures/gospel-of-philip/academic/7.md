## Resurrection and the Living

Chapter 7 articulates the Valentinian teaching that resurrection must be received before death — a "realized eschatology" that relocates the resurrection from the future to the present. Academic scholarship has connected this teaching to the broader early Christian debate about the timing and nature of resurrection, attested in canonical texts like 2 Timothy 2:17-18 (condemning those who say "the resurrection has already taken place") and in other Nag Hammadi texts such as the Treatise on the Resurrection (NHC I,4).

Scholars have analyzed the social implications of this realized eschatology. If resurrection has already occurred through gnosis and sacramental initiation, the Valentinian community possesses a present certainty that the proto-orthodox church, with its future-oriented eschatology, does not. This theological position may have functioned as a marker of communal identity and superiority. Elaine Pagels and others have argued that debates about resurrection in early Christianity were simultaneously debates about authority: who could claim the risen Christ's endorsement, and on what basis? The Gospel of Philip's position on resurrection thus has implications not only for theology but for the sociology of early Christian communities.
