## Knowledge and Ignorance

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Philip addresses the nature of names, language, and truth. Scholars have identified this section as establishing the epistemological framework of the text: names belong to the world of appearances and can deceive, while truth lies beyond conventional language. Wesley Isenberg and Bentley Layton have noted the Platonic resonances of this argument — the distinction between the world of appearances and the world of forms — filtered through a Valentinian theological lens.

Academic analysis of this chapter focuses on several questions. First, the literary form: is this a coherent argument or a collection of discrete sayings loosely organized around a theme? Martha Lee Turner has argued for greater coherence than earlier scholars recognized. Second, the philosophical context: the chapter's treatment of names intersects with Stoic, Platonic, and Jewish traditions of language theory. Third, the Coptic translation itself: several passages in this chapter are damaged or ambiguous, and scholars continue to debate key readings that affect interpretation of the text's epistemology.
