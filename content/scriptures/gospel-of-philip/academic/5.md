## Light and Darkness

Chapter 5 develops the Valentinian understanding of light and darkness as cosmic principles. Academic analysis of this section focuses on the text's sources and philosophical context. The light-darkness dualism of the Gospel of Philip has clear parallels with the Gospel of John (1:5, 3:19-21, 8:12), and scholars have debated whether Philip directly depends on John or whether both draw on common traditions — possibly rooted in Jewish wisdom literature and sectarian movements like those represented by the Dead Sea Scrolls.

The chapter's cosmology also reflects Platonic and Middle Platonic influences, particularly the concept that the visible world is a shadow or copy of a higher reality. Birger Pearson and others have traced the specific terminology used in this chapter to a confluence of Platonic, Jewish, and Iranian dualistic traditions. The question of whether Valentinian dualism is "radical" (two independent principles) or "mitigated" (darkness as privation of light) has implications for understanding the text's relationship to Manichaean and other dualistic systems. Most scholars classify Philip's dualism as mitigated — darkness is derivative and ultimately destined to be absorbed when the light reclaims its own.
