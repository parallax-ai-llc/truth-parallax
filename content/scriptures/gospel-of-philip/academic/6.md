## Adam and the Fall

Chapter 6 presents a Valentinian reinterpretation of the Genesis creation and fall narratives. Academic study of this section examines how the text engages in "rewritten Bible" — a practice well attested in Second Temple Judaism (Jubilees, 1 Enoch, Genesis Apocryphon) and continued in Gnostic literature. The text's reading of the separation of Eve from Adam as a catastrophic division rather than a creative act of God reflects the Platonic myth of the androgyne, most famously articulated in Plato's *Symposium* (189d-193d).

Scholars have debated the relative weight of Jewish and Platonic sources in Philip's anthropology. Wayne Meeks's influential study "The Image of the Androgyne" traced the tradition of primordial androgyny through Jewish, Gnostic, and early Christian sources, demonstrating that the Gospel of Philip participates in a widespread ancient discourse about human wholeness and its loss. Jorunn Buckley has analyzed the gender dynamics of this chapter, noting that Philip's treatment of the male-female division is more nuanced than simple misogyny or proto-feminism — it reflects a Valentinian theology in which both genders are incomplete without their complement.
