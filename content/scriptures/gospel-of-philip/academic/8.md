## The Perfect Human

The final chapter of the Gospel of Philip presents the vision of the "perfect human" — one who has achieved complete unity and restoration. Academic scholars have analyzed this concept in relation to the Valentinian Anthropos myth, in which the heavenly Human (*Anthropos*) is one of the primary Aeons of the Pleroma. The "perfect human" of Philip is thus not merely a moral ideal but a theological claim: the initiated Gnostic who completes the sacramental journey has recovered the nature of the primordial Anthropos.

The chapter's statement that the perfected individual is "no longer a Christian but a Christ" has attracted particular scholarly attention. Thomassen interprets this as a claim about ontological transformation: the Valentinian initiate participates in the Christic nature through the sacraments, becoming what Christ is. This raises questions about the boundary between Valentinianism and other forms of early Christianity — was this claim a radical departure from apostolic teaching or an intensification of Pauline language about being "in Christ" (2 Corinthians 5:17)? Academic analysis of this closing chapter thus circles back to the fundamental question about the Gospel of Philip's relationship to the broader Christian tradition.
