## Knowledge and Ignorance

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Philip explores the relationship between names, truth, and deception. Catholic theologians note that while the text raises legitimate questions about the capacity of language to express divine reality — a concern shared by the apophatic tradition from Pseudo-Dionysius to Thomas Aquinas — its conclusions diverge sharply from Catholic teaching. The Church affirms that human language, while always inadequate to the divine mystery, can nonetheless convey real truth about God through analogy, as the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) taught.

The chapter's privileging of esoteric knowledge over communal faith reflects the Valentinian elitism that the Church Fathers consistently opposed. Irenaeus argued that the truth of the gospel was publicly proclaimed by the apostles and entrusted to the churches through episcopal succession, not hidden in secret teachings accessible only to the spiritually elite. Catholic theology insists that saving knowledge of God is available to all through the Church's public teaching, the sacraments, and the reading of Scripture under the guidance of the magisterium.
