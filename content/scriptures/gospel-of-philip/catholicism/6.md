## Adam and the Fall

Chapter 6 reinterprets the Genesis fall narrative, presenting the separation of Eve from Adam as a cosmic fragmentation rather than the result of free moral choice. Catholic doctrine on original sin, defined at the Council of Trent (Session V, 1546), teaches that Adam's sin was an act of disobedience by which he lost original holiness and justice for himself and all his descendants. The fall is thus a moral event with ontological consequences, not a cosmological accident caused by archonic forces.

Catholic anthropology affirms the complementarity of male and female as part of God's original creative design (Genesis 1:27, 2:18-24), not as a division imposed by hostile spiritual powers. The Catechism teaches that man and woman were created for each other and that their union in marriage is a reflection of God's love. The Valentinian reading in Philip, which views sexual differentiation as a catastrophe to be overcome through mystical reunion, conflicts with the Catholic understanding of the body and sexuality as inherently good dimensions of human existence.
