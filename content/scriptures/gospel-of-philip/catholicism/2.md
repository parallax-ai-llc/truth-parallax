## The Sacraments

Chapter 2 describes five Valentinian sacraments: baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, and the bridal chamber. Catholic sacramental theology recognizes seven sacraments instituted by Christ, three of which — baptism, confirmation (chrism), and eucharist — overlap terminologically with Philip's list. This overlap has intrigued Catholic scholars, who see evidence that Valentinian communities adapted authentic Christian sacramental practice to fit their Gnostic theology.

However, the theological content of these rites differs fundamentally. Catholic baptism effects the remission of original sin and incorporation into the Body of Christ; Philip's baptism is a stage of esoteric illumination. Catholic confirmation completes baptismal grace through the gift of the Holy Spirit; Philip's chrism confers a spiritual nature superior to baptism. The Catholic eucharist is the true Body and Blood of Christ under the appearances of bread and wine (transubstantiation); Philip's eucharist involves spiritual participation in the Logos. The Catholic Church teaches that the sacraments are efficacious signs of grace, instituted by Christ and entrusted to the Church (*ex opere operato*), not stages of secret initiation into cosmological knowledge.
