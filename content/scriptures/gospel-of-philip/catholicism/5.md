## Light and Darkness

Chapter 5 develops the Valentinian dualism between light and darkness, spirit and matter. Catholic teaching, rooted in the doctrine of creation *ex nihilo* and affirmed by the Fourth Lateran Council and Vatican I, holds that the material world is the good creation of the one God. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (338) teaches that "nothing exists that does not owe its existence to God the Creator." The Valentinian suggestion that matter is inherently inferior or the product of a lesser deity contradicts this fundamental affirmation.

The Catholic tradition has consistently rejected cosmological dualism. The condemnation of Manichaean and Cathar teachings reinforced the Church's insistence that matter, while affected by the consequences of sin, is not evil in itself. The incarnation — the Word becoming flesh (John 1:14) — is the definitive Catholic argument against any theology that devalues the material order. Catholic interpreters read Philip's light-darkness dualism as a distortion of the biblical imagery of light and darkness, which in Catholic theology describes the moral and spiritual struggle between grace and sin, not an ontological divide between spirit and matter.
