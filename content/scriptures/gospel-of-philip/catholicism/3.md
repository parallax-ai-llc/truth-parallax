## Christ and Mary Magdalene

Chapter 3 describes Mary Magdalene as the "companion" of Christ, stating that he used to kiss her and loved her more than the other disciples. The Catholic tradition venerates Mary Magdalene as a saint and "apostle to the apostles" (*apostola apostolorum*) — a title affirmed by Pope Francis's elevation of her liturgical memorial to a feast day in 2016. However, the Catholic Church does not accept the Gospel of Philip's characterization of her relationship with Jesus as historically reliable, since the text was composed long after the apostolic period and reflects Valentinian theological concerns.

Catholic scholars note that the "companion" language in Philip serves a theological function within Valentinianism — expressing the syzygy principle — rather than recording historical fact. The Catholic understanding of Mary Magdalene is grounded in the canonical Gospels, which present her as a faithful disciple, a witness to the crucifixion, and the first to encounter the risen Christ. The Pontifical Biblical Commission and Catholic exegetes have clarified that the Church's teaching about Mary Magdalene is derived from the canonical accounts, not from Gnostic sources.
