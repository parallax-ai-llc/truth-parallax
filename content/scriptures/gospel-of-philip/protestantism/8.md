## The Perfect Human

The final chapter of the Gospel of Philip envisions the "perfect human" as one who has achieved complete spiritual reunification — no longer male or female, no longer Christian but "a Christ." Protestant theology has a robust doctrine of sanctification — the progressive growth of the believer in holiness through the work of the Holy Spirit — but it does not teach that believers become divine or transcend their creaturely status. The Westminster Confession affirms that sanctification is "imperfect in this life" and that "remnants of corruption" remain in every part of the believer.

The Valentinian claim that the perfected Gnostic becomes "a Christ" conflicts with the Protestant understanding of the unique person and work of Jesus Christ. While believers are united to Christ by faith and share in his benefits (justification, adoption, sanctification), they do not become Christ or acquire divine nature in the ontological sense Philip implies. The Protestant distinction between Creator and creature is fundamental and permanent.

Protestant readers may note that the aspiration toward wholeness and spiritual maturity expressed in this chapter echoes genuine biblical themes — Paul's call to "grow up in every way into him who is the head, into Christ" (Ephesians 4:15). However, the Valentinian framework transforms these themes into something the apostolic writers would not recognize, replacing grace-enabled growth with esoteric self-realization.
