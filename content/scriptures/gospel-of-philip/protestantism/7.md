## Resurrection and the Living

Chapter 7 teaches that resurrection must occur during one's lifetime rather than after death — a position that directly contradicts the Protestant understanding of bodily resurrection grounded in 1 Corinthians 15. Paul's teaching is emphatic: "If there is no resurrection of the dead, then not even Christ has been raised" (1 Corinthians 15:13). The future bodily resurrection of believers is a non-negotiable article of the Christian faith as articulated in the Apostles' Creed and affirmed by all major Protestant confessions.

The Gospel of Philip's "realized eschatology" — its insistence that resurrection is a present spiritual experience rather than a future bodily event — reflects the same error that Paul addressed in 2 Timothy 2:17-18, where Hymenaeus and Philetus are condemned for teaching that "the resurrection has already taken place." Protestant theologians see in Philip's teaching a clear example of the Gnostic tendency to spiritualize away the concrete, historical character of biblical eschatology.

While Protestant theology affirms that believers experience a kind of spiritual resurrection in conversion (Ephesians 2:5-6, Colossians 3:1), this is understood as a foretaste and guarantee of the future bodily resurrection, not as a replacement for it. The Gospel of Philip collapses this eschatological tension into a purely present, spiritual experience, losing the biblical hope for the redemption of the body and the renewal of all creation.
