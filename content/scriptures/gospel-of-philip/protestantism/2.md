## The Sacraments

Chapter 2 describes five Valentinian sacraments: baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, and the bridal chamber. Protestant theology, grounded in *sola scriptura*, recognizes only two ordinances (or sacraments) instituted by Christ in the canonical Gospels: baptism and the Lord's Supper. The additional rites described in Philip — chrism, redemption, and the bridal chamber — have no foundation in the New Testament and reflect Valentinian theological innovation rather than apostolic practice.

The chapter's understanding of baptism and eucharist also diverges from Protestant teaching in fundamental ways. Philip treats these rites as vehicles for transmitting secret spiritual power, whereas Protestant theology understands them as signs and seals of God's covenant promises, effective through faith rather than through ritual mechanism. The eucharist in Philip involves partaking of the "flesh and blood" of the Logos in a spiritual-mystical sense distinct from both the Roman Catholic doctrine of transubstantiation and the Protestant memorial or spiritual-presence views.

The sacramental proliferation described in this chapter illustrates the kind of extra-biblical accretion that the Reformers sought to strip away. For Protestant readers, Philip serves as a cautionary example of what happens when the church adds to the simple ordinances Christ established, layering them with speculative theology and mystery-religion practices.
