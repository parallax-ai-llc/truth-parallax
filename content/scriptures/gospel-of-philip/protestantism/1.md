## Knowledge and Ignorance

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Philip addresses the relationship between names, truth, and deception — asserting that ordinary language conceals reality rather than revealing it. From a Protestant perspective, this epistemological skepticism stands in tension with the Reformation confidence in the clarity of Scripture (*perspicuitas Scripturae*). Where Philip suggests that names inherently deceive, Protestant theology affirms that God has communicated truthfully and intelligibly through the written Word.

The chapter's claim that knowledge (*gnosis*) is the path to salvation diverges sharply from the Protestant emphasis on salvation by grace through faith (Ephesians 2:8-9). While Protestants affirm the importance of knowing God, this knowledge is relational and covenantal — mediated through Scripture and the Holy Spirit — rather than the esoteric, elite knowledge described in the Valentinian system. The text's suggestion that ordinary believers remain trapped in ignorance contradicts the Protestant doctrine of the priesthood of all believers.

Protestant scholars nonetheless find value in studying this chapter as evidence of how second-century Christians wrestled with questions about language, truth, and revelation. The concerns raised by Philip — can words adequately convey divine reality? — resonate with Protestant debates about biblical hermeneutics, even if the answers given are fundamentally different.
