## Overview

Protestant engagement with the Gospel of Philip is shaped by the Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* and the conviction that the canonical Scriptures constitute the sole authoritative witness to the apostolic faith. The Gospel of Philip, as a non-canonical Gnostic text, is not regarded as Scripture but is studied as evidence of the theological diversity — and deviation — that existed in the early centuries of Christianity. Protestant scholars approach it primarily through historical-critical methods.

## Theological Critique

From a Protestant perspective, the Gospel of Philip's sacramental system raises significant concerns. The text presents five sacraments rather than the two (baptism and the Lord's Supper) that most Protestants recognize, and its understanding of the eucharist and baptism is suffused with Gnostic cosmology rather than grounded in the plain teaching of the canonical Gospels. The "bridal chamber" sacrament has no basis in apostolic teaching and reflects Valentinian speculation. Protestant theologians also reject the text's apparent denigration of the material world and its implicit claim that salvation comes through secret knowledge rather than faith in Christ.

## Value for Protestant Scholarship

Despite these theological objections, Protestant scholars have contributed significantly to the study of the Gospel of Philip. The text illuminates the diversity of early Christianity and the process of canon formation, demonstrating why the early church drew boundaries around acceptable teaching. It also provides a foil against which the distinctiveness of canonical theology — grace, faith, the goodness of creation — becomes clearer.
