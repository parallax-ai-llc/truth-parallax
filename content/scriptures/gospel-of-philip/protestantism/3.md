## Christ and Mary Magdalene

Chapter 3 presents Mary Magdalene as the "companion" of Christ, stating that he loved her more than the other disciples and kissed her frequently. Protestant scholars approach these claims through historical-critical analysis, noting that the Gospel of Philip was composed well over a century after the events it describes and reflects Valentinian theological concerns rather than historical memory. The canonical Gospels present Mary Magdalene as a devoted follower and the first witness of the resurrection, but they do not assign her the role Philip describes.

The text's elevation of Mary Magdalene serves a theological function within Valentinianism: she represents the feminine principle necessary for the syzygy (pairing) that structures the Pleroma. Protestant interpreters note that this theological framework is imposed on the historical figures rather than derived from the earliest sources. The canonical Gospels, which Protestants regard as the authoritative witnesses, present a different picture of the relationships among Jesus and his disciples.

Protestant engagement with this chapter has also addressed the broader cultural fascination with the "companion" passage, particularly in the wake of popular treatments. Scholars emphasize the importance of reading the text within its Valentinian theological context rather than as a suppressed historical report about the life of Jesus.
