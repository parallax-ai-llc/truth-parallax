## Adam and the Fall

Chapter 6 reinterprets the Genesis account of Adam and the fall through a Valentinian lens, presenting the separation of Eve from Adam as a catastrophic division of an originally unified being rather than the creation of a complementary partner. Protestant theology, rooted in the canonical Genesis narrative, understands the creation of Eve as a purposeful act of a good God who declared that "it is not good for man to be alone" (Genesis 2:18). The fall results from human disobedience, not from cosmic fragmentation.

The Protestant doctrine of original sin, developed from Romans 5:12-21, understands the fall as a historical event with universal consequences: all humanity inherits Adam's guilt and corruption. The Gospel of Philip's reinterpretation removes moral responsibility from the equation, attributing the fall to archonic forces rather than to human choice. This fundamentally different anthropology has implications for soteriology: if the problem is fragmentation rather than sin, the solution is reunion rather than atonement.

Protestant interpreters note that Philip's reading of Genesis, while creative, is eisegetical — reading Valentinian cosmology into a text that does not support it. The canonical reading of Genesis 1-3, interpreted through the rest of Scripture, presents a coherent narrative of creation, fall, and redemption that the Valentinian system distorts.
