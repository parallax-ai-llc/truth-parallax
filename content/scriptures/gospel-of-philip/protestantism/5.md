## Light and Darkness

Chapter 5 explores Valentinian dualism — the opposition between light and darkness, spirit and matter. Protestant theology affirms a distinction between light and darkness in Scripture (John 1:5, 1 John 1:5-7), but this is a moral and spiritual distinction, not the ontological dualism of the Valentinian system. In the biblical framework, darkness is the result of sin and rebellion, not an inherent quality of the material world.

The Protestant doctrine of the goodness of creation (Genesis 1:31) stands in direct tension with Philip's implicit devaluation of the material order. The Reformers insisted that the physical world, though marred by the fall, remains God's good creation and the theater of his glory (Calvin). Matter is not a prison for the divine spark but the medium through which God works, blesses, and sustains human life. The incarnation itself — God becoming flesh (John 1:14) — is the ultimate affirmation of the material world's value.

Protestant interpreters recognize that the light-darkness imagery in Philip resonates with Johannine language, suggesting that the Valentinians drew on the Gospel of John in constructing their theology. However, the Valentinian appropriation transforms Johannine dualism from a moral-spiritual framework into a cosmological-ontological one, fundamentally altering its meaning.
