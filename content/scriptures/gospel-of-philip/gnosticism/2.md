## The Sacraments

Chapter 2 presents the five sacraments of Valentinian practice: baptism, chrism (anointing), eucharist, redemption, and the bridal chamber. For Gnostic readers, these are not merely ritual acts but stages of spiritual transformation through which the initiate progressively recovers the divine knowledge lost in the fall into matter. Each sacrament corresponds to a deeper level of awakening, culminating in the bridal chamber — the supreme mystery.

Baptism in the Valentinian system is understood as the beginning of illumination, not the washing away of sin in the orthodox sense. Chrism (anointing with oil) is considered superior to baptism, as it confers the "Christ" nature — the anointed one. The eucharist involves partaking of the "flesh and blood" of the Logos, understood spiritually as assimilating divine knowledge. Redemption (*apolytrosis*) is a distinct rite, possibly involving a second baptism or a deathbed ritual, that frees the soul from the archons' power during its ascent through the heavenly spheres.

The five sacraments form an ascending hierarchy, with each building upon the last. Valentinian communities debated the precise nature and order of these rites, but the Gospel of Philip consistently presents them as the practical means by which the theoretical knowledge of Gnostic cosmology becomes lived, embodied experience — transforming the initiate from a being of ignorance into a being of light.
