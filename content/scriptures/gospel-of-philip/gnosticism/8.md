## The Perfect Human

The final chapter of the Gospel of Philip brings together the text's major themes in a vision of the "perfect human" — the Gnostic who has completed the sacramental journey from ignorance to gnosis, from fragmentation to unity. For Valentinian readers, this chapter describes the telos of the entire spiritual path: the restoration of the divine image that was fractured in the fall and obscured by the archons' creation.

The perfect human is one who has reunited with their angelic counterpart in the bridal chamber, overcome the dualism of light and darkness, and received the spiritual resurrection while still alive. This person is no longer male or female but has recovered the original androgynous wholeness of the Pleroma. The text describes such a person as "no longer a Christian but a Christ" — one who has fully realized the anointed nature conferred in chrism.

This concluding vision summarizes the Gospel of Philip's radical anthropology: humanity's true nature is divine, and the task of the spiritual life is not to earn salvation through moral effort but to remember and recover what was always already present — the divine spark hidden within the material body by forces that sought to keep it imprisoned. The perfect human is not a future ideal but a present possibility for those who receive the sacraments and awaken to gnosis.
