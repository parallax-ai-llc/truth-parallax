## Resurrection and the Living

Chapter 7 articulates one of the most distinctive Gnostic teachings in the Gospel of Philip: that resurrection is not a future bodily event but a present spiritual reality. The text insists that "those who say they will die first and then rise are in error" — one must receive the resurrection while still alive. For Gnostic readers, this teaching overturns the orthodox eschatological framework and locates salvation in the present moment of gnosis.

The Valentinian understanding of resurrection is tied to the broader Gnostic anthropology: the physical body is not the true self but a garment worn by the divine spark during its sojourn in the material world. To "rise" is to awaken the pneumatic self from the sleep of ignorance, to recognize one's true identity as a being of light temporarily clothed in flesh. Physical death merely removes the outer garment; the resurrection that matters — the spiritual one — must occur before death if it is to occur at all.

This present-tense eschatology had profound implications for the Valentinian community. It meant that the sacraments — especially baptism, chrism, and the bridal chamber — were not preparations for a future resurrection but the actual enactment of resurrection in the here and now. The initiated Gnostic was already "risen," already a citizen of the Pleroma, already free from the power of the archons and the fear of death.
