## Adam and the Fall

Chapter 6 presents the Valentinian reinterpretation of the Genesis account of Adam and the fall. For Gnostic readers, the separation of Eve from Adam is not the creation of a helper but a catastrophic division of an originally unified being — a microcosmic reflection of the cosmic fragmentation that occurred when Sophia fell from the Pleroma. The fall is not about disobedience to a divine command but about the loss of primordial wholeness.

The archons who separated Adam and Eve did so out of jealousy and fear: a unified, androgynous Adam possessed a power that threatened their authority. By dividing Adam into male and female, the archons weakened humanity and introduced desire, death, and ignorance. The Gnostic reading thus inverts the orthodox understanding: the "fall" is not Adam's fault but the archons' strategy. The serpent's role is ambiguous in Philip — less the liberator of some Gnostic texts, more a complex figure in the drama of knowledge and deception.

The remedy for the fall, according to the Gospel of Philip, is the bridal chamber — the sacrament that reunites what the archons divided. Marriage in the ordinary sense is a pale reflection of this cosmic reunion, but the spiritual marriage of the bridal chamber restores the original androgynous wholeness. The chapter thus connects the Genesis narrative directly to the central sacramental teaching of the text.
