## Knowledge and Ignorance

The Gospel of Philip opens with a meditation on the relationship between names, knowledge, and truth — a theme central to Valentinian Gnostic theology. For the Gnostic reader, this chapter establishes the epistemological framework for the entire text: the world of ordinary language is a realm of deception, where names obscure rather than reveal reality. The word "God," the word "Father," the word "Son" — all are conventional labels that point beyond themselves to realities accessible only through gnosis.

In Valentinian theology, ignorance is not merely a lack of information but the fundamental condition of existence in the material world. The Demiurge himself is defined by ignorance — he does not know the Pleroma above him. Human beings, trapped in the Demiurge's creation, inherit this ignorance as their default state. The opening sayings of Philip challenge the reader to recognize that what they think they know — through names, categories, and conventional religion — is precisely what keeps them from true knowledge.

The Gnostic emphasis on the inadequacy of names reflects a broader philosophical concern shared with Platonic and Hermetic traditions. Names belong to the world of appearances; truth belongs to the world of forms or, in Valentinian terms, the Pleroma. The initiated Gnostic learns to see through names to the realities they imperfectly represent, beginning the journey from ignorance to gnosis that the rest of the Gospel of Philip will map in sacramental detail.
