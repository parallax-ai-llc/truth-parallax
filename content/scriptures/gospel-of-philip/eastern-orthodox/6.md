## Adam and the Fall

Chapter 6 reinterprets the fall of Adam as a cosmic fragmentation — the separation of Eve from an originally unified being — rather than an act of moral disobedience. The Eastern Orthodox understanding of the fall, while distinct from the Western Augustinian doctrine of original sin, nonetheless treats Adam's transgression as a genuine moral failure with cosmic consequences. Orthodox theology speaks of "ancestral sin" — the inheritance of mortality and the corruption of human nature — rather than inherited guilt, but it firmly locates the fall in human choice, not in archonic conspiracy.

Orthodox anthropology, informed by the Fathers, affirms the complementarity of male and female as part of God's creative design. Maximus the Confessor taught that the overcoming of the male-female division is indeed one of the five mediations that humanity is called to accomplish — but this is achieved through ascetic purification and the virtuous life, not through a secret Gnostic sacrament. The Orthodox vision of restored humanity is corporate, liturgical, and eschatological, pointing toward the transfiguration of all creation in the age to come.
