## The Perfect Human

The final chapter of the Gospel of Philip describes the "perfect human" who has transcended all divisions and become "a Christ." The Orthodox doctrine of theosis (deification) teaches that human beings are called to become "partakers of the divine nature" (2 Peter 1:4), and the saints of the Church demonstrate the reality of this transformation. Athanasius's famous dictum — "God became man so that man might become God" — expresses the heart of Orthodox soteriology.

However, Orthodox theosis preserves the ontological distinction between Creator and creature: the human person participates in God's uncreated energies without becoming God in essence. The Valentinian claim that the perfected Gnostic is "no longer a Christian but a Christ" suggests a collapse of this distinction that Orthodox theology does not permit. Furthermore, Orthodox perfection is communal and liturgical — achieved within the Body of Christ through the sacraments, prayer, fasting, and love of neighbor — not through esoteric individual initiation. The perfect human, in Orthodox understanding, is the saint who reflects the divine image fully restored by grace within the life of the Church.
