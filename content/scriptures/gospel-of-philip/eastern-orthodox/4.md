## The Bridal Chamber Mystery

Chapter 4 presents the bridal chamber as the supreme Valentinian sacrament — the rite of spiritual reunion with one's angelic counterpart. Orthodox mystical theology employs bridal imagery extensively: Origen's and Gregory of Nyssa's commentaries on the Song of Songs describe the soul's journey toward union with God in nuptial terms. However, the Orthodox understanding of this union is fundamentally different from the Valentinian bridal chamber.

In Orthodox theology, the soul's union with God occurs through theosis (deification) — a real participation in the divine life made possible by the incarnation, sustained by the sacraments, and perfected in the age to come. This union preserves the distinction between Creator and creature while enabling genuine communion. The Valentinian bridal chamber, by contrast, involves reunion with an angelic double — a concept rooted in Platonic mythology rather than biblical theology. Orthodox interpreters note that the text's language of "entering the bridal chamber" before death reflects a realized eschatology that eliminates the tension between the "already" and "not yet" that characterizes Orthodox eschatological hope.
