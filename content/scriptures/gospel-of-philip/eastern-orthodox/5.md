## Light and Darkness

Chapter 5 develops the Valentinian dualism between light and darkness, treating matter as inherently inferior to spirit. The Eastern Orthodox tradition affirms that God is light (1 John 1:5) and that the uncreated divine light — manifested at the Transfiguration on Mount Tabor — is accessible to the faithful through prayer and the sacraments. Gregory Palamas's distinction between God's essence and his uncreated energies provides the theological framework for Orthodox teaching on divine illumination.

However, Orthodox theology emphatically rejects the cosmological dualism that undergirds Philip's light-darkness imagery. The material world is God's good creation, and the incarnation of the Logos sanctifies matter definitively. Orthodox liturgical practice reflects this conviction: icons are painted on wood, incense fills the temple, bread and wine become the Body and Blood of Christ. The Valentinian suggestion that matter is a prison for divine sparks conflicts with the Orthodox understanding of creation as a sacramental theophany — a revelation of God's glory through the material order.
