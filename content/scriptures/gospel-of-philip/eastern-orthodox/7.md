## Resurrection and the Living

Chapter 7 teaches that resurrection is a present spiritual event rather than a future bodily one. The Eastern Orthodox Church confesses in the Nicene Creed the "resurrection of the dead and the life of the world to come," and the celebration of Pascha (Easter) — the central feast of the Orthodox liturgical year — proclaims Christ's bodily resurrection as the foundation of all Christian hope. The Paschal troparion declares: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life."

Orthodox theology does affirm that participation in Christ's resurrection begins in this life through baptism and the eucharist — "we have been buried with him through baptism into death, so that as Christ was raised from the dead, we too might walk in newness of life" (Romans 6:4). But this present participation is an anticipation, not a replacement, of the future resurrection. The Gospel of Philip's elimination of the eschatological horizon conflicts with the Orthodox understanding of salvation as a process that culminates in the bodily resurrection and the transformation of the entire cosmos.
