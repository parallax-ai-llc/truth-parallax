## Knowledge and Ignorance

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Philip explores how names and language relate to truth — arguing that names deceive and that true knowledge transcends verbal categories. Eastern Orthodox theology shares a deep concern with the inadequacy of language to express divine reality, as articulated in the apophatic (negative) theology of Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, Gregory of Nyssa, and Maximus the Confessor. However, the Orthodox tradition balances apophatic unknowing with cataphatic (positive) affirmation: God is genuinely revealed in Scripture, the liturgy, and the incarnation, even as he transcends all human categories.

The Valentinian epistemology of this chapter differs fundamentally from the Orthodox understanding. For Orthodoxy, knowledge of God is participatory — achieved through prayer, asceticism, and the sacraments within the ecclesial community — not through the acquisition of secret information. The Fathers consistently taught that saving knowledge is public, communal, and available to all the baptized, not reserved for a spiritual elite. The Gospel of Philip's division of humanity into categories of spiritual capacity contradicts the Orthodox affirmation that all persons are called to theosis.
