Shankara's Advaita tradition reads Brihadratha's disillusionment as the quintessential example of *vairagya* (detachment) — one of the four prerequisites for Vedantic inquiry. The king's recognition that worldly life in an impermanent body is ultimately futile represents the *viveka* (discrimination) between the eternal and the transient that qualifies a person for self-knowledge.

The chapter establishes the existential motivation for philosophical inquiry: the dissatisfaction with finite existence that drives the seeker toward the infinite. Brihadratha's question — what is the point of enjoyment in this body? — is the starting point of the Advaita path: recognizing the inadequacy of the phenomenal world and seeking the unconditioned reality that underlies it.
