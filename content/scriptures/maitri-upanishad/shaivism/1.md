King Brihadratha's despair in Chapter 1 reflects the first stirring of spiritual awakening — the recognition that phenomenal existence is marked by suffering and transience. Shaiva interpreters read this not as nihilistic pessimism but as the necessary dissolution of worldly attachment that precedes the recognition of one's true nature as Shiva.

The king's meditation and his approach to the sage Shakayanya represent the seeker's turn inward — from the external world of power and pleasure to the internal reality of consciousness. The Shaiva tradition recognizes *vairagya* (dispassion) not as an end in itself but as the clearing of the ground for the direct recognition (*pratyabhijna*) of unlimited awareness.
