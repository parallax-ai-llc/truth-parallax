Chapter 2's account of the self bound by the gunas reflects the influence of proto-Samkhya thought on the Maitri Upanishad. The three gunas — sattva (purity), rajas (activity), and tamas (inertia) — appear here as binding qualities of material nature, a concept that becomes central to classical Samkhya philosophy. Scholars note that the Maitri's guna theory is more developed than what appears in earlier Upanishads, suggesting a later date of composition.

The chapter's description of the self as distinct from the body yet bound by the gunas raises the philosophical question of how a non-material self can be affected by material qualities — a problem that later philosophers (Shankara, Ramanuja, Madhva) would address in very different ways.
