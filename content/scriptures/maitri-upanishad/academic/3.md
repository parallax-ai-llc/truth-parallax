Chapter 3's distinction between two forms of Brahman — temporal and eternal — reflects a philosophical development that scholars trace through the Upanishadic corpus. The earlier Brihadaranyaka (2.3.1) distinguishes two forms (mortal and immortal), and the Maitri elaborates this into a more systematic framework. Van Buitenen argues that this chapter may show the influence of early astronomical and cosmological speculation on Upanishadic thought.

The identification of time with Brahman is philosophically significant, anticipating later debates about whether time is real or illusory, divine or natural. The sun as a symbol of Brahman connects the Upanishad to the ancient Vedic solar theology of the Rig Vedic hymns.
