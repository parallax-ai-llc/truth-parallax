## Overview

The Maitri Upanishad's comprehensive system — combining cosmology, psychology, and soteriology — invites comparison with similarly synthetic philosophical-religious systems in other traditions. Its starting point — a king's despair at the transience of worldly life — echoes the Buddha's encounter with old age, sickness, and death, as well as Ecclesiastes' lament that "all is vanity."

## Comparative Themes

The three *gunas* (sattva, rajas, tamas) as qualities constituting material nature compare with the four elements of Greek philosophy, the yin-yang polarity of Chinese thought, and the three *alchemical principles* (sulphur, mercury, salt) of Western Hermeticism. The Maitri's two forms of Brahman — temporal and eternal, manifest and unmanifest — parallel the Kabbalistic distinction between *Ein Sof* (the infinite) and the *Sefirot* (divine emanations), and the Neoplatonic contrast between the transcendent One and its emanated hypostases.
