The raising of Lazarus (11:1-44) is the seventh and climactic sign in John's Gospel. Jesus' declaration 'I am the resurrection and the life' (11:25) is the fifth 'I AM' saying and one of the most powerful statements of Protestant hope. The combination of present and future dimensions — 'whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live, and everyone who lives and believes in me shall never die' (11:25-26) — grounds Protestant teaching on both the intermediate state and the final resurrection.

The detail that 'Jesus wept' (11:35) is the shortest and among the most beloved verses in the Bible, affirming Christ's full humanity and his compassion in the face of death. The political consequence of the miracle — the decision of the Sanhedrin to put Jesus to death (11:47-53) — and Caiaphas' unwitting prophecy that 'it is better for one man to die for the people' (11:50) demonstrate divine providence operating through human hostility. Protestant interpreters see in Caiaphas' words a divinely orchestrated statement of substitutionary atonement.
