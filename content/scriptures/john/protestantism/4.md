The encounter with the Samaritan woman (4:1-42) is one of the most richly developed narratives in John and a favorite text for Protestant preaching. Jesus crosses ethnic, gender, and religious boundaries to offer 'living water' (4:10) — a metaphor for the Spirit and eternal life. The woman's progressive recognition of Jesus — from 'a Jew' (4:9) to 'Sir' (4:11) to 'prophet' (4:19) to 'the Christ' (4:29) — models the journey of faith.

Jesus' declaration that true worshippers will worship 'in spirit and truth' (4:23-24) has been foundational for Protestant worship theology, emphasizing the priority of inward disposition over outward form or sacred location. The harvest imagery (4:35-38) grounds Protestant missiology. The healing of the official's son (4:46-54), the second sign, demonstrates that faith in Jesus' word alone — without visible signs — is sufficient. The official 'believed the word that Jesus spoke to him' (4:50), a model of *sola fide*.
