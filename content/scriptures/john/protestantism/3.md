John 3 contains the most frequently cited verse in Protestant Christianity: 'For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life' (3:16). This verse encapsulates the Protestant gospel: salvation is grounded in God's love, accomplished through the gift of his Son, and received through faith. The dialogue with Nicodemus (3:1-15) introduces the concept of being 'born again' (*anothen* — from above/again), which has become central to evangelical conversion theology.

The contrast between belief and condemnation (3:18-21) and the imagery of light versus darkness establish the dualistic framework that runs through John's Gospel. John the Baptist's final testimony (3:22-36) includes the statement 'He must increase, but I must decrease' (3:30), a model of Christ-centered ministry. The declaration that 'whoever believes in the Son has eternal life' (3:36) grounds the Protestant doctrine of assurance — eternal life is a present possession, not merely a future hope.
