John 15's vine imagery — 'I am the true vine' (15:1), the seventh 'I AM' saying — establishes the organic relationship between Christ and believers that defines Protestant spirituality. The command to 'abide in me' (15:4) has shaped Protestant devotional theology, particularly in the Holiness and Keswick traditions, which emphasize the 'abiding life' as the secret of spiritual fruitfulness.

The pruning imagery (15:2) raises questions about the possibility of apostasy — can branches be 'cut off'? Calvinist interpreters argue that the cut branches represent false professors who were never truly united to Christ, while Arminian interpreters see a genuine warning against falling away. The transition from servant to friend (15:15) reflects the Protestant emphasis on personal relationship with Christ. The warning about the world's hatred (15:18-25) has been read as prophetic during periods of Protestant persecution.
