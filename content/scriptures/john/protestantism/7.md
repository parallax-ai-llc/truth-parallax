John 7 is set during the Feast of Tabernacles, and Jesus' teaching generates sharp division. His offer of 'rivers of living water' (7:37-38) to any who thirst is read by Protestant interpreters as an open invitation to salvation — there is no prerequisite of merit or institutional mediation. The narrator's comment that 'this he said about the Spirit, whom those who believed in him were to receive' (7:39) connects the water imagery to Pentecost and the Protestant emphasis on the Spirit's indwelling of every believer.

The division among the people (7:40-44) — some calling Jesus a prophet, others the Christ, still others rejecting him — models the range of responses to the gospel that Protestant evangelists encounter. The Pharisees' dismissal of the crowd as ignorant of the law (7:49) and Nicodemus' cautious defense of due process (7:50-52) reveal the dynamics of institutional resistance to truth. The passage about the woman caught in adultery (7:53-8:11), while textually disputed, has been deeply influential in Protestant teaching on grace and forgiveness.
