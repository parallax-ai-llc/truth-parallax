The wedding at Cana (2:1-11) is identified as the first of Jesus' seven signs in John. Protestant interpreters generally focus on the sign's revelatory function — it 'manifested his glory, and his disciples believed in him' (2:11) — rather than developing the sacramental Eucharistic symbolism emphasized in Catholic and Orthodox traditions. The abundance of wine (six stone jars of twenty to thirty gallons each) points to the messianic banquet and the eschatological abundance promised by the prophets.

The cleansing of the temple (2:13-22) is placed at the beginning of Jesus' ministry in John, unlike its position near the end in the Synoptics. Protestant scholars debate whether this represents a different event or John's theological rearrangement. Jesus' statement 'Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up' (2:19) is a programmatic statement about his death and resurrection, understood only retrospectively by the disciples. The chapter closes with a note about Jesus' knowledge of human nature (2:24-25), establishing his divine omniscience.
