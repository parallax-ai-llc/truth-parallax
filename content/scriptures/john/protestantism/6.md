John 6 contains the feeding of the five thousand (6:1-15), the walking on water (6:16-21), and the Bread of Life discourse (6:22-71). The feeding miracle is the fourth sign, and Jesus' self-identification as 'the bread of life' (6:35) is the first of seven 'I AM' sayings. Protestant interpretation of the Bread of Life discourse is one of the sharpest points of divergence from Catholic and Orthodox traditions.

The statement 'Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you' (6:53) is read by most Protestant interpreters as a metaphor for believing in Christ — receiving him by faith, not literally consuming his body in the Eucharist. The explanatory verse 'It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh is no help at all' (6:63) is the key Protestant hermeneutical lens: the discourse is about spiritual reception through faith, not physical consumption. Many disciples leave after this hard teaching (6:66), and Peter's response — 'Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life' (6:68) — expresses the Protestant commitment to Christ alone.
