John 13 opens the Book of Glory with the foot washing (13:1-20), which Protestant interpreters read primarily as a model of humble service rather than a sacramental act. Jesus' statement 'I have given you an example, that you also should do what I have done to you' (13:15) establishes servant leadership as the defining characteristic of Christian community. Some traditions (Anabaptists, certain Baptist churches) practice literal foot washing as an ordinance.

The departure of Judas — 'and it was night' (13:30) — is one of John's most dramatic literary moments, contrasting the darkness of betrayal with the light of the farewell discourse that follows. The new commandment — 'love one another as I have loved you' (13:34) — defines the ethical content of discipleship. Peter's confident declaration and Jesus' prediction of his denial (13:36-38) warn against self-reliance and point to the necessity of grace.
