## Overview

The Gospel of John has held a place of special honor in Protestant theology since the Reformation. Luther called it "the one, fine, true, and chief Gospel" and ranked it above the Synoptics. John's emphasis on faith (*pistis*) as the means of receiving eternal life resonates deeply with the Protestant doctrine of *sola fide*. The Gospel's high Christology, its extended theological discourses, and its dramatic portrayal of individual encounters with Jesus have made it the most preached and most memorized Gospel in Protestant churches.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Reformation Reading
Luther and Calvin both produced extensive commentaries on John. Luther prized John's emphasis on faith over works, while Calvin drew on the Prologue's Logos theology to articulate the doctrine of Christ's eternal deity. Both read John's discourses as direct theological teaching, less concerned with historical-critical questions.

### Historical-Critical
Protestant scholars from F.C. Baur through Rudolf Bultmann developed critical theories about John's composition, sources (a hypothetical "Signs Source"), and relationship to Gnosticism. Bultmann's existentialist reading of John as a call to authentic decision remains influential.

### Evangelical
Conservative Protestant scholars like D.A. Carson and Craig Keener defend John's historical reliability and apostolic authorship while engaging seriously with the Gospel's literary and theological artistry.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sola Fide**: John 3:16 — "whoever believes in him" — is the most cited verse in Protestant evangelism
- **Assurance**: "These things are written so that you may believe... and that believing you may have life" (20:31) grounds Protestant assurance of salvation
- **New Birth**: The Nicodemus dialogue (3:1-21) is foundational to Protestant conversion theology and the "born again" experience
- **The Paraclete**: John 14-16 shapes Protestant pneumatology, emphasizing the Spirit's role in teaching, convicting, and guiding
- **Priesthood of All Believers**: Jesus' direct encounters with individuals (Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, the man born blind) are read as modeling unmediated access to God
