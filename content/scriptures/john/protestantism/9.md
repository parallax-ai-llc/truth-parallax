John 9 narrates the healing of the man born blind, the sixth sign, which Protestant interpreters read as both a physical miracle and a parable of spiritual illumination. Jesus' rejection of the disciples' assumption that blindness was caused by sin (9:2-3) challenges simplistic theologies of suffering and blessing. The statement 'that the works of God might be displayed in him' (9:3) reframes suffering as an occasion for divine glory.

The progressive confession of the healed man — from 'the man called Jesus' (9:11) to 'a prophet' (9:17) to 'Lord, I believe' (9:38) — parallels the Samaritan woman's journey and models the process of coming to faith. The Pharisees' interrogation and the man's expulsion from the synagogue (9:22, 34) reflect the social cost of following Jesus — a theme that resonates with Protestant communities that have experienced persecution. The chapter's ironic conclusion — the blind see and the seeing become blind (9:39-41) — warns against the spiritual blindness of religious certainty.
