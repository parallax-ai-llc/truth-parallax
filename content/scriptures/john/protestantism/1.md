The Prologue of John (1:1-18) is the most theologically dense passage in Protestant biblical theology. 'In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God' (1:1) establishes the pre-existence and deity of Christ — the twin foundations of orthodox Christology. Luther regarded this as the clearest biblical statement of Christ's divinity. Calvin devoted extensive attention to the Logos concept, arguing that John deliberately chose this term to counter both Jewish and Greek misconceptions about God's self-revelation.

The incarnation — 'the Word became flesh and dwelt among us' (1:14) — is the hinge of Protestant theology. The divine Logos takes on full humanity, not merely the appearance of humanity. John the Baptist's witness (1:19-34) introduces the title 'Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world' (1:29), which grounds the Protestant understanding of substitutionary atonement. The calling of the first disciples (1:35-51) and Nathanael's confession — 'Rabbi, you are the Son of God! You are the King of Israel!' (1:49) — establish the pattern of faith-response that characterizes the entire Gospel.
