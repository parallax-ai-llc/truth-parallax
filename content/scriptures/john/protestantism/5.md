John 5 recounts the healing at the pool of Bethesda (5:1-18) and the extended discourse on the Son's relationship to the Father (5:19-47). The Sabbath healing provokes controversy, and Jesus' response — 'My Father is working until now, and I am working' (5:17) — is read by Protestant interpreters as a direct claim to divine prerogative. The charge that Jesus was 'making himself equal with God' (5:18) confirms this reading.

The discourse that follows articulates the relationship between Father and Son with a clarity that has shaped Protestant Trinitarian theology. The Son does nothing on his own but only what he sees the Father doing (5:19); the Father has given all judgment to the Son (5:22); honoring the Son is honoring the Father (5:23). The witnesses to Jesus — John the Baptist, the works, the Father, and the Scriptures (5:33-47) — establish a fourfold testimony. The final challenge — 'If you believed Moses, you would believe me, for he wrote of me' (5:46) — grounds the Protestant hermeneutical principle of reading the Old Testament christologically.
