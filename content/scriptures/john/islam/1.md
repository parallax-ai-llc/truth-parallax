The Prologue of John (1:1-18) represents the most fundamental point of theological divergence between Islam and Christianity. The claim that 'the Word was God' (1:1) and 'the Word became flesh' (1:14) directly contradicts the Quranic affirmation that God is absolutely one and does not beget or incarnate. Surah Al-Ikhlas (112:1-4) states: 'Say: He is Allah, the One. Allah, the Eternal Refuge. He neither begets nor is born, nor is there to Him any equivalent.' Muslim scholars attribute the Logos theology to Hellenistic philosophical influence — particularly Platonic and Stoic concepts — imposed upon the original monotheistic teaching of Jesus.

The designation of Jesus as 'the Lamb of God' (1:29) is reinterpreted in Islamic thought. While Islam recognizes Jesus' prophetic status and his miraculous birth, the concept of a sacrificial lamb who 'takes away the sin of the world' is rejected. Islamic theology holds that each person is responsible for their own sins and that God forgives directly without the need for a mediating sacrifice. The calling of disciples (1:35-51) is broadly consistent with the Quranic account of the *hawariyyun* (disciples) who supported Jesus.
