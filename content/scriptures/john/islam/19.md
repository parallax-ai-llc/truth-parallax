John 19's crucifixion narrative is the passage where Islamic and Johannine theology diverge most absolutely. The Quran states categorically: 'They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but so it was made to appear to them' (4:157). Muslim scholars offer several explanations: God cast Jesus' likeness upon another person (Judas, Simon of Cyrene, or a volunteer), and this substitute was crucified while Jesus was raised to heaven. Alternatively, some modern Muslim scholars propose that Jesus was placed on the cross but did not actually die — he swooned and was later revived.

The cry 'It is finished' (19:30) is interpreted by some Muslim scholars as the cry of the substitute, not of Jesus himself. The blood and water from the pierced side (19:34) may be cited by those who hold the swoon theory as evidence that Jesus was still alive (blood flows from the living, not the dead). The burial (19:38-42) is acknowledged, but the occupant of the tomb, in the Islamic reading, was either the substitute or a still-living Jesus who would be taken up to God.
