## Overview

The Gospel of John occupies a distinctive place in Islamic engagement with the New Testament. On the one hand, its high Christological claims — the divinity of the Logos, the "I AM" sayings, the unity of Father and Son — represent precisely the theological assertions that Islam most emphatically rejects. On the other hand, John's Paraclete passages (14:16, 14:26, 15:26, 16:7) have been central to the Islamic argument that Jesus himself foretold the coming of Muhammad. This dual dynamic — rejection of Johannine Christology and appropriation of the Paraclete promise — defines the Islamic approach to this Gospel.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Paraclete Controversy
Muslim scholars have argued since the medieval period that the Greek *parakletos* ("advocate") is a corruption of *periklytos* ("praised one"), which corresponds to the meaning of the name Muhammad/Ahmad. The Quran (61:6) records Jesus as saying: "I bring good tidings of a messenger who will come after me, whose name is Ahmad." Muslim apologists read the Paraclete passages as this very prophecy, stripped of its original meaning by Christian editors.

### Critique of Logos Theology
Islamic theology categorically rejects the identification of Jesus with a pre-existent divine Word who "was God" (1:1). Muslim commentators argue that the Prologue reflects Hellenistic philosophical influence (Platonic and Stoic logos concepts) imposed upon the original monotheistic teaching of Jesus.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tawhid vs. Incarnation**: John's central claim — "the Word became flesh" (1:14) — directly contradicts the Quranic insistence that God does not beget nor is begotten (112:3)
- **Jesus' Humanity**: Passages showing Jesus' human emotions (11:35, 12:27, 13:21) are cited as evidence of his prophetic rather than divine nature
- **The Paraclete**: The most contested passages; Muslim interpretation sees Muhammad as the fulfillment of Jesus' promise
- **Rejection of Crucifixion**: John's detailed passion narrative is considered a distorted account; the Quran affirms Jesus was raised to God, not killed (4:157-158)
- **Signs, Not Divinity**: Jesus' miracles in John are understood as God-given signs (*ayat*) confirming his prophetic mission, not evidence of inherent divine power
