John 3's dialogue with Nicodemus contains teachings that Muslim scholars engage with selectively. The concept of spiritual rebirth (3:3-8) resonates broadly with Islamic teaching about spiritual transformation through submission to God, though the specific connection to baptism is not relevant to Islamic practice. The wisdom of Jesus' teaching is acknowledged as befitting a great prophet.

However, John 3:16 — 'God gave his only Son' — is categorically rejected. The concept of God having a son, whether 'only-begotten' or otherwise, contradicts Quranic theology: 'It is not befitting for Allah to take a son' (Quran 19:35). Muslim scholars view this verse as a later theological interpolation that distorts Jesus' original monotheistic teaching. The contrast between 'the one who believes' and 'the one who does not believe' (3:18) is reframed: true belief, in Islamic understanding, means believing in God's oneness and following all his prophets, culminating in Muhammad.
