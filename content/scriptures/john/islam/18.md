John 18's arrest and trial narrative is read by Muslim scholars with the conviction that the apparent defeat of Jesus is not what it seems. The 'I AM' declaration that causes the soldiers to fall back (18:5-6) demonstrates divine protection — God shields his prophet even in the moment of apparent capture. Some Muslim interpreters see this as evidence that the substitution occurred at this point: the real Jesus was taken up to heaven, and a likeness was cast upon another.

The trial before Pilate and the exchange about truth and kingship (18:33-38) are appreciated as revealing prophetic dignity in the face of political power. Jesus' statement 'My kingdom is not of this world' (18:36) is consistent with the Islamic understanding that prophets come with spiritual authority, not political ambition (though Islam also recognizes Muhammad's role as a political leader). Pilate's question 'What is truth?' is answered, from the Islamic perspective, by the Quran itself — the final, preserved, and uncorrupted truth from God.
