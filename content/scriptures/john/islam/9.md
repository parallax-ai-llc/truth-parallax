The healing of the man born blind (9:1-41) is accepted by Muslim scholars as a genuine prophetic miracle. The Quran explicitly affirms that Jesus healed the blind (3:49, 5:110), making this one of the most Quranically compatible passages in John. Jesus' rejection of the idea that the man's blindness was caused by sin (9:2-3) resonates with the Islamic principle that 'no bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another' (Quran 6:164, 53:38).

The progressive recognition of Jesus — from 'a man called Jesus' to 'a prophet' (9:17) — is read by Muslim scholars as the appropriate trajectory: Jesus is indeed a prophet, and recognizing him as such is correct belief. However, the final confession 'Lord, I believe' and the act of worship (9:38) cross the boundary of acceptable recognition in Islamic theology. Worship (*ibadah*) is reserved for God alone, and directing it toward any created being, however exalted, constitutes *shirk* (associating partners with God).
