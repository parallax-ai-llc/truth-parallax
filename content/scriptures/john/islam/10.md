John 10's Good Shepherd discourse contains elements that Muslim scholars engage with selectively. The imagery of a caring shepherd who protects his flock resonates with the Quranic and prophetic description of Muhammad as 'a mercy to the worlds' (Quran 21:107) and the hadith tradition that describes prophets as shepherds. Jesus' willingness to sacrifice for his flock illustrates prophetic devotion, though Islamic theology does not accept the substitutionary atonement theology that Christians derive from this passage.

The statement 'I and the Father are one' (10:30) is the second major claim (after 8:58) that Islam categorically rejects. This assertion of unity with God contradicts *tawhid* and is interpreted as either a later interpolation or a misunderstanding of a statement about unity of purpose. The Quran warns against those who say 'Allah is the Messiah, son of Mary' (5:72) and 'Allah is the third of three' (5:73). Muslim scholars see John 10:30 as the kind of statement that led to the Christian doctrines the Quran explicitly condemns.
