## Overview

The Gospel of John stands apart from the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke) in both structure and theology. Traditionally attributed to the apostle John ("the beloved disciple"), it was likely composed between 90 and 100 CE. The Gospel presents Jesus not primarily as a teacher of parables or an exorcist but as the eternal Logos made flesh, whose seven miraculous "signs" and seven "I AM" declarations reveal his divine identity.

## Structure

John's Gospel follows a distinctive architecture:

1. **Prologue** (1:1-18): The cosmic hymn to the Logos
2. **The Book of Signs** (1:19-12:50): Seven signs revealing Jesus' glory, interwoven with long discourses
3. **The Book of Glory** (13:1-20:31): The Farewell Discourse, the passion, crucifixion, and resurrection
4. **Epilogue** (21:1-25): The appearance at the Sea of Tiberias and the restoration of Peter

## Cross-Traditional Significance

John's Gospel has exerted enormous influence across traditions:

- **Christianity**: The primary source for high Christology, Trinitarian theology, and sacramental theology
- **Judaism**: A complex and contested text, reflecting late first-century tensions between church and synagogue
- **Islam**: The Paraclete passages (14-16) are interpreted by Muslim scholars as foretelling Muhammad
- **Gnosticism**: The language of light and darkness, knowledge and hiddenness, attracted early Gnostic commentators
- **Academic**: Central to debates about the relationship between history and theology in Gospel writing

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Logos | The pre-existent Word who was with God and was God (1:1) |
| Signs | Seven miraculous deeds revealing Jesus' glory |
| I AM Sayings | Seven self-declarations echoing the divine name (Exodus 3:14) |
| Light and Darkness | Dualistic imagery framing the cosmic conflict |
| Eternal Life | Not merely future hope but present reality through faith |
| Glory | The cross as the moment of glorification, not humiliation |
| Paraclete | The Holy Spirit as advocate, teacher, and guide |
