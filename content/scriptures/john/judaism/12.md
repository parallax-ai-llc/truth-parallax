John 12 contains the triumphal entry and the Greeks' request to see Jesus. The entry into Jerusalem (12:12-19) fulfills Zechariah 9:9 and draws on Psalm 118:26, which was part of the Hallel liturgy recited during Passover. Jewish scholars note the political volatility of this scene — messianic acclamations during Passover, when Jewish nationalism was at its peak, would have alarmed both the Roman garrison and the temple authorities.

The arrival of 'some Greeks' (12:20-22) seeking Jesus signals the extension of Jesus' influence beyond Jewish boundaries. Jesus' response — the grain of wheat must die (12:24) — and the voice from heaven (12:28-30) are analyzed by Jewish scholars in relation to the *bat qol* ('daughter of a voice'), the post-prophetic form of divine communication described in rabbinic literature (b. Berakhot 3a, Sanhedrin 11a). The citation of Isaiah 6:10 to explain Jewish unbelief (12:40) raises painful questions about John's characterization of Israel's rejection.
