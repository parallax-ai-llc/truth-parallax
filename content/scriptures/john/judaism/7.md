John 7 is set during Sukkot (Tabernacles), one of the three pilgrimage festivals. Jewish scholars note the rich liturgical background: the water libation ceremony (*nisukh ha-mayim*), performed daily in the temple during Sukkot, involved drawing water from the Pool of Siloam and pouring it on the altar. Jesus' cry 'If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink' (7:37) directly engages with this ritual, claiming to fulfill what the ceremony symbolizes.

The debate about Jesus' identity (7:40-44) reflects genuine messianic questions: Could the Messiah come from Galilee? The expectation that the Messiah would be a descendant of David from Bethlehem (7:42) is well attested in Jewish tradition (Micah 5:2). The Pharisees' contempt for the crowd — 'This crowd that does not know the law is accursed' (7:49) — reflects tensions between the educated elite and the *am ha-aretz* (common people) that are documented in rabbinic literature. Nicodemus' defense of due process (7:50-52) shows that voices of moderation existed within the establishment.
