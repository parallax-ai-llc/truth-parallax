## Overview

The Gospel of John presents a uniquely complex challenge for Jewish interpretation. More than any other Gospel, John uses the phrase "the Jews" (*hoi Ioudaioi*) in an apparently hostile manner, and its high Christological claims have historically fueled Christian supersessionism and anti-Jewish sentiment. Yet modern Jewish scholars have also recognized that John is deeply embedded in Jewish thought — its Logos theology has roots in Jewish wisdom traditions, its narrative is structured around Jewish festivals, and its debates reflect intra-Jewish arguments about messianic identity and scriptural authority.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Anti-Judaism Critique
Scholars like Adele Reinhartz and Daniel Boyarin have analyzed John's rhetoric about "the Jews," debating whether *hoi Ioudaioi* refers to Judean authorities, a subset of Jewish leaders, or Jews as a whole. The *Birkat haMinim* (blessing against heretics) and the expulsion from the synagogue (9:22, 12:42, 16:2) reflect a painful historical rupture that shaped the Gospel's polemical tone.

### Jewish Roots Analysis
Scholars like Peder Borgen and Daniel Boyarin have argued that John's Christology — including the Logos concept and "two powers in heaven" theology — fits within the spectrum of first-century Jewish theology rather than representing a break from Judaism.

## Theological Emphases

- **"The Jews" in John**: Jewish scholars insist that responsible interpretation must grapple with how the Gospel's rhetoric has been weaponized against Jewish communities throughout history
- **Festival Cycle**: John structures Jesus' ministry around Passover, Tabernacles, Dedication (Hanukkah), and Sabbath — each sign and discourse engages with the theological meaning of these Jewish festivals
- **Wisdom and Logos**: The Prologue's Logos echoes the personified Wisdom of Proverbs 8, Sirach 24, and the Wisdom of Solomon, situating Johannine theology within Jewish intellectual tradition
- **The Shema and Monotheism**: Jesus' statements "I and the Father are one" (10:30) and "before Abraham was, I AM" (8:58) are read by Jewish scholars as the point at which Johannine claims cross the boundary of Jewish monotheistic acceptability
