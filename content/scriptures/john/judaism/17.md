John 17's prayer for unity — 'that they may all be one' (17:21) — is examined by Jewish scholars primarily in terms of its implications for the definition of the community. The prayer assumes a clearly defined in-group ('those whom you have given me,' 17:9) distinguished from 'the world' (17:9). Jewish scholars note the sectarian dynamics: the prayer's language of being 'in the world but not of the world' (17:11, 14-16) resembles the self-understanding of the Qumran community and other Second Temple Jewish sectarian groups.

The use of the divine name — 'I have made your name known' (17:6, 26) — draws on the theology of the divine name central to Jewish tradition. The revelation of God's name to Moses (Exodus 3:14) and the sanctification of the name (*kiddush hashem*) are foundational Jewish concepts. Jesus' claim to have revealed the name suggests an intimate knowledge of and authority over the divine name that Jewish theology reserves for God alone. The prayer for protection 'in your name' (17:11-12) echoes the priestly blessing of Numbers 6:24-26.
