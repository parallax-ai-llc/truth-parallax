John 10's shepherd imagery draws on rich Old Testament traditions. Ezekiel 34, where God condemns Israel's false shepherds and promises to shepherd his people directly, is the primary background. Psalm 23's 'The Lord is my shepherd' establishes God as the true shepherd of Israel. Jewish scholars note that Jesus' claim to be the 'good shepherd' who 'lays down his life for the sheep' (10:11) positions him in the role that Ezekiel reserves for God himself — a theologically significant claim.

The Feast of Dedication (Hanukkah) setting (10:22) provides the backdrop for the discussion about Jesus' identity. The festival commemorates the Maccabean rededication of the temple after its desecration by Antiochus IV. Jesus' claim 'I and the Father are one' (10:30) provokes the charge of blasphemy (10:33). Jewish scholars observe that while 'two powers in heaven' theology existed in some Jewish circles (as Alan Segal documented), the mainstream tradition rejected any suggestion of divine plurality. The attempt to stone Jesus (10:31) reflects the seriousness of the claim.
