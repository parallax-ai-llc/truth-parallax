John 2's wedding at Cana provides valuable data about Galilean Jewish social customs. The wedding feast, the purification jars, and the role of the master of the feast reflect well-documented Jewish practices. Jewish scholars note that the six stone jars 'for the Jewish rites of purification' (2:6) situate the narrative within a thoroughly Jewish ritual context. The abundance of wine resonates with prophetic imagery of messianic abundance (Amos 9:13-14, Joel 3:18).

The temple action (2:13-22) is placed by John at the beginning of Jesus' ministry, during Passover. Jewish historians note the significance of the Passover setting — the festival commemorating liberation from Egyptian bondage was a time of heightened national feeling and Roman anxiety. The temple was the economic and religious center of Jewish life, and any disturbance there carried enormous political implications. The statement about destroying and raising the temple in three days (2:19) is read by Jewish scholars as a claim that later served as evidence against Jesus at his trial.
