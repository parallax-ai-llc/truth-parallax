John 18's passion narrative presents the most significant points of tension between Jewish and Christian historical claims. The arrest in the garden (18:1-11) involves a cohort of Roman soldiers alongside temple police (18:3), a detail unique to John that Jewish scholars consider historically plausible — Roman involvement from the outset would explain the ultimately Roman execution. The interrogation before Annas (18:13-14, 19-24) and the reference to Caiaphas are set against the complex politics of the high-priestly families under Roman rule.

The trial before Pilate (18:28-40) raises the chronological question: John places the trial on the day before Passover, while the Synoptics place the Last Supper on Passover evening. Jewish scholars note that John's chronology serves a theological purpose — Jesus dies at the hour when the Passover lambs are slaughtered in the temple. The statement 'We have no king but Caesar' (19:15) is historically implausible as a sincere Jewish declaration, given the depth of Jewish opposition to Roman rule, and is read by Jewish scholars as Johannine polemic.
