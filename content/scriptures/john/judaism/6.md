John 6's Bread of Life discourse engages extensively with the Exodus manna tradition. The crowd's citation of Psalm 78:24 — 'He gave them bread from heaven to eat' (6:31) — situates the dialogue within a thoroughly Jewish interpretive context. The midrashic exchange between Jesus and the crowd about the true giver of the bread from heaven (Moses or the Father) follows patterns attested in rabbinic literature.

However, the discourse takes a radical turn with 'unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood' (6:53). The consumption of blood is categorically prohibited in Jewish law (Genesis 9:4, Leviticus 17:10-14, Deuteronomy 12:23), making this statement deeply offensive to Jewish sensibility. Jewish scholars read this as evidence of the Johannine community's alienation from the synagogue — the language deliberately transgresses Jewish dietary taboos, signaling a community that has moved beyond the boundaries of Jewish practice. The murmuring of the crowd (6:41, 61) echoes the Israelites' murmuring against Moses, a deliberate typological connection.
