Nicodemus, a 'ruler of the Jews' (3:1), represents the Pharisaic elite who engaged seriously with Jesus' teaching. Jewish scholars note that his night visit may reflect caution rather than cowardice — a respected teacher seeking private dialogue with a controversial figure. The concept of being 'born again' or 'born from above' (*anothen*) has parallels in rabbinic literature, where conversion to Judaism is described as becoming 'like a newborn child' (b. Yevamot 22a).

The reference to the bronze serpent (3:14-15) draws on Numbers 21:4-9, a passage that became problematic in later Jewish history when King Hezekiah destroyed the bronze serpent because it had become an object of worship (2 Kings 18:4). Jewish scholars observe that Jesus' typological use of this passage reflects a hermeneutical method common in Second Temple Judaism. The famous verse 3:16, with its claim about God's 'only Son,' represents a theological assertion that Judaism cannot accept — the concept of divine begetting is incompatible with Jewish monotheism.
