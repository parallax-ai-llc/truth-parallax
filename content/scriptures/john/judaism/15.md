John 15's vine imagery draws on one of the most important biblical metaphors for Israel. Isaiah 5:1-7, Psalm 80:8-16, Jeremiah 2:21, and Ezekiel 15 all use the vine or vineyard to represent Israel. Jesus' claim to be the 'true vine' (15:1) implicitly positions him as the authentic Israel, with the branches representing those who abide in him. Jewish scholars read this as a supersessionist move — claiming that the 'true' Israel is the community of Jesus' followers, not the historical Jewish people.

The warning about 'the world's' hatred (15:18-25) and the prediction of persecution (15:20) are analyzed by Jewish scholars in light of the historical situation of the Johannine community. The language of 'the world' hating the disciples 'as it hated me' (15:18) reflects the experience of a marginalized community that understood its rejection by the synagogue as parallel to Jesus' rejection. The citation of Psalm 35:19 / 69:4 — 'They hated me without a cause' (15:25) — applies an Israelite psalm of lament to the Christian experience, a hermeneutical move that appropriates Jewish Scripture for a new purpose.
