The raising of Lazarus (11:1-44) is set in Bethany, near Jerusalem, and involves a family closely connected to Jesus. Jewish burial customs described in the narrative — the stone-covered tomb, the four-day interval, the mourning period — are well attested in archaeological and literary evidence. The belief that the soul lingered near the body for three days before departing permanently (as recorded in later midrashic sources) may explain the significance of the four-day delay: Lazarus is unambiguously dead.

The Sanhedrin's response (11:47-53) introduces political pragmatism: 'If we let him go on like this, everyone will believe in him, and the Romans will come and take away both our place and our nation' (11:48). Jewish historians note the genuine dilemma facing the priestly leadership — maintaining order under Roman occupation required controlling messianic movements. Caiaphas' statement about one man dying for the people (11:50) reflects the political calculus of a colonial ruling class trying to preserve its precarious autonomy.
