John 9's narrative of the man born blind contains the clearest reference to the expulsion of Jewish Christians from the synagogue. The parents' fear 'because the Jews had already agreed that if anyone should confess Jesus to be Christ, he would be put out of the synagogue' (9:22) reflects a historical situation that scholars associate with the *Birkat haMinim* — the reformulation of the twelfth benediction of the Amidah to exclude heretics (*minim*), probably introduced around 85-90 CE.

Jewish scholars debate the nature and scope of this expulsion. Some argue that the *Birkat haMinim* was not specifically anti-Christian but targeted various heretical groups. Others note that the formulation varied by region and period. In any case, the passage reflects the traumatic experience of the Johannine community being separated from the synagogue — an experience that colors the entire Gospel's rhetoric about 'the Jews.' The ironic reversal — the blind man sees, the religious authorities are blind — employs a prophetic trope (Isaiah 6:9-10) that is deeply rooted in the Hebrew Bible.
