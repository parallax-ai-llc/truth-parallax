The encounter with the Samaritan woman (4:1-42) illuminates the complex relationship between Jews and Samaritans in the first century. Jewish scholars note that the woman's surprise — 'How is it that you, a Jew, ask a drink of me, a Samaritan woman?' (4:9) — reflects real social tensions attested in rabbinic and historical sources. The dispute about whether to worship on Mount Gerizim or in Jerusalem (4:20) was a centuries-old controversy between the two communities.

Jesus' statement that 'salvation is from the Jews' (4:22) is one of the most explicitly pro-Jewish statements in the Gospel, yet it is immediately qualified by the claim that the hour is coming when worship will not be localized in either Gerizim or Jerusalem (4:21). Jewish scholars read this as a supersessionist move — claiming that Jewish worship, while historically valid, is being transcended. The harvest imagery (4:35-38) may reflect the early Christian mission to Samaria described in Acts 8.
