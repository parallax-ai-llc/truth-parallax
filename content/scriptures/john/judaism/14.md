John 14's exclusivist claim — 'I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me' (14:6) — is one of the most challenging passages in the Gospel for Jewish-Christian dialogue. Jewish scholars note that this statement, if taken at face value, excludes all non-Christians from relationship with God — a claim that Judaism categorically rejects. Jewish theology affirms that the righteous of all nations have a share in the world to come (t. Sanhedrin 13:2).

The Paraclete passages (14:16-17, 26) are analyzed by Jewish scholars for their pneumatological implications. The concept of a divine Spirit guiding the community has Jewish roots in the prophetic tradition and the concept of *ruach ha-kodesh* (the Holy Spirit). However, the identification of the Paraclete as 'another advocate' who continues Jesus' work represents a development beyond the Jewish framework. Jewish scholars also note the polemical edge in 'the world cannot receive' the Spirit (14:17) — the 'world' in John often functions as a code for those who reject Jesus, including the Jewish community.
