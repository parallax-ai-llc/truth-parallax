Jewish scholarly engagement with John 20's resurrection narrative focuses on the historical and sociological questions it raises. The empty tomb tradition is examined critically: Jewish scholars note that the claim of an empty tomb does not in itself prove resurrection — alternative explanations (theft of the body, wrong tomb) were proposed as early as Matthew 28:13. However, the strong and early attestation of the resurrection belief among Jesus' followers is a historical datum that requires explanation.

Thomas' doubt and subsequent confession (20:24-29) is of particular interest. His declaration 'My Lord and my God!' (20:28) represents the highest Christological claim in the Gospels — the identification of Jesus with God. Jewish scholars identify this as the decisive theological moment where the Johannine community crosses the boundary of Jewish monotheism. The fact that this confession comes from the most skeptical of the disciples suggests the community's awareness that its claims were extraordinary and required extraordinary evidence.
