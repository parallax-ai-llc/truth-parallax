The wedding at Cana (2:1-11) is read by Gnostic interpreters as a symbolic narrative of spiritual transformation. The water of the Jewish purification rites — representing the Demiurge's religious system — is transformed into the wine of gnosis. The six stone jars correspond to the six days of the Demiurge's creation; the wine represents the seventh, spiritual reality that transcends the material order.

The cleansing of the temple (2:13-22) is interpreted as the Savior's confrontation with the Demiurge's central institution. The merchants and money changers represent the archonic powers who profit from directing worship toward the false god. Jesus' statement about destroying and raising the temple in three days (2:19) refers not to the physical temple but to the spiritual body — the pneumatic self that will be destroyed through the passion and reconstituted in the Pleroma.
