John 3's dialogue with Nicodemus is rich with Gnostic resonance. The concept of being 'born from above' (*anothen*, 3:3) describes the pneumatic birth — the awakening of the divine spark that originates in the Pleroma. Nicodemus' inability to understand (3:4, 9) identifies him as a psychic type — intellectually engaged but lacking the spiritual capacity to receive gnosis directly. His nighttime visit symbolizes his condition: he comes from the darkness of ignorance toward the light of the Revealer.

The bronze serpent lifted up in the wilderness (3:14) is particularly significant for Ophite (serpent-venerating) Gnostics, who identified the serpent as a symbol of salvific knowledge. The serpent in Genesis 3 brought gnosis to humanity, and the lifted serpent of Numbers 21 brings healing — both images converge in Jesus, who is 'lifted up' to reveal the saving knowledge that liberates from the Demiurge's world. The light-darkness contrast (3:19-21) maps directly onto the Gnostic cosmology: the realm of light is the Pleroma, and the darkness is the material world ruled by ignorant archons.
