The healing of the man born blind (9:1-41) is a paradigm of Gnostic illumination — the movement from the blindness of ignorance to the sight of gnosis. The man's blindness from birth represents the condition of the divine spark trapped in matter from the moment of creation — not the result of personal sin but of the cosmic situation. The clay made from spittle (9:6) — fashioned from the Savior's own substance — and the washing in Siloam ('Sent') enact the process of gnosis: the divine substance touches the human, and the enlightenment follows.

The progressive recognition of Jesus — 'a man' (9:11), 'a prophet' (9:17), 'Lord, I believe' (9:38) — models the stages of gnosis, from initial awareness to full recognition of the Savior's divine nature. The Pharisees' increasing blindness (9:39-41) illustrates the archonic condition: those who claim to 'see' through the Demiurge's religious system are the most deeply blind. True sight comes only through gnosis, which the Demiurge's servants cannot grant and actively oppose.
