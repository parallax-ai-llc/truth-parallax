The raising of Lazarus (11:1-44) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the supreme demonstration of the Savior's power to awaken the divine spark from the death-sleep of material existence. Lazarus represents the pneumatic soul buried in the tomb of the body, bound by the burial wrappings of material attachments. Jesus' command 'Lazarus, come out!' (11:43) is the Gnostic call to awakening — the voice from the Pleroma that penetrates the Demiurge's realm and summons the sleeper to life.

The declaration 'I am the resurrection and the life' (11:25) is understood as referring to spiritual resurrection — the awakening of gnosis — not to bodily resurrection at the end of time. Gnostic theology generally rejected the concept of physical resurrection, holding that salvation consists in the liberation of the spirit from the body, not the reconstitution of the body. Jesus' weeping (11:35) is read by some Gnostic interpreters as compassion for humanity's entrapment in matter, while others (following docetic principles) view it as an appearance of emotion adopted for the benefit of the psychic disciples.
