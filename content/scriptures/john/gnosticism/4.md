The Samaritan woman at the well (4:1-42) received extensive attention from Valentinian Gnostics, particularly in Heracleon's commentary. Heracleon identified the woman as a pneumatic type — a spiritual person trapped in material existence (symbolized by her five husbands, which Heracleon interpreted as the five senses or five archonic powers that had dominated her). Jesus' offer of 'living water' (4:10) represents gnosis — the knowledge that, once received, becomes an internal spring of eternal life.

The worship 'in spirit and truth' (4:23-24) is central to the Gnostic rejection of material religion. True worship transcends both Gerizim and Jerusalem — both are institutions of the Demiurge. The pneumatic worshipper needs no temple, no sacrifice, no priesthood — only the direct knowledge of the true God beyond the Demiurge. The Samaritan woman's evangelistic activity (4:28-30, 39) models the dissemination of gnosis: once received, it compels transmission to those ready to hear.
