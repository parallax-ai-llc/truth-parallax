John 20's resurrection narrative is read by Gnostic interpreters not as the resuscitation of a physical body but as the manifestation of the Savior's spiritual, glorified form. The empty tomb (20:1-10) is empty because the material body has been left behind — the Savior now appears in a pneumatic body appropriate to his divine nature. Mary Magdalene's encounter (20:11-18) — 'Do not cling to me, for I have not yet ascended to the Father' (20:17) — confirms that the risen Christ exists between the material world and the Pleroma, in a transitional state.

Thomas' doubting and touching (20:24-29) is ambiguous for Gnostic readers: the emphasis on physical contact seems to contradict docetism, yet Jesus' ability to appear in locked rooms (20:19, 26) demonstrates his transcendence of material limitations. The Gnostic resolution is that the Savior accommodated himself to the psychic understanding of Thomas, allowing physical verification while actually existing in a spiritual mode. The purpose statement (20:31) — 'that you may believe' — describes the public, exoteric purpose of the Gospel; the pneumatic reader knows there is a deeper, esoteric meaning beneath the surface.
