## Overview

The Gospel of John was the most important canonical text for ancient Gnostic Christians. Its dualistic language of light and darkness, its emphasis on knowledge and truth, its Logos theology, and its sharp distinction between those "from above" and those "from below" resonated powerfully with Gnostic cosmology and soteriology. The earliest known commentary on any New Testament text was Heracleon's commentary on John (c. 170 CE), produced by a Valentinian Gnostic. This historical fact underscores John's centrality to the Gnostic movement.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Valentinian Exegesis
The Valentinian school produced the most sophisticated Gnostic readings of John. Heracleon's commentary, preserved in fragments by Origen, interpreted the Gospel's characters as representing different classes of spiritual beings — the Samaritan woman as a pneumatic (spiritual) type, the royal official as a psychic (soulish) type. Ptolemy, another Valentinian, mapped the Prologue's terms (Logos, Life, Light, Grace, Truth) onto the Aeons of the Pleroma.

### The Prologue as Cosmogony
Gnostic readers interpreted John 1:1-18 not merely as a theological introduction but as a compressed account of the emanation of divine beings from the ineffable Father. "In the beginning was the Word" described the first emanation; "the light shines in the darkness" described the fall of divine sparks into the material world.

## Theological Emphases

- **Logos as Aeon**: The Word is not merely a title for Christ but the name of a divine being within the Pleroma hierarchy
- **Dualism**: John's light-darkness opposition (1:5, 3:19-21, 8:12, 12:35-36) was read as ontological, not merely moral — the material world is darkness, the Pleroma is light
- **Knowledge over Faith**: While John uses *pistis* (faith), Gnostics elevated *gnosis* — the Samaritan woman "knows" (4:25), the disciples are given to "know" the Father (17:3)
- **Docetic Christology**: John's statement that "the Word became flesh" (1:14) was reinterpreted: the divine Logos appeared in flesh without truly becoming material
- **The World as Hostile**: "The world did not know him" (1:10) and "the ruler of this world" (12:31, 14:30, 16:11) were read as references to the Demiurge and the archons
