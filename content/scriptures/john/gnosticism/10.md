John 10's Good Shepherd discourse is reinterpreted in Gnostic thought as a contrast between the true Shepherd (the heavenly Christ) and the false shepherds (the Demiurge and his archons). The 'thieves and robbers' who came before Jesus (10:8) are the Demiurge's prophets and lawgivers who kept the sheep in bondage. The gate (10:7, 9) is the passage from the material world to the Pleroma — and only the true Savior can open it.

The statement 'I and the Father are one' (10:30) describes the unity of the Savior with the transcendent Father of the Pleroma — not the Demiurge. The sheep who 'hear my voice' (10:27) are the pneumatic elect who recognize the Savior's call because they share his divine nature. The Dedication (Hanukkah) setting — a festival celebrating the defeat of pagan oppressors and the restoration of true worship — is ironically reread: the true restoration is not of the Demiurge's temple but of the pneumatic soul to the Pleroma.
