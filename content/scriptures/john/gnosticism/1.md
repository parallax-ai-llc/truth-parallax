The Prologue of John is the single most important passage in Gnostic biblical interpretation. Valentinian Gnostics mapped the terms of the Prologue onto the Aeons of the Pleroma: the Word (*Logos*), Life (*Zoe*), Light (*Phos*), Grace (*Charis*), and Truth (*Aletheia*) are not abstract concepts but divine beings emanating from the ineffable Father. Ptolemy, a student of Valentinus, provided a detailed exposition in which each term of the Prologue corresponds to a specific Aeon within the divine hierarchy.

The statement 'the Word became flesh' (1:14) required careful reinterpretation. Docetic Gnostics maintained that the divine Logos did not truly become flesh but only appeared to do so — the material body was a garment or vehicle, not a genuine union. Valentinian Gnostics offered a more nuanced reading: the Logos passed through Mary 'like water through a pipe' (Irenaeus' report), taking on the appearance of flesh without being contaminated by matter. The Prologue's dualism — light shining in darkness, the world not knowing its maker — powerfully reinforced the Gnostic worldview.
