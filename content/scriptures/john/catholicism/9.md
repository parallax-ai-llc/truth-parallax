John 9's healing of the man born blind is read in Catholic tradition as a baptismal narrative. The early Church interpreted the washing in the Pool of Siloam (9:7) as a type of baptism — the font of illumination. The catechumenal process reflected the blind man's journey from darkness to light, and early Christian art frequently depicted this scene in baptisteries. The man's progressive confession of faith mirrors the stages of catechesis: learning, questioning, confessing, and worshipping.

The Pharisees' expulsion of the healed man from the synagogue (9:34) and Jesus' subsequent self-revelation to him (9:35-38) illustrate the Catholic conviction that exclusion from earthly institutions can be the occasion for a deeper encounter with Christ. The ironic reversal — 'for judgment I came into this world, that those who do not see may see, and those who see may become blind' (9:39) — warns against the spiritual blindness that can accompany religious authority.
