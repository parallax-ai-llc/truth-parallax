John 6 is the most important chapter in the Gospel for Catholic Eucharistic theology. The Bread of Life discourse (6:22-71) is read as Jesus' definitive teaching on the Eucharist. The statement 'my flesh is true food, and my blood is true drink' (6:55) is understood literally — Christ is really, truly, and substantially present in the Eucharistic elements. The Council of Trent defined this as the doctrine of transubstantiation: the substance of bread and wine is changed into the substance of Christ's Body and Blood.

The reaction of many disciples — 'This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?' (6:60) — and their departure (6:66) are read by Catholic interpreters as confirmation that Jesus meant his words literally. If he had intended only a symbolic meaning, he would have clarified rather than allowing disciples to leave. Peter's confession — 'Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life' (6:68) — models the Catholic response: trusting Christ's word even when its implications are challenging.
