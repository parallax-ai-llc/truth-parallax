The raising of Lazarus (11:1-44) is the supreme sign in John's Gospel, demonstrating Christ's power over death. Jesus' declaration 'I am the resurrection and the life' (11:25) is proclaimed at every Catholic funeral liturgy, grounding the Church's hope in the resurrection of the body. The detail that Jesus 'wept' (11:35) reveals his full humanity and compassion, a source of consolation in Catholic pastoral ministry.

Caiaphas' prophecy that 'it is expedient that one man should die for the people' (11:50) is read by the evangelist as an unwitting statement of substitutionary sacrifice — the high priest, by virtue of his office, prophesied the universal scope of Christ's atoning death. Catholic theology sees in this passage a confirmation that God works through institutional structures even when their occupants act from unworthy motives, a principle that applies to the ex opere operato efficacy of the sacraments.
