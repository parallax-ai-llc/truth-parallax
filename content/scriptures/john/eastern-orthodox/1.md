The Prologue of John (1:1-18) is the Paschal Gospel of the Orthodox Church, chanted during the midnight Liturgy of Pascha in the most solemn moment of the liturgical year. 'In the beginning was the Word' (*En arche en ho Logos*) echoes Genesis 1:1 and establishes the co-eternity of the Son with the Father. St. Cyril of Alexandria's commentary on the Prologue is definitive for Orthodox Christology: the Logos is not a creature but the eternal Son, 'true God from true God,' as the Nicene Creed confesses.

The incarnation — 'the Word became flesh and tabernacled among us' (1:14) — is the foundational mystery of Orthodox theology. The verb *eskenosen* ('tabernacled') connects the incarnation to the Old Testament tabernacle and temple, revealing that God's dwelling among humanity has now become permanent in the person of Christ. This grounds the Orthodox theology of icons, relics, and sacred matter: if the Logos took on flesh, then material reality can bear divine presence. John the Baptist's witness (1:29-34) and the calling of disciples (1:35-51) unfold the initial revelation of Christ's identity.
