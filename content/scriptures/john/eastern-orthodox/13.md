John 13 opens the Johannine passion narrative with the foot washing, which is reenacted in the Orthodox Holy Thursday liturgy. The bishop washes the feet of twelve clergy, enacting Christ's command of humble service. The act is understood not merely as a moral example but as a revelation of the divine nature: God is self-giving love, and true authority is exercised through service.

The departure of Judas into the night (13:30) is charged with symbolic meaning in Orthodox hymnography: 'The disciple agreed on a price for the Master' — betrayal of the divine for material gain represents the ultimate spiritual catastrophe. The new commandment — 'love one another as I have loved you' (13:34) — defines the life of the Orthodox community. This love is not sentimental but sacrificial, modeled on Christ's self-offering. Peter's confident declaration and the prediction of his denial (13:36-38) remind the faithful of the fragility of human resolve and the necessity of divine grace.
