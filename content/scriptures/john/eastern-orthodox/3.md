John 3's dialogue with Nicodemus is foundational for Orthodox baptismal theology. The statement 'unless one is born of water and the Spirit' (3:5) is understood as referring directly to the sacrament of baptism, which in Orthodox practice includes triple immersion in water and the immediate administration of chrismation (the seal of the Holy Spirit). Birth 'from above' (*anothen*) describes the ontological transformation effected in baptism — the baptized person becomes a 'new creation,' receiving the capacity for theosis.

The image of the bronze serpent lifted up in the wilderness (3:14-15) prefigures the cross — in Orthodox iconography, the crucifixion is depicted as the source of healing and life, not merely an instrument of death. The statement that 'God so loved the world' (3:16) expresses the cosmic scope of salvation: God's love encompasses not just humanity but the entire created order, which will be transfigured in the final restoration. The light-darkness contrast (3:19-21) shapes Orthodox ascetical theology, which describes the spiritual life as a journey from the darkness of passion toward the uncreated light.
