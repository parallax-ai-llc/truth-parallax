The healing of the man born blind (9:1-41) is read on the sixth Sunday after Pascha in the Orthodox Church and serves as a baptismal catechesis. The washing in the Pool of Siloam is understood as a type of baptism — the transition from blindness to sight, from darkness to light, from ignorance to knowledge of Christ. The early Church called baptism 'illumination' (*photismos*), and this narrative provides the scriptural basis for that language.

The progressive confession of the healed man — from 'a man called Jesus' (9:11) to 'a prophet' (9:17) to 'Lord, I believe' and worship (9:38) — models the catechetical journey from initial encounter to mature faith. The Pharisees' blindness, by contrast, illustrates the danger of preferring institutional certainty to the transformative encounter with Christ. Orthodox spiritual theology identifies this pattern as the difference between knowledge *about* God and knowledge *of* God — the latter requiring the purification of the heart.
