John 6 is central to Orthodox Eucharistic theology. The Bread of Life discourse is read not as metaphor but as a direct teaching about the Eucharist. 'My flesh is true food, and my blood is true drink' (6:55) is understood literally — in the Divine Liturgy, the faithful receive the actual Body and Blood of Christ under the forms of bread and wine. The epiclesis (invocation of the Holy Spirit over the gifts) is the moment of transformation, though Orthodoxy avoids the Western philosophical term 'transubstantiation,' preferring the language of mystery (*mysterion*).

The connection to the manna in the wilderness (6:31-35) situates the Eucharist within the continuous story of God's provision for his people. But the bread from heaven surpasses the manna: 'Your fathers ate the manna in the wilderness, and they died... whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise him up on the last day' (6:49, 54). The departure of many disciples (6:66) is understood as a warning against unworthy or faithless reception of the Eucharist.
