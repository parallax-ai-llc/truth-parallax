## Overview

The Gospel of John holds a uniquely exalted place in Eastern Orthodox theology and worship. It is the "Theological Gospel," the Gospel read during the Paschal season, and the text that most directly articulates the theology of the incarnation, theosis, and the divine light. The Prologue (1:1-18) is chanted at the Paschal Liturgy, and the entire Gospel is read aloud during Bright Week. St. Cyril of Alexandria's massive commentary on John remains the definitive Orthodox patristic treatment, alongside Chrysostom's homilies.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Commentary
Cyril of Alexandria's twelve-book commentary defends the full divinity of the Logos against Nestorian and Arian interpretations. Chrysostom's 88 homilies on John emphasize the practical moral implications of the text. The Fathers read John as both historical narrative and theological revelation, refusing to separate the two.

### Liturgical Hermeneutics
Orthodox interpretation insists that John's meaning is disclosed in worship. The Gospel's references to water, bread, light, and vine are understood as icons of the sacramental life — baptism, Eucharist, and the illumination of the faithful. The Gospel is not merely a text to be studied but a proclamation to be heard within the Divine Liturgy.

## Theological Emphases

- **Logos and Incarnation**: The Prologue is the foundation of Orthodox Christology — the eternal Word became flesh, making theosis possible
- **Theosis**: "I said, you are gods" (10:34) and "that they may be one even as we are one" (17:22) ground the Orthodox doctrine of deification
- **Divine Energies**: John's language of glory, light, and life is read through the Palamite distinction between God's unknowable essence and his uncreated energies
- **The Paschal Mystery**: John's distinctive passion chronology (Jesus dies as the Paschal lamb) and the resurrection appearances are central to Orthodox Paschal theology
- **The Spirit**: The Paraclete passages (14-16) shape Orthodox pneumatology, particularly regarding the Spirit's procession (the *Filioque* controversy)
