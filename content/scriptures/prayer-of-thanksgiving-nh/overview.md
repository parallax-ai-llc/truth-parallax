## Overview

The Prayer of Thanksgiving (NHC VI,7) is a short Hermetic prayer expressing gratitude for the gift of gnosis — the direct knowledge of God received through the divine mind (*nous*). The prayer follows immediately after the Discourse on the Eighth and Ninth in the same codex and may represent the concluding liturgical element of the initiation described in that text. What makes this text particularly significant is the scribal note that follows the prayer, describing a ritual meal: "When they said these things in prayer, they embraced each other and went to eat their holy food, which has no blood in it." This note provides rare evidence that Hermetism was not merely a philosophical tradition but a living religion with communal worship, ritual meals, and liturgical practices. A Latin parallel exists in Asclepius 41.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: One of the few direct witnesses to Hermetic liturgical practice, confirming Hermetism as a living religious tradition
- **Academic**: Critical evidence for the social reality of Hermetic communities and their worship practices
- **Comparative**: Parallels to sacred meals in early Christianity (Eucharist), Judaism (Kiddush), Mithraism, and other ancient religions
- **Christian**: Illuminates the broader context of sacred meal practices in the ancient Mediterranean world
- **Jewish**: Resonates with Jewish traditions of blessing and thanksgiving (*berakhah*) and communal sacred meals
- **Interfaith**: Demonstrates the universal practice of communal thanksgiving and sacred eating across religious traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Gratitude for Gnosis | The prayer gives thanks for the supreme gift: direct knowledge of God |
| Communal Worship | The text attests to a community that prayed, embraced, and ate together |
| Vegetarian Sacred Meal | The ritual food "has no blood in it" — suggesting a vegetarian or ascetic practice |
| Hermetic Liturgy | The prayer and meal together constitute evidence of a Hermetic liturgical order |
