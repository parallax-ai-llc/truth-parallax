## Overview

The Digambara tradition holds the Kalpa Sutra's biographical narratives of the Tirthankaras in high esteem while maintaining that the text, as preserved in the Shvetambara canon, is not an original canonical work but a later compilation. Digambara scholars have their own biographical traditions concerning Mahavira and the other Tirthankaras, drawn from texts such as the Adipurana and Uttarapurana, which sometimes differ in details from the Kalpa Sutra's accounts.

The Digambara reading emphasizes the Tirthankaras' supreme renunciation and equanimity, finding in the Kalpa Sutra's narratives support for the Digambara insistence on complete ascetic nudity and the rejection of all possessions as essential to the path of liberation.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Digambara interpreters read the biographical sections of the Kalpa Sutra critically but appreciatively, accepting the broad outline of the Tirthankaras' lives while preferring their own recensions where details differ. The monastic regulations in the later sections are evaluated against Digambara standards of ascetic rigor, with Digambara readers often arguing that their own tradition preserves a more authentic understanding of the rules Bhadrabahu originally intended.
