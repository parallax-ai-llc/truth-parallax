## Overview

Comparative religious studies examines the Kalpa Sutra as a significant example of sacred biography, hagiographic tradition, and the ritual use of narrative in religious communities. The text's biographical accounts of the Tirthankaras invite comparison with the life stories of the Buddha, Jesus, Muhammad, and other religious founders, while its monastic regulations can be compared with the Vinaya, the Rule of Saint Benedict, and other monastic codes.

The Kalpa Sutra's central role in the Paryushana festival provides material for the comparative study of liturgical reading and the communal performance of sacred narrative — a practice found in traditions ranging from the Jewish reading of Torah to the Christian liturgy of the Word and the Buddhist recitation of sutras.

## Key Interpretive Principles

Comparative scholars analyze the Kalpa Sutra's narrative structure, identifying the typological patterns that characterize sacred biography across traditions: miraculous birth, youthful signs of greatness, crisis of vocation, ascetic trial, enlightenment, teaching mission, and triumphant death. The Jain tradition's distinctive emphasis on the Tirthankara's renunciation of power and privilege is highlighted as a significant variation on universal hagiographic themes.
