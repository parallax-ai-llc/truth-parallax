## Overview

For Advaita Vedanta, the Bhagavad Gita is one of the three pillars of the *prasthanatraya* (triple canon), alongside the Upanishads and Brahma Sutras. Adi Shankara's *Gitabhashya* (8th century) established the definitive Advaita reading, interpreting the Gita as ultimately teaching the identity of the individual self (*atman*) with the absolute reality (*Brahman*).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Shankara's Reading
Shankara reads the Gita as teaching a hierarchy of spiritual practice: karma yoga prepares the mind, bhakti yoga purifies the heart, and jnana yoga — the direct knowledge of non-duality — alone liberates. Action and devotion are preparatory, not ultimate.

### Neo-Advaita
Modern teachers in the Advaita tradition, such as Nisargadatta Maharaj and Ramana Maharshi, frequently cite the Gita's teachings on self-inquiry (especially 2.16-25 and 13.1-6) as pointing directly to non-dual awareness.

## Theological Emphases

- **Self-Knowledge as Liberation**: Knowing "I am Brahman" (*aham brahmasmi*) dissolves ignorance and ends suffering
- **Maya**: The phenomenal world, including the battlefield, is an appearance superimposed on the changeless Brahman
- **Nishkama Karma**: Selfless action purifies the mind but does not itself liberate
- **Renunciation of Doership**: The wise person acts without the sense of being the agent (5.8-9)
