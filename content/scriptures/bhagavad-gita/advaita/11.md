Shankara's interpretation of the universal form (*vishvarupa*) in Chapter 11 is nuanced. While acknowledging the dramatic power of the vision, he treats it as a form of *saguna* (qualified) Brahman-experience rather than the highest realization. Seeing God in a cosmic form is still seeing God as an object — as something separate from the seer. The non-dual realization transcends all forms, including the universal form.

The Advaita reading notes that Arjuna requires "divine eyes" (*divya chakshus*, 11.8) to see the vision, suggesting it is a special grace-given experience rather than the natural state of self-knowledge. True *jnana* requires no special faculties — it is the recognition of what one already is. Nevertheless, the chapter serves an important function in demonstrating that the personal God worshipped through bhakti is a genuine manifestation of Brahman, albeit not the ultimate non-dual reality.
