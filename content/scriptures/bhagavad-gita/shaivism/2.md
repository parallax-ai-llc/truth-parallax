For Shaiva interpreters, Chapter 2's teaching on the indestructible self aligns perfectly with the Kashmir Shaiva doctrine of the eternal conscious self (*atman*) that is ultimately identical with Paramashiva. The famous passage on the self that is never born and never dies (2.20) is read not merely as affirming individual immortality but as pointing to the universal consciousness that transcends all particular forms.

The teaching on equanimity (*samatva*) and steady wisdom (*sthitaprajna*) resonates with the Shaiva ideal of *sahaja samadhi* — natural, effortless abidance in one's true nature even while engaged in worldly activity. Unlike interpretations that emphasize withdrawal, the Shaiva reading sees equanimity as the natural expression of one who has recognized their identity with all-pervasive consciousness.
