## Overview

Although the Bhagavad Gita is ostensibly a Vaishnava text featuring Krishna, Shaiva interpreters have long read it as expressing universal principles compatible with Shaiva theology. Abhinavagupta's *Gitarthasangraha* is the most celebrated Shaiva commentary, reading the Gita through the lens of Kashmir Shaivism's non-dual recognition philosophy.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Kashmir Shaivism
Abhinavagupta interprets Krishna not as a sectarian deity but as the universal consciousness (Paramashiva) that manifests as all reality. The Gita's teachings on yoga, knowledge, and action are read as methods of recognizing one's identity with this supreme consciousness.

### Shaiva Siddhanta
South Indian Shaiva Siddhanta accepts the Gita's philosophical framework while understanding the Supreme described therein as Shiva. The emphasis on grace, surrender, and the guru-disciple relationship resonates strongly with Shaiva devotional practice.

## Theological Emphases

- **Non-dual Consciousness**: The "field and knower" teaching (Chapter 13) maps onto Shaiva categories of Shiva and Shakti
- **Recognition (*pratyabhijna*)**: Liberation is not attaining something new but recognizing one's true nature
- **Dynamic Action**: Unlike quietist readings, Shaiva interpretation celebrates engagement with the world as divine play (*lila*)
- **Integration of Paths**: All yogas are aspects of one integral recognition
