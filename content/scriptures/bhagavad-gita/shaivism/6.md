The meditation practices described in Chapter 6 are read through the Shaiva lens as techniques for recognizing the ever-present reality of universal consciousness. While the text describes withdrawal of senses and concentration on a single point, Shaiva interpreters understand the ultimate goal not as emptiness or cessation but as the vibrant recognition of one's identity with Shiva — the consciousness that is both the meditator and the object of meditation.

Abhinavagupta gives special significance to 6.29-30: "One who sees Me everywhere and sees everything in Me — I am never lost to that one, nor is that one ever lost to Me." This mutual recognition between devotee and deity is read as the Shaiva teaching of *pratyabhijna* — Shiva recognizing Himself in the aspirant and the aspirant recognizing themselves as Shiva. The chapter's final verse (6.47) is accepted but reinterpreted: the supreme yogi is one who has fully recognized non-dual consciousness.
