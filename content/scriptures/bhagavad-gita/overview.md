## Overview

The Bhagavad Gita ("Song of the Lord") is a 700-verse scripture that forms part of the Indian epic Mahabharata (Book 6, chapters 25-42). Set on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, it presents a dialogue between the warrior prince Arjuna and his charioteer Krishna, who reveals himself as the Supreme Being. The Gita addresses Arjuna's moral crisis about fighting in a fratricidal war and expands into a comprehensive philosophical treatise on duty, action, knowledge, and devotion.

## Structure

The Gita's 18 chapters are traditionally grouped into three sections of six chapters each:

1. **Karma Kanda** (Chapters 1-6): The path of action and selfless duty
2. **Bhakti Kanda** (Chapters 7-12): The path of devotion and divine knowledge
3. **Jnana Kanda** (Chapters 13-18): The path of knowledge and liberation

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Bhagavad Gita occupies a central position across Hindu traditions and beyond:

- **Vaishnavism**: Krishna's direct teaching as the Supreme Personality of Godhead
- **Shaivism**: A universal scripture expressing Shaiva principles through Vaishnava vocabulary
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Shankara's commentary established the Gita as one of the triple canon (*prasthanatraya*)
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Madhva's commentary emphasizes the eternal distinction between God and soul
- **Academic**: A foundational text for the study of Indian philosophy and religion
- **Comparative**: Widely studied alongside the Sermon on the Mount, Dhammapada, and Tao Te Ching

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Dharma | Righteous duty appropriate to one's nature and station |
| Karma Yoga | Selfless action without attachment to results |
| Bhakti | Loving devotion to the Divine as a path to liberation |
| Jnana | Discriminative knowledge of self and reality |
| Yoga | Union of the individual self with the Supreme |
