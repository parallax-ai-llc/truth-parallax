Chapter 6's meditation teachings invite comparison with contemplative practices across traditions. The Gita's description of one-pointed concentration on the divine (6.10-15) parallels Christian *centering prayer*, Buddhist *samatha* meditation, and Sufi *muraqaba*. All share the basic structure of withdrawing attention from sensory experience and focusing it on a single point of awareness.

However, the traditions differ in their understanding of what meditation ultimately achieves. For the Gita, meditation leads to union with the divine (*yoga*); for Theravada Buddhism, it leads to insight into impermanence and the cessation of suffering; for Christian mystics, it leads to contemplative union with God (*unio mystica*). The Gita's reassurance that no spiritual effort is wasted (6.40) parallels the Buddhist teaching on merit (*punya*) carrying across lifetimes.
