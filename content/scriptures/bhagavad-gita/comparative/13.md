Chapter 13's distinction between the body-field and its knower resonates with mind-body dualisms across philosophical traditions. The Cartesian distinction between *res cogitans* (thinking substance) and *res extensa* (extended substance), the Buddhist distinction between consciousness (*vijnana*) and form (*rupa*), and the Platonic distinction between soul and body all grapple with the relationship between the experiencing subject and the experienced world.

However, the Gita adds a dimension absent from most Western dualisms: the presence of a divine knower who knows all fields simultaneously (13.3). This concept of an omniscient witness within each being parallels the Quaker "Inner Light," the Sufi concept of the "divine spark" (*latifa*) within the heart, and the Christian mystical tradition of God as *intimior intimo meo* (more intimate to me than I am to myself, Augustine).
