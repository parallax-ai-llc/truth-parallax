The cosmic tree in Chapter 15 belongs to a universal archetype found across world mythologies. The Norse Yggdrasil, the Kabbalistic Tree of Life (*Etz Chaim*), the Siberian shamanic world-tree, and the Tree of Life in Genesis all employ the tree as a cosmic symbol connecting different levels of reality. The Gita's distinctive contribution is the tree's inversion — rooted above in Brahman and branching below into the material world.

The teaching on the Supreme Person (*Purushottama*) transcending both perishable and imperishable reality (15.16-18) parallels theological discussions in other traditions about God's relation to the world. The Christian concept of God as *actus purus* (pure actuality) beyond both the mutable and immutable created orders, and the Islamic concept of God as beyond both *tanzih* (transcendence) and *tashbih* (immanence), address similar metaphysical territory.
