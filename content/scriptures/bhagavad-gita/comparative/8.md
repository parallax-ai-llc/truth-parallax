Chapter 8's teaching on remembering God at the moment of death connects with eschatological practices across traditions. The Tibetan Buddhist tradition of *phowa* (consciousness transference at death), the Christian practice of *ars moriendi* (the art of dying), and the Islamic emphasis on dying with the *shahada* on one's lips all share the conviction that the quality of consciousness at death determines the soul's trajectory.

The chapter's cosmological framework — cycles of creation and dissolution spanning billions of years (8.17-19) — is distinctive in its temporal scale. While Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions typically envision a linear history with a single creation and final judgment, the Gita's cyclical cosmology has more in common with some Buddhist and Jain temporal frameworks, though none match the Gita's specific numeric calculations.
