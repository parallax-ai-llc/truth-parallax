The Gita's resolution of the tension between renunciation and engagement in Chapter 5 addresses a universal religious question. Every major tradition struggles with the relationship between worldly action and spiritual liberation. Christian history oscillates between monastic withdrawal and active engagement ("contemplation vs. action"), while Buddhism debates whether the arahat ideal of personal liberation or the bodhisattva ideal of compassionate action is superior.

The Gita's position — that true renunciation is inner detachment maintained through outer engagement — anticipates Martin Luther's doctrine of "inner freedom and outer service" and the Jesuit ideal of being "contemplatives in action." The Sufi concept of being "in the world but not of it" (*dar dunya, dar dunya nist*) closely parallels the Gita's vision of the liberated person who acts without attachment.
