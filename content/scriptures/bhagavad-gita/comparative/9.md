Chapter 9's declaration of divine accessibility — Krishna accepts a leaf, flower, or water offered with devotion (9.26) — parallels the democratization of the sacred found across world religions. The Hebrew prophets' critique of empty ritual in favor of sincere heart-devotion (Hosea 6:6), Jesus's affirmation that the poor widow's mite exceeds the rich man's offering (Mark 12:41-44), and the Sufi emphasis on the heart's intention over external form all share this principle.

The chapter's universalism (9.32-33) — that women, merchants, and servants can attain God through devotion — invites comparison with Paul's declaration that "in Christ there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female" (Galatians 3:28). Both texts assert spiritual equality while operating within hierarchical social contexts, creating a productive tension between inclusive theology and stratified society.
