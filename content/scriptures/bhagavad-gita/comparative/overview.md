## Overview

The Bhagavad Gita has been one of the most widely studied texts in comparative religion and philosophy since its first English translation by Charles Wilkins in 1785. Its themes of moral duty, divine revelation, paths to liberation, and the nature of the self invite rich comparisons with texts and traditions across the world's religions.

## Key Comparative Themes

### War and Ethics
The Gita's battlefield setting invites comparison with just war theory in Christian ethics (Augustine, Aquinas), the Quran's treatment of jihad, and Buddhist approaches to violence. Arjuna's moral dilemma resonates with Hamlet's paralysis and Abraham's binding of Isaac.

### Paths to the Divine
The Gita's three yogas (karma, bhakti, jnana) parallel the Christian via activa, via devotiva, and via contemplativa. Sufi Islam similarly distinguishes between *sharia* (law/action), *tariqa* (path/devotion), and *haqiqa* (truth/knowledge).

### Mystical Union
Krishna's revelation of the universal form (Chapter 11) and the Gita's teachings on absorption in the divine compare with Meister Eckhart's concept of *Gelassenheit*, Sufi *fana*, and Buddhist *samadhi*.

## Cross-Cultural Reception

- **Transcendentalism**: Emerson and Thoreau drew heavily on the Gita
- **Gandhian Nonviolence**: Gandhi's allegorical reading transformed the battlefield into an inner moral struggle
- **Nuclear Age**: Oppenheimer's famous quotation of 11.32 ("Now I am become Death") entered Western consciousness
- **Interfaith Dialogue**: The Gita's pluralism (4.11: "In whatever way people approach Me, I reward them") has been central to modern interfaith conversation
