Chapter 12's teaching on devotion as the supreme path invites comparison with bhakti-like movements across religions. The Gita's assertion that personal, loving devotion surpasses impersonal philosophical knowledge parallels the Sufi elevation of love (*ishq*) over law (*sharia*), Pascal's "God of Abraham, God of Isaac, God of Jacob — not the God of the philosophers," and the Pure Land Buddhist trust in Amitabha's saving grace over self-power meditation.

The qualities of the ideal devotee (12.13-20) — compassion, equanimity, freedom from ego — resemble the Christian "fruits of the Spirit" (Galatians 5:22-23), the Buddhist paramitas (perfections), and the Sufi *maqamat* (spiritual stations). The convergence across traditions on these qualities suggests either a universal phenomenology of spiritual maturity or shared cultural exchange across the ancient world.
