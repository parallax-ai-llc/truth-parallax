The vision of the universal form in Chapter 11 stands as one of world literature's most powerful theophanies, inviting comparison with visionary experiences across traditions. Ezekiel's vision of the divine chariot (*merkavah*, Ezekiel 1), Isaiah's temple vision (Isaiah 6), John's apocalyptic visions in Revelation, and Muhammad's Night Journey (*Isra and Mi'raj*) all share the structure of an overwhelming encounter with the divine that transcends ordinary categories of perception.

The Gita's distinctive contribution is the simultaneous revelation of creation, preservation, and destruction within the divine form — what Rudolf Otto would call the *mysterium tremendum*. While biblical theophanies typically reveal God's glory and holiness, the *vishvarupa* reveals the entire cosmic process, including death and destruction, as divine activity. This integration of destruction into the divine challenges the sharp good/evil distinction characteristic of Abrahamic theologies.
