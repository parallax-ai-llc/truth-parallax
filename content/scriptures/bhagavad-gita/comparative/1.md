Arjuna's moral paralysis on the battlefield invites comparison with other great moments of ethical crisis in world literature. Like Abraham facing the command to sacrifice Isaac (Genesis 22) and Hamlet paralyzed by the demand for vengeance, Arjuna confronts a situation where duty and human feeling stand in direct conflict. The Gita's resolution — divine instruction transcending human moral calculus — parallels the structure of divine command ethics across traditions.

The warrior's crisis also resonates with the opening of the Tao Te Ching, where Lao Tzu questions the foundations of conventional morality, and with the Book of Job, where suffering challenges established theological frameworks. What makes the Gita distinctive is that the crisis occurs in a public, martial setting rather than in private contemplation, connecting ethical philosophy directly to political action.
