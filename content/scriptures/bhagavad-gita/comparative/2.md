Chapter 2's teaching on the immortal self invites rich comparisons across traditions. The Gita's assertion that the self is never born and never dies (2.20) resonates with the Platonic doctrine of the soul's immortality in the Phaedo, though the Gita's self is more radically transcendent — it is not merely immortal but entirely beyond the categories of being and non-being.

The concept of *nishkama karma* (selfless action without attachment to results) has been compared with Kant's categorical imperative, which similarly demands that moral action be performed from duty rather than desire. However, where Kant grounds duty in reason alone, the Gita roots it in cosmic and divine order (*dharma*). The Stoic concept of acting "according to nature" (*kata physin*) while remaining detached from outcomes provides perhaps the closest Western parallel.
