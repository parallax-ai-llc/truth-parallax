The Gita's contrast between divine and demonic natures in Chapter 16 resonates with moral dualism across traditions. Zoroastrianism's fundamental division between *asha* (truth/order) and *druj* (lie/chaos), the Christian opposition of virtues and vices, and the Buddhist opposition of *kusala* (wholesome) and *akusala* (unwholesome) states all share the intuition that human character falls into opposing orientations.

However, the Gita's framework is subtler than simple moral dualism. The divine and demonic are tendencies within consciousness, not cosmic forces in eternal combat (as in Zoroastrianism). This resembles the rabbinic concept of the *yetzer hatov* (good inclination) and *yetzer hara* (evil inclination) coexisting within each person, and the Buddhist understanding of defilements (*kleshas*) as removable obscurations rather than inherent evil.
