The catalog of divine glories in Chapter 10 belongs to a widespread genre of divine self-revelation found across ancient religions. The Egyptian "Hymn to Aten," the Zoroastrian *Yasna*, and the biblical theophany in Job 38-41 ("Where were you when I laid the earth's foundation?") all present the divine through the enumeration of cosmic powers and excellences.

The comparative significance lies in the Gita's method of divine self-identification. Unlike monotheistic traditions that emphasize God's radical otherness from creation, Krishna identifies himself *as* the best in every category — the Ganges among rivers, the lion among animals, the mind among the senses. This "inclusive" theism, where God is simultaneously creator and creation's finest expression, differs markedly from the Abrahamic insistence on the creator-creature distinction.
