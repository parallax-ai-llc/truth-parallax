The doctrine of divine incarnation in Chapter 4 invites fundamental comparisons with Christology. Both Krishna's descent (*avatara*) and Christ's incarnation assert that the transcendent God enters history in personal form. However, significant differences emerge: Krishna incarnates repeatedly across cosmic cycles, while Christian orthodoxy affirms a single, unique incarnation. Krishna's body is said to be eternal and spiritual, while Christ assumes genuine human nature.

Buddhist conceptions of the *bodhisattva* — one who delays final liberation to help other beings — provide another comparative lens. Like Krishna, the bodhisattva descends from a higher state to assist those trapped in suffering. The Mahayana doctrine of *upaya* (skillful means) parallels the Gita's teaching that the divine adapts its teaching to different levels of understanding (4.11).
