The three gunas of Chapter 14 — sattva, rajas, tamas — provide a tripartite model of psychological and spiritual states that invites comparison with other classificatory systems. The Christian tradition of the three "ways" — purgative (tamas → removing darkness), illuminative (rajas → active seeking), and unitive (sattva → luminous union) — maps loosely onto the guna framework, though the correspondence is imperfect.

The Taoist concept of yin and yang offers a related but distinct dual-polarity model, while the Kabbalistic *sefirot* present a ten-fold typology. What makes the guna system distinctive is its insistence that even the highest quality (sattva) is ultimately binding — transcendence requires going beyond all three qualities, not simply maximizing goodness. This insight parallels the Zen teaching that attachment to enlightenment is itself a form of bondage.
