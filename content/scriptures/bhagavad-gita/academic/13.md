Academic analysis of Chapter 13 identifies it as drawing heavily on Samkhya philosophical categories while transforming them in a theistic direction. The field/knower distinction (13.1-7) maps onto the Samkhya purusha/prakriti dualism, but the introduction of a supreme knower who knows all fields (13.3) adds a theistic dimension absent from classical Samkhya.

The twenty qualities of knowledge (13.8-12) have been compared with similar lists in the Mokshadharma section of the Mahabharata and in Buddhist texts, suggesting a shared ethical vocabulary. Scholars debate whether these verses reflect the Gita's original composition or represent an interpolation from another source. The chapter's syncretic vocabulary — combining Samkhya, Vedanta, and Yoga terminology — exemplifies the Gita's characteristic method of philosophical synthesis.
