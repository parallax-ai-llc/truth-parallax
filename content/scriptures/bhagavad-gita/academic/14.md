Chapter 14's systematic exposition of the three gunas provides important evidence for the development of Samkhya categories. Scholars note that the Gita's treatment differs from classical Samkhya (as found in the Samkhya Karika) in several respects: the gunas are subordinated to a theistic framework, and liberation is achieved through devotion rather than through discriminative knowledge (*viveka*) alone.

The chapter's psychological analysis — sattva binds through attachment to happiness and knowledge (14.6), rajas through attachment to action (14.7), tamas through negligence and sleep (14.8) — has attracted attention from scholars interested in Indian psychology. The guna theory provides a non-moralistic framework for understanding human behavior, treating virtue and vice as natural tendencies rather than moral absolutes.
