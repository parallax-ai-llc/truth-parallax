## Overview

Academic scholarship approaches the Bhagavad Gita as a historically situated text, examining its composition, dating, literary structure, and philosophical content through critical methods. Scholars generally date the Gita to between the 3rd century BCE and the 2nd century CE, viewing it as a later interpolation into the Mahabharata that synthesizes multiple philosophical and religious currents of its era.

## Key Scholarly Approaches

### Textual and Historical Criticism
Scholars such as Richard Garbe and E.W. Hopkins proposed that the Gita was composed in stages, with an original theistic poem later expanded with Samkhya, Yoga, and Vedantic material. More recent scholarship (e.g., Angelika Malinar) has questioned these layer theories.

### Philosophical Analysis
The Gita's relationship to earlier Samkhya-Yoga, Upanishadic, and emerging Bhakti traditions is a major focus. Robert Minor's *Bhagavad-Gita: An Exegetical Commentary* traces the internal logic of the text's philosophical arguments.

## Research Themes

- **Dating**: Consensus places composition between 200 BCE and 200 CE, with most scholars favoring the 2nd-1st century BCE
- **Interpolation Theory**: The Gita's relationship to the broader Mahabharata narrative remains debated
- **Synthesis vs. Eclecticism**: Whether the Gita achieves a genuine philosophical synthesis or merely juxtaposes different traditions
- **Colonial Reception**: The Gita's elevation to "Hindu Bible" status in the 19th century under colonial and nationalist pressures
