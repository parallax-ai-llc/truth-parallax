Chapter 12's direct comparison between personal and impersonal worship (12.1-5) has been analyzed by scholars as reflecting a real debate within Indian religious culture between devotional theism and philosophical monism. The Gita's preference for personal worship likely reflects its Bhagavata theological roots, though the acceptance of the impersonal path (12.3-4) suggests a desire to incorporate rather than reject the Upanishadic tradition.

The list of devotional qualities (12.13-20) has been compared with Buddhist virtue lists and Jain ethical teachings, leading some scholars to posit a shared "renunciant ethic" across Indian traditions. The emphasis on equanimity, freedom from ego, and compassion toward all beings transcends sectarian boundaries and may reflect the cosmopolitan ethical climate of the Gita's milieu.
