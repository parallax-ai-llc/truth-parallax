Scholars analyze Chapter 10's catalog of divine glories (*vibhutis*) as reflecting the religious culture of the Gita's milieu. The specific items mentioned — Vishnu among the Adityas, Shankara (Shiva) among the Rudras, Meru among mountains — provide evidence for the pantheon and cosmology current in the text's compositional period.

The literary technique of listing divine manifestations has parallels in other ancient texts, including Vedic hymns and the Hymn to Aten in Egyptian literature. Scholars note that by claiming to be the "best" in every category, Krishna/Vishnu asserts supremacy not by negating other deities but by subsuming them. This strategy of hierarchical inclusion is characteristic of what scholars call "inclusive monotheism," a theological mode particularly prominent in Hindu traditions.
