Scholars read Chapter 5 as attempting to reconcile two competing religious ideals that had emerged in Indian culture by the late first millennium BCE: the householder's active engagement in Vedic ritual life and the renunciant's withdrawal from society. The Gita's solution — that inner renunciation of ego and attachment is compatible with outer action — has been described by scholars as a "middle way" between these extremes.

The chapter's philosophical language shows influence from multiple sources. The enumeration of senses and their activities (5.8-9) draws on Samkhya categories, while the concept of the self as non-doer reflects Upanishadic thought. The closing verses on meditation (5.27-28) introduce yogic terminology that will be developed more fully in Chapter 6, suggesting to some scholars a thematic bridge between compositional layers.
