Chapter 9's claim that Krishna is the creator, sustainer, and destroyer of the universe (9.7-8) has been analyzed as representing a mature form of Indian monotheism that absorbs the earlier polytheistic framework. The critique of Vedic ritualism (9.20-21) — those who worship through sacrifice attain heaven but must return — positions the Gita's bhakti as superior to the traditional Vedic path.

The universalist declaration of 9.32-33, opening salvation to women, vaishyas, and shudras, has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate. Some read it as genuinely inclusive, while others note the embedded hierarchy ("even those of sinful birth"). The verse's social implications were extensively discussed during India's colonial period, with reformers using it to challenge caste discrimination and traditionalists arguing it presupposes the varna system.
