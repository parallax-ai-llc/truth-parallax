Scholars have given special attention to Chapter 15's metaphor of the cosmic *ashvattha* (fig) tree, tracing it to Rig Veda 1.24.7 and Katha Upanishad 6.1. The inverted tree — rooted above and branching below — appears across Indo-European mythology and may reflect a shared cosmological archetype. In the Gita's context, it serves as a comprehensive image for the manifest universe emanating from the transcendent source.

The chapter's three-fold division of reality into perishable, imperishable, and Supreme Person (15.16-18) has been analyzed as the Gita's most explicit metaphysical statement. Scholars debate whether this represents a form of Samkhya modified by theism, an independent theological innovation, or a synthesis of multiple sources. The claim that this teaching is "the most secret science" (*guhyatamam shastram*, 15.20) suggests the Gita's author(s) considered this the text's philosophical culmination.
