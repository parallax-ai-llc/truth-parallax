Academic analysis of Chapter 3 focuses on its innovative synthesis of Vedic ritual action (*karma*) with Upanishadic renunciation (*sannyasa*). The concept of *nishkama karma* — action without desire for fruits — represents what many scholars consider the Gita's most original philosophical contribution, resolving a tension that had divided Indian religious thought for centuries.

The chapter's reference to Janaka (3.20) as a model of active engagement connects the Gita's karma yoga to earlier Upanishadic traditions (Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 4.2) where Janaka appears as a philosopher-king. Scholars note that Krishna's argument from cosmic order (3.22-24) — that God Himself acts though having nothing to gain — provides a theological grounding for activism that distinguishes the Gita from the world-negating tendencies of contemporary Jain and Buddhist movements.
