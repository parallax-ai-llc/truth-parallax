Chapter 8's eschatological teachings — on the paths of light and darkness (8.24-26) and the ultimate abode beyond the manifest cosmos (8.20-21) — draw on material found in the Chandogya and Brihadaranyaka Upanishads. Scholars trace the "path of the gods" (*devayana*) and "path of the ancestors" (*pitriyana*) to Vedic ritual cosmology, noting how the Gita reinterprets these paths in a devotional framework.

The doctrine of remembering God at the moment of death (8.5-8) has parallels in Buddhist practice (*sati*) and may reflect shared meditative traditions of the Ganges plain. Scholars note that this teaching democratizes access to the divine — one need not be a Vedic ritualist to attain liberation, only a devoted practitioner. This accessibility may be part of the Gita's broader strategy of incorporating and transcending existing religious options.
