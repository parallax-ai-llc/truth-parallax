Academic analysis of Chapter 7 identifies it as marking a shift from the Gita's earlier philosophical discourse to a more explicitly theological mode. The language of personal devotion (*bhakti*) becomes prominent, and Krishna begins revealing his divine identity in terms that go beyond philosophical abstraction. Scholars debate whether this shift reflects a different compositional layer or a deliberate rhetorical progression.

The distinction between higher and lower natures (7.4-5) has been analyzed in relation to Samkhya dualism, though the Gita modifies Samkhya by making both natures dependent on a theistic supreme being. The mention of four types of devotees (7.16) provides sociological evidence for the diversity of religious motivation in the Gita's contemporary world, while the criticism of worshippers of other gods (7.20-23) suggests sectarian competition.
