Chapter 2 is widely recognized by scholars as containing some of the Gita's earliest material, with the self/soul passages (2.12-25) likely drawing on pre-existing Samkhya and Upanishadic sources. The concept of the indestructible self bears clear parallels to the Katha Upanishad (1.2.18-19), leading scholars like Franklin Edgerton to argue for direct literary dependence.

The chapter's juxtaposition of three distinct philosophical vocabularies — Samkhya dualism (purusha/prakriti), Vedantic monism (Brahman/atman), and emerging Bhakti theism — has led to debate about the Gita's compositional history. Richard Garbe influentially proposed that 2.39 marks a seam between an original Samkhya-Yoga text and later Vedantic additions, though this layer theory has been challenged by more recent holistic readings such as Angelika Malinar's.
