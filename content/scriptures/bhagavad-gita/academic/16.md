Academic analysis of Chapter 16 provides insights into the social and moral world of the Gita's composition. The twenty-six divine qualities and six demonic qualities (16.1-4) reflect the ethical vocabulary of the late Vedic and early classical period. Scholars note parallels with Dharmashastra literature and with Buddhist virtue/vice lists, suggesting a shared ethical discourse.

The description of demonic persons (16.7-20) — materialistic, arrogant, enslaved by desire — has been read by scholars as a critique of specific philosophical and social tendencies in the Gita's milieu, possibly targeting materialist schools like Charvaka/Lokayata. The emphasis on following scriptural authority (16.23-24) may reflect concerns about antinomian tendencies within devotional movements.
