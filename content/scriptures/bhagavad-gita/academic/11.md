The vision of the universal form in Chapter 11 has been the subject of extensive academic analysis as a literary, theological, and phenomenological text. Scholars note its affinities with theophany accounts in other traditions — Ezekiel's vision of the chariot, Muhammad's Night Journey — while recognizing its distinctive Indian features, particularly the simultaneous revelation of creation and destruction within God's body.

The chapter's rhetoric of terror and awe has been analyzed through the lens of Rudolf Otto's concept of the *mysterium tremendum et fascinans*. Scholars also note a structural shift: Arjuna moves from requesting the vision (11.3-4) to being overwhelmed and begging for its withdrawal (11.45). This narrative arc suggests a sophisticated understanding of the limits of visionary experience and the human need for accessible divine forms.
