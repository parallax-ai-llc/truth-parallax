Dvaita reads Chapter 15's teaching on the Supreme Person (*Purushottama*) as the climactic metaphysical declaration of the Gita. The three categories — the perishable (matter), the imperishable (individual souls), and the Supreme Person (Vishnu) — are ontologically distinct and eternally real. Madhva rejects any interpretation that collapses these categories into a single reality.

The cosmic *ashvattha* tree (15.1-4) is read as a genuine cosmological description of the material world's structure, rooted in Brahman and extending through the realms of existence. "Cutting down" the tree means liberating the soul from material entanglement through knowledge of God and devotion to Him. The declaration that the Supreme Person is "celebrated in the Vedas as Purushottama" (15.18) is read as establishing that the Vedas themselves testify to Vishnu's absolute supremacy.
