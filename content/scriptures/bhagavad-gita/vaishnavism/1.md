In Vaishnava reading, Arjuna's despair on the battlefield of Kurukshetra is not mere emotional weakness but the divinely arranged catalyst for Krishna's supreme teaching. Arjuna's collapse — dropping his Gandiva bow and refusing to fight — reveals the inadequacy of worldly reasoning when confronted with transcendent duty. Krishna will systematically dismantle Arjuna's objections over the following chapters.

Vaishnavas emphasize that Arjuna's attachment to family members (1.26-28) represents the fundamental error of bodily identification. His grief over "destroying the family" reflects genuine moral sensitivity, yet it is ultimately misplaced because it ignores the eternal nature of the soul. This chapter sets the stage for Krishna's revelation that the self is indestructible (2.17-25) and that divine will supersedes human calculation.
