## Overview

In Vaishnava tradition, the Bhagavad Gita is the direct speech of Lord Krishna, the Supreme Personality of Godhead (Bhagavan). It is not merely a philosophical text but a divine revelation spoken on the battlefield to guide Arjuna — and through him, all humanity — toward loving surrender to God.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Gaudiya Vaishnavism
Following Chaitanya Mahaprabhu and later Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, this school reads the Gita as culminating in pure *bhakti* (devotion). The final instruction — "Abandon all varieties of religion and surrender unto Me" (18.66) — is taken as the supreme teaching.

### Sri Vaishnavism
Ramanuja's *Gitabhashya* interprets the Gita through *vishishtadvaita* (qualified non-dualism), emphasizing that the individual soul is real, distinct from yet dependent on God, and that loving service is the highest goal.

## Theological Emphases

- **Krishna's Supremacy**: Chapter 11's universal form (*vishvarupa*) demonstrates Krishna's absolute lordship
- **Bhakti as Supreme Path**: While karma and jnana are valid, devotion surpasses both (12.1-7)
- **Grace (*prasada*)**: Liberation comes ultimately through divine grace, not human effort alone
- **Eternal Individuality**: The soul retains its identity even in liberation
