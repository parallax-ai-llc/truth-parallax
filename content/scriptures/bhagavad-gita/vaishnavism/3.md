Karma Yoga, as taught in Chapter 3, holds special significance in Vaishnava theology because it establishes the principle of acting for Krishna's pleasure rather than for personal gain. Krishna declares that no one can remain inactive even for a moment (3.5) and that action performed as sacrifice to Vishnu frees one from bondage (3.9). This verse is central to Vaishnava practice.

The Vaishnava reading emphasizes that all action should be offered to Krishna as *yajna* (sacrifice). Krishna's own example is instructive: though He has nothing to gain, He continues to act to set the standard for humanity (3.22-24). The chapter's teaching on desire and anger as enemies of the soul (3.36-43) underscores the Vaishnava emphasis on purifying one's intentions through devotional practice.
