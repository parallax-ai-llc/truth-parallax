Chapter 2 is regarded by Vaishnavas as containing the essence of the entire Gita. Krishna begins by rebuking Arjuna's grief as unbecoming of an enlightened person (2.2-3) and then delivers the foundational teaching on the immortality of the soul (*atman*). The famous verses 2.13-25 establish that the self is eternal, unborn, and indestructible — the cornerstone of all subsequent Vaishnava philosophy.

Krishna introduces *nishkama karma* (selfless action) and *buddhi yoga* (yoga of the intellect), urging Arjuna to fight without attachment to outcomes. The description of the *sthitaprajna* (person of steady wisdom) in 2.54-72 provides the Vaishnava ideal: one who has withdrawn the senses from their objects and rests in Krishna-consciousness. Prabhupada emphasizes that this steady wisdom is ultimately rooted in devotion to Krishna, not mere philosophical detachment.
