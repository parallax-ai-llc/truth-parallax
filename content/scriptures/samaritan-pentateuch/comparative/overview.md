## Overview

The comparative study of the Samaritan Pentateuch illuminates the broader phenomenon of scriptural divergence within related religious communities. The Samaritan-Jewish split — traditionally dated to the fourth century BCE — produced two communities sharing the same foundational text yet differing in precisely those passages that define communal identity, particularly the choice of sacred mountain (Gerizim versus Jerusalem).

This pattern of strategic textual variation within a shared scriptural framework invites comparison with other traditions: Sunni and Shia variations in hadith literature, Theravada and Mahayana differences in Buddhist canon, and Catholic and Protestant disagreements over the biblical apocrypha. The Samaritan Pentateuch demonstrates that scriptural divergence is not merely a matter of accidental corruption but often reflects deliberate theological choices made by communities in the process of defining their identity.
