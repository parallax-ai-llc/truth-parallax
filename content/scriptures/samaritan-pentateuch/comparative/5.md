## Comparative Reading

The comparative study of the Samaritan Deuteronomy reveals how a single text can encode competing claims to sacred space. The struggle between Gerizim and Jerusalem as the divinely chosen sanctuary mirrors similar disputes across religious traditions: the competition between Mecca and Medina in early Islam, the rivalry between Bodh Gaya and other Buddhist pilgrimage sites, and the contested sanctity of various Hindu *tirthas*.

The Samaritan Tenth Commandment demonstrates that sacred geography is not merely a matter of history or tradition but is actively constructed through scriptural interpretation. The ability to ground a territorial claim in the text of scripture — by addition, subtraction, or reinterpretation — is a recurring feature of religious communities across cultures and centuries.
