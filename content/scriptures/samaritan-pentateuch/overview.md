## Overview

The Samaritan Pentateuch is the Samaritan community's version of the Torah — the five books of Moses — written in the Samaritan script (a descendant of Paleo-Hebrew) and preserving a textual tradition that diverges in approximately 6,000 instances from the Masoretic Text used in mainstream Judaism. The Samaritan community, centered on Mount Gerizim near modern-day Nablus, considers their Pentateuch to be the authentic, uncorrupted text of the Torah, faithfully transmitted since the time of Moses and the high priest Abisha.

## Structure

The Samaritan Pentateuch follows the same five-book division as the Jewish Torah:

1. **Genesis** — Creation, patriarchs, and the origins of Israel
2. **Exodus** — Deliverance from Egypt, the covenant at Sinai, and the tabernacle
3. **Leviticus** — Priestly law, sacrificial regulations, and holiness codes
4. **Numbers** — Wilderness journeys, census data, and legal supplements
5. **Deuteronomy** — Moses' farewell speeches, including the distinctive Samaritan Tenth Commandment regarding Mount Gerizim

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Samaritan**: The sole canonical scripture of the Samaritan religion, read in its entirety during annual liturgical cycles
- **Jewish**: A crucial textual witness for understanding the transmission history of the Hebrew Bible
- **Academic**: An indispensable resource for textual criticism of the Pentateuch, especially in light of Dead Sea Scroll parallels
- **Comparative**: Offers insight into how related communities preserve and adapt shared sacred texts
- **Protestant**: Informs scholarly Bible translation by providing alternative readings for critical passages
- **Historical-Critical**: Essential for reconstructing the editorial history of the Pentateuch

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mount Gerizim | The central sanctuary chosen by God, replacing Jerusalem in the Samaritan tradition |
| Textual Fidelity | The Samaritan claim to possess the original, unaltered Torah |
| Mosaic Authority | The exclusive authority of the five books of Moses, with no prophetic or wisdom canon |
| Tenth Commandment | The distinctive commandment to build an altar on Mount Gerizim |
| Ancient Script | Preservation of the Paleo-Hebrew script as a mark of antiquity and authenticity |
