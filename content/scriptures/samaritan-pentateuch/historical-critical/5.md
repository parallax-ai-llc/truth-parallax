## Historical-Critical Reading

Historical-critical scholars regard the Samaritan Deuteronomy as the most important section of the Samaritan Pentateuch for understanding the editorial history of the Torah. The Tenth Commandment's construction from dispersed Deuteronomic verses illustrates how ancient editors created new textual units by reorganizing existing material — a technique that has implications for understanding the composition of the Pentateuch as a whole.

The verb tense variant ('will choose' versus 'has chosen') is analyzed within the framework of Deuteronomistic theology. Historical-critical scholars note that the centralization formula in Deuteronomy is widely regarded as a product of the seventh-century BCE reform movement associated with King Josiah. The Samaritan reading may preserve a pre-Josianic form of the text that identified the chosen place explicitly, or it may represent a post-schism modification. This question remains one of the most debated issues in Pentateuchal criticism.
