## Jewish Reading

Jewish scholars note that the Samaritan Leviticus contains fewer major variants than Genesis, Exodus, or Deuteronomy, reflecting the relatively stable transmission of priestly legal material across both communities. The shared preservation of Levitical law suggests a common priestly heritage predating the Samaritan-Jewish division.

The minor variants that do exist — differences in sacrificial procedures, purity regulations, and festival observances — illuminate the ways in which priestly law was understood and applied in different communal contexts. Jewish scholars find in the Samaritan Leviticus evidence for the diversity of priestly practice in the Second Temple period, when multiple temple communities (Jerusalem, Gerizim, Leontopolis) maintained parallel sacrificial systems.
