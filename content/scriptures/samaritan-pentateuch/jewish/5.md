## Jewish Reading

Jewish scholars find the Samaritan Deuteronomy the most theologically significant section of the Samaritan Pentateuch. The Tenth Commandment — composed of material from Deuteronomy 11:29a, 27:2b-3a, 27:4a, 27:5-7, and 11:30 — is the clearest example of sectarian modification in the entire Samaritan textual tradition, designed to elevate Mount Gerizim to the status of divinely chosen sanctuary.

The verb tense change from 'will choose' to 'has chosen' throughout Deuteronomy is equally significant, as it transforms the book's forward-looking theology of a future chosen place into a backward-looking theology of an already-revealed sacred site. Jewish scholars debate whether the Masoretic future tense represents the original reading or whether it was itself modified to support Jerusalem's claim after the construction of Solomon's temple.
