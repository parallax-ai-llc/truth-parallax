**1** [Theological Significance] The Samaritan Pentateuch represents an independent textual tradition of the Five Books of Moses, preserved by the Samaritan community centered on Mount Gerizim near modern-day Nablus.

**2** The Samaritans accept only the Torah — the five books of Moses — as Scripture. They do not accept the Prophets, the Writings, or any other books of the Hebrew Bible.

**3** The central theological difference is the status of Mount Gerizim as the divinely chosen place of worship, rather than Mount Zion in Jerusalem. This is supported by multiple textual variants throughout the Samaritan Pentateuch.

**4** The Samaritan community has maintained an unbroken tradition of Passover sacrifice on Mount Gerizim to the present day, following the prescriptions of the Torah as preserved in their text.

**5** [Deuteronomy 34:10] And there arose not a prophet since in Israel like unto Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face. [Note: The Samaritan reads "has not arisen" rather than "arose not," reflecting the belief that Moses remains the supreme and final prophet.]

**6** The Samaritan Pentateuch, along with the Dead Sea Scrolls and the Septuagint, provides essential evidence for understanding the history of the biblical text and the diversity of textual traditions in ancient Israel.
