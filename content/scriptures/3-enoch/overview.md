## Overview

3 Enoch (Sefer Hekhalot, "The Book of the Heavenly Palaces") is a Hebrew pseudepigraphical text attributed to the tanna Rabbi Ishmael ben Elisha, who narrates his ascent to the seventh heaven and his encounters with the archangel Metatron. The text belongs to the Hekhalot literature — a body of Jewish mystical writings concerned with visionary ascent through the heavenly palaces (hekhalot) to the divine throne (merkabah). It is generally dated to the fifth or sixth century CE, though it incorporates much earlier traditions.

The central revelation of 3 Enoch is the identification of Metatron with the transformed patriarch Enoch: having been taken up to heaven (Genesis 5:24), Enoch was transformed into the supreme angel Metatron, the "Prince of the Divine Presence" (Sar ha-Panim), endowed with a throne, a crown, and seventy names. Metatron serves as the heavenly scribe, the mediator between God and the angelic hosts, and the guide who reveals the secrets of the divine realm to Rabbi Ishmael.

3 Enoch is a pivotal text for understanding the development of Jewish mysticism from its Second Temple roots through the rabbinic period and into the medieval Kabbalah. The Enoch-Metatron tradition it preserves became a cornerstone of later Jewish esoteric thought.

## Structure

3 Enoch comprises 48 chapters organized around Rabbi Ishmael's heavenly journey and Metatron's revelations:

1. **The Ascent** (Chapters 1-2): Rabbi Ishmael's ascent to the seventh palace and his encounter with the hostile gatekeeping angels
2. **Metatron's Identity** (Chapters 3-16): The transformation of Enoch into Metatron, his exaltation, his names, titles, and attributes
3. **The Heavenly Court** (Chapters 17-28): The structure of the heavenly palaces, the angelic hierarchies, and the divine throne
4. **Cosmic Mysteries** (Chapters 29-40): The celestial bodies, the souls of the righteous and wicked, the cosmic curtain (Pargod) revealing all of history
5. **Divine Judgment and Worship** (Chapters 41-48): The heavenly liturgy, the divine judgment, and the proclamation of God's sovereignty

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: A primary source for the study of Jewish mysticism, Hekhalot literature, and the development of angelic mediation in Judaism
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels with Gnostic archon-systems, Islamic angelology, and Christian hierarchical angelology
- **Protestant**: Illuminates the development of Jewish angelology and its influence on New Testament conceptions of Christ and angels
- **Catholic**: Significant for understanding Jewish mystical traditions and the development of angelological doctrine
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Resonates with Orthodox traditions of the divine liturgy, angelic hierarchies, and the vision of the divine glory
- **Interfaith**: A bridge text connecting Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions of angelic mediation and mystical ascent

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Metatron | The supreme angel, identified as the transformed Enoch, bearing seventy names and serving as the Prince of the Divine Presence |
| Hekhalot / Heavenly Palaces | The seven concentric heavenly palaces through which the mystic ascends to the divine throne |
| Merkabah / Divine Throne | The throne-chariot of God, the ultimate goal of the mystical ascent |
| Angelic Hierarchies | Detailed descriptions of the orders, ranks, and functions of the angelic hosts |
| Heavenly Liturgy | The angelic worship of God, with the Kedushah (trisagion) as its centerpiece |
| Cosmic Secrets | Hidden knowledge about the structure of the universe, the course of history, and the fate of souls |
