## Elders and Community Leadership

1 Peter 5:1–4 addresses the elders (*presbyteroi*) of the community, instructing them to shepherd the flock willingly and without selfish gain. Jewish readers note the parallels with the structure of the Jewish community, where *zekenim* (elders) provided both spiritual and practical leadership. The elder model of community governance is deeply rooted in Jewish tradition, from Moses's seventy elders (Numbers 11:16) to the later rabbinic establishment.

## Humility in Jewish Tradition

1 Peter 5:5–6 — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" — quotes Proverbs 3:34 (LXX). Jewish readers appreciate the grounding of this ethical teaching in the Hebrew Bible's wisdom tradition. Humility is a paramount virtue in rabbinic thought: Moses is described as the most humble person on earth (Numbers 12:3), and the Talmud teaches that the Shekhinah rests only on the humble (Nedarim 38a).

## Babylon and Jewish Memory

1 Peter 5:13's reference to "Babylon" carries profound resonance for Jewish readers. Babylon represents the paradigmatic exile — the destruction of the First Temple and the deportation of Judah. Whether 1 Peter uses "Babylon" as a code for Rome (as is commonly assumed) or draws on the symbol more broadly, Jewish readers hear in this word centuries of exile, memory, and longing for restoration.
