## Overview

Jewish scholars engage with 1 Peter primarily through the letter's extensive appropriation of Israel's identity language. The letter addresses Gentile Christians as "a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation" (2:9) — titles drawn directly from Exodus 19:5–6 and originally applied to Israel. This transfer of covenantal identity raises fundamental questions about the relationship between the Church and Israel and the persistence of supersessionist theology in early Christianity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Identity Transfer and Supersessionism
The most pressing issue for Jewish readers is 1 Peter's application of Israel's covenant titles to a predominantly Gentile Christian audience. Jewish scholars note that this language, read uncritically, implies that the Church has replaced Israel as God's chosen people — a claim that Jewish theology firmly rejects and that has historically fueled anti-Jewish sentiment.

### Diaspora as Shared Experience
1 Peter addresses its audience as "exiles of the Dispersion" (1:1), using the language of Jewish diaspora existence. Jewish readers recognize the experience of minority communities maintaining their identity amid a hostile majority culture. The letter's pastoral strategy — maintaining distinctiveness while engaging respectfully with surrounding society — parallels Jewish diaspora experience across centuries.

### Suffering and Minority Identity
The letter's extensive treatment of suffering for one's faith resonates with Jewish experience of persecution. The counsel to endure unjust suffering with dignity while maintaining faithful practice speaks to a shared reality of religious minorities under imperial pressure.

## Theological Emphases

- **Covenant Language**: The appropriation of Exodus 19 titles as a question for Jewish-Christian dialogue
- **Diaspora Identity**: Shared experiences of exile and minority existence
- **Suffering with Integrity**: Endurance under persecution as a mark of covenant faithfulness
- **Supersessionism Critique**: The need to read 1 Peter's identity claims in light of Romans 9–11
