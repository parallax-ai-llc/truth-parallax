## Living as a Minority

1 Peter 4:1–6, with its description of believers who are "surprised" and "maligned" by their former companions for refusing to join in dissolute behavior, resonates with the Jewish experience of maintaining distinctive practices in a Gentile world. Jewish readers recognize the social cost of religious distinctiveness — the suspicion, mockery, and hostility that comes from refusing to conform to majority cultural norms.

## Hospitality and Community

1 Peter 4:8–11, emphasizing love, hospitality, and the use of gifts for mutual service, reflects values deeply rooted in Jewish community life. The practice of *hachnasat orchim* (hospitality to strangers) is a central Jewish virtue, and the emphasis on communal responsibility for one another echoes the rabbinic principle that all Israel is responsible for one another (*kol Yisrael arevim zeh bazeh*).

## Judgment Beginning with God's House

1 Peter 4:17 — "It is time for judgment to begin with the household of God" — echoes Ezekiel 9:6, where judgment begins at the sanctuary. Jewish readers recognize the prophetic principle that God's own people are held to a higher standard and face divine scrutiny before others. This principle of covenantal accountability is central to Jewish prophetic theology.
