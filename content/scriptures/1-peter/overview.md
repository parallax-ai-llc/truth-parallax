## Overview

1 Peter is a letter attributed to the Apostle Peter, written to Christian communities scattered across Asia Minor (modern Turkey) who were experiencing social marginalization and suffering for their faith. Composed likely between 60 and 90 CE, the letter offers encouragement through the lens of Christ's own suffering and resurrection, calling believers to holy conduct, mutual love, and steadfast hope in the midst of trials.

## Structure

1 Peter divides into several thematic sections:

1. **Greeting and Living Hope** (1:1–12): Praise for the new birth and the inheritance kept in heaven
2. **Call to Holiness** (1:13–2:10): Holy living, spiritual growth, and identity as God's chosen people
3. **Social Conduct** (2:11–3:12): Submission within social structures, the example of Christ's suffering
4. **Suffering for Righteousness** (3:13–4:19): Enduring unjust suffering, Christ's descent, living for God
5. **Final Exhortations** (5:1–14): Instructions to elders, humility, and resistance to the devil

## Cross-Traditional Significance

1 Peter holds an important place across religious traditions:

- **Protestantism**: A key text on perseverance under trial and the priesthood of all believers
- **Catholicism**: Foundation for teaching on baptism, the Church as living stones, and Petrine authority
- **Eastern Orthodoxy**: Central to the theology of Christ's descent into Hades and baptismal theology
- **Judaism**: The letter's use of Israel's identity language raises questions about supersessionism
- **Islam**: Peter's call to patient endurance under suffering resonates with the Islamic virtue of sabr
- **Gnosticism**: Christ's descent to the spirits in prison attracted Gnostic cosmological interpretation

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Suffering | Participation in Christ's sufferings as the path to glory |
| Hope | Living hope grounded in the resurrection |
| Holiness | Called to be holy as God is holy |
| Identity | The Church as a chosen race, royal priesthood, holy nation |
| Submission | Conduct within social structures as a witness to the world |
| Baptism | Baptism as the antitype of Noah's flood |
