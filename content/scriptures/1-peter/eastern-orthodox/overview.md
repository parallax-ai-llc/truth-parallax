## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 1 Peter is shaped by the letter's teaching on Christ's descent to the spirits in prison (3:18–22), its baptismal theology, and its vision of the Church as a living temple. The passage on Christ preaching to the imprisoned spirits (3:19) is central to the Orthodox theology of Christ's descent into Hades — a defining element of Orthodox Paschal theology and iconography.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Christ's Descent into Hades
1 Peter 3:18–20 and 4:6 are the primary New Testament texts for the Orthodox doctrine of Christ's descent into Hades. Orthodox theology teaches that between his death and resurrection, Christ descended to the realm of the dead, proclaimed victory over death, and liberated the righteous who had died before his coming. This is the subject of the central icon of Pascha — the Anastasis — which depicts Christ pulling Adam and Eve from the tombs.

### Baptismal Theology
Orthodox interpretation reads 1 Peter 3:21 — "baptism now saves you" — as a straightforward affirmation of baptismal efficacy. Baptism is understood as a genuine participation in Christ's death and resurrection, not merely a symbol. The connection to Noah's ark (3:20) is read typologically: as the waters of the flood both destroyed and saved, so the waters of baptism bring death to the old self and birth to the new.

### The Church as Living Temple
1 Peter 2:4–10, with its imagery of living stones and a holy priesthood, is central to Orthodox ecclesiology. The Church is the living body of Christ, built on the foundation of the apostles and animated by the Holy Spirit. Every baptized member participates in the royal priesthood through the liturgical and sacramental life.

## Theological Emphases

- **Descent into Hades**: Christ's liberation of the dead as the ultimate victory over death
- **Baptismal Regeneration**: Baptism as a real participation in Christ's Paschal mystery
- **Theosis**: The call to holiness (1:15–16) as the call to participate in the divine nature
- **Cosmic Redemption**: The groaning of creation anticipated in the letter's eschatological hope
