## Living as Strangers and Ethical Witness

1 Peter 2:11–12 calls believers to live as "sojourners and exiles" and to maintain honorable conduct among non-believers. This resonates with the Islamic concept of the Muslim as a moral witness (*shahid*) in the world. The Prophet Muhammad described the believers as strangers in this world, and the Quran states: "Thus We have made you a just community that you will be witnesses over the people" (2:143).

## Submission and Social Order

1 Peter 2:13–17, which counsels submission to governing authorities "for the Lord's sake," resonates with the Islamic concept of respecting legitimate authority within the bounds of divine law. Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) affirms obedience to just rulers while reserving the right of resistance against commands that violate God's law. Both traditions navigate the tension between civic obligation and divine authority.

## The Example of Suffering

1 Peter 2:21–25 presents Christ's patient suffering as a model for believers. While Islam does not accept the crucifixion as understood in Christian theology, the concept of prophetic suffering is familiar. The prophets in Islamic tradition — from Ibrahim to Muhammad — endured persecution and hardship with patience and trust in God. Muslim readers can engage with the letter's moral teaching on patient endurance while holding a different theological framework.
