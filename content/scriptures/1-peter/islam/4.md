## Breaking with Sin

1 Peter 4:1–4, which calls believers to break with sinful behavior and expect hostility from former associates, resonates with the experience of early Muslims who faced persecution in Mecca for abandoning the practices of the *jahiliyyah* (the pre-Islamic age of ignorance). The Prophet Muhammad and his companions endured social ostracism, economic boycott, and violence for their faith. Muslim readers recognize in 1 Peter a parallel experience of conversion and its social consequences.

## Stewardship and Service

1 Peter 4:10–11, calling believers to use their gifts in service to one another, aligns with the Islamic emphasis on *khidmah* (service to others) and the responsible stewardship (*amanah*) of God-given talents. The Quran teaches that all abilities are trusts from God to be used for the benefit of the community, not for selfish gain.

## Suffering for God's Name

1 Peter 4:12–16 encourages believers not to be ashamed of suffering for Christ's name. Muslim readers connect this to the concept of suffering in the path of God (*fi sabil Allah*), which is honored in Islamic tradition. The Quran states: "And do not think of those who are killed in the way of Allah as dead. Rather, they are alive with their Lord, receiving provision" (3:169).
