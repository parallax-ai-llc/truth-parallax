## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 1 Peter emphasizes the letter's ecclesiology, baptismal theology, and its witness to Petrine authority. The letter's rich imagery of the Church as "living stones" built into a "spiritual house" (2:4–5) informs Catholic teaching on the nature of the Church, while its reference to "Babylon" (5:13) has been traditionally understood as a reference to Rome, supporting the claim that Peter ministered and died in the imperial capital.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Petrine Authority
Catholic tradition regards 1 Peter as evidence that Peter exercised pastoral authority over a wide network of churches, writing from Rome ("Babylon," 5:13) to communities throughout Asia Minor. This supports the Catholic understanding of Peter's universal pastoral role, which is continued in the papacy.

### Baptismal Catechesis
Many Catholic scholars read 1 Peter as a baptismal homily or catechetical instruction. The letter's references to new birth (1:3, 23), putting away sin (2:1), and the explicit discussion of baptism as saving "through the resurrection of Jesus Christ" (3:21) are understood as reflecting early baptismal liturgy and catechesis.

### The Church as Spiritual Temple
1 Peter 2:4–10, with its imagery of living stones, a holy priesthood, and a spiritual house, informs Catholic ecclesiology. The Church is understood as the new temple in which spiritual sacrifices are offered — a corporate identity that does not negate but fulfills the Old Testament temple worship.

## Theological Emphases

- **Baptism**: 1 Peter 3:21 as a key text for baptismal regeneration
- **Peter's Roman Ministry**: 1 Peter 5:13 as evidence for Peter's presence in Rome
- **Ministerial Priesthood**: The "royal priesthood" as complementing, not replacing, ordained ministry
- **Redemptive Suffering**: Suffering united with Christ's passion as meritorious (4:13)
