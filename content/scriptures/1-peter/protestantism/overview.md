## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 1 Peter has centered on several distinctive themes: the priesthood of all believers (2:4–10), the theology of suffering, and the nature of Christian hope. The letter's declaration that believers are "a chosen race, a royal priesthood" (2:9) was foundational for the Reformation's challenge to the clerical hierarchy, and its teaching on suffering has shaped Protestant pastoral theology across centuries.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Priesthood of All Believers
1 Peter 2:4–10 is the primary New Testament text for the Reformation doctrine of the priesthood of all believers. Luther drew on this passage to argue that every Christian has direct access to God without the mediation of an ordained priesthood. This does not eliminate the pastoral office but redefines it as a function within the community rather than a separate spiritual caste.

### Theology of Suffering
Protestant interpreters read 1 Peter's extensive teaching on suffering (1:6–7; 2:18–25; 3:13–17; 4:12–19) as pastoral encouragement for persecuted believers. The letter teaches that suffering, when endured for righteousness, participates in Christ's own suffering and results in purified faith. This has been especially important for persecuted Protestant communities throughout history.

### Living Hope
The opening doxology (1:3–12) establishes the letter's governing theme: a "living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead." Protestant interpreters emphasize the eschatological dimension — the inheritance "kept in heaven" — as the foundation for endurance in the present.

## Theological Emphases

- **Royal Priesthood**: Every believer's direct access to God and participation in Christ's priestly ministry
- **Suffering as Purification**: Trials that refine faith like gold tested by fire (1:7)
- **Submission and Witness**: Godly conduct within social structures as a form of evangelism
- **Christ's Example**: The suffering servant of 2:21–25 as the model for Christian endurance
