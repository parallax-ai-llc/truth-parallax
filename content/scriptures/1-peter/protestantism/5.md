## Shepherding the Flock

1 Peter 5:1–4 instructs elders to "shepherd the flock of God" willingly, not for selfish gain, and not by domineering. Protestant ecclesiology has drawn on this passage to define pastoral ministry as servant leadership. The "Chief Shepherd" (5:4) is Christ himself, and human pastors serve under his authority. This text supports the Protestant rejection of hierarchical clericalism.

## Humility and Vigilance

1 Peter 5:5–9 calls for humility and vigilance against "your adversary the devil" who "prowls around like a roaring lion." Protestant spiritual warfare theology draws on this passage, emphasizing the need for sober watchfulness and active resistance through faith. The promise that "after you have suffered a little while, the God of all grace will himself restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you" (5:10) provides assurance of ultimate divine faithfulness.

## Final Greetings

The closing greetings (5:12–14), including the reference to "Babylon" (5:13), are interpreted by most Protestant scholars as a coded reference to Rome. Silvanus is identified as the letter's amanuensis, and the "kiss of love" (5:14) reflects the early Christian practice of communal affection within the worshiping community.
