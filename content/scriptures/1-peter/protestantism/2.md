## Living Stones and the Priesthood of All Believers

1 Peter 2:4–10 is foundational for the Protestant doctrine of the priesthood of all believers. Believers are described as "living stones" being built into a "spiritual house" to offer "spiritual sacrifices." Luther argued that this passage demonstrates that every Christian is a priest before God, with direct access to the Father through Christ. The passage does not eliminate pastoral office but redefines the entire community as priestly.

## Submission in Social Structures

1 Peter 2:11–17 counsels submission to governing authorities and honorable conduct among Gentiles. Protestant interpreters have debated the limits of this submission, particularly in the context of unjust governments. The Reformation itself involved resistance to ecclesiastical authority, and Protestant political theology has developed nuanced frameworks for legitimate civil disobedience while honoring the general principle of ordered society.

## The Suffering Servant

1 Peter 2:21–25 presents Christ's suffering as both atoning sacrifice and moral example: "He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree" (2:24). Protestant interpreters hold together both dimensions — the substitutionary atonement that secures salvation and the exemplary suffering that models patient endurance under injustice.
