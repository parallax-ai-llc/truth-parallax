---
title: "Atma Upanishad Section 2"
scripture: "atma-upanishad"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Now the inner self, the Antaratman, is that which is constituted of mind, intellect, ego, and memory. It is the subtle body which underlies the gross body and persists after the destruction of the gross body.

**2** The inner self perceives through the mind. It reflects through the intellect. It asserts through the ego. It remembers through memory. These four constitute the internal organ, the Antahkarana.

**3** The mind is characterized by thought and doubt. The intellect is characterized by determination and discrimination. The ego is characterized by the sense of "I" and "mine." Memory is characterized by recollection.

**4** The inner self experiences the three states: waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. In the waking state, it experiences the external world through the senses. In the dream state, it creates its own world of experience. In deep sleep, it rests in bliss, knowing nothing.

**5** In the waking state, the self is called Vishva—it who experiences the gross objects through the fourteen organs: the five organs of knowledge, the five organs of action, and the four aspects of the internal organ.

**6** In the dream state, the self is called Taijasa—the luminous one—for it creates and perceives its own light. The impressions gathered during the waking state are projected by the mind, and the dreamer experiences pleasure and pain in that projected world.

**7** In deep sleep, the self is called Prajna—the one who knows—for though no objects are perceived, the self rests in bliss. Upon waking, one says: "I slept well, I knew nothing." This shows that even in deep sleep, consciousness was present as a witness.

**8** The inner self is bound by desire and action. It transmigrates from body to body, carrying with it the subtle impressions of all its deeds. As a caterpillar, having come to the end of a blade of grass, grasps another blade and draws itself over to it, so does the inner self, having come to the end of one body, grasp another body and draw itself over to it.

**9** He who knows the inner self as distinct from the external self has crossed the first veil of ignorance. But the supreme knowledge comes only when one knows the inner self to be identical with the Supreme Self.
