## Overview

The Atma Upanishad belongs to the Atharva Veda and provides a systematic teaching on the three aspects of the self (*atman*): the external self (*bahyatman*), the inner self (*antaratman*), and the supreme self (*paramatman*). Through this threefold analysis, the text guides the seeker from identification with the body and senses through the inner world of mind and intellect to the recognition of the supreme self that transcends all limitation. Its clear, pedagogical structure makes it an important introductory text in the Upanishadic tradition.

## Structure

The Atma Upanishad consists of three sections:

1. **Section 1 — The External Self (Bahyatman)**: The self identified with the body, senses, and external world
2. **Section 2 — The Inner Self (Antaratman)**: The self as mind, intellect, and the inner faculties
3. **Section 3 — The Supreme Self (Paramatman)**: The transcendent self beyond all limitation

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The three levels of self culminating in Paramatman (Vishnu as Supersoul)
- **Shaivism**: The progressive recognition of consciousness from gross to subtle to absolute
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The stripping away of false identifications to reveal the non-dual self
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: The eternal distinction between the individual self and the Supreme Self
- **Academic**: Important for understanding Upanishadic philosophical psychology
- **Comparative**: Parallels with the body-soul-spirit trichotomy in other traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Three Aspects of Self | External, inner, and supreme self as progressive realizations |
| Body-Mind-Spirit | The hierarchy from gross to subtle to transcendent |
| Self-Knowledge | The progressive recognition of one's true identity |
| Transcendence | Moving beyond identification with body and mind |
| Paramatman | The supreme self as the ultimate identity |
