The Agni Purana's opening section on the *dashavatara* (ten incarnations) is a foundational text for Vaishnava theology of divine descent. Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita reading treats each avatar as a real, bodily manifestation of Vishnu's *saulabhya* (gracious accessibility) — the Lord assumes material form not through karmic compulsion but through His sovereign will (*sankalpa*) to rescue devotees and restore dharma. Madhva's Dvaita school goes further, insisting that each avatar possesses a fully divine body (*aprakrita-deha*) untouched by material defect, citing the Agni Purana's descriptions of Vishnu's transcendent qualities persisting through every incarnation.

The Chaitanya Vaishnava tradition draws particular attention to this section's enumeration of Krishna among the avatars, reading it alongside the Bhagavata Purana's declaration that Krishna is *svayam bhagavan* — the source of all incarnations rather than merely one among them. The *bhakti-rasa* framework interprets each avatar narrative as revealing a distinct mood (*rasa*) of the Lord's redemptive activity: Matsya as protector, Narasimha as fierce defender of the devotee, Rama as the ideal of dharmic sovereignty, and Krishna as the complete manifestation of divine love. The section's comprehensive scope demonstrates the Vaishnava principle that all cosmic history is ultimately the play (*lila*) of Vishnu's compassion.
