The Agni Purana's treatment of prosody, meters, rhetorical figures, and *rasa* theory holds particular significance for the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition, which developed *bhakti-rasa* into a sophisticated theological aesthetics. Rupa Goswami's *Bhakti-rasamrita-sindhu* draws on classical rasa theory to articulate five primary moods of devotional relationship with Krishna — *shanta* (peaceful), *dasya* (servitude), *sakhya* (friendship), *vatsalya* (parental), and *madhurya* (conjugal love). The Agni Purana's analysis of aesthetic experience thus becomes, in Vaishnava hands, a framework for understanding the soul's eternal emotional relationship with God.

Ramanuja's tradition reads the section's attention to poetic beauty as reflecting Vishnu's own nature as the supreme aesthetic object — He is *sundara* (beautiful), and all earthly beauty is a fractional reflection of His form. The meters and poetic structures catalogued here are understood as Vishnu's gift to humanity for the composition of devotional hymns and stotra literature. Madhva's school appreciates the emphasis on precision in poetic expression as parallel to the need for exactitude in theological language, where even slight misstatement can lead to doctrinal error. The entire field of poetics, in the Vaishnava view, finds its highest purpose in the glorification (*kirtana*) of the Lord's names, forms, and activities.
