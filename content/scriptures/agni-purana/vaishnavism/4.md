The Agni Purana's extensive treatment of ritual — covering temple construction, *pratishtha* (image installation), *puja* procedures, and *homa* (fire sacrifice) — is read by the Vaishnava tradition as a comprehensive guide to establishing the Lord's tangible presence in the devotee's life. Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita theology holds that temple worship is not symbolic but a genuine encounter with the *archa-avatara* (icon incarnation), wherein Vishnu makes Himself accessible through the consecrated image with the same fullness as His cosmic or incarnational forms. The detailed installation rituals described here are thus understood as the process by which Vishnu consents to inhabit matter for the sake of His devotees.

Madhva's school interprets the ritual prescriptions as confirming the real distinction between worshipper and worshipped — the elaborate protocols of *puja* express the soul's eternal servitude (*nitya-dasya*) to the independent Lord. The Pancharatra Agama tradition, closely allied with Vaishnavism, finds in this section a Puranic validation of its own ritual systems, particularly the consecration of Vishnu temples through *nyasa* (ritual placement of divine presence) and the daily worship cycles that maintain the deity's active presence. The Chaitanya tradition reads the devotional rituals here as preliminary forms of *arcana-bhakti* that, when infused with genuine love, become pathways to spontaneous devotion (*raganuga-bhakti*).
