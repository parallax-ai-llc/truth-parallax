## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Baopuzi examines Ge Hong's alchemical and spiritual system alongside parallel traditions worldwide. The most obvious comparisons are with Western alchemy (Hermetic tradition, Paracelsus, the Philosopher's Stone), Indian rasayana (alchemical medicine within Ayurveda), and the broader category of transformative spiritual practices that employ physical substances and laboratory procedures as vehicles for spiritual attainment.

## Alchemy East and West

Comparative scholars note striking parallels between Ge Hong's gold elixir and the Western Philosopher's Stone: both promise physical immortality or radical longevity, both involve the transmutation of base materials into a supreme substance, and both require the practitioner's moral and spiritual preparation. Yet significant differences emerge: Chinese alchemy emphasizes ingestion of the elixir, while Western alchemy often focuses on the transformative process itself.

## Universal Aspirations

The comparative tradition situates the Baopuzi's quest for immortality within the global human response to mortality. From the Epic of Gilgamesh to transhumanist technology, human cultures have consistently sought to transcend the limits of the mortal body. Ge Hong's systematic approach to this universal aspiration represents one of the most sophisticated and comprehensive programs in the history of this enduring human project.
