## Qu Huo (Dispelling Confusions)

Many confusions surround the practice of the Way. Let them be dispelled.

Confusion: The Way is only for the gifted. Truth: The Way is for all who are sincere. Natural talent varies, but sincerity and perseverance can compensate for any lack of talent. The tortoise arrives at the destination no less surely than the hare.

Confusion: The Way requires poverty and deprivation. Truth: The Way requires simplicity, not poverty. The practitioner should have enough to sustain himself without anxiety. Extreme poverty creates anxiety, which obstructs practice. Extreme wealth creates attachment, which obstructs practice equally. The middle way is best.

Confusion: The Way is opposed to Confucian learning. Truth: The Way encompasses Confucian learning. Filial piety, loyalty, propriety, righteousness -- these are not obstacles to the Way but foundations for it. The man who neglects his duties to family and society in pursuit of spiritual attainment is building on sand.

Confusion: The practice of the Way is a form of escape from the world. Truth: The practitioner who has achieved the Way is more engaged with the world, not less. His clarity of perception allows him to see problems that others miss and to respond to them effectively.

The Master Who Embraces Simplicity concludes: The Way is simple, but men make it complicated. It is near, but men seek it afar. It is within, but men look for it without. Return to simplicity. Return to sincerity. Return to the root. This is the beginning and the end of all cultivation.
