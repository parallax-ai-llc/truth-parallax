## Za Ying (Miscellaneous Responses)

A student asks: "Must one abandon the world entirely to practice the Way?"

I answer: No. There are hermits who achieve the Way in the mountains, and there are officials who achieve it in the court. The location matters less than the quality of one's practice. A man who maintains inner stillness amid the bustle of the market has achieved more than a man who sits in mountain solitude but is inwardly agitated.

A student asks: "How does one know if one is making progress?"

I answer: By several signs. First, the body becomes healthier and more vigorous. Second, the mind becomes calmer and clearer. Third, the emotions become more stable. Fourth, sleep is deep and dreamless. Fifth, the senses become sharper. Sixth, an intuitive understanding develops that goes beyond what reason alone can provide.

A student asks: "What is the relationship between the Way and Confucian ethics?"

I answer: They are not opposed but complementary. Confucian ethics provides the moral foundation without which spiritual cultivation is impossible. The man who seeks the Way but neglects his duties to family and society builds on sand. The man who fulfills his Confucian duties and also cultivates the Way builds on rock.

A student asks: "Is it dangerous to practice the Way?"

I answer: The greatest danger is practicing without proper guidance. But practicing with a good teacher and following the established methods, the Way is as safe as walking a well-marked path.
