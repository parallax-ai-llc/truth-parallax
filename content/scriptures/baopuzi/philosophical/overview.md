## Overview

Philosophical Taoism approaches the Baopuzi as a significant attempt to ground the practical pursuit of immortality in the philosophical foundations laid by Laozi and Zhuangzi. Ge Hong's insistence that the Tao is not merely a philosophical concept but a reality that can be practically engaged through cultivation represents a distinctive philosophical position: the unity of theory and practice, knowledge and transformation.

## Philosophy of Immortality

Philosophical interpreters examine Ge Hong's arguments for the reality of immortality as exercises in natural philosophy. His reasoning draws on analogies with observable natural transformations — the cicada's metamorphosis, the caterpillar's transformation into a butterfly — to argue that the boundary between mortal and immortal is not fixed but permeable through proper cultivation.

## Synthesis of Traditions

The philosophical tradition values Ge Hong's explicit effort to synthesize Confucian and Taoist thought. Unlike earlier Taoist philosophers who defined themselves against Confucianism, Ge Hong argued that Confucian moral cultivation is a necessary foundation for Taoist spiritual practice, a position that anticipates later Neo-Confucian and Neo-Taoist syntheses.
