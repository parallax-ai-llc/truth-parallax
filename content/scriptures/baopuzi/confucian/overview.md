## Overview

Confucian interpretation of the Baopuzi finds in Ge Hong an unusual and significant interlocutor — a committed Taoist who explicitly affirmed the value of Confucian ethics as the necessary foundation for spiritual cultivation. Ge Hong's insistence that moral merit (gongde) is essential for achieving immortality, and that Confucian virtues of loyalty, filial piety, and benevolence must be cultivated alongside Taoist techniques, represents a distinctive position in the history of Chinese religious thought.

## Moral Foundation

Confucian readers highlight Ge Hong's argument that technical cultivation alone cannot achieve immortality without a foundation of moral virtue. This position aligns with the Confucian conviction that self-cultivation is fundamentally an ethical enterprise and that spiritual attainment divorced from moral character is either impossible or dangerous.

## The Outer Chapters

While this commentary focuses on the Inner Chapters, Confucian interpreters note that the Baopuzi's Outer Chapters (Waipian) present an explicitly Confucian social and political philosophy, demonstrating that Ge Hong saw no contradiction between Confucian public engagement and Taoist private cultivation.
