## Overview

The Taittiriya Upanishad belongs to the Krishna Yajur Veda and is part of the Taittiriya Aranyaka. It is celebrated for its systematic teaching on the five sheaths (*pancha kosha*) that envelop the self, its ecstatic declaration that Brahman is bliss (*ananda*), and its vivid convocation address to graduating students. The text moves from practical guidance on conduct and phonetics to profound metaphysical inquiry, culminating in the sage Bhrigu's progressive discovery of Brahman.

## Structure

The three chapters (*vallis*) have distinct characters:

1. **Shiksha Valli (Chapter 1)**: Teachings on phonetics, meditation on Om, ethical instruction, and the convocation address
2. **Brahmananda Valli (Chapter 2)**: The five sheaths doctrine and the teaching that Brahman is bliss
3. **Bhrigu Valli (Chapter 3)**: Bhrigu's progressive inquiry into the nature of Brahman, guided by his father Varuna

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The five sheaths describe layers of the soul's relationship to Vishnu, the innermost bliss
- **Shaivism**: The progressive penetration through sheaths mirrors Shaiva practices of consciousness refinement
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The five sheaths are superimpositions on the one self; Brahman as bliss is the ultimate teaching
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: The sheaths demonstrate the soul's dependence on God at every level of its being
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding Vedic educational institutions and convocation practices
- **Comparative**: The sheath doctrine invites comparison with Neoplatonic emanation and Buddhist aggregates

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Five Sheaths | Food, vital breath, mind, intellect, and bliss as layers of the self |
| Brahman as Bliss | The ultimate reality is *ananda* — joy beyond measure |
| Convocation Address | Ethical instruction for graduates entering the world |
| Bhrigu's Inquiry | Progressive discovery of Brahman through meditation |
| Satyam Jnanam Anantam | "Truth, Knowledge, Infinite" — Brahman's definition |
