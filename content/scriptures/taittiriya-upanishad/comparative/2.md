The five-sheath doctrine presents a model of the human person as layered — from gross body to subtle consciousness — that resonates with anthropologies across traditions. The Neoplatonic hierarchy from matter to the One, the Kabbalistic four worlds (Assiyah, Yetzirah, Beriah, Atzilut), the Buddhist distinction between form and formless realms, and the Sufi concept of progressive veils (*hijab*) all share the conviction that the human being exists simultaneously at multiple levels of reality.

The graduated scale of bliss — ascending from human happiness through celestial joys to infinite divine bliss — parallels Dante's ascending spheres of Paradise, the Pure Land Buddhist concept of progressively more purified buddha-fields, and the Sufi *maqamat* (stations) of increasing proximity to God. Each tradition teaches that ordinary happiness is a faint intimation of a joy beyond imagining, and that the spiritual path leads from lesser to greater participation in this ultimate bliss.
