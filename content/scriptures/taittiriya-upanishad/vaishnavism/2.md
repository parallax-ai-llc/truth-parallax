The Brahmananda Valli opens with the definition "Brahman is truth, knowledge, infinite" (*satyam jnanam anantam brahma*, 2.1) and proceeds through the five sheaths to the ecstatic realization that Brahman is bliss (*ananda*). Vaishnavas identify this bliss with Vishnu's own nature. The famous scale of bliss (2.8) — ascending from human joy through progressively higher celestial joys to the bliss of Brahman — demonstrates that Vishnu's bliss infinitely surpasses all finite happiness. The devotee experiences a share of this bliss through grace, but God's own bliss remains unimaginably greater.

The five sheaths — food (*annamaya*), vital breath (*pranamaya*), mind (*manomaya*), intellect (*vijnanamaya*), and bliss (*anandamaya*) — describe the layers of the soul's embodied existence. Vaishnavas read the innermost bliss sheath not as impersonal Brahman (as Advaita holds) but as the soul's experience of Vishnu's joy. Even this joy is mediated by the Lord's grace and remains infinitely less than God's own infinite, self-subsistent bliss.
