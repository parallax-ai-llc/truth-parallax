The Brahmananda Valli is one of the most important texts for Advaita Vedanta. Shankara's commentary on the definition "satyam jnanam anantam brahma" (2.1) argues that these three terms are not attributes of Brahman but definitions that mutually delimit each other, pointing to an attributeless reality that transcends all categories. "Truth" excludes unreality, "knowledge" excludes inertness, "infinite" excludes finitude — what remains is Brahman, which cannot be positively described.

The five-sheath analysis (*pancha-kosha-viveka*) is Shankara's primary method for discriminating the self from the not-self. Each sheath is shown to be "not the self" because it is objective (known to awareness), changing, and dependent on conditions. Critically, Shankara argues that even the bliss sheath (*anandamaya kosha*) is not the self but a modification of ignorance that must be transcended. The true self is the witness-consciousness that illuminates all five sheaths while being identical with none of them. The scale of bliss (2.8) teaches that Brahman's bliss is not a quantity but a quality of infinity — it is not "very much joy" but the transcendence of all finitude.
