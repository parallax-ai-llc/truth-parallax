Shankara reads Bhrigu's progressive inquiry as a pedagogical illustration of the *pancha-kosha-viveka* (discrimination of the five sheaths) taught in Chapter 2. Bhrigu's successive identifications of Brahman with food, breath, mind, intellect, and bliss demonstrate the method of negation in practice — each identification is tried and found inadequate, pointing beyond itself to a subtler reality. The final identification with bliss is itself a sheath, not the ultimate self; but it is the closest approximation within the realm of experience.

The ecstatic declaration "I am food" (3.10) has generated extensive commentary. Shankara reads it as the joyous realization that the self, as Brahman, is the sustainer and substance of all existence. The apparent contradiction — the self is both the eater and the eaten — dissolves in the non-dual realization that there is only one reality. The threefold exclamation "Oh wonderful!" expresses the astonishment of the self recognizing its own infinite nature after lifetimes of limited identification.
