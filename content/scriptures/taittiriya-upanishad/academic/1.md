The Shiksha Valli provides invaluable evidence for the curriculum and practices of Vedic educational institutions (*gurukula*). The phonetic instruction (1.2) represents the earliest surviving systematic treatment of Sanskrit phonetics, establishing categories (articulation point, accent, quantity, connection, and delivery) that became the foundation of the Vedic auxiliary science of *shiksha* (phonetics). This material predates Panini's grammar and provides a window into pre-Paninian linguistic analysis.

The convocation address (1.11) is one of the most remarkable passages in ancient Indian literature — a formal address to graduating students that combines practical advice, ethical instruction, and a frank acknowledgment of moral uncertainty ("if in doubt, follow the practice of thoughtful persons"). Scholars have compared it to convocation addresses in Greek educational institutions and have noted its significance as evidence for a self-conscious educational tradition in Vedic India that valued both knowledge transmission and character formation.
