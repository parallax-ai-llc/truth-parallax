The concept of repentance as a transformation of consciousness rather than a cataloguing of sins finds parallels across traditions. The Christian concept of metanoia, often translated as "repentance" but literally meaning "change of mind," suggests a transformation of perspective rather than mere remorse. The Jewish concept of teshuvah (return) similarly emphasizes the redirecting of the whole person toward God rather than the enumeration of specific transgressions.

The Islamic concept of tawbah (repentance) includes both the acknowledgment of specific sins and the more radical reorientation of the heart toward God. The Sufi understanding of tawbah as a transformation of the nafs (ego-self) shares common ground with Huineng's teaching that genuine repentance is the recognition of the mind's original nature.

The chapter's integration of vows, precepts, and repentance into a single ceremony parallels the comprehensive initiation rites found across traditions. Christian baptism, which combines purification, vows, and reception into the community, and Jewish conversion, which includes circumcision, immersion, and acceptance of the commandments, similarly integrate multiple ritual elements into a single transformative ceremony.
