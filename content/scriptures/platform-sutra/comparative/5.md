Huineng's redefinition of meditation challenges the equation of spiritual practice with specific physical postures and techniques, a challenge that resonates across traditions. The Quaker tradition of worship through silence, without prescribed forms or rituals, shares something of Huineng's conviction that genuine spiritual practice cannot be reduced to external forms.

The Jewish tradition's debate between the kabbalistic emphasis on kavvanah (intention) and the halakhic emphasis on keva (fixed form) addresses a similar tension. Is prayer primarily a matter of the heart's direction or of the body's proper positioning and the mouth's recitation of prescribed words? Huineng would side with kavvanah, but the rabbinic tradition characteristically insists on both.

The Christian contemplative tradition has also grappled with the question of whether meditation requires specific physical conditions. Brother Lawrence's "Practice of the Presence of God," which teaches awareness of the divine amid the ordinary tasks of a monastery kitchen, parallels Huineng's teaching that true meditation is not confined to the meditation hall. The twentieth-century Christian teacher Frank Laubach similarly advocated continuous awareness of God's presence throughout all daily activities.
