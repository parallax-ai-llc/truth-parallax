Huineng's awakening narrative shares structural features with spiritual autobiography across traditions. Augustine's Confessions, with its moment of conversion in the garden at Milan; al-Ghazali's Deliverance from Error, describing his spiritual crisis and resolution; and the Hasidic tales of the Baal Shem Tov all present a pattern of spiritual crisis, sudden insight, and subsequent teaching authority.

The motif of the illiterate or uneducated person who surpasses the learned in spiritual matters appears across traditions. In Christianity, the apostles are described as "unlettered men" (Acts 4:13) whose spiritual authority confounded the educated. In Islam, the Prophet Muhammad's illiteracy (ummi) is understood as a sign that the Quran is of divine rather than human origin. Huineng's illiteracy functions similarly, demonstrating that awakening depends on spiritual capacity rather than intellectual attainment.

The contest between Shenxiu and Huineng mirrors the broader cross-traditional tension between institutional learning and mystical insight. The Sufi distinction between the scholar (alim) and the mystic (arif), the Christian tension between scholastic theology and mystical experience, and the Hindu distinction between pandits and yogis all reflect this fundamental question about the relationship between knowledge and realization.
