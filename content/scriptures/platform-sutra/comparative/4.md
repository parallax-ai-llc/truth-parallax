The teaching on the inseparability of meditation and wisdom can be compared with similar integrative approaches in other traditions. The hesychast tradition in Eastern Orthodox Christianity, which unites contemplative stillness (hesychia) with the prayer of the heart, shares the conviction that genuine prayer is not an activity separate from insight but a state in which awareness and devotion are fused.

The Hindu tradition of jnana-yoga (the path of knowledge) and dhyana-yoga (the path of meditation) presents a parallel distinction that has been resolved in various ways. Shankara's Advaita Vedanta, which identifies meditation with the recognition of the Self's identity with Brahman, shares Huineng's conviction that practice and understanding are ultimately one. The Bhagavad Gita's integration of karma, bhakti, jnana, and dhyana yogas represents another approach to the same question.

The Sufi concept of muraqaba (meditation) as inseparable from ma'rifa (gnosis) offers yet another parallel. In the Sufi tradition, true meditation is not a technique for producing altered states but the direct recognition of the divine reality (haqiqa) within the heart. This mirrors Huineng's teaching that meditation without wisdom is mere quietism, and wisdom without meditation is mere intellectualism.
