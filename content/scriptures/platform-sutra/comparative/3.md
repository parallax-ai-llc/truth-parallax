The dialogue between teacher and student as a method of spiritual instruction appears across traditions. The Socratic dialogue, the Talmudic dispute (machloket), the Sufi sohbet (spiritual conversation), and the guru-shishya dialogue of Hindu tradition all employ questioning and answering as tools for deepening understanding and testing realization.

Huineng's method of redirecting external questions to internal inquiry parallels the Socratic method's emphasis on self-knowledge. Socrates' injunction to "know thyself" and his practice of turning every philosophical question into a question about the questioner's own assumptions share something of Huineng's approach, though the metaphysical frameworks differ significantly.

The chapter's presentation of the Pure Land as a state of mind rather than an external location parallels the mystical internalization of paradise found across traditions. The Sufi teaching that the true Ka'ba is the heart, the Christian mystic's understanding of the Kingdom of God as an interior reality, and the Kabbalistic concept of the inner Temple all relocate the sacred from external geography to internal experience.
