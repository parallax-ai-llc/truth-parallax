Huineng's teaching on prajna as the inherent luminosity of the mind parallels the concept of divine light or inner illumination found across traditions. The Quaker "inner light," the Sufi "light upon light" (nur ala nur), the Hindu concept of the atman as self-luminous awareness, and the Kabbalistic teaching on the primordial light (or ha-ganuz) all describe a radiance within the human person that is not created but only recognized.

The teaching that wisdom is innately present rather than acquired through study parallels the Platonic doctrine of anamnesis (recollection), where learning is understood as the soul's remembering of truths it already knows. Socrates' method of eliciting knowledge through questioning rather than instruction shares something of Huineng's conviction that the teacher does not transmit wisdom but awakens it in the student.

The chapter's emphasis on the universality of prajna raises the same questions about universal salvation and spiritual equality that have been debated across traditions. If every being possesses inherent wisdom, why do most remain in ignorance? The Christian debate between Calvinism (only the elect are saved) and universalism (all will be saved) addresses a structurally similar question about the relationship between human potential and actual realization.
