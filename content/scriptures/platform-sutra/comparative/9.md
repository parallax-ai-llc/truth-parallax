The relationship between spiritual authority and political power explored in this chapter is a universal theme across religious traditions. The Christian doctrine of the "two swords," temporal and spiritual, and its long history of church-state relations from Constantine through the medieval papacy to modern secularism, represents an extended engagement with the same question that Huineng faced: how should the spiritually realized person relate to worldly power?

The Islamic concept of the relationship between din (religion) and dawla (state) has been debated throughout Islamic history, from the caliphate's combination of religious and political authority to modern movements for the separation of mosque and state. The prophetic model of Muhammad as both spiritual teacher and political leader provides a template for integration that differs from Huineng's more detached relationship with power.

The Jewish experience of exile and diaspora created a different dynamic, where spiritual authority operated largely independent of political power for most of Jewish history. The rabbi's authority derived from learning and community recognition rather than from political appointment, creating a model closer to the Chan master's independence from institutional structures. The establishment of the State of Israel raised new questions about the relationship between religious and political authority.
