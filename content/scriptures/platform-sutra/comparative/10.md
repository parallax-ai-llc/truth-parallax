The death of a spiritual master and the transmission of their teaching to successors is a universal theme across traditions. The Christian account of Jesus's death, resurrection, and commission to his disciples; the Islamic tradition of Muhammad's final sermon and the question of succession that divided Sunni and Shia; and the Hindu tradition of the guru's mahasamadhi (conscious departure from the body) all address the question of how spiritual authority is transmitted across generations.

The emphasis on preserving authentic teaching against corruption resonates with concerns found across traditions. The Jewish concept of the oral Torah being faithfully transmitted through generations of rabbis, the Islamic science of hadith criticism developed to authenticate the Prophet's sayings, and the Christian concept of apostolic succession all represent attempts to maintain the integrity of the founding revelation across time.

The Platform Sutra's conclusion, with its emphasis on the diversity of future practitioners and the need for adaptable teaching, resonates with the universal challenge of maintaining a living tradition. Every religion must balance the preservation of its founding vision with the need to speak to new audiences in new circumstances. Huineng's final teaching, which provides principles of non-dual engagement rather than fixed doctrines, offers a model for this balance that transcends any single tradition.
