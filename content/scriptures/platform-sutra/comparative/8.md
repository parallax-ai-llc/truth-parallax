The sudden-gradual debate finds echoes across traditions in the question of whether spiritual transformation occurs instantaneously or progressively. In Christianity, the tension between the Protestant emphasis on justification by faith (sudden transformation through grace) and the Catholic emphasis on sanctification through works and sacraments (gradual growth in holiness) represents a structurally similar debate.

The Islamic tradition contains its own version of this tension. The Sufi concept of fana (annihilation in God) describes a sudden and overwhelming experience of divine union, while the systematic progress through the maqamat (stations) and ahwal (states) represents a gradual ascent. Most Sufi teachers maintain that both elements are present in the spiritual journey.

The Hindu tradition includes the teaching of sahaja (spontaneous or innate) realization, associated with Sahajiya and later Advaita Vedanta traditions, which affirms that the Self is already realized and needs only to be recognized. This teaching, with its emphasis on the naturalness and immediacy of awakening, shares structural features with Huineng's sudden enlightenment while operating within a completely different metaphysical framework.
