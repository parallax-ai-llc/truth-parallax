Huineng's teaching on prajna draws directly from the Prajnaparamita literature, particularly the Diamond Sutra, which he identifies as the text through which he attained awakening. His exposition synthesizes the Prajnaparamita emphasis on emptiness with the tathagatagarbha teaching on buddha-nature, creating a distinctively Chinese Mahayana teaching on the inherent wisdom of the mind.

The chapter's presentation of prajna as the intrinsic luminosity of the mind rather than as a faculty developed through practice reflects the tathagatagarbha understanding that awakened qualities are inherent, not acquired. This synthesis became the dominant understanding in East Asian Mahayana Buddhism, influencing not only Chan/Zen but also Huayan, Tiantai, and Pure Land traditions.

The teaching that prajna manifests as a single, unified wisdom rather than as the progressive stages described in Indian Buddhist scholasticism represents a characteristically Chinese simplification of Mahayana soteriology. This streamlined approach emphasizes direct realization over systematic cultivation, making the Mahayana path accessible to practitioners regardless of their philosophical training.
