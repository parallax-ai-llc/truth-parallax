The chapter on temperaments and circumstances demonstrates Huineng's embodiment of the Mahayana ideal of upaya-kausalya (skillful means). The ability to adapt the teaching to each student's needs is understood in Mahayana Buddhism as an expression of compassion inseparable from wisdom. The teacher who offers the same teaching to all students lacks the prajna that perceives the unique conditions of each mind.

Huineng's approach to teaching different temperaments reflects the Mahayana understanding of the tathagata's four modes of speech: the teaching that is pleasing and true, the teaching that is displeasing but true, the teaching that is both pleasing and true, and silence. The skilled teacher moves fluidly among these modes, always choosing the approach that will most effectively open the student's mind.

The chapter's practical guidelines for teaching connect with the broader Mahayana concern for the preservation and transmission of the dharma. The bodhisattva's responsibility extends not only to achieving their own realization but to ensuring that the teaching is communicated effectively to beings of different capacities across all future generations.
