The formless repentance ceremony described by Huineng represents a creative integration of Mahayana doctrine with Chinese Buddhist liturgical practice. The ceremony includes taking refuge in the three bodies of the Buddha (trikaya), taking the four great bodhisattva vows, and receiving the formless precepts. Each element is reinterpreted in light of Huineng's teaching on the mind's inherent purity.

The bodhisattva vows, "Sentient beings are numberless, I vow to save them all," are reformulated to point inward: the numberless sentient beings are the deluded thoughts of one's own mind, and saving them means transforming them through wisdom. This internalization of the Mahayana vow does not diminish its universal scope but reveals its foundation in the practitioner's own realization.

The formless precepts bypass the detailed behavioral codes of the Vinaya in favor of a single principle: the mind that is free from delusion naturally acts in accord with all precepts. This reflects the Mahayana teaching on the bodhisattva's natural morality, which arises spontaneously from the realization of emptiness rather than from adherence to external rules.
