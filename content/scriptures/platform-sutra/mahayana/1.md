Huineng's autobiography is the foundational narrative of Chan Buddhism and a powerful expression of Mahayana principles. His awakening upon hearing the Diamond Sutra's teaching to "develop a mind that does not abide in anything" demonstrates the Prajnaparamita teaching that wisdom arises when the mind is free from all attachment, including attachment to spiritual practice itself.

The poetry contest between Huineng and Shenxiu encapsulates two approaches to the Mahayana path. Shenxiu's verse, comparing the mind to a mirror that must be constantly polished, represents the conventional bodhisattva path of gradual purification. Huineng's verse, declaring that there is originally no mirror and therefore no place for dust to alight, expresses the Prajnaparamita insight that the mind's nature is primordially empty and pure.

Huineng's status as an illiterate layperson who surpasses learned monks embodies the Mahayana teaching that buddha-nature is universal and that awakening is not dependent on erudition, social status, or monastic ordination. This theme connects the Platform Sutra to the Vimalakirti Sutra's celebration of lay spiritual mastery.
