Huineng's final instructions encapsulate the essential Mahayana teaching that has been developed throughout the sutra. He transmits the method of "three categories and thirty-six pairs of opposites," a systematic approach to non-dual teaching that enables students to deconstruct any conceptual framework they encounter.

The death scene presents Huineng as a fully realized bodhisattva whose passing, like the Buddha's parinirvana, is a final teaching. His calm composure, his clear final instructions, and the auspicious signs accompanying his death demonstrate the Mahayana understanding that a realized being's death is not an ending but a transformation, a continuation of the bodhisattva's inexhaustible activity in new forms.

The transmission of the dharma to succeeding generations reflects the Mahayana concern with the perpetuation of the teaching across the vast time scales of Buddhist cosmology. Huineng's entrustment, like the Buddha's, ensures that the lineage of awakening continues beyond any individual teacher's lifetime, maintaining the possibility of liberation for all future beings.
