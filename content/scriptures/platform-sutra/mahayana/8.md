The sudden-gradual debate is the defining controversy of Chinese Mahayana Buddhism, and this chapter presents Huineng's definitive statement on the matter. He argues that the dharma itself is neither sudden nor gradual but that people's capacities differ, leading to different experiences of realization. This position avoids the extreme of dismissing gradual practice while affirming the possibility of instantaneous breakthrough.

The chapter's account of the split between the Northern and Southern Schools of Chan reflects a broader pattern within Mahayana Buddhism, where different communities emphasize different aspects of the path. The tension between sudden and gradual approaches appears in various forms throughout Mahayana history: between Madhyamaka and Yogacara, between self-power and other-power, between meditation and study.

Huineng's resolution of the debate, grounding both approaches in the universal presence of buddha-nature, provided the foundation for later syntheses. The Linji (Rinzai) and Caodong (Soto) schools of Zen, while emphasizing different practice methods, both accept Huineng's teaching that awakening is the recognition of what has always been present rather than the creation of something new.
