The teaching that meditation and wisdom are one and inseparable represents Huineng's most significant contribution to Mahayana Buddhist thought. He uses the analogy of a lamp and its light: meditation is the lamp, wisdom is the light. They cannot be separated because the lamp is the very substance of the light, and the light is the function of the lamp.

This teaching resolves a tension within Mahayana soteriology between the emphasis on meditation (dhyana) and the emphasis on wisdom (prajna). Indian Mahayana texts sometimes present these as the fifth and sixth of the six paramitas, suggesting a sequential relationship. Huineng's teaching eliminates this sequential understanding, insisting that genuine meditation is already wisdom and genuine wisdom is already meditative.

The implications for the bodhisattva path are profound. If meditation and wisdom are inseparable, then the practice of the six paramitas is not a progressive sequence but a single, integrated activity. Each paramita contains all the others. This understanding influenced the Huayan teaching on the mutual interpenetration of all practices and all stages of the path.
