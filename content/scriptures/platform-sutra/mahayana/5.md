Huineng's redefinition of sitting meditation challenges the formal meditation practices that had become central to Chinese Buddhist monastic life. He teaches that sitting meditation is not about immobilizing the body on a cushion but about the mind that remains unmoved by the arising and passing of phenomena. True sitting is the stability of awareness; true meditation is the openness of perception.

This teaching reflects the Mahayana understanding that the bodhisattva's samadhi pervades all activities, not only formal meditation sessions. The Vimalakirti Sutra's teaching that samadhi can be practiced while walking, talking, and engaging in daily affairs directly informs Huineng's position. The bodhisattva who restricts samadhi to a cushion has not yet understood its true nature.

The chapter's critique of attachment to meditation posture and technique anticipates later Mahayana developments, including the tantric tradition's emphasis on meditation in action and the Japanese Zen tradition's integration of meditation into arts, crafts, and physical practices. Huineng's teaching liberates meditation from the monastery and places it in the midst of life.
