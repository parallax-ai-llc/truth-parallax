The chapter on royal patronage reveals the practical dynamics of Buddhism's relationship with political power in Tang dynasty China. From a Mahayana perspective, the bodhisattva's engagement with rulers and governments is an expression of skillful means: by influencing those in positions of power, the bodhisattva can create conditions favorable to the dharma on a vast scale.

The Mahayana tradition includes the concept of the cakravartin (wheel-turning monarch), the ideal ruler who governs in accordance with the dharma. Huineng's interactions with the imperial court reflect the broader Mahayana vision of a society transformed by Buddhist principles, where political power serves the purposes of awakening rather than mere worldly ambition.

The chapter also reveals the tensions inherent in the relationship between spiritual authority and political power. Huineng's initial reluctance to accept imperial invitation, followed by his eventual engagement, models the Mahayana ideal of detachment from worldly honors while remaining available for the benefit of beings. The bodhisattva neither seeks nor refuses contact with power.
