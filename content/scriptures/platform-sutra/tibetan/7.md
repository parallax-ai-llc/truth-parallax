The chapter on adapting teaching to different temperaments connects with the Tibetan Buddhist system of classifying practitioners by capacity. The Tibetan tradition distinguishes practitioners of highest, medium, and lowest capacity, each requiring different approaches and teachings. This framework, elaborated in the lam rim (stages of the path) literature, provides a systematic structure for what Huineng presents more intuitively.

Tibetan Buddhism has developed the concept of different vehicles (theg pa) suited to different capacities: the Shravakayana for those seeking individual liberation, the Mahayana for those aspiring to universal awakening, and the Vajrayana for those of the sharpest faculties. This classification system provides a more elaborate framework for pedagogical adaptation than the Platform Sutra offers.

The Tibetan master's ability to assess a student's capacity and provide appropriate instruction (gdams ngag) is considered one of the essential qualities of a qualified teacher. The Tibetan tradition emphasizes the importance of examining both teacher and student before entering a dharma relationship, ensuring that the teaching offered matches the student's readiness. This careful matching process contrasts with Huineng's more spontaneous approach.
