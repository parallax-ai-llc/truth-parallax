## Overview

The Platform Sutra is not part of the Tibetan Buddhist canon, but its teachings are deeply relevant to Tibetan Buddhism through the famous Council of Samye (circa 792–794 CE), where the Chinese Chan master Moheyan debated the Indian scholar Kamalashila on precisely the sudden-versus-gradual question that defines the Platform Sutra.

## Doctrinal Significance

Tibetan tradition holds that Kamalashila's gradualist position prevailed at Samye, establishing the Indian analytical approach as normative for Tibetan Buddhism. Yet scholars have noted that elements of the sudden approach persisted in Dzogchen and Mahamudra traditions, suggesting that the debate was never fully resolved.

## Points of Connection

Huineng's teaching on seeing one's original nature parallels the Dzogchen concept of rigpa (pure awareness) and the Mahamudra teaching on recognizing the nature of mind. Both traditions affirm that the awakened state is already present and needs only to be recognized, not constructed through effort.
