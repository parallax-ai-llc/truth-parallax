Huineng's autobiography, while not part of the Tibetan Buddhist canon, is deeply relevant to Tibetan Buddhism through its central theme of sudden awakening and the recognition of the mind's inherent purity. The Dzogchen tradition's teaching on trekcho (cutting through) and the direct introduction to the nature of mind (rigpa) shares structural similarities with Huineng's instantaneous realization upon hearing the Diamond Sutra.

The Tibetan tradition maintains that Huineng's approach represents a valid but incomplete path when compared with the comprehensive system of Vajrayana Buddhism. The Council of Samye, where the gradualist Indian position was judged to have prevailed over the Chinese suddenist position, established the normative framework for Tibetan Buddhism. Yet scholars have noted that the Tibetan reception of Chan was more nuanced than the simple rejection suggested by the official account.

The poetry contest between Shenxiu and Huineng mirrors debates within Tibetan Buddhism between those who emphasize gradual purification through the stages of the path (lam rim) and those who point directly to the nature of mind (sems nyid ngal gso). The Nyingma and Kagyu schools have maintained teachings that bear remarkable similarity to Huineng's sudden approach.
