The question-and-answer format in this chapter parallels the Tibetan tradition of debate (rtsod pa), where monks test each other's understanding through rigorous logical exchange. However, Huineng's style of responding to questions differs markedly from the syllogistic reasoning of Tibetan debate. He employs paradox, redirection, and direct pointing rather than logical argument.

The chapter's dialogues can be compared with the Tibetan genre of "questions and answers" (dris lan), in which a student poses questions to a master who responds with authoritative instruction. The Tibetan tradition preserves numerous such texts, from the dialogues attributed to Atisha to the collected responses of figures such as Je Tsongkhapa and the Dalai Lamas.

Tibetan readers would note that Huineng's responses consistently redirect attention from external objects to the nature of mind. This method, while characteristic of Chan, is also found in the Tibetan Mahamudra and Dzogchen traditions, where the teacher points out the nature of mind directly rather than through graduated analytical meditation. The pith instructions (man ngag) of these traditions share Huineng's emphasis on directness and simplicity.
