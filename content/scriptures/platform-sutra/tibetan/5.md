Huineng's redefinition of sitting meditation can be compared with the Dzogchen teaching on meditation in the natural state, where the practitioner rests in the recognition of rigpa without employing any particular technique or maintaining any specific posture. The Dzogchen master Patrul Rinpoche taught that the key point of meditation is not the body's position but the mind's recognition of its own nature.

The Tibetan tradition generally maintains a more structured approach to meditation practice than Huineng advocates. The detailed instructions on posture, gaze, and breath found in texts such as Kamalashila's Bhavanakrama (Stages of Meditation) reflect the Indian analytical approach that was adopted as normative in Tibet after the Council of Samye.

Yet the highest teachings of Dzogchen and Mahamudra share Huineng's conviction that formal meditation technique is ultimately a support, not the essence. The Dzogchen teaching on the four chokzhak (ways of leaving things as they are) describes a practice that transcends technique entirely, resting in the natural state regardless of posture or activity. This convergence between the Tibetan and Chan traditions at their highest levels has been noted by numerous scholars and practitioners.
