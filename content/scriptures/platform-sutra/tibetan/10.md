Huineng's final instructions and death can be compared with the Tibetan tradition of the last testaments (zhal chems) of great masters. Tibetan Buddhism places great importance on the manner of a master's death, viewing it as the final and most powerful teaching. The phenomenon of thukdam, where a realized master's body remains in meditation posture after clinical death without decomposing, is understood as a sign of advanced realization.

The transmission of the teaching through the lineage is central to both traditions. The Tibetan concept of the unbroken lineage (brgyud pa ma chad pa) ensures that the blessings and realizations of the original master are transmitted authentically through each generation. Similarly, Huineng's transmission to his successors ensures the continuation of the Chan lineage.

The Platform Sutra's concluding emphasis on the diversity of students and the need for future teachers to adapt the dharma resonates with the Tibetan tradition's recognition that the teaching manifests in different forms across time and culture. The terma (hidden treasure) tradition, where teachings are concealed for future revelation, represents a distinctively Tibetan approach to ensuring that the dharma remains available and relevant across generations.
