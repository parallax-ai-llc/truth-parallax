Huineng's teaching on the inseparability of meditation and wisdom connects with the Tibetan understanding of the union of shamatha and vipashyana (zhi gnas and lhag mthong). The Tibetan tradition teaches that genuine realization requires the union of stable calm and penetrating insight, neither of which is complete without the other.

The specific way Huineng formulates this unity differs from the Tibetan presentation. Tibetan teachers typically describe shamatha and vipashyana as initially developed separately and then united, whereas Huineng insists they are one from the beginning. This difference reflects the broader distinction between the gradual and sudden approaches.

The Mahamudra tradition, particularly as taught by Gampopa and his successors in the Kagyu school, comes closest to Huineng's position. Gampopa taught that in Mahamudra meditation, the stability of shamatha and the insight of vipashyana are naturally unified in the recognition of the mind's nature. This "one-taste" experience, where all meditation qualities are experienced as a single awareness, parallels Huineng's lamp-and-light analogy.
