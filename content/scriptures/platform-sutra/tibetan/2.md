Huineng's teaching on prajna resonates with the Tibetan Buddhist understanding of sherab (shes rab), the Tibetan translation of prajna. In the Tibetan tradition, prajna is cultivated through the three wisdoms: hearing (thos pa'i shes rab), contemplating (bsam pa'i shes rab), and meditating (sgom pa'i shes rab). This threefold approach represents the Tibetan systematization of the path to wisdom.

Huineng's assertion that prajna is inherent rather than acquired connects with the Dzogchen teaching that rigpa is not produced by meditation but merely recognized. The Tibetan master Longchenpa taught that the natural state of awareness is pristine from the beginning and needs only to be pointed out by a qualified master. This parallels Huineng's teaching that the original mind is complete in itself.

However, mainstream Tibetan Gelug scholasticism, following Tsongkhapa, would caution against conflating the inherence of buddha-nature with the realized state of buddhahood. The potential for awakening is universal, but its actualization requires the systematic development of method and wisdom through the stages of the bodhisattva path. This represents the Tibetan critique of what is perceived as the Chan shortcut.
