The relationship between Buddhism and political authority described in this chapter has deep parallels in Tibetan Buddhist history. The patron-priest (yon mchod) relationship between Tibetan lamas and Mongol and Chinese rulers established a model where spiritual authority and political power existed in a mutually beneficial relationship, each legitimizing the other.

The Dalai Lama institution represents the most developed form of Buddhist political authority, combining spiritual and temporal leadership in a single figure. The institution's evolution from the recognition of the Third Dalai Lama by the Mongol ruler Altan Khan to the current Fourteenth Dalai Lama's role as a global spiritual leader demonstrates how the Buddhism-politics relationship has transformed over centuries.

Huineng's ambivalent relationship with the Tang court, accepting imperial honor while maintaining spiritual independence, models an ideal that Tibetan Buddhist leaders have aspired to throughout history. The tension between engaging with political power for the benefit of the dharma and the risk of compromising the teaching through political entanglement remains a live issue in the Tibetan Buddhist world today.
