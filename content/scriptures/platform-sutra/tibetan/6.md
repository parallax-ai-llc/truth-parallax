The formless repentance ceremony in this chapter can be compared with the Tibetan practice of Vajrasattva purification, which is among the most important preliminary practices (sngon 'gro) in Tibetan Buddhism. Both approaches aim at purifying the practitioner's karma and obscurations, but they employ different methods.

The Vajrasattva practice involves visualization, mantra recitation, and the four powers of purification: regret, reliance, antidote, and resolve. This structured approach contrasts with Huineng's formless repentance, which bypasses specific techniques in favor of direct recognition of the mind's original purity. Tibetan practitioners might view Huineng's approach as appropriate for those of the sharpest faculties but insufficient for ordinary practitioners.

The Tibetan tradition also includes the practice of confession before the thirty-five confession Buddhas, which involves prostrations and the recitation of the names of each Buddha. This ritualized approach to purification represents the opposite end of the spectrum from Huineng's formless approach, yet both aim at the same result: the removal of obstacles to the recognition of the mind's awakened nature.
