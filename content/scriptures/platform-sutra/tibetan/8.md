The sudden-gradual debate addressed in this chapter had direct consequences for Tibetan Buddhism through the famous Council of Samye. The Chinese Chan master Moheyan (Mahayana), representing the sudden approach, debated the Indian scholar Kamalashila, representing the gradual approach. The Tibetan king Trisong Detsen is said to have judged in favor of Kamalashila, establishing the gradual Indian approach as normative.

However, recent scholarship has complicated this narrative. Tibetan Dunhuang documents show that Chan continued to influence Tibetan Buddhism long after the alleged debate. The Dzogchen tradition, which emerged within Tibetan Buddhism, bears structural similarities to Chan's sudden approach, leading some scholars to hypothesize historical connections between the two traditions.

The Nyingma master Nubchen Sangye Yeshe's Lamp for the Eye in Meditation (bSam gtan mig sgron) classified four approaches to meditation: gradual, simultaneous (Chinese), Mahayoga, and Atiyoga (Dzogchen). This text reveals that the Tibetan tradition did not simply reject the sudden approach but integrated elements of it into its highest teachings while maintaining the gradualist framework as the standard path for most practitioners.
