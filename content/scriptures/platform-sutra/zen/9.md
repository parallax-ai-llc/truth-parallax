The relationship between Zen and political power is a complex and often uncomfortable topic. Huineng's interactions with the Tang imperial court established a pattern that continued throughout Zen history: the master who maintains inner independence while engaging with worldly authority. The ideal is the person of no rank who is not defined by any social position, even when occupying one.

Japanese Zen's historical entanglement with militarism and nationalism demonstrates the dangers of this relationship. When the "person of no rank" becomes the emperor's chaplain, the teaching can be co-opted for purposes that have nothing to do with liberation. Brian Victoria's work on Zen's role in Japanese wartime ideology has forced the tradition to confront this shadow.

Huineng's own approach models a different possibility: he initially refused the imperial invitation, demonstrating his independence, then accepted when it became clear that his presence could benefit beings. The Zen ideal is neither withdrawal from political reality nor immersion in it, but a freedom that allows engagement without entanglement. This remains one of Zen's greatest ongoing challenges.
