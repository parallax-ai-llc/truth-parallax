Huineng's radical teaching on sitting meditation has been a source of debate within Zen for over a millennium. He declares that attachment to sitting in a specific posture and cultivating a blank mind is not true meditation. True sitting is the mind that does not move when confronted with all the phenomena of the universe. True meditation is the awareness that perceives without grasping.

Dogen, the founder of Soto Zen in Japan, grappled with this teaching extensively. His resolution was the concept of shikantaza (just sitting), where the physical act of sitting becomes the complete expression of buddhahood rather than a technique for achieving it. In this reading, Huineng's critique targets instrumental sitting, sitting in order to get enlightened, not the practice of sitting itself.

Every Zen practitioner must find their own resolution of this teaching. Some traditions emphasize intensive sitting retreats (sesshin) as essential to Zen practice. Others insist that any activity performed with full attention is equally valid as meditation. Huineng's teaching keeps the question alive: what is true meditation? If you think you know the answer, you have already missed it.
