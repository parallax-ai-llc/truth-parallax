The questions posed to Huineng in this chapter represent the doubts that every Zen practitioner must face and resolve. Can an ordinary person achieve what the patriarchs achieved? Is meditation sufficient, or is intellectual understanding also necessary? How does one practice in the midst of daily life? Each question, honestly asked, becomes a gateway to deeper inquiry.

Huineng's responses demonstrate the Zen method of turning every question back on the questioner. When asked about the pure land, he does not describe a distant paradise but points to the mind of the person asking. When asked about the Buddha, he does not discuss historical or cosmological Buddhas but directs attention to the questioner's own nature. The teacher is a mirror that reflects the student's own face.

This dialogical method became the foundation of the entire Zen tradition of encounter dialogue (jiyuan wenda). The koan collections, the recorded sayings of the masters, and the practice of dokusan (private interview with the teacher) all derive from the approach modeled in this chapter. The question is not answered; it is dissolved through the direct recognition of the questioner's own nature.
