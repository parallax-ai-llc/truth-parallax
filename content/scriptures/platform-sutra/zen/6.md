The formless repentance of Huineng cuts through the accumulated weight of guilt and regret that every practitioner carries. The Zen approach to repentance is not cataloguing past sins and feeling sorry for them but seeing directly into the nature of the mind that committed them. When you see that the mind is originally empty, where is the sin? Where is the sinner?

This is not a license for immorality. The Zen precepts, derived from the formless precepts of the Platform Sutra, are taken seriously as expressions of awakened conduct. But the precepts are understood from the inside out: the awakened person naturally refrains from harming because they see the interdependence of all beings, not because an external authority prohibits harmful action.

The repentance ceremony described here includes taking the bodhisattva vows, which in Zen are understood as the most fundamental expression of practice-realization. "Sentient beings are numberless; I vow to save them all." This vow is impossible, and the Zen practitioner takes it knowing it is impossible. The impossibility is the point: the vow transcends the calculating mind and opens into the boundless space of compassion.
