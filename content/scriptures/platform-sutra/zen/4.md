Meditation and wisdom are one, not two. This is the sentence that defines Zen practice. Huineng uses the analogy of a lamp and its light: you cannot separate the lamp from its light, or the light from the lamp. Similarly, you cannot practice meditation without wisdom or cultivate wisdom without meditation. They arise together, function together, and are extinguished together.

This teaching demolishes the common misunderstanding that meditation is a technique for producing states of calm, and wisdom is a separate intellectual understanding of Buddhist doctrine. In Zen, sitting on the cushion with a quiet mind is not yet meditation if it lacks the awakened quality of prajna. Conversely, understanding Buddhist philosophy brilliantly is not yet wisdom if it has not been realized in the still depths of samadhi.

The practical implication for Zen practice is immediate: when you sit in zazen, do not seek tranquility as a goal separate from insight. When insight arises, do not grasp it as a trophy separate from the ongoing flow of meditative awareness. Let meditation and wisdom be as inseparable as the wave and the water that forms it. This is the heart of shikantaza: just sitting, which is just being awake.
