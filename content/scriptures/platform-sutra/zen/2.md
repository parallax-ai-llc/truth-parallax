Huineng's teaching on prajna strikes at the root of Zen practice. Prajna is not something you acquire through meditation, study, or accumulation of merit. It is the inherent luminosity of your own mind, present right now, whether you are sitting in the meditation hall or chopping vegetables in the kitchen. The entire Zen path is nothing more than the recognition of this ever-present wisdom.

The chapter declares: "Good friends, the wisdom of bodhi and prajna is something that worldly people originally possess in themselves. It is only because the mind is deluded that they cannot attain awakening by themselves." This single statement contains the entire teaching of Zen. There is nothing to gain, nothing to achieve. There is only the delusion that obscures what is already complete.

For the Zen practitioner, this teaching is both the greatest encouragement and the most profound challenge. If prajna is already present, why sit in zazen? Why study koans? Why seek a teacher? The answer is paradoxical: practice does not create realization but allows the realization that was always there to become manifest. The candle does not create the flame; it only provides the conditions for the light that was always possible.
