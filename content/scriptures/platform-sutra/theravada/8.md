The sudden-versus-gradual debate that this chapter addresses has its own resonances within the Theravada tradition, though the terms of the debate differ. The Pali canon contains accounts of both sudden and gradual attainment. Some practitioners, such as Bahiya of the Bark-cloth, achieved arahatship almost instantly upon hearing a brief teaching, while others required years of sustained practice.

The Theravada Abhidhamma analyzes the moment of path-consciousness (magga-citta) as technically instantaneous, arising for a single mind-moment and permanently eradicating the defilements appropriate to that stage of awakening. In this sense, the actual moment of breakthrough is always sudden, even if the preparation leading to it may be gradual.

Huineng's position that the dharma itself is neither sudden nor gradual, but that people's faculties are sharp or dull, offers a resolution that some Theravada practitioners might find congenial. The Theravada tradition similarly recognizes that individual differences in spiritual capacity affect the speed of progress, without making sudden or gradual attainment into opposing doctrinal positions.
