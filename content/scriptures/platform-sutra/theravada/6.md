The formless repentance ceremony described in this chapter presents a distinctive approach to confession and purification that differs markedly from the Theravada Vinaya's detailed system of confession and restoration. In the Theravada monastic code, specific offenses require specific forms of confession, with different procedures for different categories of transgression.

Huineng's formless repentance bypasses the enumeration of specific sins in favor of direct insight into the nature of the mind that committed them. If the mind is inherently pure and defilements are adventitious, then the most profound repentance is the recognition of original purity rather than the cataloguing and confessing of individual transgressions.

The Theravada tradition would question whether this approach adequately addresses the karmic consequences of unwholesome actions. The Pali canon teaches that karma operates according to its own laws, and that the effects of past actions must be experienced even by those who have attained realization. The story of Angulimala, who suffered physical attacks even after becoming an arahat, illustrates this principle.
