Huineng's redefinition of sitting meditation challenges assumptions that are common across Buddhist traditions, including Theravada. He teaches that true sitting meditation is not a matter of physical posture but of the mind that does not arise and does not move. This radical redefinition questions whether the formal practice of sitting meditation, as understood in most Buddhist traditions, captures the essence of what meditation truly is.

The Theravada tradition places great importance on formal sitting meditation, with detailed instructions on posture, breathing, and the progressive development of concentration. The Satipatthana Sutta prescribes specific practices for each of the four foundations of mindfulness, and the Anapanasati Sutta provides systematic instruction in mindfulness of breathing.

Yet the Theravada tradition also recognizes that mindfulness should pervade all activities, not only sitting. The practice of sati-sampajanna (mindfulness with clear comprehension) extends to walking, eating, speaking, and all other daily activities. In this broader sense, the Theravada understanding of practice shares common ground with Huineng's teaching that meditation is not limited to a specific physical posture.
