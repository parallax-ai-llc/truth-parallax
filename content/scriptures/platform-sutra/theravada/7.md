This chapter on temperaments and circumstances addresses the question of how a teacher should adapt the dharma to different students, a concern shared with the Theravada tradition. The Buddha is described in the Pali canon as a supreme teacher who possessed puggala-paropariyatti-nana, the knowledge of the temperaments and spiritual capacities of individuals, enabling him to offer exactly the right teaching at the right time.

Huineng's approach to adaptation differs from the systematic Theravada method. The Theravada commentarial tradition identifies specific personality types (carita) and prescribes particular meditation subjects for each: loving-kindness for those prone to hatred, contemplation of impurity for those prone to lust, mindfulness of breathing for those prone to discursive thinking. Huineng's approach is more spontaneous and intuitive.

Both traditions recognize that a single teaching method cannot serve all practitioners. The Theravada concept of the four modes of teaching (undha, nidha, ana, panihata) describes how the Buddha adapted his instruction to the needs of each individual. This shared concern with pedagogical flexibility reflects a practical wisdom common to both traditions.
