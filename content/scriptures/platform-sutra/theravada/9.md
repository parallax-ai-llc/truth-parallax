The chapter on royal patronage addresses the relationship between Buddhist institutions and political power, a theme with extensive parallels in Theravada Buddhist history. The great Theravada emperor Ashoka established the paradigmatic model of the Buddhist king who supports the sangha, builds monasteries and stupas, and promotes the dharma throughout his realm.

In Theravada countries such as Sri Lanka, Thailand, Myanmar, and Cambodia, the relationship between the sangha and the state has been a defining feature of Buddhist history. The king serves as the protector of the sasana (Buddhist dispensation), while the sangha provides spiritual legitimacy to the ruler. This mutual dependence mirrors the dynamics described in the Platform Sutra's account of Huineng's relationship with the Tang court.

The chapter raises questions about the effects of political patronage on the integrity of Buddhist teaching, a concern that has been debated throughout Theravada history. The reform movements that have periodically emerged in Theravada countries have often been motivated by the perception that close ties to political power had compromised the purity of monastic practice.
