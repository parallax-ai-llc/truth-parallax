Huineng's central teaching that meditation and wisdom are inseparable represents a distinctive position that can be compared with the Theravada understanding of the relationship between samatha (calm) and vipassana (insight). The Theravada tradition generally presents these as complementary but distinguishable practices, with various texts debating whether they should be developed sequentially or simultaneously.

The Visuddhimagga, Buddhaghosa's comprehensive meditation manual, presents samatha and vipassana as two wings of a bird, both necessary for the flight of liberation. Some Theravada meditation traditions emphasize samatha first, developing deep concentration before turning to insight, while others emphasize vipassana from the beginning. Huineng's teaching that the two are ultimately one challenges both approaches.

The practical implications of Huineng's position are significant. If meditation and wisdom are truly inseparable, then every moment of genuine meditation is simultaneously a moment of wisdom, and every moment of genuine wisdom is a meditative state. This view resonates with the Theravada concept of sampajanna (clear comprehension), which integrates mindfulness and wisdom in daily activity.
