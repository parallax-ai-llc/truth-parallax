The autobiography of the Sixth Patriarch Huineng presents a narrative of spiritual awakening that both resonates with and departs from Theravada accounts of realization. Huineng's sudden awakening upon hearing a single line of the Diamond Sutra can be compared with accounts in the Pali canon where monks attain arahatship upon hearing a brief teaching. The Theravada Dhammapada commentary contains numerous stories of instantaneous awakening triggered by a single verse.

However, the specific content of Huineng's realization differs from the Theravada model. His insight into the mind's original purity and the absence of any place where dust can alight goes beyond the Theravada emphasis on the elimination of defilements through progressive practice. In the Pali framework, defilements are real mental factors that must be abandoned through sustained effort, not recognized as illusory.

The dramatic narrative of Huineng's appointment as Sixth Patriarch, including the poetry contest with Shenxiu and the midnight transmission of the robe and bowl, follows a pattern of contested succession that has some parallels in Theravada Buddhist history, where different communities have claimed authentic lineage from the Buddha.
