Huineng's teaching on prajna (wisdom) draws directly from the Prajnaparamita tradition but presents it in a characteristically Chinese manner. The Theravada tradition has its own well-developed understanding of panna (the Pali equivalent of prajna) as the third component of the threefold training: sila (morality), samadhi (concentration), and panna (wisdom).

In the Theravada framework, prajna or panna is cultivated through vipassana meditation, specifically through the systematic observation of the three characteristics: impermanence (anicca), suffering (dukkha), and non-self (anatta). Huineng's presentation differs by emphasizing prajna as the mind's inherent luminosity rather than as an insight developed through analytical meditation.

The chapter's teaching that prajna is present in all beings at all times, regardless of their spiritual development, challenges the Theravada understanding that wisdom deepens gradually through the stages of insight knowledge (nana). The Theravada path describes a clear progression from preliminary insight through sixteen stages of knowledge, each building on the previous one.
