The final chapter has been analyzed by text-critical scholars as containing multiple layers of composition. The Dunhuang manuscript version of the Platform Sutra ends more abruptly than later editions, which expanded the final instructions with additional teachings and biographical details. These expansions reflect the later Chan tradition's desire to provide a more complete account of Huineng's last days and to address doctrinal questions that had emerged after the text's initial composition.

The death narrative follows established patterns of Buddhist parinirvana accounts while incorporating specifically Chinese elements. Koichi Shinohara's research on Chinese Buddhist hagiography has shown how death scenes in Chinese Buddhist biographies were constructed according to culturally specific conventions that differed from Indian Buddhist models while serving similar functions of legitimation and inspiration.

The transmission of the dharma to succeeding generations, as described in this chapter, has been studied by scholars as the foundation of the dharma transmission system that became central to Chan institutional life. T. Griffith Foulk's research has shown how the Platform Sutra's model of transmission was elaborated into a complex system of dharma lineages, lamp records (denglu), and institutional governance structures that shaped Chan Buddhism for a millennium.
