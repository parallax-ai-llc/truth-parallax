The repentance ceremony described in this chapter has been studied by scholars of Chinese Buddhist ritual as an important example of the sinification of Indian Buddhist liturgical forms. Daniel Stevenson's research on Chinese Buddhist repentance rituals has shown how Indian pratimoksha and parivasana (confession and probation) procedures were transformed into elaborate liturgical ceremonies in China.

Huineng's formless repentance represents a radical simplification of these ceremonial forms, replacing detailed ritual with direct insight. Scholars have debated whether this represents a genuine innovation or a rhetorical stance that was never intended to replace actual liturgical practice. Evidence from later Chan monasteries suggests that formal repentance ceremonies continued to be observed alongside the Platform Sutra's teaching on formless repentance.

The chapter's integration of the bodhisattva precepts and the four great vows into the repentance ceremony reflects the Chinese Buddhist practice of combining multiple liturgical elements into comprehensive ceremonies. Paul Groner's research on Chinese precept traditions has shown how the Platform Sutra's formless precepts influenced later developments in both Chan and Tiantai approaches to Buddhist ethics and ordination.
