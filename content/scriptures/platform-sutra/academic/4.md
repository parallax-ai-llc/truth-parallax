The teaching on the inseparability of meditation and wisdom has been analyzed by scholars as representing a distinctively Chinese resolution of a tension present in Indian Buddhist soteriology. Robert Sharf has argued that the emphasis on the unity of practice and realization in Chan Buddhism reflects Chinese cultural values of integration and holism rather than the Indian Buddhist tendency toward systematic analysis and classification.

Academic research has traced the historical background of the meditation-wisdom debate in Chinese Buddhism. Prior to the Platform Sutra, Chinese Buddhist teachers debated whether meditation (ding) or wisdom (hui) took priority, reflecting different interpretations of Indian Buddhist texts. Huineng's resolution, declaring them inseparable, effectively transcended the debate while establishing a new paradigm for Chan practice theory.

Scholars have also examined how this teaching functioned institutionally. By declaring meditation and wisdom inseparable, the Platform Sutra implicitly challenged the specialized meditation lineages that had developed in Chinese Buddhism, asserting the superiority of the Chan approach that integrated practice and understanding. The teaching thus served both philosophical and institutional purposes within the competitive landscape of Tang dynasty Chinese Buddhism.
