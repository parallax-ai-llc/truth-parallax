The autobiography of Huineng has been the subject of extensive academic analysis since the discovery of the Dunhuang manuscript (Pelliot chinois 2007) in the early twentieth century. Philip Yampolsky's critical edition and translation (1967) established that the earliest version of the text differs significantly from later expanded editions, raising fundamental questions about the historicity of the narrative.

Scholars including John McRae have argued that the autobiography is better understood as hagiography than as history. The elements of the narrative, the illiterate genius, the secret transmission, the flight from persecution, follow established patterns of Buddhist sacred biography rather than reflecting actual historical events. McRae's principle that the "history of early Chan is the history of people telling the story of early Chan" applies with particular force to this chapter.

The poetry contest between Shenxiu and Huineng, which may have been a later addition to the text, has been analyzed by scholars as a rhetorical device for establishing the Southern School's doctrinal superiority. The verses attributed to the two competitors are too neatly opposed to represent a spontaneous composition contest, suggesting careful literary construction to serve sectarian purposes.
