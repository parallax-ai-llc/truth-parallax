The dialogue format of this chapter reflects the emergence of the yulu (recorded sayings) genre that became characteristic of Chan literature. Albert Welter's research has traced the development of this genre from early collections of short exchanges to the elaborate encounter dialogues (jiyuan wenda) of the Song dynasty. The Platform Sutra represents an early stage in this literary evolution.

Scholars have noted that the questions posed to Huineng reflect concerns specific to Chinese Buddhist culture: the relationship between meditation and daily life, the accessibility of Pure Land practice, and the role of the teacher in facilitating awakening. These questions reveal the social context of Tang dynasty Buddhism more than they illuminate Huineng's historical teaching.

The academic study of Chan dialogues has been influenced by methodological debates about how to interpret religious texts. Dale Wright's work on the philosophical dimensions of Zen language has argued that Chan dialogues should be read as performative rather than propositional: they do not communicate information but enact a transformation in the listener's understanding. This performative reading provides a framework for understanding the chapter's dialogues beyond their literal doctrinal content.
