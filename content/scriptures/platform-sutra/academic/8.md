The sudden-gradual debate has been one of the most extensively studied topics in the academic study of Chan Buddhism. Peter Gregory's edited volume Sudden and Gradual: Approaches to Enlightenment in Chinese Thought (1987) provided a comprehensive scholarly framework for understanding this debate in its philosophical, historical, and cultural contexts.

Scholars have shown that the categories of "sudden" and "gradual" functioned primarily as sectarian labels rather than as accurate descriptions of actual practice methods. The historical Shenxiu, leader of the so-called Northern School, taught methods that included sudden elements, while Huineng's Southern School, despite its rhetoric of suddenness, incorporated gradual practice. John McRae has argued that the sudden-gradual binary was a polemical construction of the Southern School designed to discredit its rivals.

Academic research has also examined the political dimensions of the debate. The Southern School's ultimate triumph was connected to the patronage of powerful figures in the Tang imperial court. Yanagida Seizan's work has shown how the Southern School's claim to Huineng's authority was strategically promoted by his disciple Shenhui, who actively campaigned against the Northern School in a series of public debates and political maneuvers.
