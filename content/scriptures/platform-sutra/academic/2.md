Academic analysis of the prajna chapter reveals its dependence on multiple Indian Mahayana sources, particularly the Diamond Sutra and the broader Prajnaparamita literature, as well as tathagatagarbha texts such as the Awakening of Faith in the Mahayana. Scholars have debated the degree to which the Platform Sutra represents a genuine synthesis of these traditions or an eclectic combination that glosses over philosophical tensions.

Peter Gregory's work on the sudden-gradual debate has shown how the concept of prajna as presented in the Platform Sutra differs from its Indian precedents. In the Prajnaparamita literature, prajna is the perfection that sees through the emptiness of all phenomena. In the Platform Sutra, prajna is identified with the inherent nature of the mind, a shift that reflects the influence of Chinese indigenous thought, particularly the Daoist concept of naturalness (ziran).

The chapter's insistence that prajna is universally present has been compared by scholars with the Neo-Confucian teaching on the innate goodness of human nature (xing), suggesting that the Platform Sutra's soteriology was shaped by Chinese cultural assumptions about the fundamental nature of the human mind. This cross-cultural analysis reveals how Buddhist concepts were transformed as they entered the Chinese intellectual context.
