The chapter's redefinition of sitting meditation has attracted scholarly attention as a key moment in the development of Chan Buddhism's distinctive practice identity. Bernard Faure's work on the rhetoric and reality of Chan meditation has shown that despite the Platform Sutra's critique of attachment to sitting, Chan monasteries developed some of the most rigorous sitting meditation regimens in Buddhist history.

This apparent contradiction between the Platform Sutra's teaching and later Chan monastic practice has been analyzed by scholars as an example of the gap between doctrinal rhetoric and institutional reality. T. Griffith Foulk's research on Song dynasty Chan monasteries reveals that formal sitting meditation (zuochan) was a central feature of daily monastic routine, suggesting that the Platform Sutra's antiformalist rhetoric was absorbed into a tradition that continued to value formal practice.

Academic analysis has also examined how different Chan lineages interpreted this chapter. The Caodong (Soto) tradition of Hongzhi Zhengjue developed the practice of "silent illumination" (mozhao), while the Linji (Rinzai) tradition developed koan practice. Both claimed fidelity to Huineng's teaching while developing very different meditation methods, illustrating how a single text can generate divergent practice traditions.
