The chapter on temperaments and circumstances has been studied by scholars of Buddhist pedagogy as evidence for Chan teaching methods during the Tang dynasty. Morten Schlutter's research on the development of Chan pedagogy has shown how the idea of adapting teaching to the student's capacity evolved from a general Buddhist principle into a specific Chan methodology.

Academic analysis has connected this chapter's teaching with the broader Chinese cultural emphasis on the master-disciple relationship. The Confucian ideal of the sage teacher who perceives each student's nature and provides individualized instruction provided a cultural framework within which Chan pedagogy developed. Thomas Wilson's work on Confucian commemoration and the sage's legacy shows parallels between the construction of master-disciple lineages in both traditions.

Scholars have also examined the institutional implications of the Platform Sutra's emphasis on the teacher's ability to assess students. This teaching legitimized the authority of Chan masters to determine who had achieved genuine realization and who had not, creating a hierarchical system of spiritual authentication that became central to Chan institutional life. The dharma transmission system that developed in later Chan Buddhism can be traced, in part, to the principles articulated in this chapter.
