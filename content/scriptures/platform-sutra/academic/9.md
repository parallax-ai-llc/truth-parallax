The chapter on royal patronage has been analyzed by scholars of Chinese Buddhist-state relations as evidence for the complex dynamics between Chan institutions and imperial power during the Tang dynasty. Stanley Weinstein's Buddhism Under the Tang has provided the most comprehensive account of how Buddhist schools navigated the shifting political landscape of the Tang dynasty.

Academic research has examined how the Platform Sutra's account of Huineng's relationship with the court functioned to legitimize the Southern School's institutional position. By establishing that Huineng was recognized by the emperor, the text claims imperial endorsement for its lineage claims. This pattern of seeking imperial validation was characteristic of all major Buddhist schools in China.

Scholars have noted that the chapter's depiction of Huineng as reluctant to accept imperial honors follows a well-established trope in Chinese literature, where the sage demonstrates moral authority by initially refusing worldly recognition. This literary convention, rooted in Daoist hermit ideals and Confucian stories of reluctant officials, was adapted by Chan Buddhism to construct a narrative that simultaneously claimed political legitimacy and spiritual independence.
