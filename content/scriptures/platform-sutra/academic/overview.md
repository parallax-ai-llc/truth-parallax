## Overview

Academic study of the Platform Sutra has been revolutionized by the discovery of the Dunhuang manuscript (dated to around 830 CE), which differs substantially from the later expanded versions. Scholars including Philip Yampolsky, John McRae, and Morten Schlutter have analyzed the text's multiple recensions, historical context, and role in constructing Chan sectarian identity.

## Textual Analysis

The earliest Dunhuang version is considerably shorter and simpler than the Yuan dynasty edition that became standard. Academic analysis reveals how the text grew through successive editorial expansions, each layer reflecting the doctrinal concerns and institutional politics of its era. The autobiography of Huineng bears the marks of hagiographic construction rather than historical memoir.

## Historical Significance

Scholars view the Platform Sutra as a key document in the formation of Chan Buddhist identity. The sudden-gradual dichotomy it establishes served sectarian purposes, legitimizing the Southern School's claim to authentic transmission. The text reveals how religious communities construct authoritative narratives to establish institutional legitimacy.
