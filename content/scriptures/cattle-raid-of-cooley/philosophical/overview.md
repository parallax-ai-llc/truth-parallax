## Overview

The Táin raises profound philosophical questions about the nature of heroism, the ethics of violence, and the relationship between individual glory and communal welfare. Cú Chulainn's defense of Ulster enacts a heroic ideal, but the text also reveals the terrible cost of that ideal — in the death of his foster-brother Fer Diad, in the slaughter of the boy-troop, and in the devastation wrought by his own battle fury. The tale invites reflection on whether heroic violence can ever be truly just, and whether the warrior's code that demands combat is ultimately self-destructive.
