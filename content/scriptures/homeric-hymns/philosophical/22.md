Homeric Hymn 22, addressed to Poseidon, is a shorter hymnic invocation that, while primarily mythological in character, embeds theological and cosmological ideas with philosophical significance. The hymn's portrayal of Poseidon — the deity's attributes, powers, and relationship to the cosmic order — contributed to the Greek theological discourse that both nourished and provoked early philosophical reflection on the nature of the divine.

Philosophically, the hymn raises questions about the nature of divine power, the relationship between gods and the natural order, and the proper human response to divine manifestation. The tension between the hymn's anthropomorphic portrayal of Poseidon and the deity's cosmic functions anticipates the critique of traditional theology that begins with Xenophanes and continues through Plato.
