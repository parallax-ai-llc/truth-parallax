Homeric Hymn 3, addressed to Apollo, is one of the four major Homeric Hymns, an extended narrative poem that belongs to the masterful tradition of Greek hexameter poetry. As a literary work, the hymn displays the characteristic features of the Homeric style — formulaic epithets, type-scenes, ring composition, and the vivid narrative art that has made the Homeric tradition foundational to Western literature.

Literary analysis reveals the hymn's artful adaptation of epic conventions to the hymnic genre. The movement from invocation to narrative (or epithet catalogue) to closing formula creates a distinctive literary structure that serves both aesthetic and religious purposes. The hymn's language, while drawing on the shared stock of Homeric formulaic diction, is employed with precision and creativity to celebrate Apollo's distinctive nature and powers.
