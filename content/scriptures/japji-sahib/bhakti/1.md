The Mool Mantar represents a crystallization of the *nirguna* Bhakti theology that characterized the medieval Sant tradition of North India. Its insistence on the oneness, formlessness, and self-existence of the divine aligns with the theological vision of Kabir, Ravidas, and Namdev, who rejected idol worship, caste-based ritual, and sectarian religion in favor of interior devotion to the one formless God. The Bhakti reading of the Mool Mantar emphasizes its radical democratization of access to the divine — no priest, no ritual, no social standing is required to approach the One who is *Saibhang* (Self-Existent).

The Salok's negation of intellectual and ritual approaches to the divine echoes the Bhakti poets' persistent critique of Brahminical learning and empty ceremonialism. For the Bhakti tradition, the path to the divine lies not through the accumulation of knowledge or the performance of rituals but through the surrender of the ego in love and devotion — a theme that the Japji Sahib will develop extensively in subsequent pauris.
