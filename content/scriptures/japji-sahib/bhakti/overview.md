## Overview

The Bhakti tradition provides essential context for understanding the Japji Sahib. Guru Nanak emerged from the broader Sant-Bhakti movement of medieval North India — a tradition of poet-saints including Kabir, Ravidas, and Namdev who rejected caste hierarchy, empty ritual, and idol worship in favor of interior devotion to a formless (*nirguna*) God. The Japji Sahib represents a culminating synthesis of this tradition, integrating Sant themes with distinctive innovations that would become the foundation of Sikh theology.

The Bhakti reading of the Japji Sahib emphasizes its passionate devotion to the divine Name, its rejection of external religious forms as substitutes for inner transformation, and its radical egalitarianism. Nanak's declaration that spiritual realization is available to all regardless of caste, gender, or religious affiliation echoes the Bhakti conviction that love of God transcends all social and ritual boundaries. The Japji Sahib's imagery of the divine as both transcendent and intimately present draws on the Bhakti tension between *nirguna* (formless) and *saguna* (with attributes) conceptions of the divine.
