Sufi readers of the Mool Mantar find striking resonances with the Islamic declaration of divine unity (*tawhid*). The opening *Ik Onkar* (One Universal Creator) echoes *La ilaha illallah* (There is no god but God), while the subsequent attributes — Without Fear, Without Enmity, Timeless, Unborn — parallel the Sufi meditation on the ninety-nine names of Allah (*Asma ul-Husna*). The Sufi tradition of contemplating the divine names as a spiritual practice directly parallels the Sikh practice of meditating on the Mool Mantar.

The Salok's declaration that the divine cannot be reached by thinking alone resonates deeply with the Sufi emphasis on experiential knowledge (*ma'rifa*) over merely intellectual understanding (*'ilm*). The Sufi distinction between *'ilm* (book learning) and *ma'rifa* (direct mystical knowledge) maps onto Guru Nanak's distinction between futile intellectual striving and the transformative silence of submitting to the divine will.
