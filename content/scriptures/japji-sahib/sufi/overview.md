## Overview

Sufi readings of the Japji Sahib highlight the profound resonances between Guru Nanak's spiritual vision and the traditions of Islamic mysticism. The Japji Sahib's emphasis on remembrance of the divine Name (*Nam*) as the supreme spiritual practice parallels the Sufi practice of *dhikr* (remembrance of Allah). Both traditions understand this practice not as mere verbal repetition but as a transformative interior discipline that gradually dissolves the ego (*haumai* in Sikh terminology, *nafs* in Sufi).

The Japji Sahib's vision of spiritual ascent through the five realms (*khands*) resonates with the Sufi concept of *maqamat* (stations) on the path to God. Both traditions describe a progressive journey from ethical discipline through knowledge and love to the annihilation of the false self in the divine reality — *Sach Khand* (Realm of Truth) in Nanak's terminology, *fana fi'llah* in Sufi vocabulary. Historical evidence suggests Guru Nanak engaged directly with Sufi masters during his extensive travels, and the Japji Sahib reflects this creative encounter.
