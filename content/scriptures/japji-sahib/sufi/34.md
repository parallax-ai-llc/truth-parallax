Sufi readers immediately recognize in the five *khands* of the Japji Sahib a parallel to the *maqamat* (stations) and *ahwal* (states) that structure the Sufi path to God. The Dharam Khand (Realm of Righteousness) of Pauri 34 resonates with specific stations on the Sufi journey — from *tawba* (repentance) and *zuhd* (renunciation) through *sabr* (patience), *tawakkul* (trust), and *rida* (contentment) to *fana* (annihilation in God) and *baqa* (subsistence in God).

The historical proximity of Guru Nanak to the Sufi tradition of Punjab — particularly the Chishtiyya and Suhrawardiyya orders — makes these parallels especially significant. Whether Guru Nanak directly adapted Sufi frameworks or independently developed similar insights through shared spiritual experience is debated, but the resonance is unmistakable. Both traditions understand the spiritual life as a progressive journey through increasingly refined states of consciousness, culminating in the direct experience of divine reality.
