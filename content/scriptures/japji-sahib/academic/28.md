Pauri 28 — Munda Santokh — The Yoga of Contentment — has been examined by scholars in relation to the overall structure and progression of the Japji Sahib. Academic analysis considers the pauri's literary form (its use of parallelism, metaphor, and rhetorical progression), its theological content (its engagement with concepts from the Sant, Sufi, and Nath Yogic traditions), and its place within the larger argument of the composition.

Linguistic analysis reveals the pauri's characteristic multilingual texture, drawing vocabulary and imagery from Punjabi, Sanskrit, Persian, and Arabic. This polyglot quality reflects Guru Nanak's position at the confluence of multiple religious and literary traditions in medieval Punjab and supports the scholarly understanding of the Japji Sahib as a text that consciously transcends sectarian linguistic boundaries.
