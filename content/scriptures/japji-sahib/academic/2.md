Academic analysis of Pauri 2 examines *Hukam* as a philosophical concept with connections to multiple traditions. The term derives from the Arabic *hukm* (command, order), reflecting the Islamic theological milieu of medieval Punjab, while the concept's application resonates with the Hindu philosophical concept of *rta* (cosmic order) and the Daoist *Tao*. Scholars debate whether *Hukam* should be understood primarily as a personal divine command (closer to the Abrahamic model) or as an impersonal cosmic principle (closer to *rta* or *dharma*).

W.H. McLeod argued that *Hukam* in the Japji Sahib functions as an overarching concept that subsumes other key Sikh theological terms — *nadar* (grace), *bhana* (pleasure/will), and *razai* (will, from Persian). The pauri's declaration that the *Hukam* cannot be fully articulated ('How can one express the Hukam? One cannot say') establishes a theological aporia — an admission that the ultimate ordering principle of reality exceeds human conceptual capacity.
