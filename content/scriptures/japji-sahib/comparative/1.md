The Mool Mantar invites comparison with foundational creedal statements across religious traditions — the Shahada of Islam, the Shema of Judaism, the Nicene Creed of Christianity, and the Buddhist Triple Refuge formula. Like these declarations, it establishes the essential theological framework in condensed, memorable form. Its opening word *Ik* (One) resonates with the monotheistic affirmations of the Abrahamic traditions while its subsequent descriptors (*Nirbhau*, *Nirvair* — Without Fear, Without Enmity) move beyond abstract theology to ethical characterization of the divine.

The Salok's assertion that thinking alone cannot reach the divine parallels the Buddhist emphasis on the limits of conceptual thought (*vikalpa*), the Daoist *wu wei* (non-doing), and the Christian mystical tradition of *via negativa*. Comparative analysis reveals how different traditions navigate the tension between the human drive to understand the divine and the recognition that ultimate reality transcends all human categories.
