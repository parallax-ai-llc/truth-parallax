## Jewish Reading

Jewish scholarly engagement with Qolasta Section 13, "Hymns of Cosmic Creation," examines the liturgical parallels between Mandaean and Jewish prayer traditions. These parallels are particularly striking in this section, where the Qolasta's treatment of Hymns of Cosmic Creation echoes patterns familiar from the Jewish *siddur*, the *machzor*, and the liturgical practices described in rabbinic literature. The shared Aramaic linguistic heritage of both traditions adds a further dimension to the comparison, as scholars identify cognate vocabulary, parallel syntactic structures, and shared formulaic elements.

Jewish scholars are interested in what the Qolasta reveals about the diversity of Jewish liturgical practice in the Second Temple and late antique periods. The Mandaean prayers, while clearly distinct from rabbinic liturgy, may preserve echoes of liturgical traditions that flourished among Jewish baptist communities before the crystallization of the rabbinic *siddur*. The Qolasta's emphasis on water purification, its responsive prayer patterns, and its concern with the transition from impurity to purity resonate with Jewish ritual and theological categories, even as the Mandaean theological framework diverges sharply from rabbinic monotheism.

## Key Themes

- Parallels between Mandaean and Jewish liturgical structures
- Shared Aramaic liturgical vocabulary and formulae
- The relationship between *masbuta* prayers and *mikveh* blessings
- What Mandaean liturgy reveals about pre-rabbinic Jewish diversity
- The role of priestly authority in both liturgical traditions

## Key Passages

> "Praised be the Great Life from eternity to eternity. His light fills the worlds, and his truth endures forever."

Jewish scholars note the structural similarity between this praise formula and the Jewish *berakhah* pattern, as well as echoes of biblical psalmic language ("from eternity to eternity," "his truth endures forever"). These parallels suggest a shared liturgical heritage, even as the Mandaean invocation of "the Great Life" differs from the Jewish naming of God.

## Commentary Tradition

Jewish scholarship on the Qolasta is informed by the broader field of Second Temple studies and the history of Jewish liturgy. Key works include those by scholars who bridge Mandaean and Jewish studies, analyzing the shared cultural and linguistic matrix from which both traditions emerged.
