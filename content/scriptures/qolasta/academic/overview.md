## Overview

Academic study of the Qolasta encompasses philology, liturgical studies, ritual analysis, and the history of religions. E. S. Drower's 1959 critical edition and translation remains the foundational scholarly resource, based on manuscripts she collected during decades of fieldwork among the Mandaean communities of southern Iraq. Subsequent scholars — including Rudolf Macuch, Jorunn Buckley, and Charles Häberl — have refined the understanding of the Qolasta's linguistic features, textual history, and ritual context.

The Qolasta's Mandaic language presents rich material for Aramaic philologists. Its prayers preserve archaic grammatical forms and vocabulary that shed light on the development of Eastern Aramaic. The liturgical structure of the text — with its repeated formulae, responsive patterns, and ritual stage directions — is analyzed alongside other ancient Near Eastern prayer collections, including the Jewish *siddur*, Syriac Christian liturgies, and Zoroastrian *yasna* ceremonies. Scholars debate the dating of individual prayers, with some possibly reaching back to the early centuries of the Common Era while others reflect later liturgical developments.
