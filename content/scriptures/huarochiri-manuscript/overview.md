## Overview

The Huarochirí Manuscript (*Runa yn[di]o ñiscap Machoncuna*) is the only substantial narrative of Andean mythology and religion written in an indigenous language — Quechua — during the colonial period. Composed around 1598-1608, probably under the direction of the Spanish priest Francisco de Ávila, the manuscript preserves the myths, rituals, and religious traditions of the people of Huarochirí province in the central Peruvian highlands. It is often called the "Andean Popol Vuh" for its significance as a record of indigenous American religious thought.

The manuscript recounts the origins and deeds of the regional deity Pariacaca (a mountain god associated with rain, storms, and water), his rival Huallallo Carhuincho, the creator deity Cuniraya Viracocha, and numerous local huacas (sacred beings and places). It describes elaborate rituals, pilgrimage traditions, agricultural ceremonies, and the complex relationships between human communities and the divine beings inhabiting the Andean landscape. The text preserves a distinctively Andean worldview in which mountains, springs, lakes, and other natural features are animate sacred beings requiring ongoing ritual attention.

## Structure

The manuscript's 31 chapters can be grouped into major thematic sections:

| Chapters | Content |
|----------|---------|
| 1-2 | Cuniraya Viracocha — the creator-trickster deity and his pursuit of Cavillaca |
| 3-5 | The ancient age — Huallallo Carhuincho's rule and the origins of Pariacaca |
| 6-10 | Pariacaca's birth, rise to power, and conquest of Huallallo Carhuincho |
| 11-15 | Pariacaca's sons and the establishment of ritual obligations |
| 16-20 | Local huacas, community rituals, and relationships with Pariacaca |
| 21-25 | Chaupiñamca and other female deities — gender and divine power |
| 26-28 | The Inca conquest and its impact on local religious practice |
| 29-31 | Colonial-era transformations and the persistence of indigenous religion |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: The most important surviving text of Andean religious thought in an indigenous language
- **Academic**: A foundational document for Andean studies, ethnohistory, and Quechua linguistics
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with other mountain-deity traditions and landscape-based religions
- **Theological**: Presents a sophisticated theology of place, reciprocity, and divine-human relationship
- **Archaeological**: Correlates with archaeological evidence from Huarochirí and the central Andes
- **Interfaith**: Offers a distinctive Andean perspective on sacred landscape, community, and the divine

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Huacas | Sacred beings inhabiting the landscape — mountains, springs, stones — requiring ritual attention |
| Pariacaca | The mountain deity as protector, rain-bringer, and sovereign of the region |
| Reciprocity | The Andean principle of ayni — mutual obligation between humans and the divine |
| Landscape | The Andean environment as a living, sacred geography |
| Community | The relationship between social organization and religious practice |
| Colonial Contact | The impact of Spanish conquest on indigenous religious traditions |
