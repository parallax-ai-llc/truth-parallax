## Overview

The Book of Veles (Велесова книга, Velesova kniga) is a text purporting to be an ancient Slavic religious and historical chronicle, allegedly written on wooden planks in a pre-Cyrillic script. The text was reportedly discovered in Ukraine in 1919 by a White Army officer, Fyodor Arturovich Izenbek, and later transcribed by Yuriy Mirolyubov in the 1940s and 1950s. The original planks, if they existed, were lost during World War II. The text presents itself as a collection of mythological narratives, religious hymns, and historical accounts of the Slavic peoples from ancient times.

The Book of Veles is one of the most controversial texts in Slavic studies. The majority of academic scholars consider it a modern forgery, likely created by Mirolyubov or his associates, based on linguistic anachronisms, historical inconsistencies, and the suspicious circumstances of its discovery and transmission. However, the text has acquired significant cultural importance in Slavic neopagan (Rodnovery) movements and among those seeking to reconstruct pre-Christian Slavic religion. Regardless of its disputed authenticity, the Book of Veles raises important questions about the construction of religious texts, the desire for indigenous spiritual traditions, and the relationship between scholarship and faith.

## Structure

The nine planks of the Book of Veles address the following topics:

| Plank | Title | Content |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The Triglav | The three-headed Slavic deity representing the cosmic triad |
| 2 | Svarog's Creation | The creation of the world by the celestial smith-god Svarog |
| 3 | The Pantheon | The gods and goddesses of the Slavic religion and their domains |
| 4 | Origins | The mythological origins of the Slavic peoples |
| 5 | Migrations | The wanderings and settlements of the Slavic tribes |
| 6 | Wars | Conflicts with neighboring peoples — Goths, Greeks, and others |
| 7 | Rituals | Sacred laws, rituals, and religious obligations |
| 8 | Seasonal Cycles | The agricultural calendar and its sacred significance |
| 9 | Prophecies | Visions of the future and the destiny of the Slavic people |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: A case study in textual authenticity, forgery, and the construction of religious canons
- **Comparative**: Contains motifs paralleling other Indo-European mythologies regardless of authorship
- **Philosophical**: Raises questions about authenticity, authority, and the nature of sacred texts
- **Literary**: A creative synthesis of Slavic mythological themes with literary and nationalist aspirations
- **Archaeological**: Claims that can be evaluated against actual archaeological evidence of Slavic religion
- **Interfaith**: Central to Slavic neopagan movements and the revival of pre-Christian Slavic spirituality
