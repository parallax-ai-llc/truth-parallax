The ritual practices and sacred laws described in Plank 7 can be evaluated against the substantial archaeological evidence for pre-Christian Slavic cult practice, including temple architecture, sacrificial deposits, and votive offerings recovered from excavated sites across the Slavic world.

The most completely excavated Slavic temple complex is Gross Raden in Mecklenburg, Germany, where Ewald Schuldt's excavations in the 1970s and 1980s revealed a ninth-century timber temple with a double-walled construction, interior cult platform, and associated deposits of animal bone (primarily horse and cattle skulls), ceramics, and metalwork. The temple's orientation and internal arrangement suggest formalized ritual procedures consistent with the kind of organized priestly cult that the Book of Veles describes. A second major temple excavation at Feldberg, also in Mecklenburg, yielded comparable features including a large hall with central hearth and evidence of communal feasting.

Animal sacrifice, a practice central to the Book of Veles' ritual prescriptions, is extensively attested in the Slavic archaeological record. At Ralswiek on Rügen, Joachim Herrmann's excavations revealed a harbor-side cult deposit containing horse skulls, weapon fragments, and burned grain — evidence of sacrificial rites combining animal offerings with agricultural and martial elements. The deposition of horse skulls at cult sites appears to be a widespread Slavic practice, documented from the Baltic coast to the Dnieper region. Thietmar of Merseburg's eleventh-century account of hecatombs (large-scale animal sacrifices) at Rethra, the sacred center of the Lutician Slavs, receives partial archaeological corroboration from deposits of animal bone found at candidate sites for this cult center.

Water-deposited votives constitute another significant category of ritual evidence. Bog and river deposits of weapons, jewelry, and tools across the Slavic world parallel the votive traditions documented in Germanic and Celtic contexts. In the Tollense valley of northeastern Germany — the site of a massive Bronze Age battlefield excavated by Thomas Terberger — later Slavic-period deposits in the same waterway suggest the continuity of the river as a sacred landscape feature. The Book of Veles' emphasis on riverine and water-associated rituals finds material support in this pattern of wet-site deposition, which Leszek Słupecki has documented as a persistent feature of Slavic religious practice.
