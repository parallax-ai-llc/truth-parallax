The question of Slavic origins, which Plank 4 addresses through mythological genealogy, remains one of the most debated problems in European archaeology. The text locates the ancestral homeland of the Slavs in a region conventionally identified with the steppe and forest-steppe zones of eastern Europe, and this broad geographical claim intersects with the archaeological debate over the Slavic Urheimat (homeland) that has occupied scholars since the nineteenth century.

The archaeological cultures most frequently associated with early Slavic ethnogenesis include the Przeworsk culture (second century BCE to fifth century CE) in central Poland, the Zarubintsy culture (third century BCE to first century CE) in the middle Dnieper region, and the later Prague-type culture (sixth to seventh centuries CE) that spread rapidly across central and eastern Europe. Kazimierz Godłowski's influential thesis identified the Prague culture as the first definitively Slavic archaeological horizon, characterized by small semi-subterranean dwellings (Grubenhäuser), hand-made pottery with simple profiles, and cremation burial — a material assemblage strikingly modest compared to the Germanic and steppe-nomadic cultures with which the early Slavs interacted.

More recent approaches, particularly those of Florin Curta, have challenged the equation of archaeological cultures with ethnic identities, arguing that "Slavic" identity was constructed through political and social processes during the sixth and seventh centuries rather than inherited from a primordial homeland. Curta's analysis of fibulae (brooch) distributions and settlement patterns in the Danube frontier zone suggests that the Slavic ethnic label was initially applied by Byzantine observers and subsequently adopted by diverse populations along the empire's northern periphery. This constructivist perspective casts the Book of Veles' origin narrative as precisely the kind of mythological charter that communities produce to naturalize contingent ethnic identities.

Genetic and linguistic evidence offers additional data points. The distribution of Y-chromosome haplogroup R1a-M458, which reaches its highest frequencies among modern West Slavic populations, provides a genetic tracer for population movements that converges broadly with the archaeological model of Slavic expansion from a Vistula-Dnieper homeland region. Meanwhile, comparative linguistic reconstruction, particularly the work of Oleg Trubachev on Slavic hydronymy, points to the upper Dnieper basin as the area where Proto-Slavic vocabulary for local flora, fauna, and waterways is most densely preserved — a linguistic-geographical argument that the Book of Veles' origin traditions, whatever their textual authenticity, situate in the correct general region.
