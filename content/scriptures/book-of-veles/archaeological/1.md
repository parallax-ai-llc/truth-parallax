The Triglav concept presented in Plank 1 — a threefold divine structure encompassing Svarog, Perun, and Veles — can be evaluated against the archaeological and historical evidence for pre-Christian Slavic religious structures. The most significant material evidence for a tripartite divine figure comes from the multi-headed idol tradition of the western Slavs, particularly the four-faced Zbruch idol discovered in 1848 in the Zbruch River in western Ukraine. This ninth- or tenth-century limestone pillar, now in the Kraków Archaeological Museum, depicts figures arranged in three vertical registers that Boris Rybakov interpreted as representing celestial, terrestrial, and underworld realms — a cosmological division that resonates with the Triglav's triadic structure.

The historical evidence for a deity specifically named Triglav comes primarily from the twelfth-century chronicles. Herbord's Vita Ottonis and Ebbo's account of Bishop Otto of Bamberg's missionary campaigns to Pomerania (1124–1128) describe a three-headed idol at Szczecin (Stettin) whose eyes and mouths were covered with gold veils. Archaeological excavations at Szczecin's Castle Hill by Władysław Filipowiak revealed traces of an early medieval cult site, including postholes consistent with a timber temple structure, though the idol itself was destroyed during Christianization.

The cult site at Arkona on the island of Rügen, excavated by Carl Schuchhardt in 1921 and more recently investigated by Fred Ruchhöft, provides the most extensively documented example of western Slavic temple architecture. While the principal deity at Arkona was the four-faced Svantevit (described by Saxo Grammaticus), the site's archaeological features — a rectangular timber temple within a fortified enclosure, containing a polychrome idol, ritual drinking horns, and a white sacred horse — offer material evidence for the kind of organized priestly cult that the Book of Veles attributes to the worship of its Triglav.

The question of the Book of Veles' authenticity is central to any archaeological assessment. Most scholars, following the detailed paleographic and linguistic analysis of Omeljan Pritsak, Lydia Zhukovsky, and others, consider the text a modern fabrication, likely produced in the early twentieth century. The wooden-plank medium claimed for the original — birch boards inscribed with a proto-Cyrillic script — has no parallel in the authenticated archaeological record of Slavic literacy, which begins with the Glagolitic and Cyrillic scripts of the ninth century. Nevertheless, the religious concepts described in Plank 1 draw on genuine elements of Slavic religion as attested through independent archaeological and historical sources.
