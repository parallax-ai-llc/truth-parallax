The pantheon described in Plank 3 can be evaluated against the most important archaeological document for the Slavic divine hierarchy: the account of Vladimir I's pantheon at Kiev in 980 CE, described in the Primary Chronicle and partially corroborated by excavations on Starokievskaya Hill. According to the chronicle, Vladimir erected idols of Perun, Khors, Dažbog, Stribog, Simargl, and Mokosh on the hill above his court. Archaeological work by Viktor Khvoika and later Soviet-era excavations identified a stone platform with postholes consistent with an idol arrangement, though interpretation remains debated.

The Perun cult, central to the Book of Veles' pantheon, has the strongest archaeological attestation of any Slavic deity. At Perynʹ near Novgorod, excavations by V.V. Sedov in 1951–1953 revealed a circular platform with eight petal-shaped projections surrounding a central post-pit, interpreted as the remains of a Perun sanctuary. Each projection contained traces of fire, consistent with the lightning-god's association with flames. Although Sedov's interpretation has been questioned by scholars such as L.S. Klein, who proposed alternative readings of the Perynʹ features, the site remains the most widely cited archaeological evidence for organized Perun worship.

The deity Veles, who gives the text its name, appears in archaeological and epigraphic evidence primarily through place-names and the testimony of the Slovo o polku Igoreve (The Tale of Igor's Campaign), which calls the poet Boyan "grandson of Veles." The opposition between Perun (upper world, thunder, oak) and Veles (lower world, cattle, water) — reconstructed by Ivanov and Toporov as the fundamental axis of Slavic mythology — finds material expression in the topographic distribution of cult sites: Perun sanctuaries tend to occupy elevated positions (hilltops, above-settlement locations), while Veles-associated place-names cluster near rivers, lowlands, and marketplaces, as documented in Aleksandr Gieysztor's survey of Slavic religious toponymy.

The goddess Mokosh, included in Vladimir's pantheon and present in the Book of Veles' divine assembly, has been linked archaeologically to the widespread tradition of female ritual figurines found across the medieval Slavic world. Spindle whorls bearing schematic female figures, found at sites from Novgorod to the Danube basin, have been interpreted by Rybakov and others as connected to Mokosh in her capacity as patroness of spinning, weaving, and women's domestic labor — though this identification remains speculative. More securely, the persistence of "Mokosh" in Russian folk tradition as a supernatural figure associated with sheep-shearing and wet weather demonstrates the deep cultural embedding of this deity beyond any single textual attestation.
