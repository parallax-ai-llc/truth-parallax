The migration narratives of Plank 5 describe the Slavs wandering through steppe and forest zones, encountering various peoples and establishing settlements along rivers. The archaeological record of Slavic expansion during the sixth through ninth centuries provides extensive material evidence for precisely this kind of migratory process, though the chronology and routes described in the text do not correspond precisely to any authenticated historical sequence.

The most dramatic phase of Slavic migration, the colonization of the Balkans in the sixth and seventh centuries, is documented both in Byzantine written sources (Procopius, Theophylact Simocatta, the Miracles of St. Demetrius) and in the archaeological record. The appearance of Prague-type pottery, cremation cemeteries, and semi-subterranean dwellings across the former Roman provinces of Moesia, Thrace, and Illyricum marks the material trace of this expansion. Excavations at sites such as Popina on the Danube (investigated by Zhivka Văzharova) and the early Slavic settlement layers at Olympus in Greece demonstrate the rapidity and scale of this demographic movement.

The eastward dimension of Slavic migration, into the forest zone of what is now Russia, is traced archaeologically through the Long Barrow (dlinnye kurgany) culture of the Pskov-Novgorod region and the Romny-Borshevo culture of the Dnieper left bank. Valentin Sedov's comprehensive surveys mapped the distribution of these archaeological cultures, documenting the progressive Slavic colonization of territories previously inhabited by Baltic and Finno-Ugric populations. Key sites include Gnezdovo near Smolensk, where large-scale excavations have revealed a multiethnic trading settlement with Slavic, Scandinavian, and steppe-nomadic material culture — evidence of the complex cultural interactions that accompanied migration.

The Book of Veles' emphasis on rivers as migration routes accords with the archaeological evidence. Slavic settlement patterns consistently follow river systems: the Vistula, Oder, Dnieper, Don, and their tributaries served as communication and transportation corridors. Pavel Dolukhanov's environmental archaeology of the East European plain demonstrated how the river-and-forest ecology shaped settlement distribution, with Slavic communities preferring river terrace locations that combined agricultural potential with access to fishing and transportation — a settlement logic visible from the earliest Prague-type sites through the formation of the Kievan Rus' state.
