Plank 2's account of Svarog as creator deity and celestial smith can be assessed against the archaeological evidence for smithing cults and fire-worship among the early Slavs. The association of a supreme sky-god with metalworking has material correlates in the widespread deposition of smithing tools and iron implements in ritual contexts across the Slavic world. Excavations at early medieval Slavic settlements in the Danube basin and the East European forest-steppe zone have revealed smithing workshops positioned in spatially significant locations within settlement layouts, suggesting the ritual importance of metallurgy.

The Hypatian Chronicle's reference to Svarog as the divine smith who forged the first plowshare and established marriage laws connects to archaeological evidence for the adoption of iron plow agriculture among the Slavs. The transition from slash-and-burn cultivation to plow agriculture, documented through the distribution of iron plowshares and coulters at sites such as Zimno in Volhynia (excavated by V.V. Aulikh) and the Prague-type culture settlements of the sixth and seventh centuries, represents a transformative technological shift. The mythological attribution of this technology to a creator deity parallels patterns found in other Indo-European traditions where cultural innovations are ascribed to divine origin.

Fire-worship, central to the Svarog cosmogony described in this plank, is well attested in the Slavic archaeological record. Hearth-centered ritual deposits have been found at numerous sites, including the Zbručany complex and the temple sites of Gross Raden in Mecklenburg, excavated by Ewald Schuldt in the 1970s. At Gross Raden, a reconstructed seventh-century Slavic temple yielded evidence of perpetual fire maintenance and animal sacrifices, providing material context for the text's emphasis on fire as a sacred creative force. The Old Russian term svarožič (son of Svarog), used for fire itself in the eleventh-century Slovo of Christoliubets, confirms the linguistic and conceptual association between the deity and the element.

Comparative archaeological evidence from the broader Indo-European world strengthens the assessment of Svarog-traditions as reflecting genuine pre-Christian beliefs. The Vedic Tvaṣṭṛ, the divine craftsman of Hindu tradition, presents structural parallels documented by Georges Dumézil and later by Viacheslav Ivanov and Vladimir Toporov in their reconstruction of the Proto-Slavic mythological system. While the Book of Veles' specific narrative formulations cannot be archaeologically verified, the conceptual framework of a celestial smith-creator occupies a well-attested position within comparative Indo-European religious archaeology.
