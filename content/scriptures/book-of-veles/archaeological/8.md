Plank 8's description of seasonal festivals and agricultural cycles invites comparison with the archaeobotanical and settlement-pattern evidence for Slavic agricultural practice, as well as the material traces of calendar-based rituals in the archaeological record.

Archaeobotanical studies from early Slavic sites have identified the principal crops of the medieval Slavic agricultural economy: millet (Panicum miliaceum), wheat (Triticum aestivum), barley (Hordeum vulgare), rye (Secale cereale), and various legumes. The prominence of millet in the earliest Slavic archaeological contexts — documented by Marek Dulinicz at sites such as Bachórz-Chodorówka and by Eugeniusz Cnotliwy at Szczecin — reflects an agricultural system adapted to the light, sandy soils of the North European Plain. The gradual transition to rye cultivation during the ninth and tenth centuries, traced through palynological and macrobotanical evidence, represents a significant agricultural intensification that reshaped settlement patterns and, presumably, the ritual calendar associated with planting and harvest.

The seasonal festivals described in the Book of Veles correspond structurally to the solstice and equinox celebrations documented in later Slavic folklore and attested archaeologically through fire-ritual deposits. The Kupala festival (summer solstice), associated with bonfires, water rituals, and plant-gathering, has been linked by Boris Rybakov to fire deposits found at several early medieval Slavic sites, including traces of large bonfires at open-air cult sites in the Dnieper region. Similarly, the Koliada (winter solstice) traditions preserved in East Slavic folk practice have been connected to archaeological evidence of midwinter feasting at settlement sites, where concentrations of animal bone and ceramic fragments indicate communal consumption events.

Evidence for grain storage and its ritual dimensions comes from the widespread pit-grain storage features (jamy) found at virtually every excavated early Slavic settlement. At Brześć Kujawski, Konrad Jażdżewski's excavations revealed granary pits containing carbonized grain alongside small ceramic vessels that may represent ritual deposits accompanying stored harvests. The ritual protection of stored grain through votive offerings — a practice described in the Book of Veles and documented in later Slavic ethnography — thus has plausible archaeological precedents in the depositional patterns observed at these sites.
