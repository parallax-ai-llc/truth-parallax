Plank 6's account of Slavic conflicts with the Goths and Greeks can be evaluated against a substantial body of archaeological evidence for interaction between Slavic, Germanic, and Byzantine populations in the Pontic steppe and Danube frontier zone during the Migration Period (fourth to seventh centuries CE).

The Chernyakhov culture (c. 200–400 CE), distributed across modern Ukraine and Moldova, represents the principal archaeological context for Gothic-Slavic interaction in the pre-Hunnic period. This multiethnic culture, excavated at hundreds of sites including the eponymous Chernyakhov cemetery investigated by Vikentiy Khvoika in 1900–1901, combined wheel-made and hand-made pottery, inhumation and cremation burial, and a mix of Germanic, Sarmatian, and local traditions. Mark Shchukin's analysis of the Chernyakhov culture's disintegration after the Hunnic invasions of the 370s–380s demonstrated how the collapse of Gothic hegemony in the Pontic region created the conditions for the subsequent Slavic demographic expansion.

The Penkovka culture (sixth to seventh centuries CE), identified by P.N. Tretyakov and further investigated by O.M. Prikhodnyuk, occupies the forest-steppe zone between the Dnieper and Dniester and is associated with the Antes — a Slavic-speaking people mentioned by Procopius and Jordanes. The Antes' conflicts with the Goths, recorded by Jordanes in his Getica (551 CE), provide a historical frame for the Book of Veles' war narratives. Archaeological evidence of fortified settlements (horodishcha) in the Penkovka zone, some showing evidence of destruction by fire, attests to the militarized character of this frontier region.

Slavic-Byzantine military encounters, the other axis of conflict in Plank 6, are documented through both literary sources and frontier archaeology. The Danube limes, particularly sites such as Sucidava (modern Celei, Romania) and Novae (modern Svishtov, Bulgaria), show destruction layers and occupation changes consistent with the Slavic raids and settlements described by Procopius in his Wars and Buildings. Uwe Fiedler's archaeological surveys of the lower Danube frontier documented the progressive transformation of Roman-period fortifications into Slavic settlements during the seventh century, providing material evidence for the text's narrative of territorial conquest at Byzantine expense.
