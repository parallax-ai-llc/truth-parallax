From an academic perspective, Al-Fatiha presents several fascinating questions regarding its form, function, and relationship to earlier religious traditions. Structurally, it is unique among Quranic surahs as a prayer addressed *to* God rather than speech *from* God — a feature that has led some scholars to question whether it was originally part of the Quran or an independent liturgical text later incorporated.

**Angelika Neuwirth** analyzes Al-Fatiha within the context of Late Antique liturgical traditions, noting structural parallels with Jewish and Christian prayer forms, particularly the *Amidah* and the *Pater Noster* (Lord's Prayer). The tripartite structure — praise, acknowledgment of divine sovereignty, petition — follows patterns well attested in Semitic prayer literature.

**Nicolai Sinai** examines the surah's vocabulary and rhetorical structure, noting that *al-sirat al-mustaqim* ("the straight path") may reflect Syriac Christian usage, where the cognate term appears in similar spiritual contexts. The designation *al-hamd* ("praise") as the surah's name parallels the Jewish practice of naming psalms by their opening words. These observations situate Al-Fatiha within a broader monotheistic liturgical tradition while recognizing its distinctive Quranic character.
