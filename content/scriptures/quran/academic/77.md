## Overview

Surah Al-Mursalat (Q 77), consisting of 50 verses, belongs to the earliest stratum of Quranic revelation according to most Western and Muslim chronological schemes. Noldeke-Schwally places it firmly in the early Meccan period. The surah is characterized by its distinctive refrain structure, oath-series opening, and eschatological content — features typical of the early Meccan corpus.

## Academic Analysis

**Angelika Neuwirth** in *Studien zur Komposition der mekkanischen Suren* analyzes the surah's formal structure, identifying it as an example of the "oath-eschatology" pattern common to early Meccan surahs. The oath series (verses 1-7) builds toward the eschatological declaration, while the refrain "Waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin" functions as a structural marker dividing the surah into discrete rhetorical units.

**Nicolai Sinai** in *The Quran: A Historical-Critical Introduction* notes that the surah's language and style bear close resemblance to pre-Islamic Arabic *saj'* (rhymed prose), while simultaneously transforming this genre for monotheistic theological purposes. The tension between continuity with and rupture from pre-Islamic literary forms is a key area of scholarly debate.

**Michael Sells** in *Approaching the Quran: The Early Revelations* highlights the acoustic and performative dimensions of this surah, arguing that its meaning cannot be fully grasped apart from its oral/aural realization. The repetitive structure creates what Sells calls a "sound vision" — an experience of the Day of Judgment conveyed through sonic intensity as much as semantic content.

## Dating and Composition

The surah's position in the Noldeke chronology (early Meccan) is broadly accepted. Its short verses, intense rhythm, and eschatological focus align with other surahs from this period (Q 81, 82, 84). Some scholars have noted possible connections with biblical prophetic literature, particularly the "day of the Lord" tradition in the Hebrew prophets.
