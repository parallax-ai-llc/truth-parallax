## Overview

Surah An-Nas (Q 114), consisting of 6 verses, is classified as a Meccan surah in both traditional Islamic and Western academic chronologies. Its position in the final portion of the Quran (*mushaf* order) reflects the general tendency to arrange shorter surahs toward the end, though this ordering does not correspond to the chronological sequence of revelation.

## Academic Analysis

In the Noldeke-Schwally chronological scheme, this surah is placed among the Meccan revelations, characterized by short, rhythmic verses, eschatological imagery, and direct rhetorical address. **Angelika Neuwirth**'s form-critical analysis identifies structural patterns in these short surahs that link them to broader ancient Near Eastern and late antique literary traditions while maintaining their distinctive Quranic character.

**Gabriel Said Reynolds** in *The Quran and the Bible* examines possible biblical and post-biblical parallels to the themes and imagery found in this surah, situating the Quranic text within the broader milieu of late antique Abrahamic discourse. **Nicolai Sinai** approaches the surah through the lens of inner-Quranic chronology, examining how its themes develop and interact with other Quranic passages.

## Scholarly Context

Recent scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of reading the Quran within its late antique context — the cultural, religious, and literary milieu of seventh-century Arabia and the broader Near East. This surah, like others in the final *juz'* of the Quran, reflects the intense eschatological preaching that characterized the earliest phase of the Quranic proclamation, a phase that shows both continuities with and departures from Jewish, Christian, and Zoroastrian apocalyptic traditions.
