Al-Fatiha invites rich comparison with opening prayers and foundational texts across religious traditions. Its structure — praise, divine sovereignty, petition for guidance — closely parallels the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13), which likewise opens with praise ("Our Father, who art in heaven, hallowed be thy name"), affirms divine sovereignty ("thy kingdom come"), and includes petition ("lead us not into temptation").

The Jewish *Amidah*, recited three times daily, shares Al-Fatiha's liturgical centrality and its movement from praise to petition. The opening blessings of the *Amidah* praise God as "great, mighty, and awesome," while Al-Fatiha praises God as "Lord of all worlds, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful." Both prayers function as the indispensable core of their respective worship traditions.

The concept of the "straight path" (*al-sirat al-mustaqim*) resonates with the Buddhist Middle Way, the Confucian *Zhongyong* (Doctrine of the Mean), and the Zoroastrian *Asha* (truth/righteousness). Each tradition articulates a concept of a correct or balanced path that avoids extremes. While the theological frameworks differ significantly, the shared human aspiration for guidance and right direction reveals a cross-cultural spiritual grammar.
