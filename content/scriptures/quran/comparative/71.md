## Overview

Surah Nuh (Noah), the 71th chapter of the Quran with 28 verses, provides fertile ground for comparative theological analysis across Islamic interpretive traditions and between Islam and other Abrahamic faiths. The surah's themes resonate with parallel discussions in Jewish, Christian, and other religious literatures, while also reflecting distinctly Quranic theological commitments.

## Cross-Traditional Islamic Perspectives

The divergences between Sunni, Shia, Sufi, and Salafi readings of this surah illuminate the interpretive pluralism within Islamic thought. Where Sunni classical tafsir emphasizes hadith-based exegesis and juridical extraction, Shia commentary foregrounds the role of the Imams as authoritative interpreters. Sufi readings uncover mystical dimensions often invisible to legalist approaches, while Salafi methodology insists on stripping away what it considers unauthorized accretions to return to the text's plain meaning.

## Abrahamic and Broader Comparisons

The surah's theological themes find parallels in biblical and rabbinic literature, Patristic Christian writings, and Zoroastrian texts. Comparative analysis reveals both shared Abrahamic concerns — monotheism, prophetic authority, eschatological accountability — and distinctive Quranic innovations that reframe inherited motifs within a new revelatory context. Scholars such as Reuven Firestone, Sidney Griffith, and Mustafa Akyol have mapped these connections, demonstrating how the Quran participates in and transforms the broader discourse of late antique monotheism.
