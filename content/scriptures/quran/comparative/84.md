## Overview

Surah Al-Inshiqaq (Q 84), consisting of 25 verses, presents themes and imagery that resonate with traditions across the Abrahamic faiths and beyond. A comparative reading illuminates both the shared substrate of late antique religious thought and the distinctive contributions of the Quranic proclamation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Abrahamic Context

The themes of this surah — divine sovereignty, moral accountability, eschatological reckoning — belong to the common inheritance of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. The specific Quranic treatment of these themes, however, reflects a distinctive theological voice. Where biblical texts often embed eschatological warnings within extended narrative or legal frameworks, the Quran concentrates them into short, rhetorically intense units designed for oral proclamation and memorization.

### Broader Religious Parallels

The surah's emphasis on cosmic signs as evidence of divine power finds parallels not only in the Bible and Zoroastrian texts but also in Hindu concepts of *pralaya* (cosmic dissolution) and Buddhist teachings on the impermanence of all conditioned phenomena. While these parallels do not imply direct influence, they suggest shared human concerns about the ultimate fate of the cosmos and the moral significance of human action.

## Cross-Traditional Reflections

Reading this surah comparatively highlights the Quran's distinctive contribution to the broader human conversation about justice, accountability, and transcendence. The surah's insistence that cosmic power and moral order are inseparable — that the God who created the universe is the same God who will judge every soul — represents a theological conviction shared across the Abrahamic traditions but expressed with particular intensity in the Quranic idiom.
