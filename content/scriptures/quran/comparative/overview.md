## Overview

Comparative study of the Quran examines its relationship to other sacred texts and religious traditions, identifying shared narratives, theological parallels, and significant divergences. This approach recognizes that the Quran exists within a rich web of Abrahamic and Late Antique textual traditions and that many of its narratives, ethical teachings, and theological concepts have counterparts in the Hebrew Bible, the New Testament, rabbinic literature, Syriac Christian writings, and Zoroastrian texts.

## Key Comparative Frameworks

### Shared Prophetic Narratives
The Quran retells stories of many biblical figures — Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, David, Solomon, Mary, and Jesus — but often with significant variations in detail, emphasis, and theological interpretation. These retellings are not simply borrowings but represent an independent prophetic perspective on shared sacred history.

### Scriptural Reasoning
An interfaith academic practice where Jewish, Christian, and Muslim scholars read their respective scriptures together, exploring how each tradition's hermeneutical methods illuminate shared texts. This approach resists both facile harmonization and polemical contrast.

### Theological Comparison
Systematic comparison of doctrinal themes — monotheism, revelation, prophecy, law, eschatology, ethics — across the Quran, Bible, and other scriptures, attending to both convergence and irreducible difference.

## Key Areas of Comparison

| Theme | Quran | Bible | Points of Convergence/Divergence |
|-------|-------|-------|--------------------------------|
| Monotheism | Strict tawhid; no Trinity | Trinitarian (Christian); Shema (Jewish) | All affirm one God; differ on divine nature |
| Jesus | Prophet, Messiah, not divine; no crucifixion | Son of God; crucified and risen | Shared reverence; fundamental christological divergence |
| Abraham | Hanif; builder of the Kaaba with Ishmael | Father of faith; covenant through Isaac | Shared patriarch; divergent covenantal lines |
| Law | Shariah derived from Quran and Sunnah | Torah/Halakha; natural law (Christian) | Shared concern for divine law; different legal systems |
| Eschatology | Detailed Day of Judgment, paradise, hellfire | Varied apocalyptic traditions | Significant parallels in imagery and structure |
