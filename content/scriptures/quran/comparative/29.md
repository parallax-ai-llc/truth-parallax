## Comparative Analysis

Surah Al-Ankabut (The Spider), with its 69 verses from the Meccan period, is a surah using the metaphor of the spider's fragile web to describe false objects of worship, exploring how trials and persecution test genuine faith, recounting the stories of Noah, Abraham, Lot, Shu'ayb, and Moses, and affirming that God's support comes to those who strive sincerely. Comparative study examines this surah alongside related passages in the Hebrew Bible, New Testament, rabbinic literature, and other religious traditions, identifying both shared themes and significant divergences in theological emphasis.

The surah's narratives and theological claims participate in a broader Abrahamic conversation about God's nature, human responsibility, and the purpose of revelation. Where the Hebrew Bible emphasizes God's covenant with a particular people and the New Testament centers on the person and work of Jesus Christ, the Quran presents itself as a confirmation and correction of previous scriptures — affirming the essential message of all prophets while asserting its own finality and completeness.

Key comparative points emerge when this surah is read alongside its biblical and post-biblical counterparts. The Quranic retelling of shared narratives — prophetic stories, legal principles, eschatological imagery — reveals a consistent pattern: familiar elements are retained but reframed within a strictly monotheistic theology that rejects incarnation, Trinity, and divine sonship while affirming the prophetic missions of Moses and Jesus.

The comparative approach also extends beyond the Abrahamic traditions. Themes in this surah — divine justice, human accountability, the moral structure of the cosmos, and the call to ethical living — find resonance in Hindu concepts of *dharma*, Buddhist teachings on *karma*, and Confucian ideals of moral cultivation. These parallels, while not implying historical dependence, reveal shared human concerns that transcend particular scriptural traditions and invite dialogue across civilizational boundaries.
