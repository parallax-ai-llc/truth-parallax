## Overview

Surah Al-Mursalat (Q 77), with its 50 verses of eschatological warning, invites comparison with apocalyptic traditions across multiple religious traditions. The surah's structure — oath series, eschatological declaration, refrain of warning — has functional parallels in biblical prophetic literature, Zoroastrian eschatology, and late antique apocalyptic texts, while maintaining features unique to the Quranic proclamation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Biblical Parallels

The "Day of the Lord" tradition in the Hebrew prophets (Amos 5:18-20, Joel 2:1-11, Zephaniah 1:14-18) shares with this surah the motif of a coming day of cosmic reckoning that will overwhelm the complacent and the unjust. The rhetorical strategy of marshaling cosmic signs as evidence of divine power finds parallels in Job 38-41 and Psalm 104, though the Quranic deployment of this strategy serves a distinctively eschatological purpose.

### Zoroastrian Connections

The vivid imagery of cosmic dissolution and reconstitution echoes Zoroastrian concepts of *frashokereti* (the making wonderful) — the final renovation of the world. Some scholars, including **Geo Widengren**, have explored possible channels of influence between Zoroastrian and Quranic eschatology, while others emphasize independent development from shared Near Eastern mythological substrates.

### Intertextual Dynamics

The refrain structure of this surah — "Woe, that Day, to the deniers" — has been compared to the "woe oracles" of Isaiah (5:8-23) and Habakkuk (2:6-20), where a repeated formula of warning punctuates a series of accusations. Whether this represents direct literary influence, shared rhetorical convention, or independent development remains debated. The Quran's own self-understanding, of course, attributes its content to divine revelation rather than literary borrowing.
