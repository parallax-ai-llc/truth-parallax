## Overview

Shia Islam approaches the Quran with a distinctive hermeneutical framework centered on the authority of the Ahl al-Bayt — the Prophet Muhammad's family, particularly Ali ibn Abi Talib and his descendants, the twelve Imams. Shia tafsir holds that the Quran possesses both an exoteric (*zahir*) and esoteric (*batin*) dimension, and that the Imams are the divinely appointed interpreters who possess the deepest understanding of both levels.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Imamate-Centered Hermeneutics
Many Quranic verses are read as alluding to the authority of Ali and the Imams. Verses about *wilayah* (authority/guardianship), such as Quran 5:55, are understood as explicit references to Ali's divinely ordained leadership.

### Ta'wil (Inner Interpretation)
While Sunni tradition often limits *ta'wil* to a narrow range of allegorical readings, Shia scholarship embraces a broader understanding. The Imams are considered the authoritative guides to the inner meanings of the Quran, which complement rather than replace the literal sense.

### Hadith of the Ahl al-Bayt
Shia tafsir draws on hadith collections distinct from Sunni sources, particularly from Imams al-Baqir, al-Sadiq, and al-Rida. These narrations are compiled in works like *Al-Kafi* by al-Kulayni and *Tafsir al-Qummi*.

## Theological Emphases

- **Wilayah**: The Quran establishes the spiritual and temporal authority of Ali and the Imams
- **Divine Justice (*'Adl*)**: God is absolutely just; human free will is essential for moral accountability
- **Intercession (*Shafa'ah*)**: The Prophet and Imams serve as intercessors on the Day of Judgment
- **Textual Integrity**: Mainstream Twelver Shia scholarship affirms the Quran's textual preservation, rejecting claims of alteration
