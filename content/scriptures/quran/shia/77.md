## Overview

Surah Al-Mursalat, comprising 50 verses, is understood in the Shia exegetical tradition as a powerful declaration of divine justice and eschatological certainty. The Shia reading of this surah, while sharing much with the broader Islamic interpretive tradition, is distinguished by its attention to narrations from the Ahl al-Bayt — the Prophet's family — and by its theological emphasis on divine justice (*'adl*) as a fundamental attribute of God.

## Shia Exegetical Tradition

**Al-Tabrisi** in *Majma' al-Bayan* explains that "al-mursalat" may refer to angels, winds, or the prophets and messengers themselves, each sent forth in succession to guide humanity. He preserves multiple interpretive traditions, including those transmitted through Imam al-Baqir and Imam al-Sadiq.

In the tafsir attributed to **Imam al-Askari** and in **Al-Qummi's** commentary, the repeated refrain "Woe, that Day, to the deniers" is connected not only to those who denied resurrection but also to those who denied the rights of the Ahl al-Bayt and the designated successors of the Prophet. This layered reading — combining the apparent (*zahir*) with the hidden (*batin*) — is characteristic of Shia hermeneutics.

## Theological Significance

In Twelver Shia theology, this surah reinforces the principle of divine justice: God's judgment is certain, and the oppressors of every age — including those who usurped the rights of the Imams — will face reckoning. The surah's eschatological warnings are understood as applying across all historical periods, not merely to the original Meccan audience.
