## Overview

Salafi interpretation of the Quran is rooted in the principle that the understanding of the earliest Muslim generations — the *salaf al-salih* (pious predecessors), comprising the Companions, their successors (*tabi'in*), and the generation after them — represents the most authentic and authoritative reading of the text. Salafism rejects what it considers later theological innovations (*bid'ah*), including the speculative theology (*kalam*) of the Ash'ari and Maturidi schools, the esoteric interpretations of Sufism, and the philosophical rationalism that entered Islamic thought from Greek sources.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Adherence to the Apparent Meaning (*Zahir*)
Salafi hermeneutics insists on taking the Quran at its apparent meaning unless there is clear textual evidence requiring otherwise. This is especially prominent in debates about God's attributes — Salafis affirm attributes like God's "hand" or "face" as real without asking "how" (*bi-la kayf*), rejecting both anthropomorphism and metaphorical reinterpretation.

### Rejection of Allegorical Ta'wil
Unlike Sufi and certain Ash'ari approaches, Salafi methodology opposes reinterpreting Quranic verses about God's attributes as metaphors. They follow **Ibn Taymiyyah's** principle of affirming what God affirms about Himself without distortion (*tahrif*), denial (*ta'til*), comparison (*tamthil*), or asking how (*takyif*).

### Hadith-Centered Reading
Like mainstream Sunni tafsir, Salafi interpretation relies heavily on prophetic traditions, but places particular emphasis on hadith grading and chain authentication, often following the methodology of **Al-Albani** (d. 1999).

## Theological Emphases

- **Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah**: The oneness of God in worship — no intermediaries, saints, or shrine veneration
- **Tawhid al-Asma' wa al-Sifat**: Affirming God's names and attributes as they appear in the text
- **Wala' wa Bara'**: Loyalty to believers and disavowal of practices considered polytheistic
- **Anti-Innovation**: Strict opposition to practices not found in the earliest sources

| Ibn Taymiyyah | 13th-14th century | Foundational theological methodology |
