## Overview

Surah Al-Mursalat, consisting of 50 verses, is approached in the Salafi tradition through strict adherence to the interpretive methodology of the *salaf al-salih* (righteous predecessors) — the Companions, their Successors, and the early generations of Muslim scholars. The Salafi reading prioritizes *tafsir bil-ma'thur* (interpretation based on transmitted reports) and rejects speculative, philosophical, or esoteric hermeneutics.

## Salafi Exegetical Approach

**Ibn Kathir** — whose tafsir is among the most widely used in Salafi circles — explains that the "mursalat" are the winds sent forth by Allah, citing narrations from Abu Salih. He also records the opinion of Ibn Mas'ud that they are angels, but the preferred Salafi position follows the apparent meaning supported by the strongest chains of transmission.

**Shaykh al-Sa'di** in *Taysir al-Karim al-Rahman* provides a clear, straightforward commentary emphasizing the surah's central message: the certainty of the Day of Judgment and the folly of denial. The Salafi methodology insists on taking the Quran's eschatological descriptions at face value, without allegorical reinterpretation. When the Quran describes the events of the Hour, the faithful Muslim affirms them as they are stated.

**Shaykh Ibn Uthaymeen** in his tafsir lectures emphasized that the repeated refrain "Woe, that Day, to the deniers" serves as a stern warning that must be conveyed to the people without softening or reinterpretation. The Salafi da'wah (call) finds in these verses a mandate for clear, uncompromising preaching of the reality of the hereafter.

## Theological Significance

In Salafi aqidah, this surah affirms the doctrines of resurrection, judgment, paradise, and hellfire as literal realities. Any attempt to interpret these eschatological descriptions metaphorically or symbolically is rejected as an innovation (*bid'ah*) that contradicts the understanding of the salaf.
