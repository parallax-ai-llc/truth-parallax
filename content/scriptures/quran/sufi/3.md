## Sufi Commentary

Surah Ali-Imran (The Family of Imran), spanning 200 verses of the Medinan revelation, is a surah centered on the family of Imran (the family of Mary and Jesus), addressing Christological debates with the Christians of Najran, the Battle of Uhud, and the importance of steadfastness in faith and community solidarity. Sufi exegesis approaches this surah as a mirror for the soul's inner states, reading its narratives, injunctions, and imagery as descriptions of the spiritual journey (*suluk*) from the self (*nafs*) toward annihilation (*fana'*) in the Divine.

**Al-Qushayri** in *Lata'if al-Isharat* reads this surah through the lens of spiritual allusion (*ishara*), discerning in its verses the stages of the mystical path — from repentance (*tawbah*) through patience (*sabr*), gratitude (*shukr*), trust (*tawakkul*), and love (*mahabbah*) to the ultimate station of gnosis (*ma'rifah*). Each verse becomes a signpost for the wayfarer, and each narrative a parable of the soul's condition before God.

**Ibn Arabi** in his various works brings the framework of *wahdat al-wujud* (the unity of being) to bear on this surah, interpreting its descriptions of creation, judgment, and divine action as manifestations of the single divine reality expressing itself through the multiplicity of forms. For Ibn Arabi, the prophetic figures mentioned in the surah are not merely historical personages but represent eternal spiritual archetypes (*a'yan thabitah*) — fixed essences in God's knowledge that manifest in the temporal world as guides for humanity.

**Rumi** draws extensively on Quranic imagery in the *Masnavi*, and themes from this surah — divine mercy, human forgetfulness, the call to awakening — recur throughout his poetry. The Sufi reading does not negate the surah's legal or historical dimensions but insists that these outer forms contain an inner wine: the direct experience of the Divine that transforms knowledge into realization and belief into vision.
