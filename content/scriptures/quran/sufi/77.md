## Overview

Surah Al-Mursalat, with its 50 verses of thunderous warning, is read in the Sufi tradition not merely as a description of eschatological events but as a mirror held before the soul. The mystics of Islam (*ahl al-tasawwuf*) understood the Quran as operating on multiple levels simultaneously — the outward warning of Judgment Day and the inward call to spiritual awakening in this very moment.

## Sufi Interpretive Approach

**Ibn Arabi** in *Fusus al-Hikam* and his Quranic reflections understood the "sending forth" (*irsal*) as referring not only to winds and angels but to the divine emanations (*tajalliyat*) that continuously flow from the Real (*al-Haqq*) into creation. Every moment is a new creation, and every breath is an opportunity for the soul to witness its own annihilation (*fana'*) and subsistence (*baqa'*).

**Al-Qushayri** in *Lata'if al-Isharat* reads the refrain "Woe, that Day, to the deniers" as addressing not merely intellectual denial but the spiritual heedlessness (*ghaflah*) that prevents the heart from recognizing divine reality. The "Day" of judgment, in the Sufi reading, is also the perpetual moment of divine disclosure — every instant in which the soul is called to account before its Lord.

## Spiritual Practice

In many Sufi orders (*turuq*), the recitation of the short Meccan surahs, including Al-Mursalat, is incorporated into devotional practices (*awrad* and *adhkar*) as a means of cultivating *taqwa* (God-consciousness) and *khashyah* (reverential awe). The rhythmic intensity of the surah's structure is itself understood as a vehicle for spiritual transformation.
