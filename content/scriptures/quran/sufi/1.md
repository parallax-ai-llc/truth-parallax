For the Sufi tradition, Al-Fatiha is the quintessential prayer of the spiritual seeker, a microcosm of the entire journey toward God. **Al-Qushayri** in *Lata'if al-Isharat* reads each verse as corresponding to a station (*maqam*) on the spiritual path: praise corresponds to gratitude (*shukr*), "Lord of all worlds" to the recognition of God's all-encompassing reality, and "Guide us on the straight path" to the soul's longing for union.

**Ibn Arabi** interprets Al-Fatiha as a dialogue between the servant and the Divine, in which each verse draws the worshipper deeper into the reality of *tawhid*. The phrase "You alone we worship, and You alone we ask for help" (1:5) marks the pivotal moment of *fana'* — the annihilation of the ego-self in the awareness of God. Before this verse, the worshipper speaks *about* God in the third person; at this verse, the worshipper speaks *to* God directly, signaling an intimate encounter.

**Rumi** in the *Masnavi* draws on Al-Fatiha's imagery of the "straight path" as the razor's edge of spiritual discernment — the path that avoids both excess and deficiency, both spiritual arrogance and spiritual despair. The prayer is not merely for external guidance but for the inner illumination that transforms the heart.
