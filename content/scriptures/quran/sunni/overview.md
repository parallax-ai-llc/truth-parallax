## Overview

Sunni Islam, representing approximately 85-90% of the global Muslim population, approaches Quranic interpretation (*tafsir*) through a methodology that prioritizes transmitted knowledge (*tafsir bil-ma'thur*) — interpreting the Quran through the Quran itself, the Sunnah of the Prophet, the statements of the Companions (*sahabah*), and the understanding of the early generations (*salaf*). Rational interpretation (*tafsir bil-ra'y*) is accepted when grounded in sound linguistic and theological principles.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Tafsir bil-Ma'thur (Narration-Based)
The foundational method, exemplified by the tafsirs of **Al-Tabari** (d. 923) and **Ibn Kathir** (d. 1373). These works compile chains of narration (*isnad*) tracing interpretations back to the Prophet, his Companions, and the early exegetes (*tabi'in*).

### Tafsir bil-Ra'y (Reason-Based)
Employed within boundaries of Arabic grammar, theology, and legal reasoning. **Al-Zamakhshari** (d. 1144) and **Al-Razi** (d. 1210) represent this approach, though the latter integrates extensive philosophical discussion.

### Legal Tafsir
The four Sunni schools of law (*madhabs*) — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali — derive legal rulings from Quranic verses, sometimes reaching different conclusions from the same text. **Al-Qurtubi's** (d. 1273) *Al-Jami' li-Ahkam al-Quran* is the premier example.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tawhid**: God's absolute oneness in essence, attributes, and worship
- **Prophetic Authority**: The Sunnah as an indispensable interpretive key to the Quran
- **Consensus**: *Ijma'* of scholars as a binding source of interpretive authority
- **Preservation**: The Quran is perfectly preserved in its original Arabic form
