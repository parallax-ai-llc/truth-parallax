## Overview

Surah An-Nazi'at, comprising 46 verses, opens with a series of oaths by angels who carry out divine commands — extracting souls, racing forth, and administering affairs. In the Sunni exegetical tradition, this surah powerfully affirms the resurrection and recounts the story of Moses and Pharaoh as a cautionary parallel.

## Sunni Exegetical Tradition

**Al-Tabari** records that the "nazi'at" (those who pull out) refers to the angels of death who extract the souls of the disbelievers with violence, while "nashitat" (those who draw out gently) refers to the angels who take the souls of the believers with ease. This interpretation is supported by narrations from Ibn Abbas, Masruq, and Sa'id ibn Jubayr.

The central narrative section (verses 15-26) retells Moses's confrontation with Pharaoh, emphasizing Pharaoh's arrogant claim "I am your most exalted lord" (79:24) — the ultimate expression of human hubris. **Ibn Kathir** notes that this story functions as both a warning to the Quraysh and a comfort to the Prophet Muhammad, who faced similar opposition.

## Theological Themes

The surah concludes with a vivid description of the Hour and its terrors, followed by the rhetorical question about when it will come. The Prophet is told that his role is only to warn those who fear it. In Sunni aqidah, this surah reinforces that knowledge of the Hour belongs exclusively to Allah.
