## Overview

Surah Al-Mursalat takes its name from the opening verse referring to the winds or angels sent forth in succession. This Meccan surah of 50 verses delivers a powerful warning about the Day of Judgment, using the refrain "Woe, that Day, to the deniers" (*Waylun yawma'idhin lil-mukadhdhibin*) ten times throughout. In Sunni tafsir, this repetition is understood as a rhetorical device of extraordinary force, hammering home the certainty of divine reckoning.

## Sunni Exegetical Tradition

**Ibn Kathir** explains that "al-mursalat" refers to the winds sent by Allah as a mercy and a punishment, while other commentators such as **Ibn Mas'ud** understood them as angels. Al-Tabari preserves both views, noting that the Companions differed on this point. The surah proceeds through a series of oaths that build toward the declaration of Judgment Day's inevitability. Verses 16-19 recall the destruction of earlier peoples as a warning pattern.

**Al-Qurtubi** in *Al-Jami' li-Ahkam al-Quran* notes that the surah's structure mirrors the psychological arc of denial: each section presents evidence — cosmic, historical, and existential — and each is met with the refrain of woe. The final verses (48-50) challenge the deniers: if not in this message, then in what will they believe?

## Theological Significance

In mainstream Sunni theology, this surah reinforces core eschatological doctrines: the reality of bodily resurrection, the certainty of divine judgment, and the inadequacy of human denial in the face of overwhelming evidence. The surah is frequently recited in prayers and is part of the *Juz' Tabarak* (the 29th part of the Quran), which is widely memorized.
