## Overview

Surah Abasa, consisting of 42 verses, takes its name from its opening: "He frowned and turned away because the blind man came to him." This is one of the most remarkable passages in the Quran, as mainstream Sunni tafsir understands it as a gentle divine reproach directed at the Prophet Muhammad himself — a fact that Sunni scholars cite as evidence of the Quran's divine origin, since no human author would include criticism of himself.

## Occasion of Revelation

According to the majority Sunni position, drawing on narrations from Aisha and Ibn Abbas, the Prophet was engaged in conversation with leaders of the Quraysh, hoping to win them to Islam, when Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum, a blind Companion, approached seeking instruction. The Prophet frowned at the interruption. The revelation came as a correction: the sincere seeker, however humble, deserves attention over the proud who show no interest.

**Al-Qurtubi** draws from this the legal principle that a scholar must prioritize the sincere student over those of higher social standing. **Ibn Kathir** emphasizes that the Prophet never again treated Ibn Umm Maktum with anything but honor, later appointing him as governor of Medina during military campaigns.

## Theological Significance

The latter portion of the surah (verses 17-42) shifts to reflect on human ingratitude and the signs of creation, culminating in a description of the Day of Judgment when people will flee from their own families. In Sunni theology, this surah powerfully combines social ethics with eschatological warning.
