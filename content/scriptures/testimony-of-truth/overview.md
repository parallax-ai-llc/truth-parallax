## Overview

The Testimony of Truth (NHC IX,3) is a polemical Gnostic text that attacks both proto-orthodox Christianity and other Gnostic groups. The author — likely writing in Alexandria in the late second or third century CE — critiques the proto-orthodox church for its emphasis on water baptism (which the author considers ineffective), its veneration of martyrdom (which the author regards as futile), and its ignorance of the true meaning of Scripture. The text also attacks other Gnostic teachers — Valentinus, Basilides, the Simonians, and others — for various theological errors. One of its most striking passages is a retelling of the Genesis creation and fall narrative from the serpent's perspective, in which the serpent is the true revealer of knowledge and the creator God is exposed as envious and ignorant. The text is badly damaged, with many lacunae, but enough survives to reveal a fiercely independent Gnostic voice.

## Structure

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The True Witness | Opening declaration that true testimony comes through renunciation and interior knowledge, not external rites |
| 2 | Critique of Orthodox Practice | Attack on water baptism, martyrdom, procreation, and the proto-orthodox church's fundamental errors |
| 3 | Polemic Against Other Gnostics | Critique of Valentinus, Basilides, the Simonians, and other Gnostic teachers and their schools |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Reveals the internal diversity and rivalries within the Gnostic movement itself
- **Academic**: Central to understanding intra-Gnostic polemic and the range of Gnostic attitudes toward proto-orthodox Christianity
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to reformist and iconoclastic movements within other religious traditions
- **Christian**: Challenges the orthodox valorization of martyrdom and sacramental practice
- **Jewish**: Reinterprets the Genesis narrative from a radically anti-demiurgic perspective, inverting the roles of God and serpent
- **Interfaith**: Raises questions about the relationship between external religious practice and interior spiritual transformation

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Anti-Martyrdom | Martyrdom is not noble but foolish — God does not desire human suffering |
| Critique of Baptism | Water baptism is an empty ritual that does not confer true spiritual transformation |
| The Serpent as Revealer | In the Genesis narrative, the serpent reveals knowledge while the creator God conceals it out of envy |
| Intra-Gnostic Polemic | Even within Gnosticism, schools attacked each other for theological errors |
