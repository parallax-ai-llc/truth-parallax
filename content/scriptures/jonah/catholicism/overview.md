## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Jonah, rooted in the patristic and medieval tradition, reads the book as a profound teaching on the universality of God's saving will and the necessity of penance. The Church Fathers — Augustine, Jerome, and Cyril of Alexandria — uniformly interpreted Jonah's time in the fish as a type of Christ's death and descent into the realm of the dead. Nineveh's repentance, complete with fasting and sackcloth, became a model for the sacrament of penance in Catholic teaching.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The Catechism of the Catholic Church draws on Jonah to affirm that God desires the salvation of all peoples. Patristic exegesis emphasized the fourfold sense of Scripture: literally, Jonah is a prophet; allegorically, a type of Christ; morally, a lesson in obedience; anagogically, a sign of resurrection. The Lenten liturgy frequently draws on Jonah's narrative to call the faithful to repentance.

## Theological Emphases

- **Typology of Death and Resurrection**: The fish is Christ's tomb; the three days anticipate Easter
- **Universal Salvation**: God's mercy knows no ethnic or national boundary
- **Penance and Conversion**: Nineveh's response models the Catholic understanding of repentance as a total turning of life
- **Obedience to the Divine Commission**: The Church, like Jonah, is sent to proclaim mercy to the nations
