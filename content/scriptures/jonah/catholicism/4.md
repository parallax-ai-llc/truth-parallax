## God the Teacher

Catholic interpretation of Jonah 4 highlights the divine pedagogy at work throughout the chapter. God does not argue with Jonah abstractly but teaches through a concrete experience — the plant that grows and dies in a single day. This method of teaching mirrors the incarnational principle at the heart of Catholic theology: God communicates through the material, the particular, and the experiential, not merely through abstract propositions.

## The Breadth of Divine Mercy

Jonah 4:2, in which the prophet recites the divine attributes of mercy, is one of the most important verses in Catholic theology of grace. The verse echoes Exodus 34:6-7, the foundational self-revelation of God's character. Catholic interpretation reads Jonah's complaint as inadvertently testifying to the deepest truth about God: his mercy is not limited to the covenant people but extends to all creation, "even the animals" (4:11).

## The Open Question

The book's ending — an unanswered question — is cherished in Catholic spiritual reading. God asks, and waits. The question hangs in the silence, addressed to Jonah, to Israel, to the Church, and to every human heart that struggles to accept the radical generosity of divine mercy. Pope Francis has cited Jonah as an example of how God's mercy always exceeds human categories.
