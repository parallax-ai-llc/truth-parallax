## The Canticle from the Depths

Catholic interpretation of Jonah 2 treasures the prophet's prayer as one of the great canticles of the Old Testament. The liturgical tradition has incorporated its language into the Church's prayer life, and patristic commentators read it as the voice of Christ himself crying out from the depths of death. Jerome noted that the prayer's language of descent — "the deep surrounded me," "I went down to the roots of the mountains" — anticipates the Apostles' Creed's affirmation that Christ "descended into hell."

## Typology of Death and Resurrection

The three days and nights in the fish are read as a type of Christ's time in the tomb. But Catholic interpretation goes further: Jonah's prayer of thanksgiving from within the fish is read as Christ's confidence in the Father even in the midst of death. The prayer's movement from despair to praise mirrors the paschal mystery — death giving way to resurrection.

## The Vow and Sacrifice (2:9)

Jonah's promise to offer sacrifice with thanksgiving has eucharistic resonances in Catholic reading. The sacrifice of thanksgiving (*todah*) offered in gratitude for deliverance from death is seen as a precursor to the Eucharist — the Church's perpetual sacrifice of thanksgiving for Christ's victory over death.
