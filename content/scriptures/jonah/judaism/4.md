## The Thirteen Attributes and God's Nature

Jewish interpretation of Jonah 4 centers on verse 2, where Jonah recites the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy (*middot ha-rachamim*) from Exodus 34:6-7 — the same attributes recited repeatedly during Yom Kippur services. Jonah's recitation is remarkable because he cites God's mercy as a complaint: he knew God would forgive Nineveh, and he considers this unjust. The irony is profound — on Yom Kippur afternoon, the congregation reads about a prophet who is angry at the very divine mercy on which their own forgiveness depends.

## The Kikayon and the Lesson of Compassion

The *kikayon* (gourd or castor plant) episode is read in rabbinic literature as a *kal va-chomer* argument — reasoning from the lesser to the greater. If Jonah pitied a plant for which he did not labor, how much more should God pity a great city? The Midrash elaborates: God says, in effect, "You had compassion on a plant — shall I not have compassion on the work of my hands?"

## The Open Ending

The book's ending without Jonah's answer is celebrated in Jewish interpretation as a mark of literary and theological genius. The question is left for the reader — and specifically for the Yom Kippur worshipper — to answer. The implied answer is: yes, God should show mercy, and so should we.
