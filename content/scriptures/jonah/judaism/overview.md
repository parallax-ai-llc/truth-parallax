## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Jonah occupies a unique liturgical position: it is read in its entirety as the *haftarah* for the afternoon service of Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. This placement is profoundly deliberate. At the very moment when the Jewish people are engaged in the most intensive act of collective repentance, the text directs their attention to God's willingness to forgive even the people of Nineveh — Israel's enemies. The message is unmistakable: if God extends mercy to those who repent sincerely, how much more will he hear Israel's prayer on this holiest day.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The Talmud and midrashic literature explore Jonah's motivations with great subtlety. The Mekhilta de-Rabbi Ishmael suggests that Jonah fled not from cowardice but from a concern for Israel's honor — if Nineveh repented and Israel did not, Israel would stand condemned. The Zohar reads Jonah's descent into the sea as the soul's descent into the body and its eventual return to God.

## Theological Emphases

- **Teshuvah**: The book's central teaching is the power and efficacy of genuine repentance
- **Universal Divine Mercy**: God's compassion extends to all nations, not only to Israel
- **Prophetic Responsibility**: Jonah's flight raises questions about the prophet's obligation and the limits of human will before divine command
- **The Thirteen Attributes**: God's self-description in Jonah 4:2 echoes the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy (Exodus 34:6-7), the liturgical heart of Yom Kippur
