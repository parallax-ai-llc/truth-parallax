## Nineveh's Teshuvah

Jewish interpretation of Jonah 3 focuses on the nature and quality of Nineveh's repentance (*teshuvah*). The Talmud (Ta'anit 16a) discusses whether Nineveh's repentance was genuine or merely superficial — did they truly change their ways, or did they simply perform the outward gestures of fasting and sackcloth? The Mishnah (Ta'anit 2:1) uses Nineveh's response as a model for communal fast days, and the liturgy of Yom Kippur draws on the chapter's assurance that God responds to sincere turning.

## The King's Decree

The king of Nineveh's decree is notable for its specificity: "Let everyone turn from his evil way and from the violence that is in his hands" (3:8). Jewish commentators emphasize that true *teshuvah* is not merely emotional or ritual but behavioral — it requires actual change in conduct, particularly in matters of justice between persons. Rashi and the Midrash stress that Nineveh's repentance included restitution of stolen goods.

## "Who Knows?" (3:9)

The king's question — "Who knows? God may turn and relent" — is read as expressing the proper humility of repentance. One does not repent in order to manipulate God's response; one repents because it is right, and leaves the outcome to God's mercy.
