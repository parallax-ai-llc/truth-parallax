## The Great Repentance

Orthodox interpretation of Jonah 3 sees Nineveh's repentance as one of the most powerful examples of *metanoia* — the total transformation of heart, mind, and life — in all of Scripture. The entire city participates: king and commoner, human and animal. This corporate dimension of repentance is significant for Orthodox theology, which understands salvation not merely as individual but as communal, involving the transformation of entire communities and, ultimately, all of creation.

## Fasting and Asceticism

The Orthodox tradition draws on Nineveh's fasting as a biblical precedent for the Church's ascetical disciplines. The Lenten fast, the fast before communion, and the Wednesday-Friday fasts all find their theological grounding in texts like Jonah 3, which demonstrate that physical self-denial can be an authentic expression of spiritual turning. Some Eastern churches observe a "Fast of Nineveh" specifically in commemoration of this passage.

## God's Compassion

"God relented" (3:10) — Orthodox theology reads God's response not as a change in the divine nature but as the revelation of God's deepest character: compassion. God's "relenting" is the natural response of infinite love meeting genuine repentance.
