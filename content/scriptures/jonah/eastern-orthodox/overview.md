## Overview

In the Eastern Orthodox tradition, the Book of Jonah holds a privileged place in the liturgy of Holy Saturday. Jonah's three days in the belly of the great fish is the primary Old Testament type of Christ's descent into Hades between his crucifixion and resurrection. The Orthodox reading emphasizes not merely the absence of Christ in the tomb but his active conquest of death from within — as Jonah was alive inside the fish, so Christ was alive and at work in the realm of the dead, liberating the righteous who had been held captive.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The Greek Fathers — Basil, Gregory of Nazianzus, and John Chrysostom — all developed the Jonah-Christ typology with great care. Orthodox liturgical hymnography draws extensively on Jonah: the Holy Saturday canon echoes his prayer from the belly of the fish. Beyond typology, Orthodox interpreters also emphasize Nineveh's repentance as a model for the Church's call to *metanoia* — the complete transformation of heart and mind.

## Theological Emphases

- **Descent into Hades**: Jonah's entombment in the fish prefigures Christ's harrowing of Hell
- **Repentance as Metanoia**: Nineveh's turning is a model of the radical change God seeks in every human heart
- **Divine Compassion**: God's concern for Nineveh reveals the breadth of his love for all creation
- **Theosis**: The narrative moves from death to life, mirroring the Orthodox understanding of salvation as transformation
