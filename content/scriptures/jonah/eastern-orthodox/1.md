## The Prophet's Flight and God's Loving Pursuit

Orthodox interpretation of Jonah 1 emphasizes that God's pursuit of his reluctant prophet is an act of love, not mere coercion. The storm is not punishment but correction — God's refusal to let Jonah destroy himself through disobedience. The Greek Fathers noted that the sea, in biblical symbolism, represents chaos and death, and Jonah's voluntary descent into its depths foreshadows Christ's voluntary descent into death for the salvation of the world.

## The Sailors' Response

Chrysostom observed that the pagan sailors demonstrate a natural piety that puts the prophet to shame. Their fear of the LORD and their offering of sacrifices (1:16) show that the seeds of true worship exist even among those who have not received the covenant. This anticipates the Orthodox understanding that God's grace works beyond the visible boundaries of the Church.

## Descent into the Deep

Jonah's being cast into the sea is read liturgically as a descent into the abyss — a voluntary entry into the realm of death that prefigures Holy Saturday. The chapter moves from Jonah's attempt to flee God's presence to his discovery that there is no place beyond God's reach, a theme central to Orthodox spirituality.
