## The Revelation of God's Heart

Orthodox interpretation of Jonah 4 reads the chapter as the ultimate revelation of God's compassionate heart. Jonah's anger contrasts with God's patience — the prophet wants justice, but God offers mercy. The Greek Fathers saw in this contrast a lesson about *apatheia* — not the absence of feeling but the transformation of the passions into divine love. Jonah's anger is a disordered passion; God's compassion is the perfection of love.

## The Plant and the Icon of Creation

The plant (*kikayon*) that God appoints to shade Jonah and then destroys becomes, in Orthodox reading, an icon of the created order's dependence on God. Everything exists by God's will and for God's purposes. Jonah's attachment to the plant mirrors the human tendency to love creation for the comfort it provides rather than as a gift that points to the Creator.

## The Cosmic Scope of Mercy

God's final question — should he not care about Nineveh, with its 120,000 people and its many animals? — reveals a divine mercy that encompasses not only human beings but all of creation. This resonates with the Orthodox understanding of salvation as cosmic in scope: God's redemptive purpose extends to the entire created order, not merely to the human soul.
