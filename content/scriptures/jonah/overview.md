## Overview

The Book of Jonah stands apart from the other prophetic books by being primarily a narrative about the prophet rather than a collection of oracles. God commands Jonah to preach repentance to Nineveh, the capital of the Assyrian Empire and Israel's dreaded enemy. Instead of obeying, Jonah flees in the opposite direction by ship, is swallowed by a great fish, and only after three days in its belly relents and goes to Nineveh. When the city repents and God shows mercy, Jonah is furious — revealing that his flight was motivated not by fear but by the suspicion that God would indeed be merciful to Israel's enemies.

## Structure

1. **Chapter 1**: Jonah's flight and the storm at sea
2. **Chapter 2**: Jonah's prayer from inside the great fish
3. **Chapter 3**: Jonah's preaching and Nineveh's repentance
4. **Chapter 4**: Jonah's anger and God's lesson through the plant

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: Read as the *haftarah* for Yom Kippur afternoon, emphasizing God's universal mercy and the power of repentance
- **Christianity**: Jesus cites Jonah as a sign of his own death and resurrection (Matthew 12:39-41); the story prefigures the gospel going to the Gentiles
- **Islam**: Yunus (Jonah) is a named prophet in the Quran; Surah 10 bears his name, and his story illustrates repentance and divine compassion
- **Gnosticism**: The descent into the fish is read as a symbol of the soul's entrapment in matter and its eventual liberation

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Reluctant Prophecy | Jonah resists God's commission, raising questions about prophetic freedom and obedience |
| Universal Mercy | God's compassion extends beyond Israel to encompass even her enemies |
| Repentance | Nineveh's wholehearted turning illustrates the transformative power of genuine repentance |
| Divine Sovereignty | God controls sea, storm, fish, plant, and worm — the entire created order serves his purposes |
| The Question of Justice | Jonah's anger exposes the tension between human desire for justice and God's preference for mercy |
