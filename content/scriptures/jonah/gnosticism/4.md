## The Illusion of Material Comfort

Gnostic interpretation of Jonah 4 reads the plant episode as an allegory of attachment to material comfort. The *kikayon* that springs up and provides shade represents the transient pleasures of the material world — real enough to produce attachment, but destined to wither because nothing in the Demiurge's creation is permanent. Jonah's grief at the plant's destruction mirrors the suffering of every soul that has invested its hope in material things.

## The Question of the Demiurge

Some Gnostic readers interpret God's final question with suspicion: is this the true God speaking, or the Demiurge defending his creation? The question "Should I not be concerned about Nineveh?" could be read as the Demiurge's self-justification — claiming compassion for a world that he created and wishes to preserve. The Gnostic reader detects a deeper irony: the Demiurge's "mercy" keeps souls trapped in the material world rather than liberating them.

## The Unanswered Question

For Gnostic readers, the book's open ending has a different resonance. The absence of an answer suggests that the question itself is the wrong question. The real question is not whether God should care about Nineveh but whether the soul will awaken to its true nature and seek liberation from the material cosmos altogether. Jonah's silence may represent the beginning of a deeper understanding that transcends both anger and acceptance.
