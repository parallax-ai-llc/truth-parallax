## Jonah's Anger and God's Question

Protestant interpretation of Jonah 4 reads the chapter as the theological climax of the entire book. Jonah's anger at God's mercy toward Nineveh reveals the root of his original flight: "I knew that you are a gracious God and merciful, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love, and relenting from disaster" (4:2). Jonah did not fear that his prophecy would fail — he feared that it would succeed in provoking repentance and mercy. Calvin observed that Jonah here accuses God of being too good — a charge that reveals the sinfulness of the human heart more than any act of disobedience.

## The Object Lesson of the Plant

God's appointment of the plant, the worm, and the scorching wind is a masterpiece of divine pedagogy. God teaches through experience what Jonah refuses to learn through instruction. If Jonah can grieve over a plant he did not create or cultivate, how much more should God care about Nineveh — a city of 120,000 people? The argument moves from the lesser to the greater, exposing the absurdity of Jonah's position.

## The Unanswered Question

The book ends with God's question, and Jonah never answers. Protestant interpreters see this open ending as deliberately inviting the reader to answer in Jonah's place. The question is addressed to every reader who resists God's mercy toward those they consider undeserving. The book refuses to resolve the tension, leaving the challenge of grace permanently open.
