## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Jonah has consistently read the book on two levels: as a historical narrative of prophetic disobedience and divine mercy, and as a typological prefiguration of the death and resurrection of Christ. Jesus himself drew the parallel — "as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish, so will the Son of Man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth" (Matthew 12:40). This dominical endorsement has made the historicity of Jonah a significant point of discussion in Protestant theology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Calvin emphasized God's sovereign pursuit of his reluctant servant and the scandal of divine mercy extended to Israel's enemies. Luther saw in Jonah's descent into the fish a picture of the believer's descent into despair and God's deliverance. Modern evangelical scholars defend the historical reading while also appreciating the book's literary artistry and theological depth.

## Theological Emphases

- **Typology of Christ**: Jonah's three days in the fish prefigure Christ's burial and resurrection
- **Universal Grace**: God's mercy extends to pagan Nineveh, anticipating the Gentile mission
- **Prophetic Obedience**: Jonah's flight serves as a warning against resisting God's call
- **The Sovereignty of God**: Every element of the narrative — storm, fish, plant, worm — is under God's direct control
