## Jonah's Preaching and Nineveh's Repentance

Protestant interpretation of Jonah 3 highlights the astonishing success of Jonah's preaching: the entire city of Nineveh, from king to cattle, repents in sackcloth and ashes. This is the most dramatic mass conversion in the Old Testament, and Protestant commentators note the irony — Jonah, the reluctant preacher, achieves results that no willing prophet ever matched. The chapter demonstrates that the power of God's word does not depend on the enthusiasm of its messenger.

## The Sovereignty of Grace

Calvin emphasized that Nineveh's repentance was not the result of Jonah's eloquence — his message was only five words in Hebrew — but of God's sovereign grace working through even the most reluctant instrument. The chapter illustrates the Reformed principle that God's word accomplishes what God intends, regardless of human limitations.

## God's Relenting (3:10)

"God relented of the disaster that he had said he would do to them." Protestant interpreters have carefully discussed how God can "relent" without being changeable. The answer lies in the distinction between God's conditional warnings and his unchanging character: God always responds to genuine repentance with mercy. This is not a change in God but a change in the relationship brought about by Nineveh's turning.
