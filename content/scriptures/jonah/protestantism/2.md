## Jonah's Prayer from the Depths

Protestant interpretation of Jonah 2 focuses on the prophet's prayer as a model of faith in extremity. From inside the great fish — a place of utter helplessness — Jonah cries out to God and receives assurance of deliverance. Calvin noted that this prayer, composed largely of psalm quotations, demonstrates that even a disobedient prophet retains the language of faith. The prayer is not a petition for rescue but a thanksgiving psalm — Jonah speaks as though deliverance has already occurred.

## "Salvation Belongs to the LORD" (2:9)

This verse is the theological climax of the prayer and, for many Protestant interpreters, the thematic center of the entire book. Salvation is God's prerogative — not Jonah's to grant or withhold from Nineveh. Luther read this verse as a confession that all deliverance, physical and spiritual, comes from God alone.

## The Great Fish as Divine Instrument

Protestant interpreters generally read the great fish not as punishment but as preservation — God's means of saving Jonah from drowning and returning him to his mission. The fish is an instrument of grace, not judgment, demonstrating that God's sovereign purposes include even the extraordinary and the miraculous.
