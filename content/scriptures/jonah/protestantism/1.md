## Jonah's Disobedience and God's Pursuit

Protestant interpretation of Jonah 1 emphasizes the futility of fleeing from God's sovereign will. When God commands Jonah to go to Nineveh, Jonah boards a ship for Tarshish — the opposite direction. Calvin noted that Jonah's flight was not mere cowardice but a deliberate rejection of his prophetic commission, motivated by his unwillingness to see God show mercy to Israel's enemies.

## The Storm and the Sailors

The storm at sea demonstrates God's absolute control over creation. The pagan sailors' fear and their eventual worship of the LORD (1:16) foreshadow the very thing Jonah dreads: Gentiles turning to the God of Israel. Protestant commentators observe the irony that Jonah sleeps in the hold while pagans pray — the prophet is less responsive to God than the heathen.

## Theological Significance

The casting of lots that identifies Jonah illustrates God's sovereignty over even seemingly random events. Jonah's instruction to throw him overboard is read by some as the first glimmer of self-sacrifice, though others see it as continued resistance — preferring death to obedience. The chapter establishes the book's central tension: God's universal mercy versus human resistance to that mercy.
