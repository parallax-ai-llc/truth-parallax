## Overview

Canto 3, "The Horse Race," represents the pivotal moment of Gesar's public emergence — his transformation from despised outcast to sovereign king through a contest that functions as a *lung bstan* (prophetic fulfillment). Tibetan Buddhist commentators read the horse race as an enactment of the third bodhisattva bhumi, "Luminous" (*'od byed pa*), in which the practitioner's accumulated merit and wisdom blaze forth irresistibly. Tsongkhapa's commentary on the bhumis in the *Lam rim chen mo* describes this stage as the moment when the bodhisattva's inner realization can no longer be concealed — precisely what occurs when the ragged Joru mounts Kyang Go Karkar and surpasses all rivals.

The horse race as a vehicle for determining kingship carries profound Vajrayana implications. In the tantric mandala, the central sovereign position is not claimed through political maneuvering but through demonstrated realization. The race is thus a *torma*-like offering of competitive energy that, when consecrated by the bodhisattva's intention, becomes an act of dharmic installation. Gampopa's *Jewel Ornament of Liberation* describes how the merit accumulated across lifetimes manifests at the decisive moment as irresistible spiritual authority — what the Gesar tradition calls *dbang thang* (power-field).

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Horse Race | The race as spiritual contest (*rtsod pa*) — a tantric trial in which obstacles generated by adversaries function as the wrathful deities of the mandala, testing and confirming the practitioner's realization |
| Kingship | Dharmic sovereignty (*chos kyi rgyal po*) established not through lineage or force but through the spontaneous manifestation of accumulated merit, paralleling the Buddhist concept of the chakravartin |
| Kyang Go Karkar | The divine horse as *lung rta* (wind-horse) made manifest — the union of vital energy and awakened mind that carries the practitioner beyond the dualistic obstacles of hope and fear |
| Contest | Destiny as the fruition of dependent origination: the outcome is neither predetermined nor random but the natural consequence of causes and conditions ripened across countless lifetimes |

## Key Passages

### The Preparation and Mounting of Kyang Go Karkar

The scenes in which the divine horse appears and Gesar prepares for the race carry the structure of a Vajrayana *sadhana*: the practitioner (Gesar) unites with the yidam-vehicle (the horse), generating the visualization that will carry awareness through the intermediate obstacles. In the Nyingma Dzogchen tradition, the horse symbolizes *rlung* (subtle wind or prana) — the vital energy that, when mounted by awareness, traverses the channels (*rtsa*) and reaches the crown. Longchenpa's *Treasury of the Dharmadhatu* describes this as the moment when *rigpa* (pure awareness) rides the wind of accumulated karma toward its destined expression.

### The Race Itself

The race unfolds as a *thodgal* vision sequence: obstacles arise, competitors deploy cunning and sorcery, yet Gesar moves through each challenge with the effortlessness that characterizes the fourth bhumi, "Radiant" (*'od 'phro ba*). The Madhyamaka reading is essential here — Nagarjuna's *Vigrahavyavartani* teaches that the one who has no thesis cannot be defeated in debate. Similarly, Gesar, who races without the ego-investment of his competitors, cannot be outrun. His victory demonstrates *sunyata* in action: the empty nature of the self produces not paralysis but supreme skillful activity.

### The Enthronement

The post-race enthronement transforms the narrative from adventure into coronation *abhisheka* — a tantric empowerment in which sovereignty is conferred through ritual. The Kagyu tradition, following Tilopa and Naropa, understands *abhisheka* as the moment when the guru's realization passes to the student as a living transmission. Here, the cosmic order itself serves as guru, conferring authority upon the one whose realization has been publicly demonstrated. Tsongkhapa would note that this enthronement fulfills the *pratimoksha* dimension of the bodhisattva path: Gesar accepts the vow of universal responsibility.

## Commentary Tradition

Mipham Rinpoche's treatment of the horse race canto places it within the framework of the *Five Certainties* (*nges pa lnga*) of the Sambhogakaya: certain teacher, certain teaching, certain retinue, certain place, certain time. The race is the *certain time* — the moment when all conditions converge for the manifestation of awakened sovereignty. The Sakya tradition, drawing on the *Lamdre* (Path and Fruit) teachings, reads the race as an illustration of the inseparability of path and fruition: Gesar does not race *in order to* become king but rather his kingship expresses itself *as* the race.

The bard tradition (*sgrung*) treats Canto 3 as the emotional and spiritual climax of the epic's first movement. Performers report that during recitation of the race sequence, meditative states (*ting nge 'dzin*) often arise spontaneously, suggesting the narrative itself functions as a practice-text whose power operates below the level of conceptual understanding.
