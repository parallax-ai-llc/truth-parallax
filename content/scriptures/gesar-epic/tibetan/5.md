## Overview

Canto 5, "The War with Hor," is the most expansive and emotionally devastating section of the Gesar epic, centering on the abduction of Drugmo by the three kings of Hor and Gesar's prolonged campaign to recover her. Tibetan Buddhist commentators identify this as the epic's primary teaching on attachment (*zhen pa*) and the bodhisattva's relationship to loss. Tsongkhapa's *Lam rim chen mo* distinguishes between ordinary attachment — which binds beings to samsara — and the bodhisattva's compassionate engagement with specific beings, which operates through the same emotional channels but is directed by prajna. Gesar's anguish at Drugmo's abduction is neither transcended nor repressed; it is *transformed* into the fuel of liberating activity.

The Hor kingdom functions in the Tibetan Buddhist reading as a *mandala of the three poisons*: the three Hor kings embody desire (*'dod chags*), aggression (*zhe sdang*), and ignorance (*gti mug*) in their most organized and militarized forms. Unlike Lutsen's solitary demonic power, the Hor threat is systemic — a society structured around afflictive emotions. This mirrors the Yogacara analysis of the *alaya-vijnana* (storehouse consciousness), where individual kleshas organize into self-reinforcing patterns that constitute entire worlds of suffering.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Hor Kingdom | The Hor realm as a samsaric mandala of organized affliction — the three kings mapping onto the three root poisons as analyzed in Vasubandhu's *Abhidharmakosa* and their Tibetan commentarial elaborations |
| Invasion | Invasion as the aggressive outward movement of the three poisons — the way afflictive emotions do not remain contained but actively colonize and corrupt the fields of others, a teaching central to the dependent origination of suffering |
| Drugmo Abducted | The separation of wisdom (*prajna*) from skillful means (*upaya*) — the dakini principle torn from the warrior principle, creating an incompleteness that the tantric path must rectify through reunion |
| Revenge | The transformation of reactive anger into *drag shul* (wrathful mode) — not revenge in the ordinary sense but the mobilization of awakened energy against the structures of suffering, following Chandrakirti's defense of the bodhisattva's use of force |

## Key Passages

### The Abduction of Drugmo

The passages describing the Hor kings' capture of Drugmo constitute one of the most theologically dense moments in the epic. From the Dzogchen perspective articulated by Longchenpa, the separation of Gesar from Drugmo represents the fundamental fracture of *kadag* (primordial purity) and *lhun grub* (spontaneous presence) — a cosmic wound that drives the entire drama of samsara. The Yogacara reading would identify the abduction as the moment when *prajna-paramita* (perfection of wisdom) becomes obscured by the active ignorance of the Hor consciousness, producing the bifurcated experience of self and other that characterizes unenlightened existence.

### Gesar's Grief and Resolve

The depiction of Gesar's grief upon learning of Drugmo's capture is remarkable for its emotional rawness, which Tibetan commentators refuse to allegorize away. Gampopa's *Jewel Ornament of Liberation* teaches that genuine compassion (*snying rje*) arises not from philosophical reflection but from the direct experience of suffering's reality. Gesar's tears are the tears of a being who has fully realized emptiness and therefore feels the pain of separation with an intensity impossible for an ordinary, ego-defended consciousness. The Kagyu tradition calls this *snying rje chen po* (great compassion) — the compassion that arises precisely because there is no self to buffer the impact of others' suffering.

### The Campaign Against the Three Kings

The extended military campaign — with its stratagems, reversals, and moments of near-defeat — reads as an experiential map of the later bodhisattva bhumis. Each Hor king requires a distinct approach, mirroring the Madhyamaka teaching that skillful means must adapt to the specific character of each affliction. Nagarjuna's *Bodhisambhara* teaches that the bodhisattva's methods are as numerous as the types of suffering; Gesar's varied tactics against the Hor embody this principle in narrative form.

## Commentary Tradition

Mipham Rinpoche's commentary tradition regards the Hor War canto as the epic's *yang dag* (authentic) teaching on the relationship between emptiness and compassion. The fact that Gesar's most terrible and sustained violence arises from his deepest love illustrates the Nyingma principle that the wrathful deities are not separate from the peaceful ones but are their intensification under conditions of urgency. Padmasambhava's subjugation of the demons of Tibet is the historical prototype; Gesar's campaign against Hor is its mythic amplification.

The Gelug tradition, represented by commentators in the Tsongkhapa lineage, emphasizes the doctrinal precision of Gesar's motivation. At no point does the narrative suggest Gesar acts from personal anger; his campaign is consistently framed as the restoration of dharmic order. This aligns with Tsongkhapa's strict distinction between *nyon mongs pa'i las* (action motivated by affliction) and *dge ba'i las* (virtuous action) — the same external behavior carries entirely different karmic consequences depending on the motivation that drives it.
