## Overview

The Sutrakritanga (Sūtrakṛtāṅga) is the second of the twelve Angas, the principal canonical texts of Shvetambara Jainism. It is one of the oldest surviving Jain scriptures, composed in Ardhamagadhi Prakrit, and addresses the refutation of heterodox philosophical views while affirming core Jain doctrines of non-violence, asceticism, and the path to liberation.

## Structure

The text is divided into two books (shruta-skandhas) comprising 16 chapters in total:

1. **Chapter 1 — The Doctrine**: Refutation of hedonism and materialism
2. **Chapter 2 — The Destruction of Karma**: Austerities and self-discipline
3. **Chapter 3 — The Knowledge of Women**: Warnings against attachment
4. **Chapter 4 — Righteousness**: The nature of dharma
5. **Chapter 5 — The Confession of Sins**: Repentance and purification
6. **Chapter 6 — Heretical Doctrines (I)**: Critique of rival schools
7. **Chapter 7 — Heretical Doctrines (II)**: Further critiques
8. **Chapter 8 — The Law**: The path of Mahavira
9. **Chapter 9 — The Praise of Mahavira**: Devotion to the Tirthankara
10. **Chapter 10 — The Great Duty**: Non-violence as supreme dharma
11. **Chapter 11 — The Path of the Wise**: Conduct for ascetics
12. **Chapter 12 — The Creed**: Jain doctrinal affirmations
13. **Chapter 13 — The Heretic**: Identifying false teachers
14. **Chapter 14 — The True Monk**: Qualities of genuine renunciation
15. **Chapter 15 — The Clasp of Discipline**: Monastic regulations
16. **Chapter 16 — Song of Renunciation**: Final exhortation to liberation

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Digambara**: Recognize the text's antiquity while relying on their own canonical recension
- **Shvetambara**: Consider it the second Anga, essential for understanding Jain polemical philosophy
- **Academic**: A primary source for understanding early Indian philosophical debates
- **Comparative**: Rich material for comparing approaches to heterodoxy across traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Refutation of Heresy | Systematic critique of non-Jain philosophical positions |
| Ahimsa | Non-violence as the foundation of all ethical conduct |
| Asceticism | Rigorous self-discipline as the means to liberation |
| Karma | Actions and their consequences bind the soul |
| Right Knowledge | Distinguishing true doctrine from false teaching |
