## The Stone That Becomes a Mountain

Orthodox interpretation of Daniel 2 reads Nebuchadnezzar's dream of the great statue as a prophetic vision of the divine economy — God's providential plan unfolding through successive empires until the coming of Christ. The stone "cut without hands" that shatters the statue and becomes a mountain filling the earth is understood as Christ and his Church — the Kingdom of God that grows organically from a humble beginning to cosmic dimensions.

The Fathers, particularly St. Hippolytus, provided detailed identifications of the four kingdoms and emphasized that the stone's divine origin ("without hands") points to the Virgin Birth — Christ entering the world not through human generation but by the power of the Holy Spirit.

Orthodox liturgical theology sees in the mountain that fills the earth an image of the Church, whose worship sanctifies all creation. The Eucharistic transformation of bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ is the ongoing reality of the stone becoming a mountain — the material world being taken up into the divine.

## Theological Themes

- **The Divine Economy**: History unfolds according to God's providential plan
- **The Virgin Birth**: The stone "cut without hands" prefigures Christ's supernatural conception
- **The Church as Cosmic Mountain**: The Kingdom of God grows to fill and sanctify all creation
- **Eucharistic Transformation**: The material world is taken up into the divine through the sacraments
