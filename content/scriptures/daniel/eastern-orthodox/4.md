## The Humbling of the Proud and the Mercy of God

Orthodox interpretation of Daniel 4 reads Nebuchadnezzar's madness and restoration as a parable of the fall and recovery of the human soul. The great tree that is cut down represents humanity in its prideful state — impressive but disconnected from its true Source. The king's descent into bestial madness is the spiritual condition of the soul that has turned away from God, losing its rational nature and divine likeness.

The Fathers noted that Nebuchadnezzar's restoration comes when "he lifted his eyes to heaven" (4:34) — a physical gesture that symbolizes the soul's return to contemplation of God. In Orthodox theology, this upward gaze is the beginning of *metanoia* (repentance): the soul recognizes its true orientation toward the divine and begins to recover the image of God that sin has obscured.

The chapter demonstrates that God's mercy extends even to pagan kings. Orthodox universalist tendencies find here a hint that God's redemptive purpose encompasses all humanity, not only the chosen people.

## Theological Themes

- **The Fall of the Soul**: Pride leads to the loss of rational nature and divine likeness
- **Metanoia**: Lifting one's eyes to heaven is the beginning of repentance and restoration
- **Universal Mercy**: God's redemptive reach extends to all people, including pagan rulers
- **The Recovery of the Divine Image**: Repentance restores the *eikon* of God in humanity
