## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Daniel is shaped by the patristic consensus, liturgical tradition, and the theology of the divine economy (*oikonomia*). The court tales are read as models of theosis — human beings transfigured by their faithfulness to God, even in the furnace of affliction. The apocalyptic visions reveal the movement of history toward its culmination in Christ, the true Son of Man. Orthodox iconography frequently depicts the Ancient of Days (7:9) and the three youths in the fiery furnace, testifying to the book's deep integration into the life of the Church.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Typology

The Greek and Syriac Fathers read Daniel's narratives as types of Christ and the Church. The three youths in the fiery furnace are types of the Resurrection — passing through death unharmed. Daniel in the lions' den prefigures Christ's descent into Hades and his triumph over the powers of death.

### Liturgical Integration

The Song of the Three Young Men is central to Orthodox liturgical life, appearing in the Matins canon. The hymn "Blessed are you, O Lord, God of our fathers" (Daniel 3:52ff) is chanted regularly, embedding Daniel's theology of praise-in-suffering into the daily prayer of the Church.

### The Ancient of Days

Orthodox iconography uniquely depicts the "Ancient of Days" (7:9) as an image of God the Father or of Christ in his eternal divine nature. This iconographic tradition is rooted in patristic interpretation of Daniel's vision.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: Daniel and his companions are transfigured by grace, becoming icons of deified humanity
- **The Divine Economy**: History unfolds according to God's providential plan, revealed in Daniel's visions
- **The Paschal Mystery**: The furnace and the lions' den are types of death and resurrection
- **Praise in Suffering**: The Song of the Three Young Men is the supreme biblical model of doxology in affliction

## Notable Orthodox Commentators

- **St. Hippolytus of Rome**: Wrote one of the earliest Christian commentaries on Daniel
- **Theodoret of Cyrrhus**: Provided patristic exegesis of Daniel's visions
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Referenced Daniel in homilies on courage and divine providence
- **St. Andrew of Caesarea**: Drew on Daniel in his commentary on Revelation
