## Daniel's Prayer and the Seventy Weeks

Jewish interpretation of Daniel 9 treasures the chapter for its magnificent penitential prayer (9:4-19) and engages carefully with the prophecy of the seventy weeks (9:24-27). Daniel's prayer is a model of *vidui* (confession) — not for personal sins but for the sins of the entire community. Its structure — praising God, confessing communal sin, and appealing to divine mercy — has influenced the liturgy of Yom Kippur.

The seventy weeks have been the subject of extensive rabbinic calculation and debate. Rashi, following the Talmud (Nazir 32b), calculated the weeks as referring to the period from the destruction of the First Temple to the destruction of the Second Temple. Other interpreters offered different calculations, but the rabbis generally cautioned against excessive focus on apocalyptic timelines, preferring to emphasize the practical message: repent and return to God.

The phrase "an anointed one shall be cut off" (9:26) is interpreted variously in Jewish tradition — some identifying the figure with the High Priest Onias III (murdered in 171 BCE), others with King Agrippa (the last Jewish king). The subsequent destruction of "the city and the sanctuary" is understood as the Roman destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE.

## Theological Themes

- **Communal Confession**: Daniel models prayer for the sins of the whole people
- **The Seventy Weeks**: Rabbinic debate over the timeline — and warnings against messianic calculation
- **Teshuvah**: The prayer's central message is the call to repentance
- **The Destruction of the Second Temple**: The prophecy finds its fulfillment in 70 CE
