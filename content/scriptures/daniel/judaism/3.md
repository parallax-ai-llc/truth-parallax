## Kiddush Hashem in the Fiery Furnace

Jewish interpretation of Daniel 3 celebrates the three youths — Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah (their Hebrew names) — as supreme examples of *kiddush hashem*: the sanctification of God's name through willingness to die rather than commit idolatry. The refusal to bow to Nebuchadnezzar's golden image is the paradigmatic act of Jewish martyrdom.

The Talmud (Berakhot 57b) and midrashic literature celebrate the three youths extensively. The Midrash asks why Daniel himself was not present at the furnace scene, offering various explanations — the most common being that Daniel had been sent on a royal errand and was therefore absent.

The declaration "But if not" (3:18) — affirming faith even without guarantee of deliverance — has been invoked by Jewish martyrs throughout history. During the Crusades, the Inquisition, and the Shoah, Jews facing death for their faith drew strength from the example of the three who entered the fire rather than bow to a false god.

## Theological Themes

- **Kiddush Hashem**: The supreme sanctification of God's name through willingness to die
- **The Prohibition of Idolatry**: Bowing to the image violates the most fundamental of the 613 commandments
- **Faith Without Guarantee**: "But if not" — trust in God without demanding rescue
- **Martyrological Tradition**: The three youths became models for Jewish martyrs across the centuries
