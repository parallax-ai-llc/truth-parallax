## The Little Horn and the Desecration of the Sanctuary

Jewish interpretation of Daniel 8 reads the vision of the ram and the goat in light of the historical persecutions of Antiochus IV Epiphanes — the Seleucid king who desecrated the Temple, prohibited Torah observance, and provoked the Maccabean revolt. The chapter is closely connected to the story of Hanukkah, which celebrates the rededication of the Temple after Antiochus' desecration.

The "little horn" that grows exceedingly great (8:9) is unanimously identified in Jewish tradition with Antiochus Epiphanes. His assault on the Temple — setting up an idol, prohibiting sacrifices, and persecuting the faithful — is the defining act of sacrilege in Second Temple history.

The question "How long?" (8:13) expresses the perennial Jewish question in the face of persecution: How long must the righteous suffer? The answer — "2,300 evenings and mornings" — has been variously calculated, but its theological point is clear: God sets limits on persecution, and the sanctuary will be restored.

## Theological Themes

- **Antiochus and Hanukkah**: The desecration of the Temple and its rededication
- **The Limits of Persecution**: God sets a boundary on the duration of suffering
- **Prophetic Vision**: Daniel sees the future suffering of his people and is overwhelmed by it
- **The Restoration of the Sanctuary**: What is desecrated will be rededicated
