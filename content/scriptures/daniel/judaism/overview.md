## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Daniel is classified among the Ketuvim (Writings) rather than the Nevi'im (Prophets), reflecting the rabbinic view that Daniel, while divinely inspired, did not hold the prophetic office in the technical sense. The book has been enormously influential in Jewish thought nonetheless: its court tales model *kiddush hashem* (sanctification of God's name) under persecution, its visions gave rise to Jewish apocalypticism, and its reference to resurrection (12:2) became foundational for rabbinic eschatology. The rabbis both drew upon and placed limits on Daniel's apocalyptic calculations, wary of messianic speculation.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Midrashic and Talmudic

The Talmud and midrashic literature extensively reference Daniel. The stories of the fiery furnace and the lions' den are celebrated as models of faithfulness. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 97b-98a) discusses Daniel's timeline for the end of days but also warns against calculating the time of the Messiah's arrival.

### Medieval Philosophical

Maimonides and other medieval philosophers engaged carefully with Daniel's visions. Maimonides classified Daniel's experiences as a lower grade of prophecy — a "vision of the night" — while affirming the book's canonical authority.

### Kabbalistic

Kabbalistic readers found in Daniel's imagery — the four kingdoms, the Ancient of Days, the angelic hierarchies — a map of the sefirot and the cosmic struggle between the forces of holiness and the *sitra achra* (the "other side").

## Theological Emphases

- **Kiddush Hashem**: Daniel and his companions model the willingness to die rather than violate God's commandments
- **Exile Theology**: The book addresses the fundamental question of how to serve God faithfully in a foreign land
- **Resurrection**: Daniel 12:2 provides a key proof-text for the rabbinic doctrine of *tehiyyat ha-metim* (resurrection of the dead)
- **Messianic Restraint**: The rabbis valued Daniel's visions but cautioned against premature messianic calculation

## Notable Jewish Commentators

- **Rashi**: Provided concise commentary combining peshat and midrash
- **Ibn Ezra**: Offered grammatical and contextual analysis
- **Maimonides**: Discussed Daniel's prophetic status in the *Guide for the Perplexed*
- **Abarbanel**: Wrote extensively on Daniel's visions in relation to Jewish eschatology
