## Faithfulness in Exile and the Sanctification of God's Name

Jewish interpretation of Daniel 1 reads the chapter as a foundational narrative of *kiddush hashem* — the sanctification of God's name in a hostile environment. Daniel's refusal to eat the king's food is understood halakhically: the food was likely not kosher, may have been offered to idols, and its acceptance would signal assimilation into Babylonian culture. Daniel's resolve is not merely personal preference but a principled commitment to Torah observance.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 93b) praises Daniel and his companions for their faithfulness, noting that God rewarded them with exceptional wisdom and understanding. Rashi comments that "God gave them" wisdom (1:17) indicates that their intellectual achievements were divine gifts, not Babylonian acquisitions — a distinction that preserves the primacy of Torah wisdom over pagan learning.

The chapter establishes the central question of the book: How does a Jew live faithfully under foreign rule? This question has resonated throughout Jewish history — in Hellenistic Alexandria, medieval Christendom, the Ottoman Empire, and modern diaspora communities. Daniel's answer: maintain Torah observance, serve the state honorably, but draw firm lines where divine law is at stake.

## Theological Themes

- **Kiddush Hashem**: Sanctifying God's name through principled faithfulness
- **Kashrut**: Dietary law as a boundary marker of Jewish identity in exile
- **Torah Wisdom**: True wisdom is a divine gift that surpasses pagan learning
- **Diaspora Ethics**: The model for Jewish life under foreign sovereignty
