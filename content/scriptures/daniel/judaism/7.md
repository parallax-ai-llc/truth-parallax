## The Four Beasts and the Messianic Kingdom

Jewish interpretation of Daniel 7 reads the vision of the four beasts and the "one like a son of man" as a revelation of Israel's suffering under successive empires and the ultimate establishment of God's kingdom through the Messiah. The four beasts are identified with the same four empires as the statue in chapter 2: Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome.

The "one like a son of man" (*kebar enash*) has been interpreted in several ways in Jewish tradition. Some rabbis identified the figure with the Messiah (Talmud, Sanhedrin 98a), while others understood it as a symbol for the people of Israel collectively. The Talmud records a debate: Rabbi Akiva saw two thrones — one for God and one for the Messiah — while Rabbi Yose objected, arguing that such a reading compromised divine sovereignty.

The "saints of the Most High" who receive the kingdom (7:18, 27) are identified with Israel — God's people who have suffered under the beasts (empires) and will ultimately be vindicated. This reading sustains Jewish eschatological hope through centuries of exile and persecution.

## Theological Themes

- **The Succession of Empires**: Israel's suffering under four successive oppressors is part of God's plan
- **The Son of Man Debate**: Messianic or collective interpretation — a living question in Jewish thought
- **Eschatological Vindication**: Israel will ultimately receive the kingdom that the beasts tried to destroy
- **The Heavenly Court**: God judges the empires and vindicates his people
