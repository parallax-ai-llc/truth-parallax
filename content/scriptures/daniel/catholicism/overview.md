## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Daniel draws on Scripture, Sacred Tradition, and the Magisterium. The book is valued both for its court narratives — which model faithfulness, virtue, and trust in divine providence — and for its apocalyptic visions, which are read within the broader framework of Catholic eschatology. Catholic scholarship has been more open than some Protestant traditions to recognizing the Maccabean context of Daniel's final form while maintaining the book's inspired and prophetic character. The Catholic canon also includes the additions to Daniel: the Prayer of Azariah, the Song of the Three Young Men, Susanna, and Bel and the Dragon.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Allegorical

The Church Fathers read Daniel's court tales as moral exempla and his visions as prophecies of Christ and the Church. St. Jerome's commentary on Daniel was influential for centuries, defending the book's prophetic character against the pagan critic Porphyry.

### Ecclesiological

Catholic interpreters read the "stone cut without hands" (2:44-45) not merely as Christ but as the Church — the visible kingdom of God on earth that grows to fill the world. The Son of Man's reception of dominion (7:13-14) is connected to Christ's headship of the Church.

### Liturgical and Sacramental

The Song of the Three Young Men (Daniel 3:52-90 in the Catholic canon) is used in the Liturgy of the Hours. The fiery furnace is read as a type of baptismal purification and Eucharistic transformation.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Kingdom of God as the Church**: Daniel's visions of the eternal kingdom are fulfilled in the Church
- **Virtue and Resistance**: Daniel's faithfulness models the cardinal virtues under extreme pressure
- **Resurrection Hope**: Daniel 12:2 is one of the earliest witnesses to bodily resurrection
- **The Deuterocanonical Additions**: Susanna, Bel and the Dragon, and the Hymn of the Three enrich the portrait of Daniel

## Notable Catholic Commentators

- **St. Jerome**: Wrote the most influential patristic commentary on Daniel
- **St. Thomas Aquinas**: Referenced Daniel in discussions of divine providence and prophecy
- **Louis F. Hartman**: Authored the Anchor Bible commentary on Daniel
- **Pope Benedict XVI**: Drew on Daniel's vision of the Son of Man in his Christological writings
