## Overview

The Book of Daniel is one of the most influential and contested books in the biblical canon. Set during the Babylonian exile, it divides into two halves: court tales of faithfulness under foreign rule (chapters 1-6) and apocalyptic visions of successive empires and the coming kingdom of God (chapters 7-12). Daniel and his companions model unwavering loyalty to God amid imperial pressure, while the visions introduce imagery — the four beasts, the Son of Man, the Ancient of Days — that has shaped Jewish, Christian, and Islamic eschatology for over two millennia.

## Structure

1. **Chapters 1-6**: Court narratives — Daniel's diet, Nebuchadnezzar's dreams, the fiery furnace, the writing on the wall, the lions' den
2. **Chapters 7-12**: Apocalyptic visions — four beasts, the ram and goat, the seventy weeks, the final vision of kings and the end times

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: Classified among the Ketuvim (Writings) rather than the Prophets; foundational for Jewish apocalypticism and the concept of resurrection (12:2)
- **Christianity**: The "Son of Man" vision (7:13-14) is central to Christology; the seventy weeks prophecy (9:24-27) is read as pointing to Christ
- **Islam**: Daniel (Danyal) is honored as a prophet; themes of God's sovereignty over empires and the faithfulness of believers resonate with Quranic narratives
- **Gnosticism**: The heavenly court, angelic hierarchies, and hidden knowledge align with Gnostic cosmology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Faithfulness under Empire | Daniel and companions resist assimilation and remain loyal to God |
| Divine Sovereignty | God rules over all kingdoms and determines the rise and fall of empires |
| Apocalyptic Revelation | Hidden knowledge of history's end is revealed through visions and angelic interpretation |
| Son of Man | The heavenly figure who receives eternal dominion (7:13-14) |
| Resurrection | The first clear Old Testament reference to bodily resurrection (12:2) |
