## The Humbling of the Arrogant King

Islamic reading of Daniel 4 finds in Nebuchadnezzar's madness and restoration a powerful illustration of the Quranic principle that *kibr* (arrogance) is the sin most detested by God. The Quran warns: "I shall turn away from My signs those who are arrogant upon the earth without right" (Surah 7:146). Nebuchadnezzar's claim — "Is not this great Babylon, which I have built?" — is the paradigmatic statement of human pride that provokes divine correction.

The narrative resonates with the Quranic accounts of Pharaoh and Nimrod — rulers whose arrogance led to their downfall. The Prophet Muhammad said: "No one who has an atom's weight of arrogance in his heart will enter Paradise" (Sahih Muslim). Nebuchadnezzar's descent into bestial madness illustrates what happens to the human soul when it attributes to itself what belongs to God alone.

The king's restoration after acknowledging God's sovereignty demonstrates the Islamic principle of *tawbah* (repentance): no matter how great the sin, sincere repentance and submission to God can bring restoration. The Quran assures: "O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins" (Surah 39:53).

## Theological Themes

- **Kibr**: Arrogance as the sin most abhorrent to God
- **The Pharaoh Parallel**: Nebuchadnezzar's pride mirrors the arrogance of Pharaoh and Nimrod
- **Tawbah**: Sincere repentance can restore even the most fallen
- **Divine Sovereignty**: All power belongs to God; human rulers are merely stewards
