## Overview

The Book of Daniel is not part of the Quran, and Islam does not treat it as revealed scripture. However, Daniel (*Danyal*) is honored in Islamic tradition as a righteous prophet and servant of God. Muslim historians and exegetes engage with his story through the *Isra'iliyyat* framework, and the discovery of what was believed to be Daniel's tomb in Susa was an event of significance in early Islamic history. The book's themes — God's absolute sovereignty over empires, the faithfulness of a believer under pagan rule, and the revelation of hidden knowledge — resonate deeply with Quranic teaching.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Isra'iliyyat Framework

Classical Muslim scholars such as al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir recounted the stories of Daniel as part of the broader history of the prophets. These narratives are accepted insofar as they align with Quranic principles and do not contradict Islamic theology.

### Divine Sovereignty over Empires

The Quran affirms that God raises and lowers nations according to his will: "Say, 'O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will'" (Surah 3:26). Daniel's vision of successive empires illustrates this Quranic principle.

### Prophetic Faithfulness

Daniel's refusal to compromise his worship of the one God under imperial pressure mirrors the Quranic narratives of Ibrahim facing Nimrod and Musa confronting Pharaoh. The believer's duty is to maintain *tawhid* (monotheism) regardless of consequences.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tawhid**: Daniel's unwavering monotheism under pagan pressure is the supreme virtue
- **God's Sovereignty over History**: The rise and fall of empires is entirely in God's hands
- **Prophetic Courage**: Daniel models the courage demanded of all who bear witness to divine truth
- **Hidden Knowledge**: God reveals what is hidden — a theme shared by Daniel and the Quran (Surah 2:255)

## Notable Islamic Scholars

- **al-Tabari**: Recounted Daniel's story in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa'l-Muluk*
- **Ibn Kathir**: Discussed Daniel in *al-Bidaya wa'l-Nihaya* and *Qisas al-Anbiya*
- **al-Tha'labi**: Included Daniel's narrative in his *Qisas al-Anbiya*
- **al-Kisa'i**: Referenced Daniel in his collection of prophetic tales
