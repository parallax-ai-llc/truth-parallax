## The Bestial Nature of the Demiurge's Servant

Gnostic interpretation of Daniel 4 reads Nebuchadnezzar's descent into bestial madness as a revelation of the true condition of the hylic (material) soul that identifies with the Demiurge. The king who boasts "Is not this great Babylon, which I have built?" represents the ego inflated by the Demiurge's deceptions — proud of material achievements that are, from the pneumatic perspective, illusory.

The madness is not punishment from the true God but the natural consequence of identifying with the material world. When the veneer of civilization is stripped away, the hylic soul is revealed in its true condition: bestial, driven by appetite, grazing like an animal, with no awareness of the divine realm above.

The restoration of Nebuchadnezzar's sanity when he "lifts his eyes to heaven" (4:34) is read as a moment of partial awakening — a glimmer of gnosis in which the hylic soul briefly perceives the reality above the material dome. However, the Gnostic reader notes that the king's restoration leaves him still a king of Babylon — still within the material realm. True liberation would require not the restoration of his throne but the abandonment of it.

## Theological Themes

- **The Hylic Condition**: Without gnosis, the soul is bestial, driven by material appetites
- **The Illusion of Material Achievement**: Babylon's greatness is a Demiurgic deception
- **Partial Awakening**: Lifting one's eyes to heaven is a glimpse of gnosis, not its fullness
- **The Trap of Restoration**: Return to power within the material world is not true liberation
