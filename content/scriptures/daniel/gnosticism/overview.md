## Overview

Gnostic engagement with the Book of Daniel is largely reconstructive, as no surviving Gnostic text offers a systematic commentary on this book. However, Daniel's themes — the succession of oppressive empires, hidden knowledge revealed to the elect, angelic hierarchies, heavenly visions, and the coming of a transcendent "Son of Man" — align remarkably well with Gnostic cosmology. The court tales can be read as narratives of the pneumatic self resisting the archons, while the apocalyptic visions map onto the Gnostic understanding of cosmic history and the soul's liberation.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Archontic Empires

Gnostic cosmology posits a series of archons (rulers) who govern the material world under the Demiurge. Daniel's four successive kingdoms — each more oppressive than the last — can be read as the rule of the archons over the material realm, a tyranny that will be overthrown by the true God of the Pleroma.

### The Son of Man as Heavenly Redeemer

The figure "like a son of man" who comes on the clouds of heaven (7:13-14) and receives eternal dominion resonates with the Gnostic Redeemer myth — a heavenly being who descends to liberate the divine sparks trapped in matter.

### Hidden Knowledge

Daniel's ability to interpret dreams and visions — knowledge hidden from the wise men of Babylon — mirrors the Gnostic emphasis on *gnosis* as hidden, salvific knowledge available only to the elect.

## Theological Emphases

- **Archontic Oppression**: The succession of empires represents the rule of the archons over the material world
- **The Heavenly Redeemer**: The Son of Man as a figure of cosmic liberation
- **Gnosis as Hidden Wisdom**: Daniel's interpretive gift is a type of the pneumatic's access to hidden knowledge
- **Resistance to Assimilation**: Daniel's refusal to eat the king's food or worship his image represents the pneumatic's resistance to the material world

## Notable Gnostic and Scholarly Sources

- **The Apocryphon of John**: The succession of archontic powers parallels Daniel's four kingdoms
- **The Apocalypse of Adam**: Shares Daniel's genre of revealed eschatological knowledge
- **Valentinian theology**: The Pleroma/kenoma distinction illuminates Daniel's "above" and "below" cosmology
- **John J. Collins**: Modern scholarship on apocalypticism that bridges canonical and Gnostic sources
