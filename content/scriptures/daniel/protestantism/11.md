## The Prophecy of Kings and Conflicts

Protestant interpretation of Daniel 11 reads the chapter as a remarkably detailed prophecy of the wars between the Ptolemaic (South) and Seleucid (North) kingdoms following Alexander the Great's death, culminating in the persecution under Antiochus IV Epiphanes. The chapter's extraordinary historical specificity has been a focal point of debate between those who defend predictive prophecy and those who date Daniel to the Maccabean period.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Wars of the Kings (11:2-35)

Conservative Protestant scholars defend these as genuine predictions made in the sixth century BCE, demonstrating the supernatural character of biblical prophecy. The detailed correspondence between the prophecy and the historical events of the third and second centuries BCE is seen as evidence of divine inspiration.

### "Forces from him shall appear and profane the temple fortress" (11:31)

This verse describes the "abomination of desolation" — Antiochus' desecration of the Temple in 167 BCE. Jesus' reference to the "abomination of desolation spoken of by the prophet Daniel" (Matthew 24:15) gives this verse eschatological significance beyond its original historical referent.

### The Transition Point (11:36-45)

Many Protestant interpreters see a shift at verse 36 from Antiochus to a future Antichrist figure — a "willful king" who "shall exalt himself above every god." This interpretive move allows the chapter to address both past and future.

## Theological Themes

- **Prophetic Detail**: The specificity of the prophecy testifies to divine omniscience
- **The Pattern of Persecution**: Antiochus establishes a pattern that will recur in the end times
- **The Abomination of Desolation**: A concept that bridges Old Testament history and New Testament eschatology
- **The Sovereignty of God**: Even the most chaotic political events unfold according to God's plan
