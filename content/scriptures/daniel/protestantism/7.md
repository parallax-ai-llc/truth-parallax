## The Vision of Cosmic Sovereignty

Protestant interpretation of Daniel 7 reads this chapter as the pivot of the entire book — the transition from court narrative to apocalyptic vision, and the introduction of the "Son of Man" who becomes central to New Testament Christology. The four beasts rising from the sea represent successive empires, and the heavenly court scene where the Ancient of Days grants dominion to "one like a son of man" is understood as a prophetic revelation of Christ's eternal kingship.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Four Beasts (7:2-8)

Protestant interpreters generally identify the four beasts with the same four empires as the statue in chapter 2: Babylon (lion), Medo-Persia (bear), Greece (leopard), and Rome (terrifying beast). The "little horn" on the fourth beast has been variously identified with the Antichrist, the papacy (in Reformation polemic), or a future world ruler.

### "One like a son of man" (7:13-14)

This is arguably the most Christologically significant verse in the Old Testament for Protestant theology. Jesus' preferred self-designation — "the Son of Man" — is drawn directly from this vision. The Son of Man receives "dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him."

### The Saints of the Most High (7:18, 27)

The identification of "the saints of the Most High" who receive the kingdom alongside the Son of Man is read as a reference to the Church — the body of Christ who shares in his reign.

## Theological Themes

- **Christological Prophecy**: The Son of Man vision is central to understanding Jesus' identity
- **The Sovereignty of God over History**: Empires are beasts from the sea; God's kingdom is from above
- **The Heavenly Court**: God judges the empires and vindicates his people
- **The Eternal Kingdom**: The kingdom given to the Son of Man "shall not be destroyed" (7:14)
