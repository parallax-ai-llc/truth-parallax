## The Humbling of the Proud

Protestant interpretation of Daniel 4 reads the chapter as a dramatic demonstration of God's sovereignty over the most powerful ruler on earth. Nebuchadnezzar's dream of a great tree, his subsequent madness (*lycanthropy*), and his eventual restoration form a narrative arc that Protestant theology reads as a parable of pride, judgment, and grace.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The Most High rules the kingdom of men and gives it to whom he will" (4:17)

This verse, repeated three times in the chapter (4:17, 25, 32), is the theological thesis of the entire book. Protestant political theology grounds itself here: all human authority is delegated and temporary.

### Nebuchadnezzar's Pride (4:30)

"Is not this great Babylon, which I have built by my mighty power...?" The king's boast is immediately answered by divine judgment. Protestant preachers from Calvin to Spurgeon have drawn the lesson that pride invites divine opposition: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" (James 4:6).

### The King's Restoration (4:34-37)

Nebuchadnezzar's sanity and kingdom are restored when he lifts his eyes to heaven and acknowledges God's sovereignty. Protestant soteriology sees here a picture of conversion: the proud heart is broken, and the broken heart is restored.

## Theological Themes

- **The Sovereignty of God**: God rules over all kingdoms without exception
- **Pride and Humiliation**: Human pride is always met with divine opposition
- **Grace and Restoration**: Even the proud can be restored through repentance
- **The Testimony of a Pagan King**: God's grace extends even to Babylon's ruler
