## The Ram, the Goat, and the Desecration of the Sanctuary

Protestant interpretation of Daniel 8 reads the vision of the ram and the goat as a prophecy of the Medo-Persian and Greek empires, with particular attention to Antiochus IV Epiphanes — the "little horn" who desecrated the Jerusalem Temple in 167 BCE. Evangelical interpreters often see in Antiochus a type of the future Antichrist.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Ram and the Goat (8:3-8)

The angel Gabriel explicitly identifies the ram as the kings of Media and Persia (8:20) and the goat as the king of Greece (8:21). The great horn is Alexander the Great; the four horns that replace it are his successor kingdoms.

### The Little Horn (8:9-12)

The "little horn" that grows exceedingly great and tramples the sanctuary is identified with Antiochus IV Epiphanes. Protestant interpreters debate whether this figure exhausts the prophecy or also points typologically to a future Antichrist.

### "How long?" (8:13)

The question "How long shall the vision be...?" expresses the anguished cry of God's persecuted people in every age. Protestant pastoral theology finds comfort in the answer: the desecration has a divinely appointed limit.

## Theological Themes

- **Prophetic Specificity**: The vision's historical fulfillment demonstrates the reliability of biblical prophecy
- **The Desecration of the Holy**: Antiochus' abomination foreshadows future attacks on God's people
- **The Limit of Evil**: Every persecution has a divinely appointed end
- **The Faithfulness of God's Word**: What God reveals comes to pass
