## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Daniel has been shaped by the Reformation's commitment to *sola scriptura* and its keen interest in biblical prophecy. The book is read as both a historical narrative of faithfulness under persecution and a prophetic revelation of God's sovereign plan for history. The court tales (chapters 1-6) model the life of faith under hostile government, while the apocalyptic visions (chapters 7-12) reveal God's ultimate triumph over all earthly kingdoms. The "Son of Man" vision (7:13-14) is central to Protestant Christology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historicist

The classic Protestant approach, held by Luther, Calvin, and many Reformers, reads Daniel's visions as mapping the course of history from Babylon through the present age. The four kingdoms are typically identified as Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, and Rome, with the "stone cut without hands" (2:44-45) representing Christ's kingdom.

### Futurist

Dispensationalist Protestants read much of Daniel (especially chapters 9 and 11-12) as prophecy yet to be fulfilled in the end times, including a future tribulation, Antichrist, and the return of Christ.

### Christological

Across Protestant traditions, Daniel 7:13-14 — the Son of Man who receives eternal dominion — is read as a direct prophecy of Christ. Jesus' self-designation as "Son of Man" in the Gospels is understood as a deliberate claim to the authority described in Daniel.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Sovereignty of God**: God sets up and deposes kings; no empire is beyond his control
- **Faithfulness under Persecution**: Daniel and his companions model courage and integrity under pressure
- **Prophetic Fulfillment**: The visions demonstrate that God reveals and controls the course of history
- **The Kingdom of God**: The "stone" that destroys all earthly kingdoms and fills the earth

## Notable Protestant Commentators

- **John Calvin**: Provided detailed commentary emphasizing God's providence and the believer's courage
- **E.J. Young**: Offered a conservative evangelical exegesis defending the book's unity and early date
- **John Goldingay**: Provided a balanced evangelical commentary in the Word Biblical Commentary series
- **Tremper Longman III**: Combined literary and theological analysis of Daniel's narratives and visions
