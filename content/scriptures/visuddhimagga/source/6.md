---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 6: Asubha-kammatthana-niddesa (Foulness as a Meditation Subject)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 6
---

**1** One who wishes to develop the meditation on foulness should go to a cemetery or a charnel ground where corpses are exposed. He should attend to the corpse, noting its characteristics, and establish the sign of foulness in his mind.

**2** The bloated: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that has been dead one, two, or three days and has become swollen and bloated. He should compare it with his own body: "This body too is of the same nature; it will become like that; it is not exempt from that fate."

**3** The livid: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that has become discolored in patches — bluish-black, reddish, whitish — and should compare it with his own body, developing dispassion toward bodily form.

**4** The festering: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that is oozing with fluids from its openings, and should reflect on the impurity that lies beneath the surface of the living body as well.

**5** The cut up: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that has been cut apart, severed in the middle. The gnawed: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that has been gnawed by animals. In each case, he should compare the sight with his own body.

**6** The scattered: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse whose parts have been scattered in different directions — a hand here, a foot there, the head elsewhere. The hacked and scattered: a similar contemplation of a body that has been hacked to pieces.

**7** The bleeding: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that is soaked in blood, oozing from wounds. The worm-infested: a bhikkhu should contemplate a corpse that is crawling with worms and maggots.

**8** The skeleton: a bhikkhu should contemplate a skeleton, with or without flesh and blood, held together by sinews, or a skeleton that has fallen apart, with bones scattered in all directions — here a hand-bone, there a foot-bone, here a shin-bone, there a thigh-bone.

**9** Through any of these ten foulness meditations, the learning sign arises, and then the counterpart sign. Through the counterpart sign, the first jhana is attained. But only the first jhana is attainable through foulness meditation, because the object is gross and unpleasant.

**10** The purpose of foulness meditation is to overcome lust and attachment to the body. When a bhikkhu sees the true nature of the body — that it is foul, impure, and bound for dissolution — the fever of sensual desire subsides, and the mind becomes cool and dispassionate.
