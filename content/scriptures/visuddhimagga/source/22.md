---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 22: Nanadassana-visuddhi-niddesa (Purification by Knowledge and Vision)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 22
---

**1** Purification by knowledge and vision is the knowledge of the four supramundane paths: the path of stream-entry, the path of once-returning, the path of non-returning, and the path of Arahatship. Each path eradicates specific defilements and is followed by its corresponding fruit.

**2** The path of stream-entry eradicates three fetters: identity view, doubt, and distorted grasp of rules and vows. The stream-enterer has entered the stream of the Dhamma and is destined for enlightenment within seven existences at most.

**3** The path of once-returning attenuates sensual desire and ill-will, weakening them to the point where they arise only mildly. The once-returner will be reborn in this world only once more before attaining Nibbana.

**4** The path of non-returning eradicates sensual desire and ill-will completely. The non-returner is no longer bound to the desire realm and will be reborn in the Pure Abodes of the form realm, attaining Nibbana there.

**5** The path of Arahatship eradicates the five remaining fetters: desire for form, desire for the formless, conceit, restlessness, and ignorance. The Arahat has completed the task and is fully liberated from all suffering.

**6** Each path-consciousness arises for a single mind-moment and performs the function of eradicating the defilements. Immediately after the path-consciousness, the fruit-consciousness arises, experiencing the bliss of liberation for two or three mind-moments.

**7** After the fruit-consciousness, the reviewing consciousness arises. The Noble One reviews the path attained, the fruit attained, the defilements abandoned, the defilements remaining, and Nibbana. This reviewing completes the process.

**8** The four paths are supramundane because they take Nibbana as their object. Unlike all mundane consciousness, which takes conditioned phenomena as its object, path-consciousness directly apprehends the unconditioned — Nibbana.

**9** Nibbana is of one kind — the cessation of all suffering. But it is spoken of in two ways: the Nibbana-element with residue remaining and the Nibbana-element without residue remaining. The first is the liberation experienced by the living Arahat; the second is the final passing away of the Arahat, after which there is no further arising.

**10** The Blessed One declared: "There is, monks, an unborn, unbecome, unmade, unconditioned. If there were not this unborn, unbecome, unmade, unconditioned, no escape would be discerned from what is born, become, made, conditioned. But since there is an unborn, unbecome, unmade, unconditioned, therefore an escape is discerned from what is born, become, made, conditioned."
