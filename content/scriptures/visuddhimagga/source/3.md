---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 3: Kammatthana-gahana-niddesa (Taking a Meditation Subject)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 3
---

**1** When a bhikkhu has purified his virtue and has undertaken the ascetic practices suitable to his temperament, he should seek a good friend — a meditation teacher — who can give him a suitable meditation subject.

**2** A good meditation teacher should possess the following qualities: he should be lovable, venerable, an able counselor, a patient listener, and capable of giving deep instruction. Finding such a teacher, the pupil should place himself under his guidance.

**3** There are forty meditation subjects: ten kasinas, ten kinds of foulness, ten recollections, four divine abidings, four formless attainments, one perception of repulsiveness in food, and one analysis of the four elements.

**4** The ten kasinas are: the earth kasina, the water kasina, the fire kasina, the air kasina, the blue kasina, the yellow kasina, the red kasina, the white kasina, the light kasina, and the space kasina.

**5** The ten kinds of foulness are meditations on a corpse in various stages of decomposition: the bloated, the livid, the festering, the cut up, the gnawed, the scattered, the hacked and scattered, the bleeding, the worm-infested, and the skeleton.

**6** The ten recollections are: recollection of the Buddha, recollection of the Dhamma, recollection of the Sangha, recollection of virtue, recollection of generosity, recollection of deities, mindfulness of death, mindfulness of the body, mindfulness of breathing, and recollection of peace.

**7** The four divine abidings are: loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. These are called divine abidings because they are the way in which the gods dwell, and they produce rebirth in the Brahma-worlds.

**8** A meditation teacher should assign a meditation subject suited to the pupil's temperament. For one of lustful temperament, the foulness meditations are suitable. For one of hateful temperament, the divine abidings are suitable. For one of deluded temperament, mindfulness of breathing is suitable.

**9** Having received a meditation subject, the bhikkhu should find a suitable place for meditation — a secluded place, free from noise, with suitable climate and good water. He should settle there and begin his practice.

**10** The bhikkhu should practice his meditation subject diligently, not too fast and not too slow, not straining and not slackening, balancing energy with tranquility, until the sign of concentration arises and the mind becomes collected and unified.
