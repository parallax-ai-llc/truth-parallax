---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 4: Pathavi-kasina-niddesa (The Earth Kasina)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 4
---

**1** One who wishes to develop the earth kasina should take up the sign of earth. The sign should be made by marking out a disc of the size of a winnowing basket or a plate, on level ground, using clean dawn-colored clay, well worked and smooth.

**2** He should place this disc at a distance of two and a half cubits from where he sits, neither too high nor too low. He should sit on a well-prepared seat, comfortable and steady, and contemplate the kasina disc with eyes neither fully open nor fully closed.

**3** He should fix his mind upon the kasina with the thought "earth, earth" or "pathavi, pathavi." He should not attend to its color or characteristics but only to the concept of earth. He should open and shut his eyes from time to time, attending to the kasina as a mirror is being looked at.

**4** As he practices in this way, the hindrances become suppressed, the mind becomes concentrated, and the learning sign arises. The learning sign appears as if one were looking at the disc itself, with all its imperfections — any scratches, marks, or unevenness.

**5** When the learning sign has been established through repeated attention, the counterpart sign arises. The counterpart sign appears clean and purified, free from all imperfections, like a disc of the full moon emerging from behind clouds, like a mirror taken from its case.

**6** With the arising of the counterpart sign, the five hindrances are suppressed, and the jhana factors become established. Applied thought fixes the mind on the counterpart sign; sustained thought keeps it there; joy pervades the mind; happiness accompanies the joy; and one-pointedness unifies the mind.

**7** When these five factors are present and the five hindrances are absent, the first jhana is attained. The bhikkhu, secluded from sense-desires and unwholesome states, enters and abides in the first jhana, which is accompanied by applied thought, sustained thought, joy, happiness, and one-pointedness.

**8** To develop the second jhana, the bhikkhu should see the defect in applied and sustained thought as gross, and by removing them, enter the second jhana, which has internal confidence and unification of mind, is free from applied and sustained thought, and has joy, happiness, and one-pointedness.

**9** To develop the third jhana, the bhikkhu should see the defect in joy as gross, and by removing it, enter the third jhana, in which he abides equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending, experiencing happiness with the body.

**10** To develop the fourth jhana, the bhikkhu should see the defect in happiness as gross, and with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, enter the fourth jhana, which has neither pain nor pleasure and has purity of mindfulness due to equanimity. This is the highest of the form-realm absorptions attainable through the earth kasina.
