---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 9: Brahma-vihara-niddesa (The Divine Abidings)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 9
---

**1** Loving-kindness: a bhikkhu who wishes to develop loving-kindness should first review the danger of hatred and the advantage of patience. Hatred is like a fire that burns the one who holds it. Patience is like water that cools and refreshes.

**2** He should first pervade himself with loving-kindness: "May I be happy, may I be free from suffering, may I be free from enmity, may I be free from affliction." For if one cannot love oneself, one cannot love others.

**3** Then he should pervade a respected person with loving-kindness, then a dearly beloved friend, then a neutral person, and finally a hostile person. He should break down the barriers between these four, extending loving-kindness equally to all.

**4** When the barriers have been broken down, the sign of loving-kindness arises, and through it, the first three jhanas are attained. The fourth jhana is not attainable through loving-kindness because loving-kindness is associated with the wish for beings' happiness, which requires the gladness of the third jhana.

**5** Compassion: a bhikkhu who wishes to develop compassion should contemplate beings who are suffering — those in the hells, those racked by disease, those oppressed by poverty, those caught in the cycle of birth and death. His heart is moved with compassion: "May these beings be free from suffering."

**6** Compassion has the characteristic of promoting the removal of suffering. Its function is not to bear others' suffering. It is manifested as non-cruelty. Its proximate cause is seeing the helplessness of those overwhelmed by suffering.

**7** Sympathetic joy: a bhikkhu who wishes to develop sympathetic joy should contemplate beings who are happy and prosperous, and rejoice in their happiness. He should think: "How wonderful that this being is happy, how excellent, how delightful!"

**8** Sympathetic joy has the characteristic of gladness at others' success. Its function is the removal of envy. It is manifested as the absence of boredom and aversion. Its near enemy is worldly merriment; its far enemy is envy.

**9** Equanimity: a bhikkhu who wishes to develop equanimity should first develop the other three divine abidings. Then, seeing the limitations of loving-kindness, compassion, and sympathetic joy — their nearness to attachment — he should develop equanimity, regarding all beings with impartiality.

**10** Equanimity has the characteristic of promoting neutrality toward beings. Its function is seeing beings as equal. It is manifested as the quieting of resentment and approval. Its proximate cause is seeing that all beings are the heirs of their own karma. Through equanimity, the fourth jhana is attained, and the mind becomes perfectly balanced and serene.
