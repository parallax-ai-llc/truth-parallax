---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 11: Samadhi-niddesa (Description of Concentration)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 11
---

**1** Concentration has the characteristic of non-distraction, the function of eliminating distraction, and the manifestation of non-wavering. Its proximate cause is happiness, for one who is happy becomes concentrated.

**2** Concentration is of many kinds. It is momentary concentration, access concentration, and absorption concentration. Momentary concentration arises for a single mind-moment. Access concentration is the concentration proximate to jhana. Absorption concentration is jhana itself.

**3** Concentration is also classified as that accompanied by applied thought and sustained thought, that accompanied by sustained thought only, and that without either. The first jhana has both; the second has sustained thought only; the third and fourth have neither.

**4** The five factors of jhana are: applied thought, sustained thought, joy, happiness, and one-pointedness. Applied thought is the initial application of the mind to the object. Sustained thought is the continued maintenance of the mind upon the object.

**5** Joy is of five kinds: minor joy, momentary joy, showering joy, uplifting joy, and pervading joy. Pervading joy, which pervades the whole body, is the joy present in the first jhana. Happiness is the pleasant feeling that accompanies jhana.

**6** One-pointedness is the unification of the mind upon a single object. It is present in all jhanas and is the essence of concentration. Without one-pointedness, the mind scatters and the jhana cannot be maintained.

**7** The five hindrances are the enemies of concentration: sensual desire, ill-will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and worry, and doubt. Each is suppressed by a jhana factor: applied thought suppresses sloth and torpor, sustained thought suppresses doubt, joy suppresses ill-will, happiness suppresses restlessness, and one-pointedness suppresses sensual desire.

**8** The bhikkhu who wishes to develop concentration should guard the doors of the senses, be moderate in eating, be devoted to wakefulness, practice in the four postures, and dwell in a suitable place with a suitable companion.

**9** He should develop the five spiritual faculties in balance: faith balanced with wisdom, and energy balanced with concentration, with mindfulness overseeing all. Too much faith without wisdom leads to blind devotion; too much wisdom without faith leads to cunning.

**10** When concentration is fully developed, the mind becomes luminous, malleable, wieldy, and imperturbable. The concentrated mind sees things as they really are. This is the great benefit of concentration: it serves as the foundation for the arising of liberating insight.
