---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 2: Dhutanga-niddesa (The Ascetic Practices)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 2
---

**1** When his virtue has been purified, a bhikkhu who desires to develop concentration should undertake the ascetic practices. For it is these ascetic practices that perfect the purification of virtue and provide the foundation for concentration.

**2** There are thirteen ascetic practices: the refuse-rag-wearer's practice, the three-robe-wearer's practice, the alms-food-eater's practice, the house-to-house-seeker's practice, the one-session-eater's practice, the bowl-food-eater's practice, the later-food-refuser's practice.

**3** Also the forest-dweller's practice, the tree-root-dweller's practice, the open-air-dweller's practice, the charnel-ground-dweller's practice, the any-bed-user's practice, and the sitter's practice.

**4** The refuse-rag-wearer's practice: a bhikkhu rejects robes given by householders and wears only robes made from cloth picked up from refuse heaps, cemeteries, or streets. This practice develops contentment and detachment from material concerns.

**5** The alms-food-eater's practice: a bhikkhu rejects invitations to meals and eats only food collected on the alms round, going from house to house without discrimination, accepting whatever is placed in the bowl, whether much or little, coarse or fine.

**6** The one-session-eater's practice: a bhikkhu eats only once a day, at a single sitting, refusing any further food after rising from the meal seat. This practice promotes moderation and reduces the time spent in obtaining and consuming food.

**7** The forest-dweller's practice: a bhikkhu refuses to live in a village and takes up residence in the forest. The forest is conducive to meditation because it is free from the noise and distractions of village life.

**8** The tree-root-dweller's practice: a bhikkhu refuses to live under a roof and takes up residence at the root of a tree. This practice develops endurance and reduces attachment to comfort.

**9** The sitter's practice: a bhikkhu refuses to lie down and maintains the three postures of sitting, standing, and walking. This is the most austere of the thirteen practices and greatly promotes wakefulness and effort.

**10** These ascetic practices are not required of all monks but are voluntary undertakings for those who wish to develop contentment, fewness of wishes, and the other qualities praised by the Blessed One. They are aids to meditation, not ends in themselves.
