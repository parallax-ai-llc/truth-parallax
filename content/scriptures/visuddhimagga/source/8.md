---
title: "Visuddhimagga Chapter 8: Anussati-kammatthana-niddesa (Other Recollections as Meditation Subjects)"
scripture: "visuddhimagga"
chapter: 8
---

**1** Mindfulness of death: a bhikkhu should recollect death in eight ways. First, as a murderer standing before one with a drawn sword. Second, as the loss of all prosperity. Third, by comparison with those who were great and powerful and yet died.

**2** Fourth, by the body being shared with many organisms. Fifth, by the frailty of life. Sixth, by the absence of any fixed sign — death may come at any moment, from any cause. Seventh, by the shortness of the life-span. Eighth, by the brevity of the moment — life lasts only for the duration of a single mind-moment.

**3** When a bhikkhu recollects death frequently, he becomes heedful and develops urgency. He abandons delight in existence, overcomes attachment to life, and the fear of death diminishes. He dies without confusion and, if he does not attain the deathless in this life, he is at least destined for a happy rebirth.

**4** Mindfulness of the body: a bhikkhu should review the thirty-two parts of the body: head-hair, body-hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, bone-marrow, kidneys, heart, liver, diaphragm, spleen, lungs, large intestines, small intestines, stomach-contents, excrement, bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, fat, tears, grease, saliva, mucus, oil of the joints, and urine.

**5** He should review these parts in forward and reverse order, by color, by shape, by region, by location, and by delimitation. When he attends to the repulsiveness of the body in this way, the counterpart sign arises, and through it, the first jhana may be attained.

**6** Mindfulness of breathing: a bhikkhu, having gone to a forest, to the root of a tree, or to an empty room, sits down cross-legged, sets his body erect, and establishes mindfulness in front of him. Mindful, he breathes in; mindful, he breathes out.

**7** There are four tetrads of mindfulness of breathing. In the first tetrad, the bhikkhu knows a long breath as long and a short breath as short, trains to experience the whole body of the breath, and trains to calm the bodily formation.

**8** In the second tetrad, he trains to experience joy, to experience happiness, to experience the mental formation, and to calm the mental formation. In the third tetrad, he trains to experience the mind, to gladden the mind, to concentrate the mind, and to liberate the mind.

**9** In the fourth tetrad, he trains to contemplate impermanence, to contemplate fading away, to contemplate cessation, and to contemplate relinquishment. Through the sixteen steps of mindfulness of breathing, all four jhanas may be attained.

**10** Recollection of peace: a bhikkhu should recollect Nibbana, the cessation of all suffering, the unconditioned state, the deathless. He reflects: "There is that state where there is no earth, no water, no fire, no air; not the sphere of infinite space, nor the sphere of infinite consciousness; neither this world nor another; neither sun nor moon. I call it neither coming nor going nor standing still, neither passing away nor arising. Without support, without going on, without foundation: this indeed is the end of suffering."
