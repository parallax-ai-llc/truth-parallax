## Overview

Reform Judaism has increasingly embraced the Zohar as a source of spiritual depth and theological creativity. While early Reform thinkers distanced themselves from Kabbalah as superstition, the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries saw a renaissance of mystical interest within the movement. The Zohar's rich imagery of the Shekhinah as feminine divine presence has been particularly influential in Reform theology, supporting egalitarian liturgy and feminist reinterpretations of Jewish tradition.

Reform engagement with the Zohar tends to be selective and interpretive, drawing on those passages that speak to contemporary concerns — the dignity of every soul, the interconnectedness of all creation, the imperative of tikkun olam (repairing the world), and the experience of divine intimacy in prayer. The Zohar is read not as halakhic authority but as poetic theology, a wellspring of metaphor and meaning that enriches the spiritual lives of modern Jews seeking a deeper relationship with the sacred.
