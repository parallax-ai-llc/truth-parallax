## Reform Jewish Reading

Reform Jewish engagement with Zohar Parashat Acharei Mot (After Death) approaches the text as a masterwork of Jewish spiritual literature, rich in metaphor, poetry, and theological insight. Rather than reading the Zohar as a literal description of metaphysical reality, Reform interpreters draw from this section those teachings that speak to contemporary spiritual seeking — the experience of the sacred in everyday life, the dignity of every human soul, and the imperative to bring healing and justice to a fractured world.

This section's exploration of spiritual renewal resonates particularly with Reform values. The Zohar's imagery of light emerging from darkness, of the divine Presence dwelling among the people, and of the Torah as a living, inexhaustible source of meaning finds new expression in Reform liturgy, education, and pastoral practice. Reform rabbis and educators increasingly incorporate Zoharic texts into sermons, study groups, and spiritual retreats, discovering in the medieval mystical tradition unexpected resources for addressing the spiritual hungers of modern Jews.

## Key Themes

- The Shekhinah as feminine divine presence and its implications for gender-inclusive theology
- Tikkun olam as cosmic and social repair
- The infinite interpretability of Torah — the Zohar's model of open-ended spiritual reading
- The experience of awe and wonder as paths to the divine
- Ethical mysticism: the inseparability of spiritual practice and moral action

## Key Passages

> "Come and see: the Torah has a body — the commandments of the Torah. This body is clothed in garments — the narratives of the Torah. Fools see only the garments. The wise see the body. But the truly wise, the servants of the Most High King, they see the soul of the Torah."

Reform readers find in this passage a powerful endorsement of interpretive freedom — the Torah's deepest meaning is not in its literal surface but in the spiritual truths it embodies. This hermeneutical principle supports Reform Judaism's commitment to reading sacred texts through the lens of contemporary moral and spiritual understanding.

## Commentary Tradition

Reform engagement with the Zohar draws on modern scholars such as Arthur Green, Daniel Matt, and Lawrence Kushner, who have made kabbalistic ideas accessible to non-specialist audiences. The emphasis is on extracting universal spiritual insights while remaining honest about the text's historical context and its sometimes problematic assumptions about gender, non-Jews, and the natural world.
