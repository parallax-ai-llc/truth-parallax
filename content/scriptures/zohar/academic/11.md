## Academic Reading

Academic analysis of Zohar Parashat Vayigash (And He Drew Near) situates this section within the broader literary and intellectual history of medieval Jewish mysticism. Scholars examine the text's literary structure, its use of Aramaic, its relationship to earlier midrashic and mystical sources, and its place within the Zoharic corpus as a whole. The section on Vayigash (And He Drew Near) presents particular challenges and opportunities for textual criticism, as it contains passages that may represent different authorial strata or redactional layers.

The Aramaic of this section displays the characteristic features of Zoharic language — a deliberately archaizing dialect that blends Talmudic Aramaic with Targumic forms and medieval Hebrew calques. Scholars debate whether certain passages reflect genuine oral traditions predating Moses de León or represent his creative literary production. The theological content — the interplay of the Sefirot, the mystical reinterpretation of biblical narrative, the coded references to divine sexuality — is analyzed in light of the Neoplatonic, Gnostic, and Aristotelian philosophical traditions available in thirteenth-century Castile.

## Key Themes

- Textual criticism and manuscript variants in this section
- Literary analysis of narrative techniques: the *sod* (secret) within the *peshat* (plain sense)
- Aramaic philology and the Zohar's artificial dialect
- Intertextual connections with earlier midrash, Sefer ha-Bahir, and philosophical sources
- The section's reception history in later kabbalistic and Hasidic literature

## Key Passages

> "Rabbi Shimon opened and said: 'The heavens declare the glory of God' (Psalm 19:1). This verse has been explained, but come and see — when the Holy One, blessed be He, created the world..."

Scholars note that this passage employs the characteristic Zoharic literary device of the *petichta* (proem), modeled on classical midrashic openings but transformed into a vehicle for theosophical teaching. The attribution to Rabbi Shimon is understood as a literary convention establishing the text's authority.

## Commentary Tradition

Key academic works on this section include Gershom Scholem's foundational studies, Yehuda Liebes's analysis of the Zohar's authorship and literary character, Elliot Wolfson's phenomenological readings of Zoharic symbolism, and the textual-critical apparatus provided in Daniel Matt's Pritzker Edition. Ronit Meroz has contributed important studies on the Zohar's compositional layers.
