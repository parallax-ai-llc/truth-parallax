## Overview

Academic study of the Zohar represents one of the most vibrant fields in Jewish studies. The foundational work of Gershom Scholem established the modern scholarly consensus that the Zohar was composed primarily by Moses de León in late thirteenth-century Castile, though it incorporates earlier mystical traditions. Subsequent scholars — Yehuda Liebes, Elliot Wolfson, Daniel Matt, Ronit Meroz, and others — have refined this picture, identifying multiple authorial layers, diverse literary genres, and complex intertextual relationships within the Zoharic corpus.

The Zohar's literary Aramaic is itself a subject of intense philological study: it is a deliberately archaizing, artificial dialect that draws on Talmudic Aramaic, Targum traditions, and medieval Hebrew, creating a unique register designed to evoke antiquity and esoteric authority. Scholars analyze the Zohar's use of earlier sources — Sefer Yetzirah, the Bahir, Maimonides, and Neoplatonic philosophy — while tracing its enormous influence on later kabbalistic, Hasidic, and Sabbatean movements. Critical editions, especially Daniel Matt's monumental Pritzker Edition, have transformed the field by providing reliable texts with extensive annotation.
