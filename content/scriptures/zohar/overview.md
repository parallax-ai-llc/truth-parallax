## Overview

The Zohar (Hebrew: ספר הזוהר, "Book of Splendor") is the foundational work of Jewish mystical thought known as Kabbalah. Traditionally attributed to the second-century sage Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai (Rashbi), modern scholarship largely credits its composition to the thirteenth-century Spanish kabbalist Moses de León, who drew upon earlier mystical traditions. Written primarily in an ornate literary Aramaic, the Zohar presents itself as a mystical commentary on the Torah, weaving together narrative, allegory, and theosophical speculation into a vast tapestry of esoteric teaching.

The Zohar's central concern is the inner life of God — the dynamic interplay of the ten Sefirot (divine emanations) through which the infinite, unknowable Ein Sof manifests in creation. The text explores how every word, letter, and narrative of the Torah conceals layers of mystical meaning that, when properly understood, reveal the structure of divine reality and the human soul's role within it. The Shekhinah, the feminine divine presence, occupies a central place in the Zohar's theology as the aspect of God most intimately connected to the created world and to the people of Israel.

Since its appearance in medieval Spain, the Zohar has profoundly shaped Jewish spirituality, liturgy, and law. It became a canonical text for Sephardic and Hasidic communities, influenced the development of Lurianic Kabbalah, and continues to inspire both traditional study and contemporary spiritual seeking.

## Structure

The Zohar is organized as a commentary following the weekly Torah portions (parashot), interspersed with several independent compositions:

1. **Bereshit through Vayechi** (Genesis sections): Creation, patriarchal narratives, and the Sefirot
2. **Shemot through Pekudei** (Exodus sections): Revelation, Tabernacle, and sacred space
3. **Acharei Mot through Behar-Bechukotai** (Leviticus sections): Holiness, festivals, and covenant
4. **Idra Rabba and Idra Zuta**: The Greater and Lesser Assemblies — the Zohar's dramatic climax

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Orthodox Jewish**: Studied as sacred revelation alongside Torah and Talmud, especially in Sephardic and Hasidic communities
- **Reform Jewish**: Engaged as a masterpiece of Jewish spiritual literature and a resource for contemporary theology
- **Conservative Jewish**: Valued as an important stratum of Jewish intellectual and spiritual history
- **Academic**: Analyzed as a medieval literary and mystical composition with complex textual history
- **Comparative**: Studied alongside Sufi, Christian mystical, and Neoplatonic traditions for shared themes of divine emanation
- **Kabbalistic**: The supreme text of the kabbalistic tradition, the key to understanding the Sefirot and the hidden Torah

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Sefirot | The ten divine emanations through which Ein Sof creates and sustains the world |
| Ein Sof | The infinite, unknowable essence of God beyond all attributes |
| Shekhinah | The feminine divine presence, exiled with Israel and longing for reunification |
| Torah Commentary | Every Torah narrative conceals multiple layers of mystical meaning |
| Tikkun | The process of cosmic repair through human spiritual action |
| Gilgul | Transmigration of souls as part of divine justice and mercy |
