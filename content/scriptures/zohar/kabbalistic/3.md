## Kabbalistic Reading

For the kabbalistic tradition, Zohar Parashat Lekh Lekha (Go Forth) is not merely a text to be read but a portal into the living structure of divine reality. Kabbalists approach this section as a map of the Sefirot in their dynamic interplay, encoded within the Torah's narrative of Lekh Lekha (Go Forth). Every word, letter, and even the shapes of the Hebrew characters conceal references to the ten emanations through which Ein Sof creates, sustains, and governs the cosmos. This section particularly illuminates the Sefirah of Chesed (Lovingkindness) and its interactions with the other divine attributes.

The kabbalistic reading practice involves *hitbonenut* (contemplative meditation) on the Zohar's imagery, allowing the practitioner to ascend through the worlds of Asiyah (Action), Yetzirah (Formation), Beriah (Creation), and Atzilut (Emanation). Rabbi Isaac Luria taught that this section encodes specific *kavanot* (mystical intentions) that, when activated through proper study and prayer, contribute to the cosmic process of *tikkun* — the repair of the shattered vessels and the reunification of the Holy One with the Shekhinah. The passage is studied not only intellectually but devotionally, often by candlelight, in the late hours of the night when the gates of heaven are believed to be most open.

## Key Themes

- The dynamics of the Sefirot as revealed through the narrative of Lekh Lekha (Go Forth)
- Mystical meditations (*kavanot*) encoded in the text
- The interplay of divine masculine (*Kudsha Brikhu Hu*) and feminine (*Shekhinah*)
- Letter mysticism: the creative and transformative power of Hebrew letters
- Practical Kabbalah: the use of divine names for spiritual elevation and protection

## Key Passages

> "Come and see: when the righteous one occupies himself with the Torah at night, a thread of grace is drawn upon him during the day, and he dwells with the Shekhinah."

Kabbalists understand this passage literally — Torah study, especially Zohar study at night, creates real channels of divine energy (*shefa*) that flow through the Sefirot and into the practitioner's soul. This teaching is the foundation of the practice of *Tikkun Chatzot* (the Midnight Rectification), in which kabbalists rise at midnight to mourn the exile of the Shekhinah and study the Zohar.

## Commentary Tradition

The supreme kabbalistic commentaries on this section include the *Or Yakar* of Rabbi Moshe Cordovero (the Ramak), the Lurianic teachings as recorded by Rabbi Chaim Vital in *Etz Chaim* and *Sha'ar ha-Kavanot*, the *Sulam* commentary of Rabbi Yehuda Ashlag, and the teachings of Rabbi Shalom Sharabi (the Rashash) as preserved in the *Nahar Shalom*. Hasidic masters — the Maggid of Mezeritch, Rabbi Nachman of Breslov, and the Tanya's author Rabbi Schneur Zalman of Liadi — built entire spiritual systems upon Zoharic foundations.
