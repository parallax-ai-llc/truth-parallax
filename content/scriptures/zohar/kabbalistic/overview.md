## Overview

Within the kabbalistic tradition itself, the Zohar is the supreme text — the "holy Zohar" (*Zohar ha-Kadosh*) — surpassed in authority only by the Torah itself. Kabbalists from the fourteenth century onward received the Zohar as the authentic teaching of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, revealed through divine inspiration (*ruach ha-kodesh*) and concealed for a millennium before its disclosure. The Zohar is not merely studied but experienced: kabbalists practice *hitbonenut* (contemplative meditation) on its passages, using the text as a vehicle for ascent through the Sefirot and communion with the divine.

The great sixteenth-century kabbalist Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Ari) built his entire theosophical system upon the Zohar, reading its narratives as encoded descriptions of cosmic processes — *tzimtzum* (divine contraction), *shevirat ha-kelim* (shattering of the vessels), and *tikkun* (cosmic repair). Lurianic Kabbalah became the dominant framework for reading the Zohar, influencing Hasidism, Sephardic mystical practice, and even Sabbatean and Frankist movements. For kabbalists, every word of the Zohar contains infinite depths of meaning, and the text is believed to possess inherent spiritual power — its recitation can heal, protect, and elevate the soul.
