## Overview

Within Orthodox Judaism, the Zohar holds a place of extraordinary reverence, particularly among Sephardic communities and Hasidic movements. The text is attributed to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, a Tanna of the second century, and is received as a divinely inspired work of Torah commentary that reveals the hidden dimensions of the Written and Oral Torah. Many Orthodox authorities — from Rabbi Yosef Karo to the Vilna Gaon — engaged deeply with the Zohar, and its influence permeates the Shulchan Aruch, the standard code of Jewish law.

Hasidic Judaism, founded in the eighteenth century by the Baal Shem Tov, places the Zohar at the center of its spiritual practice. Hasidic masters teach that studying the Zohar, even without full comprehension, purifies the soul and draws down divine light. In Sephardic tradition, the Zohar is read communally on Shabbat afternoons and during the night vigil of Shavuot (*Tikkun Leil Shavuot*). Orthodox engagement with the Zohar is characterized by deep piety, reverence for its authority, and integration of its teachings into daily prayer and ritual observance.
