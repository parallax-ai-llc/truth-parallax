## Orthodox Jewish Reading

The Orthodox Jewish tradition receives Zohar Parashat Vayishlach (And He Sent) as sacred Torah commentary revealed by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and his companions. This section is studied with deep reverence in yeshivot and batei midrash, particularly within Hasidic and Sephardic communities where the Zohar's authority is considered second only to the Torah and Talmud. The teachings here illuminate the hidden dimensions of the Torah portion, revealing how every narrative and commandment connects to the divine Sefirot and the cosmic drama of creation and redemption.

Orthodox commentators emphasize that this section's mystical teachings must be understood within the framework of halakhah (Jewish law) and the broader tradition of Torah she-baal peh (the Oral Torah). The Zohar does not replace or contradict the Talmudic tradition but deepens it, showing how the mitzvot operate on both the revealed and concealed levels of reality. The section's references to Chokhmah and the dynamics of the Sefirot are understood as describing real metaphysical processes activated by human observance of the commandments.

## Key Themes

- The hidden Torah within the narrative of Vayishlach (And He Sent)
- The interplay between divine judgment and mercy as reflected in the Sefirot
- The role of righteous souls in maintaining cosmic balance
- Prayer and mitzvot as vehicles for drawing down divine light
- The sanctity of Shabbat and its mystical dimensions

## Key Passages

> "Rabbi Shimon said: When the Holy One, blessed be He, created the world, He engraved the letters of the Torah in the supernal light, and from them all worlds were formed."

This passage, characteristic of the Zohar's approach in this section, teaches that the Torah is not merely a book but the very blueprint of creation. Orthodox study emphasizes the literal reality of this teaching — the Hebrew letters possess creative power, and their study connects the soul to the divine source.

## Commentary Tradition

The major Orthodox commentators on this section include Rabbi Moshe Cordovero (*Pardes Rimonim*), Rabbi Isaac Luria as transmitted by Rabbi Chaim Vital (*Etz Chaim*), and the Hasidic masters, particularly the Baal Shem Tov and Rabbi Nachman of Breslov. The Sulam commentary by Rabbi Yehuda Ashlag provides a systematic interpretation that has become standard in many Orthodox study circles.
