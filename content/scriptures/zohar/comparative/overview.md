## Overview

The Zohar offers extraordinarily rich material for comparative study of mystical and theosophical traditions. Its doctrine of divine emanation through the Sefirot parallels Neoplatonic theories of procession from the One, Sufi teachings on the divine attributes (*sifat*), and Christian trinitarian theology. The Zohar's language of erotic union between masculine and feminine aspects of the divine resonates with the bridal mysticism of Bernard of Clairvaux, the Sufi poetry of Ibn Arabi, and Hindu Shakta theology.

Comparative scholars examine the Zohar alongside contemporary medieval mystical works — Meister Eckhart's sermons, the *Futuhat al-Makkiyya* of Ibn Arabi, and the Neoplatonic texts circulating in thirteenth-century Spain — to trace shared intellectual currents. The concept of Ein Sof as an utterly transcendent, unknowable absolute is compared with the apophatic theology of Pseudo-Dionysius, the Sufi concept of the divine essence (*dhat*), and the Buddhist notion of sunyata. The Zohar's doctrine of cosmic tikkun (repair) is studied alongside Manichaean and Gnostic ideas of a shattered or fallen divine light requiring restoration.
