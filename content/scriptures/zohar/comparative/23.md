## Comparative Reading

Comparative study of Zohar Parashat Idra Rabba and Idra Zuta (Greater and Lesser Assemblies) reveals striking parallels and illuminating contrasts with mystical and theosophical traditions across the world's religions. This section's treatment of divine emanation, the hidden structures of reality, and the soul's relationship to the transcendent invites comparison with Neoplatonic philosophy, Sufi metaphysics, Christian mysticism, Hindu Vedanta, and Buddhist thought.

The Zohar's account of the Sefirot as they relate to the Idra Rabba and Idra Zuta (Greater and Lesser Assemblies) narrative parallels the Neoplatonic doctrine of procession from the One through successive hypostases. The text's emphasis on the erotic dimension of divine unity — the union of the Holy One, blessed be He, with the Shekhinah — resonates with the bridal mysticism of the Song of Songs tradition in Christianity, the Sufi concept of *fana* (annihilation in the divine beloved), and the Shaiva-Shakta theology of Hindu tantra. Comparative scholars analyze these parallels not to flatten differences but to illuminate the diverse ways human beings have sought to articulate the experience of the sacred.

## Key Themes

- Emanation and the chain of being: Sefirot, Neoplatonic hypostases, and Sufi divine attributes
- Mystical hermeneutics: allegorical reading in Zohar, Sufi *ta'wil*, and Christian *lectio divina*
- The feminine divine: Shekhinah, Sophia, Shakti, and the Virgin Mary
- Apophatic theology: Ein Sof, the Neoplatonic One, *dhat* in Sufism, and Buddhist emptiness
- Cosmic repair: tikkun, Manichaean light-gathering, and bodhisattva compassion

## Key Passages

> "Before the world was created, all the souls of the righteous were concealed in the divine thought."

This concept of pre-existent souls finds parallels in Platonic philosophy (the pre-existence of forms), Sufi teaching on the covenant of *alast* (Quran 7:172), and Buddhist notions of consciousness continuity. Comparative scholars explore how different traditions resolve the tension between the soul's divine origin and its earthly embodiment.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholarship on this section draws on the work of Henry Corbin (comparing Kabbalah with Iranian Sufism), Moshe Idel (Kabbalah and ecstatic mysticism), and Elliot Wolfson (comparing Zoharic symbolism with phenomenological approaches). The field is enriched by scholars who read the Zohar alongside the *Futuhat al-Makkiyya* of Ibn Arabi, the writings of Meister Eckhart, and the *Enneads* of Plotinus.
