## Overview

Conservative Judaism engages the Zohar as a profoundly important stratum of Jewish religious literature, one that shaped the development of Jewish law, liturgy, and theology from the medieval period onward. The Conservative approach characteristically combines reverence for the text's spiritual depth with critical awareness of its historical context — acknowledging the scholarly consensus that the Zohar was composed in thirteenth-century Spain while honoring the genuine mystical insights it contains.

Conservative scholars have explored how Zoharic ideas entered mainstream Jewish practice — from the Kabbalat Shabbat liturgy to customs surrounding mourning, prayer, and Yom Kippur. The text is studied in Conservative rabbinical seminaries as part of a comprehensive engagement with Jewish intellectual history. Conservative Judaism values the Zohar's emphasis on kavvanah (spiritual intention), the cosmic significance of mitzvot, and the interplay between human action and divine response, while subjecting these ideas to the same historical-critical analysis applied to all Jewish texts.
