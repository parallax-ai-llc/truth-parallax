## Overview

The Ten Wings (十翼, Shí Yì) are a collection of commentaries and appendices traditionally attributed to Confucius, though modern scholarship dates most of them to the late Warring States and early Han periods (c. 4th–2nd centuries BCE). Together with the original hexagram texts and line statements, the Ten Wings form the received text of the *Yijing* (I Ching, Classic of Changes) — one of the Five Classics of Confucianism and one of the most influential texts in East Asian civilization.

## Structure

The Ten Wings comprise seven distinct commentarial texts, three of which are divided into upper and lower sections:

1–2. **Tuan Zhuan** (彖傳, Commentary on the Judgments): Interprets the hexagram judgments attributed to King Wen
3–4. **Xiang Zhuan** (象傳, Commentary on the Images): Explains the symbolism of each hexagram and its component lines
5–6. **Xi Ci Zhuan** (繫辭傳, Great Commentary/Appended Remarks): The most philosophical and cosmological of the Wings, offering a comprehensive theory of the *Yijing*'s meaning and method
7. **Wenyan Zhuan** (文言傳, Commentary on the Words): Detailed commentary on the first two hexagrams, *Qian* (Heaven) and *Kun* (Earth)
8. **Shuo Gua Zhuan** (說卦傳, Discussion of the Trigrams): Explains the symbolism and correlations of the eight trigrams
9. **Xu Gua Zhuan** (序卦傳, Sequence of the Hexagrams): Explains the logical order of the 64 hexagrams
10. **Za Gua Zhuan** (雜卦傳, Miscellaneous Notes): Brief contrasting descriptions of paired hexagrams

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Confucian**: The commentaries that transformed divination into moral philosophy
- **Neo-Confucian**: The metaphysical foundation for Song-Ming Neo-Confucian cosmology
- **Daoist**: A source of cosmological concepts shared between Confucian and Daoist traditions
- **Buddhist-Chinese**: Engaged by Chinese Buddhist thinkers in the project of sinifying Buddhism
- **Academic**: A key subject of Chinese textual criticism, intellectual history, and comparative philosophy
- **Comparative**: A unique text for cross-cultural dialogue on cosmology, divination, and philosophy

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Yin-Yang | The complementary interaction of cosmic forces as the basis of all change |
| Dao/Way | The underlying pattern of cosmic change that the sage discerns and follows |
| De/Virtue | The moral power cultivated through alignment with the Dao |
| Xiang/Images | The symbolic language through which the cosmos communicates its patterns |
| Bian/Change | The fundamental nature of reality as constant transformation |
