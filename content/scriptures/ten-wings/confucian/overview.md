The Confucian reading of the Ten Wings sees in them the transformation of the *Yijing* from a divination manual into a comprehensive guide for moral self-cultivation and wise governance. The traditional attribution of the Wings to Confucius — while historically questionable — reflects the genuine Confucian conviction that the *Yijing*'s hexagrams encode not merely predictions about the future but patterns of moral reality that the *junzi* (exemplary person) must learn to discern and embody.

The Ten Wings establish the interpretive framework through which the Confucian tradition reads the hexagrams: as images of ethical situations requiring the exercise of virtue (*de*), humaneness (*ren*), and propriety (*li*). The Great Commentary (Xi Ci Zhuan) in particular articulates a moral cosmology in which human virtue is continuous with cosmic order — the sage is one who aligns personal conduct with the patterns of Heaven and Earth.
