Comparative study of the Ten Wings opens dialogue between Chinese and Western philosophical traditions on fundamental questions: the nature of change, the relationship between cosmos and ethics, the role of symbolic thinking in philosophical inquiry, and the possibility of discerning meaningful patterns in apparently random events. The Wings' cosmological philosophy is compared with Greek cosmological speculation (Heraclitus on change, Pythagoras on number), Indian philosophical systems (Samkhya cosmology, Buddhist dependent origination), and Western process philosophy (Whitehead, Bergson).

The *Yijing*'s unique combination of divination and philosophy invites comparison with other traditions' approaches to the relationship between sacred knowledge and practical wisdom: the Delphic oracle, the Urim and Thummim, African *Ifa* divination, and the Tibetan *mo* oracle. Comparative analysis examines what these different systems share and how the Ten Wings' philosophical elaboration of the *Yijing*'s divinatory system represents a distinctive Chinese contribution to world philosophy.
