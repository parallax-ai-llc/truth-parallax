The Buddhist-Chinese reading of the Ten Wings represents one dimension of the broader project of Buddhism's sinification — its adaptation to Chinese cultural categories and intellectual traditions. Chinese Buddhist thinkers, particularly in the Huayan school, found in the Wings' cosmological language useful categories for expressing Buddhist philosophical concepts in Chinese terms.

The concept of the mutual interpenetration of all phenomena — central to Huayan Buddhism — found a congenial expression in the *Yijing*'s vision of a cosmos in which every element participates in and reflects every other. The relationship between *li* (principle) and *shi* (phenomena) in Huayan thought drew on the same vocabulary that the Ten Wings had established for discussing the relationship between cosmic pattern and material manifestation. This cross-pollination between *Yijing* thought and Chinese Buddhism produced a distinctive philosophical synthesis.
