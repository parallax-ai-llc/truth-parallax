The great debate at Janaka's court belongs to a worldwide tradition of intellectual contests with high stakes. Greek philosophical *agon* (contest), the Tibetan Buddhist debate tradition, medieval European university disputations, and rabbinic *machloket* (argument) all share the conviction that truth is tested and refined through adversarial dialogue. Yajnavalkya's threat that Gargi's head will "fall off" if she questions too far has parallels in the Zen tradition of the master silencing the student's conceptual mind.

The Antaryami teaching — a being who indwells all things as their inner controller while remaining unknown to them — resonates with the Stoic concept of the *logos* immanent in all matter, the Quaker "Inner Light," the Islamic concept of *al-Batin* (the Hidden, one of the ninety-nine names of Allah), and the Kabbalistic *Ein Sof* pervading all the *sefirot*. Each tradition recognizes a divine presence that is closer to us than we are to ourselves, yet remains mysteriously beyond our grasp.
