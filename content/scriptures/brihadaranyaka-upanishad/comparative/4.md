Yajnavalkya's analysis of the three states of consciousness — waking, dreaming, and deep sleep — anticipates phenomenological investigations across traditions. Augustine's analysis of memory and time in the *Confessions*, Descartes' dream argument in the *Meditations*, and the Buddhist *Abhidharma* analysis of consciousness-moments all share the project of examining experience from within to discover what is fundamental and what is constructed.

The teaching that the self creates dream worlds from its own light, without external material, resonates with the Daoist *butterfly dream* of Zhuangzi and the Buddhist *vijnanavada* (consciousness-only) school, both of which question the assumed priority of waking experience over dreaming. The description of the self in deep sleep as a "mass of consciousness" without object parallels the Plotinian vision of the One, the Sufi experience of *fana*, and the Buddhist *nirodha samapatti* — states where ordinary subject-object consciousness is transcended.
