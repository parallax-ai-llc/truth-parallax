The Brihadaranyaka's opening cosmogony — creation emerging from death, hunger, and desire — presents a vision of origins strikingly different from the creation-by-divine-word model found in Genesis, the Quran, and many other traditions. It resonates more closely with Hesiod's *Theogony*, where creation emerges from Chaos, and with the Babylonian *Enuma Elish*, where the cosmos is fashioned from the body of a slain deity. The identification of the sacrificial horse with the universe parallels the Norse myth of Ymir, whose body becomes the world.

The prayer "Lead me from the unreal to the real, from darkness to light, from death to immortality" (1.3.28) has become one of the most universally cited spiritual utterances. Its threefold structure resonates with the Christian baptismal renunciation of darkness and affirmation of light, the Zoroastrian commitment to truth (*asha*) over falsehood (*druj*), and the Buddhist Three Refuges. The aspiration from death to immortality is a universal human longing expressed across every civilization.
