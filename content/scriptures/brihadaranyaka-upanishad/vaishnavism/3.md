Chapter 3 records the great philosophical debate at King Janaka's court, where Yajnavalkya faces eight challengers. Vaishnavas read this as a dramatic demonstration that all partial knowledge leads ultimately to the recognition of Vishnu's supremacy. Each challenger represents a particular philosophical position that, when pressed to its limit by Yajnavalkya, reveals its dependence on the supreme Lord. The chapter's famous Antaryami Brahmana (3.7) — describing the inner controller who dwells within earth, water, fire, the self, and all beings — is the cornerstone of Vaishnava theology.

The inner controller (*antaryamin*) described in 3.7 is Vishnu-Narayana, who "dwells within the self, whom the self does not know, whose body the self is, who controls the self from within." This passage is Ramanuja's primary proof-text for the Vishishtadvaita doctrine that all selves are the body of God — real, conscious, and individual, yet entirely sustained and governed by the Lord who indwells them.
