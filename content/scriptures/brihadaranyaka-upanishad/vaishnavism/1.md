Chapter 1 opens with the Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice) allegory, in which the sacrificial horse is identified with the cosmos — its head is the dawn, its eye the sun, its breath the wind. Vaishnava interpreters read this cosmic identification as revealing Vishnu's *vishvarupa* (universal form): the entire universe is the Lord's body, and the horse sacrifice symbolizes the devotee's offering of all creation back to its divine source. The cosmogony that follows — in which death/hunger creates the waters and from the waters emerges the cosmic egg — is read as Vishnu's creative act through His agent Brahma.

The chapter's teaching on Prana as the supreme deity among the faculties (1.3) reinforces the Vaishnava understanding of Vishnu as the life-sustaining Lord. When the other faculties — speech, sight, hearing — are overcome by evil, only Prana (identified with Vishnu) remains pure. The hymn of the Prana (1.3.28) — "Lead me from the unreal to the real, from darkness to light, from death to immortality" — is read as a prayer to Vishnu for liberation.
