Shankara treats Chapter 3's philosophical debate as a progressive demonstration that all empirical knowledge points to, and is grounded in, the one Brahman. Each challenger's question — about the thread connecting the worlds, the inner controller, the imperishable — receives an answer that transcends the questioner's framework. Yajnavalkya's warning to Gargi — "Do not question beyond this, or your head will fall off" (3.6) — indicates the point where conceptual inquiry must give way to direct realization.

The Antaryami Brahmana (3.7) is read by Shankara as describing Brahman's relationship to the empirical world from the standpoint of *vyavahara* (conventional reality). The inner controller appears distinct from the beings He indwells only as long as the appearance of multiplicity persists. In ultimate truth, there is no controller and no controlled — there is only Brahman. The teaching on the *akshara* (imperishable, 3.8) — that which is "not gross, not subtle, not short, not long" — exemplifies the *neti neti* method at its most rigorous.
