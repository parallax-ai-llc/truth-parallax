## Overview

Shankara's commentary on the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad (*Brihadaranyakopanishad Bhashya*) is his longest and most detailed work. He treats this Upanishad as the supreme authority for Advaita Vedanta, containing the *mahavakya* "Aham Brahmasmi" ("I am Brahman," 1.4.10). The entire text is read as a progressive revelation that the individual self, stripped of all superimposed limitations, is identical with the infinite, qualityless Brahman.

## Theological Emphases

The "neti neti" method (2.3.6) is Shankara's paradigm for the Advaita approach: Brahman is known not by affirming qualities but by negating everything that is not Brahman. Yajnavalkya's teaching in the Maitreyi dialogue — that the self cannot be known as an object because it is the eternal subject — establishes the Advaita doctrine of the *sakshi* (witness-consciousness). The famous passage on deep sleep (4.3.21-32), where the self exists as pure awareness without duality, is Shankara's primary evidence that the self's essential nature is non-dual consciousness temporarily obscured by *avidya*.
