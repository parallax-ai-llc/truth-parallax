Chapter 2 contains two of Shankara's most important proof-texts. The Maitreyi dialogue (2.4) establishes the Advaita teaching that the self (*atman*) is the sole locus of value — all love, all desire, all meaning derive from the self and point back to it. Yajnavalkya's declaration that "it is for the sake of the self that all is dear" does not mean egoism but reveals that Brahman-consciousness is the hidden ground of all experience of value.

The Madhu Vidya (2.5) demonstrates the interconnectedness of all phenomena in Brahman. The "honey" metaphor shows that every element exists for and through every other — a web of mutual dependence whose center is the immortal *purusha* (Brahman). Shankara's key move is to identify this cosmic *purusha* with the individual *atman*: the same consciousness that pervades the cosmos is the consciousness reading these words. The teaching that the self is "not this, not this" (*neti neti*, first appearing at 2.3.6) establishes the method of negation as the primary Advaita approach to Brahman.
