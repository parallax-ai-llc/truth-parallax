Shankara reads the opening Ashvamedha allegory as a meditation (*upasana*) rather than a description of actual sacrifice. The identification of the horse's limbs with cosmic elements teaches that the same Brahman is present in all phenomena — the ritual correspondence reveals the non-dual reality underlying apparent multiplicity. The cosmogony of 1.2, in which death/Prajapati creates the world through desire, is treated as a provisional account of creation from the empirical standpoint (*vyavaharika*), while from the absolute standpoint (*paramarthika*) there is no creation at all.

The Prana narrative (1.3) establishes a pattern central to Advaita: the faculties (speech, sight, hearing) operate within the dualistic realm and are therefore corruptible, while Prana — symbolizing the underlying consciousness — transcends duality. The prayer "Lead me from the unreal to the real" is the quintessential Advaita aspiration: may ignorance (*avidya*) be replaced by knowledge of Brahman, which alone is real.
