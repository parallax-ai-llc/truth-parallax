Chapter 4 is Shankara's most important text for the analysis of consciousness and its states. Yajnavalkya's teaching to Janaka progresses through four dialogues (4.1-4), each deepening the understanding of the self. The key passage is the analysis of deep sleep (4.3.21-32), where the self exists as pure awareness without any object — "a mass of consciousness" (*vijnanamaya*) — which Shankara takes as direct evidence that the self's essential nature is non-dual consciousness, temporarily free from the superimpositions of *avidya*.

The dream teaching (4.3.9-14) is equally important: the self creates entire worlds in dreams from its own light, without any external material. This demonstrates that consciousness does not depend on external objects — it is self-luminous and self-sufficient. If the dream world is a creation of consciousness, the waking world too may be a projection. The teaching on liberation (4.4.6-7) — "when all desires that lodge in the heart are liberated, then the mortal becomes immortal, here he attains Brahman" — is Shankara's proof-text for *jivanmukti* (liberation while alive).
