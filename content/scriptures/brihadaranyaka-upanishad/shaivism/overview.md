## Overview

Shaiva interpreters find in the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad profound resonances with the recognition (*pratyabhijna*) philosophy of Kashmir Shaivism. Yajnavalkya's method of "neti neti" — negating everything that can be objectified to arrive at the pure subject — parallels the Shaiva practice of stripping away limited identifications to recognize one's nature as Paramashiva. The Upanishad's teaching that in deep sleep the self "is embraced by the all-knowing Self" (4.3.21) aligns with the Shaiva understanding of *turiya* as the ground of all states of consciousness.

## Theological Emphases

The Brihadaranyaka's description of the self as "the seer of seeing, the hearer of hearing, the thinker of thought" (3.4) is read through the Shaiva lens of *prakasha-vimarsha* — luminous consciousness reflecting upon itself. The Maitreyi dialogue's teaching that the self alone is the source of all love resonates with the Shaiva understanding that all desire is ultimately the longing of consciousness to recognize itself. Yajnavalkya's fearless engagement with multiple questioners at Janaka's court embodies the Shaiva ideal of the liberated sage who acts freely in the world.
