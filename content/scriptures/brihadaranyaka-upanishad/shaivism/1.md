The opening cosmogony of Chapter 1, in which the universe emerges from a primordial state of hunger/death, resonates with the Shaiva understanding of creation as Shiva's act of self-limitation. The cosmic horse sacrifice is read as the dissolution and re-creation of the universe — Shiva's cosmic dance of destruction and renewal. The identification of the horse's body with the elements of the cosmos mirrors the Shaiva concept of the universe as Shiva's body (*vishvarupa*), with every element being a manifestation of consciousness.

The supremacy of Prana over the other faculties (1.3) aligns with the Shaiva teaching that pure consciousness (*chiti*) transcends and sustains all limited functions. The famous prayer "Lead me from the unreal to the real" (1.3.28) is understood as the aspirant's plea for the removal of *anava mala* (the fundamental impurity of limited self-identification) that conceals Shiva's nature within the individual soul.
