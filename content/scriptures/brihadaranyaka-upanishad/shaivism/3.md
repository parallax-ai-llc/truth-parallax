The great debate at Janaka's court in Chapter 3 enacts the Shaiva principle that all partial perspectives must be transcended in the recognition of the one consciousness. Each challenger presents a valid but limited understanding, and Yajnavalkya's responses push beyond every limitation to the unlimited awareness that is Paramashiva. The climactic moment when Gargi asks about the ultimate ground of existence and Yajnavalkya answers with the imperishable (*akshara*) points to Shiva as the unchanging consciousness behind all change.

The Antaryami Brahmana (3.7), describing the being who dwells within all things as their inner controller while remaining unknown to them, resonates with the Shaiva concept of Shiva as the *sakshin* (witness) who pervades all experience without being affected by it. The inner controller is not separate from the beings He indwells — He is their very consciousness, the awareness by which they know without being an object of their knowledge.
