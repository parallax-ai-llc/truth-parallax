Chapter 4's systematic teaching on the states of consciousness is central to Shaiva philosophy. Yajnavalkya's analysis of waking (where the self engages the external world), dreaming (where the self creates from its own light), and deep sleep (where the self rests in undifferentiated awareness) maps the three states that the fourth state (*turiya*) transcends and pervades. The Shaiva recognition tradition sees in this analysis the template for understanding how Shiva-consciousness manifests in and through all states of experience.

The teaching that in deep sleep the self "is embraced by the all-knowing Self" (4.3.21) is read as the temporary dissolution of individual identity into Shiva's undifferentiated consciousness. The dream state's revelation that the self creates entire worlds from its own light demonstrates the creative power (*vimarsha*) inherent in consciousness — the same power by which Shiva creates the waking world. Liberation, taught in 4.4, is the permanent recognition that one's consciousness is identical with the all-creating, all-knowing Shiva-consciousness.
