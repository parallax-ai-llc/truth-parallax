The Madhu Vidya's teaching that all elements are mutually "honey" for each other resonates with the Shaiva concept of *pratibimba* (mutual reflection): all things reflect Shiva's consciousness in each other, creating the web of interconnection that constitutes the manifest world. The luminous, immortal person (*purusha*) who is the self of each element and the self of the individual (2.5.14) is recognized as Shiva — the consciousness that is simultaneously the subject of every experience and the essence of every object.

Maitreyi's refusal of mere wealth and her demand for immortality embodies the Shaiva ideal of *mumukshutva* (the burning desire for liberation). Yajnavalkya's teaching that "the self should be seen, heard, reflected upon, and meditated upon" (2.4.5) prescribes the fourfold practice of Shaiva contemplation — from initial hearing (*shravana*) to the direct recognition (*pratyabhijna*) of one's identity with Shiva. The teaching that when the self is known, "all this is known" reveals the Shaiva truth that consciousness is the sole reality.
