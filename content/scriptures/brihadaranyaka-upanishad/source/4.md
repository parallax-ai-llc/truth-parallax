---
title: "Brihadaranyaka Upanishad - Chapter 4"
scripture: "brihadaranyaka-upanishad"
chapter: 4
---

**1** When Yajnavalkya was about to enter another order of life, he said: "Maitreyi, I am going to leave this home. Let me make a settlement between you and Katyayani."

**2** Maitreyi said: "My lord, if this whole earth, full of wealth, belonged to me, should I be immortal by it, or not?" Yajnavalkya said: "No. Your life would be like that of the wealthy. There is no hope of immortality by wealth."

**3** Maitreyi said: "What should I do with that by which I do not become immortal? What my lord knows, tell that to me."

**4** Yajnavalkya said: "You who are truly dear to me, you speak what is dear to me. Come, sit down, I will explain it to you, and mark well what I say."

**5** He said: "Verily, a husband is not dear, that you may love the husband; but that you may love the Self, therefore a husband is dear. Verily, a wife is not dear, that you may love the wife; but that you may love the Self, therefore a wife is dear."

**6** "Verily, sons are not dear, that you may love the sons; but that you may love the Self, therefore sons are dear. Verily, wealth is not dear, that you may love wealth; but that you may love the Self, therefore wealth is dear."

**7** "Verily, everything is not dear that you may love everything; but that you may love the Self, therefore everything is dear. Verily, the Self is to be seen, to be heard, to be perceived, to be marked. O Maitreyi! When the Self has been seen, heard, perceived, and known, then all this is known."

**8** "As a lump of salt when thrown into water melts away and the lump cannot be taken out, but wherever we taste the water it is salt — even so, O Maitreyi, the individual self, dissolved, is the Eternal, pure intelligence only."

**9** "It rises from out these elements, and vanishes again into them. When he has departed, there is no more knowledge of particular things." So said Yajnavalkya.

**10** Then Maitreyi said: "Here thou hast bewildered me, sir, when thou sayest that having departed there is no more knowledge." Yajnavalkya replied: "O Maitreyi, I say nothing that is bewildering. This is enough for knowledge."

**11** "For where there is duality, as it were, there one sees the other, smells the other, hears the other, speaks to the other, thinks of the other, knows the other. But where everything has become the Self, then by what and whom should one see? By what and whom should one smell? By what and whom should one hear? By what and whom should one speak? By what and whom should one think? By what and whom should one know?"

**12** "By what should he know Him by whom he knows all this? That Self is to be described as Not this, Not this. He is incomprehensible, for He cannot be comprehended. He is indestructible, for He cannot be destroyed. He is unattached, for He does not attach Himself. He is unfettered — He does not suffer, He is not injured. By what, O Maitreyi, should one know the Knower?"
