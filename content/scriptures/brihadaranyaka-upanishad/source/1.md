---
title: "Brihadaranyaka Upanishad - Chapter 1"
scripture: "brihadaranyaka-upanishad"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Om! The head of the sacrificial horse is the dawn, its eye the sun, its vital breath the air, its open mouth the fire called Vaisvanara, and the body of the sacrificial horse is the year.

**2** Its back is heaven, its belly the sky, its hoof the earth, its sides the four quarters, its ribs the intermediate quarters, its members the seasons, its joints the months and half-months, its feet the days and nights, its bones the stars, its flesh the clouds.

**3** Its half-digested food is the sand, its veins the rivers, its liver and lungs the mountains, its hairs the herbs and trees. Its fore part is the ascending sun, its hind part the descending sun.

**4** When the horse shakes itself, then it lightens; when it kicks, it thunders; when it makes water, it rains; voice is its voice.

**5** In the beginning there was nothing here at all. This was covered by Death, by hunger, for hunger is death. He created the mind, thinking, "Let me have a self." Then he moved about, worshipping. From him, thus worshipping, water was produced.

**6** The water that was produced — that is what the Arka signifies. Happiness came to him who thus knew the reason why the Arka is called Arka.

**7** That which was there as the froth of the water was solidified and became the earth. On it he rested. From him, thus rested and heated, his essence of brightness came forth as fire.

**8** He divided himself threefold: one-third was fire, one-third was the sun, one-third was the air. He also is the vital breath divided threefold.

**9** In the beginning, this universe was the Self alone, in the shape of a person. He looked around and saw nothing else but himself. He first said, "I am." Therefore he was called "I." Hence, even now, when a person is called, he first says, "It is I," and then says whatever other name he may have.

**10** He was afraid. Therefore a person who is alone is afraid. He thought, "Since there is nothing but myself, of what am I afraid?" Then his fear departed, for of what should he have been afraid? It is of a second that one is afraid.

**11** He was not happy. Therefore a person who is alone is not happy. He desired a companion. He became as large as a man and wife together. He then made this his Self to fall in two, and thence arose husband and wife. Therefore Yajnavalkya said: "We two are thus each like half a shell." Therefore this space is filled by the wife.
