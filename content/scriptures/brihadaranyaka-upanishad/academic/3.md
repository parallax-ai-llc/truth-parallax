Chapter 3 preserves what may be the most detailed record of philosophical debate in ancient Indian literature. The eight challengers at Janaka's court — Ashvala, Artabhaga, Bhujyu, Ushasta, Kahola, Gargi, Uddalaka, and Shakalya — each represent different philosophical concerns, from ritual knowledge to cosmology to the nature of the imperishable. Scholars debate whether this records actual debates or is a literary construction designed to establish Yajnavalkya's philosophical supremacy.

The Antaryami Brahmana (3.7) is one of the most structurally elaborate passages in the Upanishads, with its formulaic repetition describing the inner controller in earth, water, fire, atmosphere, wind, sky, sun, moon, stars, space, darkness, light, all beings, breath, speech, eye, ear, mind, skin, intellect, and the generative organ. The escalating intensity of Gargi's questions and Yajnavalkya's warning about her head (3.6) have been analyzed as reflecting the real dangers associated with philosophical inquiry in a culture where knowledge carried power.
