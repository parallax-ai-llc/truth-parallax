Chapter 1 opens with the Ashvamedha allegory, one of the most striking examples of Vedic symbolic thinking. The identification of the sacrificial horse with the cosmos belongs to the Brahmana tradition of ritual homology (*bandhu*), in which ritual elements are systematically correlated with cosmic phenomena. Scholars debate whether this passage represents a genuine alternative to physical sacrifice or a supplementary meditation intended to accompany the actual ritual.

The cosmogony of 1.2 — creation through death/hunger — is notable for its departure from the more common Vedic creation accounts involving Prajapati or the cosmic purasha. Wendy Doniger and others have analyzed this as reflecting a distinctive philosophical strand that identifies the creative principle with desire and lack rather than with plenitude. The Prana narrative (1.3) belongs to the genre of "contest of the faculties" stories found across the Upanishads and Brahmanas, which scholars read as reflecting debates about the hierarchy of ritual specialists.
