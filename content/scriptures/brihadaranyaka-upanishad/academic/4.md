Chapter 4 represents the philosophical climax of the Brihadaranyaka, with Yajnavalkya's systematic teaching on the self, the states of consciousness, and liberation. The four dialogues with Janaka (4.1-4) have been analyzed as a pedagogical progression from preliminary teachings on the self's location and nature to the culminating realization. The analysis of dreaming and deep sleep (4.3) represents one of the earliest phenomenological investigations of consciousness in world literature.

The expanded Maitreyi dialogue in 4.5 repeats much of the material from 2.4 with additions, leading scholars to debate their relative chronology. The teaching on liberation (4.4) — particularly the passage "when all desires are released" — has been central to scholarly discussions about the Upanishadic concept of *moksha* and whether it was originally understood as a post-mortem state or an achievable condition in life. The chapter's description of the dying person's last moments and the departure of the self provides important evidence for early Indian concepts of death and the afterlife.
