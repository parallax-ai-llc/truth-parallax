## On Wealth and Poverty — Academic Analysis

Scholarly analysis of this section situates it within the broader context of Gnostic literary production and the specific theological tradition to which Sentences of Sextus belongs. The Sentences of Sextus is one of the most widely attested texts from late antiquity, known in Greek, Latin, Syriac, Armenian, and Georgian versions in addition to the Coptic Nag Hammadi copy.

This section — "On Wealth and Poverty" — raises questions about genre, literary composition, and theological classification. Its relationship to other Nag Hammadi texts, to the heresiological reports of the Church Fathers, and to the broader religious landscape of late antiquity illuminates both the text's distinctiveness and its participation in shared traditions of thought.

The Coptic text reveals features characteristic of translation from Greek, and scholars continue to debate questions of date, provenance, and community of origin based on the linguistic and theological evidence of passages like this one.
