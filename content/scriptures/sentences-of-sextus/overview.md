## Overview

The Sentences of Sextus (NHC XII,1) is a collection of Pythagorean-influenced ethical maxims that was widely circulated in both Christian and pagan forms throughout late antiquity. The Nag Hammadi version preserves a Coptic translation of a portion of the collection (approximately sentences 157-397 of the full corpus). The sentences address themes of self-knowledge, self-control, the care of the soul, the proper use of speech, and the relationship between the wise person and God. Unlike most Nag Hammadi texts, the Sentences of Sextus contains no specifically Gnostic mythology or cosmology; its inclusion in the library suggests that the Gnostic community at Nag Hammadi valued practical ethical instruction alongside theological speculation. The text was known to Origen, who attributed it to Pope Xystus (Sixtus) II, and to Jerome, who dismissed this attribution and recognized the sentences as pagan in origin.

## Structure

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | On Self-Knowledge and Wisdom | Maxims on the importance of knowing oneself and cultivating wisdom |
| 2 | On Self-Control and Desire | Instructions on mastering desire, anger, and other passions |
| 3 | On Speech and Silence | The proper use of speech, the value of silence, and the dangers of idle talk |
| 4 | On God and the Soul | The relationship between the wise person and God, and the nature of the soul |
| 5 | On Wealth and Poverty | Maxims on the proper attitude toward material possessions |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Included in the Nag Hammadi library as practical ethical guidance complementing speculative theology
- **Academic**: Central to the study of the transmission of Pythagorean ethics in Christian and Gnostic contexts
- **Comparative**: One of the clearest examples of cross-cultural ethical transmission in the ancient world, bridging Pythagorean, Stoic, and Christian traditions
- **Christian**: Widely circulated in early Christianity under the name of a bishop of Rome, demonstrating the church's appropriation of pagan ethical wisdom
- **Jewish**: Parallels the Jewish wisdom tradition (Proverbs, Sirach, Pirke Avot) in form and many themes
- **Interfaith**: Demonstrates that practical ethical wisdom often transcends sectarian boundaries

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Self-Knowledge | "Know yourself" — the Delphic maxim as the foundation of the ethical life |
| Self-Control | Mastery of desire and passion is the mark of the wise person |
| Speech and Silence | Words have power; the wise person speaks little and listens much |
| The Soul's Care | The soul requires constant attention and cultivation — more important than the body |
