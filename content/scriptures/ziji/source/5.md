---
title: "Ziji Section 5: The Ethical Foundations"
scripture: "ziji"
chapter: 5
---

The ethical teachings of Bon are rooted in the understanding that all beings are interconnected through the web of cause and effect. Every action of body, speech, and mind generates consequences that ripple outward through time and space, affecting not only the actor but all beings with whom they are connected.

The ten virtuous actions form the foundation of ethical practice. Three relate to the body: refraining from killing, from stealing, and from sexual misconduct. Four relate to speech: refraining from lying, from divisive speech, from harsh words, and from idle gossip. Three relate to the mind: refraining from covetousness, from ill will, and from wrong views. The practitioner who maintains these ten virtues establishes the basis for all higher practices.

Compassion is the supreme ethical principle. The Bon teachings distinguish between three levels of compassion. The first is the natural sympathy that arises when we witness the suffering of others. The second is the compassion that arises from understanding the causes of suffering and the possibility of liberation. The third is the unconditional compassion of the enlightened ones, which extends equally to all beings without exception and is inseparable from the recognition of the primordial state.

The practice of generosity is closely linked to compassion. The practitioner gives material goods to those in need, protection to those in danger, and the teachings to those who seek liberation. The highest form of generosity is the gift of fearlessness, which comes from helping beings understand that their true nature is beyond birth, death, and suffering.

The ethical teachings are not mere rules imposed from outside but natural expressions of the primordial state. When awareness recognizes its own nature, ethical behavior arises spontaneously, without effort or deliberation, because the realized being naturally acts in harmony with the wellbeing of all. The ethical precepts thus serve as both the foundation for practice and the natural expression of its fruition.
