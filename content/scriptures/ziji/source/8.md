The ritual practices of Bon serve multiple functions: purifying obstacles, generating merit, maintaining harmony with the natural world, and creating the conditions for spiritual realization. These practices range from simple daily observances to elaborate multi-day ceremonies requiring the participation of trained ritual specialists.

The fumigation ritual, the sang, is performed daily by many Bon practitioners. Fragrant substances including juniper, rhododendron, and various herbs are burned on a mountaintop or other high place, and the rising smoke carries offerings to the deities of the upper realms while purifying the environment. The sang prayer invokes the blessings of the enlightened ones and requests their protection for all beings.

The water purification ritual, the tru, addresses the beings of the water element and the naga spirits who dwell in rivers, lakes, and underground waters. Offerings are made to these powerful beings to maintain their goodwill and to heal environmental imbalances caused by human activity. The Bon tradition has long recognized the intimate connection between human behavior and environmental health, and these rituals express that understanding in practical form.

The longevity rituals, the tse, aim to extend the practitioner's life so that they may have more time to practice the teachings and benefit other beings. These rituals involve the visualization of longevity deities, the recitation of specific mantras, and the performance of life-force retrieval practices that gather scattered vital energies and consolidate them within the practitioner's body.

The elaborate ritual dances, the cham, performed by monks in elaborate costumes and masks, enact the victory of wisdom over ignorance, the subjugation of harmful forces, and the establishment of the enlightened mandala. These dances serve as both meditation practices for the performers and teachings for the observers, conveying profound spiritual truths through movement, music, and visual symbolism.
