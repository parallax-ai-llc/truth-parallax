---
title: "Ziji Section 6: The Practice of Meditation"
scripture: "ziji"
chapter: 6
---

Meditation in the Bon tradition encompasses a vast range of practices, from preliminary calming exercises to the most advanced methods of Dzogchen. The practitioner progresses through these stages according to their capacity and the guidance of their master, each stage building upon the foundation established by the previous ones.

The practice of shamatha, or calm abiding, establishes the stability of mind necessary for all further practice. The practitioner sits in the proper posture, with the body straight and relaxed, the breath natural, and the gaze directed into space. The mind is trained to rest without following thoughts, without suppressing them, and without becoming dull or drowsy. Through sustained practice, the turbulent mind gradually settles, like muddy water becoming clear when left undisturbed.

Vipashyana, or clear seeing, builds upon the stability of shamatha to investigate the nature of mind itself. The practitioner examines the arising, dwelling, and ceasing of thoughts, seeking to find their source, their location, and their destination. Through this investigation, the practitioner discovers that thoughts have no inherent existence, no fixed location, and no solid substance. They arise from emptiness, display themselves as luminous clarity, and dissolve back into emptiness.

The union of shamatha and vipashyana creates the basis for the direct recognition of the natural state. In this recognition, the practitioner does not look at the mind as an object but rests as the mind itself, in its natural condition, without alteration or fabrication. This is the entry point to the Dzogchen teachings, where all effortful practice gives way to the simple recognition of what has always been present.

The meditation practices are supported by specific physical exercises, breathing techniques, and the use of sound and visualization. These methods work with the subtle body, the channels, winds, and essences through which consciousness manifests in the physical form. By purifying and balancing these subtle elements, the practitioner creates the optimal conditions for the recognition of the natural state.
