---
title: "Ziji Section 10: The Lineage of Transmission"
scripture: "ziji"
chapter: 10
---

The teachings of Bon have been transmitted through an unbroken lineage of realized masters stretching from Tonpa Shenrab to the present day. This lineage is the living heart of the tradition, for without the direct transmission from master to student, the teachings remain mere words on paper, lacking the transformative power that comes from direct experience.

The lineage of transmission operates on three levels. The mind lineage of the Buddhas transmits the teaching directly from mind to mind, without the need for words or symbols. This is the highest form of transmission, available only to beings of the most advanced capacity. The symbolic lineage of the knowledge holders transmits the teaching through gestures, symbols, and direct pointing-out instructions that bypass the conceptual mind. The oral lineage of realized masters transmits the teaching through spoken instruction, commentary, and guided practice.

The treasure tradition, or terma, represents a unique form of transmission in which teachings hidden by Tonpa Shenrab or his early disciples are rediscovered by predestined masters at the time when they are needed by the world. These hidden teachings may be found in rocks, lakes, temples, or even within the mind-stream of the treasure revealer, where they were placed in a previous life. The terma tradition ensures that the teachings remain fresh and relevant, adapted to the needs and capacities of each generation.

The devotion of the student to the master is considered the most essential element of spiritual practice. Through devotion, the student opens their mind to receive the blessings and transmissions that flow through the lineage. This devotion is not blind obedience but intelligent trust, based on the student's own investigation and experience of the teacher's qualities.

The preservation of the Bon lineage through periods of persecution and exile testifies to the dedication of successive generations of practitioners. When the tradition faced suppression in Tibet, masters hid texts, memorized entire libraries, and transmitted the teachings in secret. When exile scattered the community across the world, the lineage holders established new centers of practice and training, ensuring that these ancient teachings would continue to benefit beings in the modern age.
