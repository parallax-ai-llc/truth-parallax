## The Ascent Narrative: Phenomenology of Mystical Experience

Allogenes' ascent through the three levels of the Barbelo aeon is described in language that has attracted the attention of scholars interested in the phenomenology of mystical experience. At each level, Allogenes reports a distinct mode of awareness — a progressive simplification of consciousness that moves from discursive thought through intuitive vision to an awareness beyond the subject-object distinction.

This phenomenological progression has been compared to the stages of contemplative ascent described in Plotinus (Enneads I.6, V.1, VI.9), in Gregory of Nyssa's *Life of Moses*, and in the medieval Christian mystical tradition. The comparison raises the question of whether these texts describe a universal structure of mystical experience or whether their similarities are the product of shared literary and philosophical traditions.

April DeConick's work on "mystical ascent" in early Christian and Jewish literature has situated Allogenes within a broader pattern of visionary ascent texts that includes the *hekhalot* literature, the Ascension of Isaiah, and 2 Corinthians 12:1-4. She argues that these texts reflect genuine visionary practices — not merely literary conventions — and that they provide evidence of a widespread culture of contemplative ascent in late antiquity.
