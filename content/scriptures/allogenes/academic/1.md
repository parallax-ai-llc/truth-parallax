## Youel and the Sethian Revealer Tradition

The opening section of Allogenes introduces Youel as the primary revealer figure — a role attested in other Sethian texts, including the Three Steles of Seth and the untitled treatise of the Bruce Codex. Youel's name has been etymologized variously: John Turner connects it to Hebrew *El* ("God") with a possible pronominal element, while other scholars have proposed connections to the angel Yahoel known from the Apocalypse of Abraham and other Jewish texts.

The use of a female revealer is characteristic of Sethian literature and distinguishes it from most other ancient revelatory traditions. Scholars have debated whether this reflects a genuine sociological openness to women's leadership in Sethian communities or whether the female revealer functions as a literary and theological figure without direct sociological implications. The prominence of female revealers in Sethian texts (Barbelo, Epinoia, Youel, Protennoia) contrasts with the male-dominated revelatory traditions of proto-orthodox Christianity, Mandaeism, and Manichaeism.

The text's opening formula — "I am Allogenes the Stranger" — identifies the composition as a first-person pseudepigraphical revelation, a genre well attested in both Jewish (1 Enoch, 4 Ezra) and Gnostic (Zostrianos, Marsanes) literature. The choice of a first-person frame creates an immediacy absent from third-person revelation dialogues and invites the reader to identify with the visionary protagonist.
