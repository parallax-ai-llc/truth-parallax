## The Triple Power and Trinitarian Theology

The Triple-Powered One of Allogenes — Existence, Vitality, Mentality — presents both parallels and contrasts with the Christian doctrine of the Trinity. The structural similarity (a threefold divine reality that is simultaneously one) invites comparison, while the content (impersonal philosophical categories rather than personal relations) marks a fundamental divergence.

Christian trinitarian theology, as developed from Nicaea (325) through Constantinople (381) to the mature formulations of the Cappadocian Fathers, insists on the full personal reality of each "hypostasis" — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are not modes or aspects of the divine but distinct persons in eternal relation. The Sethian Triple Power, by contrast, describes modes of divine self-manifestation: Existence is the sheer givenness of the divine; Vitality is its dynamic energy; Mentality is its self-reflective awareness. These are dimensions of a single reality, not persons in relationship.

The comparison illuminates a fundamental choice in theological method: whether the divine is best understood through personal categories (love, will, address) or through philosophical categories (being, life, mind). Christian theology has consistently privileged the personal, insisting that the God revealed in Jesus Christ is not an impersonal principle but a living, willing, loving subject who addresses and is addressed.
