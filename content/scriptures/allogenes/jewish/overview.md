## Overview

For Jewish readers, Allogenes is significant for its transformation of Jewish mystical traditions — particularly the merkabah ascent tradition and the figure of Seth — into a Sethian Gnostic framework. The text's radical negative theology also invites comparison with the later Kabbalistic concept of *Ein Sof* (the Infinite) as the unknowable ground of divine being.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Merkabah Parallels

Allogenes' visionary ascent through multiple levels of the divine realm, guided by an angelic revealer (Youel), closely parallels the *merkabah* tradition's description of the mystic's ascent through the seven *hekhalot* (heavenly palaces) to the divine throne. The structural similarity has led scholars to posit a shared tradition of visionary ascent underlying both Jewish and Gnostic mysticism.

### Seth in Jewish Tradition

The identification of Allogenes with Seth draws on Jewish interpretive traditions surrounding Genesis 4:25-5:8. In Jewish exegesis, Seth represents the righteous line that culminates in Noah, as distinct from the line of Cain. The Sethian appropriation transforms this genealogical distinction into a cosmic drama of spiritual versus material humanity.

### Ein Sof and the Unknowable One

The Unknowable One of Allogenes — beyond being, beyond thought, beyond predication — anticipates in remarkable ways the Kabbalistic concept of *Ein Sof*, the infinite divine essence that precedes and transcends even the ten sefirot. While direct historical influence is unlikely, the structural parallel suggests a shared impulse within Jewish-influenced traditions to posit an ultimate divine mystery beyond all human categories.
