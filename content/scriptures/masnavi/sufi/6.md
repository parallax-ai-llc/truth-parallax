## Unity of Being: Annihilation and Subsistence

Book Six, the final book of the Masnavi, addresses the deepest mystery of the Sufi path: the relationship between annihilation (*fana*) and subsistence (*baqa*) — the paradox that the soul must die to itself in order to live in God. The story of the merchant and the parrot (distinct from the parrot story of Book Two) explores the theme of returning to the world after the experience of *fana*, now seeing with divine eyes.

For the Sufi reader, Book Six represents the summit of Rumi's teaching: *wahdat al-wujud* (the unity of being), the realization that there is only one Being — God — and that all apparent multiplicity is the manifestation of this single divine reality. "I looked and saw that lover and beloved are one — mirror and beauty are one." This is not philosophical monism but mystical experience: the lover who has been annihilated in the Beloved discovers that there was never anything other than the Beloved.

The Masnavi's final book is incomplete — tradition holds that Rumi's death interrupted its composition. For the Sufi reader, this incompleteness is itself a teaching: the journey of love has no end. The soul's exploration of the divine mystery continues beyond any text, beyond any teacher, beyond death itself. The reed's song, which opened the Masnavi, continues to play — its music the eternal lament and celebration of the soul's infinite journey toward the Infinite Beloved.
