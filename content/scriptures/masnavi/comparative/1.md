## The Song of Separation: Longing Across Traditions

The Masnavi's opening Song of the Reed — the soul's lament for separation from its divine source — belongs to one of the most universal themes in world mystical literature: the experience of longing for the divine and the conviction that the soul's true home lies elsewhere.

The Hindu bhakti tradition, particularly the *viraha* (longing in separation) poetry of Mirabai and the Gopi songs of the Bhagavata Purana, expresses a nearly identical emotion: the soul as lover, separated from the divine Beloved (Krishna), crying out in longing for reunion. The Song of Songs in the Jewish and Christian traditions gives voice to a similar longing: "On my bed at night I sought him whom my soul loves; I sought him but found him not" (3:1).

The Buddhist concept of *dukkha* (suffering, unsatisfactoriness) provides a structural parallel, though without the theistic framework: all conditioned existence is characterized by a subtle dissatisfaction that points toward a condition (nirvana) beyond the cycle of craving and loss. The Masnavi's Song of the Reed and the Buddha's First Noble Truth both begin from the same experiential observation — something is wrong with ordinary existence — but proceed in radically different directions.
