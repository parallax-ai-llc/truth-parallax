## Unity of Being: Mystical Monism Across Traditions

Book Six's culminating theme of *wahdat al-wujud* (the unity of being) connects the Masnavi to the stream of mystical monism that runs through many of the world's contemplative traditions. The conviction that reality is ultimately one — that the apparent multiplicity of the world conceals an underlying unity — is perhaps the most radical and most widely attested of all mystical insights.

Shankara's Advaita Vedanta teaches that only Brahman is real; the world of multiplicity is *maya* (illusion). Plotinus's Neoplatonism traces all reality back to the One, from which Intellect, Soul, and Matter emanate and to which all things return. The Tao Te Ching's "The Tao gives birth to one, one gives birth to two, two gives birth to three, three gives birth to the ten thousand things" traces multiplicity back to an original unity. Meister Eckhart's declaration that "all things are one in the Godhead" echoes across the mystical traditions of Christianity.

The Masnavi's *wahdat al-wujud* — "lover and beloved are one, mirror and beauty are one" — belongs to this tradition while giving it a distinctively Islamic inflection. The unity of being is not a philosophical thesis to be argued but a mystical realization to be experienced — the culmination of the journey that began with the reed's cry of separation and ends with the discovery that separation was always an illusion.
