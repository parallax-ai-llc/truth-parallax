## Overview

The Masnavi invites comparison with mystical and narrative traditions across the world's religions. Its use of story as spiritual teaching, its emphasis on love as the path to the divine, and its integration of philosophy, theology, and poetry create points of contact with texts from Hindu bhakti, Buddhist parable literature, Christian mysticism, and Jewish Hasidic storytelling.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Story as Spiritual Teaching

Rumi's use of narrative as a vehicle for spiritual truth parallels Jesus's parables, the Hasidic tales of the Baal Shem Tov, Buddhist Jataka tales, and Hindu puranic narratives. In each tradition, the story functions not as illustration but as revelation — the narrative form is itself the teaching, not merely a wrapper for a doctrinal point.

### Love Mysticism

The Masnavi's central theme of divine love connects it to the bhakti tradition in Hinduism (Mirabai, Tukaram), the Song of Songs tradition in Judaism and Christianity, and the devotional poetry of the Sikh tradition (Guru Nanak, Bulleh Shah). The experience of the divine as Beloved — simultaneously present and absent, intimate and transcendent — is one of the most widespread motifs in world mystical literature.

### Poetry and the Ineffable

Rumi's choice of poetry as the medium for mystical teaching reflects a conviction shared across traditions: that the deepest spiritual truths resist prose exposition and require the indirection, ambiguity, and emotional resonance of verse.
