## Divine Wisdom and Prophetic Medicine

Book Three's story of the king, the handmaiden, and the divine physician resonates with the Sunni understanding of prophetic medicine (*tibb al-nabawi*) — the tradition of healing wisdom attributed to the Prophet Muhammad. The Sunni reader recognizes in the divine physician a figure who heals not through human learning alone but through divine guidance (*hidaya*) — the same guidance that the Prophet brought to a spiritually sick humanity.

The book's exploration of the limits of reason complements the Sunni theological tradition's careful delineation of the respective roles of reason and revelation. The Ash'ari school, predominant in Sunni theology, affirms the value of reason while insisting on revelation's priority in matters that exceed rational capacity. Rumi's subordination of *aql* (reason) to *ishq* (love) echoes this prioritization, though it extends it into the experiential domain of mystical encounter.

For Sunni readers, Book Three also illustrates the importance of trusting in God's wisdom (*hikma*) even when human understanding fails. The king's initial reliance on human physicians represents the believer's temptation to trust in worldly solutions; the divine physician's success represents the transformative power of turning to God in sincere dependence (*tawakkul*).
