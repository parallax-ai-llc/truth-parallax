## Jealousy, Attachment, and Islamic Ethics

Book Four's treatment of jealousy and attachment connects to the Islamic ethical tradition's analysis of the diseases of the heart (*amrad al-qulub*). Islamic moral psychology, developed by scholars such as al-Ghazali in the *Ihya 'Ulum al-Din*, identifies specific spiritual diseases — envy (*hasad*), pride (*kibr*), avarice (*bukhl*), anger (*ghadab*) — and prescribes specific remedies for each.

Rumi's story of the Sufi and his wife dramatizes the disease of attachment (*ta'alluq*) and the jealousy that arises from it. For Sunni readers, the story illustrates al-Ghazali's teaching that attachment to worldly goods — even legitimate goods such as spousal love — becomes a spiritual disease when it displaces the heart's primary orientation toward God.

The remedy, as both Rumi and al-Ghazali teach, is not the elimination of human love but its proper ordering. The Prophet Muhammad loved his wives, his companions, and his community — but his love for God remained supreme. The Sunni reader finds in Book Four a poetic exploration of this prophetic model: love of creation as a path to the Creator, not a substitute for the Creator.
