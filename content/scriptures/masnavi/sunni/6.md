## The Seal of the Masnavi: Return to the Source

Book Six, the final and incomplete book of the Masnavi, brings the poem full circle to the themes announced in the opening Song of the Reed. The soul that has journeyed through the six books — through separation and longing (Book 1), ego-death (Book 2), the limits of reason (Book 3), the purification of desire (Book 4), the paradox of will (Book 5) — arrives at the threshold of its ultimate destination: *fana fi-Allah* (annihilation in God) and *baqa bi-Allah* (subsistence through God).

For Sunni readers, the Masnavi's culmination in the theme of divine unity echoes the central Islamic declaration: *La ilaha illa-Allah* — "There is no god but God." The entire Masnavi can be read as an extended commentary on this *shahada*: the systematic negation of all false gods (ego, intellect, desire, worldly attachment) until only the one true God remains. The Sufi experience of *fana* is not the annihilation of the believer but the annihilation of everything in the believer that is not God.

The Masnavi's incompleteness — Rumi died before completing Book Six — has been interpreted by Sunni readers as a sign that the divine reality the poem approaches can never be fully captured in human language. The Quran itself declares: "Say: if the sea were ink for the words of my Lord, the sea would be exhausted before the words of my Lord were exhausted" (18:109). The Masnavi's unfinished final book testifies to the same inexhaustibility.
