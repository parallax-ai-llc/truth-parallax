## The Reed and Quranic Longing

The Masnavi opens with the Song of the Reed — a passage that Sunni readers connect directly to the Quranic theme of the human being's covenant with God. In the primordial *mithaq* (covenant, Quran 7:172), God asked the unborn souls, "Am I not your Lord?" and they answered, "Yes, we testify." The reed's cry of separation echoes this primordial memory: the soul remembers a closeness to God that earthly existence has obscured.

For Sunni readers, the Masnavi's opening thus functions as a Quranic commentary — an imaginative exploration of what it means to live in the aftermath of the primordial covenant. The longing the reed expresses is not mere sentiment but a theological reality: every human soul has known God directly and bears within it the memory of that encounter, however deeply buried beneath the distractions of worldly life.

Book One's stories elaborate this theme through narrative. The tale of the king and the handmaiden, the story of the grocer and the parrot, and the account of the lion and the hare all dramatize the consequences of forgetting one's divine origin and the transformative power of remembering (*dhikr*). For Sunni readers, the Masnavi's call to remembrance aligns perfectly with the Quranic injunction: "Remember Me, and I will remember you" (Quran 2:152).
