## Overview

Within Sunni Islam, the Masnavi has been received as a devotional and ethical masterpiece that illuminates Quranic principles through narrative and poetry. Rumi himself was a trained *alim* (scholar of Islamic law) in the Hanafi school, and his poem is saturated with Quranic allusions, hadith quotations, and references to the prophetic tradition. For Sunni readers, the Masnavi is not a heterodox or esoteric text but a work firmly rooted in the Islamic sciences, extending their insights through the medium of Persian verse.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Quranic Illumination

The Masnavi has been called "the Quran in Persian" — a tribute to its role as a commentary on and extension of the Quranic message. Rumi does not simply quote the Quran; he inhabits it, drawing out its inner meanings through stories that dramatize Quranic themes: the mercy of God, the folly of heedlessness, the struggle between the lower self and the divine call.

### Prophetic Models

The stories of the prophets — Moses, Jesus, Abraham, Muhammad — serve as models of spiritual development. Rumi draws on both Quranic and extra-Quranic traditions about the prophets, presenting them not merely as historical figures but as archetypes of the soul's journey toward God.

### Ethical Formation

For Sunni readers, the Masnavi's stories serve a moral function: they expose the diseases of the ego (pride, envy, greed, anger) and prescribe the remedies of humility, generosity, patience, and love.
