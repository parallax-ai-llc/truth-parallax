## Qadar and Kasb: The Free Will Debate in Islamic Context

Book Five's treatment of free will and predestination places the Masnavi squarely within one of the most important debates in Sunni Islamic theology. The Ash'ari concept of *kasb* (acquisition) — the doctrine that God creates human actions but that humans "acquire" them, thereby bearing moral responsibility — represents the mainstream Sunni attempt to reconcile divine omnipotence with human accountability.

Rumi's story of the lion and the beasts dramatizes this tension without resolving it doctrinally. The lion's power represents God's overwhelming sovereignty (*qudra*); the beasts' negotiations represent human effort and ingenuity. For Sunni readers, the story illustrates the lived reality of the *kasb* doctrine: human beings act as if they have free will (negotiating, planning, choosing) while knowing that nothing happens outside God's decree.

The book's resolution — not through theological argument but through the transformative experience of surrender (*islam*, literally "submission") — reflects Rumi's conviction that the deepest theological questions are answered not by the intellect but by the heart. The believer who truly submits to God no longer experiences the free will/predestination dichotomy as a contradiction; it dissolves in the experience of trust (*tawakkul*) in the divine wisdom.
