## The Reed and the Longing for the Hidden Imam

For Shia readers, the Masnavi's opening Song of the Reed carries an additional resonance: the reed's cry of separation from the reed bed echoes the Shia community's longing for the Hidden Imam (the Twelfth Imam, Muhammad al-Mahdi), who entered occultation (*ghaybah*) in 874 CE and whose return the faithful await. The separation that Rumi describes is not merely individual but communal — the Shia community lives in a state of cosmic grief and expectation, separated from the rightful guide of the *ummah*.

The reed's song also resonates with the *rawdah-khani* tradition of Shia devotional literature — the recitation of mourning narratives commemorating the martyrdom of Imam Husayn at Karbala (680 CE). The note of lament that opens the Masnavi connects to the deep well of Shia grief: the pain of unjust suffering, the memory of righteous leaders cut down by tyrannical power, and the conviction that justice, though delayed, will ultimately prevail.

For Shia readers, Book One's exploration of separation and longing thus operates on multiple levels: the individual soul's separation from God, the community's separation from its rightful imam, and the cosmic separation of justice from power that characterizes the present age. The Masnavi's promise that separation will end in reunion provides comfort and hope to a community defined by waiting.
