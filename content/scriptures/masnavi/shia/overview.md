## Overview

Shia readers have long found in the Masnavi resonances with distinctively Shia themes: the authority of spiritual guidance (*walaya*), the esoteric dimension of revelation (*batin*), and the longing for the hidden imam. While Rumi's own sectarian affiliation was Sunni Hanafi, his emphasis on the inner meaning of religious forms, the authority of the spiritual master, and the hidden dimensions of divine truth aligns with Shia theological sensibilities.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Walaya and the Spiritual Master

The Masnavi's emphasis on the necessity of a spiritual guide (*murshid*) resonates with the Shia concept of *walaya* — the authority of the imam (and, by extension, the ayatollah or marja') to guide the community in both exoteric and esoteric matters. Rumi's devotion to his own master, Shams-i Tabrizi, provides a model of the master-disciple relationship that Shia readers recognize as structurally parallel to the imam-community relationship.

### Batin and Zahir

The Masnavi's consistent distinction between outward form (*zahir*) and inner meaning (*batin*) corresponds to a fundamental Shia hermeneutical principle: the Quran and the Prophetic tradition have an outer, apparent meaning accessible to all and an inner, esoteric meaning accessible only through the guidance of the imam or the Ahl al-Bayt (the Prophet's household).

### The Hidden Beloved

The Masnavi's central theme of longing for the absent Beloved resonates with the Shia experience of the Hidden Imam's occultation (*ghaybah*) — the conviction that the twelfth imam is present but hidden, and that the faithful must live in a state of longing and expectation until his return.
