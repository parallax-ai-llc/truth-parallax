## Divine Decree and the Justice of God

Book Five's treatment of free will and predestination intersects with the Shia theological emphasis on divine justice (*adl*). The Mu'tazili influence on Shia theology — particularly the insistence that God is just and that human beings possess genuine free will — provides a distinctive framework for reading the Masnavi's treatment of this theme.

For Shia readers, the tension between divine sovereignty and human freedom is resolved not through the Ash'ari concept of *kasb* but through the conviction that God's justice requires genuine human agency. The lion's power in Rumi's story represents divine sovereignty, but the beasts' negotiations represent the real choices that human beings must make — choices for which they will be held accountable by a just God.

The theme of justice also connects to the Shia historical experience. The Umayyad caliphate's usurpation of power from the Ahl al-Bayt represents, for Shia readers, the ultimate injustice — an injustice that the Masnavi's teaching on divine sovereignty does not excuse but places within a larger framework of divine justice that will ultimately prevail, whether in this world or the next.
