## Desire, Gender, and Power in the Masnavi

Book Four's treatment of the Sufi's relationship with his wife has been examined by scholars through the lens of gender studies, raising questions about Rumi's representation of women, the role of the feminine in Sufi symbolism, and the power dynamics embedded in medieval Islamic literary conventions.

The beloved in Sufi poetry is typically gendered female (in Persian, the pronoun is gender-neutral, but the conventions of the ghazal tradition feminize the beloved). Scholars such as Annemarie Schimmel and Fatemeh Keshavarz have explored the complex interplay between literal and allegorical readings of gender in the Masnavi — noting that the feminine beloved can represent the divine, the soul, the spiritual master, or the feminine dimension of the divine itself.

The story's treatment of jealousy and possession raises questions about the relationship between mystical love and social power. The Sufi's jealousy over his wife reflects a patriarchal social structure in which women were understood as possessions — a structure that Rumi neither fully endorses nor fully critiques. Scholars have debated whether the Masnavi's use of gendered love imagery reinforces or transcends the social norms of its time.
