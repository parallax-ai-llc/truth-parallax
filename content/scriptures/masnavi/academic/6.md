## The Incomplete Sixth Book: Scholarly Perspectives

The Masnavi's sixth book is incomplete — Rumi died on December 17, 1273, before finishing it. Scholars have debated whether the incompleteness is accidental (the poet's death interrupted a work in progress) or meaningful (the work was deliberately left open-ended as a testimony to the inexhaustibility of the divine reality it describes).

Nicholson's edition, based on the oldest available manuscripts, presents Book Six as ending abruptly in the middle of a story — suggesting accidental interruption. However, Lewis and other scholars have noted that the Masnavi's structure is inherently open-ended: each book begins with a new preface and a new framing story, and there is no internal evidence that Rumi planned a specific number of books. The "completion" of the Masnavi may have been a living process — the poem growing as long as its author lived.

The reception of Book Six has also been shaped by its incompleteness. Later Sufi commentators treated the unfinished text as a koan — a deliberate silence that points beyond language. The Mevlevi tradition's decision not to supplement or "complete" the text reflects a respect for its open-endedness that contrasts with literary traditions (such as the completion of Virgil's *Aeneid* or Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales*) in which unfinished masterworks have been supplemented by later hands.
