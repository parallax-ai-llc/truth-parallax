## Narrative Structure and Digressive Method

Book Two has been studied by literary scholars for its distinctive narrative technique: the "Chinese box" or "mise en abyme" structure in which stories contain stories that contain stories, with digressions that prove to be as important as — or more important than — the framing narrative. This technique, while found in other medieval narrative traditions (the *Arabian Nights*, the *Decameron*, the *Canterbury Tales*), achieves a particular density and theological purpose in the Masnavi.

Jerome Clinton and Franklin Lewis have analyzed the Masnavi's digressive method as a deliberate rhetorical strategy rather than a sign of compositional disorder. The digressions mimic the circuitous nature of the spiritual path itself: the Sufi does not proceed in a straight line from ignorance to enlightenment but spirals, returns, gets lost, and finds the way again. The reader's experience of the text — following a story, losing the thread in a digression, finding it again in an unexpected place — replicates the experience of the spiritual journey.

Book Two's parable of the parrot has been analyzed through multiple scholarly frameworks: as an allegory of *fana* (Nicholson), as a narrative about the relationship between teacher and student (Chittick), and as a meta-literary reflection on the nature of storytelling itself (Lewis).
