## The Ney-Nameh: Literary and Philosophical Analysis

The opening eighteen couplets of the Masnavi — the Song of the Reed (*Ney-nameh*) — have been the subject of more scholarly commentary than perhaps any other passage in Persian literature. Nicholson's commentary provides a line-by-line analysis; Chittick's philosophical treatment demonstrates how the passage encodes the entirety of Rumi's metaphysical system; and Mojaddedi's recent translation offers a fresh English rendering informed by contemporary literary theory.

The *Ney-nameh*'s literary structure is deceptively simple: a series of couplets in the *ramal* meter spoken in the voice of the reed flute. But scholars have identified multiple layers of meaning: the autobiographical (Rumi's separation from Shams-i Tabrizi), the cosmological (the soul's descent from the divine realm), the philosophical (the One's self-differentiation into multiplicity), and the meta-poetic (the poet's struggle to express the inexpressible through language).

The *Ney-nameh*'s reception history is itself a scholarly subject. The passage has been set to music by Mevlevi practitioners, painted by miniaturists, commented upon by philosophers, and quoted by politicians. Its transformation from Sufi devotional text to global spiritual commodity raises questions about cultural translation and the politics of literary reception.
