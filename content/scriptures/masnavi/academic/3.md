## Reason and Love in Islamic Intellectual History

Book Three's subordination of reason to love places Rumi within a specific tradition of Islamic intellectual history — one that includes al-Ghazali's critique of the philosophers (*Tahafut al-Falasifah*), Ibn Arabi's doctrine of the "knowledge of the heart" (*ilm al-qalb*), and the broader Sufi insistence on experiential knowledge (*dhawq*, "taste") as superior to discursive reasoning.

Scholars have debated the relationship between Rumi's critique of reason and the broader Islamic philosophical tradition. Rumi's position is not simply anti-intellectual: he was trained in the Islamic sciences and quotes Aristotelian and Neoplatonic sources with familiarity. His critique targets not reason per se but the pretension of reason to serve as the sole or supreme faculty of knowledge — a pretension that blinds the philosopher to dimensions of reality accessible only through love and mystical experience.

The story of the king and the divine physician has been compared by scholars to al-Ghazali's own spiritual crisis (described in *al-Munqidh min al-Dalal*), in which the great theologian found that his scholarly learning had become an obstacle to genuine faith. Both narratives describe the insufficiency of external knowledge and the necessity of an inward transformation that no amount of study can produce.
