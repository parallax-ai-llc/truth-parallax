## Free Will and Predestination: The Masnavi's Contribution

Book Five's treatment of the free will/predestination debate represents Rumi's most sustained engagement with one of the central problems of Islamic theology. Scholars have analyzed his position as a creative synthesis of competing approaches: neither fully Ash'ari (emphasizing divine omnipotence) nor fully Mu'tazili (emphasizing human freedom) but occupying a distinctive middle ground informed by mystical experience.

Chittick has demonstrated that Rumi's resolution of the problem is not theological but experiential: the contradiction between free will and predestination dissolves in the experience of *fana*, in which the distinction between the individual will and the divine will ceases to be meaningful. This experiential resolution has been compared by scholars to analogous moves in other philosophical traditions — Spinoza's equation of freedom with the recognition of necessity, Hegel's dialectical sublation of opposites, and the Buddhist concept of *pratityasamutpada* (dependent origination), which dissolves the free will question by denying the existence of an independent agent.

Book Five's narrative complexity — multiple stories that address the theme from different angles without resolving it into a single doctrinal position — has been analyzed as a deliberate hermeneutical strategy. Rumi presents the reader with a field of perspectives rather than a definitive answer, inviting the reader to discover their own resolution through practice rather than through argument.
