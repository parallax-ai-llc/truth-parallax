## All Is One: The Mystic's Vision

Book Six's culminating vision of the unity of all being — *wahdat al-wujud* — represents the most radical and most universal of all mystical insights. The conviction that beneath the apparent diversity of the world there lies a single, undivided reality has been expressed in virtually every contemplative tradition, from Vedantic *Tat tvam asi* ("That thou art") to Eckhart's "The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me" to the Hasidic *Ein od milvado* ("There is nothing besides God").

This insight does not erase differences — the Sufi remains a Muslim, the Vedantin remains a Hindu, the Hasid remains a Jew — but it relativizes them, placing them within a larger unity that no single tradition can claim to possess exclusively. Rumi's *wahdat al-wujud* is not a call to abandon one's tradition but an invitation to discover the universal within the particular, the infinite within the finite, the Beloved within every beloved.

The Masnavi's unfinished ending is, from an interfaith perspective, its most appropriate conclusion. If all being is truly one, then no text can contain it, no tradition can exhaust it, and no poem — however magnificent — can bring the journey to a final close. The reed continues to sing. The search continues. The Beloved remains both utterly near and infinitely beyond, drawing all seekers — of every tradition and none — toward a unity that can be tasted but never fully told.
