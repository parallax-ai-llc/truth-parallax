## Overview

Rumi's Masnavi has become one of the most widely read mystical texts across religious and cultural boundaries, embraced by readers of every tradition and none. His famous lines — "Come, come, whoever you are... even if you have broken your vow a thousand times, come, come again" — have been adopted as a motto by interfaith movements worldwide. The poem's emphasis on love as the universal spiritual language, its critique of religious formalism, and its insistence that the divine exceeds all human categories make it a natural catalyst for interfaith dialogue.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Beyond Religious Labels

Rumi repeatedly challenges the boundaries between religious traditions: "The lamps are different, but the light is the same." This does not mean that Rumi rejects Islam — he does not — but that he recognizes the divine light shining through all genuine spiritual seeking, regardless of the formal tradition in which it occurs.

### The Human Condition

The Masnavi's stories address the universal human condition: longing, pride, fear, love, loss, transformation, homecoming. These themes are not the property of any single tradition but the shared heritage of all human beings. Interfaith readers find in the Masnavi a mirror for their own spiritual struggles, regardless of their religious background.

### Love as Common Ground

Rumi's insistence that love is the fundamental reality — "Lovers don't finally meet somewhere. They're in each other all along" — provides a meeting point for contemplatives across traditions. The experience of being drawn toward the divine by a force beyond one's control is attested in Christian mysticism, Hindu bhakti, Buddhist compassion practice, and Jewish devekut (cleaving to God).
