## The Universal Cry of Longing

The Masnavi's opening Song of the Reed speaks to a longing that transcends religious boundaries — the human experience of incompleteness, of feeling that something essential is missing, of searching for a home that cannot be found in the visible world. This longing is the engine of every spiritual journey, whatever tradition it begins from.

Augustine's restless heart ("You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our heart is restless until it rests in you"), the Buddhist recognition of *dukkha* (the unsatisfactoriness of conditioned existence), the Hindu concept of *mumukshutva* (the intense desire for liberation), the Jewish concept of *tikkun* (the soul's longing to repair the world and return to wholeness) — all point to the same existential condition that Rumi's reed expresses.

The interfaith power of the Masnavi's opening lies in its universality: it does not name the source of longing as "Allah" or "God" or any tradition-specific concept. It simply presents the longing itself — raw, beautiful, painful — and invites the listener to recognize it as their own. Whatever one calls the reed bed from which the soul has been cut, the cry of separation is the same.
