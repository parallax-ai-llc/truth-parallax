## Overview

The *Masnavi-ye-Ma'navi* (Spiritual Couplets) is the magnum opus of Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi (1207–1273 CE), composed over the last twelve years of his life in Konya, Anatolia. Comprising approximately 25,700 couplets in six books, it is one of the longest mystical poems ever written and has been called "the Quran in Persian" (*Quran dar zaban-i Pahlavi*) — a tribute to its spiritual authority within the Persian-speaking Islamic world. The poem was composed in the *masnavi* meter (rhyming couplets in the *ramal* meter), and Rumi reportedly dictated much of it to his disciple Husam al-Din Chelebi, who served as both scribe and inspiration.

The Masnavi defies easy classification. It is at once a collection of teaching stories, a mystical commentary on the Quran and Hadith, a philosophical treatise on the nature of the soul's journey to God, and an extended conversation between a master and his disciples. Stories begin, are interrupted by digressions, resume chapters later, and nest within one another — creating a literary structure that mirrors the Sufi path itself: circuitous, surprising, and ultimately oriented toward a unity that transcends rational order.

## Structure

| Book | Opening Story | Central Themes |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| 1 | The Song of the Reed | Separation from the divine, longing, love as the fundamental force of creation |
| 2 | The Parable of the Parrot | The death of the ego (*nafs*), the difference between outward form and inner meaning |
| 3 | The Story of the King and the Handmaiden | The relationship between reason and love, the limitations of intellect |
| 4 | The Story of the Sufi and His Beautiful Wife | The nature of jealousy, attachment, and the purification of desire |
| 5 | The Story of the Lion and the Beasts | Free will and predestination, the struggle between the animal and divine natures |
| 6 | The Merchant and the Parrot | The unity of being (*wahdat al-wujud*), annihilation (*fana*) and subsistence (*baqa*) in God |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Sufi**: The supreme literary expression of Sufi mysticism, used as a teaching text in Mevlevi and other Sufi orders for seven centuries
- **Sunni**: Embraced as a devotional and ethical masterpiece that illuminates Quranic principles through narrative and poetry
- **Shia**: Read for its emphasis on spiritual authority (*walaya*), inner knowledge, and the hidden meanings of revelation
- **Academic**: Studied as a masterwork of Persian literature, a key text in the history of Islamic thought, and a rich source for comparative poetics
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to Dante's *Divine Comedy*, the parables of Jesus, the Upanishads, and Buddhist Jataka tales
- **Interfaith**: Rumi's emphasis on love as the universal spiritual language has made the Masnavi one of the most widely read mystical texts across cultures and traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Divine Love | Love (*ishq*) is the fundamental reality — the force that draws all creation back to its source |
| The Reed's Lament | The soul's longing for reunion with the divine, symbolized by the reed flute's cry of separation |
| Ego-Death | The lower self (*nafs*) must be annihilated for the divine self to emerge |
| Form and Meaning | Outward appearances conceal inner spiritual realities that the seeker must learn to perceive |
| Parable and Story | Nested narratives function as spiritual mirrors, reflecting the reader's own condition |
| Unity of Being | All apparent multiplicity is the manifestation of a single divine reality |
