---
title: "Advaya Taraka Upanishad - Chapter 2"
scripture: "advaya-taraka-upanishad"
chapter: 2
---

**1** The Rajayoga is thus described: the Manas and the Prana are mixed together, and by that union the Yogi attains the state of Unmani, the mindless state, which is the supreme goal.

**2** One should fix the gaze between the eyebrows with the practice of Shambhavi Mudra, and should contemplate the self-luminous Atman.

**3** The Atman is self-effulgent, pure consciousness, devoid of modifications, beyond all attributes, the eternal witness, free from all limitations.

**4** When the individual self merges with the supreme Self through this practice, there arises the state of Taraka Yoga, the Yoga of the deliverer.

**5** The three kinds of Lakshyas (objects of meditation) are Antara Lakshya (internal), Bahya Lakshya (external), and Madhya Lakshya (intermediate).

**6** The Antara Lakshya is the light perceived within. The Bahya Lakshya is the light perceived in the ether of the heart reflected outward. The Madhya Lakshya is the light of the Atman perceived between the two eyebrows.

**7** By the practice of Shambhavi Mudra, the Yogi perceives the Akasha within, radiant like ten million suns. This is the Antara Lakshya, the internal object of contemplation.

**8** The Bahya Lakshya is perceived at a distance of four, six, eight, ten, and twelve finger-breadths from the tip of the nose, and appears variously as the blue sky, the dark cloud, the red dawn, the yellow flame, and the white brilliance.

**9** The Madhya Lakshya is the perception of the five kinds of Akasha: Guna-rahita (attributeless), Parama (supreme), Maha (great), Tattva (reality), and Surya (solar).

**10** He who practises these three Lakshyas attains liberation even while living in the body. He becomes a Jivanmukta.
