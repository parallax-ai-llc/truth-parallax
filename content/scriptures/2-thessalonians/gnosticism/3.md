## Warning Against Idleness

The request for prayer that "the word of the Lord may speed ahead and be honored" (3:1) is understood in Gnostic terms as the hope that gnosis will spread rapidly through the material cosmos, awakening divine sparks before the archons can intervene. The prayer for deliverance from "wicked and evil men" (3:2) refers to those who serve the Demiurge's agenda, whether knowingly or unknowingly, obstructing the transmission of liberating knowledge.

The warning against "idleness" (3:6) is reinterpreted by Gnostic readers as a warning against spiritual torpor, the most dangerous state for a pneumatic soul. Idleness in the Gnostic sense is not laziness about physical labor but the failure to pursue gnosis with urgency. The divine spark that does not actively seek its liberation sinks deeper into the sleep of matter. Paul's rule that "if anyone is not willing to work, let him not eat" (3:10) becomes a spiritual maxim: those who do not labor in the cultivation of inner knowledge cannot partake of the pneumatic nourishment that sustains the community. The "bread" of the Gnostic fellowship is gnosis itself, and it must be earned through the hard work of self-examination, contemplation, and detachment from archontic illusion.

The instruction not to "grow weary in doing good" (3:13) sustains the pneumatic on the long path of awakening. The archons' strategy includes discouragement, making the prison of matter seem inescapable so that even those who glimpse the light of the Pleroma lose hope. Paul counters this with the assurance that "the Lord is faithful" (3:3), meaning the transcendent Father, beyond the reach of the Demiurge, never abandons the scattered sparks. The "peace at all times in every way" invoked in the closing blessing (3:16) is not worldly tranquility but the deep stillness of the pneumatic self resting in its knowledge of its divine origin, untouched by the turbulence of the archontic cosmos.
