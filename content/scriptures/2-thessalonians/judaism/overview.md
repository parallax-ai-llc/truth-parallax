## Overview

Jewish scholarly engagement with 2 Thessalonians centers on the letter's apocalyptic imagery and its roots in Jewish eschatological tradition. The figure of the "man of lawlessness" (2:3-4) has parallels in Jewish apocalyptic literature, including the traditions about Belial and the wicked king who exalts himself.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Apocalyptic Jewish Roots
Jewish scholars recognize that the eschatological scenario in chapter 2 draws heavily on Daniel 11:36, the Belial traditions of the Dead Sea Scrolls, and other Jewish apocalyptic texts. The "man of lawlessness" who seats himself in the temple echoes Antiochus IV Epiphanes and similar figures in Jewish memory.

### The Temple Reference
The mention of the "temple of God" (2:4) raises questions about whether the Jerusalem Temple still stood when the letter was written, a matter of interest for dating early Christian literature and understanding Jewish-Christian relations before 70 CE.

### Work and Torah
The ethical instruction to work and contribute (3:6-12) resonates with rabbinic teachings about the dignity of labor. The Talmudic tradition that Torah study should be combined with a worldly occupation (*derekh eretz*) offers an interesting parallel.
