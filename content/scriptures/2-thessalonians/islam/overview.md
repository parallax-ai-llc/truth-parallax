## Overview

Islamic scholarship finds significant points of contact with 2 Thessalonians' eschatological teaching, particularly in the figure of the "man of lawlessness," which Muslim scholars compare to the Islamic tradition of the Dajjal (the Antichrist figure in hadith literature).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Dajjal and the Man of Lawlessness
The description of the man of lawlessness who "opposes and exalts himself against every so-called god" (2:4) invites comparison with the Dajjal of Islamic tradition — a deceiver who claims divine authority before being destroyed at the return of Isa. Muslim scholars trace both traditions to common Semitic apocalyptic roots.

### Divine Judgment and Justice
The letter's assurance that God will repay with affliction those who afflict the righteous (1:6) aligns with the Qur'anic emphasis on divine justice (*'adl*). Islam's robust eschatology of judgment resonates with Paul's vision of vindication for the oppressed.

### Work and Provision
The command to work and not be idle (3:10-12) parallels the Islamic emphasis on productive labor. The Prophet Muhammad said, "No one has ever eaten food better than what he earns from the work of his own hands" (Sahih al-Bukhari), a sentiment Paul would endorse.
