## Ordination and Consecration: Becoming Sacred

The consolamentum's function as an ordination rite — transforming a lay believer into a perfectus or perfecta — connects it to the universal human practice of ritually marking the transition into a sacred role. Every tradition that distinguishes between ordinary and consecrated persons must provide a ritual mechanism for the transition.

In Catholic and Orthodox Christianity, ordination involves the laying on of hands by a bishop in apostolic succession. In Buddhism, monastic ordination (*upasampada*) involves a ceremony before the assembled *sangha* in which the candidate takes the full set of precepts. In Hinduism, the *sannyasa* (renunciation) ceremony involves the ritual death of the householder identity and rebirth as a renunciant. In indigenous traditions, the shaman's initiation often involves a literal or symbolic death and resurrection.

The consolamentum shares structural features with many of these rites: the candidate leaves one social status and enters another; the transition is marked by specific gestures (laying on of hands), sacred objects (the Gospel of John), and formal exchanges (questions and responses); the new status entails specific obligations (ascetic discipline). The cross-cultural comparison illuminates what is universal (the need for ritual transformation) and what is distinctive (the Cathar rejection of material sacramental elements).
