## The Consolamentum of the Dying: Liberation at the Threshold

The consolamentum administered to the dying was, for many Cathar believers, the most significant religious event of their lives. Many credentes postponed receiving the consolamentum until their deathbed, choosing to live under the less demanding expectations of the believer's status while ensuring that they would receive the sacrament before death freed their souls from the material world.

The deathbed consolamentum followed the same basic ritual as the standard consolamentum — the laying on of hands, the placement of the Gospel of John, the recitation of the Lord's Prayer — but was adapted to the physical condition of the dying person. If the candidate was too ill to kneel, the ceremony could be performed while the person lay in bed. If the candidate could not speak, the responses could be made by a representative.

The *endura* — the ritual fast unto death that sometimes followed the deathbed consolamentum — remains the most controversial aspect of Cathar practice. If a person who had received the deathbed consolamentum unexpectedly recovered, they faced a dilemma: either live under the strict disciplines of the perfecti or risk losing the grace of the consolamentum through inevitable transgressions. Some chose the endura — a deliberate fast that ensured death would follow swiftly, preserving the soul's newly conferred purity. Catholic polemicists condemned the endura as a form of suicide; Cathar apologists understood it as the ultimate act of faith — the final rejection of the material world in favor of the spiritual realm to which the soul truly belonged.
