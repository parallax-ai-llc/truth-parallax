## The Aparelhament: Communal Discipline in a Persecuted Community

The aparelhament (also spelled *apparelhamentum* in Latin sources) is attested in both the ritual texts and the depositions of inquisitorial witnesses, allowing scholars to compare the prescribed form of the rite with its actual practice. The depositions of former Cathar believers before the Inquisition of Toulouse and Carcassonne provide detailed accounts of aparelhament ceremonies attended in private homes, barns, and woodland clearings — the clandestine settings required by persecution.

The communal structure of the aparelhament distinguishes it from Catholic confession, which became increasingly individualized and clericalized during the medieval period (particularly after the Fourth Lateran Council of 1215 mandated annual individual confession). The Cathar practice of mutual, communal confession has been compared by scholars to the monastic practice of the *chapter of faults* in Benedictine and Cistercian communities, as well as to the *exagoreusis* (confession of thoughts) practiced in desert monasticism.

The aparelhament's role in maintaining communal solidarity under persecution has been analyzed through the lens of sociology of religion. The monthly gathering for confession and renewal reinforced the boundaries of the perfecti community, distinguished insiders from outsiders, and provided a regular occasion for the transmission of news, encouragement, and practical support in communities living under constant threat of arrest and execution.
