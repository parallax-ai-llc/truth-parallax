## The Deathbed Consolamentum and the Endura: Historical Evidence

The deathbed consolamentum and the associated practice of the *endura* are attested primarily through inquisitorial depositions rather than through the ritual texts themselves (which describe the procedure without mentioning the endura by name). The depositions collected by Bernard Gui, Jacques Fournier (later Pope Benedict XII), and other inquisitors provide vivid — though potentially biased — accounts of deathbed ceremonies and the fasting that sometimes followed.

The frequency and character of the endura remain debated among scholars. Catholic sources portrayed it as widespread and essentially suicidal — a practice in which the dying were starved to death by Cathar perfecti eager to prevent them from recovering and thereby invalidating their consolamentum. Modern historians have generally moderated this picture, noting that the endura was likely practiced by a minority of believers and that its motivations were more complex than simple theological calculation.

Jacques Fournier's detailed registers from the Inquisition of Pamiers (1318-1325), published by Jean Duvernoy, provide the most nuanced evidence. The testimonies describe a range of practices: some dying persons who received the consolamentum recovered and continued to live as perfecti; others who recovered chose to fast; still others received the consolamentum only when death was already certain. The variation suggests that Cathar communities exercised pastoral judgment rather than following a rigid rule.
