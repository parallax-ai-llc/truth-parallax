## The Aparelhament and Catholic Penitential Practice

The aparelhament's practice of communal confession among the perfecti invites comparison with Catholic penitential practice, which underwent significant development during the very period of Cathar activity. The Fourth Lateran Council (1215) mandated annual individual confession to a priest — a requirement that standardized and clericalized a practice that had previously taken multiple forms, including communal and monastic confession.

The Cathar aparelhament preserves a more archaic form of communal confession that has parallels in early monastic practice. The Benedictine *chapter of faults* — a regular meeting in which monks confessed infractions of the Rule before the abbot and community — shares the aparelhament's communal structure and its focus on maintaining the discipline of a consecrated community. The Catholic Church's decision to move toward individual, private confession under the seal of secrecy represented a different pastoral theology, one that emphasized the individual's relationship with God mediated by the priest rather than the community's mutual accountability.

For Catholic readers, the aparelhament raises questions about the relative merits of communal and individual confession — questions that the Catholic Church itself has revisited in the post-Vatican II period, with the introduction of communal penitential services alongside individual confession.
