## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Romans is rooted in the Greek patristic tradition, especially the homilies of John Chrysostom, whose thirty-two homilies on Romans remain the most influential Orthodox commentary. Unlike the Western tradition, which read Romans primarily through the lens of guilt, merit, and forensic justification, the Orthodox tradition reads the letter through the lens of participation in the divine life — theosis (deification).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Reading
Chrysostom's homilies emphasize the pastoral and moral dimensions of Romans. Justification is understood as God's healing of the whole person, not merely a legal verdict. Origen's earlier commentary, preserved in Rufinus's Latin translation, brought an allegorical and spiritual depth that influenced Eastern exegesis for centuries.

### Theosis Framework
Orthodox theologians read Romans 8 — life in the Spirit, adoption as children of God, and the glorification of creation — as describing the process of theosis. The believer participates in God's uncreated energies (Gregory Palamas) rather than receiving an external legal status.

### Synergy
Orthodoxy rejects both Pelagian self-salvation and what it sees as the Protestant over-emphasis on monergism. Romans is read as affirming the cooperation (*synergeia*) between divine grace and human freedom, particularly in Romans 6–8 where Paul exhorts believers to active participation in the life of holiness.

## Theological Emphases

- **Ancestral Sin, not Original Guilt**: Romans 5:12 ("in whom all sinned") is read as mortality inherited, not guilt transferred
- **Baptism as Real Death and Resurrection**: Romans 6:3–4 understood as sacramental participation in Christ's Paschal mystery
- **The Cosmic Scope of Salvation**: Romans 8:19–22 (creation groaning) as anticipation of cosmic transfiguration
- **Divine Election as Foreknowledge**: Romans 9 read through the lens of God's foreknowledge, not unconditional predestination
