## Divine Election and Human Freedom

Orthodox interpretation resists the Calvinist reading of unconditional double predestination. The examples of Jacob and Esau (9:10-13) illustrate God's providential use of particular historical figures, not a universal decree determining individual destinies. God's sovereignty and human freedom are held in mystery.

## The Potter and the Clay

Chrysostom interprets the potter-and-clay metaphor (9:20-23) as teaching humility before God's wisdom, not fatalism. God has the right to shape history, but this does not negate human freedom. If Paul meant God arbitrarily damns people, the moral exhortation of Romans 12-15 would be meaningless.

## God's Mercy as the Key

Orthodox interpreters emphasize that the chapter's central theme is mercy. God's freedom is the freedom to be merciful. The "hardening" of Pharaoh (9:18) is understood as God permitting the natural consequences of Pharaoh's own resistance.
