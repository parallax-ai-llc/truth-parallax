## The Gospel as Divine Power

Romans 1 opens with Paul's programmatic statement that the gospel is "the power of God for salvation" (1:16). Catholic interpretation, following the Fathers, reads this not as a forensic declaration alone but as a transformative force that effects what it proclaims. Augustine drew on this chapter to argue that apart from grace, humanity is incapable of turning to God, a position codified at the Council of Orange (529).

## Natural Knowledge of God

Paul's assertion that God's invisible attributes are "clearly perceived" through creation (1:19-20) is foundational for Catholic natural theology. The First Vatican Council (1870) formally defined that God can be known with certainty by the natural light of reason from created things, citing this passage. Thomas Aquinas built his Five Ways on the philosophical foundation Paul establishes here.

## The Catalogue of Vices

The descent into idolatry and moral corruption described in 1:21-32 is read by Catholic moral theology as illustrating the consequences of rejecting natural law. The sins enumerated represent the disordering of human nature when it is cut off from its proper end in God. The Catechism of the Catholic Church draws on this passage in its treatment of the virtues and vices.
