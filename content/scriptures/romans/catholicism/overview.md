## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Romans engages directly with the Reformation debate over justification while drawing on a patristic and scholastic tradition that long predates it. The Council of Trent (1545–1563) articulated the Catholic position in explicit dialogue with Protestant readings of Romans, affirming that justification involves not merely the imputation of Christ's righteousness but the real interior transformation of the believer through infused grace.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Foundations
The Latin Fathers — especially Augustine, Ambrosiaster, and Origen's Latin translation — shaped the Catholic reading of Romans. Augustine's anti-Pelagian works drew heavily on Romans 5 and 9 to establish doctrines of original sin and prevenient grace, which became normative for Catholic theology.

### Scholastic Synthesis
Thomas Aquinas's commentary on Romans integrates Aristotelian philosophy with Pauline theology. For Aquinas, grace perfects nature; justification is a real change in the soul (*iustificatio impii*), not merely a forensic declaration. Faith working through love (*fides caritate formata*, Galatians 5:6) is the key to reading Romans alongside James 2.

### Post-Conciliar and Ecumenical
The 1999 Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification between the Catholic Church and the Lutheran World Federation represents a significant convergence on Romans, affirming that justification is by grace through faith while maintaining the Catholic emphasis on transformation and cooperation with grace.

## Theological Emphases

- **Infused Righteousness**: Justification as real interior renewal, not merely imputation
- **Baptism**: Romans 6 read as referring to sacramental baptism and its regenerative power
- **Natural Law**: Romans 1:19–20 and 2:14–15 as the foundation for natural theology
- **Merit**: Good works, empowered by grace, have genuine salvific value (Romans 2:6–7)
