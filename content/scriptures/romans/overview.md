## Overview

Romans is a letter written by the Apostle Paul to the Christian community in Rome, likely composed around 57 CE from Corinth. It is widely regarded as Paul's most theologically comprehensive epistle and has exerted unparalleled influence on Christian thought, from Augustine to Luther to Karl Barth. The letter addresses the relationship between divine grace and human effort, the role of the Mosaic Law, and the inclusion of Gentiles in God's covenant people.

## Structure

Romans divides into several major sections:

1. **Introduction and Theme** (1:1–17): The gospel as the power of God for salvation
2. **The Problem of Sin** (1:18–3:20): All humanity — Gentile and Jew — stands under God's judgment
3. **Justification by Faith** (3:21–5:21): Righteousness through faith in Christ, apart from the Law
4. **Sanctification and the Spirit** (6:1–8:39): Freedom from sin, Law, and death through life in the Spirit
5. **Israel and God's Plan** (9:1–11:36): The fate of Israel and the mystery of divine election
6. **Ethical Exhortation** (12:1–15:13): Practical instructions for communal life
7. **Personal Greetings and Closing** (15:14–16:27): Travel plans and commendations

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Romans holds a pivotal position across multiple religious and intellectual traditions:

- **Protestantism**: The catalyst of the Reformation; Luther's reading of Romans 1:17 reshaped Western Christianity
- **Catholicism**: A foundational text for soteriology, read alongside James and the broader canon
- **Eastern Orthodoxy**: Interpreted through the lens of theosis and the patristic tradition
- **Judaism**: Romans 9–11 is the most sustained New Testament engagement with Israel's covenant status
- **Islam**: Paul's theology of grace and law is studied comparatively alongside Quranic soteriology
- **Gnosticism**: Valentinian interpreters appropriated Paul's spirit/flesh dualism for their own cosmology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Justification | Being declared righteous before God through faith |
| Grace | God's unmerited favor as the basis of salvation |
| Law and Gospel | The relationship between Mosaic commandments and the gospel |
| Election | God's sovereign choice of individuals and peoples |
| Spirit vs. Flesh | The contrast between life guided by the Spirit and life in the flesh |
| Israel | The ongoing role of ethnic Israel in God's redemptive plan |
