## Abraham as the Paradigm of Faith

Romans 4 presents Abraham as the model of justification by faith, citing Genesis 15:6 — "Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness." Protestant interpreters emphasize the word "counted" (*elogisthe*) as evidence that righteousness is imputed, not infused. Abraham was declared righteous before circumcision (4:10) and before the giving of the Law, demonstrating that justification depends on faith alone.

## Faith, Not Works

Paul's argument that "to the one who does not work but believes in him who justifies the ungodly, his faith is counted as righteousness" (4:5) is the cornerstone of Protestant soteriology. Luther called this the most radical verse in the Bible: God justifies the *ungodly* — not those who have become godly first. This distinguishes the Protestant doctrine from any scheme in which human moral improvement precedes divine acceptance.

## The Promise to All Who Believe

Paul extends the promise of Abraham to all who share his faith, whether circumcised or uncircumcised (4:11-12). Protestant covenant theology sees this as the unity of the covenant of grace across testaments: Abraham's faith and the Christian's faith have the same object — the God who gives life to the dead and calls into existence the things that do not exist (4:17).
