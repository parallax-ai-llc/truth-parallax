## Baptism into Christ's Death

Romans 6 opens with the rhetorical question "Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase?" (6:1) and answers with the theology of baptism: believers have been "baptized into his death" and "buried with him" so that they might "walk in newness of life" (6:3-4). Protestant interpreters differ on whether this refers to water baptism as a sacramental act or to spiritual union with Christ that baptism signifies.

## Dead to Sin, Alive to God

Paul's exhortation to "consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus" (6:11) is central to Protestant sanctification theology. The indicative (what God has done) grounds the imperative (what believers must do). Lutherans emphasize the daily return to baptismal identity; Reformed theologians stress the "definitive sanctification" that occurs at conversion.

## Slaves of Righteousness

The metaphor of slavery — freed from sin's mastery to become "slaves of righteousness" (6:18) — illustrates that Christian freedom is not autonomy but a transfer of lordship. Protestant ethics draws on this passage to argue that genuine faith necessarily produces a changed life, answering the charge that justification by faith promotes moral indifference.
