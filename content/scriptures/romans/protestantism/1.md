## The Power of the Gospel

Romans 1 opens with Paul's declaration that the gospel is "the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek" (1:16). Protestant interpreters regard this verse as the thesis statement of the entire letter. Luther called it the "chief part and central point" of Romans, and it encapsulates the Reformation principle that salvation comes through faith, not human achievement.

## Natural Revelation and Human Accountability

Paul argues that God's "invisible attributes" have been "clearly perceived" through creation (1:19-20), leaving humanity "without excuse." Protestant theology has drawn on these verses to articulate the doctrine of general revelation — the knowledge of God available to all people through nature and conscience. Calvin developed this into his teaching on the *sensus divinitatis*, an innate awareness of God that sin suppresses but cannot eradicate.

## The Descent into Idolatry

The latter half of the chapter traces a downward spiral: humanity exchanged the glory of God for images, and God "gave them up" to their desires (1:24, 26, 28). Protestant commentators emphasize this as the paradigm of sin — not primarily individual moral failures but the fundamental refusal to honor God as God. The threefold "God gave them up" indicates divine judgment operating through the natural consequences of idolatry.
