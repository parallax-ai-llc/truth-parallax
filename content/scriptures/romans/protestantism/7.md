## The Law's Role and Limitation

Romans 7 addresses the vexing question of the Law's relationship to sin. Paul declares that the Law is "holy and righteous and good" (7:12), yet it became the instrument through which sin produced death. Protestant interpreters see this as the core of the Law-Gospel distinction: the Law diagnoses the disease but cannot cure it.

## The Inner Conflict

The autobiographical passage in 7:14-25 — "I do not do the good I want to do, but the evil I do not want to do — this I keep on doing" (7:19) — has generated one of the most enduring debates in Protestant exegesis. Does Paul describe his pre-conversion experience or his ongoing struggle as a believer? Luther and the Reformed tradition generally favor the latter reading, seeing the passage as an honest depiction of the Christian's simultaneous existence as *simul iustus et peccator* (simultaneously justified and sinner).

## Cry for Deliverance

Paul's anguished cry — "Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death?" (7:24) — and his answer — "Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord!" (7:25) — encapsulate the Protestant understanding that deliverance comes not through moral effort but through Christ alone. This sets up the triumphant declaration of chapter 8.
