## Submission to Governing Authorities

Romans 13:1-7 is one of the most consequential political texts in Western history. Paul's command to "be subject to the governing authorities" because "there is no authority except from God" (13:1) has shaped Protestant political theology from Luther's doctrine of the two kingdoms to Calvin's theology of civil magistracy.

## The Limits of Obedience

Protestant interpreters have debated whether Romans 13 teaches unconditional obedience or qualified submission. Luther used it to condemn the Peasants' Revolt; the Confessing Church in Nazi Germany invoked Acts 5:29 ("we must obey God rather than men") as a necessary complement. Most Protestant ethicists today read Romans 13 as describing the legitimate function of government under God's sovereignty, not as a blank check for tyranny.

## Love Fulfills the Law

Paul's statement that "love is the fulfilling of the law" (13:10) brings the ethical section back to its theological ground. The commandments — "You shall not commit adultery, murder, steal, covet" — are summed up in the command to love one's neighbor (13:9). Protestant ethics sees this as confirming that the moral law remains valid as a guide for Christian life, even though it cannot justify. The chapter closes with an eschatological urgency: "the night is far gone; the day is at hand" (13:12).
