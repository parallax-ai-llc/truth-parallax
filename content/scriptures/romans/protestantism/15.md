## Christ's Example of Service

Romans 15 opens with the exhortation that "we who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak" (15:1), grounded in the example of Christ, who "did not please himself" (15:3). Protestant interpreters see this as the culmination of the ethical argument that began in chapter 12: the strong voluntarily limit their freedom for the sake of unity and love.

## The Hope of the Gentiles

Paul cites a series of Old Testament texts (15:9-12) to demonstrate that Gentile inclusion was always part of God's plan. Isaiah's prophecy of "the root of Jesse" who will "rule the Gentiles" (15:12; cf. Isaiah 11:10) is read as fulfilled in Christ. Protestant mission theology draws on this section to ground the Great Commission in the Old Testament promises.

## Paul's Apostolic Mission

The personal section (15:14-33) reveals Paul's missionary strategy and travel plans, including his ambition to preach in Spain (15:24, 28). Protestant missiology has found in Paul's "pioneer" mentality — "not where Christ has already been named" (15:20) — a model for frontier evangelism. His request for prayer regarding the Jerusalem collection (15:30-31) also illustrates the unity between Jewish and Gentile churches that the letter as a whole commends.
