## Overview

Romans stands at the very heart of Protestant theology. Martin Luther's encounter with Romans 1:17 — "the righteous shall live by faith" — ignited the Reformation, and the letter has remained the primary locus for Protestant soteriology ever since. The doctrine of justification by faith alone (*sola fide*), the distinction between Law and Gospel, and the doctrine of election all find their most developed Pauline expression here.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Reformation Reading
Luther and Calvin read Romans as Paul's systematic exposition of how sinful humanity is made right with God through faith, not works. The Law exposes sin; the Gospel declares pardon. This Law-Gospel dialectic became structural for Lutheran and Reformed theology alike.

### New Perspective on Paul
Since E.P. Sanders, James Dunn, and N.T. Wright, a significant strand of Protestant scholarship has reframed "works of the law" as ethnic boundary markers (circumcision, food laws, Sabbath) rather than moral achievement. This shifts the letter's central question from "How can I be saved?" to "Who belongs to God's people?"

### Evangelical and Reformed
Conservative evangelical interpreters maintain the traditional reading of imputed righteousness and penal substitutionary atonement, while Reformed scholars emphasize the covenant framework (federal theology) linking Adam's fall to Christ's obedience in Romans 5.

## Theological Emphases

- **Total Depravity**: Romans 1:18–3:20 demonstrates that all people, Jew and Gentile, are under sin
- **Imputed Righteousness**: Christ's righteousness is credited to the believer (3:21–26; 4:1–25)
- **Assurance of Salvation**: Romans 8:28–39 as the high point of Christian confidence
- **Predestination and Election**: Romans 9–11 as the basis for Reformed doctrines of election
