## The Remnant of Grace

Romans 11 opens with Paul's reassurance that God has not rejected his people, citing his own Jewish identity as evidence (11:1). He invokes the precedent of Elijah's seven thousand who had not bowed to Baal (11:2-4) to argue for a present "remnant, chosen by grace" (11:5). Protestant theology has used the remnant concept to explain how God's promises to Israel remain valid even amid widespread Jewish rejection of the gospel.

## The Olive Tree

Paul's olive tree metaphor (11:17-24) — natural branches broken off, wild branches grafted in — warns Gentile believers against boasting. Protestant interpreters debate whether the tree represents ethnic Israel, the covenant community, or the people of God across all ages. Reformed covenant theology tends to see continuity between Israel and the church; dispensationalists maintain a sharper distinction.

## The Mystery: All Israel Will Be Saved

Paul's declaration that "all Israel will be saved" (11:26) is one of the most debated verses in Protestant eschatology. Three major views prevail: (1) a future mass conversion of ethnic Jews; (2) "Israel" meaning the elect from all nations; (3) Israel saved through the ongoing process of remnant-plus-Gentile inclusion. The chapter concludes with a doxology (11:33-36) praising the depth of God's wisdom, marking the climax of Romans' theological argument.
