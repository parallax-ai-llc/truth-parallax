## Disputable Matters

Romans 14 addresses the tension between "weak" and "strong" believers over matters of diet and holy days. The "weak" abstain from meat and observe special days; the "strong" eat freely and consider all days alike. Protestant interpreters generally identify the "weak" as Jewish Christians maintaining Torah observances and the "strong" as Gentile believers (or Jews like Paul) who regarded these as non-essential.

## Christian Liberty and Conscience

This chapter is foundational for the Protestant doctrine of *adiaphora* — matters neither commanded nor forbidden by Scripture. Calvin's treatment of Christian liberty (*Institutes* III.19) draws heavily on Romans 14. The principle that "each one should be fully convinced in his own mind" (14:5) protects the freedom of conscience while Paul's warning against causing a brother to stumble (14:13-21) limits that freedom by love.

## The Kingdom of God

Paul's declaration that "the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking but of righteousness and peace and joy in the Holy Spirit" (14:17) provides a Protestant touchstone for distinguishing essentials from non-essentials. The Reformation motto attributed to Rupertus Meldenius — "In essentials unity, in non-essentials liberty, in all things charity" — captures the spirit of Romans 14.
