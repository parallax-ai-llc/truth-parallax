## Commendations and Greetings

Romans 16 opens with Paul's commendation of Phoebe, a *diakonos* (deacon or minister) of the church at Cenchreae (16:1-2). Protestant interpreters have debated the significance of this title: egalitarian scholars see Phoebe as holding a formal ministry office; complementarian scholars read *diakonos* as denoting service without implying ordained leadership. The greeting to Junia (16:7), described as "outstanding among the apostles," has generated similar debate.

## The Roman House Churches

The extensive list of greetings (16:3-16) reveals the social texture of early Roman Christianity: multiple house churches, a mix of Jewish and Gentile names, enslaved persons and freedpersons, prominent women in leadership roles. Protestant social historians draw on this chapter to reconstruct the diversity and vitality of the early church.

## Warning Against Divisive People

Paul's warning against those who "cause divisions and create obstacles contrary to the doctrine that you have been taught" (16:17) introduces an unexpected note of sharpness. Protestant interpreters connect this to the perennial threat of false teaching and the responsibility of church leaders to guard doctrinal integrity. The letter closes with a doxology praising "the only wise God" (16:27), bringing the theological argument of Romans to its liturgical conclusion.
