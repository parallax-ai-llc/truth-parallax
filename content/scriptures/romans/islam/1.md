## Idolatry and Tawhid

Muslim scholars find common ground with Romans 1's condemnation of idolatry. Paul's argument that humanity "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images" (1:23) resonates with the Quran's pervasive critique of shirk. The Quran states: "Indeed, Allah does not forgive association with Him" (4:48). Both Paul and the Quran diagnose idolatry as the fundamental human error.

## Natural Knowledge of God and Fitrah

Paul's claim that God's attributes are "clearly perceived" through creation (1:19-20) aligns with the Islamic concept of fitrah, the innate disposition toward recognizing God. However, while Paul uses natural revelation to establish universal condemnation, Islam sees fitrah as evidence of God's mercy: humanity is oriented toward God from birth, and social corruption, not inherited sin, leads people astray.

## The Moral Critique

The vice catalogue of 1:29-31 overlaps with Quranic moral teaching. Muslim readers appreciate Paul's moral seriousness while noting that the Islamic framework does not require the doctrine of total depravity. Human beings are capable of both good and evil, and God's guidance through the prophets provides the means to choose rightly.
