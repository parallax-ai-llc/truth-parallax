## Overview

Islamic engagement with Romans occurs primarily through comparative theology and polemical literature. Muslim scholars have long regarded Paul as the figure who transformed the simple monotheistic message of Jesus (*Isa*) into a complex theological system centered on vicarious atonement and justification by faith — doctrines that Islam firmly rejects. Romans, as Paul's most systematic theological statement, is therefore a key text in Muslim critiques of Christianity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Theological Critique
Classical and modern Muslim scholars challenge the core argument of Romans: that humanity is universally condemned under sin and can be saved only through faith in Christ's atoning death. Islam teaches that every person is born in a state of natural purity (*fitrah*) and is individually accountable. The concept of inherited original sin (Romans 5:12–21) is rejected as contrary to Quranic teaching: "No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another" (Quran 6:164).

### Comparative Soteriology
Islamic soteriology emphasizes the balance of faith (*iman*) and righteous deeds (*amal salih*). Paul's apparent dichotomy between faith and works in Romans 3–4 is viewed as a false dilemma. The Quran consistently pairs belief with action: "Those who believe and do righteous deeds" is among the most repeated phrases in the Quran.

### Paul and the Historical Jesus
Muslim scholars such as Ahmad Deedat and Seyyed Hossein Nasr argue that Paul's theology in Romans departs from the original message of Jesus. The Jesus of the Quran is a prophet who called people to worship God alone, not a divine savior whose death provides justification.

## Theological Emphases

- **Fitrah**: Humanity's original purity, contra original sin
- **Individual Accountability**: Each person judged by their own deeds (Quran 99:7–8)
- **Tawhid**: God's absolute oneness against any mediatorial soteriology
- **Balance of Faith and Works**: Salvation through belief paired with righteous action
