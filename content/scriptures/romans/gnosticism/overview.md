## Overview

Paul was the most important apostle for Gnostic Christians, and Romans was among their most prized texts. Valentinus and his followers claimed to possess a secret apostolic tradition tracing back to Paul through his disciple Theudas. The letter's sharp contrasts between spirit and flesh, law and grace, death and life provided a ready-made vocabulary for Gnostic cosmology and soteriology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Valentinian Reading
Valentinian Gnostics read Romans through a tripartite anthropology: humanity is divided into pneumatics (spiritual), psychics (soulish), and hylics (material). Paul's statements about those "led by the Spirit" (Romans 8:14) refer to the pneumatic elect who possess the divine spark. The "psychics" — ordinary Christians — may be saved through faith and works, but the pneumatics are saved by nature and gnosis.

### Spirit vs. Flesh
Romans 7–8, with its dramatic contrast between the "law of the mind" and the "law of sin in my members," was read as describing the cosmic struggle between the pneumatic self trapped in material existence and the liberating power of gnosis. The "body of death" (7:24) is the Demiurge's creation; the "Spirit of life" (8:2) is the call from the Pleroma.

### Law as Demiurgic
Paul's complex statements about the Law — "holy and just and good" yet bringing death (7:12–13) — were interpreted as referring to the Demiurge's law: well-intentioned within its limited sphere but ultimately belonging to the lower, material order and incapable of producing true salvation.

## Theological Emphases

- **Pneumatic Election**: Romans 8:28–30 as describing the predetermined salvation of the spiritual seed
- **The Groaning of Creation**: Romans 8:19–22 as the material world yearning to return to the Pleroma
- **Adam and Christ Typology**: Romans 5:12–21 reread as the fall into matter and the restoration through gnosis
- **Rejection of the Demiurge's Law**: Romans 7 as evidence that the Law belongs to the inferior creator
