## Paul's Prophetic Critique

Jewish scholars recognize in Romans 2 a pattern familiar from the Hebrew prophets: the critique of external observance without moral integrity. Paul's argument that "it is not the hearers of the law who are righteous before God, but the doers" (2:13) echoes prophetic traditions. Jewish readers note this does not critique Torah itself but hypocrisy.

## Circumcision and Covenant

Paul's relativization of circumcision (2:25-29) strikes at a central marker of Jewish identity. Jewish interpreters observe that circumcision in Judaism is not a guarantee of righteousness but a covenant sign (ot brit). Paul's argument conflates the sign with a claim to automatic justification that normative Judaism does not make.

## The Law Written on the Heart

Paul's claim that Gentiles have the law "written on their hearts" (2:15) invites comparison with Jeremiah 31:33. Jewish readers ask whether Paul is appropriating Israel's covenant promise and applying it to Gentiles, raising fundamental questions about the relationship between the original covenant community and the emergent Christian movement.
