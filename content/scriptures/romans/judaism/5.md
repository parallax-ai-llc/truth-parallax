## Adam and Original Sin

Romans 5:12-21 is perhaps the most significant point of theological divergence between Paul and Jewish thought. Judaism does not hold the doctrine of original sin as developed by Augustine. The Talmud recognizes the yetzer hara (evil inclination) as part of human nature but does not teach that Adam's sin transmitted guilt. Each person has the capacity to choose life or death (Deuteronomy 30:19).

## Death in Jewish Theology

Paul's assertion that "death spread to all because all sinned" (5:12) raises questions about the Jewish understanding of mortality. While some Jewish texts connect death to Adam's sin, the dominant rabbinic view is that death is a natural part of creation. The Talmud (Shabbat 55a-b) debates whether death can occur without sin.

## Grace in Judaism

Paul's emphasis on grace sometimes implies that Judaism lacks grace. Jewish scholars vigorously contest this: the covenant itself is an act of divine grace (hesed), and the daily liturgy is saturated with thanksgiving for unmerited divine gifts. The question is not whether Judaism has grace but whether it requires Paul's Christological framework.
