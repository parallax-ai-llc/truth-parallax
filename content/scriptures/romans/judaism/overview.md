## Overview

Romans is the New Testament letter most directly engaged with Jewish theology, and Jewish scholars have increasingly turned their attention to it, especially Romans 9–11. Paul himself claims to be "an Israelite, a descendant of Abraham" (11:1), and the letter's arguments about Torah, righteousness, and Israel's covenant status place it squarely within intra-Jewish debate — or at least on its boundary.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historical-Critical
Jewish scholars like Alan Segal, Daniel Boyarin, and Pamela Eisenbaum read Paul as a first-century Jew whose arguments in Romans reflect intra-Jewish diversity rather than a break from Judaism. Paul's critique of "works of the law" is understood as addressing specific halakhic requirements for Gentile inclusion, not Torah observance as such.

### Interfaith Dialogue
Romans 9–11, with its affirmation that "all Israel will be saved" (11:26) and that "the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable" (11:29), has become central to Jewish-Christian dialogue. The 2015 Vatican document *The Gifts and the Calling of God Are Irrevocable* explicitly draws on these verses.

### Critical Engagement
Some Jewish readers remain critical of Romans, seeing Paul's arguments in chapters 1–8 as constructing a rhetorical case against Torah that misrepresents Jewish theology. The notion that the Law brings only knowledge of sin (3:20) is challenged as a caricature of rabbinic teaching, which views Torah as life-giving (*Psalm 19:7*).

## Theological Emphases

- **Torah as Gift, Not Burden**: Jewish readers challenge the Pauline-Lutheran framing of Law as condemning
- **Israel's Irrevocable Covenant**: Romans 11:25–32 as affirmation of ongoing Jewish election
- **Paul as Jew**: Recovering Paul's Jewish identity against later supersessionist readings
- **Righteousness in Judaism**: Jewish *tzedakah* as relational faithfulness, not legalistic merit
