## Abraham in Jewish Tradition

In rabbinic tradition, Abraham is not merely a model of passive trust but an exemplar of active obedience. The Mishnah (Avot 5:3) credits Abraham with ten trials, each demonstrating faithfulness through action. Genesis 15:6 is read as one moment in a life of comprehensive obedience, not the paradigmatic act that supersedes all others.

## Faith and Works in Tension

Paul's sharp distinction between "the one who works" and "the one who believes" (4:4-5) does not map neatly onto Jewish theology, where faith (emunah) and works (ma'asim tovim) are inseparable aspects of covenantal life. Abraham's faith in Genesis 15:6 is immediately followed by the covenant of circumcision in Genesis 17.

## The Promise and Its Scope

Paul's argument that Abraham is "the father of all who believe," including uncircumcised Gentiles (4:11), represents a significant reinterpretation. While Jewish tradition recognizes righteous Gentiles and universal blessings from Abraham, the covenant of circumcision remains specific to Israel.
