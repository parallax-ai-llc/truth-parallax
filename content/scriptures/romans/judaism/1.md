## A Jewish Apostle's Rhetoric

Jewish scholars read Romans 1 with attention to Paul's rhetorical strategy. The chapter's condemnation of idolatry and sexual immorality echoes standard Jewish polemic against pagan culture, found also in the Wisdom of Solomon (13-14) and Philo. Paul writes as a Jew addressing a mixed audience, deploying familiar Jewish arguments.

## Natural Knowledge of God

Paul's assertion that God is known through creation (1:19-20) parallels rabbinic teachings. The Talmud records that Abraham recognized God through observation of the natural world. However, Jewish interpreters note that Paul uses natural theology to intensify condemnation, presupposing a Christological framework foreign to Judaism.

## The Vice List and Jewish Ethics

The catalogue of vices in 1:29-31 overlaps substantially with lists found in Jewish wisdom literature and the Dead Sea Scrolls. Paul's moral analysis of the Gentile world would have been unremarkable to a first-century Jewish audience. The distinctively Pauline move comes in chapter 2, where he turns this critique against his fellow Jews.
