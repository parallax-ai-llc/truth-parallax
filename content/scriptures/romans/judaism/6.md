## Baptism and Jewish Immersion

Paul's baptismal theology invites comparison with Jewish tevilah (immersion in the mikveh). Ritual immersion marks transitions in Judaism. Paul's innovation is connecting immersion with participation in Christ's death and resurrection (6:3-4), transforming a purification rite into an initiation into a new mode of existence.

## Slavery and Freedom

Paul's metaphor of slavery echoes the Exodus narrative. The Passover Haggadah declares that one must see oneself as having personally come out of Egypt. Paul draws on this liberation imagery but redirects it from historical-national liberation to spiritual-cosmic liberation through Christ.

## The Wages of Sin

The famous concluding verse (6:23) expresses a dichotomy that Jewish theology nuances. In rabbinic thought, sin brings consequences but repentance (teshuvah) is always available. The Talmud teaches that "nothing stands in the way of repentance." Paul's stark binary reflects his eschatological urgency.
