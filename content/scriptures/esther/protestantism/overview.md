## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Esther has a complex history. Martin Luther famously expressed reservations about the book, wishing it did not exist. Yet Protestant theology has come to appreciate Esther as a powerful narrative of divine providence — God working behind the scenes even when His name is never mentioned.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Providence Theology
The absence of God's name is understood as a literary device that highlights hidden providence. Every "coincidence" in the book — Esther's selection as queen, the king's sleepless night, the timing of events — points to divine orchestration.

### Typological
Some Protestant interpreters read Esther as a type of the Church, interceding before the King on behalf of God's people. Mordecai's challenge — "Who knows whether you have come to the kingdom for such a time as this?" (4:14) — is applied to every believer's calling.

### Literary
Modern Protestant scholarship appreciates Esther's sophisticated literary artistry: chiastic structure, ironic reversals, and comedic elements that serve theological purposes.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hidden providence**: God works even when invisible
- **Courage and calling**: "For such a time as this" defines vocational purpose
- **Reversal motif**: God inverts the plans of the wicked
- **Jewish identity**: Respect for Israel's ongoing story
