## The Banquet and Vashti's Refusal

Esther 1 sets the stage with an extravagant display of Persian imperial power. King Ahasuerus (Xerxes I) hosts a 180-day display of wealth followed by a seven-day banquet. When Queen Vashti refuses to appear at the king's command, she is deposed — creating the vacancy that will bring Esther to the throne.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Extravagant Display (1:1-9)

The description of imperial wealth and excess establishes the contrast between human power and divine sovereignty. Protestant interpreters note that the entire Persian Empire, with all its glory, serves as the stage for God's providential plan to save His people.

### Vashti's Refusal (1:10-12)

Protestant commentators are divided on Vashti. Some see her as admirably dignified in refusing to be displayed; others focus on her disobedience within the narrative's logic. Either way, her removal is the first "coincidence" in a chain of providence.

### The King's Decree (1:13-22)

The irrevocable nature of Persian law (1:19) establishes a legal framework that will become critical later: once a decree is issued, it cannot be revoked — only countered by a new decree. Protestant interpreters see this as God's sovereignty working within and through human legal systems.

## Theological Significance

- **Providence begins**: God arranges events long before the crisis emerges
- **Human power relativized**: Imperial might serves divine purposes
- **Legal framework**: The immutability of Persian law sets up the later crisis
- **Sovereignty**: God's plan operates through human customs and decisions
