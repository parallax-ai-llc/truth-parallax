## Haman's Genocidal Plot

Esther 3 introduces the antagonist: Haman the Agagite, whose wounded pride escalates into a plan to annihilate every Jew in the Persian Empire. The casting of *pur* (lots) to determine the date gives the festival of Purim its name.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Mordecai's Refusal to Bow (3:1-4)

Mordecai refuses to bow before Haman. Protestant interpreters see this as a principled stand, possibly connected to Haman's Agagite (Amalekite) identity — the ancient enemy of Israel. The conflict between Mordecai (a Benjaminite) and Haman (an Agagite) reprises the Saul-Agag conflict of 1 Samuel 15.

### The Casting of Lots (3:7)

Haman casts *pur* (lots) to determine the auspicious date for the genocide. Protestant commentators note the irony: the lots that Haman casts to determine Israel's destruction actually serve God's timing. Proverbs 16:33 states, "The lot is cast into the lap, but its every decision is from the LORD."

### The Royal Decree (3:12-15)

The edict to destroy all Jews is issued in the king's name and sealed with his ring. The irrevocable nature of Persian law means the decree cannot simply be rescinded — only countered. Protestant interpreters see this as establishing the impossible situation that only divine intervention can resolve.

## Theological Significance

- **Ancient enmity**: The Mordecai-Haman conflict has deep biblical roots
- **Providential timing**: Even "random" lots serve God's purposes
- **Totalitarian evil**: Genocide arises from wounded pride and ethnic hatred
- **Irrevocable law**: The crisis seems humanly unsolvable
