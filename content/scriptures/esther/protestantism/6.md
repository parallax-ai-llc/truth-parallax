## The Night the King Could Not Sleep

Esther 6 contains the book's most stunning reversal. The king's insomnia leads to the reading of court chronicles, the discovery of Mordecai's unrewarded loyalty, and a scene of excruciating irony as Haman unwittingly designs his enemy's honor.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Sleepless Night (6:1)

"On that night the king could not sleep." Protestant interpreters regard this as the clearest evidence of divine providence in the book. The king's insomnia is not a medical condition but God's hand turning the narrative. Every detail follows from this single, divinely orchestrated sleepless night.

### The Ironic Question (6:6)

"What should be done for the man the king delights to honor?" Haman, assuming the king means him, designs an elaborate public honor — which he then must personally bestow on Mordecai. Protestant commentators celebrate this as divine comedy at its finest.

### Haman's Humiliation (6:10-12)

Haman leads Mordecai through the city, proclaiming his honor. Protestant interpreters see in this the biblical principle of reversal: "He has brought down the mighty from their thrones and exalted those of humble estate" (Luke 1:52).

## Theological Significance

- **Divine insomnia**: God orchestrates through ordinary events
- **Ironic reversal**: The arrogant design their own humiliation
- **Delayed reward**: Mordecai's faithfulness is vindicated in God's timing
- **Providence**: The "coincidences" are too precise to be accidental
