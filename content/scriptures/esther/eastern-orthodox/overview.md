## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church reads Esther from the Septuagint, which includes the deuterocanonical additions with their explicit prayers and references to God. Orthodox interpretation emphasizes the themes of divine economy (God's providential governance of history), the power of fasting and prayer, and the dramatic reversal that vindicates the righteous.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Divine Economy
The entire narrative is read within the framework of God's providential plan. Even the seemingly secular events — Vashti's refusal, Esther's beauty, the king's insomnia — serve the divine economy of salvation.

### Ascetical
Esther's three-day fast before approaching the king (4:16, expanded in Addition C) is read as a model of ascetical preparation. Orthodox fasting practice finds biblical warrant in Esther's example.

### Liturgical
The book of Esther is read during Orthodox observances connected to the history of Israel's deliverance.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine economy**: God governs all history providentially
- **Fasting**: Esther's fast models ascetical preparation
- **Reversal**: God vindicates the righteous and overthrows the wicked
- **Courage**: Faithfulness may require risking one's life
