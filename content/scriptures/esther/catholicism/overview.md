## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Esther includes the deuterocanonical additions (Additions A-F) found in the Septuagint but absent from the Hebrew text. These additions explicitly mention God, include prayers by Mordecai and Esther, and provide theological commentary on the narrative. Esther has also been read as a type of the Virgin Mary — a woman whose intercession saves her people.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Deuterocanonical Additions
The Catholic Bible includes six additions that transform the book's theological character by making God's role explicit. Addition C contains Esther's moving prayer before approaching the king, and Addition D dramatically describes her appearance before the king.

### Marian Typology
Catholic tradition has seen in Esther a type of Mary: a woman of humble origin elevated to royal dignity who intercedes before the King on behalf of her people. This reading informs Marian devotion and theology.

### Providence and Intercession
The Catholic reading emphasizes both divine providence and human intercession. Esther's approach to the king is read as a model of intercessory prayer.

## Theological Emphases

- **Deuterocanonical text**: The additions provide explicit theological content
- **Marian typology**: Esther prefigures Mary's intercessory role
- **Providence**: God governs even when not named
- **Intercessory prayer**: Esther models effective intercession
