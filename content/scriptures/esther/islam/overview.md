## Overview

The book of Esther is not referenced directly in the Quran, and the figures of Esther, Mordecai, and Haman do not appear in Islamic scripture. However, the narrative's Persian setting, its themes of divine protection of a vulnerable community, and the figure of Haman (who shares a name with the Haman of the Quran, though they are different figures) provide points of connection with Islamic theological concerns.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historical Context
Muslim historians like al-Tabari include the Persian-period Jewish community in their universal histories, drawing on Isra'iliyyat traditions. The story of Esther, while not Quranic, is part of the broader history of the Bani Isra'il that Islamic scholarship acknowledges.

### Theological Parallels
The theme of God protecting a vulnerable community from genocide resonates with the Quranic narratives of divine intervention on behalf of the faithful. The reversal of Haman's plot parallels the Quran's teaching that "they plan, and Allah plans, and Allah is the best of planners" (8:30).

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine protection**: God shields vulnerable communities
- **Divine planning**: God's plan supersedes human plotting
- **Justice**: Oppressors are ultimately overthrown
- **Historical interest**: The Persian-period Jewish community in Islamic historiography
