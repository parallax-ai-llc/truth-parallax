## Mordecai's Legacy

Jewish interpretation of Esther 10 focuses on Mordecai's characterization as one who "sought the welfare of his people and spoke peace to all his offspring" (10:3). The Talmud (Megillah 16b) notes, however, that "some of his brothers" were not pleased — a subtle hint that his political involvement came at the cost of full devotion to Torah study.

## Key Themes

### Mordecai's Rank

The Talmud observes that Mordecai's name appears in Ezra 2:2 among the returnees from exile, placing him historically. His elevation to second-in-command parallels Joseph's position in Egypt — a pattern of Jewish flourishing in foreign courts.

### A Subtle Critique

The phrase "popular with the multitude of his brothers" (10:3) uses "multitude" (*rov*) rather than "all" — the rabbis infer that some scholars disapproved of Mordecai's political career, preferring that he devote himself entirely to Torah study. This gentle critique reflects the rabbinic tension between public service and Torah scholarship.

## Theological Significance

- **Joseph parallel**: Jewish leadership in foreign courts is a recurring pattern
- **Doresh tov**: "Seeking the welfare" of one's people is a sacred obligation
- **Subtle critique**: Even heroes face rabbinic scrutiny
- **Diaspora theology**: Jews can flourish and serve in exile
