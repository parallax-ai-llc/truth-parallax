## The Institution of Purim

Jewish interpretation of Esther 9 is inseparable from the extensive halakhic framework governing Purim observance. The Talmud (Megillah) devotes detailed attention to every aspect of the festival.

## Key Themes

### The Four Mitzvot of Purim

The chapter establishes four obligations that the halakhah codifies:

| Mitzvah | Source | Description |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Megillah reading | 9:28 | Public reading of the entire scroll |
| Mishloach manot | 9:22 | Sending food portions to friends |
| Matanot la-evyonim | 9:22 | Gifts to the poor |
| Se'udah (feast) | 9:22 | The festive Purim meal |

### V'nahafokh Hu (9:1)

"It was reversed" — this phrase encapsulates the entire book's theology and becomes the thematic motto of Purim celebration. The tradition of costume-wearing on Purim reflects this theme of reversal and hidden identity.

### Ad D'lo Yada

The Talmud (Megillah 7b) teaches that one should drink on Purim "until one cannot distinguish between 'cursed be Haman' and 'blessed be Mordecai'" — a remarkable rabbinic injunction to celebrate beyond the limits of ordinary sobriety.

## Theological Significance

- **Halakhic framework**: Purim is governed by detailed legal requirements
- **V'nahafokh hu**: Reversal is the festival's governing principle
- **Joy as obligation**: Purim mandates celebration and generosity
- **Community and charity**: Joy overflows into care for the poor
