## The Night of Reversal

Jewish interpretation of Esther 6 delights in the exquisite irony of the scene. The Talmud (Megillah 15b-16a) provides detailed commentary on every element.

## Key Themes

### The King's Insomnia

The Talmud interprets "the king's sleep was disturbed" as referring not only to the earthly king but also to the "King of Kings" — God Himself, who orchestrates the events of this crucial night.

### "What Has Been Done for Mordecai?" (6:3)

The discovery that Mordecai was never rewarded sets the reversal in motion. The midrash imagines the heavenly court arranging events: the page falls open to exactly the right entry because angels guide the reader's hand.

### Haman's Humiliation

The image of Haman leading Mordecai through the city while Haman's daughter dumps waste on her father's head (thinking the rider was Haman and the leader was Mordecai) is a classic midrashic addition (Megillah 16a) that intensifies the comic reversal.

## Theological Significance

- **Double meaning**: The "king" whose sleep is disturbed is also the King of Kings
- **Divine orchestration**: Angels guide the details of the reversal
- **Comic justice**: The midrash adds layers of humiliation to the proud
- **V'nahafokh hu**: The principle of reversal governs the entire narrative
