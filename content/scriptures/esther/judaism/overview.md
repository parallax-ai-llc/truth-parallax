## Overview

In the Jewish canon, Esther is one of the Five Megillot (Scrolls) and is read aloud in its entirety on the festival of Purim. The book holds a unique status in Jewish tradition: the Talmud (Megillah 7a) records debates about its sanctity, yet it became one of the most beloved texts in Jewish life. The rabbis found profound theological meaning in the very absence of God's name, developing the concept of *hester panim* — the "hiding of God's face."

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Talmudic
The Talmud devotes an entire tractate (Megillah) to the laws and customs surrounding the reading of Esther. The rabbis derive numerous halakhic and aggadic teachings from the text, including the obligation to read the Megillah, give gifts to the poor, send food portions to friends, and feast joyfully.

### Midrashic
The Midrash expansively fills in the narrative's gaps, providing backstories for characters, explaining motivations, and drawing moral lessons. Esther Rabbah is a rich collection of these interpretive traditions.

### Philosophical
Medieval and modern Jewish thinkers have explored the theological implications of divine hiddenness, using Esther as a case study for understanding God's presence in a seemingly godless world.

## Theological Emphases

- **Hester panim**: God's hidden face still guides events
- **Purim**: The joyful festival commemorating deliverance
- **Diaspora identity**: Maintaining Jewish identity in a foreign land
- **Divine reversal**: *V'nahafokh hu* — "it was reversed" (9:1)
