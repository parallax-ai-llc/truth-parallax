## Haman the Agagite: The Amalekite Enemy

Jewish interpretation identifies Haman as a descendant of Agag, king of Amalek (3:1). This identification transforms the Esther narrative into the latest chapter in the cosmic conflict between Israel and Amalek — the archetypal enemy whom God commands Israel to remember and destroy (Deuteronomy 25:17-19).

## Key Themes

### The Pur (Lot)

The Talmud (Megillah 13b) explains that Haman cast lots to find an auspicious month and was pleased when the lot fell on Adar, the month of Moses' death. But he did not know that Moses was also born in Adar — the month of death is also the month of redemption.

### Haman's Accusation (3:8)

"There is a certain people scattered and dispersed among the peoples... whose laws are different from every other people." The rabbis note that Haman's accusation is both true and false: the Jews are indeed different, but this difference is their greatest virtue, not a threat.

## Theological Significance

- **Amalek**: The cosmic enemy of Israel appears in every generation
- **Lot as providence**: The pur that seems random serves God's purpose
- **Antisemitic rhetoric**: Haman's accusations model timeless patterns of hatred
- **Adar**: The month of death becomes the month of deliverance
