## Haman's Downfall

Jewish interpretation of Esther 7 celebrates the dramatic reversal with characteristic rabbinic attention to every detail. The Talmud and midrash explore the psychological and spiritual dimensions of the scene.

## Key Themes

### Esther's Accusation (7:6)

"A foe and enemy, this wicked Haman!" The midrash notes that Esther initially pointed at Ahasuerus as well as Haman, and an angel redirected her hand — even in her righteous anger, divine guidance intervened.

### The Gallows (7:9-10)

Harbona's suggestion to use Haman's own gallows is celebrated by the rabbis. The Talmud (Megillah 16a) identifies Harbona as either a righteous man or even the prophet Elijah in disguise. The liturgical tradition includes "Cursed be Haman; blessed be Mordecai" as part of Purim celebrations.

### Measure for Measure

The principle of *middah k'neged middah* (measure for measure) reaches its climax: the gallows intended for the righteous receives the wicked.

## Theological Significance

- **Middah k'neged middah**: Divine justice operates proportionally
- **Angelic intervention**: Divine guidance operates even in human gestures
- **Purim liturgy**: "Cursed be Haman; blessed be Mordecai" structures Purim celebration
- **Complete reversal**: The wicked receive exactly what they planned for others
