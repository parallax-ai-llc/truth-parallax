## The Royal Banquet in Jewish Tradition

Jewish interpretation of Esther 1 is enriched by extensive midrashic commentary. The Talmud (Megillah 12a) identifies Ahasuerus with Xerxes I and interprets the 180-day feast as a celebration of what Ahasuerus believed was the failed prophecy of Jewish restoration — after seventy years, the temple had not been rebuilt (by his reckoning).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Banquet's Timing

The Talmud (Megillah 11b-12a) records that Ahasuerus calculated the seventy years of exile and, believing the prophecy had lapsed, celebrated by using the temple vessels at his feast — a detail that explains why divine retribution follows.

### Vashti's Refusal

Rabbinic tradition (Megillah 12b) offers various explanations for Vashti's refusal: she was afflicted with a skin disease (divine punishment for her cruelty to Jewish women), or she recognized the impropriety of the king's command. The midrash presents Vashti not as a heroine but as a wicked queen justly removed.

### The Irony of the Decree

The decree that "every man should be master in his own house" (1:22) is read by the rabbis with considerable humor: a decree so obvious it undermines itself.

## Theological Significance

- **Midrashic richness**: The rabbis fill every gap with interpretive tradition
- **Divine retribution**: Misuse of temple vessels brings consequences
- **Humor**: Jewish tradition embraces the book's comedic elements
- **Providential irony**: Human decrees serve divine purposes
