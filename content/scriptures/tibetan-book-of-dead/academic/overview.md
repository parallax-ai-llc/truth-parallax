## Overview

The Tibetan Book of the Dead has been the subject of extensive academic study since W.Y. Evans-Wentz's pioneering 1927 translation brought it to Western attention. Scholarship has examined the text's historical origins, its place within the *terma* tradition, the accuracy and influence of various translations, and its significance for understanding Tibetan Buddhist ritual, psychology, and eschatology.

## Translation History and Controversy

Evans-Wentz's 1927 translation, based on Kazi Dawa Samdup's English rendering, introduced the text to the West under the title "The Tibetan Book of the Dead" — an explicit analogy to the Egyptian Book of the Dead. Scholars have since critiqued this framing as misleading, arguing that it obscured the text's specifically Buddhist context and imposed Western categories of "death and dying" onto a tradition concerned primarily with the nature of consciousness and liberation. Robert Thurman's 1994 translation and Gyurme Dorje's 2005 critical edition have offered more contextually grounded renderings.

## The Terma Tradition

The Bardo Thodol's claim to have been composed by Padmasambhava in the 8th century and concealed as a *terma* for later discovery raises complex questions about authorship, authority, and textual transmission in Tibetan Buddhism. Bryan Cuevas's *The Hidden History of the Tibetan Book of the Dead* (2003) examines the historical evidence for the text's emergence, tracing it to the 14th-century *terton* Karma Lingpa and situating it within the broader phenomenon of treasure revelation in the Nyingma tradition.

## Psychological and Neuroscientific Interest

The Bardo Thodol has attracted significant interest from psychologists and neuroscientists, beginning with Carl Jung's psychological commentary on the Evans-Wentz translation. Contemporary researchers have explored parallels between the bardo experiences described in the text and near-death experiences (NDEs) reported in clinical settings. While these comparisons remain controversial, they have opened productive dialogue between Buddhist contemplative traditions and the science of consciousness.
