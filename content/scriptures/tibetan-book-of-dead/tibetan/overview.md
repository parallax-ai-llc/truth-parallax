## Overview

The Tibetan Book of the Dead (*Bar do thos grol chen mo*, "The Great Liberation Through Hearing in the Bardo") is one of the most important practice texts in Tibetan Buddhism, particularly within the Nyingma tradition. Attributed to Padmasambhava and revealed by the *terton* Karma Lingpa in the 14th century, it is an integral part of the Nyingma *terma* (treasure) literature and is used as a practical guide for the dying, the dead, and their living companions.

## The Six Bardos

While the Bardo Thodol focuses on the three bardos between death and rebirth, Tibetan teaching recognizes six bardos in total: the bardo of this life (*skye gnas bar do*), the bardo of dreaming (*rmi lam bar do*), the bardo of meditation (*bsam gtan bar do*), the bardo of dying (*'chi kha'i bar do*), the bardo of dharmata (*chos nyid bar do*), and the bardo of becoming (*srid pa'i bar do*). The text's instructions are most effective when the practitioner has trained in all six bardos during life.

## Ritual Practice

In Tibetan Buddhist funeral practice, the Bardo Thodol is read aloud beside the body of the deceased for up to forty-nine days — the traditional duration of the bardo between death and rebirth. A qualified lama guides the consciousness of the deceased through the successive stages, calling out the names of the peaceful and wrathful deities and instructing the consciousness to recognize them as projections of its own nature. Phowa (*'pho ba*) — the practice of consciousness transference — may also be performed to help the deceased achieve liberation or a favorable rebirth.

## Dzogchen and the Clear Light

The Bardo Thodol's teaching on recognizing the clear light at the moment of death is closely connected to Dzogchen (*rdzogs chen*) — the Great Perfection teaching of the Nyingma school. In Dzogchen, the practitioner trains during life to recognize *rigpa* (pure awareness) as the fundamental nature of mind. At the moment of death, this recognition becomes spontaneous liberation. The Bardo Thodol can be understood as a Dzogchen practice text adapted for the specific circumstances of death and the post-mortem state.
