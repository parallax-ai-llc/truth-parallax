## Mahayana Interpretation

This chapter presents the arising of the hundred peaceful deities in the Chonyid Bardo, appearing as brilliant lights, sounds, and forms over a period of days, each representing a pure aspect of the practitioner's own consciousness. From a broader Mahayana perspective, the teachings here represent a specialized application of the Yogacara understanding of consciousness and the *tathagatagarbha* teaching that the fundamental nature of mind is pure, luminous awareness.

The chapter's central instruction — to recognize all appearances as projections of one's own mind and to rest in the recognition of their empty, luminous nature — directly enacts the Yogacara teaching that the three realms are mind-only (*cittamatra*). When the practitioner recognizes the visions of the bardo as mental projections, they actualize the Yogacara insight at the most critical moment of the spiritual journey.

The compassionate dimension of the Mahayana is expressed in the very existence of this text: it is designed to be read to another being in their most vulnerable moment, embodying the bodhisattva's vow to benefit others by every available means. The text's promise that liberation is possible even after death reflects the Mahayana confidence in the universal accessibility of awakening.
