## Overview

From a Theravada perspective, the Tibetan Book of the Dead (Bardo Thodol) is a Tibetan Vajrayana text that lies outside the Pali Canon and reflects doctrinal and practical developments specific to Tibetan Buddhism. Nevertheless, Theravada scholars engage with its content in light of the Pali teachings on consciousness at the time of death, the process of rebirth, and the significance of mental states at the moment of dying.

## Death and Consciousness in the Pali Canon

The Pali Canon contains significant teachings on the death process. The Mahaparinibbana Sutta describes the Buddha's own death as a model of mindful dying, and the Abhidhamma analyzes the *cuticitta* (death consciousness) and *patisandhicitta* (relinking consciousness) that connect one life to the next. The concept of *bhavanga* (life-continuum consciousness) provides a framework for understanding the transition between lives, though it differs substantially from the Tibetan bardo model.

## Points of Convergence and Divergence

Both Theravada and the Bardo Thodol agree that the quality of consciousness at death significantly influences the rebirth that follows — hence the emphasis in both traditions on mindful dying and the recitation of teachings to the dying person. However, the elaborate visionary content of the Bardo Thodol — the succession of peaceful and wrathful deities, the lights of the six realms — reflects specifically Vajrayana tantric practices and Nyingma cosmology that have no counterpart in the Pali tradition.

## Comparative Value

Theravada practitioners and scholars find comparative value in the Bardo Thodol as an example of how Buddhist cultures have developed practical guidance for the dying process. The underlying principle — that awareness at the moment of death can lead to liberation — is shared across Buddhist traditions, even if the methods and cosmological frameworks differ significantly.
