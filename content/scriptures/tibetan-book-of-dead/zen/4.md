## Zen Reading

This chapter presents the Sidpa Bardo or bardo of becoming, in which the consciousness, having failed to attain liberation through recognizing the peaceful and wrathful deities, now seeks rebirth and must navigate the pull of karmic forces toward the six realms of existence. While the Bardo Thodol belongs to the Tibetan Vajrayana tradition, Zen practitioners find in its core instruction a teaching that resonates with the deepest levels of Zen practice: the recognition of the nature of mind in the midst of whatever arises.

The instruction to neither grasp nor flee from the appearances of the bardo parallels the Zen teaching of *shikantaza* — just sitting, just being present with whatever manifests without adding the overlay of conceptual judgment. Whether on the meditation cushion or at the moment of death, the practice is the same: meet what arises with clear, open awareness and let it be.

From a Zen perspective, the chapter's teaching need not be understood only in the context of physical death. Every moment of distraction, fear, or grasping is a "small death" — a momentary loss of awareness. And every moment of returning to presence is a "small liberation." The Bardo Thodol's instructions, read in this light, become a manual for moment-to-moment awakening in daily life.
