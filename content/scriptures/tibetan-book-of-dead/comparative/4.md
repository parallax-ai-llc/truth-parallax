## Comparative Perspective

This chapter presents the Sidpa Bardo or bardo of becoming, in which the consciousness, having failed to attain liberation through recognizing the peaceful and wrathful deities, now seeks rebirth and must navigate the pull of karmic forces toward the six realms of existence. The themes addressed here — the fate of consciousness after death, the visionary journey through otherworldly realms, judgment, and the possibility of liberation or rebirth — are among the most universal concerns of human religious experience.

The chapter's description of the post-mortem journey invites comparison with the Egyptian Book of the Dead's account of the soul's passage through the Duat (underworld), the Greek *katabasis* (descent to the underworld) as depicted in Homer and Virgil, and the Islamic *mi'raj* (the Prophet Muhammad's night journey through the heavens). In each tradition, the journey through otherworldly realms serves both as a cosmological statement and as a moral-spiritual teaching about the consequences of one's actions during life.

The judgment scene described in this chapter — in which the deceased's actions are weighed before Yama — has particularly close parallels with the Egyptian weighing of the heart against the feather of Ma'at and the Christian tradition of particular judgment after death. The Bardo Thodol's distinctive contribution, however, is its teaching that the judge, the judgment, and the one being judged are all projections of the mind itself — a teaching that transforms the judgment from an external event into an opportunity for self-recognition and liberation.
