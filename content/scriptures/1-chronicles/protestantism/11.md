## David's Coronation and Conquest of Jerusalem

Chapter 11 opens the Chronicler's account of David's reign with his anointing at Hebron, the capture of Jerusalem, and the catalog of his mighty warriors. The Chronicler presents David as a king who unites all Israel from the very beginning.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### All Israel Anoints David (11:1-3)

Unlike Samuel's account of David's gradual rise, the Chronicler depicts all Israel gathering at Hebron to make David king. Protestant interpreters see the Chronicler's theological emphasis: David is God's chosen king for all the people, not merely a tribal leader who gradually consolidated power.

### The Capture of Zion (11:4-9)

David conquers Jerusalem — the Jebusite stronghold — and makes it his capital. Joab leads the assault and is rewarded with military command. The city becomes 'the City of David,' establishing the theological geography that will define Israel's worship. **Selman** notes that Jerusalem's capture fulfills the need for a neutral capital that belongs to no single tribe.

### The Mighty Warriors (11:10-47)

The extended list of David's warriors — their names, exploits, and loyalties — demonstrates that David's kingdom was built on covenantal loyalty, not mere political convenience. Three warriors risking their lives to bring David water from Bethlehem's well exemplifies devotion beyond duty.

## Theological Significance

1. **United kingdom**: All Israel gathers to David, modeling the unity God desires for His people
2. **Jerusalem as God's city**: The capture of Zion establishes the sacred center of Israel's worship
3. **Loyal community**: David's warriors exemplify covenantal devotion
4. **Christological typology**: David as the anointed king gathering all God's people foreshadows Christ
