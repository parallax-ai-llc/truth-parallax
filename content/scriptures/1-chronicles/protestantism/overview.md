## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 1 Chronicles emphasizes the Chronicler's theological reshaping of Israel's history around covenant faithfulness, temple worship, and the Davidic promise. The book is read as a divinely inspired reinterpretation that highlights the spiritual dimensions of David's reign — particularly his passion for worship and his preparations for the Temple — as a model for the New Testament church.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Redemptive-Historical
Reformed and evangelical scholars read 1 Chronicles within the arc of redemption history. The genealogies trace God's covenant purposes from Adam to David, and David's temple preparations point forward to Christ, the true Temple (John 2:19-21). The Chronicler's idealized portrait of David is understood typologically as anticipating the perfect King.

### Covenantal
The Davidic covenant in chapter 17 is the theological center of the book. Protestant interpreters connect this promise — "I will establish his throne forever" — to the New Testament's identification of Jesus as the son of David who fulfills this eternal kingship.

### Worship-Centered
The detailed organization of Levitical musicians, singers, and gatekeepers (chapters 23–26) is read as evidence that worship is central to the life of God's people. Protestant reformers drew on these texts to argue for the priority of congregational worship ordered by Scripture.

## Theological Emphases

- **David as worshiper**: The Chronicler's David is above all a man of worship, not merely a warrior-king
- **Typology**: David's temple preparations prefigure Christ's building of the Church
- **Providence in genealogy**: The genealogies demonstrate God's sovereign ordering of history toward redemption
- **Retribution theology**: The Chronicler's emphasis on divine justice reinforces the Protestant understanding of God's moral governance
