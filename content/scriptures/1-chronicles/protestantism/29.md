## Overflowing Generosity and the King's Farewell

Chapter 29, the climax of 1 Chronicles, records the people's generous offerings for the Temple, David's magnificent prayer of praise, Solomon's coronation, and David's death. The chapter is a crescendo of worship, generosity, and doxology.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### David's Personal Gift (29:1-5)

David donates his personal treasure — gold, silver, bronze, iron, wood, and precious stones — beyond what he has already provided from royal resources. He then asks: 'Who is willing to consecrate themselves to the LORD today?' Protestant interpreters see this as a model of sacrificial leadership: the leader gives first and most generously.

### The People's Response (29:6-9)

The leaders and people give willingly and abundantly. 'The people rejoiced at the willing response of their leaders, for they had given freely and wholeheartedly to the LORD. David the king also rejoiced greatly.' **Selman** notes that the Chronicler's ideal of giving is characterized by willingness, joy, and wholeheartedness.

### David's Prayer (29:10-19)

David's prayer is among the greatest in Scripture: 'Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendor, for everything in heaven and earth is yours' (29:11). He acknowledges that all gifts come from God and are merely returned: 'Everything comes from you, and we have given you only what comes from your hand' (29:14). This prayer shapes Protestant doxology and theology of stewardship.

### Solomon's Coronation and David's Death (29:21-30)

Solomon is crowned a second time with full public ceremony, and David dies 'at a good old age, having enjoyed long life, wealth and honor.' The Chronicler's final portrait of David is a man of worship, generosity, and covenant faithfulness.

## Theological Significance

1. **Stewardship**: Everything belongs to God; giving is returning what is already His
2. **Joyful generosity**: True giving is willing, abundant, and full of joy
3. **Doxology**: David's prayer is a model of worship that acknowledges God's sovereignty over all things
4. **Leadership by example**: The king gives first, inspiring the people's response
