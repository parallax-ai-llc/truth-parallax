## The Chronicler's Omission and Prophetic Dignity

Islamic engagement with chapter 20 notes with approval the Chronicler's omission of the Bathsheba-Uriah narrative. The Islamic doctrine of prophetic infallibility (*ismah*) holds that prophets do not commit major sins, and the Chronicler's silence on this episode aligns more closely with the Islamic understanding of Dawud's character.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### The Omission of Bathsheba (20:1)

Islamic scholars view the Chronicler's omission of the Bathsheba narrative as evidence that this tradition recognized the problematic nature of attributing such sins to a prophet. The Quran's own account of Dawud's "test" (Sad 38:21-25) is interpreted by most Muslim scholars as involving a judicial error, not adultery and murder.

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)

The Islamic doctrine of *ismah* holds that prophets are protected from major sins. The Chronicler's presentation of David — without the Bathsheba episode — is closer to the Islamic ideal of prophetic dignity than the Samuel account.

### Military Victories (20:4-8)

The defeat of Philistine giants continues the pattern of divinely aided victory. Islamic tradition celebrates Dawud's defeat of Jalut as paradigmatic of God's power working through faithful servants.

## Theological Significance

1. **Prophetic dignity**: The Chronicler's omission aligns with Islamic prophetic theology
2. **Ismah**: Prophets are protected from major sins by divine grace
3. **Tahrif**: The Samuel narrative's accusations against David may represent textual corruption
4. **Divine aid**: God continues to grant victory to the faithful
