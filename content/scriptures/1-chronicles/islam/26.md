## Guardians and Stewards of Sacred Property

Islamic engagement with the gatekeepers and treasurers draws parallels to Islamic institutions of sacred stewardship — particularly the *waqf* (religious endowment) and the guardianship of mosques and holy sites.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Guarding Sacred Space (26:1-19)

The gatekeepers who guard the Temple's entrances parallel the guardians (*khadam*) of Islamic holy sites — the custodians of the two Holy Mosques, the guardians of al-Masjid al-Aqsa, and the caretakers of local mosques worldwide.

### Temple Treasuries (26:20-28)

The management of dedicated gifts and temple finances parallels the Islamic *waqf* — endowments dedicated to religious purposes that must be managed with scrupulous integrity. Islamic law provides detailed regulations for waqf administration, echoing the Chronicler's concern for faithful financial stewardship.

### Sacred Trust (*Amanah*) (26:20-32)

The Quran commands: "Indeed, Allah commands you to render trusts to whom they are due" (An-Nisa 4:58). The treasurers' role embodies the Islamic concept of *amanah* (trust) — the faithful stewardship of resources entrusted to one's care.

## Theological Significance

1. **Waqf parallels**: Dedicated temple resources mirror Islamic religious endowments
2. **Amanah**: Sacred trust requires scrupulous honesty in administration
3. **Guardianship**: Protecting sacred spaces is a universal religious duty
4. **Financial integrity**: Religious institutions require transparent financial management
