## Dawud's Praise and the Zabur Tradition

Islamic engagement with David's psalm of thanksgiving connects directly to the Quranic affirmation that Dawud received the Zabur (Psalms): "And to Dawud We gave the Zabur" (An-Nisa 4:163). David's praise in this chapter is read as an expression of the prophetic devotion that characterizes the Zabur.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### The Psalm as Zabur (16:8-36)

The composite psalm of praise, thanksgiving, and proclamation of God's sovereignty reflects the kind of devotional expression Islam associates with the Zabur. While Islam does not accept the biblical Psalms as perfectly preserved revelation, the themes of divine sovereignty, creation's praise, and human gratitude align with Islamic worship.

### Dhikr — Remembrance of God (16:8-12)

"Give thanks to the LORD, call upon his name, make known his deeds among the peoples." The Islamic practice of *dhikr* (remembrance of God) echoes this call to constant awareness of God's deeds. The Quran commands: "Remember Me, and I will remember you" (Al-Baqarah 2:152).

### God's Sovereignty over Creation (16:23-33)

The psalm's proclamation of God's sovereignty over all creation resonates with the Islamic concept of *tawhid al-rububiyyah* — God's lordship over all things. "The heavens declare his glory" parallels the Quranic teaching that all creation glorifies God (Al-Isra 17:44).

## Theological Significance

1. **Zabur connection**: The psalm reflects the devotional tradition attributed to Dawud
2. **Dhikr**: Constant remembrance of God is a shared value
3. **Tawhid**: God's absolute sovereignty over creation is affirmed
4. **Universal praise**: All creation joins in glorifying the one God
