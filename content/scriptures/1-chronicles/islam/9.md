## Jerusalem (Al-Quds) and the Restored Community

Islamic engagement with chapter 9 touches on the significance of Jerusalem — known in Islamic tradition as Al-Quds (The Holy) and the site of al-Masjid al-Aqsa. The restoration of a worshipping community in Jerusalem resonates with Islam's own reverence for the city.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Jerusalem in Islamic Tradition

Al-Quds holds a special place in Islam as the site of the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey (*Isra'*) and Ascension (*Mi'raj*). The Quran states: "Exalted is He who took His Servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed" (Al-Isra 17:1). The post-exilic community's return to Jerusalem connects to Islam's own sacred geography.

### Temple Personnel (9:10-34)

The detailed listing of priests and temple servants reflects a worship system that Islam acknowledges historically but does not adopt theologically. Islamic worship requires no priestly intermediary — every Muslim stands before God directly in salat (prayer).

### Exile and Return

The pattern of exile for unfaithfulness and return through God's mercy aligns with the Quranic narrative of the Bani Isra'il. God punishes disobedience but remains merciful to those who repent and return.

## Theological Significance

1. **Al-Quds**: Jerusalem's sanctity is affirmed across all three Abrahamic traditions
2. **Direct worship**: Islam requires no priestly mediation for access to God
3. **Exile and mercy**: God's punishment is real but His mercy is always available
4. **Sacred continuity**: Jerusalem's religious significance endures across millennia
