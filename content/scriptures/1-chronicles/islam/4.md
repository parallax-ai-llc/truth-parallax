## Prayer and Divine Response

Islamic engagement with chapter 4 focuses on the prayer of Jabez as an example of *du'a* (supplication) — a practice central to Islamic worship. The narrative demonstrates that sincere prayer, offered with trust in God, receives divine response.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### The Prayer of Jabez (4:9-10)

Jabez's prayer for blessing, expanded territory, God's hand, and protection from evil resonates with the Islamic understanding of *du'a*. The Quran teaches: "Call upon Me; I will respond to you" (Ghafir 40:60). Jabez's prayer models the Islamic virtues of sincerity (*ikhlas*), trust (*tawakkul*), and directness in addressing God.

### Craftsmen and Workers (4:14, 21-23)

The mention of potters and craftsmen aligns with the Islamic value of honest labor. The Prophet Muhammad said: "No one has ever eaten food better than what is earned by the work of his own hands" (Bukhari). Every lawful vocation is honorable before God.

### Genealogical Details

The specific tribal genealogies are of limited theological interest in Islam, which emphasizes individual faith and righteousness over tribal identity. The Quran states: "The most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (Al-Hujurat 49:13).

## Theological Significance

1. **Du'a**: Sincere supplication receives divine response
2. **Tawakkul**: Trust in God accompanies active effort
3. **Dignity of labor**: All honest work is honorable before God
4. **Individual righteousness**: Personal piety surpasses tribal identity
