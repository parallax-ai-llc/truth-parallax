## Dawud's Ascension to the Throne

Islamic engagement with David's coronation and conquest of Jerusalem centers on Dawud as a divinely appointed prophet-king. The Quran affirms: "We gave Dawud favor from Us" (Saba 34:10), and his kingship represents the ideal union of temporal authority and prophetic wisdom.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Dawud's Kingship (11:1-3)

Islamic tradition affirms that Dawud received both *mulk* (kingdom) and *hikmah* (wisdom) from God: "And Allah gave him the kingdom and wisdom and taught him whatever He willed" (Al-Baqarah 2:251). The political maneuvering described in the biblical text — tribal negotiations, military alliances — is viewed as secondary to the divine appointment.

### Jerusalem (11:4-9)

The conquest of Jerusalem establishes the city's sacred character. Islamic tradition reveres Al-Quds as the third holiest city in Islam and the first qiblah (direction of prayer) before Mecca. Dawud's establishment of Jerusalem as his capital connects to Islam's own sacred geography.

### The Warriors (11:10-47)

The mighty warriors are acknowledged as historical figures, though Islamic tradition places less emphasis on military heroism and more on the prophetic dimensions of Dawud's leadership — his justice, wisdom, and devotion to worship.

## Theological Significance

1. **Prophetic kingship**: Dawud unites temporal authority with prophetic wisdom
2. **Al-Quds**: Jerusalem's sacred status is affirmed in Islamic tradition
3. **Divine appointment**: Dawud's kingship is God's gift, not human achievement
4. **Wisdom over warfare**: Dawud's prophetic qualities surpass his military accomplishments
