## Overview

Islam does not regard the Books of Chronicles as preserved divine revelation, but their central figure — David (Dawud) — is recognized as a major prophet in the Quran and Hadith literature. Islamic interpretation engages with 1 Chronicles primarily through the lens of Quranic parallels, evaluating biblical narratives against the standard of prophetic infallibility (*ismah*) and strict monotheism (*tawhid*).

## Relationship to the Quran

Key 1 Chronicles themes that appear in Islamic tradition:

|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| David's kingship | Dawud as prophet-king | Al-Baqarah 2:251; Sad 38:17-26 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
The Chronicler's positive portrait of David — omitting the Bathsheba episode — aligns more closely with the Islamic understanding of prophetic dignity than the parallel account in 2 Samuel. Muslim scholars note this as evidence of the Chronicler's greater fidelity to prophetic truth.

### Tawhid (Divine Unity)
David's prayers and psalms are affirmed insofar as they express pure monotheism. The elaborate temple worship described in Chronicles is evaluated against Islamic principles of worship simplicity and divine transcendence.

### Sacred Architecture
David's preparations for the Temple connect to the Islamic understanding of sacred spaces. While Islam does not attribute the same significance to Solomon's Temple, the concept of preparing a house for divine worship resonates with the traditions surrounding the Kaaba and al-Masjid al-Aqsa.

## Theological Emphases

- **Dawud as model prophet-king**: Combining justice, worship, and governance under divine guidance
- **The Zabur**: David's psalms as divinely revealed scripture
- **Rejection of census narrative**: The census and plague (chapter 21) as described raises questions about prophetic dignity
- **Genealogical continuity**: The prophetic lineage from Adam through David affirms Islam's universal prophetic history
