## Generosity, Gratitude, and the Final Prayer

Islamic engagement with chapter 29 finds strong resonance in its themes of generous giving (*sadaqah*), gratitude (*shukr*), and the acknowledgment that everything belongs to God. David's great prayer articulates principles central to Islamic theology.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Voluntary Giving (29:1-9)

David's personal generosity and the people's willing response model *sadaqah* — voluntary charity that exceeds obligatory *zakat*. The Quran teaches: "Who is it that would loan Allah a goodly loan so He will multiply it for him?" (Al-Baqarah 2:245). Generous giving is "lending to God" — an investment with guaranteed divine return.

### Everything Belongs to God (29:11, 14)

"Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory... Everything comes from you, and we have given you only what comes from your hand." This declaration aligns perfectly with Islamic *tawhid*: God owns everything, and human giving is merely returning what was always His. The Quran affirms: "To Allah belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth" (Al-Baqarah 2:284).

### Shukr — Gratitude (29:10-19)

David's prayer of gratitude models the Islamic virtue of *shukr*. The Quran teaches: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase your favor" (Ibrahim 14:7). Gratitude is both a duty and a means of receiving further blessing.

### Dawud's Death (29:26-30)

Dawud's peaceful death "at a good old age" reflects the Islamic ideal of *husn al-khatimah* (a good ending) — dying in a state of faith, surrounded by the fruit of a righteous life.

## Theological Significance

1. **Sadaqah**: Voluntary generosity is a supreme act of faith in Islam
2. **Tawhid in ownership**: Everything belongs to God; human giving is returning His gifts
3. **Shukr**: Gratitude is both a duty and a path to increased divine favor
4. **Husn al-khatimah**: A good death crowns a life of prophetic faithfulness
