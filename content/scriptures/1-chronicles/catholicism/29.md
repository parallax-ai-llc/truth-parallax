## The Eucharistic Vision of Giving and Praise

Catholic interpretation reads chapter 29 as the climax of the Chronicler's theology of worship: everything belongs to God, and human giving is merely returning what has been received. David's great prayer anticipates the Eucharistic prayer's own logic of offering back to God what God has first given.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Sacrificial Leadership (29:1-5)

David gives from his personal wealth before asking others to give. Catholic leadership models this principle: the pastor, the bishop, the religious superior gives first and most generously, inspiring the community's response.

### The Great Prayer (29:10-19)

'Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory...' (29:11). This verse shapes the Catholic doxology and the Lord's Prayer's conclusion in some liturgical traditions. David's acknowledgment — 'Everything comes from you, and we have given you only what comes from your hand' (29:14) — articulates the Eucharistic principle: the bread and wine offered are first God's gifts, returned in thanksgiving (*eucharistia*).

### Solomon's Coronation and David's Death (29:21-30)

Solomon's second anointing — this time with full public ceremony — and David's peaceful death complete the Chronicler's portrait. David dies 'at a good old age, having enjoyed long life, wealth and honor' — the Chronicler's vision of a life blessed by faithful service to God.

## Theological Significance

1. **Eucharistic offering**: All giving is returning to God what is already His
2. **Doxology**: David's prayer shapes Christian praise across traditions
3. **Sacrificial leadership**: Leaders give first, inspiring the community's generosity
4. **A good death**: David's peaceful end models the fruit of a life lived in covenant faithfulness
