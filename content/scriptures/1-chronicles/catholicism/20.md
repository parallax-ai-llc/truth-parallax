## The Silence on Bathsheba and the Chronicler's Purpose

Catholic interpretation notes the Chronicler's significant omission of the Bathsheba-Uriah episode (2 Samuel 11-12), reading this not as denial but as theological focus. The Chronicler presents David's public, covenantal role rather than his personal failures, already well-known from Samuel.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Rabbah Campaign (20:1-3)

The brief account of Rabbah's capture — without the Bathsheba narrative — demonstrates the Chronicler's selective method. Catholic hermeneutics distinguishes between suppression and focus: the Chronicler does not deny David's sin but emphasizes his liturgical and covenantal significance for the post-exilic community.

### Philistine Giants (20:4-8)

Three encounters with Philistine warriors of extraordinary size demonstrate God's continued protection. Catholic theology sees in these victories a continuation of the David-and-Goliath pattern: God's power working through faithful warriors against seemingly impossible odds.

## Theological Significance

1. **Theological selectivity**: Sacred authors select material for theological purposes
2. **Complementary accounts**: Chronicles supplements, not replaces, Samuel-Kings
3. **Ongoing victory**: God's power continues through David's community of faith
4. **Canonical reading**: Catholic interpretation reads Scripture as a unified whole
