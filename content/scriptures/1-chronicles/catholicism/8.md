## Benjamin: The Royal Tribe Before David

Catholic interpretation reads the extended Benjaminite genealogy as a tribute to the tribe that produced Israel's first king and remained loyal to the Davidic dynasty. The chapter's culmination in Saul's house prepares for the theological transition in chapter 10.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Benjamin's Prominence (8:1-28)

The detailed Benjaminite genealogy reflects the tribe's significance: its territory included Jerusalem, and after the schism, Benjamin remained with Judah. Catholic interpreters note that faithfulness to legitimate authority — even when a different tribe holds the kingship — is itself a form of covenant loyalty.

### Saul's House at Gibeon (8:29-40)

The genealogy of Saul is recorded with dignity despite his failed kingship. Catholic theology distinguishes between the rejection of Saul's disobedient rule and the recognition of his office's legitimacy during its time. Even failed leaders are part of salvation history.

## Theological Significance

1. **Legitimate authority**: Benjamin's loyalty to the Davidic throne models fidelity to God's appointed leaders
2. **Dignity in failure**: Saul's genealogy is preserved even after his rejection
3. **Jerusalem connection**: Benjamin's association with the holy city underscores the tribe's sacred significance
4. **Transition**: The chapter prepares for the shift from genealogy to narrative
