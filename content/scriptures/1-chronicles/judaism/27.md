## Organized Governance under Torah Principles

Jewish interpretation reads David's administrative structure as evidence that good governance — military, tribal, and agricultural — operates under divine mandate. The chapter demonstrates that Torah principles apply to all dimensions of communal life.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Twelve Monthly Divisions (27:1-15)

**Radak** notes the practical wisdom of rotating military service, balancing national defense with civilian life. The system ensures readiness without permanent military burden — a principle the rabbis value as balancing communal obligation with individual freedom.

### The Incomplete Census (27:23-24)

David does not count those under twenty because "the LORD had promised to make Israel as numerous as the stars." **Rashi** connects this to the prohibition against counting Israel directly (Exodus 30:12). David's restraint reflects Torah learning — he has internalized the lesson of chapter 21.

### Royal Stewards (27:25-34)

Agricultural overseers — vineyards, olive groves, cattle — manage the kingdom's material resources. **Ralbag** observes that the Torah's concern for land management (sabbatical year, gleaning laws) extends to royal administration.

## Theological Significance

1. **Torah-governed administration**: All governance operates under divine law
2. **Census restraint**: David applies the Torah's prohibition against direct counting
3. **Agricultural stewardship**: Land management follows Torah principles
4. **Balanced obligation**: Military service must not overwhelm civilian life
