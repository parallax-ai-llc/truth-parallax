## The Psalm of Hodaah and the Establishment of Daily Worship

Jewish interpretation reads David's psalm as the foundation of the daily liturgical *hodaah* (thanksgiving) that structures Jewish worship. The composite psalm — drawn from Psalms 105, 96, and 106 — becomes a model for the Siddur's own praise sections.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Give Thanks to the LORD" (16:8)

**Rashi** connects this psalm to the daily *pesukei de-zimra* (verses of praise) in the morning service. The psalm's opening call — *hodu l'Adonai* — echoes throughout Jewish liturgy as the fundamental posture of worship.

### Covenant Memory (16:15-22)

"Remember his covenant forever" — the psalm rehearses God's promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. **Radak** notes that remembrance (*zekhirah*) is active, not passive — it involves recitation, study, and communal celebration of God's saving deeds.

### Continuous Worship Established (16:37-42)

David appoints Asaph and his associates for regular service before the Ark. **Ralbag** draws the lesson that worship must be sustained and organized, not sporadic or spontaneous alone. The Jewish requirement of daily *tefillah* (prayer) reflects this principle.

## Theological Significance

1. **Hodaah as foundation**: Thanksgiving is the primary posture of Jewish worship
2. **Active remembrance**: Covenant memory requires recitation and communal celebration
3. **Liturgical structure**: David's appointments establish the pattern for organized daily worship
4. **Psalmic worship**: The Siddur's praise sections draw on this Chronicler's psalm
