## Chesed Rejected and Its Consequences

Jewish interpretation reads the Ammonite war as a lesson about the consequences of rejecting *chesed* (kindness). David's ambassadors, sent in goodwill, are humiliated — an act that violates the fundamental social virtue of hospitality.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### David's Chesed (19:2)

**Rashi** identifies David's outreach as *gemilut chasadim* (acts of loving-kindness) — extending compassion even to a potential adversary. The rabbinic tradition values chesed as one of the three pillars on which the world stands (Avot 1:2).

### Foolish Counsel (19:3-5)

Hanun's advisors convince him to humiliate David's ambassadors. **Ralbag** draws the practical lesson: leaders who surround themselves with suspicious, cynical counselors inevitably make catastrophic decisions. The Torah values wise counsel (*etzah*) as essential to good governance.

### Joab's Trust (19:13)

"The LORD will do what is good in his sight." **Radak** reads Joab's declaration as an expression of *bitachon* (trust in God) — the willingness to act courageously while accepting whatever outcome God ordains.

## Theological Significance

1. **Chesed as foundation**: Loving-kindness is a primary social and religious virtue
2. **Consequences of folly**: Rejecting kindness leads to disaster
3. **Bitachon**: Trust in God combines active effort with acceptance of divine will
4. **Wise counsel**: The quality of one's advisors determines the quality of one's decisions
