## The Great Tefillah and the Theology of Nedavah

Jewish interpretation reads David's final prayer as one of the great *tefillot* (prayers) in Scripture — a model of acknowledging that everything belongs to God and that human giving is merely restoring what was first received. The chapter's emphasis on *nedavah* (voluntary offering) shapes Jewish theology of generosity.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Voluntary Giving (29:1-9)

"Who then is willing to consecrate themselves to the LORD today?" **Rashi** reads this as a call for *nedavah* — voluntary giving that exceeds obligation. The rabbinic tradition distinguishes between obligatory contributions (*machatzit ha-shekel*) and voluntary offerings that express the heart's overflow.

### The Great Prayer (29:10-19)

"Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory..." (29:11). This verse is incorporated into the Jewish liturgy in the *kedushah* and in the Torah service. **Radak** identifies it as one of the most comprehensive statements of divine sovereignty in Scripture.

### "Everything Comes from You" (29:14)

"For all things come from You, and of Your own have we given You." This verse articulates the Jewish theology of *hakol shelkha* (everything is Yours). The Mishnah (Avot 3:7) echoes: "Give Him of His own, for you and yours are His."

### David's Death (29:26-30)

David dies "in a good old age, full of days, riches, and honor." **Ralbag** notes that the Chronicler's final portrait of David is of a man whose life was crowned by generosity, worship, and the successful transmission of his divine calling to the next generation.

## Theological Significance

1. **Hakol shelkha**: Everything belongs to God; human giving is returning what is His
2. **Nedavah**: Voluntary generosity exceeding obligation is the highest form of giving
3. **Liturgical incorporation**: David's prayer enters the Siddur as permanent worship
4. **Blessed death**: A life of faithfulness concludes in peace, honor, and generational continuity
