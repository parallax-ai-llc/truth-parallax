## The Prayer of Jabez and the Value of Torah Study

Jewish interpretation finds in the prayer of Jabez a model of petition that the rabbis connect to Torah study. The Talmud (Temurah 16a) identifies Jabez as Othniel ben Kenaz, a great sage who restored forgotten Torah teachings — his 'enlarged territory' refers to the expansion of Torah knowledge.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Prayer of Jabez (4:9-10)

**Rashi** connects Jabez's prayer to Torah study: 'enlarge my territory' means 'multiply my students,' and 'let your hand be with me' means 'let me not forget my learning.' This interpretation transforms a personal petition into a prayer for the spread of Torah.

The rabbinic reading elevates Jabez from an obscure genealogical figure to one of Israel's great Torah scholars — demonstrating the midrashic principle that every name in Chronicles conceals deeper significance.

### Craftsmen and Workers (4:14, 21-23)

**Radak** identifies the 'potters who dwelt among plants and hedges' as scholars who produced legal rulings (*as potters shape clay*). Rabbinic interpretation frequently reads occupational language as metaphors for Torah scholarship.

### Simeon's Territory (4:24-43)

The Simeonite expansion into conquered territory demonstrates the ongoing nature of Israel's settlement. **Ralbag** draws the practical lesson that faithful communities must continue to grow and occupy their allotted spiritual territory.

## Theological Significance

1. **Torah as territory**: Jabez's prayer is reinterpreted as a plea for expanded Torah knowledge
2. **Midrashic reading**: Every name and occupation in Chronicles may conceal deeper meaning
3. **Scholarship as service**: Torah study is the highest form of divine service
4. **Ongoing faithfulness**: Spiritual territory must be continually claimed and cultivated
