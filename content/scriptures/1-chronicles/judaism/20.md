## The Chronicler's Selection and Rabbinic Interpretation

Jewish interpretation notes the Chronicler's omission of the Bathsheba-Uriah narrative (2 Samuel 11-12) and reads this selection as theological rather than evasive. The Chronicler's purpose is to present David's covenantal and liturgical significance for the post-exilic community.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Omission (20:1)

**Radak** addresses the omission directly: the Chronicler assumes the reader knows the Samuel narrative and focuses instead on David's public, covenantal role. The rabbinic tradition, however, does not avoid David's sin — the Talmud (Shabbat 56a) discusses it extensively, with some rabbis arguing that David's sin was less severe than commonly assumed.

### Philistine Giants (20:4-8)

The defeat of three Philistine warriors of extraordinary stature continues the tradition of David's victory over Goliath. **Rashi** notes that these victories demonstrate that David's courage inspired his warriors to similar feats.

## Theological Significance

1. **Authorial selection**: The Chronicler's choices reflect theological purposes, not historical ignorance
2. **Canonical reading**: Jewish interpretation reads Chronicles alongside Samuel, not as a replacement
3. **Inspired courage**: David's example empowers his community
4. **Textual analysis**: The omission invites scholarly investigation of the Chronicler's method
