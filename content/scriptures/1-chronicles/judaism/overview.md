## Overview

In Judaism, 1 Chronicles (*Divrei HaYamim Aleph* — "Words of the Days") belongs to the *Ketuvim* (Writings), the third and final section of the Tanakh. The Talmud attributes its authorship to Ezra (Bava Batra 15a). The book's extensive genealogies served a vital function for the post-exilic community, establishing tribal identity and priestly legitimacy after the Babylonian exile.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
- **Rashi**: Provides concise commentary connecting the genealogies to earlier biblical narratives
- **Radak** (Rabbi David Kimchi): Grammatical-historical analysis with attention to variant readings between Chronicles and Samuel-Kings
- **Ralbag** (Gersonides): Draws practical ethical lessons from David's reign

### Derash (Homiletical)
- **Midrash** explores the theological significance of the genealogies, finding moral lessons in names and lineages
- **Talmudic discussions** address the discrepancies between Chronicles and Samuel-Kings (Pesachim 62b discusses the genealogies' depth)
- The Chronicler's portrait of David emphasizes his role in establishing temple worship, a theme central to rabbinic Judaism

### Sod (Mystical)
- The **Zohar** finds mystical significance in the genealogical structures, seeing them as reflections of the *sefirot*
- David's preparations for the Temple correspond to the human soul's preparation for divine encounter

## Theological Emphases

- **Post-exilic identity**: The genealogies reestablish Israel's tribal structure after exile
- **Levitical legitimacy**: Detailed priestly genealogies validate the Second Temple priesthood
- **David as liturgist**: David's establishment of temple worship orders takes precedence over his military exploits
- **Retribution**: The Chronicler's theology of immediate divine justice shapes rabbinic ethical teaching

## Liturgical Usage

| Context | Usage |
|---------|-------|

| Pesachim 62b | "The Book of Chronicles was given only for midrashic interpretation" |
