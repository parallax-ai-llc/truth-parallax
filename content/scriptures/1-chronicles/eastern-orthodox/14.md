## Divine Confirmation and Discernment in Governance

Orthodox interpretation reads the triple confirmation of David's kingdom — diplomatic recognition, family growth, and military victory — as divine validation of faithful kingship. David's practice of inquiring of God before battle models the Orthodox emphasis on discernment (*diakrisis*).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Hiram's Embassy (14:1-2)

David recognizes that God has established him 'for the sake of his people.' Orthodox political theology teaches that legitimate authority exists to serve the community, not the ruler's personal interests. The Byzantine ideal of the ruler as God's servant (*basileus*) reflects this principle.

### Inquiring of God (14:10, 14)

David seeks divine guidance before each battle. Orthodox spiritual direction (*pneumatikos*) continues this practice of seeking God's will before major decisions. The pattern is: prayer, discernment, then action — never action without consultation.

### Victory and the Nations (14:17)

'The LORD made all the nations fear him.' God's blessing on His anointed extends to international recognition. Orthodox missiology sees in this pattern the Church's universal witness: faithfulness to God attracts the nations.

## Theological Significance

1. **Authority as service**: Legitimate power exists for the people's benefit
2. **Discernment**: Seeking God's will before acting is essential to faithful governance
3. **Divine attribution**: All success belongs to God, not to human strength
4. **Universal witness**: Faithfulness to God attracts the attention of all peoples
