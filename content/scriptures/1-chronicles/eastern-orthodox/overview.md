## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 1 Chronicles draws on the patristic tradition to read the Chronicler's history as a theological vision of worship, covenant, and divine-human communion. The book's emphasis on the Temple, the Levitical orders, and David's liturgical piety resonates with Orthodoxy's understanding of the Church as the continuation and fulfillment of Israel's worship.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic
The Church Fathers — particularly St. John Chrysostom, St. Theodoret of Cyrus, and St. Ephrem the Syrian — read the Chronicles as both historical record and spiritual instruction. The genealogies are not mere lists but testimonies to God's providential care across generations.

### Liturgical-Typological
Orthodox theology sees in David's organization of temple worship a prefiguration of the Church's own liturgical order. The singers, musicians, and priestly rotations described in chapters 23–26 anticipate the Divine Liturgy's structured beauty.

### Theotic
David's desire to build a house for God, and God's response that He will build a house for David (chapter 17), is read through the lens of *theosis*: God's dwelling among humanity anticipates the ultimate union of divine and human in Christ.

## Theological Emphases

- **Worship as theosis**: Liturgical worship is the primary means of human participation in the divine life
- **David as liturgical model**: David the psalmist-king embodies the integration of prayer, praise, and governance
- **Sacred continuity**: The genealogies express the unbroken chain of God's covenant community
- **Temple as icon**: The Temple prefigures the Church, which is itself an icon of the heavenly liturgy
