## The Davidic Line and the Economy of Salvation

Orthodox interpretation reads the Davidic genealogy as the historical thread of God's saving economy, stretching from David through the exile to the post-exilic period — and ultimately, through the Theotokos, to Christ Himself.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### David's Sons (3:1-9)

The record of David's sons in Hebron and Jerusalem reflects the complexity of ancient kingship. Orthodox moral theology does not idealize David but recognizes that God's purposes work through flawed human vessels — a principle central to the theology of divine condescension.

### Kings to the Exile (3:10-16)

The succession of Judahite kings traces the covenant's endurance through four centuries. **St. John Chrysostom** taught that God's faithfulness to the Davidic promise, despite the kings' unfaithfulness, reveals the unconditional dimension of divine love.

### Beyond the Exile (3:17-24)

The genealogy's continuation through Zerubbabel signals hope beyond catastrophe. Orthodox theology of the cross sees in exile and restoration the paschal pattern: death is never the final word, because God's life-giving power prevails.

## Theological Significance

1. **Paschal pattern**: The exile-return pattern prefigures death and resurrection
2. **Divine faithfulness**: God's promises endure despite human unfaithfulness
3. **Incarnational preparation**: The preserved Davidic line leads to Christ's human ancestry
4. **Hope beyond catastrophe**: Genealogy after exile testifies to resurrection hope
