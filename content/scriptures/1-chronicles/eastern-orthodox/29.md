## The Doxological Climax and the Offering of All Things to God

Orthodox interpretation reads chapter 29 as the summit of the Chronicler's theology — everything belongs to God, and true worship is the joyful return of His gifts. David's great prayer has shaped Orthodox doxology for millennia.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Sacrificial Giving (29:1-9)

David gives from his personal wealth, then the people give freely and joyfully. Orthodox stewardship is not mere obligation but the joyful offering of what God has first given. The prosphora — bread brought by the faithful for the Eucharist — embodies this principle.

### The Great Doxology (29:10-13)

'Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory...' This prayer shapes Christian doxology across all traditions. In Orthodoxy, the Great Doxology (*Megalynarion*) at Orthros echoes David's praise, attributing all glory to God.

### Everything Comes from God (29:14)

'Everything comes from you, and we have given you only what comes from your hand.' This verse articulates the Eucharistic principle at the heart of Orthodox worship: the bread and wine offered are first God's gifts of creation, returned in thanksgiving and transformed by the Spirit.

### David's Death (29:26-30)

David dies 'at a good old age, having enjoyed long life, wealth and honor.' Orthodox tradition honors the blessed death — a life concluded in peace, surrounded by the community of faith. The prayers for the departed continue the Church's care beyond death.

## Theological Significance

1. **Eucharistic offering**: All giving is returning God's gifts in thanksgiving
2. **Doxology as summit**: Praise is the highest activity of the human soul
3. **Joyful generosity**: Giving is characterized by freedom and delight, not obligation
4. **Blessed repose**: A faithful life concludes in peace, commended to God's mercy
