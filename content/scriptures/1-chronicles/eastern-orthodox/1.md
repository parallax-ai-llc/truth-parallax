## The Genealogy of the Cosmos

Orthodox interpretation reads the opening genealogy as a cosmic prologue — tracing God's providential care from Adam, the first-formed, through the nations to the chosen lineage. The Fathers saw in this sweep of generations the divine oikonomia (economy) at work, preparing humanity for the Incarnation.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Adam to Noah (1:1-4)

**St. Ephrem the Syrian** read the genealogies not as bare records but as witnesses to God's patience across generations. Each name represents a life sustained by divine providence, even in a fallen world. The genealogy's beginning with Adam echoes the Orthodox understanding that all humanity shares a common nature destined for deification.

### The Nations (1:5-23)

The spread of nations through Noah's sons reflects the Orthodox teaching on divine providence extending to all peoples. God's care is not limited to Israel but encompasses every nation, preparing the world for the universal proclamation of the Gospel.

### Abraham's Line (1:24-54)

The narrowing to Abraham's descendants demonstrates the particular within the universal — God's election of a specific people as the vehicle of salvation for all. Orthodox theology holds that particular election serves universal redemption.

## Theological Significance

1. **Divine oikonomia**: God's providential plan unfolds across all generations
2. **Universal scope**: All nations fall within God's care and purpose
3. **Preparation for Incarnation**: The genealogy leads toward Christ's coming in the flesh
4. **Common human nature**: From Adam onward, all humanity shares one nature destined for theosis
