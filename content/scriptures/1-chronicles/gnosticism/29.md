## The Cosmic Doxology and the Offering of All Things

Gnostic interpretation reads chapter 29 — David's great prayer and the people's generous giving — as the climax of the Demiurge's worship system. The prayer's magnificent language carries the text to a threshold where material worship may touch genuine transcendence.

## Key Themes in Gnostic Reading

### The Great Prayer: Material or Transcendent? (29:10-19)

"Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendor, for everything in heaven and earth is yours." This prayer pushes against the boundaries of material worship. Its cosmic scope — claiming all heaven and earth for God — may inadvertently acknowledge a deity greater than the Demiurge, whose domain is limited to the material world.

### "Everything Comes from You" (29:14)

"We have given you only what comes from your hand." This statement contains a hidden truth that exceeds its intended meaning: if everything comes from God, then the divine spark within each person also comes from the divine — a teaching the Demiurge would not willingly make. The Gnostic reads here an inadvertent confession of the Pleroma's primacy.

### Material Generosity (29:1-9)

The overwhelming material generosity — gold, silver, bronze, precious stones — represents the Demiurge's system at its most seductive. The joy of giving, the communal celebration, the sense of purpose — all are real experiences that bind the soul to the material world through pleasure rather than pain.

### David's Death (29:26-30)

David dies "at a good old age, full of days, riches and honor." The Demiurge's highest reward: a long, prosperous life within the material world. The pneumatic asks: is this truly the best possible ending, or does liberation from the body offer something greater?

## Theological Significance

1. **Threshold prayer**: David's prayer pushes material worship toward genuine transcendence
2. **Inadvertent truth**: "Everything comes from You" confesses the Pleroma's primacy
3. **Seductive generosity**: Material joy binds through pleasure, not merely through fear
4. **Death as liberation?**: The pneumatic questions whether a "good death" within matter is truly good
