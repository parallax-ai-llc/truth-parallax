## Jephthah: The Demiurge's Demand for Sacrifice

Gnostic interpretation of Judges 11 reads Jephthah's vow and its consequences as the most disturbing illustration of the Demiurge's nature — a deity who demands the sacrifice of innocent life to satisfy his honor.

## The Vow as Revelation (11:29-31)

Jephthah's vow reveals the true character of the Demiurge's religion: a system in which the deity requires blood sacrifice and the devotee must offer what is most precious. The *Testimony of Truth* explicitly criticizes the sacrificial system as evidence of the Demiurge's bloodthirsty nature.

## The Daughter as Sophia Figure (11:34-40)

Jephthah's daughter, the innocent victim of her father's vow to the Demiurge, can be read as a Sophia figure — the divine feminine suffering within the material system created by the ignorant male deity:

- She is innocent of any wrongdoing
- She suffers because of the Demiurge's demands
- Her mourning for her virginity represents the loss of spiritual potential within the material world
- The annual commemoration by women preserves a feminine counter-tradition within the patriarchal narrative

## The Contrast with True Gnosis

Genuine gnosis liberates; the Demiurge's religion demands sacrifice. The true God of the Pleroma does not require blood offerings but reveals knowledge that sets the soul free. Jephthah's tragic story illustrates why the Gnostics rejected the Old Testament deity as a false god.

## Theological Themes

- **The Demiurge's bloodthirst**: The demand for sacrifice reveals the deity's character
- **The suffering Sophia**: The innocent feminine suffering under patriarchal archonic rule
- **Liberation versus sacrifice**: True gnosis frees; the Demiurge's religion kills
- **The rejection of the Old Testament God**: Jephthah's story as evidence against the Demiurge
