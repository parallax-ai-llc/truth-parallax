## Samson's Riddle: Hidden Gnosis

Gnostic interpretation of Judges 14 reads Samson's riddle as an example of esoteric knowledge communicated in encrypted form — a practice central to Gnostic pedagogy.

## The Riddle as Gnosis (14:14)

"Out of the eater came something to eat; out of the strong came something sweet." This riddle encapsulates a paradox central to Gnostic soteriology: from death comes life, from the material world comes spiritual nourishment, from the archonic lion comes the honey of gnosis.

The riddle's unsolvability without insider knowledge mirrors the Gnostic emphasis on esoteric teaching — knowledge available only to the initiated, hidden from the archonic powers and their servants.

## The Lion as Archon (14:5-9)

Samson's defeat of the lion represents the pneumatic's victory over archonic power:

- The lion attacks the pneumatic who enters its territory (the material world)
- The Spirit's power enables the pneumatic to overcome the archonic beast
- From the dead archon's body comes sweetness — the defeat of material power releases spiritual nourishment

## The Marriage as Entanglement (14:1-4)

Samson's desire for a Philistine woman represents the pneumatic's tragic attraction to the material world. The Gnostic myth of Sophia's fall begins with desire directed outward from the Pleroma — a longing that, while ultimately serving divine purposes, causes immense suffering.

## Theological Themes

- **Esoteric knowledge**: Truth communicated in coded forms accessible only to the initiated
- **The paradox of liberation**: Sweetness comes from the defeated archon
- **Material entanglement**: The pneumatic's attraction to the material world
- **Hidden wisdom**: The deepest truths are concealed within apparently simple narratives
