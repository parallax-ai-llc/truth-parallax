## Gideon's Call: Monotheism Against Idolatry

Islamic interpretation of Judges 6 focuses on the fundamental conflict between *tawhid* (monotheism) and *shirk* (polytheism/idolatry) that drives the narrative, reading Gideon's destruction of the Baal altar as the central act of the chapter.

## The Midianite Oppression (6:1-6)

The oppression is understood within the Qur'anic framework: "Whatever strikes you of disaster — it is for what your hands have earned" (42:30). The Midianite invasion is a consequence of Israel's abandonment of *tawhid*, illustrating the universal principle that turning from God brings suffering.

## The Call to Monotheism (6:25-32)

Gideon's destruction of the Baal altar is the episode most resonant with Islamic theology. It directly parallels the Qur'anic account of Ibrahim (Abraham) destroying the idols of his people (21:57-58):

- Both acts represent the rejection of false worship in favor of the One God
- Both provoke community outrage
- Both demonstrate the logical absurdity of idolatry — if a god cannot defend itself, it is no god

Joash's defense — "If he is a god, let him contend for himself" — echoes Ibrahim's challenge to the idolaters of his community.

## The Fleece and Divine Signs

Islamic theology recognizes that God sometimes provides signs (*ayat*) to strengthen the faith of His servants. The Qur'an records numerous instances where prophets requested or received signs: Moses's staff, Ibrahim's vision of the birds (2:260). Gideon's fleece can be understood within this framework, though Islamic scholars note that excessive seeking of signs may indicate weakness of faith.

## Theological Themes

- **Tawhid vs. shirk**: The fundamental struggle of human history
- **The Ibrahim parallel**: Destroying idols as an act of prophetic witness
- **Divine signs**: God accommodates human weakness through tangible signs
- **The consequences of idolatry**: Suffering follows the abandonment of monotheism
