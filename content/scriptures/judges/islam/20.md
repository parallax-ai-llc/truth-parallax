## War Against Benjamin: Justice and Its Limits

Islamic interpretation of Judges 20 examines the civil war through the lens of Islamic jurisprudence on justice, collective punishment, and the proper exercise of communal authority.

## The Assembly and Collective Decision (20:1-11)

The gathering of all Israel represents *shura* (consultation) — a principle the Qur'an commands: "And consult them in the matter" (3:159). However, *shura* requires not merely gathering but genuine deliberation guided by justice, wisdom, and mercy.

## The Three Battles (20:18-48)

### The Initial Defeats
Islamic scholars would note that seeking God's permission to fight does not guarantee easy victory. The Qur'an records that even the Prophet Muhammad suffered the setback at Uhud before the eventual triumph. Trials and defeats serve to purify intention and deepen reliance on God.

### The Victory Through Genuine Worship
Victory comes on the third day, after fasting and offerings. This pattern aligns with the Islamic teaching that divine aid (*nasr*) requires genuine spiritual preparation — not merely military readiness but sincere *tawbah* and *du'a*.

## The Problem of Disproportionate Punishment

The near-destruction of Benjamin raises serious concerns from the perspective of Islamic jurisprudence:

- Collective punishment for the crimes of individuals violates the Qur'anic principle: "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (6:164)
- The destruction of women and children who did not participate in the crime is categorically prohibited in Islamic law
- Even in war, proportionality and discrimination between combatants and non-combatants must be maintained

## Theological Themes

- **Shura**: Communal consultation as a divine command
- **The purification of intention**: Victory requires spiritual, not merely military, preparation
- **Proportionate justice**: Punishment must match the crime
- **Individual responsibility**: Collective punishment violates divine justice
