## The Danite Migration: Institutional Idolatry

Islamic interpretation of Judges 18 examines how personal *shirk* becomes institutionalized when left uncorrected, drawing parallels to the Qur'anic accounts of nations that established systems of false worship.

## The False Oracle (18:1-6)

The Danite spies' consultation of a false priest mirrors the Qur'anic condemnation of those who seek guidance from sources other than God: "Say, 'Shall we invoke besides Allah that which neither benefits us nor harms us?'" (6:71).

## The Attack on Laish (18:27-29)

Islamic just war doctrine requires legitimate authority, just cause, right intention, and proportionality. The Danite attack on peaceful, defenseless Laish violates every criterion. The Qur'an prohibits aggression: "Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress. Indeed, Allah does not like transgressors" (2:190).

## The Stolen Gods (18:14-26)

The absurdity of gods that can be stolen is a recurring theme in Qur'anic polemic against idolatry. Surah al-Hajj (22:73) states: "Those you invoke besides Allah could not create a fly, even if they gathered together for that purpose." If an idol cannot resist theft, how can it protect its worshippers?

## The Permanent Shrine (18:30-31)

The establishment of a permanent idolatrous shrine illustrates the Qur'anic pattern: individuals introduce *shirk*, which then becomes institutionalized and persists for generations. The Qur'an warns that such innovations (*bid'ah*) are among the most dangerous developments in religious life.

## Theological Themes

- **The institutionalization of shirk**: Personal idolatry becomes communal when unchecked
- **The futility of false gods**: Idols that can be stolen offer no real protection
- **Just war criteria**: Military action without divine authorization is aggression
- **The danger of bid'ah**: Religious innovations that depart from divine guidance
