## Samson's Riddle: Providence and Human Weakness

Islamic interpretation of Judges 14 examines the tension between divine providence (*qadar*) and human moral failure through the lens of Qur'anic theology.

## The Marriage Question

Samson's desire for a foreign wife, while contrary to the Torah's prohibition, is described as being "from the LORD" (14:4). Islamic theology addresses this tension through the concept of *qadar*:

- God's decree encompasses all events, including human sins
- Human beings remain morally responsible for their choices
- God can bring good from evil without being the author of evil

The Qur'an states: "Perhaps you dislike a thing and it is good for you; and perhaps you love a thing and it is bad for you. And Allah knows, while you know not" (2:216).

## The Lion and the Riddle

The symbolism of sweetness coming from strength, life from death, resonates with the Qur'anic observation that God brings the living from the dead and the dead from the living (3:27, 6:95). The natural order itself testifies to divine power and wisdom.

## The Problem of Tahrif

Islamic scholars note that the moral failures attributed to Samson — intermarriage, violence motivated by personal anger — may represent *tahrif* (textual corruption). A figure empowered by God's spirit, in the Islamic understanding, would demonstrate higher moral standards than the biblical text attributes to Samson.

## Theological Themes

- **Qadar**: Divine decree and human responsibility coexist
- **Hidden wisdom**: God's purposes are often concealed within seemingly negative events
- **The question of tahrif**: Moral ambiguity in the text may reflect corruption
- **Divine sovereignty**: God brings good from evil according to His wisdom
