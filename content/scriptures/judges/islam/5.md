## The Song of Deborah: Praise and Gratitude to God

Islamic engagement with Judges 5 reads the Song of Deborah through the lens of the Qur'anic emphasis on divine praise (*hamd*) and gratitude (*shukr*) as the proper response to divine deliverance.

## The Theology of Praise

The Song of Deborah, as a hymn of victory and praise, resonates with the Islamic practice of praising God after divine assistance:

- The Qur'an opens with "Al-hamdu lillah" (All praise belongs to God)
- After the battle of Badr, the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) praised God for the victory
- The pattern of military victory followed by divine praise is well established in Islamic tradition

## The Divine Warrior (5:4-5)

The cosmic imagery of God's march from Seir — trembling mountains and pouring heavens — connects to the Qur'anic descriptions of divine power:

- "Do you not see that to Allah prostrates whoever is in the heavens and whoever is on the earth?" (22:18)
- Nature itself submits to God's will and participates in His purposes
- The Islamic concept of *khalifah* (stewardship) encompasses the recognition that all creation serves God

## Communal Responsibility (5:12-23)

The song's praise of responsive tribes and condemnation of those who stayed behind reflects the Islamic concept of *fard kifayah* (communal obligation) — certain duties fall upon the entire community, and failure to participate when able is a sin.

The curse on Meroz (5:23) for failing to assist resonates with the hadith tradition warning against standing by while injustice occurs.

## The Mother of Sisera (5:28-30)

Islamic interpreters note the pathos of this scene without celebrating the enemy's suffering. The Qur'anic ethic emphasizes that while God grants victory, Muslims should not rejoice excessively in others' suffering. The Prophet Muhammad reportedly wept when he saw the dead of the Quraysh after Badr.

## Theological Themes

- **Hamd and shukr**: Praise and gratitude as the proper response to divine aid
- **Communal obligation**: All believers must contribute to the common cause
- **Divine sovereignty over creation**: Nature itself serves God's purposes
- **Compassion even in victory**: Islamic ethics caution against excessive celebration of enemy suffering
