## Overview

Islamic engagement with the Book of Judges comes primarily through Qur'anic references and the *Qisas al-Anbiya* (Stories of the Prophets) tradition. While the text of Judges itself is not part of the Islamic canon, Muslims recognize the period of the Israelite judges as part of sacred history, and several figures and events find parallels in Islamic sources.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Qur'anic Correspondence
Islamic scholars identify parallels between Judges narratives and Qur'anic accounts, while noting that the Qur'anic versions are considered the authoritative record. The concept of God raising up leaders (judges) for the Children of Israel resonates with the Qur'anic theme of divine guidance.

### Prophetic History
The judges period is understood within the broader framework of prophetic history (*tarikh al-anbiya*), where God continuously sends leaders and prophets to guide communities that have strayed.

### Theological Critique
Islamic scholars note the moral failures recorded in Judges as evidence of *tahrif* (textual corruption), arguing that true prophets and divinely guided leaders would not have committed such acts.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tawhid (Monotheism)**: Israel's repeated idol worship illustrates the fundamental sin of shirk (polytheism/association)
- **Divine Testing**: The cycles of oppression are understood as divine trials (*fitna*) to purify the community
- **Prophetic Guidance**: The need for continuous divine guidance through prophets and righteous leaders
- **Moral Accountability**: Communities bear collective responsibility for their faithfulness to God's law
