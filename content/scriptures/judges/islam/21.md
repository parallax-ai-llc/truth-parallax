## Wives for Benjamin: The Exhaustion of Human Schemes

Islamic interpretation of Judges 21 reads the book's concluding chapter as the final demonstration of what happens when a community attempts to solve problems through human scheming rather than genuine submission to God's will.

## The Problem of Oaths in Islam

Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) addresses the binding nature of oaths (*yamin*) and provides clear guidance:

- An oath that leads to greater harm than benefit should be broken, with expiation (*kaffarah*) offered
- The Prophet Muhammad taught: "If anyone takes an oath and then finds something better, let him do what is better and offer expiation for his oath" (Muslim)
- Israel's refusal to break their rash oath and instead devise violent workarounds contradicts this principle

## The Jabesh-Gilead Massacre (21:8-14)

The destruction of an entire community to provide wives represents a catastrophic failure of moral reasoning. Islamic law prohibits:

- Collective punishment for individual or communal failures
- The forced seizure of women for marriage (*nikah* requires consent in Islamic law)
- The use of violence to solve problems created by rash commitments

## The Shiloh Abduction (21:15-24)

Islamic marriage law requires the free consent of both parties. The scheme to seize women at a festival violates this fundamental requirement. The Prophet Muhammad explicitly taught: "A previously-married woman may not be given in marriage without her permission, and a virgin may not be given in marriage without her consent" (Bukhari and Muslim).

## "Everyone Did What Was Right in His Own Eyes" (21:25)

Islamic interpretation reads this final verse as confirming the necessity of divine guidance (*hidayah*) and revealed law (*shari'ah*) for human flourishing. Without submission to God's will, human societies inevitably descend into moral chaos.

The Qur'anic alternative is clear: "Indeed, this Qur'an guides to that which is most suitable" (17:9). Divine guidance, properly followed, prevents the confusion and violence that characterize the judges period.

## Theological Themes

- **The ethics of oaths**: Rash commitments should be broken with expiation, not circumvented through violence
- **Consent in marriage**: Islamic law requires free consent of both parties
- **The necessity of shari'ah**: Without divine law, human morality collapses
- **Hidayah**: Divine guidance as the only reliable foundation for social order
