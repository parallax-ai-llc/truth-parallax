## Samson and the Philistines: Strength and Its Limits

Islamic interpretation of Judges 15 examines Samson's exploits through the principles of divine empowerment and the moral obligations of those who receive God's gifts.

## The Cycle of Retaliation

The escalating violence between Samson and the Philistines illustrates the Qur'anic teaching on proportionality in conflict: "And if you punish, punish with an equivalent of that with which you were harmed. But if you are patient — it is better for those who are patient" (16:126).

Islam teaches that while retaliation is permitted, patience and forgiveness are superior responses. Samson's inability to break the cycle of vengeance represents a failure of the higher path.

## The Prayer for Water (15:18-19)

Samson's desperate prayer resonates with the Islamic teaching on *du'a* (supplication). The Qur'an promises: "Call upon Me; I will respond to you" (40:60). God's provision of water demonstrates that even imperfect prayer, when offered from genuine need, reaches the Divine.

## The Limits of Physical Strength

Islamic theology teaches that physical strength (*quwwah*) is a gift from God that must be used in His service. The Qur'an instructs: "And prepare against them whatever you are able of power" (8:60), but this power must be directed by faith and moral purpose, not personal anger.

## Theological Themes

- **Proportionality**: Justice requires measured, not excessive, responses
- **The superiority of patience**: *Sabr* (patience) is better than retaliation
- **Du'a**: God responds to genuine supplication
- **The right use of divine gifts**: Strength must serve God's purposes, not personal vengeance
