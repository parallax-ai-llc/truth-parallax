## Samson's Birth: Annunciation and Consecration

Catholic interpretation of Judges 13 reads the Samson birth narrative within the biblical tradition of annunciation stories, finding in it rich typological connections to the Annunciation to Mary and the birth of Christ.

## The Annunciation Pattern

Catholic exegetes note that the appearance of the angel to Manoah's wife follows the same pattern as Gabriel's appearance to Mary in Luke 1:

- A barren/virgin woman receives a divine messenger
- The messenger announces a miraculous conception
- The child will have a special divine mission
- The mother is given specific instructions

This pattern is not coincidental but represents a deliberate literary and theological tradition in which God's great saving acts are preceded by miraculous births announced by angels.

## The Nazirite Consecration (13:4-5)

The lifelong Nazirite vow imposed on Samson connects to the Catholic theology of consecration:

- **Baptismal parallels**: As Samson was set apart from before birth, so the baptized are consecrated to God from the moment of their baptism
- **Religious life**: The Nazirite restrictions (no wine, no unclean food, no cutting of hair) prefigure the evangelical counsels of poverty, chastity, and obedience
- **Total dedication**: Samson's entire life is to be lived in service to God's mission

## Manoah's Wife: A Figure of Faith

Catholic tradition has given special attention to the unnamed wife of Manoah, whose faith exceeds her husband's:

- She receives the divine message with composure and trust
- She correctly interprets the theological significance of events
- Her reassurance to Manoah — "If the LORD had meant to kill us..." (13:23) — demonstrates mature theological reasoning

Some Catholic commentators have seen in her a type of the Blessed Virgin Mary — the woman of faith who receives the divine word and mediates it to others.

## The Theophany (13:15-23)

The angel's ascent in the flame of the altar is a dramatic revelation of divine glory. Catholic sacramental theology sees in this scene an anticipation of the Eucharistic mystery:

- An offering is presented
- Fire from heaven consumes it
- The divine presence is revealed
- The participants are awestruck and fall prostrate

## The Name "Wonderful" (13:18)

The angel's refusal to give his name, calling it "wonderful" (*pele*), connects to Isaiah 9:6 — "His name shall be called Wonderful." Catholic Christological interpretation sees this as a further indication that the angel of the LORD bears the divine presence in a unique way.
