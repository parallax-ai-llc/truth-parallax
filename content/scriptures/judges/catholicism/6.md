## Gideon's Call: Divine Initiative and Human Response

Catholic interpretation of Judges 6 finds in Gideon's calling narrative a rich exploration of how God initiates salvation through grace and invites human cooperation. The chapter illustrates themes central to Catholic sacramental and spiritual theology.

## The Midianite Oppression as Divine Pedagogy (6:1-6)

Catholic tradition understands the oppression not as vindictive punishment but as divine pedagogy — God allowing suffering to bring His people to repentance. This connects to the Catholic understanding of purgative suffering, where trials serve to purify and redirect the soul toward God.

## The Prophetic Message (6:7-10)

Before sending a deliverer, God sends a prophet. Catholic interpreters note the pattern: the Word precedes the act. This mirrors the Catholic understanding of salvation history, where divine revelation prepares for divine action. The prophet's message recapitulates salvation history — a pattern reflected in the Eucharistic prayer's anamnesis.

## The Theophany and Vocation (6:11-24)

### The Angel of the LORD
Catholic tradition generally identifies this figure as a created angel, though some Fathers allowed for a Christophanic reading. The Catechism teaches that angels serve as God's messengers and play a significant role in salvation history (CCC 328-336).

### The Call Narrative
Gideon's call follows the classic biblical vocation pattern, which Catholic theology applies to religious and priestly vocations:

1. **Divine initiative**: God chooses and calls
2. **Human inadequacy**: "My clan is the weakest... I am the least" (6:15)
3. **Divine assurance**: "I will be with you" (6:16)
4. **Sign of confirmation**: The fire consuming the offering (6:21)

### The Altar Named "The LORD Is Peace" (6:24)
Gideon's construction of an altar points to the Catholic understanding of worship as the proper response to divine encounter. The name *Yahweh-Shalom* anticipates the peace that Christ brings (John 14:27).

## The Destruction of the Baal Altar (6:25-32)

Catholic interpreters read Gideon's iconoclastic act as a model for the rejection of idolatry:

- **First things first**: True reform begins with worship
- **Personal cost**: Gideon risks family and community standing
- **Joash's argument**: The true God needs no human defense — a principle Catholic apologists have employed

## The Fleece (6:36-40)

Catholic spiritual tradition has been more sympathetic to the fleece than some Protestant readings. The fleece has been interpreted:

- As a legitimate, if imperfect, act of faith seeking understanding (*fides quaerens intellectum*)
- Typologically: the fleece receiving dew while the ground remains dry has been read as a figure of the Incarnation (the Virgin receiving the divine Word)
- As an illustration of God's patient condescension to human weakness
