## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Judges draws upon the rich tradition of patristic and medieval exegesis alongside modern historical-critical scholarship. The book is read within the broader canonical context and the Church's teaching authority (Magisterium), with attention to both the literal and spiritual senses of Scripture.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Fourfold Sense of Scripture
Catholic tradition applies the classical fourfold method — literal, allegorical, moral, and anagogical — to the narratives of Judges, finding layers of meaning that point to Christ and the Church.

### Typological Reading
The judges are understood as types of Christ and the saints. Deborah, for instance, has been read as a figure of the Church or the Blessed Virgin Mary in some patristic commentaries.

### Historical-Critical
Since *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943), Catholic scholars have engaged robustly with historical and literary methods while maintaining the text's theological significance.

## Theological Emphases

- **Providence and Grace**: God's repeated deliverance despite Israel's sin illustrates the workings of grace
- **The Role of the Holy Spirit**: The Spirit's empowerment of the judges points to the charisms given to the Church
- **Moral Theology**: The complex moral situations in Judges provide material for reflection on conscience, virtue, and sin
- **Ecclesiology**: The period's chaos without central authority underscores the need for authoritative teaching
