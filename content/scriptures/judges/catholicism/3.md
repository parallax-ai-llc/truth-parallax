## The First Judges: Instruments of Divine Grace

Catholic interpretation of Judges 3 emphasizes how God works through diverse human instruments to accomplish His saving purposes. The three judges — Othniel, Ehud, and Shamgar — demonstrate that divine grace operates through varying temperaments and methods.

## Othniel: The Model of Charismatic Leadership (3:7-11)

Othniel's narrative follows the judges cycle in its purest form. Catholic commentators see in him a model of how the Spirit's empowerment works through human cooperation with grace:

- The Spirit of the LORD "came upon him" (3:10) — an infusion of grace that empowers but does not override human freedom
- Othniel "went out to war" — active cooperation with the grace received
- The result is forty years of peace — the fruit of faithful response to divine calling

### Theological Significance
Othniel illustrates the Catholic teaching on *gratia operans* and *gratia cooperans* — God's grace both initiates and sustains the work of salvation, but requires human participation.

## Ehud: The Complexity of Moral Action (3:12-30)

The Ehud narrative has generated significant discussion in Catholic moral theology. His deceptive assassination of Eglon raises questions about the morality of means versus ends:

- **Augustinian perspective**: Some patristic writers justified Ehud's action as divinely sanctioned tyrannicide
- **Thomistic analysis**: Aquinas would distinguish between the divine command (which can override normal moral prohibitions) and Ehud's personal initiative
- **Modern Catholic interpretation**: The narrative is descriptive rather than prescriptive — it records what happened without necessarily endorsing every action

### The Literary Art
Catholic scholars appreciate the narrative's dark humor and literary sophistication. The grotesque details of Eglon's death serve a theological purpose — demonstrating God's triumph over the forces of oppression through unexpected means.

## Shamgar: Grace Beyond Expected Boundaries (3:31)

Shamgar's brief notice raises interesting questions for Catholic interpretation:

- His non-Israelite name suggests God's grace extends beyond ethnic boundaries
- His unconventional weapon — an ox goad — recalls God's use of the foolish to shame the wise (1 Corinthians 1:27)
- His inclusion among the judges demonstrates that God's saving work is not limited to conventional channels

## Theological Themes

- **The universality of grace**: God works through varied human instruments
- **Cooperation with grace**: Each judge actively responds to divine empowerment
- **The complexity of moral action**: Not every action of God's instruments is morally exemplary
- **Divine providence**: God orchestrates history through both ordinary and extraordinary means
