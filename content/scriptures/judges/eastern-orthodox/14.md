## Samson's Riddle: Providence and the Passions

Orthodox interpretation of Judges 14 reads Samson's early exploits through the lens of the patristic teaching on the passions, divine providence, and the complex relationship between human freedom and God's saving purposes.

## The Marriage and Divine Economy (14:1-4)

Samson's desire for a Philistine wife is recognized as a manifestation of the passion of *epithumia* (disordered desire). Yet the narrator's comment that "it was from the LORD" (14:4) illustrates the Orthodox understanding of divine economy (*oikonomia*):

- God does not cause sin but providentially directs the consequences of human choices
- The Fathers taught that God's providence is like a skilled physician who uses even illness to bring about healing
- Samson's freedom is not overridden, but its consequences are woven into God's larger plan

## The Lion and the Honey (14:5-9)

Orthodox typological interpretation finds rich symbolism:

- **The lion**: A figure of death and the devil, conquered by the power of the Spirit
- **The honey from the carcass**: Life and sweetness emerging from death — a Paschal image
- **The secrecy**: Samson's concealment of his experience from his parents illustrates the spiritual principle that personal spiritual experiences should be shared with discernment

## The Riddle (14:10-18)

"Out of the eater came something to eat; out of the strong came something sweet." Orthodox Christological interpretation reads this riddle as a prophecy of the Paschal mystery:

- From death (the eater, the strong) comes life (food, sweetness)
- The Cross appears to be defeat but is actually the source of eternal nourishment
- The mystery is hidden from the worldly wise but revealed to those who receive divine illumination

## The Spirit and Human Passion (14:19-20)

The tension between the Spirit's empowerment and Samson's personal rage illustrates the Orthodox teaching that the gifts of the Spirit (*charismata*) do not automatically purify the recipient's character. The Fathers distinguished between:

- **Charismatic gifts**: Extraordinary abilities given for service
- **The fruit of the Spirit**: Character transformation through ascetical labor and divine grace

Samson has the former without the latter — a condition the Fathers considered spiritually dangerous.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine economy**: God weaves even human failings into His saving plan
- **Paschal typology**: Death becoming the source of life
- **The passions and the gifts**: Spiritual gifts do not guarantee spiritual maturity
- **The need for *nepsis* (watchfulness)**: Samson's lack of spiritual vigilance leads to compromise
