## The Angel's Rebuke and the Cycle of Repentance

Orthodox interpretation of Judges 2 finds in this chapter the theological key to the entire book, establishing a pattern of sin, suffering, repentance, and restoration that mirrors the Orthodox understanding of the spiritual life and the liturgical cycle.

## The Angel at Bokim (2:1-5)

### Christological Reading
Many Orthodox Fathers identify the angel of the LORD as a theophany — a manifestation of the pre-incarnate Logos. Theodoret of Cyrus understood this figure as the Second Person of the Trinity, who would later become incarnate. The rebuke at Bokim thus represents Christ's own address to His wayward people.

### The Tears of Repentance
The name Bokim ("weepers") resonates with the Orthodox theology of *penthos* — the gift of tears that accompanies genuine repentance. The Fathers taught that tears of compunction are a grace from God, softening the hardened heart and opening it to divine mercy. The people's weeping at Bokim represents the beginning of authentic spiritual renewal.

## The Generational Crisis (2:6-10)

The failure to transmit faith from one generation to the next is addressed in Orthodox tradition through the concept of *paradosis* (Holy Tradition). The Orthodox Church understands itself as the custodian of an unbroken tradition of faith, worship, and practice that extends from the apostles to the present.

The phrase "there arose another generation who did not know the LORD" (2:10) warns against any break in this living tradition — not merely intellectual knowledge but experiential, liturgical, and communal knowledge of God.

## The Cycle as Spiritual Pattern (2:11-23)

Orthodox spiritual theology recognizes the judges cycle as a pattern that recurs in the individual soul:

1. **Apostasy**: Turning from God to created things (the passions)
2. **Suffering**: The natural consequence of turning from the Source of life
3. **Repentance**: The cry of the soul to God from the depth of its distress
4. **Restoration**: God's merciful response through grace

This cycle is not mechanical but personal — God responds to genuine repentance with renewed grace, while allowing the consequences of sin to serve as pedagogy.

## The Testing Motif (2:20-23)

God's leaving the nations as a "test" connects to the Orthodox understanding of spiritual trials (*peirasmoi*). The Fathers distinguished between temptations from the devil and trials permitted by God for spiritual growth. The nations left in Canaan serve as the latter — occasions for Israel (and the Christian) to grow in faith and dependence on God.
