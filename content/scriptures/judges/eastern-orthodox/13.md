## Samson's Birth: Annunciation and Consecration

Orthodox interpretation of Judges 13 reads the Samson birth narrative as a theophanic event rich with typological connections to the Annunciation to the Theotokos and the Incarnation of Christ.

## The Annunciation Pattern

The angel's appearance to Manoah's wife follows the pattern of divine annunciation found throughout Scripture and celebrated in Orthodox liturgical life:

- A barren woman receives a divine messenger
- The child will be consecrated to God from birth
- Specific instructions are given regarding the child's upbringing
- The response of faith opens the way for God's saving action

Orthodox hymnography for the Feast of the Annunciation (March 25) draws on this pattern, connecting the Old Testament annunciations to Gabriel's appearance to the Virgin Mary.

## The Nazirite Consecration (13:4-5)

The lifelong Nazirite vow connects to the Orthodox theology of consecration at multiple levels:

- **Baptism and Chrismation**: As Samson was set apart from before birth, so the Orthodox Christian is consecrated through the sacraments of initiation
- **Monastic tonsure**: The Nazirite restrictions prefigure the monastic vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience
- **The consecrated life**: Every Christian is called to live as one set apart for God

## Manoah's Wife: The Icon of Faith

Orthodox interpreters give special attention to the unnamed wife of Manoah, whose faith exceeds her husband's:

- She receives the divine message with the calm receptivity (*hypakoe*) that the Fathers associate with the Theotokos
- Her theological reasoning (13:23) demonstrates spiritual maturity
- Her namelessness, paradoxically, makes her a more universal figure — representing all faithful women who receive and mediate God's word

## The Theophany (13:15-23)

### The Ascension in Flame
The angel's ascent in the fire of the altar is a dramatic theophany that prefigures both the Ascension of Christ and the descent of the Holy Spirit as tongues of fire at Pentecost. Orthodox iconography of the Ascension sometimes echoes this imagery.

### The Name "Wonderful"
The angel's refusal to reveal his name, calling it "wonderful" (*pele*), connects to the theology of the divine name in Orthodox tradition. God's name is beyond human comprehension — it can be invoked in prayer but never fully grasped by the human mind.

## Theological Significance

- **The divine initiative**: God's saving plan begins before the human agent is born
- **The Theotokos typology**: The barren woman made fruitful by divine grace prefigures the Virgin Birth
- **Consecration and vocation**: Every life has a divine purpose established by God
- **Theophanic encounter**: God reveals Himself through awe-inspiring manifestations
