## War Against Benjamin

Judges 20 describes the near-annihilation of the tribe of Benjamin in a devastating civil war. Protestant interpreters see this chapter as the culmination of the moral collapse depicted throughout the book.

## The Assembly at Mizpah (20:1-7)

"All the people of Israel... assembled as one man to the LORD at Mizpah" (20:1) suggests a rare moment of unity. Four hundred thousand armed men gather — demonstrating Israel's capacity for unified action, which they have consistently failed to achieve against their external enemies.

The Levite's account of events is notably self-serving, omitting his own role in sacrificing his concubine. Protestant commentators observe that even righteous outrage can be tainted by self-justification.

## The Decision for War (20:8-11)

Israel's unified determination to punish Gibeah reflects genuine moral outrage, yet the decision is made before seeking God's guidance. Benjamin's refusal to surrender the guilty parties transforms a judicial matter into tribal warfare.

## The Battles (20:18-48)

The three-day battle sequence is theologically complex:

### First Day: Israel's Defeat (20:18-21)
Despite seeking God's guidance, Israel suffers 22,000 casualties.

### Second Day: Another Defeat (20:22-25)
Israel loses another 18,000 men. The repeated defeats drive Israel to fasting, weeping, and offerings.

### Third Day: Victory (20:26-48)
After genuine repentance and worship, God grants victory through an ambush strategy echoing Joshua's conquest of Ai.

### Theological Interpretation
Protestant commentators offer explanations for the initial defeats:

- God was purifying Israel's own motives before granting victory
- The defeats exposed Israel's reliance on numerical superiority rather than divine guidance
- Israel needed to recognize that they, too, were sinful and in need of humility

## The Destruction (20:42-48)

The near-total destruction of Benjamin — only 600 men survive — is deeply troubling. Israel's punishment far exceeds the original crime. The language echoes the *herem* (ban) used against Canaanite cities. Israel treats a brother tribe as it should have treated its pagan enemies.

## Theological Significance

- **The horror of civil war**: Israel's greatest military success in Judges is against itself
- **The dangers of righteous anger**: Even justified outrage can lead to disproportionate violence
- **The need for divine guidance at every step**: Initial obedience does not guarantee continued wisdom
- **Corporate responsibility**: Communities bear responsibility for the evil they tolerate
