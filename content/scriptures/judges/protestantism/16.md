## Samson and Delilah

Judges 16 brings the Samson cycle to its dramatic conclusion. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter a profound meditation on the nature of sin, the patience of God, and the possibility of redemptive suffering.

## The Gaza Episode (16:1-3)

Samson's visit to a prostitute in Gaza continues his pattern of sexual compromise. His escape — carrying the city gates to a hilltop — demonstrates that physical strength remains while spiritual discernment has eroded.

## Delilah and the Betrayal (16:4-20)

The Delilah narrative is one of the most psychologically compelling stories in Scripture:

### The Pattern of Deception
Delilah asks the source of Samson's strength four times. Three times Samson lies; the fourth time he reveals the truth. Protestant interpreters analyze this pattern:

- **Samson's arrogance**: He treats the deadly game as entertainment
- **The persistence of temptation**: Repeated exposure to temptation erodes resistance
- **Self-deception**: Samson eventually "told her all his heart" despite clear evidence of her intentions

### "He did not know that the LORD had left him" (16:20)
This is among the most chilling verses in the Old Testament. Protestant theology draws profound lessons:

- **The gradual departure of grace**: Sin progressively dulls spiritual awareness
- **The danger of presumption**: Samson assumed God's power would always be available regardless of his behavior
- **The Nazirite violation complete**: With his hair cut, the final external sign of his consecration is gone

## Samson's Humiliation (16:21-22)

The Philistines gouge out Samson's eyes, bind him, and set him to grinding grain. Protestant commentators note the symbolism: Samson, who followed his eyes throughout, now cannot see. But his hair began to grow — God has not finished with Samson.

## Samson's Final Act (16:23-31)

### Samson's Final Prayer
"O Lord GOD, please remember me and please strengthen me only this once" (16:28). This prayer acknowledges dependence on God, yet remains mixed with personal vengeance.

### The Death
Samson kills more Philistines in death than in life. Protestant theology sees this as a type of Christ's victory through death, evidence that God's purposes are accomplished through broken instruments, and the completion of Samson's calling despite a lifetime of failure.

## Theological Significance

- **Sin's progressive nature**: Each compromise leads to greater compromise
- **God's patience and purpose**: God does not abandon Samson even when Samson abandons his calling
- **Redemptive suffering**: Humiliation becomes the context for Samson's greatest act of service
- **The need for a better deliverer**: Samson's story cries out for one who will perfectly combine power and holiness
