## Israel's Incomplete Conquest

Judges 1 opens with Israel's partial obedience in taking the Promised Land after Joshua's death. Protestant interpreters see this chapter as establishing the root cause of all subsequent failures: incomplete obedience to God's command.

## Key Themes

### The Cost of Compromise
The repeated refrain "did not drive out" (1:21, 27, 29, 30, 31, 33) illustrates a principle central to Protestant theology: partial obedience is disobedience. The Canaanites who remained became a perpetual snare, just as unconfronted sin continues to entangle believers.

### Judah's Relative Faithfulness
Judah is presented first and most favorably, which Reformed interpreters connect to the Messianic line — the tribe from which David and ultimately Christ would come. Even here, however, Judah fails to complete the task (1:19).

### The Downward Trajectory
The chapter moves geographically from south to north, and the narrative grows progressively worse. Protestant commentators note this literary structure as intentional, foreshadowing the moral decline that will characterize the entire book.

## Theological Significance

From a Protestant perspective, Judges 1 teaches several vital lessons:

- **Obedience must be complete**: Half-measures in following God's commands lead to spiritual compromise
- **God's promises require faith**: Judah could not drive out the iron chariots, suggesting a failure of faith rather than divine limitation
- **Sin left unchecked grows**: The Canaanites who remained would become the instruments of Israel's testing and downfall

## The Adoni-bezek Episode (1:5-7)

The capture of Adoni-bezek and his acknowledgment of divine justice illustrates the lex talionis principle. Protestant commentators see this as an example of God's providential justice operating even through pagan rulers.

## Application

Protestant preachers frequently apply this chapter to the believer's ongoing battle against sin, drawing parallels between Israel's half-hearted conquest and the Christian's temptation to tolerate areas of disobedience.
