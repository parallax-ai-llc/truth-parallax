## The Birth of Samson

Judges 13 introduces the Samson cycle with a birth narrative that deliberately echoes other biblical annunciation stories. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter significant Christological typology alongside themes of divine calling and parental faithfulness.

## The Annunciation Pattern (13:2-5)

The angel of the LORD appears to Manoah's wife (who remains unnamed) to announce that she will conceive despite being barren. Protestant scholars note the parallels with other biblical birth announcements:

| Element | Judges 13 | Genesis 18 | Luke 1 |
|---------|-----------|------------|--------|
| Barren woman | Manoah's wife | Sarah | Elizabeth |
| Divine messenger | Angel of LORD | Three visitors | Gabriel |
| Promise of son | Samson | Isaac | John |
| Special calling | Nazirite | Covenant heir | Prophet |

These parallels suggest that Samson's birth carries enormous theological weight — he is set apart for a divine mission from conception.

## The Nazirite Vow (13:4-5)

The Nazirite requirements — no wine or strong drink, no unclean food, no razor on his head — establish the framework for understanding Samson's entire career. Protestant interpreters note that the vow is imposed before birth, is lifelong unlike the typical temporary Nazirite vow, and each of Samson's later failures corresponds to a violation of these requirements.

## Manoah and His Wife (13:6-23)

The interaction between Manoah and his wife provides one of the book's most nuanced character studies:

- **The wife's faith**: She calmly reports the divine encounter and later reassures her terrified husband
- **Manoah's anxiety**: He seeks confirmation, asks detailed questions, and fears death after the theophany
- **The wife's theological insight**: "If the LORD had meant to kill us, he would not have accepted a burnt offering" (13:23) — sounder theology than her husband's panic

Protestant commentators frequently note that the unnamed woman demonstrates greater faith and theological perception than the named man.

## The Theophany (13:15-23)

The angel's ascent in the flame of the altar is a dramatic theophany. Protestant interpreters discuss whether this is a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ:

- The angel refuses to give his name, calling it "wonderful" (13:18) — a term associated with divine names (Isaiah 9:6)
- Manoah recognizes they have "seen God" (13:22)
- The angel accepts worship, which only God may rightfully receive

## Theological Significance

- **God initiates salvation**: Before Samson can fail, God has already set the plan in motion
- **The importance of consecration**: Samson's calling demands total dedication
- **Faith in unlikely places**: A barren woman and an anxious man become parents of a deliverer
- **Christological foreshadowing**: The miraculous birth of a deliverer points forward to Christ
