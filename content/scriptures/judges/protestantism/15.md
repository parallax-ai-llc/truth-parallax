## Samson and the Philistines

Judges 15 escalates the conflict between Samson and the Philistines through a series of increasingly violent retaliatory exchanges. Protestant interpreters see this chapter as simultaneously displaying God's power through Samson and exposing Samson's deeply flawed character.

## The Escalation of Violence (15:1-8)

The chapter opens with Samson attempting to visit his wife, only to discover her father has given her to another man. The resulting cycle of retaliation illustrates the destructive nature of vengeance:

1. Samson burns the Philistine grain fields using foxes with torches
2. The Philistines burn Samson's wife and father-in-law
3. Samson slaughters the Philistines in response

Protestant commentators note the tragic irony: the very fate the Philistines threatened Samson's wife with (14:15) befalls her anyway. Human attempts to manipulate outcomes through fear often produce the very consequences they seek to avoid.

## Judah's Betrayal (15:9-13)

Three thousand men of Judah come to bind Samson and deliver him to the Philistines. This episode is particularly striking:

- Israel has so internalized their subjugation that they police themselves on behalf of their oppressors
- Judah's words — "Do you not know that the Philistines are rulers over us?" — reveal a complete loss of covenant identity
- Samson submits to binding by his own people, trusting God for deliverance

Protestant interpreters draw parallels to Christ's submission to arrest by His own people, though they caution against pressing the typology too far given Samson's character.

## The Jawbone of a Donkey (15:14-17)

The Spirit of the LORD rushes upon Samson, and he kills a thousand Philistines with a donkey's jawbone:

- **God's unconventional weapons**: As with Shamgar's ox goad and Jael's tent peg, God uses humble instruments
- **Samson's boast**: "With the jawbone of a donkey, heaps upon heaps" (15:16) — a self-glorifying poem that contrasts with Deborah's God-centered song
- **The naming of Ramath-lehi**: Samson names the place after his own exploit, unlike earlier judges who built altars to the LORD

## The Prayer for Water (15:18-19)

Samson's desperate cry for water after his victory is the only genuine prayer attributed to him until his death. He acknowledges God as the source of deliverance, yet even here he refers to himself as God's "servant" while complaining. God graciously provides water, sustaining His flawed instrument.

## Theological Significance

- **God works through imperfect agents**: Samson's motives are consistently personal, yet God uses his actions
- **The danger of self-reliance**: Samson's boasting contrasts with the humility expected of God's servants
- **Grace amid failure**: God provides for Samson even when Samson fails to honor God properly
- **The cost of accommodation**: Judah's willingness to surrender Samson shows the depth of Israel's compromise
