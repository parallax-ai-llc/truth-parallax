## Gideon's Call

Judges 6 begins the Gideon cycle, the longest narrative in the book. Protestant interpreters find in this chapter rich material on the themes of divine calling, human weakness, and faithful obedience.

## The Midianite Oppression (6:1-6)

The Midianite oppression is described in devastating terms — Israel was reduced to hiding in caves while their crops were systematically destroyed. Protestant commentators note that the severity of the oppression corresponds to the severity of Israel's sin, illustrating the principle of divine discipline.

## The Prophetic Oracle (6:7-10)

Before raising up a deliverer, God sends an unnamed prophet to diagnose the problem. This sequence is significant for Protestant theology: repentance must precede deliverance. The prophet's message recounts God's saving acts and Israel's disobedience, following the covenant lawsuit pattern.

## The Call of Gideon (6:11-24)

The angel of the LORD's appearance to Gideon is one of the great calling narratives in Scripture:

- **"The LORD is with you, mighty warrior"** (6:12): The divine address names what Gideon will become, not what he currently is
- **Gideon's complaint** (6:13): His questioning of God's presence resonates with Protestant emphasis on honest dialogue with God
- **The commission** (6:14): "Go in this might of yours" — Protestant interpreters debate whether "this might" refers to Gideon's natural strength or the divine empowerment just received

### The Theophany Question
Many Protestant scholars identify the "angel of the LORD" as a pre-incarnate Christophany, noting that the text shifts between "the angel of the LORD" and "the LORD" (6:14, 16).

## Destruction of the Baal Altar (6:25-32)

Gideon's night-time destruction of his father's Baal altar demonstrates several Protestant principles:

- **Reformation begins at home**: The first act of reform targets personal and family idolatry
- **Courage amid fear**: Gideon acts at night because he fears his family, yet he still acts
- **Joash's defense**: Gideon's father argues that a real god should defend himself — an argument Protestant apologists have employed against idolatry

## The Fleece (6:36-40)

The fleece episode is frequently cited in popular Protestant devotion as a model for seeking God's will. However, many Protestant scholars caution against this application:

- The fleece represents Gideon's weakness of faith, not a model to emulate
- God graciously accommodates Gideon's doubt, but the narrative does not commend the request
- Seeking "signs" may reflect a lack of trust in God's already-revealed word
