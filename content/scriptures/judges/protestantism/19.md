## The Levite's Concubine

Judges 19 is among the most disturbing chapters in the Bible. Protestant interpreters approach it with pastoral sensitivity while recognizing its crucial role in demonstrating the moral abyss into which Israel has fallen.

## The Setting (19:1-10)

The chapter opens with another Levite — this one with a concubine from Bethlehem of Judah. The extended hospitality scene with the father-in-law establishes the norms of hospitality that will be so horrifically violated later in the chapter.

## The Refusal of Jebus (19:11-15)

The Levite refuses to stay in Jebus (pre-Israelite Jerusalem) because it is a "city of foreigners." The bitter irony is unmistakable: the Israelite town of Gibeah proves far more dangerous than any foreign city would have been.

Protestant commentators note this as the narrator's devastating commentary on Israel's moral condition — they have become worse than the Canaanites.

## The Horror at Gibeah (19:16-28)

The parallels with Genesis 19 (Sodom and Gomorrah) are unmistakable and intentional:

| Element | Genesis 19 | Judges 19 |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Travelers arrive at evening | Lot receives angels | Old man receives Levite |
| Mob demands the guests | "Bring them out" | "Bring out the man" |
| Host offers women instead | Lot offers daughters | Host offers daughter and concubine |
| Outcome | Angels intervene | No divine intervention |

The crucial difference: in Genesis 19, God intervenes. In Judges 19, there is no intervention. The concubine is brutalized throughout the night and found collapsed at dawn.

### Protestant Theological Reflection
- **This is not prescriptive**: The text describes what happened, not what should have happened
- **The narrator condemns through silence**: The absence of divine intervention is itself a judgment
- **The Levite's guilt**: His willingness to sacrifice his concubine reveals his moral bankruptcy

## The Dismemberment (19:29-30)

The Levite's cutting of the concubine's body into twelve pieces and sending them throughout Israel is designed to provoke outrage. The closing question — "Has such a thing ever happened?" — deliberately echoes the destruction of Sodom.

## Theological Significance

- **The depth of human depravity**: Without God's restraining grace, human evil knows no limit
- **The failure of religious leadership**: Both Levites in chapters 17-19 are morally bankrupt
- **The absence of God**: The narrative's most striking feature is divine silence
- **The need for righteous authority**: "There was no king in Israel" takes on urgent meaning
