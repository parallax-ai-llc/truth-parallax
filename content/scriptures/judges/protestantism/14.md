## Samson's Riddle

Judges 14 begins the account of Samson's adult exploits with his ill-fated marriage to a Philistine woman. Protestant interpreters find this chapter deeply troubling yet theologically significant, illustrating the tension between divine sovereignty and human sin.

## The Timnah Marriage (14:1-4)

Samson's demand to marry a Philistine woman directly contradicts the prohibition against intermarriage with Canaanite peoples (Deuteronomy 7:3-4). His parents' protest represents the voice of covenantal obedience.

Yet the narrator inserts a remarkable theological comment: "His father and mother did not know that it was from the LORD, for he was seeking an opportunity against the Philistines" (14:4).

### Divine Sovereignty and Human Responsibility
This verse is a crux for Protestant theology:

- **Reformed interpretation**: God sovereignly orchestrates events, even through Samson's sinful choices, to accomplish His purposes
- **Arminian interpretation**: God providentially uses Samson's free choices without being the cause of his sin
- **Both traditions agree**: Samson is morally responsible for his actions even as God works through them

## The Lion Episode (14:5-9)

Samson's bare-handed killing of a lion and his later discovery of honey in its carcass serve multiple functions:

- They demonstrate the Spirit's empowering (14:6)
- Samson's eating honey from the carcass violates Nazirite purity laws, beginning his pattern of compromise
- The episode provides the basis for the riddle that drives the plot

Protestant commentators note the irony: Samson can defeat a lion but cannot govern his own desires.

## The Riddle (14:10-18)

"Out of the eater came something to eat, out of the strong came something sweet" — the riddle is unsolvable without inside knowledge, making it less a test of wisdom than a trap. The Philistines' coercion of Samson's wife foreshadows the Delilah narrative.

## The Spirit's Empowering and Samson's Rage (14:19-20)

The Spirit of the LORD rushes upon Samson, and he kills thirty Philistines at Ashkelon to pay his wager. Protestant interpreters wrestle with the relationship between the Spirit's empowerment and Samson's angry motivation:

- The Spirit empowers Samson for God's purposes (creating conflict with the Philistines)
- Samson acts from personal rage, not faith
- God uses both the empowerment and the sinful motivation to advance His plan

## Theological Significance

- **The complexity of God's providence**: God works through morally compromised agents
- **The beginning of compromise**: Small violations of consecration lead to greater ones
- **The danger of unequal partnership**: Samson's choice sets the trajectory for his downfall
- **Divine patience**: God continues to use Samson despite his failures
