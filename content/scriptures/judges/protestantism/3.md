## The First Three Judges

Judges 3 introduces the first three deliverers — Othniel, Ehud, and Shamgar — each representing a different aspect of God's deliverance. Protestant interpreters see this chapter as the first full iteration of the judges cycle.

## Othniel: The Ideal Judge (3:7-11)

Othniel serves as the paradigmatic judge. His story follows the cycle perfectly and is presented without moral complications. Protestant commentators note that Othniel's narrative provides the template against which all subsequent judges should be measured — each one will deviate further from this ideal.

The Spirit of the LORD came upon him (3:10), a phrase that becomes central to understanding the judges' empowerment. Protestant theology connects this to the broader doctrine of the Spirit's work, while noting the temporary and task-specific nature of the Spirit's anointing in the Old Testament.

## Ehud: The Left-Handed Deliverer (3:12-30)

Ehud's story is one of the most vivid in the book. Protestant interpreters engage with several elements:

- **Divine irony**: God uses a left-handed Benjaminite (the name Benjamin means "son of the right hand") to deliver Israel
- **Moral questions**: The deceptive assassination of Eglon raises ethical questions that Protestant commentators address in various ways
- **Graphic realism**: The detailed account of Eglon's death demonstrates Scripture's unflinching honesty about the realities of a fallen world

### The Eglon Narrative

The grotesque details of Eglon's death serve a literary and theological purpose. His obesity symbolizes Moab's exploitation of Israel, and his death in the "upper room" (or privy) is laden with dark humor that underscores divine mockery of Israel's oppressors.

## Shamgar: The Brief Judge (3:31)

Shamgar's single-verse account raises questions for Protestant scholars: Was he an Israelite? His name may be Hurrian. His weapon — an ox goad — emphasizes that God uses unlikely instruments. Protestant interpreters see in Shamgar a demonstration that divine deliverance is not bound by conventional means.

## Theological Themes

- **God's sovereignty over nations**: He raises up both oppressors and deliverers
- **The diversity of God's instruments**: From the model judge to the assassin to the ox-goad warrior
- **Escalating complexity**: Each narrative grows more morally ambiguous
