## The Cycle of Sin and Deliverance

Jewish interpretation of Judges 2 identifies this chapter as the theological framework for understanding the entire judges period. The cycle of apostasy, oppression, repentance, and deliverance encapsulates a pattern that rabbinic thought applies broadly to Jewish historical experience.

## The Angel at Bokim (2:1-5)

### Midrashic Identification
The Midrash identifies the angel of the LORD as Phinehas, the zealous priest. This identification connects the rebuke at Bokim with the covenantal zealotry that characterized Phinehas's earlier intervention (Numbers 25). Others in the rabbinic tradition identify the figure with a prophetic messenger sent by God.

### The Tears of Israel
The name Bokim ("weepers") has generated extensive midrashic reflection on the nature of *teshuvah* (repentance). The sages taught that tears of genuine repentance have power to open the gates of heaven, even when the gates of prayer are closed (Berakhot 32b).

## The Generational Crisis (2:6-10)

The failure to transmit faith from one generation to the next is a central concern of Jewish education (*chinuch*). The Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-9) commands: "You shall teach them diligently to your children." The generation that "did not know the LORD" (2:10) represents the catastrophic failure of this commandment.

### Pedagogical Implications
The rabbinic tradition developed elaborate educational structures precisely to prevent the situation described in Judges 2:10:

- The obligation to study Torah daily
- The duty of parents to teach their children
- The establishment of communal schools (*beit midrash*)
- The chain of transmission (*mesorah*) from Sinai to the present

## The Judges Cycle (2:11-23)

The fourfold cycle maps onto the Jewish liturgical understanding of history:

1. **Sin** (*chet*): Israel turns to foreign gods
2. **Punishment** (*onesh*): God allows oppression as consequence
3. **Repentance** (*teshuvah*): Israel cries out to God
4. **Salvation** (*yeshu'ah*): God raises up a deliverer

This cycle is reflected in the High Holiday liturgy, where the *Selichot* prayers move through confession of sin, acknowledgment of consequences, heartfelt repentance, and trust in divine mercy.

## The Testing Motif (2:20-23)

The concept of divine testing (*nisayon*) is well-developed in Jewish theology. The nations left as a test serve a pedagogical function — they provide opportunities for Israel to exercise and strengthen their faithfulness. The *Mishnah* (Avot 5:3) lists ten trials of Abraham, establishing the principle that testing serves spiritual growth.
