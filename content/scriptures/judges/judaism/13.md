## Samson's Birth

Jewish interpretation of Judges 13 examines the annunciation to Manoah's wife through rabbinic commentary on the Nazirite vow, the angel's identity, and the theological significance of Samson's consecration.

## The Barren Woman (13:2-3)

### Midrashic Tradition
The Midrash places Manoah's wife (whom some traditions name Tzlelponit or Hazzelelponi, following 1 Chronicles 4:3) among the biblical matriarchs whose barrenness was resolved through divine intervention. The pattern — Sarah, Rebekah, Rachel, Hannah, and Manoah's wife — teaches that Israel's greatest leaders are born through God's direct action, not through natural processes alone.

## The Nazirite Laws (13:4-5)

### Halakhic Discussion
The Nazir tractate of the Mishnah and Talmud discusses the Nazirite vow extensively, using Samson as a key example. Samson's Nazirite status differs from the standard vow (Numbers 6) in several ways:

- It is imposed before birth, not voluntarily assumed
- It is lifelong, not temporary
- It includes no mention of the prohibition against corpse contamination

The Rambam (Hilkhot Nezirut 3:12) categorizes Samson as a "*nazir Shimshon*" — a distinct category of Nazirite whose restrictions differ from the standard vow.

## Manoah and His Wife (13:6-23)

### Rabbinic Observations
The Talmud (Berakhot 61a) praises Manoah's wife for her superior understanding:

- She correctly perceives that the visitor is an angel
- She reassures Manoah with sound theological reasoning (13:23)
- Her faith exceeds her husband's — a pattern the sages noted without embarrassment

The Midrash Tanchuma adds that God chose to reveal His message to the woman first because she was more spiritually receptive than her husband.

## The Angel's Name (13:18)

### "Wonderful" (*Pele*)
The angel's response — "Why do you ask my name? It is wonderful" — generated rabbinic discussion about the nature of angelic identity. The word *pele* (wonder) connects to the realm of the ineffable — that which transcends human comprehension. Jewish mystical tradition (*Kabbalah*) would later develop this concept of the hidden divine name.

## The Offering and the Flame (13:19-20)

Manoah's offering and the angel's ascent in the flame have halakhic implications for the rabbinic discussion of proper sacrifice:

- The offering was accepted despite being made outside the Temple
- This is understood as an extraordinary, divinely authorized exception
- The fire from heaven confirms divine acceptance

## Theological Themes

- **Divine initiative in birth**: Israel's deliverers come through God's direct intervention
- **The Nazirite ideal**: Total consecration to God's service
- **Women's spiritual insight**: The mother's faith and understanding exceed the father's
- **The ineffable divine**: God's ways transcend human comprehension
