## Samson and Delilah

Jewish interpretation of Judges 16 engages extensively with the themes of temptation, downfall, and the possibility of final redemption. Samson's story has generated some of the richest midrashic commentary in the entire book.

## The Gaza Prostitute (16:1-3)

### Talmudic Discussion
The Talmud (Sotah 9b) explicitly connects Samson's punishment to his sin: "Samson followed his eyes, therefore the Philistines gouged out his eyes." This illustrates the rabbinic principle of *middah keneged middah* (measure for measure) — divine justice that fits the punishment precisely to the crime.

## Delilah (16:4-20)

### The Name Delilah
Rabbinic etymology connects the name Delilah to *dallah* (weakened) or *dildlah* (diminished). The Talmud (Sotah 9b) says she "weakened his strength, weakened his heart, and weakened his deeds."

### The Four Attempts
The midrashic tradition analyzes the progressive erosion of Samson's resistance:

- Each lie Samson tells comes closer to the truth
- The progression from bowstrings to ropes to weaving to hair represents a gradual self-revelation
- The sages saw in this a warning about the power of persistent temptation to wear down even the strongest will

### "He did not know that the LORD had departed from him" (16:20)
This verse is among the most discussed in the rabbinic tradition on *teshuvah*. The Midrash uses it as a paradigm for spiritual insensibility — the state in which habitual sin has so deadened the soul that one no longer perceives the absence of God's presence.

## The Imprisonment (16:21-22)

### Measure for Measure
The Talmud's observation that "he followed his eyes, therefore they gouged out his eyes" extends the *middah keneged middah* principle. Additionally:

- He who broke boundaries (leaving the community) is now bound
- He who consumed what was forbidden (unclean honey) now grinds grain for others
- He who lived by his strength now serves in weakness

### "But his hair began to grow"
The rabbinic tradition reads this as a sign of potential *teshuvah*. The Midrash says that as Samson's hair grew, his repentance also grew — physical restoration symbolizing spiritual renewal.

## Samson's Death (16:23-31)

### The Final Prayer
The sages analyzed Samson's final prayer and found it simultaneously admirable and flawed:

- He acknowledges God as the source of strength (positive)
- He seeks personal vengeance (negative)
- God answers despite the mixed motives — evidence of divine compassion

### Samson in the World to Come
The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 10:2) does not list Samson among those who have no share in the World to Come, implying that his final repentance was accepted. This has significant theological implications for the doctrine of *teshuvah* — even at the last moment, genuine return to God is possible.

## Theological Themes

- **Middah keneged middah**: Divine justice precisely mirrors the sin
- **The power of persistent temptation**: Even the strongest can be worn down
- **The possibility of late repentance**: Teshuvah is accepted until the last breath
- **God's compassion**: Even flawed prayers are heard
