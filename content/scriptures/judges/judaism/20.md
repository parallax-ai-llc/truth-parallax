## War Against Benjamin

Jewish interpretation of Judges 20 examines the civil war against Benjamin through the lens of communal justice, the limits of collective punishment, and the complex dynamics of seeking God's will.

## The Assembly at Mizpah (20:1-11)

### National Unity
The gathering of "all Israel... as one man" at Mizpah represents what the sages call *achdut* (unity). The Talmud notes that when Israel is united, even in imperfect righteousness, God's presence dwells among them. However, unity of purpose does not guarantee wisdom of action.

### The Levite's Testimony
Rabbinic commentary notes the selective nature of the Levite's account — he omits his own role in pushing the concubine out to the mob. The sages derived the principle that testimony must be cross-examined, as witnesses may present self-serving versions of events.

## The Three Battles (20:18-48)

### Why the Initial Defeats?
The rabbinic tradition offers several explanations for Israel's initial losses despite divine permission:

- **The Midrash**: Israel was being punished for tolerating the idolatry at Dan (chapter 18) before addressing the violence at Gibeah
- **Rashi**: The initial question "Who shall go up first?" was about military strategy, not about whether to fight at all — Israel assumed the conclusion without genuine seeking
- **The Talmud** (Shabbat 35b): The progression from inquiry to weeping and fasting represents the deepening of Israel's repentance, which God required before granting victory

### The Third Day
The victory on the third day, following genuine worship and sacrifice, illustrates the rabbinic principle that God's help comes when accompanied by genuine *teshuvah* — not merely military preparation.

## The Destruction of Benjamin (20:42-48)

### Excessive Punishment
Rabbinic commentators express concern about the proportionality of Benjamin's destruction. While the crime was heinous, the near-elimination of an entire tribe exceeds the demands of justice:

- The Torah commands proportionate punishment (*middah keneged middah*)
- The destruction of women and children who did not participate in the crime raises moral questions
- The sages noted that righteous anger, unchecked, can become as destructive as the evil it seeks to punish

## Theological Themes

- **The complexity of communal justice**: Punishing evil without becoming evil is Israel's perpetual challenge
- **The necessity of repentance**: Divine aid requires genuine spiritual preparation
- **The danger of excessive punishment**: Justice must be proportionate
- **The value of every tribe**: The potential loss of Benjamin threatens the integrity of all Israel
