## Tola, Jair, and the Great Dialogue

Jewish interpretation of Judges 10 gives particular attention to the remarkable dialogue between God and Israel, finding in it one of the Hebrew Bible's most profound expressions of the dynamics of *teshuvah* (repentance).

## The Minor Judges (10:1-5)

### Rabbinic Principle
The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 25a) derives from the minor judges the principle that "Jephthah in his generation is like Samuel in his generation" — every generation's leaders, however seemingly inferior, deserve the same respect as the great leaders of the past. Tola and Jair, despite their brief and unremarkable notices, served Israel faithfully.

## The Sevenfold Idolatry (10:6)

Rabbinic commentary notes the unprecedented scope of Israel's apostasy — worship of the gods of seven nations. The number seven in Jewish symbolism represents completeness, suggesting that Israel's descent into idolatry is now total.

## The Dialogue of Teshuvah (10:10-16)

### Stage 1: Confession Without Change (10:10)
Israel confesses: "We have sinned." But the sages noted that initial confession without behavioral change is insufficient. As the Rambam codified (Hilkhot Teshuvah 2:2), true repentance requires verbal confession (*vidui*), genuine regret (*charatah*), and resolution to change (*kabbalat l'ha-atid*).

### Stage 2: God's Challenge (10:11-14)
God's response — "Go and cry to the gods you have chosen" — represents what the sages call the *middah* (attribute) of strict justice (*din*). This is not divine cruelty but divine truth: Israel must confront the emptiness of their idols.

### Stage 3: True Teshuvah (10:15-16)
Israel's response demonstrates the elements of authentic repentance:

- Verbal submission: "Do to us whatever seems good to you"
- Behavioral change: "They put away the foreign gods"
- Active service: "They served the LORD"

### Stage 4: Divine Compassion (10:16)
The phrase "He could no longer bear the misery of Israel" reveals what the sages call the *middah* of mercy (*rachamim*). God's compassion overcomes the strict demands of justice — a dynamic central to the High Holiday liturgy.

## The Thirteen Attributes

The movement from divine justice to divine mercy in this passage connects to the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy (Exodus 34:6-7), which Jewish liturgy invokes in times of crisis. God's ultimate response is merciful, even when justice demands otherwise.

## Theological Themes

- **The process of teshuvah**: Repentance as a structured, multi-stage process
- **The balance of justice and mercy**: *Din* and *rachamim* in dynamic tension
- **The adequacy of every generation's leaders**: Each era's leaders deserve respect
- **The power of genuine change**: Actions speak louder than words
