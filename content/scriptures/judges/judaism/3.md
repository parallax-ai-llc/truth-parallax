## Othniel, Ehud, and Shamgar

Jewish interpretation of Judges 3 examines the first three judges through the lens of rabbinic commentary, finding in their diverse stories lessons about divine providence, the nature of leadership, and the variety of ways God delivers Israel.

## Othniel ben Kenaz (3:7-11)

### The Talmudic View
The Talmud (Temurah 16a) identifies Othniel with Yabetz (1 Chronicles 4:9-10), connecting his military prowess with his excellence in Torah learning. The sages taught that Othniel restored forgotten Torah teachings through his intellectual brilliance, suggesting that true deliverance involves spiritual restoration alongside military victory.

### The Spirit of God
The descent of God's spirit upon Othniel (*va-tehi alav ruach Hashem*, 3:10) is understood in Jewish thought as a form of divine inspiration that empowers but does not override human agency. The Rambam (Maimonides) classified the judges' inspiration as a lower level of prophecy than that of Moses but genuine divine communication nonetheless.

## Ehud ben Gera (3:12-30)

### Left-Handedness in Jewish Tradition
Ehud's left-handedness (*iter yad yemino*, literally "restricted in his right hand") has generated midrashic discussion. The Midrash notes that the tribe of Benjamin ("son of the right hand") produced a left-handed deliverer — an example of divine irony that delights rabbinic interpreters.

### The Ethics of Deception
Rabbinic tradition generally justifies Ehud's deception as permissible in the context of war against an oppressor. The Talmud (Yevamot 65b) acknowledges that deception may be permitted for the sake of peace or to save lives, though the parameters of this permission are carefully debated.

### Eglon's Death
The graphic description of Eglon's death generated midrashic commentary. The Midrash Tanchuma observes that Eglon's name derives from *egel* (calf), connecting him to idolatry. His corpulent body symbolizes the excessive tribute exacted from Israel.

## Shamgar ben Anat (3:31)

### Identity Questions
Rabbinic commentators debated whether Shamgar was an Israelite. His name appears non-Semitic, and "ben Anat" may refer to the Canaanite goddess Anat. Some sages argued he was a righteous gentile whom God used to deliver Israel, while others maintained he was an Israelite with an unusual name.

### The Ox Goad
The Talmud (*Shabbat* 150b) discusses agricultural implements in various contexts. Shamgar's use of an ox goad demonstrates the principle that God's deliverance is not dependent on conventional military equipment — echoing David's later victory over Goliath with a shepherd's sling.

## Theological Themes

- **Torah and deliverance**: True liberation involves spiritual as well as physical freedom
- **Divine irony**: God delights in using unexpected means
- **The diversity of leadership**: Different situations require different types of leaders
- **Providence**: God orchestrates history through varied human instruments
