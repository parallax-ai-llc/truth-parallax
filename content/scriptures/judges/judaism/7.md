## Gideon's Three Hundred

Jewish interpretation of Judges 7 emphasizes the theological principle that Israel's victories come from God, not from military superiority — a theme central to Jewish understanding of sacred history and liturgical practice.

## The Reduction of Forces (7:1-8)

### Midrashic Interpretation
The Midrash explains the reduction from 32,000 to 300 as demonstrating the principle stated in the Torah: "lest Israel vaunt themselves against Me, saying, 'My own hand has saved me'" (7:2, echoing Deuteronomy 8:17). This principle is central to Jewish liturgical theology — the Haggadah insists that it was God, not any human agent, who brought Israel out of Egypt.

### The Water Test
Rabbinic interpreters offer several explanations for the water test:

- **Rashi**: Those who knelt were accustomed to bowing before idols; those who lapped had not formed this habit
- **Radak**: The distinction was essentially arbitrary, designed to reduce the army to God's chosen number
- **The Midrash**: Those who lapped showed greater alertness and military readiness

## The Dream (7:9-15)

### The Barley Loaf
The Talmud (Shabbat 93b) discusses the symbolism of the barley loaf. Barley is animal food and the offering of the *sotah* (suspected adulteress) — it represents lowliness. God's choice of this symbol for Gideon's army reinforces the theme that victory comes through divine power operating through humble instruments.

## The Battle (7:16-25)

### The Shofar
The trumpets (*shofarot*) used in the battle connect to the broader Jewish theology of the shofar:

- The shofar calls to repentance (Rosh Hashanah)
- The shofar proclaims divine sovereignty
- The shofar recalls the binding of Isaac (the ram's horn)
- At Jericho and here, the shofar accompanies divine victory

### The War Cry
"A sword for the LORD and for Gideon!" illustrates the Jewish concept of *shituf* — partnership between God and human agents. Victory is God's, but human instruments participate meaningfully in divine action.

## The Pursuit (7:23-25)

The call to the other tribes to join the pursuit after God granted the initial victory illustrates the rabbinic principle that one who begins a *mitzvah* should be helped to complete it.

## Theological Themes

- **Divine victory**: *Lo b'chayil v'lo b'koach ki im b'ruchi* — "Not by might, not by power, but by My Spirit" (Zechariah 4:6)
- **Humility in victory**: Israel must never attribute divine victories to human strength
- **The shofar theology**: The instrument of divine proclamation and human response
- **Partnership with God**: Human effort matters, but within the framework of divine initiative
