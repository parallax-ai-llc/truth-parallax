## The Levite's Concubine

Jewish interpretation of Judges 19 approaches this harrowing chapter with the gravity it demands, reading it as the most extreme illustration of the moral collapse that characterizes the period when "there was no king in Israel."

## The Sodom Parallel

### Rabbinic Reading
The sages explicitly connect Gibeah to Sodom (Genesis 19), drawing the devastating conclusion that Israel has become like the nations God previously destroyed. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 103b) lists the concubine's tragedy among the great sins that brought judgment upon Israel.

### Worse Than Sodom
The Midrash observes that Gibeah is worse than Sodom in one crucial respect: at Sodom, the victims were strangers; at Gibeah, the victims are fellow Israelites. The violation of the bonds of kinship makes the crime more heinous.

## The Host's Offer (19:22-24)

### Halakhic Horror
The old man's offer of his daughter and the concubine to the mob represents a catastrophic failure of both halakhah and human decency. The Talmud is clear: no person may be sacrificed for the safety of another. The principle *ein dochin nefesh mipnei nefesh* (one may not push aside one soul for another) applies absolutely.

## The Levite's Responsibility (19:25-28)

### Rabbinic Critique
Rabbinic tradition holds the Levite personally responsible for his concubine's fate. The Midrash criticizes his callous behavior — pushing her out to the mob and his cold "Get up, let us be going" — as evidence of the moral bankruptcy of the religious leadership.

## The Dismemberment (19:29-30)

### The Twelve Pieces
The cutting of the body into twelve pieces and sending them throughout Israel is compared to Saul's later action with oxen (1 Samuel 11:7). The Midrash notes the progression: Saul dismembers animals, but the Levite dismembers a human being — evidence of how far moral standards have declined.

### The Purpose
The act is designed to provoke communal outrage and response. The Talmud discusses the permissibility of shocking actions intended to mobilize the community for justice, while also noting the manipulative dimension of the Levite's behavior.

## Theological Themes

- **The sanctity of human life**: Every person, regardless of status, bears the *tzelem Elohim* (image of God)
- **Communal responsibility**: The entire nation bears responsibility for unchecked evil
- **The failure of leadership**: Religious leaders who fail to protect the vulnerable bear special guilt
- **The comparison with Sodom**: Israel must never become like the nations God has judged
