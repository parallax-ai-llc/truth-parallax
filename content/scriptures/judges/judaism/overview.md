## Overview

Jewish interpretation of Judges (Shoftim) is shaped by millennia of rabbinic commentary, from the Talmud and Midrash through medieval and modern exegesis. The book is part of the Nevi'im Rishonim (Former Prophets) and is read as both historical narrative and prophetic teaching about the consequences of abandoning the Torah.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Rabbinic (Midrashic)
The Talmud and Midrash offer extensive commentary on the judges, often filling narrative gaps, harmonizing apparent contradictions, and deriving halakhic and aggadic teachings from the text.

### Peshat (Plain Sense)
Medieval commentators like Rashi, Radak, and Ralbag emphasized the peshat — the contextual, grammatical meaning of the text — while also drawing on midrashic traditions.

### Modern Academic
Contemporary Jewish scholars engage with historical, literary, and archaeological approaches while remaining attentive to the text's place within Jewish tradition and identity.

## Theological Emphases

- **Covenant Faithfulness**: The cycle of sin and deliverance illustrates the dynamics of the covenant between God and Israel
- **The Land of Israel**: The theme of conquest and settlement is central to Jewish understanding of the divine promise
- **Leadership and Torah**: The judges represent a model of charismatic leadership rooted in divine calling
- **Teshuvah (Repentance)**: Israel's repeated return to God models the process of repentance
