## Samson's Riddle

Jewish interpretation of Judges 14 examines Samson's marriage, the lion episode, and the riddle through the lenses of halakhah, aggadah, and the concept of divine providence directing seemingly random events.

## The Intermarriage Question (14:1-4)

### Halakhic Analysis
The Torah prohibits intermarriage with the seven Canaanite nations (Deuteronomy 7:1-3). The rabbinic debate centers on whether the Philistines were included in this prohibition:

- The Talmud (Avodah Zarah 36b) discusses the scope of the intermarriage prohibition
- Some sages argued that Samson's wife first converted (*gerut*), making the marriage technically permissible
- Others accepted the narrator's explanation that "it was from the LORD" as a unique divine dispensation

### Divine Providence
The phrase "it was from the LORD" (14:4) illustrates the Jewish concept of *hashgachah pratit* (specific divine providence). God directs individual events — even those that appear sinful — toward redemptive purposes.

## The Lion and the Honey (14:5-9)

### Nazirite Implications
Samson's eating honey from the lion's carcass raises Nazirite purity concerns. While the standard Nazirite must avoid corpse contamination, Samson's unique status as a *nazir Shimshon* may not include this restriction. The Talmud (Nazir 4a) discusses this distinction.

### The Riddle's Wisdom
"Out of the eater came something to eat; out of the strong came something sweet" — Jewish wisdom tradition appreciates the riddle as an observation about the paradoxes of life. The Midrash finds in it the broader principle that God brings good from evil, sweetness from bitterness.

## The Wedding Feast (14:10-18)

### The Social Context
Rashi notes that the seven-day feast (*mishteh*) followed standard ancient Near Eastern wedding customs. The thirty Philistine companions served as both guests and guards, reflecting the political tensions underlying the marriage.

### The Coercion of the Wife
The Philistines' threat to Samson's wife illustrates the dangers of intermarriage from a rabbinic perspective — the foreign wife is caught between loyalty to her husband and her people, with tragic consequences.

## The Spirit and the Slaughter (14:19-20)

The rush of God's spirit and Samson's killing of thirty Philistines at Ashkelon raise questions about the relationship between divine empowerment and human violence. Jewish tradition acknowledges the complexity without fully resolving it — the text presents the facts and allows the reader to wrestle with the moral implications.

## Theological Themes

- **Hashgachah pratit**: Divine providence operates through human choices
- **The complexity of halakhah**: Legal questions arise from narrative contexts
- **Wisdom and riddles**: The mashal tradition values paradox and insight
- **The consequences of intermarriage**: A recurring theme in Jewish legal and narrative tradition
