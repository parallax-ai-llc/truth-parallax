## Gideon's Call

Jewish interpretation of Judges 6 examines Gideon's calling, his destruction of the Baal altar, and the fleece episode through the lens of rabbinic commentary, finding in these narratives lessons about faith, courage, and the nature of divine communication.

## The Midianite Oppression (6:1-6)

### Midrashic Context
The Midrash connects the Midianite oppression to Israel's earlier victory over Midian in the days of Moses (Numbers 31). The reversal — from victors to victims — illustrates the principle that military strength without spiritual fidelity is unsustainable.

## The Call of Gideon (6:11-24)

### Rashi on "Mighty warrior"
Rashi explains the angel's greeting — "The LORD is with you, mighty warrior" (6:12) — as referring to Gideon's merit in defending his father's household. Even before his public career, Gideon demonstrated courage in private faithfulness.

### Gideon's Challenge
Gideon's response — "Where are all His wonders that our fathers told us about?" (6:13) — is treated by the Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 25a) as a legitimate theological question rather than faithless complaint. The principle established is that the leaders of each generation (*manhigei ha-dor*) are appropriate for their generation, even if they seem inferior to earlier leaders.

### The Identification of the Angel
Jewish tradition generally identifies the angel as a created messenger, not a manifestation of God Himself. The shift between "angel of the LORD" and "the LORD" in the text is understood as the messenger speaking in God's name, with full divine authority.

## Destruction of the Baal Altar (6:25-32)

The episode of Gideon's iconoclasm is significant for Jewish interpretation:

- It demonstrates the obligation to remove idolatry (*avodah zarah*) from Israel
- Joash's defense — "If he is a god, let him contend for himself" — articulates a rational argument against idolatry that recurs throughout Jewish thought
- The name Jerubbaal ("let Baal contend") becomes a badge of honor

### Halakhic Implications
The destruction of the altar raises halakhic questions about the obligation to destroy idolatrous objects that the Talmud (*Avodah Zarah*) discusses extensively.

## The Fleece (6:36-40)

### Rabbinic Assessment
Jewish interpreters generally view the fleece episode as reflecting Gideon's caution rather than his faithlessness. Radak notes that Gideon was seeking confirmation of a specific promise, not doubting God's existence or power. The Midrash adds that God patiently accommodated Gideon's request because the mission required absolute confidence.

## Theological Themes

- **The legitimacy of questioning**: Honest questions about God's actions are not faithless
- **The obligation to fight idolatry**: Removing *avodah zarah* is a positive commandment
- **The nature of leadership**: Leaders are appropriate for their generation
- **Divine accommodation**: God works within human limitations
