## Wives for Benjamin

Jewish interpretation of Judges 21 concludes the book of Judges with a narrative that raises fundamental questions about the binding nature of oaths, the value of human life, and the limits of human problem-solving.

## The Rash Oath (21:1-7)

### Halakhic Analysis
Jewish law (*Nedarim*) provides extensive discussion of the binding nature of oaths and the conditions under which they may be annulled:

- An oath that leads to the destruction of a tribe may be classified as an oath taken in error (*neder ta'ut*)
- The Talmud teaches that such an oath should have been annulled by the proper authority
- Israel's failure to seek annulment and instead devise violent workarounds compounds the original error

### The Mishnah on Vows
Mishnah Nedarim 9:1-10 develops the framework for releasing vows when circumstances reveal them to be harmful. The tragedy of Judges 21 provides a powerful negative example — what happens when people try to circumvent rather than properly address rash commitments.

## The Jabesh-Gilead Massacre (21:8-14)

Rabbinic tradition expresses deep discomfort with this "solution." The destruction of an entire community to provide wives violates multiple Torah principles:

- The prohibition against punishing the innocent for others' failures
- The requirement of proportionate justice
- The obligation to seek the least harmful means of resolving disputes

## The Shiloh Seizure (21:15-24)

### The Corruption of the Festival
The transformation of a religious festival into an occasion for seizing wives represents the ultimate corruption of worship. The sages connected this festival to the annual celebrations at Shiloh described in 1 Samuel 1, suggesting that even this sacred institution was being perverted.

### Legal Fiction
The elaborate reasoning used to justify the seizure — claiming it technically did not violate the oath — represents what the sages call *ha'arama* (legal evasion). While *ha'arama* has legitimate uses in halakhah, using it to circumvent the spirit of a commitment while technically observing its letter is condemned.

## "Everyone Did What Was Right in His Own Eyes" (21:25)

### The Jewish Reading
This final verse encapsulates the book's central message. Jewish interpretation reads it as:

- An argument for the necessity of authoritative Torah teaching
- A diagnosis of moral relativism
- A forward-pointing statement anticipating the establishment of the monarchy
- A perpetual warning against abandoning the divine standard in favor of personal judgment

### The Need for Torah
The sages understood this verse as ultimately an argument for Torah study and observance. Without the "king" — understood broadly as authoritative divine instruction mediated through legitimate institutions — society disintegrates into moral chaos.

## The Book's Place in Jewish Thought

Judges occupies an important place in Jewish consciousness:

- It demonstrates the consequences of abandoning Torah
- It preserves stories of heroism and failure that shape Jewish identity
- It provides material for halakhic and ethical discussion
- It points forward to the need for stable, righteous leadership
