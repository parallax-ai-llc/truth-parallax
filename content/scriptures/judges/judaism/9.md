## Abimelech

Jewish interpretation of Judges 9 reads the Abimelech narrative as a sustained parable about the consequences of illegitimate power and the certainty of divine justice. The chapter's length and complexity receive extensive rabbinic attention.

## The Fratricide (9:1-6)

### Midrashic Commentary
The Midrash compares Abimelech's murder of his seventy brothers to the sin of Cain — fratricide as the ultimate violation of human brotherhood. The number seventy connects to the seventy elders of Israel and the seventy nations, suggesting that this crime has cosmic significance.

Jotham's survival — "the youngest son" who hides — echoes the biblical pattern of the youngest being preserved for God's purposes (Joseph, David).

## Jotham's Parable (9:7-21)

### The Mashal Tradition
Jewish literary tradition (*mashal*) includes parables, fables, and allegories as vehicles for moral instruction. Jotham's parable of the trees is recognized as one of the earliest examples of this genre, which would later be developed extensively by the sages.

The parable's central insight — that productive, worthy individuals refuse power while the worthless thornbush eagerly accepts — resonates throughout Jewish political and ethical thought.

## The Evil Spirit (9:23)

Jewish theology addresses the "evil spirit" sent by God between Abimelech and the Shechemites through the concept of divine justice operating within nature:

- God does not create evil *ex nihilo* but allows the natural consequences of sin to unfold
- The "evil spirit" may represent the mutual suspicion and hostility that inevitably arise between partners in crime
- The Talmud teaches that those who cooperate in sin inevitably turn against one another

## Abimelech's Death (9:50-57)

### The Measure for Measure Principle
Jewish theology emphasizes *middat ha-din* (the principle of exact justice). Abimelech's death illustrates this perfectly:

- He who killed by violence dies by violence
- He who killed on a stone (9:5) is killed by a stone (the millstone)
- He who killed through a woman's betrayal (using his mother's family) is killed by a woman

The narrator's concluding statement (9:56-57) explicitly affirms this principle of retributive justice.

## Theological Themes

- **The consequences of fratricide**: Violence against brothers brings inevitable judgment
- **The mashal tradition**: Parables as vehicles for moral truth
- **Divine justice**: *Middat ha-din* operates with precision
- **The nature of legitimate authority**: Power seized through violence carries no divine sanction
