## The Danite Migration

Jewish interpretation of Judges 18 examines the expansion of idolatry from Micah's household to the tribe of Dan, raising questions about communal sin, the corruption of sacred lineage, and the historical consequences of religious infidelity.

## The Danite Spies (18:1-6)

### The Failure of Dan
Rabbinic tradition associates Dan's inability to hold its allotted territory with spiritual weakness. The Midrash observes that Dan was the first tribe to adopt organized idolatry, and this spiritual failure manifested as territorial failure.

### The False Oracle
The Levite's casual assurance — "Go in peace" — is critiqued by rabbinic commentators as the kind of false prophecy that tells people what they want to hear. The Talmud distinguishes between true prophecy (which may be uncomfortable) and false prophecy (which confirms existing desires).

## The Attack on Laish (18:27-29)

### Moral Assessment
While the Torah commands the conquest of Canaan, it specifies which nations are subject to this commandment. The unprovoked attack on peaceful Laish lacks the divine mandate that characterized Joshua's campaigns. Rabbinic legal reasoning would classify this as an unauthorized military action.

## The Stolen Shrine (18:14-26)

### The Irony of Stolen Gods
The Talmud (*Avodah Zarah* 54b) notes the fundamental absurdity of an idol that can be stolen: "If it cannot protect itself, how can it protect its worshippers?" This argument against idolatry is developed extensively in the rabbinic anti-idol polemics found throughout the Talmud.

## Jonathan ben Gershom ben Menasheh (18:30)

### The Suspended Nun
The Hebrew text writes the name as "Menasheh" with a suspended letter *nun*, indicating that the original reading was "Mosheh" (Moses). The sages inserted the *nun* to protect Moses' reputation:

- The Talmud (Bava Batra 109b) states: "It was Moses' grandson, but because he did the deeds of Manasseh (the wicked king), Scripture attributed him to Manasseh"
- This textual tradition demonstrates the rabbinic concern for preserving the honor of the righteous while recording the historical facts
- It also teaches that noble lineage does not guarantee spiritual faithfulness

## Theological Themes

- **The contagion of idolatry**: What begins as one man's sin becomes an institution
- **The futility of false gods**: Gods that can be stolen offer no protection
- **The suspended nun**: The tension between historical truth and honor for the righteous
- **Lineage and merit**: Ancestral virtue does not automatically transfer to descendants
