## Samson and the Philistines

Jewish interpretation of Judges 15 examines Samson's escalating conflict with the Philistines through the lens of rabbinic ethics, the theology of divine empowerment, and the nature of individual versus communal responsibility.

## The Fox Torches (15:1-5)

### Midrashic Commentary
The Midrash finds symbolic significance in the foxes (*shu'alim*). Rashi connects this to the prophecy that "foxes will walk upon" the ruins (Lamentations 5:18), suggesting a link between destruction and future redemption. The three hundred foxes parallel Gideon's three hundred soldiers — God's deliverance through unusual means.

## The Cycle of Retaliation (15:1-8)

### The Ethics of Vengeance
Jewish law distinguishes between *nekamah* (personal vengeance, prohibited) and *mishpat* (justice, commanded). The rabbinic debate over Samson centers on whether his actions constitute legitimate justice or forbidden personal revenge:

- As a divinely appointed judge, Samson had authority to act against Israel's oppressors
- Yet his personal motivations (anger over his wife) complicate the picture
- The Talmud generally treats Samson's actions as divinely sanctioned, even when personally motivated

## Judah's Complicity (15:9-13)

### Communal Responsibility
The willingness of 3,000 men of Judah to bind their own deliverer and hand him over to the Philistines represents a profound failure of communal solidarity (*arvut*). The principle of *kol Yisrael arevim zeh ba-zeh* (all Israel is responsible for one another) is violated when the community sides with the oppressor against its own deliverer.

## The Jawbone Victory (15:14-17)

### The Spirit's Empowerment
The phrase "*va-titzlach alav ruach Hashem*" (the Spirit of the LORD rushed upon him) describes the charismatic empowerment that characterized the judges. Jewish tradition understands this as a temporary gift of extraordinary strength, distinct from prophetic revelation.

### Samson's Song
Samson's victory poem (15:16) uses wordplay: *chamor* (donkey) and *chamor* (heap). Jewish literary tradition appreciates such verbal artistry, even while noting that Samson's self-glorifying tone contrasts unfavorably with the God-centered songs of Moses and Deborah.

## The Spring at Lehi (15:18-19)

### Prayer in Extremity
Samson's desperate prayer for water illustrates the Jewish teaching that genuine prayer (*tefillah*) often arises from crisis. The sages taught that even imperfect prayer, when offered from genuine need, reaches God's ears. The miraculous spring demonstrates God's responsiveness even to flawed petitioners.

## Theological Themes

- **The complexity of justice and vengeance**: The line between the two is often blurred
- **Communal responsibility**: The obligation of solidarity with those who fight for Israel
- **Divine empowerment**: God's spirit enables extraordinary deeds
- **Prayer from the depths**: Crisis often produces the most genuine prayer
