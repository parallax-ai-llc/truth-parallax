Gold rings are among the most common precious metal finds at Mesopotamian archaeological sites. Seal rings — rings with carved cylinder or stamp seals — are attested from the fourth millennium BCE onward and served as personal identifiers and legal instruments. The association between rings and authority is documented in administrative texts where the impression of a seal ring validated contracts, property transfers, and royal commands.

At Ur, Woolley discovered numerous gold rings in both royal and private burials, confirming their widespread use as markers of status and personal identity. The literary motif of ring-removal thus draws on a well-understood cultural symbol.
