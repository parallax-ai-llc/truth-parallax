## Overview

The Descent of Inanna is known from over thirty fragmentary cuneiform tablets, the majority recovered from the scribal quarter at Nippur during the University of Pennsylvania excavations in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Additional fragments have been identified at Ur, Uruk, and other sites. The archaeological context of these tablets — primarily the *edubba* (scribal school) — reveals that the text served an educational function alongside its religious significance.

## Archaeological Evidence

### Tablet Provenance
The majority of tablets derive from Nippur's scribal quarter (Area TA), where Old Babylonian-period school exercises were discarded in fill. These tablets include both student practice copies (often with errors and incomplete passages) and more polished versions that may reflect library or temple holdings.

### Material Features
The tablets are typically Type II or Type III school tablets — smaller format clay tablets used for copying exercises. The presence of multiple copies with minor variants allows scholars to reconstruct a composite text, though no single tablet preserves the entire composition.

### Cult and Ritual Context
Archaeological evidence from Ur and other sites documents the ritual mourning for Dumuzi (*Tammuz*), the deity whose fate is sealed in the narrative's conclusion. Cylinder seals, terracotta plaques, and ritual texts corroborate the widespread cultic significance of the Inanna-Dumuzi mythology.

## Key Archaeological Sites

| Site | Significance |

## Material Culture Connections

The seven items of regalia removed at each gate correspond to actual types of Mesopotamian adornment known from archaeological finds: the *shugurra* crown, lapis lazuli jewelry, gold rings, and breast ornaments have parallels in the Royal Tombs of Ur and other excavated assemblages.
