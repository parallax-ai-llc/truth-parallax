The scene of Inanna naked before Ereshkigal connects to a rich body of archaeological evidence for the depiction of nude female figures in Mesopotamian art. Terracotta plaques from the Old Babylonian period frequently depict nude female figures — some clearly identified as Ishtar/Inanna — in frontal view, sometimes standing on or flanked by lions. These plaques have been interpreted as representations of the goddess in her underworld aspect.

The 'Burney Relief' (British Museum), depicting a nude, winged female figure flanked by owls and standing on lions, has been debated as a representation of either Inanna/Ishtar or Ereshkigal. If the latter, it may depict the underworld queen who receives the naked Inanna.

The hook on which Inanna's corpse is hung may correspond to actual funerary or ritual practices, though no direct archaeological evidence for such a practice has been identified. Hooks and pegs are common finds in Mesopotamian temple contexts, and their ritual significance is attested in texts describing temple furniture and cult practice.
