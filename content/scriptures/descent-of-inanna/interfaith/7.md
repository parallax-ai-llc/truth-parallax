The seventh gate — Inanna stripped naked, killed, and hung on a hook — is perhaps the most powerful image in the entire poem, and it resonates with the deepest themes of world religion:

- **Christianity**: The crucifixion — the divine stripped, killed, and hung — stands as the most direct parallel. Theologians have noted the structural similarity without claiming direct influence
- **Buddhism**: The Buddha's confrontation with Mara under the Bodhi tree — the moment of total vulnerability before awakening
- **Hinduism**: Shiva's dance of destruction in the cremation ground — the divine present at the place of death
- **Judaism**: The Akeidah — Abraham's willingness to give up what is most precious
- **Islam**: The concept of *tawakkul* (absolute surrender to God) — the willingness to stand before the divine with nothing

The seventh gate teaches that transformation — whether death and resurrection, enlightenment, or liberation — requires passing through a point of absolute zero, where all pretense and protection are gone.
