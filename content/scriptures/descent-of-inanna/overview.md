## Overview

The Descent of Inanna (Sumerian: *Inanna's Descent to the Netherworld*) is one of the oldest narrative poems in world literature, dating to approximately 1900–1600 BCE. It recounts the journey of the goddess Inanna (Akkadian: Ishtar), Queen of Heaven and Earth, into the *kur* — the underworld realm of her sister Ereshkigal. Composed in Sumerian and preserved on multiple cuneiform tablets recovered from Nippur and Ur, the text represents a foundational myth of ancient Mesopotamian religion.

## Structure

The poem is organized around seven gates through which Inanna must pass, each requiring the surrender of a piece of her divine regalia:

1. **Gate 1**: The first gate — the great crown (*shugurra*) is removed
2. **Gate 2**: The second gate — the lapis lazuli measuring rod and line are taken
3. **Gate 3**: The third gate — the lapis lazuli necklace is removed
4. **Gate 4**: The fourth gate — the sparkling stones at her breast are taken
5. **Gate 5**: The fifth gate — the gold ring is removed
6. **Gate 6**: The sixth gate — the breastplate is taken
7. **Gate 7**: The seventh gate — the royal garments are removed; Inanna stands naked before Ereshkigal

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Descent of Inanna holds significance across multiple scholarly and religious perspectives:

- **Academic**: A primary text for understanding Sumerian religion, gender in the ancient world, and early narrative poetry
- **Comparative**: Widely studied alongside the Akkadian *Descent of Ishtar*, the Greek myths of Persephone and Orpheus, and the Christian Harrowing of Hell
- **Theological**: Raises profound questions about death, sovereignty, and the limits of divine power
- **Literary**: One of the earliest examples of narrative structure built around progressive loss and transformation
- **Archaeological**: The tablets preserve invaluable evidence of Sumerian scribal tradition and religious practice
- **Interfaith**: The death-and-return motif resonates with themes across world religions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Descent and Return | The journey to the underworld and the conditions of return |
| Stripping of Power | Progressive loss of divine attributes at each gate |
| Death of a Deity | The radical idea that even the Queen of Heaven can die |
| Substitute and Sacrifice | Dumuzi's substitution as the price of Inanna's return |
| Female Divine Power | Inanna's agency, ambition, and ultimate triumph |
