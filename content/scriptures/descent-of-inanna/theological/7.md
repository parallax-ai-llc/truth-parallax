The seventh gate is the theological climax of the poem. Inanna, stripped of every attribute of divinity, stands naked before her sister Ereshkigal — and is killed. The death of Inanna is not a metaphor or a temporary setback: she is turned into a corpse (*kus2-a*) and hung on a hook (*gisgal*). The Queen of Heaven is dead.

This scene poses the most radical theological question the poem raises: if a goddess can die, what is the nature of divinity? The text's answer seems to be that the underworld operates by laws that even the gods must obey. Ereshkigal's sovereignty in her own realm is absolute — and the price of entering that realm, even for the highest deity, is death.

The subsequent rescue — Enki's creation of beings who can pass through the underworld's gates because they are neither alive nor dead — introduces a theology of wisdom overcoming death through cleverness rather than power. Inanna is revived not by force but by compassion: the beings comfort the grieving Ereshkigal, who grants them what they ask — the corpse of Inanna.
