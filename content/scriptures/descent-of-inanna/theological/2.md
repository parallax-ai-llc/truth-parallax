The removal of the measuring rod and line at the second gate strips Inanna of her capacity to judge and order. Theologically, this represents a deeper level of vulnerability than the loss of the crown: at the first gate, she lost the symbol of authority; at the second, she loses the instruments of authority — the ability to measure, discern, and decree.

This progressive stripping raises the theological question of what constitutes divine identity. If Inanna can be separated from her powers one by one, is there an essential Inanna beneath the attributes? The text's implicit answer — that Inanna stripped of all her *me* is killed — suggests a theology in which divine identity is constituted by divine powers rather than existing independently of them.
