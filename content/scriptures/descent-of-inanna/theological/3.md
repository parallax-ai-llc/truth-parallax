At the third gate, the removal moves from instruments of authority to personal adornment. The necklace represents beauty and allure — key aspects of Inanna's divine nature as goddess of love and desire. Its removal begins to strip away not just Inanna's power but her identity as the goddess who embodies attraction and desire.

This raises a theological question about the relationship between divine attributes: is the goddess of love still the goddess of love without the outward signs of beauty? The text seems to suggest that divine identity is inseparable from its expressions — that removing the signs of divinity is tantamount to removing divinity itself.
