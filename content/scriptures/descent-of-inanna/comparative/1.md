The first gate of the Descent of Inanna introduces motifs that recur across underworld journey narratives. The requirement to surrender power at the threshold finds parallels in the Egyptian Book of the Dead, where the deceased must pass through gates guarded by sentinels, and in the Greek tradition where Charon demands payment for passage across the Styx. The Akkadian Descent of Ishtar preserves the same seven-gate structure, confirming direct literary dependence.

The removal of the *shugurra* crown — the symbol of Inanna's supreme authority — at the very first gate establishes a principle found across descent narratives: the powers of the upper world have no currency in the realm of the dead. This motif resonates with the Christian concept of kenosis (Philippians 2:7) and the Buddhist teaching on non-attachment.
