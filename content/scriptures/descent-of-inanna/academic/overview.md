## Overview

Academic scholarship on the Descent of Inanna focuses on the reconstruction and interpretation of the Sumerian text from multiple fragmentary tablets, primarily from Nippur. The composite text, established through the work of Samuel Noah Kramer and later refined by scholars including Sladek and Katz, comprises approximately 410 lines. Dating of the composition is generally placed in the Old Babylonian period (c. 1900–1600 BCE), though the mythological traditions it draws upon are considerably older.

## Key Scholarly Approaches

### Textual Reconstruction
The text is known from over thirty partial copies, requiring careful collation. W.R. Sladek's 1974 dissertation provided the first comprehensive critical edition, later supplemented by the Electronic Text Corpus of Sumerian Literature (ETCSL) at Oxford.

### Relationship to the Akkadian Version
The Akkadian *Descent of Ishtar* (first millennium BCE) is a significantly shorter adaptation. Scholars debate whether it represents an independent tradition or a condensation of the Sumerian original, with the Akkadian version omitting the Dumuzi substitution motif entirely.

### Gender and Power
Scholars including Tikva Frymer-Kensky and Julia Assante have examined Inanna's role as a boundary-crossing deity whose descent challenges binary categories of life/death, male/female, and civilized/wild.
