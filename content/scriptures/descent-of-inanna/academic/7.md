At the seventh and final gate, Inanna's royal garments (*pala tug2-mah*) are removed, and she enters Ereshkigal's throne room completely naked. What follows is the poem's climactic scene: Inanna is judged by the Anunnaki (the judges of the underworld), Ereshkigal fixes upon her the 'look of death,' speaks against her the 'word of wrath,' and utters against her the 'cry of guilt.' Inanna is turned into a corpse and hung on a hook.

Scholarly analysis of this scene has focused on the legal terminology: the Anunnaki function as a court, Ereshkigal as judge, and the death of Inanna is presented as a judicial sentence. Dina Katz has argued that the underworld in Sumerian theology functions as a court of cosmic law, and Inanna's death is the lawful consequence of transgressing its boundary. The remainder of the poem — Enki's rescue, Inanna's return, and the substitution of Dumuzi — follows from this central act of cosmic jurisprudence.
