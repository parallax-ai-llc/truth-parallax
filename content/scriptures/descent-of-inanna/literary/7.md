The seventh gate is the poem's dramatic climax, achieved through the completion of the seven-fold repetitive structure. The final stripping — of the royal garments themselves, leaving nothing — creates a moment of absolute narrative exposure. The goddess who began the poem adorned in the full regalia of divine power now stands naked in the throne room of death.

The shift from the formulaic gate sequences to the rapid-fire narrative of the judgment scene is masterful. After six iterations of the slow, ritualistic gate formula, the pace suddenly accelerates: 'The Anunnaki, the seven judges, pronounced judgment before her. They looked at her — it was the look of death. They spoke against her — it was the word of guilt. The stricken woman was turned into a corpse. The corpse was hung on a hook.' The compression of these lines after the expansive repetitions creates a devastating literary effect — the equivalent of a cinematic jump cut.

The remainder of the poem — Enki's intervention, the revival, the search for a substitute, and Dumuzi's fate — unfolds as the consequences of this central scene, each episode driven by the irreversible fact of Inanna's death and the underworld's demand for a replacement.
