The second gate deepens the poem's central literary technique. The near-verbatim repetition of the gate formula — with only the specific item varying — creates a ritualistic rhythm that builds anticipation. The reader or listener, recognizing the pattern after the second iteration, can now project it forward, knowing that five more gates await and five more items will be taken.

The lapis lazuli measuring rod is a more abstract symbol than the crown, and its removal signals a shift from the visible signs of power to the functional instruments of authority. The poem's literary sophistication lies in this careful sequencing: from most visible to most intimate, from public symbols to private adornments.
