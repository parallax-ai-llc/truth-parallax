## Overview

The Descent of Inanna is a masterwork of ancient Sumerian literary art, employing sophisticated techniques of repetition, parallelism, and dramatic structure that both reflect the conventions of oral-performative poetry and achieve remarkable narrative power. The text's literary architecture — built around the sevenfold gate sequence — creates a pattern of escalating tension that has influenced literary analysis of ancient Near Eastern texts more broadly.

## Key Literary Features

### Incremental Repetition
The seven-gate sequence employs a technique of near-identical repetition with progressive variation. At each gate, the gatekeeper asks the same question, Inanna gives a variant answer, and one item of regalia is removed. This incremental stripping creates both ritual rhythm and narrative momentum.

### Parallelism
Like all Sumerian poetry, the Descent employs extensive parallelism — both synonymous and antithetical. The contrast between Inanna's glorious departure and her naked arrival before Ereshkigal exemplifies structural antithesis at the narrative level.

### Characterization
The text achieves surprising psychological complexity:
- **Inanna**: Ambitious, willful, and ultimately vulnerable
- **Ereshkigal**: Not a villain but a sovereign exercising legitimate authority
- **Dumuzi**: His festive self-indulgence during Inanna's absence seals his fate
- **Geshtinanna**: Her grief and willingness to share Dumuzi's fate introduces compassion

### The Role of the *Me*
Inanna's regalia represent the *me* — the divine decrees or powers that constitute civilization. Her stripping is simultaneously a narrative event and a theological statement about the relationship between power and mortality.

## Literary Structure

| Section | Function |
|---------|----------|
| Preparation (lines 1–13) | Inanna gathers the *me* and adorns herself |
| Seven gates (lines 14–164) | Progressive stripping and descent |
| Death and hanging (lines 165–172) | The crisis — Inanna is killed |
| Rescue (lines 173–281) | Enki's intervention and revival |
| Return and substitution (lines 282–410) | Inanna's return and Dumuzi's fate |
