## Overview

The Buddhist-Japanese reading of the Ofudesaki explores the text's engagement with Buddhist concepts that pervaded Japanese religious consciousness in the Tokugawa and Meiji periods. The Ofudesaki's metaphor of 'dust' (*hokori*) — mental attitudes such as greed, anger, arrogance, and selfishness that cloud the mind and cause suffering — resonates strongly with the Buddhist concept of *klesha* (mental defilements) and the Zen notion of obscurations that prevent awakening.

The Ofudesaki's teaching on *innen* (causality or karmic connection) draws on Buddhist ideas of *karma* while adapting them to Tenrikyo's distinctive theological framework. Japanese Buddhist scholars note both the borrowings from and the departures from Buddhist soteriology, particularly the Ofudesaki's emphasis on divine grace and parental love as the primary means of salvation, in contrast to Buddhism's emphasis on self-cultivation and the extinguishing of desire.
