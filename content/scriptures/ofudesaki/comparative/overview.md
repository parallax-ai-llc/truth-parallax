## Overview

From a comparative perspective, the Ofudesaki exemplifies the phenomenon of prophetic scripture — a text believed to be directly authored by God through a human intermediary. This places it alongside the Quran (as received by Muhammad), the Book of Mormon (as translated by Joseph Smith), the Baha'i writings (as revealed through Baha'u'llah), and the writings of other prophetic figures who claimed divine dictation.

The Ofudesaki's theology of divine parenthood — God as the literal Parent of all human beings — invites comparison with similar concepts in Christianity (God as Father), Hinduism (Brahman as source), and indigenous traditions that speak of a Creator or Great Spirit. The text's emphasis on the *Yokigurashi* (Joyous Life) as God's intention for humanity resonates with the concept of *shalom* in Judaism, *eudaimonia* in Aristotelian ethics, and the Buddhist aspiration for the happiness of all sentient beings.
