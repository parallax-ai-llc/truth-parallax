## Overview

The Ofudesaki ("Tip of the Writing Brush") is the foundational scripture of Tenrikyo, a Japanese new religion founded by Nakayama Miki (1798-1887). Written between 1869 and 1882, the Ofudesaki comprises 1,711 verses organized into seventeen parts, all composed in the *waka* poetic form (5-7-5-7-7 syllable pattern). Tenrikyo adherents believe that God the Parent (*Oyagami* or *Tenri-O-no-Mikoto*) directly guided Nakayama Miki's brush to communicate the divine will for humanity.

## Structure

The seventeen parts of the Ofudesaki were written over thirteen years, each part addressing specific themes and circumstances:

1. **Parts I-VI**: Early revelations establishing the divine identity and purpose
2. **Parts VII-XII**: Expansion of teachings on salvation, the joyous life, and the Service
3. **Parts XIII-XVII**: Later revelations addressing the growth of the faith and the urgency of the divine message

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Tenrikyo**: The most sacred scripture, studied daily and recited in worship services
- **Academic**: A major primary source for the study of Japanese new religions and modern prophetic movements
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels with other scriptures produced through automatic or inspired writing
- **Shinto**: Examined for its relationship to *kami* theology and Japanese religious vocabulary
- **Buddhist-Japanese**: Studied for its engagement with Buddhist concepts of karma, suffering, and liberation
- **Interfaith**: Valued for its universal message of divine parenthood and human brotherhood

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Oyagami | God the Parent, creator and sustainer of all human beings |
| Joyous Life | The divine intention for humanity to live in joy and harmony |
| Hinagata | The exemplary life of Nakayama Miki as a model for followers |
| Salvation | Divine rescue from suffering through understanding the truth of origin |
| The Service | The sacred dance and prayer (*Teodori*) as the means of salvation |
| Dust | Metaphor for the mental attitudes (greed, anger, etc.) that obscure the divine nature |
