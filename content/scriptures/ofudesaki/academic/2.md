## Academic Reading

Academic study of Part II focuses on the Ofudesaki's creation narrative and its relationship to existing Japanese cosmogonic traditions. The account of human creation from primordial mud at the Jiba differs significantly from both the Kojiki/Nihon Shoki creation myths and Buddhist origin stories, representing a creative theological innovation that draws on multiple sources.

Scholars analyze the creation narrative's function as a charter myth that legitimates the Tenrikyo community's claim to the Jiba as a sacred site and establishes the theological basis for the movement's universalist anthropology — the teaching that all humans share a common divine origin.
