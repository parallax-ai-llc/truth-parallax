## Academic Reading

Academic analysis of Part I examines the Ofudesaki's opening as a programmatic declaration that establishes the text's claims to divine authority. Scholars note the strategic deployment of first-person divine speech — God speaking directly through the brush — which parallels prophetic conventions in other traditions while employing distinctively Japanese literary forms.

The historical context of Part I's composition (1869) places it in the early Meiji period, a time of radical social transformation in Japan. Scholars examine how the text responds to the disruption of traditional religious structures by the Meiji government's separation of Shinto and Buddhism (*shinbutsu bunri*) and its implications for new religious movements seeking legitimacy.
