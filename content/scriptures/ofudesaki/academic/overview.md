## Overview

Academic study of the Ofudesaki situates the text within the broader phenomenon of Japanese new religions (*shinshukyo*) that emerged during the social upheavals of the late Tokugawa and early Meiji periods. Scholars examine the Ofudesaki as a product of a specific historical moment — the transformation of Japanese society from feudal to modern — while also recognizing its role as the foundational document of a religious movement that now claims millions of adherents worldwide.

The text's composition through *kamigakari* (divine possession or inspiration) is analyzed in the context of Japanese shamanistic traditions, spirit-writing practices, and the broader cross-cultural phenomenon of automatic writing. Scholars of Japanese religion note the Ofudesaki's creative engagement with Shinto and Buddhist vocabulary, its implicit critique of Meiji government religious policies, and its innovative theology of a single creator deity — a concept that both draws upon and departs from traditional Japanese religious frameworks.
