## Overview

The Exegesis on the Soul (NHC II,6) is an allegorical narrative describing the journey of the soul — personified as a feminine figure — from her original dwelling in the heavenly realm, through her fall into the material world where she is degraded and prostituted, to her eventual repentance, purification, and restoration to her divine bridegroom through the intervention of the Father. The text is notable for its extensive use of quotations from the Hebrew Bible (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Hosea), the New Testament (Paul, 1 John), and Homer's Odyssey, weaving them into a unified allegorical narrative about the soul's descent and return. The text is generally dated to the second or third century CE and likely originates from Alexandria, where the allegorical interpretation of scripture had a long tradition.

## Structure

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The Fall of the Soul | The soul's original heavenly state, her descent into the body, and the beginning of her degradation |
| 2 | The Soul's Degradation in the World | The soul is prostituted by the archons and bears illegitimate children, losing her original purity |
| 3 | The Repentance and Cry for Help | The soul recognizes her fallen state, repents, and cries out to the Father for deliverance |
| 4 | The Restoration and the Bridal Chamber | The Father sends the bridegroom (the spirit), the soul is purified, and the sacred marriage restores her to wholeness |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Read as an allegory of the pneumatic soul's descent into matter and her eventual return to the Pleroma through gnosis
- **Academic**: Studied for its hermeneutical method (combining OT, NT, and pagan sources), its gender imagery, and its relationship to Valentinian bridal chamber theology
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to the Platonic myth of the soul's descent (Phaedrus) and to Hindu and Sufi allegories of the soul's separation from and reunion with the divine
- **Christian**: Engages with biblical texts on repentance and redemption while developing them in a distinctly Gnostic direction
- **Jewish**: Draws extensively on prophetic imagery of Israel as an unfaithful wife (Hosea, Ezekiel 16) and the motif of divine-human marriage
- **Interfaith**: Addresses the universal experience of spiritual alienation and the longing for wholeness

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Feminine Soul | The soul is feminine — a bride separated from her heavenly bridegroom |
| Fall and Degradation | The soul's descent into the body subjects her to abuse by the cosmic powers |
| Repentance | True repentance is not mere regret but a turning of the soul's orientation from matter to spirit |
| The Bridal Chamber | The sacred marriage of the soul with her divine partner restores the original unity broken by the fall |
