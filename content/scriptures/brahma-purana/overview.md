## Overview

The Brahma Purana is traditionally listed as the first of the eighteen Mahapuranas and is sometimes called the Adi Purana (the first Purana). Despite its name, the text is largely Vaishnava in orientation, with significant sections devoted to the worship of Vishnu, Krishna, and Surya (the Sun god). The text likely reached its present form between the 7th and 14th centuries CE through a gradual process of accretion.

The Brahma Purana is particularly notable for its extensive treatment of sacred geography, especially the pilgrimage sites of Odisha, including the famous temple of Jagannath at Puri and the Sun temple at Konark. It also contains cosmological accounts, genealogies of royal dynasties, and sections on dharma, festivals, and funeral rites.

## Structure

The text is organized into approximately 245 chapters, which can be grouped into ten thematic sections:

1. **Section 1 — Cosmogony and Creation**: The origin of the universe, the nature of Brahma, and the process of creation
2. **Section 2 — Geography and Sacred Sites**: The seven continents, sacred rivers, and pilgrimage places of India
3. **Section 3 — Genealogies of the Solar Dynasty**: The lineage of the solar kings from Ikshvaku to Rama
4. **Section 4 — Genealogies of the Lunar Dynasty**: The lineage of the lunar kings from Pururavas to the Pandavas
5. **Section 5 — Life of Krishna**: The birth, exploits, and teachings of Krishna
6. **Section 6 — Pilgrimage of Odisha**: Sacred sites of Odisha, especially Puri and Konark
7. **Section 7 — Dharma and Duties**: Codes of conduct, varnashrama dharma, and daily observances
8. **Section 8 — Funeral Rites and Ancestors**: Shraddha ceremonies, ancestral worship, and rites for the dead
9. **Section 9 — Festivals and Vratas**: Religious observances, fasts, and festival celebrations
10. **Section 10 — Yoga and Liberation**: Meditation, knowledge, and the paths to moksha

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Brahma Purana holds significance across multiple Hindu traditions:

- **Vaishnavism**: A foundational Purana establishing Vishnu-Krishna worship as central to creation theology
- **Shaivism**: Engaged for its cosmological accounts while noting its Vaishnava sectarian orientation
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Cited for passages on the ultimate nature of Brahman beyond all personal deities
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Referenced for its affirmation of Vishnu's supremacy and its detailed creation theology
- **Academic**: Studied for its composite nature, regional sacred geography, and layers of textual accretion

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Creation through Brahma | Brahma as the demiurge acting under the authority of the supreme deity |
| Sacred Geography | Extensive accounts of pilgrimage sites, especially in Odisha |
| Krishna Narrative | The life and deeds of Krishna as an avatar of Vishnu |
| Dharma | Codes of social and religious conduct for all stages of life |
| Eschatology | The dissolution of the universe and the paths to liberation |
