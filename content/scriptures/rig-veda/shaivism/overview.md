## Overview

Shaiva tradition sees the Rig Veda as containing the primordial revelation of Rudra-Shiva, the fierce and benevolent deity who stands apart from the other gods. The Rudra hymns (especially 1.114 and 2.33) are regarded as the oldest Shaiva scriptures, presenting the god who is simultaneously the destroyer and the healer, the lord of storms and the bestower of medicines. The epithet "Shiva" (the auspicious one) already appears in the Rig Veda as a descriptor of Rudra.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Kashmir Shaiva Reading
Kashmir Shaivism interprets the Vedic deities as manifestations of Paramashiva (supreme consciousness). Rudra's cosmic role — destroying the old to make way for the new — is understood as the dynamic activity (*spanda*) of consciousness that continuously creates, sustains, and reabsorbs the universe.

### Shaiva Siddhanta
The southern Shaiva Siddhanta tradition recognizes the Vedas as valid scripture (*shruti*) that points to Shiva as the supreme lord, while holding that the Shaiva Agamas provide the fuller revelation of Shiva's nature and worship.

## Theological Emphases

- **Rudra as Proto-Shiva**: The Rig Vedic Rudra is the direct precursor of the later Shiva of the Puranas and Agamas
- **Tryambaka**: The "three-eyed one" (7.59.12), the basis of the Mahamrityunjaya Mantra, is a Shaiva treasure
- **Healing and Destruction**: Rudra's dual nature — terrifying yet benevolent — embodies the Shaiva understanding of divine paradox
- **Cosmic Dance**: The Vedic Rudra's association with storms prefigures Shiva Nataraja's cosmic dance
