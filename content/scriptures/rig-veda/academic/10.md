Mandala 10 is recognized by scholars as the latest stratum of the Rig Veda, exhibiting linguistic features, theological concepts, and social references that mark it as significantly more recent than the Family Books. The Purusha Sukta's mention of the four varnas (social classes) is one of the earliest textual references to the caste system, and scholars debate whether it reflects an established social reality or an ideological program.

The Nasadiya Sukta (10.129) has been the subject of extensive philosophical and literary analysis. Scholars such as Brereton and Jamison emphasize its radical questioning of cosmogonic knowledge, while others read it in the context of priestly competition, where claims to cosmic knowledge enhanced ritual authority. The Mandala's funeral hymns (10.14-18), marriage hymn (10.85), and gambling hymn (10.34) provide unique windows into Vedic social life and popular religion.
