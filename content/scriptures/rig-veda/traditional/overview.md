## Overview

In the traditional Hindu understanding, the Rig Veda is *apaurusheya* (not of human origin) — it is the eternal word (*shabda*) that was "seen" rather than composed by the ancient seers (*rishis*) in states of deep meditative absorption. The hymns are considered the primordial revelation from which all dharma flows, and their authority is self-evident, requiring no external validation.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Mimamsa Exegesis
The Purva Mimamsa school, especially Jaimini and Shabara, treats the Rig Veda primarily as a guide to ritual action (*karma*). Each hymn has a specific application in the sacrificial context, and the primary meaning of the Veda is injunctive (*vidhi*) rather than descriptive.

### Nirukta and Vyakarana
Yaska's Nirukta (etymological commentary) and Panini's grammatical tradition provide the traditional tools for understanding Vedic vocabulary, which often diverges significantly from classical Sanskrit usage.

## Theological Emphases

- **Eternality of the Veda**: The hymns exist before creation and are re-revealed at the beginning of each cosmic cycle
- **Rishi-Devata-Chandas**: Each hymn has a seer, a presiding deity, and a meter — the three coordinates of Vedic interpretation
- **Sacrificial Context**: The hymns function within the elaborate framework of Vedic yajna
- **Oral Transmission**: The precise preservation of sound through *svara* (tonal accent) is itself a sacred discipline
