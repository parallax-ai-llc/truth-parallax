Mandala 6's pastoral imagery — cattle, horses, fields, rivers — connects it to the pastoral-nomadic traditions found in other ancient scriptures. The Psalms of David, composed in a similarly pastoral society, share the Bharadvaja hymns' sensitivity to the sacred presence in the natural world. The Book of Job's divine speeches about animals and weather phenomena parallel the Vedic celebration of nature as the arena of divine activity.

The Bharadvaja hymns' invocations of Pushan, the divine herdsman and guide of the dead, compare with Hermes Psychopompos in Greek religion, Anubis in Egyptian religion, and the Good Shepherd imagery in Christianity. The archetype of the divine guide who leads both flocks and souls appears across cultures, reflecting the deep connection between pastoral economy and religious imagination.
