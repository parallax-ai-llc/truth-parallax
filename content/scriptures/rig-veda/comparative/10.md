The Nasadiya Sukta (10.129) stands as one of the world's most philosophically sophisticated creation narratives. Its agnostic questioning — 'Who really knows? Who can declare it?' — compares with the pre-Socratic philosophers' speculations about the *arche* (first principle), the Daodejing's opening line about the nameless Dao, and the Buddhist refusal to answer metaphysical questions about the origin of the universe. All these traditions recognize the limits of language and thought before the mystery of origins.

The Purusha Sukta (10.90) presents a cosmogony through sacrifice that parallels creation myths in other traditions: the Norse giant Ymir from whose body the world was made, the Babylonian Tiamat whose body became heaven and earth, and the Chinese Pangu myth. These 'body cosmogonies' — creation from the dismemberment of a primordial being — constitute one of the most widespread mythological patterns identified by comparative scholars.
