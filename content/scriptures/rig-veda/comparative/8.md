Mandala 8's stylistic affinities with the Avesta highlight the close relationship between Vedic and Zoroastrian religious traditions. The shared vocabulary of sacrifice — the Vedic *yajna* and Avestan *yasna*, the Vedic *hotr* and Avestan *zaotar* — reveals a common ancestral religion that diverged into the two traditions. The Mandala's Soma/Haoma hymns are particularly significant, as both traditions preserve the ritual pressing of a sacred plant juice.

The Vedic-Avestan comparison illuminates a remarkable religious divergence: the Vedic *devas* (gods) became the Zoroastrian *daevas* (demons), while the Vedic *asuras* (a class of deities) correspond to the Avestan *ahuras* (supreme gods, especially Ahura Mazda). This inversion of divine and demonic categories provides one of comparative religion's most instructive case studies in how related traditions can develop in opposite theological directions.
