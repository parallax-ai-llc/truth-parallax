## Overview

For Advaita Vedanta, the Rig Veda's hymns to multiple deities ultimately point beyond themselves to the one non-dual reality, Brahman. Adi Shankara and the Advaita tradition read the Vedic hymns through the lens established by the Upanishads, seeing the many gods as provisional expressions of the single absolute truth. The famous Rig Vedic declaration "Ekam sat vipra bahudha vadanti" (1.164.46) — "Truth is one; the wise call it by many names" — is treated as the foundational insight of the entire Vedic corpus.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Upanishadic Culmination
Advaitins hold that the *karmakanda* (ritual portion) of the Rig Veda is preparatory, while the *jnanakanda* (knowledge portion) found in the Upanishads represents the Veda's true and final teaching. The hymns purify the mind through ritual, but liberation comes only through knowledge of Brahman.

### Symbolic Reading of Deities
Agni, Indra, Varuna, and other deities are understood symbolically: Agni as the fire of consciousness, Indra as the supreme Self conquering ignorance, and the cosmic order (*rta*) as the manifestation of Brahman's inherent nature.

## Theological Emphases

- **Unity of the Godhead**: The multiplicity of Vedic gods is appearance (*vyavaharika*), not ultimate reality (*paramarthika*)
- **Ekam Sat**: The Rig Vedic statement of oneness is the seed of Advaita philosophy
- **Preparatory Function**: Ritual action (*karma*) purifies but does not liberate; knowledge alone frees
- **Continuity with Upanishads**: The Rig Veda and its Upanishads (Aitareya) form a single teaching arc
