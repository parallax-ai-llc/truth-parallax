## Overview

Vaishnava tradition reads the Rig Veda as containing the seeds of Vishnu's supreme lordship, even though Vishnu appears in relatively few hymns compared to Agni or Indra. The celebrated Vishnu Sukta (1.154) — describing Vishnu's three cosmic strides that measured out the entire universe — is regarded as revealing the all-pervading nature of the Supreme Lord who later descends as Rama, Krishna, and other avataras.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Vishnu's Supremacy in the Veda
Though Indra and Agni receive more hymns numerically, Vaishnavas argue that Vishnu's role is qualitatively supreme. He is the friend and ally of Indra (e.g., 6.69), the one who makes Indra's victories possible, and the lord of the highest heaven (*paramam padam*) to which all beings aspire.

### Sri Vaishnava Reading
Ramanuja and the Sri Vaishnava commentators interpret Vedic passages about "the supreme abode" and "the highest light" as references to Vaikuntha and Vishnu's transcendent form. The Purusha Sukta (10.90) is read as describing Vishnu-Narayana as the cosmic person from whom all creation emanates.

## Theological Emphases

- **Trivikrama**: Vishnu's three strides symbolize his pervasion of earth, atmosphere, and heaven
- **Paramam Padam**: Vishnu's "highest step" or supreme abode, the goal of all spiritual seekers
- **Purusha Sukta**: The cosmic sacrifice of the Purusha is identified with Vishnu-Narayana
- **Vedic Roots of Avatara**: Vishnu's Vedic deeds prefigure the later doctrine of divine descent
