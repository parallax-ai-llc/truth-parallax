## Overview

The Rig Veda is the oldest and most revered of the four Vedas, composed in archaic Sanskrit between approximately 1500 and 1200 BCE. It consists of 1,028 hymns (*suktas*) organized into ten books called Mandalas, totaling over 10,600 verses. These hymns are primarily addressed to deities such as Agni (fire), Indra (king of gods), Soma (the sacred plant), Varuna (cosmic order), and the Ashvins (divine twins), and they constitute the earliest literary monument of the Indo-European language family.

## Structure

The Rig Veda's ten Mandalas are traditionally organized as follows:

1. **Mandalas 2-7** (the "Family Books"): The oldest core, each attributed to a specific priestly family (*rishi* lineage)
2. **Mandala 1 and 10**: Later compositions framing the collection, containing some of the most philosophically significant hymns
3. **Mandala 8**: Associated with the Kanva family, with stylistic affinities to the Avesta
4. **Mandala 9**: Entirely devoted to Soma Pavamana (the purified Soma)

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Rig Veda occupies a foundational position across all Hindu traditions and in broader scholarship:

- **Traditional**: The eternal, uncreated sound (*shabda*) revealed to the ancient seers (*rishis*)
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The source of *shruti* authority, pointing to the non-dual Brahman behind the many deities
- **Vaishnavism**: Contains early hymns to Vishnu, including the celebrated "three strides" (*trivikrama*) motif
- **Shaivism**: Rudra hymns (especially 1.114 and 2.33) are regarded as the earliest Shaiva scriptures
- **Academic**: The primary source for reconstructing early Indo-Aryan religion, language, and society
- **Comparative**: Studied alongside the Avesta, Homeric hymns, and other Indo-European sacred literature

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Rta | Cosmic order and truth that sustains the universe |
| Yajna | The centrality of sacrificial ritual in maintaining cosmic harmony |
| Deva-stuti | Praise of the gods through poetic invocation |
| Cosmogony | Origin hymns, especially the Nasadiya Sukta (10.129) and Purusha Sukta (10.90) |
| Soma | The sacred plant and its role in ritual and visionary experience |
