The two birds parable (2.2.1) is, for Shankara, a perfect illustration of the distinction between the *jiva* (individual soul identified with the body-mind) and the *atman/Brahman* (the unaffected witness). The eating bird is the *jiva* experiencing the fruits of karma; the watching bird is the *sakshi* (witness) that is always free. Liberation is realizing that one was always the witness, never the eater — the sense of being bound was itself part of the illusion.

The teaching that one who sees multiplicity "goes from death to death" (2.2.10) is the cornerstone of Advaita: seeing many where there is only one is ignorance. Shankara emphasizes that this does not mean the empirical world of many objects does not exist conventionally but that from the ultimate standpoint, only Brahman is real, and the apparent multiplicity is a superimposition.
