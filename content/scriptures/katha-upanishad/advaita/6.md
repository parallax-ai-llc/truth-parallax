Shankara reads Chapter 6's cosmic tree as a vivid metaphor for samsara — the beginningless cycle of existence rooted in Brahman but extending through ignorance into the material world. The instruction to cut the tree with the "weapon of detachment" points to *vairagya* — the renunciation that is born from discriminative knowledge. When one knows the self as Brahman, attachment to the tree of samsara naturally dissolves.

The Upanishad's conclusion declares that Nachiketa, "having gained this knowledge from Yama, and the entire discipline of yoga, attained Brahman" (2.3.18). Shankara reads this as confirming that liberation is the direct knowledge (*aparoksha jnana*) of one's identity with Brahman. Yoga and discipline prepare the mind, but knowledge alone liberates. The final verse's promise that "anyone who knows this supreme secret" attains the same result universalizes the teaching beyond Nachiketa's individual story.
