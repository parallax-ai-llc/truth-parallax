The razor's edge metaphor is central to Shankara's teaching on the difficulty of self-knowledge. The path is razor-sharp not because of external dangers but because of the extreme subtlety of the distinction between the self (pure awareness) and the non-self (body, senses, mind, intellect). Mistaking any aspect of the non-self for the self is the fundamental error (*avidya*) that binds.

Shankara gives extensive attention to the hierarchy of 1.3.10-11: the progression from objects to senses to mind to intellect to the great self (*mahat*) to the unmanifest (*avyakta*) to the Purusha. He interprets this as an ascending negation — each higher level negates the previous as the self — until one arrives at the Purusha, beyond which there is nothing. "Beyond the Purusha there is nothing; that is the goal, that is the highest state" (1.3.11). For Shankara, this Purusha is Brahman.
