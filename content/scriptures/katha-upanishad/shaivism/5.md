The two birds parable (2.2.1) — one eating and one watching — receives a distinctive interpretation in Shaiva thought. Rather than two permanently distinct entities (as in Dvaita), the Shaiva reading sees them as two aspects of one consciousness: the aspect that is involved in experience (*sakala*) and the aspect that witnesses without involvement (*nishkala*). Liberation is the recognition that the watcher and the eater are both Shiva.

The teaching that the self pervades everything while remaining subtler than the subtlest (2.2.8-9) expresses the Shaiva paradox of omnipresence and transcendence. Consciousness is the substance of every experience yet cannot be reduced to any particular experience. One who sees only multiplicity "goes from death to death" (2.2.10) because they miss the single consciousness manifesting as the many.
