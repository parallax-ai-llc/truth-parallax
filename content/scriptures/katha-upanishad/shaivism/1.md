The Shaiva reading of Chapter 1 sees Nachiketa's journey to Yama's abode as a metaphor for consciousness confronting its own apparent limitation. Death represents the ultimate boundary of the empirical self, and Nachiketa's willingness to face it directly embodies the Shaiva ideal of fearless inquiry into one's own nature. His three nights of waiting — traditionally interpreted as representing the three states of waking, dreaming, and deep sleep — prepare him for the revelation of the fourth state (*turiya*).

Nachiketa's rejection of worldly pleasures offered by Yama parallels the Shaiva aspirant's disidentification from the contracted states of consciousness. The pleasures represent the attractive but binding aspects of *maya* — Shiva's own power of self-concealment. By refusing them, Nachiketa demonstrates readiness for the direct recognition of his nature as pure, unlimited consciousness.
