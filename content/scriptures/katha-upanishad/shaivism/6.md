The eternal *ashvattha* tree of Chapter 6 is interpreted in Shaiva thought as Shakti's creative unfolding — the dynamic manifestation of consciousness in progressively denser forms. Its roots above represent Shiva (the transcendent source), and its branches below represent the multiplicity of phenomenal existence. Cutting the tree does not mean destroying creation but seeing through its apparent independence to its source in consciousness.

The Upanishad's conclusion — that Nachiketa attained Brahman through Yama's teaching — is read by Shaivas as the triumph of awareness over its own self-imposed limitations. Death (Yama) is the ultimate boundary of the finite self, and transcending death means recognizing that consciousness was never truly limited. The closing declaration that "anyone who knows this" can attain the same realization emphasizes the universal accessibility of the Shaiva path of recognition.
