Chapter 2's distinction between the good (*shreyas*) and the pleasant (*preyas*) is read by Shaiva interpreters as the distinction between the expansive recognition of one's true nature and the contractive pursuit of limited gratification. The pleasant binds because it reinforces identification with the finite self; the good liberates because it points toward the infinite consciousness that one already is.

The verse declaring that the self cannot be attained through learning, intellect, or instruction (1.2.23) resonates deeply with the Shaiva emphasis on direct recognition (*pratyabhijna*) over conceptual understanding. Knowledge of Shiva cannot be manufactured by the mind — it arises spontaneously when the contractions of limited consciousness relax. The self "reveals itself to the one it chooses" is read as consciousness recognizing itself through an act of grace (*anugraha*) that cannot be forced.
