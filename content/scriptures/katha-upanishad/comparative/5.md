The two birds parable — one eating, one watching — has been compared with the Christian concept of the soul's relationship to God: the active soul engaged in worldly experience and the divine witness (the Holy Spirit, or the "ground of the soul" in Eckhart's mysticism) silently present. The Buddhist doctrine of Buddha-nature likewise posits an awakened awareness present within every being alongside the deluded, grasping mind.

The Upanishad's teaching that seeing only multiplicity leads to death resonates with the mystical traditions' emphasis on unity. The Sufi concept of *tawhid* (divine unity) perceived beneath the multiplicity of creation, the Jewish mystical teaching that God's unity underlies the fragmented world of the *kelipot* (husks), and the Buddhist *pratityasamutpada* (interdependent origination) — all insist that surface diversity conceals a deeper unity, and that seeing only the surface perpetuates suffering.
