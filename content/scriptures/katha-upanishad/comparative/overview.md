## Overview

The Katha Upanishad's narrative of a youth journeying to the realm of death to gain secret knowledge resonates with descent myths across world cultures. Nachiketa's encounter with Yama parallels Odysseus's visit to Hades, Orpheus's descent for Eurydice, Gilgamesh's quest for immortality, and Dante's journey through the afterlife — each involving a living person confronting death and returning transformed.

## Comparative Themes

The chariot analogy (Chapter 3) invites direct comparison with Plato's chariot of the soul in the Phaedrus. Both use the image of a charioteer (reason/intellect) guiding horses (passions/senses) to convey a psychology of self-mastery. The Katha Upanishad's teaching that the self is "not born, does not die" parallels both the Platonic doctrine of the soul's immortality and the Buddhist rejection of annihilationism, though it differs from the Buddhist denial of a permanent self (*anatta*).
