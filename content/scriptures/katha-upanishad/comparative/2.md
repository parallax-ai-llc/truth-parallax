Yama's teaching on the pleasant versus the good resonates with ethical traditions worldwide. Aristotle's distinction between pleasure (*hedone*) and happiness (*eudaimonia*), the Buddhist middle way between asceticism and indulgence, and the Stoic distinction between apparent and genuine goods all address the same fundamental insight: what feels immediately gratifying may not lead to lasting fulfillment.

The Katha Upanishad's claim that the self "reveals itself to the one it chooses" introduces a grace-element that transforms the ethical choice into a theological one. In Christian theology, Augustine similarly argued that the will cannot choose the good without divine grace. In Sufi thought, the distinction between *nafs* (ego-desire) and *qalb* (heart-knowledge) mirrors the *preyas/shreyas* distinction, with the heart's turning toward God dependent on divine attraction (*jadhba*).
