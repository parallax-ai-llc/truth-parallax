Nachiketa's journey to the realm of death belongs to a universal category of mythic narrative: the descent to the underworld (*katabasis*). Odysseus consults the dead in Hades (Odyssey 11), Gilgamesh seeks immortality from Utnapishtim beyond the waters of death, Orpheus descends for Eurydice, and Dante traverses Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise. In each case, confrontation with death yields knowledge unavailable to ordinary consciousness.

What distinguishes the Katha Upanishad is that Nachiketa does not seek to rescue someone or gain a specific boon but to understand the nature of death itself. His question — "Does the self exist after death?" — is the most fundamental philosophical question the descent myth can pose. The answer he receives — that the self was never born and therefore cannot die — transcends the narrative framework entirely, moving from myth to metaphysics.
