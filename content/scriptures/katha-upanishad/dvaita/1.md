Madhva's reading of Chapter 1 emphasizes Nachiketa's exemplary devotion and moral courage. His willingness to go to death rather than disobey his father demonstrates the Dvaita virtue of absolute faithfulness to dharma. That Yama — the god of death and cosmic judge — becomes Nachiketa's teacher illustrates the Dvaita principle that genuine seekers are guided by divinely appointed authorities.

The Dvaita reading highlights Nachiketa's rejection of worldly boons (1.1.23-29) as demonstrating the distinction between *nitya* (eternal) and *anitya* (temporary) goods. No amount of wealth, power, or longevity can substitute for knowledge of the Supreme. Madhva reads this as establishing the absolute priority of God-knowledge over all other pursuits — a priority grounded not in world-negation but in the recognition that only Vishnu can satisfy the soul's deepest longing.
