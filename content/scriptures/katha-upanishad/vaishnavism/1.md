The Katha Upanishad opens with young Nachiketa being sent to the abode of death by his father during a sacrifice. After waiting three nights at Yama's door, Nachiketa is offered three boons. His third and most significant request — to know what happens after death — demonstrates the Vaishnava ideal of prioritizing eternal knowledge over temporal gain. Nachiketa rejects Yama's lavish offers of wealth, power, and sensory pleasure, embodying the *vairagya* (detachment) that qualifies a soul for divine revelation.

Vaishnavas read Nachiketa's steadfastness as the devotee's single-minded focus on the Supreme Lord. Yama's testing — offering every conceivable worldly enjoyment — mirrors the material world's constant temptations. Nachiketa's refusal (1.1.23-25) models the surrender required for receiving transcendent knowledge. That even the god of death must serve as a teacher of the self's immortality demonstrates Vishnu's supremacy over all cosmic powers, including death itself.
