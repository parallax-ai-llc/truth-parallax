The cosmic tree in Chapter 6 (2.3.1) has been studied as part of a cross-cultural archetype. The inverted tree — rooted above, branching below — appears in Norse mythology (Yggdrasil, though not inverted), Kabbalistic tradition (the Tree of Life), and shamanic cosmologies. Scholars debate whether these parallels reflect diffusion from a common source or independent expressions of a universal symbolic pattern.

The Katha Upanishad's conclusion — affirming that Nachiketa attained Brahman through Yama's teaching — has been analyzed as a literary and theological resolution that synthesizes the narrative and philosophical dimensions of the text. The final verse's universalization ("anyone who knows this") transforms a particular story into a general teaching, a rhetorical move characteristic of Upanishadic literature. Scholars note that this conclusion also serves an institutional purpose, establishing the authority of the teacher-student lineage as the means of transmitting liberating knowledge.
