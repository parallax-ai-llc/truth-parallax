The two birds parable (2.2.1) appears also in Mundaka Upanishad 3.1.1 and Rig Veda 1.164.20, with scholars debating which represents the earliest version. The image has been analyzed as a folk motif adapted for philosophical purposes. The relationship between the two birds — one active, one passive — has been compared with similar dual-principle doctrines in other ancient traditions, including Zoroastrian dualism and Platonic soul theory.

Scholars note that the parable's interpretation varies significantly depending on the commentator's philosophical commitments. For Shankara, the two birds are ultimately one (the eating bird is an appearance within the watching bird). For Ramanuja and Madhva, they are genuinely two. This interpretive divergence illustrates how the same Upanishadic image can support radically different metaphysical systems.
