The razor's edge metaphor (1.3.14) has entered popular culture and comparative literature — Somerset Maugham used it as a novel title. Scholars note its effectiveness as a pedagogical image: it conveys both the difficulty and the precision required for spiritual realization. The metaphor appears in later Buddhist texts as well, suggesting widespread currency in Indian religious discourse.

The cosmological hierarchy of 1.3.10-11 has been extensively analyzed as proto-Samkhya. The ascending series — objects, senses, mind, intellect, *mahat*, *avyakta*, Purusha — maps loosely onto the Samkhya categories, though the Upanishad adds a theistic Purusha at the summit that is absent from classical Samkhya. Scholars debate whether this represents an early stage of Samkhya development or an independent philosophical framework that later influenced Samkhya.
