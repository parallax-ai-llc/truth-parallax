Scholars trace the Nachiketa story to the Taittiriya Brahmana (3.11.8), where it appears as a brief ritual narrative about a boy sent to Yama and returning with knowledge of the fire sacrifice (*Nachiketa agni*). The Katha Upanishad transforms this simple ritual story into an elaborate philosophical dialogue, exemplifying the broader Upanishadic tendency to reinterpret Vedic ritual material in contemplative terms.

The three-night wait at Yama's door has been analyzed as a literary device creating dramatic tension while also reflecting the Vedic symbolic significance of the number three. Yama's role as both death-god and teacher of immortality embodies a paradox that scholars connect with broader Indo-European mythology — the figure who governs death is also the one who possesses knowledge of what transcends it.
