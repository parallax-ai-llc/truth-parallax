The *shreyas/preyas* distinction in Chapter 2 has been compared with Aristotle's distinction between apparent and genuine goods and with Epicurus's classification of desires. Scholars note that this ethical framework places the Katha Upanishad within a broader ancient discourse on the good life that extended across Eurasian cultures during the "axial age" (800-200 BCE).

Verse 1.2.18 — "The self is not born, does not die" — is nearly identical to Bhagavad Gita 2.20, leading scholars to debate the direction of borrowing. Most scholars believe the Katha Upanishad is earlier and that the Gita drew upon it, though some argue for a common source. The verse on the self "choosing" to reveal itself (1.2.23) has been analyzed as reflecting a shift from the earlier Upanishadic model of self-effort to a grace-oriented theology.
