## Overview

The Katha Upanishad belongs to the Krishna Yajur Veda and is one of the most widely studied Upanishads. It presents a dramatic dialogue between the young seeker Nachiketa and Yama, the god of death, who reveals the secret of the immortal self. The text combines narrative power with philosophical depth, addressing the nature of the self, the distinction between the pleasant and the good, and the path to liberation beyond death.

## Structure

The Katha Upanishad's six chapters (*vallis*) are traditionally grouped into two sections:

1. **Adhyaya 1 (Chapters 1-3)**: Nachiketa's encounter with Yama, the choice between pleasure and wisdom, and the teaching on the self beyond the senses
2. **Adhyaya 2 (Chapters 4-6)**: The chariot analogy, the inner self, and the eternal tree of existence

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: Yama's teaching reveals the Supreme Lord dwelling in the heart
- **Shaivism**: The self beyond death is Shiva, pure consciousness
- **Advaita Vedanta**: A primary source for the identity of atman and Brahman
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Demonstrates God's supreme sovereignty even over death
- **Academic**: One of the earliest Upanishads to systematically address the afterlife
- **Comparative**: Nachiketa's journey to the underworld parallels descent myths worldwide

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Death and Immortality | What survives death and how to attain the deathless |
| The Pleasant vs. the Good | Choosing wisdom over pleasure |
| The Self Beyond the Senses | Atman cannot be perceived through ordinary faculties |
| The Chariot Analogy | Body, senses, mind, and intellect as the chariot's components |
| The Inner Ruler | Brahman dwelling in the heart as the thumbsized person |
