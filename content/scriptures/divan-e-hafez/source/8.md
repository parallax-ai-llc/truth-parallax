## Ghazals on the Preacher and the Drunkard

**1.** The preacher stood at the pulpit and spoke of sin. But when the assembly dispersed, he went out by the back door to the tavern.

**2.** O preacher, thou who tellest others of the straight path—what crooked lane didst thou follow home last night?

**3.** I am not ashamed of my love, for it is honest. But those who are ashamed of nothing commit every shame in secret.

**4.** The drunkard who confesses his drunkenness is closer to sobriety than the sober man who denies his intoxication.

**5.** They closed the door of the tavern. O Lord, do not let them open the door of hypocrisy!

**6.** If the wine of the tavern is a sin, then what shall we say of the poison of backbiting that is served in the mosque?

**7.** The zephyr carries the scent of the rose whether the thorn approves or not. Love speaks whether the preacher approves or not.

**8.** I saw a holy man who said: "I have given up everything for God." I asked: "What have you given up?" He could not answer, for he had given up nothing but the appearance of things.

**9.** The beggar at the tavern door is richer than the king in the mosque, if the beggar has love and the king has only ritual.

**10.** Do not trust the man who wears the robe of piety on the outside—look at what he wears on the inside.

**11.** A dervish in rags with a heart full of God is worth more than a sultan in silk with a heart full of the world.

**12.** The wine-seller's honesty puts the preacher's righteousness to shame, for the wine-seller says: "This is wine," and the preacher says: "This is water," while serving the same cup.

**13.** God sees the heart, not the garment. He hears the intention, not the words. Dress as you will—it is the heart that He weighs.

**14.** I would rather be an honest sinner than a dishonest saint, for the honest sinner knows his place before God, and the dishonest saint has lost his.

**15.** Hafez, leave the preacher to his pulpit and come to the tavern, where at least the hypocrisy is diluted with wine.
