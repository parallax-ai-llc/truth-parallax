Comparative study of Hafez places him within the global tradition of lyric poetry and love mysticism. His ghazals invite comparison with the Song of Songs, Sappho's fragments, the troubadour *canso*, Dante's *Vita Nuova*, the Japanese *waka*, and the love poetry of Rumi, Kabir, and Mirabai. Each of these traditions negotiates the boundary between human and divine love in distinctive ways, and Hafez's systematic ambiguity represents perhaps the most radical version of this negotiation.

Goethe's *West-östlicher Divan* (1819) marks a watershed in the European reception of Hafez, inaugurating a tradition of comparative appreciation that recognizes in the Persian poet a kindred spirit of universal humanism and spiritual freedom. Emerson, Nietzsche, and García Lorca are among the Western writers who have responded creatively to Hafez, testifying to the cross-cultural power of his poetic vision.
