## Overview

The Divan-e-Hafez is the collected poetry of Khwaja Shams-ud-Din Muhammad Hafez-e Shirazi (c. 1315–1390 CE), universally regarded as the supreme lyric poet of the Persian language. The Divan consists primarily of *ghazals* — short lyric poems of typically seven to twelve couplets — along with a smaller number of *qasidas*, *rubaiyat*, and other verse forms. Hafez's poetry is characterized by a dazzling ambiguity that allows each verse to be read simultaneously as a celebration of earthly love and wine, a mystical allegory of divine intoxication, and a subtle critique of religious hypocrisy.

## Structure

The Divan is traditionally organized alphabetically by the final letter of the *radif* (refrain) or rhyme, though thematic groupings have been proposed by modern editors. This edition presents the Divan in fifteen thematic sections:

1. **Divine Love and Longing** — The soul's passionate desire for the Beloved
2. **Wine and the Tavern** — Celebration of wine as symbol of divine intoxication
3. **The Beloved's Beauty** — Descriptions of the beloved as theophany
4. **Separation and Sorrow** — The anguish of distance from the Beloved
5. **Fate and Fortune** — Reflections on destiny, time, and impermanence
6. **Hypocrisy of the Pious** — Critique of performative religiosity
7. **Union and Ecstasy** — Moments of mystical communion and joy
8. **Garden and Spring** — Nature as mirror of divine beauty
9. **The Path of Love** — Love as spiritual discipline and way of knowing
10. **Night and Solitude** — Darkness as the setting for intimate encounter
11. **Renunciation and Poverty** — Spiritual freedom through detachment
12. **The Saqi and the Cup** — The cupbearer as divine grace personified
13. **Hair and Mole of the Beloved** — The details of beauty as spiritual symbols
14. **Death, Eternity, and Remembrance** — Mortality and the promise of enduring love
15. **Shiraz and the Poet's World** — The poet's beloved city as earthly paradise

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Sufi**: The supreme poetic expression of mystical love, consulted as an oracle (*fal-e Hafez*)
- **Sunni**: A pinnacle of Islamic literary culture studied for its language, imagery, and wisdom
- **Shia**: Iran's most beloved poet, whose tomb in Shiraz is a national pilgrimage site
- **Academic**: An inexhaustible subject of literary, linguistic, and hermeneutical study
- **Comparative**: A touchstone for the comparative study of love poetry, mysticism, and lyric form
- **Interfaith**: A voice of spiritual universalism that speaks across religious boundaries

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Divine Ambiguity | The deliberate conflation of sacred and profane love |
| Wine (*may*) | Wine as symbol of mystical intoxication and divine grace |
| The Beloved (*yar*) | The object of desire as simultaneously human and divine |
| Rendi | The libertine or rogue as the authentic spiritual seeker |
| Anti-hypocrisy | Critique of performative piety and religious pretension |
