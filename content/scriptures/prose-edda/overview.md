## Overview

The Prose Edda (also known as the Younger Edda or Snorra Edda) was composed around 1220 CE by the Icelandic scholar, poet, and chieftain Snorri Sturluson. It is the most systematic and comprehensive medieval account of Norse mythology and the art of skaldic poetry. Snorri wrote the Prose Edda as a handbook for poets, providing the mythological knowledge and technical skills necessary for composing and understanding traditional Icelandic verse. In doing so, he preserved an extraordinary wealth of mythological narrative that might otherwise have been lost.

The Prose Edda is both a literary masterpiece and an invaluable historical document. Snorri's retelling of Norse myths — from the creation of the world to the twilight of the gods at Ragnarök — is informed by his deep knowledge of skaldic poetry and the Poetic Edda, but shaped by his own Christian worldview and literary artistry. The tension between pre-Christian content and post-Christian framing makes the Prose Edda a fascinating document of cultural negotiation and literary transformation.

## Structure

The Prose Edda consists of four parts:

| Part | Title | Content |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Prologue | A euhemeristic introduction tracing the Norse gods to human kings from Troy |
| 2 | Gylfaginning | A systematic retelling of Norse mythology through the frame narrative of King Gylfi's visit to Asgard |
| 3 | Skáldskaparmál | A treatise on poetic diction, explaining kennings and other poetic devices through mythological stories |
| 4 | Háttatal | A technical poem demonstrating 102 verse forms with commentary |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: The primary systematic source for Norse mythology, essential for Old Norse studies
- **Comparative**: Enables comparison of Norse cosmology with other Indo-European and world mythologies
- **Philosophical**: Raises questions about mythology, literary tradition, and cultural transmission
- **Literary**: A masterwork of medieval prose and a foundational text for poetic theory
- **Archaeological**: Provides narrative context for interpreting Viking Age material culture
- **Interfaith**: Demonstrates medieval Christian engagement with pre-Christian religious traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mythology | Systematic preservation of Norse cosmological and theological narratives |
| Poetics | The art and technique of skaldic verse composition |
| Kennings | Metaphorical compounds that encode mythological knowledge |
| Cultural Transmission | The preservation of pre-Christian traditions in a Christian literary context |
| Cosmology | The creation, structure, and fate of the Norse universe |
| Euhemerism | The interpretation of gods as deified historical figures |
