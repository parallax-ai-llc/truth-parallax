## Overview

2 Enoch (also known as the Slavonic Apocalypse of Enoch or the Book of the Secrets of Enoch) is a Jewish pseudepigraphical text preserved only in Old Church Slavonic manuscripts. The work recounts the patriarch Enoch's ascent through the seven heavens, his reception of divine secrets about creation and cosmology, his transformation into an angelic being, and his return to earth to transmit this knowledge before his final translation to heaven. The text concludes with a remarkable appendix about the miraculous birth of Melchizedek.

Scholars debate the date and provenance of 2 Enoch, with proposals ranging from the first century CE to the medieval period. The most widely accepted view places its composition in first-century Alexandria or Palestine, with subsequent Christian interpolation and Slavonic transmission. The text exists in two recensions — a longer and a shorter — and their relationship remains a contested question.

2 Enoch significantly expands the Enochic tradition known from 1 Enoch, offering a distinctive cosmology with seven heavens, a creation narrative told from God's own perspective, and extensive ethical instruction. The Melchizedek appendix has attracted particular scholarly attention for its connection to the Melchizedek traditions found at Qumran and in the Epistle to the Hebrews.

## Structure

2 Enoch is structured in three main sections across its 23 chapters:

1. **Enoch's Heavenly Ascent** (Chapters 1-10): Enoch's journey through the seven heavens, encountering angels, celestial phenomena, and increasingly exalted divine realms
2. **God's Revelation to Enoch** (Chapters 11-17): God's first-person account of creation, cosmic secrets, and the structure of reality
3. **Enoch's Return and Instruction** (Chapters 18-22): Enoch's descent to earth, his ethical instruction to his children, and his final translation
4. **The Melchizedek Appendix** (Chapter 23): The miraculous birth and priestly investiture of Melchizedek

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: A key text for the study of Second Temple Judaism, Enochic traditions, and the transmission of Jewish texts through Slavonic Christianity
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels with heavenly ascent traditions in Merkabah mysticism, Islamic mi'raj literature, and Zoroastrian cosmology
- **Protestant**: Studied for its illumination of New Testament backgrounds, especially Melchizedek typology and cosmological assumptions
- **Catholic**: Valued as a witness to Second Temple Jewish theology and the development of angelological and cosmological traditions
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Preserved within the Slavonic manuscript tradition; significant for understanding the transmission of Jewish pseudepigrapha in Orthodox Christianity
- **Interfaith**: A bridge text connecting Jewish Enochic traditions, Christian Melchizedek theology, and broader mystical ascent literature

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Seven Heavens | A detailed cosmology of seven celestial realms, each with distinct inhabitants and functions |
| Heavenly Ascent | Enoch's guided journey through the cosmos, receiving divine knowledge at each level |
| Creation from God's Perspective | A unique first-person divine account of the creation of the world |
| Ethical Instruction | Enoch's moral teachings to his children before his final translation |
| Melchizedek | The miraculous birth and priestly investiture of the mysterious figure Melchizedek |
| Angelic Orders | Detailed descriptions of the hierarchies and functions of the angelic hosts |
