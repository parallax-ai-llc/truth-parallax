## Comparative Reading

The placement of a region of punishment within the same heaven as paradise is a distinctive feature of 2 Enoch that invites extensive cross-traditional comparison. This juxtaposition of reward and torment in close spatial proximity has parallels in the Lukan parable of Lazarus and the rich man (Luke 16:19-31), where the blessed and the damned can see each other across an unbridgeable chasm. The structural principle, that the awareness of proximity intensifies both bliss and suffering, appears independently in the Buddhist *Peta Vatthu*, where hungry ghosts can perceive but not access the merit of others.

The specific tortures described in 2 Enoch 5, including fire, ice, and darkness, correspond to a remarkably stable cross-cultural inventory of postmortem punishments. The Zoroastrian *Arda Viraz Namag* catalogs nearly identical torments (molten metal, freezing cold, consuming darkness) applied to specific categories of sinners. The Egyptian *Book of the Dead* similarly threatens the unworthy with fire-pits and dismemberment. Mircea Eliade noted in *Death and Afterlife* that the combination of fire and ice as complementary punishments appears to be a virtually universal motif, reflecting the human experience of extreme temperatures as the primary physical metaphors for suffering.

Joseph Campbell observed that underworld and punishment topographies tend to mirror the moral taxonomy of the culture that produces them. In 2 Enoch, the punished are those who dishonored God and practiced idolatry, reflecting the priorities of Second Temple Judaism. By contrast, the Greek *Nekuia* in Homer's *Odyssey* Book 11 punishes those who offended the gods through hubris, while the Buddhist *Naraka* traditions in the *Abhidharmakosha* organize punishments around the moral categories of greed, hatred, and delusion. The convergence lies in the structural principle of cosmic moral accounting; the divergence lies in what each culture considers the most grievous transgression.
