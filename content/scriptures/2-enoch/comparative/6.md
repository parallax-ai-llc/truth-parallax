## Comparative Reading

The fourth heaven devoted to the movements of the sun and moon in 2 Enoch 6 reflects a cosmological preoccupation shared across the ancient world. The detailed description of solar and lunar circuits, including chariots, wings, and angelic attendants, parallels the elaborate solar mythology of multiple traditions. The Greek Helios driving his chariot across the sky, the Vedic Surya traversing the heavens in a seven-horsed chariot (*Rig Veda* 1.50), and the Egyptian Re sailing his solar bark through the hours of day and night all share the structural pattern of the sun as a vehicle-borne divine entity requiring active governance.

The calendrical concerns embedded in this chapter, particularly the emphasis on the solar calendar, connect to one of the most consequential disputes in Second Temple Judaism. The *Astronomical Book* of 1 Enoch (chapters 72-82) similarly privileges a 364-day solar calendar, a position associated with the Qumran community against the lunisolar calendar of the Jerusalem Temple. Comparatively, the tension between solar and lunar calendrical systems appears in Hindu astronomy (the distinction between *saura* and *chandra* months in the *Surya Siddhanta*), in Mesoamerican dual calendar systems (the solar *haab* and ritual *tzolkin*), and in the tension between solar and lunar reckonings in Islamic and Christian liturgical traditions.

The angelic governance of luminaries reflects what Mircea Eliade identified as the "desacralization" of celestial bodies in monotheistic frameworks. Where Babylonian religion treated Shamash (sun) and Sin (moon) as autonomous deities with their own cults, 2 Enoch subordinates the luminaries to angelic functionaries carrying out divine orders. This same transition is visible in the Quran (41:37: "Do not prostrate to the sun or the moon, but prostrate to God who created them") and represents a cross-cultural pattern in which the emergence of strict monotheism requires the demotion of formerly independent celestial powers to the status of servants or signs.
