## Comparative Reading

The creation of Adam and Eve on Day 6 in 2 Enoch 14 introduces distinctive anthropogonic details absent from Genesis: Adam is formed from seven substances (flesh from earth, blood from dew, eyes from the sun, bones from stone, reason from angelic and cloud mobility, veins and hair from grass, spirit from wind and God's own spirit). This sevenfold composition has no direct biblical precedent but finds structural parallels in Iranian cosmogony, where the *Bundahishn* describes Gayomart (the first man) composed of specific elemental correspondences, and in Greek philosophical anthropology, where Empedocles identified the human body as a mixture of the four elements in specific proportions.

The microcosm-macrocosm principle underlying this passage, where the human body mirrors the cosmos, represents what Joseph Campbell identified as one of the most persistent ideas in the history of religious thought. The Upanishadic equation of *atman* (individual soul) with *Brahman* (cosmic principle) expresses the same structural logic, as does the Daoist concept of the body as a microcosmic replica of heaven and earth elaborated in texts like the *Huangting Jing* (Yellow Court Canon). The *Emerald Tablet* of Hermetic tradition crystallizes this principle in its famous axiom "as above, so below."

The institution of the sabbath on Day 7 engages a cross-cultural pattern of sacred time that Mircea Eliade analyzed extensively in *The Sacred and the Profane*. The concept of a cyclically recurring sacred rest day appears in the Babylonian *shapattum* (a monthly rest on the full moon), which likely provided the etymological ancestor of "sabbath," though the weekly observance appears to be a distinctive Israelite innovation. The Hindu *tithi* system assigns sacred significance to particular lunar days, while the Buddhist *uposatha* observance of rest and reflection on full and new moon days provides an independent parallel to the sabbatical principle. The divergence between 2 Enoch's cosmic grounding of the sabbath (God's own rest modeled for creation) and these other traditions' more pragmatic or astronomical bases reveals different theological justifications for a structurally similar practice.
