## Comparative Reading

The creation of luminaries on Day 4 and sea creatures and birds on Day 5 in 2 Enoch 13 provides a cosmogonic sequence that, while following Genesis 1, introduces distinctive elaborations inviting cross-traditional comparison. The placement of luminaries after the creation of light (Day 1) reflects a theological distinction between primordial light and physical luminaries that the rabbis would develop extensively (*Genesis Rabbah* 3:6) and that parallels the Zoroastrian distinction between *xvarnah* (divine glory/light) and the created luminaries fashioned by Ohrmazd to combat the darkness of Angra Mainyu.

The creation of sea creatures, including great sea monsters, resonates with the widespread mythological tradition of primordial marine beings. The biblical *tanninim* of Genesis 1:21 connect to the Canaanite sea dragon *Litan* (Leviathan), the Mesopotamian Tiamat, the Egyptian serpent Apophis who threatens the solar bark, and the Norse Jormungandr, the world-serpent encircling Midgard. Joseph Campbell traced the "cosmic serpent" motif across cultures in *The Masks of God: Occidental Mythology*, arguing that the sea monster represents the chaos that creation simultaneously subjugates and incorporates. In 2 Enoch, as in Genesis, the sea creatures are created rather than conquered, representing the Priestly source's distinctive "demythologization" of the combat myth.

The creation of birds introduces what Mircea Eliade identified as the "symbolism of flight" connecting earthly and heavenly realms. Birds as mediators between cosmic zones appear in shamanic traditions worldwide: the eagle of Siberian shamanism, the Garuda of Hindu-Buddhist mythology who carries Vishnu, the thunderbird of Native American traditions, and the *simurgh* of Persian mythology. The structural position of birds as Day 5 creatures, created from the waters yet inhabiting the air, makes them liminal beings par excellence, a symbolism exploited in many traditions where bird imagery signifies spiritual transcendence.
