## Comparative Reading

The creation of the firmament and the separation of waters in 2 Enoch 12 engages one of the most widely attested cosmogonic motifs: the division of primordial waters to create habitable space. The *Enuma Elish* provides the closest ancient Near Eastern parallel, where Marduk splits Tiamat (the salt-water sea) to form the upper and lower waters separated by the firmament. The Egyptian cosmogony similarly depicts Shu separating Nut (sky) from Geb (earth), with the celestial ocean above and the terrestrial waters below. This tripartite division of the cosmos into upper waters, solid firmament, and lower waters appears as the default ancient Near Eastern cosmological model.

The emergence of dry land and vegetation on Day 3 connects to creation-from-water narratives found globally. The Hindu *Shatapatha Brahmana* describes Prajapati raising the earth from the cosmic waters as a boar (*Varaha avatar*), while the Iroquois creation narrative features Earth Diver animals retrieving mud from beneath the primordial sea to form the land on Turtle's back. The Polynesian *Kumulipo* creation chant similarly begins with a dark watery chaos from which land progressively emerges. Mircea Eliade catalogued these as examples of the "aquatic cosmogony" pattern, in which water represents the undifferentiated potentiality from which ordered creation proceeds.

The specific mention of vegetation being created according to its "kinds" (species) in 2 Enoch's creation account reflects the taxonomic impulse also visible in Genesis 1. Wendy Doniger has noted that creation narratives frequently embed classification systems, effectively projecting contemporary biological categories back onto the origin of the world. The Chinese *Shanhaijing* (Classic of Mountains and Seas) similarly catalogs the flora and fauna of each region as part of its cosmological geography, while the Aztec *Legend of the Five Suns* assigns different plant species to different cosmic epochs, each governed by a distinct deity.
