## Comparative Reading

God's first-person account of creation in 2 Enoch 11 introduces one of the most distinctive cosmogonic elements in Second Temple literature: the figure of Adoil, a primordial being commanded to "descend and become visible," from whose dissolution the foundations of light emerge. This creation-through-primordial-being pattern has striking parallels in the Hindu *Purusha Sukta* (*Rig Veda* 10.90), where the cosmic being Purusha is dismembered to form the universe, and in the Norse creation narrative where the giant Ymir's body is fashioned into the world by the gods (*Prose Edda*). The Mesopotamian *Enuma Elish* follows a similar pattern with Marduk splitting Tiamat's body to form heaven and earth.

The priority of light in this creation account echoes Genesis 1:3 but introduces a mediating figure (Adoil) absent from the biblical text. Comparatively, the emergence of light as the first creative act appears in the Egyptian Heliopolitan cosmogony (the first light emerging from the primordial mound), in the Maori creation narrative where the separation of Rangi and Papa allows light to enter the world, and in the Hopi emergence narrative where the people ascend from darkness to light through successive worlds. Joseph Campbell treated the "light from darkness" motif as one of the most universal cosmogonic themes, arguing in *The Masks of God* that it reflects the phenomenological priority of visual consciousness in human experience.

The concept of creation *ex nihilo* that 2 Enoch approaches, where God commands non-existent things into being, represents a significant theological development that the *Religionsgeschichtliche Schule* traced to the intersection of Jewish and Hellenistic thought. This stands in contrast to the predominant ancient Near Eastern pattern of creation through ordering pre-existing chaos, visible in the *Enuma Elish* and Egyptian cosmogonies. The Chinese *Daodejing* presents yet another model: emergence from the Dao through progressive differentiation ("The Dao produces one, one produces two, two produces three, three produces the ten thousand things"), which neither creates from nothing nor orders pre-existing material but rather unfolds from an undifferentiated source.
