## Comparative Reading

Enoch's transformation before the divine face, including his anointing with luminous oil, his clothing in garments of glory, and his metamorphosis into an angelic being, represents one of the most theologically significant examples of human divinization (*theosis*) in Second Temple literature. This transformation narrative participates in a widespread cross-cultural pattern that Mircea Eliade termed "ritual deification." The anointing motif connects to ancient Near Eastern royal investiture ceremonies documented in Mesopotamian and Egyptian sources, where the king was ritually transformed into a divine or semi-divine representative through anointing with sacred oils.

The closest structural parallel appears in the Egyptian tradition of the deceased pharaoh's transformation into Osiris through mortuary rituals, as described in the *Pyramid Texts* and *Coffin Texts*. The garments of glory that replace Enoch's earthly clothes mirror the investiture of the dead in the *Book of the Dead*, where the justified soul receives a new spiritual body. In Zoroastrian tradition, the *daena* (the soul's beautiful maiden form) meets the righteous dead, corresponding to Enoch's encounter with a transformed version of himself. The Hindu concept of *moksha* achieved through divine vision (*darshan*), particularly in Vaishnava bhakti traditions where the devotee is transformed by beholding the deity's form, provides another structural analogue.

Wendy Doniger's analysis of "transformation myths" highlights a crucial divergence: in 2 Enoch, the transformation is permanent and involves a complete ontological change from human to angel-like being, whereas in Greek traditions such as the *Odyssey*'s depictions of divine disguise, transformations are typically temporary. The permanence of Enoch's transformation more closely aligns with the Daoist concept of *xian* (immortal transcendents) who undergo irreversible bodily transformation through alchemical and spiritual practices, as described in Ge Hong's *Baopuzi*. Joseph Campbell noted that such permanent transformations represent the ultimate stage of the hero's journey: the "apotheosis" in which the hero transcends the human condition entirely.
