## Comparative Reading

Enoch's call to ascend in 2 Enoch 1 participates in a widespread pattern that Mircea Eliade identified as the *axis mundi* complex: the summons of a human figure to traverse cosmic levels and receive divine knowledge. The angelic visitors who appear to Enoch sleeping on his couch closely parallel the call narratives in 1 Enoch 14, the Ascension of Isaiah 6-11, and the Mandaean accounts of the soul's heavenly journey in the *Ginza Rabba*. Joseph Campbell would classify this as the "call to adventure" stage of the monomyth, where the hero receives supernatural aid before crossing the threshold.

The structural parallels with shamanic ascent traditions are particularly striking. As Andrei Orlov has demonstrated, the two angelic figures who wake Enoch mirror the spirit-helpers of Siberian shamanic journeys documented by Eliade in *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*. The motif of being summoned while sleeping also appears in the Quranic *Isra and Mi'raj* tradition (Surah 17:1), where Muhammad is transported from Mecca to Jerusalem and thence through the heavens, and in the Hindu *Bhagavata Purana* accounts of Dhruva's celestial ascent.

A key divergence emerges in the mode of authorization. In 2 Enoch, the call comes through angelic intermediaries acting on divine command, whereas in Greek traditions such as the *Phaedrus* myth of the soul's chariot, the ascent is driven by the soul's own philosophical eros. Wendy Doniger's work on "implied spider" narratives suggests that the passive versus active modes of ascent reflect different cultural assumptions about human agency in relation to the divine. The Zoroastrian *Arda Viraz Namag* presents an interesting middle case, where Viraz volunteers but requires ritual preparation and divine permission, blending both active and passive elements.
