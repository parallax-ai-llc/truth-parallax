## Comparative Reading

The second heaven as a realm of imprisoned angels in darkness presents one of the most widely attested mythological patterns in world religion: the confinement of rebellious celestial beings. The imprisoned angels of 2 Enoch 3 parallel the Watchers bound in valleys of darkness in 1 Enoch 10:4-6 and the *spirits in prison* of 1 Peter 3:19, but the structural pattern extends far beyond the Jewish-Christian orbit. In Greek mythology, the Titans imprisoned in Tartarus after their revolt against Zeus represent a nearly identical narrative structure, a connection explored by scholars of the *Religionsgeschichtliche Schule* and more recently by Henryk Drawnel.

Mircea Eliade's analysis of "cosmic prisons" in *Patterns in Comparative Religion* suggests that the motif of bound supernatural beings expresses a universal cosmogonic principle: the ordering of the cosmos requires the subjugation of chaotic forces, which remain present but restrained. The Vedic tradition preserves an analogue in the *Asuras* confined beneath the earth after their defeat by the *Devas*, particularly in the *Shatapatha Brahmana*. Norse mythology similarly places Loki in underground bondage until Ragnarok, while the Zoroastrian *Vendidad* describes the confinement of *daevas* in places of darkness.

The weeping of these imprisoned angels, who ask Enoch to pray for them, introduces the theme of intercessory mediation between cosmic realms. This finds parallels in the Mesopotamian tradition where the dead petition the living for offerings, and in the Buddhist concept of *pretas* (hungry ghosts) who benefit from the merit-transfer rituals of the living. The structural inversion, where a human ascends to intercede for fallen celestial beings, inverts the more common pattern (angels interceding for humans) and represents what Wendy Doniger calls a "narrative reversal" that generates theological meaning through structural surprise.
