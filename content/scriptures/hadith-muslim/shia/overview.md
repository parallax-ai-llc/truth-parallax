## Overview

Shia engagement with Sahih Muslim parallels the critical approach applied to al-Bukhari, though Muslim's collection receives particular scrutiny for its treatment of politically sensitive traditions. Shia scholars evaluate Muslim's narrators through their own biographical dictionaries, identifying those considered reliable or unreliable based on their relationship to Ahl al-Bayt and the historical events surrounding the early Muslim community.

## Interpretive Approach

Shia scholars find Sahih Muslim valuable for specific categories of hadith. Traditions that affirm the virtues of Ali ibn Abi Talib, Fatimah, Hasan, and Husayn are cited as corroborating evidence from a Sunni source. The collection's hadiths about the Prophet's final illness, his statements at Ghadir Khumm, and his relationship with his family are subjects of particularly close Shia analysis. At the same time, traditions perceived as elevating certain companions at the expense of Ahl al-Bayt are examined critically for possible bias in transmission.

## Scholarly Contributions

Shia hadith scholars have produced comparative studies examining Sahih Muslim alongside the four principal Shia hadith collections (*al-Kutub al-Arba'a*). These studies highlight areas of convergence — such as shared traditions about worship, ethics, and eschatology — as well as divergences that reflect the historical split between Sunni and Shia communities. This comparative methodology has enriched both traditions' understanding of the hadith corpus.
