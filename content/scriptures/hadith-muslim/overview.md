## Overview

Sahih Muslim is the second of the two most authoritative hadith collections in Sunni Islam, compiled by Imam Muslim ibn al-Hajjaj (815–875 CE). Distinguished by its rigorous methodology and systematic arrangement, the collection is prized for grouping all variant narrations of a single hadith together, making it an invaluable resource for hadith scholars and jurists alike.

## Structure

The collection is organized into thematic books that cover the full spectrum of Islamic belief and practice:

1. **Faith and Its Branches** — The pillars of belief, the nature of iman, and its manifestations
2. **Purification and Prayer** — Ritual cleanliness, the five daily prayers, and congregational worship
3. **Zakat, Fasting, and Hajj** — Charitable obligations, Ramadan fasting, and pilgrimage rites
4. **Business Transactions and Inheritance** — Commercial law, fair dealing, and estate distribution
5. **Marriage, Divorce, and Family Law** — Marital contracts, dissolution, and familial duties
6. **Judicial Matters and Governance** — Legal judgments, leadership, and political ethics
7. **Virtues, Manners, and Remembrance** — Noble character, social etiquette, and dhikr
8. **Paradise, Hell, and the Day of Judgment** — Eschatological descriptions and the afterlife

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Sahih Muslim holds a distinctive place across interpretive communities:

- **Sunni Islam**: Regarded as the second most authentic hadith collection, often preferred for its superior arrangement
- **Shia Islam**: Engaged critically, with attention to narrator reliability and doctrinal content
- **Sufi traditions**: Cherished for hadiths on spiritual virtues, remembrance, and prophetic character
- **Salafi movement**: A foundational textual authority for establishing correct creed and practice
- **Academic scholarship**: Studied for its advanced methodology and contribution to hadith science
