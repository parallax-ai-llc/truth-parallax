The Comparative reading of Sahih Muslim, Chapter 3 (Zakat, Fasting, and Hajj) engages with the obligatory acts of worship that structure the Muslim year and life cycle. Through the lens of inter-religious and inter-school dialogue, this section's 26 traditions are examined with attention to parallels with other religious and legal traditions. These traditions cover the regulations, spiritual significance, and practical details of communal obligations that bind the Muslim community together across time and geography.

Comparative scholars situate these hadiths within a broader framework of religious law, ethical teaching, and textual transmission across cultures. In this chapter, the interplay between specific prophetic directives and broader ethical principles provides rich material for cross-traditional and inter-religious analysis. Each hadith is situated within the larger architecture of Sahih Muslim's thematic organization, where its placement illuminates connections to related traditions elsewhere in the collection.

By reading these traditions alongside parallel materials from other religious and legal traditions, comparative scholars illuminate both the universal dimensions of prophetic ethical teaching and the distinctive features of Islamic hadith methodology. This approach fosters a richer understanding of how different communities have grappled with similar questions of authority, authenticity, and the application of ancient teachings to changing circumstances.
