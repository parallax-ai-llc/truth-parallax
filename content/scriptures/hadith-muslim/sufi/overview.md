## Overview

Sufi readers of Sahih Muslim are drawn especially to its extensive coverage of virtues (*fada'il*), remembrance of God (*dhikr*), and the inner qualities of the Prophet Muhammad. Muslim's systematic collection of traditions about supplication (*du'a*), patience (*sabr*), gratitude (*shukr*), and spiritual excellence (*ihsan*) provides rich material for Sufi spiritual formation and devotional practice.

## Interpretive Approach

The Sufi approach to Sahih Muslim emphasizes the transformative dimensions of prophetic traditions. The Book of Virtues and Manners, for instance, is read not merely as a catalog of ethical instructions but as a guide to the spiritual stations (*maqamat*) through which the seeker progresses on the path to God. Hadiths about the Prophet's night vigils, his weeping in prayer, and his intimate conversations with God are treasured as models for the aspirant's own spiritual life. Sufi masters such as Imam al-Ghazali drew extensively on Muslim's collection in works like *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences).

## Spiritual Themes

Key themes that Sufi interpreters extract from Sahih Muslim include the centrality of intention in all worship, the remembrance of death as a spiritual discipline, the purification of the heart from envy, pride, and worldly attachment, and the vision of God (*ru'yat Allah*) in the hereafter as the supreme aspiration of the believing soul.
