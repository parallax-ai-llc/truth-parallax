## Overview

The Teachings of Silvanus (NHC VII,4) is a wisdom instruction text that stands apart from most Nag Hammadi literature in its relatively orthodox Christian theology and its extensive engagement with Hellenistic philosophy. The text blends Christian exhortation with Stoic and Middle Platonic ethics, urging the reader to guard the soul, cultivate the mind, resist the passions, and seek God through Christ, who is identified as divine Wisdom (Sophia) and the Word (Logos). Unlike many Gnostic texts, the Teachings of Silvanus affirms the goodness of the creator God, does not employ the demiurge myth, and presents a Christology compatible with proto-orthodox theology. The attribution to Silvanus may refer to the companion of Paul and Peter mentioned in the New Testament (1 Peter 5:12; 2 Corinthians 1:19). The text is generally dated to the late second or early third century CE, likely originating in Alexandria.

## Structure

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Guard Your Soul | Opening exhortation to protect the soul from ignorance and the passions |
| 2 | The Dangers of Ignorance | Warning against spiritual blindness and the destructive power of the lower passions |
| 3 | Christ as Divine Wisdom | Identification of Christ with Sophia and Logos, the agent of creation and salvation |
| 4 | The Battle Against Passions | Practical instruction on resisting anger, desire, and other passions through rational self-discipline |
| 5 | The Mind and God | The cultivation of the rational mind as the means of knowing God |
| 6 | Final Exhortation | Concluding encouragement to persevere in the pursuit of wisdom and the care of the soul |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Notable for its inclusion in the Nag Hammadi library despite its relatively orthodox theology, raising questions about the boundaries of "Gnostic" literature
- **Academic**: Central to debates about the classification of Nag Hammadi texts and the diversity of Alexandrian Christianity
- **Comparative**: Offers rich parallels to Stoic, Platonic, and Jewish wisdom literature
- **Christian**: One of the most accessible Nag Hammadi texts for orthodox readers, blending Christian Christology with classical philosophical ethics
- **Jewish**: Engages extensively with the Jewish wisdom tradition (Proverbs, Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach)
- **Interfaith**: Addresses universal ethical concerns about the care of the soul, the cultivation of virtue, and the pursuit of wisdom

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Care of the Soul | The soul requires constant vigilance and cultivation, like a garden or a city under siege |
| Christ as Wisdom | Christ is identified with divine Sophia and Logos — the rational principle of creation and salvation |
| Ethics and Passion | The passions (anger, desire, ignorance) are spiritual enemies to be resisted through reason and discipline |
| The Mind as Path to God | The rational mind, properly cultivated, is the faculty through which the soul knows God |
