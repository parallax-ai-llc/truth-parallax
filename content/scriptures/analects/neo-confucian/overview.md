## Overview

Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Analects, shaped decisively by Zhu Xi (1130–1200) and Wang Yangming (1472–1529), reads the text through a metaphysical framework centered on principle (li), vital energy (qi), and the cultivation of the heart-mind (xin). Zhu Xi's commentary on the Analects, part of his Four Books curriculum, became the standard interpretation for imperial examinations and remained authoritative for centuries across East Asia.

## Interpretive Approach

Where earlier Confucian commentators focused on the practical and social dimensions of the Analects, Neo-Confucian readers sought to uncover the metaphysical foundations underlying Confucius's moral teachings. Zhu Xi interpreted ren as the principle of love inherent in the human heart-mind, and li (ritual) as the external expression of heavenly principle. Wang Yangming later emphasized the unity of knowledge and action, reading the Analects as evidence that moral insight and moral practice are inseparable.

## Scholarly Legacy

The Neo-Confucian reading of the Analects transformed East Asian intellectual culture by establishing the text as the gateway to a comprehensive philosophical system. Its influence extended to Korea (through Yi Hwang and Yi I), Japan (through Hayashi Razan and Itō Jinsai), and Vietnam, making the Neo-Confucian Analects a shared foundation of East Asian civilization.
