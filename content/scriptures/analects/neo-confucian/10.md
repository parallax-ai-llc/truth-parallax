Neo-Confucian reading of Analects 10, the chapter describing Confucius's daily conduct, is interpreted as showing how heavenly principle governs every detail of the sage's life. Zhu Xi's commentary treats each description of the Master's dress, diet, and deportment as an illustration of principle operating in concrete circumstances. The sage does not follow arbitrary rules but responds appropriately to each situation because his heart-mind is perfectly attuned to principle.

This chapter is particularly important for the Neo-Confucian teaching that moral cultivation is not confined to meditation or study but pervades all activities. The investigation of things (gewu) extends to the most mundane aspects of daily life, and the sage's conduct in eating, sleeping, and social interaction demonstrates that principle is present in all things without exception.
