Neo-Confucian interpretation of Analects 5 uses Confucius's character evaluations as illustrations of how heavenly principle manifests differently in different temperaments. Zhu Xi's commentary analyzes each figure's moral achievements and deficiencies in terms of the clarity or turbidity of their qi (vital energy), which either allows principle to shine through or obscures it. This framework enables Neo-Confucian readers to understand moral diversity without abandoning the universality of principle.

The chapter's assessments demonstrate that moral cultivation involves not only the apprehension of principle but the transformation of one's material endowment of qi. Some individuals possess naturally clear qi and achieve virtue easily, while others must exert greater effort to overcome the limitations of their temperamental endowment. This teaching grounds the Neo-Confucian commitment to universal education.
