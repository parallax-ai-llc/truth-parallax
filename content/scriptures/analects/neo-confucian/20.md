Neo-Confucian reading of Analects 20 interprets the concluding chapter as establishing the continuity of the daotong from the sage-kings through Confucius. Zhu Xi reads the words of Yao, Shun, and Tang as expressing the fundamental principles of governance rooted in heavenly mandate, and sees Confucius as the final link in this chain of sages who grasped and transmitted the Way of principle. The chapter thus provides the historical foundation for the Neo-Confucian claim to represent the authentic transmission of moral truth.

The final verse (20.3) on the necessity of understanding fate, ritual, and words is interpreted as a summary of the three dimensions of Neo-Confucian competence: metaphysical understanding (fate as the operation of principle in history), practical cultivation (ritual as the embodiment of principle in conduct), and moral discernment (understanding words as the ability to recognize principle or its absence in human discourse).
