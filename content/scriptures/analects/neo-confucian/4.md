Neo-Confucian reading of Analects 4 finds in this chapter's concentrated teachings on humaneness (ren) the core of Confucian moral metaphysics. Zhu Xi interprets ren as the principle of love and the virtue of the heart-mind, the fundamental principle that generates all particular virtues. The teaching that the humane person "rests in humaneness" (4.2) is read as describing the state of one whose heart-mind is fully aligned with principle, experiencing the natural ease that comes from acting in accordance with one's original nature.

The distinction between the exemplary person who understands rightness and the petty person who understands profit (4.16) is given metaphysical depth in Neo-Confucian commentary. Zhu Xi explains that the junzi follows heavenly principle while the petty person follows selfish human desires, establishing the fundamental Neo-Confucian moral distinction between the public principle of Heaven and the private desires that obscure it.
