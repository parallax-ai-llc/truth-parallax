Neo-Confucian reading of Analects 14 addresses the complex question of how the sage relates to an imperfect world where principle is not fully realized. Zhu Xi's commentary examines each historical figure mentioned in the chapter through the lens of their relationship to principle, assessing whether they maintained moral integrity in adverse circumstances or compromised with the demands of a disordered age.

The chapter's emphasis on self-examination over concern with others' recognition (14.30) is read as a corollary of the Neo-Confucian teaching that principle is internal to the heart-mind. Since moral truth is apprehended through inward reflection rather than external validation, the cultivated person focuses on the investigation of principle within rather than the pursuit of recognition without. This teaching supports the Neo-Confucian practice of daily self-examination and moral accounting.
