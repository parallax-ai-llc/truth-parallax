Neo-Confucian reading of Analects 2 interprets Confucius's teaching on governance by virtue through the metaphysics of principle and vital energy. The ruler who governs by virtue embodies heavenly principle so fully that his moral influence radiates outward like the North Star, ordering the state without coercive effort. Zhu Xi reads this as evidence that political order is ultimately grounded in the metaphysical order of principle, and that the ruler's primary task is the cultivation of his own heart-mind.

Confucius's intellectual autobiography (2.4) receives detailed Neo-Confucian commentary as a description of the stages of moral-metaphysical development. The culmination at seventy, when desire and duty are spontaneously aligned, represents for Zhu Xi the complete actualization of heavenly principle in human conduct, the state in which the distinction between self and principle dissolves entirely.
