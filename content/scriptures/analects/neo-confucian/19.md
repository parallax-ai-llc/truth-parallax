Neo-Confucian interpretation of Analects 19 examines the sayings of Confucius's disciples as evidence for the early transmission of the Way (daotong). Zhu Xi reads the divergent emphases of Zizhang, Zixia, and Ziyou as reflecting different partial apprehensions of the Master's comprehensive teaching. No single disciple fully grasped the entirety of principle as Confucius did, which is why the succession of the Way required further development through Zengzi and Zisi to reach its fullest articulation in Mencius.

Zigong's defense of Confucius's stature (19.23-25) is read by Neo-Confucian commentators as affirming the incommensurability of the sage's realization of principle. Just as the sun and moon cannot be surpassed, the sage's complete apprehension of heavenly principle sets a standard that later learners can approach but never exceed. This passage supports the Neo-Confucian veneration of the sage-kings and Confucius as uniquely authoritative exemplars.
