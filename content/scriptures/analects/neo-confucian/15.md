Neo-Confucian interpretation of Analects 15 gives special attention to the Golden Rule passage (15.24), which Zhu Xi reads as the practical expression of the fundamental principle of humaneness (ren). The capacity to take oneself as a measure for treating others reflects the unity of principle underlying the diversity of human experience. Because all human beings share the same heavenly nature, what is harmful to oneself is necessarily harmful to others.

The chapter's teachings on moral perseverance in adversity are interpreted as the commitment to principle regardless of circumstances. The exemplary person who is "distressed by their own lack of ability" (15.19) is one who measures himself against the standard of heavenly principle rather than against the variable opinions of others. This internal standard provides the Neo-Confucian practitioner with a stable basis for moral effort that is independent of social recognition.
