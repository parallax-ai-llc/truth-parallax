Neo-Confucian interpretation of Analects 11 uses Confucius's evaluations of his disciples to illustrate the relationship between natural endowment (qi) and moral achievement. The classification of disciples by their strengths (11.3) reveals that heavenly principle manifests differently depending on the quality of an individual's material constitution, but that genuine cultivation can overcome natural limitations. Yan Hui's preeminence reflects his exceptionally clear qi, which allowed principle to shine through with minimal obstruction.

The celebrated final passage (11.26) receives extensive Neo-Confucian commentary. Zhu Xi interprets Confucius's endorsement of Zeng Xi's simple aspiration as recognition that the highest realization of principle is found not in ambitious political projects but in the spontaneous harmony between the cultivated heart-mind and the natural world. This reading emphasizes the Neo-Confucian ideal of "quiet sitting" (jingzuo) and effortless accord with principle.
