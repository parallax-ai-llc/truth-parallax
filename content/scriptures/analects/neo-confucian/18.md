Neo-Confucian reading of Analects 18 addresses the relationship between the sage's commitment to principle and the practical reality of political disorder. The recluses who have withdrawn from the world represent one response to the obscuration of principle in public life, but Confucius's insistence on remaining engaged (18.6) reflects the Neo-Confucian conviction that principle must be realized in the world, not merely contemplated in isolation.

Zhu Xi interprets this chapter as demonstrating that the true sage differs from the recluse precisely in his refusal to abandon the project of bringing principle to bear on human affairs. The recluse may preserve his personal purity, but the sage accepts the burden of working within an imperfect world because principle demands actualization in the totality of human relationships and institutions.
