Neo-Confucian interpretation of Analects 13 reads the doctrine of the rectification of names (zhengming) through the lens of the correspondence between principle and language. For Zhu Xi, names are not arbitrary conventions but reflect the principles inherent in things. When names are rectified, human understanding aligns with the structure of reality, enabling appropriate action. The disorder that follows from incorrect naming is ultimately a disorder in the apprehension of principle.

The chapter's teaching on governance through moral example rather than legal coercion (13.6) is grounded in the Neo-Confucian conviction that principle, being the ultimate reality, has greater transformative power than any external mechanism. Laws and punishments address only the symptoms of disorder, while the ruler's moral cultivation addresses its root cause: the obscuration of principle by selfish desire.
