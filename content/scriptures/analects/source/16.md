---
title: "Analects Book 16 — Chi Shih"
scripture: "analects"
chapter: 16
---

**1** The head of the Chi family was going to attack Chuan-yu. Jan Yu and Chi-lu had an interview with Confucius, and said, "Our chief, Chi, is going to commence operations against Chuan-yu." Confucius said, "Chiu, is this not a fault of yours?"

**2** "Now Chuan-yu is in the midst of the territory of Lu; it is an integral part of the state. Why should it be attacked?" Jan Yu said, "Our master wishes it. Neither of us two ministers wishes it." Confucius said, "Chiu, I have heard that he who has charge of a state or a family does not worry that his people are few, but worries that wealth is unevenly distributed."

**3** "For when wealth is evenly distributed, there is no poverty; when there is harmony, there is no scarcity of people; and when there is contentment, there will be no upheavals."

**4** Confucius said, "There are three things which the superior man guards against. In youth, when the physical powers are not yet settled, he guards against lust. When he is strong, and the physical powers are full of vigor, he guards against quarrelsomeness. When he is old, and the animal powers are decayed, he guards against covetousness."

**5** Confucius said, "There are three things of which the superior man stands in awe. He stands in awe of the ordinances of Heaven. He stands in awe of great men. He stands in awe of the words of sages. The mean man does not know the ordinances of Heaven, and consequently does not stand in awe of them. He is disrespectful to great men. He makes sport of the words of sages."

**6** Confucius said, "Those who are born with the possession of knowledge are the highest class of men. Those who learn, and so readily get possession of knowledge, are the next. Those who are dull and stupid, and yet compass the learning, are another class next to these. As to those who are dull and stupid and yet do not learn — they are the lowest of the people."

**7** Confucius said, "There are nine things which the superior man thinks about: In seeing, he thinks about clarity. In hearing, he thinks about distinctness. In his expression, he thinks about warmth. In his demeanor, he thinks about respectfulness. In his speech, he thinks about sincerity. In business, he thinks about reverence. In doubt, he thinks about questioning. In anger, he thinks about the consequences. In the presence of gain, he thinks about righteousness."

**8** Confucius said, "Contemplating good, and pursuing it, as if they could not reach it; contemplating evil, and shrinking from it, as they would from thrusting the hand into boiling water — I have seen such men, as I have heard such words."
