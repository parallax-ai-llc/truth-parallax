---
title: "Analects Book 15 — Duke Ling of Wei"
scripture: "analects"
chapter: 15
---

**1** The Duke Ling of Wei asked Confucius about tactics. Confucius replied, "I have heard all about sacrificial vessels, but I have not learned military matters." On this, he took his departure the next day.

**2** When he was in Chan, their provisions were exhausted, and his followers became so ill that they were unable to rise. Tzu-lu, with evident dissatisfaction, said, "Has the superior man likewise to endure in this way?" The Master said, "The superior man may indeed have to endure want, but the mean man, when he is in want, gives way to unbridled license."

**3** The Master said, "Tzu, you think, I suppose, that I am one who learns many things and keeps them in memory?" Tzu-kung replied, "Yes — but perhaps it is not so?" "No," was the answer; "I seek a unity all pervading."

**4** The Master said, "Yu, those who know virtue are few."

**5** The Master said, "May not Shun be instanced as having governed efficiently without exertion? What did he do? He did nothing but gravely and reverently occupy his royal seat."

**6** Tzu-chang asked how a man should conduct himself, so as to be everywhere appreciated. The Master said, "Let his words be sincere and truthful, and his actions honorable and careful — such conduct may be practised among the rude tribes of the South or the North."

**7** The Master said, "Truly straightforward was the historiographer Yu. When good government prevailed in his state, he was like an arrow. When bad government prevailed, he was like an arrow. A superior man indeed is Chu Po-yu! When good government prevails in his state, he is to be found in office. When bad government prevails, he can roll his principles up, and keep them in his breast."

**8** The Master said, "When a man may be spoken with, not to speak to him is to err in reference to the man. When a man may not be spoken with, to speak to him is to err in reference to our words. The wise err neither in regard to their man nor to their words."

**9** The Master said, "The determined scholar and the man of virtue will not seek to live at the expense of injuring their virtue. They will even sacrifice their lives to preserve their virtue complete."

**10** Tzu-kung asked about the practice of virtue. The Master said, "The mechanic, who wishes to do his work well, must first sharpen his tools. When you are living in any state, take service with the most worthy among its great officers, and make friends of the most virtuous among its scholars."

**11** The Master said, "A man who does not say to himself, 'What shall I think of this? What shall I think of this?' — indeed, I can do nothing with such a person."

**12** The Master said, "What the superior man seeks, is in himself. What the mean man seeks, is in others."

**13** The Master said, "The superior man is dignified, but does not wrangle. He is sociable, but not a partisan."

**14** The Master said, "A superior man cannot be known in little matters, but he may be entrusted with great concerns. A small man may not be entrusted with great concerns, but he may be known in little matters."

**15** The Master said, "What I do not wish men to do to me, I also wish not to do to men."

**16** Tzu-kung asked, saying, "Is there one word which may serve as a rule of practice for all one's life?" The Master said, "Is not Reciprocity such a word? What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others."
