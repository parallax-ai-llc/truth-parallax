---
title: "Analects Book 17 — Yang Huo"
scripture: "analects"
chapter: 17
---

**1** Yang Huo wished to see Confucius, but Confucius would not go to see him. On this, he sent a present of a pig to Confucius. Confucius, having chosen a time when Huo was not at home, went to pay his respects for the gift. He met him, however, on the way.

**2** Huo said to Confucius, "Come, let me speak with you." He then asked, "Can he be called benevolent who keeps his jewel in his bosom, and leaves his country to confusion?" Confucius replied, "No." "Can he be called wise, who is anxious to be engaged in public employment, and yet is constantly losing the opportunity of being so?" Confucius again said, "No." "The days and months are passing away; the years do not wait for us." Confucius said, "Right; I will go into office."

**3** The Master said, "By nature, men are nearly alike; by practice, they get to be wide apart."

**4** The Master said, "Only the very wisest and the very stupidest do not change."

**5** The Master said, "Yu, have you heard the six words to which are attached six becloudings?" Yu replied, "I have not." "Sit down, and I will tell them to you."

**6** "There is the love of being benevolent without the love of learning — the beclouding here leads to a foolish simplicity. There is the love of knowing without the love of learning — the beclouding here leads to dissipation of mind. There is the love of being sincere without the love of learning — the beclouding here leads to an injurious disregard of consequences."

**7** "There is the love of straightforwardness without the love of learning — the beclouding here leads to rudeness. There is the love of boldness without the love of learning — the beclouding here leads to insubordination. There is the love of firmness without the love of learning — the beclouding here leads to extravagant conduct."

**8** The Master said, "The study of the Odes serves to stimulate the mind. It may be used for purposes of self-contemplation. It teaches the art of sociability. It shows how to regulate feelings of resentment. From it you learn the more immediate duty of serving one's father, and the remoter one of serving one's prince."

**9** The Master said to Po-yu, "Do you give yourself to the Chou-nan and the Shao-nan? The man who has not studied these is like one who stands with his face right against a wall."

**10** The Master said, "Does anyone say of me that I merely hoard up my knowledge and am unwilling to teach others? I am not so. I am simply one who is not resentful, not fearful of difficulties, and does not give up before making three corners of a subject clear to the student — leaving only the fourth for the student to turn."

**11** The Master said, "I would prefer not to speak." Tzu-kung said, "If you, Master, do not speak, what shall we, your disciples, have to record?" The Master said, "Does Heaven speak? The four seasons pursue their courses, and all things are continually being produced, but does Heaven say anything?"
