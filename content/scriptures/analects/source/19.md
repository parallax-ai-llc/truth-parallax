---
title: "Analects Book 19 — Tzu Chang"
scripture: "analects"
chapter: 19
---

**1** Tzu-chang said, "The scholar, trained for public duty, seeing threatening danger, is prepared to sacrifice his life. When the opportunity of gain is presented to him, he thinks of righteousness. In sacrificing, his thoughts are reverential. In mourning, his thoughts are about the grief which he should feel. Such a man commands our approbation indeed."

**2** Tzu-chang said, "When a man holds fast to virtue, but without seeking to enlarge it, and believes in right principles, but without firm sincerity, what account can be made of his existence or non-existence?"

**3** The disciples of Tzu-hsia asked Tzu-chang about the principles that should characterize mutual intercourse. Tzu-chang asked, "What does Tzu-hsia say?" They replied, "Tzu-hsia says: 'Associate with those who can advantage you. Put away from you those who cannot do so.'"

**4** Tzu-chang observed, "This is different from what I have learned. The superior man honors the talented and virtuous, and bears with all. He praises the good, and pities the incompetent. Am I possessed of great talents and virtue? — who is there among men whom I will not bear with? Am I devoid of talents and virtue? — men will put me away from them. What have we to do with the putting away of others?"

**5** Tzu-hsia said, "Even in inferior studies and employments there is something worth being looked at; but if it be attempted to carry them out to what is remote, there is a danger of their proving inapplicable. Therefore, the superior man does not practice them."

**6** Tzu-hsia said, "He, who from day to day recognizes what he has not yet, and from month to month does not forget what he has attained to, may be said indeed to love to learn."

**7** Tzu-hsia said, "There are learning extensively, and having a firm and sincere aim; inquiring with earnestness, and reflecting with self-application: virtue is in such a course."

**8** Tzu-hsia said, "The mechanic, who wishes to do his work well, must first sharpen his tools. When you are living in any state, take service with the most worthy among its great officers, and make friends with the most virtuous among its scholars."

**9** Tzu-kung said, "The faults of the superior man are like the eclipses of the sun and moon. He has his faults, and all men see them; he changes again, and all men look up to him."

**10** Tzu-kung said, "What occasion is there for such a question as 'Was not Chung-ni greater than Tzu-kung?' The talents of Confucius cannot be surpassed, just as the heavens cannot be gone up to by the steps of a stair."

**11** "Were our Master in the position of authority, he would establish the people and they would forthwith stand; he would lead them and they would follow; he would make them happy and multitudes would resort to him; he would stimulate them and they would be harmonious. Living, he would be glorious. Dead, he would be mourned. How is it possible for him to be equaled?"
