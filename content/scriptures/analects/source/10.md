---
title: "The Analects Book X"
scripture: "analects"
chapter: 10
---

**1** Confucius, in his village, looked simple and sincere, and as if he were not able to speak.

**2** When he was in the court or the temple, he spoke minutely on every point, but cautiously.

**3** When he was waiting at court, in speaking with the great officers of the lower grade, he spoke freely, but in a straightforward manner; in speaking with those of the higher grade, he did so blandly, but precisely.

**4** When the ruler was present, his manner displayed respectful uneasiness; it was grave, but self-possessed.

**5** When the prince called him to employ him in the reception of a visitor, his countenance appeared to change, and his legs to move forward with difficulty.

**6** He inclined himself to the other officers among whom he stood, moving his left or right arm, as their position required, but keeping the skirts of his robe before and behind evenly adjusted.

**7** He hastened forward, with his arms like the wings of a bird.

**8** When the guest had retired, he would report to the prince, "The visitor is not turning round any more."

**9** When he entered the palace gate, he seemed to bend his body, as if it were not sufficient to admit him.

**10** When he was standing, he did not occupy the middle of the gateway; when he passed in or out, he did not tread upon the threshold.

**11** He did not eat meat which was not cut properly, nor what was served without its proper sauce.

**12** Though his food might be coarse rice and vegetable soup, he would offer a little of it in sacrifice with a grave, respectful air.

**13** If his mat was not straight, he did not sit on it.

**14** When eating, he did not converse. When in bed, he did not speak.

**15** When the villagers were drinking together, upon those who carried staffs going out, he also went out immediately after.

**16** When the villagers were going through their ceremonies to drive away pestilential influences, he put on his court robes and stood on the eastern steps.

**17** When he was sending complimentary inquiries to any one in another State, he bowed twice as he escorted the messenger away on his mission.

**18** Chi K'ang having sent him a present of physic, he bowed and received it, saying, "I do not know it. I dare not taste it."

**19** The stable being burned down, when he was at court, on his return he said, "Has any man been hurt?" He did not ask about the horses.

**20** When the prince sent him a gift of cooked meat, he would adjust his mat, first taste it, and then give it away to others. When the prince sent him a gift of undressed meat, he would have it cooked, and offer it to the spirits of his ancestors. When the prince sent him a living animal, he would keep it alive.

**21** When he was in attendance on the prince and joining in the entertainment, the prince only sacrificed. He was the first to taste the offerings.

**22** When he was ill and the prince came to visit him, he had his head to the east, made his court robes be spread over him, and drew his girdle across them.

**23** When the prince's order called him, without waiting for his carriage to be yoked, he went at once.

**24** When he entered the ancestral temple of the State, he asked about everything.

**25** When any of his friends died, if he had no relations who could be depended on for the necessary offices, he would say, "I will bury him."

**26** When a friend sent him a present, though it might be a carriage and horses, he did not bow.

**27** The only present for which he bowed was that of the flesh of sacrifice.

**28** In bed, he did not lie like a corpse. At home, he did not put on any formal deportment.

**29** When he saw any one in a mourning dress, though it might be an acquaintance, he would change countenance; when he saw any one wearing the cap of full dress, or a blind person, though he might be in his undress, he would salute them in a ceremonious manner.

**30** He would bow forward to the crossbar of his carriage to any one in mourning attire, and in the same way to any one carrying the tables of population.

**31** When he was at an entertainment where there was an abundance of provisions set before him, he would change countenance and rise up.

**32** On a sudden clap of thunder, or a violent wind, he would change countenance.
