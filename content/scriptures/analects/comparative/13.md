Comparative reading of Analects 13 places the doctrine of the rectification of names in dialogue with other traditions' philosophies of language and truth. The conviction that social order depends on the correct use of language parallels the Greek logos tradition, the Hindu understanding of Vac (sacred speech) as the foundation of cosmic order, and the biblical tradition in which God creates through speech. In each case, language is not merely a tool for communication but a constitutive element of reality that must be handled with care and responsibility.

The chapter's emphasis on governance through moral example rather than legal coercion (13.6) invites comparison with the natural law tradition's argument that positive law derives its authority from its conformity to higher moral principles, and with Gandhi's concept of satyagraha, which relies on moral persuasion rather than coercive force. These parallels illuminate the cross-cultural debate between those who ground political order in moral transformation and those who rely on institutional mechanisms.
