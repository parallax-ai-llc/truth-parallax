Comparative interpretation of Analects 6 brings the teaching on substance and refinement (6.18) into dialogue with similar concerns in other traditions about the relationship between natural virtue and cultural formation. Aristotle's distinction between natural virtue and full virtue, which requires practical wisdom acquired through education and experience, offers a close parallel. Both thinkers recognize that raw moral impulse, however genuine, requires cultivation and refinement to become reliable virtue.

Yan Hui's contentment in poverty (6.11) invites comparison with the Christian tradition of voluntary poverty exemplified by Francis of Assisi, the Buddhist ideal of the mendicant monk, and the Stoic teaching on the sufficiency of virtue for happiness. The convergence of multiple traditions on the theme that true well-being is independent of material conditions suggests a widespread human intuition about the nature of genuine flourishing.
