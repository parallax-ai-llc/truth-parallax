Comparative interpretation of Analects 20 places the concluding appeal to the sage-kings in dialogue with other traditions' legitimating narratives. The claim that the current teaching transmits the wisdom of ancient founders parallels the Christian apostolic succession, the Buddhist lineage (parampara) of dharma transmission, the Hindu guru-shishya tradition, and the Islamic chain of transmission (isnad). Each tradition grounds its authority in an unbroken chain of transmission from a foundational source, whether human or divine.

The final verse's three requirements—understanding fate, ritual, and words (20.3)—invite comparison with the threefold structures of wisdom found in other traditions: the Christian theological virtues of faith, hope, and charity; the Buddhist three trainings of morality, concentration, and wisdom; and the Hindu three paths (margas) of knowledge, devotion, and action. These threefold summaries suggest a cross-cultural recognition that the complete moral or spiritual life integrates multiple dimensions of human experience.
