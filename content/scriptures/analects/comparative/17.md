Comparative reading of Analects 17 places Confucius's teaching on the moral functions of poetry (17.9) in dialogue with other traditions' accounts of the relationship between art and moral formation. Plato's discussion of poetry's power to shape character in the Republic, the Hindu rasa theory of aesthetic experience, and the Islamic tradition of nasheed (devotional poetry) as a vehicle for spiritual cultivation all address the same fundamental question: how does aesthetic experience contribute to moral and spiritual development?

The chapter's exploration of the tension between moral principle and practical engagement resonates with the Christian casuistic tradition's navigation between moral absolutes and pastoral realities, the Buddhist concept of upaya (skillful means), and the Jewish distinction between the letter and spirit of the law. These parallels illuminate the universal challenge of maintaining moral integrity while engaging effectively with an imperfect world.
