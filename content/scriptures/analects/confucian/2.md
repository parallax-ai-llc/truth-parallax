Confucian reading of Analects 2 focuses on Confucius's vision of governance through moral example rather than coercion. The chapter's opening image of the ruler as the North Star, remaining in place while all other stars revolve around it (2.1), encapsulates the Confucian ideal of government by virtue (de). This passage has been foundational for Confucian political thought, establishing that legitimate authority rests not on force but on the moral character of the ruler.

The chapter also contains Confucius's famous intellectual autobiography (2.4), tracing his development from setting his heart on learning at fifteen to following his heart's desire without transgressing moral bounds at seventy. Confucian interpreters read this passage as evidence that moral cultivation is a lifelong process requiring sustained effort, and that the highest achievement is the spontaneous alignment of desire with duty.
