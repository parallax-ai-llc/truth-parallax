Confucian interpretation of Analects 3 addresses the proper performance of ritual (li) and its relationship to political legitimacy. Confucius's distress at the Ji family's usurpation of royal ritual prerogatives (3.1) illustrates the Confucian principle that ritual order reflects and sustains the moral order of society. When rituals are performed by those not entitled to them, the entire fabric of social trust is undermined.

The chapter's discussion of the essence of ritual (3.3-3.4) reveals Confucius's conviction that outward ceremony without inner sincerity is empty. His teaching that it is better to be frugal than extravagant, and to grieve genuinely than to observe formalities meticulously, places the Confucian understanding of ritual squarely within the domain of moral authenticity rather than mere external compliance.
