Confucian reading of Analects 14 presents a wide-ranging collection of reflections on historical figures, political ethics, and the challenges of moral life in a disordered age. The chapter addresses the complex question of how the virtuous person should act when the Way does not prevail in the world, with Confucius offering nuanced assessments of those who withdrew from public life and those who continued to serve under imperfect conditions.

The chapter includes the important teaching that the exemplary person is concerned about their own lack of ability rather than about others' failure to recognize them (14.30). This emphasis on self-examination over resentment is characteristic of Confucian moral psychology, which locates the source of moral progress in one's own effort rather than in external circumstances or the behavior of others.
