Confucian reading of Analects 20, the brief concluding chapter, invokes the words of the sage-kings Yao, Shun, and Tang, connecting Confucius's teachings to the moral authority of the ancient founders. The chapter establishes that the Confucian Way is not an innovation but a transmission of the moral wisdom that enabled the sage-kings to bring order and prosperity to the world. This appeal to antiquity has been central to the Confucian claim that its teachings represent not one philosophical perspective among others but the perennial Way of civilization.

The chapter concludes with Confucius's statement that one who does not understand fate cannot be a gentleman, one who does not understand ritual cannot take a stand, and one who does not understand words cannot understand people (20.3). This summary encapsulates three essential dimensions of Confucian moral competence: metaphysical awareness, ritual cultivation, and practical judgment in human affairs.
