Confucian interpretation of Analects 13 develops the theme of governance through the crucial concept of the rectification of names (zhengming). When Zilu asks what Confucius would do first if given political responsibility, the Master replies, "Surely rectify names" (13.3). This teaching establishes the Confucian principle that social and political order depends upon the accurate correspondence between language and reality: when names are not correct, speech does not accord with truth, and affairs cannot be carried out successfully.

The chapter also addresses the relationship between moral example and legal coercion in governance. Confucius teaches that when the ruler is upright, the people will act rightly without being commanded; when the ruler is not upright, commands will not be obeyed (13.6). This emphasis on moral leadership over administrative technique remains central to Confucian political philosophy.
