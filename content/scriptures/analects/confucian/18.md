Confucian reading of Analects 18 presents encounters between Confucius and various recluses and worthies who have withdrawn from public life. The chapter raises the fundamental question of whether the morally serious person should engage with a disordered world or withdraw from it. Confucius's consistent response is that one cannot associate with birds and beasts—the sage must live among human beings and strive to improve the world, however imperfect it may be (18.6).

The chapter's portraits of historical figures who either served faithfully or withdrew honorably illustrate the range of morally acceptable responses to political disorder. Confucian interpreters emphasize that withdrawal is justified only in the most extreme circumstances, and that the default Confucian stance is active engagement in the work of moral and political reform.
