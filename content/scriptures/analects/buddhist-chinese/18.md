Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 18 reads Confucius's encounters with recluses through the Buddhist distinction between the pratyekabuddha (solitary realizer) who withdraws from the world and the bodhisattva who remains engaged for the benefit of others. The recluses embody the path of individual spiritual accomplishment, while Confucius represents the commitment to universal welfare that the Mahayana tradition regards as superior.

Chinese Buddhists found in Confucius's insistence on living among human beings (18.6) a parallel to the bodhisattva's vow not to enter final nirvana until all sentient beings are liberated. While the metaphysical frameworks differ profoundly, both Confucius and the bodhisattva choose engagement over withdrawal, accepting the suffering that comes from living in an imperfect world in order to serve those who need their help.
