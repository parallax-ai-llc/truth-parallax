Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 4 finds in the teachings on humaneness (ren) a close parallel to the Buddhist cultivation of compassion (karuna) and loving-kindness (metta). The teaching that the humane person "rests in humaneness" (4.2) echoes the Buddhist description of the awakened mind that naturally and effortlessly radiates compassion to all beings. Both traditions affirm that the highest moral achievement is not forced benevolence but a spontaneous disposition of care that arises from the transformed heart-mind.

The distinction between the exemplary person and the petty person (4.16) parallels the Buddhist distinction between the person who follows the dharma and the person trapped in worldly concerns. Chinese Buddhists interpreted this passage as evidence that Confucius, like the Buddha, recognized the fundamental human choice between spiritual aspiration and attachment to material gain, even if Confucius did not articulate this choice in terms of samsara and nirvana.
