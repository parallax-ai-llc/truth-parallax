Chinese Buddhist reading of Analects 9 appreciates Confucius's restraint in speaking of ultimate matters as analogous to the Buddha's "noble silence" on metaphysical questions that do not contribute to liberation. The Master's reluctance to discourse on fate and humaneness (9.1) parallels the Buddha's refusal to answer the "unanswerable questions" about the origin of the universe and the nature of the self, recognizing that some truths can only be realized through direct experience rather than verbal instruction.

Confucius's lament about the failure of the Way to prevail (9.9) invites Buddhist reflection on the nature of suffering and impermanence. From a Buddhist perspective, the Master's grief reflects an attachment to a particular vision of how the world should be—an attachment that, while morally admirable, generates the suffering (dukkha) that comes from the gap between aspiration and reality.
