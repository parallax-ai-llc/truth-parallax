Chinese Buddhist reading of Analects 17 engages with Confucius's teaching on the Odes (17.9) through the lens of the Buddhist use of skillful means in teaching. Just as the Buddha employed stories, parables, and imagery to communicate the dharma to audiences of varying capacity, Confucius recognized in poetry a vehicle for moral and emotional education that operates through aesthetic experience rather than direct instruction.

The chapter's exploration of the relationship between moral principle and practical engagement with an imperfect world resonates with the Mahayana Buddhist teaching on the bodhisattva's skillful engagement with samsara. The bodhisattva, like Confucius, cannot simply withdraw from the world of suffering but must find ways to bring wisdom and compassion to bear within the constraints of an imperfect reality.
