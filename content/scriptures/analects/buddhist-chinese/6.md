Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 6 highlights Yan Hui's contentment in poverty (6.11) as exemplifying a detachment from material conditions that resonates deeply with Buddhist teachings on non-attachment. The ability to find joy independently of external circumstances parallels the Buddhist cultivation of equanimity (upekkha) and the teaching that true happiness arises from the quality of one's mind rather than from the possession of material goods.

The teaching on the balance of substance and refinement (6.18) invites Buddhist reflection on the Middle Way between ascetic withdrawal and worldly indulgence. Just as the Buddha rejected both extreme austerity and sensual excess in favor of a balanced path, Confucius teaches that genuine moral achievement requires neither raw uncultivated sincerity nor empty cultural sophistication but a harmonious integration of both.
