Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 10 reads the detailed description of Confucius's daily conduct as illustrating a form of mindfulness practice. The Master's attentiveness to every detail of dress, diet, and deportment parallels the Buddhist cultivation of moment-to-moment awareness (sati/nian) in all activities. The Chan tradition's teaching that "chopping wood and carrying water" are themselves the practice of the Way finds an unexpected echo in the Confucian sage's transformation of mundane activities into expressions of moral awareness.

Chinese Buddhist interpreters noted that Confucius's careful conduct, while oriented toward social propriety rather than liberation from suffering, nevertheless demonstrates the integration of awareness and action that is central to Buddhist practice. The difference lies in the ultimate aim: for Confucius, mindful conduct serves social harmony; for the Buddhist, it serves the realization of the mind's true nature.
