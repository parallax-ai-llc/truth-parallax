Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 14 engages with the chapter's reflections on withdrawal and engagement through the Buddhist concept of the bodhisattva who remains in the world for the benefit of all beings. While some Buddhist practitioners withdraw into monastic seclusion, the Mahayana ideal of the bodhisattva who postpones final liberation to serve others parallels Confucius's insistence on remaining engaged with the imperfect world.

The teaching on self-examination over concern with recognition (14.30) resonates with the Buddhist practice of introspection and the cultivation of non-attachment to praise and blame. The Buddhist practitioner is trained to examine their own mental states with rigorous honesty, just as Confucius directs the exemplary person to look inward rather than outward for the source of moral progress.
