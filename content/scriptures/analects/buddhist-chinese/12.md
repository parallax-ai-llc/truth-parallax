Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 12 engages with the definition of humaneness as "restraining oneself and returning to ritual" (12.1) through the lens of Buddhist moral discipline (sila). The Buddhist practitioner similarly restrains unwholesome impulses through the observance of precepts, not as an end in itself but as a foundation for deeper meditative and wisdom practices. Both traditions understand that moral discipline creates the conditions for the transformation of the heart-mind.

The chapter's teaching on governance through personal rectitude (12.17) parallels the Buddhist understanding that the transformed individual naturally benefits all beings through the quality of their presence. In the Vimalakirti Sutra, the bodhisattva's "pure land" is purified through the purification of his own mind. Similarly, Confucius teaches that the ruler's moral integrity transforms the people without the need for external commands.
