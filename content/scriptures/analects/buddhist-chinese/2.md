Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 2 draws parallels between Confucius's model of governance by virtue and the Buddhist concept of the dharma-king (dharmaraja) who rules through moral authority rather than coercion. The image of the ruler as the North Star (2.1) echoes Buddhist descriptions of the Buddha as the "turner of the wheel of dharma" whose teaching naturally orders the world without the use of force.

Confucius's intellectual autobiography (2.4) invites comparison with the Buddhist stages of spiritual development. The progressive refinement from youthful aspiration through mature wisdom to the spontaneous alignment of desire with moral law parallels the bodhisattva's journey through the stages (bhumis) from initial aspiration to the effortless compassion of full awakening. Chinese Buddhist interpreters found in this passage evidence of a natural affinity between the Confucian sage and the Buddhist ideal of the perfected person.
