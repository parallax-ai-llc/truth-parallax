Chinese Buddhist reading of Analects 13 finds in the doctrine of the rectification of names an echo of the Buddhist emphasis on right speech (samyag-vac). While the Confucian concern with naming focuses on the correspondence between language and social-moral reality, the Buddhist emphasis on right speech addresses the relationship between language and the deeper reality of interdependent arising. Both traditions recognize that careless or dishonest speech creates confusion and suffering.

The chapter's teaching on governance through moral example (13.6) resonates with the Buddhist understanding that the dharma is transmitted not primarily through words but through the teacher's embodied realization. When Confucius teaches that the upright ruler transforms the people without commands, Chinese Buddhists recognize a parallel to the Chan master whose presence and conduct teach more powerfully than any verbal instruction.
