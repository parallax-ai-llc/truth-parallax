Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Analects 8 engages with the Confucian veneration of the sage-kings through the lens of the Buddhist concept of the cakravartin, the ideal wheel-turning king who rules the world through moral power. Both traditions affirm that legitimate political authority is grounded in moral virtue rather than military force, and that the ideal ruler serves the welfare of the people rather than his own aggrandizement.

Zengzi's teaching on the gentleman's heavy burden (8.7) resonates with the bodhisattva's vow to liberate all sentient beings, a burden far heavier than any worldly responsibility. Chinese Buddhists interpreted this parallel as evidence that the Confucian commitment to moral transformation, while limited in scope to the human social world, shares the same fundamental orientation as the bodhisattva's universal compassion.
