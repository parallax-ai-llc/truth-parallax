Chinese Buddhist reading of Analects 3 engages with the Confucian understanding of ritual (li) through the lens of Buddhist skillful means (upaya). While Buddhism generally regards ritual as provisional and instrumental rather than intrinsically valuable, Chinese Buddhists recognized that Confucian ritual serves a function analogous to Buddhist practice forms: it disciplines the mind, habituates virtuous conduct, and creates a supportive community of practitioners.

The chapter's insistence that ritual must express genuine inner feeling (3.3-3.4) resonates with the Buddhist teaching that practice without right intention is spiritually fruitless. Just as meditation performed mechanically without mindfulness yields no insight, ritual performed without sincerity yields no moral transformation. This parallel allowed Chinese Buddhists to appreciate the Confucian understanding of ritual while maintaining that Buddhist practice addresses a deeper dimension of human suffering.
