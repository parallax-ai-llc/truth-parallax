Academic study of Analects 20 examines the brief concluding chapter as a complex editorial construction that connects Confucius's teachings to the mythological sage-kings of antiquity. Scholars have noted that the chapter's style and content differ markedly from the rest of the Analects, and most regard it as a late addition by editors who sought to ground the Confucian Way in the deepest stratum of Chinese cultural memory. The invocation of Yao, Shun, and Tang serves a legitimating function similar to the genealogies found in other ancient texts.

The final verse (20.3) has been analyzed as a carefully composed summary of the essential qualities of the Confucian gentleman. Some scholars read it as a genuine saying of Confucius that was deliberately placed at the end of the collection, while others regard it as an editorial composition designed to provide a memorable conclusion. Either way, its three requirements—understanding fate, ritual, and words—have been interpreted as encapsulating the intellectual, practical, and hermeneutical dimensions of the Confucian Way.
