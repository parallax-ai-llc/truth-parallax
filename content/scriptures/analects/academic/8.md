Academic study of Analects 8 analyzes the chapter's references to historical figures, particularly the sage-kings, as evidence for the early Confucian construction of an idealized past. Scholars have examined how the Analects' portrayal of Yao, Shun, and other ancient rulers compares with their treatment in other early texts, revealing both the distinctiveness of the Confucian appropriation and the broader cultural significance of these figures in Warring States intellectual life.

The chapter's association with Zengzi (through the prominence of his sayings) has led scholars to attribute it to a stage of compilation influenced by Zengzi's school. This hypothesis is part of the larger effort to reconstruct the redaction history of the Analects, distinguishing earlier from later strata and associating different portions with different branches of the early Confucian movement.
