Academic interpretation of Analects 17 examines the chapter's literary qualities and its evidence for Confucius's engagement with the political realities of his time. The encounter with Yang Huo has been subjected to extensive historical analysis, with scholars debating its authenticity and its implications for understanding Confucius's relationship with the power holders of Lu. The chapter's discussions of nature and nurture (17.2-3) have attracted attention from scholars interested in the early Chinese understanding of human moral psychology.

The teaching on the functions of the Odes (17.9) has been analyzed as foundational for the Chinese literary critical tradition. Scholars of comparative poetics have examined Confucius's account of poetry's moral, social, and cognitive functions alongside Western theories of literature, finding both significant parallels and fundamental differences in the way ancient Chinese and Greek thinkers understood the relationship between aesthetic experience and moral formation.
