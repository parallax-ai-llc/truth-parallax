Academic study of Analects 16 examines the chapter's political teachings and its relationship to other portions of the text. Many scholars regard this chapter as part of a later stratum of the Analects, noting its more systematic and didactic style compared with the dialogue-based earlier chapters. The chapter's concern with the descent of political authority from the Son of Heaven through the nobility to household servants has been analyzed as reflecting the specific political conditions of the late Warring States period rather than the historical Confucius's own time.

The chapter's taxonomic approach to ethics—categorizing pleasures, friendships, and other phenomena into beneficial and harmful types—has been analyzed as reflecting a pedagogical method that became increasingly important in Confucian education. Scholars have compared this method with similar taxonomic approaches in other ancient educational traditions, including Buddhist abhidharma and Aristotelian classification of virtues and vices.
