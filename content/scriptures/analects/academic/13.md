Academic interpretation of Analects 13 focuses on the doctrine of the rectification of names (zhengming, 13.3) as one of the most philosophically significant and debated concepts in the Analects. Scholars of Chinese philosophy have analyzed this doctrine in relation to Western philosophy of language, examining whether Confucius's concern is primarily semantic (the correct use of words), political (the alignment of official titles with actual roles), or moral (the correspondence between moral language and moral reality).

Historians have connected the rectification of names to specific political conflicts in Confucius's time, arguing that it addressed the gap between the nominal hierarchy of the Zhou feudal system and the actual power relations in states like Lu, where ministerial families had usurped ducal authority. This historical context illuminates the practical urgency behind what might otherwise appear as an abstract philosophical doctrine.
