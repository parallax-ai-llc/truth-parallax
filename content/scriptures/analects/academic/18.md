Academic study of Analects 18 examines the chapter's encounters between Confucius and recluses as evidence for the intellectual diversity of the Spring and Autumn period and the existence of hermit traditions that predated and existed alongside early Confucianism. The recluses' critiques of Confucius's political ambitions provide a rare glimpse of alternative philosophical positions that were not fully preserved in the transmitted textual tradition.

Scholars have analyzed the chapter's structure and literary character, noting that its anecdotal form resembles the narrative style of the Zhuangzi more closely than the aphoristic style of most Analects chapters. This stylistic observation has contributed to debates about the dating and compilation of the later portions of the Analects and their relationship to other genres of early Chinese philosophical literature.
