Academic interpretation of Analects 3 analyzes the chapter's evidence for the ritual practices and political conflicts of the Spring and Autumn period. Confucius's critique of the Ji family's ritual transgressions (3.1) provides historians with evidence of the tensions between the ducal house and powerful ministerial families in the state of Lu. Scholars have used these passages to reconstruct the political dynamics of Confucius's homeland and the specific institutional arrangements that he sought to defend.

The chapter's discussions of the relationship between ritual form and inner feeling (3.3-3.4) have attracted attention from scholars of comparative religion and ritual studies. These passages anticipate debates about the nature of ritual that continue in contemporary anthropology and philosophy. Some scholars read Confucius as a ritual reformer who sought to internalize the meaning of ceremonies that had become empty formalities, while others argue that he was defending traditional forms against the corrosive effects of political and social change.
