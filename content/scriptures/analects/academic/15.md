Academic interpretation of Analects 15 has given particular attention to the Golden Rule formulation (15.24) and its place in the history of ethical thought. Scholars have compared this negative formulation ("Do not impose on others what you do not desire yourself") with the positive Golden Rule traditions found in other cultures, debating whether the negative form is weaker, equivalent, or in some respects stronger than positive versions. This passage is central to discussions of the universality of basic moral principles across cultures.

The chapter's emphasis on humaneness, wisdom, and courage as the three virtues of the exemplary person has been analyzed in comparison with other ancient virtue catalogues, including Plato's four cardinal virtues and the Buddhist paramitas. These comparisons have contributed to the growing field of comparative virtue ethics and raised questions about the extent to which different cultures converge on similar accounts of human moral excellence.
