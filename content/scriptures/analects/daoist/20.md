Daoist interpretation of Analects 20 views the concluding appeal to the sage-kings as a final confirmation of the Confucian dependence on historical authority that Daoists reject. The Way does not require the endorsement of ancient rulers; it is eternally present and accessible to anyone who releases attachment to cultural tradition and moral ambition. The sage-kings themselves, insofar as they governed with genuine wisdom, must have acted in accordance with the Dao rather than in accordance with the moral principles that later Confucians attributed to them.

Confucius's final statement on the necessity of understanding fate, ritual, and words (20.3) is reinterpreted by Daoists as pointing, despite itself, toward Daoist truths. Understanding fate means accepting the natural course of things; understanding ritual means recognizing the limits of human convention; understanding words means grasping that language never captures the living reality of the Way.
