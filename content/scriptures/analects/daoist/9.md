Daoist reading of Analects 9 finds in Confucius's restraint and rare utterances on profound topics a hint of the Daoist understanding that the deepest truths resist verbal expression. The Master's reluctance to speak of fate, profit, and humaneness (9.1) parallels the Daodejing's opening insight that "the Way that can be spoken is not the constant Way." Daoist interpreters suggest that Confucius's silence on these matters reflects an intuition that he did not fully develop: that the ultimate ground of moral reality transcends discursive articulation.

Confucius's lament that the phoenix does not arrive (9.9) is read by Daoists as the inevitable frustration of one who seeks to impose moral order on a world that follows its own natural rhythms. The Daoist sage accepts the transformations of things without longing for supernatural confirmation, finding peace in the recognition that the Way operates whether or not human civilization recognizes it.
