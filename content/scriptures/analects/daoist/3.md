Daoist reading of Analects 3 critiques the Confucian preoccupation with ritual propriety as an attachment to artificial forms that obstruct the natural flow of the Way. Confucius's distress at improper ritual performance (3.1) reveals, from a Daoist perspective, the limitations of a moral vision fixated on social hierarchy and ceremonial correctness. The Zhuangzi's sage would respond to such violations with equanimity, recognizing that the rise and fall of social conventions is part of the natural transformation of things.

Yet Daoists appreciate Confucius's teaching that the essence of ritual lies in genuine feeling rather than outward display (3.3-3.4). This acknowledgment that inner authenticity matters more than external form approaches the Daoist conviction that true virtue (de) flows naturally from alignment with the Dao, not from the scrupulous observance of humanly devised ceremonies.
