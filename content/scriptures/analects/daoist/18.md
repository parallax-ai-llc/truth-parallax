Daoist interpretation of Analects 18 reads the recluses encountered by Confucius as exemplary Daoist figures who have achieved the wisdom that Confucius approaches but cannot fully embrace. The madman of Chu who sings to Confucius (18.5) and the farmers who mock the disciples' political ambitions embody the Daoist rejection of moral activism in favor of natural simplicity. Their withdrawal from public life is not moral failure but spiritual accomplishment.

Confucius's reply that one cannot live among birds and beasts (18.6) is the crux of the Confucian-Daoist disagreement. Daoists argue that the distinction between human society and the natural world is itself an artificial construction that prevents the sage from recognizing the continuity of all life within the Dao. The recluse who lives simply and close to nature has not abandoned humanity but has recovered a more authentic mode of human existence.
