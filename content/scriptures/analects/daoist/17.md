Daoist reading of Analects 17 finds in Confucius's encounters with Yang Huo and other figures the characteristic Confucian tension between principle and pragmatism. The analogy of the uneaten gourd (17.7) is read by Daoists as a confession of Confucius's inability to achieve the wu wei state in which the sage is content simply to be, without needing to be useful. The gourd that merely hangs in one place represents, in Daoist symbolism, the emptiness and purposelessness that is the highest spiritual attainment.

In the Zhuangzi, the useless tree that survives because it is good for nothing illustrates the Daoist reversal of Confucian values: it is precisely uselessness and purposelessness that allow for the fullest expression of natural life. Confucius's rejection of this possibility reveals his fundamental attachment to social utility as the measure of human worth.
