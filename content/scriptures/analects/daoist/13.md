Daoist reading of Analects 13 engages critically with the doctrine of the rectification of names (zhengming). From a Daoist perspective, the very project of fixing names to things reflects a misunderstanding of the fluid, indeterminate nature of reality. The Zhuangzi's philosophy of language emphasizes that names are conventional impositions that distort the ceaseless transformation of things. Rather than rectifying names, the Daoist sage releases attachment to names altogether, recognizing their provisional and ultimately arbitrary character.

The chapter's emphasis on governance through moral example rather than law (13.6) receives more favorable Daoist treatment. The principle that commands are unnecessary when the ruler is upright approaches the wu wei ideal, though Daoists would add that even the concept of "uprightness" imposes an artificial moral standard on the natural Way.
