## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Analects has historically been characterized by creative tension. From the Zhuangzi's satirical portrayals of Confucius to later Daoist commentators who found points of convergence, the Daoist tradition has used the Analects as a foil for articulating its own vision of naturalness, spontaneity, and non-action (wu wei). Yet certain passages in the Analects—particularly those expressing love of simplicity and distrust of cleverness—have invited genuine Daoist appreciation.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist readers tend to highlight moments in the Analects where Confucius expresses weariness with the artificiality of social conventions or admires the unforced simplicity of certain disciples. The famous passage where Confucius endorses the aspiration of Zeng Xi to simply bathe in the river and enjoy the breeze (11.26) has been read by Daoist interpreters as evidence that even Confucius recognized the value of spontaneous harmony with nature over rigid moral striving.

## Points of Convergence and Divergence

While Daoists critique the Confucian emphasis on ritual propriety and deliberate moral cultivation as contrary to the natural Way, they acknowledge a shared concern with authenticity and the dangers of hypocrisy. The Daoist reading of the Analects ultimately serves to sharpen the distinctiveness of both traditions while revealing unexpected common ground on the importance of genuine inner transformation.
