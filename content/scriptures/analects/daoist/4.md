Daoist interpretation of Analects 4 engages with Confucius's teachings on humaneness (ren) as an example of the Confucian tendency to fragment the natural unity of the Way into discrete moral categories. From a Daoist perspective, the very effort to cultivate ren as a specific virtue introduces the artificial distinctions between virtue and vice, self and other, that the Dao transcends. The Daodejing teaches that when the great Way is abandoned, benevolence and righteousness appear—suggesting that ren is a symptom of moral decline rather than its remedy.

The chapter's distinction between the junzi who understands rightness and the petty person who understands profit (4.16) is viewed by Daoists as itself a form of moral dualism that creates the very categories it seeks to overcome. The Daoist sage operates beyond the dichotomy of rightness and profit, acting in effortless accord with the Way without categorizing actions as moral or immoral.
