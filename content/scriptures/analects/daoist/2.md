Daoist interpretation of Analects 2 finds both points of agreement and divergence with Confucian governance theory. The image of the ruler as the North Star, governing without active intervention (2.1), echoes the Daoist ideal of wu wei governance, in which the sage-ruler leads by non-action rather than by imposing policies. Daoist readers emphasize this passage as evidence that Confucius himself intuited the superiority of non-coercive governance, even if he did not fully develop this insight.

However, Daoists critique Confucius's intellectual autobiography (2.4) as a narrative of progressive moral domestication rather than genuine liberation. From a Daoist perspective, the culmination at seventy, when desire conforms to moral bounds, represents not freedom but the complete internalization of social constraints. The Daoist sage, by contrast, achieves spontaneity by transcending moral categories altogether.
