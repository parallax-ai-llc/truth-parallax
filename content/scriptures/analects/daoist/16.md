Daoist interpretation of Analects 16 critiques the chapter's concern with political hierarchy as reflecting the Confucian attachment to structured social order. The anxiety about authority flowing from the wrong level of the hierarchy reveals, from a Daoist perspective, a fixation on institutional arrangements that the Way continually disrupts and reconfigures through natural processes. The rise and fall of political power follows its own organic rhythm that human attempts to control only disturb.

The categorization of beneficial and harmful pleasures is viewed by Daoists as an example of the moral discrimination that fragments experience and prevents the spontaneous enjoyment of life. The Daoist sage takes pleasure in whatever arises naturally, without categorizing experiences as morally beneficial or harmful. This freedom from moral evaluation is itself the deepest pleasure, the joy of aligning with the Way without the burden of constant ethical judgment.
