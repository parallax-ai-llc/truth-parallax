Daoist interpretation of Analects 10, the chapter describing Confucius's meticulous daily conduct, serves as a primary example of the Confucian ritualization of life that Daoists critique. From a Daoist perspective, the detailed prescriptions for dress, diet, and comportment represent an extreme form of the artificiality that obscures natural spontaneity. The Zhuangzi's Cook Ding moves with effortless skill precisely because he has abandoned prescribed methods in favor of direct responsiveness to the Way.

Yet Daoists acknowledge that Confucius's conduct, as described here, possesses a certain grace that transcends mere rule-following. If the Master's behavior truly expressed his natural disposition rather than conscious adherence to ritual codes, then it would approach the Daoist ideal of spontaneous action (ziran). The question is whether Confucius's propriety arose from within or was imposed from without—a question on which Confucians and Daoists fundamentally disagree.
