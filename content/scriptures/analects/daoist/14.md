Daoist interpretation of Analects 14 finds in Confucius's reflections on withdrawal and engagement a tension that Daoists resolve differently. The various historical figures who withdrew from public life are viewed by Daoists not as failures of moral courage but as sages who recognized the futility of imposing moral order on a world that has its own natural processes. The recluse who washes his ears after hearing of a political appointment embodies the Daoist rejection of worldly entanglement.

Confucius's teaching on self-examination over concern with recognition (14.30) again approaches Daoist territory. The sage who is indifferent to others' opinions and focused on inner reality comes close to the Daoist ideal, though Daoists would question whether Confucius's "inner reality" is genuinely natural or merely the internalized voice of social convention masquerading as moral truth.
