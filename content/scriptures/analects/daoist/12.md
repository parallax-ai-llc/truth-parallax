Daoist interpretation of Analects 12 critiques the definition of humaneness as "restraining oneself and returning to ritual propriety" (12.1) as fundamentally at odds with the Daoist understanding of virtue. For Daoists, genuine virtue cannot arise through self-restraint, which presupposes a conflict between natural impulse and moral duty. The Daodejing's teaching that "superior virtue is not virtuous, therefore it has virtue" suggests that the truly virtuous person acts without the consciousness of restraining anything.

The chapter's teaching on governance through personal rectitude (12.17) receives more sympathetic Daoist attention. The idea that the ruler transforms the people through the power of his own moral being, like wind bending grass, resembles the Daoist concept of the sage-ruler whose non-action naturally orders the world. However, Daoists would insist that this transformative power comes from alignment with the Dao rather than from deliberate moral cultivation.
