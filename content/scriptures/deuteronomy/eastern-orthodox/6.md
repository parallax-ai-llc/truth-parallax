## The Greatest Commandment

Deuteronomy 6 contains the Shema — "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one" — the most foundational confession of faith in the Old Testament and the verse Christ Himself identified as the greatest commandment (Mark 12:29-30). For the Orthodox Church, this chapter is the heart of Deuteronomy.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one. You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your strength" (6:4-5)

The Shema proclaims divine unity with a totality of human response. Orthodox theology reads the oneness of God through the lens of Trinitarian theology — the one divine nature shared by Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. The command to love God with heart, soul, and strength encompasses the entire human person — intellect, will, desire, and bodily action. St. Maximus the Confessor teaches that the human person is created for this total orientation toward God, and that sin is precisely the fragmentation of love into competing passions.

Christ's citation of the Shema as the first and greatest commandment (Matthew 22:37) elevates it from national confession to universal calling. The Orthodox baptismal liturgy echoes this total commitment — the catechumen turns westward to renounce Satan and eastward to unite himself to Christ, enacting the Shema in bodily gesture.

### "You shall teach them diligently to your children" (6:7)

The domestic transmission of faith is central to Orthodox life. The home is understood as the "little church" (*kat' oikon ekklesia*), where prayer, fasting, and the reading of Scripture form the next generation. St. John Chrysostom's homilies on family life insist that parents are the first catechists and that the failure to teach children the faith is a graver neglect than the failure to provide material goods.

### "You shall bind them as a sign on your hand, and they shall be as frontlets between your eyes" (6:8)

While Judaism developed the practice of phylacteries from this verse, Orthodox interpretation follows the Fathers in reading this as a call to internalize the commandments. The hand signifies action; the forehead signifies thought. Every deed and every thought should be governed by love for God. The sign of the Cross, made by the Orthodox faithful upon the forehead and body, fulfills this injunction in a Christological key.

### "You shall not put the Lord your God to the test" (6:16)

Christ quotes this verse in His temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 4:7), definitively linking Deuteronomy to the spiritual combat against the devil. Orthodox ascetical theology teaches that testing God is a form of pride — a demand for proof that substitutes for trust.

## Theological Significance

The Shema is the axis around which all of Deuteronomy turns. Orthodox theology sees in it not merely a monotheistic declaration but the foundation of theosis — for to love God with the whole heart is to become united with Him, to participate in His divine life. The journey from hearing the Shema to living the Shema is the journey of sanctification.
