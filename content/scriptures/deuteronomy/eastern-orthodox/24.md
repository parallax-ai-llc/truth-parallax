## Laws of Divorce, Fair Treatment, and Provision for the Poor

Deuteronomy 24 addresses divorce, just treatment of workers and debtors, individual accountability, and the rights of the poor to glean from the harvest. Orthodox theology engages this chapter in dialogue with Christ's teaching on marriage and the Church's pastoral practice of *oikonomia*.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "When a man takes a wife and marries her, and it happens that she finds no favor in his eyes because he has found some uncleanness in her, and he writes her a certificate of divorce" (24:1)

Christ addresses this passage directly in Matthew 19:3-9, explaining that Moses permitted divorce "because of the hardness of your hearts, but from the beginning it was not so." Orthodox theology upholds the indissolubility of marriage as the ideal while exercising pastoral *oikonomia* (economy) — permitting divorce and remarriage in cases of grave necessity, recognizing that the hardness of hearts persists in a fallen world. The Orthodox wedding service includes prayers for the forgiveness of sins, acknowledging that even sacramental marriage is entered by imperfect persons in need of grace.

### "You shall not oppress a hired servant who is poor and needy" (24:14)

The daily payment of wages is not merely an economic rule but a moral imperative. St. James echoes this in his epistle: "The wages of the laborers who mowed your fields, which you kept back by fraud, cry out; and the cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth" (James 5:4). Orthodox social teaching insists that labor must be justly compensated and that the exploitation of workers is a sin that cries out to heaven.

### "When you reap your harvest in your field, and forget a sheaf... you shall not go back to get it; it shall be for the stranger, the fatherless, and the widow" (24:19)

The gleaning laws institutionalize generosity as a structural feature of economic life. The poor are not dependent on charity alone but have a right to the surplus of the harvest. St. John Chrysostom teaches that the surplus of the rich belongs by right to the poor, and that failure to share is equivalent to theft. Orthodox monasteries have historically practiced this principle, distributing food and goods to the surrounding community.

### "Fathers shall not be put to death for their children, nor shall children be put to death for their fathers; a person shall be put to death for his own sin" (24:16)

This principle of individual accountability marks a significant development in biblical jurisprudence, later expanded by the prophet Ezekiel (18:20). Orthodox theology affirms that each person bears responsibility for his or her own choices. While acknowledging the social dimensions of sin — the ways in which one generation's failures affect the next — the Church insists that every soul stands before God as a free and accountable person. The sacrament of Confession is always individual, never collective, precisely because repentance is a personal act.

## Theological Significance

Deuteronomy 24 reveals the Torah's deep concern for the dignity of every person — the divorced woman, the hired worker, the stranger, the orphan, the widow. Orthodox canon law inherits this concern, addressing not only matters of doctrine and worship but also the practical welfare of the vulnerable. The principle of *oikonomia* — applying the law with pastoral flexibility for the sake of the soul's salvation — is itself a Deuteronomic legacy.
