## Release of Debts and Freedom for Slaves

Deuteronomy 15 legislates the sabbatical year, when debts are released and Hebrew slaves are freed. Orthodox social teaching draws extensively on this chapter's vision of economic justice as inseparable from covenant faithfulness.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "At the end of every seven years you shall grant a release of debts" (15:1)

The periodic release of debts prevents the permanent concentration of wealth and the enslavement of the poor. The Fathers see in the sabbatical year a type of the Jubilee proclaimed by Christ: "The Spirit of the Lord is upon Me, because He has anointed Me to preach the gospel to the poor... to proclaim the acceptable year of the Lord" (Luke 4:18-19). St. Ambrose of Milan, widely read in the Orthodox East, teaches that withholding debt release is a form of theft — for the earth and its fruits belong ultimately to God.

### "There shall be no poor among you" (15:4)

This is not naive utopianism but a divine imperative grounded in the abundance God provides. St. John Chrysostom's homilies on poverty are relentless: the existence of poverty in the midst of abundance is a communal sin. The early Church's practice of holding goods in common (Acts 2:44-45) is the apostolic fulfillment of this Deuteronomic vision. Orthodox monastic communities continue to embody this ideal.

### "You shall not harden your heart nor shut your hand from your poor brother" (15:7)

The hardened heart is the central biblical image of spiritual death. St. Isaac the Syrian teaches that the merciful heart is the heart that has been softened by prayer and communion with God — it feels compassion for every creature because it has begun to share in the divine compassion. Almsgiving in Orthodox practice is not optional charity but a sacramental act that participates in God's own generosity.

### "You shall supply him liberally from your flock, from your threshing floor, and from your winepress" (15:14)

The freed slave is not to be sent away empty-handed but richly provisioned. This principle of generous severance reflects God's own manner of giving — abundantly and without reproach (James 1:5). Orthodox practice at Pascha — the distribution of food, the opening of doors, the invitation to all — enacts this spirit of lavish generosity.

## Theological Significance

The sabbatical year reveals that Israel's economic life is meant to mirror God's character. Wealth is not absolute but held in trust. The poor are not failures but brothers. Freedom is not earned but given. These principles undergird the Orthodox Church's social witness and her critique of economic systems that treat persons as commodities.
