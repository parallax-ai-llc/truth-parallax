## The Command to Destroy the Canaanite Nations

Deuteronomy 7 contains God's command to Israel to utterly destroy the seven nations of Canaan and to make no covenant with them. Orthodox tradition reads this chapter not as a license for violence but as a typological instruction on the spiritual warfare against the passions. St. Maximus the Confessor identifies the nations to be destroyed with the capital vices that occupy the soul's promised land, preventing the faithful from attaining union with God.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "For you are a holy people to the Lord your God; the Lord your God has chosen you to be a people for His own possession" (7:6)

The doctrine of divine election here is not arbitrary favoritism but a call to holiness. The Fathers consistently read Israel's election as prefiguring the Church — the new Israel called out of the world to be consecrated to God. St. Cyril of Alexandria emphasizes that holiness is not a natural property of the elect but a gift of grace requiring human cooperation (*synergy*). The liturgical designation of the faithful as "a royal priesthood, a holy nation" (1 Peter 2:9) draws directly on this Deuteronomic language.

### "The Lord did not set His love on you nor choose you because you were more in number... but because the Lord loves you" (7:7-8)

God's love is the sole ground of election. St. John Chrysostom teaches that divine love is uncaused — it does not respond to merit but creates worth in its object. This passage is foundational for the Orthodox understanding of grace as the free and unmerited initiative of God. The small number of Israel mirrors the "little flock" of Christ (Luke 12:32), reminding the Church that her significance lies not in worldly power but in divine love.

### "He will love you and bless you and multiply you" (7:13)

The blessings promised to faithful Israel — fertility, health, abundance — are read by the Fathers as figures of spiritual fruitfulness. St. Gregory of Nyssa sees in the multiplication of Israel the soul's growth in virtue, which is the true multiplication of life. Barrenness and disease, conversely, signify the sterility of a life alienated from God.

### "The Lord your God will deliver them over to you, and will inflict defeat upon them until they are destroyed" (7:23)

The gradual nature of the conquest — "little by little" (7:22) — is significant for Orthodox ascetical theology. St. John Cassian teaches that the passions are not overcome in a single moment but through patient, sustained effort sustained by grace. The spiritual life is not instant transformation but a lifelong process of purification, illumination, and union — the threefold path that the Fathers describe as the journey to theosis.

## Theological Significance

The command to destroy idols and their altars (7:5) finds its liturgical echo in the exorcisms of the Orthodox baptismal rite, where the catechumen renounces Satan and all his works. The destruction of false worship is fulfilled not by physical violence but by the interior cleansing of the heart. Orthodox ascetical tradition treats every passion as an idol to be overthrown so that the temple of the soul may be consecrated wholly to God.
