## The Commissioning of Joshua and the Deposit of the Law

Deuteronomy 31 narrates Moses' final preparations: he commissions Joshua as his successor, commands the reading of the Torah every seven years, and writes the Song of Moses. God warns that Israel will apostatize after Moses' death. Orthodox tradition reads this chapter as a model of apostolic succession and the transmission of sacred tradition.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Be strong and of good courage; do not fear nor be afraid of them; for the Lord your God, He is the One who goes with you. He will not leave you nor forsake you" (31:6)

This exhortation, repeated to Joshua in 31:7-8, is one of the most beloved passages in Scripture. The Fathers apply it to every Christian facing trial. St. John Chrysostom preaches that the command "do not fear" is not a denial of danger but a promise of divine accompaniment. The martyrs of the Church — from St. Stephen to the new martyrs of the twentieth century — embody this courage, entering suffering and death with the confidence that God will not forsake them.

### "Take this Book of the Law, and put it beside the ark of the covenant of the Lord your God" (31:26)

The physical deposit of the Torah beside the Ark is a type of the apostolic deposit of faith entrusted to the Church. St. Paul instructs Timothy: "Guard what was committed to your trust" (1 Timothy 6:20). Orthodox theology understands Sacred Tradition as the living continuation of this deposit — not a static text but a dynamic reality that includes Scripture, the Creed, the liturgy, the canons, the writings of the Fathers, and the icons. The bishop, as successor of the apostles, is the guardian of this deposit in each generation.

### "For I know that after my death you will become utterly corrupt" (31:29)

God's foreknowledge of Israel's apostasy does not prevent Him from making the covenant. Orthodox theology holds that God's love is not conditional on the certainty of human faithfulness. He enters into covenant with full knowledge of human weakness, providing in advance the means of restoration — repentance, sacrifice, and ultimately the Cross. St. Athanasius teaches that the Incarnation was planned from eternity, not as an afterthought in response to sin but as the fulfillment of God's original purpose for creation.

### "Assemble the people — men and women and little ones, and the stranger who is within your gates — that they may hear and that they may learn to fear the Lord your God" (31:12)

The septennial public reading of the Torah is a model of catechesis — the formation of the entire community in the knowledge of God. Orthodox catechetical practice follows this pattern: Scripture is read publicly in the Liturgy, the Creed is confessed communally, and the homily expounds the sacred text for all present. The inclusion of children and strangers in the assembly teaches that formation in the faith is not reserved for the educated or the initiated but is the right and need of every person.

## Theological Significance

The transition from Moses to Joshua prefigures the transition from the Old Covenant to the New. Joshua's very name — *Yehoshua*, "the Lord saves" — is the Hebrew form of Jesus. The Fathers consistently read Joshua as a type of Christ, who leads His people into the true Promised Land. The commissioning of Joshua also provides the model for Orthodox ordination: authority is transmitted personally, from one leader to the next, in an unbroken chain stretching back to the apostles.
