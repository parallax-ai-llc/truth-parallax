## Passover, Weeks, and Tabernacles

Deuteronomy 16 prescribes the three pilgrimage festivals — Passover, the Feast of Weeks, and the Feast of Tabernacles — along with instructions for the appointment of judges. Orthodox tradition reads these feasts typologically as fulfilled in the Paschal mystery, Pentecost, and the Transfiguration.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Observe the month of Abib, and keep the Passover to the Lord your God, for in the month of Abib the Lord your God brought you out of Egypt by night" (16:1)

The Passover of the Old Covenant is fulfilled in the Pascha of Christ — the central feast of the Orthodox liturgical year. The Paschal homily of St. John Chrysostom, read at every Paschal Matins, proclaims: "Christ is risen, and the demons are cast down. Christ is risen, and the angels rejoice." The lamb of Deuteronomy 16 is the type of Christ, the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world. The transition from Old Passover to New Pascha is not abolition but fulfillment — the same God who delivered Israel from Egypt now delivers all humanity from sin and death.

### "Seven weeks you shall count... then you shall keep the Feast of Weeks" (16:9-10)

The Feast of Weeks, falling fifty days after Passover, becomes the Christian Pentecost — the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the apostles. Orthodox theology emphasizes that Pentecost is not merely a historical event but a continuing reality: the Spirit is perpetually given to the Church in the sacraments, in prayer, and in the gathered assembly. The kontakion of Pentecost declares: "When the Most High came down and confused the tongues, He divided the nations; but when He distributed the tongues of fire, He called all to unity."

### "Three times a year all your males shall appear before the Lord your God" (16:16)

The obligation of pilgrimage — appearing before God in the appointed place — is spiritually fulfilled in the Orthodox expectation that the faithful attend the Divine Liturgy every Sunday and on the great feasts. The liturgy is the pilgrimage of the baptized to the heavenly Jerusalem, where they stand before the throne of God in the company of angels and saints.

### The Feast of Tabernacles (16:13-15)

The Feast of Tabernacles, celebrated after the harvest, commemorates Israel's wilderness dwelling in tents. The Fathers connect it to the Transfiguration, where Peter proposed building three tabernacles (Matthew 17:4), and to the eschatological promise that God will "tabernacle" among His people (Revelation 21:3). Orthodox theology reads the feast as anticipating the final dwelling of God with humanity — the new heavens and the new earth.

## Theological Significance

The concluding instructions about judges (16:18-20) connect worship to justice: "Justice, justice you shall pursue." In Orthodox thought, right worship (*orthodoxia*) and right action (*orthopraxia*) are inseparable. A society that worships God truly will pursue justice for all its members. The feast and the courtroom are both sacred spaces where God's will must be enacted.
