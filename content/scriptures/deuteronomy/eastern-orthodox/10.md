## The Tablets of the Covenant Restored

Deuteronomy 10 narrates the restoration of the stone tablets after Israel's apostasy with the golden calf. God commands Moses to hew new tablets and place them in the Ark. Orthodox theology reads this restoration as a type of divine mercy — God does not abandon His covenant even when His people are faithless. The new tablets signify the possibility of renewal after sin, a truth fulfilled in the sacrament of Confession.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Hew for yourself two tablets of stone like the first" (10:1)

St. Ephrem the Syrian notes that while the first tablets were wholly God's work, the second required Moses to prepare the stone. The Fathers see here a pattern of synergy: after the fall, restoration requires both divine initiative and human labor. The Ark that receives the tablets prefigures the Theotokos, who bore within herself the incarnate Word — the living Law of God.

### "Circumcise the foreskin of your heart, and be stiff-necked no longer" (10:16)

This is one of the great prophetic calls of the Old Testament, anticipating the New Covenant emphasis on interior transformation. St. Paul quotes this principle (Romans 2:29), and the Orthodox baptismal liturgy fulfills it sacramentally. Circumcision of the heart means the removal of all that prevents the soul from receiving God's grace — the cutting away of pride, self-will, and attachment to the passions. St. Maximus the Confessor identifies this inner circumcision with *apatheia*, the state of freedom from disordered passions that opens the way to theosis.

### "For the Lord your God is God of gods and Lord of lords, the great God, mighty and awesome, who shows no partiality and accepts no bribes" (10:17)

The Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom echoes this doxological language. God's impartiality is a cornerstone of Orthodox social teaching — the Church insists that every human being bears the image of God and must be treated with equal dignity. The prohibition of bribes extends to spiritual corruption: no one can purchase God's favor through external observances alone.

### "He administers justice for the fatherless and the widow, and loves the stranger, giving him food and clothing" (10:18)

God's care for the vulnerable is not peripheral but central to His character. Orthodox monasteries have historically served as hospitals, orphanages, and places of refuge, embodying this divine attribute. St. John Chrysostom's famous homilies on wealth and poverty draw directly on Deuteronomic passages like this one.

## Theological Significance

The restoration of the covenant in chapter 10 demonstrates that God's faithfulness exceeds Israel's faithlessness. This is the foundation of Orthodox hope — the Church does not despair of sinners because God does not despair of His people. The call to circumcise the heart transforms Deuteronomy from external law code into an interior spiritual program, preparing the way for the New Covenant written on the heart (Jeremiah 31:33).
