## Who Enters the Assembly of the Lord

Deuteronomy 23 defines who may enter the assembly of the Lord, legislates camp purity during warfare, and addresses vows, usury, and the rights of travelers. Orthodox theology reads the regulations on assembly membership in light of the universal call of the Gospel while preserving the principle that holiness requires boundaries.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "He who is emasculated... shall not enter the assembly of the Lord" (23:1)

This exclusion is dramatically overturned in the prophetic tradition (Isaiah 56:3-5) and fulfilled in the New Testament when Philip baptizes the Ethiopian eunuch (Acts 8:26-39). Orthodox theology reads this progression as revelatory: the restrictions of the Old Covenant are gradually lifted as God's saving purpose expands to include all humanity. The baptismal font has no barriers of ethnicity, social status, or physical condition — all who believe and repent are welcomed.

### "You shall not charge interest to your brother" (23:19)

The prohibition against usury — lending at interest to a fellow Israelite — was a cornerstone of patristic economic ethics. St. Basil the Great devotes an entire homily to denouncing usury as the exploitation of desperation, calling it worse than robbery because it wears the mask of benevolence. St. Gregory of Nyssa follows him, arguing that charging interest on loans to the poor is a violation of the image of God in the borrower. Orthodox canonical tradition historically forbade clergy from engaging in moneylending.

### "When you make a vow to the Lord your God, you shall not delay to pay it" (23:21)

Vows taken before God are binding. Orthodox monasticism is built on the three great vows — poverty, chastity, and obedience — which the monastic takes in the presence of God, the bishop, and the community. The seriousness of vows extends to the laity as well: the baptismal promises to renounce Satan and unite oneself to Christ constitute the most fundamental vow of every Orthodox Christian, renewed liturgically and lived out daily.

### "You shall not abhor an Edomite, for he is your brother. You shall not abhor an Egyptian, because you were an alien in his land" (23:7)

Even within the restrictions on assembly membership, compassion and historical memory temper exclusion. The Edomite is a brother (descended from Esau), and Egypt, though a place of bondage, was also a place of refuge. Orthodox theology recognizes that even outside the visible boundaries of the Church, God's providence is at work. The principle of *oikonomia* allows pastoral flexibility in the application of canonical boundaries, always for the sake of the soul's salvation.

## Theological Significance

The chapter's regulations on camp purity (23:9-14) — maintaining cleanliness because "the Lord your God walks in the midst of your camp" — express the foundational Orthodox conviction that God is present among His people and that His presence demands holiness. The Church building, the home, and the body itself are all "camps" in which God dwells, and all are to be kept pure through prayer, fasting, and the sacraments.
