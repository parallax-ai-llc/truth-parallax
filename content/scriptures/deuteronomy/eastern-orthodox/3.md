## Victories East of the Jordan

Moses recounts the defeat of Og king of Bashan and the distribution of Transjordan lands to Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh. The chapter concludes with Moses' deeply personal plea to enter the Promised Land and God's firm refusal.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Do not fear him, for I have delivered him into your hand" (3:2)

God's reassurance before battle with Og parallels His words before every decisive moment in salvation history. St. Athanasius reads such divine encouragements as evidence that faith, not military might, is the true weapon of God's people. In the Orthodox liturgical tradition, the troparion of the Cross proclaims victory through the Cross rather than through earthly power — a fulfillment of these ancient patterns.

### "O Lord God, You have begun to show Your servant Your greatness" (3:24)

Moses' prayer reveals the posture of the true servant of God — one who recognizes that even after a lifetime of service, he has only begun to glimpse divine greatness. St. Gregory of Nyssa's concept of *epektasis* (perpetual striving forward into God) finds resonance here: the spiritual life is an endless advance into the infinite depths of God, never reaching a final boundary.

### "Go up to the top of Pisgah and lift your eyes" (3:27)

God denies Moses entry but grants him a vision from the mountaintop. The Fathers universally see this as typological: Moses (the Law) can see the Promised Land but cannot enter it. Only Joshua (whose name is the Hebrew form of "Jesus") leads Israel across the Jordan. St. Justin Martyr in his *Dialogue with Trypho* emphasizes this typology — the Law brings humanity to the threshold of salvation, but it is Christ who brings entry into the Kingdom.

## Theological Significance

Moses' exclusion from the Promised Land is one of the most poignant moments in Scripture. Orthodox theology does not read this merely as punishment but as a profound typological necessity. The Law, holy and good as it is, cannot itself bring salvation. Moses sees, desires, and prays — but the crossing belongs to another. This is precisely the relationship between the Old and New Covenants as understood by the Orthodox Church.

The distribution of land to the eastern tribes, with the condition that their warriors must still cross over to fight with their brothers (3:18-20), teaches that inheritance brings obligation. In the Church, those who have received gifts of the Spirit are called to labor alongside their brothers and sisters in Christ.
