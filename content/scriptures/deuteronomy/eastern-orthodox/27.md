## The Altar on Ebal and the Twelve Curses

Deuteronomy 27 commands Israel to set up plastered stones inscribed with the law upon entering Canaan, to build an altar on Mount Ebal, and to pronounce twelve curses against specific transgressions. Orthodox tradition reads this chapter as a solemn covenant ceremony that prefigures the Church's own penitential liturgies.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You shall set up for yourself large stones, and whitewash them with lime, and you shall write on them all the words of this law" (27:2-3)

The public inscription of the law makes the divine commandments visible to all. The Fathers see in this a type of the public proclamation of the Gospel — the faith is not esoteric or hidden but declared openly to all nations. Orthodox churches display icons, Scripture texts, and the Creed prominently, continuing this principle of visible witness to divine truth. St. Cyril of Jerusalem insists that the faith must be both publicly taught and personally received.

### The Altar of Uncut Stones (27:5-6)

The altar must be built of unhewn stones — no iron tool may be used. The Fathers interpret this as a sign that God's altar requires no human embellishment; divine grace works through natural and unadorned means. St. Irenaeus of Lyon sees in the uncut stones a figure of the apostles — rough, unpolished men whom God chose as the foundation of His Church. Orthodox liturgical practice maintains a reverence for simplicity and authenticity, even amid great beauty.

### The Twelve Curses (27:15-26)

The curses address secret sins — idolatry, dishonoring parents, moving boundary markers, misleading the blind, perverting justice, and various sexual transgressions. Each curse is answered by the people: "Amen." Orthodox penitential theology recognizes that secret sins are as destructive as public ones. The sacrament of Confession brings hidden sins into the light, where they can be healed by divine grace. The communal "Amen" teaches that sin is never merely private — it wounds the entire body of the faithful.

### "Cursed is the one who does not confirm all the words of this law by observing them" (27:26)

St. Paul quotes this verse in Galatians 3:10 to demonstrate that no one can be justified by the works of the law, since no one keeps every commandment perfectly. This "curse of the law" is precisely what Christ bears on the Cross. Orthodox soteriology teaches that Christ does not merely forgive sins externally but enters into the human condition — including the curse — in order to transform it from within. The Cross is not merely payment but the destruction of the curse through the voluntary suffering of the sinless One.

## Theological Significance

The division of Israel between Mount Gerizim (blessing) and Mount Ebal (cursing) dramatizes the choice set before every soul: life or death, blessing or curse. Orthodox Great Lent begins with a similar choice — the faithful are called to turn from sin and embrace the way of repentance. The Lenten Triodion's penitential hymns echo the spirit of Deuteronomy 27, calling the faithful to hear the curses, recognize their own sinfulness, and seek the mercy of God.
