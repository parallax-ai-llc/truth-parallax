## A Holy People: Food Laws and Tithes

Deuteronomy 14 reiterates the dietary laws distinguishing clean from unclean animals and establishes the practice of tithing. Orthodox tradition reads the food laws typologically while maintaining its own rigorous fasting discipline, and understands tithing as the foundation of Christian generosity.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You are the children of the Lord your God" (14:1)

This declaration of filial relationship grounds all the laws that follow. The dietary restrictions and tithing requirements are not arbitrary but flow from Israel's identity as God's children. Orthodox theology emphasizes that baptism makes the faithful children of God by adoption, and that the entire moral and ascetical life flows from this identity. The fasting rules of the Church — abstinence from meat, dairy, fish, wine, and oil during the prescribed seasons — are similarly grounded in the baptismal identity of the Christian.

### The Dietary Laws (14:3-21)

The apostolic council of Jerusalem (Acts 15) freed Gentile Christians from the Mosaic dietary code, and Orthodox Christianity does not observe these specific prohibitions. However, the Fathers read the laws allegorically: the clean animals represent virtues, the unclean represent vices. St. Clement of Alexandria interprets the prohibition of animals that do not chew the cud as a warning against failing to meditate on Scripture. The Orthodox fasting tradition, while not following the Mosaic categories, preserves the principle that what we eat and how we eat forms the soul.

### "You shall truly tithe all the increase of your grain" (14:22)

The tithe is the acknowledgment that all good things belong to God. Orthodox canonical tradition enjoins generosity without specifying a fixed percentage, but the Deuteronomic tithe — a tenth of all produce — has served as a benchmark for Christian giving throughout history. St. John Chrysostom teaches that the tithe is the minimum, not the maximum, of what love demands.

### "You shall not boil a young goat in its mother's milk" (14:21)

This enigmatic prohibition, repeated three times in the Pentateuch, has generated extensive commentary. The Fathers read it as a prohibition against cruelty and the perversion of natural relationships — that which gives life (mother's milk) must not become an instrument of death. Orthodox ethical teaching draws from this a broader principle: the gifts of God must be used in accordance with their nature, not twisted into instruments of harm.

## Theological Significance

The holiness code of Deuteronomy 14 expresses the principle that every dimension of life — including the most mundane acts of eating and spending — is subject to sanctification. Orthodox spirituality makes no division between sacred and secular: the blessing of food before meals, the fasting calendar, and the practice of giving to the Church and the poor all consecrate daily existence as an offering to God.
