## Summary

Deuteronomy 22 presents a diverse collection of laws reflecting the Torah's concern for both ethical conduct and ritual purity. It opens with the obligation to return lost property (*hashavat aveidah*) and assist a neighbor's fallen animal. Gender-specific clothing prohibitions follow, along with the law of the bird's nest (*shiluach ha-ken*) — sending away the mother bird before taking the eggs. The chapter then legislates safety (building a parapet on a roof), prohibitions against mixed species (*kilayim*), the *sha'atnez* prohibition (mixing wool and linen), and the commandment of *tzitzit* (fringes). The latter half addresses laws of marriage, virginity, and sexual offenses.

The Mishnah (*Bava Metzia* 2:1-11) builds an elaborate halakhic framework on the lost property obligation, establishing that returning another's possession is not merely ethical courtesy but a binding legal obligation with detailed procedural requirements.

## Key Verses

**Verse 22:1-3** — "You shall not see your brother's ox or sheep straying and hide yourself from them; you shall surely return them." The Talmud (*Bava Metzia* 30a) derives from "you shall not hide yourself" that there are circumstances where one is exempt — an elder for whom the task is beneath his dignity, though the Sages encouraged going beyond the letter of the law (*lifnim mi-shurat ha-din*).

**Verse 22:6-7** — *Shiluach ha-ken*: "If a bird's nest happens to be before you... you shall not take the mother upon the young. You shall surely send away the mother... that it may be well with you and your days prolonged." This is one of only two commandments (the other being honoring parents) for which the Torah promises long life. The Mishnah (*Chullin* 12:1-5) codifies the detailed laws of this commandment.

**Verse 22:8** — "When you build a new house, you shall make a parapet (*ma'akeh*) for your roof." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Rotze'ach* 11:1-4) extends this into a general obligation to remove all hazards from one's property — the parapet is a paradigm for comprehensive safety responsibility.

**Verse 22:11** — "You shall not wear *sha'atnez* — wool and linen together." The prohibition of this specific mixture is classified as a *chok* (statute without a revealed reason). The Ramban suggests it relates to the cosmic order — certain combinations violate the divinely established boundaries of creation.

## Halakhic Significance

*Hashavat aveidah* (returning lost property) is codified in *Choshen Mishpat* 259-271 with detailed rules about identification, custody, and when the finder may claim ownership. *Shiluach ha-ken* is codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 292. The *ma'akeh* obligation (*Hilkhot Rotze'ach* 11) extends to covering pits, securing ladders, and removing aggressive animals. *Sha'atnez* (*Yoreh De'ah* 298-304) requires laboratory testing of garments when the composition is uncertain.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Ki Teitzei** (21:10-25:19), which contains the densest concentration of mitzvot in the Torah. The apparently random sequence of laws is understood by the Sages as deliberately juxtaposed — each commandment illuminates its neighbor. Rashi's teaching that one mitzvah leads to another (*mitzvah goreret mitzvah*) is exemplified here.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The sequence teaches causation — one who builds a proper house, plants properly, and wears tzitzit will merit a faithful marriage.
- **Ramban**: *Sha'atnez* and *kilayim* reflect the principle that God created distinct categories which humans must not confuse.
- **Rambam**: The *ma'akeh* is paradigmatic — every preventable hazard on one's property generates a Torah-level obligation of removal.
- **Kli Yakar**: *Shiluach ha-ken* teaches compassion not for God's sake (He does not need it) but for the refinement of human character.
