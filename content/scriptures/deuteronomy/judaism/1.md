## Summary

Deuteronomy 1 opens the book of *Devarim* ("Words"), the fifth and final book of the Torah. Moses begins his farewell address to the people of Israel on the first day of the eleventh month in the fortieth year of the wilderness wandering. He recounts the journey from Horeb (Sinai) to the edge of the Promised Land, rehearsing both divine promises and Israel's failures. The chapter sets the tone for the entire book as *Mishneh Torah* — a "repetition of the Torah" — delivered with prophetic urgency before Moses' death.

Rashi notes that the opening verse lists place names that are actually veiled references to Israel's sins, demonstrating that Moses offered *tochacha* (rebuke) through allusion rather than direct accusation, preserving the people's dignity. The Ramban, by contrast, sees the geographical markers as literal waypoints that frame the historical narrative.

## Key Verses

**Verse 1:6-8** — "The LORD our God spoke to us at Horeb, saying: 'You have stayed long enough at this mountain...'" God's command to leave Sinai and proceed to the land is understood by the Sages as teaching that even the holiest spiritual experience must eventually translate into practical action in the world (*Sifrei Devarim* 1).

**Verse 1:13** — "Get you wise men (*chakhamim*), understanding (*nevonim*), and known (*yedu'im*) among your tribes." The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 88b) derives from this verse key qualifications for judges and communal leaders. Rashi distinguishes between *chakhamim* (those who deduce new knowledge) and *nevonim* (those who discern truth between competing claims).

**Verse 1:17** — "You shall not show favoritism in judgment (*lo takiru panim*)." This verse is a foundational halakhic text for judicial impartiality. The Sifrei explains that a judge must not favor even the poor litigant out of sympathy.

**Verse 1:22** — The people's request to send spies. Unlike the account in Numbers 13, here Moses attributes the initiative to the people themselves. Rashi reconciles the two accounts: God agreed to the plan but did not command it, testing whether the people trusted the divine promise.

## Halakhic Significance

The appointment of judges (1:13-17) provides the Torah's blueprint for the structure of the *beit din* (Jewish court system). The Rambam codifies these qualifications in *Hilkhot Sanhedrin* 2:1-7, requiring judges to possess wisdom, humility, fear of God, and communal respect. The prohibition of judicial favoritism (*lo takiru panim*) appears in the *Choshen Mishpat* section of the Shulchan Arukh.

## Parashah Context

This chapter falls within Parashat **Devarim** (1:1-3:22), always read on the Shabbat before Tisha B'Av (*Shabbat Chazon*). The Haftarah is Isaiah 1, the "Vision of Isaiah," linking Moses' rebuke of Israel with Isaiah's later prophetic critique. The juxtaposition underscores the recurring pattern of divine faithfulness and human unfaithfulness.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The place names in verse 1 are coded rebukes referring to the golden calf, the complaint about manna, and other wilderness sins.
- **Ramban**: Moses' address is a new prophetic speech, not a mere repetition, containing deeper layers of meaning appropriate for the generation entering the Land.
- **Sforno**: The purpose of recounting failures is *teshuvah* — the people must confront past errors to avoid repeating them.
- **Ohr HaChaim**: Moses spoke "to all Israel" (1:1) because the rebuke concerned collective, not individual, sin.
