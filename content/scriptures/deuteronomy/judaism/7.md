## Summary

Deuteronomy 7 commands Israel to destroy the seven Canaanite nations and their idolatrous altars upon entering the land, forbidding intermarriage and covenants with them. Moses explains that Israel's election is not due to numerical greatness but to God's love and His oath to the patriarchs. The chapter promises divine blessing — fertility, health, and military victory — to those who keep the commandments, and assures Israel that God will drive out the nations gradually (*me'at me'at*) so that wild beasts do not overrun the depopulated land.

The Ramban sees the prohibition of intermarriage as protecting the theological integrity of Israel — the danger is not ethnic but spiritual, as a foreign spouse will "turn your son away from following Me" (7:4). Rashi identifies this verse as the Talmudic source (*Kiddushin* 68b) for the halakhic principle that Jewish identity follows the mother.

## Key Verses

**Verse 7:3-4** — "You shall not intermarry with them... for they will turn your son away from following Me." The Talmud (*Kiddushin* 68b) derives from "your son" (born of an Israelite mother) versus "her son" (born of a non-Israelite mother) the rule of matrilineal descent — the child of a Jewish mother is Jewish regardless of the father's identity.

**Verse 7:6** — "For you are a holy people (*am kadosh*) to the LORD your God; the LORD your God has chosen you to be a treasured people (*am segulah*)." The concept of *segulah* — a treasured possession — defines Israel's unique covenantal relationship. The Kuzari (1:27) explains that chosenness entails responsibility, not superiority.

**Verse 7:7-8** — "Not because you are the most numerous... but because the LORD loved you." Rashi explains that God chose Israel precisely because they are humble — when given greatness, they diminish themselves, unlike the nations.

**Verse 7:22** — "The LORD your God will drive out those nations before you little by little (*me'at me'at*)." The Talmud (*Sotah* 36a) discusses whether this gradualism reflects practical wisdom or a test of faith. Rashi applies it literally: sudden depopulation would create an ecological vacuum.

## Halakhic Significance

The prohibition of intermarriage (7:3) is codified by the Rambam (*Hilkhot Issurei Bi'ah* 12:1) and extended by rabbinic decree to all non-Jews, not only the seven nations. The commandment to destroy idolatrous objects (7:5) forms the basis for the laws of *avodah zarah* (idol worship) in the Shulchan Arukh (*Yoreh De'ah* 146). The *Sefer HaChinukh* counts the prohibition against showing favor (*lo techannem*, 7:2) as including three aspects: no gifts, no praise, and no territorial concessions.

## Parashah Context

The first eleven verses belong to Parashat **Va'etchanan** (3:23-7:11), while the remainder begins Parashat **Eikev** (7:12-11:25). This division places the promise of blessing for obedience as the opening theme of Eikev — "because (*eikev*) you heed these ordinances." The two *parashot* together establish the framework of covenantal theology: election brings obligation, and obligation brings blessing.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The matrilineal principle is derived from the precise wording of "your son" in verse 4.
- **Ramban**: The prohibition of intermarriage is theological, aimed at preventing religious assimilation.
- **Sforno**: God chose Israel not for inherent merit but for their potential to be a "kingdom of priests" — the choice is aspirational.
- **Abarbanel**: The gradual conquest teaches patience — redemption unfolds in stages, not in a single dramatic moment.
