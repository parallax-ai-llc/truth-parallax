## Summary

Deuteronomy 9 delivers a bracing message of humility: Israel is not inheriting the land because of its own righteousness (*tzidkatekha*) but because of the wickedness of the Canaanite nations and God's faithfulness to the patriarchal covenant. Moses recounts the sin of the golden calf (*egel ha-zahav*) in vivid detail — how he shattered the first tablets, prostrated himself before God for forty days and nights, and interceded to prevent Israel's destruction. He catalogues additional rebellions at Taberah, Massah, and Kibroth-hattaavah to drive home the point: Israel is a stiff-necked people (*am k'sheh oref*).

The Midrash (*Avot d'Rabbi Natan* 2:3) teaches that Moses broke the tablets through a logical inference (*kal va-chomer*): if the Passover sacrifice, which is only one commandment, may not be shared with apostates, how much more so the entire Torah engraved on the tablets.

## Key Verses

**Verse 9:4-5** — "Do not say in your heart... 'Because of my righteousness the LORD has brought me to possess this land.' ... Not because of your righteousness... for you are a stiff-necked people." This double negation is a cornerstone of Jewish theological humility. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Teshuvah* 9:1) uses it to distinguish between merit and grace in God's dealings with Israel.

**Verse 9:10** — "The LORD gave me the two tablets of stone written with the finger of God." The phrase "finger of God" (*etzba Elokim*) indicates that the first tablets were entirely divine in origin. The Talmud (*Nedarim* 38a) notes that the letters were miraculously legible from both sides.

**Verse 9:17** — "I took hold of the two tablets and cast them from my two hands and broke them before your eyes." Rashi, citing the Talmud (*Shabbat* 87a), records that God later endorsed Moses' decision: "Yasher koach she-shibarta" — "Well done that you broke them." The broken fragments were placed alongside the intact second tablets in the Ark.

**Verse 9:18-19** — Moses' forty-day intercession. He neither ate bread nor drank water. The Talmud (*Yoma* 4b) compares this to the angelic state, demonstrating that Moses transcended ordinary human limitations during his advocacy for the people.

## Halakhic Significance

The practice of placing the broken tablets alongside the whole ones in the Ark (*Berakhot* 8b, *Bava Batra* 14b) is the source for the custom of honoring a Torah scholar who has forgotten his learning due to illness or old age — one must treat him with the same reverence as before. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Teshuvah* 2:1) draws on this chapter's theme of repeated sin and forgiveness to establish that *teshuvah* (repentance) is always possible.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Eikev** (7:12-11:25). The recounting of the golden calf within Eikev serves as a counterweight to the promises of blessing — Israel's history of rebellion demonstrates that the covenant endures not because of human perfection but because of divine patience and mercy. The Haftarah of Eikev (Isaiah 49:14-51:3) echoes this theme: "Can a mother forget her child? Even these may forget, but I will not forget you."

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Moses' breaking of the tablets was an act of legal reasoning endorsed by God Himself.
- **Ramban**: "Stiff-necked" (*k'sheh oref*) refers to the refusal to turn one's neck — a metaphor for the inability to redirect oneself through *teshuvah*.
- **Sforno**: The land is given as a trust (*pikadon*), not a reward — Israel holds it conditionally.
- **Ohr HaChaim**: Moses' intercession reveals the power of a *tzaddik* to alter heavenly decrees through prayer.
