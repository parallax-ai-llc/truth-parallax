## Summary

Deuteronomy 2 continues Moses' historical review, recounting Israel's long detour through the wilderness and their passage around the territories of Edom, Moab, and Ammon. God explicitly forbids Israel from warring against these nations, as their lands were divinely granted to the descendants of Esau and Lot. The chapter concludes with the defeat of Sihon, king of Heshbon, marking the beginning of Israel's military conquests east of the Jordan.

The Midrash (*Devarim Rabbah* 1:19) highlights that God's concern for the territorial rights of Edom, Moab, and Ammon demonstrates that the Holy One, blessed be He, is just with all nations — not only with Israel. The Ramban sees in this a theological principle: God assigns lands to all peoples according to His sovereign will.

## Key Verses

**Verse 2:5** — "Do not provoke them, for I will not give you any of their land." Rashi explains that Esau's descendants received Mount Seir as reward for the honor Esau showed his father Isaac. The Talmud (*Bava Batra* 56a) derives from this verse principles about the inviolability of divinely sanctioned territorial boundaries.

**Verse 2:7** — "The LORD your God has blessed you in all the work of your hand; He knows your wandering through this great wilderness." The Sifrei reads this as a rebuke: God provided everything, yet the people complained. Ibn Ezra reads it as comfort — God was attentive to every step of the journey.

**Verse 2:14-16** — The thirty-eight years of wandering are summarized in a few verses. The generation of the spies had to die out before Israel could advance. The Talmud (*Ta'anit* 30b) teaches that the dying ceased on the fifteenth of Av, making that date a future festival (*Tu B'Av*).

**Verse 2:30** — "Sihon king of Heshbon would not let us pass through, for the LORD your God hardened his spirit." This raises the classical question of free will (*bechirah chofshit*). Rashi explains that God withdrew the opportunity for repentance as a punishment for Sihon's prior wickedness. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Teshuvah* 6:3) uses this as one of several cases illustrating the forfeiture of free will through persistent sin.

## Halakhic Significance

The prohibitions against attacking Edom, Moab, and Ammon are codified by the Rambam (*Hilkhot Melakhim* 5:4). These territorial restrictions define the halakhic boundaries of permissible warfare. The Sefer HaChinuch (Mitzvah 563-565) counts three separate negative commandments from this chapter.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is part of Parashat **Devarim** (1:1-3:22). The recounting of the wilderness period resonates with the themes of *Shabbat Chazon*, as Israel's failures in the desert foreshadow the failures that would lead to the destruction of the Temple. The transition from wandering to conquest signals hope — even after prolonged exile, redemption comes.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Edom received its territory as merit for Esau's *kibbud av* (honoring his father); Moab and Ammon for Lot's loyalty to Abraham.
- **Ramban**: God's sovereignty over all nations' territories is a fundamental theological claim — "He gives it to whomever is right in His eyes."
- **Abarbanel**: The thirty-eight silent years reveal that Torah only records events with enduring moral or legal significance.
- **Malbim**: The hardening of Sihon's spirit was a natural consequence of his character, not an arbitrary divine act.
