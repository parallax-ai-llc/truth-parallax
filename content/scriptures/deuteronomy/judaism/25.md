## Summary

Deuteronomy 25 addresses judicial corporal punishment (*malkot*), limiting lashes to forty and requiring that the punishment be administered in the presence of the judge to prevent excess. The law of *yibbum* (levirate marriage) follows: when a man dies childless, his brother shall marry the widow to raise offspring in the deceased's name; if the brother refuses, the *chalitzah* ceremony releases both parties. The chapter mandates honest weights and measures, and concludes with the command to remember Amalek's attack on Israel's stragglers and to "blot out the memory of Amalek from under heaven."

The Talmud (*Makkot* 22b) reduces the maximum lashes from forty to thirty-nine — "forty less one" — and uses this as an example of how the Oral Torah interprets and modifies the Written Torah's apparent plain meaning.

## Key Verses

**Verse 25:2-3** — "The judge shall cause him to lie down and be beaten in his presence... Forty stripes he may give him, he shall not exceed." The Talmud (*Makkot* 22b) famously observes: "How foolish are people who stand for a Torah scroll but not for a Torah scholar — the Torah says forty and the Sages come and reduce it by one." This passage illustrates the authority of rabbinic interpretation.

**Verse 25:4** — "You shall not muzzle an ox when it treads out the grain." The Sifrei extends this prohibition to all working animals. The principle of allowing a laborer to eat from the produce he handles is derived analogically and applied to human workers as well (*Bava Metzia* 87a-92a).

**Verse 25:5-10** — The *yibbum/chalitzah* laws. The Mishnah (*Yevamot* 1:1-4:13) devotes extensive discussion to which brothers qualify, which widows are eligible, and the precise ritual of *chalitzah* — the widow removes the brother-in-law's shoe, spits before him, and declares: "So shall be done to the man who will not build up his brother's house." Ashkenazic practice universally prefers *chalitzah* over *yibbum*.

**Verse 25:17-19** — "Remember what Amalek did to you on the way as you came out of Egypt... you shall blot out the memory of Amalek." This creates a positive commandment to remember and a positive commandment to destroy — paradoxically, the memory must be preserved in order to fulfill the obligation of erasure. The Shulchan Arukh requires this passage to be read publicly on the Shabbat before Purim (*Shabbat Zachor*).

## Halakhic Significance

*Malkot* (corporal punishment) is codified in *Hilkhot Sanhedrin* 16-18. The *yibbum/chalitzah* laws are codified in *Even HaEzer* 156-169 and remain operative today through *chalitzah* proceedings in rabbinic courts. The laws of honest weights (*Choshen Mishpat* 231) prohibit keeping false measures even if one does not use them. The remembrance of Amalek generates the annual obligation of *Parshat Zachor*, which many authorities consider a Torah-level (*d'oraita*) obligation.

## Parashah Context

This chapter concludes Parashat **Ki Teitzei** (21:10-25:19). The command regarding Amalek at the parashah's end connects to the Haftarah — I Samuel 15, in which Saul's failure to destroy Amalek completely costs him the kingship. *Shabbat Zachor*, when Ki Teitzei's final verses are re-read, always precedes Purim, linking Haman (an Agagite, descendant of Amalek) to this ancient enmity.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The juxtaposition of honest weights with Amalek teaches that commercial dishonesty invites Amalek's attack — ethical failure brings military vulnerability.
- **Ramban**: *Yibbum* ensures the continuation of the deceased's name and portion in Israel — it is an act of *chesed* (lovingkindness) toward the dead.
- **Rambam**: The obligation to remember Amalek is fulfilled through verbal recitation — memory requires articulation, not merely thought.
- **Sforno**: The limitation of lashes preserves the criminal's dignity — even the guilty retain the image of God.
