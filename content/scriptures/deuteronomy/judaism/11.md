## Summary

Deuteronomy 11 concludes Moses' extended exhortation by contrasting the agricultural dependence of the Land of Israel with the irrigation-based agriculture of Egypt. The land drinks rain from heaven, and God's "eyes are upon it" — its prosperity depends directly on Israel's obedience. The chapter contains the second paragraph of the *Shema* (11:13-21), which links rainfall to the observance of commandments and warns of drought and exile as consequences of idolatry. It concludes with the command to place blessing and curse before the people on Mounts Gerizim and Ebal.

The Mishnah (*Ta'anit* 1:1) builds the entire halakhic framework of prayer for rain upon the theology of this chapter — that rain in the Land of Israel is a direct expression of divine providence conditioned on covenantal faithfulness.

## Key Verses

**Verse 11:10-12** — "For the land... is not like the land of Egypt... the land drinks water from the rain of heaven... the eyes of the LORD your God are always upon it." The Talmud (*Ta'anit* 10a) explains that dependence on rain — unlike Egypt's Nile — forces Israel into a constant relationship of prayer and trust with God.

**Verse 11:13-17** — The second paragraph of the *Shema*: "If you will diligently obey My commandments... I will give the rain of your land in its season, the early rain (*yoreh*) and the late rain (*malkosh*)." This passage establishes the theological principle of collective reward and punishment (*s'khar va-onesh*). The Rambam (*Hilkhot Teshuvah* 9:1) explains that material blessings facilitate Torah observance rather than constituting the ultimate reward.

**Verse 11:18-20** — Repetition of the commands for tefillin and mezuzah, closely paralleling 6:6-9. The Talmud (*Menachot* 43b) derives that both passages are included in the tefillin parchments, creating four sections (*parashiyot*) within the head tefillin.

**Verse 11:26-28** — "See, I set before you today a blessing and a curse — the blessing if you obey... the curse if you do not." This declaration previews the dramatic ceremony on Gerizim and Ebal (chapter 27) and frames the entire Deuteronomic covenant as a choice placed before the people.

## Halakhic Significance

The second paragraph of the Shema (11:13-21) is recited twice daily as part of *Keriat Shema*. Its inclusion in the *tefillin* and *mezuzah* parchments is codified in *Orach Chaim* 32 and *Yoreh De'ah* 288. The Talmud (*Berakhot* 13a) debates whether kavanah (focused intention) is required for this paragraph as it is for the first. The laws of prayer for rain (*Hilkhot Ta'aniyot*) are rooted in the conditional promise of 11:14.

## Parashah Context

The first part of this chapter concludes Parashat **Eikev** (7:12-11:25). Verse 11:26 begins Parashat **Re'eh** (11:26-16:17), which opens with the dramatic declaration of blessing and curse. The transition from Eikev to Re'eh marks a shift from theological exhortation to specific legislation — the general principles now yield to detailed commandments governing worship, diet, and social life.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The Land of Israel's dependence on rain is a spiritual advantage — it keeps the people attentive to God's will throughout the agricultural year.
- **Ramban**: The second Shema paragraph addresses the community collectively (plural verbs), while the first addresses each individual (singular).
- **Ibn Ezra**: Gerizim and Ebal represent the perpetual choice facing Israel — the mountains stand as permanent witnesses to the covenant.
- **Sforno**: Rain "in its season" means precisely when needed — divine providence is not random but calibrated to agricultural and spiritual cycles.
