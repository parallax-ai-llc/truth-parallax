## Summary

Deuteronomy 34, the Torah's final chapter, recounts the death and burial of Moses. He ascends Mount Nebo to the summit of Pisgah, and God shows him the entire Promised Land — from Gilead to Dan, Naphtali, Ephraim, Manasseh, Judah to the Western Sea, the Negev, and the plain of Jericho. God declares: "I have shown it to your eyes, but you shall not cross over there." Moses dies "at the mouth of the LORD" (*al pi Hashem*), and God buries him in a valley in the land of Moab — "no one knows his burial place to this day." Moses was 120 years old; his eye was undimmed and his vigor unabated. Israel mourned thirty days. Joshua, filled with the spirit of wisdom, assumed leadership. The Torah concludes: "There has not arisen a prophet in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face."

The question of who wrote these final verses — describing Moses' death — is one of the oldest in Jewish biblical interpretation. The Talmud (*Bava Batra* 15a) records two views: Rabbi Yehudah holds that Joshua wrote the last eight verses; Rabbi Shimon says Moses himself wrote them "in tears" (*b'dema*), transcribing God's dictation of his own death prophetically.

## Key Verses

**Verse 34:4** — "I have shown it to your eyes, but you shall not cross over there." The Midrash (*Devarim Rabbah* 11:10) records Moses' final plea and God's response: the land was shown to Moses in prophetic vision encompassing all future history — he saw every generation and its leaders, every generation and its scholars.

**Verse 34:5-6** — "So Moses the servant of the LORD died there... and He buried him in the valley in the land of Moab... and no one knows his burial place to this day." The expression *al pi Hashem* (literally "by the mouth of the LORD") is understood by the Talmud (*Mo'ed Katan* 28a) as *mitah bi-neshikah* — "death by a kiss," the most gentle and exalted form of departure from life. God Himself served as *chevra kadisha* (burial society), teaching that attending to the dead is among the highest acts of *chesed shel emet* (true lovingkindness).

**Verse 34:7** — "Moses was 120 years old when he died; his eye was not dim nor his natural force abated." The Midrash divides his life into three periods of forty years: forty in Egypt, forty in Midian, forty leading Israel. The number 120 became the traditional Jewish blessing for longevity — *ad me'ah v'esrim shanah* ("until 120 years").

**Verse 34:10** — "There has not arisen a prophet in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 7:6) codifies this as the seventh of the Thirteen Principles: Moses' prophecy is unique and unsurpassed — he received revelation while fully conscious, without intermediary, and with absolute clarity.

## Halakhic Significance

The Rambam's seventh Principle of Faith — the supremacy of Mosaic prophecy — is derived from 34:10 and is foundational to the immutability of Torah law. The hidden burial site (34:6) prevents the grave from becoming a site of worship, teaching that even the greatest prophet must not be deified. The thirty-day mourning period (34:8) is the source for *sheloshim*, the thirty-day mourning period observed after burial, codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 395.

## Parashah Context

This chapter concludes Parashat **V'Zot HaBrachah** (33:1-34:12) and the entire Torah. It is read on Simchat Torah, immediately followed by the reading of Genesis 1 — the Torah has no end and no beginning, forming an eternal cycle. The Sages instituted the practice of *chatan Torah* (bridegroom of the Torah) for the person called up for this final reading and *chatan Bereshit* for the first reading of Genesis, celebrating the seamless continuity of Torah.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Moses wrote these final verses in tears, recording his own death as God dictated — prophecy transcending personal experience.
- **Ramban**: The hidden burial prevents pilgrimage that might devolve into veneration — Moses serves God, not the reverse.
- **Rambam**: "No prophet like Moses" is a permanent theological principle — no future prophet can alter the Torah, for none will attain Moses' level of prophecy.
- **Ibn Ezra**: The phrase "to this day" suggests these verses were written by a later hand — a view the tradition reconciles through the two opinions in *Bava Batra* 15a.
