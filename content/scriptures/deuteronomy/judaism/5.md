## Summary

Deuteronomy 5 presents Moses' restatement of the *Aseret HaDibrot* (Ten Commandments) to the new generation standing at the plains of Moab. While closely paralleling the Exodus 20 version, several significant differences appear — most notably the Shabbat commandment, which here uses *shamor* ("observe/guard") rather than *zakhor* ("remember"). Moses emphasizes that the covenant at Horeb was made not only with the ancestors but with "us, the living, every one of us who is here today" (5:3). The chapter concludes with the people's fear at hearing God's voice and their request that Moses serve as intermediary.

The Talmud (*Shevuot* 20b) famously resolves the *zakhor/shamor* discrepancy by teaching that God uttered both words simultaneously — *b'dibbur echad ne'emru* — something the human mouth cannot say nor the human ear can hear, demonstrating the supernatural character of the Sinai revelation.

## Key Verses

**Verse 5:3** — "Not with our fathers did the LORD make this covenant, but with us, we who are here alive today." The Ramban explains that the covenant transcends the historical generation — it binds every subsequent generation of Israel as direct participants.

**Verse 5:12** — "*Shamor* (Observe) the Sabbath day to keep it holy." The Mekhilta and Talmud (*Berakhot* 20b) teach that *shamor* encompasses the negative prohibitions of Shabbat (the thirty-nine *melakhot*) while *zakhor* encompasses the positive observances (Kiddush, Shabbat meals). Women are obligated in Kiddush despite it being a time-bound positive commandment because *shamor* and *zakhor* were said as one.

**Verse 5:16** — "Honor your father and your mother... that it may go well with you." This version adds "that it may go well with you" (*u-lema'an yitav lakh*), absent from Exodus 20. The Talmud (*Kiddushin* 39b) uses this addition to discuss whether reward for mitzvot is granted in this world or the World to Come.

**Verse 5:25-26** — The people plead: "If we hear the voice of the LORD our God any more, we shall die." Rashi explains this was both a genuine fear and a failure of spiritual capacity — the people could not endure ongoing direct revelation.

## Halakhic Significance

The *Aseret HaDibrot* in this chapter undergird vast areas of halakhah. The Shabbat commandment alone generates the elaborate legal framework of the *Hilkhot Shabbat*. The command to honor parents is codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 240, specifying obligations of feeding, clothing, and escorting one's parents. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 8:1) derives from the Sinai experience the epistemological foundation for belief — Israel knows the Torah is true because they witnessed the revelation themselves.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is central to Parashat **Va'etchanan** (3:23-7:11). The repetition of the Ten Commandments at the threshold of the Promised Land underscores that entry into the land is conditional upon covenantal fidelity. The ancient custom of standing during the Torah reading of the *Aseret HaDibrot* was debated by the Sages; the Rambam ruled against it to avoid implying that these commandments are more important than the rest of the Torah.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: *Shamor* and *zakhor* were spoken simultaneously, a miracle unique to divine speech.
- **Ramban**: The differences between the Exodus and Deuteronomy versions reflect Moses' prophetic rewording, faithful to the original intent.
- **Ibn Ezra**: The variations are not contradictions but complementary perspectives — one records what God said, the other what Moses explained.
- **Abarbanel**: The people's request for an intermediary was spiritually regrettable — direct revelation was the ideal.
