## Summary

Deuteronomy 6 contains the *Shema Yisrael* (6:4), the supreme declaration of Jewish faith and the centerpiece of daily liturgy. Moses commands Israel to love God "with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your might" (*b'khol levavekha, u-v'khol nafshekha, u-v'khol me'odekha*), and to teach these words diligently to their children. The chapter prescribes binding them as a sign upon the hand and between the eyes (tefillin) and writing them upon the doorposts (mezuzah). It concludes with warnings against forgetting God amid the prosperity of the Promised Land.

The Mishnah (*Berakhot* 1:1) opens with the question "From what time may one recite the Shema in the evening?" — making this verse the starting point of the entire Oral Torah as arranged in the Mishnah. The Talmud (*Berakhot* 61b) records that Rabbi Akiva died reciting the Shema, prolonging the word *echad* ("One") as his soul departed.

## Key Verses

**Verse 6:4** — "*Shema Yisrael, Hashem Elokeinu, Hashem Echad* — Hear, O Israel, the LORD our God, the LORD is One." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Keriat Shema* 1:2) rules that this verse must be recited with focused intention (*kavanah*) on the unity and sovereignty of God. The enlarged letters *ayin* and *dalet* in the Torah scroll spell *ed* ("witness") — Israel bears witness to God's oneness.

**Verse 6:5** — "And you shall love the LORD your God with all your heart." The Sifrei explains: "with all your heart" — with both inclinations, the good (*yetzer ha-tov*) and the evil (*yetzer ha-ra*); "with all your soul" — even if He takes your life; "with all your might" — with all your wealth, or alternatively, whatever measure He metes out to you, acknowledge Him.

**Verse 6:7** — "And you shall teach them diligently (*v'shinantam*) to your children." Rashi, citing the Sifrei, interprets *v'shinantam* as "sharpen them" — Torah should be so clear in your mouth that if asked, you do not stammer but answer immediately.

**Verse 6:16** — "You shall not test the LORD your God as you tested Him at Massah." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 10:5) derives from this a prohibition against testing prophets excessively, and against demanding supernatural proof of God's providence.

## Halakhic Significance

This chapter is the source for multiple daily obligations: the recitation of the *Shema* twice daily (*Berakhot* 1:1-2), the wearing of *tefillin* (codified in *Orach Chaim* 25-37), the affixing of *mezuzot* (*Yoreh De'ah* 285-291), and the fundamental commandment of *chinukh* — educating one's children in Torah. The Shulchan Arukh devotes extensive sections to the laws derived from these few verses, reflecting their extraordinary halakhic density.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is the theological heart of Parashat **Va'etchanan** (3:23-7:11). The Shema serves as the bridge between the Sinai revelation recounted in chapter 5 and the practical instructions for life in the land that follow. On *Shabbat Nachamu*, the reading of Va'etchanan offers consolation: the God who is One is also the God who redeems and restores.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: *V'shinantam* — words of Torah must be sharpened in the mouth, ready for immediate and precise articulation.
- **Ramban**: "With all your might" refers to loving God regardless of one's material condition — whether blessed with wealth or tested with poverty.
- **Rambam**: The commandment to love God is fulfilled through contemplation of His works and Torah, which naturally inspire love.
- **Maharal**: The Shema is recited with the hand covering the eyes to remove all sensory distraction, directing consciousness entirely toward the Divine.
