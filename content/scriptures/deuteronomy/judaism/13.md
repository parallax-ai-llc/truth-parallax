## Summary

Deuteronomy 13 addresses three increasingly intimate scenarios of enticement to idolatry: the false prophet who performs signs and wonders, the close relative or friend who secretly entices (*meisit*), and the subverted city (*ir ha-niddachat*) whose inhabitants have turned to idol worship. In each case, the Torah demands uncompromising rejection — the false prophet is executed, the secret enticer receives no pity, and the apostate city is destroyed entirely. The chapter establishes that no miracle or personal bond may override fidelity to the God of Israel.

The Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 8:2-3) derives from this chapter the principle that miracles cannot validate false theology — the Torah given at Sinai is the ultimate criterion, and any prophet who contradicts it, regardless of signs, is false.

## Key Verses

**Verse 13:2-4** — "If a prophet arises among you and gives you a sign or wonder, and the sign comes to pass, and he says 'Let us follow other gods'... you shall not listen." The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 90a) emphasizes that even a verified miracle does not authorize deviation from Torah. God permits such tests to reveal what is in the people's hearts.

**Verse 13:7** — "If your brother, the son of your mother, or your son... or your wife... or your friend who is as your own soul, entices you secretly..." The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 85b) derives from the ascending intimacy of these relationships that the prohibition applies regardless of the closeness of the bond — love of God supersedes all human attachment.

**Verse 13:9** — "You shall not consent to him nor listen to him; your eye shall not pity him." The Rambam codifies this as a distinct prohibition — one may not advocate, defend, or express sympathy for the enticer (*meisit*) in court. The normal rules of judicial mercy are suspended.

**Verse 13:13-17** — The *ir ha-niddachat* (subverted city): if an entire city turns to idolatry, it must be destroyed and never rebuilt. The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 71a) records Rabbi Eliezer's opinion that such a city never existed and was written "to expound and receive reward" — the law is theoretical, teaching the severity of communal idolatry.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of the false prophet (*navi sheker*) are codified by the Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 9:1-5), establishing criteria for distinguishing true from false prophecy. The *meisit* (enticer) occupies a unique position in halakhah — the Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 29a) permits entrapment to catch him, an exception to normal judicial procedure. The *ir ha-niddachat* laws are codified in *Hilkhot Avodah Zarah* 4:1-6 with numerous qualifying conditions that make the law practically unenforceable.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Re'eh** (11:26-16:17). After the positive command to centralize worship (chapter 12), this chapter addresses the negative: what happens when worship goes astray. The three scenarios — prophet, intimate, city — move from public deception to private temptation to communal corruption, covering all vectors through which idolatry might infiltrate.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The false prophet's sign actually comes true — God permits it as a test of faith, not as validation of the message.
- **Ramban**: The Torah enumerates relatives and friends to emphasize that no human relationship may override divine loyalty.
- **Rambam**: The Sinai revelation is the epistemological foundation that trumps all subsequent miracles — what was seen by 600,000 cannot be overturned by what is seen by one.
- **Sefer HaChinukh**: The severity of these laws reflects the existential threat idolatry poses to Israel's covenantal identity.
