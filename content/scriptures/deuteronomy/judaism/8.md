## Summary

Deuteronomy 8 warns Israel against the spiritual danger of prosperity. Moses reminds the people that God humbled them in the wilderness, feeding them *manna* to teach that "man does not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceeds from the mouth of the LORD" (8:3). He describes the goodness of the Promised Land — its wheat, barley, vines, figs, pomegranates, olive oil, and honey — and commands them to bless God after eating. The chapter concludes with a severe warning: if Israel forgets God and attributes its wealth to its own power, it will perish like the nations before it.

The Talmud (*Berakhot* 48b) derives the obligation of *Birkat HaMazon* (Grace After Meals) from verse 10: "And you shall eat and be satisfied and bless the LORD your God." This is one of the few positive commandments whose biblical source is universally agreed upon.

## Key Verses

**Verse 8:2** — "Remember the entire path on which the LORD your God led you these forty years in the wilderness, to humble you, to test you, to know what is in your heart." The Ramban explains that the "test" was not for God's knowledge (He already knows) but to actualize the potential for faithfulness within the people.

**Verse 8:3** — "He fed you manna... that He might make you know that man does not live by bread alone." Rashi explains that the manna's daily arrival taught trust — one could not hoard, and each day required renewed faith. The Sifrei reads this as the Torah's endorsement of spiritual sustenance over material security.

**Verse 8:8** — "A land of wheat and barley, and vines and fig trees and pomegranates; a land of olive oil and honey." These are the *shiv'at ha-minim* (seven species) of the Land of Israel. The Talmud (*Berakhot* 41b) establishes that these species take precedence in blessings, and the Mishnah (*Bikkurim* 1:3) specifies that only these species are brought as first fruits.

**Verse 8:10** — "And you shall eat and be satisfied and bless the LORD your God for the good land He has given you." This is the Torah source for *Birkat HaMazon*. The Talmud (*Berakhot* 48b) attributes the four blessings of the Grace to Moses, Joshua, David and Solomon, and the Sages of Yavneh, respectively.

## Halakhic Significance

*Birkat HaMazon* (8:10) is among the most practiced daily commandments. The Shulchan Arukh (*Orach Chaim* 184-201) devotes extensive treatment to its laws — when it is obligatory, its text, and the requirement of a *zimun* (quorum invitation) when three or more eat together. The seven species (8:8) determine the special *berakhah acharonah* (concluding blessing) known as *al ha-michyah* and *al ha-etz*.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Eikev** (7:12-11:25). The theme of Eikev is the conditional nature of blessing: prosperity follows obedience, but prosperity itself becomes a test. The Torah's concern that wealth leads to spiritual amnesia (*v'ramah levavekha v'shakhachta*, "your heart will grow haughty and you will forget," 8:14) is a leitmotif of rabbinic ethical literature (*mussar*).

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The manna fell daily to cultivate dependence on God — those who trusted would find it at their doorstep; those who doubted had to search.
- **Ramban**: "Not by bread alone" means that God can sustain life through any means, not only natural ones — the manna proved this.
- **Sforno**: The warning against self-attribution of wealth ("my power and the might of my hand," 8:17) addresses the perennial human tendency toward arrogance.
- **Kli Yakar**: The seven species symbolize the spiritual abundance of the land — each corresponds to a dimension of Torah service.
