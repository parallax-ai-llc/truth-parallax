## Summary

Deuteronomy 3 recounts the defeat of Og, king of Bashan, and the allocation of the Transjordan territories to the tribes of Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh. The chapter reaches its emotional climax when Moses describes his plea to God to be allowed to enter the Promised Land — a prayer that God firmly denies. Moses is permitted only to view the land from the summit of Pisgah.

The Midrash (*Devarim Rabbah* 2:5) records that Moses offered 515 prayers to enter the Land — the numerical value (*gematria*) of *va'etchanan* ("and I pleaded"). God stopped him because had Moses prayed once more, the prayer would have been answered, and the divine decree would have been overturned.

## Key Verses

**Verse 3:2** — "Do not fear him, for I have delivered him into your hand." Rashi, citing the Midrash (*Niddah* 61a), explains that Moses feared Og because Og had once informed Abraham that Lot was captured (Genesis 14:13), and this ancient merit might protect him. God reassured Moses that Og's merit was exhausted.

**Verse 3:11** — "For only Og king of Bashan remained of the remnant of the giants (*Refa'im*)." The Talmud (*Berakhot* 54b) describes Og's enormous bedstead of iron, nine cubits long. The Aggadah uses Og's gigantic stature as a symbol of seemingly insurmountable obstacles that fall before divine power.

**Verse 3:23-25** — Moses' personal plea: "O Lord GOD, You have begun to show Your servant Your greatness... Let me cross over and see the good land." The Sifrei notes that Moses did not invoke his own merits but appealed to God's grace (*matnat chinam*). This teaches that even the righteous rely on divine mercy, not personal achievement.

**Verse 3:26** — "But the LORD was angry with me on your account (*lema'anchem*) and would not listen to me." Rashi interprets this as Moses attributing the decree to Israel's sins at the waters of Meribah. The Ramban disagrees, arguing Moses accepted personal responsibility but softened the statement out of love for the people.

## Halakhic Significance

The allocation of Transjordan land (3:12-17) raises halakhic questions about the sanctity of territory east of the Jordan. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Terumot* 1:2-3) distinguishes between the *kedushah* (holiness) of the land west of the Jordan, which was sanctified permanently by Ezra's return, and the Transjordan, whose sanctity is debated. The obligation of the eastern tribes to fight alongside their brothers (3:18-20) establishes a principle of communal solidarity (*arvut*) in Jewish law.

## Parashah Context

Chapter 3 concludes Parashat **Devarim** (1:1-3:22). Moses' denied prayer bridges into Parashat **Va'etchanan** (3:23-7:11), which opens with this very plea. The transition from historical recounting to personal supplication shifts the tone of the book from narrative to the deeply personal voice of a leader confronting mortality while entrusting his people to God's care.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Moses feared Og's ancient merit; God responded that no earthly power can withstand divine will.
- **Ramban**: Moses' prayer to enter the Land was motivated by the desire to fulfill mitzvot that can only be performed there (*mitzvot ha-teluyot ba'aretz*).
- **Sforno**: The denial of Moses' prayer teaches that God's justice, once decreed, may be tempered by mercy but not annulled.
- **Kli Yakar**: Moses used the word *na* ("please") — the language of a servant, not a demand — modeling the proper posture of prayer.
