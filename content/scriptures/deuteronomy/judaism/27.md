## Summary

Deuteronomy 27 commands Israel to erect large plastered stones inscribed with the Torah upon crossing the Jordan, and to build an altar of uncut stones on Mount Ebal for burnt offerings and peace offerings. The twelve tribes are divided between Mounts Gerizim (for blessing) and Ebal (for curse). The Levites then pronounce twelve curses (*arur*) — each targeting a specific sin committed in secret — and the people respond "Amen" to each. The curses address idolatry, dishonoring parents, moving boundaries, misleading the blind, perverting justice for the vulnerable, sexual transgressions, secret murder, and taking bribes. The chapter concludes with a comprehensive curse upon anyone who does not uphold the words of the Torah.

The Talmud (*Sotah* 32a-37b) describes the Gerizim-Ebal ceremony in elaborate detail, including the arrangement of the tribes, the way the Levites turned to face each mountain, and the writing of the Torah in seventy languages on the plastered stones.

## Key Verses

**Verse 27:5-6** — "You shall build the altar of the LORD your God of whole (*shelemot*) stones; you shall not wield any iron tool upon them." The Mishnah (*Middot* 3:4) applies this principle to the Temple altar — iron, the metal of weapons, is excluded because the altar promotes peace. The Ramban sees the uncut stones as preserving the natural state of creation in divine service.

**Verse 27:15** — "Cursed is the man who makes a carved or molten image... and sets it up in secret." The Rambam notes that the twelve curses specifically target sins done in hiding — the public sins are addressed by the court; these curses invoke divine punishment for sins beyond human detection.

**Verse 27:18** — "Cursed is he who misleads a blind person on the road." The Talmud (*Mo'ed Katan* 17a) extends "blind" to include the figuratively blind — one who gives misleading advice to someone ignorant of a matter violates this principle (*lifnei iver lo titen mikhshol*).

**Verse 27:26** — "Cursed is he who does not uphold the words of this Torah to do them." The Ramban interprets this as a comprehensive obligation — one must affirm the truth and validity of the entire Torah, even commandments one cannot personally fulfill. The Talmud Yerushalmi (*Sotah* 7:4) understands this as encompassing the obligation to study, teach, observe, and perform.

## Halakhic Significance

The prohibition of using iron on the altar stones is codified in *Hilkhot Beit HaBechirah* 1:15. The principle of *lifnei iver* (placing a stumbling block before the blind, derived from Leviticus 19:14 and reinforced by 27:18) is among the most broadly applied prohibitions in halakhah — the Shulchan Arukh invokes it in contexts ranging from serving wine to a Nazirite to providing harmful business advice. The twelve curses function as a public acceptance of divine justice for hidden sins.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Ki Tavo** (26:1-29:8). The Gerizim-Ebal ceremony is the dramatic centerpiece of the covenant renewal — the blessings of chapter 28 follow directly from this framework. Ki Tavo is traditionally read in the weeks before Rosh Hashanah, and the curses serve as a sobering prelude to the season of repentance (*teshuvah*).

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The curses target hidden sins because public sins are punished by the court — these curses address what escapes human justice.
- **Ramban**: The final curse (27:26) encompasses the entire Torah — whoever denies even one commandment's divine origin falls under this curse.
- **Sforno**: The altar of whole stones symbolizes wholeness (*shleimut*) — divine service requires integrity, not human embellishment.
- **Abarbanel**: The Gerizim-Ebal ceremony was a national covenant renewal distinct from Sinai — each generation must formally accept the Torah's obligations.
