## Summary

Deuteronomy 21 addresses several distinct but thematically linked laws. The *eglah arufah* (heifer whose neck is broken) ritual responds to an unsolved murder found in the open field — the elders of the nearest city must declare their innocence of the blood. The chapter then addresses the captive woman (*eshet yefat to'ar*), permitting marriage under specific conditions. The law of the firstborn's double portion protects the rights of a less-favored wife's son. The rebellious son (*ben sorer u-moreh*) who will not obey his parents is brought before the elders. The chapter concludes with the requirement to bury the executed criminal before nightfall — "for a hanging body is a curse of God" (21:23).

The Talmud (*Sotah* 45b-47a) extensively discusses the *eglah arufah* ceremony, noting that it ceased when murderers multiplied — its purpose was to arouse communal conscience, which became untenable in lawless times.

## Key Verses

**Verse 21:1-9** — The *eglah arufah* ceremony. The elders wash their hands and declare: "Our hands did not shed this blood, nor did our eyes see it." The Mishnah (*Sotah* 9:6) asks: Would we suspect the elders of murder? Rather, they declare that they did not see the victim and dismiss him without food or escort — the community bears responsibility for failing to provide hospitality and protection.

**Verse 21:11-14** — The captive woman: if a soldier desires a woman taken in war, she must shave her head, trim her nails, and mourn for thirty days before he may marry her. Rashi explains this as a concession to the evil inclination (*yetzer ha-ra*) — the Torah provides a legal framework rather than prohibiting what men will do anyway. The juxtaposition with the "hated wife" and rebellious son teaches that such marriages lead to domestic strife.

**Verse 21:17** — "He shall acknowledge the firstborn... by giving him a double portion." The Talmud (*Bava Batra* 123a) derives from this the laws of *bechor* (firstborn) inheritance, codified extensively in *Choshen Mishpat* 278.

**Verse 21:18-21** — The *ben sorer u-moreh* (rebellious son). The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 71a) records the opinion that this law was never implemented and was written "to expound and receive reward." The stringent requirements — both parents must agree, have identical voices, and appear identical — make its application virtually impossible.

## Halakhic Significance

The *eglah arufah* is codified in *Hilkhot Rotze'ach* 9:1-10. The captive woman laws appear in *Hilkhot Melakhim* 8:2-9. The firstborn inheritance laws are codified in *Choshen Mishpat* 278 and generate the principle that a father may not disinherit a firstborn in favor of a preferred son. The *ben sorer u-moreh* is codified in *Hilkhot Mamrim* 7:1-8 with conditions so restrictive that the Talmud considers the case theoretical.

## Parashah Context

Verses 21:1-9 conclude Parashat **Shoftim** (16:18-21:9). Verse 21:10 begins Parashat **Ki Teitzei** (21:10-25:19), which contains more individual commandments (74) than any other parashah in the Torah. The transition from *eglah arufah* to *eshet yefat to'ar* shifts from communal responsibility to individual moral regulation — from the public sphere to the domestic.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The Torah's juxtaposition (captive woman, hated wife, rebellious son) traces a chain of consequences — one sin leads to another.
- **Ramban**: The *eglah arufah* ceremony is an atonement for the land, which is polluted by innocent blood.
- **Rambam**: The rebellious son is judged for his future trajectory (*niddon al shem sofo*) — a rare case of preemptive justice.
- **Sforno**: The burial requirement before nightfall reflects the dignity inherent in every human being, created in God's image.
