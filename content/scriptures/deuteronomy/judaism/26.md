## Summary

Deuteronomy 26 prescribes two liturgical declarations that anchor Israel's religious life in historical memory and ethical accountability. The *mikra bikkurim* (first fruits declaration, 26:5-10) begins with "A wandering Aramean was my father" (*Arami oved avi*) and recounts the descent into Egypt, the oppression, the exodus, and the arrival in the land. The *vidui ma'aser* (tithe confession, 26:12-15) is a declaration that one has properly distributed the required tithes to Levites, strangers, orphans, and widows. The chapter concludes with the mutual covenant: "Today the LORD has declared you... and you have declared the LORD" — a reciprocal commitment between God and Israel.

The Haggadah shel Pesach takes the *mikra bikkurim* passage (26:5-8) as the core text for the Seder's narrative — "Go and learn what Laban the Aramean sought to do to our father Jacob." The entire Pesach storytelling obligation is built upon this Deuteronomic declaration.

## Key Verses

**Verse 26:5** — "*Arami oved avi* — A wandering Aramean was my father." Rashi, following the Haggadah's interpretation, reads this as "An Aramean (Laban) sought to destroy my father (Jacob)." Ibn Ezra reads it literally: "My father was a wandering Aramean" — Jacob was a nomad before settling in Egypt. Both readings are preserved in the tradition.

**Verse 26:8** — "The LORD brought us out of Egypt with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm, with great terror, with signs and wonders." The Haggadah expounds each phrase: "mighty hand" refers to the plague of *dever* (pestilence); "outstretched arm" refers to the sword; "great terror" refers to the revelation of the *Shechinah*.

**Verse 26:13-14** — "I have removed the sacred portion from my house... I have not eaten of it in mourning (*b'oni*)... I have not given of it for the dead." The Talmud (*Ma'aser Sheni* 5:10-12) derives from this "confession" that tithes become ritually disqualified if handled during mourning or used for funerary purposes.

**Verse 26:17-18** — "Today you have declared the LORD to be your God... and the LORD has declared you to be His treasured people." The Talmud (*Berakhot* 6a) reads this as a mutual declaration of uniqueness — God says: "You have made Me a unique entity in the world, and I shall make you a unique entity in the world."

## Halakhic Significance

The *bikkurim* (first fruits) laws are codified in *Hilkhot Bikkurim* 1-4, including the requirement that the declaration be recited in Hebrew. The *vidui ma'aser* is recited at the conclusion of the third and sixth years of the *shemittah* cycle, codified in *Hilkhot Ma'aser Sheni* 11:1-6. The Haggadah's use of 26:5-8 as the Seder's narrative backbone gives these verses a living liturgical presence in every generation.

## Parashah Context

This chapter opens Parashat **Ki Tavo** (26:1-29:8). The first fruits declaration and tithe confession provide a fitting opening for a parashah that will culminate in the blessings and curses on Mounts Gerizim and Ebal. The liturgical recitations of chapter 26 remind Israel that possession of the land is contingent on remembering the past and fulfilling ethical obligations in the present.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: *Arami oved avi* — Laban sought to uproot everything, making him worse than Pharaoh, who targeted only the males.
- **Ramban**: The first fruits declaration is a formal act of gratitude that transforms agricultural harvesting into a theological statement.
- **Rambam**: The tithe confession ensures that each person takes personal responsibility for the distribution of sacred portions — it cannot be delegated.
- **Sforno**: The mutual declaration (26:17-18) is the climax of Deuteronomy's covenant theology — both parties commit to exclusivity and faithfulness.
