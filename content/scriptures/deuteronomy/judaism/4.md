## Summary

Deuteronomy 4 marks a transition from historical recounting to urgent theological exhortation. Moses calls upon Israel to observe the statutes (*chukim*) and ordinances (*mishpatim*) faithfully, warning that deviation — especially the worship of graven images — will lead to exile and destruction. The chapter contains one of the Torah's most powerful statements of monotheism: "The LORD is God; there is none else" (4:35). Moses establishes three cities of refuge east of the Jordan, and the chapter closes with a superscription introducing the laws that follow.

Rashi notes that the injunction "do not add to it and do not diminish from it" (4:2) applies to the entirety of Torah legislation — one may not invent a fifth species for the *lulav* nor use only two *parashiyot* in the tefillin. The Ramban sees this chapter as the theological foundation for the entire book of Devarim, linking obedience to the covenant at Horeb.

## Key Verses

**Verse 4:6** — "For this is your wisdom and your understanding in the sight of the peoples." The Talmud (*Shabbat* 75a) applies this verse to the study of astronomy and the calculation of the calendar, which demonstrate Israel's intellectual stature among the nations.

**Verse 4:9** — "Only take heed and guard your soul diligently, lest you forget the things your eyes have seen." The Ramban derives from this verse a positive commandment to remember the Sinai revelation for all generations. The Talmud (*Berakhot* 21a) uses the language of *hishamer* ("take heed") to identify a negative prohibition against forgetting Torah.

**Verse 4:15** — "You saw no form (*temunah*) on the day the LORD spoke to you at Horeb out of the midst of the fire." This verse is the theological cornerstone of the prohibition against depicting God. The Rambam (*Moreh Nevukhim* 1:3) builds his entire doctrine of divine incorporeality upon this principle.

**Verse 4:35** — "You have been shown to know that the LORD is God; there is none else besides Him." This verse is chanted during the *Hakafot* on Simchat Torah. The Zohar (*Va'etchanan*) identifies it as a declaration of absolute divine unity encompassing all existence.

## Halakhic Significance

The command "do not add and do not diminish" (4:2) is counted by the Rambam as two separate commandments (*Sefer HaMitzvot*, Negative Commandments 313-314). The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 89a) applies this principle to prophets who claim to alter Torah law permanently. The designation of three cities of refuge in 4:41-43 initiates the halakhic framework of the *arei miklat*, codified in *Hilkhot Rotze'ach* 8:1.

## Parashah Context

This chapter opens Parashat **Va'etchanan** (3:23-7:11), one of the richest *parashot* in the Torah, containing the Shema, the Ten Commandments' repetition, and foundational theological principles. Va'etchanan is always read on *Shabbat Nachamu*, the Shabbat of consolation after Tisha B'Av, with the Haftarah from Isaiah 40: "Comfort, comfort My people."

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: "Do not add" — the prohibition includes adding a fifth compartment to tefillin or a fifth species to the lulav.
- **Ramban**: The command to remember Sinai is a distinct positive commandment, obligating every generation to transmit the experience.
- **Rambam**: The incorporeality of God, derived from verse 15, is the third of his Thirteen Principles of Faith.
- **Sforno**: Israel's wisdom is recognized by the nations only when the people actually observe the commandments — theory without practice earns no respect.
