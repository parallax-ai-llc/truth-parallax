## Summary

Deuteronomy 15 legislates the *shemittah* (sabbatical year) release of debts, commanding that every seventh year all outstanding loans between Israelites are cancelled. Moses warns against the "evil thought" (*davar beliya'al*) of refusing to lend as the sabbatical year approaches. The chapter declares the ideal — "there shall be no poor among you" (15:4) — while realistically acknowledging "the poor will not cease from the land" (15:11). It then addresses the release of Hebrew slaves (*eved ivri*) after six years and the consecration of firstborn animals.

Hillel's institution of the *prozbul* — a legal mechanism allowing debt collection after *shemittah* through court assignment — was created precisely to counteract the "evil thought" of withholding loans described in 15:9. The Mishnah (*Sheviit* 10:3-4) records this enactment as a paradigmatic exercise of rabbinic legislative authority (*tikkun ha-olam*).

## Key Verses

**Verse 15:4** — "There shall be no needy among you, for the LORD will surely bless you." The Sifrei reconciles this with verse 11 ("the poor shall not cease"): verse 4 is the ideal when Israel fulfills God's will; verse 11 is the reality when they do not.

**Verse 15:7-8** — "You shall not harden your heart nor shut your hand from your needy brother, but you shall open your hand wide to him." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Matanot Aniyim* 7:1-2) derives from this verse the fundamental obligation of *tzedakah* and codifies that giving grudgingly violates the spirit of the law.

**Verse 15:9** — "Beware lest there be an evil thought in your heart, saying, 'The seventh year, the year of release, is near,' and your eye be evil against your poor brother and you give him nothing." The Torah here legislates against a calculating mindset — the refusal to lend because the debt will be cancelled. This is one of the rare instances where the Torah explicitly addresses internal motivation.

**Verse 15:16-17** — If a slave declares "I love my master... I will not go free," his ear is pierced against the doorpost. Rashi, citing *Kiddushin* 22b: "The ear that heard at Sinai 'For the children of Israel are My servants' and yet went and acquired a master for himself — let it be pierced."

## Halakhic Significance

The *shemittah* release of debts is codified in *Hilkhot Shemittah V'Yovel* 9:1-4. The *prozbul* (Mishnah *Sheviit* 10:3) represents a landmark in halakhic jurisprudence — the Sages' power to enact measures that appear to circumvent biblical law for the welfare of society. The laws of the Hebrew slave (*eved ivri*) are codified in *Hilkhot Avadim* 1:1-3:12 and have not been operative since the Jubilee ceased.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Re'eh** (11:26-16:17). The economic legislation — debt release, generosity toward the poor, humane treatment of slaves — follows the religious and dietary laws of the preceding chapters, demonstrating that covenantal faithfulness encompasses social and economic justice alongside ritual observance.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The piercing of the slave's ear is a punishment for choosing human servitude over divine service heard at Sinai.
- **Ramban**: The debt release is an expression of trust in divine providence — God promises to compensate the lender's loss with blessing.
- **Rambam**: *Tzedakah* is not merely charity but an obligation of justice (*tzedek*), calibrated to each person's capacity to give and the recipient's actual need.
- **Abarbanel**: The tension between "no poor" (15:4) and "the poor will not cease" (15:11) reflects the gap between covenantal ideal and historical reality.
