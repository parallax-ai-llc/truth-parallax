## Summary

Deuteronomy 12 introduces the revolutionary requirement of centralized worship — sacrifices and offerings may be brought only to "the place that the LORD your God will choose" (*ha-makom asher yivchar Hashem*), which tradition identifies as Jerusalem. All Canaanite sacred sites must be destroyed. The chapter distinguishes between sacred slaughter (*shelamim*) at the central sanctuary and secular slaughter (*basar ta'avah* — meat of desire) permitted anywhere, provided the blood is poured out upon the ground. Israel is warned repeatedly against adopting Canaanite worship practices.

The Rambam (*Hilkhot Beit HaBechirah* 1:1) identifies the "chosen place" as the Temple Mount in Jerusalem, where the *Shechinah* rests. The Sifrei counts the phrase "the place that the LORD will choose" as appearing throughout Deuteronomy without naming Jerusalem explicitly — the Sages explain this was to prevent premature territorial disputes.

## Key Verses

**Verse 12:5** — "But to the place that the LORD your God will choose from all your tribes to put His name there — to His dwelling (*shikhno*) shall you seek and there shall you come." The Rambam derives from *tidreshu* ("you shall seek") the obligation to actively seek out the location of the Temple, not merely wait for it to be revealed.

**Verse 12:8** — "You shall not do according to all that we do here today, every man whatever is right in his own eyes." Rashi explains this refers to the permissibility of private altars (*bamot*) during the wilderness period, which would be abolished once the Temple was established. The Talmud (*Megillah* 9b-10a) traces the history of *bamot* through Shiloh, Nov, and Givon until their permanent prohibition with Solomon's Temple.

**Verse 12:20-21** — Permission for secular meat consumption when one lives far from the Temple. The Talmud (*Chullin* 16b-17a) derives the laws of *shechitah* (ritual slaughter) from the phrase "you shall slaughter... as I have commanded you" — indicating an oral tradition of slaughter technique not written in the Torah.

**Verse 12:23** — "Only be strong not to eat the blood, for the blood is the life (*nefesh*)." The prohibition of blood consumption is one of the Torah's most emphatic laws. The Sifrei notes the word "be strong" (*chazak*), suggesting that refraining from blood requires conscious effort because it was a common practice among surrounding nations.

## Halakhic Significance

The centralization of worship abolishes the system of private altars and establishes the halakhic framework for the *Beit HaMikdash* (Temple). The laws of *shechitah* — derived from verse 21's reference to an oral command — constitute one of the most elaborate areas of halakhah, codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 1-28. The distinction between sacred and secular slaughter (*Chullin* 2a) is foundational to the laws of kashrut.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Re'eh** (11:26-16:17), which transitions from theological exhortation to detailed legislation. The command to centralize worship is the organizing principle of Deuteronomy's legal code — the chosen place anchors Israel's religious life. Re'eh is typically read in the month of Elul, when themes of renewed commitment and proper worship prepare the community for the High Holidays.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: "Every man what is right in his own eyes" refers to the transitional period of *bamot*; once the Temple stands, individual discretion in sacrificial worship ceases.
- **Ramban**: The centralization of worship prevents the fragmentation of Israel's religious consciousness into local cults.
- **Rambam**: The phrase "as I have commanded you" proves the existence of the Oral Torah — the written text refers to laws transmitted orally.
- **Abarbanel**: The destruction of Canaanite sacred sites is not mere iconoclasm but a precondition for monotheistic worship in the land.
