## Summary

Deuteronomy 29 initiates the covenant renewal at the plains of Moab, distinct from the Sinai covenant. Moses addresses "all Israel" — leaders, elders, officers, men, women, children, and even the strangers within the camp — emphasizing that the covenant binds every member of the community. He recounts the wilderness miracles (clothes that did not wear out, bread and wine not consumed) and warns against the "root bearing poisonous and bitter fruit" — the individual who thinks he can privately defy the covenant while the community remains faithful. The chapter alludes to the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah as the paradigm of divine judgment, and concludes with the famous declaration: "The hidden things (*ha-nistarot*) belong to the LORD our God, but the revealed things (*ha-niglot*) belong to us and to our children forever."

The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 43b) derives from "the revealed things belong to us" the principle of collective responsibility (*arvut*) — once Israel crossed the Jordan, each individual became responsible for the public conduct of the community.

## Key Verses

**Verse 29:3** — "But the LORD has not given you a heart to know and eyes to see and ears to hear until this day." Rashi explains that Israel could not fully appreciate God's providence until the wilderness journey was complete — gratitude requires the perspective of hindsight.

**Verse 29:9-10** — "You are standing today, all of you, before the LORD your God — your leaders, your tribes, your elders... from the hewer of your wood to the drawer of your water." The Midrash (*Tanchuma, Nitzavim* 2) teaches that the covenant's inclusivity — from the highest leader to the most humble laborer — establishes the principle that all Israel shares equal standing before God.

**Verse 29:18** — "Lest there be among you a root bearing poison and wormwood (*rosh v'la'anah*)." The Ramban identifies this as the individual who practices idolatry secretly while the community is faithful — a cancer within the body of Israel that eventually destroys the whole.

**Verse 29:28** — "*Ha-nistarot la-Hashem Elokeinu, v'ha-niglot lanu u-l'vaneinu ad olam* — The hidden things belong to the LORD our God, but the revealed things belong to us and to our children forever." The dotted letters above *lanu u-l'vaneinu* in the Torah scroll indicate, according to the Talmud, that collective punishment for hidden sins was suspended until after the Jordan crossing — once in the land, communal responsibility for public sins became operative.

## Halakhic Significance

The principle of *arvut* (mutual responsibility) derived from 29:28 is foundational to Jewish communal halakhah — it is the basis for one person fulfilling a mitzvah obligation on behalf of another (*Rosh Hashanah* 29a). The Moab covenant's inclusion of all social classes (29:9-10) is cited by the Rambam (*Hilkhot Teshuvah* 3:11) as evidence that the covenant's obligations are universal within the Jewish people, regardless of status.

## Parashah Context

The first eight verses conclude Parashat **Ki Tavo** (26:1-29:8). Verse 29:9 begins Parashat **Nitzavim** (29:9-30:20), which is always read on the Shabbat before Rosh Hashanah. The opening word *nitzavim* ("you are standing") conveys resilience — despite the terrifying curses of chapter 28, Israel still stands before God. This message of endurance and renewal sets the tone for the High Holiday season.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: "Until this day" — only now, after forty years, does Israel possess the maturity to understand God's ways.
- **Ramban**: The "root bearing poison" refers to the secret idolater whose private sin ultimately corrupts the community.
- **Rambam**: *Arvut* (mutual responsibility) is the social consequence of covenant — each Jew is accountable for the spiritual welfare of fellow Jews.
- **Sforno**: The dotted letters on *lanu u-l'vaneinu* signal a transition — from individual to communal accountability upon entering the land.
