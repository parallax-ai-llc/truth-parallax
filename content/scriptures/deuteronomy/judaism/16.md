## Summary

Deuteronomy 16 legislates the three pilgrimage festivals (*shalosh regalim*): Pesach (Passover), Shavuot (Weeks), and Sukkot (Tabernacles). Each festival requires appearance before God at the chosen place, and "no one shall appear before the LORD empty-handed" (16:16) — each person must bring an offering proportionate to his blessing. The chapter concludes with the appointment of judges and officers in every city and the ringing declaration: "Justice, justice shall you pursue" (*tzedek tzedek tirdof*, 16:20). Asherah trees and sacred pillars are forbidden near God's altar.

The Talmud (*Chagigah* 2a) derives from the *re'iyah* (appearance) obligation that every male Israelite must ascend to Jerusalem three times a year, bringing an *olat re'iyah* (burnt offering of appearance) — a requirement that shaped the religious geography of ancient Israel.

## Key Verses

**Verse 16:1** — "Observe the month of Aviv (*chodesh ha-aviv*) and keep the Passover." The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 11b) derives from "the month of Aviv" the obligation to intercalate the calendar — adding a leap month when necessary to ensure Passover falls in spring, linking the lunar calendar to the agricultural season.

**Verse 16:3** — "You shall eat no leavened bread with it; seven days you shall eat *matzot* — bread of affliction (*lechem oni*)." The Talmud (*Pesachim* 36a) interprets *lechem oni* as also meaning "bread over which one recites many things (*she-onin alav devarim harbeh*)" — the matzah at the Seder is the vehicle for the Haggadah's narrative.

**Verse 16:16** — "Three times a year all your males shall appear before the LORD your God... and they shall not appear before the LORD empty-handed." The Mishnah (*Chagigah* 1:1) specifies the exemptions: the deaf, the mentally incompetent, minors, and those unable to ascend on foot.

**Verse 16:20** — "*Tzedek tzedek tirdof* — Justice, justice shall you pursue." The repetition of *tzedek* is explained by the Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 32b) as referring to seeking a qualified court — one should pursue the best available tribunal. Others read it as requiring just means as well as just ends.

## Halakhic Significance

The festival laws of this chapter are elaborated across tractates *Pesachim*, *Chagigah*, *Sukkah*, and *Beitzah*. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Chagigah* 1:1) codifies the obligation of pilgrimage, while *Hilkhot Chametz U-Matzah* addresses the Passover laws. The appointment of judges (16:18) is the basis for the Rambam's *Hilkhot Sanhedrin* 1:1, requiring courts in every city and district.

## Parashah Context

Verses 16:1-17 conclude Parashat **Re'eh** (11:26-16:17). Verse 16:18 begins Parashat **Shoftim** (16:18-21:9), which transitions from the festival calendar to the establishment of judicial and governmental institutions. The juxtaposition of festivals and justice teaches that a society's worship and its legal system are inseparable — both must reflect divine standards.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: *Lechem oni* — bread of affliction and bread of responsive discourse; the matzah serves dual functions at the Seder.
- **Ramban**: The pilgrimage festivals bind the nation together at the central sanctuary, preventing regional religious fragmentation.
- **Rambam**: "Justice, justice" — the pursuit of justice requires effort; it is not passive but demands active seeking of truth.
- **Sforno**: "Not empty-handed" — the offering reflects gratitude proportional to blessing, training the heart in recognition of divine generosity.
