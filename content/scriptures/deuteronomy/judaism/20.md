## Summary

Deuteronomy 20 establishes the Torah's laws of warfare. Before battle, the *kohen mashu'ach milchamah* (priest anointed for war) addresses the troops, urging them not to fear. Officers then announce four categories of exemption: one who has built a new house but not dedicated it, planted a vineyard but not enjoyed its fruit, betrothed a woman but not married her, or is simply fearful. The chapter distinguishes between *milchemet reshut* (optional war) and *milchemet mitzvah* (obligatory war), requiring a peace offer before besieging distant cities. Trees bearing fruit may not be cut down during a siege — "for is the tree of the field a man, that it should be besieged by you?" (20:19).

The Talmud (*Sotah* 42a-44b) elaborates extensively on the priest's address and the exemptions, developing a theology of warfare in which spiritual readiness is as important as military capacity.

## Key Verses

**Verse 20:1** — "When you go out to war against your enemies and see horses and chariots and an army greater than yours, do not be afraid of them, for the LORD your God is with you." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Melakhim* 7:15) codifies that fear in battle is a violation of a positive commandment — the soldier must trust in God's deliverance.

**Verse 20:5-8** — The four exemptions from military service. The Mishnah (*Sotah* 8:1-6) discusses whether these apply only to *milchemet reshut* or also to *milchemet mitzvah*. The "fearful and faint-hearted" exemption is debated: Rabbi Akiva takes it literally; Rabbi Yose HaGelili says it refers to one who fears because of his sins.

**Verse 20:10** — "When you approach a city to fight against it, you shall offer it peace (*shalom*)." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Melakhim* 6:1) extends this obligation to all wars, including the conquest of Canaan — peace must always be offered first. The Ramban disagrees, limiting the peace offer to optional wars.

**Verse 20:19** — "When you besiege a city... you shall not destroy its trees (*lo tashchit et etzah*)... for is the tree of the field a man?" The Talmud (*Bava Kamma* 91b) extends *bal tashchit* (the prohibition of wanton destruction) far beyond wartime — it prohibits all needless destruction of useful property.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of warfare are codified in *Hilkhot Melakhim* chapters 5-8. The principle of *bal tashchit* (20:19) has become one of Judaism's most broadly applied environmental principles, codified in *Hilkhot Melakhim* 6:8-10 and extended by the Sefer HaChinukh (Mitzvah 529) to include wasting food, tearing clothing, and demolishing buildings without purpose.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Shoftim** (16:18-21:9). The laws of warfare follow the institutional chapters (courts, kings, priests, prophets) and complete the picture of a society governed by Torah — even in war, Israel must act with restraint, compassion, and faith. The military exemptions reveal that personal fulfillment and family life take precedence over military ambition.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: "Is the tree of the field a man?" — the tree is innocent and should not suffer because of human conflict.
- **Ramban**: The peace offer demonstrates that Israel's wars are instruments of justice, not aggression.
- **Rambam**: *Bal tashchit* extends to all forms of wasteful destruction — it cultivates the trait of restraint and respect for creation.
- **Sforno**: The military exemptions ensure that soldiers fight wholeheartedly — those distracted by personal concerns would undermine morale and divine protection.
