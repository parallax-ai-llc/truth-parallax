## Summary

Deuteronomy 24 addresses the law of divorce (*get*), establishing that a man who sends away his wife with a document of severance may not remarry her if she has been married to another man in the interim. The chapter legislates a newlywed's exemption from military service for one year, prohibits taking a millstone as a pledge (it is essential for livelihood), and reiterates the prohibition of kidnapping. Laws protecting workers follow: wages must be paid on the same day, and the stranger, orphan, and widow must not be oppressed. The chapter concludes with the laws of *leket* (gleanings), *shikchah* (forgotten sheaves), and *pe'ah* (corner of the field) — portions left for the poor.

The Mishnah *Gittin* opens with the procedural laws of the *get* (bill of divorce), which the Talmud traces to the phrase "he writes her a document of severance (*sefer keritut*)" in verse 1. Jewish divorce law in its entirety flows from these few verses.

## Key Verses

**Verse 24:1** — "When a man takes a wife and marries her, and it comes to pass that she finds no favor in his eyes because he has found some unseemly thing (*ervat davar*) in her, he shall write her a document of severance." The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel (*Gittin* 90a) centers on *ervat davar*: Shammai requires sexual misconduct; Hillel permits divorce for any cause, even burning his food. Rabbi Akiva extends it further — even finding a more beautiful woman suffices.

**Verse 24:5** — "When a man takes a new wife, he shall not go out to war, nor shall any duty be imposed upon him; he shall be free for his house one year and shall cheer his wife." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Melakhim* 7:10) codifies this exemption for the first year of marriage.

**Verse 24:14-15** — "You shall not oppress a hired worker who is poor... On the same day you shall give him his wages." The Talmud (*Bava Metzia* 111a) derives the prohibitions of *bal talin* (withholding wages overnight) from this verse, establishing that delayed payment is a form of oppression.

**Verse 24:19** — "When you reap your harvest in your field and forget a sheaf... you shall not go back to get it; it shall be for the stranger, the orphan, and the widow." The Mishnah (*Pe'ah* 5-7) codifies the laws of *shikchah* — the forgotten sheaf belongs to the poor by divine mandate, not merely by charitable impulse.

## Halakhic Significance

The laws of *gittin* (divorce documents) are codified in *Even HaEzer* 119-154 and constitute one of the most technically demanding areas of halakhah — an improperly written *get* can result in *mamzerut* (illegitimacy) for future children. The *bal talin* prohibition (*Choshen Mishpat* 339) applies to all employment relationships. The agricultural gifts to the poor (*leket, shikchah, pe'ah*) are codified in *Hilkhot Matanot Aniyim* 1-6.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Ki Teitzei** (21:10-25:19). The laws here reflect the parashah's comprehensive concern for social justice — from the domestic sphere (divorce, newlyweds) to the economic (worker wages) to the agricultural (harvest gifts). Each law protects the vulnerable: the divorced woman, the new bride, the day laborer, and the landless poor.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: *Ervat davar* — the dispute between Shammai and Hillel reflects genuinely different readings of the phrase, both legitimate within the oral tradition.
- **Ramban**: The prohibition of remarrying a divorced wife after an intervening marriage prevents the commodification of marriage — women must not be "passed around."
- **Rambam**: Wages must be paid immediately because the poor worker "lifts his soul" (*noseh et nafsho*) toward his pay — delay causes genuine suffering.
- **Sforno**: The harvest gleanings train the landowner in generosity and remind him that the land's produce ultimately belongs to God.
