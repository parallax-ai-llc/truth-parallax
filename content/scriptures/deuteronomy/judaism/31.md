## Summary

Deuteronomy 31 narrates the formal transition of leadership from Moses to Joshua and the provisions for the Torah's perpetuation. Moses, 120 years old, tells the people he can "no longer go out and come in" and introduces Joshua as his successor. God commands the *Hakhel* assembly — every seven years, at Sukkot following the sabbatical year, the king must read the Torah aloud before the entire nation: men, women, children, and strangers. Moses writes the Torah and entrusts it to the Levitical priests, commanding them to place it beside the Ark of the Covenant as a witness (*ed*). God then warns Moses that Israel will indeed forsake the covenant, and He will "hide His face" (*hester panim*) — a terrifying theological concept. The Song of Moses (chapter 32) is introduced as a prophetic witness for future generations.

The concept of *hester panim* (divine concealment, 31:17-18) becomes one of the most discussed theological categories in Jewish thought, particularly in the context of suffering and the apparent absence of God during catastrophe.

## Key Verses

**Verse 31:6** — "Be strong and of good courage (*chizku v'imtzu*), do not fear nor be afraid of them, for the LORD your God goes with you; He will not fail you nor forsake you." This formula of encouragement recurs throughout the chapter (31:6, 7, 23) and becomes the liturgical basis for communal reassurance — the congregation blesses those facing difficulty with *chazak chazak*.

**Verse 31:10-13** — The *Hakhel* commandment: "At the end of every seven years... you shall read this Torah before all Israel in their hearing." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Chagigah* 3:1-6) codifies that the king reads from Deuteronomy on a wooden platform in the Temple courtyard. The Mishnah (*Sotah* 7:8) describes the ceremony in vivid detail.

**Verse 31:17-18** — "I will hide My face (*v'histarti panai*) from them... and I will surely hide My face (*haster astir panai*) on that day." The double concealment — *hester b'tokh hester* (hiddenness within hiddenness) — is interpreted by the Hasidic masters as the most severe form of divine absence: not only is God hidden, but the very fact of the hiding is hidden, so that people are unaware of their distance from God.

**Verse 31:19** — "Now write for yourselves this song and teach it to the children of Israel." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Sefer Torah* 7:1) derives from this verse the obligation upon every Jew to write a Torah scroll — though the verse refers to the Song (chapter 32), the Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 21b) extends it to the entire Torah.

## Halakhic Significance

The commandment to write a Torah scroll (31:19) is the 613th and final commandment according to the Rambam's enumeration. The *Hakhel* assembly (31:10-13) is codified in *Hilkhot Chagigah* 3:1-7 and was renewed in modern Israel as a public ceremony. The placement of the Torah beside the Ark (31:26) establishes the principle that the Torah serves as a permanent witness — the written text is the anchor of all subsequent interpretation.

## Parashah Context

This chapter constitutes Parashat **Vayelech** (31:1-30), the shortest parashah in the Torah. It is read either together with Nitzavim or on the Shabbat between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur (*Shabbat Shuvah*). The theme of transition — from Moses to Joshua, from the wilderness generation to the land generation — resonates powerfully during the season of *teshuvah*, when each person transitions from the old year to the new.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: "I can no longer go out and come in" — not physical infirmity, but the withdrawal of prophetic authority as leadership passes to Joshua.
- **Ramban**: The *Hakhel* assembly recreates the Sinai experience for each new generation — it is a covenant renewal ceremony.
- **Rambam**: The obligation to write a Torah scroll applies to every individual Jew, and one who writes it himself is considered as if he received it from Sinai.
- **Sefat Emet**: *Hester panim* is not punishment but a deeper mode of relationship — God hides so that Israel will seek Him.
