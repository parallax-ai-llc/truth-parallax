## Summary

Deuteronomy 33 contains *Birkat Moshe* — the Blessing of Moses — his final prophetic words addressed to each of the twelve tribes before his death. The blessing opens with a theophanic description of God's revelation at Sinai and His love for the people, declaring: "Moses commanded us the Torah, an inheritance (*morashah*) of the congregation of Jacob" (33:4). Moses then blesses each tribe individually — Reuben for survival, Judah for military success, Levi for priestly and teaching roles, Benjamin for divine protection, Joseph for agricultural bounty, Zebulun and Issachar for commercial and scholarly partnerships, Gad for valor, Dan for strength, Naphtali for divine favor, and Asher for prosperity. Simeon is notably absent from explicit mention. The chapter concludes with a hymn to God as Israel's eternal refuge.

The Talmud (*Berakhot* 11b) derives from verse 4 that the blessings before Torah study are biblically mandated — if Torah is Israel's "inheritance," then receiving it requires a formal blessing, as one blesses upon acquiring an inheritance.

## Key Verses

**Verse 33:1** — "And this is the blessing with which Moses, the man of God (*ish ha-Elohim*), blessed the children of Israel before his death." The title "man of God" appears here for the first time applied to Moses — the Midrash (*Devarim Rabbah* 11:4) explains that in his lower half he was a man (*ish*), in his upper half he was of God (*Elohim*), reflecting his unique status between heaven and earth.

**Verse 33:4** — "*Torah tzivah lanu Moshe, morashah kehillat Ya'akov* — Moses commanded us the Torah, an inheritance of the congregation of Jacob." This verse is traditionally among the first taught to children learning to speak. The Talmud (*Sukkah* 42a) records that a father must teach his child this verse as soon as the child can speak. The term *morashah* (inheritance) — not *yerushah* (bequest) — implies active acquisition, not passive reception.

**Verse 33:8-11** — The blessing of Levi: "Your *Thummim* and *Urim* belong to Your pious one... They shall teach Your ordinances (*mishpatekha*) to Jacob and Your Torah to Israel." The dual role of the Levites — *Urim v'Thummim* (oracular consultation) and Torah teaching — establishes the tribe as both ritual and educational leaders. The Rambam (*Hilkhot Shemittah V'Yovel* 13:12-13) extends the concept of Levi's consecration to any person who devotes himself entirely to God's service.

**Verse 33:27** — "*Me'onah Elohei kedem* — The eternal God is your dwelling place, and beneath are the everlasting arms." This verse is recited in the burial liturgy, affirming that even in death, God's presence sustains and shelters.

## Halakhic Significance

The obligation to teach children Torah (derived from *Torah tzivah lanu Moshe*, 33:4) is codified in *Hilkhot Talmud Torah* 1:1-3. The Rambam's extension of the Levitical ideal (any person who consecrates himself to Torah service) provides the halakhic basis for the exemption of Torah scholars from certain communal obligations. The blessings of the tribes inform the territorial allocations discussed in the book of Joshua and the halakhic questions of tribal identity.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **V'Zot HaBrachah** (33:1-34:12), the final parashah of the Torah, read on Simchat Torah. Unlike all other *parashot*, V'Zot HaBrachah is never read on a regular Shabbat — it is reserved for the festival celebrating the completion and renewal of the Torah cycle. Moses' blessings to the tribes parallel Jacob's blessings in Genesis 49, creating a literary frame encompassing the entire Torah.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Simeon is omitted because of the tribe's role in the sin of Ba'al Peor — but Moses alluded to Simeon within Judah's blessing, as Simeon's territory was within Judah's.
- **Ramban**: The blessings are prophetic, not merely wishful — each tribe's future is foreseen and affirmed by Moses' prophetic vision.
- **Rambam**: Levi's consecration is paradigmatic — anyone who separates himself for divine service "becomes sanctified as holy of holies."
- **Sforno**: "An inheritance of the congregation of Jacob" — the Torah belongs to the entire community, not to an elite; every Jew has an equal share.
