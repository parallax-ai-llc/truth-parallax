## Summary

Deuteronomy 10 recounts the carving of the second tablets and their placement in the Ark, the consecration of the tribe of Levi for divine service, and Moses' recollection of key wilderness events. The chapter reaches its rhetorical summit in verses 12-13 with the celebrated question: "And now, Israel, what does the LORD your God require of you?" The answer — to fear God, walk in His ways, love Him, serve Him, and keep His commandments — encapsulates the Torah's ethical and spiritual demands. Moses then calls for the "circumcision of the heart" (*u-maltem et orlat levavkhem*) and declares God's impartial justice toward the orphan, widow, and stranger.

The Talmud (*Berakhot* 33b) asks: Is fear of God (*yir'at Shamayim*) a small thing, that Moses says "what does the LORD require" as though it were easy? The Gemara answers: Yes, for Moses it was a small thing — and teaches that each person must strive until it becomes natural.

## Key Verses

**Verse 10:1-2** — "Carve for yourself two tablets of stone like the first... and I will write on the tablets the words that were on the first tablets, which you broke." The second tablets represent *teshuvah* — the possibility of restoration after rupture. The Midrash (*Shemot Rabbah* 46:1) teaches that the second tablets were even greater because they came with the gift of the Oral Torah.

**Verse 10:12** — "What does the LORD your God require of you? Only to fear the LORD your God." The Talmud (*Makkot* 24a) lists this verse among the attempts by prophets to distill the Torah's 613 commandments into core principles. Rashi sees *yir'ah* (fear/awe) as the foundation upon which all other observance is built.

**Verse 10:16** — "Circumcise the foreskin of your heart (*u-maltem et orlat levavkhem*) and stiffen your neck no more." This metaphor of inner transformation — removing the barrier (*orlah*) that prevents genuine spiritual receptivity — anticipates the promise in 30:6 that God will perform this circumcision Himself.

**Verse 10:18-19** — "He executes justice for the orphan and widow, and loves the stranger (*ger*), giving him food and clothing. You shall love the stranger, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt." The Talmud (*Bava Metzia* 59b) notes that the Torah commands love of the stranger in thirty-six places — more than any other commandment.

## Halakhic Significance

The designation of the Levites (10:8-9) for carrying the Ark, standing before God in service, and blessing in His name provides the halakhic basis for the Levitical and priestly roles codified by the Rambam (*Hilkhot Klei HaMikdash* 3:1). The commandment to love the stranger is counted as an independent positive commandment (*Sefer HaMitzvot*, Positive Commandment 207) and informs the extensive body of law protecting converts (*geirim*).

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Eikev** (7:12-11:25). The progression from the broken tablets (chapter 9) to the second tablets and ethical exhortation (chapter 10) mirrors the liturgical arc from Tisha B'Av to the season of repentance — destruction followed by renewal. The Sages connected the forty days Moses spent on Sinai receiving the second tablets to the period from Rosh Chodesh Elul through Yom Kippur.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: The second tablets came with the Oral Torah — what was lost in the breaking was restored and amplified.
- **Ramban**: "Circumcision of the heart" is a commandment to remove inner resistance to God's will through deliberate spiritual effort.
- **Rambam**: Fear of God is the gateway to love of God — one begins with awe and ascends to love through knowledge.
- **Malbim**: The five requirements of verse 12 (fear, walk, love, serve, keep) correspond to five ascending levels of religious consciousness.
