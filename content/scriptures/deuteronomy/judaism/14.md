## Summary

Deuteronomy 14 opens with the prohibition against self-laceration and shaving for the dead — practices associated with Canaanite mourning rites — grounding the prohibition in Israel's status as "children of the LORD your God" (*banim atem la-Hashem*). The chapter then restates the dietary laws (*kashrut*): permitted and forbidden animals, the signs of kosher land animals (split hooves, cud-chewing), kosher fish (fins and scales), forbidden birds, the prohibition of *neveilah* (unslaughtered carcasses), and the thrice-repeated ban on cooking a kid in its mother's milk. The chapter concludes with the laws of tithing — *ma'aser rishon* (first tithe), *ma'aser sheni* (second tithe), and *ma'aser ani* (poor tithe).

Rashi explains that the juxtaposition of mourning prohibitions with dietary laws teaches that both derive from Israel's holiness — "you are a holy people" connects physical conduct to spiritual identity.

## Key Verses

**Verse 14:1** — "You are children of the LORD your God; you shall not cut yourselves (*lo titgodedu*) nor make any baldness between your eyes for the dead." The Talmud (*Yevamot* 13b) extracts a secondary meaning: *lo titgodedu* — do not form factions (*agudot agudot*), a prohibition against sectarian fragmentation within a single community.

**Verse 14:3-20** — The dietary laws, paralleling Leviticus 11 with slight variations. The Ramban explains that forbidden animals correspond to spiritual qualities — predatory and scavenging animals reflect traits (*middot*) Israel must avoid internalizing. The Rambam (*Moreh Nevukhim* 3:48) offers a rationalist perspective: the laws promote physical health and self-discipline.

**Verse 14:21** — "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk." The Talmud (*Chullin* 115b) derives from its three appearances in the Torah three separate prohibitions: cooking, eating, and benefiting from the mixture of meat and milk.

**Verse 14:22-29** — The laws of tithing. *Ma'aser sheni* is consumed by the owner in Jerusalem; *ma'aser ani* is given to the poor in the third and sixth years of the *shemittah* cycle. The Talmud (*Ta'anit* 9a) plays on the word *aser* (tithe) and *ashir* (become wealthy): "Tithe so that you may become rich."

## Halakhic Significance

The dietary laws of this chapter, together with Leviticus 11, form the foundation of *kashrut* as codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 61-100 (animals), 79-86 (fish), and 87-97 (meat and milk). The tithing laws are elaborated in tractates *Ma'aserot* and *Ma'aser Sheni*. The Rambam codifies the mourning prohibitions in *Hilkhot Avodah Zarah* 12:13-14, and the *lo titgodedu* anti-factionalism principle in *Hilkhot Avodah Zarah* 12:14.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Re'eh** (11:26-16:17). The dietary and tithing laws follow the worship-centralization laws of chapter 12 and the anti-idolatry laws of chapter 13, forming a comprehensive program for holy living — what Israel eats, how Israel worships, and how Israel distributes its wealth all reflect covenantal identity.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Dietary prohibitions and mourning restrictions are linked by the concept of *kedushah* — Israel's holiness extends to both body and grief.
- **Ramban**: The forbidden animals embody spiritual coarseness (*gasut ha-ruach*) that would affect the soul of one who consumes them.
- **Rambam**: Dietary laws serve both rational hygienic purposes and the cultivation of self-control.
- **Sforno**: Tithing trains Israel to recognize that material wealth is a trust from God, not an absolute possession.
