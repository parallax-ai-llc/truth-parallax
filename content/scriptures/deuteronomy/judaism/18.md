## Summary

Deuteronomy 18 addresses the Levitical priests' inheritance — "the LORD is their inheritance" — specifying the portions they receive from sacrifices and agricultural gifts. The chapter then condemns a catalogue of occult practices prevalent among the Canaanites: divination (*kosem*), soothsaying (*me'onen*), sorcery (*mekhasheif*), charming (*chover chaver*), consulting mediums (*ov*), and necromancy (*doresh el ha-metim*). Israel is to be "wholehearted (*tamim*) with the LORD your God" (18:13). The chapter concludes with the promise that God will raise up a prophet like Moses from among Israel, and provides criteria for distinguishing true from false prophecy.

The Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 7:1-6) builds his entire framework for prophecy on this chapter, establishing that prophecy requires wisdom, moral perfection, and divine selection — it cannot be achieved by human effort alone.

## Key Verses

**Verse 18:10-11** — "There shall not be found among you one who passes his son or daughter through fire, a diviner, a soothsayer, a sorcerer, a charmer, one who consults a medium or a familiar spirit, or a necromancer." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Avodah Zarah* 11:1-16) codifies each of these as a separate prohibition. The Ramban views them as real spiritual forces that Israel is forbidden to access — the prohibition is not based on their inefficacy but on their theological danger.

**Verse 18:13** — "*Tamim tih'yeh im Hashem Elokekha* — You shall be wholehearted with the LORD your God." Rashi explains: walk with God in simple trust and do not inquire into the future; accept whatever comes from Him. The Ramban counts this as a positive commandment — to rely on God rather than on predictive systems.

**Verse 18:15** — "A prophet from your midst, from your brothers, like me, the LORD your God will raise up for you — to him you shall listen." The Rambam (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 7:1) identifies this as the Torah's mandate for the prophetic institution. The Sifrei explains "like me" as meaning "from Israel" rather than from any other nation.

**Verse 18:21-22** — "If you say in your heart, 'How shall we know the word which the LORD has not spoken?' — when a prophet speaks in the name of the LORD and the thing does not come to pass, that is the word the LORD has not spoken." This provides the empirical test for prophecy, though the Rambam notes that only negative prophecies are subject to this test — positive prophecies may be withheld due to repentance.

## Halakhic Significance

The priestly and Levitical gifts (*matanot kehunah*) are codified in *Hilkhot Bikkurim* 9-12. The prohibitions against occult practices generate eight separate negative commandments according to the Rambam's enumeration. The laws of prophecy (*Hilkhot Yesodei HaTorah* 7-10) define the criteria for true prophecy and the consequences for false prophecy — execution by the court.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Shoftim** (16:18-21:9). Following the institutions of courts and kings (chapters 16-17), this chapter addresses the priestly and prophetic institutions — completing the four pillars of Israelite governance. The Torah places prophecy after the prohibition of divination, presenting it as the legitimate alternative to occult knowledge-seeking.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: *Tamim* — walk with God in simplicity, trusting in His providence without attempting to peer behind the veil of the future.
- **Ramban**: Occult practices access real spiritual forces, which makes their prohibition all the more serious — Israel must relate to the supernatural only through Torah and prophecy.
- **Rambam**: Prophecy is the highest form of human intellectual and spiritual attainment, granted only to those who meet its rigorous prerequisites.
- **Sforno**: The promise of a "prophet like Moses" ensures that Israel will never be left without divine guidance — the chain of prophetic authority continues through each generation's scholars.
