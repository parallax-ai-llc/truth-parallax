## Summary

Deuteronomy 23 regulates admission into the *kahal Hashem* (congregation of the LORD). Certain categories of persons are excluded from the assembly: the *mamzer* (child of a forbidden union), Ammonites and Moabites (permanently), and Edomites and Egyptians (until the third generation). The chapter legislates ritual purity in the military camp, the prohibition against returning a runaway slave to his master, the ban on cult prostitution (*kedeshah/kadesh*), prohibitions on charging interest (*neshekh/tarbit*) to a fellow Israelite, and the obligation to fulfill vows promptly. The laws balance communal boundary-setting with remarkable protections for the vulnerable.

The Talmud (*Yevamot* 76b-78a) extensively discusses the categories of exclusion, with significant implications for conversion law and the status of various nations in subsequent halakhic history.

## Key Verses

**Verse 23:2-4** — "A *mamzer* shall not enter the assembly of the LORD... An Ammonite or Moabite shall not enter the assembly of the LORD... because they did not meet you with bread and water." The Mishnah (*Yevamot* 8:3) discusses the scope of these exclusions — they apply to marriage within the Jewish community, not to conversion itself. The case of Ruth the Moabite is reconciled by the Talmud (*Yevamot* 69a): "Moabite but not Moabitess" — the exclusion applies only to males.

**Verse 23:8** — "You shall not abhor an Edomite, for he is your brother; you shall not abhor an Egyptian, for you were a stranger in his land." Despite centuries of slavery, gratitude toward Egypt is commanded. Rashi explains that Egypt provided refuge during the famine, and this initial hospitality creates a permanent claim on Israel's gratitude.

**Verse 23:16** — "You shall not deliver a slave who has escaped from his master to you." The Ramban understands this as referring to a gentile slave who fled to the Land of Israel — he is freed and protected. This law stands in stark contrast to the fugitive slave laws of other ancient Near Eastern codes.

**Verse 23:20-21** — "You shall not charge interest to your brother... to a foreigner you may charge interest." The prohibition of *ribbis* (interest) among Israelites is a cornerstone of Jewish economic ethics. The Talmud (*Bava Metzia* 61a-75b) develops an elaborate body of law distinguishing between direct interest, indirect benefit, and even the appearance of interest (*ha'aramas ribbis*).

## Halakhic Significance

The *mamzer* laws are codified in *Even HaEzer* 4:1-13 and represent one of halakhah's most difficult areas, as the status is hereditary and perpetual. The prohibition of interest (*ribbis*) is codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 159-177 and remains fully operative — affecting modern banking, loans between Jews, and investment structures. The laws of vows (*nedarim*) are codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 203-235.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Ki Teitzei** (21:10-25:19). The laws of communal membership follow the domestic laws of chapter 22, expanding the scope from the household to the community. The prohibition of interest and the requirement to fulfill vows reflect Ki Teitzei's overarching concern with integrity — in relationships, in commerce, and before God.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Egypt deserves gratitude despite the slavery — the initial hospitality creates an obligation that transcends later mistreatment.
- **Ramban**: The runaway slave is protected because the Land of Israel provides sanctuary — its holiness extends even to the bondsman seeking freedom.
- **Rambam**: The prohibition of interest (*ribbis*) reflects the Torah's vision of an economy based on mutual aid rather than exploitation.
- **Sforno**: Vows must be fulfilled promptly because delay breeds negligence — the longer one waits, the less likely fulfillment becomes.
