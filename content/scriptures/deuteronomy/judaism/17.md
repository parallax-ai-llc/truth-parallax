## Summary

Deuteronomy 17 establishes the judicial hierarchy of Israel. Cases too difficult for local courts are referred to the central tribunal at the chosen place — the Great Sanhedrin (*Beit Din HaGadol*). The chapter mandates that capital punishment requires the testimony of at least two witnesses, and forbids the sacrifice of blemished animals. It then introduces the law of the king (*melekh*), permitting monarchy but constraining royal power: the king must not multiply horses, wives, or wealth, and must write a Torah scroll (*sefer Torah*) that stays with him always, "that his heart not be lifted above his brothers" (17:20).

The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 2a) derives from this chapter the structure of the 71-member Great Sanhedrin, the 23-member lesser Sanhedrin, and the authority of rabbinic courts. The Rambam regards the command to obey the Great Sanhedrin as the Torah's source for rabbinic authority in every generation.

## Key Verses

**Verse 17:6** — "By the mouth of two witnesses or three witnesses shall the one condemned to death be put to death; he shall not be put to death by the mouth of one witness." The requirement of multiple witnesses is a foundational principle of Jewish jurisprudence, codified throughout *Choshen Mishpat* and applied by extension to all monetary and personal-status matters.

**Verse 17:11** — "According to the Torah which they shall teach you and the judgment they shall tell you, you shall do; you shall not deviate from the thing they tell you, to the right or to the left." The Rambam (*Sefer HaMitzvot*, Positive Commandment 174) derives from this verse the obligation to obey the rulings of the highest rabbinic court. Rashi interprets "right or left" as: even if they tell you right is left and left is right, obey — their authority is binding.

**Verse 17:15** — "You shall surely set over you a king whom the LORD your God chooses; from among your brothers you shall set a king over you." The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 20b) records a dispute between Rabbi Yehudah (monarchy is a commandment) and Rabbi Nehorai (it is a concession to human weakness). The Rambam counts it as a positive commandment (*Hilkhot Melakhim* 1:1).

**Verse 17:18-19** — The king must write "a copy of this Torah (*mishneh ha-Torah hazot*) in a scroll... and he shall read in it all the days of his life." The Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 21b) specifies that the king carries two Torah scrolls — one that stays in his treasury and one that accompanies him everywhere.

## Halakhic Significance

The Great Sanhedrin's authority (17:8-13) is codified in *Hilkhot Mamrim* 1:1-2, establishing the binding nature of rabbinic enactments (*takkanot*) and interpretations. The laws of the king — limitations on horses, wives, and wealth — are codified in *Hilkhot Melakhim* 3:1-4. The two-witness requirement permeates Jewish civil and criminal law as codified in *Choshen Mishpat* 28-36.

## Parashah Context

This chapter is within Parashat **Shoftim** (16:18-21:9), which establishes Israel's institutional framework: courts, kings, priests, prophets, and military leadership. The progression from judges (16:18) to the supreme court (17:8-13) to the king (17:14-20) reflects a carefully calibrated system of distributed authority — no single institution holds unchecked power.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: "To the right or the left" — obey the Sanhedrin's rulings even when they appear counterintuitive; their authority is divinely mandated.
- **Ramban**: The king's Torah scroll is a personal obligation — he writes it himself (or has it written in his presence) to internalize its teachings.
- **Rambam**: Monarchy in Israel is a commandment, but the king is subject to Torah law and the Sanhedrin's authority.
- **Abarbanel**: Monarchy is a concession, not an ideal — the Torah's restrictions on royal power reveal ambivalence about human kingship.
