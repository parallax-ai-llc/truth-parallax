## Summary

Deuteronomy 32 contains the *Shirat Ha'azinu* — the Song of Moses — a sweeping poetic composition that calls heaven and earth as witnesses and surveys Israel's entire covenantal history: God's faithfulness, Israel's rebellion, divine punishment, and ultimate redemption. The Song opens with the metaphor of Torah as rain and dew, declares God "the Rock, His work is perfect" (*ha-Tzur tamim pa'alo*), recounts how God found Israel in the wilderness and nurtured them, describes Israel's ingratitude and idolatry, and concludes with God's vengeance upon Israel's enemies and the cleansing of the land. The chapter ends with Moses commanding the people to set their hearts to the Song's words and Moses' final instruction to ascend Mount Nebo.

The Torah scroll writes this Song in a distinctive two-column layout (*ariach al gabei levenah* — "a half-brick upon a whole brick"), distinguishing it visually from all other Torah text. The Talmud (*Rosh Hashanah* 31a) records that the Levites sang portions of Ha'azinu in the Temple during the Shabbat *musaf* service.

## Key Verses

**Verse 32:1** — "*Ha'azinu ha-shamayim va-adabera, v'tishma ha-aretz imrei fi* — Give ear, O heavens, and I will speak; and hear, O earth, the words of my mouth." The Sifrei contrasts Moses' "give ear, heavens" with Isaiah's "hear, O heavens" (Isaiah 1:2) — Moses, closer to heaven, uses the language of proximity (*ha'azinu*); Isaiah, on earth, uses the language of distance (*shim'u*).

**Verse 32:4** — "*Ha-Tzur tamim pa'alo, ki khol derakhav mishpat* — The Rock, His work is perfect, for all His ways are justice." This verse is the theological anchor of the Song — all apparent injustice is ultimately reconciled within God's perfect justice. It is recited at funerals as an affirmation of divine righteousness (*tzidduk ha-din*) in the face of death.

**Verse 32:7** — "Remember the days of old, consider the years of many generations; ask your father and he will tell you, your elders and they will say to you." The Ramban sees this as a commandment of historical consciousness — every generation must learn from the past through intergenerational transmission.

**Verse 32:39** — "See now that I, I am He, and there is no god beside Me; I kill and I make alive, I wound and I heal, and there is none that can deliver from My hand." This verse is incorporated into the *Ne'ilah* service on Yom Kippur, affirming God's absolute sovereignty at the moment of the sealing of judgment.

## Halakhic Significance

The Song's distinctive layout in the Torah scroll is a halakhic requirement — a scroll that writes Ha'azinu as regular prose is *pasul* (invalid), as codified in *Hilkhot Sefer Torah* 8:4. The Levitical singing of Ha'azinu during the Temple service (*Rosh Hashanah* 31a) divided the Song into six sections corresponding to six Shabbatot. The recitation of 32:4 at funerals (*tzidduk ha-din*) is codified in *Yoreh De'ah* 339:4.

## Parashah Context

This chapter constitutes the majority of Parashat **Ha'azinu** (32:1-52), always read between Rosh Hashanah and Sukkot. Its placement during the season of judgment is deliberate — the Song's themes of divine justice, human failure, and ultimate redemption mirror the spiritual trajectory of the High Holidays. The Haftarah for Ha'azinu is II Samuel 22, David's Song, creating a parallel between Moses' farewell and David's farewell.

## Classical Commentators

- **Rashi**: Heaven and earth are invoked as witnesses because they are eternal — unlike human witnesses, they cannot be disqualified or die.
- **Ramban**: The Song is a prophecy encompassing all of Jewish history — from the exodus through exile to the messianic redemption.
- **Ibn Ezra**: "The Rock" (*Tzur*) connotes stability and permanence — God is the unchanging foundation amid the flux of history.
- **Netziv**: Ha'azinu is structured as a *shirah* (song) because poetry conveys truths that prose cannot — emotional and spiritual realities require the language of art.
