## Various Laws: Assembly and Community

Deuteronomy 23 addresses who may enter the LORD's assembly, camp hygiene during warfare, the treatment of escaped slaves, prostitution, usury, and vows. The chapter reveals the standards of holiness and justice that govern Israel's communal and economic life.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Exclusions from the Assembly (23:1-8)

Certain categories of people are excluded from the LORD's assembly, while others (Edomites and Egyptians) may enter after the third generation:

- **Calvin** noted that these exclusions were provisional and typological — in Christ, all such barriers are removed (Isaiah 56:3-8; Acts 8:27-39, the Ethiopian eunuch)
- **Wright** emphasizes that the exclusions are national and cultic, not soteriological — foreigners could still worship the LORD
- Protestant theology reads these restrictions in light of the gospel's radical inclusion

### Camp Purity (23:9-14)

During military campaigns, the camp must be kept ritually clean because "the LORD your God walks in the midst of your camp":

- **Luther** drew from this the principle that God's presence demands holiness in every sphere — not only the sanctuary but the battlefield
- **Block** notes the theological reasoning: God's presence is not limited to sacred space

### The Escaped Slave (23:15-16)

An escaped slave must not be returned to his master but allowed to live freely in any town of his choosing:

- **Wright** calls this one of the most remarkable laws in the ancient Near East — no other code protected runaway slaves this way
- This passage was invoked during the abolitionist movement in Protestant countries

### Prohibition of Interest Among Israelites (23:19-20)

No interest may be charged on loans to fellow Israelites, though foreigners may be charged:

- **Calvin** wrestled with this text in the context of early modern commerce, ultimately allowing moderate interest but insisting on the principle of mercy toward the poor
- **Luther** was stricter, condemning usury as exploitation of the needy

### Keeping Vows (23:21-23)

"If you refrain from vowing, you will not be guilty." But once made, a vow must be fulfilled. **Calvin** applied this against rash monastic vows, arguing that vows contrary to Scripture or beyond one's capacity are not binding.

## Theological Significance

- **Holiness of community**: God's people must maintain standards of purity and justice
- **Gospel inclusion**: The New Testament breaks down the barriers this chapter erects — a movement anticipated by the prophets
- **Protection of the vulnerable**: Slaves, the poor, and the marginalized receive legal protection
- **Integrity of speech**: Vows are sacred and must not be made lightly
