## Three Festivals

Deuteronomy 16 prescribes the three annual pilgrimage festivals — Passover/Unleavened Bread, the Feast of Weeks, and the Feast of Booths — all to be observed at the central sanctuary. The chapter closes with instructions for appointing judges. Worship and justice are linked as twin pillars of covenant life.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Passover and Unleavened Bread (16:1-8)

Israel must sacrifice the Passover at the place the LORD chooses, eat unleavened bread for seven days, and remember the exodus:

- **Calvin** saw the Passover as the clearest Old Testament type of Christ's atoning death — the lamb, the blood, the deliverance
- **Luther** taught that Passover demonstrates the sacramental principle: a physical sign that conveys spiritual reality
- Protestant theology connects Passover directly to the Lord's Supper as its New Testament fulfillment

### The Feast of Weeks (16:9-12)

Celebrated seven weeks after the grain harvest begins, this festival includes freewill offerings and communal rejoicing. **Wright** notes the explicitly inclusive character — sons, daughters, servants, Levites, sojourners, orphans, and widows all participate.

### The Feast of Booths (16:13-15)

A seven-day harvest celebration involving temporary shelters. **Block** observes that living in booths recalled Israel's wilderness dependence on God — prosperity must never erase the memory of vulnerability.

### "Justice, and only justice, you shall follow" (16:20)

The chapter's conclusion on the appointment of judges yields one of Scripture's most powerful justice texts:

- **Calvin** called this the magna carta of judicial integrity
- The prohibition against partiality and bribery (16:19) grounded Protestant insistence on equitable civil government
- **Luther** applied this to ecclesiastical courts, which he saw as corrupted by financial interests

## Theological Significance

- **Christ in the festivals**: Protestant typology sees Passover, Weeks (Pentecost), and Booths as pointing to Christ's death, the Spirit's outpouring, and the eschatological harvest
- **Inclusive worship**: God's festivals include the marginalized — covenant blessing is not hoarded
- **Remembrance**: Each festival combats spiritual amnesia through ritual reenactment
- **Justice as worship**: The transition from festivals to judges shows that worship without justice is incomplete
