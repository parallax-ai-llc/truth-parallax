## Various Laws: Property and Purity

Deuteronomy 22 contains a wide-ranging collection of laws addressing lost property, building safety, prohibited mixtures, and sexual ethics. The chapter moves from neighborly responsibility to marital purity, reflecting the Deuteronomic vision that every sphere of life falls under God's ordering.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Caring for a Neighbor's Property (22:1-4)

Lost animals and fallen donkeys must be restored to their owner, not ignored. **Calvin** saw here the positive dimension of the eighth commandment — you shall not steal means you shall actively protect your neighbor's goods.

### Prohibition Against Cross-Dressing (22:5)

"A woman shall not wear a man's garment, nor shall a man put on a woman's cloak." Protestant commentators have generally understood this as:

- Maintaining the created distinction between male and female (Genesis 1:27)
- **Luther** associated it with prohibiting the confusion of God-given roles
- **Block** notes the text likely addresses practices associated with Canaanite cult rituals

### Prohibited Mixtures (22:9-11)

No mixing of seeds, plowing with ox and donkey together, or wearing wool and linen blended:

- **Calvin** saw these as pedagogical — teaching Israel to maintain holy distinctions in all of life
- Protestant theology generally classifies these as ceremonial laws fulfilled in Christ, though the underlying principle of order remains

### Building a Parapet (22:8)

A rooftop must have a railing to prevent falls. **Wright** observes this as an early building code — protecting life through responsible construction. The principle of duty of care extends to all situations where negligence could cause harm.

### Sexual Ethics and Marriage Laws (22:13-30)

The chapter's latter half addresses accusations against a bride's virginity, adultery, rape, and seduction:

- Protestant theology affirms the sanctity of marriage and the seriousness of sexual sin
- **Block** carefully distinguishes the cases: consensual adultery (22:22), assault in the city (22:23-24), assault in the field (22:25-27), and seduction of an unbetrothed woman (22:28-29)
- The distinction between cases where the woman cries out (and is innocent) and where she does not reflects a concern for justice to both parties

## Theological Significance

- **Neighbor love in practice**: The chapter translates "love your neighbor" into concrete daily actions
- **Created order**: Distinctions between male and female, and the ordering of creation, are to be respected
- **Sexual holiness**: Marriage is protected by serious sanctions against its violation
- **Responsibility for safety**: Negligence that endangers life is a moral failing
