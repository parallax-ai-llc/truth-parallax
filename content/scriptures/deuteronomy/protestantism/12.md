## Centralized Worship

Deuteronomy 12 begins the specific legislation of the Deuteronomic code with its most distinctive command: Israel must worship only at "the place that the LORD your God will choose." All pagan shrines must be destroyed, and sacrifice must be centralized. This chapter shaped Israel's worship history and profoundly influenced Protestant theology of worship.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You shall surely destroy all the places where the nations whom you shall dispossess served their gods" (12:2-3)

The command to demolish pagan altars, pillars, and Asherim was applied by the Reformers to their own context:

- **Calvin** drew a direct line from this text to the removal of images, relics, and unauthorized devotional objects from churches
- **Zwingli** cited Deuteronomy 12 in support of stripping Zurich's churches of icons and statues
- The principle: worship must conform to God's prescription, not human invention

### "The Place the LORD Will Choose" (12:5, 11, 14, 18, 21, 26)

This repeated phrase — occurring six times in one chapter — establishes the regulative principle:

- **Reformed tradition**: God alone determines how He is to be worshipped; the *regulative principle of worship* derives significantly from this chapter
- **Lutheran tradition**: Luther took a somewhat different approach, permitting whatever Scripture does not forbid, but still appealed to this passage against papal innovations
- **Block** notes that the anonymity of "the place" kept Israel focused on God's sovereignty in choosing

### Permission for Secular Slaughter (12:15-16, 20-25)

With sacrifice centralized, ordinary meat consumption is permitted anywhere — but blood must not be eaten. **Craigie** observed that this was a practical accommodation: centralizing worship required distinguishing between sacred sacrifice and ordinary eating.

### "Everything that I command you, you shall be careful to do. You shall not add to it or take from it" (12:32)

This verse became a rallying cry of the Reformation:

- **Calvin** used it against Roman additions to biblical worship
- Protestant theology sees here the foundation of *sola Scriptura* in worship practice

## Theological Significance

- **Regulative principle**: God prescribes the form and manner of worship; human additions corrupt it
- **Destruction of false worship**: Tolerance of paganism leads to syncretism — a lesson the Reformers applied to medieval accretions
- **Unity of worship**: One God, one place, one people — anticipating the unity of the church in Christ
- **Blood prohibition**: Reverence for life as belonging to God alone
