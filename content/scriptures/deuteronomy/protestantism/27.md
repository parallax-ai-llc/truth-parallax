## Curses at Ebal

Deuteronomy 27 prescribes a covenant ceremony to be performed upon entry into the Promised Land. Large plastered stones inscribed with the law will be set up on Mount Ebal, an altar will be built, and twelve curses will be pronounced — to which all the people respond "Amen." The chapter marks the transition from Mosaic exhortation to covenant ratification.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Law Written on Stones (27:1-8)

Moses commands that upon crossing the Jordan, Israel must set up large stones, plaster them, and write "all the words of this law" upon them:

- **Calvin** saw in this a commitment to the public accessibility of God's word — the law must not be hidden or reserved for an elite
- **Luther** connected this to the Reformation's insistence on translating Scripture into the vernacular
- The altar of uncut stones (27:5-6) emphasizes that worship must be as God prescribes, not as humans embellish

### The Tribal Division: Gerizim and Ebal (27:11-13)

Six tribes stand on Gerizim for blessing, six on Ebal for cursing. **Block** observes that the entire nation participates in the covenant — blessing and curse are communal realities, not merely individual.

### The Twelve Curses (27:15-26)

Each curse begins "Cursed be the one who..." and addresses sins typically committed in secret — idolatry, dishonoring parents, moving boundary markers, misleading the blind, perverting justice, sexual sins, murder for hire:

- **Wright** notes that these curses target hidden sins — precisely those that human courts cannot detect
- **Calvin** emphasized that God sees what is done in secret; the curse reaches where human justice cannot
- The communal "Amen" makes the entire congregation witnesses and participants in the covenant sanctions

### "Cursed be everyone who does not confirm the words of this law by doing them" (27:26)

Paul quotes this in Galatians 3:10 to demonstrate that anyone under the law is under a curse, since no one perfectly obeys. **Luther** saw this as the verse that drives sinners to Christ — the law's demand is total, and its curse is inescapable apart from the gospel.

## Theological Significance

- **Public Scripture**: God's word must be visible and accessible — a principle the Reformation championed
- **The curse of the law**: The law's demands are perfect and its penalties comprehensive — justification by works is impossible
- **Secret sins exposed**: God's judgment reaches what human courts cannot see
- **Christ bears the curse**: Galatians 3:13 declares that Christ became a curse for us, redeeming us from the curse pronounced here
