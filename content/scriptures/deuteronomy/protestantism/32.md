## Song of Moses

Deuteronomy 32 contains the Song of Moses — one of the greatest poems in the Old Testament. It is a prophetic lawsuit (*rib*) against Israel, contrasting God's perfect faithfulness with Israel's persistent rebellion. Heaven and earth are called as witnesses. The song traces the arc from God's gracious election to Israel's apostasy to divine judgment and ultimate vindication.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Give ear, O heavens, and I will speak" (32:1)

The cosmic summons recalls the covenant lawsuit genre of the prophets. **Calvin** noted that heaven and earth are invoked as permanent witnesses — more reliable than fickle human memory.

### "The Rock, his work is perfect, for all his ways are justice" (32:4)

God is the Rock — stable, unchanging, righteous. **Luther** saw this as the fundamental confession: whatever questions arise from Israel's history, God's character remains unimpeachable.

### "He found him in a desert land... he kept him as the apple of his eye" (32:10)

God's tender care for Israel in the wilderness is described in intimate terms:

- **Wright** emphasizes the undeserved nature of this care — Israel was found in a howling wasteland, not in a place of merit
- The "apple of his eye" language reappears in Zechariah 2:8 and reflects God's jealous protection

### "Jeshurun grew fat, and kicked" (32:15)

"Jeshurun" (a poetic name for Israel meaning "upright one") grew prosperous and abandoned God. **Block** notes the tragic irony — the "upright" one becomes crooked through prosperity. This echoes chapter 8's warning about wealth-induced forgetfulness.

### "I will hide my face from them; I will see what their end will be" (32:20)

God's hiddenness is one of the most painful theological realities. **Luther** knew the experience of *deus absconditus* — the hidden God — and found in this text both the reality of divine withdrawal and the assurance that it is purposeful, not arbitrary.

### "Vengeance is mine, and recompense" (32:35)

Quoted in Romans 12:19 and Hebrews 10:30. **Calvin** drew from this the principle that believers must not take justice into their own hands — retribution belongs to God alone.

### "He will atone for his land and his people" (32:43)

The song ends not with judgment but with atonement. **Reformed theology** sees here the gospel embedded in the law — even the Song of Moses points beyond judgment to restoration.

## Theological Significance

- **God's perfection**: The Rock metaphor establishes God's unchanging righteousness as the standard against which Israel is measured
- **The pattern of rebellion**: Prosperity leads to self-sufficiency, self-sufficiency to idolatry, idolatry to judgment
- **Divine judgment and mercy**: Judgment is real but not final — atonement has the last word
- **Scripture as song**: The song form aids memorization and transmission — God accommodates His truth to human capacity
