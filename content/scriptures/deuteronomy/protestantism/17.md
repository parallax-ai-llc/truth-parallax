## Kings and Judges

Deuteronomy 17 addresses judicial procedures for difficult cases, warns against sacrificial defects and idolatry, and provides the famous "law of the king" — regulating future monarchy in Israel. The chapter establishes that all human authority, whether judicial or royal, is subordinate to God's word.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Central Tribunal for Difficult Cases (17:8-13)

When local courts cannot resolve a case, it must be brought to the central sanctuary where priests and a judge render a binding decision:

- **Calvin** saw this as establishing the principle of appellate jurisdiction — a model for orderly civil governance
- Protestant ecclesiology has drawn on this passage to support church courts and synods that adjudicate disputes according to Scripture

### The Law of the King (17:14-20)

This passage anticipates and regulates Israel's future monarchy with remarkable restrictions:

1. The king must be chosen by God, not by human ambition (17:15)
2. He must not multiply horses (military power), wives, or wealth (17:16-17)
3. He must write a personal copy of the law and read it daily (17:18-19)

**Luther** applied this to all rulers — civil authority is legitimate only when subordinate to God's word. Kings who exalt themselves above the law become tyrants.

### "He shall read in it all the days of his life, that he may learn to fear the LORD" (17:19)

**Calvin** called this the charter of constitutional monarchy — the king is not above the law but under it. This verse profoundly shaped Reformed political theory:

- The king's daily Scripture reading models submission to divine authority
- **Samuel Rutherford** drew on this passage in *Lex Rex* (1644) to argue that law is king, not the king law
- The resistance theory of the Reformed tradition — the right to resist tyrannical rulers — finds partial footing here

## Theological Significance

- **Authority under the word**: No human office — royal, priestly, or ecclesiastical — stands above Scripture
- **Constitutional governance**: The law of the king limits power and prevents absolutism
- **Daily Scripture**: The king's obligation to read the Torah daily anticipates the Protestant emphasis on personal Bible reading
- **Against human autonomy**: The accumulation of horses, wives, and silver represents self-reliance replacing trust in God
