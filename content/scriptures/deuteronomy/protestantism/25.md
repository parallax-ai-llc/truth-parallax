## Various Laws: Justice and Levirate Marriage

Deuteronomy 25 concludes the miscellaneous law section with regulations on corporal punishment, levirate marriage, honest business practices, and the mandate to remember Amalek. The chapter's diverse laws are unified by a concern for dignity, justice, and memory.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Proportionate Punishment (25:1-3)

A guilty man may be beaten, but not more than forty stripes, "lest your brother be degraded in your sight":

- **Calvin** emphasized the phrase "your brother" — even a criminal retains dignity as a member of the covenant community
- Paul received "forty lashes less one" (2 Corinthians 11:24), reflecting Jewish practice of stopping at thirty-nine to avoid accidentally exceeding the limit
- **Luther** saw here the principle that punishment must be humane and proportionate

### "You shall not muzzle an ox when it is treading out the grain" (25:4)

Paul quotes this twice (1 Corinthians 9:9; 1 Timothy 5:18) to argue that ministers of the gospel deserve material support:

- **Calvin** noted that if God cares for the ox's welfare during labor, how much more does He care for His servants
- This became a key text in Protestant arguments for adequate pastoral compensation

### Levirate Marriage (25:5-10)

If a man dies childless, his brother should marry the widow and raise offspring in the deceased's name. If he refuses, the widow publicly shames him:

- **Block** observes this law protects the widow's security and preserves the family name in Israel
- **Luther** noted that this institution is superseded in the New Testament but the principle of caring for the bereaved remains
- The book of Ruth illustrates this custom in action

### Honest Weights and Measures (25:13-16)

"You shall not have in your bag two kinds of weights, a large and a small." Dishonest trade is an "abomination to the LORD":

- **Calvin** applied this to all forms of commercial deception
- **Wright** notes that economic justice is not a secondary concern but a covenant requirement

### Remember Amalek (25:17-19)

Israel must remember how Amalek attacked the weakest stragglers and must eventually blot out Amalek's memory:

- Protestant commentators see this as God's judgment against those who prey on the vulnerable
- **Block** notes the irony: Israel must remember in order to blot out the memory

## Theological Significance

- **Human dignity**: Even in punishment, the offender retains worth before God
- **Ministerial support**: The ox proverb establishes that those who labor in God's work deserve compensation
- **Economic integrity**: Honest dealings are a covenant obligation, not mere good manners
- **Justice for the vulnerable**: Both the levirate law and the Amalek mandate protect those who cannot protect themselves
