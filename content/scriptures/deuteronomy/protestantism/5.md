## Ten Commandments Restated

Deuteronomy 5 presents Moses' restatement of the Decalogue — the Ten Commandments first given at Horeb. This second presentation, addressed to a new generation, underscores that the covenant is not merely historical but perpetually binding. The chapter is of immense importance to Protestant theology and ethics.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The LORD our God made a covenant with us at Horeb. Not with our fathers did the LORD make this covenant, but with us" (5:2-3)

Moses insists the covenant belongs to the present generation. **Calvin** saw here a principle of covenant renewal that applies to each generation of the church — baptized children must personally embrace the faith.

### The Sabbath Commandment — A Key Difference (5:12-15)

Unlike Exodus 20, which grounds the Sabbath in creation, Deuteronomy 5 grounds it in the exodus — liberation from slavery:

- **Lutheran tradition**: Luther saw the Sabbath commandment as partly ceremonial, fulfilled in Christ; Christians rest in the gospel
- **Reformed tradition**: The Westminster Confession maintains a continuing Sabbath obligation, transferred to the Lord's Day
- **Evangelical tradition**: Views vary, but many emphasize the Sabbath principle (rest and worship) without strict Sabbatarian requirements

### The Three Uses of the Law

Protestant theology, building on passages like Deuteronomy 5, identifies three uses of the moral law:

1. **Civil use** (*usus politicus*): To restrain evil in society
2. **Pedagogical use** (*usus elenchticus*): To convict of sin and drive to Christ (Galatians 3:24)
3. **Normative use** (*usus in renatis*): To guide the believer's life — the "third use" especially emphasized by Calvin and the Reformed tradition

### "Oh that they had such a heart as this always, to fear me and to keep all my commandments" (5:29)

**Luther** saw this verse as revealing the fundamental human problem: the law commands, but the heart is unwilling. This is precisely why the gospel — a new heart by the Spirit (Ezekiel 36:26) — is necessary.

## Theological Significance

- **Moral law as permanent**: Protestant theology distinguishes moral, ceremonial, and civil law; the Decalogue represents the abiding moral law
- **Law and gospel**: The Decalogue reveals both God's character and humanity's inability to keep it perfectly
- **Mediatorial role of Moses**: The people's fear leads them to request Moses as mediator (5:27), prefiguring Christ as the true Mediator
- **Heart religion**: God desires internal transformation, not mere external compliance

## The Decalogue in Protestant Catechesis

The Ten Commandments hold a central place in Protestant catechisms — Luther's Small and Large Catechisms, the Heidelberg Catechism, and the Westminster Shorter Catechism all organize significant sections around them.
