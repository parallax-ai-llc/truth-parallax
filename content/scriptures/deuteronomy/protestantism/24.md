## Various Laws: Divorce and Social Justice

Deuteronomy 24 contains the famous divorce legislation that Jesus interprets in Matthew 19, alongside humanitarian laws protecting workers, debtors, sojourners, orphans, and widows. The chapter balances regulation of a fallen world with aspiration toward justice.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Divorce Legislation (24:1-4)

If a man divorces his wife because he finds "some indecency" in her, and she remarries and is divorced again, the first husband may not remarry her:

- Jesus teaches in Matthew 19:8 that Moses permitted divorce "because of your hardness of heart, but from the beginning it was not so"
- **Calvin** understood this as regulation, not endorsement — the law manages a sinful reality without approving it
- **Luther** saw divorce as a concession to human weakness; the gospel ideal is lifelong fidelity
- Protestant traditions vary on the permissibility of divorce: Reformed tradition allows it for adultery and desertion (Westminster Confession 24.5-6); Lutherans allow it more broadly as a civil matter

### Protection of the Newlywed (24:5)

A newly married man is exempt from military service for one year "to be happy with his wife." **Block** sees this as God's priority on the marital bond — marriage is not subordinate to the state's demands.

### Prohibition Against Taking Millstones as Pledge (24:6)

This would deprive a family of their means of making bread. **Wright** notes the principle: debts must not be collected in ways that destroy the debtor's livelihood.

### Treatment of Workers (24:14-15)

"You shall not oppress a hired worker who is poor and needy... You shall give him his wages on the same day." **Luther** applied this directly to the exploitation of laborers by landowners in his day.

### Gleaning Laws (24:19-22)

Sheaves, olives, and grapes left behind belong to sojourners, orphans, and widows:

- **Wright** sees this as a welfare system built into the economic structure — not charity but entitlement
- The motive clause again invokes Egypt: "You shall remember that you were a slave" (24:22)

## Theological Significance

- **Divorce as concession**: Moses regulated divorce but did not institute it — the gospel calls for a higher standard
- **Economic justice**: Workers must be paid promptly; the poor must have access to sustenance
- **Remembering slavery**: Israel's identity as former slaves shapes their treatment of the vulnerable
- **Individual accountability**: "Fathers shall not be put to death because of their children" (24:16) — individual responsibility before God
