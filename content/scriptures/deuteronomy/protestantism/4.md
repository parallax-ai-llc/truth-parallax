## Exhortation to Obedience

Deuteronomy 4 transitions from historical review to passionate exhortation. Moses urges Israel to obey God's statutes, warning against idolatry and grounding obedience in the incomparable nature of Israel's God, who revealed Himself through word rather than visible form.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You shall not add to the word that I command you, nor take from it" (4:2)

This verse is foundational to the Protestant doctrine of *sola Scriptura*:

- **Luther** invoked this text to oppose Roman Catholic additions to biblical teaching
- **Calvin** applied it against both Roman traditions and Anabaptist claims to new revelation
- The **Westminster Confession** (1.6) echoes this principle: "The whole counsel of God... is either expressly set down in Scripture, or by good and necessary consequence may be deduced from Scripture"

### The Aniconic Command (4:15-20)

Moses emphasizes that Israel saw "no form" at Horeb — they heard a voice. This prohibition against images is central to Protestant theology:

| Tradition | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Reformed (Heidelberg Catechism Q96-98) | Prohibits all images of God in worship |
| Lutheran | Allows instructional images but not worship of them |
| Evangelical | Emphasizes the primacy of the Word over visual representation |

### "For the LORD your God is a consuming fire, a jealous God" (4:24)

**Edwards** and other Puritan preachers drew heavily on this imagery. God's jealousy is not petty but reflects the intensity of covenantal love that will not tolerate rivals.

### "The LORD your God is a merciful God" (4:31)

Placed alongside the "consuming fire" image, this creates the tension that Protestant theology navigates through the cross — where divine justice and mercy meet.

## Theological Significance

- **Sola Scriptura in seed form**: The prohibition against adding to or subtracting from God's word
- **The second commandment applied**: God is to be worshipped as He prescribes, not as humans imagine
- **Monotheism affirmed**: "There is no other besides him" (4:35) — a text Paul echoes in Romans 3:30
- **Eschatological hope**: Even in exile, God can be found by those who seek Him "with all your heart" (4:29)

## The Regulative Principle of Worship

Deuteronomy 4 is a key text for the **regulative principle** held by Reformed and Presbyterian traditions: worship must be ordered by what God commands, not by human invention. **John Owen** and the **Westminster divines** built extensively on this chapter.
