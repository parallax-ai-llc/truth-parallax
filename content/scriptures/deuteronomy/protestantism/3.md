## Victories East of Jordan

Deuteronomy 3 records the defeat of Og king of Bashan, the allocation of Transjordan territories to Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh, and Moses' poignant plea to enter the Promised Land — a plea God denies.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Defeat of Og (3:1-11)

Og's enormous iron bedstead (roughly 13 feet long) underscores the formidable nature of the opposition. **Calvin** noted that God's victory over such giants demonstrated that no earthly power can resist divine purpose.

**Evangelical scholars** see the Transjordan conquests as confirming God's faithfulness: the same God who promised the land also delivered tangible victories as down payments on that promise.

### Distribution of Transjordan Territory (3:12-20)

The allocation to Reuben, Gad, and half of Manasseh established a principle that Protestant commentators have applied broadly:

- **Stewardship**: Land belongs to God and is distributed by His will
- **Communal obligation**: These tribes must still fight alongside their brothers (3:18-20)
- **Matthew Henry** drew a spiritual lesson: those who receive blessings early are not exempt from the ongoing work of the community

### Moses' Plea and God's Refusal (3:23-29)

Moses' prayer to enter the land is one of the most affecting scenes in Scripture. God's firm refusal teaches several Protestant theological principles:

1. **Even the godliest face consequences**: Moses' sin at Meribah (Numbers 20) is not annulled by his faithfulness
2. **God's "no" is not rejection**: Moses is told to view the land from Pisgah, a gesture of grace within discipline
3. **Christ as the greater Moses**: Protestant typology sees Joshua (whose name means "salvation") leading Israel where Moses could not — foreshadowing Jesus leading believers where the law cannot

### "Charge Joshua, and encourage and strengthen him" (3:28)

The commissioning of Joshua foreshadows apostolic succession in a distinctly Protestant key — authority is transmitted not through institutional office but through God's direct calling and the community's recognition.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine warrior theology**: God fights for His people, a theme central to the entire book
- **The limits of the law**: Moses representing the law cannot bring Israel into rest; that requires Joshua/Jesus (cf. Hebrews 4:8)
- **Grace in judgment**: Even God's discipline contains merciful provisions
- **Community solidarity**: No tribe inherits alone; all must participate in the common mission

## Reformed Typological Reading

**Reformed interpreters** from Calvin onward have seen the Moses-Joshua transition as programmatic for understanding the relationship between law and gospel. The law (Moses) reveals sin and guides, but it is the gospel (Joshua/Jesus) that brings God's people into their inheritance.
