## Not for Your Righteousness

Deuteronomy 9 delivers one of the Old Testament's most emphatic statements of grace: God is giving Israel the land not because of their righteousness but because of the wickedness of the nations and God's own covenant promise. Moses then rehearses the golden calf incident to prove the point.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Not because of your righteousness or the uprightness of your heart are you going in to possess their land" (9:5)

This verse is a pillar of Protestant soteriology. **Luther** called it one of the clearest Old Testament declarations against works-righteousness:

- God acts on the basis of His promise and His justice against the Canaanites
- Israel's merit plays no role whatsoever
- **Calvin** connected this directly to justification by faith — God saves sinners despite themselves

### "For you are a stubborn people" (9:6)

Three times in this chapter Moses calls Israel stubborn (*qesheh oref* — stiff-necked). Protestant anthropology draws on this to establish total depravity — the natural inclination of the human heart is away from God.

### The Golden Calf Revisited (9:8-21)

Moses recounts the golden calf disaster in vivid terms — forty days on the mountain, Israel's idolatry below, the smashed tablets:

- **Block** notes that Moses' retelling emphasizes the speed of Israel's apostasy — barely forty days after hearing God's voice
- **Reformed theology** sees here a demonstration that even the most dramatic spiritual experiences do not guarantee perseverance apart from grace
- Moses' intercession (9:18-20, 25-29) is seen as a type of Christ's intercessory work

### Moses as Intercessor (9:25-29)

Moses' prayer appeals not to Israel's goodness but to God's reputation and His promise to the patriarchs. **Calvin** observed that this is the only legitimate ground of prayer — God's own character and promises.

## Theological Significance

- **Grace alone**: Salvation and blessing rest entirely on God's initiative, not human merit
- **Total depravity**: Israel's record demonstrates the depth of human sinfulness
- **Intercessory prayer**: Moses prefigures Christ, who intercedes on the basis of God's covenant faithfulness
- **Against moralism**: This chapter demolishes any notion that God rewards the righteous; He saves the undeserving
