## Blessing of Moses

Deuteronomy 33 records Moses' final blessing over the tribes of Israel before his death, paralleling Jacob's blessing in Genesis 49. The chapter opens and closes with magnificent theophanic poetry celebrating God as warrior, king, and protector. Each tribe receives a specific blessing reflecting its future character and role.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Theophanic Introduction (33:1-5)

"The LORD came from Sinai and dawned from Seir upon them; he shone forth from Mount Paran":

- **Calvin** saw this majestic opening as celebrating God's glorious self-revelation — He is not distant but has come to His people
- **Block** notes the military imagery: God arrives as a victorious warrior leading His heavenly hosts
- "He was king in Jeshurun" (33:5) — God's kingship over Israel is the foundation of all blessing

### Selected Tribal Blessings

**Levi** (33:8-11): Entrusted with the Thummim and Urim, teaching Jacob's ordinances and offering incense. **Calvin** applied this to the ministerial office — those who teach God's word must be faithful above all other loyalties, even family.

**Judah** (33:7): A brief prayer for Judah's strength. Protestant commentators see here the messianic tribe — from Judah comes the King.

**Joseph/Ephraim and Manasseh** (33:13-17): The longest blessing, celebrating abundance from heaven, the deep, the sun, the moon, and the ancient mountains. **Wright** notes the lavish agricultural imagery, reflecting the fertility of the central hill country.

**Benjamin** (33:12): "The beloved of the LORD dwells in safety. The High God surrounds him all day long." **Luther** treasured this verse as a picture of the believer's security in God's love.

### The Grand Conclusion (33:26-29)

"There is none like God, O Jeshurun, who rides through the heavens to your help":

- **Calvin** called this one of Scripture's greatest declarations of divine incomparability
- "The eternal God is your dwelling place, and underneath are the everlasting arms" (33:27) — **Luther** found here the supreme comfort: God sustains His people from below when they cannot sustain themselves
- "Happy are you, O Israel! Who is like you?" (33:29) — the blessedness of being God's covenant people

## Theological Significance

- **God as protector**: The frame of the chapter presents God as warrior-king who fights for and shelters His people
- **Diverse gifts, one people**: Each tribe has a unique role — Protestant theology applies this to the diversity of gifts within the body of Christ
- **The everlasting arms**: One of the Bible's most comforting images — God's sustaining support is beneath every trial
- **Blessing before death**: Moses' final act is benediction, not judgment — grace characterizes his farewell
