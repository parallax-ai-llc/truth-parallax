## Cities of Refuge

Deuteronomy 19 establishes cities of refuge for those who commit accidental homicide, regulates property boundaries, and prescribes the law of witnesses. The chapter demonstrates that justice in Israel must distinguish between intentional murder and unintentional killing, and that legal proceedings require corroboration.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Three Cities of Refuge (19:1-10)

Israel must designate three cities of refuge (with provision for three more) so that anyone who kills accidentally can flee there and be safe from the avenger of blood:

- **Calvin** saw the cities of refuge as a type of Christ — the sinner's only safe haven from the wrath deserved by sin
- **Luther** similarly taught that as the manslayer runs to the city, the sinner runs to Christ for refuge
- **Wright** emphasizes the humanitarian purpose: preventing blood feuds and ensuring proportionate justice
- **Block** notes the care taken to make the cities accessible — roads must be prepared and distances reasonable

### Intentional Murder Distinguished (19:11-13)

If a man lies in wait and kills his neighbor, the elders must extradite him from the city of refuge. "Your eye shall not pity him." Protestant theology affirms the legitimacy of capital punishment for murder as grounded in the sanctity of life (cf. Genesis 9:6).

### Boundary Markers (19:14)

"You shall not move your neighbor's landmark." **Calvin** applied this broadly to all forms of economic fraud — encroaching on another's property violates the eighth commandment.

### The Law of Witnesses (19:15-21)

No one may be convicted on a single witness; two or three are required. False witnesses receive the punishment they intended for the accused:

- This principle is carried into the New Testament (Matthew 18:16; 2 Corinthians 13:1)
- **Reformed church discipline** adopted the two-witness rule directly from this passage
- "Life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth" (19:21) — the *lex talionis* — establishes proportionate justice, not personal vengeance

## Theological Significance

- **Christ as refuge**: The cities of refuge typologically point to Christ as the believer's shelter from judgment
- **Due process**: Justice requires evidence, multiple witnesses, and careful inquiry
- **Proportionate justice**: Punishment must fit the crime — neither excessive nor insufficient
- **Protection of the innocent**: The legal system must guard against both wrongful conviction and blood vengeance
