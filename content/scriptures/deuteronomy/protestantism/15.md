## Sabbatical Year

Deuteronomy 15 legislates the release of debts every seven years, commands open-handed generosity toward the poor, and regulates the release of Hebrew slaves. The chapter envisions a community shaped by grace — where economic life reflects covenant relationship.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Debt Release Every Seven Years (15:1-6)

At the end of every seven years, creditors must cancel debts owed by fellow Israelites:

- **Wright** sees this as a structural mechanism against permanent poverty and the concentration of wealth
- **Calvin** noted that while the specific legislation is civil and no longer binding, the principle of mercy in economic dealings is permanent
- **Luther** emphasized that generosity toward debtors reflects the gospel — God has cancelled our immeasurable debt

### "There will be no poor among you... if only you will strictly obey" (15:4-5)

The conditional promise that poverty can be eliminated stands in tension with verse 11: "there will never cease to be poor in the land." Protestant commentators reconcile these:

- The ideal (no poverty) depends on perfect obedience, which Israel never achieves
- The realistic command (open your hand) deals with the world as it is
- **Block** observes that this tension mirrors the "already/not yet" of the kingdom of God

### "You shall not harden your heart or shut your hand against your poor brother" (15:7)

**Luther** saw this as addressing the root of economic injustice — a hardened heart that refuses to see need. The command is internal before it is economic.

### Release of Slaves (15:12-18)

Hebrew slaves must be freed after six years and sent away generously provisioned. **Wright** draws attention to the motive clause: "You shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the LORD your God redeemed you" (15:15). Grace received must produce grace extended.

## Theological Significance

- **Economics shaped by grace**: The sabbatical year embodies the principle that God's people must reflect His character in material dealings
- **Generosity over calculation**: The warning against hard-hearted reluctance (15:9) condemns strategic selfishness
- **Redemption as model**: Israel's experience of liberation from Egypt is the pattern for how they treat others
- **Structural justice**: The Reformers valued systemic provisions against poverty, not only individual charity
