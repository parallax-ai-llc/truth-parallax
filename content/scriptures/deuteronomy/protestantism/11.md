## Love and Obey

Deuteronomy 11 concludes Moses' second address with a comprehensive call to love God and keep His commandments. The chapter holds out the prospect of blessing in the Promised Land while warning that disobedience will bring drought and exile. It closes with the command to proclaim blessing and curse from Mounts Gerizim and Ebal.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You shall therefore love the LORD your God and keep his charge" (11:1)

Love and obedience are inseparable in Deuteronomic theology. **Calvin** insisted that true love for God necessarily produces obedience — not as a condition of salvation but as its fruit.

### The Land as Gift and Test (11:10-15)

Unlike Egypt, where irrigation was manual, Canaan depends on rain from heaven. This means Israel will be perpetually dependent on God:

- **Block** observes that the land's dependence on rainfall was a built-in mechanism for ongoing trust
- **Luther** saw here a picture of the Christian life — daily dependence on divine provision rather than self-sufficient security

### "Take care lest your heart be deceived, and you turn aside and serve other gods" (11:16)

The heart's susceptibility to deception is a persistent biblical warning. Protestant theology uses such texts to argue for:

1. The necessity of ongoing vigilance against sin
2. The means of grace (word, sacrament, prayer) as guards against apostasy
3. The deceitfulness of the human heart (Jeremiah 17:9)

### Blessing and Curse Set Before Israel (11:26-32)

The chapter culminates in a stark choice: blessing for obedience, curse for disobedience. **Wright** notes this is not abstract theology but a concrete, historical reality — Israel will literally stand on two mountains and hear the alternatives proclaimed.

**Reformed theology** recognizes the covenant of works principle here while insisting that since the fall, only Christ perfectly fulfills the conditions of blessing.

## Theological Significance

- **Love as the heart of law**: The Reformers insisted that cold legalism misses the point — God desires heartfelt devotion
- **Providence over the land**: God's ongoing control of rain and harvest teaches daily dependence
- **The two ways**: Blessing and curse represent the fundamental human choice — a theme running from Deuteronomy through the Psalms to the Sermon on the Mount
- **Teaching in the home**: Verses 18-21 reiterate the command from chapter 6 to embed God's word in daily family life
