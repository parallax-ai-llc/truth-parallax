## Wanderings Recalled

Deuteronomy 2 continues Moses' historical review, recounting Israel's long circuit through the wilderness and their encounters with Edom, Moab, and Ammon. The chapter reveals God's sovereign governance over all nations, not Israel alone.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You have been traveling around this mountain long enough" (2:3)

God's command to cease wandering and move forward carries deep spiritual significance. **Luther** preached that God's people must not remain paralyzed by past failures but press forward in obedience when God opens a new path.

**Evangelical interpreters** often apply this verse devotionally: seasons of spiritual stagnation have a divinely appointed end.

### God's Provision for Edom, Moab, and Ammon (2:4-12, 19)

Remarkably, God forbids Israel from attacking Edom (Esau's descendants), Moab, and Ammon because He had given them their own lands. Protestant commentators draw several conclusions:

- **Calvin** noted that God's sovereignty extends to the territorial boundaries of all nations, not only Israel
- **Matthew Henry** emphasized divine fairness — God is not merely a tribal deity but ruler of all peoples
- This anticipates Paul's speech in Acts 17:26 about God determining "the boundaries of their dwelling place"

### The Defeat of Sihon (2:24-37)

God commands Israel to engage Sihon king of Heshbon in battle, hardening Sihon's spirit to ensure conflict. This raises the question of divine hardening:

| Commentator | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| Calvin | God's hardening is a judicial act upon an already sinful heart |
| Luther | God withdraws His restraining grace, allowing sin to run its course |
| Craigie | The hardening language reflects ancient Near Eastern conventions of divine sovereignty |

### "The LORD your God hardened his spirit and made his heart obstinate" (2:30)

This parallels the hardening of Pharaoh's heart. **Reformed theology** sees a pattern: God's sovereign will operates even through human resistance. The Westminster Confession (5.4) teaches that God may judicially "give them over to their own lusts."

## Theological Significance

- **God's universal sovereignty**: Protestant theology affirms God as Lord over all nations and their boundaries
- **Divine patience and timing**: Thirty-eight years of wandering were not wasted but served God's purifying purpose
- **The problem of divine hardening**: A key locus for Reformed discussions of sovereignty and human responsibility
- **Total provision**: Moses notes that "the LORD your God has blessed you... you have lacked nothing" (2:7)

## Connection to Protestant Soteriology

The interplay of divine sovereignty and human responsibility in the Sihon narrative anticipates debates central to Protestant theology. **Arminius** and **Calvin** both engaged this text, arriving at different conclusions about the nature of God's relationship to human choice.
