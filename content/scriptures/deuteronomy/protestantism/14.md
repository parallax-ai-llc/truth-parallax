## Clean and Unclean Foods

Deuteronomy 14 addresses dietary laws (paralleling Leviticus 11), mourning practices, and tithing regulations. The chapter grounds these distinctions in Israel's identity as a holy people. Protestant theology has given careful attention to the question of which elements of this legislation remain binding.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You are a people holy to the LORD your God" (14:2)

The dietary laws are prefaced by Israel's identity as a set-apart people. **Calvin** argued that the food laws served to distinguish Israel from surrounding nations and to teach discipline in daily life.

### The Dietary Lists (14:3-20)

Clean and unclean animals are catalogued — land animals, fish, birds. Protestant theology has generally held these laws to be ceremonial rather than moral:

- **Luther** taught that Christ declared all foods clean (Mark 7:19), freeing the Christian conscience from dietary restrictions
- **Calvin** agreed that the ceremonial law is fulfilled in Christ but noted that the underlying principle of self-discipline and moderation remains
- **Reformed confessions** (Westminster, Belgic) classify dietary laws as abrogated ceremonial legislation

### "You shall not boil a young goat in its mother's milk" (14:21)

This enigmatic prohibition appears three times in the Pentateuch. **Craigie** suggested it prohibited a Canaanite ritual practice. Protestant commentators generally see it as a boundary marker against pagan custom rather than a permanent dietary norm.

### The Tithe (14:22-29)

Israel is commanded to tithe annually, eating the tithe before the LORD at the central sanctuary. Every third year, the tithe is stored locally for Levites, sojourners, orphans, and widows:

- **Wright** emphasizes that tithing in Deuteronomy is simultaneously worship and social welfare
- **Evangelical tradition** debates whether the tithe is a binding norm or a general principle of generosity
- **Block** notes the communal and celebratory nature of tithing — it involves feasting in God's presence

## Theological Significance

- **Ceremonial law fulfilled**: Protestant theology affirms Christian freedom from dietary restrictions (Acts 10; Romans 14)
- **Holiness in daily life**: Even eating and drinking are arenas of devotion to God
- **Generosity as worship**: Tithing serves both God's glory and the community's welfare
- **Christian liberty**: The Reformation's insistence on freedom of conscience in matters not commanded by Scripture
