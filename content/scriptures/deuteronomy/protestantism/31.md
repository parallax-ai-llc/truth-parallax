## Joshua Commissioned

Deuteronomy 31 marks the transition from Moses to Joshua. Moses, at 120 years old, commissions his successor, commands the regular public reading of the law, and receives God's sobering prediction that Israel will rebel after his death. The Song of Moses (chapter 32) is introduced as a prophetic witness against future apostasy.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Moses' Farewell and Joshua's Commission (31:1-8)

"Be strong and courageous. Do not fear or be in dread of them, for it is the LORD your God who goes with you. He will not leave you or forsake you":

- **Luther** treasured this promise as applicable to every believer facing trials — God's presence is the ground of all courage
- **Calvin** noted that Moses' inability to enter the land demonstrates the insufficiency of the law; Joshua ("Yeshua" — the same name as Jesus) leads into the inheritance
- The author of Hebrews draws on this promise in Hebrews 13:5-6

### The Law Read Every Seven Years (31:9-13)

Moses commands that the entire law be read publicly every seven years during the Feast of Booths — men, women, children, and sojourners must hear it:

- **Calvin** saw this as establishing the regulative necessity of public Scripture reading in worship — a practice the Reformation restored
- **Block** observes that the purpose is explicitly pedagogical: "that they may hear and learn to fear the LORD"
- Protestant liturgy's emphasis on lengthy Scripture readings draws on this precedent

### God's Prediction of Apostasy (31:16-21)

The LORD tells Moses that Israel will forsake Him, break the covenant, and turn to other gods. The Song of Moses is given as a preemptive witness:

- **Calvin** saw this as demonstration of God's exhaustive foreknowledge — He knows the future perfectly
- **Luther** connected this to the doctrine of human depravity — God's assessment of Israel's future is not pessimistic but realistic
- **Reformed theology** notes that God proceeds with the covenant despite foreknowing its violation — grace is not contingent on human faithfulness

### "I know what they are inclined to do even today, before I have brought them into the land" (31:21)

**Block** observes the pathos: God knows Israel's heart but brings them into the land anyway. This tension between divine gift and human failure drives the entire biblical narrative toward the need for a new covenant.

## Theological Significance

- **Succession under God**: Leadership transitions are under divine direction — Joshua is God's appointment
- **The law must be heard**: Public reading of Scripture is a non-negotiable element of covenant life
- **Divine foreknowledge**: God knows the future exhaustively, yet His purposes are not defeated
- **Law's insufficiency**: Moses cannot lead into the land; only Joshua (Jesus) can bring God's people to their inheritance
