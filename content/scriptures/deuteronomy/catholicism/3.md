## Conquest of Og and Moses Denied Entry

Deuteronomy 3 continues the historical retrospective with the defeat of King Og of Bashan, the distribution of Transjordan to the eastern tribes, and the poignant episode in which Moses is denied entry to the Promised Land. Catholic tradition reads this chapter through both historical and typological lenses, finding in Moses' exclusion a prefiguration of the relationship between the Old Law and the New.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### The Defeat of Og (3:1-11)

The victory over Og completes Israel's conquest of Transjordan. Catholic exegesis, following Origen and St. Jerome, often reads these military victories typologically. Og, whose iron bedstead measured nine cubits, represents the oversized power of sin and death that can only be overcome by divine intervention. St. Ambrose drew a parallel between these victories and the Christian's spiritual combat described by St. Paul in Ephesians 6:10-17.

### Distribution of Land (3:12-20)

Moses allocates the conquered territory to Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh, with the condition that their warriors must still cross the Jordan with their brothers. Catholic moral theology sees here the principle of solidarity: those who have received blessings bear obligations to the wider community. The Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church identifies solidarity as a fundamental principle of social life (CSDC 192-196).

### Moses' Plea Denied (3:23-29)

Moses begs God to let him cross the Jordan, but the Lord refuses, telling him to ascend Pisgah and look upon the land from afar. This passage is among the most theologically significant in the Pentateuch for Catholic interpretation. The Church Fathers, especially St. Augustine and St. Cyril of Alexandria, read Moses' exclusion as a sign that the Law alone cannot bring entry into the fullness of God's promises. It is Joshua (whose name in Greek is Jesus) who leads the people into the land — a typological anticipation of Christ bringing humanity into the Kingdom (CCC 2574).

## Theological Significance

- **Law and Grace**: Moses representing the Law cannot enter the Promised Land; Joshua representing Christ brings the people in — this typology is central to Catholic soteriology
- **Solidarity**: The eastern tribes' obligation to fight alongside their brothers models the communal dimension of Christian life and Catholic social teaching
- **Divine sovereignty**: God's refusal of Moses' prayer teaches that even the holiest petition may be denied according to God's providential plan — a theme the Catechism addresses in its teaching on prayer (CCC 2735-2737)
- **Spiritual combat**: The military victories prefigure the baptismal renunciation of Satan and the spiritual warfare of the Christian life

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Cyril of Alexandria developed the Moses-Joshua typology extensively, arguing that the entire narrative of Deuteronomy points toward the insufficiency of the Law and the necessity of Christ. Joseph Ratzinger (Pope Benedict XVI) echoed this reading in *Jesus of Nazareth*, noting that "the Torah of Moses points beyond itself" toward the definitive Prophet announced in Deuteronomy 18.
