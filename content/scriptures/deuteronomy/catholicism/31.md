## Moses Commissions Joshua and Entrusts the Law

Deuteronomy 31 narrates Moses' commissioning of Joshua as his successor, the writing of the Torah and its deposit beside the Ark, and God's warning that Israel will eventually apostatize. Catholic ecclesiology finds in this chapter a model for apostolic succession, the transmission of the deposit of faith, and the realistic assessment of the Church's ongoing struggle with unfaithfulness.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### "Be strong and courageous" (31:6-8)

Moses exhorts Joshua and all Israel with these words, grounding courage not in human strength but in God's promise to "go before you" and "not fail you or forsake you." Catholic spirituality reads this as a paradigm for Christian confidence: courage is not the absence of fear but trust in God's presence. The Catechism identifies fortitude as a gift of the Holy Spirit that empowers believers to face trials (CCC 1831). St. Theresa of Avila counseled her sisters that courage in the spiritual life depends entirely on trust in God's fidelity.

### The Commissioning of Joshua (31:14-15, 23)

God personally commissions Joshua in the tent of meeting. Catholic ecclesiology sees in this transfer of authority from Moses to Joshua a type of apostolic succession. Just as Moses did not choose his own successor but received the appointment from God, the Church's bishops receive their authority through the sacramental laying on of hands in an unbroken chain from the apostles (CCC 1555-1561). The Second Vatican Council taught that bishops are successors of the apostles "by divine institution" (*Lumen Gentium* 20).

### Writing and Depositing the Torah (31:24-26)

Moses writes the Torah and commands the Levites to place it beside the Ark of the Covenant as "a witness against you." Catholic theology sees in this act the deposit of a sacred text that must be preserved and transmitted faithfully. The Catechism teaches that Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition constitute "one sacred deposit of the word of God" entrusted to the Church (CCC 97). The physical deposit of the Torah beside the Ark models the Church's custody of the deposit of faith.

### God's Prediction of Apostasy (31:16-18, 29)

God foretells that Israel will turn to other gods after Moses' death. Catholic theology reads this sober prediction as a reminder that the Church, while indefectible in its essential mission, is composed of sinful members who are always capable of infidelity. The Catechism acknowledges that the Church is "at once holy and always in need of purification" (CCC 825). The Second Vatican Council's *Lumen Gentium* adopted this same realism, teaching that the Church is a "pilgrim" church marked by both holiness and the need for continual reform.

## Theological Significance

- **Apostolic succession**: The Moses-Joshua transfer of authority models the Church's episcopal succession
- **Deposit of faith**: The Torah deposited beside the Ark prefigures the Church's custody of Scripture and Tradition
- **Fortitude**: Christian courage rests on God's promises, not human resources
- **Ecclesial realism**: The prediction of apostasy models the Church's honest acknowledgment of sin among its members

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Clement of Rome, writing to the Corinthians (c. 96 AD), appealed to the principle of ordered succession demonstrated in the Old Testament — including the Moses-Joshua transition — to argue for proper ecclesial authority (1 Clement 42-44). Joseph Ratzinger noted that the deposit of the Torah beside the Ark establishes the principle that the written word has a normative function within the covenant community, a principle that the Church continues in its reverence for Sacred Scripture.
