## Centralization of Worship

Deuteronomy 12 opens the Deuteronomic Code with the command to centralize worship at the place the Lord will choose. All pagan sanctuaries must be destroyed, and sacrifice may be offered only at the one divinely designated site. Catholic theology reads this centralizing impulse as a providential preparation for the Church's unified liturgy centered on the Eucharistic sacrifice.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### "The place which the LORD your God will choose" (12:5)

This formula — repeated throughout Deuteronomy — refers historically to Jerusalem and its Temple. Catholic tradition sees in the single sanctuary a principle: authentic worship requires communion with the one visible center that God establishes. The Church, with its visible unity under the Bishop of Rome, continues this Deuteronomic logic. The Second Vatican Council taught that the Eucharistic assembly, united with the bishop, is the normative expression of the Church (*Sacrosanctum Concilium* 41-42).

### "You shall not do according to all that we are doing here today, every man doing what is right in his own eyes" (12:8)

This prohibition of liturgical individualism resonates with Catholic insistence on the objective normativity of liturgical law. Worship is not a matter of private preference but of communal obedience to divinely established forms. The Catechism teaches that "no sacramental rite may be modified or manipulated at the will of the minister or the community" (CCC 1125).

### The Permission for Secular Slaughter (12:15-16, 20-25)

The centralization of sacrifice created a practical problem: people living far from the sanctuary needed to eat meat. Deuteronomy 12 solves this by distinguishing sacred sacrifice from ordinary slaughter, while maintaining the absolute prohibition on consuming blood. Catholic theology connects the blood prohibition to the theology of sacrifice: blood belongs to God because it represents life (CCC 2260). The Eucharistic cup, in which Christ's blood is truly received, fulfills what the Deuteronomic prohibition protected.

### "You shall destroy their altars" (12:2-3)

The command to demolish pagan shrines reflects the radical incompatibility of true worship with idolatry. Catholic tradition, especially in missionary history, has wrestled with the application of this principle. The Church's approach has evolved from literal destruction of pagan sites (as in the early medieval missions) to the inculturation theology of Vatican II, which distinguishes between pagan worship (rejected) and cultural forms (potentially redeemed).

## Theological Significance

- **Liturgical unity**: The one sanctuary anticipates the Church's universal Eucharistic communion
- **Objective worship**: Liturgy is governed by divine institution, not human preference
- **Theology of blood**: The prohibition on consuming blood points toward the Eucharistic mystery
- **Inculturation**: The Church must destroy idolatry while respecting legitimate cultural diversity

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Cyril of Jerusalem, in his *Mystagogical Catecheses*, drew on the Temple-centralization tradition to explain why Christians gather at one altar for one Eucharist. The Jesuit scholar Dennis McCarthy argued that the centralization law is fundamentally about covenant loyalty — Israel must worship the one God in one place, just as the Church celebrates one sacrifice at one altar.
