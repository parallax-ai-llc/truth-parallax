## The Three Pilgrimage Feasts and Justice

Deuteronomy 16 prescribes the three great pilgrimage festivals — Passover, the Feast of Weeks, and the Feast of Tabernacles — and concludes with instructions for the appointment of judges. Catholic tradition reads the liturgical calendar of Israel as the providential preparation for the Church's own sacramental and liturgical life.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### Passover (16:1-8)

The Passover is the preeminent Old Testament type of the Eucharist. Catholic theology teaches that Christ's Last Supper was a Passover meal in which he transformed the paschal sacrifice into the sacrifice of the New Covenant (CCC 1339-1340). The Paschal Lamb of Exodus and Deuteronomy is fulfilled in "Christ, our Passover" (1 Cor 5:7). St. Melito of Sardis, in his *Peri Pascha* (c. 160 AD), developed the Passover typology in exquisite detail, showing how every element of the Israelite feast finds its fulfillment in Christ's Passion.

### The Feast of Weeks (16:9-12)

The Feast of Weeks (Shavuot), celebrated seven weeks after Passover, commemorated the wheat harvest and was later associated with the giving of the Law at Sinai. Catholic tradition identifies Pentecost — the descent of the Holy Spirit fifty days after Easter — as the fulfillment of this feast. Just as the Law was given at Sinai, the Spirit writes God's law on hearts at Pentecost (CCC 696, 1287). The Catechism connects Pentecost with the sacrament of Confirmation, in which the baptized receive the fullness of the Spirit.

### The Feast of Tabernacles (16:13-15)

The Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot) celebrated the autumn harvest and commemorated Israel's wilderness dwelling in tents. Catholic liturgical theology sees in this feast a sign of the pilgrim nature of the Church. The Catechism teaches that the Christian life is a journey toward the heavenly homeland (CCC 1013). The joy prescribed for this feast — "you shall rejoice" — anticipates the eschatological joy of the heavenly banquet.

### "You shall not pervert justice" (16:18-20)

The chapter's conclusion links liturgy and justice. Catholic social teaching insists on this connection: authentic worship requires just social structures. The Catechism presents the pursuit of justice as an integral dimension of Christian life (CCC 1807, 2411). Pope Benedict XVI, in *Deus Caritas Est*, argued that justice and charity are inseparable — the Church's liturgical life must overflow into social action.

## Theological Significance

- **Passover and Eucharist**: The paschal feast is the primary Old Testament type of the Mass
- **Pentecost and the Spirit**: The Feast of Weeks prefigures the outpouring of the Holy Spirit
- **Pilgrimage spirituality**: The Feast of Tabernacles models the Church's self-understanding as a pilgrim people
- **Liturgy and justice**: Worship and social ethics are inseparable in Deuteronomic and Catholic theology

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Melito of Sardis remains the classical patristic source for Passover typology. Jean Danielou, in *The Bible and the Liturgy*, systematically traced the connections between Israel's feasts and the Christian sacramental calendar, showing how Catholic liturgy organically developed from its Old Testament roots.
