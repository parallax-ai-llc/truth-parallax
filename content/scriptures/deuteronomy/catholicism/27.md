## The Altar on Ebal and the Twelve Curses

Deuteronomy 27 prescribes a covenant ceremony to be enacted upon entry into the Promised Land: large stones inscribed with the law, an altar on Mount Ebal, and a series of twelve curses pronounced by the Levites. Catholic theology reads this chapter as a dramatic enactment of moral accountability and a prefiguration of the Church's own penitential liturgies.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### The Stones of the Law (27:1-8)

Moses commands that large stones be set up, plastered, and inscribed with "all the words of this law." The public inscription of the law makes it accessible to all — no one can claim ignorance. Catholic moral theology affirms that the natural law is inscribed in every human heart and that ignorance of the moral law does not excuse violations committed with negligence (CCC 1790-1794). The public stones also model the Church's obligation to proclaim the moral law openly and clearly.

### The Altar on Ebal (27:5-7)

The altar of uncut stones on Mount Ebal — the mountain of curses — suggests that sacrifice is offered precisely where human failure is most acutely acknowledged. Catholic theology sees in this the logic of the Cross: Christ offers his sacrifice at the place of the curse, transforming condemnation into redemption. The Eucharistic sacrifice is offered for the forgiveness of sins (Matt 26:28), not in a place of innocence but in the midst of a sinful world (CCC 1365-1367).

### The Twelve Curses (27:15-26)

The Levites pronounce twelve curses on specific sins: idolatry, dishonoring parents, moving boundary markers, misleading the blind, perverting justice for the vulnerable, sexual transgressions, secret murder, bribery, and failure to keep the whole law. The people respond "Amen" to each curse, publicly accepting the moral standard. This liturgical form resembles the examination of conscience in Catholic penitential practice. The Catechism's treatment of particular sins draws on biblical catalogs like this one (CCC 1852-1853).

### "Cursed be anyone who does not confirm the words of this law by doing them" (27:26)

The final curse encompasses all who fail to keep the entire law. St. Paul quotes this verse in Galatians 3:10 to argue that no one can be justified by works of the law alone. Catholic theology, following the Council of Trent, teaches that justification comes through grace received in faith, though the justified are enabled by grace to fulfill the law's demands (CCC 1987-1995). The curse reveals the human predicament that only Christ's redemption can resolve.

## Theological Significance

- **Public moral accountability**: The law must be publicly proclaimed and publicly acknowledged
- **Sacrifice at the place of the curse**: The Ebal altar prefigures the logic of the Cross
- **Examination of conscience**: The twelve curses model the practice of naming specific sins before God
- **The curse of the law**: Paul's use of 27:26 is central to Catholic understanding of justification by grace

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Paul's use of Deuteronomy 27:26 in Galatians 3 generated extensive patristic and conciliar reflection on the relationship between law and grace. The Council of Trent carefully balanced Paul's teaching with the affirmation that grace empowers genuine obedience. Henri de Lubac noted that the Ebal-Gerizim ceremony is one of the most architecturally significant liturgies in the Old Testament, establishing the principle that worship shapes communal identity.
