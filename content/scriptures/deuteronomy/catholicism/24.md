## Divorce, Justice, and Care for the Vulnerable

Deuteronomy 24 contains the well-known regulation on divorce, along with humanitarian laws protecting newlyweds, pledges, kidnapping victims, lepers, hired workers, and the gleaning rights of the poor. Catholic theology finds in this chapter both a key text on the indissolubility of marriage and foundational principles of social justice.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### The Divorce Regulation (24:1-4)

Moses permits a man to give his wife a certificate of divorce if he finds "some indecency" in her. Jesus directly addresses this passage in Matthew 19:3-9, teaching that Moses permitted divorce "because of your hardness of heart, but from the beginning it was not so." Catholic teaching on marriage's indissolubility is built on Christ's authoritative reinterpretation of this Deuteronomic text. The Catechism affirms that a ratified and consummated sacramental marriage cannot be dissolved by any human power (CCC 1640-1641, 2382-2386). The Deuteronomic permission is understood as a concession to human weakness, not a statement of God's original intention.

### Protection of the Newlywed (24:5)

A newly married man is exempt from military service for one year "to be happy with his wife." Catholic theology reads this as an affirmation of the goodness of marriage and conjugal love. The Second Vatican Council described marriage as an "intimate partnership of life and love" (*Gaudium et Spes* 48), and this Deuteronomic provision demonstrates that even in the Old Testament, the marital bond was valued as a good worthy of protection.

### "You shall not oppress a hired worker who is poor and needy" (24:14-15)

The obligation to pay wages promptly and justly is a cornerstone of Catholic social teaching on labor. The Catechism teaches that withholding wages is one of the sins that cry to heaven for vengeance (CCC 2434, 1867). Pope Leo XIII in *Rerum Novarum* and subsequent papal encyclicals drew on biblical texts like this to insist on the just wage as a matter of strict justice, not mere generosity.

### Gleaning Rights (24:19-22)

The requirement to leave grain, olives, and grapes for "the sojourner, the fatherless, and the widow" establishes what Catholic social teaching calls the universal destination of goods. Private ownership does not extinguish the right of the needy to a share of the earth's produce. The Catechism teaches that the right to private property does not abolish "the original gift of the earth to the whole of mankind" (CCC 2403).

## Theological Significance

- **Marriage indissolubility**: Christ's teaching on marriage corrects and perfects the Deuteronomic divorce concession
- **Dignity of labor**: Just wages are a requirement of divine law, not human convention
- **Preferential option for the poor**: Gleaning laws instantiate the principle that the goods of the earth serve all
- **Progressive revelation**: The divorce regulation illustrates how God's pedagogy gradually leads humanity to the fullness of moral truth

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Jerome, commenting on Matthew 19, insisted that Christ's abrogation of the Deuteronomic divorce provision demonstrated the superiority of the New Covenant to the Old. Pope John Paul II's "Theology of the Body" audiences developed the connection between Genesis 1-2 and Jesus' appeal to "the beginning" as the basis for Catholic sexual ethics. The Pontifical Biblical Commission's treatment of marriage in Scripture emphasizes the trajectory from Deuteronomy's concession to the Gospel's restoration of the original divine plan.
