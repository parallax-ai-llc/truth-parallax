## The Decalogue Restated

Deuteronomy 5 presents the Deuteronomic version of the Ten Commandments, which the Catholic Church follows as its standard enumeration. This chapter is arguably the single most influential Old Testament text in Catholic moral theology, as the Catechism's entire treatment of the moral life (Part Three, Section Two) is structured around the Decalogue as presented here.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### "The LORD our God made a covenant with us at Horeb" (5:2)

Moses insists that the covenant was not made with the ancestors alone but "with us, who are all of us here alive today." Catholic liturgical theology sees here the principle of contemporaneity: the faithful in every age participate in the saving events of the past. This is the logic of the Eucharistic memorial — the Paschal Mystery is not a distant event but one in which believers are made present (CCC 1362-1367).

### The Commandments and Their Catholic Enumeration

The Catholic numbering, following St. Augustine, combines the prohibition of other gods and the prohibition of graven images into the First Commandment, and divides the prohibition of coveting into two separate commandments (Ninth and Tenth). This enumeration shapes the structure of Catholic moral catechesis (CCC 2052-2557).

### "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt" (5:6)

The preface to the Commandments establishes that law follows grace. God first liberates, then commands. Catholic moral theology insists that the Commandments are not arbitrary impositions but expressions of the love that sets free. As the Catechism teaches, "The Commandments take on their full meaning within the covenant" (CCC 2061).

### The Sabbath Commandment (5:12-15)

The Deuteronomic Sabbath command uniquely grounds rest in the Exodus liberation rather than creation (as in Exodus 20). Catholic teaching holds that the Lord's Day fulfills and transforms the Sabbath through Christ's Resurrection (CCC 2174-2176). The social dimension — rest for servants, animals, and strangers — informs Catholic teaching on workers' rights and the dignity of labor (*Rerum Novarum*, *Laborem Exercens*).

### "Honor your father and your mother" (5:16)

The Fourth Commandment (in Catholic numbering) is the bridge between duties to God and duties to neighbor. The Catechism develops this extensively as the foundation of family ethics, social authority, and civic obligation (CCC 2197-2257).

## Theological Significance

- **Natural law**: The Decalogue expresses the natural moral law inscribed in every human heart, accessible to reason and confirmed by revelation (CCC 1954-1960)
- **Grace precedes law**: The covenantal context of the Commandments means that obedience is a response to prior divine love, not a means of earning it
- **Liturgical contemporaneity**: Moses' insistence that the covenant is "with us" today grounds the Catholic theology of sacramental participation
- **Social morality**: The Sabbath commandment's concern for servants and strangers founds Catholic social teaching on labor and human dignity

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Augustine's enumeration of the Commandments, which the Catholic Church adopted, was not arbitrary but theological: by combining the prohibition of other gods with the ban on images, Augustine emphasized that idolatry is fundamentally a disorder of worship rather than a question of art. St. Thomas Aquinas systematized the Decalogue within his natural law framework, arguing that the Ten Commandments articulate what reason can discover about the moral order (*Summa Theologiae* I-II, q. 100).
