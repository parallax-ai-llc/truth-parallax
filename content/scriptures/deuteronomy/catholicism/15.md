## The Sabbatical Year and Release of Debts

Deuteronomy 15 legislates the septennial release of debts, generosity toward the poor, and the release of bondservants in the seventh year. Catholic social teaching draws extensively from this chapter, finding in it the biblical foundations of economic justice, the preferential option for the poor, and the principle that economic life must serve human dignity.

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### "At the end of every seven years you shall grant a release" (15:1)

The periodic cancellation of debts reflects the conviction that economic arrangements must be subordinate to communal well-being. Catholic social teaching, from Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) to Francis' *Fratelli Tutti* (2020), insists that the economy must serve the person, not the reverse. The Catechism teaches that the goods of creation are destined for the whole human race (CCC 2402-2406). The Jubilee tradition, rooted in passages like this, was invoked by John Paul II when he called for debt relief for developing nations during the Jubilee Year 2000.

### "There will be no poor among you... if only you will strictly obey" (15:4-5)

This utopian vision — a community without poverty — represents God's intention for human society. Catholic theology holds this as an eschatological goal that the Church must work toward in history, while recognizing its full realization awaits the Kingdom. The tension between the ideal (15:4) and the realistic acknowledgment that "the poor will never cease out of the land" (15:11) informs Catholic social realism.

### "You shall not harden your heart or shut your hand against your poor brother" (15:7)

This is among the most direct biblical commands regarding care for the poor. The Catechism identifies love of the poor as inseparable from love of God (CCC 2443). The Latin American bishops at Medellin (1968) and Puebla (1979) developed the concept of the "preferential option for the poor" from biblical texts like this one, a concept subsequently endorsed by John Paul II and the universal Magisterium.

### Release of Bondservants (15:12-18)

The law requiring release of Hebrew slaves after six years, with generous provision upon departure, establishes human dignity as a limit on economic exploitation. Catholic teaching draws from this the principle that labor must never reduce the worker to a mere instrument. *Laborem Exercens* (1981) insists on the priority of labor over capital and the inherent dignity of every worker.

## Theological Significance

- **Preferential option for the poor**: Deuteronomy 15 is a foundational text for Catholic social doctrine on poverty
- **Economic justice**: Periodic debt release establishes the principle that economic structures must serve human dignity
- **Generosity as duty**: Open-handedness toward the needy is commanded, not merely recommended
- **Human dignity and labor**: The bondservant laws establish that no person may be permanently reduced to an instrument of another's profit

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

St. Basil the Great, in his homilies on wealth, drew directly on Deuteronomy 15 to argue that hoarding wealth while others suffer is a form of theft. St. Ambrose similarly taught that "the earth belongs to all, not only to the rich" (*De Nabuthae*). The modern Catholic social tradition, from *Rerum Novarum* through *Laudato Si'*, continues this Deuteronomic concern for economic justice and the common good.
