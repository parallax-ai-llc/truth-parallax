## The Shema: Love of God with Whole Heart

Deuteronomy 6 contains the Shema — "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one" — followed by the command to love God with all one's heart, soul, and strength. Jesus identified this as the greatest commandment (Mark 12:29-30), making it the cornerstone of Catholic moral and spiritual theology. The Catechism treats the Shema as the foundation of the First Commandment and of the entire moral life (CCC 2083).

## Key Verses and Catholic Interpretation

### "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one" (6:4)

The confession of divine unity is the bedrock of monotheistic faith. Catholic theology affirms this oneness while developing it through the doctrine of the Trinity: God is one in substance and three in persons. The Catechism teaches that the Trinity does not compromise the Shema but fulfills it — the three persons are one God (CCC 200-202). St. Augustine's *De Trinitate* devoted extensive attention to showing how Trinitarian faith is consistent with the Deuteronomic confession.

### "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your might" (6:5)

This is the Great Commandment, cited by Jesus as the summary of the Law. Catholic spirituality understands this as a call to total self-gift — the response of the creature to the Creator's prior love. St. Bernard of Clairvaux's *On Loving God* develops this Deuteronomic command into a systematic account of the degrees of love, from self-interest to pure love of God for God's sake. The Catechism identifies faith, hope, and charity as the theological virtues by which this total love is made possible (CCC 1812-1829).

### "You shall teach them diligently to your children" (6:7)

The command to transmit faith within the family is foundational to Catholic catechetical theology. The Catechism identifies the family as "the domestic church" and the primary setting for faith formation (CCC 2204-2206). The Apostolic Exhortation *Familiaris Consortio* (1981) of John Paul II developed this theme extensively, calling Christian parents "the first heralds of the faith."

### "You shall not put the LORD your God to the test" (6:16)

Jesus quotes this verse when Satan tempts him in the desert (Matt 4:7). Catholic moral theology treats the testing of God as a sin against the virtue of religion — an attempt to manipulate divine power rather than trust divine providence (CCC 2119). St. Thomas Aquinas classified it as a species of irreligion (*Summa Theologiae* II-II, q. 97).

## Theological Significance

- **Greatest Commandment**: The Shema, as interpreted by Christ, is the organizing principle of Catholic morality
- **Trinitarian monotheism**: The unity of God proclaimed in the Shema is deepened, not contradicted, by Trinitarian revelation
- **Family catechesis**: The domestic transmission of faith is a divine command, not merely a pastoral preference
- **Temptation and trust**: Jesus' use of 6:16 against Satan makes this chapter a key text in Catholic spiritual theology on discernment

## Patristic and Scholarly Voices

Origen of Alexandria taught that the threefold love — heart, soul, strength — corresponds to three dimensions of the human person, each of which must be oriented to God. Pope Benedict XVI's encyclical *Deus Caritas Est* (2005) begins with the Shema and argues that the biblical command to love God is not an imposition from outside but the response to a love that has first been given.
