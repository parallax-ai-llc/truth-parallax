## The Decalogue at Horeb

Deuteronomy 5 restates the Ten Commandments as delivered at Horeb, emphasizing that the covenant was made not only with the ancestors but with the living generation. Moses recounts the people's fear at hearing God's voice and their request that Moses serve as intermediary.

## Islamic Parallels

### The Ten Commandments and Islamic Moral Law

The Decalogue's core prohibitions — idolatry, murder, theft, adultery, false witness — are all affirmed in the Quran and Sunnah. Surah Al-An'am (6:151-153) presents a strikingly parallel list of moral imperatives that scholars call the "Quranic Decalogue": do not associate anything with God, honor parents, do not kill your children, do not approach immoralities, do not kill the soul God has made sacred, do not consume the orphan's wealth, and be just.

### The Sabbath Commandment (5:12-15)

While Islam does not observe a Sabbath rest, Friday (*Jumu'ah*) holds a parallel communal worship function. **Ibn Kathir** noted that the Sabbath was specific to the Mosaic dispensation, while Islam's Friday does not carry the same prohibitions on labor, reflecting a different relationship between worship and daily life.

### Moses as Intermediary (5:23-31)

The people's plea for Moses to speak with God on their behalf establishes the prophetic mediator model. In Islam, Muhammad similarly intercedes (*shafa'ah*) for his community. **Al-Bukhari** records hadiths about Muhammad's intercession on the Day of Judgment, connecting him to the Mosaic prophetic office.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Decalogue's commandments correspond to the five *daruriyyat* (necessities) of Islamic *maqasid al-shari'ah*: protection of religion (no idolatry), life (no murder), intellect (implicitly), lineage (no adultery), and property (no theft). **Al-Shatibi** articulated these as the universal objectives underlying all divine law across prophetic dispensations.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars generally regard the ethical content of the Decalogue as authentic prophetic teaching preserved despite textual alterations elsewhere. **Shah Waliullah al-Dihlawi** argued that moral commandments represent the most reliably transmitted element of earlier scriptures because they were reinforced by continuous prophetic practice.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Qurtubi** compared the Decalogue with Quranic ethical prescriptions and concluded that the moral core of divine law is universal, differing only in ritual specifics (*shari'ah*) while sharing fundamental principles (*din*).

**Abdulaziz Sachedina** discusses how Islamic ethics draws on the shared Abrahamic moral heritage reflected in texts like Deuteronomy 5, viewing them as part of a continuous prophetic tradition.
