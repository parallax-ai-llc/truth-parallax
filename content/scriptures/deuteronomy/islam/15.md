## Release, Generosity, and Freedom

Deuteronomy 15 establishes the sabbatical year release of debts, commands open-handed generosity toward the poor, and regulates the release of Hebrew slaves after six years of service. The chapter insists that there should be no poor among them if they obey God's commandments.

## Islamic Parallels

### Debt Release and the Ethics of Lending (15:1-6)

The seven-year debt release resonates with Islamic prohibitions on *riba* (usury/interest) and the strong encouragement to forgive debts. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:280): "If the debtor is in hardship, then grant him time until ease. But if you give from your right as charity, then it is better for you." **Ibn Rushd** in *Bidayat al-Mujtahid* discussed the obligation to extend grace to struggling debtors.

### Open-Handedness Toward the Poor (15:7-11)

Moses' command to not harden one's heart or shut one's hand toward a poor brother mirrors the Quranic injunction in Surah Al-Ma'un (107:1-7), which condemns those who repulse the orphan and do not encourage feeding the poor. **Al-Ghazali** identified generosity (*sakha'*) as one of the highest virtues of the purified soul.

### Manumission of Slaves (15:12-18)

The requirement to free Hebrew slaves after six years — and to provide them with resources — parallels Islam's strong encouragement of manumission (*'itq*). Surah Al-Balad (90:12-13) identifies freeing a slave as the steep path of righteousness. **Al-Bukhari** records numerous hadiths praising the freeing of slaves as an act of supreme piety.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Deuteronomic vision that "there will be no poor among you" (15:4) echoes the *maqasid al-shari'ah* objective of economic justice. Islamic institutions of *zakat*, *sadaqah*, *waqf*, and the prohibition of *riba* collectively aim at the same goal. **Al-Qaradawi** argued that poverty elimination is a communal obligation in Islamic law.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the social justice legislation of Deuteronomy 15 as authentic prophetic teaching. **Shah Waliullah** noted that laws protecting the poor and regulating debt are too consistent across prophetic traditions to be the product of human invention, suggesting reliable transmission of divine intent.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Mawdudi** compared the Deuteronomic debt-release system with the Islamic economic framework, arguing that both aim to prevent the concentration of wealth and the exploitation of the vulnerable.

**Muhammad Hashim Kamali** observes that the principle of periodic debt relief in Deuteronomy 15 finds structural parallels in Islamic bankruptcy and insolvency jurisprudence.
