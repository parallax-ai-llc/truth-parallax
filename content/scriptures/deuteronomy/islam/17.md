## Judicial Authority and the Law of the King

Deuteronomy 17 addresses judicial procedure for difficult cases, mandating reference to the Levitical priests and the judge in office. It then establishes the "law of the king" — limiting royal authority by requiring the king to write a copy of the Torah, read it daily, and not accumulate excessive horses, wives, or wealth.

## Islamic Parallels

### The Constrained Ruler (17:14-20)

The Deuteronomic king is subject to God's law, not above it — a principle that directly parallels the Islamic concept of the caliph as bound by *shari'ah*. **Abu Bakr**, the first caliph, declared in his inaugural address: "Obey me so long as I obey God and His Messenger. If I disobey them, you owe me no obedience." **Al-Mawardi** formalized this principle in *Al-Ahkam al-Sultaniyyah*.

### The King Must Study the Law (17:18-19)

The requirement that the king personally copy and study the Torah parallels the Islamic expectation that rulers be knowledgeable in religion. **Ibn Taymiyyah** in *Al-Siyasa al-Shar'iyya* argued that a ruler ignorant of divine law cannot govern justly, echoing the Deuteronomic vision of a Torah-reading king.

### Judicial Escalation (17:8-13)

The system of referring difficult cases to a higher court parallels the Islamic *qada* system, where complex cases are escalated to more senior jurists. **Al-Sarakhsi** in *Al-Mabsut* discussed the hierarchy of judicial authority and the obligation to accept the ruling of qualified scholars.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The limitation on royal wealth, horses, and wives parallels the Islamic ethical framework for rulers. **Umar ibn al-Khattab** was famous for his austere lifestyle as caliph, and Islamic scholars viewed his conduct as implementing the prophetic ideal of a ruler constrained by divine law — the same ideal found in Deuteronomy 17.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the law of the king as a remarkable example of prophetic legislation limiting political power. **Ibn Khaldun** argued that the Deuteronomic insistence on royal accountability represents an authentic Mosaic principle that later Israelite kings frequently violated, demonstrating the gap between prophetic law and historical practice.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Farabi** in *The Virtuous City* described the ideal ruler as one guided by divine wisdom, a philosophical elaboration of the Deuteronomic-prophetic principle that governance must be rooted in revealed law.

**Patricia Crone** draws extensive parallels between the Deuteronomic law of the king and Islamic theories of governance, arguing that both traditions share a vision of divinely constrained political authority.
