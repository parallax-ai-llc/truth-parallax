## "Hear, O Israel" — The Great Commandment

Deuteronomy 6 contains the Shema — "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one" — the foundational declaration of Israelite monotheism. Moses commands total love of God with heart, soul, and strength, instructs parents to teach these words diligently to their children, and warns against forgetting God when prosperity comes.

## Islamic Parallels

### Shema and Shahada — Twin Declarations of Oneness

The Shema ("The LORD is one") is the closest biblical parallel to the Islamic *Shahada* ("There is no god but Allah"). Both function as the supreme declaration of *tawhid* (divine unity). **Ibn Taymiyyah** in *Majmu' al-Fatawa* argued that all prophets proclaimed the same foundational truth of God's absolute oneness, and Deuteronomy 6:4 is evidence of this continuity.

### Loving God Totally (6:5)

The command to love God with all one's heart, soul, and strength corresponds to the Islamic ideal of *ikhlas* (sincerity) and *mahabbah* (divine love). Surah Al-Baqarah (2:165) states: "Those who believe are stronger in love for Allah." **Al-Ghazali** devoted an entire book of the *Ihya* to divine love as the highest station of faith.

### Teaching the Next Generation (6:6-9)

Moses' instruction to teach children God's words "when you sit in your house, when you walk by the way, when you lie down, and when you rise" mirrors the Islamic emphasis on continuous remembrance (*dhikr*). The Prophet Muhammad said: "Teach your children prayer at seven" (Abu Dawud), reflecting the same principle of early religious education.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The comprehensive claim on the believer's life — heart, soul, strength — parallels the Islamic legal concept that *'ibadah* (worship) encompasses all of life, not merely ritual. **Ibn al-Qayyim** argued that true servitude to God means every action, intention, and resource is oriented toward divine pleasure.

The warning against forgetting God amid prosperity (6:10-12) connects to Islamic rulings on *zakat* and gratitude as obligations that prevent wealth from becoming a spiritual distraction.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars consider Deuteronomy 6:4 one of the most authentically preserved verses in the Torah, precisely because it affirms the *tawhid* that the Quran confirms. **Fakhr al-Din al-Razi** cited the Shema as proof that Moses' original message was identical in substance to Muhammad's, despite later textual alterations elsewhere in the Torah.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Shahrastani** placed the Shema at the center of his analysis of Jewish theology, arguing that this declaration represents the uncorrupted Mosaic teaching that Islam restores and perfects.

**Reza Aslan** observes that the structural parallel between Shema and Shahada reflects the deep continuity of Abrahamic monotheism, with Islam self-consciously positioning its creed as the restoration of this original Mosaic proclamation.
