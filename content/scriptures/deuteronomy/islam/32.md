## Moses' Song — A Prophetic Poem

Deuteronomy 32 contains the Song of Moses (*Ha'azinu*), a powerful poem that recounts God's faithfulness, Israel's ingratitude and idolatry, the divine punishment, and God's ultimate vindication. It begins with the heavens and earth as witnesses and ends with God's promise to avenge His servants' blood.

## Islamic Parallels

### Heaven and Earth as Witnesses (32:1)

Moses' invocation of heaven and earth as witnesses parallels the Quranic use of cosmic witnesses. Surah Al-Buruj (85:1-3): "By the sky containing great stars, and by the promised Day, and by the witness and what is witnessed." **Al-Razi** noted that calling creation to witness is a prophetic rhetorical device shared across revelations.

### The Rock — God's Faithfulness (32:4, 15, 18, 30-31)

The repeated reference to God as "the Rock" — faithful, just, and true — echoes the Quranic *asma' al-husna* (Beautiful Names) of God: *Al-Haqq* (the Truth), *Al-'Adl* (the Just). **Al-Ghazali** in *Al-Maqsad al-Asna* discussed these divine attributes as expressions of God's unwavering nature.

### Israel's Ingratitude and Idolatry (32:15-18)

"Jeshurun grew fat and kicked" — Israel's turn to idolatry amid prosperity — mirrors the Quranic pattern of communities who prosper and then forget God. Surah Al-'Alaq (96:6-7): "No! Indeed, man transgresses because he sees himself self-sufficient." **Sayyid Qutb** identified this as the fundamental human spiritual disease.

### Divine Vengeance and Mercy (32:35-43)

God's declaration "Vengeance is Mine" parallels the Quranic assurance that God will ultimately judge between truth and falsehood. Surah Al-Hajj (22:39-40) permits defensive fighting precisely because God intervenes in history to prevent the total suppression of worship.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Song's structure — blessing, warning, punishment, restoration — embodies what **Al-Shatibi** called the Quranic *sunan* (patterns) of divine dealing with communities. These patterns form the basis for Islamic legal reasoning about collective moral responsibility and its consequences.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars recognize the Song of Moses as an ancient poetic composition with theological depth. **Ibn Khaldun** argued that prophetic poetry is among the most resistant literary forms to textual corruption, since poetic structure constrains editorial alteration, suggesting the Song preserves authentic Mosaic themes.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** compared the Song of Moses to the Quranic surahs that recount the histories of destroyed nations, noting the shared rhetorical pattern of divine faithfulness met by human ingratitude, followed by punishment and eventual mercy.

**Angelika Neuwirth** observes that the Song of Moses and certain Meccan surahs share formal features — cosmic invocations, historical recitations, divine judgment proclamations — suggesting common roots in ancient prophetic oratory.
