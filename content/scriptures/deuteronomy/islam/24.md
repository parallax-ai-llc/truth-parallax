## Divorce, Protection of the Vulnerable, and Gleaning Laws

Deuteronomy 24 regulates divorce procedures, protects newlyweds from military service, prohibits taking millstones as pledges, addresses kidnapping, prescribes prompt payment of wages, and establishes gleaning rights for the poor, the stranger, the orphan, and the widow.

## Islamic Parallels

### Divorce Law (24:1-4)

The Deuteronomic divorce procedure — a written certificate of divorce — parallels the Islamic *talaq* system. The prohibition against remarrying a former wife after she has married another man is directly echoed in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:230): a divorced wife cannot return to her first husband until she has married and been divorced by another. **Al-Sarakhsi** noted this convergence as evidence of shared prophetic legislation on marriage.

### Prompt Payment of Wages (24:14-15)

The command to pay a hired worker before sunset parallels the hadith: "Give the laborer his wages before his sweat dries" (Ibn Majah). **Al-Qaradawi** identified workers' rights as a core Islamic economic principle with deep prophetic roots visible in the Torah.

### Gleaning Rights (24:19-22)

The instruction to leave forgotten sheaves and remaining olives and grapes for the poor, stranger, orphan, and widow mirrors the Islamic institution of *sadaqah* and the broader ethic of sharing agricultural abundance. Surah Al-An'am (6:141): "Eat of their fruit when they ripen and give its due on the day of its harvest."

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The prohibition against punishing children for their parents' sins (24:16) is a foundational principle of Islamic criminal law. Surah Al-An'am (6:164): "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another." **Al-Shafi'i** cited this as a bedrock principle of individual criminal responsibility.

The prohibition against taking essential tools (millstones) as debt pledges (24:6) parallels the Islamic rule that items necessary for livelihood cannot be seized for debt. **Al-Kasani** discussed this in the context of *rahn* (pledge/collateral) law.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the social justice legislation of Deuteronomy 24 as among the Torah's most reliably preserved prophetic content. **Shah Waliullah** argued that these laws protecting workers, widows, orphans, and strangers represent the universal prophetic ethic that cannot be corrupted because it is reinforced across all revelations.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Mawdudi** compared the Deuteronomic labor protections with Islamic workers' rights, arguing that both traditions prioritize the dignity of labor and the obligation to pay fair and timely wages.

**Wael Hallaq** notes that the structural parallels between Deuteronomic and Islamic family law — particularly divorce procedures — suggest a shared legal heritage that Islamic law consciously reformed and refined.
