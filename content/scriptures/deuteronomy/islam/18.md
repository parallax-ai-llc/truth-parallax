## The Promise of a Future Prophet

Deuteronomy 18 is among the most theologically significant chapters for Islamic-biblical interpretation. After addressing the Levitical priesthood's provisions and prohibiting divination and sorcery, Moses declares that God will raise up "a prophet like me from among your brothers" — a promise that Islamic tradition identifies as a prophecy of Muhammad.

## Islamic Parallels

### "A Prophet Like Me From Among Your Brothers" (18:15, 18)

This is the single most important Deuteronomic verse in Islamic theology. Muslim scholars argue that "from among your brothers" refers not to fellow Israelites but to the Ishmaelite branch of Abraham's descendants — the Arabs. **Ibn Taymiyyah** in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* provided an extensive argument that Muhammad fulfills every criterion of this prophecy: he was a lawgiving prophet, a national leader, and a mediator of divine revelation, "like Moses" in ways that other prophets were not.

### The Test of a True Prophet (18:21-22)

The criterion for testing prophets — whether their predictions come true — is affirmed in Islamic hadith. The Prophet Muhammad's verified prophecies (the conquest of Constantinople, internal Muslim conflicts, etc.) are cited by scholars like **Al-Bayhaqi** in *Dala'il al-Nubuwwah* as evidence that he passes the Deuteronomic test.

### Prohibition of Divination and Sorcery (18:9-14)

The comprehensive ban on sorcery, divination, necromancy, and soothsaying directly parallels Islamic prohibitions. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:102) condemns sorcery, and the Prophet declared: "Whoever goes to a fortune-teller and believes what he says has disbelieved in what was revealed to Muhammad" (Muslim). **Al-Nawawi** classified these practices as major sins.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Deuteronomic distinction between legitimate prophecy and illegitimate divination underpins the Islamic legal distinction between *wahy* (divine revelation to prophets) and *kahanah* (soothsaying). **Ibn al-Qayyim** in *Miftah Dar al-Sa'adah* elaborated on the epistemological difference between prophetic knowledge and occult claims.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars argue that the "prophet like Moses" prophecy is one of the most significant preserved passages in the Torah, precisely because it points to Muhammad. **Rahmatullah al-Hindi** in *Izhar al-Haqq* devoted extensive analysis to this verse, arguing that Jewish and Christian reinterpretations were motivated by the desire to avoid acknowledging Muhammad's prophethood.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Qurtubi** provided detailed comparisons between Moses and Muhammad — both were lawgivers, both led migrations, both established communities and states — to demonstrate that Muhammad fulfills Deuteronomy 18:15 more completely than any other prophet.

**Jamal Badawi** summarizes the Islamic case: Muhammad is the only prophet who matches Moses in receiving a comprehensive law, leading a community in both spiritual and political dimensions, and dying a natural death after completing his mission.
