## Inscribing the Law and Pronouncing Curses

Deuteronomy 27 instructs the Israelites to set up plastered stones inscribed with the law upon crossing the Jordan, build an altar on Mount Ebal, and pronounce twelve curses on those who violate specific commandments. The people affirm each curse with "Amen."

## Islamic Parallels

### Inscribing Divine Law (27:2-4, 8)

The command to write the law on stones for public display resonates with the Islamic emphasis on preserving and disseminating divine revelation. The Quran's collection into written form under Abu Bakr and its standardization under Uthman reflect the same prophetic concern for textual preservation. **Al-Suyuti** in *Al-Itqan* discussed the meticulous process of Quranic compilation.

### Communal Oath and Accountability (27:15-26)

The twelve curses, each affirmed by "Amen," constitute a communal oath binding the entire people. This parallels the Islamic *bay'ah* (pledge of allegiance) — a communal commitment to uphold divine law and righteous governance. **Al-Mawardi** described *bay'ah* as a binding contract between ruler and community, echoing the Deuteronomic covenant ceremony.

### Specific Curses and Islamic Moral Law

The cursed offenses — idolatry, dishonoring parents, moving boundary stones, misleading the blind, perverting justice for vulnerable persons, sexual immorality, and secret murder — all correspond to grave sins (*kaba'ir*) in Islamic law. **Al-Dhahabi** in *Kitab al-Kaba'ir* compiled a list of major sins that overlaps substantially with the Deuteronomic curses.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The public inscription of law connects to the Islamic principle that legal obligations must be promulgated and known before they can be enforced. **Al-Shatibi** argued that ignorance of the law is a valid excuse (*'udhr*) only if the law was not properly publicized — a principle the Deuteronomic stone inscription was designed to prevent.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars note that the ethical content of the twelve curses aligns precisely with Quranic moral teaching. **Ibn Hazm** argued that such universal moral prohibitions represent the original prophetic core that survived textual alteration because they were embedded in communal practice and memory.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** discussed the Ebal ceremony as an example of prophetic pedagogy — using public ritual to embed divine law in collective consciousness, a technique also employed in Islamic worship.

**Camilla Adang** observes that Muslim scholars frequently cited the Deuteronomic curses as evidence that the Torah's moral framework confirms Quranic teaching, even if other aspects of the text were altered.
