## The Death and Legacy of Moses

Deuteronomy 34, the final chapter of the Torah, records Moses' ascent to Mount Nebo, his view of the Promised Land he would never enter, his death at 120 years of age, his burial by God in an unknown grave, and the tribute that "there has not arisen a prophet since in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face."

## Islamic Parallels

### Moses' Death and Prophetic Finality

The death of Moses — the greatest prophet of the Israelite dispensation — carries deep significance in Islamic theology. **Al-Tabari** records traditions about Moses' death drawn from both Quranic commentary and *Isra'iliyyat*, noting that Moses initially resisted the Angel of Death, striking him and blinding him in one eye, before accepting God's decree. This narrative emphasizes that even the greatest prophets are mortal.

### "No Prophet Like Moses" (34:10) — Until Muhammad

The Torah's closing tribute — that no prophet like Moses arose in Israel — is central to Islamic exegesis. Muslim scholars argue this implicitly leaves open the possibility of such a prophet arising outside Israel. **Ibn Kathir** connected this verse to Deuteronomy 18:15-18, arguing that Muhammad is the prophet "like Moses" who arose from among Israel's "brothers" (the Ishmaelites), fulfilling the Torah's own prophecy.

### The Hidden Grave (34:6)

God's burial of Moses in an unknown location — "no one knows his burial place to this day" — resonates with the Islamic concern about grave veneration. The Prophet Muhammad said: "Do not make my grave an idol that is worshipped" (Musnad Ahmad). **Ibn al-Qayyim** argued that God concealed Moses' grave precisely to prevent the idolatrous veneration that afflicted other prophets' tombs.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Moses' death at 120 with undiminished vigor (34:7) reinforced the Islamic legal principle that prophets maintain their intellectual and physical integrity until death, ensuring the reliability of their final instructions. **Al-Qadi 'Iyad** discussed prophetic *'ismah* (protection) as encompassing physical as well as moral integrity.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars have long noted that Moses could not have written the account of his own death, making Deuteronomy 34 the clearest evidence of post-Mosaic editorial activity in the Torah. **Ibn Hazm** cited this chapter as proof that the Torah as it exists is not entirely from Moses' hand, supporting the broader *tahrif* argument. This observation does not, however, invalidate the chapter's theological content in the Muslim view.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tha'labi** in *Qisas al-Anbiya* devoted extensive coverage to Moses' final moments, portraying them as the culmination of the greatest prophetic career before Muhammad. Moses' submission to death is presented as the ultimate act of *islam* — surrender to God's will.

**Brannon Wheeler** analyzes the Islamic traditions about Moses' death, noting that they simultaneously honor Moses as the greatest pre-Muhammadan prophet while affirming that Muhammad surpassed him as the Seal of the Prophets (*Khatam al-Nabiyyin*).
