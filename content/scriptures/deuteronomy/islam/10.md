## Restoration and the Essence of True Religion

Deuteronomy 10 recounts the making of new stone tablets to replace those Moses shattered, the death of Aaron, and the Levites' appointment to carry the Ark. Moses then articulates the essence of what God requires: to fear Him, walk in His ways, love Him, serve Him with all one's heart and soul, and to circumcise the heart.

## Islamic Parallels

### New Tablets — Renewal of Revelation (10:1-5)

The replacement of the broken tablets represents divine mercy and the renewal of the covenant. In Islam, the concept of successive revelations — Torah, Psalms, Gospel, Quran — embodies this same principle. **Al-Razi** commented that God's willingness to restore the tablets after human failure demonstrates that divine guidance is never permanently withdrawn from humanity.

### Circumcision of the Heart (10:16)

The metaphor of heart circumcision — removing spiritual hardness — directly parallels the Quranic concept of purifying the heart (*tazkiyat al-nafs*). Surah Ash-Shams (91:9-10): "He has succeeded who purifies it, and he has failed who instills it with corruption." **Al-Ghazali** built his entire spiritual psychology around the purification of the heart.

### What Does God Require? (10:12-13)

Moses' summary — fear God, walk in His ways, love Him, serve Him — corresponds precisely to the Islamic framework of *taqwa* (God-consciousness), *ittiba'* (following the prophetic way), *mahabbah* (divine love), and *'ibadah* (worship). **Ibn al-Qayyim** identified these as the four pillars of the spiritual life.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The command to love the stranger "for you were strangers in the land of Egypt" (10:19) parallels the Islamic obligation of hospitality and care for travelers (*ibn al-sabil*). **Al-Kasani** in *Bada'i al-Sana'i* classified hospitality as a communal obligation (*fard kifayah*), rooted in the same prophetic ethic found here.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view Deuteronomy 10:12-13 as a remarkably well-preserved summary of authentic prophetic teaching. **Shah Waliullah** argued that such passages represent the *usul* (foundations) of the Mosaic message that survived textual alteration because they were too widely known and practiced to be changed.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Mawardi** connected the Deuteronomic requirement to "fear and love God" with the Islamic balance between *khawf* (fear) and *raja'* (hope), arguing that both are necessary for a complete relationship with the Divine.

**John Kaltner** notes that the ethical summary of Deuteronomy 10 resonates powerfully with Islamic moral theology, creating a bridge between the traditions.
