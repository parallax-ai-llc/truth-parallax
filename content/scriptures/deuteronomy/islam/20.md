## Rules of Engagement

Deuteronomy 20 provides Israel's laws of warfare: a priest addresses the troops before battle, certain exemptions from military service are granted, terms of peace must be offered to distant cities before attack, and fruit trees must not be destroyed during siege. The Canaanite cities, however, are to receive no terms.

## Islamic Parallels

### Trust in God, Not Numbers (20:1-4)

The priestly exhortation not to fear large armies parallels the Quranic assurance: "How many a small company has overcome a large company by permission of Allah" (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:249). **Khalid ibn al-Walid** exemplified this principle, and **Ibn Kathir** cited divine assistance in battle as a constant theme across prophetic history.

### Exemptions from Military Service (20:5-8)

The Deuteronomic exemptions — for those who built a new house, planted a vineyard, betrothed a wife, or are fearful — parallel Islamic jurisprudence on *jihad* exemptions. **Al-Shaybani** in *Kitab al-Siyar* discussed categories of persons exempt from military obligation, including the newly married and those with legitimate fears.

### Offering Peace Before Battle (20:10-12)

The requirement to offer terms of peace before attacking parallels the Islamic obligation to invite the enemy to Islam or a peace treaty before engaging in battle. The Prophet Muhammad instructed: "Do not fight them until you have invited them" (Muslim). **Al-Awza'i** emphasized this as a binding condition for legitimate *jihad*.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The prohibition against destroying fruit trees during siege (20:19-20) is one of the most celebrated biblical-Islamic convergences in military law. Abu Bakr's famous ten rules of warfare include: "Do not cut down fruit-bearing trees." **Al-Shaybani** codified environmental protections during warfare, directly echoing the Deuteronomic principle.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars distinguish between the universal warfare ethics of Deuteronomy 20 (offering peace, sparing trees) and the specific command to annihilate the Canaanite nations (20:16-18). **Al-Razi** argued that the total destruction command may be a later insertion reflecting nationalist ideology, since prophetic religion does not mandate ethnic annihilation.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** discussed the military campaigns of the Israelites alongside those of early Islam, noting structural parallels in the theological framing of divinely authorized warfare.

**James Turner Johnson** compares the Deuteronomic and Islamic just war traditions, finding shared principles of proportionality, discrimination, and the preference for peace that distinguish both from purely pragmatic military ethics.
