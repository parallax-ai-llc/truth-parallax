## Moses Recounts the Journey

Deuteronomy 1 opens with Moses addressing the Israelites on the plains of Moab, recounting their journey from Horeb (Sinai) through the wilderness. He recalls the appointment of leaders and the failed attempt to enter the Promised Land due to the people's disobedience.

## Islamic Parallels

### Moses as Prophet and Leader

In Islam, Musa (Moses) is one of the five greatest prophets (*Ulul Azm*). His recounting of Israel's history parallels the Quranic theme of prophets reminding their communities of God's blessings and warnings. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:30-39) similarly recounts divine covenants and human failure to uphold them.

### The Appointment of Judges (1:9-18)

Moses' delegation of authority to tribal leaders resonates with the Islamic principle of *shura* (consultation), established in Surah Ash-Shura (42:38): "and whose affair is determined by consultation among themselves." **Ibn Taymiyyah** emphasized that governance in Islam requires both divine guidance and structured human leadership.

### The Rebellion at Kadesh-Barnea (1:26-33)

The people's refusal to enter the land mirrors the Quranic account in Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:20-26), where the Israelites tell Moses: "Go, you and your Lord, and fight. Indeed, we are remaining right here." This disobedience is a central cautionary narrative in Islamic tradition.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The appointment of judges reflects principles found in Islamic *qada* (judicial) theory. **Al-Mawardi** in *Al-Ahkam al-Sultaniyyah* outlined qualifications for judges that echo Moses' criteria: wisdom, tribal knowledge, and fear of God. The Islamic legal tradition sees this as evidence of the universal prophetic concern for just governance.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the narrative framework of Deuteronomy 1 with interest, noting that while the core events — the wilderness journey, the rebellion, the divine punishment — are confirmed by the Quran, the specific framing as Moses' farewell speech may reflect later editorial arrangement. **Ibn Hazm** argued that the Torah underwent textual changes after Moses, though the underlying prophetic message remained discernible.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** in his *Tarikh* (History) provides extensive commentary on the wilderness period, drawing on both Quranic revelation and Israelite traditions (*Isra'iliyyat*). He notes that the forty-year wandering was a divine pedagogical measure, purifying the community before they could inherit the land.

**Brannon Wheeler** observes that Islamic retellings of the wilderness narrative tend to emphasize divine mercy alongside punishment, a balance that shapes how Muslim scholars read these Deuteronomic recollections.
