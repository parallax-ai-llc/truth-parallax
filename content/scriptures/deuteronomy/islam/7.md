## Separation and Election

Deuteronomy 7 commands the Israelites to destroy the Canaanite nations and their religious artifacts, forbids intermarriage, and explains Israel's election: God chose them not for their greatness but out of love and faithfulness to the patriarchal covenant. God promises blessings for obedience and warns against adopting pagan practices.

## Islamic Parallels

### Chosenness and the Islamic Ummah

The concept of Israel as a chosen people resonates with — and is reframed by — the Quranic designation of the Muslim community as "the best nation produced for mankind" (Surah Al Imran 3:110). **Al-Mawdudi** emphasized that chosenness in Islam is conditional on commanding good and forbidding evil, not ethnic identity.

### Destruction of Idols (7:5, 25-26)

The command to tear down altars and burn images directly parallels the Prophet Muhammad's destruction of the idols in the Ka'bah upon the conquest of Mecca. **Ibn Hisham** records this event as the restoration of Abrahamic monotheism, fulfilling the same prophetic mandate found in Deuteronomy.

### God's Love as Motivation (7:7-8)

God's love for Israel as the basis of election parallels the Quranic affirmation of God's love for the righteous: "Indeed, Allah loves those who are constantly repentant and loves those who purify themselves" (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:222). **Al-Ghazali** taught that divine love precedes human devotion.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The prohibition on intermarriage with idolaters (7:3-4) parallels the Quranic prohibition on Muslim women marrying polytheists (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:221). Islamic law permits Muslim men to marry women of the People of the Book, a nuance that extends the Deuteronomic principle while modifying its scope.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars have questioned the ethnic exclusivism of Deuteronomy 7, viewing the emphasis on national chosenness as a post-Mosaic addition that narrows the universalist prophetic message. **Al-Biruni** in *Tahqiq ma li-l-Hind* argued that the original Torah carried a more universal mandate, subsequently constricted to serve national interests.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** discussed the concept of Israel's election within the broader framework of God's testing various communities, noting that chosenness brings responsibility and that failure to uphold the covenant nullifies special status.

**Uri Rubin** notes that the Quranic recasting of chosenness from ethnicity to righteousness represents a deliberate theological response to passages like Deuteronomy 7.
