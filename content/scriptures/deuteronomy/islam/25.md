## Punishment, Marriage, and Honest Dealings

Deuteronomy 25 limits corporal punishment to forty lashes, establishes levirate marriage (a brother's duty to marry his deceased brother's childless widow), commands honest weights and measures, and concludes with the injunction to remember and blot out Amalek.

## Islamic Parallels

### Limiting Corporal Punishment (25:1-3)

The cap of forty lashes to prevent excessive punishment parallels the Islamic *hadd* punishment regulations. The Prophet Muhammad reportedly said: "No one should be given more than ten lashes except in one of the punishments prescribed by Allah" (al-Bukhari), establishing even stricter limits. **Al-Nawawi** discussed the jurisprudence of corporal punishment, emphasizing that its purpose is correction, not degradation.

### Honest Weights and Measures (25:13-16)

The prohibition against having "two differing weights" parallels one of the Quran's most emphatic ethical demands. Surah Al-Mutaffifin (83:1-3): "Woe to those who give short measure, who demand full measure when they receive from others but give less when they measure or weigh for them." **Al-Ghazali** identified commercial honesty as a pillar of Islamic social ethics.

### The Ox That Treads Grain (25:4)

The command not to muzzle an ox while it treads grain expresses a principle of animal welfare. Islamic tradition similarly emphasizes kindness to animals. The Prophet said: "A woman entered the Fire because of a cat" that she had confined and starved (al-Bukhari). **Al-Izz ibn Abd al-Salam** articulated animal rights in Islamic law.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Levirate marriage (25:5-10) has no direct Islamic equivalent; Islam does not obligate a brother to marry his deceased brother's wife. However, Islamic inheritance law (*fara'id*) addresses the same underlying concern — protecting the widow and preserving family lineage — through mandatory inheritance shares rather than mandatory marriage. **Ibn Qudamah** contrasted the two approaches.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the commercial ethics of Deuteronomy 25 as fully consonant with Quranic teaching. **Al-Biruni** observed that fair dealing in commerce is among the most consistently taught prophetic principles, suggesting these verses reliably preserve Moses' original instruction.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Mawardi** connected the Deuteronomic weights-and-measures legislation to the Islamic institution of *hisbah* (market regulation), arguing that state enforcement of commercial honesty is a prophetic mandate spanning all dispensations.

**Ze'ev Maghen** analyzes the comparative jurisprudence of corporal punishment in biblical and Islamic law, noting that both traditions seek to balance deterrence with human dignity.
