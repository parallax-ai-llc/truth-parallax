## Boundaries of the Community

Deuteronomy 23 defines who may enter the assembly of the Lord, prescribes regulations for camp purity during wartime, addresses fugitive slaves, cult prostitution, interest on loans, and the obligation to fulfill vows.

## Islamic Parallels

### Ritual Purity in the Camp (23:9-14)

The requirement for physical cleanliness in the military camp because "the LORD walks in the midst of your camp" parallels the Islamic emphasis on *taharah* (ritual purity) as a prerequisite for worship. Surah Al-Muddathir (74:4): "And your clothing purify." **Al-Nawawi** in *Al-Majmu'* stressed that purity is prerequisite to prayer, especially communal prayer.

### Prohibition of Interest (23:19-20)

The prohibition against charging interest to a fellow Israelite directly parallels the Islamic prohibition of *riba*. Surah Al-Baqarah (2:275): "Allah has permitted trade and has forbidden interest." Notably, both traditions permit charging interest to outsiders in the Deuteronomic context, though Islamic law universalizes the prohibition. **Ibn Rushd** discussed the nuances of *riba* prohibition in *Bidayat al-Mujtahid*.

### Fulfilling Vows (23:21-23)

The obligation to keep vows made to God parallels the Islamic treatment of *nadhr* (religious vows). Surah Al-Insan (76:7): "They fulfill their vows and fear a Day whose evil will be widespread." **Al-Bukhari** records hadiths requiring fulfillment of vows made to God, with specific conditions and exceptions.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The protection of fugitive slaves (23:15-16) — commanding that a runaway slave not be returned to his master — has no direct Islamic parallel but resonates with Islam's strong encouragement of manumission. **Al-Jassas** noted that Islam systemically incentivized the freeing of slaves through *kaffarah* (expiation) obligations, working toward the same humanitarian end through different legal mechanisms.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars note that the Deuteronomic permission to charge interest to foreigners (23:20) contradicts the universal Islamic prohibition of *riba*. **Al-Razi** argued this represents a later corruption narrowing the original universal prophetic ban on exploitative lending to apply only within the Israelite community.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Ghazali** discussed the prohibition of interest as one of the most important economic principles shared across prophetic traditions, viewing the Deuteronomic prohibition as confirming Islam's stance.

**Timur Kuran** analyzes the parallels and differences between biblical and Islamic usury prohibitions, noting that both emerge from a shared Abrahamic concern with economic justice.
