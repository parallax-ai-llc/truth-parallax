## Transition of Leadership

Deuteronomy 31 records Moses' final preparations: he announces he is 120 years old and can no longer lead, commissions Joshua as his successor, commands the law to be written down and read publicly every seven years, and receives God's warning that the people will eventually rebel.

## Islamic Parallels

### Prophetic Succession (31:1-8, 14-15, 23)

The commissioning of Joshua as Moses' successor parallels the Islamic concern with succession after the Prophet's death. While Sunni and Shia traditions disagree on how Muhammad's succession was determined, both acknowledge the centrality of legitimate leadership transition. **Al-Mawardi** discussed the qualifications for succession, echoing the Deuteronomic themes of courage, divine appointment, and communal trust.

### Writing Down the Law (31:9, 24-26)

Moses' command to write the Torah and place it beside the Ark parallels the Islamic emphasis on preserving revelation in written form. The Quran was written on various materials during Muhammad's lifetime and compiled into a codex afterward. **Al-Suyuti** drew parallels between the Mosaic and Muhammadan processes of scriptural preservation.

### God's Foreknowledge of Rebellion (31:16-21)

God's prediction that Israel will turn to other gods after Moses' death parallels the Quranic theme of communities straying after their prophets. Surah Al Imran (3:144): "Muhammad is not but a messenger. Other messengers have passed on before him. So if he was to die or be killed, would you turn back on your heels?"

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The seven-year public reading of the law (31:10-13) embodies the principle that legal knowledge must be regularly renewed in public consciousness. **Al-Ghazali** argued that scholars bear a communal obligation (*fard kifayah*) to teach divine law to every generation, preventing the ignorance that leads to communal decline.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars find irony in Deuteronomy 31's command to preserve the Torah in writing, given their view that the text was subsequently altered. **Ibn Hazm** argued that the very need to command preservation implies awareness that the text was vulnerable to corruption — a vulnerability that, in the Muslim view, was eventually realized.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** compared Moses' farewell to Muhammad's Farewell Sermon (*Khutbat al-Wada'*), noting the structural parallel: both prophets deliver final instructions, warn against future deviation, and entrust their communities with preserved scripture.

**Hava Lazarus-Yafeh** observes that Muslim scholars consistently used Deuteronomy 31's anxiety about future apostasy as evidence that the Mosaic dispensation anticipated its own imperfection, requiring the final prophetic correction through Muhammad.
