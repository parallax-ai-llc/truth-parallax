## One Place of Worship

Deuteronomy 12 commands the destruction of all Canaanite worship sites and establishes that sacrifices and offerings must be brought only to the place God chooses. The chapter also permits the slaughter of animals for food outside the sanctuary, provided the blood is not consumed.

## Islamic Parallels

### Centralized Worship and the Ka'bah

The Deuteronomic concept of one divinely chosen place of worship finds its Islamic counterpart in the Ka'bah at Mecca. Surah Al Imran (3:96): "Indeed, the first House of worship established for mankind was that at Bakkah [Mecca]." **Ibn Kathir** noted that God's selection of a specific sacred site is a recurring feature of prophetic religion, from the Tabernacle to the Temple to the Ka'bah.

### Destruction of Pagan Shrines (12:2-3)

The command to demolish altars and sacred pillars parallels the early Islamic destruction of pagan sites around the Ka'bah and throughout Arabia. **Al-Waqidi** records that the Prophet sent expeditions to destroy idols at sites like al-'Uzza, Manat, and Suwa'.

### The Blood Prohibition (12:16, 23-25)

The prohibition against consuming blood is one of the clearest Torah-Quran convergences. Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:3) lists blood among forbidden foods. **Al-Shafi'i** regarded this as evidence of continuity between Mosaic and Muhammadan dietary law.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Deuteronomic distinction between sacred slaughter (at the sanctuary) and ordinary slaughter (for food) parallels the Islamic distinction between *udhiyah* (sacrificial offering) and regular *dhabihah* (permissible slaughter). Both traditions regulate where, how, and for what purpose animals may be killed.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the centralization of worship as an authentic prophetic principle, though **Ibn Hazm** argued that the specific identification of Jerusalem as God's chosen place was a later interpolation, since the Quran identifies Mecca as the original and eternal center of Abrahamic worship.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Mawardi** discussed the principle of sacred space in Islamic governance, drawing on the concept of divinely designated worship sites that runs from the Mosaic sanctuary through to the Meccan *haram*.

**Reuven Firestone** analyzes the competition between Jerusalem and Mecca as sacred centers, noting that both traditions claim Deuteronomy 12's principle of centralized worship as support for their respective sanctuaries.
