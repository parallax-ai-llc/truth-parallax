## Festivals, Pilgrimage, and Justice

Deuteronomy 16 prescribes the three annual pilgrimage festivals — Passover (Pesach), the Feast of Weeks (Shavuot), and the Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot) — when all males must appear before God at the central sanctuary. The chapter concludes with instructions for appointing just judges.

## Islamic Parallels

### Pilgrimage Festivals and Hajj

The requirement for all males to appear before God three times a year at the chosen place parallels the Islamic obligation of *Hajj* — pilgrimage to Mecca at least once in a lifetime. Surah Al Imran (3:97): "And pilgrimage to the House is a duty owed to Allah by all who are able." **Ibn al-Arabi** noted that sacred pilgrimage is a universal prophetic institution linking Moses' festivals to Muhammad's Hajj.

### Passover and the Sacrifice of Eid al-Adha (16:1-8)

The Passover sacrifice commemorating liberation from Egypt finds its Islamic counterpart in the *udhiyah* (Eid al-Adha sacrifice) commemorating Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his son. Both are communal acts of remembrance involving animal sacrifice, shared meals, and gratitude. **Al-Nawawi** discussed the *udhiyah* as a Sunnah rooted in Abrahamic precedent.

### Justice and Judges (16:18-20)

"Justice, and only justice, you shall pursue" is one of the Torah's most celebrated ethical statements. It parallels the Quranic command: "Be just; that is nearer to righteousness" (Surah Al-Ma'idah 5:8). **Al-Mawardi** established that the appointment of qualified judges is a communal obligation (*fard kifayah*) in Islamic governance.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The Deuteronomic prohibition against bribes (16:19) is a cornerstone of Islamic judicial ethics. The Prophet Muhammad cursed both the bribe-giver and the bribe-taker (Abu Dawud). **Ibn Farhun** in *Tabsirat al-Hukkam* elaborated on the conditions that ensure judicial impartiality.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the festival legislation as specific to the Mosaic dispensation, abrogated by Islam's own liturgical calendar. However, **Al-Shahrastani** recognized the structural continuity: both traditions mandate periodic communal gatherings centered on sacrifice, remembrance, and pilgrimage.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** compared the Israelite pilgrimage festivals with the Islamic Hajj, noting that both involve journeying to a sacred site, performing sacrificial rites, and experiencing communal spiritual renewal.

**Josef van Ess** observes that early Islamic scholars were aware of the structural parallels between Deuteronomic festivals and Islamic worship, using them to argue for the continuity of prophetic religion.
