## Guarding Against Apostasy

Deuteronomy 13 addresses three sources of enticement to worship other gods: a prophet performing signs, a close family member, and wicked men leading a city astray. In each case, the penalty is death, and the community must purge the evil from its midst.

## Islamic Parallels

### False Prophets and the Seal of Prophecy

The warning against prophets who perform signs but lead people away from God resonates with the Islamic concept of false prophets (*mutanabbiyun*). The Prophet Muhammad warned: "There will be thirty liars (*dajjalun*) among my community, each claiming to be a prophet" (Abu Dawud). **Al-Qadi 'Iyad** in *Al-Shifa* discussed criteria for distinguishing true from false prophets.

### Apostasy (Riddah) in Islamic Law (13:6-11)

The severe Deuteronomic penalty for enticing others to worship false gods parallels the Islamic legal treatment of *riddah* (apostasy). While Quranic verses on apostasy emphasize divine punishment in the hereafter (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:217), the hadith tradition and classical jurists debated temporal penalties. **Ibn Taymiyyah** distinguished between private doubt and public enticement to apostasy.

### Family Loyalty vs. Divine Loyalty (13:6-8)

The instruction to prioritize God over family bonds — even reporting a beloved relative — parallels Surah At-Tawbah (9:24): "If your fathers, your sons, your brothers... are more beloved to you than Allah and His Messenger... then wait until Allah executes His command."

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

Deuteronomy 13's communal punishment for cities led astray connects to the Islamic jurisprudential concept of *hisbah* (public enforcement of moral order). **Al-Mawardi** argued that the state bears responsibility for preventing organized apostasy, though he distinguished between individual conscience and public sedition.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the severity of Deuteronomy 13 as reflecting authentic prophetic concern for preserving monotheism, though **Abu Hamid al-Ghazali** cautioned that the legal specifics may have been altered over time while the underlying principle — protecting the community from *shirk* — remains valid.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Juwayni** discussed the tension between mercy and justice in apostasy cases, noting that both the Deuteronomic and Islamic traditions struggle to balance individual freedom with communal religious integrity.

**Patricia Crone** observes that the parallels between Deuteronomic and Islamic apostasy law suggest a shared Near Eastern legal culture concerned with the social stability that monotheistic commitment provides.
