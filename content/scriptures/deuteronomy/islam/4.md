## Call to Obedience and Warning Against Idolatry

Deuteronomy 4 presents Moses' exhortation to obey God's statutes, a stern prohibition against making graven images, and a reminder that Israel heard God's voice at Horeb but saw no form. Moses warns that disobedience will lead to exile, but repentance will bring restoration.

## Islamic Parallels

### The Invisible God (4:12, 15-18)

Moses' emphasis that Israel saw no form when God spoke at Horeb strongly parallels the Islamic doctrine of *tanzih* — God's absolute transcendence beyond physical representation. Surah Ash-Shura (42:11) declares: "There is nothing like unto Him." **Al-Ash'ari** used this principle to establish orthodox Islamic theology against anthropomorphism.

### Prohibition of Idolatry

The comprehensive ban on images of any creature — male, female, animal, bird, fish — mirrors the Islamic prohibition of idol worship as the gravest sin (*shirk*). Surah An-Nisa (4:48): "Indeed, Allah does not forgive association with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills."

### God's Jealousy and Exclusivity (4:24)

The description of God as a "consuming fire, a jealous God" resonates with the Islamic concept of God's exclusive right to worship. **Ibn al-Qayyim** discussed divine *ghayrah* (protective jealousy) in *Ighathat al-Lahfan*, affirming that God does not accept divided devotion.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The injunction "do not add to what I command you and do not subtract from it" (4:2) parallels the Islamic legal principle that divine law is complete and not subject to human modification. **Al-Shatibi** in *Al-Muwafaqat* argued that *bid'ah* (innovation in religion) violates this prophetic principle of preserving revealed law intact.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars find Deuteronomy 4's theology of divine invisibility remarkably consonant with Islamic teaching. **Fakhr al-Din al-Razi** argued that such passages demonstrate the original monotheistic purity (*hanifiyyah*) of the Mosaic revelation before later distortions. The very presence of anti-iconic theology in the Torah supports the Islamic claim that all prophets taught *tawhid*.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Shahrastani** in *Al-Milal wa al-Nihal* classified the Mosaic prohibition of images as evidence that the original Torah upheld pure monotheism. He contrasted this with later Jewish and Christian practices that, in his view, departed from this Deuteronomic standard.

**Gabriel Said Reynolds** observes that Islamic commentators frequently cite passages like Deuteronomy 4 as proof that the Torah's theological core aligns with Quranic monotheism.
