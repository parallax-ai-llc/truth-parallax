## Remembrance and Warning Against Self-Sufficiency

Deuteronomy 8 urges Israel to remember God's provision during the forty years of wilderness wandering — the manna, the preserved clothing, the discipline. Moses warns that when they enjoy the prosperity of the Promised Land, they must not say "my power and the might of my hand have gotten me this wealth."

## Islamic Parallels

### Manna and Divine Sustenance (8:3, 16)

The Quran confirms that God sent manna and quails to the Israelites: "We shaded you with clouds and sent down to you manna and quails" (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:57). **Al-Qurtubi** commented that this provision was a test of gratitude, and those who were ungrateful suffered divine punishment.

### "Man Does Not Live by Bread Alone" (8:3)

This famous declaration parallels the Islamic understanding that spiritual sustenance through revelation is more essential than material provision. Surah Al-A'raf (7:96) connects blessing to faith: "If only the people of the cities had believed and feared Allah, We would have opened upon them blessings from the heaven and the earth."

### The Danger of Prosperity (8:11-17)

Moses' warning against attributing wealth to one's own power is a central Quranic theme. The story of Qarun (Korah) in Surah Al-Qasas (28:76-82) illustrates the same lesson: "He said, 'I was only given it because of knowledge I have,'" before God caused the earth to swallow him.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The injunction to remember God's provision connects to the *fiqh* of *shukr* (gratitude). Islamic law mandates *zakat* partly as a mechanism to prevent the spiritual disease of self-sufficiency. **Al-Qarafi** argued that gratitude for divine provision is a legal obligation, not merely a moral sentiment.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view Deuteronomy 8's theology of divine provision and human humility as fully consistent with Quranic teaching. **Ibn Kathir** cited the manna narrative as one of the strongest convergences between Torah and Quran, suggesting this chapter preserves authentic prophetic instruction.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Ghazali** in the *Ihya* used the wilderness manna narrative to illustrate *tawakkul* (reliance on God), teaching that believers should trust in God's provision without neglecting lawful effort.

**Mustansir Mir** observes that the Quran's treatment of the manna episode shares Deuteronomy 8's concern with gratitude as a test of faith, making this chapter a key intersection of biblical and Quranic ethics.
