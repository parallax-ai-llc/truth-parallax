## Neighborly Duty and Sexual Ethics

Deuteronomy 22 covers a range of social and moral regulations: returning lost property, not wearing clothing of the opposite sex, protecting bird nests, building parapets on roofs, prohibiting mixed seeds and fabrics, and extensive laws regarding sexual morality including adultery, rape, and premarital relations.

## Islamic Parallels

### Returning Lost Property (22:1-4)

The duty to return a neighbor's stray animal or lost goods parallels the Islamic institution of *luqatah* (found property). The Prophet Muhammad prescribed specific rules for found items: announce it for a year, and if unclaimed, it may be used (al-Bukhari). **Al-Nawawi** elaborated the detailed *fiqh* of found property, echoing the Deuteronomic duty of care.

### Gender Distinction in Dress (22:5)

The prohibition against cross-dressing resonates with hadith prohibitions: "The Prophet cursed men who imitate women and women who imitate men" (al-Bukhari). **Ibn al-Qayyim** discussed this as rooted in maintaining the divinely established order of creation, the same rationale underlying the Deuteronomic law.

### Sexual Morality and Zina (22:13-30)

The extensive sexual legislation parallels Islamic *hadd* punishments for *zina* (illicit sexual relations). Both traditions require evidence (witnesses) before punishment and distinguish between consensual adultery and rape. Surah An-Nur (24:2-4) prescribes punishment for fornication and demands four witnesses for accusation.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The requirement for evidence in sexual accusations (22:13-21) parallels the Islamic four-witness requirement for *zina* accusations (Surah An-Nur 24:4). **Al-Shafi'i** emphasized that the high evidentiary bar serves to protect the accused, reflecting both traditions' concern with preventing false accusations.

The rooftop parapet requirement (22:8) embodies the Islamic legal principle of *la darar wa la dirar* (no harm shall be inflicted or reciprocated), codified by **Al-Suyuti** as one of the five universal maxims of Islamic law.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars generally accept the sexual morality provisions as reflecting authentic prophetic moral teaching, while noting that specific penalties differ between the Mosaic and Muhammadan dispensations. **Al-Razi** argued that the underlying moral principles are universal even when procedural details vary.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Kasani** compared Deuteronomic and Islamic sexual offenses law, finding convergence in the categories of offense but differences in evidentiary requirements and penalties that reflect Islam's emphasis on procedural protections.

**Judith Tucker** observes that both Deuteronomic and Islamic family law traditions balance communal moral standards with procedural safeguards for the accused.
