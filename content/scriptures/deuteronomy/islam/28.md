## The Covenant's Two Paths

Deuteronomy 28 is the longest and most dramatic chapter, presenting the full scope of blessings for obedience (28:1-14) and an overwhelming catalogue of curses for disobedience (28:15-68). The curses culminate in exile, disease, famine, siege, and ultimate dispersion among the nations.

## Islamic Parallels

### Blessings for Obedience (28:1-14)

The promise that obedience brings prosperity in every domain — city, field, offspring, livestock — parallels the Quranic conditional blessing: "If only the people of the cities had believed and feared Allah, We would have opened upon them blessings from the heaven and the earth" (Surah Al-A'raf 7:96). **Sayyid Qutb** emphasized that divine blessing is always conditional on moral conduct.

### The Catalogue of Curses (28:15-68)

The devastating curses — plague, drought, defeat, madness, exile — mirror Quranic descriptions of divine punishment on disobedient communities. Surah Al-A'raf (7:133) describes plagues sent upon Pharaoh's people. **Al-Tabari** noted that the pattern of escalating punishment for persistent disobedience is universal across prophetic history.

### Exile and Dispersion (28:64-68)

The prophecy of scattering among the nations has been understood by Muslim scholars as fulfilled in Jewish history. **Ibn Kathir** cited the Babylonian and Roman exiles as confirmations of the Deuteronomic curse, viewing them as evidence that the covenant's terms were operative — disobedience brought precisely the consequences Moses foretold.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The covenant blessings and curses embody the Islamic jurisprudential principle that God's law is not arbitrary but consequential. **Al-Shatibi** argued that every divine command carries inherent benefits (*masalih*) and every prohibition prevents harms (*mafasid*), making the blessing-curse structure a natural expression of moral causality.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view Deuteronomy 28 as largely authentic, since the Quran itself describes the consequences of Israelite disobedience in parallel terms. **Rahmatullah al-Hindi** argued that the chapter's prophetic accuracy — foretelling events that demonstrably occurred — supports its preservation as genuine Mosaic speech, even if other parts of the Torah were altered.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Mawdudi** analyzed the blessing-curse structure as the universal prophetic framework: every community receives divine guidance, and its response determines its fate. He cited Deuteronomy 28 alongside Quranic passages about destroyed civilizations (*'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lot*) as expressing the same divine principle.

**Hava Lazarus-Yafeh** notes that Muslim polemicists frequently invoked the fulfilled curses of Deuteronomy 28 as evidence that Jewish suffering resulted from covenant violation, a reading with significant theological implications for Jewish-Muslim dialogue.
