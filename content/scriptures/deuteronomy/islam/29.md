## A Second Covenant

Deuteronomy 29 records a covenant renewal ceremony on the plains of Moab, distinct from the Horeb covenant. Moses addresses all Israel — leaders, elders, children, strangers, even future generations — warning against secret idolatry and declaring that "the secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things revealed belong to us and to our children forever."

## Islamic Parallels

### Covenant Binding All Generations (29:10-15)

The extraordinary scope of this covenant — binding present and absent, born and unborn — parallels the Quranic primordial covenant (*mithaq*) of Surah Al-A'raf (7:172), where God addressed all of Adam's descendants: "Am I not your Lord?" and they responded "Yes, we testify." **Al-Razi** interpreted this as establishing universal divine accountability across all generations.

### Warning Against Secret Idolatry (29:18-21)

The concern with someone who privately thinks "I shall be safe, though I walk in the stubbornness of my heart" parallels the Quranic condemnation of *nifaq* (hypocrisy). Surah Al-Baqarah (2:8-10) describes those who claim belief while concealing disbelief. **Al-Ghazali** devoted extensive treatment to the diseases of the heart, including secret idolatry of the ego.

### "The Secret Things Belong to the LORD" (29:29)

This celebrated verse resonates with the Islamic distinction between *'ilm al-ghayb* (knowledge of the unseen), which belongs solely to God, and revealed knowledge accessible to humans. Surah Al-Jinn (72:26-27): "He is the Knower of the unseen, and He does not disclose His unseen to anyone except whom He has approved of His messengers."

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The inclusion of strangers (*ger*) in the covenant community (29:11) parallels the Islamic concept that anyone who accepts the *shahada* enters the community of believers regardless of ethnic or national origin. **Al-Mawdudi** argued that Islam's universalism fulfills the expansive trajectory visible in passages like Deuteronomy 29.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars find the covenant theology of Deuteronomy 29 remarkably consonant with Quranic teaching. **Ibn Taymiyyah** argued that the inclusion of future generations in the covenant demonstrates that divine accountability is not limited to those present at revelation — a principle Islam affirms through the concept of the primordial *mithaq*.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tha'labi** discussed the Moab covenant as an example of prophetic renewal, noting that prophets regularly recommit their communities to divine obedience, as Muhammad did at Hudaybiyyah.

**Andrew Rippin** observes that the structural parallels between the Deuteronomic and Quranic covenant narratives suggest a shared theological grammar of divine-human commitment that transcends individual scriptural traditions.
