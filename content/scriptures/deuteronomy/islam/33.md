## Moses Blesses the Tribes

Deuteronomy 33 contains Moses' final blessings upon the twelve tribes of Israel before his death. The chapter opens with a theophany describing God's revelation at Sinai, proceeds through individual tribal blessings, and concludes with a hymn to God as Israel's incomparable protector.

## Islamic Parallels

### The Sinai Theophany (33:1-5)

The opening description of God coming from Sinai, rising from Seir, and shining forth from Mount Paran has profound significance for Islamic exegesis. Muslim scholars interpret these three locations as representing the three major prophetic dispensations: Sinai for Moses, Seir for Jesus, and Paran for Muhammad (as Paran is associated with Mecca via Ishmael). **Ibn Taymiyyah** in *Al-Jawab al-Sahih* argued this verse prophesies the sequence of Abrahamic revelations.

### Prophetic Farewell Blessings

Moses' blessing of his community before death parallels Muhammad's Farewell Sermon and his supplications for the Muslim ummah. **Al-Tabari** noted that farewell blessings are a prophetic institution: the greatest prophets — Abraham, Jacob, Moses, Muhammad — all blessed their communities as their final prophetic act.

### God as Incomparable Protector (33:26-29)

"There is none like God, O Jeshurun" echoes the Quranic declaration of divine incomparability. Surah Al-Ikhlas (112:1-4): "Say: He is Allah, the One. Allah, the Eternal Refuge. He neither begets nor is begotten, nor is there any equivalent to Him." **Al-Ash'ari** used such convergences to argue for the unity of prophetic theology across dispensations.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The tribal blessings' focus on divine protection, agricultural prosperity, and military security reflects the *maqasid al-shari'ah* concern with preserving the five necessities: religion, life, lineage, property, and intellect. **Al-Juwayni** argued that prophetic blessings articulate the same goods that divine law is designed to protect.

## Tahrif Perspective

The Paran reference (33:2) is one of the most debated Torah passages in Muslim-Jewish-Christian polemics. Muslim scholars insist Paran refers to the Hijaz region and thus prophesies Muhammad's coming. **Rahmatullah al-Hindi** devoted extensive analysis to this claim in *Izhar al-Haqq*, arguing that the geographical identification of Paran with the Arabian Peninsula is supported by biblical and historical evidence.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Qurtubi** cited Deuteronomy 33:2 as one of the key Torah passages foretelling Muhammad, alongside Deuteronomy 18:15-18, forming the two pillars of the Islamic "biblical prophecy" argument.

**Gordon Nickel** examines the Muslim exegetical tradition on Deuteronomy 33:2, noting that the identification of Paran with Muhammad's revelation has been a consistent feature of Islamic biblical interpretation from the earliest period.
