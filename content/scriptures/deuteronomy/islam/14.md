## Clean and Unclean, Tithes and Generosity

Deuteronomy 14 prohibits pagan mourning practices, details the dietary laws — distinguishing clean from unclean animals — and establishes the tithe system, including a provision for the poor every third year.

## Islamic Parallels

### Dietary Laws — Kashrut and Halal

The Deuteronomic categories of clean and unclean animals overlap significantly with Islamic dietary law. Both prohibit swine (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:173). However, Islamic law is generally less restrictive: most marine life is permitted, and the distinction between animals with split hooves that chew cud does not apply. **Al-Qaradawi** in *The Lawful and the Prohibited in Islam* argued that Islamic dietary law represents a lightening of earlier Mosaic restrictions, as indicated in Surah Al-An'am (6:146).

### The Prohibition of Swine (14:8)

The shared prohibition of pork is one of the most visible Torah-Quran convergences. Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:3) explicitly lists swine among forbidden foods. **Ibn Kathir** noted this continuity as evidence that Islamic dietary law preserves the Abrahamic prophetic tradition while modifying certain Mosaic specifics.

### Tithing and Zakat (14:22-29)

The Deuteronomic tithe — one-tenth of agricultural produce — parallels the Islamic institution of *zakat* (2.5% of wealth annually) and *ushr* (one-tenth of agricultural produce). The third-year tithe for Levites, strangers, orphans, and widows mirrors the Quranic distribution categories for zakat (Surah At-Tawbah 9:60).

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The principle underlying both Deuteronomic dietary law and Islamic *halal/haram* categories is that God has the sole right to determine what is permissible. **Al-Shatibi** argued that dietary regulations serve the *maqsad* (objective) of protecting health and spiritual purity, a rationale applicable across prophetic dispensations.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the extensive Mosaic dietary code as partially abrogated by Islam. **Al-Razi** argued that the Quran's statement that certain foods were forbidden to the Jews "for their wrongdoing" (Surah An-Nisa 4:160) suggests some Mosaic dietary restrictions were punitive rather than universal, and Islam restored the original broader permissions.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** discussed dietary law as one area where prophetic dispensations legitimately differ, maintaining that the underlying principle — eating what is wholesome (*tayyib*) and avoiding what is harmful (*khabith*) — remains constant.

**David Freidenreich** analyzes the comparative dietary laws of Judaism and Islam, noting that Deuteronomy 14 represents the biblical foundation that Islamic law both builds upon and modifies.
