## Offering, Confession, and Covenant

Deuteronomy 26 prescribes the ceremony of bringing firstfruits to the sanctuary, accompanied by a historical confession beginning "A wandering Aramean was my father." It also addresses the third-year tithe declaration and concludes with a mutual covenant affirmation between God and Israel.

## Islamic Parallels

### Gratitude for Provision (26:1-11)

The firstfruits offering as an act of gratitude for the land's produce parallels the Islamic practice of *sadaqah* and giving thanks for divine provision. Surah Ibrahim (14:7): "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you." **Al-Qurtubi** taught that tangible offerings are the proper expression of gratitude, not merely verbal thanks.

### Historical Confession and Communal Memory (26:5-10)

The recitation of salvation history — "A wandering Aramean was my father" — parallels the Islamic emphasis on remembering God's blessings in congregational worship. The *khutbah* (sermon) regularly recounts prophetic history to reinforce communal identity. **Al-Tabari** noted that all prophetic communities maintained liturgical recitations of sacred history.

### Covenant Affirmation (26:16-19)

The mutual declaration — God claiming Israel as His people, Israel acknowledging God as their Lord — parallels the Quranic *mithaq* (covenant): "Am I not your Lord?" to which humanity responds "Yes" (Surah Al-A'raf 7:172). **Al-Razi** interpreted this primordial covenant as the universal template for all prophetic covenants, including the Mosaic.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The third-year tithe declaration (26:12-15) — a formal statement that the tithe has been properly distributed to Levites, strangers, orphans, and widows — parallels the Islamic principle of *zakat* accountability. **Al-Qaradawi** discussed the obligation of *zakat* distributors to ensure funds reach their proper recipients, echoing the Deuteronomic concern for verifiable compliance.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the covenant theology of Deuteronomy 26 as reflecting authentic prophetic religion. **Shah Waliullah** argued that the bilateral covenant structure — God's claim and human acknowledgment — is preserved across all revelations, from the Mosaic covenant to the Quranic *mithaq*.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tha'labi** discussed the Israelite liturgical confessions as examples of how prophetic communities maintain their identity through ritualized remembrance, comparing them to the Islamic practices of *dhikr* and *du'a*.

**Michael Pregill** observes that the covenant affirmation formula of Deuteronomy 26 finds structural echoes in Quranic covenant language, suggesting a shared Abrahamic liturgical heritage.
