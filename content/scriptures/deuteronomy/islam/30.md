## The Way Back and the Choice Before You

Deuteronomy 30 promises that even after exile, if Israel returns to God with all their heart, God will restore them, circumcise their hearts, and gather them from among the nations. Moses then presents the ultimate choice: "I have set before you today life and good, death and evil... therefore choose life."

## Islamic Parallels

### Repentance and Divine Restoration (30:1-10)

The promise of restoration after repentance is one of the most powerful parallels between Deuteronomy and the Quran. Surah Az-Zumar (39:53): "Say, O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins." **Al-Ghazali** identified *tawbah* (repentance) as the gateway to spiritual restoration, citing the universal prophetic promise that God accepts the return of the sincere.

### Circumcision of the Heart (30:6)

God's promise to circumcise the heart — removing the obstacle to true devotion — parallels the Quranic concept of God guiding and opening the heart. Surah Al-An'am (6:125): "Whomever Allah wills to guide, He expands his breast to Islam." **Ibn al-Qayyim** described spiritual transformation as a divine gift that follows human effort in repentance.

### "Choose Life" (30:19)

Moses' climactic exhortation to choose life parallels the Quranic emphasis on human responsibility and free will within divine sovereignty. Surah Al-Kahf (18:29): "And say, 'The truth is from your Lord, so whoever wills — let him believe; and whoever wills — let him disbelieve.'" **Al-Maturidi** used such passages to establish the Sunni position on human moral agency.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The accessibility of divine law — "this commandment is not too hard for you, nor is it far off" (30:11-14) — parallels the Islamic legal principle of *yusur* (ease). Surah Al-Baqarah (2:185): "Allah intends for you ease and does not intend for you hardship." **Al-Shatibi** established this as a foundational principle: divine law is designed to be practicable, not burdensome.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view Deuteronomy 30's theology of repentance and mercy as perfectly consonant with Quranic teaching. **Shah Waliullah** argued that this chapter represents the authentic voice of Moses, since its message — God's boundless mercy for the repentant — is the hallmark of all genuine prophecy.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tabari** discussed the prophetic pattern of warning followed by promise of mercy, noting that Deuteronomy 28-30 mirrors the Quranic structure of threat (*wa'id*) followed by hope (*wa'd*), a rhetorical pattern shared across all divine scriptures.

**Abdulaziz Sachedina** identifies Deuteronomy 30's "choose life" as a foundational text for the shared Abrahamic ethic of moral responsibility, arguing that it underpins both Jewish and Islamic conceptions of human dignity.
