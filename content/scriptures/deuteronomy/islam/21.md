## Criminal and Family Legislation

Deuteronomy 21 addresses several distinct legal matters: the ritual for unsolved murder (the heifer ceremony), the treatment of captive women taken in war, the rights of the firstborn son of an unloved wife, and the punishment of a rebellious son.

## Islamic Parallels

### The Heifer Ceremony and Surah Al-Baqarah (21:1-9)

The ritual slaughter of a heifer to expiate an unsolved murder resonates with the Quranic account of the cow in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:67-73), where God commands the Israelites to slaughter a cow to resolve a murder case. **Al-Tabari** noted the thematic connection between these rituals, both involving animal sacrifice to establish justice in cases of unknown guilt.

### Captive Women (21:10-14)

The regulation of marriage to captive women has a direct Islamic parallel. Surah An-Nisa (4:25) and hadith literature address the status of *malakat aymanukum* (those whom your right hand possesses). **Al-Sarakhsi** discussed the rights of captive women in Islamic law, including the requirement to treat them with dignity, echoing Deuteronomy's provision for a mourning period and prohibition of selling them as slaves.

### Firstborn Rights (21:15-17)

The protection of the firstborn's inheritance rights regardless of the father's preference between wives connects to Islamic inheritance law, which fixes shares by divine command (Surah An-Nisa 4:11-12). **Ibn Qudamah** in *Al-Mughni* discussed how Islamic *fara'id* (inheritance law) prevents arbitrary disinheritance.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The concept of communal responsibility for unsolved crimes (21:1-9) resonates with the Islamic institution of *qasamah* — a system of collective oaths in cases of unresolved murder. **Al-Nawawi** discussed *qasamah* as a mechanism for maintaining justice when direct evidence is unavailable, noting its ancient precedent.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars view the captive women legislation as reflecting authentic but dispensation-specific prophetic law. **Shah Waliullah** argued that Islam reformed and refined the Mosaic regulations on captive women, granting them greater protections while acknowledging the continuity of the underlying legal category.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Qurtubi** extensively compared the heifer ritual of Deuteronomy 21 with the Baqarah narrative, concluding that both represent divinely mandated procedures for achieving justice in extraordinary circumstances.

**Jonathan Brown** notes that Islamic scholars were aware of the Deuteronomic parallels to their own laws on captives and inheritance, using them to demonstrate the continuity of prophetic legislation.
