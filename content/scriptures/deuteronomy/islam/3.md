## Defeat of Og and Division of the Land

Deuteronomy 3 continues the recounting of Israel's military victories east of the Jordan, focusing on the defeat of King Og of Bashan. Moses then describes the allocation of the Transjordan territories to the tribes of Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh. The chapter closes with God's refusal to let Moses cross into the Promised Land.

## Islamic Parallels

### The Giant King Og

The tradition of Og as a giant figure resonates with Islamic accounts of ancient peoples of extraordinary stature. **Al-Tabari** records traditions about Og (*'Uj ibn 'Anaq*) as a remnant of a pre-flood race, reflecting the Quranic theme that God destroyed mighty civilizations when they rejected His guidance (Surah Al-Fajr 89:6-13).

### Divine Victory Over Tyrants (3:1-7)

God's command to Moses not to fear Og parallels the Quranic assurance to believers facing powerful enemies. Surah Al-Anfal (8:17) declares: "You did not kill them, but it was Allah who killed them." Victory belongs to God, not to human military strength.

### Moses Denied Entry (3:23-27)

God's refusal to let Moses enter the land is a profound moment of prophetic humility. In Islamic tradition, even the greatest prophets face limitations set by God. **Al-Ghazali** noted in *Ihya Ulum al-Din* that true submission (*islam*) means accepting divine decree without objection, as Moses ultimately did.

## Islamic Jurisprudence Connections

The distribution of conquered territory among the tribes introduces principles relevant to Islamic *fay'* and *ghanimah* (war spoils) law. **Abu Yusuf** in *Kitab al-Kharaj* discussed how the Prophet Muhammad allocated conquered lands, drawing on precedents that echo this Deuteronomic model of tribal distribution under prophetic authority.

## Tahrif Perspective

Muslim scholars note that while the Quran confirms the general narrative of Israel's conquests under divine command, the detailed tribal allocations in Deuteronomy 3 likely reflect later administrative records incorporated into the text. **Ibn Khaldun** observed that sacred texts sometimes blend prophetic revelation with historical documentation.

## Scholarly Connections

**Al-Tha'labi** discusses Moses' plea to enter the land as an example of prophetic *du'a* (supplication) that God declines for a higher wisdom. This narrative teaches that divine refusal is itself a form of mercy.

**Khaleel Mohammed** notes that the Islamic reception of Moses' exclusion from the land avoids the punitive framing found in some Jewish and Christian interpretations, instead emphasizing God's sovereign prerogative.
