## Overview

Deuteronomy (*Devarim*, "Words") is the fifth and final book of the Torah/Pentateuch. Cast as Moses' farewell addresses to Israel on the plains of Moab, it recapitulates and expands the law given at Sinai, calls for covenant faithfulness, and prepares the people for life in the Promised Land without Moses. The book culminates in Moses' death on Mount Nebo, within sight of the land he would never enter.

## Structure

Deuteronomy is structured as a series of speeches and a concluding narrative:

1. **First Address** (Chapters 1–4): Historical retrospective — lessons from the wilderness
2. **Second Address** (Chapters 5–26): The law restated and expanded — the Decalogue, the Shema, the Deuteronomic Code
3. **Third Address** (Chapters 27–28): Blessings and curses at Shechem
4. **Final Words** (Chapters 29–34): Covenant renewal, the Song of Moses, the Blessing of Moses, and the death of Moses

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Deuteronomy is among the most theologically influential books across traditions:

- **Judaism**: The source of the Shema (6:4), the most fundamental Jewish confession of faith; the basis of vast halakhic development
- **Christianity**: The most quoted Old Testament book in the New Testament; Jesus answers Satan's temptations with three Deuteronomy quotations
- **Islam**: Moses' farewell and the theme of prophetic succession resonate with Islamic prophetology
- **Gnosticism**: The covenant curses and the jealous God of Deuteronomy confirm the Gnostic portrait of the Demiurge

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Covenant Renewal | Moses calls a new generation to recommit to the Sinai covenant |
| The Shema | "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one" (6:4) |
| Love and Obedience | The command to love God with all heart, soul, and strength |
| Centralization of Worship | The "place where the LORD will choose" as the single legitimate sanctuary |
| Blessings and Curses | The two paths: faithfulness leads to blessing, disobedience to curse |
| Moses' Death | The great leader's final act — seeing the land but not entering it |
