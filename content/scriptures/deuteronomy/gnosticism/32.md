## The Song of Moses

Deuteronomy 32 presents the Song of Moses, a poetic composition that recounts the Demiurge's relationship with Israel from election through betrayal to vengeance. For Gnostic interpreters, this song is the single most revealing text in the Torah — a sustained lyrical confession in which the Demiurge exposes his jealousy, his rage, and his fundamental insecurity.

## The Rock and His Work

The song opens by declaring that God is "the Rock, his work is perfect, and all his ways are just" (32:4). Valentinian interpreters noted the Demiurge's characteristic self-praise. He calls his own work perfect — a claim the *On the Origin of the World* (NHC II,5) directly contradicts. The Demiurge's cosmos is flawed precisely because its maker is ignorant. He calls it perfect because he knows nothing beyond it.

The designation "Rock" is itself significant. The Demiurge identifies with mineral solidity — unchanging, unyielding, dense. This is the opposite of the Pleroma's dynamic fullness. The true Father is not a rock but a living silence, an abyss of infinite depth. The Demiurge's self-image as stone reveals his materialist nature.

## Jealousy Unveiled

The central theological disclosure comes in verses 32:16-21: Israel provoked God to jealousy with foreign gods, so he will provoke them to jealousy with a "no-people." The Demiurge explicitly names his own passion — jealousy. For Gnostic readers, this is the passage the *Apocryphon of John* (NHC II,1) references when it describes Yaldabaoth's self-incriminating declaration. Having said "I am a jealous God," the Demiurge inadvertently reveals that other gods exist. As the *Apocryphon* asks: of whom is he jealous if there is no other god?

Hans Jonas identified divine jealousy as the defining trait that separates the Demiurge from the transcendent Father. The true God cannot be jealous because the Pleroma lacks nothing. Jealousy presupposes deficiency — and deficiency is the Demiurge's essential condition.

## The Demiurge as Warrior

In verses 32:39-42, the Demiurge declares: "I kill and I make alive; I wound and I heal... I will make my arrows drunk with blood, and my sword shall devour flesh." For Sethian interpreters, these verses strip away any pretense of benevolence. The Demiurge boasts of killing, wounding, and bloodshed. He is not a reluctant punisher but an enthusiastic warrior who celebrates slaughter.

The *Testimony of Truth* (NHC IX,3) uses precisely this kind of passage to argue that the God of the Torah is malevolent. What kind of deity makes his arrows "drunk with blood"? Only a being intoxicated with its own violent power — which is precisely what the archons are.

## The Allotment of Nations

Verse 32:8-9 contains a cosmological claim of immense Gnostic significance: "When the Most High gave to the nations their inheritance, when he divided mankind, he fixed the borders of the peoples according to the number of the sons of God. But the LORD's portion is his people." The text distinguishes between the "Most High" (Elyon) and the LORD (YHWH). For Gnostic interpreters, this is canonical evidence of the Demiurge's subordinate status. The Most High distributes the nations; the LORD receives only one portion. YHWH is not the supreme deity but one archon among many, assigned a single people by a higher power.

Birger Pearson noted that this verse became a cornerstone text for Gnostic biblical interpretation, demonstrating from the Torah itself that the God of Israel is not the highest God.

## Contrast with Gnosis

The Song of Moses is poetry in service of domination — a lyrical celebration of jealousy, vengeance, and blood. Gnostic hymns, such as those preserved in the *Odes of Solomon* and the *Gospel of Truth*, offer a radically different music: songs of recognition, return, and rest in the Pleroma. Where the Song of Moses ends with arrows drunk on blood, the Gnostic hymn ends with the soul's quiet reunion with the Father.

## Theological Significance

Chapter 32 is the Demiurge's self-portrait in verse. He declares himself jealous, vengeful, and violent — and he is proud of it. For the Gnostic reader, the Song of Moses is the prosecution's closing argument: here, in the Demiurge's own words, is the evidence that the God of Deuteronomy is not the unknowable Father but a lesser, passionate, and ultimately ignorant power.
