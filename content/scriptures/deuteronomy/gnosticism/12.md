## Centralizing Worship to the Demiurge

Deuteronomy 12 commands the destruction of all Canaanite cultic sites and the centralization of Israelite worship at a single location chosen by the Demiurge. For Gnostic interpreters, this chapter reveals the Demiurge's strategy of cultic monopoly — by concentrating worship in one place, he eliminates competing spiritual pathways and tightens his control over religious experience.

## "Destroy All Their Places of Worship"

The command to demolish Canaanite altars, pillars, sacred poles, and carved images (12:2-3) is the Demiurge's war on spiritual pluralism. Valentinian interpreters recognized that the "high places" (*bamot*) were sites where alternative spiritual experiences might occur — encounters with powers or realities beyond the Demiurge's jurisdiction. Their destruction ensures that all worship flows through the Demiurge's approved channel.

Hans Jonas described the Demiurge's centralization as analogous to the archontic consolidation of power in the cosmic spheres. Just as each archon governs his sphere exclusively, the Demiurge demands exclusive control over the terrestrial cult.

## "The Place the LORD Your God Will Choose"

The unnamed central sanctuary (12:5, 11, 14, 18, 21, 26) — the place "to put his name" — is the Demiurge's projection of himself into the material landscape. By placing his name in one location, he creates a geographic focal point for archontic power. Sethian interpreters saw this as the Demiurge establishing his throne on earth, mirroring the archontic thrones described in *On the Origin of the World* (NHC II,5).

Birger Pearson observed that the Gnostic rejection of temple worship followed logically from the identification of the temple's patron as the Demiurge. If the God who dwells in the temple is Yaldabaoth, then temple worship is archon-worship.

## Blood and the Demiurge's Appetite

The detailed regulations about blood (12:15-16, 23-25) — "the blood is the life" — reveal the Demiurge's fascination with the vital force of living creatures. The prohibition against eating blood, combined with the requirement of sacrifice, means that the blood-life belongs exclusively to the Demiurge. Valentinian interpreters read this as the Demiurge feeding on vital energy, a concept elaborated in Gnostic texts where archons consume the psychic energy of the souls trapped in their spheres.

## Contrast with Gnosis

The Gnostic does not worship at a single chosen place because gnosis is not localized. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 77) declares: "Split a piece of wood, and I am there. Lift up the stone, and you will find me." The divine spark is everywhere the material veil is pierced — not concentrated in a temple that the Demiurge controls. True worship, in the Valentinian system, is the interior ascent of the soul, requiring no altar, no sacrifice, and no geographic pilgrimage.

## Theological Significance

Chapter 12 is the Demiurge's blueprint for cultic monopoly. By destroying all alternatives and centralizing worship, he ensures that every religious act reinforces his authority. For the Gnostic reader, this chapter is a warning: the temple is the Demiurge's stronghold, and true seekers must find the divine spark outside his approved channels.
