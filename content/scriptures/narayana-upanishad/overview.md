## Overview

The Narayana Upanishad belongs to the Atharva Veda (or in some recensions, the Krishna Yajur Veda) and is one of the most important Vaishnava Upanishads. It declares Narayana — a name of Vishnu meaning "the refuge of all beings" — as the supreme Brahman, the ultimate reality from which all creation proceeds and to which all creation returns. The text is closely related to the Mahanarayana Upanishad and provides the scriptural foundation for the Vaishnava identification of the Upanishadic Brahman with the personal God Vishnu-Narayana.

## Structure

The Narayana Upanishad consists of two sections:

1. **Section 1 — Narayana as Supreme Brahman**: The identification of Narayana with the ultimate reality and the source of all creation
2. **Section 2 — Meditation on Narayana**: The mantras and practices for meditating on Narayana as the supreme self

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The foundational Upanishadic text for the supremacy of Vishnu-Narayana
- **Shaivism**: Narayana as one name for the supreme reality that Shaivas call Shiva
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Narayana as the saguna (qualified) form of nirguna Brahman
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Primary scriptural authority for Narayana/Vishnu as the Supreme Being
- **Academic**: Important evidence for the development of Vaishnava theology within the Vedic tradition
- **Comparative**: The personal God as ultimate reality parallels monotheistic theologies worldwide

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Narayana as Brahman | The identification of the personal God with the absolute reality |
| Creation from Narayana | All worlds, beings, and gods originate from Narayana |
| Devotional Meditation | Mantras and practices for realizing Narayana as the self |
| Divine Immanence | Narayana dwelling within all beings as the inner self |
| Supreme Sovereignty | Narayana as the lord of all gods, beings, and worlds |
