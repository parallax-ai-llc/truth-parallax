## Overview

The Interpretation of Knowledge (NHC XI,1) is a Valentinian homily addressing a practical pastoral problem: divisions and jealousy within a Christian community over the unequal distribution of spiritual gifts. The author argues that all members of the church form one body — the body of Christ — and that jealousy over charismata (prophecy, teaching, healing, etc.) reflects ignorance of the deeper spiritual unity established by the Savior's sacrifice. The text draws explicitly on Paul's body-of-Christ metaphor from 1 Corinthians 12, interpreting it through a Valentinian theological framework. This is one of the rare Nag Hammadi texts that directly addresses congregational life and pastoral concerns, providing evidence that Valentinian Christians participated in worshipping communities with real social dynamics.

## Structure

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The Savior's Sacrifice for Unity | The Savior descended from the Pleroma and gave himself as a sacrifice to establish the unity of the church |
| 2 | Spiritual Gifts and the Body | Drawing on 1 Corinthians 12, the author argues that diverse gifts serve the unity of the one body |
| 3 | Overcoming Jealousy Through Knowledge | Jealousy over gifts reflects ignorance; true knowledge (*gnosis*) reveals the underlying unity of all members |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Provides rare evidence of Valentinian pastoral theology and congregational life
- **Academic**: Studied for its Valentinian exegesis of Paul and its evidence of Gnostic community social dynamics
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to discussions of spiritual gifts and community unity in other religious traditions
- **Christian**: Engages directly with Pauline ecclesiology (1 Corinthians 12) and the perennial problem of division over spiritual gifts
- **Jewish**: Reflects Jewish concepts of community solidarity and the diverse gifts of members within the covenant community
- **Interfaith**: Addresses the universal challenge of maintaining community unity amid diversity of roles and gifts

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Body of Christ | The church is one body with many members, each contributing a necessary gift |
| Jealousy as Ignorance | Envy of others' spiritual gifts stems from not understanding the deeper unity of the community |
| The Savior's Sacrifice | Christ's descent and self-giving establish the unity that the community is called to embody |
| Valentinian Ecclesiology | The Valentinian church is not merely a school of thought but a worshipping community with real social needs |
