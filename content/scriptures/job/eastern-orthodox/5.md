Eliphaz's counsel to accept divine discipline (5:17) contains an insight that Orthodox spiritual theology affirms within proper limits. The tradition of *pedagogical suffering* — suffering as divine instruction — has deep roots in Orthodox ascetical theology. St. Isaac the Syrian taught that God sometimes permits suffering to redirect the soul toward its true end, which is union with God. However, the Orthodox tradition insists that this principle cannot be mechanically applied: not all suffering is pedagogical, and it is never appropriate for one person to identify another's suffering as divine punishment without explicit prophetic authority.

The hymnic praise of God's power (5:9-16) resonates with the Orthodox liturgical tradition of doxology. The Divine Liturgy, the Akathist hymns, and the Psalter all celebrate God's sovereign power over creation in terms similar to Eliphaz's description. Orthodox worship maintains a robust sense of divine transcendence — God who "does great things and unsearchable" — while simultaneously affirming divine accessibility through the incarnation. Eliphaz correctly praises God's power but fails to account for the mystery dimension that Orthodox theology calls *apophatic* theology: the recognition that God's ways ultimately exceed human comprehension.

The promise of material restoration (5:19-26) is evaluated in Orthodox thought through the eschatological horizon. While earthly blessings are genuinely good — the Orthodox tradition blesses homes, harvests, marriages, and all dimensions of material life — they are not the ultimate good. The kingdom of God, which Orthodox theology understands as both present and future, relativizes all earthly prosperity. Eliphaz's error is not in valuing material well-being but in treating it as the definitive sign of divine favor, a confusion that Orthodox theology resolves by orienting all goods toward the eschatological telos of theosis.
