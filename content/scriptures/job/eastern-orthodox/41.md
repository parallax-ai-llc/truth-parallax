## Leviathan

The description of Leviathan in Job 41 — armored, fire-breathing, utterly fearless (41:13-21, 33) — has received extensive attention in Orthodox patristic commentary. The Fathers generally read Leviathan as a symbol of the cosmic forces of chaos, death, and evil that only God can subdue. The Paschal homily attributed to St. John Chrysostom declares that Christ "despoiled Hades" and "trampled down death by death" — language that echoes God's mastery over Leviathan.

Orthodox iconography of the *Anastasis* (Resurrection) depicts Christ descending into the realm of death, shattering its gates and lifting Adam and Eve from their tombs. Leviathan, the primordial monster who is "king over all that are proud" (41:34), represents the power of death that held humanity captive until Christ's victory. The Orthodox Paschal celebration — the most joyous moment of the liturgical year — is the answer to Leviathan: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life."

The theological function of the Leviathan passage is to demonstrate that God's sovereignty extends over powers that utterly exceed human capacity. Orthodox theology insists that this is not abstract doctrine but lived reality: the sacraments, the prayers for the departed, the exorcism in the baptismal rite — all presuppose that God has conquered the forces of chaos and death, and that this victory is communicated to the faithful through the life of the Church.
