Job's lament over human mortality — "Man who is born of a woman is few of days and full of trouble" (14:1) — is heard in Orthodox funeral services, which frame death within the broader context of resurrection hope. The Orthodox *panikhida* (memorial service) moves between grief and hope, acknowledging the reality of death while affirming Christ's victory over it. The troparion for the departed — "Give rest, O Christ, to the soul of your servant with the saints, where there is neither sickness, nor sorrow, nor sighing, but life everlasting" — answers Job's lament with the Paschal faith that transforms death from terminus to transition.

The tree metaphor (14:7-9) — hope for a felled tree but none for a dead man — represents the limits of pre-incarnational understanding. Orthodox theology reads this through the lens of Christ's own image: "Unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies, it remains alone; but if it dies, it bears much fruit" (John 12:24). The botanical principle of death-leading-to-life, which Job observes in trees but cannot apply to human beings, is fulfilled christologically. The Orthodox Cross is sometimes depicted with branches sprouting from its base, visually connecting the tree that Job envied with the tree of the Cross that accomplishes what nature alone cannot.

Job's question "If a man dies, shall he live again?" (14:14) receives its definitive answer in the Orthodox Paschal proclamation: "Christ is risen from the dead!" The entire Paschal liturgy — the most important celebration in the Orthodox calendar — is structured as the answer to Job's question. The midnight procession from the dark church into the light, the exchange of the Paschal greeting, the reading of Chrysostom's Paschal homily that proclaims "death, where is your sting?" — all respond to Job's anguished uncertainty with the certainty of resurrection experience.
