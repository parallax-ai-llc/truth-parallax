## Elihu Defends God's Justice

Elihu's assertion that "it is unthinkable that God would do wrong" (34:10) articulates a conviction that Orthodox theology holds absolutely. The divine nature is identical with goodness, justice, and love — God cannot act contrary to His own nature. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that God's will and God's nature are one: there is no arbitrary divine will lurking behind a benevolent divine face.

The argument that God sustains all life by His spirit (34:14-15) aligns with the Orthodox doctrine of divine energies. Gregory Palamas taught that the uncreated energies of God permeate all creation, sustaining it in existence. If God withdrew His energies, all things would cease to be. This is not merely a philosophical point but a liturgical one: every Orthodox prayer begins with an invocation of the Holy Spirit as the one who is "everywhere present and filling all things."

Elihu's accusation that Job "adds rebellion to his sin" (34:37) is challenged by the Orthodox understanding of *lament* as a legitimate form of prayer. The Psalms of lament, which constitute a significant portion of the Orthodox daily office, demonstrate that crying out against apparent injustice is not rebellion but faith in its most honest expression. The Orthodox tradition, shaped by centuries of persecution, knows that the most authentic prayer often comes from the deepest pain.
