Job's plea for a divine pledge (17:3) connects to the Orthodox understanding of Christ as the guarantor of a new covenant. The Eucharistic liturgy's words of institution — "This is my blood of the new covenant" — provide the pledge Job seeks. Orthodox sacramental theology understands the Eucharist as *arrabon* (pledge, earnest) of the kingdom, a foretaste of the eschatological banquet that guarantees its ultimate realization. Job's longing for guarantee is a longing for sacramental assurance that, within the narrative, remains unfulfilled but is proleptically answered in the Church's liturgical life.

Job's social degradation — becoming "a byword" and "one before whom men spit" (17:6) — is read typologically in Orthodox tradition as a prefiguration of Christ's humiliation. The Orthodox service of the Twelve Gospels, read during Holy Thursday evening, includes the accounts of Christ being spat upon, mocked, and rejected — the same experiences Job describes. Orthodox iconography depicts Christ's mocking (*empaigmos*) with the same combination of dignity and degradation that characterizes Job's self-description, reinforcing the typological connection between the righteous sufferer of the Old Testament and the righteous sufferer of the Cross.

The closing question — "Where then is my hope?" (17:15) — represents the experience of spiritual desolation that Orthodox ascetical writers call *akedia* (acedia, spiritual listlessness). The desert fathers identified *akedia* as the "noonday demon" that attacks the spiritual person with a comprehensive sense of meaninglessness. The remedy, according to St. John Cassian and others, is not to flee the desert but to remain, enduring the darkness in the trust that it will eventually yield to light. Job's persistence in dialogue with God, even in hopelessness, embodies this ascetical principle.
