## Elihu on Dreams and Discipline

Elihu's assertion that God speaks through dreams and visions (33:14-16) resonates with the Orthodox tradition of divine communication beyond rational discourse. The lives of the saints are filled with accounts of revelatory dreams, and the Orthodox liturgical calendar commemorates several visions — from Jacob's ladder to Peter's vision in Acts 10. Elihu correctly identifies a mode of divine speech that the three friends ignored.

The figure of the heavenly mediator — "an angel, a mediator, one out of a thousand" who finds "a ransom" for the sufferer (33:23-24) — is read in Orthodox Christology as a prefiguration of the Incarnation. Christ is the ultimate Mediator who bridges the gulf between God and humanity not by external transaction but by uniting divine and human natures in His own person. The Orthodox understanding of salvation as *theosis* transforms the mediator concept: Christ does not merely intercede but shares His divine life with those He ransoms.

Elihu's theology of suffering as divine pedagogy (33:19-22) contains elements that Orthodox ascetic theology affirms. The *Philokalia* teaches that trials (*peirasmoi*) can serve the soul's purification when received with patience and prayer. Yet Orthodox theology insists that this framework must not be mechanically applied — not all suffering is pedagogical, and the mystery of innocent suffering ultimately points beyond all human explanatory systems to the cross of Christ.
