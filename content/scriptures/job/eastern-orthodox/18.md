Bildad's portrait of the wicked person's destruction (18:5-21) is read in Orthodox tradition against the backdrop of the Church's understanding of death and the afterlife. The "king of terrors" (18:14) — death personified — is precisely the enemy that Christ defeats in His descent into Hades. The Paschal homily attributed to St. John Chrysostom proclaims: "Hades was embittered when it encountered You in the lower regions... It was embittered, for it was abolished. It was embittered, for it was mocked." The terrifying figure Bildad invokes has been dethroned by Christ's resurrection.

Bildad's assertion that the wicked person's "memory perishes from the earth" and he "has no name in the street" (18:17) contradicts the Orthodox practice of commemorating the dead. The Book of the Living (*Diptychs*) maintained in Orthodox parishes records the names of both living and dead members of the community for liturgical commemoration. The Orthodox conviction that the bonds of love transcend death — expressed in the prayer "Memory Eternal" (*Aionia i mnimi*) sung at funerals and memorial services — directly opposes Bildad's theology of complete erasure. In Orthodox understanding, no one who belongs to the community of faith is truly forgotten.

The claim that the wicked person "has no posterity or progeny" (18:19) reflects Bildad's biological understanding of immortality — survival through descendants. Orthodox theology offers a radically different vision of immortality through participation in divine life (*theosis*). The saints, many of whom had no biological descendants, enjoy an imperishable presence in the Church through their icons, their feast days, their writings, and the prayers of the faithful. Immortality in Orthodox theology is relational and participatory, not biological, which renders Bildad's criterion irrelevant.
