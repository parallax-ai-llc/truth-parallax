## The Thunderstorm and God's Approach

Elihu's final chapter is dominated by the approaching storm, and Orthodox theology reads this as a genuine theophanic preparation. The thunder that makes Elihu's heart "leap from its place" (37:1) recalls the Orthodox understanding of divine encounter as simultaneously terrifying and beautiful — the *tremendum et fascinans* that characterizes all authentic experience of the Holy.

The rhetorical questions — "Do you know how the clouds hang poised?" (37:16), "Can you join him in spreading out the skies, hard as a mirror of cast bronze?" (37:18) — function as a catechesis in divine mystery. Orthodox theology insists that the proper response to creation is not explanation but *wonder* (*thaumazein*). The monk who gazes at the night sky and the scientist who studies stellar composition should both arrive at the same place: astonishment before the Creator's wisdom.

Elihu's assertion that "the Almighty is beyond our reach" (37:23) articulates the *apophatic* principle that lies at the heart of Orthodox theology. Yet the irony is immediate: the God who is "beyond our reach" is about to speak. Orthodox theology holds these truths together in the mystery of revelation: God is utterly transcendent and yet freely self-communicating. The whirlwind from which God will speak is the meeting point of transcendence and immanence — the place where the unreachable God reaches out.
