Job's observation that the wicked "live, reach old age, and grow mighty in power" (21:7) confronts the same empirical challenge that Orthodox theology addresses through its eschatological horizon. The Orthodox understanding of history as moving toward the Parousia (Second Coming of Christ) and the final judgment provides a framework within which the apparent injustice of present arrangements can be held in tension with the certainty of ultimate justice. The Creed's affirmation that Christ "will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead" answers Job's challenge not by denying the observation but by extending the timeline beyond earthly existence.

Job's argument that the wicked prosper without consequence (21:7-16) and that suffering is distributed without regard to moral character (21:23-26) represents a form of theological realism that Orthodox tradition values. St. Isaac the Syrian taught that God's providence is genuinely mysterious — not merely complex but truly beyond human comprehension. The difference between Orthodox theology and Job's friends is not that Orthodox theology can explain why the wicked prosper but that it refuses to explain away the mystery with false certainty. Contemplation of divine mystery (*theoria*) requires the discipline of unknowing, which the friends consistently refuse.

Job's final dismissal of his friends' theology — "How then will you comfort me with empty nothings?" (21:34) — clears the theological ground for the divine speech that comes in chapters 38-41. Orthodox interpretation reads the dialogue cycles as a progressive demonstration that human theology, however well-intentioned, cannot adequately account for the mystery of suffering. Only the divine voice, speaking from the whirlwind, can provide the response that satisfies — not by answering Job's questions but by revealing a divine presence so overwhelming that the questions themselves are transformed. This is the apophatic resolution that Orthodox theology finds uniquely satisfying.
