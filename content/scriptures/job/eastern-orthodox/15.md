Eliphaz's accusation that Job "does away with the fear of God" (15:4) misunderstands the nature of authentic spiritual struggle. Orthodox tradition, particularly in the hesychast writings, teaches that spiritual combat (*pneumatike pale*) — including intense questioning of God — is an essential dimension of spiritual maturity. The Orthodox saints who engaged most deeply with God often experienced periods of profound darkness and questioning. St. Silouan of Mount Athos, in the twentieth century, described his own experience of divine absence in terms remarkably similar to Job's, yet the Church recognizes his spiritual journey as exemplary rather than impious.

Eliphaz's assertion that "the heavens are not pure in his sight" (15:15) reflects an extreme pessimism about creation that Orthodox theology tempers through its doctrine of divine glory pervading the cosmos. The Palamite theology of the divine energies teaches that God's uncreated light permeates all creation, making it capable of transfiguration. The Transfiguration of Christ on Mount Tabor — a feast of central importance in Orthodox theology — demonstrates that created matter can be illuminated by divine glory. Eliphaz's vision of universal impurity is corrected by the Taboric vision of creation's capacity for glorification.

The extended description of the wicked person's fate (15:20-35) serves as Eliphaz's theology of consequences. Orthodox soteriology, while affirming the reality of divine judgment, frames it primarily in therapeutic rather than juridical terms. Sin is sickness; judgment is diagnosis; punishment is the painful consequence of separation from the source of life. The Orthodox understanding of hell itself follows this pattern: hell is not God's active punishment but the experience of divine love by those who have rejected it. Eliphaz's legalistic framework misses the therapeutic dimension that Orthodox theology considers primary.
