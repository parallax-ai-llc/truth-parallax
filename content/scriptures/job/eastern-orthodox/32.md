## Elihu's Angry Entrance

Orthodox commentary on Elihu has been relatively sparse compared to the Western tradition, but the Fathers who address him tend toward a cautious assessment. Elihu's claim that "the spirit in a person, the breath of the Almighty, gives them understanding" (32:8) aligns with the Orthodox theology of the Holy Spirit as the source of all genuine wisdom. Yet the Fathers also recognized that not every claim to spiritual inspiration is authentic — discernment (*diakrisis*) is essential.

The Orthodox monastic tradition provides a lens for reading Elihu. His anger (32:2-5), his lengthy self-justification (32:6-22), and his claim to burst with words "like new wineskins" (32:19) suggest what the Desert Fathers called *kenodoxia* (vainglory) — the subtle passion that mimics authentic spiritual authority. Evagrius Ponticus taught that vainglory is the most dangerous passion because it can disguise itself as virtue, even as prophetic zeal.

Yet Orthodox reading does not dismiss Elihu entirely. His role in the narrative — bridging human argument and divine speech — suggests a providential function. In Orthodox theology, even imperfect instruments serve God's purposes. The storm that Elihu begins to describe at the end of his speeches (chapters 36-37) becomes the very whirlwind from which God speaks, suggesting that God can use flawed vessels to prepare the way for His own revelation.
