Job's description of sleepless nights "filled with tossing to the dawn" (7:4) connects to the Orthodox tradition of night vigil (*agrypnia*). While Orthodox monks embrace the night watch as a voluntary spiritual discipline, Job experiences involuntary wakefulness as torture. The contrast illuminates a principle central to Orthodox ascetical theology: suffering willingly embraced for spiritual purposes differs fundamentally from suffering imposed without consent. Yet Orthodox tradition also recognizes that involuntary suffering can become transformative when united to Christ's passion — a possibility Job cannot yet perceive within his pre-incarnational context.

The parody of Psalm 8 (7:17-18) — transforming wonder at divine attention into terror of divine surveillance — represents what Orthodox theology calls the distortion of the natural relationship between Creator and creature caused by the Fall. In the unfallen state, God's attentiveness would be experienced as pure love; in the fallen state, the same attentiveness can be experienced as judgment. Orthodox theology, following St. Maximus the Confessor, teaches that divine love is constant — it is human perception that varies. The same divine fire that warms the righteous scorches the unrepentant. Job experiences God's attention as hostile not because God has changed but because suffering has distorted his capacity to perceive divine love.

Job's closing plea for pardon (7:21) anticipates the Orthodox understanding of forgiveness as healing rather than legal acquittal. In Orthodox theology, sin is primarily understood as sickness rather than legal transgression, and salvation is therapy (*therapeia*) rather than juridical pardon. Job's plea "why do you not pardon my transgression?" is thus, in Orthodox reading, a cry for healing — for the restoration of the divine-human relationship that suffering has strained but not destroyed.
