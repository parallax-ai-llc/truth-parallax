## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Job is grounded in the patristic consensus and the liturgical life of the Church. The Greek Fathers read Job as a drama of cosmic significance — a righteous man's struggle against the powers of evil, sustained by divine grace and vindicated by the God who is beyond all human comprehension. Job's story is understood within the broader Orthodox framework of *theosis* (deification): suffering can become a path toward union with God.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Exegesis
The Orthodox Church draws on the Greek and Syriac Fathers:
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Homilies emphasizing Job's patience and moral virtue
- **Olympiodorus of Alexandria**: Detailed commentary on the Septuagint text of Job
- **Didymus the Blind**: Allegorical readings connecting Job to Christ
- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Poetic-typological interpretation

### Liturgical Context
Job is read during Holy Week in the Orthodox liturgy, linking his innocent suffering to the Passion of Christ. The connection between Job's vindication and the Resurrection is central to the Orthodox reading.

### The Septuagint Text
Orthodox interpretation works from the Septuagint (LXX), which differs significantly from the Masoretic Hebrew text. The LXX of Job is considerably shorter and contains a distinctive appendix (42:17b-e) that identifies Job with Jobab of Genesis 36:33.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine mystery**: God's ways are beyond human comprehension — apophatic theology
- **Patience as spiritual warfare**: Job's endurance is a struggle against demonic assault
- **Suffering and theosis**: Affliction can become a means of purification and union with God
- **Job as prophet**: Job prophesies the resurrection — "I know that my Redeemer lives" (19:25)
- **The Satan as tempter**: The adversary operates only within divinely permitted limits
