## The Hymn to Wisdom

The Wisdom Poem of Job 28 occupies a central place in Orthodox theology, which identifies the Wisdom (*Sophia*) of God with the Second Person of the Trinity. When the poem declares that wisdom was with God "when he established the force of the wind and measured out the waters" (28:25), Orthodox tradition reads this as a revelation of the Logos through whom all things were made (John 1:3). The great cathedral of *Hagia Sophia* in Constantinople is dedicated to this divine Wisdom.

The mining metaphor (28:1-11) — humanity's ability to penetrate rock and discover hidden treasures — contrasts sharply with the inaccessibility of wisdom. Orthodox epistemology, rooted in the patristic tradition, distinguishes between *episteme* (scientific knowledge of the created world) and *theologia* (knowledge of God). The first is achievable through human effort; the second is given only through revelation and participation in divine life. "Where then does wisdom come from?" (28:20) — from God's own self-disclosure.

The concluding verse — "The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom" (28:28) — is read in Orthodox tradition as pointing to the liturgical and ascetic life. The "fear of the Lord" is not servile terror but the reverential awe (*eulabeia*) that characterizes authentic worship. The Orthodox liturgy, with its incense, icons, and chanted prayers, cultivates precisely this disposition — an embodied recognition that wisdom is not an intellectual achievement but a mode of being before God.
