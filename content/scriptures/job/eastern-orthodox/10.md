Job's meditation on divine craftsmanship (10:8-12) — "Your hands fashioned and made me... you clothed me with skin and flesh, and knit me together with bones and sinews" — connects to the Orthodox understanding of the human person as the crown of creation. St. Gregory of Nyssa, in *On the Making of Man*, developed the patristic doctrine that humanity was created as a microcosm — a being that unites the material and spiritual dimensions of creation in a single person. Job's reflection on his own formation acknowledges this dignity while agonizing over its apparent meaninglessness in the face of suffering.

The paradox of a Creator who seems to destroy His own work (10:8, 13-17) raises the problem of evil in terms that Orthodox theology addresses through the doctrine of divine *economia* (God's providential management of creation). St. Irenaeus taught that God's plan for humanity unfolds through stages, including stages of suffering, that are part of a larger narrative of growth toward deification. The apparent contradiction between God's creative care and God's destructive power is resolved eschatologically: the full picture will be visible only when the divine economy reaches its completion in the final restoration (*apokatastasis*) of all things.

Job's description of Sheol (10:21-22) — "the land of gloom like thick darkness" — is transformed in Orthodox theology by the doctrine of Christ's descent into Hades. The Paschal troparion proclaims: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life." The darkness Job describes is precisely the darkness that Christ enters and illumines. Orthodox iconography consistently depicts this event with Christ standing on the shattered gates of Hades, surrounded by the light of His divinity, raising the dead from their tombs — including, by implication, righteous sufferers like Job.
