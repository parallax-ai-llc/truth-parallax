The Orthodox reading of Job 1 begins with the liturgical context: the Book of Job is read during Holy Week in the Byzantine tradition, connecting Job's suffering directly to the Passion of Christ. This liturgical placement shapes Orthodox interpretation fundamentally — Job is read not primarily as a philosophical treatise on suffering but as a participation in the mystery of the Cross. St. John Chrysostom, whose homilies on Job profoundly influenced Orthodox interpretation, read Job's trials as a form of spiritual athletics (*askesis*), a training ground for the soul's journey toward *theosis* (deification).

The heavenly council scene (1:6-12) is understood in Orthodox theology within the framework of spiritual warfare. The Orthodox tradition, following the desert fathers, takes the reality of demonic opposition seriously while insisting on its subordination to divine sovereignty. Satan's need to obtain permission before testing Job reflects the Orthodox teaching that evil has no independent existence — it is parasitic upon the good and operates only within divinely established limits. St. Gregory the Theologian taught that God permits trials not to discover what is in the human heart (since God already knows) but to reveal it, both to the individual and to the angelic hosts.

Job's response — "The LORD gave, and the LORD has taken away; blessed be the name of the LORD" (1:21) — exemplifies the Orthodox virtue of *eucharistia* (thanksgiving) in all circumstances. The Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom contains the exclamation "for all things we give thanks," which reflects this same disposition. Orthodox theology understands Job's thanksgiving not as passive resignation but as active participation in the divine will — a foretaste of the deified life in which the human will is perfectly aligned with God's purposes.
