## Bildad's Brief Final Word

Bildad's six-verse speech emphasizes God's transcendence — "Dominion and awe belong to God" (25:2) — and human insignificance — "how much less a mortal, who is but a maggot" (25:6). Orthodox theology affirms God's absolute transcendence with even greater vigor than Western traditions, insisting through the *essence-energies distinction* (Gregory Palamas) that God in His essence is utterly beyond all creaturely comprehension.

However, Orthodox theology corrects Bildad's one-sided transcendence with the equally emphatic affirmation of divine *condescension* (*synkatabasis*). The God who is beyond all being freely chose to create, to enter into covenant, and ultimately to become incarnate. Bildad knows that God is high; he does not know that God bends low. The Orthodox hymn "O Heavenly King" addresses the Holy Spirit as one who is "everywhere present and filling all things" — transcendence and immanence held together.

Bildad's question "How can a mortal be righteous before God?" (25:4) is answered in Orthodox soteriology not through forensic declaration but through *theosis* — genuine participation in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). The human person is not merely declared righteous from the outside but is actually transformed from within by the divine energies. Bildad cannot imagine this answer because his theology has no room for divine-human communion — only distance.
