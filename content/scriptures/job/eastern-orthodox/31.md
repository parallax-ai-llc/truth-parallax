## Job's Oath of Innocence

Job 31 presents an examination of conscience that the Orthodox tradition recognizes as anticipating the practice of *exomologesis* (confession). Job examines not only external actions but interior dispositions: lust of the eyes (31:1), deceitful intention (31:5), coveting a neighbor's wife (31:9). The Orthodox preparation for Holy Communion includes a similar comprehensive self-examination, acknowledging that sin begins in the heart before manifesting in action.

Job's ethical vision is remarkable in its breadth. His treatment of servants as equals before God — "Did not he who made me in the womb make them?" (31:15) — articulates a principle that Orthodox social theology grounds in the doctrine of the *image of God* (*imago Dei*). Every human person, regardless of social status, bears the divine image and is therefore of infinite worth. The Orthodox baptismal rite emphasizes this equality: the newly baptized, whether slave or free, rich or poor, is clothed in the same white garment.

Job's climactic demand — "Let the Almighty answer me!" (31:35) — is read in Orthodox tradition with a blend of admiration and caution. Job's boldness reflects the *parrhesia* that characterizes authentic prayer, but his demand for an answer on his own terms will be transcended by God's response. The Orthodox liturgical prayer "Lord, I am not worthy" expresses the complementary truth: even the righteous approach God in humility, knowing that encounter with the Holy exceeds all human categories.
