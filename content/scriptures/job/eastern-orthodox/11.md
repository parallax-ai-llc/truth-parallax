Zophar's description of divine transcendence (11:7-9) — "Can you find out the deep things of God?" — articulates a truth that Orthodox theology affirms with particular emphasis. The Orthodox apophatic tradition, rooted in the writings of St. Gregory of Nyssa, pseudo-Dionysius, and St. Gregory Palamas, teaches that God's essence (*ousia*) is utterly unknowable to created beings. Zophar correctly identifies divine incomprehensibility but draws the wrong conclusion: instead of recognizing that this mystery should humble his own theological claims, he uses it to silence Job's questions while leaving his own theology unchallenged.

The Palamite distinction between God's essence (unknowable) and God's energies (knowable through experience) provides a framework for evaluating Zophar's theology. Zophar claims to know that Job is guilty and that repentance will bring restoration, effectively claiming knowledge of divine purpose that belongs to God's essence, not His revealed energies. Orthodox theology insists that human knowledge of God comes through God's self-revelation in His energies — in creation, in Scripture, in the sacraments, in prayer — not through human speculation about divine motives. Zophar speculates where he should contemplate.

Zophar's conditional promise (11:13-19) — "If you direct your heart rightly... then you will lift up your face without blemish" — represents a semi-Pelagian tendency that Orthodox theology corrects through its synergistic soteriology. Orthodox teaching affirms that salvation involves the cooperation (*synergeia*) of divine grace and human freedom, but it insists that grace always initiates. Zophar's formula implies that human effort alone can restore the divine-human relationship, overlooking the necessity of grace. The Orthodox prayer "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner" (the Jesus Prayer) embodies the correct ordering: petition for mercy precedes and enables human transformation.
