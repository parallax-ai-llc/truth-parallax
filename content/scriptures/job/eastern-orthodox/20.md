Zophar's description of the wicked person who "swallows down riches and vomits them up again" (20:15) employs imagery that Orthodox ascetical theology associates with the passion of *pleonexia* (greed, acquisitiveness). The Philokalia, the great anthology of Orthodox spiritual texts, contains extensive analysis of the passions and their dynamics. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the passions are not evil in themselves but become destructive when misdirected — desire for God becomes greed, anger at injustice becomes wrath. Zophar's description of wealth-as-poison captures the patristic insight that disordered desire ultimately destroys the one who harbors it.

The theme of divine reversal — God casting out the contents of the wicked person's belly (20:15) — resonates with the Orthodox theology of judgment as revelation rather than arbitrary punishment. Orthodox theology, following the Cappadocian Fathers, understands the Last Judgment as the moment when all things are revealed in their true nature under the light of divine truth. The wicked do not receive punishment from an external source; rather, the inner reality of their choices is made manifest. Zophar's imagery of involuntary disgorging captures this dynamic, even though he applies it unjustly to Job.

Zophar's claim that "this is the wicked man's portion from God" (20:29) asserts a determinist theology that Orthodox soteriology complicates through its emphasis on human freedom. Orthodox theology insists that God genuinely desires the salvation of all (1 Timothy 2:4) and that the divine will does not predetermine anyone's destruction. The possibility of repentance remains open until the moment of death — a principle expressed in the Orthodox hymn for Great Lent: "Open to me the doors of repentance, O Life-giver." Zophar's closed system leaves no room for the transformative mercy that Orthodox theology considers central to God's character.
