## Repentance and Restoration

Job's final words — "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you" (42:5) — express the transition from discursive knowledge to direct experience that Orthodox theology calls *theoria* (contemplation, vision of God). The entire ascetic and liturgical tradition of Orthodoxy aims at precisely this: moving the faithful from hearing about God to seeing God — not with physical eyes but with the "eyes of the heart" that are opened through purification, illumination, and union.

God's vindication of Job over his friends — "You have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has" (42:7) — is read in Orthodox theology as confirmation that honest wrestling with God is truer than comfortable piety. The Orthodox tradition of *holy fools* (*yurodivy*) — saints who spoke uncomfortable truths in scandalous ways — finds its biblical precedent in Job's audacious honesty. Job's intercession for his friends (42:8-9) further reveals his priestly character and prefigures the great High Priest who intercedes for all.

Job's restoration (42:10-17) is read in Orthodox eschatology as an icon of the resurrection. The doubled possessions, the new family, the long life are not merely narrative closure but typological signs of the age to come. Orthodox funeral prayers express hope not for mere restoration but for *transfiguration* — the transformation of all creation in the light of Christ's risen glory. Job's story ends in this life, but its meaning extends into the life of the world to come.
