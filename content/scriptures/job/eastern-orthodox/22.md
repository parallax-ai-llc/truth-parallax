## Eliphaz's Final Accusation

Orthodox patristic reading of Job 22 draws primarily on St. John Chrysostom, who saw in Eliphaz's false accusations a warning against the spiritual disease of *pleonexia* — the grasping desire to impose one's own understanding on the mystery of another's suffering. Eliphaz fabricates specific sins (22:6-9) because his theological system demands them. Chrysostom taught that such reasoning reveals more about the accuser's spiritual state than the accused's.

The Orthodox liturgical tradition, which reads Job extensively during Holy Week, finds in Eliphaz's speech a parallel to the false witnesses at Christ's trial. Just as Eliphaz invents charges to explain Job's suffering, the Sanhedrin sought false testimony to justify their condemnation of the Righteous One. The pattern of the innocent sufferer, falsely accused, is central to Orthodox Christology and soteriology.

Eliphaz's exhortation to "receive instruction from God's mouth" (22:22) contains genuine wisdom that the Orthodox tradition affirms — but the irony is that Eliphaz himself has not done so. He speaks from human reasoning rather than divine revelation. Orthodox theology insists on the distinction between *theologians* in the patristic sense — those who have experienced God — and mere rationalists who construct systems about God. Eliphaz belongs to the latter category.
