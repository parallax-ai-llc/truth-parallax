## Job's Hymn to God's Power

Job's hymn to divine power in chapter 26 evokes the Orthodox theology of creation as a free act of divine love. God "suspends the earth over nothing" (26:7) — the world has no foundation other than God's creative will. Orthodox cosmology, following the Cappadocian Fathers, insists that creation is *ex nihilo* and is sustained moment by moment by divine energy. Without God's continuous creative act, all things would return to nothingness.

The imagery of God conquering Rahab and piercing the "gliding serpent" (26:12-13) connects to the Orthodox understanding of Christ's cosmic victory. The Paschal troparion — "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death" — celebrates the same divine power over chaos that Job glimpses. Orthodox iconography of the Resurrection (*Anastasis*) depicts Christ descending into Hades and binding the powers of chaos, an image with deep roots in passages like this.

Job's concluding observation — "these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him!" (26:14) — is the quintessential apophatic statement. Orthodox theology distinguishes between what we can know of God (His energies, revealed in creation and Scripture) and what remains forever beyond knowledge (His essence). Even the most magnificent revelation — creation itself — is merely a "whisper" of the divine reality. This is why Orthodox worship is characterized by awe and mystery: the God we worship infinitely exceeds our capacity to understand.
