## Does God Benefit from Human Righteousness?

Elihu's question about whether human righteousness affects God (35:6-7) touches the heart of the Orthodox essence-energies distinction. God in His essence (*ousia*) is utterly beyond all creaturely influence — nothing adds to or diminishes God's essential being. Yet in His energies (*energeiai*), God truly engages with creation, and human response genuinely matters. The Orthodox answer to Elihu is paradoxical: God gains nothing from human righteousness, yet God genuinely delights in it.

This paradox is liturgically expressed in the Orthodox Eucharistic prayer: "We offer to You, Yours of Your own, on behalf of all and for all." God needs nothing from us, yet He receives our offerings with genuine joy. Elihu's theology, which emphasizes only divine self-sufficiency, misses this relational dimension. The God of Orthodox theology is not the unmoved mover of Aristotle but the Trinity of loving Persons who freely chose to create beings capable of communion.

Elihu's critique of unanswered prayer — that people cry out but do not truly seek God (35:10-12) — contains insight that the Orthodox tradition of *hesychasm* (inner stillness) develops. Authentic prayer requires *nepsis* (watchfulness) and the turning of the whole heart toward God. Yet applying this critique to Job, whose entire struggle has been directed toward God, reveals its inadequacy. Job's prayer may be anguished, but it is anything but inattentive.
