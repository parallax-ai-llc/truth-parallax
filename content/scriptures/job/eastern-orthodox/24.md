## The Problem of Unpunished Evil

Job 24's catalogue of injustice — the displacement of boundaries, the seizure of orphans' animals, the forced hiding of the poor (24:2-4) — is read in Orthodox social theology as evidence of the fallen world's distortion of God's intended order. The Orthodox concept of *kenosis* (self-emptying) extends to social ethics: where Christ emptied Himself for others, the wicked in Job 24 grasp and exploit, reversing the divine pattern.

The vivid descriptions of suffering — the hungry gleaning bare fields, the naked shivering without shelter (24:6-8) — echo the Orthodox liturgical texts for the Last Judgment, where Christ identifies Himself with the hungry, naked, and homeless (Matthew 25:35-36). Orthodox social teaching, rooted in the Cappadocian Fathers, insists that neglect of the poor is not merely a social failure but a theological one — it is a refusal to recognize Christ in the suffering.

Job's question about why God does not schedule times of judgment (24:1) is addressed in Orthodox eschatology through the distinction between the present age and the age to come. God's patience (*makrothymia*) with evil is not indifference but long-suffering love, granting time for repentance. Yet this patience has a limit, and the Orthodox tradition of the Second Coming affirms that divine justice will ultimately and publicly vindicate the oppressed and judge the oppressor.
