Eliphaz's first speech is evaluated in Orthodox tradition through the lens of spiritual discernment (*diakrisis*), one of the most important gifts in the patristic understanding of the spiritual life. The desert fathers, particularly Evagrius of Pontus and St. John Cassian, developed detailed criteria for discerning genuine spiritual insight from demonic deception. Eliphaz claims a nocturnal visionary experience (4:12-16), but Orthodox spiritual directors would question its source: authentic divine communication typically produces peace and humility, while this vision produces fear and leads to judgment of others.

Eliphaz's principle that "those who plow iniquity and sow trouble reap the same" (4:8) states a general truth that Orthodox theology recognizes within the doctrine of divine providence. However, the Orthodox tradition, following the Cappadocian Fathers, distinguishes between God's *boulema* (deliberate will) and God's *synchoreseos* (permissive will). Suffering may result from sin, from demonic attack, from the natural conditions of a fallen world, or from God's mysterious purpose of spiritual purification. Eliphaz reduces this complex theology to a single mechanism, which is a theological impoverishment.

The spirit's declaration that no mortal can be righteous before God (4:17) touches the Orthodox understanding of human nature as damaged but not destroyed by the Fall. Orthodox theology, unlike some Western formulations, teaches that the *image* of God in humanity is retained even after the Fall, though the *likeness* (understood as the dynamic capacity for deification) is impaired. Eliphaz's sweeping nihilism about human righteousness goes further than Orthodox anthropology warrants, overlooking the grace-enabled capacity for genuine holiness that the saints demonstrate.
