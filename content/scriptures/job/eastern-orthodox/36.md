## Elihu Exalts God's Majesty

Elihu's statement that God "delivers the afflicted by their affliction" (36:15) resonates with the Orthodox theology of redemptive suffering. The Orthodox tradition of the *Passion-bearers* — saints who accepted suffering with patience and without resistance — exemplifies this principle. Boris and Gleb, the first saints canonized in Rus', chose to suffer death rather than resist, and their suffering itself became a source of grace for others.

The chapter's transition from theological argument to natural contemplation (36:26-33) mirrors the Orthodox spiritual movement from *praktike* (ascetic struggle) to *theoria physike* (contemplation of God through nature). Evagrius Ponticus taught that the person who has been purified through struggle begins to see God's *logoi* (creative intentions) in every creature and phenomenon. Elihu's turning toward the storm is a turning toward this contemplative mode.

The storm imagery — clouds laden with moisture, thunder, lightning (36:27-33) — fills the Orthodox liturgical imagination. The Great Blessing of Waters at Theophany celebrates God's presence in the elements, and the Orthodox understanding of creation as *theophanic* (revealing God) gives Elihu's meteorological observations a sacramental depth. In Orthodox perspective, the storm Elihu describes is not mere weather but a revelation of the God who is about to speak.
