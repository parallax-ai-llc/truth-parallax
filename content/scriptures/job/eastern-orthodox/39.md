## The Wild Kingdom

God's survey of the animal kingdom in Job 39 reveals a creation that Orthodox theology describes as *logikos* — endowed with divine reason and purpose, even in its wildest forms. The mountain goats birthing in hidden places (39:1), the wild donkey ranging free across the salt flats (39:5-8), the eagle nesting on inaccessible crags (39:27-28) — all bear witness to the Creator's care for creatures that exist beyond human knowledge and utility.

Orthodox theology reads the diversity and freedom of the animal world as a reflection of God's own creative abundance. St. Basil the Great's *Hexaemeron* celebrates each creature as an expression of divine artistry, and the Orthodox blessing of animals on certain feast days acknowledges that all creatures participate in the divine economy. The ostrich, deliberately made without wisdom (39:17), and the war horse, clothed in thunder (39:19-25), are equally manifestations of divine creativity.

The ecological implications are significant for contemporary Orthodox theology. Ecumenical Patriarch Bartholomew, known as "the Green Patriarch," has drawn on the Orthodox sacramental understanding of creation — rooted in passages like Job 39 — to articulate a theology of environmental responsibility. If God cares for wild creatures that serve no human purpose, then creation's value is intrinsic, not merely instrumental, and humanity's role is stewardship, not exploitation.
