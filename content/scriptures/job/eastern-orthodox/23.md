## Job's Longing for God's Court

Job's desperate search for God — "If only I knew where to find him" (23:3) — resonates deeply with the Orthodox theology of divine desire (*eros*). St. Gregory of Nyssa taught that the soul's longing for God is itself a form of participation in God, since the desire for the Infinite can only be ignited by the Infinite. Job's searching in every direction (23:8-9) is not futile but is itself a spiritual ascent.

The Orthodox tradition of *apophatic* (negative) theology illuminates Job's experience. God is not found in the expected places because God transcends all locations and categories. The *Philokalia* teaches that the experience of God's apparent absence can be a higher form of divine encounter than any consolation — the soul is being drawn beyond images and concepts into the darkness of divine mystery, which is in reality "a darkness beyond light" (Pseudo-Dionysius).

Job's confidence that he will emerge "as gold" (23:10) from his testing finds its Orthodox parallel in the theology of *theosis* (deification). The refining fire of suffering is not merely punitive or pedagogical but transformative — it participates in the process by which the human person is conformed to the divine likeness. Yet Job's terror before God (23:15-17) reminds us that *theosis* is not comfortable but awesome, involving the death of the old self before the birth of the new.
