## Does God Benefit from Human Righteousness?

Elihu's question — whether human righteousness or sin affects God at all (35:6-7) — touches on a central issue in Catholic theology. The doctrine of divine aseity affirms that God is entirely self-sufficient and gains nothing from creation. Yet Catholic theology simultaneously affirms, through the doctrine of the covenant, that God has freely chosen to be affected by human response. The two truths are held in paradox.

Elihu's explanation for unanswered prayer — that people "cry out under a load of oppression" but do not truly seek God (35:9-12) — contains a kernel of truth that Catholic spiritual direction recognizes. The difference between self-centered cries for relief and genuine prayer that seeks God's will is central to Ignatian discernment. Yet applying this analysis to Job, who has consistently addressed God directly and passionately, reveals its inadequacy.

Catholic theology, through the *analogia entis*, maintains that while Creator and creature are infinitely different, there remains a real, if analogical, relationship between them. God's transcendence does not negate His immanence. Elihu grasps the first truth but misses the second, producing a theology that is technically correct but spiritually incomplete — a God of majesty without intimacy.
