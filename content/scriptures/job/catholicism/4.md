Catholic interpretation of Eliphaz's first speech draws on a long tradition of moral discernment about the relationship between truth and its application. Aquinas noted in his *Expositio super Iob* that Eliphaz represents a position that is partially true — suffering can indeed result from sin — but errs by absolutizing a general principle into a universal rule. The Catholic emphasis on analogical reasoning (neither univocal nor equivocal) provides a framework for understanding how Eliphaz's theology fails: he applies a principle univocally where only an analogical application is warranted.

The nocturnal vision Eliphaz reports (4:12-16) — a spirit passing before his face, causing his hair to stand on end — has generated extensive discussion in Catholic theology regarding the discernment of spirits. Gregory the Great treated this vision as genuinely supernatural but noted that its theological content, while true in a limited sense, was misapplied. Catholic tradition's criteria for discerning authentic spiritual experience (outlined in the writings of Ignatius of Loyola and John of the Cross) would question a vision that produces fear and leads to judgment of others rather than charity and humility.

The spirit's question — "Can mortal man be in the right before God?" (4:17) — states a truth that Catholic theology affirms through the doctrine of original sin and the necessity of grace. However, the Catholic tradition also insists on the reality of sanctifying grace that genuinely transforms the human person, making real righteousness possible through divine assistance. Eliphaz's error is not in affirming human frailty but in denying the possibility of genuine, grace-enabled righteousness — a denial that the narrator's description of Job as "blameless and upright" (1:1) explicitly contradicts.
