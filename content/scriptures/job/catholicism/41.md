## Leviathan

The extended description of Leviathan in Job 41 — fire-breathing, armored, utterly fearless (41:18-21, 33) — has generated rich Catholic theological reflection. St. Gregory the Great devoted extensive commentary to Leviathan as a figure of Satan: "nothing on earth is its equal" (41:33) because the demonic operates with a power that no merely human effort can overcome. Only God is Leviathan's master.

Catholic demonology, while affirming the reality of spiritual evil, insists on its subordination to divine sovereignty. Leviathan is terrifying, but God describes it with something approaching playfulness — as if this fearsome creature is, in God's eyes, a remarkable specimen rather than an existential threat. The Catechism affirms that "the power of Satan is, nonetheless, not infinite. He is only a creature, powerful from the fact that he is pure spirit, but still a creature" (CCC 395).

The Christological reading of Leviathan's defeat connects to the Catholic theology of Christ's descent into hell (*descendit ad inferos*). The Apostles' Creed affirms that Christ, in His death, entered the realm of the dead and conquered the powers of chaos and death. The Leviathan who is "king over all that are proud" (41:34) meets its match in the one who "humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death" (Philippians 2:8). Catholic Easter liturgy celebrates this cosmic victory.
