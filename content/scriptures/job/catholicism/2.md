The intensification of Job's trial from material loss to bodily affliction holds special significance in Catholic theology, which has a developed theology of the body and of physical suffering. Pope John Paul II's apostolic letter *Salvifici Doloris* (1984) draws on Job as the paradigmatic Old Testament figure who confronts the mystery of suffering. The pope argued that Job's experience demonstrates that suffering cannot be adequately explained by retributive justice and must ultimately be understood in the light of the Cross.

God's acknowledgment that Job "still holds fast his integrity, although you incited me against him to destroy him without reason" (2:3) is significant for Catholic moral theology. The word "integrity" (*tummah*) suggests wholeness of character — Job's virtue is not a single act but a stable disposition, what Aquinas would call a *habitus* (virtue as a stable quality of the soul). Catholic tradition sees Job's perseverance under escalating trial as evidence of infused virtue, a grace given by God that enables human cooperation with divine purposes through suffering.

Job's wife's counsel to "curse God and die" (2:9) has been interpreted variously in Catholic tradition. While some patristic writers (notably Augustine) viewed her harshly as a tool of Satan, Gregory the Great offered a more nuanced reading, noting that Satan left her alive precisely to serve as an instrument of temptation. Catholic commentators have increasingly recognized her own suffering as a bereaved mother and the wife of a man afflicted beyond recognition. The friends' seven days of silent solidarity (2:11-13) embody what Catholic social teaching calls accompaniment — the ministry of presence before the ministry of words.
