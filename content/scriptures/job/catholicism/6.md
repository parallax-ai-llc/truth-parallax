Job's wish that his suffering could be weighed on scales (6:2-3) reflects a concern for proportionality that resonates with Catholic moral theology's emphasis on justice as rendering to each what is due. His insistence that his complaint is rational and proportionate to his suffering, rather than the reckless outburst Eliphaz implied, demonstrates what Catholic tradition calls *recta ratio* — right reason operating even under extreme duress. Job is not abandoning reason but applying it to his experience with painful honesty.

The wadi metaphor (6:15-20) — friends who are like seasonal streams that overflow when water is abundant but vanish in the desert heat — has deep resonance in Catholic teaching on friendship. Aquinas, drawing on Aristotle, distinguished between friendships of utility, pleasure, and virtue. Job accuses his friends of failing even the lowest standard: they offer neither practical help nor emotional comfort. Catholic social teaching's principle of solidarity demands more than theoretical sympathy; it requires concrete accompaniment, especially in times of suffering.

Job's challenge — "Teach me, and I will be silent; make me understand how I have gone astray" (6:24) — embodies the Catholic intellectual tradition's commitment to reasoned argument. He does not refuse correction but demands that his accusers provide specific evidence. Catholic canon law's insistence on due process and the rights of the accused (Canons 1717-1731) reflects a similar conviction: accusations must be substantiated, not merely asserted. Job's friends condemn without evidence, a violation of justice that Catholic tradition has consistently rejected.
