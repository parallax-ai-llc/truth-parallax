## Elihu's Angry Entrance

Catholic interpretation of Elihu has varied considerably. St. Gregory the Great saw him as a figure of pride — a young man who claims spiritual authority he does not possess. St. Thomas Aquinas, by contrast, gave Elihu a more sympathetic reading, seeing his theology as a genuine advance over the three friends. This interpretive diversity reflects Catholic theology's comfort with multiple valid readings within the tradition.

Elihu's claim that wisdom comes not from age but from "the spirit in a person, the breath of the Almighty" (32:8) raises questions about the nature of theological authority. Catholic theology affirms that the Holy Spirit can speak through any believer, but it also insists on the role of the Magisterium in discerning authentic inspiration from mere enthusiasm. Elihu's angry certainty (32:2-5) — he burns against both Job and the friends — may reflect genuine inspiration or youthful presumption; the text remains ambiguous.

The Catholic lectionary does not include Elihu's speeches in the liturgical cycle, which some scholars interpret as a quiet judgment on their theological weight. Nevertheless, the Elihu section serves a literary function that Catholic exegesis values: it creates a dramatic pause between Job's challenge and God's response, building anticipation for the theophany. In the economy of the narrative, even incomplete theology serves the divine purpose.
