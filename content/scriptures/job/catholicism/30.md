## Job's Present Humiliation

The devastating contrast between chapters 29 and 30 mirrors the theological movement from consolation to desolation that St. Ignatius of Loyola described in his *Spiritual Exercises*. Job, who was once honored by nobles, is now mocked by the lowest of society (30:1-8). His body is ravaged by disease, his skin blackened and peeling, his bones burning (30:30). Catholic theology of suffering insists on taking this physical agony with full seriousness — the body matters.

John Paul II's *Salvifici Doloris* identifies Job as the paradigmatic sufferer of the Old Testament, one whose experience raises the question of suffering's meaning with unparalleled force. Job's cry "I cry to you, God, but you do not answer" (30:20) is not sin but the authentic prayer of the suffering just — a prayer that finds its ultimate voicing in Christ's cry of dereliction from the cross (Matthew 27:46).

Catholic devotional tradition has connected Job's physical sufferings to the Passion of Christ. Job's becoming "a brother of jackals" (30:29), his social ostracism, and his sense of divine abandonment all find Christological resonance. The tradition of offering one's sufferings in union with Christ's Passion (*redemptive suffering*) finds its Old Testament foundation in precisely this chapter: suffering that is real, undeserved, and yet, within God's mysterious providence, not meaningless.
