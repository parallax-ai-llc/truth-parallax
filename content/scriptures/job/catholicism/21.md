Job's observation that "the wicked live, reach old age, and grow mighty in power" (21:7) raises the classic problem of theodicy that Catholic theology addresses through its eschatological framework. The Church teaches that final judgment belongs to God alone and occurs at the end of time (CCC 1038-1041). The apparent prosperity of the wicked in this life does not negate divine justice but defers it to the eschaton. Augustine argued in *De Civitate Dei* that the mixture of righteous and wicked in this life is part of God's providential design, which will be fully revealed only at the Last Judgment.

Job's empirical challenge to retribution theology — "How often is it that the lamp of the wicked is put out?" (21:17) — employs a method that Catholic intellectual tradition values. The Thomistic synthesis of faith and reason insists that theology must account for observed reality, not merely assert doctrinal positions against the evidence. Catholic moral theology's tradition of casuistry (careful analysis of specific cases) reflects this same commitment to attending to the particular rather than reasoning only from abstract principles. Job is the book's casuist, insisting that his specific case cannot be resolved by general rules.

Job's concluding judgment — "How then will you comfort me with empty nothings?" (21:34) — echoes the prophet Ezekiel's condemnation of those who "whitewash" false visions (Ezekiel 13:10-16). Catholic theology recognizes a prophetic function in Job's speech: he speaks truth to religious power, challenging a theological establishment that has substituted its own certainties for divine revelation. The Church's tradition of prophetic witness — from the Old Testament prophets through the saints and the papal social encyclicals — continues this work of speaking uncomfortable truths against comfortable theologies.
