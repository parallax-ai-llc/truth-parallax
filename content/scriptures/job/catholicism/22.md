## Eliphaz's Final Accusation

Catholic interpretation of Job 22 draws on the patristic tradition, particularly St. Gregory the Great's *Moralia in Job*, which reads Eliphaz's false accusations as a portrait of how spiritual blindness leads to calumny. Eliphaz charges Job with specific sins — oppressing widows, withholding bread, stripping the naked (22:6-9) — that the prologue has already refuted. Gregory sees in Eliphaz a warning against judging others' interior states based on external circumstances.

The Magisterium's teaching on the mystery of suffering, articulated in John Paul II's *Salvifici Doloris*, provides a framework for reading this chapter. Eliphaz represents the persistent human temptation to reduce suffering to a mathematical equation of sin and punishment. Catholic theology insists that suffering, while it may have pedagogical value, is ultimately a mystery that finds its deepest meaning in union with Christ's Passion.

Eliphaz's call to repentance (22:21-30) contains language that is, paradoxically, beautiful: "Submit to God and be at peace with him; in this way prosperity will come to you" (22:21). Catholic spiritual writers have noted that these words, when detached from Eliphaz's false premise, articulate a genuine truth about the spiritual life. The tradition of *lectio divina* allows even misapplied words to yield spiritual fruit when read in the light of the whole canon.
