## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Job draws on the rich patristic and medieval tradition of reading the book through Christological and moral lenses. The most influential Catholic commentary on Job is St. Gregory the Great's *Moralia in Iob* (Morals on the Book of Job), a massive 35-book work that reads Job simultaneously as history, allegory, and moral instruction.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Four Senses of Scripture
Following Catholic hermeneutical tradition, Job is read on multiple levels:

1. **Literal**: The historical narrative of a righteous man's suffering and restoration
2. **Allegorical**: Job as a type of Christ — the innocent sufferer who is vindicated by God
3. **Moral**: Practical instruction on patience, humility, and trust in divine providence
4. **Anagogical**: The restoration of Job as a figure of the resurrection and final vindication

### Redemptive Suffering
Catholic theology sees in Job a foundation for the doctrine of redemptive suffering — that suffering, united to Christ's passion, can have salvific value. Pope John Paul II's *Salvifici Doloris* (1984) draws heavily on Job.

## Theological Emphases

- **Job as type of Christ**: The innocent sufferer who intercedes for others (42:8-10)
- **Redemptive value of suffering**: Suffering is not meaningless but participates in divine purposes
- **The virtue of patience**: Job's endurance as a model of *patientia* in the face of trial
- **Divine providence**: God's governance of all things, even when hidden
- **The limits of human wisdom**: Only God possesses true wisdom (chapter 28)
