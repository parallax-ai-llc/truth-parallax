## Job's Remembrance of Former Glory

Job's recollection of his former life in chapter 29 provides a portrait of sanctity that Catholic hagiography recognizes: a man who combined intimate friendship with God (29:4) with active care for the vulnerable. He rescued the poor, helped the fatherless, made the widow's heart sing (29:12-13), and served as "eyes to the blind and feet to the lame" (29:15). Catholic social teaching identifies these as the corporal works of mercy.

St. Gregory the Great read Job's former glory as an image of the Church in her fullness — a community where divine friendship and social justice are inseparable. Job's role as a just leader who "sat as chief" and "dwelt as a king among his troops" (29:25) prefigures the ideal of the Christian ruler guided by the virtues, a theme developed extensively in Catholic political theology from Augustine's *City of God* to the social encyclicals.

The chapter's emotional dimension — Job's aching memory of when "God's intimate friendship blessed my house" (29:4) — speaks to the Catholic contemplative tradition. The mystics describe seasons when God's presence was palpable, followed by withdrawal that deepens desire. Job's nostalgia is not mere sentimentality but a spiritual reality that St. John of the Cross called the memory of "past visits" that sustains the soul through present darkness.
