## Job's Longing for God's Court

Job's cry in 23:3 — "If only I knew where to find him" — resonates profoundly with the Catholic mystical tradition. St. John of the Cross described the "dark night of the soul" as precisely this experience: the soul that has known God's presence is plunged into apparent abandonment, yet this very darkness is a deeper form of divine encounter. Job's longing is not evidence of faithlessness but of a love that cannot bear God's absence.

The Catechism teaches that "the desire for God is written in the human heart" (CCC 27). Job's restless searching — forward, backward, left, right (23:8-9) — dramatizes this innate desire at its most intense. Catholic theology reads Job's inability to find God not as divine cruelty but as the purgative stage of the spiritual life, where the soul is weaned from consolations in order to seek God for His own sake.

Job's confidence that he will emerge "as gold" from testing (23:10) has been a treasured text in Catholic spirituality. St. Thérèse of Lisieux, who endured intense spiritual darkness, drew consolation from this image. The refining fire of suffering purifies faith, burning away attachment to God's gifts in order to deepen attachment to God Himself. Yet the chapter's final verses (23:15-17) maintain the tension: purification is real, but so is the terror of God's inscrutable purposes.
