## Elihu Exalts God's Majesty

Job 36 presents Elihu at his theological best. His assertion that God "delivers the afflicted by their affliction" (36:15) articulates a principle that Catholic theology embraces: suffering itself can be a vehicle of grace. The tradition of redemptive suffering, from Paul's "I fill up in my flesh what is lacking in regard to Christ's afflictions" (Colossians 1:24) to John Paul II's theology of the body, finds an Old Testament root in this verse.

The chapter's transition from theological argument to natural theology (36:26-33) — thunder, clouds, lightning as evidence of God's power — resonates with the Catholic theological tradition of reading creation as a "book" written by God. The Catechism states that "God speaks to man through the visible creation" (CCC 1147), and Elihu's attention to the storm illustrates this principle. Creation is not merely scenery but sacramental — pointing beyond itself to the Creator.

Catholic commentators note that as Elihu turns from human reasoning to contemplation of nature, his theology improves. His arguments about suffering were partially correct but misapplied; his wonder at creation approaches the reverence that God's own speeches will demand. The movement from system-building to contemplation mirrors the Catholic spiritual life's trajectory from the active to the contemplative, from theology to prayer.
