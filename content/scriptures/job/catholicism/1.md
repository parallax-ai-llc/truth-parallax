Catholic interpretation of Job 1 begins with the recognition that Job's story has been read within the Church's liturgical and theological tradition since the patristic era. Pope Gregory the Great's monumental *Moralia in Iob* (Morals on the Book of Job) established the interpretive framework that shaped Catholic reading for over a millennium. Gregory read Job simultaneously on three levels: the literal-historical, the allegorical-christological, and the moral-tropological. In this reading, Job himself prefigures Christ, and his sufferings anticipate the Passion.

The heavenly council scene (1:6-12) is understood in Catholic theology as demonstrating the reality of Satan's limited agency under divine sovereignty. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 395) teaches that Satan's power is "not infinite" and that he is "only a creature, powerless against the mystery of God." The fact that Satan must obtain divine permission before striking Job illustrates the Catholic principle that evil operates within providentially ordained limits. Thomas Aquinas, in his commentary on Job, emphasized that God permits evil not because He wills suffering but because He can draw greater good from it — a principle central to the theology of redemptive suffering.

Job's response — "The LORD gave, and the LORD has taken away; blessed be the name of the LORD" (1:21) — is understood in Catholic tradition as an act of supreme virtue, specifically the theological virtue of fortitude combined with the theological virtue of hope. The liturgical use of Job in the Office of the Dead reflects the Church's conviction that Job's patient endurance models the disposition Christians should maintain in the face of death and loss. His words are not mere resignation but active surrender to divine providence, a disposition that Catholic spiritual theology calls *abandonment to Divine Providence* (following Jean-Pierre de Caussade).
