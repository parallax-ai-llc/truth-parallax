## Bildad's Brief Final Word

Bildad's third speech, at only six verses, reveals the exhaustion of purely rational theology. Catholic interpretation, following St. Gregory the Great, reads Bildad's brevity as evidence that human argumentation about God's ways inevitably reaches a limit. The friends have talked themselves into silence — a silence that will be filled, ultimately, only by God's own voice.

Bildad's assertion of divine transcendence — "Dominion and awe belong to God; he establishes order in the heights of heaven" (25:2) — aligns with Catholic theology's affirmation of God's absolute sovereignty. Yet the tradition of analogical reasoning (*analogia entis*) insists that the gap between Creator and creature, while infinite, is bridgeable through grace. Bildad knows God is transcendent but has no concept of divine condescension — the very principle that Catholic theology locates at the heart of Incarnation and sacrament.

Bildad's question "how can a mortal be righteous before God?" (25:4) is answered in Catholic soteriology through the doctrine of justification: not by human effort alone, but by grace received through faith and the sacraments. The Council of Trent affirmed that justification involves genuine interior renewal, not merely forensic declaration — God does not merely declare the "maggot" (25:6) righteous but actually transforms the creature through sanctifying grace.
