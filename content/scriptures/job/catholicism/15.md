Eliphaz's second speech opens with the accusation that Job is "doing away with the fear of God" (15:4). Catholic theology distinguishes between servile fear (fear of punishment) and filial fear (reverential awe rooted in love). The Book of Sirach teaches that "the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom" (Sirach 1:14), a text in the Catholic canon that the deuterocanonical tradition preserves. Job's honest questioning does not undermine authentic fear of the Lord but rather demonstrates the filial form — a reverence so deep that it dares to engage God directly rather than retreat into formulaic piety.

Eliphaz's question "What is man, that he can be pure?" (15:14) and his assertion that even "the heavens are not pure in his sight" (15:15) articulate a radical depravity that Catholic theology moderates through its understanding of grace. The Council of Trent taught that while original sin wounded human nature, it did not totally corrupt it (Session 5, Canon 5). Concupiscence remains but does not constitute sin in itself. Catholic anthropology thus offers a more hopeful assessment of human capacity than Eliphaz's vision, while still affirming the universal need for grace.

The extended portrayal of the wicked person's torment (15:20-35) functions as an implicit sermon directed at Job. Catholic moral theology's tradition of examining conscience — a practice recommended before confession — provides a framework for honest self-assessment, but it insists that this examination be guided by the Holy Spirit and the teaching of the Church, not by the judgments of external observers. Eliphaz appoints himself as Job's confessor without authority and pronounces a judgment that Catholic sacramental theology reserves to God alone, mediated through the priest in the seal of confession.
