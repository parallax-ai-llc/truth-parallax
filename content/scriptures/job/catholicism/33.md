## Elihu on Dreams and Discipline

Elihu's first address to Job in chapter 33 introduces two significant theological concepts. First, God communicates through dreams and nocturnal visions to "turn them from wrongdoing and keep them from pride" (33:17). Catholic theology, informed by the scholastic tradition, affirms that God may use extraordinary means — including dreams, visions, and even suffering — to guide the faithful, while insisting on the primacy of Scripture, Tradition, and the Magisterium for doctrinal discernment.

Second, Elihu envisions a heavenly mediator: "an angel, a mediator, one out of a thousand" who declares "I have found a ransom for them" (33:23-24). Catholic Christology reads this passage as a significant Old Testament anticipation of Christ's mediatorial role. The Epistle to the Hebrews identifies Christ as the definitive mediator of a new covenant (Hebrews 9:15), and the Catholic liturgy echoes this in the prayer "per Christum Dominum nostrum" — through Christ our Lord.

Elihu's theology of suffering as divine pedagogy (33:19-22) aligns more closely with Catholic teaching than the retribution theology of the three friends. *Salvifici Doloris* affirms that suffering can be a "call" — an invitation to conversion and deeper union with God. Yet Catholic theology also insists, with the book of Job itself, that not all suffering is pedagogical. The mystery exceeds every explanatory framework.
