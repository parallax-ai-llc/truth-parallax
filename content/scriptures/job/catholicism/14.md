Job's meditation on human mortality — "Man who is born of a woman is few of days and full of trouble" (14:1) — has been incorporated into Catholic funeral liturgy for centuries. The prayer *Subvenite* and the readings at the Rite of Christian Burial draw on the same themes of human frailty and divine transcendence. Catholic theology understands mortality not as natural but as a consequence of original sin (CCC 1008): "death entered the world through sin" (Romans 5:12). Job's experience of mortality's weight thus reflects the universal human condition under the effects of the Fall.

The tree metaphor (14:7-9) — a felled tree that sprouts again at the scent of water — followed by the contrast with human death (14:10-12) contains what Catholic interpreters identify as a seed of resurrection hope. The question "If a man dies, shall he live again?" (14:14) is not merely rhetorical in Catholic reading. The Church teaches that the Old Testament contains real anticipations of resurrection faith (CCC 992), and Job's question, while unanswered within the narrative, points toward the definitive answer given in Christ's resurrection. The *sensus plenior* of Scripture allows Catholic interpreters to read forward from Job's question to its christological fulfillment.

Job's image of God sealing up transgression "in a bag" (14:17) and the gradual erosion of hope "as water wears away stones" (14:19) reflect a vision of cumulative divine judgment that Catholic theology addresses through the doctrine of purgatory. The Church teaches that temporal punishment for sin may persist even after guilt is forgiven (CCC 1472-1473), and the process of purification continues beyond death. While Job does not articulate this doctrine, his intuition that sin has lasting consequences beyond simple forgiveness aligns with the Catholic understanding of the spiritual life as a process of ongoing purification.
