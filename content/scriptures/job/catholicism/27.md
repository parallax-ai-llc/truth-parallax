## Job's Oath of Integrity

Job's oath of integrity in chapter 27 — "Till I die, I will not deny my integrity" (27:5) — resonates with the Catholic tradition of moral conscience. The Catechism teaches that "a human being must always obey the certain judgment of his conscience" (CCC 1790). Job's refusal to confess sins he has not committed, even under intense social and theological pressure, exemplifies the inviolability of conscience that Catholic moral theology defends.

St. Gregory the Great interpreted Job's oath as an act of *fortitudo* — the cardinal virtue of fortitude. Under persecution from friends and, seemingly, from God Himself, Job maintains his truthful self-assessment. Catholic moral theology distinguishes this from pride: Job does not claim sinlessness but refuses to accept false guilt. St. Thomas Aquinas taught that truthfulness about oneself is an integral part of the virtue of justice.

The second portion of the chapter, describing the fate of the wicked (27:13-23), has been read in Catholic exegesis as Job's affirmation that divine justice is real even if its timing is mysterious. Catholic eschatology provides the framework: particular judgment at death and general judgment at the end of time ensure that no sin goes unaddressed and no righteousness unrewarded, even when this life suggests otherwise.
