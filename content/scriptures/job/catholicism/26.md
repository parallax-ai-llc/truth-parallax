## Job's Hymn to God's Power

Job's hymn to divine power in chapter 26 has been treasured in Catholic theology as one of the Old Testament's finest statements on God as Creator. The imagery of God stretching out the heavens over empty space and suspending the earth over nothing (26:7) anticipates the doctrine of *creatio ex nihilo* — creation from nothing — that became a cornerstone of Catholic cosmology.

St. Gregory the Great read Job's description of God's power over Rahab and the sea (26:12-13) typologically: the conquest of the primordial sea monster prefigures Christ's victory over sin and death. The Catholic liturgical tradition draws on this same typology in the Easter Vigil, where the crossing of the Red Sea and the stilling of chaos are celebrated as anticipations of baptismal liberation.

The chapter's concluding verse — "these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him!" (26:14) — is foundational for Catholic natural theology. The First Vatican Council affirmed that God can be known through creation by the natural light of reason (Dei Filius, ch. 2), but this knowledge is like a whisper compared to the thunder of His full reality. The *via eminentiae* of Thomistic theology begins here: what creation reveals about God is true but infinitely exceeded by what God is in Himself.
