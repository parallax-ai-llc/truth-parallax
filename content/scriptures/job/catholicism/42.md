## Repentance and Restoration

Job's final words — "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you" (42:5) — express the transition from notional to real knowledge of God that Catholic theology, following Newman, considers essential to authentic faith. Job's previous theology was correct but secondhand; now he has encountered God directly. This movement from *fides* (faith) to *visio* (vision) anticipates the eschatological beatific vision that is the ultimate goal of human existence.

God's rebuke of the three friends — "You have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has" (42:7) — is one of the most remarkable divine verdicts in Scripture. Catholic theology reads this as confirmation that honest wrestling with God is more pleasing than pious platitudes. The tradition of "holy boldness" (*parrhesia*) — speaking truthfully to God even in anguish — finds its Old Testament justification here. Job's intercession for his friends (42:8-9) further reveals his priestly character, prefiguring Christ's intercessory role.

Job's restoration — double possessions, new children, long life (42:10-17) — is read in Catholic eschatology as a proleptic sign of the resurrection. The restoration does not negate the suffering but transforms it. Catholic theology of the resurrection body affirms that the wounds of this life are not erased but glorified, as Christ's risen body still bears the marks of the nails. Job's story thus points beyond itself to the ultimate Catholic hope: not mere restoration but transfiguration.
