## The Hymn to Wisdom

The great Wisdom Poem of Job 28 holds a privileged place in Catholic theology. The extended mining metaphor (28:1-11) — humans tunneling through rock, overturning mountains, discovering hidden treasures — demonstrates that even the most impressive human achievement cannot uncover wisdom. "Where then does wisdom come from?" (28:20). Catholic theology answers: wisdom is a gift of the Holy Spirit, not an intellectual acquisition.

St. Thomas Aquinas connected Job 28 to his theology of *sapientia* — wisdom as the highest of the intellectual virtues, ordering all things to their ultimate end in God. The chapter's declaration that "God understands the way to it" (28:23) affirms that wisdom resides in God as its source and that human participation in wisdom is always participatory — a sharing in God's own knowledge through grace.

The concluding verse — "The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom" (28:28) — is read in Catholic tradition as anticipating the fullness of the Wisdom literature's teaching. The seven gifts of the Holy Spirit include both wisdom and fear of the Lord; here they are identified as aspects of the same reality. The liturgical reading of this chapter during Lent invites the faithful to recognize that the path to wisdom passes through humility and reverence, not through intellectual mastery or theological system-building.
