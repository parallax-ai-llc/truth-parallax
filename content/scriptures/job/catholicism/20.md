Zophar's second speech presents the wicked person's downfall in terms of ill-gotten wealth disgorged: "He swallows down riches and vomits them up again" (20:15). Catholic social teaching resonates with Zophar's concern about unjust economic gain, though it applies this principle systemically rather than individually as Zophar does. The Church's social encyclicals, from *Rerum Novarum* (1891) to *Fratelli Tutti* (2020), consistently teach that wealth acquired through exploitation of the poor carries a moral debt that must be repaid through justice and charity.

The image of the wicked person who "knew no contentment in his belly" (20:20) touches on the Catholic virtue tradition's treatment of temperance and avarice. Aquinas identified avarice as one of the capital sins — not because wealth is inherently sinful but because the disordered desire for wealth enslaves the soul. Catholic moral theology distinguishes between the right to private property (affirmed) and the universal destination of goods (the principle that the earth's resources are meant for all). Zophar's description of economic self-destruction through greed is theologically sound as a general moral principle, even though its application to Job is unjust.

Zophar's theological error lies not in his moral principles but in his application. Catholic hermeneutics, guided by the Magisterium's teaching authority, insists that Scripture must be read in the context of the whole canon and the Tradition of the Church. Reading Zophar's speech in isolation could produce a coherent theology of retribution; reading it within the full narrative of Job, and within the broader biblical witness, reveals its inadequacy. The Church's practice of lectio continua (continuous reading of Scripture in the liturgy) guards against precisely this kind of selective proof-texting.
