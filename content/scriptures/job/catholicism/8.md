Bildad's blunt defense of divine justice — "Does God pervert justice?" (8:3) — raises a question that Catholic theology addresses through its understanding of divine attributes. Aquinas argued in the *Summa Theologiae* (I, q. 21) that justice and mercy are both essential to God's nature and are never in conflict. God's justice is always tempered by mercy, and God's mercy never contradicts justice. Bildad's error is not in affirming divine justice but in reducing it to a simple calculus of reward and punishment that excludes the mystery of mercy.

The suggestion that Job's children died because of their own sin (8:4) is handled with particular sensitivity in Catholic pastoral tradition. The Church's teaching on the communion of saints and the efficacy of intercessory prayer (CCC 958) suggests a different framework: the living can pray for the dead, and the dead are not simply consigned to their fate by a mechanical principle of retribution. Job's own practice of offering sacrifices for his children (1:5) already demonstrated this intercessory impulse, which Bildad's theology effectively denies.

Bildad's appeal to ancestral wisdom (8:8-10) resonates with the Catholic emphasis on Sacred Tradition as a source of authority alongside Sacred Scripture. However, Catholic theology distinguishes between Apostolic Tradition (which is binding) and merely human traditions (which can err). Bildad treats ancestral opinion as infallible, a mistake that Catholic hermeneutics avoids by subjecting all traditions to the authoritative interpretation of the Magisterium. The content of Bildad's inherited wisdom — a rigid retribution theology — demonstrates that tradition, while valuable, requires discerning interpretation.
