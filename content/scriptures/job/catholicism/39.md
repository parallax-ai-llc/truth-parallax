## The Wild Kingdom

God's tour of the animal kingdom in Job 39 reveals a creation that exceeds human comprehension and control. The mountain goats birthing in secret (39:1), the wild donkey's untamable freedom (39:5-8), the war horse's terrifying eagerness for battle (39:19-25) — all testify to a Creator whose purposes extend far beyond human utility. Catholic theology reads this as an expression of the *gratuity* of creation: God creates not out of need but out of overflowing love.

Pope Francis's *Laudato Si'* draws directly on this theological tradition: "Each creature possesses its own particular goodness and perfection... Each of the various creatures, willed in its own being, reflects in its own way a ray of God's infinite wisdom and goodness" (LS 69). The ostrich, deliberately made foolish by God (39:17), and the eagle, nesting on inaccessible heights (39:27-28), are equally expressions of divine creativity. Catholic ecology finds here a mandate for respecting all creatures, not merely those useful to humans.

The theological implication for Job's suffering is indirect but powerful: if God's creative purposes encompass this vast diversity of creatures with their own modes of flourishing, then His purposes for Job's life may similarly exceed Job's ability to comprehend. Catholic providence theology affirms that God's plan is not a human plan writ large but something genuinely other — encompassing all creatures in a wisdom that surpasses human categories.
