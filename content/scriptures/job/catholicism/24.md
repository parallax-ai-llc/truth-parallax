## The Problem of Unpunished Evil

Job 24 presents a devastating catalogue of social injustice that resonates with Catholic social teaching. The exploitation of the poor — moving boundary stones, seizing orphans' donkeys, forcing the needy off the road (24:2-4) — violates what Catholic moral theology calls the "preferential option for the poor." Job's outrage at these injustices reflects the prophetic tradition that the Church has always claimed as its own.

The chapter's vivid descriptions of destitution — the hungry gleaning in fields, the naked without covering, the shelterless drenched by mountain rains (24:6-8) — appear almost verbatim in Catholic social encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* to *Fratelli Tutti*. The Church reads Job's protest not as doubt about God's existence but as a demand for the kingdom's justice. Job asks why God does not "set times for judgment" (24:1) — a question that Catholic eschatology answers with the doctrine of final judgment.

St. Thomas Aquinas addressed the problem of unpunished evil by distinguishing between God's antecedent will (that all should act justly) and His consequent will (which permits evil for greater goods humans cannot always perceive). Job 24 dramatizes the anguish that lives within this distinction. Catholic theology does not dismiss Job's pain but insists that divine justice, though delayed, is never denied.
