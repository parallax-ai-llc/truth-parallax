## Elihu Defends God's Justice

Elihu's second speech vigorously defends the proposition that "it is unthinkable that God would do wrong" (34:10). Catholic theology affirms this absolutely — God's justice is essential to His nature, not an arbitrary attribute. The *Summa Theologiae* locates justice within God's very being: God cannot act unjustly because His will is the standard of justice itself (ST I, q. 21).

Elihu's argument about divine sustenance of all life (34:14-15) — that if God withdrew His spirit, all flesh would perish — articulates what Catholic theology calls the doctrine of divine conservation. The Catechism teaches that "God does not abandon his creatures to themselves" but sustains them in being at every moment (CCC 301). This is not merely a philosophical truth but a spiritual one: every breath is a gift.

However, Catholic exegesis notes Elihu's limitation. His accusation that Job "adds rebellion to his sin" (34:37) reveals the same error the friends committed: assuming that questioning God's administration is itself sinful. The Catholic spiritual tradition, enriched by mystics who wrestled intensely with God — from Job through Jeremiah to John of the Cross — affirms that honest questioning is not rebellion but an expression of faith seeking understanding (*fides quaerens intellectum*).
