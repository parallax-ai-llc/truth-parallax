Job's lament over human mortality (14:1-2) — "few of days and full of trouble" — confirms the Gnostic assessment of material existence. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches: "In this world, there is good and evil. Its good things are not good, and its evil things not evil. But there is evil after this world which is truly evil." Job's description of life as brief and troubled is not merely an emotional response to suffering but an accurate appraisal of material existence's inherent inadequacy. The Gnostic does not mourn mortality so much as recognize it as further evidence that the material cosmos is a failed creation.

The tree metaphor (14:7-12) — a tree can sprout again, but a dead man does not rise — raises questions about *metempsychosis* (transmigration of souls) in Gnostic thought. Some Gnostic systems, particularly those influenced by Platonic and Pythagorean ideas, taught that the divine spark passes through multiple incarnations before achieving the *gnosis* necessary for final liberation. In this framework, the tree's regeneration becomes an image of reincarnation: the trunk is cut but the root remains and produces new growth. The human being *does* return — not in the same body but through the recycling of the divine spark into new material forms until liberation is achieved.

Job's question "If a man dies, shall he live again?" (14:14) is answered differently in Gnostic theology than in orthodox Christian or rabbinic traditions. The Gnostic answer is: yes, but not through bodily resurrection. The divine spark returns to the Pleroma — the fullness of divine reality — leaving the material body behind permanently. The *Treatise on the Resurrection* (to Rheginos) teaches that the "resurrection" is the awakening of the spiritual self that occurs when *gnosis* is received. This can happen before or after physical death. Job's question, in Gnostic reading, is answered by the experience of *gnosis* rather than by eschatological promise.
