## Memory of the Pleroma

Job's recollection of his former blessedness — "when God's lamp shone on my head, when by his light I walked through darkness" (29:3) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a dim memory of the soul's pre-existence in the Pleroma. Before the divine spark was imprisoned in matter, it dwelt in the fullness of divine light. Job's nostalgia is not merely personal but cosmic: it is the anamnesis (remembering) of a primordial state of unity with the true God.

The *Hymn of the Pearl* tells a similar story: a prince sent to a foreign land forgets his origin, but a letter from his parents awakens his memory and he recovers his royal garment. Job's detailed recollection — divine intimacy (29:4), social honor (29:7-10), charitable activity (29:12-16) — represents the soul remembering its original state of wholeness and purpose before the fall into matter.

The contrast between chapters 29 and 30 mirrors the Gnostic narrative of fall: from Pleromatic fullness to material imprisonment. Yet the very capacity to remember — to feel the ache of what was lost — is evidence that the divine spark has not been fully extinguished. Memory of the light is itself a form of gnosis, and Job's nostalgia is the first step toward the awakening that the book's conclusion will bring.
