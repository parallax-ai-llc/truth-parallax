Job's description of life as "hard service" (7:1) resonates with the Gnostic understanding of earthly existence as enslavement. The *Hymn of the Pearl* (in the *Acts of Thomas*) describes the soul's journey into the material world as a descent into slavery, where the divine prince forgets his true identity and labors under foreign masters. Job's language of forced labor and hired servitude captures this Gnostic mythological pattern: the pneumatic is a royal being trapped in a servile condition, laboring under the archons' regime without remembering his true origin.

The parody of Psalm 8 (7:17-18) — God's attentiveness experienced as oppressive surveillance — is perhaps the most Gnostic-compatible passage in the book. In Gnostic cosmology, the Demiurge and his archons monitor the divine sparks trapped in human bodies to prevent their escape. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the archons placing "counterfeit spirits" alongside the divine spark to keep it confused and obedient. Job experiences this archontic surveillance with growing horror: "Will you not look away from me, nor leave me alone till I swallow my spit?" (7:19). The Demiurge's attention is not loving care but custodial vigilance — the warden watching his prisoner.

Job's wish for God to look away (7:19) inverts the normal religious desire for divine attention. In Gnostic thought, this makes perfect sense: the Demiurge's gaze is a prison beam, not a searchlight of love. The true God — the Monad, the Bythos, the unknowable depth beyond the Demiurge's cosmos — does not "look at" the created world in the surveillance mode Job describes. The true divine attention is *gnosis* itself: the inward illumination that the Pleroma sends through emanation, not the external observation of a cosmic overseer. Job's rejection of the Demiurge's gaze is, unknowingly, a step toward the true God's liberating self-disclosure.
