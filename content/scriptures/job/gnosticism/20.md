Zophar's imagery of swallowed riches being disgorged (20:15) — "He swallows down riches and vomits them up again; God casts them out of his belly" — provides a vivid illustration of the Demiurge's economy of reward and punishment. In Gnostic analysis, the material world's entire system of acquisition and loss operates as a mechanism of archontic control. The desire for material goods keeps the divine sparks focused on the Demiurge's realm rather than seeking their true home in the Pleroma. Wealth gained and lost, consumed and disgorged — this is the cyclical trap of *samsara* that the Gnostic *gnosis* breaks.

The description of wealth-as-poison (20:12-14) — sweetness that turns to venom — resonates with the Gnostic understanding of material pleasure as a spiritual narcotic. The *Apocryphon of John* describes how the archons created material pleasures to distract the divine sparks from their imprisonment. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 87) warns: "Wretched is the body that is dependent upon a body, and wretched is the soul that is dependent upon these two." Zophar correctly identifies the self-destructive nature of material acquisition but fails to recognize that the entire system — not just its abuses — is the problem. The Gnostic insight transcends Zophar's moral reform by questioning the framework itself.

Zophar's final verdict — "this is the wicked man's portion from God" (20:29) — is a statement about the Demiurge's retributive system that Gnostic theology neither disputes nor celebrates. The Demiurge does indeed operate a reward-and-punishment economy, and Zophar describes it accurately. The Gnostic response is not to deny the system's reality but to transcend it through *gnosis*. The pneumatic who achieves liberation is no longer subject to the Demiurge's economy of portions and punishments — not because the system ceases to exist but because the pneumatic has exited it entirely, returning to the Pleroma where such categories do not apply.
