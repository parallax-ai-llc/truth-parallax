## The Pneumatic's Defiance

Job's oath — "Till I die, I will not deny my integrity" (27:5) — is read in Gnostic tradition as the ultimate pneumatic declaration: the refusal to accept the Demiurge's guilt-system. The divine spark within Job recognizes its own purity, regardless of the suffering imposed by the material world's ruler. This is not arrogance but gnosis — the knowledge that one's true identity is not defined by the cosmic powers' judgment.

The paradox of Job swearing by the God who has "denied me justice" (27:2) is resolved in Gnostic reading by distinguishing between the two "Gods" — the Demiurge who denies justice (because his system is unjust) and the true God by whom oaths have genuine meaning. Job instinctively appeals to a higher authority than the one tormenting him, even though he cannot yet name this authority. This is the Gnostic pre-awakening: the intuition that the cosmic ruler is not the ultimate reality.

The description of the wicked's fate (27:13-23) is read by some Gnostic interpreters as describing the fate of the *hylic* (material) person — one who is entirely identified with the Demiurge's world and has no divine spark to survive its dissolution. The pneumatic, by contrast, endures suffering in this world but possesses an imperishable inner life that transcends all material destruction.
