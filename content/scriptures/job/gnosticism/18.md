Bildad's sustained portrait of the wicked person's destruction (18:5-21) reads in Gnostic interpretation as a catalog of archontic punishments — the Demiurge's enforcement mechanisms described by one of his faithful agents. The imagery of nets, traps, and snares (18:8-10) is particularly resonant: the *Pistis Sophia* describes the archons as setting traps for descending and ascending souls, placing obstacles in the path of those who attempt to escape the material realm. Bildad's description of the wicked person caught in nets is, unknowingly, a description of every soul trapped in the Demiurge's cosmos — not just the wicked but the righteous as well.

The "king of terrors" (18:14) — death personified — is identified in Gnostic thought with the chief archon or with one of the archontic powers that governs the transition between material life and what follows. In the *Pistis Sophia*, the soul encounters various archontic gatekeepers after death who attempt to prevent its ascent. Death is not liberation but another stage of archontic processing — unless the soul possesses the *gnosis* (knowledge of the divine names, seals, and passwords) needed to pass through the archontic checkpoints. Bildad's "king of terrors" is one of these gatekeepers, and his terror is real for those who lack *gnosis*.

Bildad's claim that the wicked person "has no name" and "no posterity" (18:17, 19) touches on the Gnostic concern with spiritual lineage as opposed to biological lineage. The pneumatic's true lineage is not from Adam (the Demiurge's creation) but from the Pleroma — from Sophia and ultimately from the Monad. Biological extinction is irrelevant to the pneumatic because their genuine identity is not rooted in the material world. Bildad's threats about the loss of earthly name and progeny carry no weight for the Gnostic who has recognized that true identity and true lineage transcend the Demiurge's domain entirely.
