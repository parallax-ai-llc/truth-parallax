The attack on Job's body (2:7) carries particular significance in Gnostic theology, which views the material body as a prison for the divine spark. The Gnostic principle of *soma-sema* (the body as a tomb) — attested in Plato and adopted by many Gnostic schools — means that physical suffering represents an intensification of the soul's already-imprisoned condition. Job's boils are not merely affliction but a revelation of the body's fundamental inadequacy as a container for spiritual reality. The Demiurge's attack on Job's flesh exposes what Gnostic theology already assumes: that the material body is a site of suffering by its very nature.

Job's wife's counsel to "curse God and die" (2:9) is read in some Gnostic traditions as unwitting wisdom — a call to reject the Demiurge's authority and transcend the material realm through death. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the world is a realm of illusion, and death can be understood as liberation rather than punishment. However, most Gnostic interpreters would note that cursing the Demiurge without possessing *gnosis* achieves nothing — mere rebellion without knowledge leads only to another form of bondage. Job's wife speaks more truly than she knows, but her counsel lacks the *gnosis* that would give it transformative power.

The friends' arrival and silence (2:11-13) is read Gnostically as the arrival of those who represent conventional wisdom — the *psychikoi* (soul-people) who possess moral knowledge but lack the *pneumatic* (spiritual) insight that would allow them to perceive the true nature of Job's situation. In the Valentinian tripartite anthropology (hylic/psychic/pneumatic), the friends are psychics: they have genuine religious insight but are limited by their allegiance to the Demiurge's system. Their theology, while internally consistent, operates within a framework that the pneumatic (spirit-person) must ultimately transcend.
