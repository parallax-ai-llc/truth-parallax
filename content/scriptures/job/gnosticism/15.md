Eliphaz's accusation that Job undermines "the fear of God" (15:4) reveals the mechanism by which the Demiurge's system maintains control. In Gnostic analysis, fear is the primary tool of archontic governance. The *Apocryphon of John* describes how the archons use fear, ignorance, and forgetfulness to keep the divine sparks imprisoned. Eliphaz is, consciously or not, an enforcer of this fear-based system: he threatens Job with divine consequences for questioning the established theological order. The Gnostic *pneumatic* must overcome precisely this fear to achieve liberation — a process Job is undergoing through his defiant questioning.

Eliphaz's claim that "the heavens are not pure in his sight" (15:15) inadvertently supports the Gnostic cosmology. If even the heavens — the domain of the Demiurge and his archons — are impure, then the entire cosmic structure is flawed. Gnostic theology would agree emphatically: the heavens, which the Demiurge created and populated with archons, are as corrupt as the earth below. The Valentinian system describes the heavens as archontic spheres through which the soul must ascend, encountering resistance and deception at each level. Eliphaz's statement about celestial impurity confirms, unwittingly, the Gnostic critique of the entire cosmic order.

The extended description of the wicked person's torment (15:20-35) functions in Gnostic reading as archontic propaganda — threats designed to enforce compliance. The Demiurge's system relies on the promise of reward and the threat of punishment to keep its subjects obedient. Eliphaz recites these threats with the conviction of a true believer, but the pneumatic recognizes them as the Demiurge's control mechanisms. The Gnostic *Soter* liberates precisely by revealing that these threats are hollow: the Demiurge's power over the divine spark is limited by the true God's ultimate authority, even though the Demiurge does not know this.
