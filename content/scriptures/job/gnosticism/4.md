Eliphaz represents, in Gnostic reading, the voice of conventional religious wisdom operating within the Demiurge's system. His retributive theology (4:7-8) — the innocent do not perish, the wicked reap what they sow — accurately describes how the Demiurge's cosmos is *supposed* to work. The Gnostic insight is that this system is itself unjust: it was designed by an ignorant or malevolent creator, and its "justice" is merely the mechanical operation of archontic law (*heimarmene*, fate) rather than genuine divine equity. Eliphaz faithfully reports the Demiurge's rules, but these rules are part of the problem, not the solution.

The nocturnal vision (4:12-16) — a spirit passing before Eliphaz's face — is interpreted in Gnostic tradition as a communication from an archon rather than from the true God. The Gnostic texts describe various archontic beings who communicate through dreams and visions to reinforce the Demiurge's authority. The *Testimony of Truth* warns against visions that produce fear rather than *gnosis*, and Eliphaz's trembling response (4:14-15) suggests an archontic rather than truly divine source. The content of the vision — "Can mortal man be in the right before God?" (4:17) — reinforces the Demiurge's control by convincing humans of their utter worthlessness before the creator.

The assertion that even God's angels are charged with error (4:18) takes on particular significance in Gnostic cosmology, where the angelic beings associated with the Demiurge (the archons) are indeed flawed — they are ignorant of the Pleroma and serve a flawed creator. Eliphaz speaks more truly than he knows: the heavenly beings in the Demiurge's court are indeed untrustworthy. But Eliphaz draws the wrong conclusion, using angelic imperfection to diminish human worth rather than to question the system that produced such flawed spiritual authorities.
