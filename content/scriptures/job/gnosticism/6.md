Job's anguished response to Eliphaz takes on particular significance in Gnostic interpretation as the voice of the *pneumatic* (spirit-bearing) individual discovering that the religious community cannot understand his experience. The divine spark within Job is in agony — not merely physical agony but the deeper suffering of spiritual alienation. The Gnostic experience of being a stranger in the material world (*xenos*, alien) is precisely what Job describes: he is surrounded by people who share his language and culture but cannot perceive the spiritual reality of his situation.

The wadi metaphor (6:15-20) — friends who are like seasonal streams that vanish in the heat — captures the Gnostic experience of discovering that conventional religious community cannot sustain the pneumatic's spiritual journey. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 49) declares: "Blessed are the solitary and elect, for you will find the kingdom." The pneumatic's path often leads through isolation, as the communities built on *psychic* (faith-based) religion prove unable to accompany the one who has begun to see through the Demiurge's system. Job's friends, like the wadi, offer plenty when nothing is at stake but disappear when genuine spiritual crisis arrives.

Job's insistence that his complaint is rational (6:24-30) reflects the Gnostic conviction that genuine spiritual knowledge is not irrational but super-rational — it does not abandon reason but transcends it. The *pneumatic* does not reject logic; rather, he sees that the logic of the Demiurge's system, while internally consistent, is based on false premises. Job's challenge to his friends — "make me understand how I have gone astray" (6:24) — is a demand for evidence that they cannot produce, because the system they defend cannot account for the anomaly Job represents: a genuinely righteous person suffering within a supposedly just cosmos.
