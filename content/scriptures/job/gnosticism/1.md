The Gnostic reading of Job 1 begins with a fundamental reorientation: the God who presides over the heavenly council and authorizes Satan's attack on Job is identified not with the true, transcendent God (the *Monad* or *Bythos*) but with the Demiurge — the lower creator deity who fashioned the material world in ignorance of the Pleroma (the fullness of divine reality). In Valentinian and Sethian Gnostic systems, the Demiurge believes himself to be the supreme God ("I am a jealous God; there is no other beside me"), but this very claim reveals his ignorance. The heavenly council of Job 1:6 is thus the court of the Demiurge and his archons (rulers), not the true divine assembly.

Satan's role in this framework is ambiguous. Some Gnostic readings identify Satan as an archon who operates within the Demiurge's system of control, while others — particularly in the Ophite and Cainite traditions — view the serpent/adversary figure more positively, as one who challenges the Demiurge's authority and potentially liberates the divine sparks trapped in matter. The "wager" between God and Satan (1:8-12) is read as an intra-archontic power struggle rather than a genuine test of faith. Job, who possesses the divine spark (*pneuma*) within him, is caught between archontic forces that neither understand nor care about his true spiritual nature.

Job's patient response (1:21) — "blessed be the name of the LORD" — is evaluated ambiguously in Gnostic interpretation. Some Gnostic readers see Job's acceptance as evidence that he has not yet awakened to *gnosis* (saving knowledge): he still attributes his suffering to the "LORD" (the Demiurge) and accepts the Demiurge's authority as legitimate. The deeper journey of the book, in Gnostic reading, will involve Job's progressive disillusionment with the Demiurge's justice system, which prepares him for an encounter with the true divine reality that transcends the Demiurge's domain.
