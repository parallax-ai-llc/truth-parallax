Job 19:25-27 represents, in Gnostic reading, the supreme moment of pneumatic breakthrough within the book — the instant when the divine spark within Job pierces through the Demiurge's veil and glimpses the true divine reality. "I know that my Redeemer lives" is not a statement about the Demiurge (who is Job's oppressor, not his redeemer) but about the *Soter* — the divine emissary from the Pleroma whose existence Job intuits even though he has not yet received the fullness of *gnosis*. The *goel* (redeemer) is the Pleromatic Christ who will "at the last stand upon the earth" to vindicate the imprisoned sparks.

The affirmation "in my flesh I shall see God" (19:26) is reinterpreted in Gnostic theology. Most Gnostic systems would not affirm bodily resurrection — the flesh is the prison, not the vehicle of salvation. However, the phrase can be read as describing a visionary experience that occurs while still embodied: the pneumatic's reception of *gnosis* through a visionary theophany. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the resurrection is not a future event but a present spiritual transformation: "Those who say they will die first and then rise are in error. If they do not first receive the resurrection while they live, when they die they will receive nothing." Job's vision of God "in my flesh" describes the *gnosis* experience itself — divine encounter within the present life.

The catalog of suffering preceding this declaration (19:7-22) is read Gnostically as the necessary stripping away of all material supports that enables pneumatic awakening. The *Hymn of the Pearl* describes the prince removing his "heavy and unclean garment" as he awakens to his true identity. Job has been stripped of wealth, family, health, social standing, and now even theological certainty — every material and psychic support has been removed. It is precisely this radical stripping that creates the conditions for the divine spark to pierce through to the Pleromatic reality. The Gnostic path runs through loss, not around it.
