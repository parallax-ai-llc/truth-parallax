Bildad's defense of divine justice (8:3) — "Does God pervert justice?" — is answered by Gnostic theology with a resounding yes, though with the crucial qualification that the "God" in question is the Demiurge, not the true God. The Demiurge's justice (*heimarmene*, cosmic fate) operates mechanistically: reward for obedience, punishment for disobedience. But this system is itself a perversion of the true divine reality, which operates through *gnosis*, grace, and the restoration of the divine sparks to the Pleroma. Bildad correctly describes the Demiurge's rules but mistakes those rules for genuine cosmic justice.

The suggestion that Job's children died for their sin (8:4) exemplifies the cruelty of the archontic system. In Gnostic reading, the death of innocents is evidence against the Demiurge's justice, not for it. The *Testimony of Truth* argues that the biblical God's actions — from the expulsion from Eden to the Flood to the destruction of Sodom — reveal a deity who is jealous, wrathful, and unjust. Bildad's attempt to rationalize the death of Job's children as divine justice inadvertently supports the Gnostic case that this "God" is not worthy of worship.

Bildad's botanical metaphors — papyrus withering without water (8:11-13) — are read Gnostically as images of the divine spark's condition in the material world. The spark, separated from the waters of the Pleroma (the true divine reality), withers in the dry environment of the Demiurge's cosmos. The image is more apt than Bildad intends: it is not Job who withers through wickedness but the divine spark that withers through its imprisonment in matter. The solution is not moral reform (as Bildad suggests) but reconnection with the Pleromatic source — the living water that the *Gospel of John* associates with Christ and that Gnostic texts identify with *gnosis* itself.
