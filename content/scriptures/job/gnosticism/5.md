Eliphaz's counsel to accept divine discipline (5:17) is read in Gnostic tradition as an exhortation to submit to the Demiurge's system — precisely the disposition that *gnosis* liberates one from. The Gnostic salvation narrative is fundamentally about awakening from the sleep imposed by the archons and recognizing that the apparent "God" of this world is not the true God. Eliphaz urges Job to remain asleep, to accept the Demiurge's discipline as genuine divine correction, and to trust in the system's ultimate fairness. From a Gnostic perspective, this is the counsel of the *psychikoi* — well-meaning but spiritually limited.

The promise of material restoration (5:19-26) — protection from famine, safety from wild beasts, numerous descendants, death in "ripe old age" — perfectly illustrates the Demiurge's economy of reward. In Gnostic theology, material blessings are the Demiurge's currency, used to maintain allegiance to his system. The *Hypostasis of the Archons* describes how the archons use material pleasures and threats to keep the divine sparks trapped in matter. Eliphaz's promise of earthly prosperity is thus a spiritual trap: it redirects attention from the true goal (return to the Pleroma) toward the false goal (comfort within the material prison).

Eliphaz's concluding certainty — "this we have searched out; it is true" (5:27) — exemplifies the *psychic* confusion of systematic theology with genuine knowledge. Gnostic epistemology distinguishes between *pistis* (belief or faith, the *psychic* mode of knowing) and *gnosis* (direct spiritual knowledge, the *pneumatic* mode). Eliphaz operates entirely within *pistis* — he believes in the Demiurge's system and teaches it faithfully. But *pistis* without *gnosis* is ultimately a form of ignorance, however sincerely held. The pneumatic must move beyond belief in the system to knowledge of the reality behind it.
