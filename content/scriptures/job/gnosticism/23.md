## The Longing for the True God

Job's desperate search for God — "If only I knew where to find him" (23:3) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as the cry of the divine spark seeking its origin in the Pleroma. The God Job seeks is not the Demiurge (who is all too present in the suffering he inflicts) but the hidden, true God beyond the material cosmos. The *Gospel of Truth* (NHC I,3) describes a similar longing: the Aeons searching for the Father from whom they originated.

Job's searching in every direction — forward, backward, left, right (23:8-9) — without finding God dramatizes the Gnostic condition of alienation. The divine spark, trapped in matter, cannot find its way home through spatial movement. The true God is not located in any direction within the cosmos but transcends the cosmos entirely. Gnostic texts like the *Hymn of the Pearl* (Acts of Thomas 108-113) tell of the soul's forgetting and reawakening to its divine origin — Job is in the stage of desperate searching that precedes the moment of gnosis.

Job's confidence that he will emerge "as gold" (23:10) is read Gnostically as an unconscious prophecy: the divine spark within him is indeed imperishable, and no amount of material suffering can destroy it. The gold is not Job's moral character but his pneumatic nature — the indestructible divine element planted by the true God before the Demiurge shaped the material world. Suffering refines by separating the gold (spirit) from the dross (matter).
