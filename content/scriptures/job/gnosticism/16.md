Job's dismissal of his friends as "miserable comforters" (16:2) captures the pneumatic's disillusionment with conventional religious community. The Gnostic experience of spiritual isolation — being surrounded by the religiously devout who nevertheless lack *gnosis* — is one of the tradition's most consistent themes. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 28) records Jesus saying: "I stood in the midst of the world, and I appeared to them in flesh. I found all of them intoxicated; I found none of them thirsty." Job's friends are "intoxicated" by the Demiurge's theology: they consume it freely but it leaves them unable to perceive the spiritual reality before their eyes.

The violent imagery of divine assault (16:7-14) — God tearing, gnashing teeth, setting Job as a target — provides the Gnostic interpreter with evidence for the Demiurge's hostile nature. These descriptions do not require reinterpretation or apology in Gnostic theology; they accurately describe what the Demiurge does. The *Apocryphon of John* and the *Hypostasis of the Archons* consistently portray the Demiurge as violent, jealous, and destructive. Job's experience of divine hostility is not a subjective misperception but an accurate report of the Demiurge's behavior toward the pneumatic who begins to question the archontic order.

The declaration of a "witness in heaven" (16:19) is read in Gnostic Christology as an intuition of the *Soter* — the divine emissary from the Pleroma who descends to liberate the imprisoned sparks. Valentinian theology teaches that the *Soter* exists in the Pleroma prior to his descent and that the pneumatic's cry for help is itself evidence of the Pleromatic connection that the archons cannot sever. Job's witness is not the Demiurge (who is Job's accuser, not his advocate) but a being from beyond the Demiurge's cosmos — the true divine presence that the Gnostic tradition identifies with the Christ of the Pleroma.
