## Another Archonic Voice

Gnostic interpretation is generally hostile to Elihu, reading him as another voice of the Demiurgic system — a new variant of the same failed theology. His anger at both Job and the friends (32:2-5) represents the system's frustration when its categories are challenged. Where the three friends used age and tradition, Elihu claims direct spiritual inspiration (32:8) — but in Gnostic reading, the "spirit" he invokes is not the Spirit of the true God but the *pneuma* of the Demiurge.

Elihu's claim to speak with irresistible urgency — "I am full of words, and the spirit within me compels me" (32:18) — resembles the false inspiration that Gnostic texts attribute to the archons. The *Second Treatise of the Great Seth* (NHC VII,2) warns against those who speak with seeming authority but serve the cosmic powers. Elihu's prolixity contrasts sharply with authentic gnosis, which comes in a flash of recognition rather than a torrent of words.

Yet even the Gnostic reader can find significance in Elihu's role: his failure, like the friends' failure, demonstrates that no voice within the cosmic system can provide the answer Job needs. Human wisdom, whether old or young, whether traditional or inspired, cannot break through the archonic veil. Only direct revelation from beyond the system — which in the book comes as the divine voice from the whirlwind — can shatter the prison of ignorance.
