## The Demiurge's Indifference

Elihu's question — does human righteousness affect God at all? (35:6-7) — receives a startling answer in Gnostic theology: the Demiurge is indeed largely indifferent to human moral life. He demands worship and obedience not because he cares about human flourishing but because he feeds on the energy of worship. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the archons as parasitic powers who sustain themselves through human ignorance and devotion.

By contrast, the true God — the unknown Father of the Pleroma — is genuinely affected by the plight of the divine sparks. The *Gospel of Truth* describes the Father's compassion for the Aeons who have lost their way, and the sending of the Savior (Christ/Logos) is motivated by love, not self-interest. The distinction between the Demiurge's indifference and the true God's compassion is precisely the knowledge that constitutes gnosis.

Elihu's explanation for unanswered prayer — that people cry out in distress without truly seeking God (35:10) — contains, in Gnostic reading, an accidental truth: most people pray to the Demiurge, who cannot or will not answer. True prayer — the soul's cry for liberation directed toward the unknown God — is always heard, but it is heard beyond the cosmic veil. The challenge is not the adequacy of the prayer but the identity of the deity addressed.
