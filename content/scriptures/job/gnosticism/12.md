Job's sarcastic dismissal of his friends (12:2) — "No doubt you are the people, and wisdom will die with you" — captures the Gnostic pneumatic's frustration with those who mistake conventional religious knowledge for genuine spiritual insight. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 3) teaches: "If your leaders say to you, 'Look, the kingdom is in the sky,' then the birds of the sky will precede you." Gnostic tradition consistently warns against accepting the authority of those whose knowledge does not extend beyond the Demiurge's system. Job's sarcasm reflects the lonely clarity of the pneumatic who sees through what others accept uncritically.

Job's appeal to creation (12:7-10) — "Ask the beasts, and they will teach you" — is given a Gnostic twist: nature itself bears witness to the Demiurge's chaotic sovereignty. The natural world's cycles of predation, decay, and death are not evidence of wise providence but of the material world's fundamentally disordered condition. The *On the Origin of the World* describes the Demiurge's creation as inherently unstable, subject to entropy and violence. When Job observes that "in his hand is the life of every living thing" (12:10), Gnostic readers hear not reassurance but a description of archontic captivity — every living being is held in the Demiurge's grip.

The portrait of divine sovereignty as disruption (12:14-25) — tearing down, withholding waters, overthrowing, stripping, pouring contempt — aligns remarkably well with the Gnostic characterization of the Demiurge. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the Demiurge as Yaldabaoth, a being characterized by ignorance and power — a dangerous combination that produces exactly the kind of arbitrary, destructive sovereignty Job describes. Job's hymn to divine power is, in Gnostic reading, an indictment: this is not the behavior of the true God but the conduct of a cosmic tyrant whose power operates without wisdom or love.
