## Defending the Indefensible

Elihu's passionate defense of divine justice — "it is unthinkable that God would do wrong" (34:10) — is precisely the claim that Gnostic theology challenges most directly. The Demiurge does wrong constantly — not because he is purely evil (in most Gnostic systems) but because he is ignorant of the true divine order. His justice is the justice of an inferior deity operating with limited knowledge and unlimited power, a combination that produces the very suffering Job endures.

Elihu's argument that God sustains all life (34:14-15) is true in the Gnostic framework but carries a different valence: the Demiurge sustains material existence, but material existence is itself the problem. Sustaining the prison is not an act of benevolence toward the prisoners. The *Tripartite Tractate* (NHC I,5) describes the material world as sustained by the Demiurge's will, but this sustenance serves his purposes, not the pneumatic's liberation.

Elihu's accusation that Job "adds rebellion to his sin" (34:37) reveals the archonic mentality: any questioning of the system is defined as rebellion. Gnostic texts celebrate precisely this "rebellion" — the pneumatic's refusal to accept the Demiurge's authority as ultimate. The *Testimony of Truth* reinterprets the Eden narrative so that the serpent (who encourages questioning) is the liberator and the jealous God (who forbids knowledge) is the oppressor. Job's questioning, in this light, is not rebellion but awakening.
