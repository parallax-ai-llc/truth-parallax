## The Wild Kingdom

God's survey of the animal kingdom in Job 39 has been read in Jewish tradition through the lens of the *Perek Shirah* (Chapter of Song) — an ancient text that assigns a verse of praise to each creature. The mountain goats, wild donkeys, ostriches, and eagles of Job 39 are all singing their particular song of praise to the Creator, whether humans hear it or not. The world is a chorus of divine praise that extends far beyond human awareness.

The descriptions of creatures that serve no human purpose — the wild donkey who laughs at the city (39:7), the ostrich who leaves her eggs in the dust (39:14) — challenge anthropocentric readings of creation. The Talmud (Eruvin 100b) teaches that humans can learn ethical lessons from animals: modesty from the cat, fidelity from the dove, industry from the ant. But Job 39 goes further — these creatures exist not primarily as human teachers but as independent expressions of divine creativity.

Jewish ecological ethics draws on this chapter's vision of creation as intrinsically valuable. The principle of *bal tashchit* (do not destroy — Deuteronomy 20:19) extends the Torah's concern from fruit trees to all of creation. If God cares for the eagle's nest on inaccessible crags (39:27-28) and the wild ox's untamable strength (39:9-12), then creation's purpose includes dimensions that human utility alone cannot measure.
