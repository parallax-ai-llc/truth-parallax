## Overview

In Judaism, Job (*Iyov*) is part of the Ketuvim (Writings), the third section of the Tanakh. The Talmud records extensive debate about the book — whether Job was a historical figure or a parable (Bava Batra 15a-b), whether he was Jewish or a righteous Gentile, and whether his suffering was deserved or undeserved. Jewish interpretation engages deeply with the problem of *tzaddik v'ra lo* — the righteous person who suffers.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Talmudic Debates
The Talmud (Bava Batra 15a-16b) preserves remarkable discussions:
- **Authorship**: Some attribute the book to Moses; others to Job himself
- **Historicity**: Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says Job never existed — the book is a parable (*mashal*)
- **Dating**: Opinions range from the time of Abraham to the Persian period
- **Job's identity**: Some say he was Jewish; others say he was a righteous Gentile from Uz

### Midrashic Elaboration
Midrashic sources expand the narrative extensively, placing Job among Pharaoh's advisors alongside Jethro and Balaam. When Pharaoh planned to enslave the Israelites, Jethro opposed and fled, Balaam supported the plan, and Job remained silent — his suffering is sometimes explained as punishment for this silence.

### Medieval Commentators
- **Rashi**: Concise, peshat-oriented commentary
- **Ralbag (Gersonides)**: Philosophical analysis of providence and theodicy
- **Ramban (Nachmanides)**: References Job in his *Sha'ar HaGemul* on reward and punishment
- **Maimonides**: Extensive treatment in *Guide for the Perplexed* III:22-23

## Theological Emphases

- **Theodicy**: The central Jewish theological problem — why do the righteous suffer?
- **The Satan as prosecutor**: Not a fallen angel but a member of the heavenly court with a defined role
- **Limits of human wisdom**: "The fear of the LORD, that is wisdom" (28:28)
- **Divine justice and mystery**: God's ways are ultimately beyond human comprehension
- **No doctrine of original sin**: Job's suffering is not attributed to a fallen human nature

## Maimonides on Job

In the *Guide for the Perplexed* (III:22-23), Maimonides offers an influential philosophical reading. He identifies each character with a different theory of providence:
- **Job**: Initially holds a naive view of divine justice
- **Eliphaz**: Represents the view of the Torah (sin causes suffering)
- **Bildad**: Represents the Mu'tazilite view (God compensates in the afterlife)
- **Zophar**: Represents the Ash'arite view (God's will is inscrutable)
- **Elihu**: Approaches the truth — suffering can serve as instruction
- **God's speech**: Reveals that true providence requires knowledge humans cannot attain
