## Elihu Exalts God's Majesty

Elihu's assertion that God "delivers the afflicted by their affliction" (36:15) aligns with an important strand of rabbinic theology. The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 2:2) teaches that God tested Moses through shepherding before entrusting him with Israel — suffering and humiliation served as preparation for greater responsibility. This pedagogical understanding of suffering provides a framework for reading Job's ordeal, though the framework remains incomplete.

The chapter's turn toward natural theology (36:26-33) connects to the Jewish blessing tradition. The *berakhot* (blessings) recited upon witnessing natural phenomena — thunder, lightning, mountains, the sea — express the conviction that nature is not secular but sacred, a continuous revelation of divine power. Elihu's attention to the storm anticipates this blessing practice: the proper response to nature's grandeur is not scientific explanation alone but *berakhah* (blessing).

Elihu's declaration that God is "exalted in his power" (36:22) resonates with the *Kedushah* prayer in the daily liturgy, where God is praised as "holy" and "exalted" beyond all creation. Yet Jewish liturgy always pairs God's exaltation with His nearness: the same God who is praised by angels "in the heights" also "fills the whole earth with His glory." Elihu captures the exaltation but misses the nearness — a theological imbalance that the divine speeches will correct.
