## Job's Remembrance of Former Glory

Job's recollection of his former life engages the rabbinic concept of *tzedakah* (righteousness expressed through justice and charity). His portrait of himself as one who rescued the poor, helped the fatherless, and made the widow's heart sing (29:12-13) exemplifies the *tzaddik* (righteous person) as defined in Jewish tradition. The Talmud (Sukkah 49b) teaches that *tzedakah* is greater even than all the sacrifices, and Job's self-description is that of a man who practiced *tzedakah* comprehensively.

Rashi draws attention to Job's claim that he "put on righteousness as a garment" and "justice was my robe and turban" (29:14). This imagery suggests that ethical behavior was not occasional but constitutive of Job's identity — he wore justice the way a priest wears sacred vestments. In rabbinic thought, this exemplifies the ideal of *middot* (character traits) becoming second nature through habitual practice.

The communal dimension of Job's former glory — elders rising, princes silent, the community looking to him as leader (29:7-10, 21-25) — reflects the Jewish concept of *kavod* (honor) that accrues to a person of genuine merit. The Mishnah (Avot 4:1) asks "Who is honored?" and answers "One who honors others." Job's honor was not self-aggrandizement but the natural community response to a life of active justice and compassion.
