## Leviathan

Leviathan occupies a major place in Jewish aggadic tradition. The Talmud (Bava Batra 74b-75a) describes Leviathan as the great sea creature that God will slaughter for the messianic banquet, fashioning a sukkah (booth) from its skin for the righteous. This imagery transforms Leviathan from a terror into a promise: the forces of chaos will not merely be subdued but will be redeemed and repurposed for the joy of the world to come.

The midrashic tradition elaborates: God originally created a male and female Leviathan, but destroyed the female lest they reproduce and overwhelm creation (Bava Batra 74b). This aggadah reveals rabbinic awareness that the forces of chaos, if unchecked, would annihilate the created order. Only God's sovereign power keeps chaos within bounds — a theological point that Job 41 powerfully reinforces. Leviathan is "king over all that are proud" (41:34), but God is king over Leviathan.

The Zohar reads Leviathan as symbolizing the *sitra achra* (the "other side" — the dimension of evil in Kabbalistic thought) that will be overcome in the messianic age. The mystical tradition thus connects Job 41 to the deepest hopes of Jewish eschatology: the world contains forces of terrifying power and apparent evil, but they are not ultimate. God's sovereignty extends over all of them, and the age to come will reveal their final subjugation and transformation.
