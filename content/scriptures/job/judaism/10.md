Job's meditation on divine craftsmanship (10:8-12) — "you poured me out like milk and curdled me like cheese, clothed me with skin and flesh" — provides imagery that rabbinic commentary uses to discuss embryology and the formation of the human being. The Talmud (Niddah 31a) describes the formation of the embryo in terms that parallel Job's language, attributing to God the detailed work of fashioning each person individually. The blessing *asher yatzar* (recited after using the bathroom) praises God for the complexity of the human body, reflecting the same wonder at divine craftsmanship that Job expresses here, though Job's wonder has turned to accusation.

The paradox of a Creator who destroys His own creation (10:8-17) engages a tension the Midrash addresses through the concept of God's internal deliberation. Genesis Rabbah 8:5 describes God consulting with the angels before creating humanity: the angels of *chesed* (love) and *tzedek* (justice) favored creation, while *emet* (truth) and *shalom* (peace) opposed it. God threw *emet* to the ground and created humanity anyway. Job's complaint that God created him only to destroy him echoes the angels' objection — and the Midrash's resolution (that God values *chesed* above *emet*) provides an indirect answer to Job's accusation.

Job's description of Sheol (10:21-22) reflects the biblical concept of the underworld before the development of the rabbinic doctrine of *olam ha-ba* (the World to Come) and *Gehinnom* (the place of purification). Maimonides (Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Teshuvah 8) argued that the ultimate reward of the righteous is the spiritual bliss of the World to Come, while Nachmanides insisted on bodily resurrection. Job's vision of Sheol as undifferentiated darkness represents the earliest stratum of Jewish thought about death, which later tradition would transform dramatically.
