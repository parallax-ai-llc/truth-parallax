## Eliphaz's Final Accusation

Rabbinic tradition treats Eliphaz's final speech with particular severity. The Talmud (Bava Batra 16a) debates whether Eliphaz spoke from prophecy or from presumption, and the consensus leans toward the latter. His specific accusations — that Job oppressed widows, withheld bread, and stripped the naked (22:6-9) — are understood as fabrications that violate the prohibition against bearing false witness (*lo ta'aneh*). Rashi reads Eliphaz as projecting his own moral anxiety onto Job.

The Midrash notes the irony that Eliphaz's theological premise — "Can a man be profitable to God?" (22:2) — contradicts a fundamental principle of Jewish theology. The *midrash* on Psalms teaches that God takes genuine delight in Israel's obedience and is genuinely grieved by disobedience. While God is self-sufficient, the covenant relationship is real, not merely instrumental. Eliphaz's deistic theology misrepresents the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

Eliphaz's call to repentance (22:21-30), though built on false premises, contains phrases that entered Jewish liturgical consciousness. The expression "receive Torah from His mouth" (22:22) echoes in the daily prayer liturgy, and the promise that the penitent will "decree a thing and it shall be established" (22:28) resonates with rabbinic teachings about the power of righteous prayer. Jewish interpretive tradition thus salvages genuine wisdom even from flawed speakers.
