## Job's Oath of Integrity

Job's oath — "Till I die, I will not deny my integrity (*tummati*)" (27:5) — is deeply significant in Jewish moral theology. The Hebrew *tummah* (integrity, wholeness) is the same quality attributed to Job in the prologue (1:1) and is a central virtue in Jewish ethics. The Mishnah (Avot 2:13) teaches that a "good heart" — moral integrity — is the single most important human quality. Job's refusal to compromise this integrity, even under extreme pressure, makes him a model for Jewish ethical life.

The paradox of Job swearing by God while accusing God of injustice (27:2) has generated extensive rabbinic commentary. Rashi reads this as evidence of Job's fundamental faith: even in his most bitter moments, he does not deny God's existence or sovereignty. He disputes God's justice in his particular case while affirming God as the ultimate source of truth by which oaths are sworn. This tension — loyalty to God combined with honest complaint — is characteristic of Jewish prayer.

The description of the wicked's fate (27:13-23) is read by some rabbinic commentators as Job quoting the friends' position back to them, while others see it as Job's genuine affirmation that divine justice is real, even if its timing is mysterious. Ramban (Nachmanides) suggested that Job distinguishes between the wicked who prosper temporarily and the righteous who suffer temporarily — both will eventually receive their due, but the timeframe belongs to God alone.
