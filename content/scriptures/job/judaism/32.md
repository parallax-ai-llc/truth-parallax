## Elihu's Angry Entrance

Rabbinic opinion on Elihu is notably divided. The Talmud (Bava Batra 16b) identifies him with Balaam, the pagan prophet, while other traditions see him more positively. The Midrash suggests that Elihu was angry because both sides had failed: the friends had dishonored God by misrepresenting His justice, and Job had honored himself at God's expense. This balanced anger suggests a more nuanced perspective than either party possessed.

Elihu's claim that wisdom is not the monopoly of the aged (32:9) but comes from "the spirit in a person" (32:8) engages the rabbinic tension between the authority of tradition (*mesorah*) and fresh insight (*chiddush*). The Talmud values both: Torah study requires learning from one's teacher, but the student who never offers a novel interpretation has not fully engaged with Torah. Elihu represents the *chiddush* principle — the insistence that truth can emerge from unexpected sources.

Yet rabbinic tradition also recognizes the danger of Elihu's excessive self-confidence. The Mishnah (Avot 4:7) warns against claiming honor for oneself, and Elihu's elaborate self-justification (32:6-22) — apologizing for his youth while simultaneously asserting his superiority — displays the very pride the tradition warns against. The rabbis noted that God never responds to Elihu directly, a silence that may itself constitute a judgment.
