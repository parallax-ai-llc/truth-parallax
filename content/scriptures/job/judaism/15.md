Eliphaz's accusation that Job undermines "the fear of God" (*yirat Elohim*, 15:4) misapplies a concept central to Jewish ethics. *Yirat shamayim* (fear of heaven) is not mere terror but reverential awe that motivates ethical behavior. The Talmud (Shabbat 31b) teaches that *yirat shamayim* is the treasury in which all other merits are stored: without it, nothing else matters. But the same tradition insists that authentic fear of God produces humility and compassion, not the harsh judgmentalism Eliphaz displays. His accusation reveals his own deficiency in the very virtue he invokes.

Eliphaz's rhetorical questions — "Were you born before Adam? Were you brought forth before the hills?" (cf. 15:7) — sarcastically deny Job any special claim to wisdom. Jewish tradition answers this implicit question about the source of wisdom through the concept of Torah study as an equalizing force. The Mishnah (Avot 4:1) defines the wise person as "one who learns from every person," not one who claims chronological or experiential priority. Eliphaz's appeal to seniority and ancestral tradition contradicts this democratic vision of wisdom, which values insight wherever it appears.

The description of the wicked person's fate (15:20-35) is read by Jewish commentators as Eliphaz's indirect accusation that Job belongs among the wicked. Metzudat David and other traditional commentators note that each detail of the wicked person's suffering suspiciously matches Job's own experience. Rabbinic hermeneutics (*middot*) would recognize this as an argument by analogy (*gezerah shavah*), but one illegitimately constructed: superficial resemblance between Job's suffering and the punishment of the wicked does not prove that Job is wicked. The Talmudic principle *ein mazkirin avonot* (one does not remind a person of past sins) extends to the broader prohibition against using someone's misfortune as evidence of their guilt.
