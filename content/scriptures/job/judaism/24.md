## The Problem of Unpunished Evil

Job's catalogue of social injustice in chapter 24 engages one of the central questions of rabbinic theology: *tzaddik v'ra lo, rasha v'tov lo* — why do the righteous suffer and the wicked prosper? The Talmud (Berakhot 7a) records that even Moses asked this question, and the various rabbinic answers — punishment in the world to come, suffering as refinement, reward for ancestors' merits — are all partial responses to a mystery that Job lays bare.

The vivid descriptions of exploitation — moving boundary stones, seizing orphans' donkeys, forcing the poor to hide (24:2-4) — violate numerous Torah commandments. The prohibition against moving boundary stones (Deuteronomy 19:14), the obligation to leave gleanings for the poor (Leviticus 19:9-10), and the command to shelter the vulnerable (Exodus 22:21-24) are all implicitly invoked. Job's protest is thus not merely personal but covenantal: the wicked are violating the Torah with impunity.

The Midrash addresses the delay of divine judgment by distinguishing between *olam hazeh* (this world) and *olam haba* (the world to come). Rabbinic tradition teaches that God may grant the wicked prosperity in this world as payment for their few merits, reserving punishment for the next world, while the righteous may suffer here so that they enter the world to come with a clean account. This framework does not eliminate the problem but locates its resolution beyond the temporal horizon.
