## Job's Hymn to God's Power

Job's hymn to divine power in chapter 26 engages with Jewish cosmological traditions that extend far beyond the biblical text. The description of God stretching out the heavens over *tohu* (void) and suspending the earth over nothing (26:7) connects to the mystical traditions of *ma'aseh bereshit* (the work of creation) that the Mishnah (Hagigah 2:1) restricts to qualified students. The mysteries of creation are not for casual discussion.

The conquest of Rahab (26:12) is read in Jewish tradition as God's subjugation of the primordial forces of chaos — a theme that appears throughout the Psalms and prophets. The Talmud (Bava Batra 74b) contains elaborate aggadic traditions about the sea monsters and God's mastery over them, reflecting a cosmological imagination that sees creation as the ongoing triumph of divine order over chaos.

Job's concluding statement — "these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him!" (26:14) — articulates the theological humility that characterizes the best of Jewish thought. Maimonides built his negative theology on precisely this principle: we can know that God exists and what God is not, but God's essential nature exceeds all positive description. The whisper of creation points to a thunder of divine reality that human language cannot capture.
