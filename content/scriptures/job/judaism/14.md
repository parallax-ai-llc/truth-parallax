Job's meditation on mortality (14:1-6) — "Man who is born of a woman is few of days and full of trouble" — has been incorporated into Jewish mourning and liturgical practice. The prayer *El Malei Rachamim* ("God, full of compassion"), recited at funerals and memorial services, draws on the same sensibility: human life is fragile, fleeting, and ultimately dependent on divine mercy. The contrast between human transience and divine permanence that Job draws is a persistent theme in Jewish theology, from Psalm 90 ("Teach us to number our days") to the High Holy Days liturgy's meditation on human mortality.

The tree metaphor (14:7-12) and the question "If a man dies, shall he live again?" (14:14) touch on one of the most significant developments in Jewish theology: the emergence of resurrection belief. The Pharisees affirmed *techiyat ha-metim* (resurrection of the dead), while the Sadducees denied it. The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 10:1) includes belief in resurrection among the requirements for a share in the World to Come. Maimonides listed it as the thirteenth of his thirteen principles of faith. Job's question, asked from a position of uncertainty, would receive a definitive affirmative answer in rabbinic Judaism, though the precise nature of resurrection remains debated.

Job's image of God sealing transgression "in a bag" (14:17) resonates with the Yom Kippur imagery of the divine Book of Life and the Book of Death. The liturgical poem *Unetaneh Tokef* envisions God as recording all deeds — a cosmic accounting that determines each person's fate for the coming year. However, the Yom Kippur liturgy also proclaims that "repentance, prayer, and charity avert the severe decree" — a hopeful counterpoint to Job's image of sealed, irrevocable judgment. The Jewish liturgical tradition thus offers the very remedy that Job seeks but cannot find within his narrative.
