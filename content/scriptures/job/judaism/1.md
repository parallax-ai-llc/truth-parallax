The Book of Job (*Iyov*) occupies a unique place in the Ketuvim (Writings), the third division of the Tanakh. The Talmud (Bava Batra 14b-15a) records a famous debate about Job's historicity: Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan that Job never existed and was merely a parable (*mashal*). Others insist on his historical reality. This ambiguity is itself theologically significant — Jewish tradition is less concerned with Job's historicity than with the theological and moral questions his story raises. The Midrash places Job in various periods, some identifying him as a contemporary of Moses, others as living during the Babylonian exile.

The figure of *ha-Satan* (the Adversary) in 1:6-12 is understood in Jewish tradition not as the cosmic devil of later Christian theology but as a member of the divine court whose role is prosecutorial. The definite article ("the Satan") indicates a function rather than a proper name. The Talmud (Bava Batra 16a) identifies the Satan with the *yetzer ha-ra* (evil inclination) and with the Angel of Death, suggesting a complex understanding of adversarial forces as serving divine purposes. Satan operates within God's court, not as an independent rebel, which preserves strict monotheism — a core concern of Jewish theology.

Job's response — "The LORD gave, and the LORD has taken away; blessed be the name of the LORD" (1:21) — is recited in Jewish mourning practice and has been incorporated into the liturgical consciousness of the community. The blessing of God's name (*barukh shem*) even in the face of devastating loss exemplifies the Jewish principle of *berakhah* (blessing) as a response to all of life's circumstances. The Mishnah (Berakhot 9:5) teaches that one must bless God for the evil just as one blesses God for the good, and Job is the paradigmatic exemplar of this principle.
