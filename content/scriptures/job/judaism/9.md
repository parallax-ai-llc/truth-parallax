Job's assertion that no human can be righteous before God (9:2) takes on a specific meaning in Jewish context: it is not a statement of total depravity but a recognition of the vast asymmetry between human and divine perspectives. The Talmud (Berakhot 33b) records Rabbi Chanina's teaching that "everything is in the hands of Heaven except the fear of Heaven," affirming both divine sovereignty and genuine human moral agency. Job's frustration is not that righteousness is impossible but that it is unrecognizable from God's perspective — a complaint about divine inscrutability rather than human incapacity.

The cosmic hymn describing God's power (9:5-10) employs mythological imagery that Jewish commentary has carefully interpreted. Ibn Ezra treated these descriptions as poetic rather than literal, while Ramban found in them allusions to the ma'aseh bereshit (work of creation) that the Mishnah guards as esoteric teaching. The reference to God treading on the waves of the sea (9:8) evokes the Exodus narrative — God's sovereignty over the waters — and rabbinic commentary connects Job's cosmic theology to Israel's foundational experience of divine power at the Red Sea.

Job's wish for a *mokhiach* (mediator, arbiter) between himself and God (9:33) is read in Jewish tradition not as an anticipation of a messianic figure but as an expression of the fundamental human need for justice to be mediated fairly. The Jewish legal system's insistence on impartial judges (*dayanim*) and the prohibition against a judge hearing one party without the other present (Sanhedrin 7b) reflect the same principle Job articulates: justice requires a neutral third party. Job's complaint is that in a dispute with God, no such neutral party exists — a genuine theological problem that Jewish thought acknowledges without fully resolving.
