Bildad's sustained portrait of the wicked person's fate (18:5-21) is evaluated in Jewish tradition against the principle of *tzelem Elohim* — every person, however wicked, retains the divine image. The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 4:5) teaches that the first human was created singly to teach that whoever destroys a single life destroys an entire world. Bildad's dehumanization of the wicked — reducing them to objects of divine wrath — violates this principle by treating certain people as beyond the reach of divine mercy. Even the Talmud's descriptions of *Gehinnom* (the place of post-mortem purification) emphasize its temporal, purifying nature rather than permanent destruction.

The "king of terrors" (18:14) — death as sovereign — is a concept Jewish tradition addresses through the theology of divine sovereignty over death itself. The second blessing of the Amidah (*G'vurot*) praises God as *m'chayei ha-metim* — "the one who gives life to the dead." This liturgical affirmation, recited three times daily, directly counters the notion that death has ultimate sovereignty. In Jewish eschatology, the "king of terrors" is ultimately subject to the King of Kings, who alone determines the final disposition of every soul.

Bildad's claim that the wicked person "has no name in the street" (18:17) and "no posterity" (18:19) contradicts the Jewish emphasis on *zikaron* (memory) as a sacred obligation. The *Yizkor* service, recited on major holidays, keeps the memory of the departed alive within the community. The practice of naming children after deceased relatives ensures continuity of memory across generations. Jewish tradition resists the erasure Bildad describes, insisting instead on the enduring significance of every life and the community's responsibility to remember.
