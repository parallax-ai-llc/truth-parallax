## God Speaks from the Whirlwind

The divine speeches from the whirlwind represent one of the most significant theophanies in the Hebrew Bible. God's opening question — "Where were you when I laid the earth's foundation?" (38:4) — is not merely rhetorical but pedagogical: it positions Job, and every reader, within the cosmic order. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 38b) teaches that Adam was created last among all creatures to remind humanity that "even the gnat preceded you in creation." God's questions to Job serve the same humbling function.

The cascade of divine questions about creation — the sea's boundaries (38:8-11), the storehouses of snow (38:22-23), the constellations (38:31-33) — connects to the *ma'aseh bereshit* tradition that the Mishnah (Hagigah 2:1) treats as one of the deepest mysteries. Jewish mysticism teaches that the act of creation reveals God's *middot* (attributes) — each element of the natural world embodies a dimension of divine wisdom. God's questions invite Job to contemplate these middot.

Significantly, God does not explain Job's suffering. Rabbinic commentators noted this with characteristic honesty. The Midrash observes that God's response is an overwhelmingly magnificent non-answer — or rather, the answer is the encounter itself. Maimonides read Job 38 as teaching that knowledge of God through contemplation of creation is the highest form of human fulfillment, and that this knowledge renders the question of suffering, while not intellectually resolved, existentially transformed.
