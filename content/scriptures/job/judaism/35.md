## Does God Benefit from Human Righteousness?

Elihu's question — whether human righteousness or wickedness affects God (35:6-7) — engages a classic debate in Jewish theology. Maimonides argued that God is utterly unaffected by human action, consistent with His absolute unity and self-sufficiency. But the Kabbalistic tradition, following the Zohar, teaches that human actions have cosmic significance — righteous deeds (*mitzvot*) strengthen the flow of divine blessing into the world, while sins obstruct it. The two traditions represent different emphases within Jewish thought.

The Talmud (Yoma 69b) records that the Men of the Great Assembly restored the prayer praising God as "great, mighty, and awesome" because they recognized that God's greatness is manifest precisely in His patience with human failing. This suggests that God is affected by human behavior — not in His essential nature but in the mode of His self-revelation. Elihu's theology of divine indifference, while philosophically defensible, does not capture the full range of Jewish teaching.

Elihu's critique of those who cry out in distress but do not ask "Where is God my Maker?" (35:10) has a parallel in Jewish prayer theology. The Talmud distinguishes between prayer that is mere petition (*bakashah*) and prayer that is genuine communion (*tefillah*). The former may go unanswered; the latter always "reaches" God, even when no visible response follows. Yet applying this distinction to Job, whose entire struggle is directed toward God, is problematic — Job's prayer is anything but self-centered.
