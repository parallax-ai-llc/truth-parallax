## Elihu on Dreams and Discipline

Elihu's assertion that God communicates through dreams (33:14-16) aligns with a significant strand of Jewish thought. The Talmud (Berakhot 55a-57b) contains extensive discussion of dreams as potential vehicles of divine communication, while also warning that "dreams follow their interpretation." Rabbinic tradition maintains a careful balance: dreams may contain genuine revelation, but they require discernment and should not replace Torah as the primary source of divine guidance.

The figure of the mediating angel — "one out of a thousand" who finds a ransom (33:23-24) — is read in Jewish tradition through the concept of the *melitz yosher* (advocate of righteousness). The liturgy of the High Holy Days invokes heavenly advocates who intercede on Israel's behalf. While Judaism rejects the notion of a divine mediator who replaces the direct relationship between God and the individual, the angelic intercessor of Job 33 represents a legitimate aspect of Jewish angelology.

Elihu's theology of suffering as divine communication (33:19-22) — God using pain to prevent a person from descending to the pit — partially aligns with the rabbinic concept of *yissurin* (afflictions) as corrective. The Talmud (Berakhot 5a) teaches that afflictions should prompt self-examination. However, rabbinic wisdom also recognizes the limits of this framework: Rabbi Yannai declared, "We do not have in our hands an explanation for the suffering of the righteous or the prosperity of the wicked" (Avot 4:15).
