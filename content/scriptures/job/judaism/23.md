## Job's Longing for God's Court

Job's desire to bring his case before God — "If only I knew where to find him" (23:3) — resonates with the Jewish legal tradition of *din Torah* (a case brought before a divine court). The Talmud (Berakhot 32a) teaches that the righteous may argue their case before God, citing Moses, Abraham, and Job as exemplars. Job's demand is not impious but reflects the covenantal framework in which God invites human partners into dialogue — even disputation.

Rashi interprets Job's searching in every direction (23:8-9) as expressing the universal human condition of *hester panim* — the hiding of God's face. This concept is central to Jewish theology of suffering: God sometimes withdraws His manifest presence, not as punishment but as a test or as part of an inscrutable divine plan. The Zohar teaches that *hester panim* is itself a form of divine presence — God is present in His very hiddenness.

Job's declaration that he will emerge "as gold" from testing (23:10) is read in Jewish tradition alongside the principle that *yissurin shel ahavah* (sufferings of love) refine the righteous. The Talmud (Berakhot 5a) teaches that God may send afflictions to those He loves in order to increase their merit in the world to come. Yet the tradition also acknowledges, through Job's continued terror (23:15-17), that this theological framework does not eliminate the anguish of the one who suffers.
