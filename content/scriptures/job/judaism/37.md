## The Thunderstorm and God's Approach

Elihu's final speech, filled with meteorological imagery, connects to the Jewish theological tradition of God's presence in natural phenomena. The Talmud (Berakhot 59a) prescribes specific blessings for thunder ("Blessed is He whose power and might fill the world") and lightning ("Blessed is He who makes the work of creation"), transforming natural events into occasions of religious awareness. Elihu's storm description is, in effect, an extended *berakhah*.

The rhetorical questions — "Do you know how the clouds hang poised?" (37:16) — function in Jewish reading as a preparation for *yirat Hashem* (fear/awe of God). The Talmud (Berakhot 33a) identifies fear of God as the one thing not predetermined by heaven — it must be freely chosen. Elihu's questions, by revealing the limits of human knowledge, create the conditions in which genuine awe becomes possible. The storm is not merely weather but a summons to reverence.

Elihu's concluding statement — "The Almighty is beyond our reach" (37:23) — anticipates Maimonides' theology of divine unknowability. The *Guide for the Perplexed* argues that all positive attributes applied to God are either negations or descriptions of His actions, not His essence. Jewish theology stands with Elihu here: God's essential nature exceeds human comprehension. But what Elihu cannot anticipate is that this unreachable God is about to speak — proving that while God may be beyond our reach, we are not beyond His.
