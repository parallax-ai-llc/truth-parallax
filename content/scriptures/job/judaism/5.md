Eliphaz's counsel to accept divine discipline (5:17) invokes the concept of *musar* (discipline, instruction), which is central to the wisdom tradition in which Job is situated. Proverbs 3:11-12 — "My son, do not despise the LORD's discipline" — is the locus classicus for this principle, and Eliphaz explicitly applies it. Jewish commentators note that *musar* in the wisdom tradition carries a dual sense: both chastisement and education. The Vilna Gaon, in his commentary on Proverbs, emphasized that divine discipline is always purposeful, aimed at the moral and spiritual growth of the recipient.

The description of God's providential care (5:9-16) — setting on high those who are lowly, frustrating the devices of the crafty — echoes themes found throughout the Psalms and the prophets. Rabbinic literature frequently celebrates God's reversal of human expectations: the Midrash on the Song of the Sea (Mekhilta de-Rabbi Ishmael) elaborates at length on how God overturns the plans of the powerful on behalf of the vulnerable. Eliphaz's theology is correct in its general orientation but fails in its specific application: God does indeed lift up the lowly, but Job is already lowly, and Eliphaz's speech pushes him lower.

The promise of material blessing (5:19-26) — "in famine he shall redeem you from death" and "you shall come to your grave in ripe old age" — articulates a *this-worldly* eschatology that is characteristic of much biblical wisdom literature. Jewish thought has generally maintained a strong emphasis on the goodness of life in this world (*olam ha-zeh*) alongside belief in the World to Come (*olam ha-ba*). Eliphaz's error is not in valuing earthly blessing but in treating it as the infallible indicator of divine approval, a position the book of Job exists to complicate.
