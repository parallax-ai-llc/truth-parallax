Job's question "Why do the wicked live, reach old age, and grow mighty in power?" (21:7) articulates the classic Jewish formulation of the theodicy problem. The Talmud (Berakhot 7a) records that Moses himself asked this question: *tzaddik v'ra lo, rasha v'tov lo* — "Why does the righteous person suffer while the wicked person prospers?" The Talmud offers multiple answers — testing, atonement, reward in the World to Come — but ultimately acknowledges the mystery. Rabbi Yannai's conclusion, cited above (in chapter 4 discussion), represents the dominant rabbinic stance: honest agnosticism about specific cases.

Job's empirical challenge to retribution theology (21:7-16) resonates with the Ecclesiastes tradition (*Kohelet*), which similarly observes that "there are righteous people to whom it happens according to the deeds of the wicked" (Ecclesiastes 8:14). The inclusion of both Job and Ecclesiastes in the biblical canon demonstrates Judaism's willingness to preserve voices that challenge dominant theological frameworks. The *machloket* between the optimism of Proverbs and the skepticism of Job/Ecclesiastes is itself a form of Torah study, generating insight through the friction of opposing perspectives.

Job's devastating conclusion — "How then will you comfort me with empty nothings? There is nothing left of your answers but falsehood" (21:34) — has been cited by Jewish thinkers confronting the Holocaust as a legitimate response to theodicies that explain too much. Rabbi Irving Greenberg's principle — "no statement, theological or otherwise, should be made that would not be credible in the presence of burning children" — echoes Job's rejection of comfortable theology that fails before the reality of innocent suffering. Jewish thought after the Shoah has found in Job a conversation partner who refuses to accept inadequate answers and who insists that theological honesty must take precedence over theological comfort.
