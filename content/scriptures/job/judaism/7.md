Job's description of life as forced labor (7:1-2) resonates with Jewish memory of slavery in Egypt. The Hebrew word *tzava* (hard service, military service) evokes the experience of conscripted labor, and rabbinic commentary connects Job's language to the broader theme of human bondage that the Exodus narrative addresses. The Passover Haggadah's statement "In every generation, a person is obligated to see themselves as though they personally went out of Egypt" suggests that Job's experience of existential servitude participates in a universal Jewish narrative of bondage and anticipated liberation.

The inversion of Psalm 8 (7:17-18) is recognized in Jewish commentary as a daring rhetorical move. Rashi notes that Job takes a verse meant to express God's gracious attentiveness and reinterprets it as oppressive surveillance. The Midrash Tanchuma discusses the tension between God's knowledge of human actions (which could be experienced as judgment) and God's compassion (which transforms that knowledge into care). Job experiences only the judgmental dimension, which rabbinic theology would correct by pointing to the thirteen attributes of divine mercy (*midot ha-rachamim*) proclaimed in Exodus 34:6-7.

Job's plea for pardon (7:21) — "Why do you not pardon my transgression?" — anticipates themes central to the High Holy Days liturgy. The prayers of Yom Kippur, especially the *Vidui* (confession) and the *Selichot* (penitential prayers), are built on the conviction that God is fundamentally disposed toward forgiveness. The liturgical refrain *ki anu amekha v'atah eloheinu* ("for we are your people and you are our God") expresses the covenantal framework within which forgiveness operates. Job seeks this forgiveness outside a covenantal framework, which intensifies his isolation.
