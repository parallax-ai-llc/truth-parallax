Jewish interpretation of Eliphaz generally views him as the most thoughtful of the three friends, though ultimately mistaken. The Talmud (Bava Batra 15b) identifies Eliphaz as coming from Teman, an Edomite region associated with wisdom. Rashi notes that Eliphaz was the most senior of the friends and therefore spoke first. His initial courtesy (4:2-4) — acknowledging Job's past record of comforting others — reflects what the rabbis value as *derekh eretz* (proper conduct), though his subsequent argument undermines this initial sensitivity.

Eliphaz's retributive principle — "who that was innocent ever perished?" (4:7) — is evaluated in rabbinic tradition against the broader testimony of Scripture and experience. The Talmud (Berakhot 7a) records Rabbi Yannai's statement: "It is not in our power to explain either the prosperity of the wicked or the afflictions of the righteous." This agnostic position regarding retribution represents the mature rabbinic stance, which acknowledges the principle of divine justice while refusing to apply it mechanically to individual cases. Eliphaz's error, in Jewish reading, is not in affirming divine justice but in claiming to administer it.

The nocturnal vision (4:12-16) is treated with particular interest in Jewish mystical tradition. The Zohar discusses angelic and demonic communications that come through dreams and night visions, developing criteria for distinguishing between them. Maimonides (Guide for the Perplexed II:41-45) analyzed prophetic experience and argued that genuine prophecy requires specific intellectual and moral qualifications. By these criteria, Eliphaz's vision — which produces fear rather than understanding, and leads to judgment rather than compassion — falls short of authentic prophetic experience as Judaism defines it.
