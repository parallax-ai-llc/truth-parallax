## Elihu Defends God's Justice

Elihu's declaration that "it is unthinkable that God would do wrong" (34:10) articulates a principle that rabbinic theology affirms categorically: *tzedek tzedek tirdof* — "Justice, justice shall you pursue" (Deuteronomy 16:20), and God Himself is the ultimate exemplar of justice. The Talmud (Berakhot 33b) teaches that everything is in the hands of heaven except the fear of heaven — God's governance is just, even when its justice is hidden from human perception.

Elihu's observation that God sustains all life by His spirit (34:14-15) connects to the Jewish daily prayer *Elohai Neshamah*: "My God, the soul You have given me is pure. You created it, You formed it, You breathed it into me." Every breath is a divine gift, and the continuation of life is not automatic but depends on God's ongoing will. This theology of divine sustenance (*hashgachah*) is fundamental to Jewish consciousness.

Elihu's charge that Job "adds rebellion to his sin" (34:37) is challenged by the Jewish tradition of *chutzpah klapei shamaya* — audacity directed toward heaven. The Talmud records numerous instances of righteous figures arguing boldly with God — Abraham bargaining for Sodom, Moses challenging God after the golden calf, Jeremiah questioning divine justice. In Jewish tradition, such audacity is not rebellion but the deepest expression of covenantal relationship.
