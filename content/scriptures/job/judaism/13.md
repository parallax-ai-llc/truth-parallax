Job's desire to "argue my case with God" (13:3) stands within the distinguished Jewish tradition of *chutzpah klapei shamaya* — audacity directed toward heaven. Abraham argued with God over the fate of Sodom (Genesis 18:23-32), Moses challenged God's plan to destroy Israel (Exodus 32:11-14), and Jeremiah questioned divine justice (Jeremiah 12:1). The Hasidic master Levi Yitzchak of Berditchev famously put God "on trial" for the suffering of the Jewish people. Job's bold demand for a hearing is not impiety but the expression of a relationship strong enough to withstand confrontation.

Job's charge that the friends "speak falsely for God" (13:7-8) raises a principle that rabbinic ethics takes very seriously. The prohibition of *sheker* (falsehood) extends even to statements made in defense of God or Torah. The Talmud (Shabbat 55a) declares: "The seal of the Holy One, blessed be He, is *emet* (truth)." To defend God with lies is to deny God's essential attribute. Rashi's commentary on this passage emphasizes that God does not need human fabrications in His defense — a principle that would later inform Maimonides' insistence on intellectual honesty in theology, even when the conclusions are uncomfortable.

"Though he slay me, I will hope in him" (13:15) — reading with the Qere — is one of the most debated verses in Jewish exegesis. The Ketiv (written text) reads *lo* (not) rather than *lo* (to him), yielding "I will not hope." Jewish tradition preserves both readings, reflecting a characteristically Jewish comfort with ambiguity. The Zohar reads the verse as expressing the highest form of divine service: service without expectation of reward, which the Mishnah (Avot 1:3) attributes to Antigonus of Sokho: "Be not like servants who serve the master for the sake of receiving a reward."
