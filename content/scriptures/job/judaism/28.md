## The Hymn to Wisdom

The Wisdom Poem of Job 28 is central to Jewish *chokhmah* (wisdom) tradition. The extended mining metaphor (28:1-11) demonstrates that *chokhmah* is fundamentally different from technical knowledge: humans can mine gold and sapphires, but wisdom cannot be extracted from the earth or purchased with treasure (28:15-19). The Talmud (Shabbat 31a) records that the first question asked of a person in the heavenly court is not about wisdom but about integrity — yet wisdom remains the supreme aspiration.

Rabbinic tradition identifies the wisdom of Job 28 with Torah. The Midrash (Bereishit Rabbah 1:1) teaches that God looked into the Torah and created the world, echoing Job 28:27 — "he looked at wisdom and appraised it." Torah is not merely a legal code but the blueprint of creation, the divine wisdom through which all things were made. To study Torah is therefore to participate in the very wisdom by which the world exists.

The concluding verse — "The fear of the Lord (*yirat Adonai*), that is wisdom" (28:28) — is foundational for the Jewish understanding of the relationship between piety and knowledge. The Talmud (Berakhot 33a) asks, "Is the fear of heaven such a small thing?" and answers that for Moses, it was. The point is that *yirat Adonai* is the prerequisite for all genuine wisdom — not an emotional state but a fundamental orientation of the whole person toward God's reality and authority.
