## Job's Oath of Innocence

Job 31 presents what rabbinic tradition recognizes as a comprehensive *cheshbon hanefesh* (accounting of the soul) — a practice that later became central to Jewish ethical discipline, especially in the *Mussar* movement. Job examines his conduct across every dimension of moral life: sexual purity (31:1, 9-12), honesty in business (31:5-8), justice toward servants (31:13-15), generosity to the poor (31:16-23), avoidance of idolatry (31:24-28), and environmental stewardship (31:38-40).

Job's treatment of servants deserves special attention in Jewish interpretation. His declaration that his servant was made by the same God who made him (31:15) anticipates the rabbinic teaching that all human beings descend from a single ancestor so that no person can claim superior lineage (Sanhedrin 37a). The Mishnah's insistence on the infinite value of each human life finds a powerful precursor in Job's ethical vision.

Job's concluding demand — "Oh, that I had someone to hear me! Here is my signature — let the Almighty answer me!" (31:35) — is read in Jewish tradition as the formal submission of a *din Torah* (legal case before God). The audacity of this act is mitigated by its deep respect for divine justice: Job does not deny God's authority but appeals to it. This mirrors the Jewish legal principle that every person — even one who disputes a judge's ruling — has the right to be heard.
