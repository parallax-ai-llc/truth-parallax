## Overview

Job is one of the great wisdom books of the Hebrew Bible and Christian Old Testament. It addresses the most profound question in theology: why do the righteous suffer? The book is set in the land of Uz and tells the story of a blameless man named Job who loses everything — his children, his wealth, and his health — in a test permitted by God at the instigation of "the Satan" (the Accuser).

## Structure

The Book of Job is composed of both prose and poetry:

1. **Prologue** (Chapters 1–2): Prose narrative — the heavenly court, Job's losses, the Satan's challenge
2. **Job's Lament** (Chapter 3): Job curses the day of his birth
3. **Three Cycles of Dialogue** (Chapters 4–27): Debates between Job and his three friends — Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar
4. **Wisdom Poem** (Chapter 28): An interlude on the inaccessibility of wisdom
5. **Job's Final Defense** (Chapters 29–31): Job's oath of innocence
6. **Elihu's Speeches** (Chapters 32–37): A young man's intervention
7. **God's Speeches from the Whirlwind** (Chapters 38–41): The divine response
8. **Epilogue** (Chapter 42): Prose narrative — Job's restoration

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Job occupies a distinctive place across religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: Part of the Ketuvim (Writings); the Talmud debates Job's historicity and dating
- **Christianity**: A key text for theodicy, patience, and prefiguration of Christ's suffering
- **Islam**: Job (Ayyub) is honored as a prophet of extraordinary patience (*sabr*)
- **Gnosticism**: The God who torments Job is read as evidence of the Demiurge's cruelty
- **Academic**: One of the finest examples of ancient Near Eastern wisdom literature

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Suffering | The experience of undeserved suffering and its meaning |
| Theodicy | The justice of God in the face of innocent suffering |
| Wisdom | The limits of human understanding and the fear of the LORD |
| Divine Speech | God's self-revelation from the whirlwind |
| Patience | Job's endurance as a model of faith under trial |
| Restoration | God's vindication and restoration of the righteous sufferer |
