## Job's Silence and Behemoth

Job 40 contains two pivotal moments: Job's first response to God (40:3-5) and the beginning of God's second speech with the description of Behemoth (40:15-24). Job's initial reply is brief and submissive: "I am unworthy — how can I reply to you? I put my hand over my mouth" (40:4). Protestant interpreters note that this is not yet the full repentance of 42:6 but a necessary first step — recognition of his own smallness before God.

God's second speech opens with a direct challenge: "Would you discredit my justice? Would you condemn me to justify yourself?" (40:8). Protestant theology recognizes this as the heart of the matter. Job has not merely questioned God's ways but has implicitly placed himself in the position of moral judge over the Creator. The Reformation's emphasis on *Deus absconditus* (the hidden God) finds its Old Testament roots here — God's justice operates on a plane that human moral reasoning cannot fully access.

Behemoth (40:15-24) — whether hippopotamus, mythological creature, or cosmic symbol — represents created power at its most overwhelming. "He ranks first among the works of God" (40:19). Protestant commentators see Behemoth as evidence that God's creation contains forces far beyond human control or comprehension, and yet God made them. If God governs Behemoth, He can be trusted with Job's suffering.
