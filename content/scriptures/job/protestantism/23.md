## Job's Longing for God's Court

Job 23 contains one of the most poignant expressions of desire for God in the Old Testament. Job longs to find God and present his case before the divine tribunal (23:3-4), confident that he would be acquitted (23:7). Protestant theology reads this as a legitimate cry of faith — not rebellion against God, but desperate desire for encounter with Him.

The chapter pivots on the paradox of divine hiddenness: "I go forward, but he is not there; backward, but I cannot perceive him" (23:8-9). Luther, who knew seasons of God's apparent absence (*Anfechtung*), found deep resonance here. The Protestant spiritual tradition affirms that God's hiddenness does not negate His presence — Job's very desire to appear before God is itself evidence of a faith that persists through darkness.

Job's declaration "he knows the way that I take; when he has tested me, I shall come out as gold" (23:10) is a cornerstone text in Protestant pastoral theology. It affirms both the reality of suffering as a refining process and the confidence that God's purposes are ultimately redemptive, even when incomprehensible. Yet 23:15-17 shows that this confidence coexists with genuine terror — Protestant honesty about the Christian life does not flatten this tension.
