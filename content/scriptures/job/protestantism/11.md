Zophar the Naamathite is the bluntest of Job's three friends, and his speech lacks even the courteous opening that Eliphaz offered. He immediately accuses Job of babbling (11:2-3) and insists that if God were to speak, He would reveal that "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" (11:6). Protestant interpreters note the irony: Zophar claims to know what God would say, yet when God does speak in chapters 38-41, He says nothing remotely resembling Zophar's predictions.

Zophar's description of divine mystery (11:7-9) — "Can you find out the deep things of God? Can you find out the limit of the Almighty?" — contains genuinely profound theology. His recognition that God's wisdom is "higher than heaven" and "deeper than Sheol" anticipates Paul's doxology in Romans 11:33-36. Protestant commentators observe that Zophar correctly identifies God's transcendence but incorrectly concludes that this transcendence justifies his own theology of retribution. He uses divine mystery to shut down Job's questions rather than to humble his own certainties.

The conditional promise Zophar offers (11:13-19) — that if Job will "set your heart aright" and "stretch out your hands toward him," then security, hope, and rest will follow — represents the friends' theology at its most transactional. Protestant interpreters connect this to the broader biblical warning against reducing faith to a formula for prosperity. The book of Job exists precisely to shatter such frameworks, revealing a God who cannot be manipulated by human performance.
