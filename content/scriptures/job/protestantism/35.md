## Does God Benefit from Human Righteousness?

Elihu's third speech poses a provocative question: does human righteousness or wickedness affect God at all? "If you sin, how does that affect him? If your sins are many, what does that do to him?" (35:6). Protestant theology engages this question seriously — God is indeed self-sufficient and not dependent on human goodness for His well-being.

Yet Protestant interpreters challenge Elihu's conclusion. While God is ontologically independent, Scripture consistently portrays Him as relationally engaged. The God of the covenant is grieved by sin (Genesis 6:6), delighted by obedience (Psalm 147:11), and moved by prayer. Elihu's theology of divine transcendence, pushed to its logical extreme, produces a deistic God — precisely what the book of Job, with its portrait of a God who speaks from the whirlwind, will refute.

Elihu's explanation for unanswered prayer — that people cry out in distress but do not ask "Where is God my Maker?" (35:10) — contains partial truth. Protestant pastoral theology acknowledges that suffering can be self-focused rather than God-focused. But applying this to Job, who has consistently directed his complaints to God rather than away from Him, reveals Elihu's inability to see past his theological framework.
