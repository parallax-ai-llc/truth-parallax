## Bildad's Brief Final Word

Bildad's third speech is the shortest in the dialogue cycle — a mere six verses. Protestant commentators note that this brevity itself is significant: the friends' arguments have exhausted themselves. Bildad can only repeat the theme of God's transcendence and human insignificance: "How can a mortal be righteous before God?" (25:4). His theology has reached a dead end.

The speech affirms truths that are, in isolation, unimpeachable: God's dominion and fearsome power (25:2), the impurity of even the moon and stars before Him (25:5). The Reformers agreed with Bildad's assessment of human unworthiness — *simul justus et peccator* — but insisted that this is the beginning of theology, not its conclusion. Bildad's error is not what he affirms but what he omits: he has no gospel, no mechanism by which a sinful human might stand before a holy God.

Protestant interpretation reads Bildad's final question — "how can one born of woman be pure?" (25:4) — as inadvertently prophetic. The answer Bildad cannot imagine is precisely what the gospel provides: justification by grace through faith, the imputation of Christ's righteousness to those who are, in themselves, "maggots" and "worms" (25:6).
