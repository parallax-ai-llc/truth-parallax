## Repentance and Restoration

Job 42 brings the book to its stunning conclusion. Job's final response to God — "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you. Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes" (42:5-6) — is among the most discussed verses in Protestant exegesis. What does Job repent of? Not the sins the friends alleged, but his presumption that he could comprehend God's governance of the universe.

The epilogue (42:7-17) delivers a sharp rebuke to the three friends: "You have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has" (42:7). Protestant theology finds this stunning — God vindicates Job's honest anguish over the friends' orthodox platitudes. The Reformation's emphasis on honest faith over performative piety finds powerful support here. God prefers Job's agonized questions to Eliphaz's tidy answers.

Job's restoration — double wealth, new children, 140 more years of life (42:10-17) — has troubled some Protestant commentators, who note that it seems to reinstate the very retribution theology the book has dismantled. Yet the restoration comes after and because of Job's encounter with God, not as a reward for correct theology. Moreover, Job must first intercede for his friends (42:8-9), an act of grace toward those who have wronged him. Protestant interpretation sees here a typological foreshadowing of Christ's intercession for sinners and the ultimate restoration that lies beyond suffering.
