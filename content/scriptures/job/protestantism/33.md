## Elihu on Dreams and Discipline

In Job 33, Elihu addresses Job directly, proposing that God communicates through dreams and visions (33:14-16) and through suffering that confines a person to bed (33:19-22). Protestant interpreters note that Elihu introduces a concept the three friends never considered: suffering as divine pedagogy rather than punishment. God may use pain to turn a person "back from the pit" (33:18).

The most striking element is Elihu's vision of a heavenly mediator: "Yet if there is an angel at their side, a mediator, one out of a thousand, sent to tell them how to be upright" (33:23). This figure intercedes and declares, "Spare them from going down to the pit; I have found a ransom for them" (33:24). Protestant Christological interpretation sees here a foreshadowing of Christ as the one mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5).

Elihu's theology is more nuanced than the three friends' retribution formula, but Protestant commentators observe that it still does not fully apply to Job's case. Job is not suffering because God is teaching him a lesson — the prologue makes this clear. Elihu's theology is better than Eliphaz's, but it remains an incomplete answer to the mystery of innocent suffering.
