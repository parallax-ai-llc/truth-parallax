## Job's Remembrance of Former Glory

Job 29 begins his final defense with a poignant recollection of his former life. He recalls God's intimate friendship — "when God's lamp shone on my head" (29:3) — and his honored standing in the community. Young men stepped aside, elders rose, princes fell silent (29:7-10). This is not empty nostalgia but evidence: Job's former blessedness proves he was once in right relationship with God.

Protestant interpreters note the social dimension of Job's self-portrait. He rescued the poor and the fatherless (29:12), was eyes to the blind and feet to the lame (29:15), and broke the fangs of the wicked (29:17). This chapter demolishes Eliphaz's accusations in chapter 22 and reveals Job as a model of the Reformed ethic: faith expressed through active justice and mercy in the community.

The chapter's emotional arc — from the warmth of divine intimacy (29:2-6) to public honor (29:7-11) to charitable action (29:12-17) to confidence in a long future (29:18-20) — creates the backdrop against which his present suffering is measured. Protestant pastoral theology recognizes this pattern: the memory of God's past faithfulness intensifies the pain of His apparent present absence, yet it also serves as an anchor of hope.
