The opening chapter of Job introduces a man of extraordinary piety and wealth in the land of Uz. Protestant interpreters note that Job is described as "blameless and upright" (1:1), which does not mean sinless perfection but rather a genuine, consistent devotion to God. Calvin emphasized that Job's righteousness was real but not meritorious — it flowed from God's grace rather than earning divine favor. The description of Job's sacrificial intercession for his children (1:5) demonstrates a priestly concern that Protestant commentators connect to the universal priesthood of believers.

The heavenly council scene (1:6-12) raises profound theological questions central to Protestant thought. Satan appears not as an independent power but as one who must seek divine permission before acting. Luther saw in this passage a powerful affirmation of God's absolute sovereignty — even the adversary operates within divinely set boundaries. The phrase "Have you considered my servant Job?" (1:8) indicates that God initiates the test, a point that Reformed theologians use to distinguish between God's permissive will and His active will without diminishing His ultimate sovereignty.

Job's response to catastrophic loss — "The LORD gave, and the LORD has taken away; blessed be the name of the LORD" (1:21) — is one of the most quoted verses in Protestant pastoral theology. It exemplifies what the Reformers understood as true faith: trust in God's character even when circumstances suggest His absence. The narrator's closing affirmation that "in all this Job did not sin or charge God with wrong" (1:22) establishes the baseline against which the entire subsequent dialogue must be measured.
