## Job's Oath of Innocence

Job 31 is one of the most remarkable ethical statements in the Old Testament — a series of self-imprecatory oaths in which Job invites divine punishment if he has committed any of the sins enumerated. Protestant interpreters recognize this chapter as evidence of an extraordinarily developed moral conscience that anticipates the ethics of the Sermon on the Mount.

Job's ethical inventory covers not only actions but inner attitudes: lust of the eyes (31:1), deceit of the heart (31:5), covetousness (31:9-12), injustice toward servants (31:13-15), neglect of the poor (31:16-23), idolatry of wealth (31:24-25), secret sin (31:33), and inhospitality (31:31-32). Protestant commentators note that Job's treatment of servants as moral equals — "Did not he who made me in the womb make them?" (31:15) — is radically egalitarian for the ancient world.

The chapter concludes with Job's final challenge: "Oh, that I had someone to hear me! I sign now my defense — let the Almighty answer me" (31:35). This is the climax of the entire dialogue section. Protestant theology reads Job's signature as both legally formal and spiritually audacious — he demands a hearing from the Judge of all the earth, confident in his integrity. The silence that follows (before Elihu's intervention) is deafening.
