## The Wild Kingdom

God's first speech continues in Job 39 with a tour of the animal kingdom: mountain goats giving birth (39:1), the wild donkey's freedom (39:5-8), the untamable wild ox (39:9-12), the foolish ostrich (39:13-18), the war horse's fearless strength (39:19-25), and the hawk and eagle soaring by divine wisdom (39:26-30).

Protestant interpreters note a crucial theological point: these animals are not domesticated, not useful to humans, and not part of the human economy. God delights in creatures that serve no human purpose. This challenges anthropocentric readings of creation — the world was not made solely for human benefit, and God's providential care extends to realms humans never see. Calvin drew from this the principle that God's sovereignty encompasses the entirety of creation, not merely human affairs.

The ostrich passage (39:13-18) is particularly striking: God describes a creature He has made deliberately foolish, "for God did not endow her with wisdom" (39:17), yet she outruns the horse. Protestant commentators see here a playful divine humor — God is not embarrassed by His creation's apparent imperfections but delights in diversity and wildness. This implicitly challenges the friends' tidy theology: God's world is wilder, stranger, and more wonderful than their system allows.
