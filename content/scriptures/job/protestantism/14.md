Job's meditation on human mortality in chapter 14 reaches the deepest theological register of the first cycle of speeches. His opening image — "Man who is born of a woman is few of days and full of trouble. He comes out like a flower and withers" (14:1-2) — has become a classic expression of human frailty in Protestant liturgy and hymnody. The plea "look away from him and leave him alone" (14:6) continues Job's paradoxical desire for divine absence as the only form of mercy available.

The tree metaphor (14:7-12) draws a painful contrast between botanical resilience and human finality. A tree, when cut down, can sprout again from its stump, "though its root grows old in the earth" (14:8). But "man lies down and rises not again" (14:12). Protestant interpreters see in this passage the Old Testament at its most honest about death's apparent finality. Yet a flicker of hope appears: "If a man dies, shall he live again? All the days of my service I would wait, till my renewal should come" (14:14). Calvin and Luther both saw this as a seed of resurrection hope struggling to break through the soil of despair.

The chapter's concluding vision of divine erosion — "the waters wear away the stones" and "you destroy the hope of man" (14:19) — returns to pessimism. Protestant interpreters read Job 14 as the theological low point that makes the resurrection affirmation of Job 19:25-27 possible. Without the depths of chapter 14, the heights of chapter 19 would lack their transformative power. The arc from hopelessness to hope mirrors the broader Protestant understanding of the gospel: one must know the full weight of sin and death before the resurrection can be properly received.
