Job turns from addressing his friends to addressing God directly, and his words take on the character of a lament psalm turned inside out. His description of life as "hard service" and hired labor (7:1-2) reflects the ancient Near Eastern reality of forced labor, but Protestant commentators see it as a broader metaphor for the human condition under suffering. The swift passage of days "without hope" (7:6) and the image of life as a breath that vanishes (7:7) emphasize mortality's weight.

The most theologically provocative passage is Job's parody of Psalm 8. Where the psalmist marvels, "What is man that you are mindful of him?" as an expression of wonder at divine care, Job inverts it bitterly: "What is man, that you make so much of him, and that you set your heart on him, visit him every morning and test him every moment?" (7:17-18). Protestant interpreters recognize this as a daring rhetorical move — Job wishes God would look away and leave him alone rather than subjecting him to relentless scrutiny. Luther saw in this passage the experience of the soul under divine law without the comfort of the gospel.

Job's concluding question — "If I sin, what do I do to you, you watcher of mankind? Why have you made me your mark?" (7:20) — pushes toward a theology of divine transcendence. If God is truly infinite, why would human sin matter enough to provoke such punishment? Protestant theology answers this question christologically, through the cross, but within the narrative world of Job, the question hangs unanswered, creating the theological tension that drives the book forward.
