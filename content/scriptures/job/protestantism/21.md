Job 21 represents his most systematic dismantling of the friends' retribution theology. He begins by asking them simply to "look at me, and be appalled, and lay your hand over your mouth" (21:5) — the same silence they initially offered in 2:13 would serve better than their speeches. His central argument is devastatingly empirical: the wicked often prosper. "Why do the wicked live, reach old age, and grow mighty in power?" (21:7). Their children are established, their houses safe, their livestock productive (21:8-10). They live in prosperity and "go down to Sheol in peace" (21:13).

Protestant interpreters recognize that Job is making the same observation that vexes Psalm 73, Ecclesiastes, and Habakkuk. The difference is that Job presses the point against friends who refuse to acknowledge it. His rhetorical question — "How often is it that the lamp of the wicked is put out?" (21:17) — directly contradicts Bildad's assertion in 18:5. Job argues from observation rather than from theological axiom, and Protestant empirical theology has valued this approach. The Reformers insisted that theology must account for reality as experienced, not merely as theorized.

Job's concluding challenge is pointed: "Will you teach God knowledge, seeing that he judges those who are on high?" (21:22). One person dies in full prosperity while another "dies in bitterness of soul, never having tasted of prosperity. They lie down alike in the dust" (21:25-26). Protestant commentators see this chapter as the necessary destruction of simplistic theodicy that clears ground for God's own answer in chapters 38-41. Job's honesty about the world's moral complexity is closer to divine truth than the friends' tidy theological system. The final verdict — "How then will you comfort me with empty nothings? There is nothing left of your answers but falsehood" (21:34) — closes the second cycle with a devastating judgment on conventional religious wisdom.
