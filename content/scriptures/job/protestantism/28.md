## The Hymn to Wisdom

Job 28 is one of the great poems of the Old Testament — a meditation on wisdom's inaccessibility that stands as an interlude between the dialogue and Job's final defense. The mining imagery (28:1-11) illustrates human ingenuity: mortals can tunnel through rock and find precious metals, but wisdom lies beyond all human discovery. "Where then does wisdom come from?" (28:20).

Protestant interpreters read this chapter as a pivotal theological statement. The friends have claimed to possess wisdom about how God operates; Job has demanded that God explain Himself. Chapter 28 declares that both approaches fail — wisdom is not a human achievement but a divine prerogative. "God understands the way to it, and he alone knows where it dwells" (28:23).

The chapter's conclusion — "The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom, and to shun evil is understanding" (28:28) — has been foundational for Protestant wisdom theology. It does not resolve the problem of suffering but reframes it: wisdom is not an intellectual attainment but a relational posture. The Reformation's insistence on *sola fide* resonates with this — right relationship with God is not achieved through knowledge but through reverent trust.
