Zophar's second speech responds to Job's powerful testimony with a conventional portrait of the wicked person's inevitable downfall. He opens by claiming his "thoughts" compel him to answer and that an inner "spirit" drives his understanding (20:2-3), suggesting divine inspiration for what Protestant commentators recognize as merely human theology. His central thesis — "the exulting of the wicked is short, and the joy of the godless but for a moment" (20:5) — restates the retribution principle without addressing any of Job's arguments.

The extended metaphor of ill-gotten wealth as food that turns to poison in the stomach (20:12-18) is vivid: the wicked person "swallows down riches and vomits them up again; God casts them out of his belly" (20:15). Protestant interpreters note that Zophar's theology contains a grain of truth — Scripture does teach that wickedness ultimately self-destructs — but his application is devastatingly wrong when aimed at Job. The principle that "he will give back the fruit of his toil and will not swallow it down" (20:18) becomes an implicit accusation that Job's former wealth was dishonestly gained.

Zophar's conclusion — "This is the wicked man's portion from God, the heritage decreed for him by God" (20:29) — claims divine authority for his theological system. Protestant commentators see in Zophar the danger of absolute confidence in one's interpretation of providence. The friends have made an idol of their theology, substituting a system about God for an encounter with God. Job, for all his anguish and theological confusion, remains in relationship with the living God, while the friends worship their own certainties.
