## The Problem of Unpunished Evil

Job 24 presents a searing catalogue of social injustice: the wicked move boundary stones, seize flocks, drive away orphans' donkeys, and force the poor into hiding (24:2-4). Protestant social ethics has drawn heavily on this chapter's unflinching depiction of systemic oppression. Job's question "Why are not times of judgment kept by the Almighty?" (24:1) challenges every easy theodicy.

The chapter's vivid portraits — the hungry gleaning in fields not their own (24:6), the naked shivering without covering (24:7-8), the murderer rising at twilight (24:14-16) — function as evidence in Job's legal case against the retribution principle. If God punishes the wicked and rewards the righteous in this life, why does gross injustice persist? Protestant interpreters from Calvin to contemporary scholars acknowledge the force of this argument.

The chapter's conclusion, where Job seems to concede that the wicked are eventually brought low (24:22-24), has generated significant debate. Some Protestant commentators read these verses as Job quoting his friends' position sarcastically. Others see Job acknowledging that divine judgment occurs but on God's timetable, not ours. Either way, the chapter's lasting contribution is its refusal to minimize the reality of evil in God's world.
