Eliphaz the Temanite opens the friends' side of the dialogue with a speech that begins gently but rests on a theological foundation that the book will ultimately challenge. His opening question — "If one ventures a word with you, will you be impatient?" (4:2) — shows initial tact. Protestant commentators note that Eliphaz's early appeal to Job's own record of comforting others (4:3-4) contains genuine pastoral wisdom, yet his underlying premise is flawed: that suffering always indicates personal sin.

The heart of Eliphaz's argument appears in 4:7-8: "Who that was innocent ever perished? Or where were the upright cut off? As I have seen, those who plow iniquity and sow trouble reap the same." This represents what Protestant theologians call "retribution theology" in its rigid form — a mechanistic view of divine justice that equates prosperity with righteousness and suffering with sin. While Proverbs often presents this pattern as a general principle, Eliphaz absolutizes it into an iron law, which the book of Job exists to critique.

Eliphaz's nocturnal vision (4:12-21) adds a mystical dimension to his argument. A spirit whispers, "Can mortal man be in the right before God? Can a man be pure before his Maker?" (4:17). Protestant interpreters observe that this statement is technically true — no human achieves perfect righteousness before God — but Eliphaz weaponizes it to imply that Job's suffering proves specific guilt. The Reformers recognized this as a perversion of the doctrine of total depravity: while all humans are sinful, it does not follow that every particular suffering is punishment for particular sin.
