## Job's Hymn to God's Power

Job 26 opens with biting sarcasm directed at Bildad — "How you have helped the powerless!" (26:2) — before launching into a magnificent hymn to divine power that surpasses anything the friends have offered. Protestant interpreters note the irony: Job, the supposed sinner, articulates a grander theology of God's majesty than his orthodox accusers.

The cosmological imagery is striking: God "spreads out the northern skies over empty space" and "suspends the earth over nothing" (26:7). He stirs the sea and strikes down Rahab (26:12), and by His breath the skies become fair (26:13). Protestant commentators have appreciated how these verses anticipate both the divine speeches (chapters 38-41) and the Christological hymns of the New Testament (Colossians 1:16-17).

The chapter's climax is its most profound verse: "And these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him! Who then can understand the thunder of his power?" (26:14). This is a foundational text for Protestant natural theology — creation reveals God's power, but only as a whisper. Full knowledge of God requires His self-revelation, culminating in Christ.
