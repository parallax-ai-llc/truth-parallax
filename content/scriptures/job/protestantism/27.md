## Job's Oath of Integrity

Job 27 presents Job swearing a solemn oath by the very God who has "denied me justice" (27:2) — a remarkable act of faith. He refuses to concede his friends' position: "Till I die, I will not deny my integrity" (27:5). Protestant theology finds here a model of honest faith that holds fast to personal integrity even when God seems to be an adversary.

The second half of the chapter (27:13-23), where Job describes the fate of the wicked, has puzzled commentators. Some Protestant scholars attribute these verses to Zophar's missing third speech, which would explain why Job appears to argue his opponents' position. Others read Job as distinguishing his own case from that of genuine sinners — he is not arguing for moral relativism but insisting that his suffering does not fit the retribution template.

Luther found Job 27 especially significant because it demonstrates that true righteousness is not self-righteousness. Job's oath is not a claim to sinless perfection but a declaration that he has not committed the specific sins his friends allege. The distinction between acknowledging one's general sinfulness before God and rejecting false accusations is central to Protestant pastoral care.
