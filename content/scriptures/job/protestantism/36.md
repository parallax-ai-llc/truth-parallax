## Elihu Exalts God's Majesty

Job 36 marks a transition in Elihu's rhetoric from argumentation to doxology. He begins with another defense of divine pedagogy — God uses suffering to speak to the righteous (36:8-10) and gives them the choice to "listen and serve him" or "die without knowledge" (36:11-12). But the chapter's second half soars into a hymn to God's power in nature: thunderclouds, lightning, rain (36:26-33).

Protestant interpreters recognize that Elihu is at his best here. His theology of suffering as divine communication (36:15 — "he delivers the afflicted by their affliction") contains genuine insight, even if it does not fully explain Job's situation. The Puritan tradition in particular drew on this principle: God's "severe mercies" shape the believer's character in ways that comfort alone cannot.

The transition from theology to meteorology in 36:26-33 anticipates the divine speeches. Elihu begins pointing away from human reasoning toward the overwhelming evidence of God's power in creation. Protestant commentators note that Elihu may be observing an actual approaching storm — the very storm from which God will speak in chapter 38. If so, Elihu functions as a kind of prophetic forerunner, preparing the way for the divine encounter.
