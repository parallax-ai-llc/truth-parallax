The second cycle of speeches begins with Eliphaz adopting a markedly harsher tone. He accuses Job of undermining piety itself: "You are doing away with the fear of God and hindering meditation before God" (15:4). Protestant interpreters note the escalation — Eliphaz has moved from pastoral concern to theological accusation. His charge that Job's own mouth condemns him (15:6) reflects an inability to distinguish between honest lament and impiety, a confusion that Protestant pastoral theology has consistently warned against.

Eliphaz's rhetorical questions — "Are you the first man who was born? Or were you brought forth before the hills?" (15:7) — sarcastically challenge Job's claim to wisdom. He appeals again to ancestral tradition (15:18-19) and to his own experience, but Protestant commentators observe that he merely repeats the same retribution framework with louder volume. His description of the wicked man's fate (15:20-35) — living in torment, wandering in darkness, destined for desolation — is powerful poetry but transparently aimed at Job. The friends' argument has become circular: Job suffers, therefore Job is wicked, therefore the description of the wicked applies to Job.

The closing image of the wicked man whose "branches will not be green" and who is "like a vine that sheds its unripe grape" (15:32-33) continues the botanical metaphors that run through the dialogue. Protestant interpreters note that Eliphaz's descriptions of the wicked often match Job's actual experience, which is precisely the point — the friends read Job's suffering as a text and arrive at the wrong interpretation because their hermeneutic is flawed.
