## Eliphaz's Final Accusation

In his third and final speech, Eliphaz abandons subtlety and levels specific charges against Job: oppressing the poor, withholding bread from the hungry, and stripping the naked (22:6-9). Protestant interpreters note that these accusations are entirely fabricated — the prologue has already established Job's righteousness (1:1, 1:8). Eliphaz's descent from theological generalities to slanderous specifics reveals the inevitable end of retribution theology when pressed against reality.

The Reformers recognized in Eliphaz a portrait of legalistic religion. Calvin observed that Eliphaz represents those who, unable to reconcile suffering with their theological system, resort to blaming the victim. His call to "agree with God, and be at peace" (22:21) is ironically good advice built on a false premise — Job does not need to repent of sins he has not committed.

Protestant exegesis draws attention to the theological error at work: Eliphaz assumes that God's transcendence (22:2-4) means God has no relational concern for human righteousness, reducing the divine-human relationship to mere transaction. The Reformation's emphasis on grace over works finds a cautionary tale here — Eliphaz's religion is all law and no gospel.
