Job continues his direct address to God with a bold demand for explanation: "I will say to God, Do not condemn me; let me know why you contend against me" (10:2). Protestant interpreters note that Job's willingness to confront God directly, while audacious, reflects a genuine relationship rather than mere religion. The Reformers distinguished between Job's honest wrestling with God and the blasphemy of those who dismiss God entirely. Job's faith is paradoxically demonstrated by his refusal to let God go without an answer.

The most poignant section is Job's reflection on his own creation (10:8-12). He reminds God that divine hands fashioned him "like clay," granted him "life and steadfast love," and that divine care "preserved my spirit." The shift from creation language to accusation — "Yet these things you hid in your heart" (10:13) — suggests that God created Job only to destroy him. Protestant commentators see this as Job's distorted perception under extreme suffering, not as theological truth, but they honor the honesty with which Job expresses it.

Job's closing wish — "Are not my days few? Then cease, and leave me alone, that I may find a little cheer before I go" (10:20) — echoes the death wish of chapter 3 but with added pathos. He describes Sheol as a land of "darkness and deep shadow" (10:21-22) with no return. Protestant interpreters note that Job's limited understanding of the afterlife heightens the book's dramatic tension. Without resurrection hope, suffering becomes unbearable, which makes Job's later declaration in 19:25-26 all the more remarkable.
