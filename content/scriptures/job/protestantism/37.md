## The Thunderstorm and God's Approach

Elihu's final speech in Job 37 is dominated by the imagery of a thunderstorm — God's voice thundering, lightning flashing, ice and snow falling by divine command (37:2-13). Protestant interpreters read this chapter as the dramatic transition between human speech and divine revelation. The storm Elihu describes is the very whirlwind from which God will speak.

The rhetorical questions Elihu poses to Job — "Do you know how God controls the clouds?" (37:15), "Can you join him in spreading out the skies?" (37:18) — directly anticipate God's own interrogation in chapters 38-41. Protestant theology reads this convergence as theologically significant: even flawed human theology, when it turns from accusation to wonder, can point toward authentic encounter with God.

Elihu's concluding statement — "The Almighty is beyond our reach and exalted in power; in his great righteousness, he does not oppress" (37:23) — is both his finest theological moment and his most ironic. He affirms what the book will ultimately confirm: God's ways are beyond human comprehension. Yet his next assertion — that "he does not have regard for any who are wise in heart" (37:24) — may be his final misstatement, for God is about to have very direct regard for Job.
