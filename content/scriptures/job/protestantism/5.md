Eliphaz continues his first speech with a mixture of proverbial wisdom and misapplied theology. His observation that "man is born to trouble as the sparks fly upward" (5:7) is a statement Protestant commentators widely affirm as capturing the universal human condition after the Fall. Yet Eliphaz uses this general truth to set up a specific prescription: that Job should simply submit to divine discipline and expect restoration.

The hymnic passage praising God's power (5:9-16) contains beautiful theology — God "does great things and unsearchable, marvelous things without number" (5:9), who "sets on high those who are lowly" (5:11). Protestant interpreters note the irony that Eliphaz correctly describes God's character but misapplies it to Job's situation. His counsel, "Behold, blessed is the one whom God reproves; therefore despise not the discipline of the Almighty" (5:17), echoes Proverbs 3:11-12 and is quoted in Hebrews 12:5-6. The theology is sound in principle but wrongly applied to Job, who is not being disciplined for sin.

Eliphaz's concluding promise of restoration (5:19-26) — deliverance from famine, war, and wild beasts, with a peaceful death in old age — represents what Protestant theologians identify as a prosperity gospel in embryonic form. The final verse, "Behold, this we have searched out; it is true. Hear, and know it for your good" (5:27), reveals Eliphaz's overconfidence in his theological system. The Reformers cautioned against this kind of theological certainty that closes itself off from the mystery of God's ways.
