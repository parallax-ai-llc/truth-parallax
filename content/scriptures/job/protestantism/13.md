Job escalates his case with one of the book's most memorable declarations: "But I would speak to the Almighty, and I desire to argue my case with God" (13:3). Protestant interpreters have long admired Job's courage here. Unlike his friends, who theologize about God from a safe distance, Job insists on a direct encounter. The Reformers saw in Job a prefiguring of the Protestant principle of unmediated access to God — no human intermediary can substitute for a personal encounter with the divine.

Job's indictment of his friends is devastating: "As for you, you whitewash with lies; worthless physicians are you all" (13:4). His charge that they "speak falsely for God" and "show partiality toward him" (13:7-8) raises a profound ethical question that Protestant theology takes seriously: Is it acceptable to defend God with bad arguments? Job argues emphatically that it is not. God does not need human lies in His defense, and those who distort truth to protect divine honor actually dishonor God. This principle undergirds the Protestant commitment to honest theological inquiry.

The climactic declaration — "Though he slay me, I will hope in him; yet I will argue my ways to his face" (13:15) — is one of the most debated verses in the book. The Hebrew text is ambiguous: the Ketiv (written text) reads "I will not hope" while the Qere (reading tradition) reads "I will hope." Protestant translations have generally followed the Qere, finding in this verse the supreme expression of faith that persists even when God appears as enemy. This is faith at its most stripped-down — trust without evidence, hope without comfort.
