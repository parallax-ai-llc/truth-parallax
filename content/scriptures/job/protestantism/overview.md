## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Job is shaped by the Reformation emphasis on *sola scriptura* and the sovereignty of God. The book is valued as a powerful exploration of theodicy — the question of God's justice in permitting suffering — and is read as a corrective to simplistic "prosperity theology" that equates righteousness with material blessing.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Sovereignty and Mystery
Reformed interpreters, following Calvin, emphasize God's sovereign freedom. God is not obligated to explain His purposes to humanity. The divine speeches (chapters 38–41) are read as God asserting His sovereign wisdom beyond human comprehension.

### Justification and the Mediator
Luther saw in Job a prefiguration of the believer's struggle — justified by faith yet experiencing God as hidden (*Deus absconditus*). Job's cry for a mediator (9:33, 16:19, 19:25) is read as pointing toward Christ.

### Pastoral and Practical
Many Protestant pastors turn to Job for counsel on suffering. The book dismantles the "retribution theology" of the friends while affirming that God is present even in silence.

## Theological Emphases

- **The sovereignty of God**: God permits suffering for purposes beyond human understanding
- **The failure of retribution theology**: Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar represent a false equation of suffering with sin
- **The mediator figure**: Job's longing for an advocate (19:25) is understood as anticipating Christ
- **Faith under trial**: Job's faith endures despite the absence of explanation
- **Restoration as grace**: The epilogue demonstrates God's restorative mercy
