## Elihu's Angry Entrance

Elihu bursts onto the scene in Job 32 — a young man burning with anger at both Job and the three friends. Protestant interpreters are sharply divided on Elihu's role. Some, following Calvin, view him as a brash young man who adds little to the argument. Others see him as a genuine theological advance, preparing the way for God's speeches.

Elihu's rationale for speaking is significant: "It is not only the old who are wise, not only the aged who understand what is right" (32:9). His claim is that the "spirit in a person, the breath of the Almighty" (32:8) gives understanding regardless of age. Protestant theology, with its emphasis on the priesthood of all believers, resonates with this democratization of wisdom — though it also recognizes that Elihu's self-important preamble (32:14-22) undermines his credibility.

The narrative detail that the three friends "stopped answering Job because he was righteous in his own eyes" (32:1) is crucial. Protestant exegesis notes that the author does not say Job was wrong — only that the friends perceived him that way. The reader, who knows the prologue, understands what the friends cannot: Job's self-righteousness is, in this case, justified.
