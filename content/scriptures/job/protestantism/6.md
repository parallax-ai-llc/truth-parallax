Job responds to Eliphaz with a passionate defense of his anguish. His wish that his "vexation were weighed, and all my calamity laid in the balances" (6:2) challenges Eliphaz's implied accusation that his complaint is disproportionate. Protestant interpreters see in Job's argument a legitimate insistence that suffering be taken seriously rather than explained away with theological formulas. The imagery of God's "arrows" and "terrors" arrayed against him (6:4) frames his experience as divine assault, not mere misfortune.

The extended metaphor of the friends as unreliable wadis — seasonal streams that overflow in winter but vanish when most needed in summer heat (6:15-20) — is among the most powerful images of failed friendship in Scripture. Protestant pastoral theology draws from this passage the principle that theological correctness without empathy becomes cruelty. Job expected comfort from his friends but received only lectures. His pointed question, "Teach me, and I will be silent; make me understand how I have gone astray" (6:24), challenges Eliphaz to provide specific evidence of wrongdoing rather than relying on general principles.

Job's closing plea — "Is there any injustice on my tongue? Cannot my palate discern the cause of calamity?" (6:30) — asserts his ability to distinguish between deserved punishment and undeserved suffering. Protestant commentators note that Job is not claiming sinlessness but rather protesting the disproportionality between any sin he may have committed and the devastation he has experienced. This distinction between general sinfulness and specific guilt is crucial to Reformed theology's understanding of suffering.
