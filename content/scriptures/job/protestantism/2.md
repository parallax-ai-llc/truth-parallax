Job 2 intensifies the trial from material loss to bodily suffering. The second heavenly council scene (2:1-6) repeats the structure of chapter 1, but now Satan requests permission to strike Job's own flesh. Protestant interpreters observe the escalating logic: Satan's argument that "skin for skin" (2:4) reveals a cynical anthropology — the claim that all human devotion is ultimately self-serving. This is the fundamental question the book addresses, and Protestant theology answers it by pointing to grace-empowered faithfulness rather than autonomous human goodness.

God's statement that Job "still holds fast his integrity, although you incited me against him to destroy him without reason" (2:3) is theologically significant. The phrase "without reason" (Hebrew *hinnam*) echoes 1:9 where Satan asked whether Job fears God "for nothing" (*hinnam*). Protestant commentators note the irony: Satan accused Job of serving God for profit, but God acknowledges that Job suffers without cause. This wordplay frames the book's central theological tension between retributive justice and gratuitous grace.

Job's wife urges him to "curse God and die" (2:9), and Job's rebuke — "Shall we receive good from God, and shall we not receive evil?" (2:10) — demonstrates what Protestant interpreters regard as mature faith that accepts divine providence in its totality. The arrival of the three friends (2:11-13) and their seven days of silent mourning represent genuine compassion before their theology leads them astray. Their initial silence, Protestant pastors often note, was the wisest thing they did throughout the entire book.
