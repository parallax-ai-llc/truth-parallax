## Elihu Defends God's Justice

Elihu's second speech in chapter 34 is a vigorous defense of divine justice. He rejects Job's claim that God has denied him justice (34:5-6) and asserts the foundational principle: "It is unthinkable that God would do wrong, that the Almighty would pervert justice" (34:10). Protestant theology broadly affirms this principle while recognizing that Elihu applies it too mechanically.

Elihu's argument about divine governance is theologically sophisticated: God sustains all life by His will, and if He withdrew His spirit, "all humanity would perish together" (34:14-15). Therefore God's justice is not like a human ruler's — it is intrinsic to His very nature as the sustainer of existence. Protestant systematic theology draws on passages like this in articulating the doctrine of divine conservation.

However, Protestant commentators note Elihu's overreach when he declares that Job "adds rebellion to his sin" by multiplying words against God (34:37). Like the three friends before him, Elihu assumes that challenging God's administration is itself sinful. The divine speeches that follow will vindicate neither Job's claim to understand God's purposes nor Elihu's claim that questioning God is rebellion — God simply redirects the entire conversation.
