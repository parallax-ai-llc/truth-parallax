Job's anguished curse upon the day of his birth (3:1-10) marks a dramatic shift from the patient sufferer of chapters 1-2. Protestant interpreters have long wrestled with this transition: Job does not curse God, but he curses his own existence with a ferocity that echoes Jeremiah 20:14-18. Calvin argued that Job's outburst, while extreme, remained within the bounds of faith because it was directed at his circumstances rather than at God's character. The raw honesty of Job's lament has become foundational to Protestant pastoral theology's insistence that authentic faith can coexist with profound grief.

The central question of the chapter — "Why is light given to him who is in misery?" (3:20) — frames the existential crisis that drives the entire poetic dialogue. Job longs for death as rest (3:13-19), describing Sheol as a place where "the wicked cease from troubling, and there the weary are at rest." Protestant commentators note that Job's understanding of the afterlife here is limited and pre-Christian; the fuller revelation of resurrection hope comes later, particularly in Job 19:25-27. This progressive revelation is a key hermeneutical principle in Protestant reading of the Old Testament.

Job's concluding words — "For the thing that I fear comes upon me, and what I dread befalls me" (3:25) — reveal that beneath his former prosperity lay an undercurrent of anxiety. Luther found pastoral comfort in this admission: even the most righteous experience fear, and acknowledging that fear before God is more honest than pretending invulnerability. Protestant preaching has consistently used Job 3 to validate the place of lament in the life of faith.
