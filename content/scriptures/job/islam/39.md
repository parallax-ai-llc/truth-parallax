## The Wild Kingdom

God's survey of the animal kingdom in Job 39 resonates profoundly with the Quranic theology of animals as communities (*umam*) before God. The Quran declares: "There is no creature on earth or bird that flies with its wings but that they are communities like you" (6:38). The mountain goats, wild donkeys, ostriches, and eagles of Job 39 are not merely examples of divine power but communities in their own right, each with its own relationship to the Creator.

The diversity of the creatures — the wild donkey's freedom (39:5-8), the ostrich's foolishness (39:13-18), the war horse's courage (39:19-25) — reflects the Quran's celebration of creation's variety as a sign of divine wisdom: "And of His signs is the creation of the heavens and the earth and the diversity of your languages and your colors. Indeed in that are signs for those of knowledge" (30:22). Each creature manifests a different *sifah* (attribute) of the Creator.

Islamic environmental ethics (*fiqh al-bi'ah*) draws on the principle articulated in both Job 39 and the Quran: creation has value independent of human utility. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) established the concept of the *hima* (nature reserve) and forbade the needless destruction of animals and plants. If Allah cares for creatures that serve no human purpose — the wild ox, the hawk, the eagle — then creation's value extends far beyond human calculation, and humanity's role is that of *khalifah* (steward), not owner.
