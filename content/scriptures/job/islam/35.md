## Does God Benefit from Human Righteousness?

Elihu's question — whether human righteousness or sin affects God (35:6-7) — engages the Islamic concept of *ghina* (divine self-sufficiency). The Quran declares: "O mankind, you are those in need of Allah, while Allah is the Free of need, the Praiseworthy" (35:15). Allah is *al-Ghani* (the Self-Sufficient) — human worship adds nothing to His essential reality, and human sin diminishes nothing. This is a fundamental principle of Islamic theology.

Yet Islamic tradition nuances this divine self-sufficiency with a theology of divine love. A hadith qudsi records Allah saying: "I was a hidden treasure and I loved to be known, so I created creation" (cited by Ibn Arabi). While this hadith's authenticity is debated, the sentiment it expresses — that God created out of love and delights in His creatures' worship — represents an important dimension of Islamic spirituality. Allah is self-sufficient yet chooses to be in relationship.

Elihu's critique of those who cry out in distress without truly seeking God (35:10-12) resonates with the Islamic distinction between *du'a* (genuine supplication) and mere complaint. The Quran teaches: "Call upon Me; I will respond to you" (40:60), but true *du'a* requires sincerity (*ikhlas*) and the orientation of the heart toward Allah. However, applying this critique to Ayyub — a prophet whose entire struggle is directed toward God — misses the mark, for prophetic complaint is itself a form of worship.
