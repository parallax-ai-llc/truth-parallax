## Job's Oath of Innocence

Job 31's comprehensive ethical self-examination — covering lust (31:1), dishonesty (31:5), adultery (31:9), injustice to servants (31:13), neglect of the poor (31:16), and idolatry (31:24) — presents a moral inventory that corresponds closely to the Islamic concept of *muhasabah* (self-reckoning). The Quran commands: "O you who believe, fear Allah, and let every soul look to what it has put forth for tomorrow" (59:18). Ayyub's detailed self-examination exemplifies this practice.

Job's declaration of equal dignity with his servants — "Did not he who made me in the womb make them?" (31:15) — articulates a principle central to Islamic social ethics. The Prophet Muhammad's Farewell Sermon declared: "An Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab, nor does a non-Arab have any superiority over an Arab... except by piety and good action." Job's egalitarian ethics, rooted in shared creation, align perfectly with Islam's rejection of all human hierarchies except that of *taqwa* (God-consciousness).

Job's concluding challenge to God (31:35) is read in Islamic tradition with respectful caution. While Islam honors Ayyub's integrity, it also emphasizes *adab ma'a Allah* — proper comportment before the Divine. The Quran records prophetic supplications of extraordinary boldness — Ibrahim arguing for the people of Lut, Musa speaking frankly to Allah — but always within the framework of ultimate submission. Ayyub's demand for an answer, in Islamic reading, hovers at the boundary of prophetic boldness and the limits of creaturely propriety.
