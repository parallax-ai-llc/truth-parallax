The escalation of Ayyub's trial to physical suffering is central to the Islamic narrative. The Quran states: "And [mention] Ayyub, when he called to his Lord, 'Indeed, adversity has touched me, and you are the Most Merciful of the merciful'" (21:83). Muslim commentators, including al-Tha'labi in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* (Stories of the Prophets) and Ibn Kathir, describe Ayyub's illness in vivid detail — afflictions covering his entire body, social ostracism, loss of everything except his faith. The *hadith* literature and *tafsir* traditions expand on the Quranic account, drawing partially on biblical and rabbinic sources while framing the narrative within Islamic theological categories.

The role of Ayyub's wife in Islamic tradition differs significantly from the biblical account. While the Bible records her saying "Curse God and die" (2:9), Islamic tradition generally presents her more sympathetically. Al-Tha'labi identifies her as Rahmah (or in some traditions, Liya, a descendant of Yusuf/Joseph) and describes her as faithfully serving Ayyub throughout his illness, working as a servant to provide for him when all other resources were exhausted. The hadith traditions mention a moment of wavering when she suggested Ayyub ask God for healing, but even this is treated as human weakness rather than impiety. Her steadfast service is often cited as a model of marital devotion.

The Islamic emphasis on Ayyub's *integrity* during bodily suffering connects to the broader Quranic theme of prophetic infallibility (*ismah*). While scholars debate the precise scope of prophetic protection from sin, there is consensus that the prophets maintained their faith and moral character through every trial. Ayyub's refusal to despair or lose faith, even at the extremity of physical agony, demonstrates the *ismah* that Islamic theology attributes to God's chosen messengers. His patience is not ordinary human endurance but prophetically enhanced *sabr*.
