## God Speaks from the Whirlwind

The divine speech from the whirlwind in Job 38 represents, from an Islamic perspective, a profound instance of divine self-revelation (*wahy*). Allah's opening question — "Where were you when I laid the earth's foundation?" (38:4) — parallels the Quranic rhetorical questions that invite humanity to recognize its dependence on the Creator: "Were they created by nothing, or were they the creators?" (52:35). Both the Bible and the Quran use interrogation as a pedagogical instrument of divine humility.

The cascade of questions about creation — the sea's boundaries (38:8-11), the dawn (38:12-15), the storehouses of snow (38:22-23), the constellations (38:31-33) — closely parallels the Quranic *ayat al-khalq* (signs of creation) found throughout Surahs like al-An'am (6), al-Nahl (16), and ar-Rahman (55). The Quran asks: "Then which of the favors of your Lord will you deny?" (55:13). Both texts invite the reader from intellectual knowledge to reverential wonder before the Creator's power and wisdom.

Significantly, God does not explain Ayyub's suffering. Islamic theology finds this deeply meaningful: the ultimate answer to the problem of suffering is not a theological proposition but an encounter with Allah Himself. The Sufi master Rumi wrote that explanations are "the foam on the surface of the sea" while divine encounter is "the sea itself." Job 38 confirms the Islamic teaching that *ma'rifah* (experiential knowledge of God) transcends *ilm* (intellectual knowledge about God).
