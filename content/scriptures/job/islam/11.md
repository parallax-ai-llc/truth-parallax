Zophar's meditation on divine transcendence (11:7-9) — the unfathomable depth and height of God's wisdom — connects to the Islamic concept of *ghayb* (the unseen, the hidden reality beyond human perception). The Quran opens with the declaration that the believers are "those who believe in the unseen" (2:3). Islamic theology categorizes certain knowledge as belonging exclusively to *'ilm al-ghayb* (knowledge of the unseen), accessible only to God and shared with whom He wills. Zophar correctly identifies divine incomprehensibility but, ironically, then proceeds to claim knowledge of precisely the sort that belongs to the *ghayb* — namely, Job's hidden guilt and its relationship to his suffering.

The assertion that "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" (11:6) is evaluated against the Islamic principle that God's mercy exceeds His wrath. A hadith qudsi (sacred hadith, words of God transmitted through the Prophet) records God saying: "My mercy prevails over My wrath" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Zophar's implication that God is being lenient with Job inverts this principle, suggesting that Job deserves worse — a claim incompatible with the Islamic vision of a God whose fundamental disposition toward His servants is merciful.

Zophar's call for repentance (11:13-14) employs language that resonates with the Islamic concept of *tawbah* (repentance, turning back to God). The Quran promises: "And whoever does evil or wrongs himself but then seeks forgiveness of Allah will find Allah Forgiving and Merciful" (4:110). Islamic theology affirms the efficacy of sincere repentance unconditionally. The problem with Zophar's counsel is not the call to repentance itself — Islam values *tawbah* as a perpetual practice for all believers — but the false premise that Job has committed specific sins requiring special repentance. The Quran's own characterization of Ayyub as "an excellent servant" (38:44) contradicts Zophar's accusation.
