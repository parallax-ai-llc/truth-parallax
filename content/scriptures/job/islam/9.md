Job's overwhelming sense of divine power (9:5-10) — God who moves mountains, shakes the earth, commands the stars — resonates profoundly with the Quranic descriptions of divine omnipotence (*qudrah*). The Quran states: "Indeed, Allah holds the heavens and the earth, lest they cease. And if they should cease, no one could hold them after Him" (35:41). Islamic theology understands divine power not merely as the ability to act but as the sustaining force that keeps all of creation in existence moment by moment. Job's hymn to cosmic power, while expressed in anguish, accurately reflects the Islamic vision of God's absolute sovereignty over creation.

Job's complaint that God "destroys both the blameless and the wicked" (9:22) raises a challenge that Islamic theology addresses through the doctrine of *qadar* (divine decree). The Quran affirms: "No disaster strikes upon the earth or among yourselves except that it is in a register before We bring it into being" (57:22). Islamic theology holds this in tension with divine justice: apparent injustice in this world will be rectified in the afterlife. The Quran promises: "So whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it" (99:7-8). Job's observation is empirically accurate for *dunya* but does not account for the eschatological dimension that Islamic theology considers decisive.

Job's wish for a mediator (9:33) is reframed in Islamic theology through the concept of *shafa'ah* (intercession). The Quran teaches that intercession belongs to God alone (39:44) but that God may grant permission for certain intercessors on the Day of Judgment. The Prophet Muhammad is understood in Islamic tradition as the supreme intercessor (*al-shafi'*) who will intercede for his community before God. Job's longing for an intermediary is thus fulfilled, in Islamic understanding, through the prophetic institution itself — the prophets serve as mediators between God and humanity, with Muhammad holding the ultimate intercessory role.
