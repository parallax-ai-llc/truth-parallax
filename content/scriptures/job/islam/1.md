In Islamic tradition, Job is known as *Ayyub* (peace be upon him) and is recognized as one of the prophets mentioned in the Quran. The Quran references Ayyub in several passages (4:163, 6:84, 21:83-84, 38:41-44), praising his patience (*sabr*) in the face of extreme affliction. The opening chapter of the biblical Job, which describes Ayyub's prosperity and subsequent trial, is understood by Muslim scholars within the framework of *ibtila'* (divine testing) — the Quranic principle that God tests those He loves most, particularly the prophets, to demonstrate their faithfulness and elevate their spiritual rank.

The heavenly council scene (1:6-12), in which Satan (*Shaytan* or *Iblis*) challenges Job's sincerity before God, is treated cautiously by Islamic commentators. The Quran presents Iblis as a jinn who refused to prostrate before Adam (2:34, 7:11-18, 15:28-35) and was granted respite until the Day of Judgment to tempt humanity. Muslim scholars accept the general principle that Shaytan seeks to lead the righteous astray but emphasize that such activity is always under divine control — consistent with *tawhid* (the absolute oneness and sovereignty of God). Al-Tabari records traditions about Iblis seeking permission to afflict Ayyub, while Ibn Kathir notes that the details of Satan's role come primarily from *Isra'iliyyat* (Judeo-Christian traditions) and must be evaluated against Quranic principles.

Job's patient response — praising God in the face of loss — exemplifies the Islamic virtue of *sabr* (patience, steadfast endurance). The Quran states: "Indeed, We found him patient, an excellent servant. Indeed, he was one who repeatedly turned back [to Allah]" (38:44). Muslim commentators see in Ayyub the model of prophetic *sabr*, which is not passive resignation but active trust in divine wisdom. The Quran promises that "indeed, with hardship comes ease" (94:5-6), a principle that Ayyub's narrative ultimately fulfills.
