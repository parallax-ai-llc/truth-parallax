Job's observation that the wicked prosper (21:7-16) addresses a challenge the Quran explicitly acknowledges and responds to. The Quran states: "Let not the [uninhibited] movement of the disbelievers throughout the land deceive you. It is but a small enjoyment; then their [final] refuge is Hell, and wretched is the resting place" (3:196-197). Islamic theology addresses the prosperity of the wicked through its robust eschatological framework: earthly prosperity is temporary (*mata' al-dunya* — the enjoyment of the world), while the consequences of the Day of Judgment are eternal. The Quran consistently warns against being deceived by the apparent success of the ungodly.

Job's empirical challenge — "How often is it that the lamp of the wicked is put out?" (21:17) — finds its Islamic answer in the concept of *imhal* (divine respite, delay). The Quran explains: "And let not those who disbelieve ever think that [because] We extend their time [of enjoyment] it is better for them. We only extend it for them so that they may increase in sin, and for them is a humiliating punishment" (3:178). Islamic theology teaches that God's delay in punishing the wicked is not evidence of approval but an extension of opportunity for repentance — or, for the persistently unjust, an increase of their ultimate accountability.

Job's closing dismissal of his friends' theology (21:34) — "How then will you comfort me with empty nothings?" — resonates with the Quranic critique of those who speak about God without knowledge: "And of the people is he who disputes about Allah without knowledge and follows every rebellious devil" (22:3). Islamic epistemology requires that claims about divine purposes be grounded in revelation (*wahy*) rather than human speculation (*zann*). The friends' speeches represent *zann* — conjecture presented as certainty — which the Quran repeatedly warns against: "Indeed, assumption avails not against the truth at all" (53:28). Job's rejection of their theology aligns with the Quranic standard of epistemic humility before the divine.
