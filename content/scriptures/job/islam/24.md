## The Problem of Unpunished Evil

Job 24's catalogue of social injustice — the powerful exploiting the vulnerable, moving boundaries, seizing orphans' property (24:2-4) — engages the Islamic concept of *zulm* (oppression/injustice), which the Quran condemns in the strongest terms. Allah declares in a hadith qudsi: "O My servants, I have forbidden *zulm* for Myself and have made it forbidden among you, so do not oppress one another" (Muslim). The exploitation Job describes is an offense against the divine order.

The vivid descriptions of poverty — the hungry gleaning bare fields, the naked shivering without shelter (24:6-8) — evoke the Quranic obligation of *zakat* (obligatory charity) and *sadaqah* (voluntary charity). Islam considers the failure to care for the poor a grave sin: "Have you seen the one who denies the religion? That is the one who drives away the orphan and does not encourage the feeding of the poor" (107:1-3). The injustices of Job 24 represent a comprehensive violation of Islamic social ethics.

Job's question about why God does not schedule times of judgment (24:1) is answered in Islamic eschatology with absolute clarity: the Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*) will bring every act of injustice to account. The Quran repeatedly assures believers that "not an atom's weight of injustice" will go unaddressed (4:40). The delay of visible punishment in this life is part of Allah's wisdom — granting time for repentance while reserving ultimate judgment for the Hereafter.
