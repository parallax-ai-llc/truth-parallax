Job's wish that his suffering could be weighed (6:2-3) resonates with the Quranic imagery of the *mizan* (scales) that will be used on the Day of Judgment: "And We place the scales of justice for the Day of Resurrection, so no soul will be treated unjustly at all" (21:47). Islamic eschatology envisions a cosmic weighing of deeds in which every action receives its just measure. Job's demand for proportional assessment anticipates this eschatological principle, insisting that his suffering be measured against any possible wrongdoing rather than assumed to indicate guilt.

The wadi metaphor (6:15-20) — unreliable friends who fail when most needed — is evaluated within the Islamic ethic of *ukhuwwah* (brotherhood). The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) taught: "A Muslim is the brother of a fellow Muslim. He should not oppress him, nor should he hand him over to the oppressor. Whoever fulfills the needs of his brother, Allah will fulfill his needs" (Sahih al-Bukhari). The friends' failure to support Job violates this foundational principle of Islamic brotherhood, which requires active solidarity rather than theological lecturing.

Job's demand for specific charges (6:24) aligns with Islamic legal principles of *bayyinah* (evidence) and *hujjah* (proof). Sharia law requires that accusations be substantiated — the standard for serious accusations (*qadhf*, false accusation of sexual immorality) is four witnesses, reflecting the extreme seriousness with which Islamic jurisprudence treats unsubstantiated accusations. The Quran warns: "O you who have believed, if there comes to you a disobedient one with information, investigate, lest you harm a people out of ignorance" (49:6). Job's friends fail this Quranic standard by accusing without evidence.
