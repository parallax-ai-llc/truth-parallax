## The Hymn to Wisdom

The Wisdom Poem of Job 28 engages the Islamic concept of *hikmah* (wisdom), which the Quran identifies as a divine gift of surpassing value: "He gives wisdom to whom He wills, and whoever has been given wisdom has certainly been given much good" (2:269). The poem's contrast between human technological achievement (mining, 28:1-11) and wisdom's inaccessibility mirrors the Islamic distinction between worldly knowledge (*ilm al-dunya*) and divine wisdom (*hikmah ilahiyyah*).

The declaration that wisdom cannot be purchased with gold or precious stones (28:15-19) aligns with the Quranic teaching that true knowledge comes from Allah alone. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) prayed: "O Allah, I ask You for beneficial knowledge" (Ibn Majah), distinguishing knowledge that draws one closer to God from knowledge that is merely technical. Islamic intellectual tradition, from al-Kindi to al-Ghazali, has maintained this hierarchy: the highest *ilm* (knowledge) is *ma'rifah* (knowledge of God).

The chapter's conclusion — "The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom" (28:28) — is virtually identical to the Quranic statement: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous (*atqakum*) of you" (49:13). *Taqwa* — consciousness of God, translated variably as "fear," "piety," or "God-consciousness" — is the Islamic equivalent of "fear of the Lord." Both traditions affirm that wisdom is not intellectual brilliance but a fundamental orientation of the heart toward the Divine.
