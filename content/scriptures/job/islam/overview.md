## Overview

In Islam, Job is known as Ayyub (Arabic: أيوب) and is honored as a prophet of extraordinary patience (*sabr*). The Quran mentions Ayyub in several passages (4:163, 6:84, 21:83-84, 38:41-44), presenting him as a model of faithful endurance through suffering. While the biblical Book of Job is not accepted as preserved revelation, the Quranic account and hadith literature preserve a distinctive Islamic narrative of the prophet's trial and restoration.

## Quranic References

The Quran's treatment of Ayyub is concise but theologically rich:

- **Quran 21:83-84** — "And [mention] Job, when he called to his Lord, 'Indeed, adversity has touched me, and You are the most merciful of the merciful.' So We responded to him and removed what afflicted him of adversity. And We gave him [back] his family and the like thereof with them as mercy from Us."
- **Quran 38:41-44** — The fuller account: Ayyub strikes the ground with his foot and a healing spring emerges; God commands him to strike with a bundle of grass (to fulfill an oath without harming his wife).
- **Quran 4:163, 6:84** — Ayyub listed among the prophets to whom God sent revelation.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
Islamic theology holds that prophets are protected from major sin. Unlike the biblical Job who questions God's justice, the Quranic Ayyub is presented as a model of patience who never wavers in faith. His cry in 21:83 is understood as a supplication, not a complaint.

### *Sabr* (Patient Endurance)
Ayyub is the supreme exemplar of *sabr* in Islamic tradition. His patience is not passive resignation but active trust in God's wisdom and mercy. The hadith literature describes his suffering lasting 18 years, during which he never complained.

### Isra'iliyyat
Many details in Islamic retellings of Ayyub's story derive from biblical and Jewish sources (Isra'iliyyat). Scholars distinguish between:
- **Accepted**: Details confirmed by the Quran (trial, patience, restoration)
- **Rejected**: Elements suggesting Ayyub questioned or challenged God

## Theological Emphases

- **Patience as prophetic virtue**: Ayyub's *sabr* is the defining quality of his prophethood
- **Divine mercy and healing**: God responds to sincere supplication with direct intervention
- **Trial as purification**: Suffering tests and refines faith
- **No questioning of God**: The Islamic Ayyub does not engage in the theological disputations found in the biblical text
- **Restoration as divine promise**: God always rewards patient endurance

## Major Commentators

| Commentator | Era | Work |
|-------------|-----|------|
| Al-Tabari | 9th-10th century | *Jami' al-Bayan* and *Tarikh* — extensive Ayyub narratives |
| Ibn Kathir | 14th century | *Qisas al-Anbiya'* — prophetic biography of Ayyub |
| Al-Tha'labi | 11th century | *Arais al-Majalis* — narrative expansion |
| Al-Qurtubi | 13th century | *Al-Jami'* — legal-theological commentary |
| Sayyid Qutb | 20th century | *Fi Zilal al-Quran* — modern reflection on patience |
