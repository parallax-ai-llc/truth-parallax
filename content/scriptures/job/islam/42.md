## Repentance and Restoration

Job's final response — "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you" (42:5) — represents the transition from *ilm* (knowledge about God) to *ma'rifah* (experiential knowledge of God) that Islamic mysticism considers the goal of the spiritual journey. Al-Ghazali described this as the difference between knowing that honey is sweet and tasting its sweetness. Ayyub's encounter with Allah transforms his understanding from theological propositions to lived experience.

The Quranic account of Ayyub's restoration directly parallels Job 42. The Quran records: "And We restored his family to him and [gave him] the like thereof with them as mercy from Us and a reminder for those of understanding" (38:43). Islamic interpretation emphasizes that the restoration is an act of divine *rahmah* (mercy), not a reward earned through patience. Ayyub's *sabr* was genuine, but the restoration flows from Allah's generosity, not from any human claim.

God's rebuke of the three friends (42:7) and Job's intercession for them (42:8-9) are read in Islamic tradition through the concept of prophetic *shafa'ah* (intercession). The prophets, by virtue of their closeness to Allah, are given the privilege of interceding for others. Ayyub's willingness to pray for those who wronged him demonstrates the prophetic character that Islam celebrates: magnanimity, forgiveness, and the desire for others' welfare even at personal cost. The Quran affirms: "The good deed and the evil deed are not equal. Repel evil by that which is better, and then the one between whom and you there was enmity will become a devoted friend" (41:34).
