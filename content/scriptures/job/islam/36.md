## Elihu Exalts God's Majesty

Elihu's assertion that God "delivers the afflicted by their affliction" (36:15) connects to the Islamic understanding that trial (*bala'*) can be a vehicle of divine mercy. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) taught: "Wonderful is the affair of the believer, for there is good for him in every matter — and this is not the case with anyone except the believer. If he is happy, he thanks Allah, and thus there is good for him; and if he is harmed, he shows patience, and thus there is good for him" (Muslim). Elihu articulates a principle that Islam develops extensively.

The chapter's transition to natural theology — thunder, clouds, lightning (36:26-33) — reflects the Quranic *ayat* tradition. The Quran repeatedly invites contemplation of natural phenomena as signs of Allah's power: "Do you not see that Allah drives clouds? Then He brings them together, then He makes them into a mass, and you see the rain emerge from within it" (24:43). Every natural phenomenon is an *ayah* — a sign pointing to the Creator. Elihu's meteorological attention mirrors this Quranic practice.

Islamic theology reads the approach of the storm as a manifestation of *jalal* (divine majesty) — one of the two complementary aspects of God's self-revelation, alongside *jamal* (divine beauty). The storm is terrifying and beautiful simultaneously, evoking both *khawf* (fear) and *raja'* (hope). This balance of *jalal* and *jamal* characterizes the Islamic experience of God and prepares for the divine speech from the whirlwind.
