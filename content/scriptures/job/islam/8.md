Bildad's assertion of divine justice — "Does God pervert *mishpat*?" (8:3) — resonates with the Islamic theological concept of *'adl* (divine justice). The Mu'tazili school of Islamic theology made divine justice a central principle, arguing that God necessarily acts justly and that human reason can discern the principles of divine justice. The Ash'ari school, which became dominant in Sunni Islam, countered that God's actions define justice rather than conforming to a pre-existing standard — what God does is just because God does it. Bildad's theology aligns more with the Mu'tazili position, while the book of Job's ultimate resolution (God answering from the whirlwind rather than explaining) resonates with the Ash'ari emphasis on divine sovereignty beyond human rational categories.

Bildad's suggestion that Job's children died for their own sin (8:4) is evaluated against the Quranic principle of individual moral responsibility: "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (6:164, 17:15, 35:18, 53:38). This principle, repeated multiple times in the Quran, directly contradicts any theology that attributes children's death to parents' sin or vice versa. While Islamic tradition acknowledges collective trials (*fitna*) that affect communities, the moral responsibility for sin is strictly individual. Bildad's casual attribution of guilt to the dead violates this Quranic axiom.

Bildad's appeal to ancestral tradition (8:8-10) is evaluated within the Quranic critique of uncritical adherence to inherited beliefs. The Quran repeatedly challenges those who say, "We found our fathers upon a religion, and we are guided by their footsteps" (43:22-23). While Islamic theology values *ijma'* (scholarly consensus) and the *sunnah* (prophetic tradition), it insists that these be grounded in revelation rather than mere custom. Bildad's error, in Islamic perspective, is not in respecting tradition but in failing to submit tradition to the criterion of divine revelation.
