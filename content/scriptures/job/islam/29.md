## Job's Remembrance of Former Glory

Job's recollection of his former blessedness in chapter 29 presents a portrait of prophetic virtue that Islamic tradition recognizes in Ayyub. His rescue of the poor, care for the orphan, and justice for the stranger (29:12-16) exemplify the *akhlaq* (moral character) that the Quran praises: "And what will make you know what the steep path is? It is the freeing of a slave, or feeding on a day of severe hunger an orphan of near relationship, or a needy person in misery" (90:12-16).

The intimacy with God that Job recalls — "when God's lamp shone on my head" (29:3) — reflects what Sufi tradition calls *uns* (divine intimacy). The great mystics describe seasons of extraordinary closeness to Allah, where the heart is illuminated by divine light (*nur*). Ibn Ata'illah taught that these experiences of *uns* are gifts that cannot be earned or retained by human effort — they come and go according to divine wisdom.

Job's social prominence (29:7-10, 21-25) is read in Islamic tradition through the concept of *wali* — a friend of God whose inner state radiates outward as natural authority. The Quran affirms: "Surely the friends of Allah — no fear will there be concerning them, nor will they grieve" (10:62). Ayyub's former glory was not worldly ambition but the visible fruit of *wilayah* (divine friendship), which the test of suffering will deepen rather than destroy.
