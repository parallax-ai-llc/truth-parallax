Bildad's sustained description of the wicked person's destruction (18:5-21) is compared in Islamic reading with the Quranic passages describing the fate of those who reject God's guidance. The Quran's descriptions of punishment are typically eschatological — occurring on the Day of Judgment and in the afterlife — rather than within earthly history. "The Day the punishment will cover them from above them and from below their feet and it is said, 'Taste what you used to do'" (29:55). Bildad's error, from an Islamic perspective, is conflating temporal suffering with eschatological punishment, a confusion the Quran carefully avoids by consistently placing ultimate judgment in the afterlife.

The "king of terrors" (18:14) — death personified — is understood in Islamic tradition through the figure of the Angel of Death (*Malak al-Mawt*), identified in some traditions as Azrael (*Izra'il*). The Quran states: "Say, 'The angel of death who has been entrusted with you will take you. Then to your Lord you will be returned'" (32:11). Islamic theology views death not as a terrifying enemy but as a divinely appointed transition — serious and solemn, but ultimately a return to God rather than an annihilation. The Muslim greeting to the dying and the dead — "To Allah we belong and to Him we shall return" (*inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'un*, 2:156) — reframes death as homecoming.

Bildad's claim that the wicked have "no posterity" and "no survivor" (18:19) is countered by the Islamic understanding that ultimate legacy is not biological but spiritual. The Quran values righteous deeds over lineage: "The Day when there will not benefit [anyone] wealth or children, but only one who comes to Allah with a sound heart" (26:88-89). Islamic theology measures a person's legacy by their *amal salih* (righteous deeds) and their contribution to *da'wah* (calling others to God), not by their number of descendants.
