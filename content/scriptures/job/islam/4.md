The speeches of Job's friends do not appear in the Quran, and Islamic commentators who discuss them draw primarily on *Isra'iliyyat* traditions. Eliphaz's theology of retribution (4:7-8) — the innocent do not perish, and wrongdoers reap what they sow — is evaluated within the Islamic framework of *qadar* (divine decree). Islamic theology affirms that God is perfectly just (*'Adl* is one of the divine names in some theological schools), but it also teaches that divine wisdom may include testing the righteous through suffering. The Quran states: "Do the people think that they will be left to say, 'We believe' and they will not be tried?" (29:2).

Eliphaz's nocturnal vision (4:12-16) is assessed through the Islamic science of dream interpretation (*ta'bir al-ru'ya*). The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) taught that true dreams (*ru'ya sadiqah*) are one-forty-sixth part of prophecy (Sahih al-Bukhari). However, Islamic tradition also warns of dreams from Shaytan (*hulm*) that cause distress and lead to error. Eliphaz's vision, which produces fear and leads to harsh judgment of an innocent man, would likely be classified in Islamic dream science as unreliable — a product of demonic influence or personal anxiety rather than genuine divine communication.

The assertion that no mortal can be righteous before God (4:17) is reframed in Islamic theology through the concept of *fitrah* — the innate disposition toward recognizing and worshipping God with which every person is born. While Islam teaches that all humans are sinners ("Every son of Adam sins, and the best of sinners are those who repent," Sunan al-Tirmidhi), it also affirms the *fitrah* as a genuine capacity for righteousness. Eliphaz's extreme pessimism about human nature is thus moderated by the Islamic conviction that God has equipped humanity with the capacity for genuine goodness through the *fitrah* and through prophetic guidance.
