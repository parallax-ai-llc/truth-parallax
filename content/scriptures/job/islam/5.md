Eliphaz's counsel to accept divine correction (5:17) resonates with the Islamic concept of *ibtila'* (trial) as a form of divine purification. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "No fatigue, nor disease, nor sorrow, nor sadness, nor hurt, nor distress befalls a Muslim, even if it were the prick of a thorn, but that Allah expiates some of his sins for that" (Sahih al-Bukhari). This hadith affirms the principle that suffering can serve a redemptive purpose, aligning with Eliphaz's general point while differing in application — the hadith applies universally to believers, not as an accusation against a specific individual.

The hymnic praise of God's power (5:9-16) parallels Quranic descriptions of divine sovereignty. The Quran repeatedly describes God as the one who "gives life and causes death" (2:258), who "provides for whom He wills without account" (3:37), and who "humbles whom He wills and exalts whom He wills" (3:26). Islamic theology's ninety-nine divine names (*al-Asma' al-Husna*) capture the same comprehensive vision of divine power that Eliphaz articulates. The difference is that Islamic theology explicitly pairs power with mercy — *al-Rahman* (the Most Merciful) and *al-Rahim* (the Most Compassionate) — while Eliphaz emphasizes power and justice to the exclusion of mercy.

Eliphaz's promise of material restoration (5:19-26) touches on the Quranic promise that *tawbah* (repentance) leads to blessing: "And said, 'Ask forgiveness of your Lord. Indeed, He is ever a Perpetual Forgiver. He will send rain to you in abundance and give you increase in wealth and children'" (71:10-12). However, Islamic theology distinguishes between this-worldly provision and the ultimate reward of paradise (*Jannah*), which transcends all earthly blessings. Eliphaz's exclusively this-worldly vision of restoration falls short of the eschatological horizon that Islamic theology maintains.
