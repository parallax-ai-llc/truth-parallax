Job's despairing tone in chapter 17 raises the question of whether despair is permissible in Islamic spirituality. The Quran warns against losing hope in God's mercy: "Indeed, no one despairs of relief from Allah except the disbelieving people" (12:87). However, Islamic scholars distinguish between the momentary experience of emotional anguish (*huzn*) — which even the Prophet Muhammad experienced — and the settled theological conviction that God has abandoned the sufferer (*ya's*). Job's lament expresses the former without crossing into the latter, as his continued address to God demonstrates ongoing relationship even in desolation.

Job's social degradation — becoming "a byword" and an object of scorn (17:6) — resonates with the experience of prophets throughout the Quran. Nearly every prophet described in the Quran faced rejection, mockery, and social ostracism from their communities. Noah was mocked (11:38), Abraham was threatened with death (21:68), Moses was challenged (26:18-19), and Muhammad himself endured years of persecution in Mecca. The pattern of prophetic suffering followed by divine vindication is one of the Quran's most consistent themes, and Ayyub's experience fits squarely within it.

Job's closing question — "Where then is my hope?" (17:15) — is answered in Islamic theology by the concept of *raja'* (hope in God's mercy). Al-Ghazali, in his *Ihya' Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences), taught that *raja'* and *khawf* (fear) should be held in balance: the believer hopes for God's mercy while remaining aware of divine justice. The Quran assures: "And whoever fears Allah — He will make for him a way out, and will provide for him from where he does not expect" (65:2-3). Job's experience in the broader narrative fulfills this Quranic promise: God provides a resolution Job could not have anticipated.
