## Job's Hymn to God's Power

Job's hymn to divine creative power in chapter 26 finds extensive parallels in the Quran's creation verses (*ayat al-khalq*). God suspending the earth over nothing (26:7) echoes the Quran's description: "Allah is the one who raised the heavens without pillars that you can see" (13:2). Islamic cosmology celebrates these signs (*ayat*) as evidence of Allah's power and wisdom, inviting contemplation (*tafakkur*) and gratitude (*shukr*).

The imagery of God subduing Rahab and piercing the fleeing serpent (26:12-13) connects to the Quranic theme of Allah's sovereignty over all forces, including those of chaos and destruction. The Quran declares: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is between them" (5:17). No force, however powerful, stands outside Allah's governance. Islamic theology sees in creation's order the evidence of *tadbir* — God's wise management of all affairs.

Job's conclusion — "these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him!" (26:14) — resonates deeply with the Islamic principle that Allah's reality infinitely exceeds human comprehension. The Quran states: "They encompass nothing of His knowledge except what He wills" (2:255). Islamic mystical tradition (Sufism) has emphasized this theme: the greatest knowledge of God is the knowledge that God exceeds all knowledge. Ibn Arabi called this *hayra* — the bewilderment of the mystic before divine infinity.
