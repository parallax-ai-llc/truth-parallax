Job's desire to present his case directly to God (13:3) is understood in Islamic tradition as an expression of *du'a* (supplication) at its most intense. The Quran encourages direct address to God: "And when My servants ask you concerning Me — indeed I am near. I respond to the invocation of the supplicant when he calls upon Me" (2:186). Islamic theology affirms that no intermediary is needed between the servant and God in prayer. Ayyub's insistence on speaking directly to God, bypassing his friends' theological mediation, exemplifies the Islamic principle of immediate access to the divine.

Job's accusation that his friends "speak falsely for God" (13:7-8) connects to the Islamic virtue of *sidq* (truthfulness). The Quran condemns those who "say about Allah what they do not know" (7:33) and warns against attributing false statements to God: "And do not say about what your tongues assert of untruth, 'This is lawful and this is unlawful,' to invent falsehood about Allah" (16:116). Islamic theology considers false speech about God among the gravest of sins, and Job's friends — who confidently assert divine purposes they cannot know — exemplify this transgression.

The declaration "though he slay me, I will hope in him" (13:15) expresses a form of *tawakkul* (trust in God, reliance upon God) that Islamic mysticism (*tasawwuf*) considers the highest spiritual station. The great Sufi masters, including Rabia al-Adawiyya and al-Ghazali, taught that *tawakkul* means complete surrender to divine will regardless of personal outcome. Rabia famously prayed that she loved God neither for fear of hell nor desire of paradise but for God's own sake. Job's declaration of hope beyond death approaches this Sufi ideal of disinterested devotion.
