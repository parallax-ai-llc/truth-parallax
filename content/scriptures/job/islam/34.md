## Elihu Defends God's Justice

Elihu's declaration that "it is unthinkable that God would do wrong" (34:10) articulates a foundational principle of Islamic theology: Allah is *al-Adl* (the Just) and *al-Hakam* (the Judge), and injustice is impossible for Him. The Quran states definitively: "Indeed, Allah does not do injustice, even as much as an atom's weight" (4:40). Islamic creedal theology (*aqidah*) affirms this categorically — God's justice is intrinsic to His nature.

Elihu's argument about divine sustenance of all life (34:14-15) corresponds directly to the Islamic doctrine of *rububiyyah* — Allah's lordship over all creation. The Quran declares: "There is no creature on earth but that upon Allah is its provision" (11:6). Every living thing depends on Allah's sustaining power at every moment. This is not merely a philosophical truth but a source of worship: the Muslim recognizes in every breath a gift from *al-Razzaq* (the Provider).

Elihu's accusation that Job "adds rebellion to his sin" (34:37) is evaluated in Islamic tradition through the distinction between *shikwa* (complaint to God, which is permissible) and *shakwa* (complaint about God, which is problematic). The Quran records Ya'qub (Jacob) saying: "I only complain of my suffering and my grief to Allah" (12:86) — complaint directed to God is an act of faith. Ayyub's questioning, in Islamic reading, remains largely within the bounds of *shikwa*, though it tests those bounds intensely.
