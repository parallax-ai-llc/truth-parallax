## Bildad's Brief Final Word

Bildad's brief speech about God's transcendence and human insignificance (25:2-6) resonates with the central Islamic affirmation of *tawhid* — the absolute oneness and sovereignty of Allah. The Quran declares: "There is nothing like unto Him" (42:11), and "Vision cannot grasp Him, but He grasps all vision" (6:103). Bildad's theology of divine majesty, taken in isolation, aligns with Islamic teaching about Allah's incomparable greatness.

However, Islamic theology corrects Bildad's one-sidedness. While Allah is utterly transcendent (*tanzih*), He is also intimately near (*tashbih*): "We are closer to him than his jugular vein" (50:16). The Quran holds these truths in balance through the concept of *tawazun* (equilibrium). Bildad's error, from an Islamic perspective, is reducing God to pure transcendence, leaving no room for the divine mercy (*rahmah*) and nearness that the Quran emphasizes.

Bildad's question "how can a mortal be righteous before God?" (25:4) is answered in Islam through the concept of *fitrah* — the innate disposition toward God that every human being possesses. While Islam acknowledges human weakness, it does not teach a doctrine of radical corruption. The Quran affirms: "We have certainly created the human being in the best of forms" (95:4). Human beings are capable of righteousness through submission to Allah's guidance, not merely "maggots" before an indifferent deity (25:6).
