Zophar's imagery of ill-gotten wealth being disgorged (20:15-18) connects to the extensive Quranic and jurisprudential treatment of economic ethics. The Quran prohibits *riba* (usury/interest) in the strongest terms: "Those who consume interest cannot stand [on the Day of Resurrection] except as one stands who is being beaten by Satan into insanity" (2:275). The prohibition of *riba*, along with the obligation of *zakat* (charitable giving) and the prohibition of *ghish* (fraud), creates a comprehensive Islamic economic ethic that condemns the acquisition of wealth through exploitation. Zophar's description of wealth that turns to poison captures an Islamic moral principle, even though his application to Job is unjust.

The theme of stolen sweetness turning bitter (20:12-14) resonates with the Quranic principle that ill-gotten gains provide no lasting satisfaction. The Quran warns: "And do not consume one another's wealth unjustly or send it [in bribery] to the rulers in order that [they might aid] you [to] consume a portion of the wealth of the people in sin, while you know [it is unlawful]" (2:188). The hadith literature reinforces this: "A body nourished on *haram* (forbidden wealth) will not enter paradise" (Ahmad). Zophar's economic theology is sound as a general Islamic principle but is weaponized against an innocent man.

Zophar's conclusion that the wicked person's fate is "decreed for him by God" (20:29) asserts a determinism that Islamic theology nuances through the concept of *kasb* (acquisition). The Ash'ari school teaches that while God creates all actions, the human being "acquires" moral responsibility for them. This preserves both divine sovereignty and human accountability without the rigid determinism Zophar implies. The Quran's consistent call to repentance ("Turn to Allah in repentance, all of you, O believers," 24:31) presupposes genuine human choice, which Zophar's sealed verdict effectively denies.
