## Job's Longing for God's Court

Job's longing to find God and present his case (23:3-4) resonates with the Islamic concept of *du'a* — supplication to Allah with urgency and sincerity. The Quran teaches that Allah is near to those who call upon Him: "When My servants ask you about Me, indeed I am near. I respond to the call of the supplicant when he calls upon Me" (2:186). Ayyub's searching in every direction (23:8-9) reflects the human experience of seeking Allah during trial.

The Islamic tradition of *tawakkul* (trust in God) illuminates Job's paradox: he cannot find God, yet he trusts that God knows his way and will vindicate him (23:10). The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) taught that true *tawakkul* involves acting while trusting — not passive resignation but active faith during uncertainty. Ayyub's continued search for God, even when God seems absent, exemplifies this active trust.

Job's declaration that he will emerge "as gold" from testing (23:10) aligns with the hadith tradition that views tribulation as purification. The Prophet said: "No calamity befalls a Muslim but that Allah expiates some of his sins because of it, even though it were the prick of a thorn" (Bukhari). Islamic theology affirms that suffering, when borne with patience and faith, elevates the believer's spiritual station and purifies the soul for the Hereafter.
