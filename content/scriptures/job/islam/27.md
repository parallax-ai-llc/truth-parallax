## Job's Oath of Integrity

Job's oath of integrity — "Till I die, I will not deny my integrity" (27:5) — exemplifies the Islamic virtue of *sidq* (truthfulness). The Quran commands: "O you who believe, fear Allah and be with the truthful" (9:119), and the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) taught that truthfulness leads to righteousness and righteousness leads to Paradise (Bukhari). Ayyub's refusal to confess sins he did not commit demonstrates *sidq* under the most extreme pressure.

The paradox of Job swearing by God while accusing God of denying him justice (27:2) is read in Islamic tradition through the concept of *adab* (proper conduct) with Allah. While Islam permits supplication and even complaint to God — the Quran records numerous prayers of distress by prophets — it insists that such expressions remain within the bounds of reverence. Ayyub's oath acknowledges Allah's authority even while questioning His judgment in this particular case, maintaining *adab* even in agony.

The fate of the wicked described in 27:13-23 aligns with Quranic descriptions of divine retribution against those who persist in injustice and disbelief. The Quran warns: "Do not think that Allah is unaware of what the wrongdoers do. He only delays them for a Day when eyes will stare in horror" (14:42). Islamic theology affirms that divine justice is certain, even when delayed, and that the apparent prosperity of the wicked is temporary — a *fitna* (test) for both them and the observers.
