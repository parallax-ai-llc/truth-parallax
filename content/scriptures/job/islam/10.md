Job's meditation on divine creation (10:8-12) — "Your hands fashioned and made me" — parallels Quranic descriptions of God's creation of humanity. The Quran states: "We have certainly created man in the best of stature" (95:4) and describes the detailed process: "We created man from an extract of clay, then We placed him as a sperm-drop in a firm lodging, then We made the sperm-drop into a clinging clot, and We made the clot into a lump of flesh, and We made from the lump bones, and We covered the bones with flesh; then We developed him into another creation" (23:12-14). Islamic theology sees divine creation as an expression of *hikmah* (wisdom) and *rahmah* (mercy), not arbitrary power.

The paradox Job identifies — a Creator who seems to destroy His creation (10:8-17) — is addressed in Islamic theology through the concept of *bala'* (trial) and the assurance that suffering has divine purpose even when that purpose is hidden. The Quran teaches: "Perhaps you hate a thing and it is good for you; and perhaps you love a thing and it is bad for you. And Allah knows, while you know not" (2:216). This verse is frequently cited by Muslim scholars discussing Ayyub's suffering: the divine wisdom behind affliction may be completely opaque to the sufferer yet perfectly coherent within God's comprehensive knowledge.

Job's description of death and Sheol (10:21-22) is transformed in Islamic eschatology, which provides detailed descriptions of the afterlife that the biblical text lacks. Islamic teaching describes the experience of the grave (*barzakh*), the intermediate state between death and resurrection, during which the deceased is questioned by angels and experiences either comfort or torment. The Quran and hadith literature provide a richly detailed eschatological vision that fills the void Job describes, giving meaning and structure to what he experiences only as "darkness and deep shadow."
