## Elihu's Angry Entrance

Islamic reading of Elihu's entrance in Job 32 evaluates him through the lens of *adab* (proper conduct) and *hikmah* (wisdom). His anger at both Job and the three friends (32:2-5) resonates with the Islamic concept of *ghadab lillah* — anger for Allah's sake, which is permissible when directed at genuine injustice or falsehood. However, the extended self-justification (32:6-22) raises concerns about *riya'* (showing off) and *ujb* (self-admiration), both of which Islam considers spiritual diseases.

Elihu's claim that wisdom comes from "the spirit in a person, the breath of the Almighty" (32:8) rather than from age alone connects to the Islamic principle that Allah grants *hikmah* to whom He wills, regardless of worldly status or seniority. The Quran affirms that Isa (Jesus) spoke with wisdom in the cradle (19:29-33), demonstrating that divine wisdom is not bound by age. Yet Islamic tradition also emphasizes respect for elders (*ikram al-kibr*), and Elihu's tone may violate this principle.

The Islamic tradition of *shura* (consultation) provides a framework for evaluating Elihu's intervention. While fresh perspectives are valued, they should be offered with humility and respect. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "He who does not show mercy to our young ones and does not respect our old ones is not one of us" (Tirmidhi). Elihu's theological contribution may contain truth, but his manner of delivery falls short of prophetic *adab*.
