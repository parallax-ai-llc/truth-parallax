Job's appeal to the animals and the earth as witnesses to divine sovereignty (12:7-10) connects to the Quranic concept of *ayat* (signs). The Quran repeatedly directs attention to natural phenomena as evidence of God's existence, power, and wisdom: "Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth and the alternation of the night and the day are signs for those of understanding" (3:190). Islamic theology understands the entire natural world as a book of divine signs that complements the written revelation of the Quran. Job's argument that even animals recognize divine sovereignty aligns with the Quranic teaching that all creation glorifies God: "The seven heavens and the earth and whatever is in them exalt Him" (17:44).

The portrait of God overturning human institutions (12:14-25) — stripping counselors, deposing kings, silencing the trusted — resonates with the Quranic depiction of God as *Malik al-Mulk* (Owner of Sovereignty): "Say, 'O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will'" (3:26). Islamic political theology understands all human authority as delegated and provisional, subject to divine withdrawal at any moment. The rise and fall of civilizations in Islamic historiography, as articulated by Ibn Khaldun in his *Muqaddimah*, reflects this theological conviction that God alone is the ultimate sovereign.

Job's sarcasm toward his friends (12:2) — "No doubt you are the people, and wisdom will die with you" — is evaluated in Islamic ethics through the concept of *adab* (proper conduct). While Islamic tradition values respectful discourse, it also recognizes the legitimacy of sharp correction when the stakes are high. The Quran itself employs rhetorical questions and pointed critique when addressing those who reject truth: "Do you order righteousness of the people and forget yourselves while you recite the Scripture? Then will you not reason?" (2:44). Job's sarcasm serves a corrective function analogous to Quranic rhetorical questioning.
