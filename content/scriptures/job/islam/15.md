Eliphaz's accusation that Job undermines fear of God (15:4) touches on the Islamic concept of *taqwa* — God-consciousness, reverential awareness, piety. The Quran identifies *taqwa* as the highest virtue: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous [most endowed with *taqwa*] of you" (49:13). Islamic theology would evaluate Job's questioning not as a violation of *taqwa* but as its expression: genuine God-consciousness includes the courage to seek truth, even when that search involves difficult questions. The Quran itself poses challenging theological questions and invites reflection rather than demanding passive acceptance.

Eliphaz's assertion that even "the heavens are not pure in his sight" (15:15) is assessed against the Islamic understanding of creation's inherent goodness. The Quran teaches that God created everything in the best form: "He who perfected everything which He created" (32:7). While Islamic theology acknowledges human weakness and the reality of sin, it does not share the extreme pessimism about creation that Eliphaz expresses. The Islamic concept of *fitrah* — the innate disposition toward good with which every person is born — affirms a fundamental orientation toward righteousness that Eliphaz's theology denies.

The portrait of the wicked person's torment (15:20-35) is compared in Islamic reading with the Quranic descriptions of the fate of the disbelievers and the unjust. The Quran provides vivid depictions of punishment for those who reject God's guidance (e.g., 56:41-56, 78:21-30). However, Islamic eschatology reserves such judgment for the Day of Resurrection (*yawm al-qiyamah*) and does not authorize human beings to pronounce it upon their contemporaries. Eliphaz's error, in Islamic perspective, is not in describing divine punishment but in usurping God's role as judge — a violation of *tawhid* that attributes to human beings a prerogative that belongs to God alone.
