## The Thunderstorm and God's Approach

Elihu's final chapter, dominated by the thunderstorm, resonates with the Quranic theology of *ra'd* (thunder). The Quran declares: "The thunder exalts His praise, and the angels too, out of fear of Him" (13:13). In Islamic understanding, thunder is not merely a meteorological event but an act of divine praise — creation itself worshipping its Creator. Elihu's description of the storm (37:2-13) captures this sacred dimension of natural phenomena.

The rhetorical questions — "Do you know how the clouds hang poised?" (37:16) — function as what the Quran calls *tafakkur* (contemplative reflection on creation). The Quran repeatedly asks similar questions: "Do they not look at the sky above them — how We structured it and adorned it?" (50:6). These questions are not meant to embarrass but to awaken *khushu'* (reverent humility) — the recognition that creation's complexity and beauty point to a Creator of infinite wisdom.

Elihu's concluding assertion — "The Almighty is beyond our reach" (37:23) — aligns with the Islamic affirmation of *tanzih* (divine transcendence). Allah is *al-Mutakabbir* (the Supreme) and *al-Aziz* (the Almighty), beyond all human comprehension. Yet the Quran's very existence — as God's direct speech to humanity — demonstrates that the unreachable God chose to reach out. The approaching whirlwind will confirm what the Quran proclaims: Allah speaks, and His speech transforms those who hear.
