The declaration "I know that my Redeemer lives" (19:25) is interpreted in Islamic tradition not through the Christian lens of a divine redeemer figure but as an expression of certainty in divine vindication. Muslim commentators understand Job's confidence as *yaqin* (certainty) in God's ultimate justice — the conviction that God will clear his name and restore his honor. The Quran describes this same certainty in reference to Ayyub: "And We gave him [back] his family and the like thereof with them as mercy from Us and a reminder for the worshippers [of Allah]" (21:84). Divine vindication comes as mercy (*rahmah*), not as legal acquittal through a third-party redeemer.

The affirmation "in my flesh I shall see God" (19:26) is reframed within Islamic eschatological theology. While the *ru'yat Allah* (vision of God) in the afterlife is affirmed by the majority of Sunni scholars — based on Quranic verses like "Some faces, that Day, will be radiant, looking at their Lord" (75:22-23) — this vision is understood as an eschatological gift of paradise rather than an earthly experience. The Mu'tazili school denied the possibility of seeing God, while the Ash'ari school affirmed it as an experience beyond physical sight. Job's longing to see God "in the flesh" anticipates the eschatological vision that Islamic theology promises to the righteous in paradise.

The Quranic account of Ayyub's restoration (21:83-84, 38:41-44) provides the resolution that this chapter's suffering anticipates. "And We answered him, and We removed what afflicted him of adversity. And We gave him [back] his family and the like thereof with them as mercy from Us" (21:84). The mechanism of healing is described in 38:42: "Strike [the ground] with your foot; this is a spring for a cool bath and drink" — a miraculous spring that healed Ayyub's body. Islamic commentators see this divine intervention as the ultimate answer to all of Job's complaints: God acts directly and miraculously when the appointed time arrives.
