## Elihu on Dreams and Discipline

Elihu's assertion that God communicates through dreams (33:14-16) aligns with the Islamic theology of *ru'ya* (true dreams), which the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) identified as "one-forty-sixth part of prophethood" (Bukhari). Islam distinguishes between true dreams (*ru'ya sadiqah*) from Allah, confused dreams from the self, and disturbing dreams from Shaytan. Elihu correctly identifies divine dreams as a mode of guidance.

The figure of the heavenly mediator who finds "a ransom" for the sufferer (33:23-24) is read in Islamic theology through the concept of *shafa'ah* (intercession). Islam affirms that angels and prophets may intercede with Allah's permission: "Who is it that can intercede with Him except by His permission?" (2:255). However, Islamic theology firmly insists that no intermediary is necessary for the individual's direct relationship with Allah — intercession operates within, not against, the principle of *tawhid*.

Elihu's theology of suffering as divine communication (33:19-22) partially aligns with the Islamic understanding of *ibtila'* (testing). The Quran teaches: "We will surely test you with something of fear and hunger and a loss of wealth and lives and fruits, but give good tidings to the patient" (2:155). Suffering can be a divine message calling the believer to deeper faith. Yet Islam also insists that not all suffering is communicative — some is simply the nature of *dunya*, and the believer's task is *sabr* (patience) regardless of the cause.
