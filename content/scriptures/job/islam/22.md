## Eliphaz's Final Accusation

Islamic engagement with Job 22 reads Eliphaz's false accusations through the Quranic principle that bearing false witness is among the gravest sins. The Quran warns against suspicion (*zann*) and slander (*gheebah*): "O you who believe, avoid much suspicion; indeed, some suspicion is sin" (49:12). Eliphaz's fabricated charges against Ayyub — oppressing widows, withholding bread (22:6-9) — exemplify precisely this prohibited behavior.

The Islamic understanding of *sabr* (patient endurance) provides a framework for evaluating Eliphaz's theology. While he correctly urges turning to God (22:21-23), his premise — that Ayyub's suffering proves his sinfulness — contradicts the Quranic portrait of Ayyub as a righteous prophet tested by Allah's decree. The Quran (21:83-84) presents Ayyub's suffering as a test of faith, not a consequence of sin, and his patience as exemplary for all believers.

Islamic theology rejects the strict retribution theology that Eliphaz represents. While the Quran affirms that righteous deeds are rewarded and sinful deeds punished, it also teaches that God tests those He loves (Quran 2:155-157) and that worldly suffering is not a reliable indicator of divine displeasure. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "The greatest reward comes from the greatest trial. When Allah loves a people, He tests them" (Tirmidhi). Eliphaz's error is to deny this dimension of divine wisdom.
