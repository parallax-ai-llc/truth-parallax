Job's curse upon the day of his birth (3:1-10) presents a challenge for Islamic interpretation, which maintains the doctrine of prophetic dignity (*ismah*). Muslim commentators generally handle this passage in one of several ways: some attribute the extreme language to *Isra'iliyyat* — material from Jewish and Christian sources that entered Islamic tradition but may not accurately represent the prophetic experience. Others, like al-Qurtubi, accept that Ayyub experienced genuine anguish but distinguish between the expression of pain (which is permissible) and complaint against God's decree (which is not). The Quranic account focuses on Ayyub's prayer rather than his curse, modeling the proper response to suffering.

The Quran records Ayyub's prayer in measured terms: "Indeed, adversity has touched me, and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful" (21:83). Islamic commentators contrast this restrained supplication with the more extreme language of Job 3, using the contrast to illustrate the principle that the Quran corrects and purifies previous scriptures. The concept of *tahrif* (alteration of earlier scriptures) allows Muslim scholars to acknowledge the emotional content of Job 3 while maintaining that the Quranic version more accurately represents the prophetic experience. Ayyub's prayer is a model of *du'a* (supplication) — expressing need while affirming divine attributes.

The theme of longing for death (3:11-19) is addressed in Islamic theology through the prophetic hadith: "None of you should wish for death because of a misfortune that has befallen him. If he must wish for anything, let him say: 'O Allah, keep me alive as long as life is better for me, and cause me to die when death is better for me'" (Sahih al-Bukhari). This hadith provides a framework for understanding Ayyub's anguish: genuine suffering can prompt the desire for death, but Islamic teaching channels this impulse into trusting submission (*tawakkul*) to divine wisdom regarding the timing of death.
