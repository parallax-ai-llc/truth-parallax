Job's meditation on human mortality (14:1-2) — few days, full of trouble, like a flower that withers — is consistent with the Quranic depiction of worldly life as transient: "The example of this worldly life is but like rain which We have sent down from the sky that the plants of the earth absorb... until, when the earth has taken on its adornment and is beautified... there comes to it Our command by night or by day, and We make it as a harvest, as if it had not flourished yesterday" (10:24). Islamic theology uses the brevity of *dunya* not to induce despair but to redirect attention toward the eternal *akhirah* (afterlife).

The tree metaphor (14:7-12) — a felled tree that sprouts again versus a dead man who does not rise — is directly addressed by Islamic resurrection theology (*ba'th*). The Quran uses botanical imagery to argue for resurrection: "And it is He who sends the winds as good tidings before His mercy, and We send down from the sky pure water, that We may bring to life thereby a dead land and give it as drink to those We created of numerous livestock and men" (25:48-49). The Quran explicitly states: "And He is the one who begins creation and then repeats it, and that is even easier for Him" (30:27). Job's observation that trees can regenerate becomes, in Islamic theology, evidence *for* rather than against human resurrection.

Job's question "If a man dies, shall he live again?" (14:14) receives an emphatic affirmative in the Quran: "How can you disbelieve in Allah when you were lifeless and He brought you to life; then He will cause you to die, then He will bring you back to life, and then to Him you will be returned" (2:28). The Quran addresses skeptics who deny resurrection by pointing to God's power in original creation: "Does man not remember that We created him before, while he was nothing?" (19:67). Islamic theology transforms Job's uncertain question into a certainty grounded in divine power and promise.
