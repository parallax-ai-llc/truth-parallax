Job's description of life as hard service (7:1) and his nights of restless torment (7:4) are understood in Islamic thought through the concept of *dunya* — the present world characterized by transience, hardship, and trial. The Quran teaches: "Know that the life of this world is but amusement and diversion and adornment and boasting to one another and competition in increase of wealth and children — like the example of a rain whose [resulting] plant growth pleases the tillers; then it dries and you see it turned yellow; then it becomes debris" (57:20). Job's experience of life's brevity and suffering illustrates the Quranic characterization of *dunya* as inherently unstable.

Job's complaint about divine surveillance (7:17-19) — "What is man, that you make so much of him... Will you not look away from me?" — is reframed in Islamic theology through the doctrine of *muraqabah* (divine watchfulness, mindfulness of God's observation). In Sufi spirituality, awareness of God's constant observation is not a source of terror but of comfort and moral guidance. The Prophet Muhammad defined *ihsan* (spiritual excellence) as "worshipping Allah as though you see Him, and if you cannot see Him, then indeed He sees you" (Sahih Muslim). Job's distress at being watched represents a distortion of *muraqabah* — he experiences as surveillance what should be experienced as loving attentiveness.

Job's plea for pardon (7:21) connects to the Islamic emphasis on divine forgiveness as one of God's primary attributes. The Quran describes God as *al-Ghafur* (the All-Forgiving) and *al-Tawwab* (the Accepter of Repentance) — names that appear together over seventy times. The Quran assures: "Say, 'O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins'" (39:53). Job's plea for forgiveness finds its answer in the overwhelming Quranic testimony to divine mercy as the defining attribute of God's relationship with humanity.
