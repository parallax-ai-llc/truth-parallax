## Overview

The Shiva Purana is one of the eighteen Mahapuranas and the principal Puranic text of the Shaiva tradition. It celebrates Shiva as the Supreme Being — the creator, preserver, and destroyer of the universe — and provides extensive teachings on Shiva worship, theology, mythology, and philosophy. The text likely reached its present form between the 6th and 12th centuries CE, though it incorporates much older material.

The Shiva Purana is distinguished by its comprehensive treatment of Shiva's many aspects: the transcendent Absolute (Paramashiva), the cosmic dancer (Nataraja), the supreme ascetic (Mahayogi), the devoted husband of Parvati, and the fierce destroyer of evil. The text weaves together narrative, ritual instruction, philosophical discourse, and devotional hymns into a rich theological tapestry.

## Structure

The text is divided into seven Samhitas (sections):

1. **Samhita 1 — Vidyeshvara Samhita**: The nature of Shiva, the supremacy of the linga, and foundational theology
2. **Samhita 2 — Rudra Samhita**: The mythology of Shiva — his marriages, the birth of his sons, and cosmic exploits
3. **Samhita 3 — Shatarudra Samhita**: The hundred Rudras, Shiva's manifestations, and the greatness of Shiva worship
4. **Samhita 4 — Kotirudra Samhita**: The ten million Rudras, sacred sites, and the power of Shiva's names
5. **Samhita 5 — Uma Samhita**: The goddess Uma (Parvati), her austerities, and the Shiva-Shakti relationship
6. **Samhita 6 — Kailasa Samhita**: Philosophical teachings, liberation, and the nature of reality from Shiva's perspective
7. **Samhita 7 — Vayaviya Samhita**: Shiva's relationship with Vayu, cosmic functions, and eschatological teachings

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Shiva Purana holds significance across multiple Hindu traditions:

- **Shaivism**: The foundational Puranic text establishing Shiva's supreme sovereignty
- **Vaishnavism**: Acknowledged as a Mahapurana, though Vaishnava readers reinterpret its theological claims
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Cited for its philosophical passages on the nature of ultimate reality
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Engaged with polemically as representing the Shaiva theological position
- **Academic**: Essential for understanding Shaiva sectarian development and Hindu theology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Shiva's Supremacy | Shiva as the ultimate reality, transcending all other deities and principles |
| Linga Worship | The linga as the primary symbol and object of Shiva worship |
| Shiva-Parvati | The divine marriage as a model for cosmic complementarity and devotion |
| Asceticism and Grace | The tension and synthesis between Shiva's ascetic withdrawal and his compassionate engagement |
| Cosmic Dance | Shiva as Nataraja, whose dance creates, sustains, and destroys the universe |
