## Overview

Vaishnava interpreters approach the Shiva Purana with respect for its status as a Mahapurana while maintaining that its theological claims must be understood within the larger framework of Vishnu's supremacy. The Vaishnava tradition has developed sophisticated strategies for engaging with Shaiva Puranic literature without conceding Shiva's ultimate supremacy.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Shiva as Vishnu's Greatest Devotee
A central Vaishnava strategy is to acknowledge Shiva's greatness while interpreting it as derived from his devotion to Vishnu. In this reading, Shiva is the foremost among Vishnu's devotees — powerful, wise, and venerable, but not the ultimate reality.

### Tamo-guna Purana Classification
Some Vaishnava authorities classify Puranas according to the three gunas, placing the Shiva Purana among the tamasic Puranas. This classification, while contested, allows Vaishnavas to acknowledge the text's existence while questioning the ultimate validity of its theological claims.

### Harihara Synthesis
More irenic Vaishnava interpreters emphasize the unity of Hari (Vishnu) and Hara (Shiva), reading the Shiva Purana as describing aspects of the divine that are ultimately unified in Vishnu.

## Theological Emphases

- **Subordination of Shiva**: Shiva's powers as delegated by or derived from Vishnu
- **Vishnu's Hidden Presence**: Finding Vaishnava theology within Shaiva narratives
- **Hierarchy of Puranas**: Using Puranic classification to relativize Shaiva claims
- **Complementary Theology**: Shiva and Vishnu as performing complementary cosmic functions under Vishnu's overall sovereignty
