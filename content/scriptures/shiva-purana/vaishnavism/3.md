## Overview

The Shatarudra Samhita describes the hundred manifestations of Rudra and elaborates on the greatness of Shiva worship. Vaishnava interpreters approach this material by distinguishing between the legitimate veneration of Shiva and the theological claim that Shiva is the Supreme Being.

Vaishnava readers acknowledge the multiple forms of Rudra described here as demonstrations of divine power but interpret them as manifestations of a deity who, however powerful, operates within the cosmic order established and maintained by Vishnu. The hundred Rudras are understood as expressions of the destructive and transformative aspect of the divine, a function that is real and important but subordinate to Vishnu's comprehensive sovereignty.

The worship practices described in this samhita — the use of sacred ash (vibhuti), rudraksha beads, and specific mantras — are treated by Vaishnavas with varying degrees of acceptance, from those who incorporate some Shaiva elements into their practice to those who maintain strict separation between Vaishnava and Shaiva worship forms.

## Key Themes

- **Qualified Reverence**: Respecting Shiva's power while maintaining Vishnu's supremacy
- **Functional Interpretation**: The Rudras as serving a specific cosmic role under Vishnu
- **Worship Boundaries**: Maintaining distinctions between Vaishnava and Shaiva practice
- **Multiple Forms**: Shiva's many manifestations as analogous to but less than Vishnu's avatars
- **Selective Engagement**: Accepting some aspects of Shaiva worship while rejecting supremacy claims

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Hundred Rudras

Vaishnava interpreters read the hundred manifestations of Rudra as demonstrations of power within the destructive domain that Vishnu has assigned to Shiva. These are impressive but functionally limited expressions of divine energy.

### Vibhuti and Rudraksha

Vaishnava attitudes toward Shaiva worship symbols vary by sub-tradition. Some (especially Smarta-influenced groups) accept these practices; others (strict Vaishnavas) maintain that only Vaishnava symbols (tulasi, tilaka, conch, disc) are appropriate.

## Commentary Tradition

Vaishnava commentary on this samhita is relatively sparse compared to more theologically central texts, as the material is considered specific to Shaiva practice rather than universally applicable.
