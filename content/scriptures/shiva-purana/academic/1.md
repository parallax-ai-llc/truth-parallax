## Overview

Academic study of the Vidyeshvara Samhita examines its compositional history, theological content, and relationship to other Shaiva and Puranic literature. Scholars generally regard this samhita as one of the later additions to the Shiva Purana corpus, as its systematic theology reflects a more developed stage of Shaiva sectarian thought.

The linga theology presented here is studied in relation to archaeological and epigraphic evidence for the development of linga worship in India. The twelve jyotirlingas, while presented as primordial manifestations, correspond to historically datable temple sites, providing evidence for the text's compositional context.

The cosmic linga narrative — one of the most analyzed passages in Puranic literature — is studied as a key document in the history of Hindu sectarian competition. Scholars trace variants of this story across multiple Puranas and note how each recension adapts the narrative to support its particular sectarian position.

## Key Themes

- **Compositional Dating**: Placing the Vidyeshvara Samhita within the Purana's textual history
- **Linga Cult Origins**: Archaeological and textual evidence for the development of linga worship
- **Sectarian Polemics**: The cosmic linga narrative as a document of inter-sectarian competition
- **Jyotirlinga Geography**: The twelve jyotirlingas as evidence for Shaiva sacred geography
- **Theological Systematization**: How this samhita organizes earlier Shaiva theological ideas

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Cosmic Linga Variants

Scholars have documented versions of the cosmic linga narrative in the Linga Purana, Kurma Purana, and Skanda Purana, noting how each version adjusts the narrative details to favor its own sectarian position.

### Archaeological Evidence

The earliest archaeological evidence for linga worship (Gudimallam linga, 2nd century BCE) predates the Shiva Purana by several centuries, helping scholars reconstruct the history of Shaiva worship practice.

## Commentary Tradition

Academic commentary on this samhita draws on art historical, archaeological, and epigraphic evidence alongside textual analysis. Stella Kramrisch's The Presence of Siva provides a comprehensive scholarly treatment, while Doniger O'Flaherty's work examines the mythological dimensions.
