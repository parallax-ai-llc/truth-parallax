## Overview

A comparative approach to the Shiva Purana reveals how its central themes resonate with and diverge from patterns found in other religious and mythological traditions worldwide. Shiva's paradoxical nature — simultaneously ascetic and erotic, destructive and creative, transcendent and immanent — finds parallels in divine figures across many cultures.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Paradoxical Deity
Shiva's combination of apparently contradictory attributes — destroyer and creator, ascetic and lover, terrifying and gentle — parallels similar divine paradoxes in other traditions: Dionysus in Greek religion, Odin in Norse mythology, and certain aspects of Yahweh in the Hebrew Bible.

### Sacred Sexuality and Asceticism
The tension between asceticism and sexuality in Shiva's mythology finds parallels in many traditions that struggle to integrate bodily and spiritual dimensions of religious life. The linga as a symbol of creative power can be compared with phallic religious symbols in many ancient cultures.

### The Dancing God
Shiva Nataraja, the cosmic dancer, can be compared with dancing deities in other traditions — the dancing Krishna, the ecstatic Sufi whirling, and the ritual dance traditions of indigenous cultures worldwide.

## Theological Emphases

- **Coincidentia Oppositorum**: The union of opposites in the divine, compared across traditions
- **Phallic Symbolism**: The linga compared with sacred phallic symbols in other cultures
- **Ascetic Traditions**: Shaiva asceticism compared with Christian monasticism, Buddhist renunciation, Sufi austerity
- **Sacred Dance**: Nataraja compared with dance as divine expression elsewhere
- **Divine Marriage**: Shiva-Parvati compared with sacred marriages (hieros gamos) in other traditions
