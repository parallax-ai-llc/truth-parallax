## Overview

The Vidyeshvara Samhita's theological and ritual content provides rich material for comparative analysis. The concept of an aniconic representation of the supreme deity — the linga — invites comparison with aniconic worship traditions in other religions, including early Israelite worship (before images), Islamic aniconism, and the Omphalos stones of ancient Greece.

The cosmic linga narrative, in which a pillar of fire manifests between heaven and earth with no discernible top or bottom, bears structural resemblance to axis mundi myths worldwide and to theophanic experiences in other traditions — the burning bush of Moses, the pillar of fire leading Israel through the wilderness, and the Zoroastrian sacred fire.

The twelve jyotirlinga pilgrimage network can be compared with sacred geographical systems in other traditions: the Christian Holy Land pilgrimage sites, the Islamic hajj and ziyarat traditions, and the Buddhist circuit of places associated with the Buddha's life.

## Key Themes

- **Aniconic Worship**: The linga compared with aniconic traditions in other religions
- **Sacred Fire Theophanies**: The cosmic linga compared with fire manifestations of the divine elsewhere
- **Pilgrimage Networks**: Jyotirlingas compared with sacred site systems in other traditions
- **Supreme Deity Claims**: Shiva's supremacy compared with monotheistic claims in other traditions
- **Axis Mundi**: The cosmic linga as world-axis compared with similar symbols globally

## Key Verses and Passages

### Aniconic Worship

The linga as an aniconic representation of the divine finds parallels in the Jewish prohibition of images, Islamic aniconism, and the early Buddhist use of symbols (footprints, empty thrones) rather than images of the Buddha.

### Sacred Fire

The cosmic linga of fire parallels the Zoroastrian sacred fire (Atar), the burning bush (Exodus 3), and the pillar of fire (Exodus 13:21) as manifestations of divine presence through fire.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholars note that the tension between iconic and aniconic worship — central to the Shiva Purana's linga theology — is a recurring theme across religious traditions, reflecting fundamental questions about the nature of the divine and the limits of human representation.
