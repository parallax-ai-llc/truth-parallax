## Overview

The Shaiva tradition regards the Shiva Purana as its most important Puranic authority. For Shaivas, this text reveals the true nature of Shiva as Paramashiva — the Supreme Being who transcends the Trimurti while containing all cosmic functions within himself. The text is read as direct divine revelation (agama) transmitted through the Puranic literary form.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Shiva as Para Brahman
Shaiva interpreters read the Shiva Purana as establishing that Shiva alone is the ultimate reality — not one god among many but the Absolute from whom all other deities, including Brahma and Vishnu, derive their being and power.

### The Linga as Supreme Symbol
The Shiva Purana's extensive treatment of linga worship is central to Shaiva practice. The linga is understood not as a phallic symbol (a common misconception) but as the aniconic representation of Shiva's transcendent nature — formless, infinite, and self-luminous.

### Panchakritya
The five cosmic functions — creation (srishti), preservation (sthiti), destruction (samhara), concealment (tirodhana), and grace (anugraha) — are all attributed to Shiva, making him the sole agent of all cosmic activity.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sadashiva**: The eternal, unchanging aspect of Shiva beyond all transformation
- **Shakti**: The dynamic power of Shiva, personified as Parvati/Uma, through whom he acts
- **Bhakti-Jnana Synthesis**: Devotion and knowledge as complementary paths to Shiva
- **Shiva's Grace (Anugraha)**: The ultimate means of liberation, bestowed by Shiva on the devoted soul
- **Asceticism and Householder Life**: Shiva as the model for both renunciation and married life
