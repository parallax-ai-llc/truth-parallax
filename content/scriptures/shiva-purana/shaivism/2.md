## Overview

The Rudra Samhita is the narrative heart of the Shiva Purana, containing the great mythology of Shiva in all his aspects — the terrible destroyer (Rudra), the supreme ascetic (Mahayogi), the passionate lover, and the compassionate husband. For Shaivas, these narratives reveal Shiva's nature as the coincidence of opposites — the deity who unites asceticism and eroticism, destruction and creation, withdrawal and engagement.

The Daksha sacrifice narrative is foundational for Shaiva self-understanding. Daksha's refusal to honor Shiva represents the arrogance of Vedic orthodoxy that fails to recognize Shiva's supremacy. Sati's self-immolation (from which the term 'sati' derives) and Shiva's devastating response demonstrate both the intensity of divine love and the futility of opposing the Supreme Lord.

The courtship of Shiva and Parvati is read as revealing the deepest mystery of Shaiva theology: the relationship between Shiva (pure consciousness) and Shakti (dynamic energy). Their union is not merely a marriage but the cosmic principle that makes creation possible.

## Key Themes

- **Shiva's Paradoxical Nature**: The union of asceticism and eroticism in the divine
- **Daksha's Punishment**: The consequences of failing to recognize Shiva's supremacy
- **Shiva-Shakti Union**: The cosmic marriage as the foundation of all reality
- **Sati's Sacrifice**: The power of devotion and the intensity of divine love
- **Parvati's Tapas**: The goddess's austerities as the model for devoted spiritual practice

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Daksha Sacrifice

This is the definitive narrative of Shiva's wrathful power. When Daksha insults Shiva and Sati dies of shame, Shiva creates Virabhadra, who destroys the sacrifice and punishes the gods. For Shaivas, this demonstrates that no cosmic order can stand without Shiva's blessing.

### Shiva and Parvati's Marriage

The divine wedding is the cosmic event par excellence in Shaiva theology. Their union represents the joining of consciousness (Shiva) and energy (Shakti), without which nothing can exist or function.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva commentators treat the Rudra Samhita as the supreme mythological revelation. The narratives are not merely stories but sacred history (itihasa) revealing eternal truths about Shiva's nature. Temple iconography, ritual practice, and devotional poetry all draw extensively on these narratives.
