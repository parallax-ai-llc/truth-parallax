## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the Kotirudra Samhita's expansion to ten million Rudras as further evidence for its hierarchical theology. The proliferation of Rudra forms demonstrates the derivative, distributed nature of Shiva's power — in contrast to Vishnu's singular, self-sufficient sovereignty. While ten million Rudras are impressive, they represent delegated authority rather than ultimate supremacy.

The Dvaita tradition has its own extensive pilgrimage traditions centered on Vishnu temples and sacred sites. While not dismissing Shaiva sacred geography entirely, Dvaitins maintain that the holiest sites are those associated with Vishnu's presence and his avatars. The Dvaita reading of this samhita's sacred geography emphasizes Vishnu's hidden presence within even Shaiva sites.

The nama-smarana practices described here are paralleled by the Dvaita tradition's own emphasis on the recitation of Vishnu's names, which Dvaitins regard as the supreme spiritual practice. Madhvacharya taught that Vishnu's names carry his full divine power and that their recitation, with proper understanding and devotion, leads to liberation.

## Key Themes

- **Distributed Power**: Ten million Rudras as evidence of delegated rather than supreme authority
- **Vaishnava Sacred Geography**: Dvaita pilgrimage traditions centered on Vishnu sites
- **Vishnu Nama Superiority**: Vishnu's names as carrying the highest salvific power
- **Hidden Presence**: Vishnu as the antaryamin within all Shaiva sacred sites
- **Hierarchical Devotion**: The ranking of devotional practices with Vishnu worship at the apex

## Key Verses and Passages

### Vishnu's Names vs. Shiva's Names

Dvaitins maintain that while recitation of Shiva's names has spiritual value, it does not lead to the highest liberation. Only the names of Vishnu carry the full power of the Supreme Person, and only devotion to Vishnu brings complete moksha.

### Sacred Site Hierarchy

Madhvacharya and his followers maintain a hierarchy of sacred sites with Vishnu temples and tirthas at the apex, reflecting the overall taratamya principle applied to sacred geography.

## Commentary Tradition

Dvaita commentary on Shaiva pilgrimage and nama traditions is found in the broader context of Madhvacharya's hermeneutical works and the devotional literature of the Haridasa tradition, which emphasizes the supremacy of Hari-nama (Vishnu's name recitation).
