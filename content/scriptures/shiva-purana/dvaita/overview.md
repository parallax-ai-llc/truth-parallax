## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta, founded by Madhvacharya, engages with the Shiva Purana primarily as a text whose theological claims must be corrected or reinterpreted in light of what Dvaitins regard as the clear Vedic and Puranic teaching of Vishnu's absolute supremacy. Madhvacharya developed detailed hermeneutical strategies for reading Shaiva Puranic literature.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Puranic Hierarchy
Madhvacharya classified Puranas into sattvic, rajasic, and tamasic categories, placing the Shiva Purana among the tamasic texts. This classification does not deny the text's canonical status but subordinates its authority to sattvic Puranas like the Vishnu Purana and Bhagavata Purana.

### Reinterpretation of Supremacy Claims
Where the Shiva Purana declares Shiva supreme, Dvaita interpreters apply the principle that such statements must be understood in light of clearer Vedic passages establishing Vishnu's supremacy. Apparent Shaiva supremacy claims are read as contextual, hyperbolic, or referring to Shiva's relative greatness among created beings.

### Taratamya Application
In the Dvaita hierarchy of beings, Shiva occupies an exalted position — often second only to Lakshmi and above Brahma — but remains categorically distinct from and subordinate to Vishnu.

## Theological Emphases

- **Vishnu Sarvottamatva**: The absolute supremacy of Vishnu over all beings including Shiva
- **Hermeneutical Priority**: Vedic and sattvic Puranic texts as interpretive keys for tamasic Puranas
- **Shiva's Exalted Status**: Acknowledging Shiva's greatness while maintaining his subordination
- **Anti-mayavada**: Rejecting the Advaita reading that equates Shiva with nirguna Brahman
- **Scriptural Consistency**: The need to harmonize all scriptural statements under Vishnu's supremacy
