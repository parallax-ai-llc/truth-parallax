## Overview

Dvaita interpreters approach the mythological narratives of the Rudra Samhita with their characteristic hermeneutical rigor. While acknowledging the literary and devotional power of these stories, Dvaitins insist on reading them within the framework of Vishnu's absolute supremacy and Shiva's subordinate position in the cosmic hierarchy.

The Daksha sacrifice narrative is particularly significant for Dvaita interpretation. Where Shaivas read it as demonstrating Shiva's supremacy, Dvaitins note that the narrative ultimately requires divine mediation to resolve — and that this mediation, in their reading, involves Vishnu's sovereign will. The destruction wrought by Virabhadra is seen as an expression of divinely permitted wrath rather than ultimate sovereignty.

Dvaitins also engage with the question of Shiva's asceticism and its relationship to Vishnu. They argue that Shiva's meditation and tapas are themselves forms of worship directed toward Vishnu, the supreme object of all devotion — even Shiva's.

## Key Themes

- **Subordinate Reading**: Shiva's cosmic actions as permitted by Vishnu's will
- **Shiva's Devotion to Vishnu**: Shiva's meditation as directed toward Vishnu
- **Hierarchical Mythology**: The narratives read within the taratamya framework
- **Controlled Destruction**: Shiva's wrath as a delegated function
- **Vishnu's Hidden Sovereignty**: Finding Vishnu's governance behind Shaiva narratives

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Daksha Sacrifice — Dvaita Reading

Dvaita interpreters read the Daksha sacrifice narrative as demonstrating the cosmic disorder that results when the proper hierarchy is disrupted — not by Daksha's failure to honor Shiva, but by the broader failure to maintain the correct order of worship with Vishnu at the apex.

### Shiva as Vishnu's Devotee

Dvaitins cite passages from other Puranas where Shiva worships Vishnu and apply this framework to the Rudra Samhita's narratives, reading Shiva's meditation as devotion directed toward the Supreme Person.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's approach to Shaiva mythology is detailed in his Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya. His followers Jayatirtha and Vyasatirtha developed these readings further, creating a systematic Dvaita mythological hermeneutic.
