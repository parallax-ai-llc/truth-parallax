## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta's engagement with the Shatarudra Samhita follows its general approach to Shaiva Puranic material: acknowledging the text's canonical status while reinterpreting its theological claims within the framework of Vishnu's supremacy. The hundred manifestations of Rudra are understood as describing the real but limited power of a being who, however exalted, remains subordinate to Vishnu.

Madhvacharya's classification of this text as tamasic Puranic literature means that its worship prescriptions are not considered binding on Vaishnavas. However, the Dvaita tradition does not dismiss Shiva worship as invalid — rather, it regards such worship as appropriate for those souls whose inherent nature (svarupa-yogyata) inclines them toward Shiva, while insisting that the highest worship is directed toward Vishnu.

The Dvaita reading of the Shatarudriya (the Vedic text to which this samhita connects) differs significantly from both Shaiva and Advaita readings. Madhvacharya interpreted the Vedic Shatarudriya as ultimately praising Vishnu, who is the inner controller (antaryamin) of Rudra.

## Key Themes

- **Limited Manifestations**: The hundred Rudras as real but finite expressions of delegated power
- **Worship Hierarchy**: Shiva worship as valid but subordinate to Vishnu worship
- **Antaryamin Doctrine**: Vishnu as the inner controller even of Shiva's manifestations
- **Svarupa-yogyata**: Different souls naturally inclined toward different deities
- **Shatarudriya Reinterpretation**: The Vedic hymn as ultimately praising Vishnu

## Key Verses and Passages

### Madhva's Shatarudriya Interpretation

Madhvacharya's reading of the Vedic Shatarudriya as praising Vishnu (who dwells within Rudra as antaryamin) is one of the most controversial and distinctive features of Dvaita hermeneutics, directly challenging both Shaiva and Advaita readings.

### The Hierarchy of Worship

Dvaitins maintain that while Shiva worship is not wrong, it does not lead to the highest liberation (moksha), which comes only through devotion to Vishnu. This samhita's worship prescriptions are thus seen as valid for a particular class of devotees but not as the universal path.

## Commentary Tradition

The Dvaita engagement with this material is found primarily in Madhvacharya's commentary on the Shatarudriya and in the polemical works of later Dvaita scholars like Vyasatirtha and Vadiraja Tirtha.
