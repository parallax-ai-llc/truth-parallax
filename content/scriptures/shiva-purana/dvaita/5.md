## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta engages with the Uma Samhita's goddess theology within its hierarchical framework. The Dvaita tradition acknowledges the importance of the divine feminine but locates it within the taratamya (hierarchy) governed by Vishnu. In the Dvaita scheme, Lakshmi occupies the highest position after Vishnu, and all other goddess forms are subordinate to her.

Parvati/Uma is respected in the Dvaita tradition as Shiva's consort and as a powerful divine being, but her theological status is understood as categorically different from that of Lakshmi. Where Lakshmi is the eternal consort of the Supreme Person and the mediator of his grace, Parvati is the consort of a being (Shiva) who is himself subordinate to Vishnu.

The philosophical dialogues between Shiva and Parvati are read by Dvaitins with attention to passages that can be harmonized with Dvaita theology — particularly teachings on the reality of the world, the distinctness of souls, and the necessity of devotion to the supreme deity.

## Key Themes

- **Hierarchical Goddess Theology**: Parvati as subordinate to Lakshmi in the cosmic hierarchy
- **Taratamya Applied**: The divine feminine arranged within the Dvaita graded hierarchy
- **Selective Reading**: Extracting Dvaita-compatible teachings from Shaiva goddess narrative
- **Lakshmi's Primacy**: All goddess forms understood in relation to Lakshmi-Narayana
- **Real Feminine Power**: The divine feminine as genuinely powerful within the hierarchical order

## Key Verses and Passages

### Goddess Hierarchy

Dvaitins maintain a clear hierarchy: Lakshmi as the supreme feminine divine, with all other goddess forms occupying lower positions. Parvati is great but not ultimate.

### Philosophical Dialogues

Dvaita interpreters extract passages from the Shiva-Parvati dialogues that support key Dvaita positions: the reality of the world, the eternal distinctness of souls, and the necessity of devotion to a personal God.

## Commentary Tradition

Dvaita commentary on goddess theology is found primarily in the broader context of Madhvacharya's systematic theology and the devotional literature of the Haridasa tradition, which celebrates Lakshmi alongside Vishnu.
