## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the Uma Samhita's treatment of the goddess as an exploration of the relationship between Brahman and its creative power (maya-shakti). In the Advaitic framework, Uma/Parvati represents maya — the inscrutable power of Brahman that projects the appearance of the manifold world without affecting Brahman's essential nature.

The philosophical dialogues between Shiva and Parvati in this samhita are particularly valued by Advaitins, as they echo the question-and-answer format of the Upanishads. Parvati's questions about the nature of reality represent the sincere aspirant's inquiry (jijnasa), while Shiva's responses point toward non-dual realization.

Shankaracharya's association with the Shakta tradition — evident in hymns attributed to him like the Saundaryalahari — provides a precedent for Advaitic engagement with goddess theology. For Advaitins, the divine feminine, like the divine masculine, is a provisional characterization of the attributeless Brahman, valuable for worship but transcended in realization.

## Key Themes

- **Maya-Shakti**: Uma as the creative power (maya) of Brahman
- **Philosophical Dialogue**: The Shiva-Parvati exchanges as echoing Upanishadic inquiry
- **Beyond Gender**: The divine feminine and masculine as provisional characterizations of nirguna Brahman
- **Devotional Engagement**: Goddess worship as a valid form of saguna Brahman devotion
- **Inquiry and Realization**: Parvati's questions representing the aspirant's journey to knowledge

## Key Verses and Passages

### Parvati's Inquiry

Advaitins read Parvati's philosophical questions as representing the moment of jijnasa — the sincere desire to know the truth that, according to the Brahma Sutra's opening, is the prerequisite for Vedantic study.

### Maya as Goddess

The Advaitic identification of the goddess with maya provides a philosophical framework for understanding the Uma Samhita's theology. Maya is not negative but the creative power through which Brahman appears as the world.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on goddess theology balances philosophical rigor with devotional openness. The Shankaracharya mathas (monasteries) maintain traditions of goddess worship alongside their commitment to non-dual philosophy.
