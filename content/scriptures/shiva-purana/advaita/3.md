## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the Shatarudra Samhita's descriptions of Shiva's multiple manifestations as illustrating the relationship between the one Brahman and its apparent multiplicity. The hundred Rudras are not ontologically distinct beings but expressions of the single non-dual reality appearing in various forms through the power of maya.

The worship practices described — vibhuti, rudraksha, mantra recitation — are valued by Advaitins as upasana (meditation aids) that purify the mind and prepare it for the ultimate realization of non-duality. The application of vibhuti, in particular, carries symbolic resonance with Advaita: ash represents the burning away of all phenomenal distinctions in the fire of knowledge.

The connection to the Vedic Shatarudriya is significant for Advaitins because it links Puranic devotion to the Vedic tradition, from which Advaita derives its primary authority. Shankaracharya's commentary on the Shatarudriya reads it as describing Brahman in both its saguna and nirguna aspects.

## Key Themes

- **One in Many**: The hundred Rudras as illustrations of Brahman's apparent multiplicity
- **Upasana**: Worship practices as means of mental purification
- **Vibhuti Symbolism**: Sacred ash as representing the dissolution of phenomenal distinctions
- **Vedic Foundation**: The Shatarudriya as Vedic authority for Shaiva-Advaitic devotion
- **Mantra as Jnana**: Sacred sound as a vehicle for transmitting non-dual knowledge

## Key Verses and Passages

### The One and the Many

Advaitins read the hundred manifestations as a Puranic illustration of the Upanishadic teaching: 'Ekam sat vipra bahudha vadanti' — the one reality is spoken of by the wise in many ways (Rig Veda 1.164.46).

### Shankaracharya's Shatarudriya Commentary

Shankaracharya's commentary on the Vedic Shatarudriya provides the interpretive key for the Advaita reading of this samhita, identifying Rudra with the all-pervading Brahman.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary connects this samhita's devotional content to the broader Advaitic framework through Shankaracharya's own Shaiva devotional works and his commentary on the Shatarudriya. Later Advaitins like Appaya Dikshita further developed the Advaita-Shaiva synthesis.
