## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the mythological narratives of the Rudra Samhita as allegorical expressions of philosophical truths. The stories of Shiva's marriages, battles, and cosmic interventions are understood as Puranic dramatizations of the relationships between consciousness and energy, knowledge and ignorance, the Absolute and the phenomenal world.

The Daksha sacrifice narrative, in particular, lends itself to Advaitic interpretation. Daksha, representing conventional Vedic ritualism (karma-kanda), fails to recognize the supremacy of Shiva, representing the highest knowledge (jnana-kanda). The destruction of Daksha's sacrifice symbolizes the transcendence of ritual by knowledge — a central Advaitic theme.

The Shiva-Parvati relationship is read as the relationship between nirguna Brahman (Shiva) and maya-shakti (Parvati). Their union represents the apparent conjunction of the Absolute with its creative power, which produces the phenomenal world without affecting the Absolute's essential nature.

## Key Themes

- **Allegorical Reading**: Mythology as expressing philosophical truths
- **Daksha as Karma-kanda**: The sacrifice narrative as the transcendence of ritual by knowledge
- **Shiva-Parvati as Brahman-Maya**: The divine couple as a metaphor for consciousness and illusion
- **Kama's Destruction**: The burning of desire as the prerequisite for Self-realization
- **Marriage as Non-dual Union**: The divine wedding as symbolizing the resolution of duality

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Burning of Kama

Advaitins read Shiva's destruction of Kamadeva (desire) as the quintessential spiritual act — the destruction of desire through the fire of knowledge (jnana-agni). Only when desire is consumed can the self recognize its true nature as Brahman.

### Parvati's Tapas

Parvati's austerities to win Shiva are interpreted as the aspirant's intense practice (sadhana) required to realize Brahman. The fact that Shiva initially resists and then accepts Parvati mirrors the Advaitic understanding that Brahman seems distant until the moment of realization.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin interpretation of Shaiva mythology has a long history, from Shankaracharya's Shaiva hymns through Vidyaranya's synthetic works. The allegorical method allows Advaitins to appreciate the narrative richness while extracting non-dual philosophical content.
