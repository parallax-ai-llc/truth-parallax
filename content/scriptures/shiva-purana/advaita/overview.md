## Overview

Advaita Vedanta interprets the Shiva Purana through the lens of non-dualism, reading its theological affirmations about Shiva as descriptions of nirguna Brahman — the attributeless Absolute that is the ultimate reality beyond all personal conceptions of the divine. Many prominent Advaitins, including Shankaracharya himself, maintained strong connections to the Shaiva tradition.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Shiva as Nirguna Brahman
For Advaitins, the Shiva Purana's descriptions of Shiva as the transcendent reality beyond all attributes (particularly in the Kailasa Samhita) point directly to nirguna Brahman. The name "Shiva" itself, meaning "auspicious" or "pure," is taken as a descriptor of the Absolute.

### The Linga as Symbol of the Formless
The Advaita reading of linga worship emphasizes the linga's aniconic character — it represents the formless Brahman, making Shiva worship a natural expression of Advaitic devotion to the attributeless Absolute.

### Shankaracharya's Shaiva Connections
Tradition associates Shankaracharya with Shaiva worship and the composition of Shaiva devotional hymns. Advaita interpreters see no contradiction between non-dualism and devotion to Shiva as saguna Brahman.

## Theological Emphases

- **Formless Absolute**: Shiva as pointing to the nirguna Brahman beyond all form
- **Dakshinamurti**: Shiva as the silent teacher who transmits knowledge through silence — the quintessential Advaitic guru
- **Maya and Lila**: Shiva's cosmic play as illustrating the nature of apparent creation
- **Jivanmukti**: Shiva as the model of the liberated being — free while engaged in cosmic activity
- **Neti Neti**: The Shiva Purana's descriptions of Shiva's transcendence as echoing the Upanishadic "not this, not this"
