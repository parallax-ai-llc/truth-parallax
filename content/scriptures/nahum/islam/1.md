## The Sovereignty of God Over Creation

Islamic interpretation of Nahum 1 finds powerful resonance with the Quranic descriptions of God's absolute power over creation. The theophanic imagery — storm, earthquake, drying seas — echoes passages like Surah 99 (*al-Zalzalah*): "When the earth is shaken with its [final] earthquake." God's sovereignty over nature is a foundational Islamic principle, and Nahum's hymn affirms it with vivid force.

## Divine Patience and Its Limits

The verse "The LORD is slow to anger and great in power" aligns with the Islamic understanding of God as *al-Halim* (the Forbearing). God's patience is real but not infinite in application — those who persist in sin after repeated warnings will face judgment. The Quran records: "And your Lord is the Forgiving, full of mercy. If He were to impose blame upon them for what they earned, He would have hastened for them the punishment" (18:58).

## The Destruction of Oppressive Power

Nahum 1 announces the end of Assyrian tyranny, and Islamic interpretation reads this as consistent with the Quranic pattern: God destroys oppressive civilizations when their sin reaches its limit. The chapter serves as a warning to all powers that abuse their authority — a warning the Quran reinforces with accounts of Pharaoh, 'Ad, and Thamud.
