## The Sins of an Empire Laid Bare

Islamic interpretation of Nahum 3 reads the woe oracle as consistent with the Quranic pattern of divine judgment on oppressive civilizations. The catalogue of Nineveh's sins — bloodshed, plunder, sorcery, the seduction of nations — mirrors the Quran's descriptions of the sins of Pharaoh, 'Ad, and Thamud. The underlying principle is the same: empires that build their greatness on the suffering of others are storing up judgment for themselves.

## The Example of Thebes

The reference to Thebes, destroyed by the very power that now faces its own destruction, illustrates the Quranic warning: "Has there not reached you the news of those before you — the people of Nuh and 'Aad and Thamud and those after them? No one knows them but Allah" (14:9). The rise and fall of civilizations follows a pattern: those who forget God and oppress the weak are ultimately brought low, regardless of their military might.

## A Universal Warning

Muslim scholars read Nahum 3 as a warning that transcends its historical context. Every civilization, every government, every institution that perpetuates injustice is subject to the same divine judgment that fell on Nineveh. The chapter's closing line — "upon whom has not come your unceasing evil?" — is addressed not only to Nineveh but to every power that abuses its authority.
