## Overview

While the Book of Nahum is not part of the Quran, its themes are deeply consistent with Quranic teaching about the fate of nations that reject God's guidance. The Quran repeatedly recounts the destruction of peoples who persisted in sin after being warned — the people of 'Ad, Thamud, Pharaoh, and others. Nineveh's fall, as described by Nahum, fits this pattern: a civilization that was once spared (as the Quran affirms in Surah 10:98) but returned to its wickedness and was ultimately destroyed.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Muslim scholars engage with Nahum through the *Isra'iliyyat* framework, treating the book as Israelite prophetic literature whose moral lessons are consistent with Quranic revelation. The emphasis falls on the universal principle that God is sovereign over the rise and fall of civilizations, and that no nation is immune to divine judgment when it persists in oppression and idolatry.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Sovereignty**: God alone determines the rise and fall of nations and empires
- **Consequences of Sin**: Persistent wickedness, even after reprieve, leads to destruction
- **Warning to the Living**: Nineveh's fate serves as a warning to all communities that reject justice
- **Justice for the Oppressed**: God's judgment on oppressive powers vindicates those who suffered under them
