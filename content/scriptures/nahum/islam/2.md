## The Fall of a Civilization

Islamic interpretation of Nahum 2 reads the vivid siege narrative as an illustration of the Quranic principle that civilizations fall when God decrees their end: "When We intend to destroy a city, We command its affluent but they defiantly disobey therein; so the word comes into effect upon it, and We destroy it with [complete] destruction" (17:16). Nineveh's fall, despite its massive fortifications, demonstrates that no material defense can withstand God's decree.

## The Lion of Imperial Hubris

The lion imagery (2:11-12) resonates with the Quranic critique of imperial arrogance. Empires that prey upon the weak, consuming nations like a lion consumes its prey, are ultimately brought low by God. The Quran presents Pharaoh as the archetype of such imperial hubris, and Nineveh fits the same pattern — a power that trusted in its own might rather than in God.

## Warning for All Nations

Muslim scholars read Nahum 2 as a warning that applies to every civilization: military power, economic wealth, and political dominance do not guarantee survival. Only righteousness and justice sustain a society. When a nation abandons justice, God withdraws his protection, and no army or wall can compensate for the loss.
