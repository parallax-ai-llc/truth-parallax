## Overview

Gnostic interpreters read the Book of Nahum as a prophetic vision of the fall of the archontic powers that rule the material world. Nineveh, as the seat of the most brutal empire of the ancient Near East, represents the archontic order at its most oppressive — the Demiurge's regime of violence, domination, and false order. Nahum's prophecy of Nineveh's total destruction is read as an anticipation of the ultimate dissolution of the material cosmos and the liberation of the divine sparks imprisoned within it.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The theophanic hymn of chapter 1, in which God appears as a cosmic warrior commanding storm, fire, and flood, is read as a description of the true God's power breaking through the Demiurge's domain. The vivid imagery of military collapse in chapters 2-3 — chariots overturned, gates thrown open, the city emptied — symbolizes the crumbling of the archontic structures that maintain the prison of matter.

## Theological Emphases

- **Fall of the Archons**: Nineveh's destruction symbolizes the eventual overthrow of the material world's rulers
- **The True God's Power**: The theophany reveals a God above and beyond the Demiurge, capable of shattering his order
- **Liberation of the Sparks**: The fall of oppressive power means freedom for the divine particles trapped in matter
- **Cosmic Dissolution**: The destruction of Nineveh prefigures the dissolution of the material cosmos itself
