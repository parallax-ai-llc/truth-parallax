## Overview

The Book of Nahum is an oracle against Nineveh, the capital of the Assyrian Empire, announcing its imminent and total destruction. Writing in the late seventh century BCE, Nahum prophesies the fall of the city that had terrorized the ancient Near East for over a century. Where Jonah told of Nineveh's repentance and reprieve, Nahum declares that reprieve exhausted — Assyria's cruelty, militarism, and idolatry have reached their limit, and God will act as both judge and warrior. The prophecy was fulfilled when Nineveh fell to the Babylonians and Medes in 612 BCE.

## Structure

1. **Chapter 1**: A theophanic hymn declaring God's power and justice; comfort for Judah
2. **Chapter 2**: The siege and fall of Nineveh described in vivid military imagery
3. **Chapter 3**: A woe oracle cataloguing Nineveh's sins and sealing its fate

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: Nahum's name means "comfort"; the book reassures Judah that the oppressor will fall and God's justice prevails
- **Christianity**: The fall of Nineveh prefigures the ultimate judgment of all unjust powers; God's wrath serves his redemptive purposes
- **Islam**: The destruction of a once-great civilization for its sins echoes the Quranic narratives of destroyed nations (*qawm*)
- **Gnosticism**: The violent overthrow of imperial power is read as a symbol of the defeat of the Demiurge's archons

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Divine Vengeance | God is "slow to anger" but will not leave the guilty unpunished |
| The Fall of Empire | Even the mightiest empire is subject to God's judgment |
| Comfort for the Oppressed | Nineveh's fall means liberation for those crushed under Assyria's heel |
| The Warrior God | God appears as a cosmic warrior who fights on behalf of the vulnerable |
| The Irony of Power | Nineveh's strength becomes its undoing; its fortifications crumble like ripe figs |
