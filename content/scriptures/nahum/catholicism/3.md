## The City of Blood

Catholic interpretation of Nahum 3 reads the woe oracle as a prophetic exposure of the structural sin embedded in the Assyrian Empire. Nineveh is not merely a city with sinful individuals but a system built on blood — military conquest, deportation, forced labor, and the systematic exploitation of subject peoples. Catholic Social Teaching's concept of "structures of sin" finds a biblical precedent in Nahum's comprehensive indictment.

## The Seduction of the Nations

The imagery of Nineveh as a harlot who "sells nations through her whorings and peoples through her charms" (3:4) is read in Catholic interpretation as a description of cultural and political seduction. Imperial power sustains itself not only through military force but through the allure of wealth, culture, and sophisticated religion. The chapter warns against the seductive power of any civilization that offers prosperity at the cost of justice.

## The Irreversibility of Judgment

The chapter's closing declaration that Nineveh's wound is incurable marks a solemn theological boundary. Catholic theology affirms that God's mercy is always available to the repentant, but Nahum illustrates the reality that persistent refusal of mercy eventually reaches a point of no return. The door that was open in Jonah's day is now closed — not because God's mercy has diminished but because Nineveh's capacity for repentance has been extinguished.
