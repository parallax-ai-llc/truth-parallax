## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Nahum situates the book within the broader framework of divine providence and moral theology. The Church Fathers read Nineveh's fall as an illustration of the principle articulated in the Wisdom literature: "Pride goes before destruction" (Proverbs 16:18). Jerome, who composed a commentary on Nahum, emphasized that the same God who showed mercy to Nineveh in Jonah's day now executes judgment — not because God has changed, but because Nineveh has returned to its sins.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Catholic social teaching draws on Nahum's denunciation of imperial violence to affirm God's solidarity with oppressed peoples. The book's vivid imagery of military destruction is read not as glorification of violence but as a prophetic assurance that unjust power will not endure. The liturgical tradition has occasionally drawn on Nahum 1:15 ("How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news") in connection with evangelization.

## Theological Emphases

- **Providence and Justice**: God governs the rise and fall of nations according to his righteous purposes
- **Solidarity with the Oppressed**: Nineveh's fall means liberation for those crushed by Assyrian tyranny
- **The Moral Order**: Empires that violate the moral order carry within themselves the seeds of their own destruction
- **Prophetic Judgment**: The prophet speaks God's verdict over the powerful on behalf of the powerless
