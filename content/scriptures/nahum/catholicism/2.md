## The Fall of the Mighty

Catholic interpretation of Nahum 2 reads the vivid siege narrative as a demonstration of the theological principle that all human power is contingent on God's will. The chapter's graphic military imagery — chariots crashing, soldiers stumbling, the palace collapsing — is not gratuitous but serves to drive home the message that no fortification, however massive, can withstand divine judgment. Jerome noted that Nineveh's walls, among the most formidable in the ancient world, were as nothing before God.

## "I Am Against You" (2:13)

This divine declaration is the theological center of the chapter. Catholic interpretation reads it as a solemn pronouncement of judgment that no army or alliance can reverse. When God sets himself against a power, its fall is inevitable. The Church Fathers applied this principle broadly: any institution, nation, or individual that persists in injustice will eventually face the God who is "against" all oppression.

## The Empty Lions' Den

The mockery of Nineveh's lions (2:11-12) — once the terror of the nations, now scattered and destroyed — illustrates the vanity of trusting in military power. Catholic moral theology draws on such passages to warn against the idolatry of power and to affirm that true security comes only from God.
