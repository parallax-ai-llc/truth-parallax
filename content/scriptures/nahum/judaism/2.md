## The Fall of Nineveh in Jewish Memory

Jewish interpretation of Nahum 2 reads the chapter as a prophetic celebration of the historical fall of Nineveh in 612 BCE. The Assyrian Empire had destroyed the Northern Kingdom of Israel in 722 BCE and had terrorized Judah for over a century. Nineveh's fall was remembered in Jewish tradition as a paradigmatic act of divine justice — proof that God does not forget the suffering of his people and that imperial power, however overwhelming, is not permanent.

## The Vivid Imagery

Rashi and Radak attend closely to the chapter's military imagery, noting the precision of Nahum's descriptions: the chariots, the uniforms, the breach of the walls, the river gates. Some commentators connect the flood imagery to the historical tradition that the Tigris River's flooding aided the besieging armies — a detail that later historians confirmed. The precision of fulfilled prophecy is taken as evidence of its divine origin.

## The Lion Metaphor

The mockery of Assyria's lion symbol (2:11-12) is read with satisfaction in Jewish interpretation. The lion that once prowled and devoured — seizing territory, deporting populations, extracting tribute — is now hunted and destroyed. The reversal of the predator-prey relationship illustrates the Psalmist's confidence: "The LORD is king forever and ever; the nations perish from his land" (Psalm 10:16).
