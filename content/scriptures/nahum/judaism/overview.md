## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Nahum is read as the sequel to Jonah. Where Jonah records Nineveh's repentance and God's decision to spare the city, Nahum records the end of that grace period — Nineveh has returned to its violent ways, and God's judgment is now irrevocable. The prophet's name, Nahum, derives from the root *n-ch-m*, meaning "comfort," and Jewish interpretation emphasizes that the book's primary audience is not Nineveh but Judah, which is being comforted with the assurance that its oppressor will fall.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Rabbinic commentary on Nahum is relatively sparse compared to other prophets, but the Dead Sea Scrolls preserve a *pesher* on Nahum (4QpNah) that applies the book's prophecies to events of the Hasmonean period. Medieval commentators such as Rashi and Radak focused on the historical fulfillment of Nahum's prophecy in the actual fall of Nineveh in 612 BCE.

## Theological Emphases

- **Nechamah (Comfort)**: The book's primary purpose is to comfort Judah, not merely to condemn Nineveh
- **Divine Justice**: God does not tolerate oppression indefinitely; empires that abuse their power will be judged
- **The Jonah-Nahum Arc**: Repentance that does not endure does not ultimately avert judgment
- **The Book of the Twelve**: Nahum's place within the Twelve Prophets creates a canonical conversation about Nineveh's fate
