## The Sins of Assyria Catalogued

Jewish interpretation of Nahum 3 reads the woe oracle as a comprehensive catalogue of Assyrian atrocities. The Assyrians were notorious in the ancient world for their cruelty — mass deportations, impalement, flaying alive, the destruction of entire peoples. Nahum's language, while harsh, is measured against the reality of Assyrian practice. Jewish commentators from Rashi to modern scholars note that Nahum does not exaggerate; if anything, he understates the horror of Assyrian imperial policy.

## The Fall of Thebes as Precedent

The reference to Thebes (No-Amon, 3:8-10) serves a rhetorical purpose: Nineveh itself had participated in the sack of Thebes in 663 BCE. Now the same fate that Nineveh inflicted on Thebes will be inflicted on Nineveh. The principle of measure-for-measure (*middah k'neged middah*) — a cornerstone of Jewish theology of justice — is at work. What you did to others will be done to you.

## "All Who Hear Clap Their Hands" (3:19)

The book's final image — the nations clapping with joy at Nineveh's fall — is read in Jewish interpretation without embarrassment. After generations of terror, the liberation that comes with the oppressor's fall is a legitimate cause for celebration. The Midrash connects this to the Song of the Sea (Exodus 15): when tyranny ends, the appropriate response is joyful praise.
