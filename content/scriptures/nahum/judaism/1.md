## The Opening Hymn and Divine Attributes

Jewish interpretation of Nahum 1 notes that the theophanic hymn draws on the language of God's self-revelation at Sinai (Exodus 34:6-7). The phrase "slow to anger and great in power" echoes the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy, but here the emphasis falls on the other side: "the LORD will by no means clear the guilty." The rabbis read this as a reminder that the same God who forgives the repentant also judges the impenitent — and Nineveh, having returned to its sins after the reprieve recorded in Jonah, now faces the justice it deferred.

## The Alphabetic Acrostic

Scholars have detected the remnants of an alphabetic acrostic in Nahum 1:2-8, suggesting that the passage was composed as a formal hymn. Jewish commentators note that this literary form — proceeding from *aleph* to *tav* — symbolizes the totality and comprehensiveness of God's sovereignty: from beginning to end, all things are under God's governance.

## Comfort for Judah

The prophet's name, Nahum, means "comfort" (*nacham*), and Jewish interpretation emphasizes that the book's primary audience is Judah, not Nineveh. The fall of the oppressor is the comfort of the oppressed. The chapter assures Judah that the Assyrian yoke will be broken and that God's justice ultimately prevails over imperial violence.
