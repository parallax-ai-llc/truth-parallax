## The Siege of Nineveh

Protestant interpretation of Nahum 2 reads the chapter as a vivid, almost cinematographic depiction of Nineveh's fall. The imagery is concrete and military: scarlet-clad warriors, flashing chariots, the opening of the river gates, the palace dissolving in flood. Protestant commentators note that Nahum's prophecy was fulfilled with remarkable precision when the coalition of Babylonians and Medes breached Nineveh's walls in 612 BCE, reportedly aided by a flood of the Tigris River.

## God as Divine Warrior

Behind the human armies stands God himself: "Behold, I am against you, declares the LORD of hosts" (2:13). Protestant theology emphasizes that the fall of Nineveh is not merely a military event but a divine act of judgment. God uses human agents but is the ultimate actor. The divine warrior motif connects Nahum to the broader biblical theme of God fighting on behalf of the oppressed.

## The Lions' Den Reversed (2:11-12)

Nahum mocks Nineveh with the image of a lions' den — Assyria had used the lion as its imperial symbol. The den that once sheltered the lions and their cubs is now empty, desolate. Protestant commentators see in this reversal a demonstration of the principle that the symbols of oppressive power will be turned against the oppressor.
