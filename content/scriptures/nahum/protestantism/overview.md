## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Nahum takes the book as a powerful affirmation of God's sovereign justice over the nations. The Reformers read the fall of Nineveh as a historical demonstration that no empire, however mighty, is beyond God's judgment. Calvin emphasized that God's patience, while real, has limits — the Assyrians had been granted time to repent (as seen in Jonah) but had returned to their violence, and now judgment was final.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Conservative Protestant scholars defend the book's prophetic authenticity and read it as fulfilled prophecy — Nineveh fell exactly as Nahum described. The book is also read canonically alongside Jonah: Jonah records Nineveh's temporary reprieve, Nahum records the end of that reprieve. The theological principle is that grace refused eventually gives way to judgment.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Justice**: God is a righteous judge who will not let oppression go unpunished indefinitely
- **The Fall of Empire**: Nineveh's destruction demonstrates that all human power is subject to God's sovereignty
- **Comfort for God's People**: Nahum's name means "comfort" — the book assures the oppressed that God will act
- **The Limits of Patience**: God's slowness to anger does not mean God will never act
