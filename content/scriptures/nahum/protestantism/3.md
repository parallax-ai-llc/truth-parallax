## Woe to the City of Blood

Protestant interpretation of Nahum 3 reads the chapter as a final, devastating indictment of Nineveh's sins. The city is condemned for its "unceasing evil" (3:19) — lies, plunder, military violence, and the spiritual seduction of the nations through sorcery and harlotry. Protestant commentators understand the "harlotry" metaphorically as Nineveh's seductive political alliances and cultural influence, by which it corrupted the nations it conquered.

## The Example of Thebes (3:8-10)

Nahum's reference to the fall of Thebes (No-Amon) in 663 BCE serves as a historical precedent: if mighty Thebes fell, how can Nineveh presume to be invulnerable? Protestant historical criticism values this reference for dating the prophecy — after 663 but before 612 BCE — and for demonstrating the prophetic method of learning from history.

## The Incurable Wound (3:19)

"There is no easing your hurt; your wound is grievous." The book ends not with hope for Nineveh but with finality. Protestant commentators observe that Nahum differs from Jonah in this respect: where Jonah offered repentance, Nahum announces irrevocable judgment. The difference lies in Nineveh's response — grace refused becomes grace exhausted.
