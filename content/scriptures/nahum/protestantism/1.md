## The LORD Is Slow to Anger

Protestant interpretation of Nahum 1 reads the opening theophanic hymn as a comprehensive statement about God's character: he is both jealous and patient, both wrathful and merciful. The verse "The LORD is slow to anger and great in power, and the LORD will by no means clear the guilty" (1:3) holds together two truths that human beings tend to separate — God's patience and his justice. Calvin emphasized that God's slowness to anger is real patience, not indifference; when judgment finally comes, it is the action of a God who has waited long.

## The Theophany (1:2-8)

The description of God's power over storm, sea, and mountain echoes earlier theophanies (Exodus 19, Psalm 18). Protestant commentators read this as a reminder that the God who judges Nineveh is the same God who delivered Israel from Egypt — the covenant God who acts in history. The theophany establishes that Nineveh's fall is not merely a geopolitical event but a divine act.

## Good News for Judah (1:15)

"Behold, upon the mountains, the feet of him who brings good news!" — this verse, applied to evangelism in Isaiah 52:7 and Romans 10:15, here announces specifically the good news of Nineveh's fall. For Judah, the end of Assyrian terror is gospel. Protestant interpreters note that liberation from oppression is itself good news, anticipating the ultimate liberation from sin and death.
