## The Theophany of Divine Glory

Orthodox interpretation of Nahum 1 reads the opening hymn as a revelation of divine glory comparable to the theophanies at Sinai and in Ezekiel. God's appearance in storm, earthquake, and fire reveals his absolute sovereignty over creation. The mountains tremble, the sea retreats, and the rivers dry up — all nature acknowledges its Creator. Orthodox hymnography draws on such imagery to celebrate God's power, particularly in the canons and troparia of major feasts.

## The Jealousy of God

"The LORD is a jealous and avenging God" (1:2) — Orthodox theology interprets divine jealousy not as a petty emotion but as the intensity of divine love. God's jealousy is his passionate commitment to his people and his refusal to share them with false gods. It is the zeal of a lover, not the possessiveness of a tyrant.

## The Refuge in Trouble

"The LORD is good, a stronghold in the day of trouble" (1:7). Orthodox spirituality finds in this verse the assurance that sustains believers through persecution and suffering. The same God whose power shakes the mountains provides tender shelter for those who trust in him — a paradox that is central to the Orthodox experience of God.
