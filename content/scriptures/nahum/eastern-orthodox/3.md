## The Exposure of Evil

Orthodox interpretation of Nahum 3 reads the woe oracle as God's act of exposing evil in all its reality. The language of uncovering and shaming (3:5-6) — "I will lift your skirts over your face; and I will let nations look on your nakedness" — is understood as God stripping away the pretenses and propaganda by which empires conceal their violence. The fall of Nineveh is an act of truth-telling: the world sees what the empire truly was.

## Sorcery and False Religion

The condemnation of Nineveh's "sorceries" (3:4) is read in Orthodox interpretation as a judgment on all forms of spiritual deception. The archontic powers — whether understood as demonic forces or as the ideologies that sustain unjust systems — use spiritual seduction to maintain their hold. Nineveh's sorcery represents the religious dimension of imperial control, and God's judgment strips it away.

## The Finality of Judgment

The book ends without hope for Nineveh: "All who hear the news about you clap their hands over you. For upon whom has not come your unceasing evil?" (3:19). Orthodox theology holds this in tension with the universal scope of God's mercy: Nineveh's judgment is final because Nineveh's sin was final — not because God's love is limited, but because Nineveh exhausted every opportunity for repentance.
