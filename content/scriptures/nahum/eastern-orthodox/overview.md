## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox tradition reads the Book of Nahum within the broader framework of God's cosmic governance and the eschatological defeat of evil. The theophanic hymn of chapter 1 — in which God appears in storm, earthquake, and fire — is read alongside other biblical theophanies as a revelation of divine glory and power. The fall of Nineveh is understood not merely as a historical event but as a type of the final judgment, when all unjust powers will be overthrown by God's justice.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The Greek Fathers, including Cyril of Alexandria and Theodoret, commented on Nahum with attention to both its historical and spiritual senses. Orthodox hymnography occasionally draws on Nahum's imagery of divine deliverance. The tradition emphasizes that God's wrath is not arbitrary anger but the necessary expression of his justice and holiness in the face of unrepentant evil.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theophany**: God's appearance in power reveals his glory and sovereignty over all creation
- **Eschatological Judgment**: Nineveh's fall is a type of the final judgment of all evil
- **Divine Comfort**: God's justice is the ultimate source of comfort for those who suffer under oppression
- **The Defeat of Evil**: The fall of Nineveh anticipates the ultimate triumph of good over evil in God's kingdom
