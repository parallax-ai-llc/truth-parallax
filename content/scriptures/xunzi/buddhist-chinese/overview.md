## Overview

Chinese Buddhist engagement with the Xunzi has been shaped by parallel concerns about human nature and the possibility of moral transformation. Xunzi's insistence that human beings are born with selfish desires that must be overcome through disciplined practice resonates with Buddhist teachings on the afflictions (klesha) and the necessity of cultivation. Yet his rejection of any innate goodness stands in tension with Mahayana doctrines of Buddha-nature.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist readers note that Xunzi and Buddhism share a realistic assessment of the untrained human condition: both traditions acknowledge that without deliberate effort, human beings are driven by desires that lead to suffering and social disorder. The Buddhist path of ethical discipline (sila), concentration (samadhi), and wisdom (prajna) parallels Xunzi's program of ritual, education, and moral transformation in significant structural ways.

## Key Differences

The fundamental divergence concerns ultimate aims. Xunzi seeks to produce the exemplary person (junzi) who contributes to social harmony within the human world. Buddhism aims at liberation from the cycle of rebirth altogether. Xunzi's emphasis on ritual as the mechanism of transformation also differs from the Buddhist emphasis on meditation and insight into the nature of mind.
