Chinese Buddhist reading of Xunzi chapter 6, "Against the Twelve Masters," finds points of contact with Buddhist understandings of moral cultivation and the transformation of consciousness. Xunzi's recognition that untrained human desires lead to conflict and suffering parallels the Buddhist analysis of the three poisons — greed, hatred, and delusion — as the root causes of dukkha.

However, Buddhist interpreters note that Xunzi's program of transformation through ritual and social conformity differs fundamentally from the Buddhist path of inner liberation through meditation and insight. Where Xunzi seeks to reshape desire into socially constructive channels, Buddhism aims ultimately to uproot the attachment that gives rise to desire altogether. This chapter thus illuminates both the convergences and the irreducible differences between Confucian and Buddhist approaches to the human condition.
