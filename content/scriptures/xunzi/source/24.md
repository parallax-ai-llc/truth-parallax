## Jun Zi (The Gentleman)

The gentleman speaks of what is within the scope of his knowledge and remains silent about what is beyond it. He speaks what is true and practices what he preaches.

The gentleman says: Learning should be like coloring silk -- once the dye is absorbed, it cannot be removed. Therefore the choice of what one studies is of the utmost importance.

The gentleman is broad in his learning and examines himself three times a day: Have I been loyal in my dealings? Have I been trustworthy with my friends? Have I practiced what I have been taught?

The gentleman prizes three things: a countenance that is warm and genial, a manner that is courteous and respectful, and words that are sincere and truthful.

The gentleman avoids three things: reckless speech, reckless action, and reckless thought.

When the gentleman is at home, he is respectful. When serving in office, he is diligent. When dealing with others, he is loyal. Even when among barbarians, he does not abandon these principles.

The gentleman takes the Way as his standard and adjusts his conduct accordingly. He does not bend the Way to suit himself. When the world follows the Way, the gentleman flourishes. When the world does not follow the Way, the gentleman still maintains his virtue.

The petty man is the opposite: When the world follows the Way, he conforms. When it does not, he abandons principle.
