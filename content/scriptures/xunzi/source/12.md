## Jun Dao (The Way of the Ruler)

The ruler is the fountainhead of the state. When the fountainhead is clear, the stream runs pure. When the fountainhead is muddy, the stream runs foul.

The ruler who wishes to govern well does not try to do everything himself. He appoints the right men to the right positions and oversees them. He establishes the principles and allows his ministers to carry them out.

The Way of the ruler lies in knowing men. If the ruler knows how to select the worthy and employ them, and how to identify the unworthy and remove them, then government will proceed of itself.

The ruler must be impartial. If he favors one faction, the other factions will be resentful. If he allows personal feelings to influence his appointments, the worthy will withdraw and the unworthy will advance.

The ruler should be like a balance scale: perfectly level, showing no bias. He weighs men's abilities and assigns them their duties. He judges their performance and rewards or punishes accordingly.

The ruler should be slow to act and quick to listen. He should deliberate carefully before making decisions. Once he has decided, he should act firmly and without hesitation.

Three things the ruler must guard against: favoritism in appointments, inconsistency in rewards and punishments, and neglect of public business for private pleasure. If the ruler guards against these three, the state will be well ordered.
