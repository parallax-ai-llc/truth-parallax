## Da Lue (The Grand Survey)

The great principles of government are few but fundamental. From these few principles, all the details of governance follow.

The first principle: Distinguish clearly between the worthy and the unworthy. Place the worthy in office; remove the unworthy. This alone will accomplish more than all other measures combined.

The second principle: Make the laws clear and enforce them consistently. When the laws are unclear, the people are confused. When enforcement is inconsistent, the people are resentful.

The third principle: Be sparing with the people's labor. Do not call them away from their fields during planting and harvest. Do not exhaust them with excessive construction projects or military campaigns.

The fourth principle: Lead by example. The ruler's conduct is the model for the people. If the ruler is virtuous, the people will follow. If the ruler is corrupt, no amount of law enforcement will prevent corruption.

When these principles are followed, order arises naturally. When they are neglected, disorder follows inevitably.

The sage does not invent new principles. He applies the ancient principles to the present age. The principles themselves never change -- they are as constant as Heaven and Earth. But their application must be adapted to the circumstances of the time.

Therefore the true statesman combines knowledge of the ancient Way with understanding of the present situation. He is neither rigidly traditional nor recklessly innovative.
