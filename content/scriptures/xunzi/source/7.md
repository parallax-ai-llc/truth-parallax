## Zhong Ni (On Confucius)

Among the rulers of the age, there were the hegemons such as Duke Huan of Qi and Duke Wen of Jin. They were not true kings but they brought a measure of order to the world.

The hegemon unifies the feudal lords through strength and good faith. He keeps his promises and rewards and punishes with consistency. This is sufficient to maintain order, but it falls short of the Way of the true king.

The true king acts from humanity and righteousness. He transforms the people through moral influence. He does not rely on force or cleverness, but on the power of virtue. The people follow him as water flows downhill -- naturally and without compulsion.

Confucius could have been such a king, but the age was not right for him. Though he held no territory, his influence was greater than that of any feudal lord. Though he commanded no army, his power was greater than that of any general.

The Way of Confucius was this: To make the distinctions clear -- between right and wrong, between noble and base, between worthy and unworthy. To establish the rites and music. To cultivate the person and order the state. To love the people and use them wisely.

If a sage arose in the present age and followed the Way of Confucius, within a generation the world would be at peace. This is the greatness of the Confucian Way -- it is not bound by time or circumstance.
