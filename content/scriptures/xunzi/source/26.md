## Fu (Rhyme-Prose Poems)

The fu poems use imagery to convey philosophical ideas:

On ritual: "What is it that transforms the raw into the refined, the crude into the elegant? It was made by man but serves Heaven. It is not born with us but accompanies us to the grave. It shapes the outer form and orders the inner life. It is ritual."

On wisdom: "What is it that does not move yet reaches everywhere? It does not speak yet all things are made clear. It does not strive yet all things are accomplished. It is the mind of the sage, guided by the Way."

On the gentleman: "What is it that is firm yet not rigid? Flexible yet not weak? Bright yet not dazzling? Dark yet not obscure? It is the virtue of the gentleman."

On knowledge: "What is it that grows greater the more it is shared, and diminishes the more it is hoarded? It benefits all who receive it, yet is never exhausted. It is knowledge."

On government: "What is it that flourishes when neglected by the ruler, and withers when the ruler attends to it too closely? It is the natural life of the people. Govern them lightly, and they prosper. Govern them heavily, and they wither."

These poems demonstrate that even abstract philosophical concepts can be rendered vivid through poetic form. The sage expresses truth through all means available -- argument, song, poetry, and ritual.
