## Zheng Lun (Rectifying Theories)

Theories must be rectified so that the truth can be known. In our age, many hold theories that are partial, contradictory, or simply false. These must be examined and corrected.

Some say that human nature is good. This is wrong. Some say that order can be achieved without a ruler. This is wrong. Some say that rites and music are unnecessary. This is wrong.

The error of Mencius: He held that human nature is good. But if nature were good, what need would there be for sages, for rites, for education? The very existence of these things proves that nature must be corrected.

The error of Mo Di: He held that all should be loved equally. But if all were loved equally, there would be no distinction between father and stranger, between kin and outsider. This would destroy the bonds of family and society.

The error of Shen Dao: He held that following circumstances is sufficient for governance. But circumstances change, and without fixed principles, the ruler is adrift.

The correct theory is this: Human nature tends toward disorder, but it can be corrected through education, rites, and moral cultivation. The sages established standards of conduct -- not because nature is good, but precisely because nature must be shaped and guided.

Rectifying theories is the work of the philosopher. Without correct theories, good governance is impossible. Without good governance, the people cannot live in peace.
