## Overview

Neo-Confucian engagement with the Xunzi has been predominantly critical. When Zhu Xi (1130–1200) established the Four Books as the core Confucian curriculum, he deliberately excluded the Xunzi and elevated Mencius, enshrining the doctrine that human nature is originally good (xing shan) as orthodoxy. Xunzi's teaching that human nature is evil was condemned as dangerously close to Legalism.

## Interpretive Approach

Neo-Confucian readers typically approach the Xunzi as a cautionary example of what happens when Confucian thought loses its metaphysical grounding in the original goodness of human nature. Zhu Xi acknowledged Xunzi's intellectual brilliance but argued that his error on the question of human nature led inexorably to his students Han Fei and Li Si embracing authoritarian Legalism.

## Rehabilitation in Qing Dynasty

The Qing dynasty evidential research movement (kaozheng) rehabilitated Xunzi as a serious thinker whose emphasis on ritual, institutions, and empirical observation offered a valuable corrective to what they saw as Neo-Confucian metaphysical speculation. Scholars such as Wang Zhong and Ling Tingkan argued that Xunzi's understanding of ritual was closer to the original Confucian vision than the Song dynasty moralists had recognized.
