## Overview

The Florentine Codex (*Historia General de las Cosas de Nueva España*) is the most comprehensive ethnographic document of Aztec (Nahua) civilization, compiled by the Franciscan friar Bernardino de Sahagún between approximately 1545 and 1590 with the collaboration of indigenous Nahua scholars and informants. The work consists of twelve books covering virtually every aspect of Nahua life — gods and ceremonies, calendrical systems, omens and divination, philosophy and rhetoric, the natural world, social organization, merchants and artisans, kings and warfare, and the conquest of Mexico.

Written in parallel Nahuatl and Spanish columns with over 2,000 illustrations, the Florentine Codex is simultaneously a work of missionary anthropology, indigenous self-representation, and collaborative scholarship. Sahagún's Nahua collaborators (the trilingüe students of the College of Santa Cruz de Tlatelolco) drew on elder informants' knowledge to create a systematic record of pre-conquest culture. The result is a document of extraordinary richness that preserves Nahua voices, perspectives, and knowledge systems even as it was produced within a colonial framework.

## Structure

| Book | Title | Content |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The Gods | The major Aztec deities, their attributes, ceremonies, and temples |
| 2 | Ceremonies | The eighteen monthly festivals (veintenas) and associated rituals |
| 3 | The Origin of the Gods | Mythological narratives including Quetzalcoatl and the creation of humanity |
| 4 | The Soothsayers | Divination, the tonalpohualli calendar, and day-sign interpretation |
| 5 | Omens | Signs, portents, and auguries |
| 6 | Rhetoric and Philosophy | Huehuetlatolli (ancient speeches), moral philosophy, prayers, and proverbs |
| 7 | The Sun, Moon, and Stars | Astronomical knowledge and celestial mythology |
| 8 | Kings and Lords | Political organization, governance, and royal customs |
| 9 | Merchants and Artisans | The pochteca (long-distance merchants) and skilled craftspeople |
| 10 | The People | Social roles, virtues, vices, diseases, and bodily descriptions |
| 11 | Earthly Things | Natural history — animals, plants, minerals, and the land |
| 12 | The Conquest | The Nahua account of the Spanish conquest of Mexico |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: Preserves Nahua voices, knowledge systems, and cultural memory within a colonial framework
- **Academic**: The foundational text for Aztec studies and Mesoamerican ethnohistory
- **Comparative**: Enables comparison of Nahua philosophy, religion, and science with other civilizations
- **Theological**: Contains rich theological content including prayers, hymns, and philosophical discourses
- **Archaeological**: Provides detailed descriptions of material culture, architecture, and ceremonial practice
- **Interfaith**: Reveals a sophisticated religious and philosophical tradition deserving of interfaith recognition

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Ethnography | Systematic recording of an entire civilization's knowledge and practices |
| Collaboration | The complex dynamics between Sahagún, his Nahua collaborators, and indigenous elders |
| Colonial Context | The production of indigenous knowledge within a framework of colonial power |
| Nahua Philosophy | Sophisticated moral, metaphysical, and aesthetic thought (huehuetlatolli) |
| Religious Practice | Detailed descriptions of Aztec ceremonies, prayers, and theological concepts |
| Conquest Narrative | The indigenous perspective on the fall of the Aztec empire |
