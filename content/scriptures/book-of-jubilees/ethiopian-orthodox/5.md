## Moses and the Fulfillment of the Covenant

Ethiopian Orthodox tradition reads Jubilees' concluding chapters on Moses and the Exodus as the culmination of the covenantal narrative that began with creation. The text's treatment of Israel's enslavement in Egypt, the Passover, and the crossing of the Red Sea is received as divinely revealed supplement to the Exodus account. The Angel of the Presence's role in narrating these events to Moses on Sinai frames the entire Exodus as part of the heavenly revelation, establishing continuity between the patriarchal and Mosaic dispensations.

## Passover and Liturgical Practice

Jubilees' detailed Passover legislation — specifying the age of participants, the timing of the sacrifice, and the prohibition against celebrating it outside the sanctuary — has influenced Ethiopian Orthodox Passover observance and its integration into the Easter (Fasika) celebration. The text's insistence on the precise and unchangeable character of the Passover ordinance supports the Ethiopian Orthodox emphasis on liturgical exactitude.

## Sabbath as Climax

Jubilees concludes with an extensive sabbath legislation (chapter 50) that Ethiopian Orthodox interpreters read as the capstone of the entire revelation. The text's presentation of the sabbath as the sign of the eternal covenant, observed by God and the angels from creation, reinforces the Ethiopian Orthodox practice of sabbath observance alongside Sunday worship — a distinctive feature of Ethiopian Christianity.
