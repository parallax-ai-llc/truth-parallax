## Divine Justice and the Flood

Catholic interpreters engage Jubilees' Flood account as a theological meditation on divine justice and mercy. The text's careful explanation of the Flood's causes — the transgression of the Watchers, the corruption of all flesh, and the universal spread of violence — provides a more detailed theodicy than the Genesis account alone. Catholic scholars note how Jubilees' emphasis on the Flood as a response to cosmic disorder rather than merely human sin reflects a sophisticated theology of evil that resonates with the Catholic tradition's understanding of the Fall's cosmic dimensions.

## Demonology and Spiritual Warfare

Jubilees' account of Mastema's retention of one-tenth of the evil spirits offers an early Jewish explanation for the persistence of evil after the Flood. Catholic interpreters compare this tradition with the development of Catholic demonology in the patristic and medieval periods, noting structural similarities in the understanding of demonic forces as permitted by God to operate within defined limits. The text's emphasis on Noah's prayer as the means by which the demons are bound reflects a theology of intercessory prayer consonant with Catholic devotional practice.

## The Division of the Earth

The detailed geographical division of the earth among Noah's sons in Jubilees 8–9 reflects a concern with cosmic order and divine allocation that Catholic interpreters compare with the providentialist reading of history in Augustine's City of God and subsequent Catholic historiography.
