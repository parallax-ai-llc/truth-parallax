## Overview

Catholic scholarship engages the Book of Jubilees as a significant witness to Jewish biblical interpretation in the Second Temple period. The text's method of expanding and supplementing the biblical narrative resonates with the Catholic understanding that scripture is received and interpreted within a living tradition. Patristic writers including Epiphanius and the author of the Decretum Gelasianum were aware of Jubilees, and its themes have informed Catholic reflection on the relationship between the Old and New Testaments.

## Interpretive Approach

Catholic interpreters note Jubilees' emphasis on the antiquity and universality of divine law — the patriarchs observing sabbath, festivals, and purity regulations before Sinai. This retrojection of Torah observance into the patriarchal period supports the theological claim, important in Catholic tradition, that God's moral law is inscribed in nature and known to the righteous of all ages. The text's angelic mediation of revelation to Moses is also studied in relation to Catholic angelology and the theology of divine communication.

## Historical Value

Catholic scholars value Jubilees as evidence for the diversity of Jewish thought in the centuries immediately preceding Christianity. The text's strict legal interpretation, its calendar reform, and its polemics against assimilation illuminate the religious tensions that shaped the world into which Jesus was born and the early Church emerged.
