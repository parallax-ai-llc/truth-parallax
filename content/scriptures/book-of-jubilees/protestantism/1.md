## Rewriting Genesis

Protestant scholars study Jubilees' opening chapters as a masterful example of "rewritten Bible," in which the author retells the creation narrative and early Genesis while embedding theological and legal commentary directly into the narrative. The addition of precise chronological data, the naming of previously unnamed figures, and the harmonization of apparent contradictions in Genesis reveal the exegetical methods of a Second Temple Jewish author deeply concerned with the coherence and authority of the scriptural text.

## The Sabbath Before Sinai

Jubilees' claim that the sabbath was observed by the angels from creation and was ordained for Israel before the giving of the Torah at Sinai represents a significant theological move. Protestant interpreters note how this retrojection serves to establish the sabbath as an eternal, cosmic institution rather than a historical commandment — a strategy that parallels debates within Protestant theology about the continuity of sabbath observance between the Old and New Testaments.

## Hermeneutical Significance

The prologue's framing of the entire book as angelic dictation to Moses represents one of the strongest claims to authority in Jewish pseudepigraphical literature. Protestant scholars analyze this literary device as evidence of how Second Temple Jewish communities understood the processes of revelation and scriptural inspiration, providing context for later Christian debates about the nature of biblical authority.
