## Abraham in Second Temple Interpretation

Protestant scholars study Jubilees' Abraham narrative as evidence of how Second Temple Judaism interpreted the patriarchal stories. The text's emphasis on Abraham's active obedience — his rejection of idolatry, his separation from his father's household, his meticulous observance of tithes and festivals — contrasts with the Pauline emphasis on Abraham's faith (Genesis 15:6). Protestant interpreters use this contrast to illuminate the theological debates about faith and works that shaped both early Judaism and early Christianity.

## The Aqedah and Mastema

Jubilees' introduction of Mastema as the instigator of the Binding of Isaac has attracted significant Protestant scholarly attention. The parallel with Job's framing narrative transforms the Aqedah from a test of Abraham's obedience into a cosmic contest between God and the adversary. Protestant interpreters compare this tradition with the New Testament's understanding of temptation and testing, noting how the Mastema figure bridges the gap between the Satan of Job and the devil of the Gospels.

## Covenant and Calendar

Jubilees' careful integration of the Abrahamic covenant into its jubilee chronology and solar calendar demonstrates how the author sought to unify narrative, law, and time into a comprehensive covenantal framework. Protestant scholars note the text's assumption that covenant faithfulness includes correct calendar observance — a perspective that illuminates the calendrical disputes reflected in the Dead Sea Scrolls and possibly in the Gospel accounts of the Passion.
