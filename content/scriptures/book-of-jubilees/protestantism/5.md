## The Exodus in Jubilees

Protestant scholars study Jubilees' Exodus account as the text's most explicitly halakhic section, where narrative retelling gives way to detailed legal instruction. The Passover legislation and sabbath laws that conclude the book demonstrate the author's primary concern: establishing the eternal and unchangeable character of Israel's covenant obligations. Protestant interpreters note the contrast between this legal rigorism and Paul's teaching on Christian freedom from the ceremonial law.

## Mastema and the Plagues

Jubilees' assignment of a role to Mastema in the Egyptian plague narrative — he assists the Egyptian magicians and opposes Moses — adds a demonological dimension to the Exodus story that Protestant scholars connect to New Testament spiritual warfare themes. The text's portrayal of the Exodus as a cosmic battle between God and Mastema provides background for Paul's language of cosmic conflict and Christ's victory over the powers (Colossians 2:15).

## Sabbath Theology

The concluding sabbath legislation, with its severe penalties for violation and its cosmic grounding in God's own rest on the seventh day, provides Protestant scholars with important comparative material for understanding the development of sabbath theology. The text's strict sabbath regulations, which exceed even the later rabbinic norms in severity, represent one end of a spectrum of sabbath observance that illuminates the sabbath controversies recorded in the Gospels.
