## Rewriting the Flood Narrative

Protestant scholars study Jubilees' Flood account as a careful harmonization and expansion of the Genesis narrative. The author resolves tensions in the biblical text (such as the two different accounts of the animals entering the ark) and adds significant theological material, including a detailed angelology and demonology. The text's integration of the Watchers tradition from 1 Enoch into the Genesis Flood narrative demonstrates how Second Temple authors wove together multiple scriptural and parabiblical traditions into a unified account.

## Mastema and Demonology

Jubilees' introduction of Mastema — an adversarial figure who negotiates with God to retain one-tenth of the fallen spirits — has attracted particular Protestant scholarly attention. The parallel with the Satan figure in Job and the adversary in Zechariah 3 illuminates the development of Jewish demonology in the Second Temple period. Protestant interpreters note how this tradition contributed to the New Testament's assumptions about demonic activity and spiritual warfare.

## The Noahide Covenant

Jubilees' account of the laws given to Noah after the Flood — prohibiting the consumption of blood, mandating justice, and establishing basic moral principles — provides an early witness to the concept of Noahide laws that became important in rabbinic Judaism. Protestant scholars compare these with the apostolic decree in Acts 15, noting possible connections between the two traditions.
