## Chapter 10 — Demonic Spirits and Healing

In Jubilees 10, the flood serves as cosmic judgment against a world corrupted by fallen angels and their offspring. Gnostic interpreters read this chapter through the lens of cosmic dualism, finding in it early expressions of the struggle between spiritual light and material darkness that Gnostic mythology elaborates in full.

## Gnostic Interpretive Lens

Jubilees 10 is the chapter where Mastema — prince of the demonic spirits — emerges as a fully articulated cosmic antagonist, and for Gnostic interpreters this is the text's most explicit approach to their own mythology. When God commands the angels to bind all the evil spirits, Mastema intervenes, requesting that one-tenth remain under his authority to afflict humanity. God grants this request. In Gnostic terms, this negotiation reveals the Demiurge's complicity with the archon of darkness: the world-ruler does not eliminate evil but *licenses* it, allowing Mastema to function as a subordinate archon who tests and torments the souls trapped in matter.

Mastema's role here closely parallels that of Yaldabaoth or Saklas in Sethian Gnostic texts — a ruler of the material realm who operates with the permission of a higher (but still limited) divine authority. The fact that God negotiates with Mastema rather than simply overruling him suggests, to Gnostic readers, that the God of Jubilees is not the supreme, transcendent deity but a Demiurgic figure who must manage the forces of his own flawed creation through compromise rather than absolute sovereignty.

The healing knowledge revealed to Noah by the angels — medicinal herbs and remedies against demonic affliction — represents a form of practical *gnosis* granted to those who serve within the Demiurge's system. This is *psychic* knowledge: genuinely helpful within the material world but incapable of achieving ultimate liberation. The *pneumatic* gnosis that transcends the entire system of demons and remedies alike remains unspoken in Jubilees' framework, available only to those who read beyond its surface.

## Scholarly Assessment

Birger Pearson's analysis of Jewish demonology and Gnostic archontology identifies Mastema in Jubilees 10 as a crucial intermediate figure between the *satan* of Job and the fully developed archon-rulers of Gnostic mythology. The negotiation scene — where God deliberately retains evil spirits in the world — represents precisely the kind of theological problem that Gnostic thinkers resolved by positing a Demiurge distinct from the true God, one whose governance of the cosmos necessarily includes collaboration with malevolent powers.
