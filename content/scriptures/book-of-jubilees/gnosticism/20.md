## Chapter 20 — Abraham's Farewell to His Sons

Jubilees 20 presents Abraham's farewell address to his sons, charging them to keep the way of the Lord, avoid idolatry, and maintain purity. Gnostic interpreters read this testament as the pneumatic patriarch's final attempt to transmit spiritual knowledge within the constraints of the Demiurgic system.

## Gnostic Interpretive Lens

Abraham's farewell speech in Jubilees 20 focuses on ethical commandments: reject idols, avoid fornication, maintain purity, love one another. In Gnostic terms, this represents the *psychic* level of teaching — moral instruction that governs life within the material world but does not address the soul's transcendent origin or destiny. Abraham, who earlier in Jubilees showed signs of pneumatic awakening (rejecting idols, praying against evil spirits), now speaks as a patriarch firmly embedded in the Demiurge's ethical framework.

The prohibition against intermarriage with the Canaanites encodes the Gnostic concern with maintaining the pneumatic lineage. Just as Valentinian theology posited that the spiritual seed must be transmitted through specific channels to preserve its integrity, Abraham's insistence on endogamy ensures that the pneumatic inheritance passes through the proper line. The Canaanites, in this reading, represent the *hylic* population — those without the divine spark — with whom mixture would dilute the spiritual inheritance.

Abraham's address to Ishmael and his other sons alongside Isaac reveals the Demiurge's hierarchical system: all receive the ethical commandments, but the covenantal inheritance — the deeper *gnosis* — passes to Isaac alone. This reflects the Valentinian tripartite anthropology where *psychic* humanity receives the moral law, but only the *pneumatic* elect receive the salvific knowledge that transcends it.

## Scholarly Assessment

Einar Thomassen's work on Valentinian soteriology has demonstrated that the Gnostic distinction between moral instruction (*psychic*) and salvific knowledge (*pneumatic*) maps onto precisely the kind of hierarchical transmission found in Jubilees 20. Abraham's farewell gives ethical teaching to all his sons but reserves the deeper covenantal relationship for Isaac — a pattern that Gnostic interpreters read as confirming their understanding of differentiated spiritual natures and correspondingly differentiated revelations.
