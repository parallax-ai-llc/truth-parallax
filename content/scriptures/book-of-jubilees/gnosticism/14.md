## Chapter 14 — War of the Kings and Melchizedek

Jubilees 14 narrates the war of the kings, Abram's rescue of Lot, the encounter with Melchizedek, and the covenant of the pieces. Gnostic interpreters find in this chapter a dense web of cosmic warfare, mysterious priesthood, and visionary covenant that lends itself to esoteric reading.

## Gnostic Interpretive Lens

Melchizedek, the priest-king of Salem who appears without genealogy to bless Abram, is one of the most powerful Gnostic figures in the Hebrew Bible and its expansions. In the Nag Hammadi text *Melchizedek*, he is an emanation from the highest divine realm, a figure who transcends the Demiurgic priestly order entirely. Jubilees 14's presentation of Melchizedek as "priest of the Most High God" suggests a priesthood not derived from the Demiurge's institutional structures but originating in a higher, pre-cosmic order — a Pleromatic priesthood that Abram recognizes and honors.

The covenant of the pieces — where Abram cuts animals in half and a divine fire passes between them — is a visceral enactment of cosmic division. In Gnostic reading, the splitting of the animals represents the fundamental Demiurgic act: the division of the original unity into opposing pairs (spirit/matter, light/darkness, male/female). The fire passing between the pieces is the pneumatic spark traversing the divided cosmos, the divine presence that moves through the material world's fragmentations without being consumed by them.

The war of the kings, with Abram defeating the four kings to rescue Lot, reads as a pneumatic victory over the archons. Lot, trapped in Sodom (a Gnostic figure for the material world at its most degraded), is rescued by the one who possesses spiritual power. Yet this military victory is temporary and material — it does not liberate Lot from the conditions that will ultimately lead to Sodom's destruction.

## Scholarly Assessment

Gilles Quispel and Claudio Gianotto have traced the Melchizedek tradition from Jubilees through Hebrews to the Nag Hammadi Melchizedek text, showing a trajectory of progressive esotericization. Jubilees 14's portrait of a priest-king outside the normal genealogical order provided the seed for Gnostic interpretations of Melchizedek as a heavenly revealer figure operating independently of the Demiurgic priesthood established through Levi and Aaron.
