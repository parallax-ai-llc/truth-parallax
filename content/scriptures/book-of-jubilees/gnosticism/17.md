## Chapter 17 — Binding of Isaac

Jubilees 17 introduces the Binding of Isaac (*Aqedah*) with a crucial detail absent from Genesis: it is Mastema, not God, who instigates the test. Gnostic interpreters regard this attribution as one of the most revealing moments in the entire Book of Jubilees.

## Gnostic Interpretive Lens

The *Aqedah* in Jubilees 17 is reframed as a cosmic contest between Mastema and God, with Abraham's faithfulness as the stakes. For Gnostic readers, this triangular dynamic — Mastema proposing the test, God permitting it, Abraham enduring it — recapitulates the Job drama and confirms the Demiurgic system's essential cruelty. The God who allows Mastema to demand the sacrifice of Abraham's son is not the transcendent, benevolent source of all being but a ruler who governs through trials, tests, and suffering — the hallmarks of archontic governance.

Mastema's role as instigator reveals the collaboration between the Demiurge and his chief archon. In Sethian Gnostic mythology, Yaldabaoth and Saklas often operate in tandem — one commanding, the other provoking — to keep the pneumatic souls in a state of suffering and obedience. The *Aqedah* becomes the supreme example of this archontic strategy: the demand for absolute obedience, proven through willingness to sacrifice what is most precious, is the Demiurge's ultimate test of subjugation.

The connection Jubilees draws between the *Aqedah* and the Passover festival — placing it on the fourteenth of the first month — links the sacrifice of the son to the ongoing liturgical calendar. In Gnostic terms, the Demiurge memorializes his own cruelty as worship, transforming the archontic test into an annual celebration that reinforces the principle of obedient self-sacrifice within the material order.

## Scholarly Assessment

Jon Levenson's work on the *Aqedah* in Jewish and Christian tradition has shown how the attribution to Mastema in Jubilees 17 reflects an emerging theological discomfort with ascribing the test directly to God. Gnostic interpreters exploited exactly this discomfort, arguing that the need for a Mastema figure revealed what was implicit all along: that the God who tests through suffering is not the ultimate deity but a subordinate power whose governance requires the collaboration of malevolent forces.
