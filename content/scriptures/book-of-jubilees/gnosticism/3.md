## Chapter 3 — Garden of Eden and the Fall

Jubilees 3, part of the retelling of creation and early human history, presents creation through angelic activity and divine speech. Gnostic readers engage with this chapter's cosmology critically, noting how the angelic involvement in creation resonates with — yet ultimately differs from — the Gnostic understanding of creation as the work of ignorant or malevolent archons.

## Gnostic Interpretive Lens

The Garden of Eden narrative in Jubilees 3 is the single most resonant chapter for Gnostic interpretation. In Gnostic retellings — from the *Apocryphon of John* to the *Hypostasis of the Archons* — Eden is the Demiurge's controlled environment, designed to keep the first humans in ignorance. Jubilees' version, where God places Adam in the garden to "till and keep it," reinforces this reading: the human being is positioned as a laborer within the Demiurge's domain, assigned to serve the material creation rather than to awaken to transcendent knowledge.

The serpent's role undergoes a dramatic revaluation in Gnostic reading. Where Jubilees treats the serpent as a deceiver, many Gnostic texts (particularly Ophite traditions) regard the serpent as a messenger of *Sophia* — divine Wisdom — who brings the *gnosis* that the Demiurge sought to withhold. The tree of knowledge becomes the vehicle of salvific awakening, and God's prohibition against eating from it becomes the Demiurge's desperate attempt to maintain ignorance among his creatures.

Jubilees 3's unique detail about purification periods after the birth of male and female children, connecting Eden to purity law, exemplifies the Gnostic concern with how material regulations — bodily purity, gender distinctions, biological processes — serve as mechanisms by which the archons keep human consciousness fixated on the body rather than the spirit. The expulsion from Eden, in Gnostic terms, is not simply punishment but the soul's deeper descent into *hyle*, the beginning of its long exile from the Pleroma.

## Scholarly Assessment

Karen King's work on Gnostic reinterpretations of Genesis demonstrates that the Eden narrative was the primary biblical battleground between orthodox and Gnostic hermeneutics. Jubilees 3's distinctive additions — particularly the purification laws and the careful chronology of Eden events — show how Second Temple elaboration of Genesis created the very textual complexity that Gnostic exegetes would later exploit to construct alternative cosmologies centered on the reversal of orthodox valuations.
