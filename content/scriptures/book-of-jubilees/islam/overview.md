## Overview

The Book of Jubilees is not part of Islamic scripture, but Islamic scholars engage with it as part of the broader *Isra'iliyyat* tradition — narratives derived from Jewish and Christian sources that supplement Quranic accounts. Jubilees' retelling of prophetic histories from Adam to Moses contains numerous parallels with Quranic narratives.

## Relationship to Islamic Tradition

Islam does not recognize Jubilees as revelation, viewing the Quran as the final, uncorrupted divine message. However, several themes in Jubilees resonate with Islamic teaching:

- **Ibrahim (Abraham)**: Jubilees' account of Abraham rejecting idolatry closely parallels Surah al-Anbiya (21:51-70)
- **Angels**: The prominent role of angels in Jubilees aligns with Islamic angelology (belief in *mala'ika*)
- **Covenant**: The emphasis on divine covenant resonates with Islam's concept of *mithaq* (covenant)
- **Prophetic chain**: Jubilees' succession of righteous figures mirrors the Quranic chain of prophets

## Points of Divergence

| Issue | Jubilees | Islamic Teaching |
|---|---|---|
| Scripture | Claims Mosaic authorship | Quran is the final revelation |
| Calendar | 364-day solar calendar | Lunar Hijri calendar |
| Isaac vs. Ishmael | Isaac as the favored son | Isma'il has a prominent role |
| Textual authority | Supplements Torah | Previous scriptures subject to *tahrif* |

## Scholarly Engagement

Muslim scholars have historically engaged with Jubilees through the *Isra'iliyyat* framework, using it cautiously to illuminate prophetic narratives where it does not contradict the Quran.
