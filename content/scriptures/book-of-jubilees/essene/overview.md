## Overview

The Book of Jubilees held a position of near-canonical authority within the Essene movement, particularly the community at Qumran. The Damascus Document explicitly refers to "the book of the divisions of times into their jubilees and weeks," and the large number of Jubilees manuscripts found at Qumran (at least fifteen copies) attests to the text's centrality. Essene interpreters read Jubilees as an authoritative guide to the correct calendar, the proper interpretation of Torah, and the maintenance of covenant purity.

## Interpretive Approach

Essene reading of Jubilees emphasized the text's solar calendar as the divinely ordained reckoning of time, in contrast to the lunisolar calendar used in the Jerusalem Temple. The book's strict separatism — its warnings against intermarriage with Gentiles, its concern with purity, and its hostility toward assimilation — aligned with the Essene self-understanding as a community of the elect living in covenant faithfulness while the rest of Israel had gone astray. Jubilees' angelology, particularly its concept of the Angel of the Presence who dictates the revelation to Moses, complemented the Essene belief in angelic fellowship within the community.

## Community Identity

For the Essenes, Jubilees provided scriptural legitimation for their distinctive practices and beliefs. The text's retrojection of the solar calendar and strict halakhah onto the patriarchal period established these as eternal divine ordinances rather than sectarian innovations. This appeal to primordial authority was essential to the Essene claim that they, not the Jerusalem priesthood, represented authentic Israel.
