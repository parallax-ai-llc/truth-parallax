## The Exodus as Paradigm

The Essene community at Qumran read Jubilees' Exodus account as a paradigm for their own experience of deliverance from a corrupt religious establishment. Just as Israel was liberated from Egyptian bondage and constituted as a covenant people at Sinai, the Essenes understood themselves as a community called out of the apostate mainstream and reconstituted around the true Torah. Jubilees' emphasis on the Exodus as a cosmic conflict with Mastema reinforced the Essene dualistic worldview, in which their community struggle was part of a larger battle between the forces of light and darkness.

## Passover and Community Practice

Jubilees' strict Passover halakhah — including the sanctuary requirement and specific age and ritual provisions — informed Essene festival observance. The text's insistence that the Passover be celebrated only at the divinely appointed sanctuary raised the question, for a community that regarded the Jerusalem Temple as defiled, of how the festival could be properly observed. This tension may have contributed to the Essene development of alternative liturgical forms attested in the Qumran scrolls.

## Sabbath as Covenant Sign

The concluding sabbath legislation represented the capstone of the Essene legal program. The sabbath, as presented in Jubilees, was the supreme sign of the covenant — observed by God from creation, commanded to Israel alone among all nations, and punishable by death if violated. The Essene community's rigorous sabbath observance, as documented in the Damascus Document, reflects the influence of Jubilees' strict sabbath theology.
