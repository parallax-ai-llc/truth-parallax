## Overview

Academic study of the Book of Jubilees has been transformed by the discovery of Hebrew fragments at Qumran, which confirmed the text's original language and its importance in the Dead Sea Scrolls community. James VanderKam's critical edition and commentary established the standard for scholarly engagement with the text. Academic interpreters analyze Jubilees as a prime example of the "rewritten Bible" genre, studying its exegetical methods, theological agenda, and relationship to both the biblical text and the Qumran sectarian literature.

## Interpretive Approach

Critical scholars examine Jubilees' systematic reworking of Genesis and Exodus, identifying the author's exegetical strategies: harmonization of contradictions, filling of narrative gaps, retrojection of later legal practices, and integration of non-biblical traditions. The text's 364-day solar calendar, shared with the Astronomical Book of Enoch and several Qumran texts, has been the subject of extensive analysis. Scholars debate whether Jubilees was composed within the Qumran community, by its predecessors, or by an independent group sharing similar convictions.

## Key Debates

Central questions in academic study include the date of composition (most scholars favor the mid-2nd century BCE), the relationship between Jubilees and the Temple Scroll, the nature and extent of the text's legal innovations, and the social identity of the group or movement that produced and transmitted the work. The interplay between narrative retelling and legal argumentation in Jubilees has also generated productive methodological discussions about the boundaries between narrative, exegesis, and law in ancient Judaism.
