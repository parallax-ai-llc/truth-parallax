## Source Analysis and Composition

Scholars identify Jubilees 2 as a rewriting of Genesis 1, with significant expansions including the creation of angelic classes on the first day — a tradition absent from the biblical source. VanderKam and others note that the author systematically transforms the Priestly creation account into a foundation for the 364-day solar calendar, embedding calendrical theology into the structure of creation itself.

## The Sabbath Institution

The chapter's extended treatment of the sabbath reflects Second Temple debates about sabbath observance. The claim that Israel alone was elected to keep the sabbath "with the angels" introduces an exclusivist theology not found in Genesis. Comparative analysis with the Damascus Document and other Qumran texts reveals shared legal traditions, suggesting a common scribal milieu.

## Angelology and Cosmology

The detailed enumeration of angelic orders — angels of the presence, angels of sanctification, and nature angels — reflects a developing angelology that scholars trace through Second Temple literature. This taxonomy likely influenced later Jewish and Christian angel hierarchies. The text's cosmological framework integrates Mesopotamian and Hellenistic elements with Israelite traditions.
