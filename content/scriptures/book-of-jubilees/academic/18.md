## The Abraham Traditions

Jubilees 18 ("The Death of Sarah") exemplifies the author's extensive reworking of the Abrahamic narratives. Academic scholars analyze how the text expands, rearranges, and supplements the Genesis account to address exegetical problems, establish legal precedents, and advance theological arguments. The chapter's additions to the biblical narrative often reflect traditions attested also in later rabbinic literature, raising questions about shared oral or written sources.

## Legal and Halakhic Dimensions

The legal material embedded within the narrative — including regulations about sacrifice, purity, and calendar observance — reflects the concerns of a scribal community committed to a distinctive interpretation of Mosaic law. Comparison with the Damascus Document and the Temple Scroll reveals significant overlap, supporting the scholarly consensus that Jubilees emerged from circles closely related to the Qumran community.

## Composition and Redaction

Source-critical analysis suggests that the author of Jubilees drew on multiple traditions beyond Genesis, including early Enochic literature, Aramaic Levi, and possibly oral traditions about Abraham preserved in Second Temple scribal schools. The integration of these diverse sources into a coherent narrative demonstrates considerable literary skill.
