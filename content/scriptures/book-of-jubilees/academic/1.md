## Genre and Method

Academic scholars classify Jubilees' opening chapters as a paradigmatic example of the "rewritten Bible" genre, in which a later author retells a biblical narrative while incorporating interpretive expansions, legal rulings, and theological commentary. Scholars such as George Brooke, Moshe Bernstein, and James Kugel have analyzed the specific exegetical techniques employed: harmonization of contradictory passages, filling of narrative gaps, derivation of laws from narrative contexts, and integration of non-biblical traditions.

## The Prologue and Authority Claims

The prologue's framing device — the Angel of the Presence dictating from the heavenly tablets to Moses on Sinai — has been analyzed as a sophisticated literary strategy for claiming divine authority for the author's interpretations. Academic interpreters compare this technique with similar authority claims in other Second Temple texts (e.g., the Temple Scroll's first-person divine speech) and study its implications for understanding concepts of revelation and scriptural authority in early Judaism.

## Creation Traditions

The creation account in Jubilees 2 demonstrates the author's method of interweaving the biblical text with additional traditions. The detailed angelology — the creation of specific classes of angels on the first day — has no parallel in Genesis and likely draws on independent traditions about the heavenly world. Scholars have identified Mesopotamian and Hellenistic parallels to some of these traditions, illuminating the cosmopolitan intellectual context of Second Temple Jewish scribal culture.
