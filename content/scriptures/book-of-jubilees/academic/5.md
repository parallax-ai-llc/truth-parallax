## Legal Analysis

Academic scholars recognize the concluding chapters of Jubilees as the most explicitly halakhic section of the text, where the author's legal program reaches its fullest expression. The Passover legislation has been analyzed in detail by scholars including Lutz Doering and Michael Segal, who have compared its provisions with the biblical Passover laws, the Qumran Temple Scroll, the Damascus Document, and the later Mishnah. These comparisons reveal a complex landscape of competing legal traditions in Second Temple Judaism.

## Sabbath Regulations

The sabbath legislation in Jubilees 50 has been the subject of extensive scholarly analysis. Doering's comprehensive study demonstrates that Jubilees' sabbath rules are in many respects stricter than the rabbinic norms but share structural features with the sabbath regulations in the Damascus Document and other Qumran texts. This convergence supports the scholarly hypothesis of a shared legal tradition linking Jubilees to the Qumran community, though the precise relationship remains debated.

## The Text's Conclusion

The abrupt ending of Jubilees at the Sinai revelation — without narrating the wilderness wandering, the conquest, or the later history of Israel — is significant for academic interpreters. Scholars have debated whether the text was always intended to end at this point or whether a continuation has been lost. The concluding emphasis on sabbath and Passover may reflect the author's conviction that these institutions represent the climax of the covenantal relationship that the entire book has traced.
