## Abraham's Conversion from Idolatry

Jewish scholars note that Jubilees' account of Abraham's youthful rejection of idolatry parallels and may predate the well-known rabbinic midrash of Abraham smashing his father Terah's idols (Bereishit Rabbah 38:13). Both traditions address the same exegetical problem: Genesis says nothing about Abraham's inner transformation from polytheism to monotheism. Jubilees fills this gap with a narrative of Abraham's rational inquiry, prayer for guidance, and deliberate separation from his idolatrous environment.

## Circumcision and Covenant Law

Jubilees' exceptionally strict treatment of circumcision — declaring that any uncircumcised male "does not belong to the children of the covenant" and that the commandment is "an eternal ordinance" written on the heavenly tablets — reflects a priestly and separatist strand of Jewish legal thought. Jewish scholars compare these provisions with the rabbinic laws of circumcision (milah), noting both agreements and the distinctive rigor of Jubilees' formulations.

## The Aqedah Tradition

Jubilees' retelling of the Aqedah, with Mastema prompting the test and Abraham triumphing through unwavering faith, represents an early stage of the rich Jewish interpretive tradition surrounding Genesis 22. Jewish scholars trace the development of the Aqedah tradition from Jubilees through the targumim, the midrashim, and the medieval piyyutim, examining how each generation found in this narrative resources for addressing its own theological and existential challenges.
