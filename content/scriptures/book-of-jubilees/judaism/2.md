## Midrashic Expansion of the Flood

Jewish scholars recognize in Jubilees 5–10 an early and extensive midrashic treatment of the Flood narrative. The text harmonizes the two strands of the Genesis account, adds chronological precision using the jubilee framework, and introduces theological explanations for events that Genesis leaves unexplained. The addition of Noah's agricultural and pharmacological knowledge (Jubilees 10:10–14) reflects a tradition of Noah as a culture hero that also appears in later rabbinic and medieval Jewish literature.

## The Noahide Laws

Jubilees' account of the commandments given to Noah is studied by Jewish scholars as an important precursor to the rabbinic concept of the seven Noahide laws (sheva mitzvot b'nei Noah). While the specific list differs from the later rabbinic formulation, the underlying principle — that God established a universal moral law applicable to all humanity through Noah — is shared. This tradition has ongoing significance in Jewish legal thought and interfaith dialogue.

## Demonology and the Problem of Evil

Jubilees' introduction of Mastema and the retention of evil spirits provides a distinctively Jewish answer to the problem of evil's persistence after the Flood. Jewish scholars compare this with the rabbinic concept of the yetzer hara (evil inclination), noting that both traditions seek to explain why human beings continue to sin even after God's decisive judgment. The two approaches — external demonic influence versus internal moral struggle — represent competing but not mutually exclusive frameworks within Jewish thought.
