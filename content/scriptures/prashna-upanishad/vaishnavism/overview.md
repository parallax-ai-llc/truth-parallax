## Overview

Vaishnava interpreters identify the supreme Prana celebrated in the Prashna Upanishad with Vishnu-Narayana, the Lord who sustains all life. When Pippalada teaches that Prana is supreme among the faculties — that when Prana departs, all other faculties must follow — this is read as a parable of Vishnu's absolute sovereignty. All beings, faculties, and cosmic functions depend on the Lord's sustaining power and would collapse without it.

## Theological Emphases

The Prashna Upanishad's cosmogony (Chapter 1), in which Prajapati creates through the pairing of Rayi (matter) and Prana (spirit), is read as describing Vishnu's creative act through His *shakti* (divine energy, identified with Lakshmi). The teaching on AUM (Chapter 5) is understood as a meditation on Vishnu's sacred name, with each syllable corresponding to an aspect of the Lord's nature. The person with sixteen parts (Chapter 6) who merges into the supreme Purusha is the devotee who, at liberation, enters Vishnu's eternal abode while retaining individual identity.
