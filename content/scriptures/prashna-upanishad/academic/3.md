Chapter 3's account of the five pranas — prana, apana, samana, vyana, and udana — represents one of the most detailed early Indian analyses of the vital breath and its functions. This five-fold classification became standard in later Yoga, Ayurveda, and Samkhya systems. Scholars have debated whether the classification originated in medical observation, ritual practice, or philosophical speculation — likely all three contributed.

The analogy of the king and governors (3.4) has been analyzed as evidence for the political context of Upanishadic thought, reflecting the administrative structures of the *mahajanapada* (great kingdom) period in which the later Upanishads were composed. The relationship between Atman and the five pranas mirrors the relationship between a sovereign and provincial governors — an analogy that would be natural in the increasingly urbanized and politically complex society of the 5th-4th century BCE Gangetic plain.
