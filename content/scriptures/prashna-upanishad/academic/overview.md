## Overview

The Prashna Upanishad belongs to the Atharva Veda tradition and is typically dated to the 5th-4th century BCE, making it one of the later principal Upanishads. Its systematic question-and-answer format distinguishes it from the more discursive style of earlier Upanishads and may reflect the development of more formal pedagogical methods in Vedic education. The text's association with the Atharva Veda connects it to traditions of healing, cosmological speculation, and life-affirming philosophy.

## Research Themes

The Prashna Upanishad's theory of Prana represents one of the most developed pre-scientific models of vital force in ancient Indian thought, with detailed accounts of how breath is distributed through the body via five sub-functions (prana, apana, samana, udana, vyana). The cosmogony of Chapter 1, pairing Rayi (matter) and Prana (spirit/energy), anticipates Samkhya dualism. The sixteen-part person (Chapter 6) has generated scholarly debate about its relationship to early Samkhya categories and Buddhist aggregates. The text's six-question structure may preserve an actual catechetical format used in Atharva Veda schools.
