Chapter 6's teaching on the person with sixteen parts has generated scholarly debate about its relationship to early Samkhya enumerations of categories (*tattvas*). The list of sixteen parts — Prana, faith, space, elements, senses, mind, food, strength, austerity, mantras, action, worlds, and name — does not correspond exactly to any known Samkhya enumeration, suggesting an independent classificatory system. Some scholars see in it an Atharva Veda tradition distinct from the Samkhya-influenced categories of other Upanishads.

The rivers-and-ocean simile (6.5) has been the subject of intense hermeneutical debate between Advaita and Dvaita scholars, which extends into modern academic discussion. The original meaning of the simile — whether it teaches complete dissolution of individuality or absorption-with-continuity — cannot be determined with certainty from the text alone, as both readings are grammatically possible. The chapter provides a satisfying structural conclusion to the six-question framework, moving from cosmological origins (Chapter 1) to the ultimate nature and destiny of the person.
