Chapter 4's treatment of sleep, dreams, and deep sleep represents an early Indian contribution to the phenomenology of consciousness. The analysis of dreaming as the mind's recombination of waking impressions (4.5) anticipates elements of modern dream theory. The recognition that the self continues to exist in deep sleep — and experiences happiness there — raises philosophical questions about the nature of consciousness that remain debated in contemporary philosophy of mind.

Scholars have compared this passage with the more elaborate treatments of sleep states in the Brihadaranyaka (4.3) and Mandukya Upanishads. The Prashna's account is more physiological than philosophical, describing the mechanism by which faculties withdraw during sleep rather than analyzing the metaphysical implications. This may reflect the Atharva Veda tradition's characteristic interest in psycho-physical processes and their practical significance for health, meditation, and spiritual practice.
