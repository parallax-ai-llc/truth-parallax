The contest of the faculties — in which the senses compete for supremacy, only for Prana (breath/life-force) to prove indispensable — resonates with Aesop's fable of the body parts disputing their importance, the medieval Christian debate tradition of the "members of the body" (drawing on 1 Corinthians 12), and the Chinese medical understanding of *qi* as the fundamental principle sustaining all bodily functions.

The primacy of breath across traditions is striking. Pranayama in yoga, *qi gong* in Chinese practice, the hesychast breathing prayer in Orthodox Christianity, and the Sufi *dhikr* coordinated with breath all recognize that the breath is uniquely positioned between the voluntary and involuntary, the conscious and unconscious, making it a gateway between the mundane and the spiritual.
