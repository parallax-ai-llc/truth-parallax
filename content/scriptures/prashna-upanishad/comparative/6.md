The Prashna's concluding image of the rivers merging into the ocean is one of the most universal metaphors in mystical literature. The Sufi poet Rumi uses the same image repeatedly. The *Tao Te Ching* speaks of all streams returning to the sea. The Buddhist *Mahaparinirvana Sutra* describes the Buddha's final passing as a river reaching the ocean. In each case, the metaphor conveys the dissolution of separateness and the return to a primordial unity — though traditions differ on whether individual identity survives this merging.

The person with sixteen parts, manifesting the diversity of the cosmos from an undivided source, resonates with the Kabbalistic *Adam Kadmon* (primordial human) whose *sefirot* (emanations) structure the universe, the Hermetic *Anthropos* (cosmic human), and the Pauline Body of Christ comprising many members. Each tradition envisions a primal unity that differentiates into multiplicity while retaining an underlying coherence — and each points to a return from fragmentation to wholeness.
