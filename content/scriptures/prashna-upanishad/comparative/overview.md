## Overview

The Prashna Upanishad's central concept of Prana as the universal life-force finds parallels across world traditions: the Chinese *qi*, the Greek *pneuma*, the Hebrew *ruach*, and the Stoic *pneuma* all point to a vital breath that sustains both individual life and cosmic order. The Upanishad's systematic inquiry into the nature and distribution of this force represents one of humanity's earliest attempts to understand the relationship between breath, life, and consciousness.

## Comparative Themes

The question-and-answer format invites comparison with catechetical traditions worldwide — from the Buddhist *Milindapanha* (Questions of King Milinda) to the Socratic dialogues and rabbinic responsa literature. The meditation on AUM, with its three syllables corresponding to states of consciousness, parallels trinitarian meditations in Christianity and the Sufi practice of *dhikr* (remembrance) using sacred syllables. The person with sixteen parts dissolving into the Absolute resonates with Buddhist teachings on the dissolution of the aggregates (*skandhas*) at liberation and with Neoplatonic accounts of the soul's return to the One.
