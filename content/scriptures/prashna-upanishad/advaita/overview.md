## Overview

Shankara's commentary on the Prashna Upanishad treats Pippalada's six answers as a progressive teaching leading from empirical knowledge of creation and Prana to the ultimate realization of Brahman. The supremacy of Prana (Chapters 2-3) is a stepping-stone: just as Prana is revealed as the ground of all bodily functions, so Brahman is revealed as the ground of Prana itself and of all existence.

## Theological Emphases

The meditation on AUM (Chapter 5) receives careful treatment from Shankara, who reads the three syllables as corresponding to the three states of consciousness — waking (A), dreaming (U), and deep sleep (M) — with the undifferentiated whole syllable pointing to *turiya*, the fourth state of pure awareness that is Brahman. The person with sixteen parts (Chapter 6) is ultimately revealed to be Brahman when the parts are resolved into their source. Shankara reads the simile of rivers merging into the ocean as demonstrating that individual identity is dissolved in liberation — names and forms disappear, and only Brahman remains.
