Shankara reads Chapter 4 as an important supplement to the Brihadaranyaka and Chandogya teachings on the states of consciousness. The absorption of senses into mind during dream and mind into Prana during deep sleep illustrates the progressive resolution of multiplicity into unity — the reverse of the process by which Brahman apparently differentiates into the world. Deep sleep demonstrates that the self exists as pure awareness even when all mental and sensory activity ceases.

The happiness experienced in deep sleep is a key Advaita argument: since the self is blissful even in the absence of all objects and all mental activity, bliss must be intrinsic to the self's nature, not dependent on external conditions. This counters the objection that Brahman's bliss is merely the pleasure of object-contact. Waking consciousness obscures the self's natural bliss through the superimpositions of *avidya*; deep sleep temporarily removes these superimpositions, allowing the self's innate bliss to be felt — though without the clarity of self-knowledge.
