Shankara reads Chapter 5 as teaching a graduated meditation (*upasana*) on AUM, the *pranava* (sacred syllable). The three components A, U, and M correspond to the three states of consciousness — waking, dreaming, and deep sleep — and to the three aspects of Brahman as manifested in the empirical world. Meditation on progressively more complete versions of AUM yields progressively higher results, but all results remain within *samsara* until the meditator realizes the full import of AUM.

The complete AUM, meditated upon as a whole, points beyond the three states to the fourth (*turiya*) — pure consciousness that is Brahman itself. Shankara connects this teaching to the Mandukya Upanishad's more elaborate analysis of AUM and its four components (including silence). The highest meditation is not on AUM as a sound but on the reality that AUM symbolizes — the attributeless Brahman that is the meditator's own self.
