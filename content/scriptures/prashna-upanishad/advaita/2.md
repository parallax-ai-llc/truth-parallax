Shankara reads the parable of the faculties' contest as an illustration of the relationship between Brahman and the empirical world. Just as all faculties depend on Prana and collapse without it, so all phenomena depend on Brahman and have no independent existence. Prana is not itself Brahman but serves as a symbol pointing to the one reality that sustains all appearances.

The hymn of praise to Prana (2.5-13) is treated by Shankara as a meditation (*upasana*) on Prana as a symbol of Brahman. The meditator who identifies Prana with the cosmic sustainer purifies the mind and prepares for the higher knowledge that Brahman is the self. The key Advaita insight is that the supremacy attributed to Prana points beyond Prana itself to the witness-consciousness that is aware of Prana — the self that knows breath but is not itself breath.
