Shankara treats Chapter 6 as the culmination of the Prashna's teaching. The person with sixteen parts is Brahman in its manifested aspect (*saguna Brahman*), and the sixteen parts are the cosmic principles that emerge from Brahman through *maya*. The rivers-and-ocean simile (6.5) is one of Shankara's most important proof-texts for the dissolution of individual identity at liberation: just as rivers lose their names and forms when they merge into the ocean, so individual selves lose their apparent individuality when they realize their identity with Brahman.

The key verse — "He who knows that imperishable being wherein the self consisting of consciousness and all these parts are founded, he becomes all-knowing and enters into all" (6.6) — describes the *jnani*'s realization. Knowledge of the partless Brahman is not the addition of new information but the removal of ignorance that projected the appearance of parts. The sixteen parts were never real as independent entities; they were always Brahman, appearing as parts due to *avidya*.
