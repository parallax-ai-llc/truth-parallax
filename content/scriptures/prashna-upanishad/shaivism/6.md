The person with sixteen parts represents, in Shaiva interpretation, the fullness of Shiva's manifestation through the system of *kalas* (phases of creative expression). The sixteen parts correspond to the sixteen kalas of Sadashiva — the phases through which the undivided consciousness progressively manifests as the differentiated world. The teaching that these parts emerge from the supreme Purusha and return to it describes the eternal cycle of emanation (*sristi*) and reabsorption (*samhara*) that is Shiva's cosmic play.

The river-ocean simile (6.5) expresses the Shaiva insight that liberation is the recognition that individual streams of consciousness have always been the ocean — separation was apparent, not real. The teaching that the liberated person becomes "free from parts" does not mean the destruction of individuality but the recognition that the parts were never truly separate from the whole. This is *pratyabhijna* — the recognition that the apparently fragmented field of experience is and has always been Shiva's undivided consciousness.
