## Overview

Shaiva interpreters read the Prashna Upanishad's teaching on Prana as pointing to Shiva as the universal life-force that animates all beings. The supremacy of Prana over the senses (Chapter 2) mirrors the Shaiva teaching that consciousness (*chit*) is the ground of all experience. The cosmic Prana is not merely biological breath but the pulsation (*spanda*) of Shiva's creative consciousness, the fundamental vibration from which all manifestation arises.

## Theological Emphases

The person with sixteen parts (*shodasha-kala purusha*) taught in Chapter 6 resonates with the Shaiva tradition of Sadashiva's sixteen kalas (phases of creative manifestation). The teaching that these parts merge into the supreme Purusha as rivers merge into the ocean parallels the Shaiva understanding of individual consciousness dissolving into Paramashiva. The meditation on AUM (Chapter 5) is understood through the Shaiva lens as a practice of recognizing the three states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, sleeping) as expressions of Shiva, with the silence after AUM representing the *turiya* state — pure Shiva-consciousness.
