Pippalada's teaching on sleep and dreams provides material central to Shaiva consciousness theory. The three states — waking (engagement with external objects through the senses), dreaming (the mind recreating experiences from its own substance), and deep sleep (all faculties absorbed into Prana) — correspond to the first three states analyzed in the Shaiva tradition. The fourth state (*turiya*), implied but not named here, is the state of pure Shiva-consciousness that pervades and transcends the other three.

The teaching that in deep sleep the self experiences happiness (4.6) is significant for Shaiva philosophy: even when the mind and senses are inactive, consciousness itself is not absent but rests in an undifferentiated state of bliss. This demonstrates that bliss (*ananda*) is intrinsic to consciousness, not dependent on objects. The Shaiva practitioner aims to bring this recognition into waking life — to experience the bliss of deep sleep with the clarity of waking awareness. This is *turiya*: the fourth state that is always present.
