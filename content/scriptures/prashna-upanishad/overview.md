## Overview

The Prashna Upanishad belongs to the Atharva Veda and takes its name from *prashna* ("question"). It records six questions posed by six seekers to the sage Pippalada, each question probing deeper into the nature of reality. The structure is dialogical and pedagogical, moving from cosmological inquiry about the origin of beings to the ultimate question about the self with sixteen parts. The Upanishad offers a remarkably systematic treatment of creation, prana, the mind, sleep, and meditation on AUM.

## Structure

Each of the six chapters presents one question and its answer:

1. **Chapter 1**: Kabandhi asks about the origin of creatures — the teaching on Prajapati's creation through Rayi (matter) and Prana (life)
2. **Chapter 2**: Bhargava asks about the supremacy of Prana among the faculties
3. **Chapter 3**: Kausalya asks about the origin and distribution of Prana in the body
4. **Chapter 4**: Gargya asks about what sleeps and wakes in a person, and who experiences dreams
5. **Chapter 5**: Satyakama asks about the fruits of meditating on AUM
6. **Chapter 6**: Sukesha asks about the person with sixteen parts (*shodasha-kala purusha*)

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The supreme Prana is identified with Vishnu as the life-sustaining Lord
- **Shaivism**: The person with sixteen parts resonates with Shaiva metaphysics of Shiva's manifestation
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The sixteen parts merge into Brahman as rivers merge into the ocean
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: The hierarchy of Prana over other faculties mirrors Vishnu's supremacy
- **Academic**: Important for understanding Atharva Veda philosophy and proto-scientific inquiry
- **Comparative**: The question-answer format parallels catechetical traditions in many religions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Six Questions | A progressive inquiry from cosmology to the nature of the self |
| Prana | The vital breath as the supreme principle sustaining life |
| AUM | The three-syllable meditation and its graded fruits |
| Sixteen Parts | The person composed of sixteen aspects that merge into the Absolute |
| Sleep and Dreams | The states of consciousness as windows into the nature of mind |
