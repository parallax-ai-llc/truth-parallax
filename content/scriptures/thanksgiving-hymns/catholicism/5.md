## The Community as God's Garden

Chapter 5's garden metaphor resonates with Catholic ecclesiology's rich tradition of organic imagery for the Church. Paul's description of the Church as the Body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12), Jesus's image of the vine and branches (John 15), and the patristic tradition of the Church as the "garden enclosed" (Song of Songs 4:12) all develop themes present in the Hodayot's vision of the community as a divinely planted garden.

Catholic interpreters note the sacramental dimension of the garden imagery: the community is nourished by "streams of living water" that flow from a hidden source, anticipating the Catholic understanding of grace flowing through the sacraments from the inexhaustible source of Christ's redemptive work. The Hodayot's insistence that the garden is God's planting — not a human achievement — resonates with the Catholic teaching that the Church is constituted by divine initiative, sustained by divine grace, and ordered toward divine purposes.

The eschatological dimension of the garden imagery, with its vision of hidden trees bearing fruit for the end times, corresponds to the Catholic understanding of the Church as a pilgrim community — already holy in its sacramental life yet not yet fully manifested in glory. The Hodayot's garden is both present reality and future promise, a "mystery" in the Pauline sense (Ephesians 5:32) that points beyond itself to the eschatological fullness of God's kingdom.
