## Knowledge and Revelation: The Mysteries of God

Chapter 2's theology of revealed knowledge resonates with the Catholic understanding of divine revelation as articulated in *Dei Verbum*. The Hodayot's conviction that salvific knowledge comes from God rather than human inquiry parallels the Catholic teaching that revelation is God's free self-communication, transmitted through Scripture and Sacred Tradition and authentically interpreted by the Magisterium.

Catholic interpreters note the communal dimension of revelation in the Hodayot. Knowledge is not given to isolated individuals but to a community gathered around an authoritative teacher — a pattern that anticipates the Catholic understanding of the Church as the custodian and interpreter of revelation. The Teacher of Righteousness functions as a type of the teaching office that Christ would establish, mediating divine truth to the community of the faithful.

The hymns' language of divine mysteries (*razim*) also connects to the Catholic theology of the sacraments, which are themselves called *mysteria* in the Greek theological tradition. Both the Hodayot and Catholic sacramental theology share the conviction that God communicates hidden realities through visible signs and appointed channels — a theology of mediated grace that distinguishes Catholic from purely rationalist approaches to divine knowledge.
