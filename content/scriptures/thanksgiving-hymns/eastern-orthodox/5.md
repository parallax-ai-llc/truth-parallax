## The Community as God's Garden

Chapter 5's garden metaphor resonates with the Orthodox theology of paradise and the Church as the restoration of Eden. The Orthodox liturgical tradition, particularly in the services of Great Lent and Pascha, repeatedly invokes the imagery of paradise lost and paradise restored through Christ's resurrection. The Hodayot's vision of a divinely planted garden, nourished by living water and bearing fruit for the age to come, anticipates this liturgical theology.

Orthodox ecclesiology understands the Church as the "garden enclosed" (Song of Songs 4:12) — a sacred space set apart from the fallen world where the conditions of paradise are progressively restored through the sacramental life. The Hodayot's garden, with its hidden trees and concealed springs, shares this sense of a sacred space that is simultaneously present in the world and hidden from worldly perception.

The eschatological dimension of the garden imagery corresponds to the Orthodox understanding of the Church as an eschatological community — already participating in the life of the age to come through the Eucharist and the sacraments, while awaiting the full manifestation of God's kingdom. The Hodayot's vision of the garden bearing fruit for the end times captures precisely this "already and not yet" dimension that Orthodox theology expresses through the liturgical anticipation of the eschaton in every celebration of the Divine Liturgy.
