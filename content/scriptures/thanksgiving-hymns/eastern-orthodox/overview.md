## Overview

Eastern Orthodox engagement with the Thanksgiving Hymns draws upon the Orthodox tradition's deep appreciation for hymnographic prayer and its theology of theosis (divinization). The Hodayot's themes of human transformation through divine grace, participation in heavenly worship, and the experience of God's light resonate with central concerns of Orthodox spirituality as expressed in the writings of the Church Fathers and the liturgical tradition.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

The Hodayot's language of being brought from darkness into light, from death into life, and from dust into the assembly of the holy ones finds powerful resonance in the Orthodox theology of theosis. The hymns describe a transformation of the human person through encounter with God that, while not using the later theological vocabulary of divinization, expresses a remarkably similar spiritual dynamic. The community's conviction that its members had already begun to participate in the angelic liturgy anticipates the Orthodox understanding of the Divine Liturgy as a participation in the worship of heaven.

Orthodox scholars also note the Hodayot's affinity with the tradition of the Jesus Prayer and hesychast spirituality. The hymns' combination of profound awareness of human sinfulness with confident trust in God's mercy mirrors the spiritual dynamic of the prayer "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." Both traditions share the conviction that true self-knowledge — including knowledge of one's unworthiness — is itself a gift of grace and a precondition for authentic encounter with God.
