## Knowledge and Revelation: The Mysteries of God

Chapter 2's celebration of revealed knowledge finds direct parallels in Paul's language of divine mysteries (*mysteria*). Paul writes of "God's wisdom, secret and hidden, which God decreed before the ages for our glory" (1 Corinthians 2:7), and the Hodayot similarly celebrate access to mysteries hidden from the majority but revealed to the elect through God's spirit.

Protestant theology emphasizes the role of the Holy Spirit in illuminating Scripture and granting spiritual understanding — a conviction that resonates with the Hodayot's language of divine spirit opening the heart to hidden truths. The Reformers' insistence on the *testimonium internum Spiritus Sancti* (the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit) as the ground of Scriptural authority shares structural similarities with the Hodayot's theology of revealed knowledge, even while differing in crucial content.

The soteriological significance of knowledge in the Hodayot — to know is to be saved, to be ignorant is to be condemned — provides context for the New Testament's own association of knowledge with salvation. John's Gospel, with its repeated emphasis on knowing the Father and the Son (John 17:3), shares the conviction that salvific knowledge is a divine gift rather than a human achievement, even as it identifies the content of that knowledge differently.
