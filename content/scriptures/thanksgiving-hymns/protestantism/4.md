## Creation and Cosmic Praise

Chapter 4's celebration of God as Creator connects the Hodayot to the broader biblical theology of creation that Protestant theology affirms. The hymns' vision of the natural world — luminaries, seasons, and all living things — rendering praise to God resonates with Psalm 19:1 ("The heavens declare the glory of God") and Romans 1:20, where Paul argues that God's nature is "clearly perceived in the things that have been made."

Protestant interpreters note that the Hodayot's creation theology, unlike some Hellenistic systems, affirms the goodness of the material world. Creation is not a prison from which the soul must escape but a theater of God's glory — a perspective Calvin developed extensively in his *Institutes*. The hymns' integration of cosmic praise with the community's liturgical life reflects the conviction that human worship participates in and gives voice to the mute praise of all creation.

The chapter also provides context for the New Testament's cosmic Christology. Colossians 1:15-20, which describes Christ as the one "in whom all things hold together," extends the Hodayot's creation theology by identifying the Logos through whom all things were made. The hymn tradition represented in the Hodayot thus provides a Jewish matrix for the early Church's boldest Christological claims.
