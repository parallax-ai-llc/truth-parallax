## Overview

Academic study of the Thanksgiving Hymns has focused on questions of authorship, literary form, theological content, and the hymns' relationship to biblical psalmody and other Second Temple literature. The publication of the critical edition by Hartmut Stegemann and Eileen Schuller (DJD XL, 2009) provided a definitive reconstruction of the scroll, enabling more precise analysis of the collection's structure and development.

## Key Scholarly Debates

The most persistent scholarly debate concerns authorship. Geza Vermes and others distinguished between "Teacher Hymns" (characterized by first-person leadership language and references to persecution) and "Community Hymns" (more general expressions of piety). This distinction, while influential, has been challenged by scholars such as Carol Newsom, who argues that the category of "authorship" may be less relevant than the hymns' function in constructing communal identity. Michael Douglas has proposed that the entire collection was composed by a single author.

Form-critical analysis has classified the Hodayot within the broader category of "thanksgiving psalms" (*todah*), connecting them to the biblical psalm tradition while noting significant innovations. The Hodayot's distinctive features — extensive use of sectarian theological vocabulary, elaborate development of creation theology, and radical anthropological statements — distinguish them from biblical psalmody and provide evidence of a distinctive theological school within Second Temple Judaism.
