## Creation and Cosmic Praise: Scholarly Perspectives

The creation hymns of Chapter 4 have been studied for their cosmological content, their connection to the community's distinctive solar calendar, and their relationship to other creation texts in the Scrolls and broader Jewish literature. The hymns' detailed descriptions of luminaries, seasons, and cosmic order provide evidence for the community's cosmological worldview and its practical implications for liturgical life.

Jonathan Ben-Dov and others have analyzed the connection between the Hodayot's creation language and the calendrical texts from Qumran (4Q317-337). The community's solar calendar, based on a 364-day year with precisely ordered divisions, was understood not as a human calculation but as a reflection of divine creative order. The creation hymns thus functioned as theological legitimation for the community's calendrical practice, grounding their distinctive liturgical schedule in the structure of creation itself.

The relationship between the Hodayot's creation theology and other Jewish creation traditions — including the Enochic astronomical materials (1 Enoch 72-82) and the rabbinic creation theology of the *Yotzer* prayer — has been explored by scholars seeking to map the diversity of Jewish cosmological thought in the Second Temple period. The Hodayot represent a distinctive synthesis of psalmaic praise, cosmological speculation, and sectarian polemic that illuminates the creative theological energy of the period.
