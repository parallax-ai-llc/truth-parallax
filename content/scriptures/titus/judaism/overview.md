## Overview

Jewish scholarship on Titus examines the letter's polemical references to "those of the circumcision" (1:10) and Jewish-Christian tensions in the early church, while also noting the significant ethical parallels between the letter's moral instruction and rabbinic teaching.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Polemic Against Jewish Christians
The references to "the circumcision party" (1:10) and "Jewish myths" (1:14) reflect tensions between Pauline and Torah-observant communities. Jewish scholars analyze this polemic as evidence of the painful process of separation between Judaism and Christianity.

### Good Works and Righteousness
The letter's emphasis on "good works" (2:7, 14; 3:1, 8, 14) shares significant common ground with the Jewish concept of *mitzvot* (commandments) and *gemilut chasadim* (acts of lovingkindness). Jewish readers note that the Pauline tradition ultimately could not dispense with the ethical substance of Torah.

### Elder Governance
The appointment of elders in each city (1:5) has parallels in the governance of Jewish synagogues, where elders (*zekenim*) played a central role. This structural continuity reveals the Jewish organizational roots of early Christian communities.
