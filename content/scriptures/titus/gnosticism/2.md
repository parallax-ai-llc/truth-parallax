## Sound Doctrine and Godly Living

The exhortation to "teach what accords with sound doctrine" (2:1) is understood by Gnostic interpreters as the mandate to transmit authentic gnosis rather than the Demiurge's corrupted theology. "Sound doctrine" is not institutional orthodoxy but the teaching that heals the soul's amnesia, reminding the divine spark of its origin in the Pleroma. The detailed instructions for older men, older women, younger women, younger men, and servants (2:2-10) are read as guidance for pneumatics at different stages of awakening. Each group faces particular archontic temptations appropriate to their social position, and the teaching must address these specific snares.

The transformative declaration that "the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people" (2:11) describes the irruption of the Pleroma's light into the Demiurge's dark cosmos. This grace is not a juridical pardon but a cosmic event: the divine fullness reached across the abyss to touch the imprisoned sparks. It "trains us to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions" (2:12) by awakening the pneumatic awareness that sees through the archons' enticements. "Worldly passions" are the desires generated by the material body that keep the soul anchored to the Demiurge's realm. The grace of gnosis does not suppress these passions through external law but dissolves them from within by revealing their ultimate emptiness.

The "blessed hope" of "the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior Jesus Christ" (2:13) anticipates the final revelation that will dissolve the archontic cosmos entirely. Christ "gave himself for us to redeem us from all lawlessness" (2:14) not by satisfying the Demiurge's legal demands but by extracting the divine sparks from the entire system of law and lawlessness that the inferior creator established. To be "purified" and made "a people for his own possession who are zealous for good works" (2:14) is to be reconstituted as citizens of the Pleroma, acting from gnosis rather than from compulsion, freely expressing the divine nature rather than anxiously obeying an external code.
