## Overview

Gnostic engagement with Titus, like the other Pastoral Epistles, involves a complex interplay of polemic and counter-reading. The letter's warnings against "Jewish myths" (1:14) and those who "profess to know God but deny him by their works" (1:16) were read by orthodox Christians as anti-Gnostic polemic, while Gnostic interpreters offered alternative readings.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### "To the Pure All Things Are Pure"
The statement that "to the pure, all things are pure" (1:15) was seized upon by libertine Gnostic groups who argued that the spiritual person, having transcended material categories, is not bound by conventional moral rules. The letter's author likely intended the opposite: purity of conscience, not antinomian license.

### Rejection of Myths and Genealogies
As in 1 Timothy, the polemic against "myths" (1:14) and the emphasis on "sound doctrine" (2:1) are widely read as targeting Gnostic cosmological speculation. Gnostic teachers responded that the institutional church had reduced Paul's liberating message to mere moralism.

### The Material World and Good Works
The letter's strong emphasis on good works performed in the material world (2:7, 14; 3:1, 8, 14) stands in tension with Gnostic devaluation of the physical realm. For orthodox interpreters, this emphasis confirms that embodied ethical action — not secret knowledge — is the fruit of genuine faith.
