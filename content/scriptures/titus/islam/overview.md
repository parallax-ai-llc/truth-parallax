## Overview

Islamic engagement with Titus centers on the letter's ethical teaching, particularly its emphasis on good works and moral reform, which resonates with the Qur'anic insistence that faith must be accompanied by righteous deeds (*'amal salih*). The letter's theological claims are evaluated through the lens of Islamic monotheism.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Good Works and Righteous Deeds
The repeated emphasis on "good works" in Titus (2:7, 14; 3:1, 8, 14) finds a strong parallel in the Qur'anic pairing of faith and good deeds. Islam insists that genuine faith (*iman*) necessarily produces righteous action, a conviction this letter seems to share despite the Pauline tradition's complex relationship with "works."

### Divine Grace and Mercy
The grace passage (2:11-14; 3:4-7) echoes the Qur'anic emphasis on divine mercy (*rahmah*). While the theological frameworks differ — Paul speaks of grace through Christ, Islam speaks of Allah's mercy to all creation — both traditions affirm that human salvation originates in divine initiative.

### Community Organization
The appointment of elders to govern local communities (1:5) parallels the Islamic institution of communal leadership through appointed *imams* and *qadis* (judges), reflecting shared Semitic traditions of community governance.
