## The Queen of Sheba

Jewish interpretation of 1 Kings 10 engages extensively with the Queen of Sheba's visit through both halakhic and aggadic (narrative) traditions. The Aggadah elaborates the encounter far beyond the biblical text, making it one of the most colorful stories in rabbinic literature.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Queen's Visit (10:1-13)

The Talmud (Bava Batra 15b) and Midrash provide elaborate accounts of the Queen's visit. The "hard questions" (*chidot*) she posed are detailed in various midrashic sources:

| Riddle | Solomon's Answer |
|--------|-----------------|
| What is the land that was once cursed but is now blessed? | The land of Canaan, cursed under Noah, blessed under Israel |
| What appears from the earth like dust and nourishes like fire? | Wheat |
| Who are the beings that emerge from the earth, give voice, but are neither man nor beast? | Musical instruments |

**Targum Sheni** (on Esther) provides the most extensive version of the encounter, including stories of Solomon's throne and its automated mechanical features.

### Solomon's Wealth (10:14-25)

**Rashi** notes that the 666 talents of gold came from trade and tribute — not from taxation of Israel alone. **Radak** connects Solomon's wealth to the covenantal blessing: "I give you also what you have not asked, both riches and honor" (3:13).

However, the rabbis also see in Solomon's accumulation of gold a violation of the Torah's limits on kingship (Deuteronomy 17:17): "He shall not acquire for himself excessive silver and gold."

### Horses from Egypt (10:26-29)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 21b) explicitly connects Solomon's acquisition of horses from Egypt with the prohibition in Deuteronomy 17:16. **Rambam** cites this as one of three specific violations by Solomon (horses, wives, gold) that demonstrate even the wisest can fail when they rationalize disobedience.

## Theological Significance

1. **Aggadic elaboration**: The Midrash's expansions reveal the depths of Solomon's legendary wisdom
2. **Covenantal blessing**: Solomon's wealth fulfills God's promise of what he "did not ask"
3. **The danger of rationalization**: Even the wise can convince themselves that Torah limits do not apply
4. **The law of the king**: Kingship has specific halakhic constraints that even Solomon violated
