## Solomon's Wisdom

Jewish interpretation of 1 Kings 3 is among the richest in the tradition, as Solomon's wisdom (*chokhmah*) holds a central place in Jewish thought — connecting to Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the Song of Songs, all traditionally attributed to him.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Dream at Gibeon (3:5-15)

**Rashi** explains that Solomon sacrificed at Gibeon because the Tabernacle of Moses stood there (2 Chronicles 1:3), making it a legitimate place of worship before the Temple was built. This halakhic detail matters: Solomon did not sin by worshipping at a high place, because the *bamah* (high place) at Gibeon was sanctioned.

**Radak** observes that God's offer — "Ask what I shall give you" — was itself a test of Solomon's character. His choice of wisdom over wealth or long life demonstrated the very quality he requested.

The Talmud (Berakhot 57b) discusses dreams as a form of prophecy, noting that Solomon's encounter at Gibeon was "one-sixtieth of prophecy." The rabbis distinguish between prophetic dreams (clear divine communication) and ordinary dreams.

### The Judgment of the Two Mothers (3:16-28)

This is one of the most celebrated passages in rabbinic literature:

**Midrash Rabbah** elaborates the story extensively, imagining the inner thoughts of both women and crediting Solomon with divine inspiration in devising the test. The story demonstrates Solomon's possession of *ruach ha-kodesh* (the holy spirit of wisdom).

**Ralbag** draws a practical lesson (*to'elet*): a wise judge must understand human nature — the true mother's instinct to protect her child, even at the cost of losing him, reveals the truth.

**Malbim** analyzes the legal dimensions: the case was unsolvable by ordinary evidence (no witnesses), requiring a judge with extraordinary discernment.

### Solomon's Wisdom and Jewish Law

The rabbis saw Solomon's wisdom as paradigmatic for Jewish jurisprudence. The principle that a judge must seek truth beneath appearances, using insight rather than relying solely on testimony, enters halakhic discussion through this narrative.

## Theological Significance

1. **Wisdom as divine gift**: True *chokhmah* comes from God, not from human effort alone
2. **The priority of wisdom over wealth**: Solomon's choice establishes the hierarchy of values
3. **Judicial discernment**: The wise judge must see beneath surface appearances
4. **Torah and wisdom**: Solomon's wisdom is rooted in and serves Torah
