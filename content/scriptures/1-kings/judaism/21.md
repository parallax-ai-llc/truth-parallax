## Naboth's Vineyard

Jewish interpretation reads 1 Kings 21 as a foundational text for the Torah's teaching on property, justice, and the king's accountability to divine law. The story of Naboth's vineyard is one of the most discussed narratives in rabbinic ethics.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Naboth's Refusal (21:1-4)

**Rashi** explains that Naboth's refusal is grounded in Torah law: the ancestral *nachalah* (inheritance) was not a personal possession but a divine trust, governed by the laws of Leviticus 25 and Numbers 36. To sell ancestral land permanently was to violate God's distribution of the land among the tribes.

**Radak** notes that Ahab, as king, might have claimed the right of *eminent domain* (*din melekh*) — the king's legal authority to appropriate property for state purposes (1 Samuel 8:14). Naboth's refusal asserts that divine law (*din Torah*) supersedes royal prerogative.

### Jezebel's Plot (21:5-16)

The rabbis identify multiple violations of Torah in Jezebel's scheme:
- False witness (Exodus 20:16)
- Perversion of justice (Deuteronomy 16:19-20)
- Murder of the innocent (Exodus 20:13)
- Theft of inheritance (Exodus 20:15)

**Rambam** (Maimonides) in *Hilkhot Melachim* 3:8 establishes that a king who seizes property without legal justification is a robber — royal power does not override Torah law.

### Elijah's Confrontation (21:17-24)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 113a) records that God sent Elijah specifically because the prophetic voice must confront injustice. The rabbis derive from this the obligation of *tochacha* (rebuke): silence in the face of injustice makes one complicit.

### Ahab's Repentance (21:25-29)

**Rashi** notes that Ahab's repentance, though motivated by fear of punishment, was genuine enough to move God's mercy. The rabbis derive from this the principle that the gates of repentance (*teshuvah*) are always open — no sinner is beyond redemption if they genuinely turn.

## Theological Significance

1. **Torah above kingship**: Divine law supersedes royal authority in matters of property and justice
2. **Prophetic rebuke as obligation**: Silence in the face of injustice is complicity
3. **The sanctity of inheritance**: Ancestral land is a divine trust, not a commodity
4. **The power of teshuvah**: Repentance, even when imperfect, moves God's mercy
