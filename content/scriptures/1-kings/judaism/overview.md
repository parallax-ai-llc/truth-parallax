## Overview

In Judaism, 1 Kings (*Melachim Aleph*) belongs to the *Nevi'im Rishonim* (Former Prophets) section of the Tanakh. The book is understood not primarily as history but as prophetic instruction — the Talmud identifies its author as the prophet Jeremiah (Bava Batra 15a). Jewish interpretation spans the Talmud, Midrash, medieval commentators (Rishonim), and modern scholarship, with particular attention to Solomon's wisdom, the Temple, and the halakhic dimensions of worship.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
- **Rashi**: Provides concise commentary integrating midrashic tradition with the plain sense
- **Radak** (Rabbi David Kimchi): Grammatical-historical analysis with theological reflection
- **Ralbag** (Gersonides): Philosophical-ethical lessons drawn from the narrative

### Derash (Homiletical)
- **Midrash** on Solomon's wisdom and judgment (the two mothers, 1 Kings 3)
- **Talmudic discussions** of Temple measurements, furnishings, and rituals (Tractate Middot)
- The Aggadah elaborates extensively on Solomon's encounters with the Queen of Sheba and with demons (Gittin 68b)

### Sod (Mystical)
- The **Zohar** reads the Temple as a microcosm of the divine *sefirot*
- Solomon's wisdom is associated with the *sefirah* of *Tiferet* (beauty/harmony)

## Theological Emphases

- **Kingship and Torah**: A righteous king must be governed by Torah (Deuteronomy 17:14-20)
- **The Temple as axis mundi**: The central place of divine-human encounter
- **Prophetic rebuke**: The prophets hold kings accountable to the covenant
- **Exile as consequence**: The division of the kingdom foreshadows the eventual exile
