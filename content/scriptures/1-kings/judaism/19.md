## Elijah at Horeb

Jewish interpretation of 1 Kings 19 reads Elijah's journey to Horeb (Sinai) as a return to the source of revelation — the prophet going back to the mountain where Torah was given to understand his mission in light of the original covenant.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Elijah's Flight and Despair (19:1-4)

The Midrash records a tradition that God rebuked Elijah for his despair: "Are you greater than Moses, who also wanted to die?" (Numbers 11:15). The rabbinic tradition, while sympathizing with Elijah's exhaustion, sees in his prayer to die an excess of zeal that needs tempering.

**Radak** reads Elijah's flight as evidence of his humanity: even the greatest prophet is subject to fear and discouragement. The rabbis draw the lesson that spiritual heroism on one day does not guarantee it on the next.

### The Angelic Provision (19:5-8)

**Rashi** connects the forty-day journey to Moses' forty days on Sinai: Elijah is retracing the steps of the original lawgiver. The bread and water sustain a journey that is both physical and spiritual — a pilgrimage to the source of Torah.

### The Theophany (19:9-13)

The *kol demamah dakkah* (still small voice) is one of the most discussed phrases in Jewish theology:

| Commentator | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Rashi** | A thin, quiet sound — God's voice after the cosmic displays |
| **Radak** | The sound of fine silence — God communicates in stillness |
| **Malbim** | The voice of conscience — God speaks through inner awareness |
| **Rav Kook** | The gentle divine voice that pervades all creation beneath the noise |

The Midrash notes that God appeared to Moses in fire (the burning bush, Sinai) but to Elijah in silence. The modes of revelation differ according to the prophet and the moment.

### "I have left 7,000 in Israel" (19:18)

**Rashi** interprets the 7,000 as a literal number of faithful individuals. **Radak** reads it as a round number indicating a significant remnant. The rabbinic principle of the *she'erit* (remnant) — that God always preserves a faithful core in Israel — becomes foundational for Jewish theology of exile and survival.

## Theological Significance

1. **Return to Sinai**: Elijah's pilgrimage represents the prophetic return to the source of Torah
2. **The still small voice**: God's deepest communication occurs in silence and subtlety
3. **The remnant**: God always preserves a faithful community in Israel
4. **Prophetic humanity**: Even the greatest prophets experience fear and despair
