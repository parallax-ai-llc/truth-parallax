## The Contest on Mount Carmel

Jewish interpretation of 1 Kings 18 reads the Mount Carmel confrontation as the supreme demonstration of God's exclusive sovereignty and the nullity of idolatry. This chapter is the haftarah reading for Parashat Ki Tisa — the portion that includes the golden calf narrative (Exodus 32).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Obadiah's Hidden Prophets (18:1-16)

**Rashi** identifies Obadiah as a righteous proselyte who feared God even while serving the wicked Ahab. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 39b) praises Obadiah for hiding 100 prophets — fifty in each cave — at enormous personal risk.

### "How long will you limp between two opinions?" (18:21)

**Rashi** interprets *se'ifim* (opinions/branches) as referring to divided loyalties: the people tried to worship both Yahweh and Baal simultaneously. The rabbinic principle is clear: God demands exclusive devotion. The Shema — "Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One" — admits no divided loyalty.

### The Contest and the Fire (18:22-39)

**Rashi** notes a halakhic problem: Elijah offered sacrifice on a *bamah* (high place) outside Jerusalem, which was prohibited after the Temple was built. The Talmud (Yevamot 90b) resolves this by invoking the principle of *hora'at sha'ah* — a temporary prophetic dispensation in extraordinary circumstances. A prophet may suspend a Torah law temporarily if the situation demands it.

**Radak** emphasizes the pedagogical purpose of the contest: the people needed a dramatic demonstration because their faith had deteriorated to the point where reasoned argument was insufficient.

### Elijah's Prayer (18:36-37)

The Talmud (Berakhot 6b) analyzes Elijah's brief prayer as a model of effective intercession: concise, direct, and focused on God's honor rather than the prophet's reputation.

### The Execution of the Prophets of Baal (18:40)

**Rambam** (Maimonides) cites this episode in *Hilkhot Avodat Kokhavim* (Laws of Idolatry) as justification for the law that a prophet who leads Israel to idolatry is subject to the death penalty. Elijah acted as both prophet and judge in this extraordinary circumstance.

## Theological Significance

1. **Exclusive monotheism**: God demands undivided loyalty — the foundation of Jewish faith
2. **Hora'at sha'ah**: A prophet may temporarily suspend Torah law in extraordinary circumstances
3. **Effective prayer**: Brevity, directness, and focus on God's honor characterize true intercession
4. **The nullity of idolatry**: Baal's silence demonstrates the emptiness of all false worship
