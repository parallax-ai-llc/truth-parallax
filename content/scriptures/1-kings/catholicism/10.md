## The Queen of Sheba and Solomon's Glory

Catholic interpretation reads the Queen of Sheba's visit as a typological anticipation of the Gentile nations coming to acknowledge the wisdom of God, ultimately fulfilled in the Church's universal mission.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Queen's Journey (10:1-13)

The Church Fathers regarded the Queen of Sheba as a type of the Gentile Church coming to the true Solomon — Christ. **Origen** wrote that as the queen came from the ends of the earth bearing gifts to Solomon, so the Church comes from all nations bearing the gifts of faith to Christ.

In Catholic art and liturgy, the Queen of Sheba frequently appears as one of the great Gentile figures who foreshadow the universal scope of God's plan of salvation.

### "The half was not told me" (10:7)

Catholic spiritual writers, particularly in the mystical tradition, have applied this verse to the soul's encounter with God: no report can adequately prepare one for the direct experience of divine wisdom. **St. Bernard of Clairvaux** used this motif to describe the overwhelming reality of contemplative prayer.

### Solomon's Wealth and Splendor (10:14-25)

Catholic social teaching engages with Solomon's wealth cautiously. While material abundance can be a sign of God's blessing, the Church's tradition of voluntary poverty (from the Desert Fathers through the Franciscans) offers a counterbalance. The *Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church* affirms the universal destination of goods — wealth carries social responsibility.

### Horses from Egypt (10:26-29)

Catholic moral theology notes Solomon's violation of the Deuteronomic law concerning kings (Deuteronomy 17:16). This observation reinforces the principle that even legitimate authority must submit to a higher law — a foundation of Catholic political theology.

## Theological Significance

1. **Gentile prefiguration**: The Queen of Sheba anticipates the nations' conversion to Christ
2. **The insufficiency of report**: Direct encounter with God surpasses all secondhand knowledge
3. **Wealth and responsibility**: Material abundance carries moral obligations
4. **Christ the greater Solomon**: All of Solomon's glory finds its fulfillment in Christ
