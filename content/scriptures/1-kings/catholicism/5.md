## Preparations for the Temple

Catholic interpretation of 1 Kings 5 reads the Temple preparations through the lens of sacramental theology: the gathering of materials and labor for God's house prefigures the Church's ongoing work of building up the Body of Christ.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Solomon's Alliance with Hiram (5:1-12)

The cooperation between Israel and Tyre carries deep typological significance for Catholic theology. The cedar of Lebanon, provided by Gentile hands for God's Temple, prefigures the contributions of all nations to the building up of the Church.

**St. Bede the Venerable** read the alliance as a figure of Christ uniting Jew and Gentile in one temple — the Church — "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets" (Ephesians 2:20).

### The Labor Force (5:13-18)

Catholic social teaching engages with the forced labor described here with moral seriousness. While the narrative presents this as necessary for the Temple's construction, Catholic tradition recognizes the dignity of labor and the obligation of just treatment:

- Pope Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) established the principles of workers' rights and just wages
- The Temple's construction does not justify exploitation, but illustrates the costly sacrifice involved in building sacred space

### Cedar, Stone, and Gold

Catholic liturgical theology sees in the Temple materials a prefiguration of the elements used in church construction and decoration:

| Material | Typological Meaning |
|----------|-------------------|
| Cedar | Incorruptibility — the enduring nature of the Church |
| Hewn stone | The faithful, shaped by grace for incorporation into God's dwelling |
| Gold | The glory of God's presence and the purity of worship |

## Theological Significance

1. **Building the Church**: The Temple preparations model the ongoing construction of Christ's Body
2. **Universal contribution**: Gentile labor for God's house anticipates the Catholic (universal) Church
3. **Dignity of labor**: Sacred work demands just treatment of workers
4. **Sacred materials**: Physical elements bear spiritual meaning in sacramental theology
