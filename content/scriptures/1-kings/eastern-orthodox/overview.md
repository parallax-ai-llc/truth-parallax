## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 1 Kings is rooted in the *consensus patrum* (agreement of the Fathers) and the liturgical life of the Church. The book is read typologically, with Solomon's Temple understood as a revelation of the divine economy, and the prophet Elijah occupying a place of extraordinary honor in Orthodox theology and worship as a model of the contemplative and prophetic life.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Exegesis
The Orthodox Church privileges readings from the Greek and Syriac Fathers:
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Moral and pastoral reflections on Solomon's fall and Elijah's faith
- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Poetic-typological treatment of the Temple and the prophets
- **St. Gregory the Theologian**: The relationship between divine wisdom and human governance
- **Theodoret of Cyrrhus**: Historical and typological commentary on the Books of Kings

### Liturgical Context
1 Kings provides key readings in the Orthodox liturgical calendar:
- The dedication of Solomon's Temple is read at the consecration of churches
- Elijah's ascension (2 Kings 2) and his contest on Carmel are commemorated on the Feast of the Prophet Elijah (July 20)
- The "still small voice" theophany (1 Kings 19) informs hesychast spirituality

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis through wisdom**: Solomon's wisdom as a gift of divine illumination, corrupted by attachment to the passions
- **Temple as cosmic sanctuary**: The Temple reveals the structure of the cosmos oriented toward God
- **Elijah as proto-hesychast**: The prophet's withdrawal to the wilderness and encounter with God in silence
- **Ancestral sin**: The division of the kingdom as a consequence of cumulative apostasy, not merely individual fault
