## Preparations for the Temple

Orthodox interpretation of 1 Kings 5 reads the Temple preparations through the lens of liturgical theology: the gathering of materials for God's house prefigures the Church's ongoing liturgical construction of sacred space.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Alliance with Hiram (5:1-12)

The cooperation between Israelite and Phoenician craftsmen carries cosmological significance in Orthodox thought. **St. Maximus the Confessor** taught that Christ unites all things — Jew and Gentile, sacred and secular, heaven and earth. The Gentile contribution to God's Temple anticipates this cosmic reconciliation.

The cedar of Lebanon, renowned for its beauty and durability, becomes in Orthodox hymnography a symbol of incorruptibility and the beauty of holiness. The Psalms celebrate the "cedars of Lebanon which the Lord has planted" (Psalm 104:16).

### The Labor Force (5:13-18)

Orthodox social ethics engages with the forced labor described here by affirming the dignity of work while noting that compulsion distorts labor's sacred character. In Orthodox theology, work is understood as participation in God's creative activity — a liturgical act (*leitourgia*, "the work of the people") that should be freely offered.

### Stone and Cedar: The Materials of the Temple

Orthodox iconographic and architectural theology reads the Temple materials as laden with spiritual meaning:

| Material | Orthodox Theological Reading |
|----------|----------------------------|
| Cedar | The fragrance of holiness; incorruptible beauty |
| Hewn stone | The faithful shaped by ascetical struggle for incorporation into God's dwelling |
| Gold | The uncreated light of God's glory (*doxa*) |

The Orthodox tradition of building churches with precious materials continues this theology: the physical church building is an icon of the heavenly Temple.

## Theological Significance

1. **Cosmic reconciliation**: Gentile cooperation in building God's house anticipates the unity of all in Christ
2. **Sacred materiality**: Physical materials bear spiritual significance in Orthodox liturgical theology
3. **The dignity of labor**: Work is a liturgical act properly offered in freedom, not compulsion
4. **Church architecture**: The Temple sets the pattern for Orthodox church construction
