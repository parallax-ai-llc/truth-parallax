## The Queen of Sheba and Solomon's Glory

Orthodox interpretation reads the Queen of Sheba's visit as a theophanic encounter with divine wisdom, manifested through Solomon but pointing beyond him to Christ, "in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Queen's Pilgrimage (10:1-13)

Orthodox patristic tradition reads the Queen of Sheba as a type of the soul seeking wisdom, or of the Gentile nations drawn to the light of divine revelation. **St. Ephrem the Syrian** in his hymns portrayed the Queen's journey as a pilgrimage of the heart — from the darkness of ignorance to the light of God's wisdom.

In Orthodox iconography, the Queen of Sheba sometimes appears in the cycle of Old Testament figures who prefigure the Church's reception of divine truth.

### "The half was not told me" (10:7)

Orthodox mystical theology applies this verse to the experience of *theoria* (divine contemplation): no verbal report can substitute for the direct vision of God. **St. Gregory of Nyssa** in his concept of *epektasis* (perpetual progress into the infinite God) taught that every experience of God reveals that there is infinitely more still to discover.

### Solomon's Wealth (10:14-25)

Orthodox ascetical theology reads the catalog of Solomon's wealth with caution. Material abundance is not inherently evil in Orthodox thought — the monasteries of Mount Athos, for instance, include richly adorned churches — but attachment to wealth distorts the soul's orientation toward God.

**St. Basil the Great** preached that surplus wealth belongs to the poor: "The bread in your cupboard belongs to the hungry; the coat unused in your closet belongs to the one who needs it."

## Theological Significance

1. **Pilgrimage of the soul**: The Queen's journey models the soul's search for divine wisdom
2. **Epektasis**: Every encounter with God reveals infinite depths yet to be explored
3. **Wealth and asceticism**: Material abundance must serve God and neighbor, not self-glorification
4. **Christological fulfillment**: Solomon's wisdom is surpassed by Christ, the Wisdom of God
