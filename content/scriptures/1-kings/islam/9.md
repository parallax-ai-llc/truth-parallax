## God's Second Appearance to Solomon

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 9 centers on the conditional nature of divine promises, a theme strongly attested in the Quran.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Conditionality of Divine Favor

The conditional promise — blessing for obedience, punishment for apostasy — aligns with the Quranic principle: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you in favor; but if you deny, indeed, My punishment is severe" (Ibrahim 14:7).

Islamic theology affirms that no individual, nation, or institution can presume on God's favor without ongoing obedience. The destruction of previous peoples — 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lut — demonstrates this principle repeatedly in the Quran.

### The Warning of Destruction

God's warning that the Temple could become "a heap of ruins" (9:8) resonates with the Quranic theme of destroyed civilizations. **Sayyid Qutb** emphasized that God's warnings are sincere: no sacred space or privileged status protects a people who abandon God's commands.

### Sulayman's Kingdom

Islamic tradition affirms the vast scope of Sulayman's kingdom — his building projects, international trade, and naval expeditions — while insisting that all this power was exercised in obedience to God. The biblical hints of excess and self-aggrandizement are not accepted by Islamic commentators.

## Theological Significance

1. **Conditional blessing**: God's favor requires ongoing obedience — no presumption is permitted
2. **Lessons from destroyed peoples**: The Quran repeatedly warns of nations that lost divine favor
3. **Power as trust**: Political authority must be exercised in obedience to God
4. **No permanent privilege**: Sacred spaces and chosen peoples are not immune to divine judgment
