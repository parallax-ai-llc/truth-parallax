## The Succession of Sulayman in Islamic Perspective

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 1 centers on the figure of Solomon (Sulayman), whom Islam recognizes as both a prophet (*nabi*) and a messenger (*rasul*) endowed with extraordinary divine gifts. The succession narrative is evaluated against Quranic principles of prophetic character and divine sovereignty.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Dawud (David) and the Succession

Islamic tradition regards David (Dawud) as a prophet-king who received the Zabur (Psalms). The Quran presents Dawud as one to whom God gave "the kingdom and wisdom and taught him whatever He willed" (Al-Baqarah 2:251).

The political intrigues of the succession — Adonijah's conspiracy, Bathsheba's intervention, Nathan's scheming — are viewed by Islamic scholars as evidence of *tahrif* (textual corruption). The biblical text's portrayal of prophets engaged in political maneuvering does not align with the Islamic understanding of prophetic dignity (*ismah*).

### Sulayman's Appointment

Islamic tradition affirms that Sulayman inherited Dawud's prophetic and royal authority by divine decree: "And Sulayman inherited Dawud" (An-Naml 27:16). This is understood as both a spiritual and temporal inheritance — wisdom, prophetic insight, and kingship.

**Al-Tabari** records traditions about Sulayman's youth and his exceptional wisdom even as a child, connecting this to the Quranic statement that God granted him understanding (*fahm*) from an early age (Al-Anbiya 21:79).

### The Anointing

The concept of sacramental anointing by a priest has no direct parallel in Islamic theology. In Islam, prophetic authority comes directly from God (*wahy*) — no intermediary institution confers legitimacy. Sulayman's authority derives from divine appointment, not from priestly ritual.

## Theological Significance

1. **Prophetic succession**: Authority passes by divine decree, not by political maneuvering
2. **Prophetic dignity**: Islamic theology rejects portrayals of prophets in undignified circumstances
3. **Divine sovereignty**: God alone appoints rulers and prophets
4. **Tahrif**: Narrative elements that compromise prophetic dignity are attributed to textual corruption
