## David's Death and Solomon's Consolidation

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 2 is shaped by the Quranic presentation of Dawud as a righteous prophet whose death is an occasion for reflection on prophetic legacy rather than political intrigue.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Dawud's Legacy

The Quran presents Dawud's primary legacy as spiritual rather than political: he received the Zabur (Psalms), was granted wisdom and decisive speech (Sad 38:20), and was a model of repentance and devotion. The biblical account of David's deathbed political instructions — arranging the deaths of enemies — is regarded by Islamic scholars as inconsistent with prophetic character.

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes that Dawud's legacy to Sulayman was spiritual: wisdom, faith, and the prophetic mission. The political machinations described in the biblical text are attributed to later scribal additions or misunderstandings.

### The Consolidation of Power

The executions of Joab, Shimei, and Adonijah described in 1 Kings 2 raise significant questions for Islamic ethics. While Islam recognizes that rulers must maintain justice and order, the manner in which these individuals are eliminated is viewed with suspicion:

- A prophet-king should rule by divine guidance, not by settling personal scores
- Justice in Islam requires due process (*qada*) — not preemptive assassination
- The portrayal of Sulayman as a ruthless consolidator contradicts the Quranic image

### Sulayman's Kingdom Established

The Quran affirms that God established Sulayman's kingdom with unique power: "My Lord, forgive me and grant me a kingdom such as will not belong to anyone after me" (Sad 38:35). Islamic tradition sees this establishment as divine gift, not human political achievement.

## Theological Significance

1. **Prophetic legacy is spiritual**: The true inheritance of prophets is wisdom and faith
2. **Justice requires due process**: Islam requires legal procedures even in political matters
3. **Divine establishment**: Sulayman's kingdom is a divine gift, not a human political achievement
4. **Textual criticism**: Passages portraying prophets negatively are attributed to *tahrif*
