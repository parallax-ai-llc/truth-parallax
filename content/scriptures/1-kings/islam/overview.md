## Overview

Islam does not regard the Books of Kings as preserved divine revelation, but many of their central figures — Solomon (Sulayman), Elijah (Ilyas), and others — are recognized as prophets in the Quran and Hadith literature. Islamic interpretation engages with 1 Kings primarily through the lens of Quranic parallels, evaluating biblical narratives against the standard of prophetic infallibility (*ismah*) and strict monotheism (*tawhid*).

## Relationship to the Quran

Key 1 Kings narratives that appear in Islamic tradition:

| Biblical Narrative | Islamic Parallel | Key Quranic References |
|-------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Solomon's wisdom and judgment | Sulayman's divinely granted judgment | Al-Anbiya 21:78-79 |
| Temple construction | Sulayman commands jinn to build | Saba 34:12-13 |
| Queen of Sheba's visit | Bilqis and the throne | An-Naml 27:20-44 |
| Solomon's kingdom | Sulayman's unmatched dominion | Sad 38:35-40 |
| Elijah vs. Baal prophets | Ilyas calls people from idolatry | As-Saffat 37:123-132 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
The biblical account of Solomon's apostasy — worshipping foreign gods in old age (1 Kings 11) — is firmly rejected in Islamic theology. Sulayman is regarded as a righteous prophet who never committed idolatry. The Quran states: "Solomon did not disbelieve, but the devils disbelieved" (Al-Baqarah 2:102).

### Tawhid (Divine Unity)
All narratives are read through the lens of strict monotheism. Solomon's prayer at the Temple dedication is affirmed insofar as it acknowledges God's oneness, but any association of God with a physical dwelling is reinterpreted.

### Sulayman's Supernatural Dominion
Islamic tradition, drawing on Quran and Hadith, attributes to Sulayman powers not found in 1 Kings:
- Command over the wind (Saba 34:12)
- Authority over the jinn (An-Naml 27:17)
- Understanding the speech of birds and animals (An-Naml 27:16)

## Theological Emphases

- **Kingship as divine trust**: Sulayman's power is a test (*fitna*) and a gift from God
- **Rejection of idolatry narratives**: Solomon's apostasy as described in 1 Kings 11 is considered *tahrif* (textual corruption)
- **Ilyas (Elijah)**: A prophet who called his people away from Baal worship to pure monotheism
- **The Temple**: Not a uniquely sacred space in Islam but acknowledged as a place of monotheistic worship
