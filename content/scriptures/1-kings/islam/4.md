## Sulayman's Kingdom and Administration

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 4 evaluates Solomon's prosperity and administration through the Quranic lens of kingship as a divine trust (*amanah*) and a test (*fitna*).

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Sulayman's Unmatched Kingdom

The Quran records Sulayman's prayer: "My Lord, forgive me and grant me a kingdom such as will not belong to anyone after me. Indeed, You are the Bestower" (Sad 38:35). Islamic tradition understands this as fulfilled: Sulayman's kingdom was unique in all of history, encompassing authority over humans, jinn, animals, and the wind.

**Al-Tabari** describes Sulayman's court as attended by both humans and jinn, with the wind carrying his throne wherever he wished. This far exceeds the administrative structure described in 1 Kings 4.

### Prosperity as Divine Test

Islamic theology regards Sulayman's wealth and power as a *fitna* (test). The Quran warns repeatedly that wealth and power are trials: "Your wealth and your children are but a trial, and God has with Him a great reward" (At-Taghabun 64:15).

**Sayyid Qutb** in *Fi Zilal al-Quran* emphasized that Sulayman's uniqueness was not in his wealth but in his use of it for God's purposes. Unlike the 1 Kings narrative, which portrays increasing self-indulgence, the Quranic Sulayman consistently directs his power toward worship and justice.

### Wisdom Encompassing All Knowledge

The Islamic account of Sulayman's knowledge extends beyond the natural history described in 1 Kings 4:33. In Islamic tradition, Sulayman understands the inner nature of all creatures because God has granted him prophetic insight into the divine order underlying creation.

## Theological Significance

1. **Kingdom as trust**: Political power is a divine *amanah* that must be used for God's purposes
2. **Wealth as test**: Prosperity tests the believer's gratitude and stewardship
3. **Unique prophetic endowment**: Sulayman's kingdom was unmatched in scope and nature
4. **Wisdom directed toward worship**: True wisdom serves God, not self-glorification
