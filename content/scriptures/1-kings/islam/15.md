## Abijam and Asa of Judah

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 15 draws on the broader principles of just governance, reform, and the ongoing struggle between faith and idolatry.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Reform as Islamic Value

Asa's reforms — removing idols and deposing his grandmother for her involvement in idol worship — resonate with the Islamic concept of *islah* (reform/correction). The Quran praises those who work for reform: "Indeed, Allah does not allow to be lost the reward of those who do good" (Hud 11:115).

The principle that no family relationship overrides the demands of *tawhid* is deeply embedded in Islamic theology. Ibrahim's willingness to oppose his own father's idolatry (Maryam 19:42-48) sets the paradigm.

### The Incompleteness of Reform

The note that "the high places were not removed" during Asa's reign corresponds to the Islamic understanding that *jihad al-nafs* (the struggle against the soul's lower tendencies) is a lifelong process. Complete purification — of the individual or the community — is an ongoing effort, not a single event.

### The Cycle of Leadership

The contrast between Abijam (unfaithful) and Asa (reforming) illustrates the Quranic observation that each generation is tested individually: "That was a community which has passed on. It will have what it earned, and you will have what you have earned" (Al-Baqarah 2:134).

## Theological Significance

1. **Islah (reform)**: Active correction of religious corruption is an obligation
2. **Tawhid above kinship**: No family relationship overrides the demands of monotheism
3. **Ongoing struggle**: Purification is a continuous process, not a single achievement
4. **Individual accountability**: Each generation is judged on its own faithfulness
