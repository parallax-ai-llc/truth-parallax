## The Question of Solomon's Apostasy

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 11 is defined by a categorical rejection of its central claim: that Solomon worshipped foreign gods. This chapter represents one of the clearest points of divergence between biblical and Islamic tradition.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)

Islamic theology holds that prophets are protected from major sins, particularly *shirk* (associating partners with God). The Quranic Sulayman never commits idolatry. The Quran explicitly defends him: "And Solomon did not disbelieve, but the devils disbelieved" (Al-Baqarah 2:102).

**Ibn Kathir** states categorically: "It is not permissible to believe that a prophet of God worshipped idols. This is *tahrif* (corruption) introduced into the biblical text."

### The Quranic Account of Sulayman's Trial

The Quran does record a trial that befell Sulayman: "And We certainly tried Sulayman and placed on his throne a body; then he returned" (Sad 38:34). Islamic scholars interpret this variously:

| Interpretation | Scholar |
|---------------|---------|
| A jinn impersonated Sulayman and sat on his throne | Al-Tabari |
| Sulayman was tested with illness or loss of power | Ibn Kathir (preferred) |
| The "body" represents a lifeless child born to Sulayman | Some classical commentators |

In all interpretations, Sulayman repents and returns to God's favor — he does not commit idolatry.

### The Foreign Wives

Islamic tradition does not accept the biblical portrayal of Sulayman's 700 wives and 300 concubines. While polygamy is permitted in Islam (up to four wives under specific conditions), the excess described in 1 Kings 11 and its connection to idolatry is regarded as *tahrif*.

Some Islamic scholars acknowledge that Sulayman may have married multiple women but deny that this led to any form of worship of other gods.

### The Division of the Kingdom

Islamic tradition does not engage extensively with the division of the kingdom (which is narrated in 1 Kings 12). The focus remains on Sulayman's righteousness and the subsequent decline as the result of later generations' faithlessness, not Sulayman's personal sin.

## Theological Significance

1. **Prophetic infallibility**: Sulayman never committed idolatry — the biblical claim is *tahrif*
2. **The Quranic defense**: "Solomon did not disbelieve" (Al-Baqarah 2:102) is definitive
3. **Trial and return**: Sulayman was tested but returned to God — consistent with prophetic dignity
4. **Textual corruption**: Passages depicting prophets as idolaters are regarded as scribal alterations
