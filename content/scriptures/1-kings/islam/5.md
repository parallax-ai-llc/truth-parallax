## Preparations for the Temple

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 5 relates to Sulayman's construction projects, which Islamic tradition attributes to his command over the jinn and the wind rather than to forced human labor.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Sulayman's Builders: Jinn, Not Human Corvee

The Quran states that "among the jinn were those who worked for him by the permission of his Lord" (Saba 34:12) and that "the devils" (shayatin) built for him "whatever he willed — of elevated chambers, images, basins as large as reservoirs, and stationary kettles" (Saba 34:13).

This Quranic account fundamentally reframes the construction narrative. Where 1 Kings describes human forced labor — 30,000 men conscripted from Israel — Islamic tradition attributes the work to jinn under Sulayman's divinely granted authority. The use of forced human labor is viewed as inconsistent with prophetic justice.

### The Alliance with Hiram

Islamic tradition does not specifically address the alliance with Hiram of Tyre. However, the principle of cooperation between peoples for constructive purposes is affirmed in Islam. The Quran states: "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another" (Al-Hujurat 49:13).

### Cedar and Stone

Islamic architectural tradition values the use of fine materials in the construction of mosques and sacred spaces. The principle that God deserves the finest offerings of human craftsmanship is shared between the biblical and Islamic traditions, though the specific materials differ.

## Theological Significance

1. **Jinn as laborers**: Sulayman commanded supernatural beings, not exploited human workers
2. **Prophetic justice**: A prophet-king would not impose oppressive forced labor
3. **Divine authority over creation**: Sulayman's power over jinn demonstrates God's sovereignty
4. **Cooperation among peoples**: International collaboration for constructive purposes is valued
