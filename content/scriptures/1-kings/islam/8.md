## Dedication of the Temple

Islamic engagement with 1 Kings 8 relates to Solomon's prayer of dedication insofar as it affirms monotheism and God's transcendence — themes central to Islamic theology.

## Key Points of Islamic Engagement

### Solomon's Prayer and Tawhid

Solomon's declaration — "Will God indeed dwell on the earth? Behold, heaven and the highest heaven cannot contain you" (8:27) — resonates deeply with Islamic theology of divine transcendence (*tanzih*). God cannot be localized or contained in any physical structure.

Islamic theology affirms this principle absolutely: "Nothing is like unto Him" (Ash-Shura 42:11). The Temple is not God's dwelling in any literal sense — God is beyond all containment. Islamic scholars read Solomon's acknowledgment as consistent with authentic prophetic theology.

### Prayer and Its Direction

Solomon's instruction that the people should pray "toward this house" (8:29-30) resonates with the Islamic concept of *qiblah* — the direction of prayer. Jerusalem was the first *qiblah* for Muslims before it was changed to the Kaaba in Mecca (Al-Baqarah 2:142-150).

This connection reinforces the Islamic understanding that Sulayman's Temple was a legitimate house of monotheistic worship, part of the continuous prophetic tradition that culminates in Islam.

### Universal Scope of Prayer (8:41-43)

Solomon's prayer for the foreigner — "that all the peoples of the earth may know your name and fear you" — aligns with Islam's self-understanding as a universal religion. The Quran states: "We have not sent you [Muhammad] except as a mercy to the worlds" (Al-Anbiya 21:107).

### The Glory Cloud

The descent of the glory cloud upon the Temple is not directly addressed in the Quran. Islamic theology is cautious about anthropomorphic descriptions of God's presence. God's relationship to physical spaces is understood through his knowledge and power, not through physical inhabitation: "He is with you wherever you are" (Al-Hadid 57:4).

## Theological Significance

1. **Divine transcendence**: God cannot be contained in any structure — a core Islamic principle
2. **Qiblah**: The Temple as the original direction of prayer connects to Islamic practice
3. **Universal worship**: Solomon's prayer for all peoples resonates with Islam's universal mission
4. **Tanzih**: God's presence is understood through power and knowledge, not physical inhabitation
