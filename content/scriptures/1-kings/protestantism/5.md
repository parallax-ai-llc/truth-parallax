## Preparations for the Temple

Chapter 5 records Solomon's alliance with King Hiram of Tyre and the massive labor force assembled for the Temple project. Protestant interpreters see in these preparations the convergence of divine purpose and human effort.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Solomon's Message to Hiram (5:1-6)

Solomon informs Hiram that David could not build the Temple because of his wars, but now "the Lord my God has given me rest on every side." The theological rationale is clear: the Temple is a house of peace, built in a time of peace.

**Calvin** noted that God's timing is purposeful — the Temple could not be built amid warfare because it was to represent the peace of God's presence with his people.

### The Alliance with Tyre (5:7-12)

The cooperation between Israelite and Phoenician labor raises an important Protestant interpretive question: should God's house be built with pagan help? Most commentators see this positively, as the nations contributing to God's glory.

### The Labor Force (5:13-18)

Solomon conscripts 30,000 men from Israel for forced labor. Protestant interpreters note the irony:

- Israel was delivered from forced labor in Egypt
- Now Israel's own king imposes corvée labor for God's house
- This burden will become the catalyst for the kingdom's division (12:4)

## Theological Significance

1. **Peace as prerequisite for worship**: The Temple is built in rest, not in war
2. **International cooperation for God's glory**: Hiram's contribution foreshadows the nations streaming to Zion
3. **The cost of sacred space**: Great undertakings require sacrifice, but the cost must not crush the people
4. **Divine provision through human means**: God provides cedar, stone, and labor through natural channels
