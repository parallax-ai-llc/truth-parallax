## Solomon's Wisdom and the Famous Judgment

Chapter 3 records Solomon's encounter with God at Gibeon and the celebrated judgment between the two mothers. For Protestant theology, this chapter establishes wisdom as a divine gift inseparable from covenant obedience.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Solomon's Sacrifice at Gibeon (3:1-4)

Solomon worships at the "great high place" of Gibeon before the Temple is built. Protestant interpreters note the narrator's qualified approval — the people sacrificed at high places "because no house had yet been built for the name of the Lord."

### The Dream at Gibeon (3:5-15)

God appears to Solomon in a dream: "Ask what I shall give you." Solomon's request for "an understanding heart to judge your people" rather than wealth or long life earns divine commendation.

**Calvin** drew from this passage the principle that true wisdom begins with acknowledging one's insufficiency before God. Solomon's humility — "I am but a little child" — is the prerequisite for receiving divine wisdom.

**Luther** preached that Solomon's prayer exemplifies faith: asking God for what only God can give, rather than seeking worldly advantage.

### The Judgment of the Two Mothers (3:16-28)

The famous case of the two women claiming the same child demonstrates Solomon's wisdom in action. Protestant interpreters highlight:

- **The penetrating nature of true wisdom**: Solomon discerns the truth by testing the heart
- **Justice for the powerless**: The two women are prostitutes — the lowest social class — yet Solomon gives them a fair hearing
- **Typology**: Solomon's wisdom in judgment points to Christ, who judges with perfect righteousness

## Theological Significance

1. **Wisdom as divine gift**: Human wisdom is insufficient; true discernment comes from God alone
2. **The prayer of faith**: Solomon asks in faith and receives abundantly (cf. James 1:5)
3. **Christological typology**: "Something greater than Solomon is here" (Matthew 12:42)
4. **Justice and compassion**: True wisdom serves justice, especially for the vulnerable
