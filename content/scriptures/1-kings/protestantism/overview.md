## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 1 Kings emphasizes the book's covenantal framework: God's promises are conditional upon obedience, and the narrative demonstrates the consequences of both faithfulness and apostasy. The Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* leads Protestants to read 1 Kings as a self-interpreting account of how kingship, temple worship, and prophetic authority interact under God's sovereign rule.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Redemptive-Historical
Reformed and evangelical scholars read 1 Kings within the overarching narrative of redemption. Solomon's temple points forward to Christ, who declared, "Something greater than Solomon is here" (Matthew 12:42). The division of the kingdom reveals the inadequacy of human kingship and the need for a messianic king.

### Covenantal
The Davidic covenant (2 Samuel 7) provides the theological lens for reading Solomon's rise and fall. God's conditional promises — "if you walk before me... I will establish your royal throne" (1 Kings 9:4-5) — frame the book's central tension.

### Prophetic
Elijah's confrontation with Baal worship on Mount Carmel (chapter 18) is a paradigmatic text for Protestant theology: the sovereignty of God over all false gods, the sufficiency of faith, and the boldness of the prophetic calling.

## Theological Emphases

- **Idolatry as the root sin**: Solomon's foreign wives lead to worship of Ashtoreth, Chemosh, and Molech
- **The prophet vs. the king**: Elijah and Micaiah exemplify the prophetic office's independence from royal power
- **Typology**: Solomon as a type of Christ in wisdom and temple-building; Elijah as a type of John the Baptist
- **Justification by faith**: Elijah's prayer on Mount Carmel as an example of faith alone before God
