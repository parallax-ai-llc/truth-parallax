## Solomon's Apostasy

Chapter 11 records the most devastating reversal in Israel's history: Solomon, the wisest king, turns to idolatry under the influence of his foreign wives. Protestant interpreters regard this chapter as one of the most sobering warnings in Scripture.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Solomon's Foreign Wives (11:1-3)

"King Solomon loved many foreign women... from the nations concerning which the Lord had said to the people of Israel, 'You shall not enter into marriage with them.'" Solomon had 700 wives and 300 concubines.

**Calvin** wrote with characteristic directness: Solomon "buried his wisdom" under the weight of his passions. The wisest man became the most foolish by abandoning the very principle that made him wise — obedience to God's word.

### Turning After Other Gods (11:4-8)

"When Solomon was old his wives turned away his heart after other gods." He built high places for Chemosh (the god of Moab) and Molech (the god of the Ammonites).

**Protestant interpreters** emphasize that Solomon's sin was not sudden but gradual:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Treaty marriages | Political alliances sealed by marriage (violation of Deut 7:3-4) |
| Toleration | Permitting foreign worship in Jerusalem |
| Participation | Solomon himself worshipping at the high places |
| Construction | Building permanent shrines for foreign gods |

### God's Judgment (11:9-13)

"The Lord was angry with Solomon." God announces the kingdom will be torn from Solomon's son — though not entirely, for David's sake and Jerusalem's sake.

### Adversaries Raised Up (11:14-40)

God raises three adversaries: Hadad the Edomite, Rezon of Damascus, and Jeroboam son of Nebat. The prophet Ahijah tears a garment into twelve pieces, giving ten to Jeroboam as a symbolic prophecy.

## Theological Significance

1. **No one is immune to apostasy**: If Solomon fell, anyone can
2. **The danger of compromise**: Sin progresses from tolerance to participation to institutionalization
3. **God's judgment is measured**: The kingdom is torn but not destroyed — grace persists even in judgment
4. **The failure of human kingship**: Even the best of Israel's kings proves inadequate, pointing to Christ
