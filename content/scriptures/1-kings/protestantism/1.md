## Solomon's Rise and the Succession Crisis

1 Kings opens with the aging King David and the political maneuvering surrounding his succession. Adonijah, David's eldest surviving son, attempts to seize the throne, but the prophet Nathan and Bathsheba intervene to secure Solomon's anointing.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### David's Declining Power (1:1-4)

The opening portrayal of David — old, unable to keep warm, attended by Abishag the Shunammite — stands in stark contrast to the warrior-king of 1-2 Samuel. Protestant interpreters see here a sober reminder that even the greatest human leaders are mortal and frail.

**Calvin** observed that God humbled David in his final days to demonstrate that no earthly power endures, pointing to the need for an eternal King.

### Adonijah's Presumption (1:5-10)

Adonijah's self-declaration — "I will be king" — mirrors Absalom's earlier rebellion. Protestant theology identifies this as the sin of presumption: claiming authority that only God can bestow.

### Nathan and Bathsheba's Intervention (1:11-27)

The prophet Nathan acts decisively within the political sphere, reflecting the Protestant understanding that God works through ordinary means — even court politics — to fulfill his purposes.

### Solomon's Anointing (1:28-40)

Solomon's anointing at Gihon, performed by Zadok the priest and Nathan the prophet, establishes the pattern of divinely authorized kingship. Protestant interpreters note the collaboration of prophetic and priestly offices, both of which find their fulfillment in Christ.

## Theological Significance

1. **Divine sovereignty over succession**: God's choice of Solomon overrides natural primogeniture
2. **The inadequacy of human kingship**: Even David's house is marred by rivalry and intrigue
3. **Typology**: Solomon's peaceful accession prefigures Christ's eternal kingdom
4. **Providence**: God works through human agents (Nathan, Bathsheba) to accomplish his purposes
