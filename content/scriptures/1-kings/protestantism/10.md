## The Queen of Sheba Visits Solomon

Chapter 10 portrays Solomon at the height of his glory: the Queen of Sheba journeys from afar to test his wisdom, and the narrator catalogs his extraordinary wealth. Protestant interpreters read this chapter as both the apex of covenantal blessing and the prelude to Solomon's fall.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Queen of Sheba's Visit (10:1-13)

The queen comes "to test him with hard questions" and is overwhelmed: "The half was not told me. Your wisdom and prosperity surpass the report that I heard."

**Jesus** himself invoked this visit: "The queen of the South will rise up at the judgment with this generation and condemn it, for she came from the ends of the earth to hear the wisdom of Solomon, and behold, something greater than Solomon is here" (Matthew 12:42).

**Protestant interpreters** draw several lessons:
- A pagan queen recognized divine wisdom — Israel's own kings would not
- Her willingness to travel points to the faith of the Gentiles
- Solomon's fame draws the nations, foreshadowing the universal scope of the gospel

### Solomon's Wealth (10:14-25)

Six hundred sixty-six talents of gold per year, thrones of ivory and gold, fleets of ships — the catalog of wealth is deliberately extravagant. Protestant interpreters note the number 666 (10:14) and its later resonance in Revelation 13:18, though most caution against over-reading this parallel.

### Horses and Chariots from Egypt (10:26-29)

Solomon acquires horses and chariots from Egypt, directly violating the law of the king in Deuteronomy 17:16: "He shall not acquire many horses for himself or cause the people to return to Egypt."

**Reformed interpreters** see this as the first visible crack in Solomon's obedience — a violation of Torah that anticipates the full-scale apostasy of chapter 11.

## Theological Significance

1. **Wisdom attracts the nations**: Solomon's wisdom is a vehicle for God's glory among the Gentiles
2. **Wealth as test**: Abundance can be a gift or a snare — Solomon's case will prove the latter
3. **Deuteronomic violations**: The accumulation of gold, horses, and (soon) wives directly contravenes the Torah's limits on kingship
4. **Christological surpassing**: Christ is the "greater Solomon" who fulfills what Solomon could only preview
