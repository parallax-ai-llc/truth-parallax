## The Divine Nature and Gnostic Claims

2 Peter 1:4 — "partakers of the divine nature" — uses language that resonates with the Gnostic claim that the pneumatic possesses an inherent divine nature. However, 2 Peter immediately qualifies this participation with a list of moral virtues (1:5–7) that must be actively cultivated. This ethical framework counters the Gnostic belief that the divine nature is an innate possession requiring only awakening, not moral effort.

## Knowledge Reclaimed

The letter's repeated emphasis on "knowledge" (*epignōsis*) of Christ (1:2, 3, 8) appears to reclaim the concept of salvific knowledge from Gnostic appropriation. In 2 Peter, true knowledge produces moral transformation — faith, virtue, self-control, godliness, love. This stands in direct contrast to the Gnostic understanding of gnosis as cosmological insight that transcends moral categories.

## Eyewitness vs. Myth

2 Peter 1:16 — "We did not follow cleverly devised myths" — may represent a direct polemic against Gnostic mythological systems. The Valentinian Pleroma, with its elaborate hierarchy of aeons and emanations, could be characterized as "cleverly devised myths." 2 Peter counters by appealing to eyewitness testimony and empirical experience of the Transfiguration.
