## The Delay of the Parousia

2 Peter 3 addresses those who deny the second coming — possibly Gnostic-influenced Christians who had spiritualized eschatology. The Gnostic tradition generally rejected the idea of a future, physical return of Christ, teaching instead that salvation was a present, interior experience of gnosis. 2 Peter insists on the physical reality of cosmic transformation, countering any reduction of eschatology to interior awakening.

## The Physical Cosmos

2 Peter 3:10–12, describing the dissolution of the heavens and earth by fire, affirms the reality and significance of the material cosmos — precisely what Gnostic cosmology denied. For Gnostics, the material world was the flawed creation of the Demiurge, destined for dissolution not by divine judgment but by its own inherent deficiency. 2 Peter affirms that the present cosmos is God's creation and that its transformation is a sovereign divine act.

## New Creation vs. Pleroma

2 Peter 3:13's promise of "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells" represents a fundamentally different eschatological vision from the Gnostic return to the Pleroma. The Gnostic hope was escape from matter into pure spiritual existence. 2 Peter envisions the renewal of the material world — a physical new creation in which righteousness is at home. This affirmation of the goodness of material creation stands in direct opposition to Gnostic ontology.
