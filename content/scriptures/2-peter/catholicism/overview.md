## Overview

Catholic interpretation of 2 Peter emphasizes the letter's teaching on participation in the divine nature (1:4), the authority of apostolic tradition over private interpretation (1:20–21), and the cosmic scope of eschatological renewal. The letter's warning against private interpretation of Scripture has been especially significant for Catholic theology, supporting the Church's role as the authoritative interpreter of the Bible.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Partakers of the Divine Nature
2 Peter 1:4 — "that through these you may become partakers of the divine nature" — is a central text for Catholic theology of grace and deification. While the Western tradition has not developed the concept of theosis as extensively as the East, Catholic theology affirms that grace effects a real interior transformation, making the believer a participant in God's own life.

### Authoritative Interpretation
2 Peter 1:20 — "no prophecy of Scripture is a matter of one's own interpretation" — supports the Catholic teaching that Scripture must be interpreted within the living tradition of the Church under the guidance of the Magisterium. The Second Vatican Council's *Dei Verbum* develops this principle, affirming that Scripture and Tradition together form one sacred deposit of the Word of God.

### Cosmic Renewal
2 Peter 3:10–13, describing the dissolution of the present heavens and earth and the coming of "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells," informs Catholic eschatology. The Catechism teaches that the material universe itself will be transformed and renewed, not annihilated — a cosmic redemption that includes the bodily resurrection.

## Theological Emphases

- **Participation in the Divine Nature**: Grace as real interior transformation
- **Church Authority**: The Magisterium as guardian of scriptural interpretation
- **Apostolic Tradition**: The reliability of eyewitness testimony (1:16–18)
- **New Creation**: The transformation of the cosmos in the eschatological age
