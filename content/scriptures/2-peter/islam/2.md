## False Teachers and Corrupted Religion

2 Peter 2, with its sustained denunciation of false teachers, resonates with the Quranic concern about those who corrupt divine revelation. The Quran warns against "those who conceal what Allah has revealed of the Book and purchase thereby a small gain" (2:174). Muslim scholars read 2 Peter's warning as confirmation that the corruption of religious teaching has been a persistent problem across Abrahamic traditions.

## Prophetic Examples of Judgment

2 Peter 2:4–10, citing the flood, Sodom, and the fallen angels, draws on narratives shared across Abrahamic traditions. The Quran narrates the stories of Nuh (Noah), Lut (Lot), and the peoples destroyed by divine judgment extensively. Muslim readers recognize the shared moral framework: God is patient but ultimately just, and those who persist in corruption will face divine reckoning.

## The Way of Balaam

2 Peter 2:15's reference to Balaam resonates with the Quranic narrative of those who received divine signs but rejected them for worldly gain. While the Quran does not name Balaam specifically, the figure of one who received God's revelations but "detached himself from them" (Quran 7:175–176) is presented as a cautionary tale. Both traditions warn against the corruption of prophetic authority by greed.
