## Overview

Eastern Orthodox theology regards 2 Peter 1:4 — "partakers of the divine nature" — as the single most important verse in the New Testament for the doctrine of theosis (deification). The entire Orthodox understanding of salvation as participation in the divine life finds its scriptural anchor in this passage. The letter's witness to the Transfiguration (1:16–18) and its vision of cosmic renewal (3:13) further reinforce its importance for Orthodox theology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Theosis
2 Peter 1:4 is the locus classicus for the Orthodox doctrine of theosis. Athanasius's famous dictum — "God became man so that man might become God" — finds its New Testament foundation here. Gregory Palamas distinguished between God's essence (forever inaccessible) and God's uncreated energies (in which believers participate), and 2 Peter 1:4 is understood as referring to participation in the divine energies.

### The Transfiguration
2 Peter 1:16–18, with its eyewitness account of the Transfiguration on the holy mountain, is central to Orthodox theology of divine light. The Transfiguration reveals the uncreated light that permeates Christ's humanity and is accessible to those who pursue the life of prayer and purification. Palamas's defense of the hesychast monks drew on this passage to affirm that the light of Tabor was the uncreated divine energy.

### Cosmic Transformation
2 Peter 3:10–13 envisions the dissolution of the present cosmos and the coming of a new creation. Orthodox eschatology reads this as cosmic transfiguration, not annihilation. The material world will be purified and glorified, just as Christ's body was transfigured on Tabor. Creation is destined not for destruction but for participation in the divine glory.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: 2 Peter 1:4 as the scriptural foundation for deification
- **Uncreated Light**: The Transfiguration as revelation of the divine energies
- **Essence-Energies Distinction**: Participation in God's energies, not God's essence
- **Cosmic Transfiguration**: The new heavens and new earth as the glorification of creation
