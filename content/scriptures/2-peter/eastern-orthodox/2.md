## False Teachers and the Integrity of Faith

2 Peter 2 warns against false teachers who introduce "destructive heresies." Orthodox theology reads this in light of the ecumenical councils, which defined the boundaries of orthodox faith precisely in response to heretical teaching. The letter's language of "denying the Master who bought them" (2:1) is understood as a reference to Christological heresy — the denial of Christ's true divine and human natures.

## Examples of Judgment

The three examples of divine judgment in 2:4–10 — fallen angels, the flood, Sodom — are read in the Orthodox liturgical and homiletical tradition as types of God's justice. The preservation of Noah and Lot within the judgment demonstrates the Orthodox conviction that God's judgment always includes the salvation of the righteous. The Church is the ark of salvation within the wider judgment of the world.

## The Return to Corruption

2 Peter 2:20–22 describes those who knew "the way of righteousness" but turned back to corruption. Orthodox theology reads this as a warning about the loss of baptismal grace through persistent sin. The sacrament of Confession exists precisely for the restoration of those who have fallen, but the letter's stern warning underscores the seriousness of post-baptismal sin and the need for ongoing repentance.
