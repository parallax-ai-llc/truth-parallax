## Theosis: Partakers of the Divine Nature

2 Peter 1:4 is the single most cited verse in Orthodox theology of theosis. The promise that believers may become "partakers of the divine nature" (*theias koinōnoi physeōs*) is understood not as metaphor but as ontological reality. Through the incarnation, Christ united divine and human natures, opening the possibility for all humanity to participate in the divine life. Gregory Palamas clarified that this participation is in God's uncreated energies, not the divine essence.

## The Transfiguration and Divine Light

2 Peter 1:16–18, with its eyewitness account of the Transfiguration, is central to Orthodox theology of the uncreated light. The light that shone from Christ on Mount Tabor was not created illumination but the visible manifestation of God's uncreated energies. The hesychast tradition teaches that this same light is accessible to those who pursue the life of prayer and purification, anticipating the full vision of divine glory in the age to come.

## The Prophetic Word

2 Peter 1:19–21 affirms that Scripture is inspired by the Holy Spirit. Orthodox theology holds that Scripture is one mode of the Church's living tradition, inseparable from the patristic interpretation, liturgical usage, and conciliar definitions that together constitute the fullness of divine revelation.
