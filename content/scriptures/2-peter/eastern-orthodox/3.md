## The Day of the Lord and Cosmic Transfiguration

2 Peter 3:10–13 describes the dissolution of the present cosmos and the coming of "new heavens and a new earth." Orthodox eschatology understands this as transfiguration, not annihilation. Just as Christ's body was transfigured on Tabor and glorified in the resurrection, so the entire material cosmos will be transfigured by the divine energies. The new creation is not a replacement but a glorification of the present order.

## Divine Patience and Universal Mercy

2 Peter 3:9 — God "not wishing that any should perish" — resonates with the Orthodox emphasis on God's boundless love and desire for the salvation of all. While Orthodoxy affirms the reality of human freedom to reject God, the dominant patristic tradition emphasizes the depth and breadth of God's salvific will. Gregory of Nyssa's hope for universal restoration (*apokatastasis*) draws on this passage.

## Growth in Knowledge

2 Peter 3:18 — "Grow in the grace and knowledge of our Lord" — encapsulates the Orthodox understanding of the spiritual life as an unending journey into the infinite mystery of God. Knowledge of God is not a fixed possession but a dynamic, ever-deepening participation in the divine life. The Orthodox spiritual tradition, from Evagrius to Maximus the Confessor, articulates this as the soul's eternal movement into the inexhaustible depths of divine love.
