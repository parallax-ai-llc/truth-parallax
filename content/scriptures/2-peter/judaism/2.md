## False Prophets in Jewish Law

2 Peter 2:1 warns of "false prophets" and "false teachers," echoing the Torah's concern with false prophecy. Deuteronomy 13:1–5 prescribes the test for false prophets: does the prophet's teaching lead to the worship of other gods? Jewish readers note that the concern with false religious authority is rooted in the Torah itself and represents one of the deepest continuities between Jewish and Christian ethics.

## The Flood and Sodom

2 Peter 2:4–10 uses the flood and the destruction of Sodom as examples of divine judgment. These narratives are foundational in Jewish tradition as demonstrations of God's justice. The preservation of Noah and Lot illustrates the Jewish principle that the righteous are delivered from the judgment that falls on the wicked — a theme developed extensively in rabbinic midrash.

## Balaam in Jewish Tradition

2 Peter 2:15–16 references "the way of Balaam," who loved the "wages of unrighteousness." In Jewish tradition, Balaam is a complex figure: a genuine prophet who was corrupted by greed and led Israel into sin (Numbers 22–24; 31:16). Rabbinic literature presents Balaam as the paradigm of the religious leader who uses prophetic gifts for personal advantage — precisely the concern of 2 Peter's polemic.
