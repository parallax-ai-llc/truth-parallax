## Overview

Jewish scholars engage with 2 Peter primarily as a window into the Jewish apocalyptic traditions that shaped early Christianity. The letter's use of biblical examples of divine judgment (Noah's flood, Sodom and Gomorrah), its concern with false prophecy, and its apocalyptic cosmology all draw on resources deeply rooted in Second Temple Judaism. The letter also shares extensive material with the Epistle of Jude, which itself draws on Jewish pseudepigraphical traditions.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Apocalyptic Heritage
2 Peter's eschatological vision — the dissolution of the heavens by fire (3:10–12) and the coming of a new creation (3:13) — draws on Jewish apocalyptic traditions found in texts like 1 Enoch, 4 Ezra, and the Sibylline Oracles. Jewish scholars note that the letter's cosmology is thoroughly rooted in Jewish apocalyptic thought, even as it applies these traditions to distinctly Christian theological claims.

### False Prophecy
The letter's extended warning against false teachers (chapter 2) echoes the Torah's concern with false prophets (Deuteronomy 13:1–5; 18:20–22). Jewish readers recognize the continuity of concern: discerning true from false religious authority is a perennial challenge in both Jewish and Christian tradition.

### Divine Patience
2 Peter 3:9 — "The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise, as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish" — resonates with rabbinic teaching on God's attribute of mercy (*middat harachamim*). The delay of judgment is an expression of divine forbearance, a theme found throughout the Hebrew Bible and rabbinic literature.

## Theological Emphases

- **Apocalyptic Continuity**: 2 Peter's eschatology as rooted in Jewish apocalyptic tradition
- **False Prophecy**: Shared concern with discerning true from false religious authority
- **Divine Patience**: God's forbearance as an attribute of mercy
- **Noahic Traditions**: The flood narrative as a paradigm of judgment and deliverance
