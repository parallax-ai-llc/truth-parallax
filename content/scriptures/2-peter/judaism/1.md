## Knowledge and the Jewish Tradition

2 Peter 1:3–8, with its emphasis on the knowledge of God that produces virtue, resonates with the Jewish concept of *da'at Elohim* (knowledge of God) developed in the prophetic literature. Hosea 4:1 laments the lack of knowledge of God, and Jeremiah 31:34 envisions a future in which all will know God. Jewish readers appreciate the letter's insistence that genuine knowledge of God transforms moral conduct.

## The Transfiguration and Theophany

2 Peter 1:16–18 describes the Transfiguration as an event in which God's voice spoke from heaven. Jewish scholars note the parallels with the Sinai theophany (Exodus 19–20), where God's glory was manifest and the divine voice declared Torah. The Transfiguration narrative draws on the deep Jewish tradition of divine revelation on holy mountains.

## Prophecy and the Spirit

2 Peter 1:19–21 affirms that prophets spoke "as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit." Jewish readers recognize the continuity with the Hebrew Bible's own understanding of prophecy as divinely inspired speech. The prophets of Israel spoke the word of the Lord, not their own inventions — a conviction shared by 2 Peter and by rabbinic tradition, which affirms that the *ruach ha-kodesh* (Holy Spirit) rested upon the prophets.
