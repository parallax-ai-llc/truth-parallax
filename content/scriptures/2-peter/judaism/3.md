## Scoffers and the Problem of Theodicy

2 Peter 3:3–4, describing those who mock the promise of divine intervention — "Where is the promise of his coming?" — touches on the classic Jewish problem of theodicy: why does God delay justice? The Psalms ask this question repeatedly (Psalm 13:1; 94:3), and the rabbinic tradition develops extensive answers, centered on God's patience and the ultimate certainty of justice.

## A Day as a Thousand Years

2 Peter 3:8, citing Psalm 90:4 — "With the Lord one day is as a thousand years" — draws on a text attributed to Moses that expresses the radical difference between divine and human perspectives on time. Jewish readers appreciate the hermeneutical principle: divine time is not human time, and the apparent delay of justice does not indicate divine indifference but divine patience.

## New Heavens and New Earth

2 Peter 3:13 envisions "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells," echoing Isaiah 65:17 and 66:22. Jewish eschatological hope has always included the renewal of creation — the *olam ha-ba* (the world to come) is not an ethereal spiritual realm but a renewed and perfected material world. Jewish readers recognize in 2 Peter a shared eschatological vision rooted in the Hebrew prophets.
