## Partakers of the Divine Nature

2 Peter 1:3–4 speaks of believers becoming "partakers of the divine nature." Protestant interpreters generally read this as a metaphor for the transforming power of sanctification rather than ontological deification. The "divine nature" is shared through the "precious and very great promises" of the gospel, and participation consists in growing in Christlike character through the moral qualities listed in 1:5–7.

## The Ladder of Virtues

2 Peter 1:5–7 presents a chain of virtues: faith, virtue, knowledge, self-control, steadfastness, godliness, brotherly affection, and love. Protestant interpreters read this as a practical guide to sanctification — the progressive cultivation of Christian character. These virtues are not the basis of salvation but its evidence, and their absence indicates spiritual blindness (1:9).

## The Prophetic Word Confirmed

2 Peter 1:16–21 defends the reliability of apostolic testimony with the eyewitness account of the Transfiguration and affirms the divine origin of prophetic Scripture. Protestant theology draws on 1:20–21 as a key text for the doctrine of inspiration: the prophets spoke not from their own impulse but as they were "carried along by the Holy Spirit." This passage, alongside 2 Timothy 3:16, anchors the Protestant commitment to Scripture's authority.
