## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 2 Peter has focused on the letter's defense of scriptural authority and the certainty of Christ's return. The declaration that "no prophecy of Scripture comes from someone's own interpretation" and that "men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit" (1:20–21) is foundational for the Protestant doctrine of biblical inspiration. The letter's warnings against false teachers and its eschatological teaching have also shaped Protestant theology significantly.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Biblical Inspiration
2 Peter 1:20–21 is one of the key proof-texts for the Protestant doctrine of verbal plenary inspiration. Along with 2 Timothy 3:16, it establishes that Scripture is not the product of human initiative but the result of the Holy Spirit's superintending influence on the human authors. This has been central to evangelical theology since the Princeton school.

### Eschatological Certainty
2 Peter 3, which defends the reality of Christ's return against skeptics who ask "Where is the promise of his coming?" (3:4), shapes Protestant eschatology. The passage affirms that divine timing differs from human expectation and that the delay is an expression of God's patience, "not wishing that any should perish" (3:9).

### Growth in Grace
The letter's opening exhortation to supplement faith with virtue, knowledge, self-control, steadfastness, godliness, brotherly affection, and love (1:5–7) has been used in Protestant teaching on progressive sanctification — the believer's growth in Christlikeness through active pursuit of virtue empowered by grace.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sola Scriptura**: 2 Peter 1:19–21 as a foundation for the authority of Scripture
- **The Parousia**: Christ's return as certain despite apparent delay
- **False Teaching**: The marks and destiny of false teachers as a warning to the church
- **Growth in Knowledge**: The Christian life as progressive growth in the knowledge of Christ
