## The Rise of False Teachers

2 Peter 2:1–3 warns that false teachers will arise within the community, bringing "destructive heresies" and exploiting believers with "false words." Protestant interpreters read this as a perennial warning applicable to every generation of the church. The marks of false teaching identified here — doctrinal distortion, moral license, and financial exploitation — serve as diagnostic criteria for evaluating religious leaders.

## Historical Precedents of Judgment

2 Peter 2:4–10 cites three examples of divine judgment: the fallen angels, the flood generation, and Sodom and Gomorrah. Protestant interpreters treat these as historical demonstrations of God's willingness and ability to judge wickedness while preserving the righteous (Noah, Lot). The passage affirms divine sovereignty over history and assures believers that false teachers will ultimately face judgment.

## The Corruption of False Freedom

2 Peter 2:17–22 describes the false teachers as "waterless springs" who promise freedom while being slaves of corruption. Protestant interpreters emphasize the seriousness of apostasy: those who have "known the way of righteousness" and turned back are in a worse condition than if they had never known it (2:21). This passage has generated significant debate about the perseverance of the saints and the possibility of falling from grace.
