## Scoffers and the Delay of the Parousia

2 Peter 3:1–7 addresses those who mock the promise of Christ's return, arguing that the world has continued unchanged since creation. Peter responds by appealing to the flood as precedent: God has intervened in history before and will do so again. Protestant eschatology draws on this passage to affirm the certainty of the second coming despite its apparent delay.

## Divine Patience

2 Peter 3:8–9 explains the delay of Christ's return as an expression of divine patience: "With the Lord one day is as a thousand years" and God is "not wishing that any should perish." Protestant theology has used this passage in debates about election and universal atonement. Arminian interpreters emphasize God's desire for universal salvation, while Calvinists read "you" as referring to the elect whom God is gathering.

## New Heavens and New Earth

2 Peter 3:10–13 describes the dissolution of the present heavens and earth by fire and the coming of "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells." Protestant eschatology is divided between those who read this as cosmic annihilation followed by new creation and those who interpret it as radical purification and renewal of the existing cosmos. The ethical imperative — "what sort of people ought you to be?" (3:11) — grounds eschatological hope in present moral seriousness.
