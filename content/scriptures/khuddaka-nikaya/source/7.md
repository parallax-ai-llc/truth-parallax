---
title: "Khuddaka Nikaya - Chapter 7: Petavatthu (Stories of the Hungry Ghosts)"
scripture: "khuddaka-nikaya"
chapter: 7
---

**1** Outside the walls they stand, and at the crossroads. They stand at door-posts, returning to their former homes. But when a meal with plentiful food and drink is served, no one remembers them. Such is the karma of living beings.

**2** Thus those who are compassionate toward their dead relatives give timely offerings of proper food and drink, thinking: "May this be for our relatives. May our relatives be happy!"

**3** Those who have gathered there, the assembled relatives, gratefully approve the plentiful food and drink: "May our relatives live long because of whom we have gained this! Honour has been done to us, and the donors are not without fruit."

**4** The venerable Moggallana saw a being in the peta realm, emaciated, naked, consumed by hunger and thirst. He asked: "What evil deed did you commit in the past?"

**5** The peta said: "Venerable sir, in my former life I was miserly. Though I had great wealth, I did not give to the poor, did not offer to the monks, did not share with the needy. I hoarded everything for myself. By that miserliness, I was reborn here in this wretched state."

**6** Another peta said: "I was a slanderer. I spoke ill of the virtuous, I disparaged the noble ones, I spread rumours about the innocent. By that verbal misconduct, I was reborn in this realm of suffering."

**7** Another peta said: "I was a butcher. Day after day I killed animals for my livelihood. I felt no remorse, no compassion. By that unwholesome karma, I now suffer the fruits of my deeds."

**8** The Blessed One said: "Monks, the realm of the hungry ghosts is a realm of great suffering. Those reborn there experience constant hunger, constant thirst, constant exposure. They are tormented by desires they cannot fulfil."

**9** "But even from this realm, liberation is possible. When their living relatives make offerings of food, clothing, and other requisites, dedicating the merit to the departed, those departed ones may receive the benefit and find relief."

**10** "Therefore, the wise should make offerings on behalf of the dead, thinking: 'May this merit reach my departed relatives.' And the departed, if they are in a state to receive it, will be grateful and find relief."

**11** "Moreover, one should reflect on the suffering of those in the lower realms and develop a sense of urgency. 'I, too, if I am heedless, may be reborn in such a realm.' This reflection leads to diligence in the practice of virtue."

**12** "The wise, having reflected on the suffering of the petas, should practise generosity, morality, and meditation. They should not be miserly, not be slanderous, not be violent. For it is by one's own actions that one determines one's future destination."
