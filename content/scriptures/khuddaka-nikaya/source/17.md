---
title: "Khuddaka Nikaya - Chapter 17: Petakopadesa (Instruction on the Pitaka)"
scripture: "khuddaka-nikaya"
chapter: 17
---

**1** Homage to the Blessed One. This is the Petakopadesa, the Instruction on the Pitaka, which shows how the teaching is to be interpreted. It was spoken by the Elder Mahakaccana.

**2** The teaching of the Blessed One has one flavour: the flavour of liberation. But it is expounded in various ways, according to the dispositions and capacities of beings.

**3** There are three turnings of the Dhamma: the teaching of the aggregates, elements, and bases; the teaching of dependent origination; and the teaching of the path. All the Suttas can be classified under one of these three.

**4** The teaching of the aggregates instructs one to see: "This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self." The teaching of dependent origination instructs one to see: "When this is, that comes to be." The teaching of the path instructs one: "This is the way to liberation."

**5** How should a Sutta be interpreted? First, one should determine its topic: is it about suffering, its origin, its cessation, or the path? Then one should determine to whom it was addressed and in what circumstances.

**6** Then one should determine the level of teaching: is it a teaching of convention or a teaching of the ultimate? At the conventional level, the Buddha spoke of beings, persons, men, and women. At the ultimate level, he spoke only of aggregates, elements, and bases.

**7** The Suttas spoken at the conventional level should not be taken as expressions of ultimate truth, and the Suttas spoken at the ultimate level should not be reduced to mere convention. One must distinguish between the two.

**8** A Sutta that requires further interpretation is one whose meaning is not immediately obvious, one that uses figurative language, metaphor, or indirect expression. A Sutta whose meaning is explicit is one that can be taken at face value.

**9** The wise student should rely on Suttas of explicit meaning as the standard for interpreting those of implicit meaning, and not the reverse.

**10** There are four reliances: one should rely on the teaching, not on the teacher; on the meaning, not on the words; on Suttas of definitive meaning, not on those requiring interpretation; and on direct knowledge, not on ordinary consciousness.

**11** By following these principles, one can navigate the vast ocean of the Buddha's teaching without becoming lost in the waves of conflicting interpretations.

**12** For the Dhamma is like a great ocean: it is deep, immeasurable, and hard to fathom. But by means of these instructions, the student may find the shore of understanding and reach the dry land of Nibbana.
