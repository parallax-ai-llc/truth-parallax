---
title: "Khuddaka Nikaya - Chapter 11: Niddesa (The Exposition)"
scripture: "khuddaka-nikaya"
chapter: 11
---

**1** In the Atthakavagga it is said: "One who has entered upon the path should not swerve to this side or that." What does "not swerve" mean? It means one should not incline toward sensual pleasures on the one hand, nor toward self-mortification on the other. One should follow the Middle Path.

**2** "He should not seize upon any of the phenomena of the world as his own." What phenomena? Material form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness. Contact, feeling, craving, clinging, becoming—none of these should be seized as "mine" or "myself."

**3** To say "mine" of anything conditioned is to bind oneself. To let go of "mine" regarding all conditioned things is to free oneself. The sage neither claims ownership of phenomena nor denies their existence, but sees them as they are: arising and passing, conditioned and impermanent.

**4** "A person of wisdom should not form an attachment to what is seen, heard, or sensed." The seen includes all visible forms perceived by the eye. The heard includes all sounds perceived by the ear. The sensed includes smells, tastes, and tangibles. The cognized includes all mental objects.

**5** In regard to each of these, the wise person does not form attachment, does not form aversion, does not form delusion. He perceives without grasping, without rejecting.

**6** "The brahman who has gone beyond boundaries." What boundaries? The boundary of the sense-realm, the boundary of the form-realm, the boundary of the formless realm. These are the three boundaries of existence. The sage has gone beyond all three.

**7** "He has nothing that he has grasped at by seeing or by knowing." This means: he does not make views into possessions. He does not cling to his own knowledge as superior to others'. He is without the conceit of "I know."

**8** "He is not passionate for passion, nor is he passionate for dispassion." This means: he does not desire the state of desire, but also he does not desire the state of being free from desire as a thing to be clung to. He has gone beyond both desire and the desire for its absence.

**9** This is the supreme peace: where desire is not clung to, and where the absence of desire is not clung to. Where views are not formed, and where the absence of views is not clung to.

**10** "For the one who has no inclinations, there exist no knots." Inclinations are of three kinds: the inclination toward sense-pleasure, the inclination toward existence, and the inclination toward ignorance. When these are absent, the knots of bondage do not form.

**11** "Those who cling to perceptions and views wander the world offending people." Those who say, "Only this is true, all else is false"—these persons quarrel with others. But the sage, being free from clinging, quarrels with no one.

**12** Such is the exposition of the Atthakavagga, the most profound section of the Sutta Nipata, which teaches the complete letting go of all views, all possessions, all clingings, all formations—the direct pointing to the unconditioned, to Nibbana itself.
