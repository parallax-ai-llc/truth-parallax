---
title: "Khuddaka Nikaya - Chapter 5: Sutta Nipata (The Sutta Collection) - Atthakavagga"
scripture: "khuddaka-nikaya"
chapter: 5
---

**1** One who has entered upon the path should not swerve to this side or that. He should not seize upon any of the phenomena of the world as his own, thinking, "This is mine."

**2** A person of wisdom should not form an attachment to what is seen, heard, or sensed. Why should a sage identify himself through other things?

**3** They do not construct, they do not prefer; they do not say "this is the highest purity." Letting go of whatever bond is tied, they do not form longings for anything in the world.

**4** The brahman who has gone beyond boundaries has nothing that he has grasped at by seeing or by knowing. He is not passionate for passion, nor is he passionate for dispassion. He has nothing here declared as "held supreme."

**5** For the one who has no inclinations, there exist no knots. For one who has let go of views, there are no bonds. But those who cling to perceptions and views wander the world offending people.

**6** Without annihilating, without grasping, without having found the path through the forest of views, the sage, having shaken off the dust, goes without clinging.

**7** The Rhinoceros Sutta: Renouncing violence toward all living beings, not harming even one of them, one should not wish for a son, let alone a companion. One should wander solitary as a rhinoceros horn.

**8** Affection arises for one who has companions. Suffering following upon affection arises. Seeing the peril that arises from affection, one should wander solitary as a rhinoceros horn.

**9** Sympathizing with friends, with companions, one misses one's goal, being shackled in mind. Seeing this danger in intimacy, one should wander solitary as a rhinoceros horn.

**10** Like a great elephant in the forest, leaving the herd, roaming the wilderness at will, one should wander solitary as a rhinoceros horn.

**11** The Metta Sutta extended: Let one be strenuous, upright, and sincere, without pride, easily contented, and joyous. Let one not be submerged by the things of the world. Let one not take upon oneself the burden of riches. Let one's senses be controlled.

**12** Let one cultivate an unbounded mind toward all beings, a mind of loving-kindness toward the whole world—above, below, and across—unobstructed, without hostility, without enmity. Standing, walking, sitting, or reclining, as long as one is without drowsiness, one should develop this mindfulness. This, they say, is divine abiding here.
