## Overview

Archaeological study of the Epic of Gilgamesh focuses on the cuneiform tablets on which the epic was inscribed, the sites where they were discovered, and the broader material culture of ancient Mesopotamia that illuminates the world depicted in the text.

## Key Archaeological Discoveries

### The Library of Ashurbanipal
The most complete version of the epic was discovered among the approximately 30,000 cuneiform tablets excavated from the library of the Assyrian king Ashurbanipal (668-627 BCE) at Nineveh. Hormuzd Rassam and Austen Henry Layard conducted the primary excavations in the mid-19th century.

### George Smith's Discovery (1872)
George Smith's identification and translation of the flood narrative (Tablet XI) at the British Museum caused a sensation, fundamentally altering understanding of the relationship between Mesopotamian and biblical traditions.

### Subsequent Discoveries
Fragments of the epic have been found at numerous sites across Mesopotamia, the Levant, and Anatolia, demonstrating its widespread circulation in the ancient world.

## Key Archaeological Sites

| Site | Discovery | Significance |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Nineveh | Standard Babylonian version | Most complete text |
| Ur | Old Babylonian fragments | Earlier version evidence |
| Megiddo | Fragment | Evidence of circulation in Canaan |
| Hattusa | Hittite translation | Evidence of cross-cultural transmission |
| Ugarit | Fragment | West Semitic context |
