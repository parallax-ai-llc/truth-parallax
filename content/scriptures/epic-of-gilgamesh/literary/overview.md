## Overview

Literary study of the Epic of Gilgamesh examines this ancient Mesopotamian composition as one of the foundational works of world literature. Scholars analyze its narrative structure, poetic techniques, characterization, thematic development, and enduring literary influence.

## Key Literary Approaches

### Narrative Structure
The Standard Babylonian version exhibits sophisticated narrative architecture. The epic employs ring composition (the opening and closing descriptions of Uruk's walls), episodic structure linked by thematic development, and a clear three-part division: the adventures of Gilgamesh and Enkidu, Enkidu's death and Gilgamesh's grief, and the quest for immortality.

### Poetic Technique
The epic employs characteristic features of Mesopotamian poetry:
- **Parallelism**: Repetition with variation of lines and passages
- **Formulaic language**: Stock phrases and repeated descriptions
- **Simile and metaphor**: Vivid comparisons drawn from nature and daily life
- **Dream sequences**: Dreams as narrative foreshadowing devices

### Characterization
The epic presents remarkably developed characters:
- **Gilgamesh**: A complex protagonist who evolves from tyrant to wise king
- **Enkidu**: The wild man who represents nature and becomes the embodiment of loyal friendship
- **Siduri**: The ale-wife whose advice anticipates Epicurean philosophy
- **Utnapishtim**: The flood survivor who embodies the impossibility of human immortality

## Literary Influence

| Work | Connection |
|------|-----------|
| Homer's Odyssey | Journey narrative, divine intervention, underworld visit |
| Hebrew Bible | Flood narrative, themes of mortality and wisdom |
| Beowulf | Monster-slaying hero confronting mortality |
| Modern literature | Rainer Maria Rilke, Philip Roth, and others draw on Gilgamesh |
