## Overview

Theological analysis of the Epic of Gilgamesh examines the text's profound engagement with questions of divine-human relations, mortality, the meaning of life, and the nature of the gods. While not a religious text in the liturgical sense, the epic offers one of antiquity's most searching theological reflections on the human condition.

## Key Theological Themes

### Human Mortality and Divine Immortality
The central theological tension of the epic is the unbridgeable gap between human mortality and divine immortality. The gods have reserved eternal life for themselves, and Gilgamesh's quest to transcend this boundary ultimately fails.

### The Nature of the Gods
The epic presents a complex theology in which the gods are powerful but not always just or benevolent. They create Enkidu to check Gilgamesh's tyranny but also decree Enkidu's death. The gods send the flood but then regret it.

### The Problem of Suffering
Enkidu's death raises profound theological questions about divine justice. His suffering and death, decreed by the gods as punishment for killing Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven, occasions Gilgamesh's crisis of meaning.

### The Meaning of a Good Life
The epic's resolution — Gilgamesh's return to Uruk and recognition of the enduring value of his city's walls — suggests a theology of immanent meaning: if eternal life is impossible, meaning is found in the lasting contributions one makes to human civilization.
