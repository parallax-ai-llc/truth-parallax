**Tablet X**

**1** The tavern-keeper Siduri, who lives by the seashore,
**2** she lives by the shore of the sea.
**3** The pot-stand was made for her, the golden fermenting vat was made for her.
**4** She is covered with a veil.

**5** Gilgamesh came wandering,
**6** wearing a skin, having the flesh of the gods in his body,
**7** but grief was in his belly.
**8** His face was like that of one who has been on a long journey.

**9** The tavern-keeper was gazing off into the distance,
**10** she was talking to herself and said:
**11** "Surely this is a slaughterer of wild bulls!
**12** Why would he make directly for my door?"

**13** When the tavern-keeper saw him, she barred her door,
**14** she barred her gate, she barred the bolt.
**15** But Gilgamesh, hearing the sounds,
**16** raised his chin, set his foot against her door:

**17** "Tavern-keeper, what did you see
**18** that you barred your door, barred your gate, barred the bolt?
**19** I will break down your door! I will smash your bolt!"

**20** The tavern-keeper said to Gilgamesh:
**21** "Why are your cheeks so emaciated, your expression so desolate?
**22** Why is your heart so wretched, your features so haggard?
**23** Why is there such sadness deep within you?
**24** Why do you look like one who has been on a long journey?

**25** Why is your face weathered by cold and heat?
**26** Why do you roam the wilderness?"

**27** Gilgamesh spoke to her, to the tavern-keeper:
**28** "Tavern-keeper, why should my cheeks not be emaciated,
**29** my expression not be desolate?
**30** Why should my heart not be wretched, my features not haggard?

**31** Why should there not be sadness deep within me?
**32** Why should I not look like one who has been on a long journey?
**33** Why should my face not be weathered by cold and heat?
**34** Why should I not roam the wilderness?

**35** My friend, my younger brother who chased the wild ass,
**36** the panther of the steppe—
**37** Enkidu, my friend, my younger brother, who chased the wild ass,
**38** the panther of the steppe—

**39** we who met and scaled the mountain,
**40** seized and slew the Bull of Heaven,
**41** overthrew Humbaba who dwelt in the Cedar Forest,
**42** killed lions in the mountain passes—

**43** my friend whom I loved so dearly,
**44** who went through every danger with me,
**45** Enkidu whom I loved so dearly,
**46** who went through every danger with me—

**47** the fate of mankind has overtaken him.
**48** Six days and seven nights I wept over him,
**49** and did not allow him to be buried,
**50** until a maggot fell out of his nose.

**51** I was terrified by his appearance.
**52** I began to fear death, and so roam the wilderness.
**53** The matter of Enkidu, my friend, oppresses me,
**54** so I roam back and forth over the steppe.

**55** The matter of Enkidu, my friend, oppresses me,
**56** so I have been roaming over long trails through the wilderness.
**57** How can I stay silent, how can I be still?
**58** My friend whom I love has turned to clay.

**59** Am I not like him? Will I not lie down too,
**60** never to get up again, for ever and ever?"

**61** Gilgamesh said to the tavern-keeper:
**62** "So now, tavern-keeper, which is the way to Utnapishtim?
**63** What is its landmark? Give me the landmark!
**64** If it is possible, I will cross the sea;
**65** if it is not possible, I will roam the wilderness!"

**66** The tavern-keeper said to Gilgamesh:
**67** "There has never been, Gilgamesh, any way across,
**68** and as long as the wind has blown over the sea,
**69** no one has crossed the sea.

**70** Only valiant Shamash crosses the sea;
**71** apart from Shamash, who can cross the sea?
**72** The crossing is difficult, the way is difficult,
**73** and in between are the Waters of Death, barring the way ahead.

**74** So where, Gilgamesh, would you cross the sea?
**75** When you reach the Waters of Death, what would you do?

**76** But Gilgamesh, there is Urshanabi, the boatman of Utnapishtim.
**77** He has the Stone Things with him in the forest, picking herbs.
**78** Go, let him see your face.
**79** If it is possible, cross with him;
**80** if it is not possible, retreat back the way you came."

**81** When Gilgamesh heard this,
**82** he took up the axe in his hand,
**83** drew the dirk from his belt,
**84** and went down into the forest.

**85** He fell upon the Stone Things like an arrow.
**86** He smashed them.
**87** [Gap in text]

**88** Urshanabi said to Gilgamesh:
**89** "Why are your cheeks so emaciated, your expression so desolate?
**90** Why is your heart so wretched, your features so haggard?
**91** Why is there such sadness deep within you?
**92** Why do you look like one who has been on a long journey?

**93** Why is your face weathered by cold and heat?
**94** Why do you roam the wilderness?"

**95** Gilgamesh spoke to Urshanabi the boatman:
**96** "Urshanabi, my friend whom I loved so dearly,
**97** who went through every danger with me,
**98** Enkidu—whom I loved so dearly—
**99** the fate of mankind has overtaken him.

**100** Six days and seven nights I wept over him,
**101** and did not allow him to be buried,
**102** until a maggot fell out of his nose.

**103** I was terrified, I began to fear death,
**104** and so roam the wilderness.
**105** The matter of my friend oppresses me,
**106** so I roam the steppe.

**107** How can I stay silent, how can I be still?
**108** My friend whom I love has turned to clay.
**109** Am I not like him? Will I not lie down too,
**110** never to get up, for ever and ever?"

**111** Gilgamesh said to Urshanabi:
**112** "Now, Urshanabi, which is the way to Utnapishtim?
**113** What is its landmark? Give me the landmark!
**114** If it is possible, I will cross the sea;
**115** if it is not possible, I will roam the wilderness!"

**116** Urshanabi said to Gilgamesh:
**117** "It is your hands, Gilgamesh, that hinder the crossing!
**118** You have smashed the Stone Things, you have destroyed what carried me.

**119** Take the axe in your hand, go down to the forest,
**120** cut three hundred punting poles, each sixty cubits long.
**121** Trim them and fit them with knobs,
**122** then bring them to me!"

**123** When Gilgamesh heard this,
**124** he took the axe in his hand, drew the dirk from his belt,
**125** went down into the forest, and cut three hundred punting poles
**126** each sixty cubits in length.
**127** He trimmed them and fitted them with knobs,
**128** and brought them to Urshanabi.

**129** Gilgamesh and Urshanabi embarked on the boat,
**130** they launched the boat on the waves and sailed away.

**131** A month and a half's journey they had sailed
**132** in three days' time. Thus Urshanabi reached
**133** the Waters of Death.

**134** Urshanabi said to Gilgamesh:
**135** "Quick, Gilgamesh, take a pole,
**136** but your hand must not touch the Waters of Death!

**137** A second, a third, and a fourth pole take, Gilgamesh.
**138** A fifth, a sixth, and a seventh pole take, Gilgamesh.
**139** An eighth, a ninth, and a tenth pole take, Gilgamesh.
**140** An eleventh, a twelfth pole take, Gilgamesh."

**141** With one hundred and twenty pushes Gilgamesh had used up the poles.
**142** He stripped himself; he held up his arms for a mast,
**143** he held up his garment for a sail.

**144** Utnapishtim was looking off into the distance.
**145** He was talking to himself, saying words to himself:
**146** "Why have the Stone Things of the boat been destroyed,
**147** and one who is not my boatman rides the boat?

**148** This one who comes—he is not a man of mine.
**149** I look, but he is not my man.
**150** I look, but he is not—
**151** [Gap in text]"

**152** Utnapishtim said to Gilgamesh:
**153** "Why are your cheeks so emaciated, your expression so desolate?
**154** Why is your heart so wretched, your features so haggard?
**155** Why is there such sadness deep within you?
**156** Why do you look like one who has been on a long journey?

**157** Why is your face weathered by cold and heat?
**158** Why do you roam the wilderness?"

**159** Gilgamesh spoke to him, to Utnapishtim:
**160** "Utnapishtim, should not my cheeks be emaciated,
**161** my expression desolate?
**162** Should not my heart be wretched, my features haggard?

**163** My friend, my younger brother—
**164** who chased the wild ass, the panther of the steppe—
**165** Enkidu whom I loved so dearly,
**166** who went through every danger with me—
**167** the fate of mankind has overtaken him.

**168** Six days and seven nights I wept over him,
**169** until a maggot fell out of his nose.
**170** I was terrified, I began to fear death.

**171** How can I stay silent, how can I be still?
**172** My friend whom I love has turned to clay.
**173** Am I not like him? Will I not lie down too,
**174** never to get up, for ever and ever?"

**175** Gilgamesh said to Utnapishtim:
**176** "So it is to find you that I have come on this journey!
**177** And you lie here, taking your ease upon your back!
**178** Tell me, how was it that you found life among the gods?"
