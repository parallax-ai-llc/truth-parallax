**Tablet XII**

[Note: Tablet XII is an Akkadian translation of the latter part of the Sumerian poem "Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Netherworld." It is considered a later addition to the epic, not fully integrated with the preceding narrative. In this tablet, Enkidu is alive, contradicting the earlier story.]

**1** In those days, in those far-off days,
**2** in those nights, in those far-off nights,
**3** in those years, in those far-off years—

**4** after heaven had been moved away from earth,
**5** after earth had been separated from heaven,
**6** after the name of man had been fixed—

**7** after An had carried off heaven,
**8** after Enlil had carried off earth,
**9** after Ereshkigal had been carried off into the Netherworld as its prize—

**10** he set sail, he set sail,
**11** the father set sail for the Netherworld.
**12** Against the king the small ones were hurled,
**13** against Enki the large ones were hurled.

**14** The small stones of the hand,
**15** the large stones of the—dancing reeds—
**16** the keel of Enki's boat
**17** was struck by the waves in battle.

**18** Against the king, the water at the head of the boat
**19** devoured like a wolf;
**20** against Enki, the water at the stern of the boat
**21** struck down like a lion.

**22** At that time, a tree, a single tree, a huluppu-tree,
**23** had been planted by the bank of the Euphrates,
**24** had been watered by the Euphrates.
**25** The violence of the South Wind plucked up its roots,
**26** tore away its crown,
**27** the Euphrates carried it off on its waters.

**28** The woman, roving about in fear at the word of An,
**29** roving about in fear at the word of Enlil,
**30** took the tree in her hand, brought it to Uruk:
**31** "I shall bring it to pure Inanna's garden."

**32** The woman tended the tree with her hand, placed it by her foot.
**33** Inanna tended the tree with her hand, placed it by her foot.
**34** "When will it be a throne for me to sit on?" she said.
**35** "When will it be a bed for me to lie on?" she said.

**36** Five years, ten years had gone by,
**37** the tree had grown stout, but its bark had not split.
**38** At its roots, a snake who could not be charmed
**39** had made its nest.
**40** In its crown, the Anzu-bird was raising its young.
**41** In its trunk, the phantom maid had built her dwelling.

**42** The maid who laughs with a joyful heart—
**43** but holy Inanna—how she wept!
**44** As the dawn began to brighten into day,
**45** as birds began to sing at the coming of dawn,

**46** her brother, the warrior Utu, his sister
**47** holy Inanna addressed:

**48** "My brother, in those days—
**49** the tree I planted by the Euphrates bank,
**50** the Euphrates watered it.
**51** A snake who could not be charmed made its nest in its roots.
**52** The Anzu-bird was raising its young in its crown.
**53** The phantom maid built her dwelling in its trunk."

**54** As she wept—her brother would not help her in the matter.
**55** When the dawn began to brighten into day,
**56** as birds began to sing at dawn,

**57** her brother the hero Gilgamesh, his sister
**58** holy Inanna addressed:
**59** [Repeats the same plea]

**60** Her brother the hero Gilgamesh assisted her in this matter.
**61** He put on his armour, weighing fifty minas, about his waist.
**62** Fifty minas were handled by him like thirty shekels.

**63** With his bronze axe for expeditions,
**64** weighing seven talents and seven minas, he entered her garden.
**65** He struck the snake who could not be charmed at its root.
**66** The Anzu-bird took its young and went into the mountains.
**67** The phantom maid tore down her dwelling
**68** and fled to the wilderness.

**69** As for the tree, he plucked it at its roots and tore off its crown.
**70** The sons of his city, who accompanied him, cut off its branches.
**71** He gave it to holy Inanna for her throne.
**72** He gave it to her for her bed.

**73** As for himself, from its roots he fashioned his pukku,
**74** from its crown he fashioned his mekku.

**75** He began to play the pukku in the broad square.
**76** He began to praise the pukku in the broad square.
**77** The young men of his city were called forth by the pukku.

**78** "O my pukku, O my mekku!"
**79** The pukku, which has no equal, his pukku—
**80** [Gap in text]

**81** The moaning of the young women—
**82** "My pukku, my mekku, let it be my pukku!"

**83** In the place where the pukku had been since yesterday—
**84** there his pukku fell down into the Netherworld.
**85** His mekku fell down into the Netherworld.
**86** He reached with his hand, but could not reach it.
**87** He reached with his foot, but could not reach it.

**88** He sat down at the gate of the Netherworld,
**89** weeping and lamenting:
**90** "O my pukku! O my mekku!
**91** My pukku, whose song had no equal!
**92** My mekku, whose dance had no equal!"

**93** Enkidu said to him, to his lord:
**94** "My lord, why do you weep? Why is your heart sick?
**95** I will bring up your pukku from the Netherworld.
**96** I will bring up your mekku from the Netherworld."

**97** Gilgamesh said to Enkidu:
**98** "If you will go down to the Netherworld,
**99** let me give you instructions, pay attention to my instructions!

**100** Do not put on clean garments,
**101** they will mark you as a stranger.
**102** Do not be anointed with good oil from the flask,
**103** at its scent they will gather about you.

**104** Do not hurl the throw-stick in the Netherworld,
**105** those struck by the throw-stick will surround you.
**106** Do not carry a staff in your hand,
**107** the spirits will flutter about you.

**108** Do not put sandals on your feet.
**109** Do not shout in the Netherworld.
**110** Do not kiss your wife whom you love,
**111** do not strike your wife whom you hate.

**112** Do not kiss your child whom you love,
**113** do not strike your child whom you hate.
**114** The lament of the Netherworld will seize you."

**115** Enkidu did not heed the instructions of his lord.
**116** He put on clean garments—they marked him as a stranger.
**117** He was anointed with good oil from the flask—
**118** at its scent they gathered about him.

**119** He hurled the throw-stick in the Netherworld—
**120** those struck by the throw-stick surrounded him.
**121** He carried a staff in his hand—
**122** the spirits fluttered about him.

**123** He put sandals on his feet.
**124** He made a noise in the Netherworld.
**125** He kissed his wife whom he loved.
**126** He struck his wife whom he hated.

**127** He kissed his child whom he loved.
**128** He struck his child whom he hated.
**129** The lament of the Netherworld seized him.

**130** The Netherworld held him fast.
**131** It is not disease that held him,
**132** it is not a demon that held him—
**133** the Netherworld held him.
**134** It is not the battle-mace of Nergal, the merciless, that struck him—
**135** the Netherworld held him.

**136** On the field of battle of men he did not fall—
**137** the Netherworld held him.

**138** Then Gilgamesh, the son of Ninsun,
**139** went to Enlil's temple alone, weeping:
**140** "Father Enlil, my pukku fell into the Netherworld,
**141** my mekku fell into the Netherworld.
**142** Enkidu, whom I sent to retrieve them—
**143** the Netherworld seized him."
**144** Father Enlil would not help him in this matter.

**145** He went to Sin's temple, weeping:
**146** "Father Sin, my pukku fell into the Netherworld,
**147** my mekku fell into the Netherworld.
**148** Enkidu, whom I sent to retrieve them—
**149** the Netherworld seized him."
**150** Father Sin would not help him in this matter.

**151** He went to Ea's temple, weeping:
**152** "Father Ea, my pukku fell into the Netherworld,
**153** my mekku fell into the Netherworld.
**154** Enkidu, whom I sent to retrieve them—
**155** the Netherworld seized him."

**156** Father Ea helped him in this matter.
**157** He spoke to Nergal, the valiant hero:
**158** "O valiant hero, Nergal,
**159** open a hole in the Netherworld at once,
**160** so that the spirit of Enkidu may leave the Netherworld
**161** and speak to his brother."

**162** Nergal, the valiant hero,
**163** opened a hole in the Netherworld.
**164** The spirit of Enkidu, like a gust of wind,
**165** came forth from the Netherworld.

**166** They embraced and kissed each other.
**167** They discussed, they agonized:

**168** "Tell me, my friend, tell me, my friend,
**169** tell me the order of the Netherworld which you have seen."

**170** "I shall not tell you, my friend, I shall not tell you!
**171** But if I tell you the order of the Netherworld which I have seen,
**172** sit down and weep."

**173** "Let me sit down and weep."

**174** "My body, which you could touch and in which you rejoiced—
**175** it is infested with lice like an old garment.
**176** My body, which you could touch and in which you rejoiced—
**177** it is filled with dust."

**178** He cried "Alas!" and threw himself in the dust.

**179** "Did you see him who had one son?"
**180** "I saw him. He lies at the foot of the wall and weeps bitterly."
**181** "Did you see him who had two sons?"
**182** "I saw him. He sits on two bricks and eats bread."

**183** "Did you see him who had three sons?"
**184** "I saw him. He drinks water from a waterskin hung from the saddle."
**185** "Did you see him who had four sons?"
**186** "I saw him. His heart rejoices like a man who has four donkeys to yoke."

**187** "Did you see him who had five sons?"
**188** "I saw him. Like a good scribe, his arm is open; he enters the palace with ease."
**189** "Did you see him who had six sons?"
**190** "I saw him. His heart rejoices like a ploughman."

**191** "Did you see him who had seven sons?"
**192** "I saw him. As a companion of the gods, he sits on a throne and listens to judgements."

**193** "Did you see him who had no heir?"
**194** "I saw him. Like a defective brick, he eats bread that has no savour."

**195** "Did you see the man who fell down from the roof?"
**196** "I saw him. They cannot repair his bones."

**197** "Did you see him whose spirit has no one to tend it?"
**198** "I saw him. He eats the scraps and the crumbs tossed out in the street."
