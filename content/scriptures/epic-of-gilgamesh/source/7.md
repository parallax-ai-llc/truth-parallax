**Tablet VII**

**1** Enkidu spoke to Gilgamesh, saying:
**2** "My friend, listen to the dream I had last night.
**3** Anu, Enlil, Ea, and celestial Shamash were in council.
**4** And Anu said to Enlil:

**5** 'Because they have killed the Bull of Heaven, and have killed Humbaba,
**6** the one of them who pulled up the Cedar of the Mountain must die!'
**7** Enlil said: 'Enkidu must die; Gilgamesh must not die!'

**8** But celestial Shamash addressed valiant Enlil:
**9** 'Was it not at my command that they killed the Bull of Heaven and Humbaba?
**10** Should now innocent Enkidu die?'
**11** Enlil was angry at Shamash:
**12** 'It is you who are responsible
**13** because you travelled with them daily like one of their own!'"

**14** Enkidu lay down before Gilgamesh,
**15** his tears streaming down like canals:
**16** "O my brother, so dear to me, my brother!
**17** They will take me from my brother."

**18** "I must sit on the threshold of the dead
**19** and never again see my dear brother with my eyes."

**20** [Gap in text]

**21** Enkidu looked up and spoke to the door:
**22** "O door of the forest, unconscious, not alive—
**23** if only you had understanding!
**24** I searched for you through twenty leagues of forest,
**25** until I found the tallest cedar.

**26** Your timber is unrivalled in the land.
**27** Seventy-two cubits your height, twenty-four cubits your breadth,
**28** one cubit your thickness; your pivot, ferrule, and doorframe
**29** are of one piece.

**30** I fashioned you, I carried you to Nippur.
**31** Had I known, O door, that this would be the outcome,
**32** and that this your beauty would lead to this,
**33** I would have raised the axe and chopped you down,
**34** would have floated you as a raft to the temple of Shamash!"

**35** [Gap in text]

**36** Enkidu spoke before Gilgamesh:
**37** "My friend, the great goddess cursed me:
**38** I shall not die like one who falls in the midst of battle.
**39** I feared the battle, but I will die on a bed of shame.
**40** My friend, he who falls in battle is blessed,
**41** but I—I shall die in my bed of shame."

**42** Gilgamesh listened to the words of his friend.
**43** His tears streamed down.

**44** Gilgamesh opened his mouth and spoke, saying to Enkidu:
**45** "You who were so reasonable, now you speak strangely.
**46** Why, my friend, does your heart speak strangely?
**47** The dream was very precious, but the terror was great;
**48** we must treasure the dream whatever the terror,

**49** for the dream has shown that misery comes at last
**50** to the healthy man, the end of life is sorrow."

**51** Enkidu raised up his curse and cursed the trapper:
**52** "O trapper, trapper, who let me not attain
**53** as much as my friend—
**54** may the trapper not attain as much as his friend!

**55** Make his profit empty, diminish his gain!
**56** May his income, his portion, dwindle before you!
**57** May the paths leading to his traps be lost!"

**58** Enkidu raised up his curse and cursed the harlot Shamhat:
**59** "Come, Shamhat, I will decree your fate,
**60** a fate that will never end for all eternity!
**61** I will curse you with a mighty curse!

**62** May sunrise and sunset take your beauty!
**63** May your lover despise your favours!
**64** [Gap in text]

**65** May the potter's terrace be your dwelling,
**66** may the junction of roads be your place of business.
**67** May the wasteland be your home,
**68** may the shadow of the city wall be your standing place.

**69** May thorn and bramble skin your feet,
**70** may drunkard and sober alike slap your cheek!
**71** [Gap in text]

**72** Because you made me weak, who was undefiled!"

**73** When Shamash heard what his mouth had uttered,
**74** he called out to him from the sky:
**75** "Enkidu, why do you curse the harlot Shamhat?

**76** She who fed you bread fit for a god,
**77** she who gave you wine fit for a king,
**78** she who clothed you in noble garments,
**79** and gave you splendid Gilgamesh as a companion?

**80** And now Gilgamesh, your friend and brother,
**81** will lay you out on a great bed.
**82** He will lay you out on a bed of honour,
**83** will place you on his left on the seat of ease.

**84** The rulers of the earth will kiss your feet.
**85** He will have the people of Uruk mourn and lament for you,
**86** joyful people he will fill with woe for you.
**87** And when you are gone
**88** he will roam the wilderness with matted hair in the skin of a lion."

**89** When Enkidu heard the words of valiant Shamash,
**90** his angry heart grew calm.
**91** [Gap in text]

**92** He retracted his curse and replaced it with a blessing:
**93** "May your lovers give you gifts of gold and jewels.
**94** May governors and nobles love you.
**95** May the man at one league away slap his thigh in excitement.
**96** May the man at two leagues away let down his hair."

**97** [Gap in text]

**98** Enkidu was lying in his bed of sickness.
**99** A dream came to him.
**100** He told his dream to Gilgamesh:
**101** "My friend, I had a dream:

**102** The sky roared, the earth rumbled,
**103** I was standing between them.
**104** There appeared a man of dark visage—
**105** his face resembled the Anzu bird.

**106** His hands were the paws of a lion,
**107** his nails the talons of an eagle!
**108** He seized me by my hair and overpowered me.
**109** I struck him, but he skipped about like a rope.

**110** Then he struck me and capsized me like a raft,
**111** and trampled on me like a wild bull.
**112** He squeezed my whole body like a vice.
**113** 'Help me, my friend!' But you did not rescue me, you were afraid and did not—"

**114** "Then he turned me into a dove,
**115** my arms were feathered like a bird.
**116** He seized me, led me down to the House of Darkness,
**117** the dwelling of Irkalla,

**118** to the house which none leave who have entered it,
**119** on the road from which there is no way back,
**120** to the house where those who dwell do without light,

**121** where dust is their drink, their food is of clay,
**122** where, like a bird, they wear garments of feathers,
**123** and light cannot be seen, they dwell in the dark,

**124** and upon the door and bolt, dust has gathered.
**125** On entering the House of Dust,
**126** I looked and saw the crowns in a heap,
**127** I saw those who had worn the crowns, who from days of old had ruled the land,

**128** who had set out cooked meats before Anu and Enlil,
**129** who had set out baked bread, who had set out cold water from the waterskins.
**130** On entering the House of Dust,
**131** high priests and acolytes were there,

**132** ritual priests and ecstatics were there,
**133** the anointers of the great gods were there.
**134** Etana was there, and Shakkan,
**135** the queen of the Netherworld, Ereshkigal, was there.

**136** Belet-seri, the scribe of the Netherworld, knelt before her.
**137** She was holding a tablet and reading aloud to her.
**138** She raised her head and saw me:
**139** 'Who has brought this one here?'"

**140** [Gap in text]

**141** Through the first day, the second day, Enkidu lay in his bed;
**142** through the third day, the fourth day, Enkidu lay in his bed;
**143** through the fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth day,
**144** Enkidu's illness grew ever worse.

**145** The eleventh and twelfth day his illness grew ever worse.
**146** Enkidu drew up from his bed.
**147** He called out to Gilgamesh—his words a lament:

**148** "My friend hates me—
**149** the friend with whom I ranged the wilderness:
**150** Enkidu, the friend whom I loved so dearly,
**151** who went through every danger with me—
**152** the fate of mankind has overtaken him.

**153** Six days and seven nights I wept over him,
**154** and did not allow him to be buried,
**155** until a maggot fell out of his nose.

**156** I was terrified by his appearance,
**157** I began to fear death, and so roam the wilderness."

**158** [Note: Lines 148-157 anticipate Gilgamesh's own later words from Tablet X; the tablet is fragmentary here.]
