## Overview

The Epic of Gilgamesh is the oldest surviving work of great literature, originating in ancient Mesopotamia. The earliest Sumerian poems about Gilgamesh date to the Third Dynasty of Ur (approximately 2100 BCE), while the most complete version — the Standard Babylonian edition attributed to the scribe Sin-leqi-unninni — dates to approximately 1200 BCE. The epic was discovered in 1853 among the ruins of the library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh.

The narrative follows Gilgamesh, king of Uruk, through a transformative journey from tyrannical ruler to wise king. His friendship with the wild man Enkidu, their heroic adventures, Enkidu's death, and Gilgamesh's subsequent quest for immortality constitute one of humanity's most profound meditations on friendship, mortality, and the meaning of a well-lived life.

## Structure

The Standard Babylonian version consists of twelve cuneiform tablets:

1. **Tablets I-IV**: Introduction of Gilgamesh and Enkidu, their friendship, and the journey to the Cedar Forest
2. **Tablets V-VIII**: The battle with Humbaba, the Bull of Heaven, and the death of Enkidu
3. **Tablets IX-XI**: Gilgamesh's quest for immortality, the flood narrative, and the return to Uruk
4. **Tablet XII**: An appendix containing the Sumerian poem "Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Netherworld" 

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Epic of Gilgamesh holds significance across multiple disciplines:

- **Academic**: The foundational text of Assyriology and ancient Near Eastern literary studies
- **Comparative**: Contains the earliest known flood narrative, parallel to Genesis and other traditions
- **Theological**: Profound exploration of human mortality and the divine-human relationship
- **Literary**: One of the world's first great literary works, influencing narrative traditions globally
- **Archaeological**: Its discovery revolutionized understanding of the ancient Near East
- **Interfaith**: Universal themes of friendship, loss, and the search for meaning

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mortality | The inescapability of death and the quest for eternal life |
| Friendship | The transformative bond between Gilgamesh and Enkidu |
| Heroism | The nature of true heroism and its relationship to wisdom |
| Civilization vs. Nature | The tension between the wild and the civilized |
| Divine-Human Relations | The complex interactions between gods and mortals |
