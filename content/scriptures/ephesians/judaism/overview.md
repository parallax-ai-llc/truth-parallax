## Overview

Jewish engagement with Ephesians focuses on the letter's remarkable claim that in Christ, God has broken down "the dividing wall of hostility" between Jew and Gentile (2:14), creating "one new humanity" (2:15). This passage is central to Jewish-Christian dialogue, raising questions about supersessionism, the ongoing validity of Torah, and the nature of Israel's covenant.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### The Dividing Wall
The "wall of partition" (2:14) is often identified with the balustrade in the Jerusalem Temple separating the Court of the Gentiles from the inner courts. Jewish scholars note that this barrier reflected the Torah's distinction between Israel and the nations — a distinction Ephesians claims Christ has abolished.

### The Abolition of the Law
Ephesians 2:15 states that Christ has "abolished the law of commandments expressed in ordinances." Jewish interpreters critique this as supersessionist: it implies that the Torah — given by God to Israel — has been rendered obsolete by a new dispensation.

### One New Humanity
The vision of "one new humanity" (2:15) raises the question of whether Jewish particularity is preserved or erased. Jewish scholars ask whether this "unity" comes at the cost of Jewish identity and covenant faithfulness.

## Scholarly Engagement

Modern Jewish scholars engage Ephesians as evidence of early Christianity's evolving relationship with Judaism, noting that the letter's irenic tone differs from the sharper polemic of Galatians.
