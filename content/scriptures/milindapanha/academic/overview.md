## Overview

Academic study of the Milindapanha spans multiple disciplines, including Buddhist studies, classical studies, philosophy, and the history of cross-cultural intellectual exchange. The text is a primary source for understanding the encounter between Hellenistic Greek and Indian Buddhist philosophical traditions — an encounter that scholars such as Christopher Beckwith and Thomas McEvilley have argued was far more extensive and mutually influential than previously recognized.

Key academic questions include the text's date and compositional history (with scholarly estimates ranging from the first century BCE to the second century CE), the historical identification of King Milinda with Menander I, and the extent to which Nagasena's philosophical arguments reflect genuine engagement with Greek logical and philosophical methods. The text's treatment of personal identity has attracted particular attention from analytic philosophers, who find in the chariot analogy a sophisticated reductionist argument that anticipates contemporary debates about the nature of persons, consciousness, and identity over time.
