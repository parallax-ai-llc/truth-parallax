Book 5 of the Milindapanha addresses the problem of inference — the role of reason, evidence, and inference in Buddhist epistemology, which the Tibetan tradition engages with through its own rich dialectical heritage. Tibetan monastic education places great emphasis on philosophical debate (*rtsod pa*), and the Milindapanha's dialogue format provides an early model of the kind of rigorous, adversarial philosophical exchange that characterizes Tibetan Buddhist scholarship.

The Tibetan engagement with the examination of how one can know things not directly perceived, including past lives, karma, and nibbana situates Nagasena's arguments within the broader framework of the *grub mtha'* (tenets systems) — the hierarchical presentation of Buddhist philosophical schools. Nagasena's positions are typically classified as representing the Sautrantika or Vaibhashika perspective, providing a foundation that is then refined and transcended by the Madhyamaka and Yogacara analyses. The Milindapanha thus serves as an introduction to philosophical reasoning in the Tibetan curriculum, demonstrating the power of clear analysis to dispel confusion about basic Buddhist teachings.
