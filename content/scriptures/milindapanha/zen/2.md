Book 2 of the Milindapanha addresses the distinguishing marks of qualities — the foundational dialogue on the nature of the self, mind, and basic Buddhist concepts. Zen Buddhism finds in this section a model of the direct, dialogical mode of teaching that would become the hallmark of the Zen tradition. The sharp exchange between Milinda and Nagasena — with its unexpected turns, provocative challenges, and moments of sudden insight — anticipates the *mondo* (question-and-answer) encounters between Zen masters and students.

The Zen perspective on Nagasena's famous chariot analogy and the analysis of personal identity emphasizes the performative dimension of Nagasena's teaching. His arguments do not merely convey information but enact a transformation of understanding — they disrupt the questioner's habitual conceptual frameworks and create an opening for direct insight. This pedagogical strategy resonates deeply with the Zen emphasis on *satori* (sudden awakening) and the use of *koans* (paradoxical cases) to catalyze breakthrough beyond conceptual thought.
