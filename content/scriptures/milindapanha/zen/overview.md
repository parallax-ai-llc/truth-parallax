## Overview

Zen Buddhism finds in the Milindapanha an early exemplar of the dialogical, often paradoxical mode of teaching that would become the hallmark of the Zen tradition. The exchange between Milinda and Nagasena — with its sharp questions, unexpected responses, and sudden shifts of perspective — anticipates the *mondo* (question-and-answer) encounters between Zen masters and students, and the text's famous puzzles resonate with the *koan* tradition.

The Zen perspective especially appreciates Nagasena's method of disrupting King Milinda's conceptual assumptions rather than simply replacing them with new concepts. When Nagasena declares "I am known as Nagasena, but Nagasena is merely a conventional name — no permanent self is to be found therein," he performs a characteristically Zen gesture: using language to point beyond language, employing concepts to reveal the limits of conceptual thinking. The Milindapanha thus represents a proto-Zen moment in Buddhist history — a demonstration that direct insight into the nature of reality cannot be captured in any philosophical system.
