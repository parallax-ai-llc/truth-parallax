---
title: "Milindapanha - Chapter 1"
scripture: "milindapanha"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Thus have I heard. The city of Sagala is a delightful place, a city of commerce and trade, set in a pleasant land. There are parks and gardens and groves, tanks and lakes and pools, rivers and mountains, and places of pilgrimage.

**2** In Sagala there was a king named Milinda, learned, eloquent, wise, and able. He was a keen debater, and no one in all India could refute him in argument.

**3** One day King Milinda said to his ministers: "India is empty of learned men! There is no sage, no Brahman, no wanderer, no teacher who is able to discuss with me and dispel my doubts."

**4** Now at that time the venerable Nagasena was dwelling at the Sankheyya hermitage, and he was learned and competent, wise and sagacious, and skilled in exposition.

**5** The Elder Nagasena, accompanied by a company of monks, set out for Sagala. And King Milinda, hearing of his arrival, went to meet him.

**6** The king, having approached Nagasena, greeted him courteously and sat down at a respectful distance. And the king asked: "How is the Venerable One known? What is your name, reverend sir?"

**7** "I am known as Nagasena, O king; but this is only a designation, a name, a concept, a convention. There is no permanent individuality involved."

**8** The king said: "If there is no permanent individuality, then who is it that practices righteousness? Who is it that meditates? Who is it that attains Nibbana? Then there is no merit, no demerit, no doer of good or evil."

**9** "If, venerable Nagasena, your parents did not give you the name Nagasena, and your teachers did not give you the name, and your friends and companions did not give you the name — then who is this Nagasena?"

**10** Nagasena replied: "O king, how did you come here? Did you come on foot, or in a chariot?" The king said: "I came in a chariot, venerable sir."

**11** "If you came in a chariot, explain to me what the chariot is. Is the pole the chariot?" "No, venerable sir." "Is the axle the chariot?" "No." "Are the wheels the chariot?" "No." "Is the body of the chariot the chariot?" "No."

**12** "Is the yoke the chariot? Is the reins? Is it all these parts combined? Or is it something apart from these parts?" To all these questions the king answered "No."

**13** Then Nagasena said: "Then, O king, I can discover no chariot! The chariot is a mere empty sound. What then is the chariot? Your Majesty has spoken a falsehood!"

**14** The king replied: "I have spoken no falsehood! It is on account of its having all these parts — the pole, the axle, the wheels, the body, the reins — that it goes under the name 'chariot.'"

**15** "Very good, O king! You understand the meaning of 'chariot.' In exactly the same way, it is on account of all the constituent parts — form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness — that the word 'Nagasena' is used."

**16** "But in the ultimate sense, there is no person to be found herein. As the Sister Vajira said in the presence of the Blessed One: 'Just as the word chariot is used when the parts are rightly combined, so the word being is used when the aggregates are present.'"

**17** The king said: "Wonderful, Nagasena! Most wonderful! Were the Buddha himself here, he would approve your answer. Well spoken, well spoken, Nagasena!"
