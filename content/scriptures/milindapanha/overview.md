## Overview

The Milindapanha ("Questions of King Milinda") is a Buddhist text recording a dialogue between the Indo-Greek king Menander I (Milinda in Pali, reigned c. 155-130 BCE) and the Buddhist monk Nagasena. Composed in Pali, the text is one of the most celebrated and accessible works of Buddhist philosophical literature, presenting core Buddhist doctrines through lively debate, memorable analogies, and penetrating philosophical argument. While not included in the Pali canon proper, the Milindapanha is highly regarded in the Theravada tradition and has been influential across all Buddhist schools.

The dialogue format allows the text to address fundamental Buddhist concepts — non-self (*anatta*), dependent origination (*paticcasamuppada*), karma, rebirth, and nibbana — through the give-and-take of genuine philosophical inquiry. King Milinda, trained in Greek logic and rhetoric, poses sharp, skeptical questions that Nagasena answers with characteristic brilliance, often employing vivid similes and thought experiments that have become classics of world philosophy.

## Structure

The seven books of the Milindapanha are organized as follows:

| Book | Title | Content |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Preliminary | Background narrative — the previous lives and meeting of Milinda and Nagasena |
| 2 | Distinguishing Marks of Qualities | The initial dialogue on the nature of the self, mind, and Buddhist practice |
| 3 | The Cutting Off of Puzzles | Resolution of philosophical dilemmas (*dhammantagaha*) about Buddhist doctrine |
| 4 | The Solving of Dilemmas | Further resolution of apparent contradictions in the Buddha's teaching |
| 5 | The Problem of Inference | The role of reason, evidence, and inference in Buddhist epistemology |
| 6 | The Similes | Extended similes illustrating the qualities of the ideal Buddhist practitioner |
| 7 | The Ascetic Practices | The thirteen *dhutanga* practices and the virtues of ascetic discipline |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Theravada**: One of the most important para-canonical texts, widely studied and frequently cited
- **Mahayana**: Valued for its philosophical arguments, especially on non-self and dependent origination
- **Zen**: The dialogue format and use of paradox resonate with the koan tradition
- **Tibetan**: Studied as an example of Buddhist dialectical method and philosophical argument
- **Academic**: A key text for the study of Buddhist philosophy, Hellenistic-Buddhist cultural exchange, and Pali literature
- **Comparative**: Offers unique material for comparison between Buddhist and Greek philosophical traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Non-self (*Anatta*) | The chariot analogy and the analysis of personal identity as a conventional designation |
| Rebirth | The continuity of the mental stream across lives without a transmigrating soul |
| Dependent Origination | The interconnected arising of all phenomena based on conditions |
| Nibbana | The nature of liberation — its reality, its characteristics, and its attainability |
| Buddhist Epistemology | The role of reason, inference, and experience in knowing the truth |
| Dialogue | The philosophical dialogue as a method of inquiry and teaching |
