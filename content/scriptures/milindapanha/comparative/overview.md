## Overview

The Milindapanha is uniquely positioned for comparative philosophical analysis, as it literally stages an encounter between two great philosophical civilizations — the Hellenistic Greek and the Indian Buddhist. The dialogue format itself invites comparison with the Platonic dialogues, and scholars have noted structural and thematic parallels between Nagasena's dialectical method and the Socratic *elenchus* (cross-examination). Both traditions use dialogue to expose the inadequacy of conventional assumptions and guide the interlocutor toward deeper understanding.

Comparative analysis of the Milindapanha reveals fascinating convergences and divergences between Greek and Buddhist approaches to fundamental philosophical questions. On personal identity, Nagasena's chariot analogy parallels Hume's bundle theory and anticipates Parfit's reductionism, while differing from the Greek philosophical tradition's general commitment to the reality of the soul (*psyche*). On epistemology, the text's treatment of inference and evidence resonates with both Aristotelian logic and the Indian *pramana* tradition. On ethics, the dialogue's emphasis on intention, character, and the transformative potential of philosophical inquiry connects Buddhist and Greek virtue ethics.
