Book 7 of the Milindapanha addresses the ascetic practices — the thirteen *dhutanga* practices and the discipline of renunciation, offering uniquely rich material for comparative philosophical analysis. The dialogue literally stages an encounter between Greek and Indian philosophical traditions, and the examination of austere practices such as wearing only robes from refuse heaps, eating only one meal a day, and dwelling in the forest can be examined in relation to both Buddhist and Hellenistic philosophical frameworks. The text provides rare evidence of actual cross-cultural philosophical exchange in the ancient world.

Comparative analysis of this section reveals fascinating convergences and divergences between Buddhist and Western approaches to fundamental questions. The dialogue format itself invites comparison with Plato's dialogues, and scholars have noted both structural parallels (the use of the *elenchus* or cross-examination) and significant differences (Nagasena's more cooperative, less adversarial dialectical style). The philosophical content — dealing with questions of personal identity, causation, knowledge, and the nature of reality — connects to live debates in contemporary Western philosophy.
