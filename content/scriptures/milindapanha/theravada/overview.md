## Overview

The Milindapanha holds a position of unique authority within the Theravada tradition. While not formally included in the Tipitaka (the Pali canon), it is classified among the para-canonical texts and is treated with near-canonical reverence, particularly in Myanmar (Burma), where it is included in the traditional collection. Buddhaghosa and subsequent Theravada commentators cite the Milindapanha frequently, treating Nagasena's arguments as authoritative expositions of Buddhist doctrine.

The Theravada tradition values the Milindapanha for its unparalleled clarity in explaining difficult doctrinal points, particularly the teaching of non-self (*anatta*). The famous chariot analogy — in which Nagasena demonstrates that just as a "chariot" is merely a conventional name for a collection of parts, so a "person" is a conventional designation for the five aggregates — has become the standard Theravada illustration of this central doctrine. The text's dialogue format, sharp philosophical argumentation, and memorable similes make it an essential pedagogical resource in Theravada Buddhist education.
