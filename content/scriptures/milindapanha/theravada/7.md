Book 7 of the Milindapanha addresses the ascetic practices — the thirteen *dhutanga* practices and the discipline of renunciation. In the Theravada tradition, this section is studied as an authoritative exposition of Buddhist doctrine, with Nagasena's arguments serving as models of how to explain and defend the Dhamma. The Theravada commentarial tradition, from Buddhaghosa onward, frequently cites the Milindapanha's formulations as definitive statements on difficult doctrinal points.

The Theravada reception of the examination of austere practices such as wearing only robes from refuse heaps, eating only one meal a day, and dwelling in the forest emphasizes its practical significance for Buddhist education and practice. Nagasena's arguments are not merely philosophical exercises but demonstrations of how clear thinking can dispel confusion and deepen understanding of the path to liberation. In Theravada monastic education, particularly in Myanmar and Sri Lanka, the Milindapanha is studied as a text that trains the mind in precise doctrinal analysis while maintaining the warmth and accessibility of narrative literature.
