Book 2 of the Milindapanha addresses the distinguishing marks of qualities — the foundational dialogue on the nature of the self, mind, and basic Buddhist concepts, which Mahayana Buddhism engages with from the perspective of its own expanded philosophical framework. While the Milindapanha's arguments are grounded in early Buddhist doctrine, Mahayana readers find in them anticipations of later Mahayana developments — particularly the Madhyamaka analysis of emptiness and the Yogacara investigation of consciousness.

The Mahayana engagement with Nagasena's famous chariot analogy and the analysis of personal identity often involves reading Nagasena's arguments as preliminary analyses that point toward deeper insights. For example, Nagasena's demonstration that the 'self' is a mere conventional designation applied to a collection of aggregates anticipates the Madhyamaka teaching that all phenomena — not just persons — lack inherent existence (*svabhava*). The Mahayana tradition thus values the Milindapanha as a skillful introduction to philosophical analysis that prepares the ground for the full realization of emptiness.
