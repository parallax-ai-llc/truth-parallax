## Overview

Mahayana Buddhism engages with the Milindapanha as a philosophically sophisticated early text that anticipates several distinctively Mahayana developments. Nagasena's analysis of the self as a mere conventional designation applied to interdependent processes resonates with the Madhyamaka teaching of emptiness (*sunyata*) — the view that all phenomena, not just persons, lack inherent existence. The text's emphasis on the conventional and provisional nature of language and concepts also connects with the Mahayana doctrine of two truths (*conventional* and *ultimate*).

The Milindapanha's use of skilled dialogue, analogies, and thought experiments exemplifies what Mahayana Buddhism would later formalize as *upaya* (skillful means) — the adaptation of teaching methods to the capacities and needs of the audience. Nagasena's ability to meet King Milinda on his own intellectual terms, using Greek-influenced logical argumentation to communicate Buddhist insights, demonstrates the kind of creative cross-cultural pedagogy that Mahayana traditions have practiced throughout their history.
