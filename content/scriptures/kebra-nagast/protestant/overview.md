## Overview

Protestant engagement with the Kebra Nagast raises important theological questions about the boundaries of sacred history, the nature of biblical authority, and the relationship between covenant and national identity. The text's claim that God transferred the Ark of the Covenant — and by extension, the divine covenant itself — from Israel to Ethiopia challenges Protestant assumptions about the relationship between Old and New Testaments and the universality of the Christian gospel.

Protestant scholars and theologians have approached the Kebra Nagast with varying degrees of appreciation and critique. Some value it as a witness to the deep roots of Christianity in Africa, challenging the assumption that Christianity is an inherently Western religion. Others critique its conflation of sacred and political authority, its extension of biblical narrative beyond canonical bounds, and its particularistic claim of Ethiopian election. The text provokes productive reflection on how all Christian traditions have selectively interpreted scripture to support particular cultural and political projects.
