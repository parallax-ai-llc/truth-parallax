## Overview

The Kebra Nagast provides rich material for comparative analysis across multiple dimensions. Its narrative of the Queen of Sheba's visit to Solomon connects to one of the most widely distributed story complexes in world literature, with versions in the Hebrew Bible (1 Kings 10), the Quran (Surah 27), Jewish midrash, Islamic *qisas al-anbiya* (tales of the prophets), and various Ethiopian and Arabian traditions. Comparative analysis reveals how different religious traditions have elaborated and reinterpreted this shared narrative core.

The text's political theology — legitimating royal authority through divine election and sacred lineage — invites comparison with similar texts from other cultures: the Japanese Kojiki and Nihon Shoki (establishing the divine descent of the imperial line), the British Historia Regum Britanniae (tracing British kings to Troy), and various ancient Near Eastern royal inscriptions and foundation myths. The concept of *translatio imperii* — the transfer of sacred authority from one nation to another — connects the Kebra Nagast to Roman, Byzantine, and Holy Roman Empire political theology.
