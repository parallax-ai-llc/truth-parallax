## Overview

The Kebra Nagast ("Glory of Kings") is a fourteenth-century Ethiopian literary work that recounts the origins of the Solomonic dynasty of Ethiopia, tracing the lineage of Ethiopian emperors to the biblical King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba (known in Ethiopian tradition as Makeda). The text narrates their meeting, their union, the birth of their son Menelik I, and — most dramatically — the transfer of the Ark of the Covenant from Jerusalem to Ethiopia, establishing Ethiopia as the new Israel and the rightful guardian of God's covenant.

Compiled in its present form around 1314 CE during the early Solomonic period of Ethiopian history, the Kebra Nagast draws on biblical narrative, Jewish and Christian apocryphal traditions, patristic theology, and Ethiopian oral tradition. It served as the constitutional and theological foundation of the Ethiopian monarchy for over six centuries and remains a text of profound religious and cultural significance. For the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, it is a sacred historical text confirming Ethiopia's unique role in salvation history. For the Rastafari movement, it is a foundational scripture establishing the divine lineage of Emperor Haile Selassie.

## Structure

The Kebra Nagast is organized into sections addressing different aspects of the narrative:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| 1 | The Glory of Kings — Introduction and the greatness of Ethiopia's divine election |
| 2 | The Wisdom of Solomon — Solomon's wisdom, the visit of the Queen of Sheba (Makeda) |
| 3 | The Union of Solomon and Makeda — Their meeting, dialogue, and the conception of Menelik |
| 4 | The Birth and Youth of Menelik I — The son of Solomon grows up in Ethiopia |
| 5 | Menelik's Journey to Jerusalem — The young prince visits his father Solomon |
| 6 | The Transfer of the Ark — The Ark of the Covenant is brought from Jerusalem to Ethiopia |
| 7 | The Establishment of the Solomonic Dynasty — Ethiopia as the New Israel |
| 8 | Theological Discourses — On the Incarnation, the Trinity, and the role of Mary |
| 9 | The Covenant with Ethiopia — God's covenant transferred from Israel to Ethiopia |
| 10 | The Prophecies — Old Testament prophecies interpreted as referring to Ethiopia |
| 11 | The Christian Kings — The destiny of Ethiopian Christian monarchy |
| 12 | The Queen of the South — Expanded narratives of Makeda and her significance |
| 13 | The Ark and Its Powers — The theological significance of the Ark of the Covenant |
| 14 | The Final Glory — The eschatological destiny of Ethiopia and its kings |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Ethiopian Orthodox**: A sacred text confirming Ethiopia's unique covenantal relationship with God
- **Academic**: A key text for Ethiopian studies, African Christianity, and comparative literary analysis
- **Comparative**: Rich connections to biblical, Jewish, Islamic, and African literary and theological traditions
- **Protestant**: Raises questions about canon, sacred history, and the relationship between covenant and ethnicity
- **Catholic**: Connects to traditions of the Queen of Sheba and the theology of sacred kingship
- **Interfaith**: A text at the intersection of Jewish, Christian, Islamic, and African traditional narratives

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Translatio Imperii | The transfer of divine election and sacred authority from Israel to Ethiopia |
| Sacred Kingship | The theological legitimation of the Ethiopian monarchy through Solomonic descent |
| The Ark | The Ark of the Covenant as the supreme symbol of divine presence and covenantal relationship |
| Ethiopia as New Israel | The reinterpretation of biblical history to center Ethiopia in salvation history |
| Marian Theology | The significant role of the Virgin Mary in the text's theological framework |
| Covenant Theology | The nature and transfer of God's covenant from one nation to another |
