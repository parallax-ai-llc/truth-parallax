## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the third Skandha's Kapila teachings as supporting the non-dual position, particularly the analysis of material nature as ultimately rooted in a single undifferentiated reality. Sridhara Swami's commentary on the Kapila-Devahuti dialogue emphasizes that the Sankhya analysis of prakriti serves to demonstrate the illusory nature of the material world, thereby pointing the aspirant toward Brahman.

The distinction between purusha and prakriti presented by Kapila is read by Advaitins as a preliminary teaching that prepares the aspirant for the higher realization that purusha and prakriti are both apparent modifications of the one Brahman. The analytical discrimination between consciousness and matter is a valid step in the progression toward non-dual knowledge.

The bhakti teaching of this Skandha is read by Advaitins as describing the highest form of upasana — an intense devotional engagement with saguna Brahman that purifies the mind and creates the conditions for the arising of non-dual knowledge.

## Key Themes

- **Sankhya as Preparatory**: The analysis of prakriti as leading toward Brahman-realization
- **Beyond Purusha-Prakriti**: The non-dual Brahman underlying both consciousness and matter
- **Devotion as Purification**: Bhakti as the most effective chitta-shuddhi
- **Kapila and Vedanta**: Reading Kapila's philosophy through an Upanishadic lens
- **Progressive Realization**: From Sankhya analysis to Vedantic non-dual knowledge

## Key Verses and Passages

### Sankhya and Vedanta

Sridhara Swami reads Kapila's Sankhya as a stepping stone to Vedanta rather than a rival system. The Sankhya discrimination between purusha and prakriti prepares the mind for the higher Vedantic discrimination between Brahman and maya.

### The Nine Forms of Bhakti

Advaitins read the nine forms of devotion as progressive stages of upasana, with self-surrender (atma-nivedana) as the closest approximation to the non-dual realization of identity with Brahman.

## Commentary Tradition

Sridhara Swami's commentary on the Kapila teachings represents a masterful integration of Sankhya, Bhagavata bhakti, and Advaita Vedanta, demonstrating how diverse philosophical strands can be woven into a non-dual framework.
