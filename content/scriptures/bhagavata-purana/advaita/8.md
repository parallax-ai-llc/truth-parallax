## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the eighth Skandha's narratives as illustrations of the progressive deepening of surrender from external to internal, from conditional to unconditional. Gajendra's surrender represents the moment when the jiva, having exhausted all its own resources, turns to Brahman — not to a deity 'out there' but to the divine reality within.

Sridhara Swami reads the churning of the cosmic ocean as an allegory for the spiritual process: the ocean is the mind, the churning rod is concentration, the devas and asuras are positive and negative tendencies, and the nectar of immortality is self-knowledge (atma-jnana). The poison represents the initial suffering that arises in spiritual practice, which must be absorbed (as Shiva absorbs it) before the nectar of realization appears.

The Vamana narrative is read allegorically: the three steps of Vamana cover the three states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep), and the reclaiming of the three worlds symbolizes Brahman's pervasion of all states of experience.

## Key Themes

- **Inner Surrender**: Gajendra's prapatti as turning inward to the Self
- **Allegorical Churning**: The churning narrative as a map of spiritual practice
- **Three States**: Vamana's three steps as covering the three states of consciousness
- **Exhaustion of Self-effort**: The recognition that liberation cannot be achieved through effort alone
- **Universal Brahman**: Vamana's cosmic expansion as demonstrating Brahman's all-pervasiveness

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Churning as Spiritual Practice

Sridhara Swami's allegorical reading: the ocean of the mind is churned through concentrated practice, negative tendencies (asuras) and positive tendencies (devas) both contributing to the process, until the nectar of self-knowledge (amrita) emerges.

### Vamana's Three Steps

Advaitins read Vamana's expansion to fill all space as demonstrating the Upanishadic truth that Brahman is 'one without a second' — there is no space that is not Brahman.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on this Skandha demonstrates the distinctive Advaita method of finding philosophical allegory within devotional narrative.
