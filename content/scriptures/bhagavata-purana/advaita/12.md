## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the twelfth Skandha's Kali Yuga description as an illustration of the nature of samsara itself — the world of duality characterized by progressive manifestation of ignorance. The Kali Yuga's darkness is, from the Advaitic standpoint, not merely a historical period but a symbol of the condition of any being immersed in avidya.

The Markandeya narrative — witnessing the dissolution and re-creation of the cosmos — is read by Advaitins as a direct experience of the truth that the phenomenal world is impermanent and dependent on Brahman. Markandeya sees the cosmos dissolved and reborn, confirming that Brahman alone is the unchanging reality.

The text's self-identification as Krishna's continuing presence is read by Advaitins through the concept of shabda-brahman (Brahman as sacred sound/word). The Bhagavata Purana, as a manifestation of shabda-brahman, is a valid means of accessing the non-dual reality in literary form.

## Key Themes

- **Kali Yuga as Avidya**: The dark age as symbolizing the condition of ignorance
- **Markandeya's Vision**: Cosmic dissolution as demonstrating Brahman's sole reality
- **Shabda-Brahman**: The text as a manifestation of Brahman in linguistic form
- **Nama as Meditation**: Chanting as a means of stilling the mind for non-dual realization
- **Impermanence of All Ages**: The cosmic cycles as demonstrating the transience of all phenomenal existence

## Key Verses and Passages

### Markandeya's Cosmic Vision

Sridhara Swami reads Markandeya's experience of cosmic dissolution as the ultimate demonstration of Brahman's sole reality. When the universe dissolves, only Brahman remains — confirming the Advaitic teaching that the world is apparent (mithya) and Brahman alone is real (satya).

### Text as Brahman

The Bhagavata's self-identification as the 'sun' risen after Krishna's departure is read by Advaitins as establishing the text as shabda-brahman — a literary manifestation of the non-dual Absolute available for contemplation and realization.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on this final Skandha brings the entire Advaitic reading of the Bhagavata to its conclusion, establishing the text as a valid pramana (means of knowledge) for Brahman-realization.
