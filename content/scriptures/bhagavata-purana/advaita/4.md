## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the fourth Skandha's narratives as illustrations of the spiritual journey from ignorance to knowledge, with bhakti as the driving force of this journey. Dhruva's transformation from a child seeking worldly power to a realized soul represents the Advaitic progression from motivated (sakama) to unmotivated (nishkama) spiritual practice.

Sridhara Swami's commentary emphasizes that Dhruva's initial material desire was necessary as the catalyst for his spiritual journey — without the desire for his father's kingdom, he would never have undertaken the austerities that led to divine realization. This illustrates the Advaitic teaching that even mixed motivations can lead to liberation when directed toward the divine.

The Prithu narrative is read by Advaitins as an allegory for the mind (the king) governing the body and senses (the earth) through righteous discernment. The extraction of prosperity from the earth symbolizes the extraction of spiritual knowledge from material experience through proper understanding.

## Key Themes

- **Spiritual Progression**: Dhruva's journey as the path from desire to dispassion to knowledge
- **Catalytic Desire**: Material motivation as the initial spark for spiritual transformation
- **Allegory of Governance**: Kingship as a metaphor for self-mastery
- **Nishkama Bhakti**: The evolution from motivated to unmotivated devotion
- **Self-Realization Through Experience**: Using worldly experience as material for spiritual insight

## Key Verses and Passages

### Dhruva's Transformation

Sridhara Swami reads Dhruva's famous statement of disillusionment with material desire after his vision of Vishnu as the moment of viveka (discrimination) — the recognition that no finite attainment can satisfy the infinite longing of the self for Brahman.

### Prithu as Inner Ruler

Advaitins allegorize the Prithu narrative: the ideal king represents the atman governing the psycho-physical organism through buddhi (intellect), extracting spiritual wisdom from material experience.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on these narratives emphasizes their pedagogical function as illustrations of philosophical principles, while maintaining genuine appreciation for their devotional power.
