## Overview

Sridhara Swami's Advaita commentary on the first Skandha sets the interpretive framework for the entire Advaitic reading of the Bhagavata. He reads the opening invocation and framework narrative as establishing that the Bhagavata's ultimate subject is Brahman — the non-dual reality that is the source and ground of all existence.

The verse 'krishnas tu bhagavan svayam' is interpreted by Advaitins not as establishing Krishna's supremacy over other personal deities but as identifying Krishna with Brahman itself — the Absolute appearing in personal form. Since Brahman transcends all personal characterizations, this identification serves devotional purposes without establishing any ultimate distinction between different divine forms.

Vyasa's dissatisfaction, in the Advaita reading, represents the limitation of all knowledge that stops short of Brahma-vidya — the direct, non-dual knowledge of the Self. Narada's instruction to compose the Bhagavata is read as a call to create a text that leads the reader from devotional engagement to non-dual realization.

## Key Themes

- **Brahman as Subject**: The Bhagavata ultimately teaching about nirguna Brahman
- **Krishna as Brahman**: Personal deity as the Absolute in accessible form
- **Devotion Leading to Knowledge**: Bhakti as preparation for non-dual realization
- **Literary Incarnation**: The text as Brahman manifesting in literary form
- **Transcending Knowledge**: Vyasa's dissatisfaction as pointing beyond all conceptual knowledge

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Opening Invocation

Sridhara Swami reads the Bhagavata's opening verse (janmady asya yatah) as an echo of the Brahma Sutra's opening (janmady asya yatah), establishing that both texts ultimately discuss the same subject: Brahman.

### 'Krishnas tu Bhagavan Svayam'

The Advaita reading: 'Bhagavan' = Brahman. Krishna is identified with Brahman not as one personal deity supreme over others but as the personal face of the impersonal Absolute, revealing itself for devotees who require a personal object of meditation.

## Commentary Tradition

Sridhara Swami's Bhavartha-dipika commentary on this canto is foundational. Its opening establishes the Advaita hermeneutical principles applied throughout the text. Even Vaishnava commentators who disagree with Sridhara's Advaitic conclusions acknowledge his literary and philosophical acumen.
