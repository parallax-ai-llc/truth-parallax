## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the second Skandha's cosmic form meditation as a description of Brahman's relationship to the manifest universe. The cosmic form is understood as Brahman viewed through the lens of maya — the Absolute appearing as the totality of creation without being exhausted by or limited to that creation.

Sridhara Swami's commentary emphasizes that the cosmic form meditation (virat-upasana) is a valid but preliminary practice. The aspirant begins by meditating on Brahman as the cosmic form, then progresses to meditation on Brahman as the subtle witness (sakshi), and finally realizes Brahman as the non-dual Self beyond all forms, including the cosmic form.

The teaching that Bhagavan transcends the cosmic form even while being identical with it is read by Advaitins as pointing to the distinction between vyavaharika (conventional) and paramarthika (ultimate) perspectives. From the conventional standpoint, God is the cosmic form; from the ultimate standpoint, Brahman is formless and non-dual.

## Key Themes

- **Virat-Upasana**: Cosmic form meditation as a preliminary practice
- **Brahman Beyond Form**: The Absolute transcending even the cosmic form
- **Two Perspectives**: Vyavaharika and paramarthika readings of cosmic theology
- **Progressive Meditation**: From the gross cosmic form to the subtle non-dual Brahman
- **Maya and Manifestation**: The cosmos as Brahman's apparent manifestation through maya

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Cosmic Form as Upasana

Sridhara Swami reads the cosmic form meditation as a step in the aspirant's progressive realization — beginning with the gross form (sthula), moving to the subtle (sukshma), and culminating in the formless (arupa) Brahman.

### Transcendence of the Cosmic Form

Advaitins emphasize passages where the text declares Bhagavan's transcendence over the cosmic form, reading these as pointers to nirguna Brahman beyond all manifestation.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on this Skandha integrates Upanishadic meditation techniques (particularly the panchagni vidya and madhu vidya) with the Bhagavata's cosmic form meditation, creating a comprehensive contemplative framework.
