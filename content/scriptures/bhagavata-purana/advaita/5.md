## Overview

Advaita Vedanta finds the fifth Skandha particularly rich in philosophical content. Jada Bharata's teaching to King Rahugana on the nature of the self — distinguishing the true self (atman) from the body, mind, and social roles — is essentially an Advaitic discourse embedded within the Bhagavata's narrative.

Sridhara Swami's commentary on Jada Bharata's teaching draws out its non-dual implications: the self is not the body, not the mind, not the social persona, but the pure witness consciousness that is identical with Brahman. Bharata's fall through attachment to the deer illustrates the power of superimposition (adhyasa) — projecting the self onto an external object.

The cosmographic sections are treated by Advaitins as apara vidya (lower knowledge) — valid at the conventional level but ultimately transcended in the realization that all apparent cosmic diversity is Brahman alone.

## Key Themes

- **Atma-vichara**: Jada Bharata's teaching as self-inquiry leading to non-dual realization
- **Adhyasa**: Bharata's attachment as illustrating the error of superimposition
- **Witness Consciousness**: The true self as the uninvolved observer of all experience
- **Beyond Social Identity**: Jada Bharata's appearance as a fool demonstrating transcendence of social roles
- **Lower Knowledge**: Cosmography as conventionally valid but ultimately transcended

## Key Verses and Passages

### Jada Bharata's Discourse

Sridhara Swami reads Jada Bharata's teaching as a compact Advaita treatise: the self is not the body (which changes and dies), not the mind (which fluctuates), and not the social role (which is imposed) — it is the unchanging witness, identical with Brahman.

### The Deer Attachment

Advaitins read Bharata's attachment to the deer as a classic case of adhyasa — the superimposition of self onto an external object. The lesson is that any identification with anything other than Brahman perpetuates samsara.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on this Skandha is extensive, particularly on the Jada Bharata sections, which provide some of the most explicitly philosophical material in the Bhagavata.
