## Overview

Advaita Vedanta reads the sixth Skandha's Ajamila narrative as demonstrating the purificatory power of the divine name — a form of upasana that, according to Advaitins, operates by removing the impurities (mala) that obstruct the self's recognition of its identity with Brahman.

Sridhara Swami's commentary emphasizes that Ajamila's salvation, while mediated through the divine name, ultimately points to the self-luminous nature of Brahman within every being. The name 'Narayana' has power not because of magical properties but because it connects the mind with Brahman, the reality underlying all names and forms.

The Vritra narrative's presentation of a demon-devotee is read by Advaitins as demonstrating that spiritual realization is not limited by birth, caste, or species — a position consistent with the Advaitic teaching that the atman in every being is identical with Brahman, regardless of the being's external form or moral status.

## Key Themes

- **Nama as Upasana**: The divine name as a meditation technique purifying the mind
- **Self-luminous Brahman**: The name's power deriving from Brahman's presence within all beings
- **Universal Self**: The atman's identity with Brahman present even in demons like Vritra
- **Purification of Ignorance**: Sin as a form of avidya dissolved by Brahman-awareness
- **Beyond Birth and Status**: Spiritual realization transcending all external categories

## Key Verses and Passages

### Ajamila's Salvation

Sridhara Swami reads Ajamila's salvation as operating through the divine name's power to remove ignorance (avidya). The name connects the mind — however briefly — with Brahman, and even a moment of such connection can transform the soul's trajectory.

### Vritra as Jnani

Advaitins read Vritra's philosophical prayer as evidence that the demon had attained a level of self-knowledge (atma-jnana) that transcended his demonic birth — demonstrating that knowledge of Brahman is available to all beings.

## Commentary Tradition

Advaitin commentary on the Ajamila narrative connects it to the broader Advaita teaching on the power of sacred syllables (mantras) and the purificatory function of Brahman-awareness.
