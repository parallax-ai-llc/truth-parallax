## Overview

Advaita Vedanta has a long and complex relationship with the Bhagavata Purana. Sridhara Swami's 14th-century commentary — the earliest extant complete commentary on the text — reads the entire Bhagavata through an Advaitic lens, interpreting its devotional theology as pointing ultimately toward the non-dual Brahman. This commentary is so respected that even Vaishnava commentators (including Vallabhacharya and Chaitanya) acknowledged its authority.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Advaitic Theism
The Advaita reading of the Bhagavata represents a distinctive position: "Advaitic theism" — genuine devotion to a personal God (saguna Brahman) understood as ultimately pointing toward the attributeless non-dual reality (nirguna Brahman). The Bhagavata's devotional intensity is not rejected but recontextualized.

### Bhakti and Jnana
Advaitins read the Bhagavata's bhakti as the highest form of upasana (meditation/worship), which purifies the mind and prepares it for the arising of non-dual knowledge. Sridhara Swami's commentary consistently draws out how devotional passages point toward jnana.

### Krishna as Brahman
The Advaita reading identifies Krishna with Brahman — not as a particular personal deity supreme over other deities but as the personal face of the impersonal Absolute, revealing itself through lila for the benefit of devotees.

## Theological Emphases

- **Saguna-Nirguna Synthesis**: The Bhagavata as revealing both personal and impersonal aspects of the Absolute
- **Chitta-shuddhi through Bhakti**: Devotion as the most effective means of mental purification
- **Lila as Maya**: Krishna's divine play as the creative activity of Brahman through maya
- **Jivanmukti**: Liberation while embodied, exemplified by the sages and devotees in the text
- **Knowledge through Devotion**: Bhakti not opposed to jnana but leading to it
