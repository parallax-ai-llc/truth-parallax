## Overview

The sixth Skandha's theology of the divine name and its narrative of salvation through grace invite rich comparative analysis. The Ajamila story — salvation through the accidental utterance of a divine name — parallels Pure Land Buddhist teachings on the nembutsu (calling on Amitabha Buddha's name) and the Christian concept of salvation through calling on the name of the Lord (Romans 10:13).

The debate between grace and karma dramatized in the Vishnuduta-Yamaduta conflict parallels the Christian debate between grace and works (Paul vs. James, Luther vs. Catholic teaching) and the Buddhist debate between self-power (jiriki) and other-power (tariki) in the Pure Land tradition.

Vritra's portrayal as a demon-devotee who surpasses the gods in spiritual attainment parallels the pattern of unlikely saints in other traditions: the 'sinners' who become saints in Christian hagiography, the outcasts who become arhat in Buddhist tradition, and the Sufi tradition of divine grace falling on the unexpected.

## Key Themes

- **Salvation Through Name**: Ajamila compared with nembutsu, calling on the Lord's name in Christianity
- **Grace vs. Works/Karma**: The Vishnuduta debate compared with Paul-James, jiriki-tariki debates
- **Unlikely Saints**: Vritra compared with converted sinners in other traditions
- **Deathbed Conversion**: Ajamila's last-moment salvation compared with the repentant thief on the cross
- **Name Theology**: The power of divine names across Islam, Judaism, Christianity, and Buddhism

## Key Verses and Passages

### Salvation Through the Name

Ajamila's salvation through uttering 'Narayana' at death parallels Honen's Pure Land teaching that a single sincere nembutsu at death ensures rebirth in Amitabha's Pure Land, and the Christian promise that 'everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved' (Romans 10:13).

### The Deathbed Moment

Ajamila's last-moment salvation parallels the repentant thief on the cross (Luke 23:43) — both traditions affirming that a single moment of sincere turning toward the divine can outweigh a lifetime of sin.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholars find the sixth Skandha to be one of the most fertile sections of the Bhagavata for cross-traditional analysis, as its themes of grace, name-power, and unlikely salvation resonate across virtually all devotional traditions.
