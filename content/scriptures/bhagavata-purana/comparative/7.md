## Overview

The Prahlada-Narasimha narrative offers some of the richest material in the Bhagavata for comparative analysis. The persecuted child devotee who maintains faith despite paternal hostility parallels the stories of biblical figures like Isaac (threatened by his father Abraham), Daniel in the lion's den, and the young David facing Goliath — in each case, divine protection vindicating faithful vulnerability.

The Narasimha avatar — a divine form that defies all categories — can be compared with theophanies in other traditions that similarly transcend human conceptual frameworks: the burning bush that is not consumed (Exodus 3), the resurrection body of Christ that passes through walls yet bears wounds, and the Buddhist concept of the dharma body (dharmakaya) that is beyond all form yet manifests in form.

Prahlada's calm assurance in the face of death parallels the composure of Christian martyrs, the Buddhist bodhisattva's fearlessness, and the Sikh tradition of martyrdom exemplified by Guru Arjan Dev. In each case, the devotee's equanimity testifies to a reality that transcends death.

## Key Themes

- **Persecuted Faith**: Prahlada compared with Daniel, Christian martyrs, and Sikh martyrs
- **Category-Transcending Theophany**: Narasimha compared with the burning bush and resurrection body
- **Father-Son Conflict**: Hiranyakashipu-Prahlada compared with Abraham-Isaac and other father-son religious conflicts
- **Divine Protection**: God's intervention for the faithful compared across traditions
- **Equanimity in Persecution**: Prahlada's composure compared with the composure of saints and martyrs worldwide

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Persecuted Faithful

Prahlada's persecution by his own father for religious conviction parallels Daniel's faithfulness in the lion's den (Daniel 6), the early Christian martyrs' refusal to worship Roman gods, and the Sikh Guru Arjan Dev's martyrdom for religious principle.

### Category-Defying Divine Manifestation

Narasimha's defiance of all categories parallels other traditions' recognition that the divine exceeds human classification: the God who is 'neither this nor that' (neti neti) in the Upanishads, the God whose ways are 'unsearchable' in Paul's letters (Romans 11:33), and the Buddhist Madhyamaka teaching that ultimate reality defies all conceptual categories.

## Commentary Tradition

Comparative scholars note that the Prahlada-Narasimha narrative combines themes of persecution, divine intervention, and categorical transcendence in a way that resonates across virtually all religious traditions.
