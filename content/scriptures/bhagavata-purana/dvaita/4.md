## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the fourth Skandha's narratives as demonstrations of key Dvaita theological principles: the inherent hierarchy among souls, the reality of devotion as an eternal relationship, and Vishnu's responsive engagement with his devotees. Dhruva and Prithu represent souls of the highest class (uttama-jivas) whose devotion naturally emerges from their inherent spiritual capacity.

Madhvacharya reads Dhruva's story through the lens of taratamya (hierarchy): Dhruva's exceptional devotion reflects his exalted position in the hierarchy of souls, not merely his individual effort. While effort is necessary, the capacity for such devotion is itself a gift of Vishnu's grace reflecting the soul's inherent nature.

The Prithu narrative is significant for Dvaita political theology, establishing that legitimate earthly governance derives from Vishnu's authority. The king is not autonomous but governs as Vishnu's agent, accountable to divine law.

## Key Themes

- **Taratamya in Devotion**: Dhruva's exceptional bhakti reflecting his exalted spiritual nature
- **Inherent Capacity**: Devotional ability as rooted in the soul's svarupa (essential nature)
- **Real Relationship**: Dhruva's encounter with Vishnu as a genuine meeting between distinct beings
- **Divine Accountability**: The king answerable to Vishnu for his governance
- **Grace and Nature**: Vishnu's grace working through the soul's inherent disposition

## Key Verses and Passages

### Dhruva's Hierarchy

Madhvacharya reads Dhruva's extraordinary devotion as evidence for the Dvaita doctrine that souls differ in their inherent capacity for spiritual realization. Dhruva's success is not merely a product of effort but reflects his nature as an uttama-jiva.

### Prithu and Divine Governance

The Dvaita reading of Prithu emphasizes the king's accountability to Vishnu — legitimate authority is always delegated authority, and the ruler who forgets this becomes a tyrant.

## Commentary Tradition

Dvaita commentary on these narratives integrates them into the broader Dvaita theological framework of taratamya, svarupa-yogyata, and Vishnu's responsive grace.
