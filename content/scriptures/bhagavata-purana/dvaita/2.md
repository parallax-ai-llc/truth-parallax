## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the second Skandha as providing crucial evidence for the personal, qualified nature of the Supreme Being. The cosmic form (Virat Rupa) demonstrates that Bhagavan is not an attributeless void but a personal God with a real cosmic body — the universe exists within him, sustained by his will and power.

Madhvacharya emphasizes that the cosmic form meditation described here presupposes the reality of both God and the universe. The meditation would be meaningless if either were illusory, as Advaitins claim. The fact that the Bhagavata prescribes this meditation as a valid spiritual practice proves the reality of the cosmic form and, by extension, the reality of creation.

The Skandha's teaching that Bhagavan transcends the cosmic form is read by Dvaitins as demonstrating that God is greater than his creation — not that he is formless. Vishnu possesses both a cosmic form (the universe) and a transcendent personal form (his eternal body in Vaikuntha), both equally real.

## Key Themes

- **Real Cosmic Form**: The Virat Rupa as a real, not illusory, manifestation of Vishnu
- **Personal God**: The cosmic form proving the personal, qualified nature of the Absolute
- **Real Universe**: The meditation presupposing the reality of creation
- **Dual Form**: Vishnu's cosmic and transcendent forms as both genuinely real
- **Against Formlessness**: The cosmic form teaching as evidence against nirguna Brahman

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Reality of the Cosmic Form

Madhvacharya argues that prescribing meditation on the cosmic form only makes sense if the form is real. An illusory meditation object cannot produce a real spiritual result, therefore the cosmic form and the universe it represents must be genuinely real.

### Transcendence as Greater Reality

Dvaita interpreters read Bhagavan's transcendence of the cosmic form not as formlessness but as his possession of a form greater than the cosmos — his eternal, blissful body in Vaikuntha.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's Bhagavata Tatparya Nirnaya and Vijaydhvaja Tirtha's Padaratnavali provide the foundational Dvaita readings of this Skandha, emphasizing the reality of both God's form and the created universe.
