## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the sixth Skandha as providing powerful evidence for the saving power of Vishnu's name and the supremacy of devotion. The Ajamila narrative is taken at face value: Vishnu's name, even uttered accidentally, has the real power to counteract real sin and real death — because Vishnu's name carries his full divine authority.

Madhvacharya reads the Vritra narrative as demonstrating the Dvaita doctrine that a soul's spiritual capacity (svarupa-yogyata) is independent of its birth circumstances. Vritra, despite being born as a demon, possessed an inherent capacity for the highest devotion — a capacity rooted in his essential nature as an uttama-jiva (soul of the highest rank) who had temporarily fallen into demonic birth through past karma.

The debate between the Vishnudutas and Yamadutas over Ajamila's soul is read by Dvaitins as demonstrating the real cosmic authority of Vishnu's name — it is not symbolic or metaphorical but exercises actual power in the spiritual realm.

## Key Themes

- **Real Salvific Power**: Vishnu's name as carrying genuine cosmic authority
- **Svarupa-yogyata**: Vritra's devotion reflecting his inherent spiritual nature
- **Cosmic Authority**: The Vishnuduta-Yamaduta debate proving the supremacy of Vishnu's jurisdiction
- **Against Karma Alone**: Grace through nama transcending the mechanical operation of karma
- **Real Sin, Real Salvation**: The Ajamila narrative presupposing the reality of both sin and liberation

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Vishnuduta-Yamaduta Debate

Madhvacharya reads this as a genuine cosmic legal dispute that establishes the supremacy of Vishnu's authority (represented by the Vishnudutas) over the authority of Yama (death). The debate's outcome demonstrates that Vishnu's name overrides the claims of karma.

### Vritra's Essential Nature

The Dvaita reading: Vritra was inherently a great devotee — his demonic birth was a temporary condition caused by past karma, not a reflection of his essential spiritual nature. This supports the Dvaita doctrine of inherent differences among souls.

## Commentary Tradition

Dvaita commentary on this Skandha is extensive, drawing on Madhvacharya's nama-theology and the broader Haridasa tradition's emphasis on the supremacy of Hari's name.
