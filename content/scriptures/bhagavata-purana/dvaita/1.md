## Overview

Madhvacharya's reading of the first Skandha establishes the Dvaita interpretive framework for the entire Bhagavata. For Dvaitins, this canto unambiguously declares Vishnu/Krishna's absolute supremacy, the reality of the world, and the eternal distinction between God and souls — the three pillars of Dvaita theology.

The verse 'krishnas tu bhagavan svayam' is read by Dvaitins as the most important single verse in Puranic literature — an unequivocal declaration that Krishna is the Supreme Person, not a manifestation of some impersonal Brahman (as Advaitins claim) but the personal, qualified, all-powerful God from whom everything else derives.

Vyasa's dissatisfaction is read by Dvaitins as demonstrating that even the compiler of the Vedas could not attain spiritual perfection until he composed a text that fully glorified the Supreme Person. This reinforces the Dvaita position that knowledge of Vishnu's supremacy is the essential content of all valid scripture.

## Key Themes

- **Unambiguous Supremacy**: The first canto as clearly establishing Vishnu/Krishna's sovereignty
- **Anti-Advaita Proof**: Against impersonal interpretations of the Bhagavata's theology
- **Scriptural Hierarchy**: The Bhagavata as the highest authority among all scriptures
- **Personal God**: The irreducibly personal nature of the Absolute as revealed in this canto
- **Vyasa's Completion**: The Bhagavata as the fulfillment of all Vyasa's prior literary activity

## Key Verses and Passages

### 'Krishnas tu Bhagavan Svayam' — Dvaita Reading

Madhvacharya insists this verse must be read literally: Krishna alone is Bhagavan — the Supreme Person with all six divine qualities (jnana, bala, aishvarya, virya, shakti, tejas). This is not metaphor, not provisional, and not reducible to an impersonal Brahman. It is the definitive theological statement of the Bhagavata.

### The Opening Verse

Madhvacharya reads the Bhagavata's opening (janmady asya yatah) as proving that the source of the universe is a conscious, personal being — not the attributeless Brahman of Advaita but the personal Vishnu with full divine attributes.

## Commentary Tradition

Madhvacharya's Bhagavata Tatparya Nirnaya is the foundational Dvaita commentary. Vijaydhvaja Tirtha's Padaratnavali provides a complete verse-by-verse Dvaita commentary. Later scholars Jayatirtha, Vyasatirtha, and Raghavendra Swami further developed the Dvaita reading.
