## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta considers the Bhagavata Purana the highest authority among the Puranas — a sattvic Purana par excellence that reveals the ultimate truth of Vishnu's absolute supremacy. Madhvacharya cited the Bhagavata extensively throughout his works, and it holds a central place in the Dvaita liturgical and intellectual tradition.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Supreme Scriptural Authority
For Dvaitins, the Bhagavata Purana's declarations of Vishnu/Krishna's supremacy are to be taken as literal, unambiguous theological truths. The text's statement "krishnas tu bhagavan svayam" is read as establishing Krishna's identity with the supreme Vishnu.

### Anti-Advaita Reading
Madhvacharya's reading of the Bhagavata is explicitly anti-Advaita. He rejects Sridhara Swami's Advaitic interpretation and insists that the text's devotional theology presupposes the real, permanent distinction between God and the soul — making Advaitic non-dualism incompatible with the Bhagavata's message.

### Scriptural Realism
Dvaitins read the Bhagavata's narratives — including Krishna's miracles, cosmic descriptions, and divine activities — as descriptions of real events involving a real personal God. The literary beauty of the text serves its theological truth, not the other way around.

## Theological Emphases

- **Vishnu Sarvottamatva**: The Bhagavata as the supreme proof of Vishnu's absolute sovereignty
- **Bheda**: The eternal, real distinction between God, souls, and matter
- **Aparoksha Bhakti**: Direct, unmediated devotion to the personal Vishnu/Krishna
- **Taratamya**: The hierarchy of souls reflected in the Bhagavata's diverse characters
- **Anti-Mayavada**: The Bhagavata's realism against Advaita's interpretation of the world as illusion
