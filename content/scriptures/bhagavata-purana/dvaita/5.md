## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta reads the fifth Skandha as demonstrating the reality of bondage, the genuine danger of material attachment, and the necessity of constant vigilance in devotional practice. Bharata's fall is not merely an allegory but a real spiritual event — a real soul genuinely fell from a real state of advancement through a real attachment.

Madhvacharya reads the Bharata narrative as evidence against the Advaita position that bondage is merely apparent (mithya). If bondage were illusory, how could a realized soul fall back into it? The fact that Bharata — despite his advanced spiritual status — genuinely became bound through attachment proves that bondage is real and that constant devotion to Vishnu is necessary to maintain liberation.

The cosmographic content is taken with full seriousness as describing the real structure of the universe created and governed by Vishnu. The descriptions of hellish regions serve as genuine warnings about the consequences of adharma.

## Key Themes

- **Real Bondage**: Bharata's fall proving the genuine reality of material attachment
- **Constant Vigilance**: The necessity of unbroken devotion to maintain spiritual advancement
- **Against Illusory Bondage**: Bharata's case disproving the Advaita claim that bondage is merely apparent
- **Real Cosmography**: The universe's structure as genuinely real, including heavenly and hellish realms
- **Karma's Reality**: The consequences of attachment as real events in real lives

## Key Verses and Passages

### Bharata's Real Fall

Madhvacharya insists that Bharata's fall was a genuine spiritual event — a real devotee really losing his attainment through real attachment. This proves that bondage is not illusory and that spiritual life requires constant, sustained effort.

### Real Hells

The descriptions of hellish regions in the cosmographic sections are read by Dvaitins as descriptions of actual places where souls experience the real consequences of their actions — not allegories or metaphors.

## Commentary Tradition

Dvaita commentary on this canto emphasizes its practical spiritual warnings and its cosmological realism, using both to support the Dvaita position against Advaita.
