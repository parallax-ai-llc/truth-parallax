## Overview

The first Skandha establishes the framework for the entire Bhagavata Purana. It opens with Vyasa's dissatisfaction after composing the Vedas and the Mahabharata — Narada advises him that his work is incomplete because he has not adequately glorified Bhagavan Krishna. This narrative frame is foundational for Vaishnava reading: all knowledge and all scripture find their fulfillment only in devotion to Krishna.

The Skandha then introduces the primary narrative setting: the sage Shuka reciting the Bhagavata to King Parikshit, who has been cursed to die in seven days. This framework of urgent spiritual inquiry — a king facing death seeking the highest knowledge — sets the devotional and existential tone for the entire work.

The famous verse 'krishnas tu bhagavan svayam' (1.3.28) appears in this canto, establishing the Bhagavata's central theological claim: Krishna is Svayam Bhagavan, the Supreme Person himself, the source of all avatars. This verse is the most important proof-text for Gaudiya and other Krishna-centered Vaishnava theologies.

## Key Themes

- **Scriptural Fulfillment**: All knowledge culminates in Krishna-bhakti
- **Urgency of Spiritual Inquiry**: Parikshit's seven-day deadline as paradigmatic spiritual urgency
- **Svayam Bhagavan**: Krishna as the supreme source of all divine forms
- **Guru-Disciple Relationship**: Shuka-Parikshit as the model for receiving transcendental knowledge
- **Narada's Instruction**: The insufficiency of all knowledge that does not lead to devotion

## Key Verses and Passages

### 'Krishnas tu Bhagavan Svayam' (1.3.28)

This is the paribhasha-sutra (defining verse) of the entire Bhagavata. Vaishnava commentators read it as establishing that all avatars listed in the preceding verses are expansions of Krishna, who alone is the original Supreme Person. The verse reads: 'All these are either portions or portions of portions of the Person, but Krishna is Bhagavan himself.'

### Vyasa's Dissatisfaction (1.4-5)

Narada's instruction to Vyasa that his voluminous literary output lacks spiritual value because it does not sufficiently glorify Krishna establishes the Vaishnava principle that all knowledge, however vast, is incomplete without bhakti.

## Commentary Tradition

Every major Vaishnava commentary tradition — Sridhara Swami, Vallabha, Jiva Goswami, Vishvanatha Chakravarti, Madhvacharya, Viraraghavacharya — provides extensive commentary on this foundational canto. The interpretation of 'krishnas tu bhagavan svayam' is the key point of divergence among commentators.
