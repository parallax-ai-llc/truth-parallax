## Overview

The third Skandha is structured around the dialogue between Vidura and the sage Maitreya, covering creation accounts and culminating in the teachings of Kapila to his mother Devahuti. For Vaishnavas, this canto is significant for presenting Kapila — an avatar of Vishnu — as the founder of a theistic Sankhya philosophy that subordinates analytical knowledge to devotion.

The Kapila-Devahuti dialogue is particularly important because it presents a comprehensive soteriological framework: the analysis of matter (prakriti) and spirit (purusha) through Sankhya categories, followed by the teaching that this analytical knowledge finds its fulfillment in bhakti. The Vaishnava reading emphasizes that Kapila's Sankhya is not the atheistic Sankhya of Ishvarakrishna but a devotional philosophy that leads to Krishna.

The creation accounts in this Skandha elaborate on the process by which Bhagavan creates the universe through his various energies. For Vaishnavas, these accounts demonstrate that creation is a purposeful, willed activity of a personal God — not an automatic process or an impersonal emanation.

## Key Themes

- **Theistic Sankhya**: Kapila's philosophy as devotional analysis of reality
- **Bhakti's Supremacy**: Analytical knowledge finding its fulfillment in devotion
- **Mother-Son Teaching**: The Devahuti-Kapila dialogue as a model for spiritual instruction
- **Purposeful Creation**: The cosmos as a product of Bhagavan's personal will
- **Vidura's Inquiry**: The sincere seeker as the ideal recipient of spiritual knowledge

## Key Verses and Passages

### Kapila's Teaching to Devahuti (3.25-33)

Vaishnava commentators read Kapila's teaching as the definitive statement of theistic Sankhya: the analysis of material nature is valuable but reaches its goal only when it leads to devotion to the Supreme Person.

### The Nine Forms of Bhakti (3.29)

Kapila's enumeration of devotional practices provides the Vaishnava with a practical program: hearing, chanting, remembering, serving the lotus feet, worship, prayer, servitude, friendship, and self-surrender.

## Commentary Tradition

Vaishnava commentators treat the Kapila teachings as authoritative philosophy, using them to establish a distinctly Vaishnava version of Sankhya that differs from both classical Sankhya and Advaita Vedanta.
