## Overview

The second Skandha presents the cosmic form (Virat Rupa) of Bhagavan and the process of cosmic creation. For Vaishnavas, this canto reveals the intimate connection between the Supreme Person and the entire cosmos — every part of the universe is a manifestation of Bhagavan's body, yet he transcends the totality of creation.

The meditation on the universal form described here is foundational for Vaishnava contemplative practice. The practitioner is taught to meditate on the cosmic body of Vishnu, seeing the earth as his feet, the sky as his head, the sun and moon as his eyes, and all living beings as parts of his universal form. This practice bridges cosmology and devotion.

The Skandha also addresses the question of Bhagavan's form — establishing that the Supreme Person possesses both a transcendent form (beyond material qualities) and a cosmic form (encompassing all of creation). This dual-form theology is crucial for Vaishnava understanding of how an infinite God can be both transcendent and immanent.

## Key Themes

- **Virat Rupa**: The cosmic form as revealing Bhagavan's all-encompassing nature
- **Meditation Practice**: The contemplation of the universal form as Vaishnava sadhana
- **Transcendence and Immanence**: Bhagavan as both beyond and within the cosmos
- **Creation as Divine Activity**: The universe emerging from Bhagavan's will and body
- **Form Theology**: Bhagavan possessing both transcendent and cosmic forms

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Cosmic Form Meditation (2.1-2)

Vaishnava commentators provide detailed instructions for meditating on the cosmic form, reading this passage as practical spiritual guidance. Each element of the universe is identified with a part of Bhagavan's body.

### The Question of Form (2.5-6)

The dialogue between Shuka and Parikshit on the nature of Bhagavan's form establishes that the Absolute is not formless but possesses a supreme form (vigraha) that transcends material limitations.

## Commentary Tradition

Vaishnava commentators treat this Skandha as essential for establishing the ontological relationship between God and the world. Sridhara Swami, Jiva Goswami, and Vishvanatha Chakravarti each provide distinctive readings of the cosmic form theology.
