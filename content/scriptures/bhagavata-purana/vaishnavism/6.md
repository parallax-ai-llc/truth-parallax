## Overview

The sixth Skandha contains the dramatic story of Ajamila — a fallen Brahmin who is saved from death by accidentally calling the name of Narayana (which he had given to his youngest son). For Vaishnavas, this narrative is the definitive proof-text for the saving power of the divine name (nama), even when uttered without devotional intention.

The Skandha also contains the complex narrative of the Daksha-Narada dispute and the Indra-Vritra battle. The Vritra narrative is particularly rich for Vaishnava theology, as the demon Vritra is portrayed as a great devotee of Vishnu — demonstrating that devotion transcends the categories of 'god' and 'demon' and that birth as a demon is no bar to spiritual attainment.

For Vaishnavas, this canto's central teaching is the supremacy and accessibility of the divine name. If even the accidental utterance of 'Narayana' at the moment of death can save a sinful man like Ajamila, how much more effective is the conscious, devoted repetition of God's names?

## Key Themes

- **Nama-mahima**: The supreme power of the divine name to save
- **Accidental Salvation**: Even inadvertent utterance of Vishnu's name conferring grace
- **Transcendent Devotion**: Vritra as a demon-devotee surpassing the gods in bhakti
- **Nama-aparadha**: The proper and improper uses of the divine name
- **Death and Liberation**: The moment of death as the critical opportunity for liberation

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Ajamila Story (6.1-3)

Vaishnava commentators read Ajamila's salvation as demonstrating the absolute power of Vishnu's name. The Vishnudutas (messengers of Vishnu) defeat the Yamadutas (messengers of death) by arguing that the utterance of 'Narayana' — even accidentally — purifies all sin.

### Vritra's Devotion (6.11-12)

Vritra's moving prayer to Vishnu before his death at Indra's hands is one of the most celebrated devotional passages in the Bhagavata. A demon prays for pure devotion, while the 'god' Indra is consumed by material ambition.

## Commentary Tradition

The Ajamila narrative has generated extensive Vaishnava commentary on the theology of the divine name (nama-tattva), the nature of sin and purification, and the mechanics of salvation at the moment of death. Jiva Goswami's treatment is particularly systematic.
