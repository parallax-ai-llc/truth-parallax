## Overview

The fifth Skandha presents the stories of Rishabha and Bharata alongside an elaborate Puranic cosmography. For Vaishnavas, this canto demonstrates the perils of attachment — even spiritual attachment — and the comprehensive scope of Vishnu's created order.

Rishabha, an avatar of Vishnu who teaches renunciation and asceticism, and Bharata, a devotee-king who falls from realization through attachment to a deer, provide complementary lessons. Rishabha shows the ideal of complete detachment while Bharata demonstrates how even advanced spiritual practitioners can stumble through seemingly innocent attachments.

The cosmographic sections describe the structure of the universe in detail — the various planetary systems, the earthly continents, and the hellish regions. Vaishnavas read this as a map of Vishnu's domain, with each region under his governance and every being within it eligible for his grace.

## Key Themes

- **Dangers of Attachment**: Bharata's fall illustrating the subtlety of material bondage
- **Renunciation**: Rishabha as the model of complete detachment
- **Cosmic Governance**: The cosmography as demonstrating Vishnu's comprehensive sovereignty
- **Vigilance**: The need for constant spiritual awareness even at advanced stages
- **Jada Bharata**: The realized sage appearing as a fool — the transcendence of worldly perception

## Key Verses and Passages

### Bharata and the Deer (5.8-14)

Vaishnava commentators read this as a profound warning: Bharata, despite being a great devotee, became so attached to a baby deer that he was reborn as a deer in his next life. The lesson is that any attachment — even seemingly innocent compassion — can divert one from Vishnu.

### Jada Bharata's Teaching (5.10-13)

After his rebirth as Bharata the 'dull' one, Jada Bharata delivers a powerful teaching to King Rahugana on the nature of the self and the folly of identifying with the body. Vaishnavas read this as demonstrating that true realization transcends all external appearances.

## Commentary Tradition

Vaishnava commentators treat the Bharata narrative as one of the most instructive cautionary tales in the Bhagavata, demonstrating that devotion must be single-pointed and that even compassion for creatures can become a spiritual snare if it replaces devotion to Vishnu.
