## Overview

Shaiva interpreters read the second Skandha's cosmic form teaching through the lens of Shaiva cosmology. The concept of the universe as the body of the Supreme Being resonates with Shaiva theology, particularly the concept of Shiva as Vishvarupa (the universal form) and the Kashmir Shaiva teaching that the entire universe is a manifestation of Shiva's consciousness.

The Virat Purusha meditation described here parallels Shaiva meditation practices that identify the practitioner's body with the cosmic body of Shiva. In both traditions, the universe is experienced not as something separate from the divine but as a direct expression of divine reality.

Shaiva readers note that the concept of the cosmic body is not exclusively Vaishnava — it appears in the Vedic Purusha Sukta, which predates sectarian distinctions, and in numerous Shaiva texts. The Bhagavata's presentation is thus read as one expression of a pan-Hindu theological concept.

## Key Themes

- **Universal Form**: The Virat Purusha as a concept shared across Hindu traditions
- **Cosmic Body Meditation**: Parallels with Shaiva meditation on Shiva's cosmic form
- **Vedic Foundations**: The Purusha Sukta as the common source for cosmic form theology
- **Kashmir Shaiva Parallels**: The universe as Shiva's self-expression (svabhava)
- **Pan-Hindu Theology**: Cosmic form theology as transcending sectarian boundaries

## Key Verses and Passages

### Purusha Sukta Connections

Shaiva interpreters read the Bhagavata's cosmic form meditation in light of the Vedic Purusha Sukta (RV 10.90), which describes the universe as the body of the cosmic Purusha — a figure not identified exclusively with either Vishnu or Shiva in the Vedic context.

### Shiva's Vishvarupa

Shaiva texts describe Shiva's own cosmic form (Vishvarupa), suggesting that the concept of the divine cosmic body is a shared theological category rather than an exclusively Vaishnava one.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva engagement with cosmic form theology draws on the Purusha Sukta tradition, Kashmir Shaiva philosophy (particularly Abhinavagupta's concept of the universe as Shiva's body), and the broader Shaiva theological framework.
