## Overview

Shaiva readers find the fifth Skandha's themes of renunciation and asceticism particularly resonant, as these are central to Shaiva spiritual practice. Rishabha's extreme renunciation — wandering naked, eating minimal food, and remaining indifferent to all worldly concerns — mirrors the practices of Shaiva ascetics, particularly the Pashupata and Kapalika traditions.

The Bharata narrative's warning about attachment is a universal spiritual teaching that Shaiva interpreters apply to their own tradition. The subtlety of spiritual attachment — becoming bound through seemingly virtuous emotions — is a theme emphasized in Shaiva texts as well, where the aspirant is warned against attachment even to spiritual experiences.

The cosmographic sections are engaged by Shaiva readers as part of the shared Hindu cosmological tradition, with many elements paralleling descriptions in Shaiva Puranas.

## Key Themes

- **Ascetic Resonance**: Rishabha's renunciation mirroring Shaiva ascetic ideals
- **Universal Warning**: The Bharata narrative as applicable across traditions
- **Shaiva Asceticism**: Parallels between Rishabha's practices and Pashupata/Kapalika ascetics
- **Subtle Attachment**: The danger of bondage through spiritual emotion
- **Shared Cosmography**: The cosmographic content as common Hindu heritage

## Key Verses and Passages

### Rishabha and Shaiva Ascetics

Shaiva interpreters note the striking parallels between Rishabha's ascetic behavior and Shaiva practices — both traditions valorize radical renunciation as the path to liberation.

### The Subtlety of Bondage

The Bharata narrative resonates with Shaiva teachings on the three malas (impurities) that bind the soul — even seemingly positive emotions can reinforce the fundamental mala of anava (ego) if they strengthen the sense of separate selfhood.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva engagement with this canto focuses on the shared ascetic and renunciant themes rather than the specifically Vaishnava theological framing.
