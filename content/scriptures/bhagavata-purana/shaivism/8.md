## Overview

Shaiva interpreters engage with the eighth Skandha's narratives while noting Shiva's important presence in several of them. In the churning of the cosmic ocean, Shiva plays a crucial role by drinking the deadly poison (halahala) that emerges before the nectar of immortality — an act of cosmic self-sacrifice that saves the universe. This episode is foundational for Shaiva theology: Shiva accepts the poison that would destroy creation, earning the name Nilakantha (Blue-Throated One).

Shaiva readers argue that Shiva's role in the churning narrative — absorbing cosmic poison to save all beings — is a greater act of divine intervention than the other events described in this Skandha. This selfless absorption of cosmic evil represents Shiva's unique function as the deity who takes upon himself the suffering that creation cannot bear.

The Gajendra narrative's teaching on surrender resonates with Shaiva soteriology, where the bound soul (pashu) must ultimately surrender to the Lord (Pashupati) to be freed from bondage.

## Key Themes

- **Shiva's Cosmic Sacrifice**: Nilakantha's absorption of poison as saving all creation
- **Shared Surrender Theology**: Gajendra's prapatti paralleling Shaiva concepts of surrender to Pashupati
- **Shiva in the Churning Narrative**: Shiva's indispensable role even within a Vaishnava text
- **Poison and Grace**: Shiva transforming cosmic evil through his own body
- **Complementary Roles**: Shiva and Vishnu each contributing to cosmic salvation

## Key Verses and Passages

### Shiva Drinks the Poison

Shaiva interpreters highlight this episode as demonstrating Shiva's supreme compassion and power — he alone can absorb the cosmic poison without being destroyed, saving all beings from annihilation. This act of cosmic self-sacrifice has no parallel among the other deities described in the narrative.

### Surrender Across Traditions

The Gajendra narrative's emphasis on complete surrender parallels the Shaiva concept of the soul surrendering to Pashupati as the final step before liberation.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva commentary on the churning narrative gives extensive treatment to Shiva's role, arguing that his acceptance of the poison demonstrates the highest form of divine compassion and power.
