## Overview

Shaiva readers engage with the sixth Skandha's teaching on the power of the divine name with genuine interest, as nama-smarana (remembrance of divine names) is central to Shaiva practice as well. The principle that the divine name carries salvific power transcends sectarian boundaries — Shaivas simply apply it to the names of Shiva rather than Vishnu.

The Vritra narrative, in which a demon surpasses the gods in devotion, resonates with Shaiva traditions that emphasize Shiva's accessibility to all beings regardless of birth or status. Shaiva hagiography includes many accounts of devotees from marginal or despised backgrounds who attained liberation through Shiva's grace.

The Daksha-Narada dispute in this Skandha also resonates with Shaiva tradition, given that Daksha is a figure who appears prominently in Shaiva mythology as well — specifically in the Daksha sacrifice narrative of the Shiva Purana.

## Key Themes

- **Nama-smarana**: The power of divine names as a shared Hindu devotional principle
- **Universal Accessibility**: The divine accessible to all, including demons — a pan-Hindu theme
- **Daksha Connections**: Shared Daksha mythology across Shaiva and Vaishnava Puranas
- **Marginal Devotees**: Vritra compared with outcast devotees in Shaiva hagiography
- **Death and Liberation**: The moment of death as critical — shared across Hindu traditions

## Key Verses and Passages

### The Power of Names

Shaiva interpreters note that the principle demonstrated in the Ajamila story — the salvific power of the divine name — applies equally to Shiva's names. The Shaiva tradition of panchakshara mantra (namah shivaya) recitation rests on the same theological foundation.

### Vritra and Shaiva Saints

Vritra's devotion despite his demonic birth parallels the stories of Shaiva saints from low-caste backgrounds, such as Nandanar and Kannappa, who attained Shiva's grace despite social barriers.

## Commentary Tradition

Shaiva engagement with this canto focuses on the trans-sectarian devotional principles rather than the specifically Vaishnava theological framework.
