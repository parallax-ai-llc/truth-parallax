The bow-and-arrow metaphor of Chapter 2 receives a distinctive interpretation in Shaiva thought. The arrow (self) aimed at the target (Brahman) using the bow (Om) describes the concentrated movement of awareness toward its own source. In Kashmir Shaivism, this is not a journey from here to there but the retraction of awareness from its outward-flowing tendencies back into its own ground — a process called *nimeshana* (closing, or the inward turn of consciousness).

The chapter's teaching that Brahman is "that in which heaven, earth, and sky are woven, together with the mind and all the vital breaths" (2.2.5) expresses the Shaiva understanding of consciousness as the substratum of all experience. Space, time, and all mental functions are not independent realities but modes of consciousness's self-expression. Recognizing this transforms the world from an obstacle to a revelation.
