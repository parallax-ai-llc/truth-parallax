The Shaiva reading of Chapter 1 identifies higher knowledge (*para vidya*) with the direct recognition of Shiva-consciousness. All discursive knowledge, including mastery of the Vedas and their auxiliaries, constitutes lower knowledge because it operates through concepts and language — tools of the finite mind. Higher knowledge is the non-conceptual recognition (*pratyabhijna*) of one's identity with the infinite consciousness that pervades all things.

The emanation metaphor — beings arising from Brahman like sparks from fire (1.1.7) — is read through the Kashmir Shaiva doctrine of *abhasa* (manifestation). The sparks are real emanations of Shiva's creative power, not illusions. Each spark is consciousness appearing in a particular form, and recognizing this transforms one's relationship to the world from bondage to freedom. The critique of empty ritual (1.2.7-10) resonates with the Shaiva emphasis on interior awareness over external ceremony.
