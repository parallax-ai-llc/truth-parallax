The two birds parable in Chapter 3 receives a characteristically non-dual reading in Shaiva thought. The two birds are not two separate beings but two aspects of one consciousness: the aspect involved in experience (the eating bird) and the aspect that remains as pure witness (the watching bird). Liberation occurs when the eating bird "recognizes" the watching bird — not as another being but as its own deeper nature.

The Mundaka's declaration that the knower of Brahman "becomes Brahman" (3.2.9) is read by Shaivas as confirming the non-dual teaching: the aspirant does not become something new but recognizes what they have always been — Paramashiva, the absolute consciousness. The closing verse's promise that those who know Brahman are "freed from name and form and attain the supreme" (3.2.8) describes the dissolution of the contracted self-sense in the infinite expanse of Shiva-consciousness.
