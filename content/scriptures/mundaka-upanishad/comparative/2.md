The bow-and-arrow metaphor invites comparison with contemplative imagery across traditions. The Zen practice of archery (*kyudo*) similarly uses the archer's art as a metaphor for spiritual practice — the arrow released from the bow of discipline flies to the target of enlightenment when the archer achieves a state of "no-mind." The Christian mystic John of the Cross used the image of the "living flame of love" penetrating the soul, while the Sufi poet Rumi described the heart as an arrow aimed at the Beloved.

The chapter's teaching that all things are "woven" into Brahman (2.2.5) resonates with the mystical concept of the web of interconnection — Indra's net in Buddhism, the Kabbalistic concept of divine sparks pervading all matter, and the Lakota concept of *mitakuye oyasin* ("all my relations"). Each tradition recognizes that the fabric of reality is unified at a level deeper than surface appearances.
