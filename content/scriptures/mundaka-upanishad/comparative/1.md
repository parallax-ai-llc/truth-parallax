The Mundaka Upanishad's distinction between higher and lower knowledge resonates across world traditions. The Sufi distinction between *ilm* (intellectual knowledge) and *ma'rifa* (gnosis/direct knowledge of God), the Christian distinction between *theologia* (rational theology) and *theosis* (experiential deification), and the Buddhist distinction between *pariyatti* (study) and *patipatti* (practice) all recognize that conceptual mastery of religious texts differs fundamentally from direct spiritual realization.

The critique of ritual as insufficient for liberation parallels the Hebrew prophets' critique of sacrifice without justice (Amos 5:21-24, Isaiah 1:11-17), Jesus's teaching that inner purity matters more than external observance (Mark 7:15), and the Quran's declaration that "It is not their meat nor their blood that reaches God; it is your piety" (22:37). The universal pattern suggests that every tradition, at some point, must confront the limitations of its own external practices.
