Shankara reads Chapter 2's bow-and-arrow metaphor as describing the practice of *upasana* (meditative worship) that prepares the mind for direct knowledge. The self aimed at Brahman through Om is the individual consciousness concentrated to a single point, and "hitting the target" is the moment when the distinction between the meditator and Brahman dissolves in non-dual recognition.

The chapter's assertion that Brahman is "that in which all this is woven" (2.2.5) is read by Shankara as the classic Advaita teaching that Brahman is the sole substratum (*adhishthana*) of the phenomenal world. The world of names and forms is woven into Brahman the way a snake is superimposed on a rope — it appears real but has no independent existence. Knowing the rope dissolves the snake; knowing Brahman dissolves the world as a separately existing reality.
