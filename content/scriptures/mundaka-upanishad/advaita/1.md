Shankara's commentary on Chapter 1 treats the *para/apara vidya* distinction as the organizing principle of the entire Upanishad. Lower knowledge encompasses all mediate, conceptual, and empirical forms of learning — including the Vedas themselves when studied as objects of scholarship. Higher knowledge is the immediate, non-conceptual realization of Brahman as one's own self. This distinction is foundational to Advaita's hierarchy of knowledge.

The critique of ritual practitioners as "fools" and their sacrifices as "leaky boats" (1.2.7) is read by Shankara as establishing that no amount of action — however meritorious — can produce liberation. Action produces finite results within the realm of samsara; only knowledge (*jnana*) can reveal the infinite Brahman that transcends all cause and effect. The sparks-from-fire analogy (1.1.7) is read as *vivartavada* — the world appears to emanate from Brahman but does not alter Brahman's nature, just as sparks do not diminish the fire.
