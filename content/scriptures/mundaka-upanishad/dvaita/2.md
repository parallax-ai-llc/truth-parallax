The bow-and-arrow metaphor is read by Dvaita as describing focused devotional meditation aimed at Vishnu. The self is the arrow, Om is the bow, and Vishnu is the target. The "hitting" of the target means achieving the Lord's presence — not identity with Him but proximate, blissful relationship. The arrow does not become the target; it reaches the target. This distinction is crucial for Dvaita's rejection of Advaita's identity-reading.

The chapter's cosmogonic descriptions (2.1.1-3) are taken literally: Vishnu creates the universe — life, mind, senses, elements — as a real production, not an illusory appearance. The created universe genuinely exists and genuinely differs from its creator. Madhva emphasizes that Brahman is both the efficient cause (the potter) and the material cause (the clay) of creation, but this does not mean creation is identical with the creator.
