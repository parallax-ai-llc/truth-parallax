## Overview

The Kena Upanishad belongs to the Sama Veda and takes its name from the opening word *kena* ("by whom?"). It asks the fundamental question: Who or what powers the mind, the breath, speech, and the senses? The answer points beyond all empirical faculties to Brahman — the ultimate reality that cannot be grasped by thought or speech yet is the power behind all thought and speech.

## Structure

The Kena Upanishad's four chapters divide into two distinct sections:

1. **Chapters 1-2 (Verse)**: Philosophical inquiry into Brahman as the power behind the senses and mind
2. **Chapters 3-4 (Prose)**: The parable of the gods humbled before Brahman, with the goddess Uma as revealer

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: Brahman is identified with Vishnu as the power behind all faculties
- **Shaivism**: The humbling of the gods parallels Shaiva teachings on consciousness transcending all limited powers
- **Advaita Vedanta**: A key source for the teaching that Brahman is the witness-consciousness behind all experience
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Demonstrates God's absolute sovereignty over even the greatest celestial beings
- **Academic**: Important evidence for the development of Upanishadic epistemology
- **Comparative**: The parable of divine humility resonates with wisdom traditions worldwide

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Unknown Knower | Brahman as the subject that can never become an object |
| Limits of Knowledge | The mind cannot reach Brahman through ordinary cognition |
| Divine Humility | The gods' pride is shattered when they encounter Brahman directly |
| Uma as Revealer | The feminine divine as the source of theological knowledge |
| Power Behind Powers | Every faculty derives its capacity from Brahman |
