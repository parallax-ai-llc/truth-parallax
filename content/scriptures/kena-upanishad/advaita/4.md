Shankara's reading of Uma's revelation in Chapter 4 treats her as a personification of *Brahma-vidya* (knowledge of Brahman). Her role as intermediary between the unknowable Yaksha and the gods represents the tradition of teaching (*sampradaya*) through which self-knowledge is transmitted. The guru, like Uma, reveals what the student cannot discover through independent investigation.

The chapter's closing simile — Brahman flashes forth like lightning (4.4) — is interpreted by Shankara as describing the sudden, non-gradual nature of self-realization. Just as lightning illuminates the entire landscape in an instant, Brahman-knowledge is not a progressive accumulation of information but an instantaneous recognition of what was always already the case. The chapter's final verses, establishing the practice of austerity, self-control, and Vedic study as means to this realization, describe the preparatory disciplines (*sadhana*) that make the mind fit for the flash of recognition.
