Shankara's commentary on Chapter 1 identifies its central teaching as the distinction between Brahman (the ultimate subject) and everything else (objects). The opening question "By whom is the mind directed?" is not answered by naming another entity but by pointing to the unknowable ground of all knowing. Brahman is not another agent directing the mind from outside but the very awareness in which the mind's activity appears.

The key verse "That which is not expressed by speech, but by which speech is expressed — know that alone as Brahman" (1.5) establishes the Advaita principle that Brahman can never be objectified. Every attempt to describe, define, or conceptualize Brahman produces only another mental object, not Brahman itself. Shankara uses this to argue that Brahman is *svaprakasha* (self-luminous) — it is pure awareness that is self-evident and needs no proof, just as the sun needs no lamp to be illuminated.
