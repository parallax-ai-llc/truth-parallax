Chapter 2's paradox — "It is known to those who do not know it; those who know it do not know it" (2.3) — receives elaborate treatment from Shankara. He explains that Brahman is not "unknown" in the sense of being completely inaccessible, nor "known" in the sense of being grasped by the intellect. It is self-evident (*aparoksha*) awareness that is recognized only when all attempts at objective knowledge are abandoned.

Shankara distinguishes between *vritti jnana* (conceptual knowledge, which is always dualistic) and *svarupa jnana* (essential knowledge, which is Brahman's own self-luminosity). The sage does not "know Brahman" as an object but abides as Brahman. The chapter's statement that Brahman is realized "in every state of awareness" (2.4) is read by Shankara as pointing to the *sakshi* (witness-consciousness) that is present equally in waking, dreaming, and deep sleep — the unchanging awareness that is Brahman itself.
