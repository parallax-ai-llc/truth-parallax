## Overview

Shankara wrote two commentaries on the Kena Upanishad — a word-by-word *bhashya* and a more elaborate *vakya bhashya* — reflecting its importance for Advaita. The Upanishad's teaching that Brahman is "that which is not thought by the mind but by which the mind thinks" (1.5) is foundational to the Advaita doctrine of Brahman as the eternal witness-subject (*sakshi*) that can never become an object of knowledge.

## Theological Emphases

Shankara reads the Kena Upanishad's negative formulations — Brahman is not what people worship as "this" (1.4-9) — as employing the method of *neti neti* ("not this, not this"). Brahman cannot be defined by any positive predicate because it is the very ground of all predication. The famous verse 2.3 — "It is known to those who do not know it; those who know it do not know it" — expresses the Advaita paradox that Brahman is self-evident (*svaprakasha*) and therefore cannot be "known" as an object distinct from the knower.
