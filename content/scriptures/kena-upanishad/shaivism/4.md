Uma's appearance in Chapter 4 as the revealer of Brahman's identity holds profound significance for Shaiva theology. Uma is Shakti — the dynamic, self-revelatory aspect of consciousness. That Brahman (Shiva as transcendent awareness) disappears and Uma appears to deliver knowledge is read as expressing the fundamental Shaiva principle: Shiva is known only through Shakti. Pure transcendence, without the revelatory power of Shakti, remains inaccessible.

Kashmir Shaivism identifies Uma with *vimarsha* — the self-reflective awareness that makes consciousness aware of itself. Without vimarsha, pure luminosity (*prakasha*) would be inert light with no self-knowledge. Uma's revelation to Indra models the moment of recognition (*pratyabhijna*) when the aspirant, through the grace of Shakti, recognizes their own nature as Shiva. The chapter's closing description of Brahman as flashing forth like lightning (4.4) evokes the sudden, illuminating nature of this recognition.
