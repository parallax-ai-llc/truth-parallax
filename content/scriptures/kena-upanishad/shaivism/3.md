The parable of the gods in Chapter 3 is a favorite of Shaiva commentators. Brahman appears as a mysterious Yaksha whom the gods cannot identify — a perfect illustration of consciousness's self-concealment (*tirodhana*). The gods, representing various cosmic powers (fire, wind, sovereignty), attempt to assert their independent capacity and fail utterly. Agni cannot burn a blade of grass; Vayu cannot move it.

Shaiva interpreters read this as demonstrating that all powers (*shaktis*) in the universe are derivative from the one supreme Shakti of Paramashiva. When consciousness withdraws its enabling power, the gods' faculties are rendered inert. This is not punishment but revelation — by demonstrating the gods' dependence, Brahman reveals itself as the sole autonomous reality. The gods' humiliation is the prerequisite for their awakening to the truth of their own source.
