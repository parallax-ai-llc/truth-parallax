Chapter 2's paradoxical teaching — that Brahman is known to those who do not know it (2.3) — is especially resonant with Kashmir Shaivism's understanding of recognition (*pratyabhijna*). To "know" Brahman in the ordinary sense of subject grasping an object is impossible, because Brahman is the subject itself. The one who claims to know Brahman has reduced it to an object and therefore does not truly know. The one who "does not know" has recognized that Brahman exceeds all objectification.

For Shaiva interpreters, this paradox resolves in the experience of *sahaja* — natural, effortless abidance in one's true nature as consciousness. Brahman is not something to be found or achieved but recognized as always already present. The chapter's conclusion — that Brahman is realized in every flash of awareness (2.4) — confirms the Shaiva teaching that every moment of cognition is a moment of Shiva's self-recognition, typically overlooked due to habitual extroversion.
