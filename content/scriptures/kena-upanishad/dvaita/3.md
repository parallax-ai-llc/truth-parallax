The parable of the gods' humiliation in Chapter 3 is central to Dvaita's hierarchical theology. Agni, Vayu, and Indra represent the highest levels of the cosmic hierarchy, yet they are utterly powerless before Brahman. This demonstrates the Dvaita principle of *taratamya* — the gradation of beings from the lowest to the highest, with Vishnu standing infinitely above all.

Madhva emphasizes that the gods' failure is not merely cognitive (they cannot identify Brahman) but also practical (they cannot exercise their own powers). Agni cannot burn, Vayu cannot blow — their capacities are entirely dependent on divine will. This illustrates the Dvaita teaching that every being's power is derived from and sanctioned by Vishnu. Independence (*svatantra*) belongs to God alone; all others are dependent (*paratantra*).
