Madhva interprets Chapter 2's paradox — "known to those who do not know it" (2.3) — as teaching intellectual humility rather than a non-dual epistemology. Those who claim complete knowledge of God are deluded by pride; those who recognize the infinite depth of God's nature, knowing that their knowledge is always partial, are closer to truth. This is not a paradox about the impossibility of all knowledge but about the impossibility of exhaustive knowledge.

For Dvaita, Brahman is genuinely knowable through scripture, devotion, and grace — but never completely. Even liberated souls, who enjoy eternal proximity to Vishnu in Vaikuntha, continue to grow in their knowledge of God's infinite nature. The statement that Brahman is realized "in every state of awareness" (2.4) is read as affirming Vishnu's omnipresence rather than the identity of awareness with Brahman.
