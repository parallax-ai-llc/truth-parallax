## Overview

The Kena Upanishad's teaching on the unknowability of the ultimate reality resonates with apophatic (negative) theology across traditions. Its declaration that Brahman is "not what people worship as this" parallels the Christian apophatic tradition (Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart), the Islamic concept of God's incomparability (*tanzih*), and the Taoist teaching that "the Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao."

## Comparative Themes

The parable of the gods humbled before Brahman (Chapters 3-4) finds parallels in traditions that emphasize divine transcendence over celestial beings — the Quran's account of the angels prostrating before Adam, the Book of Job's divine speech silencing human presumption. The figure of Uma as the feminine revealer of divine knowledge resonates with Sophia (Wisdom) in Jewish and Christian traditions, the Shekinah in Kabbalah, and the role of Fatimah in Sufi thought as a bridge between the human and divine.
