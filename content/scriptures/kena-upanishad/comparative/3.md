The parable of the gods humbled before Brahman in Chapter 3 resonates with narratives of divine humility across traditions. In the Hebrew Bible, God appears to Job out of a whirlwind and humbles him with questions about creation's mysteries (Job 38-41). In the Quran, the angels are commanded to prostrate before Adam, acknowledging a wisdom they do not possess (2:34). In Zen Buddhism, the master's blows and shouts shatter the student's intellectual pretensions.

The specific motif of natural elements failing to function — fire that cannot burn, wind that cannot blow — inverts the typical theophany where God demonstrates power through natural forces (the burning bush, the parting of the Red Sea). Here, God demonstrates power precisely by *withdrawing* the capacity of natural forces, revealing that their ordinary functioning is itself a divine gift. This "negative theophany" offers a distinctive approach to revealing divine sovereignty.
