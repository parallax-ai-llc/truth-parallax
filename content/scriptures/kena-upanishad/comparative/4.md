Uma's role as revealer of Brahman in Chapter 4 connects with the widespread association of feminine figures with divine wisdom across world traditions. The Hebrew *Hokmah* (Wisdom) in Proverbs 8 was present at creation and delights in humanity; the Greek *Sophia* was adopted by Gnostic and early Christian traditions as a mediator of divine knowledge; the Tibetan Buddhist *Prajnaparamita* (Perfection of Wisdom) is personified as the "Mother of all Buddhas."

The common thread is that ultimate knowledge — particularly knowledge of the divine — is mediated through a feminine principle that bridges the gap between transcendence and the seeker. Uma does not simply point to Brahman as an external reality; she embodies the revelatory power through which the unknowable becomes known. This pattern suggests a deep structural intuition across cultures: that wisdom is not aggressive conquest of truth but receptive opening to disclosure.
