Chapter 2's teaching that Brahman "is known to those who do not know it" parallels the apophatic traditions found across religions. The Christian mystical tradition, from Pseudo-Dionysius through the anonymous author of *The Cloud of Unknowing* to Meister Eckhart, similarly teaches that God is best known through "unknowing" — the surrender of conceptual grasping. The Sufi concept of *fana* (annihilation of the ego) likewise suggests that the divine is encountered only when the self's claims to knowledge are dissolved.

The Taoist tradition offers perhaps the closest parallel: "The Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao" (Tao Te Ching, Chapter 1). Both the Kena Upanishad and the Tao Te Ching insist that ultimate reality exceeds the reach of language and conceptual thought, yet is intimately present in every moment of experience. The difference lies in the Upanishad's more explicitly epistemological framing — it analyzes the structure of knowing itself.
