Chapter 2's paradox about knowing and not-knowing (2.3) has generated extensive scholarly analysis. Some scholars read it as a genuine epistemological paradox anticipating later Madhyamaka Buddhist logic, while others see it as a rhetorical device designed to push the student beyond conceptual thinking. Paul Deussen compared it with Socrates' "I know that I know nothing," noting both the structural similarity and the different metaphysical contexts.

The chapter's verse form and abstract philosophical vocabulary place it among the more developed portions of the Upanishadic corpus, suggesting a date later than the earliest prose Upanishads (Brihadaranyaka, Chandogya). Scholars note that the teaching on Brahman as accessible "in every flash of awareness" (2.4) represents a democratization of spiritual knowledge — Brahman is not restricted to ritual contexts but available in ordinary experience.
