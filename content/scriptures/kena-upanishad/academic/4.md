The figure of Uma Haimavati in Chapter 4 has been a subject of significant scholarly interest. Her appearance as the revealer of divine knowledge represents one of the earliest instances in Upanishadic literature of a feminine divine figure playing a positive theological role. Scholars connect her with the broader tradition of *vidya* (knowledge) personified as feminine — a tradition that extends from the Vedic Vak (Speech) through later Shakta theology.

The chapter's closing section, which describes Brahman through natural analogies — lightning, the blinking of an eye — and prescribes austerity and self-control as means of realization, may represent an editorial conclusion designed to integrate the philosophical and narrative sections into a coherent teaching. Scholars note the shift from the abstract Brahman of Chapters 1-2 to the more concrete, almost personal deity of the parable, reflecting a broader tension in Upanishadic thought.
