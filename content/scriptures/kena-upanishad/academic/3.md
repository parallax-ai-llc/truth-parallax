The prose narrative of Chapter 3, with its vivid parable of the gods and the mysterious Yaksha, represents a different literary genre from the verse portions. Scholars debate whether this narrative was originally independent — perhaps a folk tale or mythological fragment — later attached to the philosophical verses of Chapters 1-2. The story's structure (sequential testing and failure) follows common folktale patterns found across Indo-European narrative traditions.

The blade of grass that Agni cannot burn and Vayu cannot move has been analyzed as a symbol of the "least significant" natural object, chosen precisely to maximize the contrast between the gods' cosmic power and their actual impotence before Brahman. Some scholars see this as reflecting a critique of the Vedic fire-sacrifice tradition, subtly undermining Agni's ritual supremacy.
