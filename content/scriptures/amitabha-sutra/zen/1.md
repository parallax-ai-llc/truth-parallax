Zen's reading of the Amitabha Sutra transforms its devotional content into a vehicle for awakening. The sutra's description of Sukhavati becomes a description of the awakened mind: the jeweled trees are the virtues spontaneously expressed by one who has realized their true nature, the lotus pools are the clarity of undisturbed awareness, and the celestial music is the dharma heard in every moment of experience. The call to recite Amitabha's name with single-minded concentration for one to seven days is understood as a meditation instruction: when the practitioner's mind becomes completely unified through recitation, the distinction between self and Buddha dissolves, and the Pure Land is realized here and now. This Zen reading preserves the sutra's practical urgency while reinterpreting its cosmological claims as descriptions of the mind's own nature.
