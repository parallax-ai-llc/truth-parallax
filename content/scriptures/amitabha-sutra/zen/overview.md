## Overview

The Amitabha Sutra's call for single-minded, undistracted recitation of the Buddha's name intersects with Zen's emphasis on concentrated awareness. In Chinese Chan monasteries, the sutra has been chanted for centuries alongside seated meditation, and the practice of nianfo has been integrated into Chan training as a complement to — or even a form of — meditation.

## Concentrated Recitation as Meditation

The sutra prescribes holding Amitabha's name with undistracted mind for one to seven days until the mind becomes unified. Chan practitioners have understood this as a description of samadhi achieved through verbal recitation — a concentration practice that, at its deepest level, dissolves the boundary between reciter and recited, self and Buddha.

## Ouyi Zhixu's Commentary

The Ming dynasty master Ouyi Zhixu (1599–1655), who practiced both Chan and Pure Land, wrote an influential commentary reading the Amitabha Sutra as a profound meditation manual. He taught that the sutra's Pure Land is the mind's true nature, and that reciting the Buddha's name is a direct method for realizing that nature.
