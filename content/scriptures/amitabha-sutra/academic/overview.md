## Overview

Academic study of the Amitabha Sutra has focused on its textual history, its role in East Asian Buddhist liturgy, and its significance for understanding popular Buddhist devotion. Kumarajiva's Chinese translation (402 CE) is the most widely used version, and scholars have compared it with Xuanzang's later translation and the surviving Sanskrit text.

## Translation Studies

Comparison of Kumarajiva's and Xuanzang's translations reveals significant differences in terminology and emphasis. Kumarajiva's more fluid, literary style contributed to the sutra's popularity, while Xuanzang's more technically precise translation is valued for philological study. The relationship between these Chinese versions and the Sanskrit original illuminates the dynamics of Buddhist textual transmission.

## Popular Buddhism

The Amitabha Sutra's role in daily monastic and lay liturgy makes it a key text for understanding the lived religion of East Asian Buddhism. Scholars such as Daniel Stevenson and Charles Jones have examined how its recitation functions as both personal devotion and communal ritual, shaping Buddhist identity and community.
