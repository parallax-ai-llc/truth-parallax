## Tibetan Buddhist Perspective

This chapter presents sixteen discourses considered among the most ancient in the Pali Canon, characterized by a radical non-attachment to views (*ditthi*) and an uncompromising insistence that the sage does not take up any fixed position in the world. While the Sutta Nipata is a Pali Canon text not directly included in the Tibetan Kangyur, Tibetan Buddhism engages with its teachings through parallel Agama traditions and through the recognition that early Buddhist teachings form the indispensable foundation of the entire graduated path (*lamrim*).

The ethical teachings and renunciation practices described in this chapter correspond to what Tibetan tradition classifies as the teachings of the initial and intermediate scope — the cultivation of ethical discipline, the recognition of the faults of cyclic existence, and the development of the determination to be free. These foundational practices are prerequisites for the higher Mahayana and Vajrayana paths.

Tibetan masters also recognize the philosophical sophistication of these early verses, particularly their treatment of non-attachment to views, as consistent with the Prasangika Madhyamaka approach championed by Chandrakirti and Tsongkhapa. The refusal to establish any fixed position is understood not as nihilism but as a direct expression of the emptiness of all phenomena — a teaching that the Tibetan tradition inherits and develops with extraordinary rigor.
