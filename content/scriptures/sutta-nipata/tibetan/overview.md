## Overview

Although the Sutta Nipata is a Pali text and not directly part of the Tibetan canonical collection, Tibetan Buddhism engages with its teachings through parallel Agama traditions preserved in the Kangyur and through the broader recognition that early Buddhist teachings form the foundation upon which the entire lamrim (graduated path) is built.

## Foundational Teachings

The Sutta Nipata's emphasis on renunciation, ethical conduct, and the cultivation of loving-kindness corresponds to the initial scope (*skyes bu chung ngu*) and intermediate scope (*skyes bu 'bring*) teachings in the Tibetan lamrim tradition. Tsongkhapa's *Lamrim Chenmo* draws on early Buddhist teachings about the faults of samsara and the benefits of renunciation that are vividly expressed in the Sutta Nipata's verses.

## Metta and Bodhicitta

The Metta Sutta's teaching on universal loving-kindness is recognized in Tibetan tradition as a precursor to the full development of *bodhicitta* (the awakening mind). Tibetan masters distinguish between the Hinayana practice of *metta* as a meditative cultivation and the Mahayana transformation of loving-kindness into the aspiration to attain Buddhahood for the sake of all beings — but they affirm the continuity between them.

## Scholarly Interest

Tibetan scholars trained in the *shedra* (monastic college) system sometimes study the Sutta Nipata in comparative contexts, noting the parallels between Pali and Agama versions of the Buddha's discourses. This comparative work enriches the understanding of how Buddhist teachings were transmitted and transformed as they moved from India into Tibet.
