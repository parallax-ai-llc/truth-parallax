## Theravada Reading

This chapter of the Sutta Nipata presents twelve discourses on the theme of shedding attachments, opening with the celebrated simile of the snake shedding its old skin — just as the snake abandons its worn-out skin, so the practitioner abandons this shore and the far shore, shedding all views and attachments. Within the Theravada tradition, these verses are revered as among the earliest and most authentic records of the Buddha's teaching, preserving his voice in a form that predates the systematic elaboration of the Abhidhamma and the commentarial tradition.

The commentator Buddhaghosa (5th century CE) provided extensive explanations of these verses in the Paramatthajotika, connecting the often terse and allusive poetry to the broader doctrinal framework of Theravada Buddhism. The canonical Niddesa commentary, attributed to Sariputta, offers a word-by-word analysis of the Atthaka Vagga and Parayana Vagga, confirming their ancient status and central importance within the tradition.

In Theravada monastic practice, verses from this chapter are regularly chanted in paritta (protective recitation) ceremonies, meditation retreats, and daily liturgy. The teachings contained here — on renunciation, ethical conduct, mindfulness, and the abandonment of craving — form the practical heart of Theravada Buddhist life.
