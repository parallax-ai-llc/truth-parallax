---
title: "Sutta Nipata - Chapter 2"
scripture: "sutta-nipata"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Whoever destroys living beings, speaks untruth, takes in the world what is not given, goes to another's wife, and is addicted to intoxicating drinks — such a one digs up his own root in this very world.

**2** Know this, O good man: evil things are difficult to control. Let not greed and wickedness drag you along to protracted misery.

**3** People give alms according to their faith and their pleasure. Therefore, he who frets about the food and drink given to others will find no peace either by day or by night.

**4** But he in whom that feeling is cut off, uprooted, and destroyed, finds peace by day and by night.

**5** There is no fire like passion, no grip like hatred, no net like delusion, no river like craving.

**6** The fault of others is easily noticed, but one's own is difficult to see. A man winnows his neighbour's faults like chaff, but conceals his own, as a crafty cheat conceals a losing throw.

**7** He who is ever mindful of the faults of others, and is irritable, his own defilements increase. He is far from the destruction of defilements.

**8** There is no track in the sky, and no monk outside the teaching of the Buddha. Mankind delights in worldly pleasures. The Tathagatas are free from worldly things.

**9** There is no track in the sky, and no monk outside the teaching of the Buddha. There are no conditioned things that are eternal. There is no instability in the Buddhas.

**10** Let one not neglect one's own welfare for the sake of another, however great. Clearly understanding one's own welfare, let one be intent upon the good.

**11** The world is afflicted by death and decay. But the wise do not grieve, having understood the nature of the world.

**12** Him I call a Brahmin who has traversed this miry road, this difficult path of defilements and samsara, who has crossed and gone beyond, who is meditative, free from craving and doubt, who, clinging to nothing, has attained Nibbana.

**13** Him I call a Brahmin who in this world has laid aside weapons and renounced violence toward all creatures. He neither kills nor helps others to kill.

**14** Him I call a Brahmin who is friendly among the hostile, who is peaceful among the violent, and who is unattached among the attached.

**15** Him I call a Brahmin from whom anger, hatred, pride, and envy have dropped, like a mustard seed from the point of a needle.

**16** Him I call a Brahmin who speaks gentle, instructive, and truthful words, by which he offends no one.

**17** Him I call a Brahmin who takes nothing in the world that is not given, be it long or short, small or great, good or bad.

**18** Him I call a Brahmin who has no desires for this world or the next, who is free from desires, who has freed himself.

**19** Him I call a Brahmin who has no longings, who through knowledge is free from doubts, who has reached the depths of the deathless.

**20** Him I call a Brahmin who has gone beyond good and evil, who is free from grief, free from defilements, and pure.
