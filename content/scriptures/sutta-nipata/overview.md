## Overview

The Sutta Nipata (*Discourse Collection*) is one of the oldest texts in the Pali Canon, belonging to the Khuddaka Nikaya of the Sutta Pitaka. Scholars widely regard many of its verses as preserving the earliest stratum of Buddhist teaching, predating the systematization found in later Theravada literature. Its language is archaic, its poetry spare and direct, and its ethical vision uncompromising.

## Structure

The Sutta Nipata is organized into five chapters (*vagga*):

1. **Uraga Vagga** (The Snake Chapter): Twelve discourses on shedding attachments, beginning with the famous simile of the snake shedding its skin
2. **Cula Vagga** (The Small Chapter): Fourteen discourses on ethical conduct, right livelihood, and social relations
3. **Maha Vagga** (The Great Chapter): Twelve discourses including the celebrated Metta Sutta (Discourse on Loving-Kindness)
4. **Atthaka Vagga** (The Chapter of Eights): Sixteen discourses on non-attachment to views, considered among the most ancient Buddhist texts
5. **Parayana Vagga** (The Way Beyond): Eighteen discourses framed as questions from brahmin students to the Buddha

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Theravada**: A canonical Pali text, central to the study of early Buddhism and regularly chanted in Theravada monasteries
- **Mahayana**: Recognized as preserving early teachings that prefigure Mahayana themes of emptiness and non-attachment to views
- **Zen**: The directness and anti-dogmatic spirit of the Atthaka Vagga resonates with Zen emphasis on immediate experience
- **Academic**: One of the most important texts for reconstructing the teachings of the historical Buddha

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Renunciation | Letting go of worldly attachments and the householder's life |
| Compassion | Universal loving-kindness (*metta*) extended to all beings |
| Non-attachment to Views | Clinging to doctrines is itself a fetter — a theme central to the Atthaka Vagga |
| Ethics | Right conduct, harmlessness, and truthful speech as foundations of the path |
| Direct Inquiry | The Buddha's dialogues model a method of direct questioning and investigation |
| The Sage Ideal | The *muni* — the silent sage who has crossed over — as the model practitioner |
