## Mahayana Perspective

This chapter presents twelve major discourses including the celebrated Metta Sutta (Discourse on Loving-Kindness), the Khaggavisana Sutta (Rhinoceros Horn Discourse on solitary practice), and teachings on the sage's relationship to the world. Mahayana interpreters engage with these early teachings with deep respect, recognizing in them the seeds of insights that the Mahayana tradition would later develop into comprehensive philosophical systems and the bodhisattva path.

The radical non-attachment to views expressed in these verses — particularly in the Atthaka Vagga — anticipates the Madhyamaka dialectic of Nagarjuna, who demonstrated through rigorous logic what these early verses express poetically: that clinging to any conceptual position, including the position of emptiness itself, is a subtle form of bondage. The Mahayana reading sees these early teachings not as superseded but as foundational.

The chapter's emphasis on compassion and the welfare of all beings is understood within the Mahayana framework as an early expression of the bodhisattva ideal. While the Sutta Nipata does not employ Mahayana terminology, its ethical vision — particularly the Metta Sutta's call for boundless loving-kindness — is embraced as fully consonant with the Mahayana aspiration to liberate all sentient beings.
