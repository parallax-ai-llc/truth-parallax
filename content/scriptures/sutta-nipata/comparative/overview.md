## Overview

The Sutta Nipata, as one of the earliest Buddhist texts, offers a valuable point of comparison with other ancient ethical, philosophical, and religious traditions. Its themes of renunciation, non-attachment, compassion, and the ideal of the wandering sage find striking parallels across cultures.

## The Wandering Sage Tradition

The Sutta Nipata's portrait of the *muni* (silent sage) and the *samana* (wandering ascetic) belongs to a broader phenomenon of the Axial Age (approximately 800–200 BCE), during which similar ideals emerged independently across Eurasia. The *muni* of the Sutta Nipata may be compared with the Cynic and Stoic sages of Greece, the Daoist immortals of China, and the Upanishadic renunciants of India. Each tradition produced a model of the wise person who has transcended social convention, worldly attachment, and the fear of death.

## The Metta Sutta and Universal Love

The Metta Sutta's teaching on boundless loving-kindness invites comparison with other traditions of universal love: the Christian *agape* (unconditional love), the Confucian *ren* (benevolence), and the Mohist *jian ai* (impartial care). While each concept differs in its philosophical underpinnings and practical expression, the convergence across traditions suggests a deep human recognition that moral maturity involves extending care beyond the boundaries of family, tribe, and nation.

## Non-Attachment to Views and Philosophical Skepticism

The Atthaka Vagga's radical non-attachment to views has been compared to the Greek skeptical traditions — Pyrrhonism in particular — as well as to the *wu wei* (non-action) of Daoism and the *neti neti* (not this, not this) of Upanishadic inquiry. Each of these traditions, in its own way, recognizes that conceptual fixation is an obstacle to genuine understanding, and that wisdom involves a certain kind of letting go of the need to be right.
