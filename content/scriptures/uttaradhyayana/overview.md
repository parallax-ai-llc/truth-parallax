## Overview

The Uttaradhyayana Sutra (Uttarādhyayana Sūtra, "Last Discourses") is one of the most beloved and widely studied texts in the Jain canon. Traditionally regarded as containing the final teachings delivered by Lord Mahavira before his liberation (nirvana), it combines ethical instruction, philosophical exposition, narrative parables, and devotional hymns into a comprehensive guide for the monastic and spiritual life.

## Structure

The text is organized into 36 chapters covering a wide range of topics:

| Chapters | Theme |
|----------|-------|
| 1-5 | Exhortations to the monastic life, impermanence, and the rarity of human birth |
| 6-10 | Stories of exemplary monks and their renunciation |
| 11-15 | Ethical teachings on non-violence, truthfulness, and self-restraint |
| 16-20 | The nature of karma, bondage, and the ten virtues |
| 21-25 | Cosmology, the different realms of existence, and samara |
| 26-30 | Doctrinal teachings on the soul, liberation, and right conduct |
| 31-36 | Final teachings, the qualities of the liberated soul, and Mahavira's last days |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Digambara**: Respect the text while maintaining their own version of Mahavira's final teachings
- **Shvetambara**: Treasure it as containing Mahavira's most intimate instructions
- **Academic**: A critical source for understanding Jain ethics and monasticism
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with the Buddha's Mahaparinibbana Sutta as "last teachings"

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Final Teachings | Mahavira's last instructions before liberation |
| Renunciation | The supreme value of leaving worldly life |
| Non-Violence | Ahimsa in thought, word, and deed |
| Karma and Liberation | How to shed karmic bondage and attain moksha |
| Exemplary Lives | Stories of monks and nuns who achieved liberation |
| The Ten Virtues | Supreme forgiveness, humility, uprightness, contentment, truthfulness, self-restraint, austerity, renunciation, non-attachment, and celibacy |
