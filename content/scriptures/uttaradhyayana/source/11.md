---
title: "Uttaradhyayana - Chapter 11"
scripture: "uttaradhyayana"
chapter: 11
---

**1** Mahavira said: The monk should cultivate friendship toward all beings, delight in the virtues of the virtuous, compassion for the suffering, and equanimity toward the hostile.

**2** Friendship, Maitri, is the first of the four sublime attitudes. The monk should regard all beings as his friends, for all share the same essential nature — the nature of the soul.

**3** Delight in virtue, Pramoda, is the second sublime attitude. The monk should rejoice in the good qualities of others, without envy or jealousy. The virtues of others should be a source of inspiration, not irritation.

**4** Compassion, Karunya, is the third sublime attitude. The monk should feel the suffering of all beings as his own and should act to relieve that suffering whenever possible.

**5** Equanimity, Madhyasthya, is the fourth sublime attitude. Toward those who are hostile, the monk should maintain perfect equanimity — neither hatred nor submission, but calm and balanced non-reaction.

**6** These four attitudes purify the mind and create a spiritual atmosphere around the monk that benefits all who come into contact with him.

**7** The monk who practises these four attitudes is like a great tree that gives shade, fruit, and shelter to all who approach it, asking nothing in return.

**8** He who cultivates friendship toward all beings attracts friendship from all. He who cultivates compassion receives compassion. This is the law of karma applied to the mental life.

**9** The four sublime attitudes are not merely emotions to be felt but virtues to be practised. They must be expressed in action — in the monk's words, his deeds, and his manner of living.

**10** He who has perfected these four attitudes has conquered the passions of anger, pride, and envy. He is established in peace and radiates goodwill to all the world.
