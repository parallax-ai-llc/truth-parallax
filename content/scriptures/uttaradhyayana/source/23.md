---
title: "Uttaradhyayana - Chapter 23"
scripture: "uttaradhyayana"
chapter: 23
---

**1** Mahavira said: The monk should understand the doctrine of manifold aspects, Anekantavada, which is the key to correct understanding.

**2** Every reality has infinite aspects. No single statement can capture the whole truth about any reality. Truth is always complex and many-sided.

**3** The wise man sees many aspects of truth; the fool sees only one. He who insists that his view alone is correct and all others are wrong is deluded.

**4** The doctrine of Syadvada teaches that every statement should be qualified: "In some respect, it is so; in some respect, it is not so."

**5** This does not mean that truth is relative or that anything goes. It means that truth is complex and that humility is required in its pursuit.

**6** The practice of non-absolutism leads to tolerance and understanding. It prevents the fanaticism that arises from the conviction that one's own view is the only truth.

**7** In spiritual matters, as in worldly matters, different perspectives reveal different aspects of the truth. The views of different traditions, when correctly understood, complement rather than contradict each other.

**8** The Jain tradition does not claim to have the exclusive truth. It claims to have a comprehensive method — Anekantavada — for approaching the truth from multiple angles.

**9** The monk who practises Anekantavada in his thinking becomes open-minded, humble, and tolerant. He is able to learn from all and to reject nothing without examination.

**10** This doctrine of non-absolutism is one of the most distinctive and valuable contributions of the Jain tradition to the world of thought and spiritual practice.
