---
title: "Uttaradhyayana - Chapter 32"
scripture: "uttaradhyayana"
chapter: 32
---

**1** Mahavira said: The wise monk should practise equanimity in all circumstances — in pleasure and pain, in praise and blame, in honour and dishonour.

**2** The world is full of pairs of opposites: hot and cold, gain and loss, victory and defeat. The monk should accept both with the same calm mind.

**3** He who is elated by good fortune and depressed by bad fortune is tossed about like a leaf in the wind. He has no stability, no peace, no progress.

**4** He who receives both with equanimity is like a mountain — unshaken by the storms that rage around him.

**5** Equanimity is not indifference. It is the balanced state of mind that sees things as they are, without the distortion of desire or aversion.

**6** The equanimous monk is not cold or uncaring. He feels compassion for all beings. But his compassion is free from personal attachment and emotional turmoil.

**7** He acts for the welfare of others not because he is driven by passion but because it is the right thing to do. His action is guided by wisdom, not by emotion.

**8** The practice of equanimity is the practice of freedom. The monk who is equanimous is free from the tyranny of circumstances. His happiness does not depend on what happens to him.

**9** His happiness comes from within — from the peace of a mind that is free from desire and aversion, from the clarity of a consciousness that is fixed upon the self.

**10** Let the monk cultivate equanimity daily, through meditation, through the contemplation of the nature of things, and through the practice of the vows. In time, equanimity becomes his natural state.
