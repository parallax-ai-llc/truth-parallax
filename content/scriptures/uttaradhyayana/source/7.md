---
title: "Uttaradhyayana - Chapter 7"
scripture: "uttaradhyayana"
chapter: 7
---

**1** Mahavira said: The parable of the lotus. As the lotus grows in muddy water but rises above it, pure and undefiled, so the wise soul, though born in the world, rises above worldly attachments.

**2** The lotus does not reject the mud; it transforms the mud into beauty. So the wise soul does not reject the world; it transforms worldly experience into wisdom.

**3** The lotus flower is not wetted by the water in which it grows. So the wise soul is not stained by the world in which it lives.

**4** The monk should be like the lotus — present in the world but not of the world, living among beings but attached to none, serving all but clinging to nothing.

**5** The sage Mrigaputra was of high birth, handsome, wealthy, and learned. Yet he renounced everything to follow the path of the Jinas.

**6** His family pleaded with him to return. His friends tempted him with worldly pleasures. But he said: "I have seen the truth. I cannot return to illusion."

**7** He practised severe austerities, conquered his passions, and attained liberation. His example shows that no worldly advantage can compare with the bliss of liberation.

**8** Another sage, Nami, was a king who ruled with justice and prosperity. Yet he too renounced his throne, seeing that all worldly glory is impermanent.

**9** He said: "The kingdom is like a painted picture — beautiful to behold but without substance. The soul alone is real, and the soul alone is worth pursuing."

**10** These examples teach the monk that renunciation is not a loss but a gain — the greatest gain possible, the gaining of one's own true self.
