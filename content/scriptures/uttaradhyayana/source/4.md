---
title: "Uttaradhyayana - Chapter 4"
scripture: "uttaradhyayana"
chapter: 4
---

**1** Mahavira said: The monk should practise non-violence with the utmost care and sincerity, for non-violence is the foundation of all virtue.

**2** Non-violence is not merely the absence of physical harm. It is the absence of all ill-will, all hostility, all indifference to the suffering of others.

**3** The monk who practises non-violence sees all beings as his own self. He feels their pain as his own pain. He rejoices in their happiness as in his own.

**4** He does not kill, nor does he cause others to kill, nor does he consent to the killing by others. He is vigilant against all forms of violence, direct and indirect.

**5** He sweeps the path before him, lest he crush a tiny creature. He strains his water, lest he swallow a living being. He checks his food, his seat, and his bed.

**6** He speaks no harsh word, for harsh words wound the spirit. He thinks no cruel thought, for cruel thoughts generate the karma of violence.

**7** He extends his compassion even to beings with one sense — the earth-bodies, the water-bodies, the fire-bodies, the air-bodies, and the plant-bodies.

**8** This universal compassion is the distinguishing mark of the Jain ascetic. It sets him apart from all other spiritual practitioners and brings him closest to the state of the Jinas.

**9** The monk who has perfected non-violence radiates peace and goodwill. In his presence, even hostile creatures become calm, even enemies become friends.

**10** Ahimsa Paramo Dharma — Non-violence is the supreme religion. This is the first and foremost teaching of the Jinas, the cornerstone of all Jain spiritual practice.
