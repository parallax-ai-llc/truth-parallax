---
title: "Uttaradhyayana - Chapter 5"
scripture: "uttaradhyayana"
chapter: 5
---

**1** Mahavira said: As a tortoise draws in all its limbs, so the monk should draw in his five senses and his mind from their objects.

**2** The eye seeks beautiful forms, the ear seeks pleasant sounds, the nose seeks sweet scents, the tongue seeks delicious tastes, and the skin seeks soft touches. But the wise monk restrains them all.

**3** He does not look at what is not proper to see. He does not listen to what is not proper to hear. He does not smell what arouses desire. He does not taste for pleasure. He does not touch for gratification.

**4** The mind is the sixth sense, and it is the most difficult to control. It wanders ceaselessly, like a monkey jumping from branch to branch.

**5** The monk should tame the mind through the practice of meditation, as a mahout tames a wild elephant with the goad of concentration.

**6** When the senses are restrained and the mind is still, the soul shines forth in its own light. This is the beginning of spiritual awakening.

**7** The restrained monk is like a deep lake — calm, clear, and undisturbed by the winds of desire. The unrestrained monk is like a shallow puddle — easily stirred and quickly muddied.

**8** The senses are like wild horses yoked to the chariot of the body. If they are not controlled, they will drag the chariot over the cliff of ruin. If they are controlled, they will carry it to the destination of liberation.

**9** The monk should not suppress the senses by force but should understand their nature and gradually withdraw them from their objects through the power of discrimination.

**10** He who has mastered his senses and his mind has won the greatest victory. He has conquered the inner enemies that are more dangerous than any outer foe.
