Shankara treats Chapter 8 as the culmination of the Chandogya's teaching, moving from meditation (*upasana*) to direct knowledge (*jnana*). The *dahara vidya* (8.1) — meditation on the tiny space within the heart — is a practice that leads the mind inward toward the self. The space within the heart is Brahman, and its paradoxical nature (as small as a grain of rice, yet containing everything) illustrates the Advaita teaching that Brahman transcends all spatial and temporal categories.

The Prajapati-Indra dialogue (8.7-12) is one of Shankara's most important texts. The progressive rejection of the waking self, dreaming self, and even the self in deep sleep as the true self demonstrates the *neti neti* method in narrative form. Each candidate is rejected because it is limited, changing, or unconscious. The final teaching reveals the self as pure consciousness — "free from evil, free from old age, free from death, free from grief, free from hunger and thirst" — which is Brahman itself, the unchanging witness of all states.
