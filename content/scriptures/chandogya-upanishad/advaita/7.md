Shankara reads the Narada-Sanatkumara dialogue as a methodical demonstration that every finite reality points beyond itself to the Infinite. Narada's learning — encompassing all the Vedas, sciences, and arts — fails to bring him peace because it is all *namarupa* (name and form), which is inherently limited. Sanatkumara's teaching ascends through a hierarchy of realities, each "greater" than the last, until reaching the Bhuma — the Infinite that is Brahman.

The definition of the Bhuma — "where one sees nothing else, hears nothing else, knows nothing else" (7.24) — is Shankara's proof-text for the non-dual nature of Brahman. In the Infinite, there is no second thing to be seen or known. This is not a state of blankness but of fullness — the self resting in its own unlimited nature. The progression from name to Bhuma demonstrates that every object of knowledge is a stepping-stone that must be transcended, and that only the Infinite — the self itself — provides the peace Narada seeks.
