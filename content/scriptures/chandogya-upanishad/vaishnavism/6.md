Chapter 6 contains the most celebrated teaching of the Chandogya — Uddalaka Aruni's instruction to his son Shvetaketu, culminating in the ninefold repetition of "Tat Tvam Asi" ("That Thou Art"). Vaishnava interpreters, following Ramanuja, read this not as asserting identity between the individual self and Brahman but as teaching that Brahman (Vishnu) is the inner essence (*antaratman*) of every self. "Thou art That" means the self's true nature is to be the body of God — inseparably related to Vishnu while remaining a distinct entity.

Uddalaka's examples — clay and pots, gold and ornaments, iron and tools — are read by Ramanuja as demonstrating that effects are real transformations of their cause, not mere illusions. The world is the real body of Brahman, and individual selves are real parts of His being. The teaching that all beings emerge from Sat (Being) and return to it (6.2) describes the soul's dependence on Vishnu, not its metaphysical identity with Him.
