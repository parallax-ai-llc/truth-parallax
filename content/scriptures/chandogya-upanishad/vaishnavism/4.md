Chapter 4 contains two major narratives: the teaching of Raikva (the cart-driver sage) to King Janashruti, and the instruction of Satyakama Jabala by his teacher Gautama. Vaishnavas read both narratives as demonstrating that true knowledge of Vishnu transcends social distinctions. Raikva, a humble cart-driver, possesses the supreme knowledge that even a king lacks — the knowledge of Vayu (wind) and Prana as the absorbers of all things, pointing to Vishnu as the ultimate absorber and sustainer of the universe.

The story of Satyakama Jabala, who is accepted as a student despite his uncertain parentage because of his truthfulness, illustrates the Vaishnava teaching that devotion and sincerity are the true qualifications for divine knowledge. The four teachings Satyakama receives from a bull, fire, a swan, and a water-bird — each revealing a quarter of Brahman — show that Vishnu reveals Himself through all of creation to the sincere seeker.
