Chapter 7 presents a remarkable progressive teaching (*shrenivada*) in which Sanatkumara leads Narada through a hierarchy of twenty-five levels of reality, from the most concrete (name) to the most abstract (the Infinite, *Bhuma*). Scholars have noted structural parallels with early Samkhya enumeration of categories (*tattvas*) and have debated whether this passage represents a proto-Samkhya teaching or an independent Upanishadic development.

The chapter is also significant for its portrayal of Narada — a figure who in later mythology becomes a great devotee of Vishnu — as a seeker who has mastered all conventional knowledge yet remains unfulfilled. This narrative framework reinforces the Upanishadic distinction between lower knowledge (*aparavidya*), which encompasses all worldly and ritual learning, and higher knowledge (*paravidya*), which is direct realization of the Absolute. The Bhuma teaching (7.23-26) has been compared with similar concepts of the Infinite in Greek philosophy, particularly Anaximander's *apeiron*.
