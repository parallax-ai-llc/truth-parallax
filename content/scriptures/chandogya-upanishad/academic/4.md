Chapter 4 contains two of the Chandogya's most celebrated narratives. The Raikva story raises important questions about social status and knowledge in Vedic society — Raikva is a *shudra* or low-status figure whose wisdom surpasses that of a king. Scholars debate whether this narrative reflects a genuinely egalitarian strand in Upanishadic thought or is a literary device that ultimately reinforces Brahmanical authority (since Raikva teaches Vedic knowledge).

The Satyakama Jabala narrative is equally significant for social history. Satyakama's acceptance as a student despite his unknown parentage — his mother Jabala tells him honestly that she does not know his father — has been read as evidence that early Upanishadic culture was more fluid in its social boundaries than later Brahmanical orthodoxy. The instruction by non-human beings (bull, fire, swan, water-bird) represents a distinctive pedagogical motif that may reflect pre-Brahmanical nature-mysticism absorbed into the Vedic framework.
