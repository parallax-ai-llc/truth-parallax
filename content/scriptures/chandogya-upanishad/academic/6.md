Chapter 6 has generated more scholarly commentary than any other section of the Chandogya. The "Tat Tvam Asi" formula, repeated nine times by Uddalaka Aruni to his son Shvetaketu, has been analyzed philologically, philosophically, and historically. Joel Brereton's influential study argues that in its original context, the sentence may have meant something closer to "you are connected to that" or "you are of that nature" rather than the strict identity asserted by Shankara or the strict difference asserted by Madhva.

The cosmogony of 6.2 — "In the beginning, this was Being alone, one without a second" — represents one of the earliest formulations of monistic philosophy in world literature. Scholars debate whether this represents an original Upanishadic insight or a development from earlier Rig Vedic cosmogonic speculations (particularly the *Nasadiya Sukta*, RV 10.129). The nine analogies Uddalaka employs — salt in water, the banyan seed, rivers and ocean — have been analyzed as a sophisticated pedagogical strategy using empirical observation to convey metaphysical truths.
