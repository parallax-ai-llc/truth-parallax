Chapter 3 contains some of the Chandogya's most layered material, combining ritual meditation (the Madhu Vidya), cosmological speculation (the sun as cosmic center), and philosophical teaching (the *tajjalan* formula and the golden *purusha*). Scholars note that the Madhu Vidya, with its elaborate cosmic correspondences, likely belongs to an earlier stratum of composition, while the philosophical passages in 3.12-14 represent a later development toward the systematic metaphysics of Chapters 6-8.

The *tajjalan* formula (3.14.1) — "All this is Brahman; it arises from That, dissolves into That, and breathes in That" — is considered by Olivelle and others to be one of the earliest formulations of what would become classical Vedantic metaphysics. The golden *purusha* meditation connects the Chandogya to the broader Vedic tradition of solar theology, in which the sun serves as the primary symbol of the divine. The Gayatri meditation (3.12) preserves evidence of this mantra's central role in Vedic religious life.
