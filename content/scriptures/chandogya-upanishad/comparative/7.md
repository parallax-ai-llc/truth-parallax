The Narada-Sanatkumara dialogue enacts a pattern found across contemplative traditions: the learned seeker who discovers that intellectual mastery does not bring peace and must be guided beyond knowledge to wisdom. This parallels Ecclesiastes' "of making many books there is no end, and much study is a weariness of the flesh," the Zen master's dismissal of the scholar's conceptual understanding, and Socrates' claim that wisdom begins with recognizing one's ignorance.

The progressive ascent through levels of reality toward the Infinite resonates with the Neoplatonic hierarchy from matter to the One, the Sufi *maqamat* (stations) of the spiritual path, and the Buddhist *jhana* progression through increasingly refined states of consciousness. The Bhuma — the Infinite where "one sees nothing else" — invites comparison with the Plotinian vision of the One, the Buddhist *nirvana* that transcends all categories, and the mystical *unio mystica* of the Christian contemplative tradition.
