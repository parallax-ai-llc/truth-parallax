## Overview

The Chandogya Upanishad's "Tat Tvam Asi" teaching has been one of the most frequently cited texts in comparative religion and philosophy. Aldous Huxley placed it at the center of his "perennial philosophy," seeing in it a universal mystical insight found across traditions — that the deepest self is identical with the ultimate reality. The text's movement from ritual to metaphysics also parallels developments in ancient Greece (from Homeric religion to Presocratic philosophy) and ancient Israel (from temple cult to prophetic interiority).

## Comparative Themes

Uddalaka's teaching that all beings emerge from one Being (*sat*) and return to it invites comparison with Neoplatonic emanation, the Kabbalistic doctrine of *tzimtzum*, and the Sufi concept of *fana* (annihilation in God). The Chandogya's use of analogies — salt dissolved in water, rivers merging into the ocean — appears across mystical traditions as a way of describing the soul's relationship to the Absolute. The Panchagni Vidya's account of the soul's journey after death compares with Plato's "Myth of Er," Egyptian afterlife literature, and the Tibetan *bardo* teachings.
