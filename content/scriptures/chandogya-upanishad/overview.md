## Overview

The Chandogya Upanishad is one of the oldest and longest of the principal Upanishads, embedded within the Chandogya Brahmana of the Sama Veda. Its name derives from the *chandoga*, the priest who chants the Sama Veda hymns. Spanning eight chapters, it weaves together ritual instruction, cosmological speculation, ethical teaching, and some of the most celebrated dialogues in Indian philosophy — including the famous "Tat Tvam Asi" ("That Thou Art") teaching of Uddalaka Aruni to his son Shvetaketu.

## Structure

The Chandogya Upanishad's eight chapters move from liturgical meditation to metaphysical inquiry:

1. **Chapters 1-2**: Meditation on the Udgitha (Om) and the Saman chant, linking ritual sound to cosmic principles
2. **Chapters 3-5**: Cosmological teachings including the Madhu Vidya (honey doctrine), the Panchagni Vidya (five-fire doctrine), and the doctrine of Prana
3. **Chapters 6-8**: The philosophical core — Uddalaka's instruction on Being (*sat*), the "Tat Tvam Asi" teaching, and the dialogue on the true self in deep sleep

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The "Tat Tvam Asi" teaching is read as affirming the soul's dependence on Vishnu as its inner essence
- **Shaivism**: The Upanishad's exploration of consciousness in waking, dreaming, and deep sleep aligns with Shaiva metaphysics
- **Advaita Vedanta**: The primary source for the *mahavakya* "Tat Tvam Asi," affirming identity of self and Brahman
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Madhva reinterprets "Tat Tvam Asi" as "Tat Atvam Asi" — "Thou art not that" — preserving difference
- **Academic**: A critical text for reconstructing early Vedic philosophy and education
- **Comparative**: The progression from ritual to philosophy mirrors developments in Greek, Chinese, and Israelite traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Tat Tvam Asi | "That Thou Art" — the identity of atman and Brahman |
| Udgitha/Om | The sacred syllable as the essence of the Saman chant |
| Prana | The vital breath as the supreme among faculties |
| Five Fires | The doctrine of transmigration and the two paths after death |
| Sat (Being) | The one reality from which all multiplicity emerges |
