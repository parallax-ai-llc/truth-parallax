The *dahara vidya* — the meditation on the tiny space within the heart that contains the entire universe — is central to Shaiva contemplative practice. This inner space is the seat of Shiva-consciousness, the point where the individual recognizes its identity with the infinite. The paradox of an infinitely small space containing the cosmos parallels the Shaiva teaching that consciousness (*chiti*) is simultaneously the smallest point of awareness and the totality of existence.

Prajapati's progressive teaching to Indra through four levels of self — waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and the transcendent — maps precisely onto the four states of consciousness in Shaiva philosophy. The final revelation of the self as "free from evil, free from death" points to *turiya* — the state of pure Shiva-consciousness that transcends and pervades the other three states. The chapter's teaching that this self "rises up from the body and reaches the highest light" (8.12.3) describes the recognition (*pratyabhijna*) of one's eternal Shiva-nature.
