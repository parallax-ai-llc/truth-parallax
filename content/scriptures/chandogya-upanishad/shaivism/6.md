The "Tat Tvam Asi" teaching resonates profoundly with the Shaiva recognition (*pratyabhijna*) tradition. When Uddalaka tells Shvetaketu "That Thou Art," the Shaiva reader hears the quintessential call to recognize one's identity with Paramashiva. The nine repetitions mirror the progressive removal of *malas* (impurities) that conceal the self's divine nature. Each analogy — salt dissolved in water, rivers merging in the ocean, the invisible essence of the banyan seed — reveals another dimension of the truth that individual consciousness is universal consciousness in disguised form.

Uddalaka's teaching that all beings emerge from one Sat (Being) aligns with the Shaiva doctrine that the universe is Shiva's self-emanation (*abhasa*). The multiplicity of names and forms does not negate the unity of consciousness any more than the diversity of waves negates the unity of the ocean. The journey from ignorance to recognition requires not acquiring something new but removing the veil that obscures what has always been the case.
