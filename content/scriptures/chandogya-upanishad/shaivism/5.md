The Panchagni Vidya's account of the soul's passage through five fires — heaven, rain, earth, man, and woman — maps the process by which consciousness descends into embodiment. Shaiva interpreters read this as describing Shiva's creative self-limitation: the infinite consciousness progressively contracts through cosmic stages until it appears as an individual being in a specific body. The two paths after death correspond to liberation (recognition of one's Shiva-nature) and continued bondage (failure to recognize).

King Ashvapati's teaching on the Vaishvanara — the universal self that encompasses the entire cosmos — resonates with the Shaiva concept of *vishvarupa* (the cosmic form of consciousness). Each householder's error is to identify the self with only one dimension of Shiva's manifestation; the complete teaching reveals that consciousness is the totality, not any single part. This anticipates the Kashmir Shaiva insight that limited self-identification is the fundamental contraction (*anava mala*) that constitutes bondage.
