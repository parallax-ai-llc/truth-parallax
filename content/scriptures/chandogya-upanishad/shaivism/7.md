The Narada-Sanatkumara dialogue maps a progressive ascent of consciousness that resonates with the Shaiva tradition of *tattva* analysis. Each stage — from name through speech, mind, will, to Prana and the Infinite — corresponds to progressively subtler levels of Shiva's manifestation. The fact that Narada's vast intellectual knowledge cannot bring peace demonstrates the Shaiva teaching that conceptual knowledge (*paroksha jnana*) is no substitute for direct recognition (*pratyabhijna*) of one's nature as Shiva.

The Bhuma — the Infinite where "one sees nothing else" — is recognized in Shaiva terms as *turiya*, the fourth state of pure consciousness in which the subject-object distinction dissolves. This is not unconsciousness but hyper-consciousness — Shiva's own nature of infinite awareness. The progressive teaching mirrors the *bhavana* (contemplative) practices of Kashmir Shaivism, where attention is gradually refined from gross objects to subtle energies to pure awareness itself.
