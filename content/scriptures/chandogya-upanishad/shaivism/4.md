The narratives of Chapter 4 demonstrate the Shaiva principle that consciousness-knowledge (*jnana*) is not bound by social convention. Raikva, an unrecognized sage living under a cart, embodies the Shaiva ideal of the *avadhuta* — the liberated being who has transcended all social identity. His teaching on Vayu and Prana as the ultimate absorbers of all phenomena points to the Shaiva understanding of consciousness as the ground into which all experiences dissolve.

Satyakama Jabala's instruction by non-human teachers — bull, fire, swan, and water-bird — resonates with the Shaiva teaching that Shiva communicates through the entire field of manifestation. Every element of nature is a potential guru because every element is an expression of Shiva's consciousness. The four quarters of Brahman revealed to Satyakama correspond to the four states of consciousness in Shaiva philosophy: waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and *turiya*.
