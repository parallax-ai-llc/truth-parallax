Madhva reads the Narada-Sanatkumara dialogue as demonstrating the inadequacy of all knowledge that falls short of devotion to Vishnu. Narada's vast learning cannot bring peace because intellectual knowledge, however extensive, does not constitute direct relationship with God. The progressive ascent from name to Prana to Bhuma is a hierarchy of knowledge that culminates in the recognition of Vishnu's infinite nature.

The Bhuma, in the Dvaita reading, is not an impersonal Infinite but the infinitely perfect Lord Vishnu, who surpasses all finite realities. The teaching that "where one sees nothing else" does not mean the dissolution of the individual into the Absolute but the devotee's total absorption in awareness of God, analogous to a lover who sees only the beloved. The hierarchy of the chapter — each level greater than the last — exemplifies the Dvaita principle of *taratamya* (graduated reality), with Vishnu as the unsurpassable summit.
