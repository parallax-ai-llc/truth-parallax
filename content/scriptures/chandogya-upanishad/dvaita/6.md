Madhva's interpretation of Chapter 6 is his most famous and controversial contribution to Vedantic hermeneutics. He reads "Tat Tvam Asi" not as "Thou art That" (identity) but as "*Tat tvam asi*" with a different grammatical analysis: the sentence teaches the soul's dependent relationship to Brahman, not identity with Brahman. Alternatively, some Dvaita interpreters read *atat tvam asi* — "thou art not that" — emphasizing the eternal difference between the finite soul and the infinite God.

Madhva reads Uddalaka's examples of clay and pot as demonstrating that God is the material and efficient cause of all things while remaining distinct from His effects. The pot is made of clay but is not the clay itself — it has its own form and function. Similarly, souls are created by Vishnu and depend on Him but possess their own eternal individuality. The teaching that all beings merge into Sat during deep sleep and dissolution does not mean they lose existence but that they rest in God's care, to be sent forth again.
