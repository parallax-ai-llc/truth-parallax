---
title: "Chandogya Upanishad Chapter 7"
scripture: "chandogya-upanishad"
chapter: 7
---

**1** Narada approached Sanatkumara and said: "Teach me, Sir." Sanatkumara said to him: "Please tell me what you know; afterwards I shall tell you what is beyond."

**2** Narada said: "I know the Rig-Veda, Sir, the Yajur-Veda, the Sama-Veda, as the fourth the Atharvana, as the fifth the Itihasa-Purana, the Veda of the Vedas, the Pitrya, the Rasi, the Daiva, the Nidhi, the Vakovakya, the Ekayana, the Deva-vidya, the Brahma-vidya, the Bhuta-vidya, the Kshatra-vidya, the Nakshatra-vidya, the Sarpa and Devajana-vidya. All this I know, Sir."

**3** "But, Sir, with all this I know the Mantras only, the sacred books; I do not know the Self. I have heard from men like you that he who knows the Self overcomes sorrow. I am in sorrow. Do, Sir, help me over this sorrow of mine."

**4** Sanatkumara said to him: "Whatever you have read is only a name. The Rig-Veda is a name, the Yajur-Veda is a name, the Sama-Veda is a name. Meditate on the name."

**5** "He who meditates on the name as Brahman is, as it were, lord and master as far as the name reaches—he who meditates on the name as Brahman." "Sir, is there something better than a name?" "Yes, there is something better than a name." "Sir, tell it me."

**6** "Speech is better than a name. Speech makes us understand the Rig-Veda, the Yajur-Veda, the Sama-Veda, and as the fourth the Atharvana, the fifth the Itihasa-Purana, as well as right and wrong, the true and the false, the good and the bad, the pleasant and the unpleasant. For if there were no speech, neither right nor wrong would be known, neither the true nor the false, neither the good nor the bad, neither the pleasant nor the unpleasant. Speech makes us understand all this. Meditate on speech."

**7** "He who meditates on speech as Brahman is, as it were, lord and master as far as speech reaches—he who meditates on speech as Brahman." "Sir, is there something better than speech?" "Yes, there is something better than speech." "Sir, tell it me."

**8** "Mind is better than speech. For as the closed fist holds two amalaka or two kola or two aksha fruits, thus does mind hold speech and name. For when a man is minded in his mind to read the sacred hymns, he reads them; when he is minded in his mind to perform sacred acts, he performs them; when he is minded to desire sons and cattle, he desires them; when he is minded to desire this world and the other, he desires them. For mind is indeed the self, mind is the world, mind is Brahman. Meditate on the mind."

**9** "He who meditates on mind as Brahman is, as it were, lord and master as far as mind reaches—he who meditates on mind as Brahman." "Sir, is there something better than mind?" "Yes, there is something better than mind." "Sir, tell it me."

**10** "Will is better than mind. For when a man wills, he thinks in his mind, then he sends forth speech, and he sends it forth in a name. In a name the sacred hymns are contained, in the sacred hymns all sacrifices."

**11** "All these, beginning with will and ending in sacrifice, centre in will, consist of will, abide in will. Heaven and earth willed, air and ether willed, water and fire willed. Through the will of heaven and earth rain wills; through the will of rain, food wills; through the will of food, the vital breaths will; through the will of the vital breaths, the sacred hymns will. Meditate on will."

**12** "He who meditates on will as Brahman, he, being himself safe, firm, and undistressed, obtains the safe, firm, and undistressed worlds which he has willed. He is, as it were, lord and master as far as will reaches." "Sir, is there something better than will?" "Yes, there is something better than will." "Sir, tell it me."

**13** "Consideration is better than will. For when a man considers, then he wills, then he thinks in his mind, then he sends forth speech. Meditate on consideration."

**14** "He who meditates on consideration as Brahman, he obtains the worlds he has considered. He is, as it were, lord and master as far as consideration reaches." "Sir, is there something better?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me."

**15** "Reflection is better than consideration. Meditate on reflection. The earth reflects, as it were, the sky reflects, heaven reflects, water reflects, the mountains reflect. Therefore he who among men is great seems to have obtained a share of reflection. Meditate on reflection."

**16** "Sir, is there something better than reflection?" "Yes, there is something better." "Sir, tell it me."

**17** "Knowledge is better than reflection. By knowledge we know the Rig-Veda, the Yajur-Veda, the Sama-Veda, the Atharvana, and all the sciences. Meditate on knowledge."

**18** "He who meditates on knowledge as Brahman is, as it were, lord and master as far as knowledge reaches." "Sir, is there something better than knowledge?" "Yes, there is something better than knowledge." "Sir, tell it me."

**19** "Power is better than knowledge. One powerful man shakes a hundred men of knowledge. When a man is powerful, he rises, rising he serves, serving he approaches and sees, hears, perceives, knows, acts, and understands. By power the earth stands, the sky stands, the mountains stand. Meditate on power."

**20** "He who meditates on power as Brahman is, as it were, lord and master as far as power reaches." "Sir, is there something better than power?" "Yes, there is something better than power." "Sir, tell it me."

**21** "Food is better than power. Therefore if a man abstain from food for ten days, though he live, he would be unable to see, hear, perceive, think, act, and understand. But when he obtains food, he is able to see, hear, perceive, think, act, and understand. Meditate on food."

**22** "Sir, is there something better than food?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me."

**23** "Water is better than food. Therefore, if there is not sufficient rain, the vital spirits fail from fear that there will be less food. Meditate on water."

**24** "Sir, is there something better than water?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me."

**25** "Fire is better than water. For fire united with air warms the ether. Meditate on fire. He who meditates on fire as Brahman, he obtains the resplendent worlds." "Sir, is there something better?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me."

**26** "Ether is better than fire. For in the ether exist both sun and moon, lightning, stars, and fire. Through the ether we call, through the ether we hear, through the ether we answer. In the ether or space we rejoice and rejoice not. In ether everything is born, and towards ether everything tends. Meditate on ether."

**27** "Sir, is there something better than ether?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me." "Memory is better than ether. Therefore where many are assembled together, if they have no memory they would hear no one, they would not perceive, they would not know. Through memory we know our sons, through memory our cattle. Meditate on memory."

**28** "Sir, is there something better than memory?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me." "Hope is better than memory. Fired by hope does memory read the sacred hymns, perform sacrifices, desire sons and cattle, desire this world and the other. Meditate on hope."

**29** "Sir, is there something better than hope?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me." "Breath is better than hope. As the spokes of a wheel hold to the nave, so does all this hold to breath. Life moves on by breath, breath gives life, and gives it to the living. Meditate on breath."

**30** "Sir, is there something better than breath?" "Yes." "Sir, tell it me."

**31** Sanatkumara said: "The True is better than breath." "But he who knows the True, stands firm. He is the True, and he stands firm in the True. He is, as it were, lord and master. When the True is found, there is bliss. There is no bliss in anything that is small or finite. The Infinite alone is bliss. But we must desire to understand the Infinite."

**32** "Where one sees nothing else, hears nothing else, understands nothing else—that is the Infinite. Where one sees something else, hears something else, understands something else—that is the finite. The Infinite is immortal, the finite is mortal." "Sir, in what does the Infinite rest?" "In its own greatness—or not even in greatness."

**33** "That Infinite is below, above, behind, before, to the right and to the left. I am all this. This Infinite is the Self. The Self is below, above, behind, before, to the right and to the left. He who sees, perceives, and understands this, loves the Self, delights in the Self, revels in the Self, rejoices in the Self—he becomes a Self-ruler; he is lord and master in all the worlds. But those who think differently from this, live in perishable worlds and have other beings for their rulers."

**34** "For him who sees, perceives, and understands this, the breath springs from the Self, hope springs from the Self, memory springs from the Self, ether springs from the Self, fire springs from the Self, water springs from the Self, everything springs from the Self."

**35** "And of this it has been said: He who sees this does not see death, nor illness, nor pain; he who sees this sees everything and obtains everything everywhere. This is the Self. From sorrow the Self is free—ageless, deathless, griefless, hungerless, thirstless, whose desire is the True, whose conception is the True."
