## Overview

The Digambara ("sky-clad") tradition represents one of the two major branches of Jainism, distinguished by its emphasis on complete renunciation, including the abandonment of all clothing by male monastics. Digambara interpretation of the Tattvartha Sutra draws on a rich commentarial tradition that emphasizes the text's metaphysical rigor and soteriological precision.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Attribution to Umasvami

Digambara tradition attributes the Tattvartha Sutra to Acharya Umasvami and accepts a slightly different recension of the text than the Shvetambara version. The Digambara text contains some variant readings and includes a different final verse that emphasizes the path of renunciation.

### Commentarial Tradition

Major Digambara commentaries include Pujyapada's *Sarvarthasiddhi* (6th century) and Akalanka's *Rajavartika* (8th century). These works elaborate the sutra's compressed aphorisms into detailed philosophical arguments, often engaging with Buddhist and Hindu opponents.

### Strict Soteriology

Digambara interpreters emphasize that complete liberation requires complete renunciation, including the abandonment of clothing, possessions, and social bonds. This strict reading informs their understanding of the Tattvartha Sutra's chapters on conduct and liberation.
