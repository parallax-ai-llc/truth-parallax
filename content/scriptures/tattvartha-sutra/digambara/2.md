## The Soul in Digambara Metaphysics

The Digambara interpretation of Chapter 2 draws heavily on the mystical philosophy of Acharya Kundakunda, who distinguished between the conventional (*vyavahara*) and ultimate (*nishchaya*) perspectives on the soul. From the ultimate standpoint, the soul is pure consciousness, infinitely blissful, and entirely distinct from karmic matter.

### Categories of Living Beings

The chapter's classification of living beings according to the number of sense organs they possess (from one-sensed beings like earth, water, fire, air, and plants, to five-sensed beings with mind) is read by Digambara interpreters as establishing the universal scope of *ahimsa*. Every living being, no matter how simple, possesses a soul capable of infinite knowledge.

### The Soul's Innate Perfection

Kundakunda's *Samayasara* provides the key Digambara framework for understanding the soul: in its essential nature (*svabhava*), the soul is identical with the qualities of the liberated being -- infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, and infinite energy. Bondage is not the soul's true condition but an adventitious state caused by karmic matter.

### Consciousness as Defining Attribute

Digambara interpreters emphasize that consciousness (*chetana*) is the defining and exclusive attribute of the soul. Unlike the Samkhya *purusha* or the Vedantic *atman*, the Jain *jiva* is an active, knowing agent whose essential nature is pure awareness, temporarily obscured by the veil of karma.
