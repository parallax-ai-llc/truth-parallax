## The Dual Path of Purification

Digambara interpretation of Chapter 9 presents the two complementary processes of spiritual purification: *samvara* (stopping the influx of new karma) and *nirjara* (shedding existing karma). Together, these processes gradually strip away the karmic matter that obscures the soul's innate perfection.

### Stopping Karmic Influx

Samvara is achieved through the cultivation of ethical conduct, careful bodily movement (*samiti*), restraint of the senses (*gupti*), the ten types of dharma (forbearance, humility, etc.), reflection (*anupreksha*), and the endurance of hardships (*parishaha*). Digambara interpreters emphasize that true samvara requires inner transformation, not merely external behavioral modification.

### Austerities and Karma Shedding

The chapter describes both external austerities (fasting, reduction of food, bodily mortification) and internal austerities (expiation, reverence, study, meditation, renunciation of the body). Digambara tradition places particular emphasis on severe ascetic practices, including prolonged fasting and the practice of *sallekhana* (voluntary death through fasting) as the ultimate form of nirjara.

### The Stages of Spiritual Progress

Digambara commentators correlate the practices described in this chapter with the fourteen stages of spiritual development (*gunasthanas*), which map the soul's progressive liberation from karmic bondage. The highest stages involve the simultaneous destruction of all remaining karma and the attainment of omniscience.
