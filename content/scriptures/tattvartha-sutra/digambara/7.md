## The Path of Ethical Practice

Digambara interpretation of Chapter 7 presents the system of vows and restraints that constitute the practical dimension of Jain ethics. The chapter establishes two levels of ethical practice: the five *anuvratas* (minor vows) for householders and the five *mahavratas* (great vows) for monastics.

### The Great Vows in Digambara Practice

Digambara tradition interprets the five great vows -- non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession -- in their most radical form. The vow of non-possession (*aparigraha*), in particular, is understood as requiring the complete abandonment of all possessions, including clothing. This strict interpretation is the defining feature of Digambara monasticism.

### Householder Ethics

For laypeople, the Tattvartha Sutra prescribes a graduated path of ethical practice. The five minor vows adapt the monastic ideals to the conditions of worldly life, while supplementary vows (*shilavratas*) provide additional structure. Digambara commentators emphasize that householder practice, while genuinely valuable, is preparatory to the full renunciation required for liberation.

### The Centrality of Ahimsa

Digambara interpreters, following Pujyapada, regard non-violence as the foundational vow from which all others derive. Truthfulness is practiced because falsehood causes harm; non-stealing because theft injures others; celibacy because sexual activity involves violence toward microscopic organisms; non-possession because attachment motivates harmful actions.
