## The Non-Living Substances

Digambara interpretation of Chapter 5 presents the Jain analysis of *ajiva* (non-living substance) with meticulous philosophical precision. The six substances (*dravyas*) that constitute reality -- soul, matter, space, time, motion, and rest -- are analyzed in terms of their essential characteristics, modifications, and interrelationships.

### Matter (Pudgala)

Digambara commentators devote particular attention to the analysis of matter, since it is the material substrate of karma. *Pudgala* possesses the qualities of touch, taste, smell, and color, and exists in both atomic (*paramanu*) and aggregate (*skandha*) forms. The interaction between material karma particles and the immaterial soul constitutes the central problem of Jain soteriology.

### Space, Time, Motion, and Rest

The four remaining non-living substances receive careful treatment. *Akasha* (space) provides the locus for all substances; *kala* (time) enables change and persistence; *dharma* (motion) and *adharma* (rest) serve as auxiliary conditions for movement and stillness. These concepts represent unique Jain contributions to Indian metaphysics, without parallel in Buddhist or Hindu systems.

### The Relationship Between Jiva and Ajiva

Digambara interpreters, following Kundakunda, emphasize the radical ontological distinction between soul and non-soul. From the ultimate perspective, the soul is entirely different from matter, and their apparent interaction is a consequence of beginningless karmic association. Liberation consists in the soul's complete disentanglement from material bondage.
