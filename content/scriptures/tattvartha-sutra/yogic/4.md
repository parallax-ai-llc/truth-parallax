The yogic practitioner's engagement with the fourth chapter's teaching on celestial beings focuses on the dangers of attachment to supernatural experiences. As meditative practice deepens, practitioners may experience visions, powers, and states of extraordinary bliss that correspond to the conditions of celestial existence. The chapter's teaching that even the highest devas remain in bondage serves as a warning against becoming attached to these experiences.

Jain yogic tradition draws from this chapter the principle of vairāgya (dispassion) toward all states of existence, including the most exalted. The goal of practice is not the acquisition of supernatural powers or heavenly pleasures but the complete destruction of karma and the realization of the soul's inherent perfection. Any experience short of kevala-jñāna, however extraordinary, remains a station on the path rather than the destination.
