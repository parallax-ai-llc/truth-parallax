The ninth chapter is the most directly relevant to yogic practice, providing detailed instructions for the stoppage and shedding of karma. The external austerities (bāhya-tapas) — fasting, reduced diet, withdrawal, bodily mortification, modesty, and meditation — and the internal austerities (abhyantara-tapas) — expiation, reverence, service, study, renunciation, and meditation — constitute the complete yogic program for spiritual purification.

The chapter's treatment of dhyāna (meditation) receives special emphasis in yogic interpretation. The four types of meditation described — painful (ārta), cruel (raudra), righteous (dharma), and pure (śukla) — represent a progression from the agitated mental states of ordinary consciousness to the perfectly still awareness that precedes kevala-jñāna. The yogic practitioner works systematically through these stages, using specific techniques to transform the quality of meditative experience.
