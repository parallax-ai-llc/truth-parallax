## Overview

The yogic reading of the Tattvartha Sutra focuses on the text's practical teachings regarding the disciplining of body, speech, and mind as the means of spiritual purification. The Jain understanding of yoga differs from the Patañjali tradition but shares its concern with the systematic control of mental and physical activity. The Tattvartha Sutra's discussion of the influx and stoppage of karma provides the theoretical foundation for Jain yogic practice.

## Theological Emphases

Central to the yogic interpretation is the text's teaching on the three guptis (restraints of mind, speech, and body) and the practice of kayotsarga (abandonment of the body) as a meditative discipline. These practices, described in the chapters on stoppage and shedding of karma, constitute a comprehensive program of psychophysical transformation aimed at freeing the soul from the weight of accumulated karmic matter.

## Interpretive Context

The yogic tradition within Jainism reads the Tattvartha Sutra's progression from right faith through right knowledge to right conduct as describing a path of interior transformation. The fourteen stages of spiritual development (guṇasthāna), while not explicitly enumerated in the text, are understood as implicit in its systematic account of the soul's journey from bondage to liberation.
