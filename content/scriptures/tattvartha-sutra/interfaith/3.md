## Suffering as a Universal Theme

Interfaith interpretation of Chapter 3 approaches the descriptions of hell realms as an occasion for inter-religious dialogue about the nature of suffering, its causes, and its role in spiritual development. Rather than debating the literal existence of hell, interfaith interpreters focus on the shared recognition that suffering is a profound dimension of sentient existence.

### The Problem of Suffering

Every major religion addresses the reality of suffering: Christianity through the theology of the Cross, Buddhism through the First Noble Truth, Islam through the concepts of trial and divine wisdom, Judaism through the book of Job. The Jain account of suffering as the natural result of harmful actions offers a distinctive perspective that invites dialogue about moral responsibility and the relationship between action and consequence.

### Compassion as Response

The contemplation of suffering in the hell realms is intended to generate compassion (*anukampa*) for all beings trapped in the cycle of birth and death. This compassion-generating function of suffering contemplation finds parallels in Buddhist meditation on suffering, Christian meditation on the Passion, and Jewish engagement with communal suffering. Across traditions, the contemplation of suffering can become a doorway to deeper compassion.

### Justice and Mercy

The Jain understanding of hellish existence as a natural karmic consequence raises interfaith questions about justice and mercy. While the Jain system operates without divine mercy, other traditions emphasize the possibility of divine intervention, forgiveness, or intercession. These different responses to the problem of suffering provide rich material for interfaith theological exchange.
