## Action, Consequence, and Responsibility

Interfaith interpretation of Chapter 6 engages the Jain theory of karmic influx as a basis for inter-religious dialogue about moral responsibility, the consequences of human action, and the relationship between individual conduct and spiritual destiny.

### Moral Causation Across Traditions

The principle that actions have moral consequences is shared across religious traditions, though the mechanism differs. The Jain concept of karmic influx offers a materialistic explanation; Christianity speaks of divine judgment and grace; Islam of divine recording and reckoning; Buddhism of mental formations and their fruits. Interfaith dialogue benefits from comparing these different accounts while recognizing their shared conviction that moral conduct matters ultimately.

### The Inner Life

The chapter's emphasis on passions as the intensifiers of karmic bondage resonates with the cross-traditional recognition that the inner life is spiritually decisive. Jesus' teaching that sin proceeds from the heart, the Buddhist emphasis on mental purification, the Islamic concept of *tazkiyah* (purification of the soul), and the Hindu discipline of the mind all converge on the insight that external actions matter less than the inner dispositions from which they spring.

### Collective and Individual Responsibility

Interfaith interpreters note that the Jain karma theory is strictly individualistic -- each soul bears the consequences of its own actions alone. This invites dialogue with traditions that emphasize collective dimensions of moral responsibility: the Christian concept of corporate sin, the Jewish idea of communal covenant, and indigenous concepts of community obligation.
