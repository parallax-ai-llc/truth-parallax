## The Human Condition

Interfaith interpretation of Chapter 8 engages the Jain analysis of karmic bondage as a contribution to the universal religious question: what is wrong with the human condition, and how can it be remedied? Every major religion offers a diagnosis of the human predicament, and the Jain account provides distinctive perspectives for interfaith reflection.

### Diagnoses of the Human Condition

The Jain understanding of bondage as the entanglement of soul with karmic matter invites comparison with other religious diagnoses: Christian original sin, Buddhist *dukkha* (suffering rooted in craving), Hindu *avidya* (ignorance of the true self), and Islamic *ghaflah* (heedlessness of God). Each tradition identifies something fundamentally problematic about the ordinary human condition while affirming the possibility of transformation.

### The Structure of Bondage

The chapter's detailed analysis of karmic bondage suggests that the human predicament is complex and multifaceted, requiring a correspondingly comprehensive response. This insight resonates with traditions that recognize multiple dimensions of human brokenness: physical, psychological, social, and spiritual.

### Hope Amid Bondage

Despite its sober analysis of karmic bondage, the Tattvartha Sutra maintains that liberation is possible for every soul. This message of universal hope resonates with the salvific promises of other traditions: Christian salvation, Buddhist enlightenment, Hindu liberation, and Islamic paradise. Interfaith interpreters find in this shared hope a basis for mutual encouragement across religious boundaries.
