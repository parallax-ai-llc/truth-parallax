## The Ultimate Hope

Interfaith interpretation of Chapter 10 engages the Jain vision of *moksha* as a contribution to the universal human longing for ultimate fulfillment, freedom, and wholeness. The chapter's description of the liberated soul provides a vision of human possibility that invites comparison and dialogue across religious boundaries.

### Visions of Fulfillment

The Jain siddha state -- characterized by infinite knowledge, perception, bliss, and energy -- resonates with visions of ultimate fulfillment in other traditions: the Christian beatific vision, the Islamic paradise, the Buddhist nirvana, the Hindu *sat-chit-ananda* (being-consciousness-bliss), and the Sikh *sachkhand* (realm of truth). Interfaith interpreters explore these parallels while respecting the distinctive features of each tradition's eschatology.

### Self-Realization and Theosis

The Jain understanding of liberation as the soul's realization of its own inherent perfection finds parallels in the Christian concept of *theosis* (deification), the Hindu concept of *atma-jnana* (self-knowledge), and the Buddhist concept of realizing one's buddha-nature. These parallels suggest a shared mystical intuition across traditions: that the ultimate goal involves not the acquisition of something new but the uncovering of what was always already present.

### A World Without Suffering

The Jain vision of liberation ultimately points toward a world in which all souls achieve freedom from suffering. This eschatological hope finds echoes in the Christian Kingdom of God, the Buddhist aspiration for the liberation of all sentient beings, the Jewish concept of *tikkun olam* (repair of the world), and the Islamic hope for a world of perfect justice. Interfaith interpreters find in these shared hopes a common motivation for collaborative work toward a more compassionate and just world.
