## Ethics as Shared Practice

Interfaith interpretation of Chapter 7 highlights the Jain system of vows and restraints as a resource for inter-religious dialogue about ethical practice, moral discipline, and the cultivation of virtue. The chapter's ethical framework offers both common ground and distinctive contributions to interfaith ethical conversation.

### Non-Violence as Meeting Point

The centrality of *ahimsa* in the Jain ethical system provides perhaps the most productive meeting point for interfaith dialogue. Non-violence in various forms -- Buddhist compassion, Christian love of enemies, Gandhian *satyagraha*, Quaker pacifism, Jewish *shalom*, Islamic *rahma* -- is valued across traditions. The Jain articulation of non-violence as the foundational ethical principle invites sustained interfaith reflection on the scope and limits of non-violent commitment.

### Simplicity and Detachment

The vow of non-possession (*aparigraha*) connects with simplicity traditions across religions: Franciscan poverty, Buddhist renunciation, Islamic *zuhd* (ascetic detachment), Hindu *vairagya* (dispassion), and Quaker simplicity. In an era of ecological crisis and economic inequality, the shared religious value of voluntary simplicity offers common ground for interfaith action.

### Truthfulness and Dialogue

The vow of truthfulness (*satya*) provides a meta-ethical foundation for interfaith dialogue itself. The commitment to honest communication, respect for the perspectives of others, and the recognition that truth is served by genuine engagement with different viewpoints aligns with the Jain philosophical principle of *anekantavada* (many-sidedness).
