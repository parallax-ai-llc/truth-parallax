## The Sacredness of All Life

Interfaith interpretation of Chapter 2 highlights the Jain doctrine of universal ensoulment as a resource for inter-religious dialogue on the moral status of non-human life, environmental ethics, and the scope of compassion.

### Expanding the Circle of Concern

The Tattvartha Sutra's teaching that all living beings -- from the simplest one-sensed organisms to the most complex five-sensed beings -- possess souls worthy of moral consideration offers a radical expansion of the moral community. Interfaith interpreters find resonances with indigenous traditions' recognition of the sacred in all natural phenomena, the Franciscan tradition of kinship with creation, and Buddhist teachings on the sentience of all beings.

### Consciousness as Common Ground

The chapter's identification of consciousness as the defining characteristic of the soul provides a meeting point for inter-religious dialogue about the nature of awareness. Whether understood as Jain *jiva*, Buddhist *vijnana*, Hindu *chaitanya*, or Christian *anima*, consciousness emerges as a shared concern across traditions that grounds the dignity and moral significance of all sentient beings.

### Environmental Ethics

The Jain recognition of souls in earth, water, fire, and air invites dialogue with traditions that share a commitment to environmental stewardship: Islamic *khalifah* (stewardship), Christian creation care, indigenous earth-centered spiritualities, and Buddhist interdependence. The Tattvartha Sutra's comprehensive scope provides philosophical grounding for ecological ethics that transcends any single tradition.
