---
title: "Tattvartha Sutra - Chapter 6"
scripture: "tattvartha-sutra"
chapter: 6
---

**1** The stopping of the influx of karma is called Samvara. It is achieved by the practice of the vows, carefulness, and self-discipline.

**2** The five great vows for monks are: non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possessiveness. These vows, when practised with completeness, stop the influx of all harmful karma.

**3** The five forms of carefulness are: carefulness in walking, carefulness in speaking, carefulness in eating, carefulness in placing and removing objects, and carefulness in attending to the calls of nature.

**4** The three guards are: the guard of body, the guard of speech, and the guard of mind. By guarding these three, the monk prevents harmful activities.

**5** The ten forms of dharma are: forgiveness, humility, straightforwardness, contentment, truthfulness, self-restraint, austerity, renunciation, non-attachment, and celibacy.

**6** The twelve contemplations are: impermanence, helplessness, the cycle of rebirth, aloneness, separateness, the impurity of the body, the influx of karma, the stopping of karma, the shedding of karma, the nature of the universe, the difficulty of attaining right faith, and the truth of the teaching.

**7** The twenty-two hardships that the monk must endure are: hunger, thirst, cold, heat, insect bites, nakedness, tedium, women, wandering, sitting, resting, abuse, beating, begging, failure to obtain food, disease, thorns, dirt, respect, knowledge, ignorance, and lack of faith.

**8** By enduring these hardships with equanimity, the monk conquers his passions and stops the influx of karma.

**9** The monk who has achieved perfect Samvara does not attract any new karma. His soul is like a clean cloth that no longer attracts dust.

**10** From this state, the monk proceeds to shed the remaining karma through the practice of austerities, leading to complete liberation.
