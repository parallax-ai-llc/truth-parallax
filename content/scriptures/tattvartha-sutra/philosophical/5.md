## Ontological Foundations

Philosophical analysis of Chapter 5 engages with the Tattvartha Sutra's ontology of six eternal substances. This ontological framework raises fundamental questions about the nature of substance, change, and the relationship between the permanent and the impermanent.

### The Doctrine of Sat

The chapter's treatment of reality (*sat*) as that which possesses origination, destruction, and persistence (*utpada-vyaya-dhrauvya*) represents a sophisticated philosophical position. Reality is neither purely permanent (as in the Vedantic *satkaryavada*) nor purely momentary (as in the Buddhist *ksanikavada*), but involves both change and continuity. Philosophical interpreters analyze this position as a form of moderate realism that avoids the extremes of eternalism and annihilationism.

### Substance and Mode

The distinction between *dravya* (substance) and *paryaya* (mode) provides the ontological foundation for the Jain theory of predication. A substance persists through its various modifications, just as gold persists through its various forms (ring, bracelet, necklace). Philosophical interpreters connect this doctrine to the theory of *anekantavada*: because every substance is both permanent (in its essential nature) and changing (in its modal expressions), any complete description of reality must accommodate both perspectives.

### The Problem of Interaction

If soul and matter are fundamentally different types of substance, how do they interact? This question -- analogous to the mind-body problem in Western philosophy -- receives its Jain answer in the doctrine of the soul's beginningless association with karma. The interaction is not newly established but has always obtained, and liberation consists in undoing this primordial entanglement.
