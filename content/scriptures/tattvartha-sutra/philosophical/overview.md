## Overview

Philosophical interpretation of the Tattvartha Sutra examines the text as a work of systematic philosophy, analyzing its contributions to metaphysics, epistemology, logic, and ethics. The sutra's rigorous categorization of reality, its theory of knowledge, and its ethical framework constitute one of the most sophisticated philosophical systems produced in ancient India.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Substance Metaphysics

The Tattvartha Sutra presents a realist ontology that recognizes six eternal substances (*dravya*): soul (jiva), matter (pudgala), space (akasha), time (kala), and the principles of motion (dharma) and rest (adharma). Philosophical interpreters analyze this framework's internal coherence and compare it with other substance ontologies, both Indian and Western.

### Epistemology

The text's opening verse -- "Right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct together constitute the path to liberation" -- establishes an epistemological framework in which knowledge is both a means to and a component of salvation. The classification of knowledge into five types (sensory, scriptural, clairvoyant, telepathic, and omniscient) raises important philosophical questions about the nature and limits of human cognition.

### Logic and Predication

The Jain theory of *syadvada* (conditional predication), which underlies the Tattvartha Sutra's approach to philosophical claims, represents a distinctive contribution to logic. Every proposition can be affirmed, denied, or declared inexpressible from different standpoints, yielding seven possible predications.

## Philosophical Significance

| Domain | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| Metaphysics | Pluralistic realism with six eternal substances |
| Epistemology | Five types of knowledge, perspectival truth |
| Logic | Seven-fold predication (saptabhangi) |
| Ethics | Non-violence as ontologically grounded duty |
