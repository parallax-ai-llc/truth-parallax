## Causation and Moral Responsibility

Philosophical analysis of Chapter 6 examines the Jain theory of karmic influx as a contribution to the philosophy of action, moral causation, and responsibility. The chapter's account of how actions produce karmic consequences raises fundamental questions about the nature of agency and the relationship between intention and outcome.

### The Causal Theory of Karma

The Tattvartha Sutra presents karma as operating through a strict causal mechanism: activities create vibrations in the soul that attract karmic particles. Philosophical interpreters analyze this as a form of moral naturalism -- the moral consequences of actions are natural, lawlike processes, not arbitrary divine rewards or punishments. This naturalistic account raises the question of whether morality is reducible to natural causation or whether normative concepts (good, bad, right, wrong) retain an irreducible significance.

### Intention and Consequence

The chapter's analysis of how passions intensify karmic influx suggests a philosophical position in which both intention and consequence matter morally. The type of karma attracted depends on the action performed (a consequentialist element), but the intensity and duration of bondage depend on the agent's passions (a virtue-theoretic element). This integrated position offers a philosophical alternative to the Western debate between consequentialism and deontology.

### The Problem of Involuntary Harm

The Tattvartha Sutra acknowledges that some karmic influx occurs through unavoidable activities (breathing, walking, eating). Philosophical interpreters examine how the tradition handles the moral status of involuntary harm, developing concepts of graduated responsibility and compensatory practice that have parallels in Western legal and moral philosophy.
