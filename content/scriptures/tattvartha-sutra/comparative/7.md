## Ethical Systems in Comparative View

Comparative study of Chapter 7 examines the Jain system of vows and restraints alongside the ethical frameworks of other world religions. The Jain approach offers a distinctive combination of rigorous non-violence, graduated commitment, and practical accommodation.

### Non-Violence Compared

The Jain *ahimsa* described in this chapter represents perhaps the most comprehensive commitment to non-violence in any world religion. Comparative scholars place it alongside Buddhist *karuna* (compassion), Christian commandments against killing, the Hippocratic principle of "do no harm," and Gandhian *satyagraha*. While each tradition values non-violence, the Jain extension of the principle to microscopic organisms and elemental beings is unparalleled.

### Monastic and Lay Ethics

The two-tier ethical system of *mahavratas* (for monastics) and *anuvratas* (for laypeople) invites comparison with similar distinctions in Buddhism (bhikkhu vinaya vs. five precepts), Catholicism (evangelical counsels vs. commandments), and Hinduism (sannyasa vs. grihastha dharma). Each tradition negotiates the relationship between radical ethical ideals and the practical demands of worldly life.

### Vows and Covenant

The concept of vows (*vratas*) as solemn commitments that structure religious life finds parallels in Christian monastic vows, Jewish covenantal obligations, Islamic submission to divine law, and Buddhist precepts. Comparative scholars examine how the formal assumption of binding commitments functions across traditions to create religious identity and community.
