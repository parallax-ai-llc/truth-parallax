## Soul Theories in World Religions

Comparative study of Chapter 2 places the Jain doctrine of *jiva* alongside other theories of the soul, self, or personhood found in world religious and philosophical traditions. The Jain position is distinctive in several respects that emerge clearly through comparison.

### The Soul in Indian Traditions

The Jain *jiva* differs from the Hindu *atman* (which is ultimately identical with Brahman in Advaita Vedanta) and the Buddhist rejection of a permanent self (*anatta*). The Jain soul is individual, eternal, and distinct from all other souls, yet it is not static -- it actively knows, perceives, and acts. This position navigates between the extremes of monism and nihilism.

### Western Parallels

The Jain theory of the soul bears interesting resemblance to certain Western philosophical positions: Leibniz's monadology (individual spiritual substances with inherent perceptual capacity), Aristotelian hylomorphism (the soul as the form of the body), and contemporary property dualism. However, the Jain insistence on the soul's materiality-interacting nature sets it apart from most Western substance dualism.

### The Scope of Ensoulment

The Tattvartha Sutra's attribution of souls to earth, water, fire, air, and plants goes far beyond what most other traditions recognize. This broad understanding of sentience has led comparative scholars to examine Jain thought alongside indigenous animistic traditions, contemporary deep ecology, and emerging scientific discussions about plant intelligence and consciousness.
