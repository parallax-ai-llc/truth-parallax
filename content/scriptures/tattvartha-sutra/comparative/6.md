## Karmic Causation Across Traditions

Comparative study of Chapter 6 examines the Jain concept of karmic influx alongside other religious and philosophical accounts of moral causation, spiritual contamination, and the mechanisms by which actions shape the soul.

### Karma East and West

The Jain theory of *asrava* is compared with Hindu *karma* (moral causation within divine providence), Buddhist *karma* (volitional action producing consequences), and even Western concepts such as Aristotelian habituation (actions shaping character) and original sin (the inherited spiritual condition). Each tradition offers a different account of how moral actions affect the agent's spiritual state.

### Pollution and Purification

The Jain notion that karma physically pollutes the soul finds parallels in pollution-and-purification concepts across world religions: the Zoroastrian concept of *druj* (pollution), Jewish concepts of *tumah* (ritual impurity), Shinto concepts of *kegare* (defilement), and Hindu concepts of *mala* (impurity). In each case, specific practices are prescribed for removing the contamination.

### The Role of Intention

Chapter 6's emphasis on the role of passions in determining karmic quality invites comparison with other traditions' treatments of intention: the Buddhist focus on *cetana* (volition), the Christian emphasis on sin as proceeding from the heart, the Islamic concept of *niyyah* (intention) in determining the moral value of actions. Across traditions, the inner state of the agent is recognized as morally decisive.
