## Metaphysics of the Non-Living

Comparative study of Chapter 5 examines the Tattvartha Sutra's analysis of non-living substances alongside other philosophical and religious accounts of the material world, space, time, and the principles underlying physical reality.

### Atomic Theories Compared

The Jain theory of *pudgala* (matter) as composed of indivisible atoms finds parallels in Greek atomism (Democritus, Epicurus), Vaisheshika atomic theory, and Buddhist *dharma* theory. Each tradition arrived independently at a particulate theory of matter, but with significant differences: Jain atoms possess sensory qualities, Greek atoms differ only in shape and size, and Buddhist dharmas are momentary events rather than persistent substances.

### Space and Time

The Tattvartha Sutra's treatment of space and time as distinct substances invites comparison with Western philosophical treatments. The Jain concept of *akasha* (space) as an infinite, all-accommodating substance resembles the Newtonian concept of absolute space, while the treatment of *kala* (time) as the enabler of change parallels aspects of the Aristotelian analysis of time as the measure of motion.

### The Concepts of Dharma and Adharma

The Jain principles of motion and rest have no parallels in other philosophical systems, making them a distinctive contribution to world metaphysics. Comparative philosophers have attempted to relate them to the concept of a medium (as in the ether theory of pre-Einsteinian physics) or to field theories in modern physics, though such comparisons remain tentative.
