## Visions of Ultimate Freedom

Comparative study of Chapter 10 examines the Jain concept of *moksha* alongside the ultimate goals described in other world religious and philosophical traditions. This comparison reveals both shared aspirations and fundamental differences in how traditions conceptualize the final state of spiritual achievement.

### Liberation in Indian Context

Jain *moksha* is compared with Hindu *mukti* (liberation from *samsara*, variously understood as union with Brahman, dwelling with God, or realization of the self), Buddhist *nirvana* (cessation of suffering, blowing out of the fires of craving), and Sikh *mukti* (union with the divine Name). The Jain concept is distinctive in maintaining the individual identity of the liberated soul and describing liberation as the removal of material karma rather than the realization of an underlying unity.

### Western and Abrahamic Parallels

The Jain siddha state is compared with the Christian beatific vision, the Islamic concept of *fana* (annihilation in God) and *baqa* (subsistence in God), and the Jewish concept of *olam ha-ba* (the world to come). The most striking difference is the Jain understanding of liberation as achievable through self-effort alone, without divine grace or intervention.

### Philosophical Questions

Chapter 10 raises philosophical questions that resonate across traditions: Is the liberated state one of knowledge or of being? Is personal identity preserved in the ultimate state? Is liberation permanent or temporary? Can the liberated being relate to the unliberated? These questions have been debated within Christianity (the nature of resurrected life), Buddhism (the nature of parinirvana), and Hinduism (the status of the *jivanmukta*) as well as within Jainism.
