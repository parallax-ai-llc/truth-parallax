## Hell Realms Across Traditions

Comparative study of Chapter 3 examines how the Jain conception of hell compares with descriptions of the afterlife in other world religions. The Jain hell realms share structural features with counterparts in other traditions while differing in crucial theological details.

### Indian Hells Compared

The Jain, Buddhist, and Hindu traditions all describe multiple hell realms of varying severity, but their underlying theologies differ significantly. Buddhist hells are products of mind and karma without material substance; Hindu hells are governed by Yama, a divine judge; Jain hells are natural consequences of karmic matter without any divine administration or judgment.

### Abrahamic Afterlife Concepts

The Jain hell differs fundamentally from the Christian and Islamic concepts of hell as eternal punishment by a just God. In Jainism, hellish existence is always temporary, mechanistically determined by karma, and devoid of any divine intentionality. This makes the Jain hell closer to a natural consequence than a punishment, more akin to certain Buddhist interpretations than to Abrahamic models.

### Moral Psychology of Fear

Comparative scholars examine the motivational function of hell descriptions across traditions. In Jainism, the fear of hellish rebirth is intended to generate *samvega* (spiritual urgency); in Christianity, the fear of hell serves as a spur to repentance and faith; in Buddhism, hell serves to motivate compassion and wisdom. The psychological mechanisms are similar even where the theological frameworks differ dramatically.
