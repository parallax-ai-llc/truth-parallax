## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Tattvartha Sutra places Jain philosophy alongside other religious and philosophical traditions to illuminate both shared concerns and distinctive contributions. The text's systematic treatment of karma, the soul, cosmology, and liberation provides rich material for cross-traditional analysis.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Karma Theories Compared

The Tattvartha Sutra's treatment of karma as a material substance that physically binds to the soul differs significantly from Hindu and Buddhist conceptions. In Hindu traditions, karma operates through a more abstract moral law; in Buddhism, karma functions without a permanent soul. These differences illuminate fundamental divergences in Indian metaphysics.

### Non-Violence Across Traditions

The Jain commitment to *ahimsa* (non-violence), as articulated in the Tattvartha Sutra, represents perhaps the most radical form of this ethical principle across world religions. Comparative scholars examine parallels with Buddhist compassion, Christian pacifism, Gandhian non-violence, and modern animal rights ethics.

### Pluralism and Tolerance

The Jain doctrine of *anekantavada* (many-sidedness), implicit throughout the Tattvartha Sutra, offers a distinctive approach to religious pluralism. Comparative theologians find parallels with perspectivism in Western philosophy, Buddhist two-truths doctrine, and contemporary pluralistic theology.

## Comparative Framework

| Concept | Jainism | Buddhism | Hinduism |
|---------|---------|----------|----------|
| Soul | Jiva (eternal, individual) | No-self (anatta) | Atman (identical with Brahman) |
| Karma | Material substance | Mental volition | Moral law |
| Liberation | Removal of karmic matter | Nirvana (cessation) | Union with Brahman |
| Non-violence | Supreme ethical duty | Compassion-based | Contextual (dharma) |
