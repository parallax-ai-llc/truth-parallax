## Practices of Liberation Compared

Comparative study of Chapter 9 examines the Jain practices of stopping and shedding karma alongside the purification, ascetic, and contemplative practices prescribed in other world religions for overcoming spiritual bondage.

### Asceticism Across Traditions

The austerities described in Chapter 9 find parallels in the ascetic traditions of other religions: Christian Desert Fathers, Hindu tapas, Buddhist asceticism (in its moderate form after the Buddha's rejection of extreme mortification), Sufi practices of self-denial, and Jewish fasting traditions. Comparative scholars note that the Jain tradition retains the most unqualified endorsement of severe austerity among major world religions.

### Meditation Traditions

The Tattvartha Sutra's treatment of meditation is compared with Buddhist *samatha* and *vipassana*, Hindu *dhyana* and *samadhi*, Christian contemplative prayer, Sufi *muraqaba*, and Daoist inner alchemy. The Jain classification of four types of meditation, with only the highest two contributing to liberation, parallels similar qualitative distinctions made in other contemplative traditions.

### The Goal of Purification

The concept of spiritual purification -- removing accumulated impurity to reveal the soul's innate purity -- appears across traditions: Christian sanctification, Buddhist path of purification, Hindu *shuddhi*, Shinto misogi, and Jain *nirjara*. Comparative scholars examine whether these parallel practices reflect a universal human religious impulse or historically connected traditions of spiritual cultivation.
