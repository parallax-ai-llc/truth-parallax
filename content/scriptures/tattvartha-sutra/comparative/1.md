## Foundations of the Path Across Traditions

Comparative interpretation of Chapter 1 examines how the Tattvartha Sutra's formulation of the threefold path -- right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct -- parallels and diverges from foundational path-formulations in other world religions.

### The Three Jewels Compared

The Jain *ratnatraya* invites comparison with the Buddhist Three Jewels (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha), the Christian theological virtues (faith, hope, charity), and the Islamic pillars of belief (*iman*), submission (*islam*), and spiritual excellence (*ihsan*). While structural parallels exist, the Jain formulation is distinctive in its equal emphasis on cognitive and ethical dimensions.

### Epistemological Frameworks

The Tattvartha Sutra's classification of knowledge types finds parallels in other traditions: the Buddhist distinction between conventional and ultimate knowledge, the Sufi levels of knowledge (rational, intuitive, mystical), and the Thomistic hierarchy of natural and supernatural knowledge. The Jain framework is unique in positing a continuous spectrum from ordinary perception to complete omniscience.

### Faith as Foundation

The chapter's treatment of *samyag-darshana* (right faith) as the prerequisite for spiritual progress parallels the role of faith in Abrahamic traditions. However, Jain right faith is not faith in a personal God but rather an intuitive conviction in the fundamental structure of reality as described by the omniscient Tirthankaras, making it closer to a philosophical commitment than religious trust.
