## Divine Beings in Cross-Cultural Perspective

Comparative study of Chapter 4 examines the Jain understanding of celestial beings alongside the concepts of divinity, angels, and spiritual beings found in other world religions. The Jain treatment is notable for its systematic classification and its thoroughly non-theistic framework.

### Gods Without a God

The Jain celestial beings are not gods in the Western theistic sense -- they are not creators, judges, or saviors. Comparative scholars note that this represents one of the most thoroughgoing non-theistic theologies in world religion, more radical even than the Buddhist treatment of *devas*, since Jainism's rejection of a creator deity is more explicit and philosophically developed.

### Angelic Parallels

The *Vyantara* and *Vaimanika* devas invite comparison with the angelic hierarchies of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. Like angels, Jain celestial beings possess superhuman powers, inhabit non-earthly realms, and interact with the human world. However, unlike angels, they are not servants of a supreme God but independent souls reaping the fruits of their own karma.

### Celestial Hierarchies

The systematic organization of celestial beings into ranked orders parallels similar hierarchical systems in Neoplatonic philosophy (the Great Chain of Being), medieval Christian angelology (the nine choirs of Pseudo-Dionysius), and Hindu cosmology (the various lokas inhabited by different classes of devas). Comparative scholars examine the different principles used to organize these hierarchies across traditions.
