## Overview

The Tattvartha Sutra (also known as Tattvarthadhigama Sutra, "That Which Is") is the most authoritative and comprehensive summary of Jain philosophy and doctrine. Composed by Acharya Umasvati (or Umasvami) around the 2nd century CE, it holds the unique distinction of being accepted as authoritative by both major Jain traditions -- Digambara and Shvetambara.

## Structure

The text is organized into ten chapters covering the entire range of Jain metaphysics, cosmology, ethics, and soteriology:

1. **Faith, Knowledge, and Conduct**: The three jewels (*ratnatraya*) as the path to liberation
2. **The Soul**: Categories and characteristics of living beings
3. **The Lower Worlds**: Descriptions of hell realms and their inhabitants
4. **The Celestial Beings**: The various orders of divine beings
5. **Non-living Substances**: Matter, space, time, and the principles of motion
6. **Influx of Karma**: How karmic matter enters the soul
7. **Vows and Restraints**: Ethical conduct for laypeople and monastics
8. **Bondage of Karma**: How karma binds to the soul
9. **Stopping and Shedding Karma**: Practices of spiritual purification
10. **Liberation**: The nature of the freed soul

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Digambara**: Attribute the text to Umasvami and consider it a foundational doctrinal work
- **Shvetambara**: Attribute it to Umasvati and likewise regard it as supremely authoritative
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding Indian philosophical systems
- **Comparative**: Important for inter-religious dialogue on karma, non-violence, and metaphysics

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Anekantavada | The doctrine of many-sidedness; reality is complex and multifaceted |
| Ahimsa | Non-violence as the supreme ethical principle |
| Karma | A material substance that binds to the soul through action |
| Moksha | Liberation through right faith, knowledge, and conduct |
| Syadvada | The doctrine of conditional predication |
