## Jain Metaphysics in Philosophical Context

Academic study of Chapter 5 situates the Tattvartha Sutra's analysis of non-living substances within the broader landscape of Indian and comparative metaphysics. The Jain theory of *dravya* (substance) represents one of the most systematically developed realist ontologies in the Indian philosophical tradition.

### Substance Theory

Scholars analyze the Jain six-substance ontology in relation to other Indian metaphysical systems: the Vaisheshika theory of *padarthas* (categories), the Samkhya dualism of *purusha* and *prakriti*, and the Buddhist rejection of permanent substances in favor of momentary dharmas. The Jain position combines elements of pluralistic realism with a dynamic theory of modal change that avoids both eternalism and annihilationism.

### The Theory of Pudgala

The Jain theory of matter (*pudgala*) as composed of indivisible atoms (*paramanus*) has attracted scholarly comparison with Greek atomism, Vaisheshika atomic theory, and modern particle physics. While the analogies should not be pressed too far, the Jain atomic theory represents an independently developed tradition of particulate matter theory with distinctive features.

### Dharma and Adharma as Unique Concepts

The Jain principles of motion (*dharma*) and rest (*adharma*) have no parallels in other Indian systems and have therefore received particular scholarly attention. Interpreted as auxiliary conditions rather than efficient causes, they represent a nuanced understanding of causation that distinguishes the Jain position from both the Buddhist emphasis on conditions and the Hindu emphasis on divine agency.
