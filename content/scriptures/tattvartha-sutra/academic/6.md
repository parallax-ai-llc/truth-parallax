## Karma Theory: Mechanism and Morality

Academic study of Chapter 6 examines the Jain theory of *asrava* (karmic influx) as one of the most detailed and systematic treatments of karma in Indian religious thought. The Jain understanding of karma as a physical, material process distinguishes it from the more abstract moral-cosmic law described in Hindu and Buddhist traditions.

### The Materiality of Karma

Scholars note that the Tattvartha Sutra's treatment of karmic influx presupposes a uniquely Jain understanding of karma as subtle material particles that physically interact with the soul. This materialistic karma theory has no exact parallel in other Indian systems, where karma typically operates as a moral law or as mental dispositions rather than as physical substance.

### Agency and Determinism

Chapter 6 raises important philosophical questions about the relationship between agency and karmic determination. Academic interpreters debate whether the Jain system allows for genuine free will or whether the soul's activities are themselves determined by prior karma. The Jain position appears to maintain a compatibilist view: the soul possesses genuine agency, but its exercise of that agency is conditioned by existing karmic circumstances.

### Ethical Implications

The chapter's analysis of how different types of actions and intentions produce different types of karmic influx has been compared by scholars with Western ethical theories, particularly consequentialism and virtue ethics. The Jain emphasis on intention as a key determinant of karmic consequence aligns with certain deontological and virtue-theoretic perspectives.
