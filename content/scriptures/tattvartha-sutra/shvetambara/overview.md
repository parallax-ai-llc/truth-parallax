## Overview

The Shvetambara ("white-clad") tradition represents the other major branch of Jainism, whose monastics wear white garments and whose scriptural canon is more extensive than the Digambara collection. Shvetambara interpretation of the Tattvartha Sutra is shaped by its broader canonical context and its somewhat more inclusive understanding of the path to liberation.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Attribution to Umasvati

Shvetambara tradition attributes the text to Acharya Umasvati and preserves a recension that includes Umasvati's own auto-commentary (*svopajnabhashya*). This auto-commentary provides invaluable insight into the author's intended meaning and is considered an integral part of the text.

### Canonical Context

Shvetambara interpreters read the Tattvartha Sutra within the larger context of the Shvetambara Agamas -- the canonical scriptures believed to preserve the teachings of Mahavira through his immediate disciples. The sutra is understood as a systematic summary of agamic teachings.

### Inclusive Path

Shvetambara interpretation tends to emphasize that the path of liberation is available to all sincere practitioners, including women and householders at various stages. While full moksha requires monastic commitment, significant spiritual progress is possible within lay life.
