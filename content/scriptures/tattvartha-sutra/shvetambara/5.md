## Analysis of Non-Living Reality

Shvetambara interpretation of Chapter 5 examines the non-living substances (*ajiva*) that, together with the soul, constitute the totality of reality. The chapter's systematic analysis of matter, space, time, and the principles of motion and rest represents one of Jain philosophy's most distinctive contributions.

### The Six Substances

Shvetambara commentators, drawing on Umasvati's auto-commentary, explain the six fundamental substances: *jiva* (soul), *pudgala* (matter), *dharma* (principle of motion), *adharma* (principle of rest), *akasha* (space), and *kala* (time). Each substance is eternal in its essential nature but undergoes modifications (*paryaya*) through time.

### Matter and Its Properties

Matter (*pudgala*) is the only substance that possesses sensory qualities -- touch, taste, smell, and color. Shvetambara interpreters explain that matter exists in both atomic and aggregate forms, and that the interaction of material karma particles with the immaterial soul constitutes the fundamental problem of existence.

### The Unique Jain Concepts

The principles of *dharma* (motion) and *adharma* (rest) are unique to Jain metaphysics. They do not cause movement or stillness but serve as necessary auxiliary conditions, much as water provides the medium in which fish swim. Shvetambara commentators defend these concepts against Buddhist and Hindu critics who questioned their necessity.
