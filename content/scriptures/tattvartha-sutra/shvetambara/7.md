## The System of Vows

Shvetambara interpretation of Chapter 7 presents the ethical framework of Jain practice, encompassing both the lay and monastic paths. The chapter describes the five main vows, supplementary vows, and the spiritual disciplines that constitute the practical core of Jain religious life.

### The Five Great Vows

For Shvetambara monastics, the five *mahavratas* -- non-violence (*ahimsa*), truth (*satya*), non-stealing (*asteya*), celibacy (*brahmacharya*), and non-possession (*aparigraha*) -- are practiced with rigor but with certain practical accommodations. Unlike the Digambara interpretation, Shvetambara tradition permits monastics to wear white garments and carry essential implements such as the mouth-cloth (*muhpatti*) and the soft broom (*rajoharana*).

### Lay Ethical Practice

The five minor vows (*anuvratas*) adapt monastic ideals to householder life. Shvetambara commentators provide detailed guidance on how laypeople can practice limited non-violence, qualified truthfulness, honest livelihood, marital fidelity, and moderated consumption. The system of supplementary vows adds further structure to lay practice.

### Women and the Spiritual Path

A significant point of Shvetambara interpretation concerns women's capacity for liberation. The Shvetambara tradition affirms that women can attain *moksha*, citing the example of the 19th Tirthankara Mallinatha, whom Shvetambara tradition regards as female. This inclusive soteriology shapes the tradition's understanding of who can fully practice the vows described in this chapter.
