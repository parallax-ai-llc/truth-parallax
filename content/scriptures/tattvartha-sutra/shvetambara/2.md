## The Nature of the Soul

Shvetambara interpretation of Chapter 2 examines the characteristics, categories, and conditions of the *jiva* (soul) with careful attention to both doctrinal precision and practical ethical implications. The chapter's teaching that all living beings possess souls grounds the Shvetambara commitment to universal compassion.

### Consciousness as the Soul's Hallmark

The defining characteristic of the soul is *upayoga* (consciousness or cognitive activity), which manifests in two forms: *darshana* (indeterminate awareness) and *jnana* (determinate knowledge). Shvetambara commentators emphasize that consciousness is present in all living beings, from single-sensed organisms to five-sensed beings with rational minds.

### Classification of Living Beings

The chapter classifies beings according to the number of sense organs: one-sensed (earth, water, fire, air, plants), two-sensed (worms), three-sensed (ants, lice), four-sensed (flies, bees), and five-sensed (animals, humans, celestial and hellish beings). Shvetambara interpreters use this classification to establish the scope of ethical responsibility, extending *ahimsa* to all categories of life.

### The Soul's Spatial Extension

A distinctive feature of Jain soul theory is the teaching that the soul pervades the entire body it inhabits, expanding or contracting to match the body's size. Shvetambara commentators explain this through the analogy of a lamp that illuminates a small room and a large hall equally, adapting its radiance to the available space.
