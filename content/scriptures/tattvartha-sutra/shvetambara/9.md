## Practices of Purification

Shvetambara interpretation of Chapter 9 describes the dual process of *samvara* (preventing new karmic influx) and *nirjara* (shedding accumulated karma) that progressively purifies the soul and leads toward liberation.

### Methods of Stopping Influx

Samvara is achieved through multiple interconnected practices: the five types of careful movement (*samiti*), the three types of restraint (*gupti*), the ten virtues (*dharma*), the twelve reflections (*anupreksha*), the twenty-two endurances (*parishaha*), and the five types of conduct (*charitra*). Shvetambara commentators provide practical guidance on integrating these practices into both monastic and lay life.

### External and Internal Austerities

The chapter describes six external austerities (fasting, reduced diet, limiting food types, giving up delicacies, solitary living, and bodily mortification) and six internal austerities (expiation, humility, service, study, meditation, and renunciation). Shvetambara interpreters, while respecting the value of external austerities, tend to emphasize the primacy of internal transformation.

### Meditation Practices

Shvetambara commentators, particularly Haribhadra in his *Yogadrishtisamuccaya*, elaborate the Tattvartha Sutra's brief treatment of meditation into a comprehensive system. Four types of meditation are described: sorrowful (*arta*), cruel (*raudra*), righteous (*dharmya*), and pure (*shukla*). Only the last two contribute to liberation, and the highest meditation leads directly to omniscience.
