The Second Sun, Nahui Ehecatl (Four Wind), was the age governed by Quetzalcoatl in his aspect as the wind god Ehecatl. The humans of this era ate pine nuts (*acocentli*), a step beyond the wild acorns of the First Sun, suggesting a progressive refinement across cosmic ages. This world was destroyed by a great hurricane sent by Tezcatlipoca, and the surviving humans were transformed into monkeys. The wind-destruction reverses the previous dynamic: where the First Sun ended through Quetzalcoatl's intervention, the Second Sun ends through Tezcatlipoca's retaliation.

The transformation of humans into monkeys carries specific meaning within Nahua cosmovision. Monkeys (*ozomatli*) are associated in the *tonalpohualli* with the day-sign that governs art, play, and social celebration but also with foolishness and mimicry without understanding. The humans of the Second Sun were blown away because they lacked the rootedness to withstand cosmic upheaval. For contemporary practitioners, the monkey transformation warns against superficiality — imitating the forms of spiritual life without cultivating the substance. Communities in the Puebla-Tlaxcala valley, where Nahua ceremonial traditions persist, invoke the Second Sun's lesson in coming-of-age teachings that distinguish genuine maturity from mere performance of adulthood.

Ehecatl as the governing principle of the Second Sun connects this age to breath, spirit, and the invisible forces that animate the world. The wind is Quetzalcoatl's breath, the animating principle of life, but also the force that uproots and destroys. Nahua communities recognize this duality in their treatment of wind (*ehecatl*) as a spiritual agent: wind-born illnesses, believed to be caused by encounters with spirits at crossroads and whirlwinds, are treated by curanderos who understand wind as both life-giving and potentially dangerous. The Second Sun's destruction by hurricane is thus not an arbitrary divine act but an expression of wind's inherent ambivalence.

The progressive destruction of the suns models a Nahua understanding of learning through failure. Each cosmic age advances beyond the previous one — from acorns to pine nuts, from giants to ordinary humans — but each also carries a fatal flaw that leads to its dissolution. The gods are experimenters, refining their creation through successive attempts. This cosmology resists fatalism: the destruction of a world-age is not final punishment but a stage in the ongoing process of cosmic education. Contemporary Nahua ritual specialists interpret personal and communal crises through this lens, understanding catastrophe as potential transformation rather than mere loss.
