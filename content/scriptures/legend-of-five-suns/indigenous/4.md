The Fourth Sun, Nahui Atl (Four Water), was governed by Chalchiuhtlicue, "She of the Jade Skirt," the goddess of lakes, rivers, and flowing water. This era's humans ate a cultivated grain (*teocentli*, an ancestor of maize), marking a further advance in the progression of cosmic ages. The Fourth Sun ended in a great flood that lasted fifty-two years — the length of a full calendar round — during which the sky collapsed and the waters rose to consume everything. The surviving humans were transformed into fish, returning to the waters from which the world's sustenance had come.

Chalchiuhtlicue's governance connects the Fourth Sun to the feminine dimension of water — not Tlaloc's storm-rain but the sustaining flow of rivers, the nourishing waters of springs, the amniotic waters of birth. In Nahua communities, springs and rivers are gendered feminine and associated with fertility, cleansing, and the continuous renewal of life. The *temazcal* (sweat bath), still central to Nahua healing and birthing practices, invokes Chalchiuhtlicue's waters as agents of purification and rebirth. The Fourth Sun's destruction by flood is thus simultaneously an ending and a cosmic-scale purification, preparing the world for the final and most complete creation.

The fifty-two-year flood duration is cosmologically precise: it equals one calendar round (*xiuhmolpilli*), the period after which the 260-day *tonalpohualli* and the 365-day *xiuhpohualli* (solar calendar) realign. This links the Fourth Sun's destruction to the New Fire Ceremony (*Toxiuhmolpilia*), which historically marked the completion of each calendar round and was the most anxious moment in the Mexica ceremonial cycle. If the new fire could not be drilled on the designated night, the current age might end as the Fourth Sun ended. Contemporary communities that maintain calendar awareness understand the completion of each cycle as a moment of cosmic vulnerability and renewal.

The fish-transformation of the Fourth Sun's survivors encodes the Nahua understanding that water is the medium of primordial life. All forms return to water before being reconstituted in the next age. This principle shapes indigenous approaches to environmental stewardship: contamination of rivers and aquifers is understood not merely as ecological damage but as an offense against the cosmic element that bridges between world-ages. Nahua environmental activists in communities threatened by mining and industrial agriculture frame their resistance in terms drawn from the Fourth Sun narrative, arguing that to poison the waters is to risk a repetition of the flood that ended the previous world.
