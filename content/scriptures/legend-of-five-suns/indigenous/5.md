The Fifth Sun, Nahui Ollin (Four Movement), is the present age — the era created at Teotihuacan when the humble god Nanahuatzin threw himself into the sacrificial fire and rose as the sun, Tonatiuh. The proud god Tecuciztecatl hesitated four times before following, and his lesser courage resulted in his becoming the dimmer moon. The assembled gods then sacrificed themselves to provide the blood (*chalchihuatl*) that set the sun in motion across the sky. This founding act of divine self-sacrifice establishes the principle of reciprocity (*nextlahualli*) that undergirds the entire Mexica ceremonial system: the sun moves because the gods gave their lives, and humans must reciprocate to sustain the cosmos.

For contemporary Nahua and Mexica-heritage communities, the Fifth Sun's creation at Teotihuacan connects them to a specific sacred landscape. Teotihuacan's pyramids — the Pyramid of the Sun and the Pyramid of the Moon — are not merely archaeological ruins but the physical site where the current age began. Indigenous ceremonial practitioners gather at Teotihuacan during solstices and equinoxes to honor the sacrifice that initiated the Fifth Sun, performing offerings and prayers that sustain the reciprocal relationship between humanity and the sun. The Mexican government's promotion of Teotihuacan as a tourist site creates ongoing tension with communities who regard it as a living ceremonial center.

The Fifth Sun's calendrical sign, Nahui Ollin (Four Movement/Earthquake), specifies that this age will end through seismic cataclysm. In a region where earthquakes are a regular reality — the devastating 1985 and 2017 earthquakes in Mexico City being recent examples — this prophecy carries existential immediacy. Rather than producing fatalism, however, the prediction shapes a posture of active responsibility: the Fifth Sun endures only through continuous human participation in the cosmic economy of sacrifice and reciprocity. When communities fail in their obligations — when ceremonies are abandoned, when the land is abused, when reciprocal relationships break down — the stability of the current age is threatened.

Nanahuatzin's example — the poorest and most diseased god who succeeds where the wealthy and beautiful Tecuciztecatl fails — carries profound social meaning. Humility and courage outweigh wealth and appearance in the cosmic order. This principle informs indigenous critiques of colonial and postcolonial social hierarchies. Contemporary Nahua Elders draw on the Fifth Sun narrative to teach that genuine authority derives from willingness to sacrifice for the community, not from the accumulation of status or resources. The narrative also grounds the pan-indigenous Mexicanidad movement, which seeks to revitalize pre-colonial spiritual practices as a framework for social justice and cultural self-determination in modern Mexico.
