The First Sun, Nahui Ocelotl (Four Jaguar), was presided over by Tezcatlipoca, the Smoking Mirror, who became the sun of this era. The world was inhabited by giants who subsisted on acorns and wild fruits. This age ended when Quetzalcoatl struck Tezcatlipoca from the sky, and in his rage Tezcatlipoca transformed into a great jaguar that devoured the giants. For Nahua communities, this first cosmic epoch establishes a foundational principle: no age is permanent, and even the most powerful force eventually yields to its complementary opposite.

The jaguar destruction of the First Sun carries deep ecological and spiritual meaning. Tezcatlipoca as jaguar represents the predatory power of the earth itself — the untamed forest, the night, the forces that consume what they have created. Contemporary Nahua and Mexica-heritage communities in central Mexico understand the jaguar as an avatar of earthly power, and ceremonial dances invoking the jaguar (*ocelotl*) acknowledge the destructive potential inherent in creation. The giants of this age represent an early, imperfect form of humanity — large in body but lacking the spiritual refinement that the gods sought.

The cycling destruction encoded in the First Sun reflects the Mesoamerican understanding of time as fundamentally cyclical rather than linear. Each sun is not a permanent dispensation but a temporary arrangement that carries the seeds of its own ending within its governing sign. Four Jaguar (Nahui Ocelotl) specifies the calendrical day on which the age ended, tying cosmic history to the *tonalpohualli* (260-day sacred calendar). For those who maintain the calendar count, this is not merely a story about the past but a warning inscribed in the structure of time itself: every epoch must reckon with the forces it suppresses.

The opposition between Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl that drives the First Sun's destruction recurs throughout Mesoamerican thought. These are not simply rival gods but complementary cosmic principles — darkness and light, earth and wind, obsidian mirror and plumed serpent. Their struggle creates and destroys worlds. Nahua Elders teach that wisdom consists not in choosing one over the other but in understanding their necessary interdependence, a principle that shapes ethics, governance, and the seasonal ceremonies that mark the agricultural year in highland Mexican communities.
