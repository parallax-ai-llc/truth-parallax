The Third Sun, Nahui Quiahuitl (Four Rain), was governed by Tlaloc, the rain deity who dwells in the mountain of sustenance (*Tlalocan*). The humans of this era subsisted on aquatic seeds (*acecentli*), reflecting their water-deity patron. This age ended catastrophically when fire rained from the sky, and the surviving humans were transformed into birds — turkeys, butterflies, or dogs in various tellings. The destruction by fire-rain represents a cosmic paradox: the rain god's age ends not through flood but through its elemental opposite.

Tlaloc's governance of the Third Sun connects this cosmic age to the entire complex of Nahua water-mountain-agricultural spirituality. Tlaloc's domain, Tlalocan, is understood as a paradise of perpetual green abundance located within the mountains, where the *tlaloque* (rain spirits) store water in great jars and release it as rain. Contemporary Nahua communities in Morelos, Guerrero, and the Sierra Norte de Puebla maintain ceremonial obligations to Tlaloc and the rain spirits, offering food, flowers, and prayer at mountain springs and cave mouths at the beginning of the rainy season. These ceremonies are understood as fulfilling a covenant established in the Third Sun's era.

The fire-rain destruction carries associations with volcanic eruption, a geological reality in central Mexico that shapes indigenous cosmological understanding. The great volcanoes — Popocatepetl, Iztaccihuatl, and the Tlaxcalan volcano Matlalcueye (La Malinche) — are living presences in Nahua communities, and their potential for eruption is understood as the cosmic fire-rain held temporarily in abeyance. Communities living in volcanic zones maintain specific ceremonial relationships with these mountains, understanding that the balance between water and fire that sustains agricultural life is actively maintained through reciprocal obligation between humans and the elemental powers.

The transformation of humans into birds at the Third Sun's end is read by indigenous scholars as a commentary on the relationship between the terrestrial and celestial realms. Birds occupy the space between earth and sky, mediating between the human and divine worlds. In Nahua thought, certain birds — particularly eagles and quetzals — carry spiritual messages, and their appearance in specific contexts is interpreted as communication from the powers that govern the cosmos. The Third Sun's survivors, elevated into the air as birds, thus represent a form of spiritual ascent even within destruction, reinforcing the principle that cosmic catastrophe contains seeds of transformation.
