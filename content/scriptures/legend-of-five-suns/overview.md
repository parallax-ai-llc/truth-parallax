## Overview

The Legend of the Five Suns (Leyenda de los Cinco Soles) is the central creation narrative of Aztec (Mexica) cosmology, describing the creation and destruction of five successive worlds or cosmic ages, each presided over by a different sun deity. The narrative survives in several colonial-era sources, most importantly the *Leyenda de los Soles* (1558), the *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus Pinturas*, and sections of Sahagún's *Florentine Codex*. It represents the Aztec understanding of cosmic history, the fragility of the present world, and the necessity of sacrifice to sustain the current Sun.

According to the legend, the gods Quetzalcoatl and Tezcatlipoca repeatedly create the world, only to see it destroyed through cosmic conflict and cataclysm. Each Sun is associated with a different element (earth, wind, fire, water), a different race of beings, and a different form of destruction. The current age — the Fifth Sun, Nahui Ollin (Four Movement) — was created at Teotihuacan through the self-sacrifice of the gods themselves and is destined to be destroyed by earthquakes. The narrative establishes the theological foundation for Aztec sacrificial practice: just as the gods sacrificed themselves to create the Fifth Sun, so humans must offer sacrifice to sustain it.

## Structure

| Sun | Name | Element | Presiding Deity | Form of Destruction |
|-----|------|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Nahui Ocelotl (Four Jaguar) | Earth | Tezcatlipoca | Jaguars devour the inhabitants |
| 2 | Nahui Ehecatl (Four Wind) | Wind | Quetzalcoatl | Hurricanes transform inhabitants into monkeys |
| 3 | Nahui Quiahuitl (Four Rain) | Fire/Rain | Tlaloc | Rain of fire destroys the world |
| 4 | Nahui Atl (Four Water) | Water | Chalchiuhtlicue | A great flood transforms inhabitants into fish |
| 5 | Nahui Ollin (Four Movement) | Movement/Earthquake | Tonatiuh | Earthquakes will destroy the current world |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Indigenous**: The foundation of Nahua cosmological understanding, still referenced in contemporary indigenous communities
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding Aztec religion, philosophy, and the concept of cyclical time
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with other traditions of multiple world ages (Hindu yugas, Hesiod's Ages of Man, Hopi worlds)
- **Theological**: A profound theology of sacrifice, cosmic fragility, and divine responsibility
- **Archaeological**: Corroborated by the Sun Stone (Piedra del Sol) and other archaeological evidence
- **Interfaith**: Offers a distinctive vision of creation as cyclical, fragile, and dependent on ongoing sacrifice

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Cosmic Cycles | The creation and destruction of multiple worlds in succession |
| Sacrifice | Self-sacrifice of the gods as the foundation of cosmic order |
| Divine Conflict | The rivalry between Quetzalcoatl and Tezcatlipoca as a creative-destructive force |
| Fragility | The present world is temporary and requires constant sustenance |
| Transformation | Each destruction transforms beings into new forms (monkeys, fish, etc.) |
| Human Responsibility | Humanity's obligation to sustain the current Sun through sacrifice |
