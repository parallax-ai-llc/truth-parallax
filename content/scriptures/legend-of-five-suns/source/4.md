---
title: "Legend of Five Suns - Chapter 4"
scripture: "legend-of-five-suns"
chapter: 4
---

**1** Then the gods spoke to Tecuciztecatl: "Cast thyself into the fire, O Tecuciztecatl!" And he made ready to throw himself into the flames. But the fire was great and hot. He felt the heat and was afraid.

**2** He turned back. He dared not. He tried again to cast himself into the fire. Four times he tried, four times he turned back. He could not do it. He could not throw himself in.

**3** For it had been ordained that no one should try more than four times. And so after his four attempts, the gods spoke to Nanahuatzin: "Onward, O Nanahuatzin! It is thy turn!"

**4** And Nanahuatzin, summoning all his courage, shut his eyes tightly, hardened his heart, and cast himself into the fire. At once he began to crackle and sizzle like something being roasted. His body burst into flames.

**5** When Tecuciztecatl saw that Nanahuatzin had cast himself in, he was ashamed. And he too threw himself into the fire. But it was too late. The first honor was not his.

**6** It is said that an eagle then swooped into the fire and burned itself. That is why the eagle has dark, singed feathers. Then a jaguar leaped in but was only scorched. That is why the jaguar has dark spots upon its skin.

**7** Then the gods sat down to wait. They wondered where Nanahuatzin would appear, now that he had cast himself into the fire, to give light to the world.

**8** A long time they waited. Then the sky began to redden. The dawn appeared on every side. And the gods went down upon their knees to await the rising of Nanahuatzin, made into a Sun.

**9** They looked in every direction. They turned round and round. They could not agree where he would appear. Some said he would rise in the north. Others pointed to the south. Still others to the west.

**10** But those who looked to the east were proved right. For it was there that the Sun appeared. And when the Sun came forth, when he burst forth, he appeared to be very red. He seemed to sway from side to side. No one could look at him, for he blinded their eyes. He shone fiercely, and his rays reached in all directions.

**11** And afterward Tecuciztecatl rose, following right behind the Sun. He too came from the east, from the same direction. He was the Moon. And they both shone equally. They both shone with the same brightness.

**12** When the gods saw that they both shone equally, they spoke among themselves again. They said: "How can this be, O gods? Is it well that both should shine equally? Is it right that they give the same light?"

**13** Then one of the gods ran forth, and he struck the face of Tecuciztecatl with a rabbit. And so he darkened his face. He killed his brightness. And that is why the Moon does not shine as brightly as the Sun. And that is why one can see a rabbit in the face of the Moon.

**14** But still neither the Sun nor the Moon could move. They remained fixed at the horizon. And the gods said: "How shall we live? The Sun does not move. Shall we live among common folk? Let us all give our lives, let us all die, that through us the Sun may be revived."

**15** Then the wind, Ehecatl, arose and exerted himself fiercely and violently. The wind killed all the gods. He slew them all. And then the Sun began to move, to follow his course across the sky.
