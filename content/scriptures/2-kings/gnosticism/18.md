## Hezekiah and the Archontic Siege

Gnostic reading of 2 Kings 18 interprets the Assyrian siege as a conflict between archontic powers, with Hezekiah's "trust in the LORD" representing loyalty to one archon (YHWH) against another (Ashur).

## Gnostic Interpretation

### Nehushtan Destroyed (18:4)

Hezekiah's destruction of the bronze serpent is read by some Gnostic traditions through a lens influenced by the Ophites (serpent-venerating Gnostics). In Ophite theology, the serpent of Eden was a liberator who brought *gnosis*. The bronze serpent that Moses made was a vestige of this liberating knowledge. Hezekiah's destruction of Nehushtan, then, can be read as the Demiurge's servant destroying a symbol of *gnosis*.

### The Rabshakeh's Speech

The Rabshakeh's argument — "no god has saved any nation from Assyria" — is, in the Gnostic reading, actually correct at a deeper level than he knows. No archon has the power to save, because all archons are limited, ignorant beings. The Demiurge cannot truly save Israel any more than Ashur can save Assyria. Salvation comes only from the transcendent God, unknown to both sides in this conflict.

### Trust as Archontic Loyalty

Hezekiah's "trust in the LORD" is, in Gnostic terms, loyalty to one archon. The pneumatic trusts not in any earthly god — not in YHWH, not in Ashur — but in the unknown Father beyond all archontic powers.

## Theological Significance

- **The serpent destroyed**: Ophite Gnostics lament the destruction of a liberating symbol
- **No archon saves**: Neither YHWH nor Ashur can truly deliver
- **Archontic conflict**: The siege is a battle between material powers, not a spiritual event
- **Beyond all archons**: True salvation transcends the God of the Old Testament
