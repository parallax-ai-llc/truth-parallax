## Manasseh: The Demiurge's Perfect Servant

Gnostic reading of 2 Kings 21 sees Manasseh's comprehensive apostasy as a paradoxical revelation: by worshiping every archon simultaneously, Manasseh inadvertently reveals the fundamental equivalence of all archontic religions.

## Gnostic Interpretation

### All Archons Worshiped

Manasseh's sins include worshiping the host of heaven, building altars to multiple gods, practicing divination, and setting up an Asherah in the temple. In Gnostic terms, Manasseh is simply expanding his devotion to include multiple archons rather than limiting it to one. The Demiurge's objection — expressed through the prophets — is not moral but territorial: he demands exclusive worship because he is a jealous archon, not because the other gods are somehow worse.

### Child Sacrifice and Archontic Demand

Manasseh's sacrifice of his son represents the most extreme form of archontic worship: the material powers demand human life itself. Every form of worship, in the Gnostic view, is a form of sacrifice — the offering of human energy, attention, and life to archontic powers. Manasseh merely makes explicit what all archontic religion implicitly demands.

### The Murder of the Innocent (21:16)

The shedding of innocent blood represents the archontic world's fundamental violence. The material order sustains itself through the consumption of life. The pneumatic stands in solidarity with the innocent who are sacrificed to the archontic system.

## Theological Significance

- **All archons are equivalent**: Worshiping many gods is no worse than worshiping one archon
- **Archontic jealousy**: The Demiurge's objection is territorial, not moral
- **Sacrifice as archontic demand**: All material worship consumes human life
- **The violence of the system**: Innocent blood is the fuel of the archontic order
