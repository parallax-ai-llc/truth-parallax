## Naaman's Healing: A Baptismal Type

The healing of Naaman the Syrian is one of the most important Old Testament passages in Catholic sacramental theology. The Church Fathers unanimously read Naaman's sevenfold washing in the Jordan as a type of baptism, and the entire narrative as an illustration of how grace operates through material signs.

## Patristic Interpretation

### The Jordan as Baptismal Waters (5:14)

**Cyril of Jerusalem** in his *Catechetical Lectures* explicitly cites Naaman's washing as a type of baptism: "Naaman was a leper, and was cleansed by washing in the Jordan. You too are leprous with sin, and are cleansed by the waters of baptism." **Ambrose** develops the same theme in *De Sacramentis*, arguing that the Jordan's waters had no inherent healing power — it was God's command that made them efficacious, just as baptismal water has no natural power to forgive sins but does so by God's institution.

### The Servant Girl's Evangelism (5:2-3)

Catholic commentators note that the most powerful testimony comes from the lowliest source. The unnamed slave girl anticipates the evangelistic vocation of every baptized Christian, regardless of social status. Catholic social teaching on the dignity of every person finds an early biblical illustration here.

### Elisha Refuses Payment (5:16)

Grace is free. The Catholic teaching that sacraments cannot be bought or sold (the sin of simony, named after Simon Magus in Acts 8) finds its Old Testament foundation here. Gehazi's attempt to profit from grace brings upon him the very disease from which Naaman was freed.

### Naaman's Request for Earth (5:17)

Naaman asks to take two mule-loads of Israelite soil home, so he can worship the LORD on holy ground. Catholic sacramental theology sees in this the intuition that material things can be bearers of the sacred — the sacramental principle that the material world mediates divine grace.

## Theological Significance

- **Baptismal typology**: Naaman's washing is the premier Old Testament type of baptism
- **Sacramental principle**: Material elements (water, earth) mediate divine grace
- **Simony condemned**: Grace cannot be commodified
- **Universal salvation**: A Gentile receives healing that Israelites did not — grace extends beyond Israel
