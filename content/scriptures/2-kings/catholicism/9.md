## Jehu's Anointing and the Fall of Jezebel

Catholic interpretation of 2 Kings 9 engages carefully with the question of divinely commissioned violence, the end of Jezebel's tyranny, and the fulfillment of prophetic words spoken generations earlier.

## Key Catholic Readings

### The Anointing of Jehu (9:1-10)

Catholic theology recognizes that God uses human instruments for judgment, but the Church's moral tradition distinguishes between divine commission and human excess. The anointing establishes Jehu's legitimate authority, but his subsequent actions must still be evaluated by moral criteria. **Augustine** argues that God can righteously commission destruction without approving every act of the commissioned agent.

### Jezebel's Death (9:30-37)

The death of Jezebel fulfills Elijah's prophecy from 1 Kings 21:23. Catholic commentators emphasize the certainty of divine justice: no matter how long delayed, God's word is fulfilled. Jezebel's act of adorning herself before death is read by some Fathers as defiant pride, by others as an attempt to die with royal dignity — a final assertion of self against God's decree.

### Prophetic Fulfillment and Providence

The chapter demonstrates the Catholic understanding of divine providence: God's plan unfolds across generations. Words spoken by Elijah decades earlier come to pass through agents who may not even be aware they are fulfilling prophecy. This is the same providential logic that governs the entire arc of salvation history.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine justice**: Prophetic words are fulfilled without fail
- **Moral complexity**: God's use of human agents does not automatically sanctify all their actions
- **Providence across generations**: God's purposes unfold over long spans of time
- **The end of tyranny**: Jezebel's fall demonstrates that no earthly power is permanent
