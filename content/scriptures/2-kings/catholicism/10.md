## Jehu's Purge and the Problem of Zealotry

Catholic moral theology engages 2 Kings 10 with particular care, as Jehu's destruction of Baal worship raises questions about the relationship between religious zeal and moral conduct.

## Key Catholic Readings

### Excessive Violence (10:1-11)

Catholic teaching distinguishes between Jehu's divine commission to end the house of Ahab and his excessive cruelty in carrying it out. The later condemnation in Hosea 1:4 — "I will punish the house of Jehu for the blood of Jezreel" — confirms that God held Jehu accountable for going beyond his commission. The principle of proportionality in Catholic just war theory applies even to divinely commissioned acts.

### Deception in God's Service (10:18-28)

Jehu's ruse to lure Baal worshipers to their deaths through deception is problematic for Catholic moral theology. The Catechism teaches that lying is intrinsically evil (CCC 2482-2486). Even when the end is the eradication of idolatry, the means of deception is not justified. **Thomas Aquinas** in the *Summa Theologiae* argues that the good end does not justify intrinsically evil means.

### Partial Obedience (10:29-31)

Jehu removes Baal but retains the golden calves. Catholic moral theology recognizes the danger of selective obedience — choosing which divine commands to follow based on personal convenience or political advantage.

## Theological Significance

- **Zeal must be governed by justice**: Excess in God's name remains sin
- **The end does not justify the means**: Deception is not sanctified by good intentions
- **Partial obedience**: Removing one sin while retaining another is insufficient
- **God's accountability extends to His agents**: Even commissioned servants answer for their methods
