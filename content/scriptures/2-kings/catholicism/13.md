## Elisha's Death and Continuing Prophetic Power

Catholic interpretation of 2 Kings 13 gives special attention to the miracle of the dead man revived by contact with Elisha's bones, as this passage has been central to Catholic teaching on relics.

## Key Catholic Readings

### The Veneration of Relics (13:20-21)

When a corpse is thrown into Elisha's tomb and touches his bones, the dead man is restored to life. This is the single most important Old Testament text for the Catholic doctrine of relics. The Council of Trent (Session XXV) explicitly affirms that "the holy bodies of the holy martyrs and of others living with Christ... are to be venerated by the faithful," and that through relics God bestows benefits upon humanity. Elisha's bones demonstrate that God's power can operate through the physical remains of holy persons.

**Thomas Aquinas** (*Summa Theologiae* III, q. 25, a. 6) argues that the bodies of the saints deserve veneration as temples of the Holy Spirit and instruments through which God worked.

### The Arrows and Half-Hearted Devotion (13:14-19)

Elisha's anger at Jehoash for striking the ground only three times teaches that half-hearted devotion limits the grace God desires to give. Catholic spiritual directors have long used this passage to urge fervent, persistent prayer.

### Covenant Compassion (13:23)

Despite Israel's continuing sin, God has compassion because of the Abrahamic covenant. Catholic theology sees here the "already/not yet" of salvation: God's mercy precedes human repentance and creates the conditions for it.

## Theological Significance

- **Relics**: God's power operates through the physical remains of the saints
- **Fervent devotion**: Half-hearted prayer limits what God desires to give
- **Prevenient grace**: God's mercy operates before and apart from human merit
- **The body's dignity**: Even after death, the body of the saint retains its sacred character
