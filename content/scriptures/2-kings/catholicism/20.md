## Hezekiah's Illness, Prayer, and the Babylonian Embassy

Catholic interpretation of 2 Kings 20 reflects on the efficacy of prayer, the nature of prophetic pronouncement, and the spiritual danger of pride after receiving extraordinary grace.

## Key Catholic Readings

### Prayer Changes the Decree (20:1-7)

Isaiah announces Hezekiah's death, but Hezekiah prays and God grants fifteen more years. Catholic theology affirms the real efficacy of intercessory prayer without denying God's eternal foreknowledge. **Thomas Aquinas** teaches that God eternally decrees both the prayer and its answer; prayer is a genuine cause within God's providential plan, not an illusion of influence.

### The Sign of the Sundial (20:8-11)

The miraculous regression of the shadow confirms God's promise. Catholic sacramental theology sees in external signs the visible confirmation of invisible grace. Just as the sundial's regression reassures Hezekiah, so the sacraments provide tangible signs of God's promises.

### The Babylonian Embassy (20:12-19)

Hezekiah's display of his treasures reveals the spiritual danger of vainglory. Catholic spiritual theology, following **John Cassian** and **John of the Cross**, identifies vainglory as a subtle temptation that strikes precisely after spiritual victories. The man who trusted God against Assyria falls to the pride of showing off before Babylon.

## Theological Significance

- **The efficacy of prayer**: Prayer is a real cause within divine providence
- **Signs and sacraments**: Visible signs confirm invisible grace
- **Vainglory**: Pride strikes hardest after spiritual triumph
- **Future consequences**: Present unfaithfulness has long-term effects
