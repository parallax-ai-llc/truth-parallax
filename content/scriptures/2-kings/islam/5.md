## The Healing of Naaman: Grace, Humility, and Monotheism

Islamic interpretation of 2 Kings 5 sees Naaman's healing as a demonstration of Allah's power operating through the prophet al-Yasa', and Naaman's subsequent confession as a recognition of *tawhid*.

## Islamic Interpretation

### Naaman's Confession (5:15)

Naaman's declaration — "Now I know that there is no God in all the earth but in Israel" — is read by Islamic scholars as a confession of monotheism approaching the *shahada*. However, Islamic theology would correct the formulation: God is not "in Israel" but is the Lord of all creation. Naaman's insight is genuine but incomplete — he recognizes the one God but associates Him with a particular people and place.

### Humility and Obedience (5:11-14)

Naaman's initial resistance to the simple command demonstrates the human tendency toward pride. Islamic spiritual literature (*tasawwuf*) emphasizes that submission (*islam*) to Allah's commands — even when they seem simple or beneath one's dignity — is the essence of faith. The word "Islam" itself means submission.

### Gehazi's Corruption (5:20-27)

Gehazi's greed illustrates the danger of hypocrisy (*nifaq*) — professing faith while pursuing worldly gain. The Quran warns severely against hypocrisy: the hypocrites are assigned to the lowest level of the Fire (Quran 4:145). Gehazi's sin is compounded by the fact that he uses the prophet's name to pursue personal profit.

### The Universality of Grace

That a Gentile military commander receives healing through an Israelite prophet demonstrates the universal scope of divine mercy. Islam teaches that Allah is *Rabb al-'alamin* (Lord of all worlds), and His mercy extends to all humanity, not merely to one nation.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid recognized**: Naaman approaches monotheistic confession
- **Submission**: Obedience to divine command, even when seemingly simple
- **Hypocrisy condemned**: Using religion for personal gain is a grave sin
- **Universal mercy**: Allah's grace extends beyond national boundaries
