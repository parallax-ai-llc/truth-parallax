## Hezekiah and the Assyrian Crisis

Islamic interpretation of 2 Kings 18 focuses on Hezekiah's trust in Allah as a model of *tawakkul*, and the Rabshakeh's speech as an example of how enemies of faith attempt to undermine trust in God.

## Islamic Interpretation

### Tawakkul: Reliance on Allah

Hezekiah's trust in God against overwhelming military odds exemplifies the Islamic virtue of *tawakkul*. The Quran teaches: "And whoever relies upon Allah — then He is sufficient for him" (Quran 65:3). Hezekiah's refusal to surrender despite the Assyrian siege models the believer's stance: trust in Allah alone, regardless of the apparent odds.

### The Rhetoric of Disbelief (18:19-35)

The Rabshakeh's speech is analyzed in Islamic interpretation as a form of *fitna* (trial/temptation) designed to shake the believers' *iman* (faith). His arguments — "no god can deliver you," "your God has not protected you" — mirror the tactics of every enemy of faith. The Quran warns: "And the disbelievers planned, but Allah planned. And Allah is the best of planners" (Quran 3:54).

### The Destruction of Nehushtan (18:4)

Hezekiah's destruction of the bronze serpent, which had become an object of veneration, parallels Ibrahim's destruction of his father's idols (Quran 21:57-58). Both acts demonstrate that no material object, however ancient or revered, should become a rival to Allah.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawakkul**: Complete reliance on Allah in the face of overwhelming odds
- **Fitna**: The enemy's strategy is to destroy faith, not merely to conquer territory
- **Destruction of idols**: No material object should rival Allah in devotion
- **Allah is sufficient**: Divine protection does not depend on human military strength
