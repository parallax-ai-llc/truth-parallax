## The Destruction of Baal Worship

Islamic reading of 2 Kings 10 focuses on the eradication of *shirk* (idolatry) and the ethical questions surrounding the means used to accomplish it.

## Islamic Interpretation

### The Destruction of the Temple of Baal (10:18-28)

The destruction of the Baal temple and the slaughter of its priests represents the eradication of *shirk* — the most severe sin in Islam. Islamic history records similar episodes: the Prophet Muhammad cleansed the Ka'bah of 360 idols upon the conquest of Makkah. The principle is the same: spaces dedicated to idol worship must be purified for the worship of Allah alone.

### The Ethics of Deception (10:18-19)

Jehu's ruse raises questions in Islamic ethics. The general principle in Islamic law is that lying (*kadhib*) is prohibited, though certain categories of permissible deception exist in warfare and reconciliation. Classical Islamic jurists debated whether strategic deception in the service of eliminating *shirk* is permissible. The majority position favors truthfulness even when the cause is righteous.

### Partial Obedience (10:29-31)

Jehu's retention of the golden calves despite destroying Baal worship illustrates the danger of selective religiosity. Islamic theology demands total submission to Allah's will, not partial compliance chosen according to personal convenience. The Quran asks: "Do you believe in part of the Scripture and disbelieve in part?" (Quran 2:85).

## Theological Significance

- **Shirk eliminated**: Idol worship must be destroyed wherever it exists
- **Total submission**: Partial obedience is insufficient in Islam
- **Ethics of means**: Even righteous ends must be pursued through permissible means
- **The Ka'bah parallel**: Muhammad's cleansing of the Ka'bah echoes Jehu's destruction of Baal's temple
