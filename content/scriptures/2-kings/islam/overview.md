## Overview

Islam does not regard 2 Kings as a preserved divine text but acknowledges that historical narratives about the prophets and kings of Israel contain elements of truth. The Quran names al-Yasa' (Elisha) among the prophets (Quran 6:86, 38:48), and Islamic tradition draws on *Isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) for supplementary details about the kings and prophets mentioned in the biblical record.

## Relationship to the Quran

The Quran does not narrate the events of 2 Kings in detail but establishes theological principles that guide Muslim reading:

| Biblical Event | Islamic Perspective |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Elisha's miracles | Al-Yasa' is a prophet; miracles are from God alone |
| Fall of Israel | Consequence of disobedience to prophets — a recurring Quranic theme |
| Destruction of the Temple | Part of the divine decree (Quran 17:4-8, *Isra'*) |
| Exile | God's punishment of a disobedient nation |

### Surah al-Isra' (17:4-8)
This passage refers to two occasions when the Children of Israel caused corruption (*fasad*) in the land, after which God sent enemies against them. Muslim exegetes frequently identify the Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem as one of these occasions.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Prophetic Infallibility (*Ismah*)
The prophets in 2 Kings (Elijah, Elisha, Isaiah) are regarded as sinless messengers. Any biblical depiction suggesting prophetic error is attributed to *tahrif* (textual alteration).

### Divine Sovereignty
The rise and fall of kingdoms demonstrates God's absolute control over history — a core tenet of Islamic theology.

### Isra'iliyyat Caution
Classical scholars like Ibn Kathir accepted historical details from 2 Kings cautiously, rejecting anything contradicting the Quran while permitting narration of neutral material.

## Major Commentators

| Commentator | Era | Approach |
|-------------|-----|----------|
| Al-Tabari | 9th-10th century | Extensive Isra'iliyyat, encyclopedic |
| Ibn Kathir | 14th century | Hadith-based, critical of Isra'iliyyat |
| Al-Tha'labi | 11th century | *Qisas al-Anbiya'* — expansive narrative |
| Isma'il Haqqi | 18th century | Sufi-oriented tafsir |
