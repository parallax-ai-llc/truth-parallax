## Hezekiah's Illness and the Babylonian Embassy

Islamic interpretation of 2 Kings 20 reflects on the power of du'a to influence divine decree, the nature of signs, and the spiritual danger of worldly pride.

## Islamic Interpretation

### Prayer and Decree (20:1-7)

The reversal of Hezekiah's death sentence through prayer raises the question of whether du'a can change *qadar* (divine decree). Islamic theology holds that du'a is itself part of qadar — Allah decrees both the prayer and its answer. The Prophet Muhammad taught: "Nothing repels qadar except du'a." This does not imply that the eternal decree changes but that du'a is the divinely appointed means by which certain outcomes are realized.

### The Sign (20:8-11)

Allah's reversal of the shadow on the sundial is an *ayah* (sign) confirming His promise. The Quran uses *ayat* (signs) extensively: "And how many a sign within the heavens and earth do they pass over while they, therefrom, are turning away" (Quran 12:105). Signs are given to strengthen faith, not to compel belief.

### Pride After Blessing (20:12-19)

Hezekiah's display of his treasures to the Babylonians illustrates the Islamic concept of *'ujb* (self-admiration) — a dangerous spiritual state in which one attributes blessings to oneself rather than to Allah. The Quran warns: "Do not consider yourselves pure" (Quran 53:32). The proper response to divine blessing is gratitude (*shukr*), not display.

## Theological Significance

- **Du'a and qadar**: Prayer is the appointed means within divine decree
- **Signs strengthen faith**: Allah provides confirmatory signs for the faithful
- **'Ujb condemned**: Self-admiration after receiving blessing is spiritually dangerous
- **Shukr**: The proper response to divine favor is gratitude, not ostentation
