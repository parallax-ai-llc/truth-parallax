## The Moabite Campaign: Divine Aid and Human Presumption

Islamic reading of 2 Kings 3 focuses on the prophet's role as intermediary between God and rulers, the conditions under which divine aid is granted, and the abomination of human sacrifice.

## Islamic Interpretation

### The Prophet as Guide

Al-Yasa's refusal to prophesy for the wicked king of Israel while consenting for the righteous Jehoshaphat illustrates the Islamic principle that prophets do not serve corrupt rulers. The Quran teaches that prophets are sent as warners (*mundhirun*) and bearers of good news (*mubashshirun*), and their mission is compromised by alliance with tyrants.

### Mesha's Sacrifice (3:27)

Human sacrifice is absolutely prohibited in Islam. The Quran's account of Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son (Quran 37:99-111) culminates in divine intervention and the substitution of a ram — establishing that Allah does not desire human sacrifice. Mesha's act represents the extreme corruption of pagan religion, the very opposite of the divine command.

### Water Without Rain

The miraculous provision of water in the desert parallels Quranic accounts of divine provision — the spring that gushed forth for Hajar (Hagar) and Isma'il in the desert (the well of Zamzam). Allah provides for His servants through means that transcend natural causation.

## Theological Significance

- **Prophets and rulers**: Prophets must not be co-opted by corrupt power
- **Human sacrifice prohibited**: Islam absolutely forbids the taking of innocent life as offering
- **Divine provision**: Allah provides through supernatural means when He wills
- **The conditions of divine aid**: Righteousness, not mere power, attracts God's help
