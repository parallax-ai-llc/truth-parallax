## Al-Yasa's Death and Continuing Signs

Islamic reading of 2 Kings 13 focuses on the compassion of Allah, the prophetic miracle at Elisha's tomb, and the persistence of divine mercy despite human disobedience.

## Islamic Interpretation

### The Miracle at the Tomb (13:20-21)

The revival of a dead man through contact with al-Yasa's bones is treated cautiously in Islamic interpretation. While Islam affirms that prophets can perform miracles by Allah's permission, the question of miracles occurring through relics (*athar*) is debated. Sunni orthodoxy generally attributes the miracle to Allah's power alone, using the bones as a means (*sabab*), not as an independent source of power. This distinguishes the Islamic position from any form of relic veneration.

### Divine Mercy (13:23)

Allah's compassion toward Israel despite their disobedience illustrates the Quranic attribute *ar-Rahman* (the Most Merciful). The Quran teaches: "My mercy encompasses all things" (Quran 7:156). Allah's mercy precedes His wrath and creates space for repentance.

### Half-Hearted Devotion (13:14-19)

The king's failure to strike the ground with full vigor demonstrates the Islamic teaching that *ibadah* (worship) and *du'a* (supplication) must be performed with full sincerity and effort. The Prophet Muhammad taught: "When you ask Allah, ask with certainty."

## Theological Significance

- **Allah's mercy**: Compassion precedes judgment
- **Miracles belong to Allah**: The power is God's, not the relic's
- **Sincerity in worship**: Half-hearted devotion yields half-hearted results
- **Prophetic dignity**: Even in death, the prophet is honored by Allah
