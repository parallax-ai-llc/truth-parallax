## Ahaziah and the Prophet Ilyas

Islamic reading of 2 Kings 1 engages the narrative through the lens of *tawhid* (divine unity) and prophetic authority. Ahaziah's consultation of Baal-Zebub is viewed as *shirk* (associating partners with Allah), the gravest sin in Islamic theology. The prophet Ilyas (Elijah) serves as Allah's messenger, delivering judgment upon the idolatrous king.

## Islamic Interpretation

### Shirk and Its Consequences

The Quran repeatedly warns against *shirk*: "Indeed, Allah does not forgive association with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills" (Quran 4:48). Ahaziah's decision to consult a foreign deity rather than seeking a prophet of God is a direct violation of *tawhid*. Islamic scholars reading this narrative see it as a cautionary example of how rulers who abandon monotheism invite divine wrath.

### Ilyas (Elijah) as Prophet

The Quran mentions Ilyas (Elijah) by name in Surah al-An'am (6:85) and Surah as-Saffat (37:123-132), listing him among the righteous prophets. Islamic tradition regards all prophets as protected from sin (*ismah*) and as bearers of the same essential message: worship Allah alone. The fire from heaven in this chapter is understood as a miracle (*mu'jizah*) confirming the prophet's divine mandate.

### Divine Sovereignty

**Al-Tabari** in his *Tarikh* narrates these events drawing on Isra'iliyyat sources, presenting them as illustrations of Allah's absolute power over kings and kingdoms. **Ibn Kathir** is more selective, emphasizing the theological principles while cautioning against accepting every detail of the biblical narrative.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: The absolute oneness of God forbids consultation of other deities
- **Prophetic authority**: Prophets carry Allah's own authority
- **Shirk condemned**: Associating partners with God is the unforgivable sin
- **Divine sovereignty**: Allah controls the fate of kings and nations
