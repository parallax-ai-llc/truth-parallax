## Josiah's Reforms and the Restoration of Monotheism

Islamic reading of 2 Kings 23 values Josiah's comprehensive purge of idolatry as an example of how a righteous ruler should restore *tawhid* and purify worship.

## Islamic Interpretation

### The Purge of Idolatry

Josiah's systematic destruction of idolatrous shrines, altars, and cult objects parallels the Prophet Muhammad's cleansing of the Ka'bah upon the conquest of Makkah. Both acts represent the restoration of pure monotheism after periods of shirk. The Quran declares: "Truth has come, and falsehood has departed. Indeed is falsehood ever bound to depart" (Quran 17:81). This verse was recited by the Prophet as he destroyed the idols.

### The Passover Restored (23:21-23)

The restoration of a foundational religious observance after centuries of neglect resonates with the Islamic understanding of Muhammad's mission: to restore the original monotheistic practice of Ibrahim (Abraham) after centuries of corruption. Islam sees itself not as a new religion but as the restoration of the primordial faith (*din al-fitrah*).

### The Limits of Reform

Despite Josiah's unprecedented efforts, the divine decree of judgment stands. Islamic theology recognizes that *tawbah* (repentance) can avert punishment for individuals but that nations that have passed a certain threshold of corruption may face inevitable consequences. The Quran teaches: "And when We intend to destroy a city, We command its affluent but they defiantly disobey therein; so the word comes into effect upon it, and We destroy it with destruction" (Quran 17:16).

## Theological Significance

- **Restoration of tawhid**: The ruler's primary duty is to purify worship
- **The Ka'bah parallel**: Muhammad's cleansing echoes Josiah's reforms
- **Din al-fitrah**: Islam as the restoration of original monotheism
- **National consequences**: Some collective punishments cannot be averted
