## The Unseen World and the Ethics of Warfare

Islamic reading of 2 Kings 6 engages the themes of the unseen world (*al-ghayb*), the prophetic ability to perceive it, and the ethics of mercy in warfare.

## Islamic Interpretation

### The Unseen Army (6:15-17)

The opening of the servant's eyes to see the angelic army illustrates the Quranic concept of *al-ghayb* — the unseen realm. The Quran teaches that belief in the unseen is a fundamental characteristic of the faithful: "Who believe in the unseen" (Quran 2:3). Islamic angelology affirms that angels are real beings who participate in worldly events at Allah's command, including in battle (Quran 3:123-125 describes angelic assistance at Badr).

### Mercy in War (6:21-23)

Al-Yasa's command to feed the captured Arameans rather than kill them aligns with Islamic ethics of warfare. The Prophet Muhammad instructed: "Do not kill the elderly, the women, or the children. Do not cut down fruit-bearing trees. Do not destroy buildings." The treatment of prisoners of war in Islam requires provision of food, water, and basic dignity (Quran 76:8: "And they give food in spite of love for it to the needy, the orphan, and the captive").

### The Floating Axe Head (6:1-7)

This miracle demonstrates Allah's control over the natural order. Islamic theology affirms that the laws of nature are habits (*'adat*) of Allah's action, not independent forces. Allah can suspend or alter them at will, and this is what occurs in prophetic miracles.

## Theological Significance

- **Al-ghayb**: The unseen world is real and essential to faith
- **Angelic assistance**: Angels participate in worldly events by divine command
- **Ethics of warfare**: Islam mandates humane treatment of captives
- **Divine control**: Natural laws are Allah's habits, not independent forces
