## Naaman's Healing: Baptism and Universal Grace

The healing of Naaman is one of the most frequently cited Old Testament passages in Orthodox baptismal theology. The Fathers unanimously read it as a type of baptism, and it features prominently in catechetical instruction.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### The Jordan and Baptism (5:14)

**Cyril of Jerusalem** in his *Mystagogical Catecheses* cites Naaman's sevenfold immersion as the definitive Old Testament type of baptism. The Jordan is the baptismal river *par excellence* in Orthodox tradition. At the Great Blessing of Waters on Theophany, the troparion recalls that Christ sanctified the Jordan's waters, transforming them into a source of healing and new life. Naaman's healing anticipates this transformation.

### The Humility of Faith (5:11-14)

Naaman expects a dramatic ritual but receives a simple command. **John Chrysostom** uses this to teach about the humility required for baptism: the catechumen must accept that salvation comes through simple water and the invocation of the Trinity, not through spectacular displays. The baptismal waters appear ordinary but carry the uncreated grace of God.

### Elisha Refuses Payment (5:15-16)

Grace is free — a principle central to Orthodox soteriology. The mystery of salvation is gift, not transaction. Gehazi's attempt to profit from grace (5:20-27) is read as a type of simony, condemned in the canons of the Ecumenical Councils.

### Naaman's Earth (5:17)

Naaman's request for Israelite soil to worship upon reflects the Orthodox intuition that material creation can be sanctified and become a vehicle for worship. Holy water, blessed icons, and consecrated ground all participate in this sacramental vision of the material world.

## Theological Significance

- **Baptismal typology**: Naaman's sevenfold immersion is the type of baptism
- **Humility in the sacraments**: Grace comes through simple material means
- **The gift character of salvation**: Grace cannot be bought or earned
- **Sanctification of matter**: The material world can mediate the divine
