## Hezekiah's Illness, Sign, and Fall

Orthodox reading of 2 Kings 20 meditates on the power of prayer, the meaning of signs, and the danger of vainglory after receiving divine favor.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### Prayer and Divine Response (20:1-7)

Hezekiah's prayer and God's reversal of the death decree illustrate the Orthodox teaching on the synergy (*synergia*) between divine will and human prayer. Prayer is not an attempt to change God's mind but a participation in God's providential action. **Maximus the Confessor** teaches that God's will and human freedom cooperate without contradiction.

### The Sign of the Sundial (20:8-11)

Signs in Orthodox theology are not mere proofs but revelations of divine truth. The retreat of the shadow demonstrates God's sovereignty over time and nature — a theme celebrated in the Orthodox hymns of Great Lent, where the reversal of natural order points to the reversal of death at Pascha.

### Vainglory and the Babylonian Embassy (20:12-19)

Hezekiah's display of his treasures is the classic biblical illustration of *kenodoxia* (vainglory). **John Climacus** in *The Ladder of Divine Ascent* warns that vainglory is the passion that most characteristically attacks those who have made genuine spiritual progress. Hezekiah's finest hour — trusting God against Sennacherib — is immediately followed by his greatest failure.

## Theological Significance

- **Synergy in prayer**: Human prayer cooperates with divine providence
- **Signs and revelation**: God uses extraordinary means to confirm His promises
- **Vainglory**: The passion that attacks the spiritually advanced
- **Consequences**: Momentary pride produces centuries of suffering
