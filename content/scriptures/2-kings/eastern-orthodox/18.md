## Hezekiah's Faith and the Siege of Jerusalem

Orthodox interpretation of 2 Kings 18 celebrates Hezekiah as a model of faith under trial, emphasizing the destruction of Nehushtan, the Rabshakeh's assault on faith, and the theological virtue of hope.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### The Destruction of Nehushtan (18:4)

Hezekiah's destruction of the bronze serpent is significant for Orthodox icon theology. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787) distinguished between proper veneration (*proskynesis*) and worship (*latreia*). The bronze serpent had degenerated from a legitimate object of remembrance into an object of worship. Icons, properly understood, direct veneration through the image to the prototype — when this direction is lost, the image becomes an idol. Hezekiah's action demonstrates that even divinely instituted objects can be misused.

### The Rabshakeh's Attack on Faith (18:19-35)

The Rabshakeh's speech represents a spiritual assault on the virtue of hope (*elpis*). Orthodox ascetical theology identifies *acedia* (spiritual torpor or despair) as one of the most dangerous temptations. The Rabshakeh's arguments are designed to produce despair: "No god has delivered any nation. Your God will not deliver you." The proper response, modeled by Hezekiah, is to bring the crisis to God in prayer.

### Hezekiah's Trust (18:5)

"He trusted in the LORD." Orthodox theology of the divine attributes emphasizes God's faithfulness (*pistis*). Hezekiah's trust is not a psychological state but a theological reality — he rests on the character of God revealed in covenant and history.

## Theological Significance

- **Proper veneration**: Even legitimate objects can become idols when misused
- **Resistance to despair**: Faith endures sophisticated attacks
- **The virtue of hope**: Trusting God's character when circumstances suggest defeat
- **Reform**: True renewal removes obstacles to genuine worship
