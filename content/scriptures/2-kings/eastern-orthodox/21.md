## The Abyss of Apostasy

Orthodox reading of 2 Kings 21 sees Manasseh's reign as the nadir of the Old Testament — a comprehensive reversal of every element of covenant faithfulness. The chapter raises the question of whether repentance can avert national judgment.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### Manasseh's Comprehensive Evil (21:2-9)

The catalog of Manasseh's sins systematically reverses every dimension of Israel's covenant vocation. Orthodox theology reads this as a portrait of humanity in full rebellion against *theosis*: instead of ascending toward God, Manasseh descends into every form of spiritual darkness. The installation of an idol in the temple represents the ultimate desecration — placing a false image in the place reserved for the living God.

### The Shedding of Innocent Blood (21:16)

Orthodox tradition preserves the martyrdom of Isaiah, said to have been sawn in two by Manasseh (cf. Hebrews 11:37). The persecution of the righteous by the powerful is a recurring pattern that reaches its climax in the Crucifixion. Orthodox hagiography sees in every martyrdom a participation in Christ's suffering.

### Manasseh's Repentance

While 2 Kings does not record Manasseh's repentance, 2 Chronicles 33:12-13 and the *Prayer of Manasseh* (included in the Orthodox Psalter) do. Orthodox theology affirms that no sin is beyond God's mercy: "Where sin increased, grace abounded all the more" (Romans 5:20). Manasseh's repentance is celebrated in the Orthodox Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete, read during Great Lent.

## Theological Significance

- **The depths of apostasy**: Manasseh represents the full rejection of the divine vocation
- **The martyrdom of the prophets**: The righteous suffer at the hands of the wicked
- **No sin beyond mercy**: Even Manasseh can repent
- **Consequences persist**: National judgment may follow even personal repentance
