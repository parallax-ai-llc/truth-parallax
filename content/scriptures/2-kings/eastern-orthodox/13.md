## The Death of Elisha and the Power of Holy Relics

2 Kings 13 holds special significance in Orthodox theology because the miracle of the dead man revived by contact with Elisha's bones provides the primary Old Testament warrant for the veneration of relics.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### Elisha's Bones and the Theology of Relics (13:20-21)

Orthodox theology of relics is grounded in the belief that the Holy Spirit sanctifies the bodies of the saints, making them vessels of divine grace even after death. The miracle at Elisha's tomb demonstrates this principle: God's uncreated energies continue to operate through the sanctified body. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787) cited this passage in defense of the veneration of relics and icons.

**John of Damascus** in *On the Orthodox Faith* argues: "The saints are not dead. For if they live with God and God is the God of the living, then they are alive. And the power of God works through their relics."

### The Arrows and Spiritual Fervor (13:14-19)

Elisha's anger at Jehoash's half-hearted striking of the arrows teaches that spiritual fervor (*zelos*) is necessary for the full reception of grace. Orthodox ascetical theology emphasizes that the life of prayer requires intensity and persistence. The *Jesus Prayer* tradition, for example, requires constant, fervent repetition.

### Covenant Compassion (13:23)

Despite Israel's unfaithfulness, God does not destroy them "because of his covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." Orthodox theology sees here the operation of *philanthropia* — God's love for humanity — which sustains the world even when human faithfulness fails.

## Theological Significance

- **Veneration of relics**: God's grace operates through the sanctified remains of the saints
- **Spiritual fervor**: Half-hearted devotion limits the reception of grace
- **Divine philanthropia**: God's love sustains even the unfaithful
- **Continuity of grace beyond death**: The saints remain active channels of divine energy
