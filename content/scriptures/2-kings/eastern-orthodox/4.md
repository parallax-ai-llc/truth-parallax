## Elisha's Miracles and the Economy of Salvation

Orthodox interpretation of 2 Kings 4 reads the four miracles as manifestations of the divine economy (*oikonomia*) — God's providential plan for the salvation and restoration of creation.

## Patristic Readings

### The Widow's Oil (4:1-7)

The multiplication of oil is connected in Orthodox theology to the sacrament of Holy Unction (*Euchelaion*), in which oil is blessed and applied for healing. **Ephrem the Syrian** reads the widow's oil as a type of the Holy Spirit's abundance — poured out without measure upon those who bring their emptiness to God.

### The Shunammite's Son (4:8-37)

**Theodoret** and **Ephrem** both read Elisha's raising of the dead child as a type of the Resurrection. Elisha's act of stretching himself on the child — mouth to mouth, eyes to eyes, hands to hands — is read as a prefiguration of the Incarnation: God assumes human nature in its entirety, conforming Himself to human measure so that humanity might be raised to divine life.

The child sneezes seven times — seven being the number of completion in Orthodox numerology, signifying the fullness of restoration.

### Death in the Pot (4:38-41)

The poisoned stew healed by the addition of flour is read by Orthodox commentators as a type of how the Incarnation heals corrupted human nature. The flour (the body of Christ) is added to the pot (the world corrupted by sin), and what was deadly becomes nourishing.

### Feeding the Hundred (4:42-44)

The multiplication of loaves directly prefigures the Eucharist. Orthodox liturgical commentary connects this to the Proskomidia — the preparation of the Eucharistic bread — where a small offering becomes the Body of Christ for the many.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine economy**: God's providential plan unfolds through concrete historical acts
- **Incarnational typology**: Elisha's bodily contact prefigures the Incarnation
- **Sacramental connections**: Oil, bread, and healing point to the sacraments
- **Resurrection hope**: The dead child raised anticipates the general resurrection
