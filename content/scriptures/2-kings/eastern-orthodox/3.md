## War, Prophecy, and Divine Provision

Orthodox reading of 2 Kings 3 emphasizes the role of the prophet as the true leader of Israel, the relationship between repentance and divine aid, and God's provision through unexpected means.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### Elisha's Discernment (3:13-14)

Elisha refuses to prophesy for Jehoram and consents only for Jehoshaphat's sake. **John Chrysostom** uses this passage to teach that the prayers of the righteous benefit those in their company. Orthodox intercessory prayer — the prayers of the saints for the living — finds a pattern here: the merit of one person creates a channel of grace for others.

### Water in the Desert (3:16-20)

God provides water without rain, filling the trenches miraculously. Orthodox theology sees this as a manifestation of God's uncreated energies operating through creation. The distinction between God's essence (unknowable) and His energies (experienced in creation and grace) is central to Orthodox theology, and miracles like this illustrate how God acts in the world without being contained by it.

### Mesha's Sacrifice (3:27)

The sacrifice of Mesha's son is read by Orthodox commentators as the ultimate perversion of worship. True worship involves self-offering (*prosphora*) to God; Mesha's act is the demonic inversion, offering another's life to a false god. Orthodox liturgical theology contrasts this with the Eucharistic offering, where Christ offers Himself.

## Theological Significance

- **Intercessory prayer**: The righteous mediate grace for others
- **Divine energies**: God acts in creation through His uncreated energies
- **True vs. false offering**: Self-offering to God vs. sacrificing others to idols
- **Prophetic discernment**: The prophet distinguishes between worthy and unworthy petitioners
