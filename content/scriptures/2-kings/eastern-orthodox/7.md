## Deliverance and the Duty to Share Good News

Orthodox reading of 2 Kings 7 emphasizes God's miraculous deliverance, the duty to proclaim salvation, and the consequences of unbelief.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### The Prophetic Word (7:1)

Elisha's prophecy of abundance in the midst of siege demonstrates the power of God's word to create new realities. Orthodox theology, following the Cappadocian Fathers, teaches that God's word is not merely informational but performative — it accomplishes what it declares. This is the same creative word that called the cosmos into being.

### The Lepers as Evangelists (7:9)

"This is a day of good news, and we remain silent." Orthodox missionary theology draws on this verse. The Great Commission is not merely a post-Resurrection command but is rooted in the Old Testament pattern: those who encounter God's salvation are obligated to share it. The liturgical dismissal — "Let us go forth in peace" — sends the baptized into the world as witnesses.

### The Trampled Officer (7:17-20)

The skeptical officer who sees the fulfillment but does not participate in it illustrates the Orthodox teaching that faith is necessary for participation in grace. The sacraments are not magical — they require the recipient's faith and openness. Seeing without believing excludes one from the feast.

## Theological Significance

- **The creative word**: God's word brings into being what it declares
- **The obligation of witness**: Encountering salvation demands proclamation
- **Faith and participation**: Grace requires the recipient's receptivity
- **Divine deliverance**: God acts suddenly and unexpectedly
