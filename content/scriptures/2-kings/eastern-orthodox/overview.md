## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of 2 Kings is grounded in the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The book is read within the broader framework of *theosis* (divinization) and the divine economy of salvation. The Fathers — particularly the Antiochene school (Theodoret of Cyrus, John Chrysostom) and the Cappadocians — provided rich commentary on the prophetic narratives and their spiritual significance.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Liturgical Context
2 Kings is read in the Orthodox lectionary, with selections from the Elisha cycle appearing during Great Lent. Elijah's ascent in the fiery chariot is celebrated as a prefiguration of Christ's Ascension and is the subject of prominent iconography.

### Patristic Typology
- **Elijah's mantle passing to Elisha**: The transmission of prophetic grace, understood as analogous to apostolic succession
- **Naaman's sevenfold washing**: A type of baptism, extensively developed by Cyril of Jerusalem in his *Catechetical Lectures*
- **The Shunammite's son raised**: A type of the resurrection, connecting to Christ's raising of the widow's son at Nain (Luke 7)

### Divine Economy
The rise and fall of kingdoms is read as part of God's providential plan (*oikonomia*) leading ultimately to the Incarnation. The exile is not simply punishment but a purification that prepares Israel for the coming of Christ.
