## Zeal and Its Limits

Orthodox interpretation of 2 Kings 10 engages the tension between righteous zeal and excessive violence, reading Jehu's purge of Baal worship through the lens of patristic moral theology.

## Orthodox Interpretation

### The Destruction of Baal (10:18-28)

The eradication of the Baal cult is affirmed as a legitimate act in defense of true worship. However, Orthodox theology distinguishes between the divine commission to destroy Baal worship and Jehu's personal methods. **Theodoret** notes that the deception used to gather the Baal worshipers is morally problematic, even if the goal is righteous.

### Partial Obedience (10:29-31)

Jehu destroys Baal but preserves the golden calves at Bethel and Dan. Orthodox spiritual theology sees this as a pattern common in the spiritual life: a person may overcome one passion while remaining enslaved to another. True *theosis* requires the purification of all passions, not merely the most visible ones.

### The Limits of Human Zeal

The Orthodox tradition, informed by the hesychast Fathers, teaches that human zeal must be tempered by *nepsis* (spiritual sobriety) and *diakrisis* (discernment). Zeal without discernment can become a form of pride. Jehu's story illustrates the danger of confusing divine commission with personal ambition.

## Theological Significance

- **Legitimate zeal**: Destroying false worship is a righteous act
- **Partial purification is insufficient**: All passions must be addressed
- **Discernment required**: Zeal without sobriety becomes dangerous
- **The persistence of structural sin**: Removing one form of idolatry while preserving another
