## The Healing of Naaman

2 Kings 5 records one of the most theologically significant narratives in the Old Testament: the healing of Naaman, the Aramean (Syrian) general, from leprosy. Protestant interpreters prize this chapter as a powerful illustration of salvation by grace through faith, the universal scope of God's mercy, and the danger of spiritual presumption.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Servant Girl's Witness (5:2-3)

An unnamed Israelite slave girl, captured in a raid, tells Naaman's wife about the prophet in Samaria. Protestant commentators frequently note the irony: the most powerful general in Aram receives the word of salvation from the least powerful person in his household. **Calvin** sees here a rebuke to those who despise humble messengers.

### Naaman's Rage at Simplicity (5:11-12)

Naaman expects a dramatic ritual but is told simply to wash seven times in the Jordan. His initial refusal illustrates the human tendency to resist the simplicity of grace. Protestant soteriology draws a direct parallel: salvation is by faith, not by impressive works, and human pride resists this.

### The Sevenfold Washing (5:14)

When Naaman obeys, his flesh is restored "like the flesh of a little child." Jesus himself cites this episode in Luke 4:27, noting that "there were many lepers in Israel in the time of the prophet Elisha, and none of them was cleansed, but only Naaman the Syrian" — a statement that provoked his own townspeople to attempt to kill him.

### Gehazi's Greed (5:20-27)

Elisha's servant Gehazi pursues Naaman to extract payment that Elisha had refused. The leprosy that left Naaman clings to Gehazi. Protestant interpretation sees Gehazi as a type of those who attempt to profit from the free grace of God — anticipating Simon Magus in Acts 8.

## Theological Significance

- **Grace is free**: Elisha refuses payment; God's salvation cannot be purchased
- **Universal scope of grace**: A Gentile receives what Israel's own lepers did not
- **Simplicity of faith**: Obedience to a simple command, not impressive ritual
- **The danger of greed**: Gehazi's attempt to monetize grace brings judgment
