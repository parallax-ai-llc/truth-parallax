## Amaziah and Jeroboam II

2 Kings 14 covers the reign of Amaziah of Judah and the prosperous reign of Jeroboam II of Israel. Amaziah achieves military success against Edom but foolishly challenges Israel and is defeated. Jeroboam II restores Israel's borders to their greatest extent since Solomon, yet the narrator devotes only a few verses to this accomplishment.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Amaziah's Qualified Righteousness (14:3-4)

Amaziah did "what was right in the eyes of the LORD, yet not like David his father." He executed his father's assassins but spared their children, following the law of Deuteronomy 24:16. Protestant commentators see this as praiseworthy legal restraint. Yet the high places remained — the persistent failure of Judah's otherwise good kings.

### The Thistle and the Cedar (14:9)

Jehoash of Israel responds to Amaziah's challenge with a parable: a thistle demanded that a cedar give its daughter in marriage, but a wild beast trampled the thistle. The message is clear — Amaziah overestimates himself. Protestant commentators note the wisdom of knowing one's limitations and the folly of pride after initial success.

### Jeroboam II's Prosperity (14:25-27)

Jeroboam II restores Israel's borders "according to the word of the LORD, the God of Israel, which he spoke by his servant Jonah the son of Amittai." This is the same Jonah of the prophetic book. Despite Jeroboam's evil, God saves Israel through him because "the LORD had not said that he would blot out the name of Israel from under heaven."

## Theological Significance

- **Success breeds pride**: Amaziah's Edomite victory leads to foolish overreach
- **Prosperity is not endorsement**: Jeroboam II's golden age coincides with deep spiritual decay
- **God's patience**: Divine mercy extends the life of a rebellious nation
- **Human evaluation vs. divine evaluation**: Worldly success is not the measure of faithfulness
