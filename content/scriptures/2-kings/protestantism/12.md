## Joash Repairs the Temple

2 Kings 12 narrates the reign of Joash (Jehoash) of Judah, focusing on his project to repair the temple. What begins as a promising reform deteriorates when Joash fails to maintain his devotion after Jehoiada's death (a detail supplied by 2 Chronicles 24) and ends by paying tribute to Hazael with temple treasures.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Joash's Dependent Righteousness (12:2)

"Jehoash did what was right in the eyes of the LORD all the days in which Jehoiada the priest instructed him." Protestant interpreters note the qualification: Joash's faithfulness was dependent on his mentor. Without Jehoiada's guidance, Joash lacked the internal conviction to sustain his reforms. This provides a sober lesson about faith that relies on others rather than on personal conviction.

### The Temple Repair System (12:4-16)

Joash establishes a system for collecting and disbursing funds for temple repair. When the priests prove negligent with the money, Joash creates a chest with a hole in its lid for direct contributions — an early example of financial accountability. Protestant commentators commend the innovation while noting that even sacred institutions require oversight.

### The Tribute to Hazael (12:17-18)

When Hazael of Aram threatens Jerusalem, Joash strips the temple of its sacred treasures to buy peace. What was repaired is now ransacked — by the very king who repaired it. Protestant exegesis sees a tragic irony: Joash's greatest achievement is undone by his own hand under pressure.

## Theological Significance

- **Dependent vs. independent faith**: Mentored righteousness must become personal conviction
- **Stewardship and accountability**: Even temple finances require systems of integrity
- **The fragility of reform**: Outward repair without inward transformation is insufficient
- **The cost of unfaithfulness**: What God builds, human fear can dismantle
