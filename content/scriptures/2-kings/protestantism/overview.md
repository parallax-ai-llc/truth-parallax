## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 2 Kings reads the book through the lens of *sola scriptura* and covenant theology. The rise and fall of Israel's and Judah's kingdoms illustrate the consequences of faithfulness and unfaithfulness to God's covenant. Protestant commentators emphasize the prophetic word as the engine of history, the typological significance of key figures, and the theological lessons for the church.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

### Prophetic Authority
The dominance of Elisha in the first half of the book demonstrates that God's word through the prophets supersedes royal power. Protestant theology sees this as affirming the priority of Scripture over institutional authority.

### Covenant and Judgment
The fall of both Israel (ch. 17) and Judah (ch. 25) is read as the outworking of covenant curses specified in Deuteronomy 28-29. This confirms the Protestant emphasis on the binding nature of God's covenant.

### Typology
- Elisha's miracles prefigure Christ's ministry (feeding the hungry, healing lepers, raising the dead)
- Josiah's reform anticipates the Reformation's return to Scripture
- The exile foreshadows the spiritual exile of sin before redemption
