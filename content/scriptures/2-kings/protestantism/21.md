## Manasseh and Amon: The Depths of Apostasy

2 Kings 21 records the longest reign in Judah's history — Manasseh's fifty-five years — and the shortest theological assessment: unmitigated evil. Manasseh reverses every reform of his father Hezekiah, introduces practices more abominable than the nations God drove out before Israel, and fills Jerusalem with innocent blood. His son Amon follows the same path before being assassinated.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Manasseh's Comprehensive Apostasy (21:2-9)

The catalog of Manasseh's sins is exhaustive: he rebuilt the high places Hezekiah destroyed, erected altars to Baal, made an Asherah pole, worshiped the host of heaven, built pagan altars in the temple, burned his son as an offering, practiced divination and sorcery, and set a carved image of Asherah in the temple itself. Protestant interpreters view this as a systematic reversal of every commandment in Deuteronomy.

### The Irrevocable Decree (21:10-15)

God sends prophets to declare that Jerusalem will be destroyed — "wiping Jerusalem as one wipes a dish, wiping it and turning it upside down" (21:13). Protestant theology regards this as the moment when Judah's fate is sealed. Even Josiah's later reform cannot reverse this decree, only delay it (22:18-20).

### "He shed very much innocent blood" (21:16)

Rabbinic tradition identifies this with the martyrdom of Isaiah (said to have been sawn in two — cf. Hebrews 11:37). Protestant commentators, while acknowledging the tradition's uncertainty, emphasize that Manasseh's violence against the righteous is the sin that makes judgment irrevocable.

## Theological Significance

- **One generation can undo decades of reform**: Manasseh destroys Hezekiah's legacy
- **Some judgments are irrevocable**: There is a point beyond which even national repentance cannot avert consequences
- **The mystery of God's patience**: Manasseh reigns for fifty-five years without immediate judgment
- **The persistence of evil**: Amon shows that Manasseh's wickedness was not merely personal but systemic
