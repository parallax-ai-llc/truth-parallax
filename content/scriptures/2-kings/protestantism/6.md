## The Invisible Army and the Siege

2 Kings 6 contains two distinct episodes: Elisha's miracles at the Jordan and in Dothan (6:1-23) and the beginning of the Aramean siege of Samaria (6:24-33). Together they contrast spiritual sight with spiritual blindness.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Floating Axe Head (6:1-7)

A borrowed axe head falls into the Jordan. Elisha causes it to float by throwing a stick into the water. While a small miracle, Protestant commentators note that God cares about everyday needs. The axe was borrowed, making its loss a matter of justice, not just inconvenience.

### "Those who are with us are more than those who are with them" (6:16)

When the Aramean army surrounds Dothan to capture Elisha, his servant panics. Elisha prays, and the servant's eyes are opened to see the hills filled with horses and chariots of fire. This verse is central to Protestant teaching on spiritual warfare: the reality of God's invisible protection is more substantial than any visible threat.

### Blindness and Mercy (6:18-23)

Elisha asks God to strike the Aramean soldiers with blindness, leads them into Samaria, and then instructs the king of Israel to feed them and send them home rather than kill them. The king's question "Shall I strike them down?" and Elisha's refusal illustrate the prophetic principle that mercy triumphs over vengeance.

### The Siege and the Donkey's Head (6:24-33)

The siege of Samaria produces famine so severe that a donkey's head sells for eighty shekels of silver. The horror of cannibalism reaches the king, who blames Elisha. Protestant interpreters see the siege as the covenant curse of Deuteronomy 28:53-57 being fulfilled literally.

## Theological Significance

- **Spiritual perception**: True sight is seeing the spiritual reality behind the material
- **God's care for small things**: The axe head matters because justice matters
- **Mercy over vengeance**: Elisha models Christ's command to love enemies
- **Covenant consequences**: The siege fulfills Deuteronomic warnings
