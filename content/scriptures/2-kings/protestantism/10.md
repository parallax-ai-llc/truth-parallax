## Jehu's Purge of Baal Worship

2 Kings 10 continues Jehu's revolution with the systematic elimination of Ahab's descendants and the destruction of Baal worship in Israel. Yet the chapter ends with a stinging qualification: Jehu did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Seventy Sons of Ahab (10:1-11)

Jehu challenges Samaria's leaders to choose a champion from Ahab's seventy sons. Instead, they capitulate and deliver the sons' heads in baskets. The ruthlessness exceeds what the prophetic commission required, and Hosea 1:4 later condemns "the blood of Jezreel" — suggesting that God held Jehu accountable for his excess even while using him as an instrument of judgment.

### The Ruse at the Temple of Baal (10:18-28)

Jehu summons all Baal worshipers to a great sacrifice, then slaughters them and demolishes the temple of Baal. Protestant commentators debate whether deception in service of a divine commission is justified. **Calvin** argues that Jehu's lie was sinful even though his purpose was legitimate — God does not need falsehood to accomplish His will.

### The Qualification (10:29-31)

"But Jehu did not turn aside from the sins of Jeroboam" — the golden calves at Bethel and Dan remained. Jehu destroyed Baal worship but preserved the state religion that Jeroboam had established. Protestant interpretation sees this as the tragedy of partial obedience: Jehu eliminated the foreign god but kept the domesticated one.

## Theological Significance

- **Partial obedience is disobedience**: Removing Baal while keeping the calves is insufficient
- **God's sovereignty over mixed motives**: God uses Jehu but does not approve all his actions
- **The limits of violence**: Revolution may remove one evil while installing another
- **The persistent problem of Jeroboam's sin**: Israel's foundational apostasy outlasts every purge
