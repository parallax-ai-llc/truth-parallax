## Ahaz and the Assyrian Alliance

2 Kings 16 records the reign of Ahaz, one of Judah's worst kings. Facing a military coalition of Israel and Aram, Ahaz refuses Isaiah's counsel (cf. Isaiah 7) and instead appeals to Tiglath-Pileser of Assyria for help, paying for the alliance with temple treasures and redesigning the temple altar after a pagan model he saw in Damascus.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Ahaz's Apostasy (16:3-4)

Ahaz "even burned his son as an offering, according to the despicable practices of the nations." He sacrificed at high places, on hills, and under every green tree. Protestant commentators view Ahaz as the antitype of the faithful king — where Hezekiah later trusts God alone, Ahaz trusts everyone and everything except God.

### The Appeal to Assyria (16:7-9)

Rather than trusting the LORD's promise through Isaiah (Isaiah 7:1-9), Ahaz sends a message to Tiglath-Pileser: "I am your servant and your son. Come up and save me." **Calvin** notes the bitter irony: Ahaz declares himself the "servant and son" of a pagan king while refusing to be a servant of God. The Assyrian alliance brings short-term relief but long-term bondage.

### The Damascus Altar (16:10-18)

After meeting Tiglath-Pileser in Damascus, Ahaz sends back plans for a pagan altar and orders the priest Urijah to build a copy. He displaces the bronze altar of Solomon to make room. Protestant Reformation interpreters saw this as a picture of what happens when human innovation replaces God's prescribed worship — a key argument for the regulative principle of worship.

## Theological Significance

- **Trust in human power versus trust in God**: Ahaz chooses Assyria over the LORD
- **The corruption of worship**: The Damascus altar represents the substitution of human preference for divine ordinance
- **The cost of alliance**: What seems like political pragmatism leads to religious compromise
- **The regulative principle**: God prescribes how He is to be worshiped
