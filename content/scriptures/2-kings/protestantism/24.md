## The Beginning of the End

2 Kings 24 records the final years of Judah's independence. Jehoiakim rebels against Nebuchadnezzar and dies. His son Jehoiachin surrenders Jerusalem after a three-month reign, and the first major deportation to Babylon takes place in 597 BCE. Nebuchadnezzar installs Zedekiah as a puppet king.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### God Sends Destroyers (24:2-4)

Bands of Chaldeans, Arameans, Moabites, and Ammonites raid Judah. The narrator explains: "Surely this came upon Judah at the command of the LORD, to remove them from his sight, for the sins of Manasseh, according to all that he had done, and also for the innocent blood that he had shed" (24:3-4). Protestant theology underscores that these geopolitical events are not random: God directs the nations to accomplish His covenant purposes.

### Jehoiachin's Surrender (24:10-16)

The eighteen-year-old king surrenders to Nebuchadnezzar, who deports the king, the queen mother, the officials, the warriors, and the craftsmen — "none remained, except the poorest people of the land" (24:14). The deportation includes the temple treasures. Protestant interpreters note that this is the first fulfillment of Isaiah's prophecy to Hezekiah (20:17-18).

### Zedekiah Installed (24:17-20)

Nebuchadnezzar renames Mattaniah as Zedekiah and places him on the throne. Zedekiah "did what was evil in the sight of the LORD." The last king of Judah follows the pattern of apostasy that has characterized most of his predecessors.

## Theological Significance

- **Historical events serve theological purposes**: Babylon is God's instrument of judgment
- **The fulfillment of prophecy**: What Isaiah predicted a century earlier now occurs
- **The cost of covenant violation**: The treasures and people are stripped away
- **The end of an era**: The Davidic kingdom, though not the Davidic line, is about to end
