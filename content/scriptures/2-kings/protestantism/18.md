## Hezekiah and Sennacherib's Invasion

2 Kings 18 introduces Hezekiah, Judah's greatest reforming king, and the Assyrian crisis that tested his faith. Hezekiah removes the high places, smashes the bronze serpent, and trusts in the LORD. Sennacherib invades Judah and sends the Rabshakeh to Jerusalem with a propaganda speech designed to undermine the people's confidence in God.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Hezekiah's Reform and Faith (18:3-7)

"He trusted in the LORD, the God of Israel, so that there was none like him among all the kings of Judah after him, nor among those who were before him" (18:5). Protestant interpreters note that this supreme commendation includes even David. Hezekiah's destruction of the bronze serpent (*Nehushtan*) is particularly significant — an object with legitimate origins (Numbers 21:8-9) had become an idol. Reformed theology sees here the principle that even God-given instruments can become objects of superstitious veneration.

### The Rabshakeh's Speech (18:19-35)

The Rabshakeh's speech is a masterpiece of psychological warfare. He argues: (1) Egypt is unreliable (18:21); (2) Hezekiah removed God's high places, so God should be angry with him (18:22); (3) the LORD himself sent Assyria against Judah (18:25); (4) no god of any nation has resisted Assyria (18:33-35). Each argument contains a grain of truth twisted to serve a lie.

Protestant preaching frequently uses this passage to illustrate how temptation works: the enemy mixes truth with falsehood to produce despair.

### "Do not let Hezekiah make you trust in the LORD" (18:30)

This is the Rabshakeh's ultimate demand — stop trusting God. The entire Assyrian strategy is to break faith. Protestant theology sees this as the definitive spiritual battle: every assault on the believer ultimately aims at destroying trust in God.

## Theological Significance

- **True reform**: Hezekiah removes even legitimate objects that have become idols
- **Faith under pressure**: Trusting God when all evidence suggests defeat
- **The rhetoric of unbelief**: The enemy's arguments are sophisticated, not crude
- **The sufficiency of God**: Hezekiah's trust is vindicated in chapter 19
