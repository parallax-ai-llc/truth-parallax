## The Death of Elisha and Partial Restoration

2 Kings 13 records the reigns of Jehoahaz and Jehoash of Israel and the death of Elisha. The chapter illustrates both God's continuing compassion for Israel despite its sins and the limitations of half-hearted obedience.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### God's Compassion Despite Sin (13:4-5, 23)

Despite Jehoahaz's continuation in Jeroboam's sins, he cries out to the LORD, and God raises a deliverer. Verse 23 explains: "The LORD was gracious to them and had compassion on them and turned toward them, because of his covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." Protestant theology sees here the unconditional dimension of the Abrahamic covenant operating alongside the conditional Mosaic covenant.

### The Arrows of Victory (13:14-19)

On his deathbed, Elisha instructs King Jehoash to strike the ground with arrows. Jehoash strikes three times and stops. Elisha is angry: "You should have struck five or six times; then you would have struck down Syria until you had made an end of it." The king's half-heartedness limits God's deliverance.

**Application**: Protestant commentators consistently draw the lesson that God's blessings are often proportional to the earnestness with which they are sought.

### Elisha's Bones Raise the Dead (13:20-21)

Even after Elisha's death, a corpse thrown into his tomb is revived upon touching Elisha's bones. Protestant interpreters are careful to distinguish this from relic veneration, emphasizing that the power was God's alone, demonstrated to confirm Elisha's prophetic ministry one final time.

## Theological Significance

- **Covenant faithfulness**: God's mercy extends even to an unfaithful people
- **The cost of half-heartedness**: Partial obedience yields partial results
- **Prophetic authority beyond death**: Elisha's ministry is vindicated even after his death
- **Grace preceding repentance**: God is compassionate before Israel fully turns back
