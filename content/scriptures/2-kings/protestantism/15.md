## Israel's Final Kings and Judah's Stability

2 Kings 15 covers a tumultuous period: the stable reigns of Azariah (Uzziah) and Jotham in Judah contrasted with the rapid succession of assassinations and coups in Israel. The chapter marks the beginning of Assyria's direct intervention in Israelite affairs under Tiglath-Pileser III.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Azariah's Leprosy (15:5)

Azariah (also called Uzziah) reigns for fifty-two years but is struck with leprosy "until the day of his death." 2 Chronicles 26:16-21 supplies the reason: he presumed to burn incense in the temple, a prerogative of priests. Protestant interpreters see this as a lesson about the distinction between kingly and priestly offices — a distinction that only Christ ultimately unites.

### Israel's Spiral of Violence (15:8-31)

In rapid succession: Zechariah reigns six months before being assassinated by Shallum; Shallum reigns one month before being killed by Menahem; Menahem pays tribute to Assyria; Pekahiah is assassinated by Pekah; Pekah is assassinated by Hoshea. The dynasty of Jehu ends with Zechariah, fulfilling God's promise that Jehu's sons would sit on the throne "to the fourth generation" (10:30).

### Tiglath-Pileser and the First Deportation (15:29)

Tiglath-Pileser III conquers parts of northern Israel and deports populations. This is the beginning of the end for the northern kingdom. Protestant interpreters view the Assyrian advance as the instrument of divine judgment.

## Theological Significance

- **The limits of human authority**: Azariah's leprosy demonstrates that even good kings cannot overstep divine boundaries
- **The self-destruction of rebellion**: Israel tears itself apart from within before Assyria destroys it from without
- **Prophetic fulfillment**: Jehu's dynasty ends exactly as prophesied
- **The Assyrian crisis**: God uses pagan empires to accomplish covenant judgment
