## The Siege Lifted

2 Kings 7 records the miraculous end of the siege of Samaria. Elisha prophesies abundance by the next day, four lepers discover the abandoned Aramean camp, and the skeptical royal officer is trampled to death — fulfilling Elisha's word exactly.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Elisha's Prophecy (7:1-2)

Elisha announces that by the next day, fine flour and barley will be sold at normal prices in Samaria's gate. The king's officer scoffs: "If the LORD should make windows in heaven, could this thing be?" Elisha replies that the officer will see it but not eat of it. Protestant interpreters note the pattern: unbelief does not prevent God's word from being fulfilled, but it does exclude the unbeliever from its blessings.

### The Four Lepers (7:3-8)

Four lepers at the city gate reason that staying means death, so they approach the Aramean camp. They find it abandoned — God had caused the Arameans to hear the sound of a great army. The lepers' logic is a model of desperate faith: when all options are deadly, move toward the unknown.

### Good News That Must Be Shared (7:9)

The lepers' declaration — "This is a day of good news. If we are silent and wait until morning light, punishment will overtake us" — is frequently cited in Protestant missionary theology as a picture of the gospel imperative. Those who discover grace are obligated to share it.

### The Officer Trampled (7:17-20)

The skeptical officer is stationed at the gate and trampled by the rushing crowd. He sees the abundance but does not eat. Protestant commentators emphasize that God's word is fulfilled to the letter — both the promise and the judgment.

## Theological Significance

- **God's word is certain**: The prophecy is fulfilled exactly as spoken
- **Unbelief has consequences**: Seeing without participating is itself a judgment
- **The gospel mandate**: Good news demands proclamation
- **God uses the unlikely**: Lepers become the agents of deliverance
