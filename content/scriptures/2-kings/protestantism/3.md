## The Campaign Against Moab

2 Kings 3 records the joint military campaign of Israel, Judah, and Edom against Moab after King Mesha's rebellion. The chapter juxtaposes the failure of human military planning with the power of prophetic intervention through Elisha.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Jehoram's Partial Reform (3:1-3)

Jehoram son of Ahab removes the pillar of Baal but continues in the sins of Jeroboam. Protestant commentators see this as a picture of incomplete repentance — removing the most egregious sin while retaining the systemic apostasy that pervades the nation.

### Elisha and the Music (3:15)

Elisha calls for a musician before prophesying. Protestant interpreters note that this does not reduce prophecy to an ecstatic technique but reflects the prophet's need to still his spirit after his anger at Jehoram. **Calvin** observes that the music was a means of composing Elisha's mind to receive God's word, not a mechanical trigger for revelation.

### Water Without Rain (3:16-20)

God provides water in the desert without rain — filling the ditches miraculously. This demonstrates God's sovereignty over natural means. He is not bound to the ordinary course of nature to accomplish His purposes.

### Mesha's Sacrifice (3:27)

The king of Moab sacrifices his eldest son on the city wall, after which "great wrath came upon Israel" and they withdrew. This verse is notoriously difficult. Protestant interpretations include: (1) the wrath was Moabite fury that intensified their resistance; (2) Israel was horrified and withdrew in revulsion; (3) God's indignation fell on Israel for provoking such desperation.

## Theological Significance

- **Incomplete obedience is insufficient**: Jehoram's half-measures do not earn God's favor
- **God provides through unexpected means**: Water without rain illustrates sovereign providence
- **The limits of military power**: Victory depends on God's word, not human alliances
- **The horror of false religion**: Mesha's sacrifice shows the ultimate end of idolatry
