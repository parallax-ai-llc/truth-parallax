## Jehu's Revolution

2 Kings 9 records the anointing of Jehu as king of Israel and his swift, violent purge of the house of Ahab. Elisha sends a young prophet to anoint Jehu, who then kills Joram of Israel, Ahaziah of Judah, and Jezebel in rapid succession. The chapter fulfills Elijah's prophecy from 1 Kings 21.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Secret Anointing (9:1-10)

Elisha sends a member of the prophetic guild to anoint Jehu privately, instructing him to "open the door and flee; do not linger." The anointing includes the commission to strike down the house of Ahab and avenge the blood of the prophets. Protestant interpreters note the contrast between Jehu's divine commission and his subsequent excess.

### Jehu's Zeal (9:20)

"The driving is like the driving of Jehu the son of Nimshi, for he drives furiously." This description captures Jehu's character — zealous, impetuous, ruthless. Protestant commentary consistently distinguishes between Jehu's legitimate commission and his personal ambition.

### The Death of Jezebel (9:30-37)

Jezebel, hearing of Jehu's approach, paints her eyes and adorns her hair — an act of royal defiance, not seduction. Jehu orders her thrown from the window, and dogs consume her body, fulfilling Elijah's prophecy (1 Kings 21:23). The narrative spares no detail in demonstrating that God's word spoken decades earlier is fulfilled to the letter.

## Theological Significance

- **Prophetic fulfillment**: Every word of Elijah's prophecy against Ahab's house comes to pass
- **Divine judgment through human agents**: God uses Jehu, even though Jehu acts from mixed motives
- **The end of tyranny**: Jezebel's death closes the most notorious chapter of royal apostasy
- **Zeal without purity**: Jehu's revolution raises questions about whether righteous ends justify violent means
