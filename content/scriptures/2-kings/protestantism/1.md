## Judgment on Ahaziah

2 Kings opens with the continuation of Ahaziah's story from 1 Kings 22. After falling through a lattice in his upper chamber, Ahaziah sends messengers to inquire of Baal-Zebub, the god of Ekron, whether he will recover. Elijah intercepts the messengers with a word of judgment: "Is it because there is no God in Israel that you are going to inquire of Baal-Zebub?" (1:3).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Fire from Heaven (1:9-14)

Ahaziah sends three successive companies of fifty soldiers to arrest Elijah. The first two captains approach Elijah with authority, and fire from heaven consumes them. The third captain humbles himself, and his company is spared. Protestant interpreters read this as a demonstration of the inviolability of the prophetic office.

**Calvin** notes that the fire is not Elijah's personal vengeance but God's vindication of His word. The prophet is merely the instrument through whom divine judgment operates.

### The Theological Question (1:3, 6, 16)

The threefold repetition of the question — "Is it because there is no God in Israel?" — establishes the central theological issue of 2 Kings. Every subsequent narrative of apostasy and judgment circles back to this fundamental question of whether Israel will acknowledge the LORD or seek other gods.

## Theological Significance

- **Exclusive worship**: The very first chapter frames the entire book around the first commandment
- **Prophetic authority**: Elijah's word overrides royal power
- **Divine judgment**: Those who reject God's word face consequences
- **The pattern of inquiry**: Seeking God's word versus seeking pagan oracles
