## Hezekiah's Illness and the Babylonian Embassy

2 Kings 20 records two episodes that shadow Hezekiah's otherwise exemplary reign: his terminal illness and miraculous healing, and his fateful reception of Babylonian envoys. The chapter bridges the Assyrian crisis and the coming Babylonian exile.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Hezekiah's Prayer and Healing (20:1-7)

Isaiah tells Hezekiah, "Set your house in order, for you shall die." Hezekiah turns his face to the wall and prays, weeping. Before Isaiah leaves the palace, God reverses the decree and grants fifteen more years. Protestant theology wrestles with how a divine decree can be changed by prayer. Most Reformed interpreters hold that God's eternal decree always included both the announcement and the reversal — the prayer was the appointed means by which God accomplished His predetermined will.

### The Sign of the Sundial (20:8-11)

The shadow retreats ten steps on the "steps of Ahaz." Protestant interpreters differ on whether this was a localized optical phenomenon or a cosmic event. In either case, it demonstrates God's absolute control over the natural order.

### The Babylonian Embassy (20:12-19)

Merodach-Baladan sends envoys, ostensibly to congratulate Hezekiah's recovery. Hezekiah shows them everything in his treasury. Isaiah pronounces judgment: everything will be carried to Babylon, and Hezekiah's descendants will be eunuchs in the Babylonian palace. Hezekiah's response — "The word of the LORD that you have spoken is good" — is troubling. He seems relieved that the disaster will come after his lifetime.

Protestant interpreters view this as Hezekiah's great failure: the man who trusted God against Assyria succumbs to pride before Babylon. The very treasures God preserved from Assyria are now displayed to the future conqueror.

## Theological Significance

- **Prayer changes things**: God is responsive to the cries of His people
- **Pride follows deliverance**: Hezekiah's finest hour (ch. 19) is followed by his worst (ch. 20)
- **Babylon foreshadowed**: The Babylonian exile is announced a century before it occurs
- **The danger of complacency**: "Peace in my time" is an inadequate response to prophetic warning
