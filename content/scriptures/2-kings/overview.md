## Overview

2 Kings continues the narrative of Israel's and Judah's monarchies begun in 1 Kings. Originally a single work with 1 Kings in the Hebrew Bible (*Melachim*), it was divided into two books in the Septuagint. The book traces the final centuries of both kingdoms — from the ministry of Elisha through the destruction of Samaria by Assyria (722 BCE) and the fall of Jerusalem to Babylon (586 BCE).

## Structure

2 Kings may be broadly divided into three sections:

1. **The Elisha Cycle** (Chapters 1–13): The prophetic ministry of Elisha, successor to Elijah, including miracles, political interventions, and the dynasty of Jehu
2. **The Divided Kingdom's Decline** (Chapters 14–17): The final kings of Israel culminating in the fall of Samaria and the Assyrian exile
3. **Judah Alone** (Chapters 18–25): The reigns of Hezekiah and Josiah as bright intervals before the Babylonian conquest and exile

## Cross-Traditional Significance

2 Kings holds importance across multiple religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: Part of the Nevi'im Rishonim (Former Prophets), read in haftarah portions; the destruction of the Temple is commemorated on Tisha B'Av
- **Christianity**: Provides the historical backdrop for the prophetic books (Isaiah, Jeremiah); key types and patterns of judgment and restoration
- **Islam**: Figures such as Elisha (al-Yasa') are recognized as prophets in the Quran
- **Academic**: A critical source for reconstructing the history of the ancient Near East in the 9th–6th centuries BCE

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Prophetic Authority | The word of God through the prophets drives history forward |
| Covenant Faithfulness | Obedience to the covenant determines the fate of kings and nations |
| Judgment | The destruction of both kingdoms as consequences of idolatry |
| Reform | The reforms of Hezekiah and Josiah as models of covenant renewal |
| Exile | The theological crisis of displacement from the promised land |
| Hope | The release of Jehoiachin at the book's end hints at restoration |
