## The Babylonian Conquest Begins

Jewish reading of 2 Kings 24 sees the Babylonian conquest as the fulfillment of prophetic warnings and the beginning of the Galut (exile) that has shaped Jewish identity ever since.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Divine Agency (24:2-4)

**Rashi** emphasizes that the raiders came "at the command of the LORD" — the destruction is not merely political misfortune but divine decree. The Talmud (Gittin 56a-b) teaches that HaShem does not punish Israel without first providing ample warning and opportunity for teshuvah.

### The First Deportation (24:10-16)

The deportation of Jehoiachin and the Temple vessels in 597 BCE is the beginning of the Babylonian exile. The Talmud (Seder Olam Rabbah) carefully calculates the chronology of the exile, linking it to Daniel's prophecy of seventy years. Among those deported were the sages and scholars — the intellectual and spiritual leadership of the nation.

### Exile and Transformation

Rabbinic Judaism emerged from the experience of exile. The synagogue replaced the Temple as the center of worship, prayer replaced sacrifice, and Torah study became the supreme religious act. The seeds of this transformation are planted in the events of 2 Kings 24.

## Theological Significance

- **Exile as divine decree**: The conquest is not random but purposeful
- **The beginning of Galut**: Exile becomes a defining Jewish experience
- **Transformation through suffering**: Judaism evolves in response to catastrophe
- **The portability of Torah**: Jewish faith survives apart from Temple and land
