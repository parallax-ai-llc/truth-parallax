## Jehu's Purge of Baal

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 10 evaluates Jehu's destruction of Baal worship while noting his failure to remove the golden calves — a pattern of selective obedience that limits the divine reward.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Seventy Sons (10:1-11)

**Rashi** interprets the execution of Ahab's descendants as the fulfillment of the prophetic decree. However, the Talmudic discussion of Hosea 1:4 — "I will punish the house of Jehu for the blood of Jezreel" — indicates that Jehu went beyond his commission. **Ralbag** derives the lesson that even legitimate authority can be exercised excessively.

### The Temple of Baal Destroyed (10:18-28)

**Radak** notes that Jehu's ruse — pretending to be a worshiper of Baal — raises ethical questions about deception in the service of God. The rabbinic consensus is nuanced: while the eradication of idolatry is a supreme value, the use of deception is not ideal. The Talmud generally prefers straightforward action.

### The Golden Calves Retained (10:29-31)

Jehu's reward — four generations on the throne — is limited precisely because he retained Jeroboam's calves. **Rashi** explains that Jehu feared that if he removed the calves and sent the people to worship in Jerusalem, they would transfer their loyalty to the Davidic king. His obedience was calculated, not wholehearted.

## Theological Significance

- **Selective obedience**: Removing one form of idolatry while preserving another is insufficient
- **Limited reward**: Partial faithfulness receives partial blessing
- **The ethics of deception**: Legitimate ends do not automatically justify all means
- **Political calculation vs. faith**: Jehu's reasoning was political, not theological
