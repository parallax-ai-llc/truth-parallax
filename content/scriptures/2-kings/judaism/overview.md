## Overview

In Judaism, 2 Kings (*Melachim Bet*) is part of the Nevi'im Rishonim (Former Prophets) in the Tanakh. The book chronicles the final chapters of Israelite monarchy, from Elisha's prophetic career through the catastrophic destruction of the First Temple (Churban Bayit Rishon) in 586 BCE. The destruction of the Temple and the Babylonian exile constitute one of the most traumatic events in Jewish history, commemorated annually on Tisha B'Av.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Haftarah Readings
Several passages from 2 Kings serve as haftarah portions:

| Haftarah | Parashah | Passage |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Vayeira | Genesis 18-22 | 2 Kings 4:1-37 (Elisha and the Shunammite) |
| Tazria | Leviticus 12-13 | 2 Kings 4:42-5:19 (Naaman's healing) |
| Metzora | Leviticus 14-15 | 2 Kings 7:3-20 (Four lepers at Samaria's gate) |

### Rabbinic Interpretation
The Talmud and Midrash extensively discuss 2 Kings narratives. The destruction of the Temple is analyzed in Tractate Gittin 55b-58a, where the rabbis identify *sinat chinam* (baseless hatred) as a cause of the Second Temple's destruction, drawing on the pattern established by the First Temple's fall.

### Medieval Commentators
- **Rashi**: Provides concise peshat explanations with selective midrashic material
- **Radak (David Kimchi)**: Offers grammatical and contextual analysis
- **Ralbag (Gersonides)**: Draws moral and philosophical lessons (*to'aliyyot*)
- **Abarbanel**: Provides extended political and theological analysis

## Theological Significance

The fall of Israel and Judah raised profound theological questions that shaped rabbinic Judaism:
- Why did God allow the Temple to be destroyed?
- How can Israel maintain its covenant identity in exile?
- What is the relationship between sin and national catastrophe?

These questions gave rise to the rabbinic emphasis on Torah study, prayer, and deeds of loving-kindness as replacements for the sacrificial system.
