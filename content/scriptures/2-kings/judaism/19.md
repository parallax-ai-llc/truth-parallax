## Hezekiah's Prayer and the Deliverance of Jerusalem

Jewish tradition celebrates the deliverance of Jerusalem from Sennacherib as one of the great miracles of the Tanakh — a vindication of trust in HaShem against overwhelming military odds.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Hezekiah's Prayer (19:14-19)

**Rashi** notes that Hezekiah spread the letter before HaShem in the Temple, as if to say: "See what this man writes against You." The Talmud (Sanhedrin 95b) records that Hezekiah prayed throughout the night, and HaShem answered through the prophet Isaiah. The Midrash adds that Hezekiah's prayer was so powerful because it was offered with complete *kavanah* (intention) and *bitachon* (trust).

### The Destruction of the Assyrian Army (19:35)

**Radak** reports that the angel destroyed 185,000 soldiers in a single night. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 95b) offers various accounts of the mechanism: some say the angel "uncovered their ears" so they heard the heavenly song and died; others say a fire consumed their bodies but left their garments intact. Whatever the mechanism, the result was unambiguous: HaShem fought for Jerusalem.

### Sennacherib's Death (19:36-37)

The irony of Sennacherib's death in the temple of his own god is noted by **Rashi**: the very deity he claimed would protect him against Israel's God becomes the location of his assassination. The Midrash records that his sons killed him because they discovered he intended to sacrifice them.

## Theological Significance

- **The power of prayer**: Hezekiah's prayer moves heaven to act
- **HaShem fights for Israel**: When Israel trusts fully, divine intervention follows
- **The irony of idolatry**: Sennacherib dies where he worships — proving his god powerless
- **Prophetic vindication**: Isaiah's word is fulfilled exactly
