## Jehu's Revolution

Jewish reading of 2 Kings 9 focuses on the fulfillment of Elijah's prophecy, the end of the house of Ahab, and the theological evaluation of divinely commissioned violence.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Anointing (9:1-10)

**Radak** notes that Jehu's anointing is secret, conducted by a member of the prophetic guild rather than by Elisha himself. The secrecy reflects the dangerous political situation and the need for the revolution to appear spontaneous to the military officers who support it.

### The Death of Jezebel (9:30-37)

**Rashi** comments that Jezebel adorned herself not to seduce Jehu but to die with royal dignity — or, alternatively, to shame Jehu by comparing him unfavorably to previous usurpers. Her death fulfills Elijah's prophecy (1 Kings 21:23) to the letter. The Midrash notes that Jehu's order to bury Jezebel — "she is a king's daughter" — shows that even in executing judgment, basic human dignity should be maintained.

### Prophetic Fulfillment

The connection between Elijah's word and Jehu's action illustrates the rabbinic principle that the words of the prophets never return empty. The Talmud (Shabbat 119b) teaches that Jerusalem was destroyed because people despised Torah scholars — a principle that 2 Kings 9 illustrates in reverse: those who heeded the prophetic word survived; those who rejected it perished.

## Theological Significance

- **Prophetic words are fulfilled**: Every detail of Elijah's prophecy comes to pass
- **Human dignity in judgment**: Even enemies deserve basic respect
- **The end of tyranny**: Ahab's house falls as God decreed
- **The complexity of divine instruments**: Jehu serves God's purpose but is not thereby sanctified
