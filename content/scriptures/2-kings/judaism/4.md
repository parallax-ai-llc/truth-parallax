## Elisha's Miracles: Divine Compassion for the Vulnerable

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 4 emphasizes HaShem's concern for the poor, the widow, and the vulnerable, reading each miracle as a manifestation of divine *chesed* (lovingkindness).

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Widow's Oil (4:1-7)

**Rashi** identifies the widow as the wife of Obadiah, who had gone into debt to feed the hundred prophets he hid from Jezebel (1 Kings 18:4). The miracle rewards the family's previous act of *chesed*. The Midrash teaches that the oil stopped flowing only when there were no more vessels — a lesson that divine blessing is limited only by our capacity to receive it.

### The Shunammite Woman (4:8-37)

The Shunammite woman (*ha-isha ha-gedolah*, "the great woman") is praised by the rabbis for her hospitality (*hakhnasat orchim*). **Rashi** notes that she recognized Elisha as holy by the fact that no fly ever crossed his table. Her insistence on going directly to the prophet when her son dies — "It is well" (*shalom*) she tells her husband — demonstrates extraordinary faith and determination.

The raising of the child is connected in rabbinic thought to the doctrine of *techiyat ha-metim* (resurrection of the dead), one of the fundamental principles of Jewish faith as articulated by Maimonides.

### Feeding the Hundred (4:42-44)

**Radak** notes that Elisha's servant doubts the sufficiency of twenty loaves for a hundred men. The miracle teaches that when food is given in fulfillment of a divine word, it does not follow natural limitations.

## Theological Significance

- **Chesed**: HaShem's lovingkindness extends to the most vulnerable
- **Hospitality rewarded**: Acts of kindness (*gemilut chasadim*) bring divine blessing
- **Resurrection affirmed**: The raising of the dead illustrates a core Jewish doctrine
- **Divine provision**: HaShem's resources exceed human calculation
