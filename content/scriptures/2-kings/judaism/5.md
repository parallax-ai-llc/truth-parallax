## Naaman the Leper: Healing and Conversion

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 5 focuses on Naaman's recognition of the God of Israel, the nature of genuine conversion, and the moral failure of Gehazi.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Naaman as Righteous Gentile

The rabbis regard Naaman's declaration — "Now I know that there is no God in all the earth but in Israel" (5:15) — as a genuine confession of faith. Some rabbinic traditions identify Naaman as a *ger toshav* (resident alien who accepts basic monotheistic principles) rather than a full convert, since he continues to accompany his master to the temple of Rimmon (5:18-19).

### The Servant Girl's Role (5:2-3)

**Rashi** emphasizes that the servant girl, despite her captivity, retained her faith in HaShem and His prophets. The Midrash sees her as an example of how Jewish faith survives even in the most adverse circumstances — a theme of immense importance throughout Jewish history.

### The Sevenfold Immersion (5:14)

**Radak** notes the connection between Naaman's immersion and the laws of *tevilah* (ritual immersion) in the *mikveh*. The number seven signifies completion and renewal. Naaman's flesh becomes "like the flesh of a little child" — he is, in a sense, reborn.

### Gehazi's Punishment (5:20-27)

The Talmud (Sotah 47a) identifies Gehazi as one of four commoners who forfeited their share in the World to Come. His sin was not merely greed but the profanation of God's name (*chillul HaShem*): by seeking payment, he gave the impression that prophets and Torah are commodities for sale.

## Theological Significance

- **Monotheistic recognition**: Even a Gentile general can recognize HaShem
- **Jewish faith in adversity**: The servant girl models faithfulness under oppression
- **Tevilah and renewal**: Immersion symbolizes spiritual rebirth
- **Chillul HaShem**: Profaning God's name is among the gravest sins
