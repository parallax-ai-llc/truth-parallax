## Josiah and the Discovery of the Torah

Jewish tradition venerates Josiah as the last righteous king of Judah. The discovery of the Sefer HaTorah (Book of the Law) in the Temple during his reign is a pivotal moment in Jewish religious history.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Lost Book (22:8)

**Rashi** identifies the book as a Torah scroll, possibly the original scroll written by Moses. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 103b) records that Manasseh had attempted to destroy all Torah scrolls; the one found by Hilkiah had been hidden by the priests. The loss and recovery of the Torah is a metaphor for the Jewish historical experience: the Torah may be suppressed but never destroyed.

### Josiah's Grief (22:11)

**Radak** explains that Josiah tore his garments because he recognized that the curses in Deuteronomy applied directly to the current situation. The practice of *keriah* (tearing garments) in mourning and upon hearing of catastrophe is connected to this biblical model.

### Huldah the Prophetess (22:14-20)

**Rashi** explains that the officials went to Huldah rather than to Jeremiah because "women are more compassionate" and might intercede more effectively. The Talmud (Megillah 14b) counts Huldah among the seven prophetesses of Israel: Sarah, Miriam, Deborah, Hannah, Abigail, Huldah, and Esther. Her prophetic authority is unquestioned — she speaks with the full authority of HaShem.

### The Personal Exemption (22:19-20)

Josiah is promised that he will be "gathered to your grave in peace" — he will not see the destruction. **Ralbag** notes that this reward is for Josiah's personal piety; it does not revoke the national decree. Individual righteousness secures individual blessing but cannot reverse accumulated national guilt.

## Theological Significance

- **The indestructibility of Torah**: The Torah may be hidden but never destroyed
- **Keriah and grief**: The proper response to encountering divine warning
- **Women prophets**: Huldah's authority is fully recognized
- **Individual vs. national destiny**: Personal piety cannot reverse collective judgment
