## Ahaz's Apostasy

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 16 condemns Ahaz as one of the worst kings of Judah, noting his child sacrifice, his alliance with Assyria, and his alteration of the Temple altar.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Child Sacrifice (16:3)

**Rashi** identifies the practice of "passing his son through the fire" as the worship of Molech, explicitly prohibited in Leviticus 18:21. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 64a-b) discusses the specific rituals involved and their absolute prohibition. Ahaz's act represents the most extreme violation of Torah law — sacrificing the future of Israel to a foreign god.

### The Damascus Altar (16:10-18)

**Radak** explains that Ahaz saw an altar in Damascus and had it replicated in Jerusalem, displacing the bronze altar built by Solomon. This represents a fundamental corruption of worship: replacing the divinely prescribed form with a humanly devised one. The principle that worship must follow divine instruction, not human innovation, is central to *halakhah*.

### Subordination to Assyria (16:7-8)

**Ralbag** draws the moral that a leader who abandons trust in HaShem will inevitably become subordinate to human powers. Ahaz's declaration — "I am your servant and your son" — to the Assyrian king is a grotesque inversion of the covenant relationship, in which Israel is supposed to be HaShem's servant and child.

## Theological Significance

- **The prohibition of child sacrifice**: The most absolute boundary in Torah
- **Worship as divinely prescribed**: Human innovation in worship is idolatry
- **Trust in HaShem vs. human alliance**: Political pragmatism without faith leads to bondage
- **Covenant inversion**: Ahaz transfers his covenantal allegiance from God to Assyria
