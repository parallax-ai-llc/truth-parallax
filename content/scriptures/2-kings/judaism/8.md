## Hazael and the Decline of Judah

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 8 meditates on the prophet's sorrow, the endurance of the Davidic covenant, and the corruption of Judah through foreign influence.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Elisha's Tears (8:11-12)

**Rashi** explains that Elisha wept because he saw prophetically the specific atrocities Hazael would commit against Israel — burning fortresses, slaying young men, dashing children, and ripping open pregnant women. The Midrash connects this to the broader pattern of prophetic suffering: the true prophet feels the pain of what he foresees.

### The Lamp of David (8:19)

"The LORD was not willing to destroy Judah, for the sake of David his servant, as he promised to give him a lamp (*ner*) for his sons forever." **Radak** explains that the *ner* (lamp) refers to the continuation of the Davidic dynasty. The promise is unconditional: no matter how wicked individual kings may be, the line itself will not be extinguished. This is the foundation of Jewish messianic hope.

### Intermarriage and Its Consequences (8:18)

The marriage of Jehoshaphat's son to Ahab's daughter introduces the worship of Baal into Judah. Rabbinic teaching on intermarriage (*Kiddushin* 68b) emphasizes the spiritual danger of marriage to those who worship other gods. The effects in 2 Kings 8 are immediate and catastrophic.

## Theological Significance

- **Prophetic suffering**: The prophet bears the burden of foreknowledge
- **Messianic promise**: The Davidic lamp will never be extinguished
- **The danger of intermarriage**: Foreign influence corrupts worship
- **Covenant faithfulness**: HaShem keeps His promises despite human unfaithfulness
