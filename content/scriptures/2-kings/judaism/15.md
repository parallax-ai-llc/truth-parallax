## Israel's Final Kings and the Assyrian Shadow

Jewish reading of 2 Kings 15 notes the rapid decline of the northern kingdom through a series of assassinations while Judah maintains relative stability.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Azariah's Leprosy (15:5)

**Rashi**, drawing on 2 Chronicles 26, explains that Azariah (Uzziah) was struck with *tzara'at* (leprosy) for burning incense in the Temple — a prerogative reserved for the *kohanim* (priests). The Talmud (Arakhin 16a) teaches that *tzara'at* comes as punishment for various sins, including arrogance. Azariah's transgression reflects the belief that he was above the Torah's restrictions.

### The Collapse of Israel (15:8-31)

**Radak** observes that the rapid succession of usurpers in Israel reflects the complete breakdown of legitimate governance. When the fear of HaShem departs, only force remains as the basis of authority, and force is inherently unstable. The Mishnah (Avot 3:2) teaches: "Pray for the welfare of the government, for were it not for the fear of it, men would swallow each other alive."

### The Beginning of Exile (15:29)

Tiglath-Pileser's deportation of parts of northern Israel marks the beginning of the exile of the Ten Tribes. Jewish tradition regards the fate of the Ten Tribes as one of the great unresolved questions of history. Various Midrashic traditions place them beyond the Sambation River or identify them with distant populations.

## Theological Significance

- **Tzara'at and transgression**: Violation of sacred boundaries brings physical consequence
- **Legitimate authority**: Government requires a moral and spiritual foundation
- **The Ten Tribes**: Their fate remains a mystery in Jewish thought
- **The Assyrian threat**: External enemies exploit internal weakness
