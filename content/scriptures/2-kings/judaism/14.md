## Amaziah and Jeroboam II

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 14 draws moral lessons about pride, the importance of proportional justice, and HaShem's patience with Israel.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Individual Responsibility (14:6)

Amaziah executes his father's assassins but spares their children, "according to what is written in the Book of the Torah of Moses." **Rashi** identifies this as Deuteronomy 24:16. This is one of the few instances where a king of Judah explicitly follows written Torah, and the rabbis commend it. The principle of individual accountability (*adam mutal b'cheto*) is fundamental to Jewish jurisprudence.

### The Parable of the Thistle (14:9)

**Radak** reads the parable as a warning against *gaavah* (arrogance). The Talmud (Sotah 5a) teaches that HaShem despises the arrogant and that pride is equivalent to idolatry. Amaziah's success against Edom inflated his self-estimation beyond reality.

### Jeroboam II and God's Compassion (14:26-27)

Despite Jeroboam's wickedness, HaShem saves Israel through him because the people's suffering is severe. **Ralbag** observes that God's compassion sometimes overrides strict justice — a principle central to the theology of the High Holy Days, where Israel appeals to divine mercy rather than demanding that God act on merit alone.

## Theological Significance

- **Individual responsibility**: Children are not punished for parents' sins
- **Pride and its consequences**: Arrogance leads to catastrophic miscalculation
- **Divine compassion**: HaShem's mercy sometimes overrides strict justice
- **The irony of prosperity**: Material success can coexist with spiritual decay
