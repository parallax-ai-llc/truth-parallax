## The Fall of Samaria and the Exile of the Northern Kingdom

2 Kings 17 marks one of the most catastrophic events in Jewish history: the destruction of the northern kingdom of Israel and the exile of the Ten Tribes. The chapter provides an extended theological explanation for the catastrophe.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Sins That Caused Exile (17:7-23)

The Talmud (Yoma 9b) identifies the sins that caused the destruction of the First Temple: idolatry (*avodah zarah*), sexual immorality (*gilui arayot*), and bloodshed (*shefichut damim*). 2 Kings 17 catalogs all three in the northern kingdom. The rabbis saw the exile as a direct application of the covenant curses in Deuteronomy 28.

### "The LORD warned Israel and Judah by every prophet" (17:13)

**Rashi** emphasizes that the exile did not come without warning. HaShem sent prophets over centuries — Ahijah, Elijah, Elisha, Hosea, Amos, Isaiah. The Midrash teaches that God's patience is immense but not infinite: "I have been patient with you, but you have exhausted My patience."

### The Samaritans (17:24-41)

**Radak** explains that the mixed population settled by Assyria created a syncretic religion that combined elements of Torah observance with worship of foreign gods. This community later became known as the Samaritans (*Kutim*), whose status in *halakhah* remained debated throughout the Talmudic period. The Talmud (Kiddushin 75b, Hullin 6a) records various opinions on whether they are to be treated as Jews or as Gentiles.

## Theological Significance

- **Covenant curses fulfilled**: The exile is the application of Deuteronomy 28
- **The patience of HaShem**: Centuries of prophetic warning preceded judgment
- **The Three Cardinal Sins**: Idolatry, immorality, and bloodshed cause exile
- **The Samaritan question**: Syncretism creates an ambiguous religious identity
