## The Lifting of the Siege

Jewish reading of 2 Kings 7 highlights the fulfillment of prophetic words, the reversal of fortune, and the obligation to share good news.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Elisha's Prophecy (7:1-2)

**Rashi** notes that the officer's skepticism — "If the LORD should make windows in heaven" — echoes a broader pattern: those who limit God's power by comparing it to natural possibilities inevitably misjudge the situation. The Midrash connects this to the manna in the wilderness: God opened windows in heaven before and can do so again.

### The Four Lepers (7:3-9)

**Radak** identifies the four lepers as Gehazi and his three sons, following a tradition in the Talmud (Sanhedrin 107b). Their status as lepers — ritually impure and socially marginal — makes them fitting vessels for God's redemptive act. HaShem frequently works through those whom society regards as least significant.

The lepers' statement — "This is a day of good news (*besorah*), and we remain silent" — establishes the moral principle that those who receive good fortune are obligated to share it. The concept of *besorah* (good tidings) connects to the prophetic proclamation of redemption (Isaiah 52:7).

### The Trampled Officer (7:17-20)

**Ralbag** derives the *to'elet*: skepticism about God's word leads to seeing the fulfillment without participating in it. This is a measure-for-measure (*middah keneged middah*) punishment: the officer questioned God's ability to provide, so he saw the provision but was excluded from it.

## Theological Significance

- **Prophetic reliability**: What the prophet declares comes to pass
- **God uses the marginalized**: Lepers become agents of deliverance
- **The obligation to share good news**: Silence in the face of blessing is punishable
- **Measure for measure**: The punishment fits the sin
