## Manasseh: The Worst King and the Power of Teshuvah

Jewish tradition presents Manasseh as the most wicked king of Judah, yet also — through the Chronicler's account — as an example of the limitless power of repentance.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Catalog of Sins (21:2-9)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 103b) lists Manasseh among the kings who have no share in the World to Come. His sins include every category of prohibition: idolatry, sorcery, child sacrifice, and the murder of the innocent. **Rashi** identifies the carved image placed in the Temple (21:7) as a four-faced idol designed to offend HaShem from every direction.

### The Murder of Isaiah (21:16)

Rabbinic tradition (Yevamot 49b) identifies the "innocent blood" shed by Manasseh with the martyrdom of the prophet Isaiah, who is said to have been sawn in two while hiding inside a cedar tree. This tradition is echoed in Hebrews 11:37.

### Manasseh's Teshuvah

While 2 Kings does not record Manasseh's repentance, 2 Chronicles 33:12-13 does, and the *Tefillat Menasheh* (Prayer of Manasseh) is preserved in some traditions. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 103a) debates whether Manasseh's repentance was accepted. One tradition states that the ministering angels tried to close the windows of heaven against his prayer, but HaShem carved a special passage beneath the Throne of Glory to receive it — teaching that no one is so wicked that genuine teshuvah is impossible.

## Theological Significance

- **The depths of wickedness**: Manasseh represents the extreme of human sin
- **The power of teshuvah**: Even the worst sinner can repent
- **The murder of prophets**: Persecuting God's messengers seals national judgment
- **Consequences persist**: Personal repentance may not avert national catastrophe
