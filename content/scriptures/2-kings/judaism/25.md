## Churban: The Destruction of the First Temple

2 Kings 25 records the *Churban Bayit Rishon* — the destruction of the First Temple — the most traumatic event in pre-modern Jewish history, commemorated annually on Tisha B'Av. The chapter also records the assassination of Gedaliah (commemorated as a fast day) and the elevation of Jehoiachin in Babylon.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Destruction of the Temple (25:8-17)

The Talmud (Ta'anit 29a) records that the Temple was set on fire on the ninth of Av and burned through the tenth. This date — Tisha B'Av — became the Jewish day of national mourning, on which both Temples are commemorated along with other catastrophes. The book of *Eikhah* (Lamentations) is read on this day, and the community sits on the floor in mourning.

**Rashi** notes the meticulous inventory of Temple vessels carried to Babylon. Each item represents irreplaceable sacred craftsmanship and centuries of accumulated holiness. The destruction is not merely of a building but of the physical center of Israel's relationship with HaShem.

### Gedaliah's Assassination (25:22-26)

The assassination of Gedaliah by Ishmael son of Netaniah is commemorated as *Tzom Gedaliah* (the Fast of Gedaliah), observed on the third of Tishrei, the day after Rosh Hashanah. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 18b) explains that the murder of Gedaliah extinguished the last ember of Jewish sovereignty in the land, making it equivalent in gravity to the Temple's destruction.

### Jehoiachin's Release (25:27-30)

**Radak** reads the final paragraph as a sign of divine mercy within judgment. The Davidic king survives, is released from prison, and eats at the royal table. The Midrash sees this as HaShem's assurance that the exile is not permanent — the covenant with David endures. Jehoiachin's survival preserves the genealogical line from which the Messiah will come.

## Theological Significance

- **Tisha B'Av**: The national day of mourning for the destroyed Temple
- **Tzom Gedaliah**: The fast commemorating the end of Jewish sovereignty
- **Exile is not permanent**: The Davidic line survives as a seed of future redemption
- **Messianic hope**: Even in Babylon, the covenant promise endures
- **Judaism transformed**: From Temple-centered to Torah-centered life
