## Elisha's Death and the Covenant with the Patriarchs

Jewish interpretation of 2 Kings 13 focuses on HaShem's continuing compassion for Israel, the prophetic significance of Elisha's death, and the miracle at Elisha's tomb.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Arrows of Victory (13:14-19)

**Rashi** explains that each strike of the arrow corresponded to a future victory over Aram. Jehoash's stopping at three limited his victories to three. **Ralbag** derives the principle that one should pursue the fulfillment of God's promises with maximum vigor — half-hearted effort yields half-hearted results.

### The Miracle at the Tomb (13:20-21)

**Radak** notes that this miracle confirmed Elisha's prophetic status even after his death. The Talmud discusses whether the revived man truly lived a full life or was merely temporarily restored. Jewish tradition generally sees this as a genuine resurrection, affirming the broader doctrine of *techiyat ha-metim*.

### The Patriarchal Covenant (13:23)

"The LORD was gracious to them and had compassion on them and turned toward them, because of his covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." **Rashi** emphasizes that this verse demonstrates that the merit of the patriarchs (*zekhut avot*) continues to protect their descendants. The *zekhut avot* is invoked in daily prayer: "Who remembers the piety of the patriarchs and brings a redeemer to their children's children."

## Theological Significance

- **Zekhut avot**: The merit of the patriarchs protects their descendants
- **Vigorous pursuit of blessing**: Half-hearted effort limits divine provision
- **Resurrection**: Even death cannot end the power of the righteous
- **Prophetic confirmation**: Miracles at the tomb validate the prophet's message
