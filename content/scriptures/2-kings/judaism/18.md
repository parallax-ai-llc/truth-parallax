## Hezekiah's Faith and the Assyrian Crisis

Jewish tradition regards Hezekiah as one of the greatest kings of Judah. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 94a) records that HaShem considered making Hezekiah the Messiah but decided otherwise because Hezekiah did not sing a song of praise after his deliverance.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Hezekiah's Reform (18:3-7)

**Rashi** notes that Hezekiah removed the high places, broke the sacred pillars, and destroyed the bronze serpent (*Nehushtan*). The Talmud (Berakhot 10b) preserves a tradition that Hezekiah hid the "Book of Remedies" (*Sefer Refu'ot*) because people were relying on it instead of praying to HaShem for healing. The Sages approved this action.

### The Rabshakeh's Speech (18:19-35)

**Radak** analyzes the Rabshakeh's rhetoric as a sophisticated attempt to undermine trust in HaShem. The speech is delivered in Hebrew (*Yehudit*) rather than Aramaic, deliberately targeting the common people on the walls. Hezekiah's officials request the use of Aramaic (the diplomatic language) to prevent demoralizing the population — an early example of information warfare.

### Hezekiah's Response (18:36-37)

The people's silence — "they did not answer him a word, for the king's command was, 'Do not answer him'" — is praised by the rabbis as an act of communal discipline and faith. **Ralbag** sees this as a model: when confronted with blasphemy, silence and trust in HaShem are preferable to futile argument.

## Theological Significance

- **Trust in HaShem alone**: Hezekiah's faith is the standard for all subsequent leaders
- **Information warfare**: The battle is as much spiritual and psychological as military
- **Disciplined silence**: Not every provocation requires a response
- **Messianic potential**: Hezekiah comes close to the messianic ideal
