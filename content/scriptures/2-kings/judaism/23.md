## Josiah's Reforms and the Great Passover

Jewish reading of 2 Kings 23 celebrates Josiah's comprehensive reform as the most thorough renewal in Israel's history, culminating in the greatest Passover since the period of the Judges.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Covenant Renewal (23:1-3)

**Rashi** notes that Josiah gathered the entire nation — elders, priests, prophets, and common people — to hear the Torah read publicly. This mirrors the *hakhel* ceremony prescribed in Deuteronomy 31:10-13, in which the king reads the Torah before all Israel every seven years. Josiah's act restores this foundational practice.

### The Purge of Idolatry (23:4-20)

**Radak** provides detailed commentary on each site Josiah destroys, connecting them to earlier narratives. The destruction of the altar at Bethel (23:15) fulfills the prophecy of the man of God from 1 Kings 13:2, spoken over three hundred years earlier. The rabbis see this as dramatic proof of prophetic reliability.

### The Passover (23:21-23)

"No such Passover had been kept since the days of the judges." **Rashi** explains that previous Passovers had been compromised by the presence of ritual impurity or the absence of the Ark. Josiah's Passover restored the full observance for the first time in centuries. The celebration of Pesach is central to Jewish identity — it is the foundational act of national memory.

### The Irrevocable Decree (23:26-27)

Despite Josiah's unprecedented faithfulness, HaShem does not relent from the decree against Jerusalem. **Ralbag** explains that the accumulation of sin over many generations created a debt that one generation's repentance could not pay. This does not diminish Josiah's personal merit but acknowledges the collective nature of covenant responsibility.

## Theological Significance

- **Public Torah reading**: The king's duty to make Torah accessible to all
- **Prophetic fulfillment**: Three-hundred-year-old prophecies are fulfilled exactly
- **Pesach**: The foundational act of Jewish memory and identity
- **Collective covenant responsibility**: Individual merit cannot reverse collective guilt
