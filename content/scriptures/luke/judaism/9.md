## Messianic Confession in Jewish Context

Peter's confession "The Christ of God" (9:20) uses the Greek translation of the Hebrew "Mashiach" (Messiah/Anointed One). Jewish scholars emphasize that first-century messianic expectations were diverse — some anticipated a royal-political Messiah, others a priestly Messiah, others a prophetic figure, and still others multiple messianic figures (as at Qumran). The association of messiahship with suffering and death (9:22) was not a prominent strand in pre-Christian Jewish messianism, though passages like Isaiah 53 were available for such interpretation.

## The Transfiguration and Jewish Mystical Tradition

The Transfiguration (9:28–36) resonates with Jewish mystical traditions about the glorification of Moses on Sinai and the visionary experiences described in Ezekiel and Daniel. Jewish scholars note parallels with Merkavah mysticism and the tradition of Moses's shining face (Exodus 34:29–35). The appearance of Moses and Elijah represents the Torah and the Prophets — the two pillars of Jewish Scripture — and their conversation with Jesus situates him within this prophetic lineage.

## Radical Discipleship and Jewish Parallels

The radical demands of discipleship (9:57–62) — "Let the dead bury their own dead" — have sometimes been read as anti-Jewish, but Jewish scholars point out that comparable demands are found in rabbinic literature about the priority of Torah study. Elisha's call to follow Elijah (1 Kings 19:19–21) provides a biblical precedent for leaving family obligations to follow a prophetic master.
