## The Lord's Prayer and Jewish Prayer Traditions

Jewish scholars note significant parallels between the Lord's Prayer (11:1–4) and Jewish liturgical prayer. "Hallowed be your name" echoes the Kaddish prayer ("May His great Name be exalted and sanctified"). "Your kingdom come" reflects the Amidah's petition for the restoration of God's reign. "Give us each day our daily bread" parallels the daily provision of manna. "Forgive us our sins" mirrors Yom Kippur prayers. These parallels demonstrate that Jesus's prayer teaching was rooted in Jewish liturgical tradition.

## The Beelzebul Controversy and Jewish Exorcism

The Beelzebul controversy (11:14–26) reveals that exorcism was practiced within Judaism. Jesus's own reference — "by whom do your sons cast them out?" (11:19) — confirms that Jewish exorcists were active contemporaries. Josephus describes exorcistic practices among Jews, and the Dead Sea Scrolls contain exorcism texts. Jewish scholars emphasize that Jesus's exorcisms place him within a recognized Jewish practice, not outside it.

## The Woes and Intra-Jewish Debate

The woes against the Pharisees and lawyers (11:37–54) must be read as intra-Jewish critique, comparable to the self-critical tradition within the prophetic and rabbinic literature itself. The Talmud contains its own criticisms of insincere Pharisees (Sotah 22b), and the prophets frequently condemned religious leaders. Jewish scholars warn against reading these woes as evidence of a blanket condemnation of Judaism or Pharisaism.
