## The Resurrection and Jewish Belief

The resurrection of Jesus is the point of greatest theological divergence between Judaism and Christianity. While the Pharisaic tradition affirmed bodily resurrection as an eschatological hope (at the end of days), the claim that one individual had been resurrected in the middle of history was unprecedented. Jewish scholars note that this claim required a fundamental reinterpretation of Jewish eschatological categories and was the decisive factor in the emergence of Christianity as a distinct movement.

## The Emmaus Road and Scriptural Interpretation

The Emmaus road narrative (24:13–35), in which Jesus interprets "in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself" (24:27), represents the foundational act of christological interpretation of the Hebrew Bible. Jewish scholars observe that this hermeneutical move — reading the Torah and Prophets as pointing to Jesus — is the root of the interpretive divergence between Judaism and Christianity. Judaism reads the same texts but finds different meanings: the messianic prophecies await future fulfillment, and the Torah's authority is independent of christological reinterpretation.

## Historical Significance

Jewish scholars approach Luke 24 as a text of immense historical significance, regardless of their theological assessment of its claims. This chapter records the convictions that launched the early Christian movement and ultimately transformed the religious landscape of the ancient world. Understanding these convictions — and the Jewish context from which they emerged — remains essential for Jewish self-understanding and for the ongoing dialogue between Judaism and Christianity.
