## The Parables of the Lost and Rabbinic Parallels

Luke 15's three parables of the lost have significant parallels in rabbinic literature. The image of God as a shepherd seeking lost sheep appears in Ezekiel 34 and in later rabbinic midrashim. The idea that heaven rejoices over the repentance of sinners is affirmed in the Talmud: "In the place where penitents stand, even the perfectly righteous cannot stand" (Berakhot 34b). Jewish scholars argue that Jesus's teaching on divine joy over repentance is not a departure from Judaism but a vivid expression of its deepest convictions.

## The Prodigal Son and Jewish Family Dynamics

The parable of the prodigal son (15:11–32) operates within the framework of Jewish inheritance law and family dynamics. The younger son's request for his inheritance is, as Jewish scholars note, tantamount to wishing his father dead — a severe social transgression. His descent into feeding pigs represents the ultimate degradation for a Jew. The father's extravagant welcome upon his return, including the robe, ring, and feast, represents a complete restoration of status that exceeds normal expectations.

## The Elder Brother and Intra-Jewish Tensions

The elder brother's resentment (15:25–32) is often read by Christians as representing the Pharisees' rejection of Jesus's ministry to sinners. Jewish scholars suggest a more nuanced reading: the elder brother's complaint — "I have served you all these years and you never gave me a feast" — raises a legitimate question about justice and reward that Jewish tradition takes seriously. The parable does not resolve this tension but holds it open as an invitation to share in God's joy.
