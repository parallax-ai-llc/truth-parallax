## The Good Samaritan and Jewish-Samaritan Relations

The parable of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) must be understood against the background of deep Jewish-Samaritan hostility in the first century. Jewish scholars note that making a Samaritan the hero of a parable about love of neighbor was deliberately provocative — it challenged ethnic and religious prejudice within Jesus's own community. The parable does not critique the Torah itself but critiques the failure of Torah interpreters (the priest and Levite) to embody its deepest demands.

## The Lawyer's Question and Torah Interpretation

The exchange between Jesus and the lawyer (10:25–28) — "What must I do to inherit eternal life?" — takes the form of a standard rabbinic halakhic discussion. The lawyer's answer, combining Deuteronomy 6:5 (love of God) and Leviticus 19:18 (love of neighbor), demonstrates that this synthesis was already known in Jewish circles. Jesus's confirmation "Do this, and you will live" (10:28) affirms the Torah's authority. Jewish scholars argue that the parable's point is not the inadequacy of the Torah but the radical scope of the command to love one's neighbor.

## The Seventy-Two and the Nations

The sending of the seventy-two (or seventy) disciples may allude to the seventy nations of Genesis 10 or the seventy elders of Numbers 11:16–25. Jewish scholars see this as reflecting a universalist strand within Jewish tradition — the recognition that God's purposes extend beyond Israel to embrace all peoples.
