## Overview

Jewish engagement with the Gospel of Luke operates on a fundamentally different plane than Christian interpretation. Judaism does not regard Luke as Scripture, but Jewish scholars have found it valuable for understanding Second Temple Judaism, early Jewish-Christian relations, and the development of anti-Jewish polemic in early Christianity. Luke preserves Jewish prayers, customs, and theological concepts that illuminate the world of first-century Palestinian Judaism.

## Historical and Cultural Value

Luke's infancy narratives are saturated with Jewish liturgical language. The Magnificat echoes Hannah's prayer (1 Samuel 2), Zechariah's Benedictus draws on prophetic expectations, and Simeon's Nunc Dimittis reflects Jewish messianic hope. Jewish scholars note that these hymns represent authentic Jewish piety of the late Second Temple period, whatever their compositional history.

## Critical Concerns

Jewish readers are attentive to Luke's portrayal of Pharisees and Jewish leaders. While Luke is sometimes considered more sympathetic to Judaism than Matthew, the "woes" against the Pharisees (11:37–54) and the parable of the Wicked Tenants (20:9–19) have historically been used to justify anti-Jewish sentiment. Modern Jewish-Christian dialogue has prompted re-examination of these passages in their original context.

## Scholarly Contributions

Jewish scholars such as Amy-Jill Levine, Daniel Boyarin, and Géza Vermes have contributed significantly to the study of Luke by situating Jesus's teachings within the context of rabbinic Judaism, demonstrating continuities between Jesus's parables and rabbinic *meshalim*.
