## Healing, Purity, and Jewish Law

Luke 5's healing narratives raise important questions about Jewish purity law. The healing of the leper (5:12–16) is followed by Jesus's instruction to "show yourself to the priest" and make the offering "as Moses commanded" (5:14) — an affirmation of the Torah's purity regulations. Jewish scholars emphasize that Jesus does not abolish the purity system but works within it, even as he extends healing to those whom the system marginalizes.

## The Forgiveness Controversy

The healing of the paralytic (5:17–26) and Jesus's claim to forgive sins provoked controversy because, in Jewish theology, forgiveness of sins is a divine prerogative. The scribes' objection "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" (5:21) reflects mainstream Jewish theology. Jewish scholars note that this episode represents a key moment of divergence: from a Jewish perspective, a human being claiming to forgive sins exceeds the bounds of prophetic authority.

## Tax Collectors and Social Boundaries

The calling of Levi and Jesus's table fellowship with tax collectors (5:27–32) is understood by Jewish scholars in terms of the social dynamics of Roman-era Palestine. Tax collectors were despised not merely for dishonesty but for collaboration with the Roman occupier. Jesus's willingness to eat with them was a deliberate challenge to social boundaries — a stance that can be understood within the prophetic tradition of solidarity with the marginalized, even as it pushed the boundaries of conventional Jewish practice.
