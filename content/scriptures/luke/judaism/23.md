## The Trial Before Pilate and Roman Justice

Jewish scholars emphasize that the crucifixion was a Roman method of execution, carried out under Roman authority. Pilate's role in condemning Jesus — despite declaring him innocent — reflects the arbitrary exercise of Roman imperial power. Jewish scholars have worked to counter the historically devastating charge of "deicide" that was leveled against the Jewish people on the basis of the passion narratives. The Second Vatican Council's Nostra Aetate (1965) formally rejected this charge.

## The Crucifixion and Jewish Suffering

Luke's crucifixion narrative (23:26–49) has been a source of immense pain in Jewish history, as it was used for centuries to justify persecution of Jews. Jewish scholars read the text critically, noting that crucifixion was a Roman punishment, that Jesus himself was Jewish, that many Jews mourned his death (23:27, 48), and that Joseph of Arimathea — a Jewish council member — gave him honorable burial. The text itself is more nuanced than the anti-Jewish readings that were imposed upon it.

## Jesus's Words from the Cross

Jesus's prayer "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do" (23:34) and his promise to the penitent thief (23:43) are of interest to Jewish scholars as expressions of compassion and mercy consistent with Jewish values. The prayer of forgiveness echoes the tradition of righteous figures who intercede for others — Abraham, Moses, and the prophets who pleaded with God on behalf of Israel.
