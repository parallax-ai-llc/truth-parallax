## Repentance and Jewish Theology

Luke 13's call to repentance (13:1–5) is deeply consonant with the Jewish theology of teshuvah (repentance/return). The rabbinic tradition teaches that repentance is always available and that God eagerly awaits the sinner's return. The parable of the barren fig tree (13:6–9) — a gardener pleading for one more year before cutting down a fruitless tree — has parallels in rabbinic parables about God's patience with Israel.

## The Narrow Door and Jewish Eschatological Judgment

The teaching on the narrow door (13:22–30) employs imagery found in Jewish apocalyptic literature. The idea that many will seek to enter the kingdom but be unable, and that "some are last who will be first, and some are first who will be last" (13:30), resonates with Jewish teachings about eschatological reversal. The rabbis taught that the righteous of all nations have a share in the world to come, while mere ethnic identity does not guarantee salvation.

## The Lament Over Jerusalem

Jesus's lament over Jerusalem (13:34–35) stands in the tradition of prophetic grief over Israel's unfaithfulness — Jeremiah's weeping over Jerusalem being the most prominent parallel. Jewish scholars read this lament with particular poignancy given the actual destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE, recognizing in it the voice of a Jewish prophet mourning over his own people and their holy city.
