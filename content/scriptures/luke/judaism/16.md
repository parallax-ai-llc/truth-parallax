## Wealth and Poverty in Jewish Ethics

Luke 16's teachings on wealth — the shrewd manager (16:1–13) and the rich man and Lazarus (16:19–31) — stand squarely within the Jewish prophetic tradition of concern for the poor. The Torah's provisions for the poor — gleaning rights, the sabbatical year, the tithe for the needy — establish a framework of economic justice that Jesus's teachings intensify. Jewish scholars note that the critique of wealth in Luke echoes the prophetic denunciations of Amos and Isaiah.

## The Rich Man and Lazarus: A Jewish Story

The parable of the rich man and Lazarus (16:19–31) draws on Jewish traditions about the afterlife, including the concept of Gehinnom (the place of post-mortem punishment) and the "bosom of Abraham" (a place of rest for the righteous). Similar stories appear in rabbinic literature, including Egyptian Jewish texts. The name "Lazarus" (Eleazar, "God helps") evokes Jewish trust in divine providence.

## "Moses and the Prophets"

The parable's conclusion — "If they do not hear Moses and the Prophets, neither will they be convinced if someone should rise from the dead" (16:31) — is of particular interest to Jewish scholars. This statement affirms the sufficiency and authority of the Torah and the Prophets, which is precisely the Jewish position. The irony that this affirmation appears in a text that Christians read as pointing to Jesus's resurrection does not escape Jewish commentators.
