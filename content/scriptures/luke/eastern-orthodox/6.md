## The Sermon on the Plain

Orthodox interpretation of the Sermon on the Plain (6:17–49) reads it as the charter of the Christian life — the ethical content of theosis. The Beatitudes and Woes (6:20–26) describe not merely future rewards and punishments but the present spiritual reality of those who follow Christ versus those who live for worldly satisfaction. Orthodox ascetical theology sees in the Beatitudes a description of the progressive stages of spiritual growth.

## Love of Enemies and the Imitation of God

The command to love enemies (6:27–36) is foundational for Orthodox ethics. The call to "be merciful, just as your Father is merciful" (6:36) grounds ethics in the imitation of God (theomimesis) — a concept central to patristic thought. St. John Chrysostom taught that loving one's enemies is the highest expression of the divine likeness in humanity and the most powerful testimony to the reality of the gospel.

## The Two Builders

The parable of the two builders (6:46–49) warns against hearing without doing. Orthodox spirituality insists on the unity of faith and practice — theology and praxis are inseparable. The true disciple is one who both hears Christ's words and acts upon them, building the house of the soul on the rock of obedience.
