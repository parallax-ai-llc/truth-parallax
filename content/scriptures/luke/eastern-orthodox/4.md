## The Temptation and Spiritual Warfare

Orthodox interpretation of the temptation (4:1–13) draws on the ascetical tradition of the Desert Fathers. The three temptations represent the fundamental passions that the ascetic life seeks to overcome: gluttony (bread), vainglory (kingdoms), and pride (Temple). Jesus's victory over Satan through fasting, humility, and Scripture is the prototype for the monastic struggle against the passions (logismoi) that is central to Orthodox spirituality.

## The Nazareth Proclamation

Jesus's reading of Isaiah 61 in the Nazareth synagogue (4:16–21) is understood by Orthodox interpreters as the inauguration of the messianic age. The "year of the Lord's favor" is connected to the Jubilee and, more broadly, to the entire economy of salvation that Christ accomplishes. The rejection at Nazareth (4:22–30) prefigures the pattern of the prophet rejected by his own, fulfilled ultimately in the crucifixion.

## Authority Over Demons and Disease

The exorcisms and healings in Capernaum (4:31–44) demonstrate Christ's authority over the fallen spiritual powers. Orthodox theology understands these events not merely as past miracles but as revealing the ongoing cosmic battle between Christ and the demonic forces — a battle that continues through the Church's ministry of prayer, exorcism, and the sacraments.
