## The Forerunner and Repentance

Orthodox tradition venerates John the Baptist as the greatest of the prophets and the "Forerunner" (Prodromos) of Christ. His preaching of repentance (3:1–18) is understood as the prophetic preparation for the sacramental life of the Church. John's call to ethical transformation — sharing food and clothing, honest dealings — reflects the Orthodox understanding that repentance (metanoia) involves the transformation of the whole person, not merely intellectual assent.

## The Baptism and Theophany

The baptism of Jesus (3:21–22) is celebrated as the Feast of Theophany (Epiphany, January 6) and is one of the most important feasts in the Orthodox calendar. The event reveals the Holy Trinity: the Father's voice, the Son in the Jordan, and the Holy Spirit descending as a dove. Orthodox theology teaches that Christ's entry into the waters of the Jordan sanctified all creation, making water the instrument of sacramental rebirth in Holy Baptism. The Great Blessing of Waters on Theophany reflects this understanding.

## The Genealogy and Universal Salvation

Luke's genealogy (3:23–38) tracing Jesus back to Adam and God is read in Orthodox theology as affirming the universality of salvation. Christ assumes the full human nature that descends from Adam, and through his incarnation, death, and resurrection, he restores the image of God in all humanity.
