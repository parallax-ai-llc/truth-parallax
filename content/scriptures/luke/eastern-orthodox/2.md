## The Nativity of Christ

The Nativity (2:1–20) is celebrated in the Orthodox Church on December 25 (January 7 in churches following the Julian calendar) and is one of the twelve Great Feasts. Orthodox interpretation emphasizes the cosmic significance of the event: God becomes human so that humans might become divine (theosis). The cave of the Nativity, depicted in Orthodox iconography, symbolizes Christ's descent into the darkness of the fallen world to illuminate it with divine light.

## The Presentation and the Meeting of the Lord

The Presentation in the Temple (2:22–40) is celebrated as the Feast of the Meeting of the Lord (Hypapante, February 2). Simeon's Nunc Dimittis — "Now you are dismissing your servant in peace" — is prayed at every Vespers service. Orthodox theology sees in this encounter the meeting of the Old and New Testaments: the aged Simeon, representing Israel's long expectation, holds in his arms the fulfillment of all prophecy. Simeon's prophecy of the sword piercing Mary's soul is reflected in the icon of the Theotokos "Softener of Evil Hearts."

## The Boy Jesus in the Temple

The finding of Jesus in the Temple (2:41–52) reveals his divine wisdom even in childhood. Orthodox commentators, particularly Theophylact of Ohrid, note that Jesus's obedience to his earthly parents while asserting his relationship to his heavenly Father models the proper balance of human and divine that characterizes the incarnate life.
