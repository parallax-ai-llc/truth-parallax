## The Mystical Supper

Luke 22's account of the Last Supper (22:14–23) is the foundation of the Orthodox Divine Liturgy. The words of institution — "This is my body" and "This cup is the new covenant in my blood" — are the core of the eucharistic anaphora. Orthodox theology teaches the real change (metabole) of the bread and wine into the body and blood of Christ, though it avoids the Western term "transubstantiation," preferring to speak of a mystery that exceeds rational explanation.

## The Agony in the Garden

Jesus's prayer on the Mount of Olives (22:39–46) is one of the most poignant passages in Orthodox Holy Week liturgy. The detail unique to Luke — the sweat "like great drops of blood" (22:44) — reveals the full reality of Christ's human suffering. Orthodox theology insists that the Incarnation involves the assumption of the whole human nature, including the capacity for suffering and anguish. Jesus's prayer "Not my will, but yours, be done" is the supreme model of submission to divine providence.

## Betrayal and Denial

Judas's betrayal with a kiss and Peter's threefold denial are read during the Orthodox service of the Twelve Gospels on Holy Thursday evening. Orthodox hymnography meditates extensively on these scenes, contrasting Judas's despair with Peter's repentance. The faithful are reminded that the difference between Judas and Peter is not the gravity of the sin but the response to it: Judas despairs, while Peter repents and is restored.
