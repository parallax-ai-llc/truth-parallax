## Sabbath Healing and the Lord's Day

The Sabbath healing (14:1–6) is read by Orthodox interpreters as demonstrating that Christ, as Lord of the Sabbath, fulfills and transcends the Mosaic legislation. The Orthodox celebration of Sunday (the Lord's Day) as the day of resurrection reflects this understanding: the Sabbath rest finds its true meaning in the new creation inaugurated by Christ's victory over death.

## Humility and the Kingdom Feast

The teaching on choosing the lowest place (14:7–11) and inviting the poor, the crippled, the lame, and the blind (14:12–14) are foundational for Orthodox ethics. Humility (tapeinophrosyne) is considered the queen of virtues in Orthodox spirituality. The great banquet parable (14:15–24) is read as an image of the heavenly liturgy — the eschatological feast to which all are invited but which requires a response of faith and self-surrender.

## The Cost of Discipleship

The teaching on the cost of discipleship (14:25–35) — carrying the cross, renouncing possessions — is applied in Orthodox tradition particularly to monastic life, but it is understood as having universal application. Every baptized Christian is called to take up the cross daily, though the specific form this takes varies according to each person's vocation and circumstances.
