## The Parables of the Lost

Luke 15 is read in the Orthodox Church on the Sunday of the Prodigal Son, the second Sunday in the pre-Lenten Triodion. The three parables — the lost sheep, the lost coin, and the prodigal son — together reveal the heart of God's love for sinners. Orthodox hymnography for this Sunday is among the most moving in the liturgical year, expressing the soul's longing for return to the Father's house.

## The Prodigal Son and the Journey of Repentance

The parable of the prodigal son (15:11–32) is the supreme Orthodox icon of repentance. The son's journey — departure from the father, dissipation in a far country, descent to feeding pigs, the moment of self-awareness ("he came to himself"), and the return — maps the entire spiritual journey of the human soul. Orthodox Lenten hymnography draws extensively on this parable, using the phrase "I have wasted the riches entrusted to me" to express the universal human experience of spiritual squandering.

## The Father's Embrace and the Elder Brother

The father's unconditional welcome — running, embracing, kissing, robing, feasting — is understood in Orthodox theology as an image of God's prodigal mercy. The "best robe" symbolizes the restoration of the baptismal garment; the ring signifies the restoration of the divine image; the feast prefigures the eucharistic banquet. The elder brother's resentment warns against the spiritual pride that is perhaps the most dangerous obstacle to theosis.
