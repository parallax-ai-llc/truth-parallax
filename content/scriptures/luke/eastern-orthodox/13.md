## The Call to Repentance

Luke 13's call to repentance (13:1–5) is central to Orthodox Lenten spirituality. The Great Lent is a sustained period of repentance (metanoia), and Jesus's warning "unless you repent, you will all likewise perish" is echoed in the penitential prayers, prostrations, and fasting of this liturgical season. The parable of the barren fig tree (13:6–9) is read as God's patient forbearance, giving the sinner time to bear the fruit of repentance.

## The Mustard Seed and the Church

The parables of the mustard seed and leaven (13:18–21) are understood in Orthodox theology as describing the Church's growth from the small community of apostles to the universal Body of Christ. This growth is not merely numerical but organic — the leaven of the gospel transforming all aspects of human culture and society, as happened in the Christianization of the Roman and Byzantine empires.

## The Narrow Door and the Lament

The teaching on the narrow door (13:22–30) warns against the presumption that external association with Christ guarantees salvation. Orthodox ascetical theology insists that the narrow way requires genuine struggle (podvig) — prayer, fasting, repentance, and the cultivation of virtue. Jesus's lament over Jerusalem (13:34–35) is chanted in Orthodox Holy Week services as an expression of divine sorrow over human obstinacy.
