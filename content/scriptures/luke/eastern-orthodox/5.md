## The Miraculous Catch and the Call to Follow

The miraculous catch of fish and Peter's call (5:1–11) are read in Orthodox tradition as revealing the transformative power of Christ's presence. Peter's response "Depart from me, for I am a sinful man" reflects the Orthodox understanding of the encounter with holiness: the closer one draws to God, the more acutely one perceives one's own unworthiness. This is not despair but the beginning of repentance and transformation.

## Healing and the Forgiveness of Sins

The healings in Luke 5 — the leper (5:12–16) and the paralytic (5:17–26) — are understood by Orthodox interpreters as signs of Christ's power to heal both body and soul. The declaration "Your sins are forgiven" (5:20) reveals that physical healing is a visible sign of the deeper spiritual restoration that Christ accomplishes. Orthodox sacramental theology sees a continuity between these Gospel healings and the Church's ministry of Holy Unction and Confession.

## New Wine and the New Life in Christ

The calling of Levi (5:27–32) and the saying about new wine in old wineskins (5:36–39) are interpreted as signaling the newness of life in Christ. Orthodox theology emphasizes that the gospel does not simply repair the old but creates something genuinely new — a "new creation" (2 Corinthians 5:17) that is realized through baptism and the ongoing process of theosis.
