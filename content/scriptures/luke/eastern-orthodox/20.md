## The Question of Authority

The confrontation over Jesus's authority (20:1–8) is read by Orthodox interpreters as revealing the difference between institutional power and divine authority. Jesus's authority derives from his relationship with the Father, not from human appointment. Orthodox ecclesiology teaches that the authority of the Church — its bishops, councils, and traditions — is authentic only insofar as it faithfully transmits the authority of Christ himself.

## The Parable of the Wicked Tenants

The parable of the wicked tenants (20:9–19) is understood in Orthodox theology as an allegory of God's dealings with Israel and, by extension, with any community that rejects its prophetic calling. The "cornerstone" (20:17) is Christ, upon whom the entire edifice of the Church is built. Orthodox theology, however, rejects supersessionism — the idea that the Church simply replaces Israel — affirming instead a mystery of divine providence that encompasses both.

## Caesar, the Resurrection, and David's Lord

The question about taxes (20:20–26) establishes the principle that loyalty to God and participation in civil society are not inherently contradictory, though God's claims always take precedence. The Sadducees' question about the resurrection (20:27–40) elicits Jesus's affirmation that "God is not God of the dead, but of the living" — a declaration that grounds the Orthodox practice of praying for the departed and the communion of saints.
