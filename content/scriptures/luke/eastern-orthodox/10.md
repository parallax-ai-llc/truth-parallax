## The Mission of the Seventy-Two

The sending of the seventy-two (10:1–24) is read by Orthodox interpreters as establishing the pattern for the Church's missionary activity. The number seventy-two (or seventy) recalls the nations of Genesis 10, symbolizing the universal scope of the gospel. Jesus's prayer of thanksgiving (10:21–22) reveals the intimate relationship between the Father and the Son and the divine initiative in revelation — themes central to Orthodox trinitarian theology.

## The Good Samaritan

Orthodox patristic interpretation of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) is predominantly allegorical. The man who fell among robbers represents Adam (humanity) fallen from paradise. The robbers are the demons. The priest and Levite represent the Law and Prophets, which could diagnose sin but not heal it. The Samaritan is Christ himself, who binds humanity's wounds with oil (anointing/chrismation) and wine (the Eucharist), brings the wounded to the inn (the Church), and promises to return (the Parousia). This reading, found in Origen and many Church Fathers, remains standard in Orthodox homiletics.

## Mary and Martha

The Mary and Martha episode (10:38–42) is central to the Orthodox theology of contemplation. Mary, sitting at Jesus's feet, represents the hesychastic tradition of silent prayer and attentiveness to the divine Word. Martha's distraction with "many things" represents the danger of allowing even good works to displace the "one thing necessary" — communion with God through prayer.
