## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Luke is rooted in the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. The Orthodox approach reads Luke not primarily as a historical document but as a living text proclaimed in the Divine Liturgy, illuminated by the Holy Spirit, and interpreted through the consensus of the Church Fathers. Luke's emphasis on prayer, the Holy Spirit, and the Theotokos resonates profoundly with Orthodox spirituality.

## Patristic Foundations

The great Church Fathers — Cyril of Alexandria, John Chrysostom, Ambrose of Milan, and Theophylact of Ohrid — produced extensive commentaries on Luke. Orthodox interpretation favors a typological and spiritual reading that sees Old Testament patterns fulfilled in Christ while also discerning the deeper mystical sense of the text.

## Theotokos and Liturgy

Luke's infancy narratives are central to Orthodox Mariology and hymnography. The Theotokos hymns of the Orthodox liturgical cycle draw heavily on Luke 1–2. The Magnificat is sung at Matins. Icons of the Annunciation, the Nativity, and the Presentation are among the most venerated in the Orthodox tradition, each grounded in Luke's account.

## Theosis and the Holy Spirit

Orthodox readers emphasize Luke's portrayal of the Holy Spirit as the agent of transformation — from the Spirit's role in the incarnation to the promise of Pentecost. This connects to the Orthodox doctrine of theosis (divinization): humanity's calling to participate in the divine nature through the Spirit's work.
