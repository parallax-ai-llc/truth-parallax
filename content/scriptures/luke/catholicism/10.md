## The Seventy-Two and the Universal Mission

The sending of the seventy-two (10:1–24) is interpreted in Catholic tradition as prefiguring the Church's universal mission. While the Twelve represent the apostolic college (the bishops), the seventy-two represent the broader participation of all the baptized in evangelization. This passage has been foundational for Catholic missiology, from the early Church's expansion to the modern missionary movement.

## The Good Samaritan

The parable of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) is one of the most frequently cited texts in Catholic social teaching. The Samaritan's care for the wounded man — binding his wounds, bringing him to an inn, paying for his care — is the model for Catholic charitable institutions: hospitals, hospices, and social services. Church Fathers such as Ambrose and Augustine also read the parable allegorically: the wounded man is fallen humanity, the Samaritan is Christ, and the inn is the Church where healing occurs through the sacraments.

## Mary and Martha

Catholic interpretation of the Mary and Martha episode (10:38–42) has shaped the theology of the contemplative and active religious life. Mary represents the contemplative vocation — prayer, adoration, lectio divina — while Martha represents the active vocation of service. Both are honored, but Jesus's affirmation that Mary has chosen "the better part" has traditionally supported the primacy of contemplation in Catholic spirituality.
