## Repentance and Divine Patience

Catholic interpretation of Luke 13 emphasizes the universal call to repentance (13:1–5). The parable of the barren fig tree (13:6–9) is read as an image of God's patience: the vinedresser (often identified with Christ as intercessor) pleads for one more year before the tree is cut down. This reflects the Catholic understanding of the relationship between divine justice and divine mercy — God's patience creates space for conversion, but the opportunity is not unlimited.

## The Kingdom's Growth

The parables of the mustard seed and the leaven (13:18–21) describe the Church's growth from small beginnings to a universal reality. Catholic ecclesiology sees in these parables a description of the Church's historical expansion from a small Palestinian movement to a global institution that encompasses diverse peoples and cultures.

## The Narrow Door and Lament Over Jerusalem

The teaching on the narrow door (13:22–30) warns against presumption — not everyone who claims familiarity with Jesus will be saved. Catholic interpretation emphasizes the necessity of cooperating with grace through faith, sacraments, and moral living. Jesus's lament over Jerusalem (13:34–35) expresses the divine desire for universal salvation and the sorrow occasioned by human refusal.
