## The Centurion's Faith

The centurion's words "Lord, I am not worthy to have you come under my roof" (7:6) are incorporated directly into the Roman Rite of the Mass, spoken by the assembly before receiving Communion. Catholic interpretation sees the centurion as a model of eucharistic piety — humble recognition of unworthiness combined with confident faith in Christ's healing power. The centurion's faith, exercised outside the boundaries of Israel, also prefigures the universality of the Church.

## The Raising of the Widow's Son

The raising of the widow's son at Nain (7:11–17) is read by Catholic interpreters as a sign of Christ's power over death, anticipating his own resurrection. Jesus's compassion for the widowed mother reflects the divine mercy that the Church is called to embody in its ministry to the bereaved and the suffering. The crowd's response — "God has visited his people!" — echoes the Benedictus and affirms the incarnation as God's definitive visitation.

## The Anointing by the Sinful Woman

The anointing of Jesus's feet by the sinful woman (7:36–50) is a paradigm of repentance and forgiveness in Catholic pastoral theology. Her tears, her kiss, and her ointment express the lavish love that flows from the experience of being forgiven. Jesus's parable of the two debtors teaches that the depth of love is proportional to the magnitude of forgiveness received — a principle that undergirds the Sacrament of Reconciliation.
