## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Luke is deeply informed by the Magisterium, the Church Fathers, and the liturgical tradition. Luke's Gospel holds a special place in Catholic devotion because of its extensive treatment of Mary, its sacramental imagery, and its emphasis on God's mercy toward the poor and marginalized — themes that have shaped Catholic social teaching.

## Marian Significance

Luke's infancy narrative is the primary biblical source for Catholic Marian theology. The Annunciation (1:26–38) grounds the doctrine of Mary's fiat and her role as Theotokos. The Magnificat (1:46–55) is prayed daily in the Liturgy of the Hours (Vespers). The Visitation, the Presentation in the Temple, and the finding of the boy Jesus are all incorporated into the Rosary.

## Sacramental and Liturgical Themes

Catholic readers find sacramental resonance throughout Luke: the meal scenes anticipate the Eucharist, the commissioning of the Twelve and the Seventy-Two prefigures apostolic ministry, and the post-resurrection breaking of bread at Emmaus (24:30–31) is read as a eucharistic epiphany. Luke's emphasis on forgiveness connects to the Sacrament of Reconciliation.

## Social Teaching

Luke's Beatitudes ("Blessed are the poor") and the parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus have been foundational texts for Catholic social doctrine, from the encyclicals of Leo XIII to Pope Francis's emphasis on a "Church of the poor."
