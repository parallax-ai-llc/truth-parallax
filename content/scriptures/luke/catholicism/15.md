## The Parables of Divine Mercy

Luke 15 is the heart of the Gospel for Catholic preaching on mercy and reconciliation. The three parables — the lost sheep (15:3–7), the lost coin (15:8–10), and the prodigal son (15:11–32) — together constitute what has been called the "Gospel of Mercy." Pope Francis made these parables central to the Jubilee Year of Mercy (2015–2016), and they remain among the most frequently cited texts in Catholic homiletics.

## The Prodigal Son and the Sacrament of Reconciliation

The parable of the prodigal son is the primary biblical image for the Sacrament of Reconciliation (Confession). The son's journey — sin, degradation, self-recognition, return, and the father's embrace — mirrors the sacramental process of examination of conscience, contrition, confession, and absolution. The father's response — running to meet the son, kissing him, clothing him with the best robe, and celebrating with a feast — reveals the extravagance of divine mercy that exceeds all human expectation.

## The Elder Brother

The elder brother (15:25–32) represents those within the community of faith who resent God's mercy toward sinners. Catholic interpreters read this figure as a warning against the spiritual pride that can afflict the devout, and as an invitation to share in the Father's joy over every repentant sinner.
