## The Sermon on the Plain

Catholic interpretation of the Sermon on the Plain (6:17–49) reads it alongside Matthew's Sermon on the Mount as a comprehensive charter of Christian discipleship. The Beatitudes and Woes in Luke's version are understood as describing not merely future eschatological realities but present ethical demands that shape the life of the baptized. The Catholic social tradition has drawn extensively on Luke's Beatitudes — "Blessed are you who are poor" — in formulating the preferential option for the poor.

## The Law of Love

The command to love enemies (6:27–36) is understood in Catholic moral theology as the perfection of the natural law and the fulfillment of the Torah's love commandments. The golden rule (6:31) is cited in the Catechism as a universal moral principle accessible to reason as well as revelation. The call to "be merciful, just as your Father is merciful" (6:36) has been a keynote of Pope Francis's pontificate, particularly during the Jubilee Year of Mercy.

## Hearing and Doing

The parable of the two builders (6:46–49) reinforces the Catholic emphasis on the inseparability of faith and works. Catholic interpreters stress that hearing Jesus's words without doing them is insufficient — salvation requires active obedience empowered by grace, expressed through the sacramental life and works of charity.
