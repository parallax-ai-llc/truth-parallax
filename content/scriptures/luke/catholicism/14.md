## Sabbath Healing and Humility

The healing of the man with dropsy on the Sabbath (14:1–6) continues Luke's theme of Jesus's authority to interpret the Law according to its deepest intention — mercy. Catholic moral theology draws on such passages to affirm the principle that the law exists to serve human well-being, not to impose arbitrary burdens. The teaching on humility at banquets (14:7–14) is understood as both practical wisdom and a parable of the kingdom, where the last are first.

## The Great Banquet

The parable of the great banquet (14:15–24) is interpreted in Catholic theology as an image of the Eucharist and the heavenly feast. The excuses offered by the invited guests represent the distractions of worldly concerns that prevent people from responding to God's invitation. The command to bring in the poor, the crippled, the blind, and the lame from the streets reflects the Church's mission to include the marginalized.

## The Cost of Discipleship

Jesus's teaching on counting the cost (14:25–35) is foundational for Catholic religious life. The call to "hate" family and "carry the cross" is understood not as literal hatred but as the radical prioritization of Christ that characterizes consecrated life — the vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience that religious orders profess.
