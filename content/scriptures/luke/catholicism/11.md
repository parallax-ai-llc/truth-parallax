## The Lord's Prayer

Catholic interpretation of the Lord's Prayer (11:1–4) is shaped by its liturgical use in the Mass and the Divine Office. The Catechism of the Catholic Church devotes an entire section to this prayer, calling it "the summary of the whole Gospel." Each petition is expounded in relation to Catholic doctrine: "hallowed be your name" as worship, "your kingdom come" as eschatological hope, "give us each day our daily bread" as both material sustenance and eucharistic nourishment.

## Persistence in Prayer and the Holy Spirit

The parables of the friend at midnight (11:5–8) and the good father (11:11–13) teach persistence and confidence in prayer. Catholic spirituality has a rich tradition of intercessory prayer, including prayer to the saints, which finds support in Luke's teaching that God responds generously to those who ask. The promise that the Father will "give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him" (11:13) is connected to the Sacrament of Confirmation and the charismatic gifts.

## The Woes

The woes against the Pharisees and lawyers (11:37–54) are read by Catholic interpreters as warnings against formalism and hypocrisy that apply to the Church in every age. The critique is directed not at the Torah itself but at those who burden others with rules while neglecting justice and the love of God — a principle that informs ongoing Catholic reform and pastoral discernment.
