## Women Disciples and the Parable of the Sower

Luke 8 opens with the mention of women who accompanied Jesus and provided for his ministry (8:1–3) — Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Susanna, and many others. Catholic tradition honors these women as models of discipleship and generous service. The parable of the sower (8:4–15) is interpreted in Catholic preaching as calling believers to prepare the soil of their hearts through prayer, the sacraments, and ascetical practice so that the Word may bear fruit.

## The Storm and the Gerasene Demoniac

The calming of the storm (8:22–25) is read typologically as Christ's power to still the storms that assail the Church — the barque of Peter — through the ages. The exorcism of the Gerasene demoniac (8:26–39) demonstrates Christ's authority over demonic powers, a theme that informs the Catholic practice of exorcism and the broader theology of spiritual warfare.

## Jairus's Daughter and the Hemorrhaging Woman

The intertwined narratives of Jairus's daughter and the woman with a hemorrhage (8:40–56) illustrate the Catholic understanding of faith as both personal encounter and communal mediation. The woman touches Jesus's garment and is healed — Catholic piety finds here a parallel to the veneration of relics and sacramentals, through which God's power operates through material means. The raising of Jairus's daughter prefigures the general resurrection.
