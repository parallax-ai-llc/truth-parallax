## The Temptation

Catholic interpretation of the temptation (4:1–13) emphasizes Jesus as the New Adam who succeeds where the first Adam failed. The three temptations correspond to the three traditional categories of sin: the lust of the flesh (bread), the lust of the eyes (kingdoms), and the pride of life (Temple pinnacle). Catholic moral theology draws on this passage to teach about the nature of temptation and the use of Scripture and prayer as spiritual weapons.

## The Nazareth Proclamation

Jesus's reading of Isaiah 61 in the Nazareth synagogue (4:16–21) is understood in Catholic tradition as a programmatic declaration of his messianic mission. The "year of the Lord's favor" is connected to the Jubilee tradition, which has inspired Catholic social teaching on debt relief, economic justice, and the preferential option for the poor. Pope Francis frequently invokes this passage in his teaching on mercy.

## Ministry in Capernaum

The exorcisms and healings in Capernaum (4:31–44) demonstrate Christ's power over evil and disease. Catholic interpretation sees these acts not merely as displays of power but as signs of the kingdom inaugurated by Christ — the same power that continues to operate through the sacraments and the ministry of the Church.
