## Humility and Tawadu

The teachings on humility in Luke 14 — choosing the lowest seat, inviting the poor — resonate with the Islamic virtue of tawadu (humility). The Prophet Muhammad said: "No one who has the weight of a mustard seed of arrogance in his heart will enter Paradise" (Sahih Muslim). Islamic scholars find in Isa's teaching a prophetic emphasis on humility before God and service to the vulnerable that is fully consistent with Islamic ethics.

## The Great Banquet and Divine Invitation

The parable of the great banquet (14:15–24) is read by Islamic scholars as illustrating the human tendency to refuse divine guidance. The Quran repeatedly describes how communities reject their prophets: "How many a prophet We sent among the earlier peoples, but there never came a prophet to them without their mocking him" (Surah 43:6–7). The inclusion of outsiders when the originally invited refuse echoes the Islamic understanding that God's guidance is offered to all and that those who reject it lose their privilege.

## The Cost of Following a Prophet

The teaching on the cost of discipleship (14:25–35) resonates with the Islamic concept of sacrifice in the path of Allah (fi sabilillah). Following a prophet requires total commitment, willingness to endure hardship, and readiness to prioritize divine obedience above worldly attachments. The Quran teaches: "You will never attain righteousness until you spend of what you love" (Surah 3:92).
