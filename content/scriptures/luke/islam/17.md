## Faith and Gratitude

The healing of the ten lepers (17:11–19) illustrates the Islamic virtue of shukr (gratitude). The Quran states: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase My blessings upon you; but if you are ungrateful, My punishment is severe" (Surah 14:7). The Samaritan leper who returns to give thanks models the proper response to divine blessing. Islamic scholars note that ingratitude (kufr al-ni'mah) is a form of spiritual blindness that prophetic teaching consistently addresses.

## The Kingdom of God and Islamic Eschatology

Jesus's teaching about the coming of the kingdom (17:20–37) intersects with Islamic eschatology at several points. The warnings about the days of Noah and Lot (17:26–30) are familiar in the Quran, which recounts these narratives as examples of divine judgment upon disobedient communities. The suddenness of the end — "one will be taken and the other left" (17:34–35) — parallels the Quranic teaching that the Hour (al-Sa'ah) will come suddenly, when people least expect it.

## Isa and the End Times

Islamic tradition assigns Isa a unique role in eschatology: he will return before the Day of Judgment, defeat the Dajjal (Antichrist), and establish justice on earth. While Luke 17 does not describe this role explicitly, Muslim scholars read Jesus's eschatological teachings through the lens of Islamic traditions about his second coming — one of the few points where Islam affirms a future role for Isa that goes beyond his original prophetic mission.
