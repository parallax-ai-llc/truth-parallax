## The Temptation and Iblis

The temptation narrative (4:1–13) resonates with the Islamic account of Iblis (Satan) and his role as the tempter of humanity. In Islamic theology, prophets are protected from sin (ismah), which means that while Isa may have been tested, he could not have succumbed to temptation. Muslim scholars read this narrative as confirming Isa's prophetic integrity — he resists Satan through recourse to divine revelation, as any true prophet would.

## The Nazareth Declaration

Jesus's reading of Isaiah 61 and declaration of his mission (4:16–21) is understood by Islamic scholars as consistent with the prophetic vocation: bringing good news, proclaiming liberation, and healing. These are functions that Islam attributes to all prophets. However, Islamic interpretation rejects any reading that equates this prophetic mission with divine identity. Isa is a messenger of Allah (rasul), not Allah incarnate.

## Healing and Prophetic Signs

The healings and exorcisms in Capernaum (4:31–44) are acknowledged in Islamic tradition as authentic prophetic signs (mu'jizat). The Quran affirms that Isa healed the blind and the lepers and raised the dead "by Allah's leave" (Surah 3:49, 5:110). The qualifying phrase "by Allah's leave" is crucial: Isa's miracles are not evidence of divinity but of divine empowerment, granted to him as they were to other prophets.
