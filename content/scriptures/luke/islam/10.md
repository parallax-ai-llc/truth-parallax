## The Mission of the Seventy-Two

The sending of the seventy-two (10:1–24) is read by Islamic scholars as the expansion of Isa's prophetic mission. The instructions — travel light, depend on God's provision, proclaim the kingdom — are consistent with the prophetic lifestyle that Islam commends. The rejection faced by some missionaries parallels the Quranic teaching that prophets are frequently rejected by their communities (Surah 36:30).

## The Good Samaritan and Islamic Ethics

The parable of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) resonates powerfully with Islamic ethical teaching. The obligation to help those in need, regardless of their identity, is a fundamental Islamic principle. The Quran teaches: "Whoever saves a life, it is as if they have saved all of humanity" (Surah 5:32). The parable's critique of religious leaders who pass by a suffering person aligns with the Quranic warning against hypocrisy (nifaq) — outward religiosity that lacks inner compassion.

## Mary and Martha

The Mary and Martha episode (10:38–42) does not have a Quranic parallel, but the tension between contemplation and action resonates with Islamic discussions about the balance between dhikr (remembrance of God) and khidmah (service). Islamic spirituality, particularly in the Sufi tradition, values both active service and contemplative devotion, recognizing that the inner life of prayer must animate all outward action.
