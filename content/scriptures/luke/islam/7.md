## The Centurion's Faith

The healing of the centurion's servant (7:1–10) is read by Islamic scholars as demonstrating the universal scope of prophetic ministry. Isa's willingness to heal a Roman soldier's servant shows that prophetic compassion extends beyond ethnic and religious boundaries — a principle affirmed in the Quran, which describes Muhammad as "a mercy to all the worlds" (Surah 21:107). The centurion's faith in Isa's authority is understood as faith in Allah's power working through the prophet.

## Raising the Dead

The raising of the widow's son at Nain (7:11–17) corresponds directly to the Quranic affirmation that Isa raised the dead "by Allah's leave" (Surah 3:49, 5:110). Islamic tradition accepts this miracle as one of the signs given to Isa but insists that the power to give life and death belongs exclusively to Allah. Isa is the instrument, not the source, of this life-giving power.

## The Anointing Woman

The anointing by the sinful woman (7:36–50) does not have a direct Quranic parallel, but the themes of repentance and divine forgiveness resonate with Islamic teaching. The concept of tawbah (repentance) is central to Islam, and the Quran affirms that "Allah loves those who turn to Him in repentance" (Surah 2:222). However, Isa's declaration "Your sins are forgiven" (7:48) would be interpreted by Muslim scholars as conveying Allah's forgiveness, not exercising an independent authority to forgive.
