## Overview

Islam does not accept the Gospel of Luke as preserved, uncorrupted Scripture, but recognizes that the original Injil (Gospel) revealed to Isa (Jesus) was a genuine divine revelation. Islamic engagement with Luke focuses on areas of convergence and divergence with the Quran, particularly regarding the birth of Isa, the nature of his mission, and the rejection of his crucifixion and divinity.

## The Birth of Isa and Maryam

Luke's infancy narrative has the most extensive parallels with Quranic accounts of any Gospel. The annunciation to Maryam (Surah 3:42–47, 19:16–22) closely parallels Luke 1:26–38. Both traditions affirm the virgin birth, the angelic announcement, and Maryam's exemplary piety. However, Islamic tradition insists that Isa is a prophet and servant of Allah, not divine — a fundamental divergence from Luke's Christology.

## Prophetic Ministry

Islamic readers recognize many of Isa's teachings in Luke as consistent with prophetic ethics: care for the poor, prayer, humility, and submission to God's will. The emphasis on monotheism (tawhid) leads Muslim scholars to read Luke's "Son of God" language as later theological addition rather than authentic prophetic speech. Isa's miracles — healing the sick, raising the dead — are acknowledged in Islamic tradition as signs granted by Allah.

## The Crucifixion Question

The most significant divergence concerns Luke 23. The Quran states that Isa was not crucified but that "it was made to appear so" (Surah 4:157). Islamic interpreters therefore regard Luke's passion narrative as reflecting a theological misunderstanding that entered the Christian tradition.
