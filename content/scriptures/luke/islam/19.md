## Zacchaeus and Sincere Repentance

The story of Zacchaeus (19:1–10) is valued by Islamic scholars as an illustration of sincere tawbah (repentance) and its practical consequences. Zacchaeus's restitution — returning fourfold what he has taken unjustly — exemplifies the Islamic principle that repentance must include righting wrongs where possible. The Prophet Muhammad taught that one of the conditions of valid repentance is making amends to those who have been wronged.

## The Temple and Prophetic Reform

The cleansing of the Temple (19:45–48) is understood in Islamic perspective as a prophetic act of purification. All prophets in Islamic tradition confront corruption in religious institutions. Muhammad himself cleansed the Ka'bah of idols upon the conquest of Mecca — a parallel that Islamic scholars draw with Isa's Temple action. Both acts represent the prophetic restoration of pure monotheistic worship against institutional corruption.

## Jesus's Weeping Over Jerusalem

Jesus's weeping over Jerusalem (19:41–44) is read by Islamic scholars as the grief of a prophet whose people refuse his message. The Quran describes similar prophetic grief: "Perhaps you would kill yourself with grief that they are not believers" (Surah 26:3). The prophetic vocation includes sorrow over human resistance to divine guidance, and Isa's tears over Jerusalem express this universal prophetic experience.
