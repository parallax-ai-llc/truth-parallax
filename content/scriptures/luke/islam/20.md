## The Question of Authority

The challenge to Jesus's authority (20:1–8) mirrors the challenges faced by all prophets in Islamic tradition. The Quran describes how communities demanded credentials from their prophets: "Why has no angel been sent down to him?" (Surah 6:8). Jesus's counter-question demonstrates the prophetic skill of exposing the questioners' insincerity — a technique also employed by Muhammad in dealing with the Quraysh.

## The Parable of the Tenants

The parable of the wicked tenants (20:9–19) resonates with the Quranic theme of communities rejecting their prophets. The Quran states: "Every time a messenger came to them with what their souls did not desire, some they denied and some they killed" (Surah 5:70). Islamic scholars read this parable as confirming the prophetic pattern of rejection — but they do not accept the Christian interpretation that the "son" who is killed is the divine Son of God. Instead, Isa is one in a long line of rejected prophets.

## Render to Caesar

The question about taxation (20:20–26) and Jesus's response are understood by Islamic scholars within the framework of Islamic political theology. Islam, like Jesus's teaching, recognizes legitimate temporal authority while insisting on the absolute sovereignty of God. The Quran teaches: "O you who believe, obey Allah and obey the Messenger and those in authority among you" (Surah 4:59) — but human authority is always subordinate to divine law.
