## The Last Supper in Islamic Perspective

Luke 22's Last Supper narrative (22:14–23) does not have a direct Quranic parallel, though Surah 5:112–115 describes a heavenly table (ma'idah) that Isa requests from Allah for his disciples. Islamic scholars see the Last Supper as a final gathering of Isa with his followers before the crisis, but they reject the Christian sacramental interpretation of the bread and wine as the body and blood of Christ — such an interpretation is incompatible with Islamic theology.

## The Betrayal and Arrest

The betrayal by Judas (22:47–53) and the arrest are recognized in Islamic tradition as part of the plot against Isa. However, Islamic theology diverges sharply from the Gospel narrative at this critical juncture. The Quran states: "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them" (Surah 4:157). Most Islamic scholars interpret this to mean that Allah rescued Isa and substituted another person in his place — often identified as Judas himself or Simon of Cyrene.

## Peter's Denial

Peter's denial (22:54–62) is understood by Islamic scholars as illustrating the weakness of even the most devoted followers under pressure. This narrative is consistent with the Islamic observation that the early Christian community was fragmented and confused by the events surrounding Isa's apparent departure — a confusion that, in the Islamic view, contributed to the theological errors that later developed in Christianity.
