## Yahya (John the Baptist) in Islamic Tradition

Luke 3's account of John the Baptist's ministry corresponds to the Quranic portrayal of Yahya ibn Zakariya. The Quran praises Yahya as "noble, chaste, and a prophet from among the righteous" (Surah 3:39). His call to repentance and ethical conduct aligns with the Islamic understanding of prophetic ministry: all prophets call humanity to submission to God (islam) and righteous living. Islamic scholars read John's preaching as consistent with the universal prophetic message.

## The Baptism of Isa

The baptism of Jesus (3:21–22) presents theological difficulties for Islamic interpretation. The descent of the Holy Spirit and the voice declaring "You are my beloved Son" are understood by Muslim scholars as later theological embellishments reflecting the Christian doctrine of the Trinity — a doctrine the Quran explicitly rejects (Surah 4:171, 5:73). Islamic interpretation holds that the original event may have involved a divine affirmation of Isa's prophetic mission, but not a declaration of divine sonship.

## Genealogy and Prophetic Lineage

Luke's genealogy (3:23–38) is of interest to Islamic scholars insofar as it establishes Isa's connection to earlier prophets recognized in Islam — Ibrahim (Abraham), Ishaq (Isaac), Yaqub (Jacob), Dawud (David), and ultimately Adam. Islam affirms a continuous chain of prophethood, and Isa's place within that chain is important. However, the claim that this genealogy traces back to "God" (3:38) is understood as affirming God as Creator, not as establishing literal divine paternity.
