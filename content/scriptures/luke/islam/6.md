## The Sermon on the Plain and Prophetic Ethics

Islamic scholars find much in the Sermon on the Plain (6:17–49) that is consistent with Islamic ethical teaching. The Beatitudes' concern for the poor, the hungry, and the persecuted aligns with the Quran's emphasis on social justice, care for orphans, and equitable distribution of wealth. The Islamic tradition of zakat (obligatory charity) and sadaqah (voluntary giving) reflects similar values.

## Love of Enemies and Islamic Ethics

The command to love enemies (6:27–36) is admired by many Muslim scholars as expressing a high ethical ideal. While Islamic ethics places greater emphasis on justice ('adl) than on unconditional love of enemies, the Quran does teach: "Repel evil with what is better, and the one who was your enemy may become your close friend" (Surah 41:34). The ethic of mercy (rahmah) is central to Islam — every surah of the Quran (except one) begins with "In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate."

## Prophetic Authority and Ethical Teaching

Islamic scholars read the Sermon on the Plain as evidence that Isa was a great ethical teacher — one of the "messengers of strong will" (ulu al-azm) mentioned in the Quran. His ethical teachings, insofar as they are consistent with the Quran, are regarded as authentic expressions of the original Injil. Where they diverge from Islamic teaching — particularly claims of divine authority — Muslim scholars attribute the divergence to later Christian editing.
