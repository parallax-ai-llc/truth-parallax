## The Parables of Mercy

Luke 15's three parables of the lost are among the Gospel texts most appreciated by Muslim scholars for their emphasis on divine mercy (rahmah). The image of God actively seeking the lost resonates with the Quranic teaching that Allah's mercy encompasses all things (Surah 7:156). The hadith qudsi (sacred tradition) records Allah saying: "My mercy prevails over my wrath" — a sentiment that these parables powerfully illustrate.

## The Prodigal Son and Return to God

The parable of the prodigal son (15:11–32) is widely appreciated in Islamic tradition. The son's departure, degradation, self-recognition, and return mirror the Quranic pattern of human forgetfulness (ghaflah), the consequences of sin, and the possibility of return (tawbah) to a merciful God. Islamic scholars have noted that this parable, stripped of its specifically Christian theological context, presents a universally compelling picture of divine mercy. Several hadith traditions attribute similar parables to Isa.

## The Joy of Heaven

The recurring refrain "there will be joy in heaven over one sinner who repents" (15:7, 10) aligns with the Quranic teaching that Allah rejoices over the repentance of His servants. A well-known hadith states: "Allah is more delighted with the repentance of His servant than a person who finds his lost camel in the desert." Islamic scholars see in Luke 15 an authentic expression of the prophetic message of divine mercy that Isa was sent to proclaim.
