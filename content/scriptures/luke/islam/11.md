## Prayer in Islamic Perspective

Luke 11's teaching on prayer (11:1–13) resonates with the Islamic emphasis on salah (the five daily prayers) and du'a (supplicatory prayer). The Lord's Prayer's address to God as Father is, however, problematic for Islamic theology, which prefers titles emphasizing God's transcendence — Rabb (Lord), Rahman (Most Merciful), Rahim (Most Compassionate). Islamic scholars would understand the original prayer of Isa as a monotheistic petition consistent with prophetic practice, while viewing the "Father" language as a later Christian development.

## The Beelzebul Controversy

The Beelzebul controversy (11:14–26) and Isa's authority over demonic forces are understood within the Islamic framework of jinn and prophetic authority. The Quran affirms the existence of jinn and shayatin (devils) and attributes to certain prophets authority over them. Isa's exorcisms are legitimate prophetic signs, and his argument that he casts out demons by divine power (not by Satan) is consistent with Islamic theological reasoning.

## The Woes and Prophetic Critique

The woes against the Pharisees (11:37–54) are read by Islamic scholars as exemplifying the prophetic function of critiquing religious hypocrisy. The Quran contains similar critiques of those who distort religion for personal gain or burden others with rules while neglecting justice and mercy. This pattern of prophetic critique is universal in Islamic understanding — every prophet confronted corruption within his community.
