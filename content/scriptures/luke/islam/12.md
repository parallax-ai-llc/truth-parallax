## Trust in Allah's Providence

Jesus's teaching on divine providence (12:22–34) — "Consider the ravens... consider the lilies" — resonates deeply with the Islamic concept of tawakkul (trust in Allah). The Quran teaches: "And whoever puts their trust in Allah, He is sufficient for them" (Surah 65:3). Islamic scholars find in these teachings an authentic prophetic emphasis on detachment from worldly concerns and reliance on God's provision.

## The Rich Fool and Worldly Attachment

The parable of the rich fool (12:13–21) aligns with the Quranic warning against the deception of worldly wealth (dunya). The Quran states: "The life of this world is nothing but an illusory enjoyment" (Surah 3:185). Islamic tradition is rich with teachings on the dangers of materialism, and the hadith literature contains sayings attributed to Isa that echo this Lukan teaching — "The world is a bridge; pass over it, but do not build upon it."

## Accountability and the Day of Judgment

The parables of watchfulness (12:35–48) resonate with the Islamic emphasis on accountability before God on the Day of Judgment (Yawm al-Qiyamah). The Quran teaches that every person will be held accountable for their deeds, and the concept of divine reckoning (hisab) is central to Islamic eschatology. Islamic scholars see in these parables an authentic prophetic warning consistent with the message of all Allah's messengers.
