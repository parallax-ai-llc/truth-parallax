## The Mission of the Disciples

The sending of the Twelve (9:1–6) is understood in Islamic perspective as the delegation of prophetic authority to followers — a pattern seen in the mission of the hawariyyun (disciples) described in the Quran. The Quran honors those who supported Isa's mission: "O you who believe, be helpers of Allah, as Isa son of Maryam said to the disciples" (Surah 61:14).

## The Feeding of the Five Thousand

The miraculous feeding (9:10–17) has a Quranic parallel in the account of the disciples asking Isa to bring down a table spread from heaven (Surah 5:112–115). While the details differ, both traditions affirm that Isa performed miracles of provision by Allah's power. Islamic scholars read these accounts as signs confirming Isa's prophetic mission rather than as evidence of divinity.

## The Transfiguration

The Transfiguration (9:28–36) presents significant interpretive challenges for Islamic readers. The glorification of Isa and the divine voice declaring him "my Son, my Chosen One" are understood as later theological embellishments reflecting Christian Christology. Islamic scholars would accept that Isa may have experienced a spiritual elevation or divine encounter on the mountain — prophets regularly receive revelation in solitary retreat — but reject the Trinitarian interpretation that Christianity has assigned to this event.
