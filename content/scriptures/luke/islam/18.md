## Persistent Prayer and Islamic Devotion

The parable of the persistent widow (18:1–8) resonates with the Islamic emphasis on consistent, devoted prayer. The five daily prayers (salah) and the practice of du'a (supplication) embody the persistence that Jesus commends. The Quran instructs: "And seek help through patience and prayer" (Surah 2:45). Islamic scholars see in this parable an affirmation of the prophetic teaching that prayer must be constant and trusting, not sporadic or discouraged.

## The Pharisee and the Tax Collector

The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector (18:9–14) powerfully illustrates the Islamic teaching against riya (ostentation in worship) and kibr (arrogance). The Quran warns: "Do not turn your cheek with contempt toward people" (Surah 31:18). The tax collector's humble prayer — "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" — models the Islamic attitude of 'ubudiyyah (servanthood before God) that should characterize all worship.

## The Rich Ruler

The encounter with the rich ruler (18:18–30) reinforces the prophetic warning against attachment to wealth. The Quran teaches that wealth is a test (fitnah): "Your wealth and your children are but a trial, and Allah has a great reward" (Surah 64:15). Islamic scholars read Isa's demand to sell everything and follow him as an expression of the radical detachment (zuhd) from worldly goods that characterizes the prophetic life.
