## Overview

The Gospel of Luke is the third book of the New Testament and the longest of the four Gospels. Traditionally attributed to Luke the physician, a companion of the Apostle Paul, it presents a carefully ordered account of the life, ministry, death, and resurrection of Jesus of Nazareth. Luke is also the first half of a two-volume work, with the Acts of the Apostles as its sequel.

## Structure

Luke's Gospel can be divided into major sections:

1. **Prologue and Birth Narratives** (Chapters 1–2): The births of John the Baptist and Jesus
2. **Preparation for Ministry** (Chapters 3–4): Baptism, genealogy, and temptation
3. **Galilean Ministry** (Chapters 5–9): Teaching, healing, and calling disciples
4. **Travel Narrative** (Chapters 9–19): The journey to Jerusalem, unique parables and teachings
5. **Jerusalem Ministry** (Chapters 19–21): Teaching in the Temple, eschatological discourse
6. **Passion and Resurrection** (Chapters 22–24): Last Supper, crucifixion, resurrection, ascension

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Luke holds significance across multiple religious traditions:

- **Christianity**: A foundational Gospel text emphasizing Jesus's compassion for the marginalized
- **Judaism**: Contains extensive engagement with Jewish Scripture, liturgy, and messianic expectation
- **Islam**: Jesus (Isa) and Mary (Maryam) are honored figures; birth narratives have Quranic parallels
- **Gnosticism**: Certain sayings and parables were reinterpreted within Gnostic frameworks

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Universal Salvation | God's saving purposes extend to all peoples — Gentiles, Samaritans, sinners |
| The Holy Spirit | The Spirit's activity pervades the narrative from the annunciation onward |
| Prayer | Jesus is depicted praying at every major turning point |
| Reversal | The rich are humbled, the poor are exalted (Magnificat, Beatitudes) |
| Women | Women play prominent roles as witnesses, disciples, and exemplars of faith |
| Outcasts | Tax collectors, sinners, Samaritans, and the poor receive special attention |
