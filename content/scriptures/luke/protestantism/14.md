## Sabbath Healing and Table Fellowship

Luke 14 opens with Jesus healing a man with dropsy on the Sabbath (14:1–6), once again challenging rigid Sabbath interpretation. Protestant commentators see Jesus asserting his lordship over the Sabbath and demonstrating that mercy takes precedence over ceremonial observance. The subsequent teaching on choosing the lowest seat at a banquet (14:7–11) illustrates the kingdom principle that "everyone who exalts himself will be humbled."

## The Parable of the Great Banquet

The parable of the great banquet (14:15–24) is a powerful text for Protestant soteriology. Those originally invited make excuses and refuse to come; the host then sends servants to bring in "the poor and crippled and blind and lame" from the streets and lanes. Protestant interpreters read this as depicting Israel's rejection of the Messiah and the inclusion of Gentiles and outcasts in the kingdom. The concluding statement "none of those who were invited shall taste my banquet" underscores the seriousness of refusing God's gracious invitation.

## Counting the Cost

The chapter closes with Jesus's stark teaching on the cost of discipleship (14:25–35). The images of the tower builder and the king going to war emphasize that following Jesus requires sober calculation and total commitment — "whoever does not bear his own cross and come after me cannot be my disciple" (14:27).
