## The Temptation in the Wilderness

Luke 4 begins with Jesus, "full of the Holy Spirit," being led into the wilderness for forty days of temptation (4:1–13). Protestant interpreters emphasize that Jesus resists Satan by quoting Scripture — a demonstration of the sufficiency of God's Word that resonates with the *sola scriptura* principle. The sequence of temptations differs from Matthew's, and Protestant scholars debate the theological significance of Luke's ordering.

## The Nazareth Manifesto

Jesus's sermon in the Nazareth synagogue (4:16–30) is often called his "mission statement." By reading from Isaiah 61 and declaring "Today this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing," Jesus identifies himself as the anointed prophet who brings good news to the poor, liberty to captives, and sight to the blind. Protestant commentators highlight that the hometown rejection foreshadows the pattern of the entire Gospel: the gospel moves from Jewish rejection toward Gentile acceptance.

## Authority in Capernaum

The exorcism in the Capernaum synagogue (4:31–37) and the healing of Simon's mother-in-law (4:38–39) demonstrate Jesus's authority over demonic powers and disease. Protestant interpreters see these miracles as authenticating signs that confirm Jesus's messianic identity and the arrival of the kingdom of God.
