## Sin, Forgiveness, and Faith

Luke 17 opens with teachings on the seriousness of causing others to stumble, the duty of forgiveness, and the nature of faith (17:1–6). The disciples' request "Increase our faith!" receives the memorable response about faith the size of a mustard seed. Protestant interpreters stress that the power of faith lies not in its quantity but in its object — the almighty God.

## The Ten Lepers

The healing of the ten lepers (17:11–19) is unique to Luke. Only one — a Samaritan — returns to give thanks, prompting Jesus to ask, "Were not ten cleansed? Where are the nine?" Protestant commentators draw two lessons: gratitude as the proper response to grace, and the recurring Lukan theme that outsiders (here a Samaritan) often display greater faith than insiders.

## The Coming of the Kingdom

Jesus's eschatological teaching (17:20–37) distinguishes between the kingdom's present spiritual reality — "the kingdom of God is in your midst" (17:21) — and its future dramatic manifestation. The comparisons to the days of Noah and Lot (17:26–30) warn of complacency before sudden judgment. Protestant eschatology has extensively engaged these verses in discussions of the rapture, the second coming, and the nature of the kingdom.
