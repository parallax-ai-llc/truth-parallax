## The Parable of the Shrewd Manager

Luke 16 opens with the puzzling parable of the dishonest manager (16:1–13), which has challenged interpreters throughout history. Protestant commentators generally argue that Jesus commends not the manager's dishonesty but his shrewdness in using material resources to secure his future. The application — "make friends for yourselves by means of unrighteous wealth" — teaches that earthly resources should be deployed for eternal purposes. The concluding maxim "You cannot serve God and money" (16:13) draws a sharp line.

## The Pharisees and the Law

Jesus's rebuke of the money-loving Pharisees (16:14–18) and his teaching on the enduring validity of the Law lead into one of the most solemn passages in the Gospels. Protestant interpreters note that Jesus does not abolish the Law but reveals its deeper spiritual intent, consistent with the Reformation distinction between the Law's condemning function and the gospel's liberating function.

## The Rich Man and Lazarus

The parable of the rich man and Lazarus (16:19–31) presents a vivid picture of post-mortem reversal. Protestant preachers have long used this passage to warn against the spiritual danger of wealth and indifference to the poor. The conclusion — "If they do not hear Moses and the Prophets, neither will they be convinced if someone should rise from the dead" (16:31) — underscores the sufficiency of Scripture, a point deeply resonant with *sola scriptura*.
