## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Luke is shaped by the Reformation principles of *sola scriptura* and *sola fide*. Luke's Gospel receives particular attention for its clear articulation of salvation by grace, its emphasis on the work of the Holy Spirit, and its portrayal of Jesus as the Savior of all people — themes that resonate deeply with Protestant soteriology.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

Protestants highlight Luke's birth narratives as foundational for understanding the incarnation and the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy. The Magnificat (1:46–55) is read as a hymn of divine grace rather than a basis for Marian devotion. Luke's unique parables — the Good Samaritan, the Prodigal Son, the Pharisee and the Tax Collector — are central to Protestant preaching on grace, repentance, and justification.

## Theological Distinctives

The travel narrative (9:51–19:27) is seen as Jesus's deliberate journey toward the cross, reinforcing the Protestant emphasis on substitutionary atonement. Luke 24, with its Emmaus road account, is treasured for its model of Christ-centered biblical interpretation — Jesus himself opening the Scriptures to reveal how all things written about him must be fulfilled.
