## The Lord's Prayer

Luke 11 opens with Jesus teaching his disciples to pray (11:1–4). Luke's version of the Lord's Prayer is shorter than Matthew's, and Protestant interpreters see it as a model of simplicity and dependence on God. The accompanying parables of the friend at midnight (11:5–8) and the good father (11:11–13) teach persistence in prayer and confidence in God's generous response — culminating in the promise of the Holy Spirit to those who ask.

## The Beelzebul Controversy

The accusation that Jesus casts out demons by Beelzebul (11:14–26) prompts Jesus's declaration that "if it is by the finger of God that I cast out demons, then the kingdom of God has come upon you" (11:20). Protestant interpreters see this as a decisive claim that Jesus's ministry inaugurates the kingdom. The warning about the unclean spirit returning to a swept house (11:24–26) cautions against moral reformation without genuine spiritual transformation.

## Woes Against the Pharisees and Lawyers

The six woes (11:37–54) directed at the Pharisees and lawyers constitute a sharp critique of religious hypocrisy. Protestant commentators read these woes as a warning against legalism, externalism, and the substitution of human tradition for God's Word — themes that deeply resonate with Reformation theology.
