## The Trial Before the Council, Pilate, and Herod

Luke 23 presents Jesus's trial before the Sanhedrin, Pilate, and Herod Antipas. Protestant interpreters note Luke's emphasis on Jesus's innocence — Pilate declares three times "I find no guilt in this man" (23:4, 14, 22), and Herod finds nothing deserving death. This juridical innocence underscores the substitutionary nature of the atonement: the sinless one dies in the place of the guilty.

## The Crucifixion

Luke's crucifixion account (23:26–49) includes several unique elements. Jesus's words to the weeping women (23:28–31), his prayer "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do" (23:34), and his promise to the penitent thief "Today you will be with me in paradise" (23:43) all emphasize divine mercy even in the hour of greatest suffering. Protestant theology reads the penitent thief as a powerful illustration of salvation by grace through faith alone — no works, no sacraments, only a desperate cry to Jesus.

## The Death and Burial

Jesus's final words "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" (23:46) express perfect trust in God. The centurion's declaration "Certainly this man was innocent!" (23:47) and Joseph of Arimathea's burial of the body (23:50–56) set the stage for the resurrection. Protestant interpreters note that Jesus's real, physical death is essential for the reality of the atonement and the bodily resurrection that follows.
