## The Sermon on the Plain

Luke 6 presents Jesus's Sermon on the Plain (6:17–49), which parallels Matthew's Sermon on the Mount but with distinctive emphases. Protestant interpreters note Luke's more direct address — "Blessed are you who are poor" rather than "poor in spirit" — and the corresponding "woes" against the rich, which sharpen the theme of eschatological reversal.

## Beatitudes and Woes

The four Beatitudes (6:20–23) and four Woes (6:24–26) present a stark contrast between those who suffer now and will be comforted, and those who prosper now and will grieve. Protestant commentators read these not primarily as social program but as eschatological promise: the kingdom of God inverts the world's values. Yet the social dimension is not dismissed — these texts have fueled Protestant social engagement from the abolition movement onward.

## Love Your Enemies

The command to love one's enemies (6:27–36) and the golden rule (6:31) are understood as the ethical fruit of grace. Protestant exegetes stress that this radical ethic is not a means of earning salvation but flows from the prior experience of God's mercy: "Be merciful, just as your Father is merciful" (6:36). The parable of the two builders (6:46–49) concludes the sermon with a warning against hearing without doing.
