## The Sending of the Seventy-Two

Luke 10 opens with Jesus sending out seventy-two disciples (10:1–24), a mission unique to Luke. Protestant interpreters see this as anticipating the wider Gentile mission of Acts. Jesus's warning to Chorazin, Bethsaida, and Capernaum (10:13–16) emphasizes the seriousness of rejecting the gospel. The disciples' joyful return and Jesus's prayer of thanksgiving (10:21–22) highlight the sovereign grace of God in revelation.

## The Good Samaritan

The parable of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) is among the most influential texts in all of Scripture. Protestant interpretation emphasizes that the parable answers the question "Who is my neighbor?" by demolishing ethnic and religious boundaries. The priest and Levite who pass by represent religious formalism without compassion, while the despised Samaritan embodies the love that fulfills the law. Some Protestant interpreters also read the Samaritan as a figure of Christ himself, who rescues the helpless.

## Mary and Martha

The episode of Mary and Martha (10:38–42) is a beloved text in Protestant spirituality. Mary, who sits at Jesus's feet listening to his teaching, has chosen "the good portion" — a statement that prioritizes hearing the Word over anxious activity. Protestant preachers have long used this passage to emphasize the primacy of faith and receptivity to God's Word.
