## The Three Parables of the Lost

Luke 15 contains three of Jesus's most beloved parables, all addressed to the Pharisees who criticize him for eating with sinners. Together they form a powerful triptych on divine grace and the joy of heaven over repentance.

## The Lost Sheep and the Lost Coin

The parable of the lost sheep (15:3–7) depicts a shepherd who leaves ninety-nine sheep to search for one that is lost, and the parable of the lost coin (15:8–10) depicts a woman who sweeps her entire house to find a single coin. Protestant interpreters emphasize the initiative of God in seeking the lost — salvation originates not in human effort but in divine pursuit. The refrain "there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents" underscores the heart of the gospel.

## The Prodigal Son

The parable of the prodigal son (15:11–32) is often called "the gospel within the gospel." Protestant theology finds here a complete picture of grace: the son's rebellion and degradation, his "coming to himself," his return, and the father's extravagant welcome. The father's response — running to meet the son, embracing him, clothing him, and throwing a feast — is read as an image of justification by grace. The elder brother's resentment represents the danger of legalistic self-righteousness that begrudges God's mercy to the undeserving.
