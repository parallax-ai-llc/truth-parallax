## John the Baptist's Ministry

Luke 3 opens with a detailed historical synchronism (3:1–2), anchoring the narrative in verifiable history — a feature Protestant apologists frequently cite. John's preaching of a "baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins" (3:3) is understood as preparatory to the gospel: genuine repentance precedes saving faith. John's ethical instructions to tax collectors and soldiers (3:10–14) demonstrate that repentance bears practical fruit.

## The Baptism of Jesus

Jesus's baptism (3:21–22) is a pivotal moment in Protestant interpretation. The descent of the Holy Spirit and the Father's declaration "You are my beloved Son" reveal the Trinitarian nature of God. Protestant commentators stress that Jesus's baptism was not for his own repentance but identified him with sinful humanity — an anticipation of his substitutionary role on the cross.

## The Genealogy

Luke's genealogy (3:23–38) traces Jesus's lineage back to Adam and ultimately to God, in contrast to Matthew's genealogy which begins with Abraham. Protestant interpreters see this as consistent with Luke's universalist emphasis: Jesus is not merely the Jewish Messiah but the Savior of all humanity, the second Adam who undoes what the first Adam wrought.
