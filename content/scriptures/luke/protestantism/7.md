## The Centurion's Faith

Luke 7 opens with the healing of the centurion's servant (7:1–10). The centurion's declaration "I am not worthy to have you come under my roof" and Jesus's response "I tell you, not even in Israel have I found such faith" illustrate that saving faith transcends ethnic and social boundaries. Protestant interpreters highlight this as an early signal of the Gentile mission.

## The Widow of Nain

The raising of the widow's son at Nain (7:11–17) is unique to Luke. Jesus's compassion for the bereaved mother — "he had compassion on her" — and his authoritative command "Young man, I say to you, arise" display both his humanity and his divine power over death. Protestant commentators connect this episode to the Elijah-Elisha cycle, reinforcing Jesus's identity as the eschatological prophet.

## John's Question and the Sinful Woman

John the Baptist's question from prison — "Are you the one who is to come?" (7:19) — receives an answer grounded in Isaiah's messianic prophecies. The chapter closes with the anointing by the sinful woman (7:36–50), whose extravagant love is contrasted with Simon the Pharisee's coldness. Jesus's declaration "Your faith has saved you; go in peace" (7:50) is a key Protestant proof-text for justification by faith.
