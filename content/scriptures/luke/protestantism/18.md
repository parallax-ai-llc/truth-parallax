## Parables on Prayer and Humility

Luke 18 opens with two parables that address prayer and spiritual posture. The parable of the persistent widow (18:1–8) teaches that God's people "ought always to pray and not lose heart," with the assurance that God will vindicate his elect. The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector (18:9–14) is a cornerstone of Protestant soteriology: the tax collector who cries "God, be merciful to me, a sinner!" goes home justified, while the self-righteous Pharisee does not. "Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted" (18:14).

## Children, the Rich Ruler, and Discipleship

Jesus's blessing of children (18:15–17) and the encounter with the rich ruler (18:18–30) continue the theme of radical dependence. The ruler's inability to part with his possessions illustrates that wealth can be a barrier to entering the kingdom. Peter's declaration "We have left our homes and followed you" prompts Jesus's promise of reward both in this age and in the age to come.

## The Blind Beggar Near Jericho

The healing of the blind beggar (18:35–43) demonstrates the saving power of faith: "Your faith has saved you" (18:42). Protestant interpreters note that the beggar's persistent cry "Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!" combines messianic confession with humble petition — a model of saving faith.
