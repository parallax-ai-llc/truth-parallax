## The Call to Repentance

Luke 13 opens with Jesus interpreting two current events — the Galileans killed by Pilate and the tower collapse at Siloam — as calls to universal repentance rather than evidence of special sinfulness (13:1–5). Protestant interpreters appreciate Jesus's refusal to engage in speculative theodicy, instead redirecting attention to personal moral urgency. The parable of the barren fig tree (13:6–9) adds a note of divine patience alongside the warning.

## The Mustard Seed and the Narrow Door

The parables of the mustard seed and leaven (13:18–21) describe the kingdom's surprising growth from insignificant beginnings. The teaching on the narrow door (13:22–30) warns against presumption: "Strive to enter through the narrow door, for many, I tell you, will seek to enter and will not be able." Protestant commentators use this passage to emphasize both the urgency of personal decision and the reversal motif — "some are last who will be first."

## Lament Over Jerusalem

Jesus's lament over Jerusalem (13:34–35) — "How often would I have gathered your children together as a hen gathers her brood" — expresses divine grief over Israel's rejection of her Messiah. Protestant interpreters see this as a poignant expression of the tension between God's universal saving will and human resistance to grace.
