## The Prologue and Birth Announcements

Luke 1 opens with a literary prologue (1:1–4) in which Luke addresses Theophilus, establishing his Gospel as an "orderly account" grounded in eyewitness testimony. Protestant interpreters emphasize Luke's concern for historical reliability — a point frequently cited in apologetics for the trustworthiness of the Gospel records.

## Zechariah, Elizabeth, and the Birth of John

The announcement to Zechariah in the Temple (1:5–25) sets the stage for the coming of the Messiah by first introducing his forerunner. Protestant commentators note the continuity with Old Testament patterns: a barren couple, an angelic announcement, and a child set apart for God's purposes. Zechariah's temporary muteness serves as a sign of both judgment for unbelief and the reliability of God's word.

## The Annunciation and the Magnificat

The angel Gabriel's announcement to Mary (1:26–38) is read by Protestants as the moment of the incarnation, fulfilling Isaiah 7:14. Mary's response — "Let it be to me according to your word" — is celebrated as a model of faith, though Protestants resist the Catholic elevation of Mary to a mediatorial role. The Magnificat (1:46–55) is interpreted as a hymn of grace, echoing Hannah's song and anticipating the gospel's theme of divine reversal.

## The Benedictus and Birth of John

Zechariah's prophetic hymn (1:67–79) is rich with covenant theology. Protestant exegetes highlight its declaration that God has "visited and redeemed his people" and raised up "a horn of salvation" — language that frames the entire Gospel narrative within the history of God's saving promises to Israel.
