## Zacchaeus

The story of Zacchaeus (19:1–10) is a signature Lukan narrative of grace. A despised chief tax collector climbs a tree to see Jesus, who invites himself to Zacchaeus's home. Zacchaeus's spontaneous generosity — giving half his goods to the poor and repaying fourfold anyone he defrauded — is the fruit of genuine conversion. Jesus's declaration "Today salvation has come to this house" and "the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost" (19:10) encapsulate the gospel in a single sentence. Protestant interpreters emphasize that Zacchaeus's transformation follows Jesus's initiative, not the reverse.

## The Parable of the Ten Minas

The parable of the ten minas (19:11–27) teaches faithful stewardship during the period between Jesus's departure and return. Protestant commentators connect this to the doctrine of vocation: all believers are entrusted with gifts and opportunities to be used for the Master's purposes. The severe punishment of the wicked servant and the rebellious citizens underscores eschatological accountability.

## The Triumphal Entry and Temple Cleansing

Jesus's entry into Jerusalem (19:28–40) fulfills Zechariah 9:9 and marks his public messianic claim. His weeping over Jerusalem (19:41–44) expresses prophetic grief over the city's coming destruction. The cleansing of the Temple (19:45–48) asserts divine authority over corrupt religious institutions — a passage that Reformers applied to their own critique of the medieval Church.
