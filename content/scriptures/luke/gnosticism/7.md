## The Centurion and Universal Gnosis

The centurion's faith (7:1–10) was interpreted by Gnostic readers as demonstrating that gnosis is not limited to any ethnic or religious group. The divine spark exists in all pneumatic individuals regardless of their outward identity. Jesus's astonishment at the centurion's faith — greater than anything found in Israel — confirmed, for Gnostics, that the Demiurge's chosen people had no special claim on spiritual knowledge.

## The Raising at Nain and Spiritual Awakening

The raising of the widow's son at Nain (7:11–17) was read by Gnostic interpreters not as a literal resuscitation but as an allegory of spiritual awakening. Death in Gnostic thought represents the condition of the divine spark trapped in ignorance and matter. To "arise" is to awaken to gnosis — to recognize one's true origin in the Pleroma. The young man rising from his bier symbolizes the soul rising from the death-sleep of material existence.

## The Anointing Woman as Sophia

The anointing of Jesus's feet by the sinful woman (7:36–50) was given rich allegorical treatment by Valentinian Gnostics. The woman was identified with Sophia — the fallen divine Aeon whose passion and grief generated the material world. Her tears represent Sophia's sorrow; her anointing represents the restoration of the divine connection; Jesus's acceptance of her represents the Pleroma's reception of the repentant Sophia. The Pharisee who judges her represents the Demiurge's moralistic religion that cannot comprehend the dynamics of divine grace and gnosis.
