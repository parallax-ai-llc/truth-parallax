## The Annunciation as Pneumatic Event

Gnostic readers approached Luke 1's annunciation narratives through the lens of their cosmological system. The announcement to Mary was not understood as the Demiurge's plan but as an intervention from the Pleroma — the fullness of the true divine realm above the material world. The Holy Spirit that "overshadows" Mary (1:35) was identified by Valentinian Gnostics not with the Demiurge's spirit but with Sophia or the divine Aeon who plants the pneumatic seed in the material world.

## Marcion's Rejection of Luke 1–2

Marcion of Sinope rejected Luke 1–2 entirely, beginning his Gospel at what corresponds to Luke 4:31. For Marcion, Jesus did not have a human birth — he descended directly from the unknown God into the world of the Demiurge. The birth narratives, with their emphasis on Jewish Temple worship, Davidic lineage, and the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy, were incompatible with Marcion's radical distinction between the God of the Old Testament and the Father of Jesus Christ.

## Valentinian Interpretation

Valentinian Gnostics, by contrast, did not reject Luke 1 but reinterpreted it. The Magnificat's themes of reversal — putting down the mighty, exalting the humble — were read as describing the cosmic reversal that occurs when gnosis enters the material world. Mary herself was understood as a vessel for the pneumatic element, her role being to provide the material body that the divine Savior would inhabit temporarily.
