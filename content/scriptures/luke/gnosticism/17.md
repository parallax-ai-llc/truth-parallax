## Faith as Gnosis

The disciples' request "Increase our faith!" (17:5) was interpreted by Gnostic readers as a request for deeper gnosis. Faith, in the Gnostic sense, is not trust in a personal God but knowledge of one's divine origin and destiny. The mustard-seed saying teaches that even a small measure of genuine gnosis is sufficient to accomplish what seems impossible — the liberation of the divine spark from its material imprisonment.

## The Ten Lepers and Spiritual Cleansing

The healing of the ten lepers (17:11–19) was read as an allegory of spiritual purification. Leprosy represents the corruption that material existence inflicts on the divine spark. Nine lepers are healed but return to the Demiurge's religious system (the priests, 17:14); only one — significantly, a Samaritan, an outsider — returns to the true source of healing. Gnostic interpreters saw this as confirming that the institutional religion of the Demiurge cannot provide genuine spiritual restoration.

## The Kingdom Within

Jesus's statement "the kingdom of God is within you" (17:21, alternative translation) was perhaps the single most important verse for Gnostic hermeneutics. This was understood as a direct declaration that the divine realm is not external but interior — the divine spark within the pneumatic soul is the kingdom itself. Gnosis is the recognition of this inner divine presence, and salvation consists in awakening to this reality rather than seeking an external eschatological event.
