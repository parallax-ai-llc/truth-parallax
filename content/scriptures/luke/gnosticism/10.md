## The Good Samaritan as Cosmic Allegory

The parable of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) received elaborate allegorical treatment from Gnostic interpreters, particularly in Valentinian circles. The man who fell among robbers is Adam (humanity), stripped of his divine garments by the archons. The priest and Levite are the Torah and the Prophets — the Demiurge's religion, which sees the wounded man but cannot heal him. The Samaritan is the divine Christ, an outsider to the Demiurge's system, who binds the wounds with oil (the chrism of gnosis) and wine (the pneumatic knowledge), brings the wounded to the inn (the Gnostic community), and promises to return (the eschatological completion of salvation).

## Mary and Martha: Contemplation and Action

The Mary and Martha episode (10:38–42) was interpreted by Gnostic readers as contrasting two levels of spiritual life. Martha represents the psychic level — busy with external works of religion and morality. Mary represents the pneumatic level — sitting at the Savior's feet receiving the secret teaching (gnosis). Jesus's declaration that Mary has chosen "the good portion" confirms the superiority of gnosis over works.

## The Seventy-Two and Esoteric Transmission

The mission of the seventy-two (10:1–24) was read as the dissemination of esoteric knowledge to those capable of receiving it. The instruction to shake dust from their feet against unresponsive towns (10:10–11) confirmed the Gnostic distinction between those who can receive gnosis and those who cannot — a distinction rooted not in moral worthiness but in ontological nature.
