## The Baptism as the Descent of the Christ

For many Gnostic schools, the baptism of Jesus (3:21–22) was the most significant event in Luke 3 — and indeed in the entire Gospel. Cerinthian Gnostics taught that the divine Christ-Aeon descended upon the human Jesus at the baptism, and the voice "You are my beloved Son" marked the moment of this divine union. The human Jesus was merely the vessel; the true Savior was the spiritual Christ who entered him from the Pleroma.

## The Genealogy and the Demiurge

Luke's genealogy (3:23–38) presented a problem for Gnostic interpreters. By tracing Jesus's lineage back through the patriarchs and ultimately to God, it connected Jesus to the Demiurge's creation. Marcion rejected the genealogy entirely. Valentinian Gnostics reinterpreted it: the "God" at the end of the genealogy was not the Demiurge but the true, transcendent Father — thus the genealogy, properly understood, traced Jesus's spiritual lineage beyond the material world to the ultimate divine source.

## John the Baptist's Role

John the Baptist occupied an ambiguous position in Gnostic thought. Some Gnostic sects honored him as a forerunner of gnosis; others, particularly the Mandaeans (a Gnostic sect that venerates John), elevated John above Jesus. In Valentinian interpretation, John represented the psychic level of religion — sincere but limited to the Demiurge's realm — while Jesus brought the pneumatic revelation that transcended it.
