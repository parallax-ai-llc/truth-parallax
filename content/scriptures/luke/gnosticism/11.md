## The Lord's Prayer and the Unknown God

Gnostic interpreters, particularly Marcion, read the Lord's Prayer (11:1–4) as addressed not to the Demiurge but to the true, unknown Father revealed by Jesus. The petition "Your kingdom come" was understood as a prayer for the Pleroma's ultimate triumph over the material realm. "Give us each day our daily bread" was interpreted as a request for spiritual nourishment — the bread of gnosis — rather than material sustenance.

## The Beelzebul Controversy and the Demiurge

The Beelzebul controversy (11:14–26) was read through the Gnostic identification of the Demiurge with the archonic powers. Some Gnostic systems collapsed the distinction between the creator god and Satan, arguing that both were aspects of the same malevolent or ignorant power that rules the material world. Jesus's exorcisms demonstrated his authority over these lower powers, confirming his origin in the true divine realm above.

## The Woes and the Religion of the Demiurge

The woes against the Pharisees and lawyers (11:37–54) were enthusiastically appropriated by Gnostic readers as a condemnation of the Demiurge's religion. The Pharisees' obsession with external purity, tithes, and legal precision represented the bondage of psychic religion — religion that follows the Demiurge's rules without penetrating to the deeper pneumatic truth. Jesus's critique confirmed, for Gnostics, the fundamental inadequacy of the Old Testament religious system.
