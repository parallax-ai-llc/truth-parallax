## The Shrewd Manager and Gnostic Pragmatism

The parable of the shrewd manager (16:1–13) was interpreted by Gnostic readers as teaching the pneumatic to use the material world's resources strategically for spiritual purposes. The "unrighteous mammon" belongs to the Demiurge's realm, but the wise pneumatic uses it to advance the cause of gnosis. The saying "You cannot serve God and mammon" confirmed the absolute separation between the true God and the material order — a core Gnostic principle.

## The Rich Man and Lazarus: Cosmic Justice

The parable of the rich man and Lazarus (16:19–31) was read as illustrating the ultimate reversal that awaits those who invest in the material world versus those who possess spiritual knowledge. The rich man, clothed in the Demiurge's blessings, discovers after death that his material prosperity was worthless. Lazarus, poor in the world's terms but rich in gnosis, enters the true divine rest. The "great chasm" represents the ontological divide between the Pleroma and the material realm.

## Moses and the Prophets

Abraham's statement "They have Moses and the Prophets" (16:29) was interpreted by Marcion as ironic: Moses and the Prophets belong to the Demiurge's revelation, which is fundamentally inadequate for salvation. The true message of salvation comes not from the Old Testament but from the alien God revealed by Jesus. Even a resurrection (16:31) is insufficient for those trapped in the Demiurge's religious framework — only gnosis can liberate.
