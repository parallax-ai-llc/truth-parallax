## Overview

Gnostic interpretation of Luke selectively appropriates and reinterprets the Gospel through the lens of Gnostic cosmology and soteriology. While no complete Gnostic commentary on Luke survives, Gnostic texts from the Nag Hammadi library and patristic reports reveal how Gnostic teachers — particularly Marcion, Valentinus, and their followers — engaged with Luke's narrative. Marcion famously adopted a heavily edited version of Luke as his sole Gospel.

## Marcion's Gospel

Marcion of Sinope (c. 85–160 CE) regarded an edited Luke as the only authentic Gospel. He removed the birth narratives (chapters 1–2) and all references to the Hebrew Bible, arguing that the God of the Old Testament was an inferior Demiurge distinct from the supreme God revealed by Jesus. Marcion's Gospel began abruptly at what corresponds to Luke 4:31, with Jesus descending from heaven into Capernaum.

## Valentinian Readings

Valentinian Gnostics read Luke's parables as allegories of the Pleroma and the fall of Sophia. The parable of the Lost Coin (15:8–10) was interpreted as Sophia searching for the lost divine spark. The Prodigal Son (15:11–32) was read as the soul's descent into matter and return to the Pleroma. The woman who anointed Jesus's feet (7:36–50) was identified with Sophia herself.

## Pneumatic Hermeneutics

Gnostic readers distinguished between the literal, psychic, and pneumatic (spiritual) levels of the text. Only the pneumatic elite — those possessing gnosis — could discern the hidden meanings. Luke's emphasis on the Holy Spirit was reinterpreted as referring to the divine spark within the Gnostic initiate rather than the Spirit of the Creator God.
