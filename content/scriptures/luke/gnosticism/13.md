## The Narrow Door and Gnosis

The teaching on the narrow door (13:22–30) was read by Gnostic interpreters as confirming the exclusivity of gnosis. Only the few — the pneumatics who possess the divine spark and receive the secret teaching — can enter through the narrow door. The "many" who seek to enter but cannot are the psychics and hylics who lack the ontological capacity for genuine spiritual knowledge. This reading reinforced the Gnostic self-understanding as a spiritual elite.

## Reversal of First and Last

The saying "some are last who will be first, and some are first who will be last" (13:30) was applied by Gnostics to the cosmic reversal they expected: those whom the Demiurge's religion honors (the pious Jews, the Temple establishment) will be revealed as spiritually impoverished, while the Gnostic "outsiders" — despised by the religious mainstream — will be recognized as the true bearers of the divine light.

## The Lament Over Jerusalem

Jesus's lament over Jerusalem (13:34–35) was read by Marcion and other Gnostics as a judgment on the Demiurge's holy city and its religion. Jerusalem, as the center of the Demiurge's worship, is judged and found wanting. The divine Savior offers gnosis, but the city — bound to its creator god — refuses. The coming desolation of Jerusalem's "house" (Temple) is the inevitable consequence of clinging to the inferior deity.
