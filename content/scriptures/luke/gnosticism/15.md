## The Lost Coin and Sophia

The parable of the lost coin (15:8–10) was one of the most frequently interpreted texts in Valentinian Gnosticism. The woman searching for her lost coin was identified with Sophia — the fallen Aeon who searches for the divine sparks scattered throughout the material world. The ten coins represent the Aeons of the Pleroma; the lost coin is the divine element that has fallen into matter. The sweeping of the house is Sophia's activity of gathering the pneumatic fragments back to their source.

## The Prodigal Son and the Fall into Matter

The parable of the prodigal son (15:11–32) was read as the definitive allegory of the Gnostic myth. The younger son's departure from the father's house represents the fall of the divine spark from the Pleroma into the material world. The "far country" is the material realm created by the Demiurge. Feeding pigs — the most degrading occupation for a Jew — symbolizes the soul's enslavement to the basest material passions. The moment of self-recognition ("he came to himself") is the awakening of gnosis — the moment when the pneumatic soul remembers its divine origin.

## The Return to the Pleroma

The son's return to the father and the father's joyful reception represent the soul's ascent back to the Pleroma and the divine embrace. The robe, the ring, and the feast symbolize the restoration of the divine image, the seal of gnosis, and the eschatological banquet of spiritual reunion. The elder brother, in this reading, represents the Demiurge or his followers — those who have remained within the creator's household but resent the pneumatic's reception of greater honor.
