## The Resurrection and Gnostic Soteriology

Luke 24's resurrection narratives were interpreted by Gnostic readers in terms of their own soteriology. The risen Christ who appears to the disciples is understood not as a resurrected physical body but as the pneumatic Christ manifesting in a spiritual form. The emphasis on Jesus eating fish (24:42–43) and inviting the disciples to touch him was problematic for docetic Gnostics, who generally preferred to understand the resurrection appearances as visionary or spiritual experiences rather than physical encounters.

## The Emmaus Road and Esoteric Teaching

The Emmaus road narrative (24:13–35) was treasured by Gnostic interpreters for its description of Jesus "opening the Scriptures" and revealing the hidden meaning of Moses and the Prophets. This was read as the model for Gnostic exegesis itself: the true meaning of Scripture is not the surface meaning but the pneumatic meaning that only the initiated can perceive. The disciples' "burning hearts" represent the awakening of gnosis — the interior fire of divine recognition.

## The Ascension and Return to the Pleroma

The ascension (24:50–53) was understood in Gnostic theology as the divine Christ's return to the Pleroma — the consummation of the salvific drama. The Savior who descended from the realm of light to bring gnosis to the imprisoned sparks now returns to his origin, having accomplished his mission. The disciples' "great joy" anticipates the eventual joy of all pneumatics who, through gnosis, will follow the same ascending path back to the fullness of divine life.
