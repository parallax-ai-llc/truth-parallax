## The Pharisee and the Tax Collector

The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector (18:9–14) was read by Gnostic interpreters as contrasting psychic and pneumatic religion. The Pharisee represents the psychic religious person — devoted to the Demiurge's law, proud of moral achievement, but lacking genuine gnosis. The tax collector represents the pneumatic who, despite appearing sinful by the Demiurge's standards, possesses the essential humility and self-knowledge that opens the door to gnosis.

## Children and Pneumatic Receptivity

Jesus's teaching on receiving the kingdom like a child (18:15–17) was interpreted by Valentinian Gnostics as describing the pneumatic disposition required for receiving gnosis. The child's openness, freedom from dogmatic assumptions, and natural receptivity model the state of consciousness in which gnosis can be received. The "wise" of this world — those learned in the Demiurge's religion — are too encumbered by their existing frameworks to receive the truly new revelation.

## The Rich Ruler and Material Attachment

The rich ruler's inability to follow Jesus (18:18–30) confirmed the Gnostic teaching that attachment to the material world is the primary obstacle to spiritual liberation. The ruler has kept all the Demiurge's commandments but remains enslaved to his possessions. Gnostic asceticism, particularly in its encratite (severely ascetic) forms, drew on texts like this to justify radical renunciation of material goods, family, and bodily pleasures.
