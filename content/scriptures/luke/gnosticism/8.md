## The Sower and the Three Natures

The parable of the sower (8:4–15) was among the most important texts for Valentinian anthropology. The four types of soil were mapped onto the Valentinian typology of human natures: the path represents the hylics (material people) who cannot receive the seed at all; the rocky ground represents the psychics (soulish people) who receive superficially; the thorns represent those choked by the Demiurge's world; and the good soil represents the pneumatics (spiritual people) who bear fruit through gnosis.

## The Storm and Cosmic Chaos

The calming of the storm (8:22–25) was read as an allegory of the divine Savior's power over the chaos of the material world. The storm represents the turbulence created by the Demiurge's flawed creation — a cosmos marked by disorder, suffering, and death. The divine Christ stills this chaos by introducing the harmony of the Pleroma into the material realm.

## The Gerasene Demoniac and the Archons

The exorcism of the Gerasene demoniac (8:26–39) was interpreted as the liberation of a divine spark from the control of the archons. The "legion" of demons represents the multitude of archonic powers that rule the material world. Their entry into the swine — unclean animals associated with the material realm — and their destruction in the sea symbolizes the ultimate dissolution of the archonic powers when gnosis triumphs.
