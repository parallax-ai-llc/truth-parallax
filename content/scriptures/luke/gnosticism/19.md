## Zacchaeus and Spiritual Seeking

Gnostic interpreters read the story of Zacchaeus (19:1–10) as an allegory of the pneumatic soul's search for the divine Savior. Zacchaeus's small stature represents the diminished condition of the divine spark in the material world. His climbing the sycamore tree represents the soul's effort to rise above the material plane. Jesus's initiative in calling Zacchaeus confirms the Gnostic principle that salvation comes from above — the divine Savior descends to meet the ascending soul.

## The Temple Cleansing and Critique of the Demiurge's Worship

The cleansing of the Temple (19:45–48) was interpreted by Marcionites and other Gnostic groups as a direct attack on the Demiurge's religious institution. The Temple, as the center of the Demiurge's worship — with its animal sacrifices, priestly hierarchy, and legal regulations — represented everything that the divine Christ came to overthrow. The cleansing was not a reform but a repudiation of the entire sacrificial system.

## The Parable of the Ten Minas

The parable of the ten minas (19:11–27) was read by Gnostic interpreters as teaching the responsible use of gnosis. The minas represent the measure of spiritual knowledge entrusted to each pneumatic. Those who develop their gnosis are rewarded with greater knowledge; those who hide or neglect it lose even what they have. The citizens who rejected the nobleman's rule represent those who refuse to acknowledge the authority of the true God, clinging instead to the Demiurge.
