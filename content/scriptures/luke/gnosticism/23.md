## The Crucifixion and Docetic Christology

Luke 23's crucifixion narrative is the point of greatest tension between Gnostic and proto-orthodox Christianity. For docetic Gnostics, the divine Christ was not and could not be crucified — only the material body suffered on the cross while the true spiritual Christ withdrew or observed from above. The Second Treatise of the Great Seth (Nag Hammadi) describes the crucifixion scene with the "living Jesus" laughing at his persecutors' ignorance: "It was another who drank the gall and the vinegar; it was not I."

## Basilides and the Substitution Theory

Basilides, an early Gnostic teacher, reportedly taught that Simon of Cyrene (23:26) was miraculously transformed to look like Jesus and crucified in his place, while Jesus took Simon's form and stood by, laughing at the deception. This substitution theory, later echoed in Islamic theology, represents one of the most radical Gnostic reinterpretations of the passion narrative.

## The "Death" and Its Meaning

Jesus's final words in Luke — "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" (23:46) — were read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the moment when the divine pneuma separates from the material body and returns to the Pleroma. The death of the body is not a tragedy but a liberation — the divine element escaping its material prison. The cosmic significance of the cross, for Gnostics, lies not in atonement for sin but in the demonstration that the material world cannot ultimately hold the divine spirit.
