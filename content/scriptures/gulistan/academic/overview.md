## Overview

Academic study of the Gulistan spans Persian literary criticism, Islamic intellectual history, comparative ethics, and reception studies. Scholars analyze Sa'di's masterful blend of prose and poetry (*nazm-o-nasr*), noting how his seemingly artless anecdotes are in fact carefully constructed literary performances that encode complex moral arguments.

The Gulistan's manuscript tradition is extensive, with copies produced across the Persian-speaking world from the 13th century onward, often with lavish illuminations. Textual scholars compare recensions to trace the work's transmission and occasional interpolation. Historians of ideas examine how Sa'di's ethical framework — pragmatic, humanistic, and rooted in both Sufi and courtly traditions — reflects the social conditions of 13th-century Shiraz and the broader post-Mongol Islamic world.
