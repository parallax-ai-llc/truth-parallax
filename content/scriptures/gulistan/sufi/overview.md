## Overview

The Sufi reading of the Gulistan recognizes in Sa'di's seemingly simple tales a deep current of mystical teaching. For the Sufi, the "Rose Garden" is not merely a book of worldly wisdom but an allegory of the spiritual path (*tariqah*). The roses represent divine beauty, the thorns the trials of the ego (*nafs*), and the garden itself the heart cultivated through *dhikr* (remembrance of God) and moral refinement.

Sa'di himself was a wandering dervish for decades, and his stories of Sufi masters, ascetics, and lovers of God carry the authority of lived experience. The Sufi reader finds in the Gulistan a practical manual for *adab* — not mere social etiquette but the spiritual courtesy that governs the relationship between the seeker and the Divine. Each anecdote is a mirror in which the reader may see the state of their own soul.
