## Overview

The Jabala Upanishad holds a position of supreme importance in the Advaita tradition, as it provides scriptural authority for the institution of sannyasa that Shankara made central to his reform of Hinduism. Shankara's own decision to take sannyasa as a young student (*brahmachari*) is justified by the Jabala's declaration that renunciation can be taken from any stage of life — one need not wait to complete the householder stage.

## Theological Emphases

For Advaita, the Jabala's teaching on renunciation is inseparable from the pursuit of self-knowledge. External renunciation — giving up home, possessions, ritual duties — is the outward expression of the inner recognition that the self is not the body, not the social role, not the performer of rituals, but pure consciousness identical with Brahman. The teaching on Avimukta — the place never abandoned — is read as the heart, where the self is eternally present and can never be lost.
