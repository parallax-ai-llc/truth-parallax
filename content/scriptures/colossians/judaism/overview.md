## Overview

Jewish engagement with Colossians centers on the letter's polemical references to Jewish practices — Sabbath, dietary laws, festivals, and circumcision — as "shadows" of the reality found in Christ (2:16-17). This language has been consequential for Jewish-Christian relations, as it has historically been used to delegitimize ongoing Jewish observance.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### Sabbath and Festivals as "Shadow"
The claim that Jewish holy days are "a shadow of the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ" (2:17) has been one of the most contested texts in Jewish-Christian dialogue. Jewish scholars argue that Sabbath and festivals are not mere shadows but divinely mandated expressions of covenant faithfulness.

### Circumcision "Made Without Hands"
The reference to a "circumcision made without hands" (2:11) — spiritual circumcision through baptism — has been read as superseding physical circumcision. Jewish interpretation maintains that *brit milah* (circumcision) remains the eternal sign of God's covenant with Abraham (Genesis 17:13).

### The "Philosophy" in Question
Scholars debate whether the "philosophy and empty deceit" (2:8) Paul combats refers to Jewish practices, pagan philosophy, or some syncretistic combination. Jewish scholars note that characterizing Torah observance as "empty deceit" reflects the polemical context rather than a fair description.

## Scholarly Engagement

Recent scholarship, including work by Mark Nanos, argues that the "Colossian philosophy" may not be Judaism at all but a pagan syncretism, which would reframe the letter's relationship to Jewish tradition.
