## Overview

Islamic engagement with Colossians addresses the letter's claims about Christ's cosmic sovereignty and divine fullness. While Islam reveres Jesus (*Isa*) as a prophet and messenger of God, it categorically rejects the identification of Christ as "the image of the invisible God" (1:15) or as one in whom "all the fullness of deity dwells bodily" (2:9), as these claims violate the principle of *tawhid* (divine oneness).

## Quranic Resonances

### Divine Sovereignty Over Creation
The affirmation that "all things were created through him and for him" (1:16) resonates with Islam's emphasis on God as sole creator — though Islam attributes this creative power to God alone, not to Jesus.

### Moral Renewal
The ethical instructions of chapter 3 — putting away anger, malice, and falsehood and putting on compassion, kindness, and humility — closely parallel Quranic moral teaching and the *hadith* literature on virtue.

### Household Ethics
The household codes (3:18–4:1) address family relationships in ways that resonate with Islamic family ethics, though the specific formulations differ.

## Points of Divergence

| Colossians Theme | Islamic Perspective |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Christ as image of God | God is beyond all images; no creature can represent God |
| Divine fullness in Christ | Fullness (*kamal*) belongs to God alone |
| Spiritual circumcision replacing physical | Islam values physical circumcision (*khitan*) |
