## Overview

The Great Learning's elevation to canonical status is entirely the achievement of Neo-Confucianism. Zhu Xi extracted it from the Record of Rites, rearranged its text, supplied a supplementary commentary on the "investigation of things" (gewu), and made it the first of the Four Books. His interpretation established the Great Learning as the architectural plan of Neo-Confucian philosophy, with the investigation of things as the foundation of all moral and intellectual endeavor.

## Interpretive Approach

The great debate within Neo-Confucianism centers on the meaning of "investigation of things" (gewu). Zhu Xi interpreted it as the exhaustive study of the principles inherent in things and affairs, building knowledge incrementally until a breakthrough of comprehensive understanding occurs. Wang Yangming radically reinterpreted gewu as "rectifying the mind in the midst of affairs," arguing that moral knowledge is innate and that the investigation of external things leads away from, rather than toward, genuine understanding.

## Lasting Impact

This debate between Zhu Xi and Wang Yangming over the Great Learning's first step shaped the entire trajectory of Neo-Confucian philosophy in China, Korea, and Japan. The text's deceptive simplicity thus generated one of the most productive philosophical controversies in East Asian intellectual history, with implications for epistemology, moral psychology, and the theory of education.
