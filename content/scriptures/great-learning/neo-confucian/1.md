Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Great Learning's chapter treats it as the architectural blueprint for the entire Neo-Confucian philosophical system. Zhu Xi's decision to make the Great Learning the first of the Four Books reflects his conviction that its systematic structure provides the essential framework within which all other Confucian teachings must be understood. His rearrangement of the text and his supplementary commentary on the "investigation of things" (gewu) represent perhaps the most consequential editorial intervention in the history of Chinese philosophy.

The debate over the meaning of gewu zhizhi (investigation of things and extension of knowledge) constitutes the most important philosophical controversy generated by this text. Zhu Xi interpreted gewu as the exhaustive investigation of the principles (li) inherent in all things and affairs, building knowledge incrementally until a moment of comprehensive breakthrough (huoran guantong) in which the fundamental unity of all principle is grasped. Wang Yangming rejected this interpretation, arguing that gewu means "rectifying affairs" by applying the innate moral knowledge (liangzhi) already present in the heart-mind. This disagreement divided Neo-Confucianism into two major schools and generated centuries of philosophical debate across East Asia.

The text's progression from inner cultivation to world peace is interpreted by Neo-Confucians as describing the natural expression of heavenly principle from its source in the individual heart-mind to its full actualization in the political and cosmic order. When principle is fully realized in the ruler's heart-mind through the eight steps, it radiates outward through the concentric circles of family, state, and world, bringing each into alignment with the natural order. This metaphysical reading transforms a practical program of moral and political development into a cosmological vision of the unity of self and world.
