Academic scholarship on the Great Learning examines its origins as chapter 42 of the Record of Rites (Liji), its extraction and rearrangement by Zhu Xi, and the philosophical and political consequences of its elevation to canonical status. Scholars have debated the text's date of composition, with most placing it in the late Warring States or early Han period, and have examined its relationship to other early Confucian texts on self-cultivation, particularly the Zhongyong (Doctrine of the Mean) and the Xunzi.

The differences between the "old text" (guben) version preserved in the Liji and Zhu Xi's rearranged version have been the subject of detailed philological analysis. Zhu Xi not only rearranged the text's sections but added a supplementary commentary (buzhuan) on the investigation of things, which he regarded as a lost passage. Later scholars, including Wang Yangming, rejected both the rearrangement and the supplementary commentary, arguing for a return to the original text. This editorial controversy has been analyzed as revealing fundamental philosophical disagreements about the nature of moral knowledge and the starting point of self-cultivation.

Historians of education have studied the Great Learning's role in shaping the civil service examination system and the broader culture of learning in imperial China. Its position as the first of the Four Books meant that it was the first text studied by every aspiring scholar-official, and its concise formulations shaped the moral vocabulary of Chinese governance for centuries. Scholars have also examined its reception in Korea, Japan, and Vietnam, tracing how different cultural contexts produced different interpretations of its key concepts.
