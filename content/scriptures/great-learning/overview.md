## Overview

The Great Learning (Daxue) is one of the Four Books of the Confucian canon, originally a chapter of the Record of Rites (Liji) that was extracted and elevated to independent status by the Neo-Confucian scholar Zhu Xi in the twelfth century. Traditionally attributed to Confucius's disciple Zengzi, the text presents a concise program for moral and political transformation, linking personal self-cultivation to the ordering of family, state, and the world.

## Structure

The Great Learning consists of a single chapter organized around three guiding principles and eight sequential steps:

1. **Three Principles (三綱領)**: Manifesting bright virtue, loving the people, and resting in the highest good
2. **Eight Steps (八條目)**: Investigation of things, extension of knowledge, sincerity of intention, rectification of the mind, cultivation of the self, regulation of the family, ordering of the state, and bringing peace to the world

The text moves from a brief classical statement (jing) attributed to Confucius to a longer commentary (zhuan) traditionally ascribed to Zengzi, which elaborates each step with citations from the Odes, Documents, and other canonical sources.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Despite its brevity, the Great Learning has exerted enormous influence:

- **Confucian**: Provides the essential blueprint for the Confucian project of self-cultivation leading to social harmony
- **Neo-Confucian**: Zhu Xi made it the first of the Four Books, the starting point of all Confucian education
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding the Confucian synthesis of ethics and politics
- **Comparative**: Its stepwise model of inner-to-outer transformation invites comparison with contemplative traditions worldwide

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Ge Wu (格物) | Investigation of things as the foundation of knowledge |
| Cheng Yi (誠意) | Sincerity of intention as essential to moral integrity |
| Xiu Shen (修身) | Self-cultivation as the root of all governance |
| Qi Jia (齊家) | Regulating the family as the basis for ordering the state |
| Ming De (明德) | Manifesting bright virtue as the ultimate aim of learning |
