## Overview

Chinese Buddhist interpreters have found in the Great Learning's concept of "manifesting bright virtue" (ming mingde) a resonance with the Buddhist teaching on revealing the inherently luminous nature of mind. The text's program of progressive purification—from sincerity of intention through rectification of mind to self-cultivation—parallels the Buddhist path of moral discipline, concentration, and wisdom, making the Great Learning a natural bridge between Confucian and Buddhist practice.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist readers interpret the Great Learning's "bright virtue" as analogous to the originally pure mind or buddha-nature that is obscured by delusion and attachment. The eight steps of the Great Learning are read as a Confucian version of the graduated path, moving from preliminary ethical discipline to the transformation of consciousness. The text's emphasis on sincerity (cheng) and the rectification of the mind resonates with Buddhist teachings on right intention and mental purification.

## Syncretic Significance

In the Song-Ming synthesis of the Three Teachings, the Great Learning served as common ground between Confucian ethics and Buddhist soteriology. Its brevity and systematic clarity made it an ideal text for demonstrating the compatibility of Confucian moral cultivation with Buddhist spiritual practice, and it continues to serve this function in contemporary East Asian religious thought.
