Chinese Buddhist interpretation of the Great Learning's chapter reads the concept of "manifesting bright virtue" (ming mingde) as a parallel to the Buddhist teaching on revealing the inherently luminous nature of mind (zixing qingjing xin). Just as the Great Learning teaches that bright virtue is already present within every person and needs only to be manifested, Mahayana Buddhism teaches that buddha-nature is inherent in all beings and needs only to be uncovered through the removal of adventitious defilements. This structural parallel provided one of the most important conceptual bridges between Confucian and Buddhist thought in Chinese intellectual history.

The eight steps of the Great Learning are read by Buddhist interpreters as a Confucian version of the graduated path (marga) that moves from preliminary ethical discipline through the purification of consciousness to the transformation of the world through compassionate action. The progression from the investigation of things to sincerity of intention to rectification of the mind parallels the Buddhist threefold training of wisdom (prajna), moral discipline (sila), and meditation (samadhi), though the order and emphasis differ. Chinese Buddhists appreciated this parallel while maintaining that the Buddhist path addresses a deeper dimension of suffering that the Confucian program does not explicitly recognize.

The text's vision of self-cultivation leading to world peace resonates with the Mahayana Buddhist concept of the bodhisattva's vow to liberate all beings through the perfection of wisdom and compassion. The Great Learning's insistence that personal moral transformation is the foundation for social harmony complements the Buddhist teaching that the purification of the individual mind is the basis for the purification of the buddha-field. This convergence facilitated the Song-Ming synthesis of the Three Teachings, in which the Great Learning served as common ground between Confucian ethics and Buddhist soteriology.
