Confucian interpretation of the Great Learning's single chapter reads it as the definitive statement of the Confucian vision linking personal moral cultivation to political and cosmic order. The opening declaration of the three principles—manifesting bright virtue, loving the people, and resting in the highest good—establishes the comprehensive scope of the Confucian project, which extends from the innermost dimension of the individual heart-mind to the ordering of the entire world. Confucian educators have traditionally treated this passage as the gateway to all subsequent study, providing the student with a map of the territory that the entire Confucian curriculum explores.

The eight steps—from the investigation of things through the extension of knowledge, sincerity of intention, rectification of the mind, and cultivation of the self to the regulation of the family, ordering of the state, and bringing peace to the world—constitute the most systematic formulation of the Confucian program of moral and political development. Confucian interpreters emphasize that self-cultivation (xiu shen) occupies the pivotal position in this sequence, serving as the hinge between the inner work of moral development and the outer work of social and political engagement. The text's insistence that this program applies equally to the Son of Heaven and the common person establishes the democratic principle at the heart of Confucian moral thought.

The commentary section's elaboration of each step through citations from the Odes, the Documents, and other canonical sources demonstrates the Confucian method of grounding moral teaching in the authoritative tradition of the sage-kings. Each step is illustrated with historical examples that show how the principles of the Great Learning were realized—or violated—in the lives of actual rulers and states, making the text both a theoretical treatise and a practical handbook for moral and political action.
