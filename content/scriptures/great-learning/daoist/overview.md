## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Great Learning focuses on those elements of the text that resonate with Daoist inner cultivation practices, particularly the emphasis on sincerity (cheng), stillness, and the priority of inner transformation over external reform. While Daoists critique the Confucian assumption that deliberate moral effort can bring peace to the world, they find in the Great Learning's inward-turning movement a partial echo of their own contemplative traditions.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist readers highlight the Great Learning's teaching that "things have their root and branches; affairs have their end and beginning" as consistent with the Daoist emphasis on returning to the root. The text's sequence of moving from inner sincerity to outer order can be read as a Confucian version of the Daoist principle that the sage governs by perfecting the self rather than imposing rules on others. However, Daoists would insist that genuine sincerity arises not from moral effort but from aligning with the natural Way.

## Critical Appreciation

The Daoist reading of the Great Learning ultimately values the text's recognition that governance begins with the inner life of the ruler, while questioning whether the Confucian program of deliberate cultivation can achieve the spontaneous harmony that Daoists regard as the hallmark of true sagely rule.
