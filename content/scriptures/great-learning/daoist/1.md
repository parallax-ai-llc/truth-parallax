Daoist reading of the Great Learning's chapter finds both points of resonance and fundamental disagreement with its Confucian program. The text's emphasis on sincerity (cheng) and the rectification of the mind resonates with Daoist inner cultivation traditions that similarly prioritize the purification of consciousness as the foundation for all genuine achievement. The teaching that the ruler who has rectified his own mind naturally brings order to the state approaches the Daoist ideal of wu wei governance, in which the sage-ruler's inner alignment with the Way spontaneously orders the world without deliberate intervention.

However, Daoists critique the Great Learning's systematic chain of eight steps as a characteristically Confucian attempt to impose rational order on a process that should be organic and spontaneous. From a Daoist perspective, the investigation of things, the extension of knowledge, and the deliberate sincerity of intention represent forms of intellectual and moral effort that actually obstruct the natural flow of the Way. The Daodejing's teaching that "the pursuit of learning is to increase day after day; the pursuit of the Way is to decrease day after day" suggests that genuine wisdom comes from releasing knowledge rather than accumulating it.

The text's vision of extending moral influence from self to family to state to world is appreciated by Daoists as recognizing the organic interconnection of all levels of reality, while being criticized for assuming that this interconnection can be managed through deliberate moral effort. The Daoist sage brings peace to the world not by cultivating virtue and extending it outward but by returning to the root of natural simplicity, allowing the Way to operate through all levels of existence without human interference.
