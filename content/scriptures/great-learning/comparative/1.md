Comparative interpretation of the Great Learning's chapter places its distinctive model of linking inner moral transformation to political order in dialogue with parallel visions across world traditions. The text's chain from self-cultivation to world peace invites comparison with Plato's philosopher-king, who governs justly because he has achieved knowledge of the Good through intellectual and moral discipline, and with the Hindu-Buddhist concept of the chakravartin, the wheel-turning king whose personal virtue ensures cosmic order. In each case, political legitimacy and effectiveness are grounded in the ruler's inner moral state.

The eight-step model invites comparison with other traditions' graduated paths of transformation. The Buddhist Eightfold Path, the Christian purgative-illuminative-unitive way, the Sufi stations (maqamat) of the spiritual journey, and the Platonic ascent from the cave all describe progressive transformations that move from preliminary discipline through deepening insight to a culminating state that integrates knowledge and virtue. The Great Learning's distinctive contribution is its seamless integration of the personal and political dimensions: each step of inner development simultaneously expands the practitioner's sphere of moral influence.

The text's insistence that "from the Son of Heaven down to the common people, all must regard self-cultivation as the root" establishes a principle of moral equality that invites comparison with the Stoic doctrine of the cosmopolis, the Christian teaching on the equality of all souls before God, the Buddhist teaching on universal buddha-nature, and the Islamic principle that all believers are equal before Allah. These parallels reveal a cross-cultural intuition that genuine moral authority transcends social hierarchy and that the obligation to cultivate virtue is universal rather than restricted to any particular class or station.
