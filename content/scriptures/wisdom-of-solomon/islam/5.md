## The Reckoning of the Ungodly

Chapter 5 depicts the wicked at judgment, confessing that their lives of luxury and arrogance amounted to nothing. Islamic eschatology contains strikingly similar scenes: the Quran describes how the wicked will wish they had been dust (78:40), will bite their hands in regret (25:27), and will acknowledge their error when it is too late. The Wisdom of Solomons literary portrait of remorse and reversal mirrors these Quranic judgment scenes.

## The Impermanence of Worldly Things

The vivid similes comparing worldly achievement to shadows, smoke, and wind echo the Quranic teaching that "the life of this world is nothing but play and amusement" (6:32) and that "whatever you have been given is but a passing enjoyment of this worldly life" (28:60). Both texts use the transience of material things to motivate serious moral reflection and preparation for the eternal life to come.

## Divine Cosmic Justice

The image of God arming Himself with creation for cosmic battle resonates with Islamic descriptions of the Day of Judgment, when the heavens will be rolled up (21:104), mountains will be scattered like dust (20:105-107), and the entire natural order will be transformed. Both traditions affirm that the cosmos itself participates in divine justice — creation is not indifferent to human wickedness but will ultimately serve Gods purposes of judgment and renewal.
