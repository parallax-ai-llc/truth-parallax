## Virtue over Longevity

Chapter 4 teaches that a short virtuous life surpasses a long wicked one. Islamic theology affirms this through the concept of *qadr* (divine decree): the length of ones life is determined by God, and what matters is not duration but the quality of faith and deeds. The Prophet Muhammad said, "The best of people is the one whose life is long and whose deeds are good" — but the Wisdom of Solomons emphasis on virtue over mere length resonates with the Islamic priority of spiritual quality.

## Divine Protection of the Righteous

The passage about the righteous taken early to prevent corruption (4:10-14) finds a parallel in the Islamic concept of divine wisdom (*hikmah*) operating behind seemingly tragic events. The Quranic story of Khidr and Moses (18:60-82) illustrates this principle: Khidr kills a young man, and Moses objects, but Khidr explains that God knew the youth would grow to cause his righteous parents grief. Both texts affirm that divine wisdom operates beyond human understanding.

## Judgment and Reversal

The chapters vision of the wicked confronting their error at judgment corresponds directly to Quranic eschatology. The Quran describes the Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*) in vivid terms: the arrogant will be humbled, the oppressed will be vindicated, and every soul will receive its due. The Wisdom of Solomons eschatological reversal thus resonates deeply with Islamic expectations of divine justice.
