## Overview

Islam does not recognize the Wisdom of Solomon as revealed Scripture, but the figure of Sulayman (Solomon) holds an exalted place in the Quran and Islamic tradition as a prophet-king endowed with extraordinary wisdom (*hikmah*) by Allah. The Quranic Sulayman commanded the winds, understood the speech of animals and birds, and ruled over jinn — themes that resonate with, yet diverge significantly from, the Wisdom of Solomon's philosophical portrait.

## Sulayman in the Quran

Solomon appears in multiple surahs (Al-Baqarah, An-Naml, Saba', Sad) as a model of divinely granted wisdom and just governance. The Quranic emphasis on Sulayman's gratitude, humility before God, and rejection of idolatry parallels the Wisdom of Solomon's insistence that true wisdom begins with the knowledge of God and that idol worship is the root of moral corruption.

## Thematic Convergences

Several themes in the Wisdom of Solomon find parallels in Islamic thought:

- **Tawhid and anti-idolatry**: The book's extended critique of idol worship (chapters 13–15) aligns closely with Islam's foundational commitment to divine unity and its rejection of *shirk* (associating partners with God)
- **Hikmah as divine gift**: Both traditions present wisdom not as human achievement but as a grace bestowed by God upon the worthy
- **Accountability after death**: The book's teaching that the wicked will face judgment corresponds to Islamic eschatology

## Scholarly Context

Muslim scholars engaging in comparative scripture studies note the Wisdom of Solomon as evidence of the broader monotheistic wisdom tradition (*hikmah*) that Islam sees as culminating in the Quranic revelation. The book's philosophical arguments against polytheism are appreciated as reflecting an authentic prophetic impulse, even if the text itself is not considered divinely preserved.
