## Wisdom and Just Governance

Chapter 8 connects wisdom with effective and just governance, a theme central to Islamic political philosophy. The ideal Muslim ruler governs with *hikmah* (wisdom), *adl* (justice), and *shura* (consultation). Islamic thinkers from al-Farabi to Ibn Khaldun developed theories of wise governance that echo the Wisdom of Solomons portrait of Solomon seeking divine guidance for statecraft.

## The Virtues and Islamic Ethics

The chapters identification of Wisdoms teachings with temperance, prudence, justice, and fortitude finds parallels in Islamic virtue ethics. Classical Muslim ethicists, particularly al-Ghazali in his *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, developed a comprehensive virtue theory that synthesized Quranic ethics with Greek philosophical categories — a synthesis remarkably similar to what the Wisdom of Solomon achieved within a Jewish framework centuries earlier.

## Wisdom as Divine Gift

Solomons acknowledgment that wisdom must be received as a gift from God aligns perfectly with the Islamic understanding of *hikmah* as a divine bestowal. The Quran makes this explicit: God gives wisdom to whom He wills (2:269). Human effort — study, prayer, righteous living — prepares the ground, but wisdom itself comes from God. This combination of human striving and divine grace characterizes the Islamic pursuit of knowledge at its best.
