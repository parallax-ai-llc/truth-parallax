## The Darkness over Egypt

Chapter 17 expands the ninth plague into a sustained meditation on darkness as both physical punishment and spiritual condition. Islamic theology draws a similar connection between physical and spiritual darkness. The Quran describes disbelief as layers of darkness: "Their deeds are like a mirage in a desert... or like darknesses within an unfathomable sea" (24:39-40). The Wisdom of Solomons equation of Egypts external darkness with internal spiritual blindness mirrors this Quranic imagery.

## The Terror of Guilty Conscience

The chapters depiction of the Egyptians tormented by their own guilty conscience resonates with Islamic psychology of sin. The Quran describes the *nafs lawwamah* — the self-reproaching soul (75:2) — that accuses the sinner from within. This internal accountability, operative even before the Day of Judgment, corresponds to the Wisdom of Solomons portrait of Egyptians imprisoned not by chains but by the darkness of their own guilt.

## Light as Divine Guidance

The contrast between Egypts darkness and Israels light reflects the universal religious symbolism of light as divine guidance. The Quran presents itself as light: "There has come to you from God a light and a clear Book" (5:15). The famous Light Verse (24:35) describes God as "the Light of the heavens and the earth." The Wisdom of Solomons contrast between those in darkness and those in light parallels the Quranic distinction between guidance (*hidayah*) and misguidance (*dalalah*).
