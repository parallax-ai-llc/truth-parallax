## The Sin of Nature Worship

Chapter 13 critiques those who worship the elements of nature instead of the Creator. Islamic theology identifies this error as *shirk* (associating partners with God) — the gravest sin in Islam. The Quran makes the same argument as the Wisdom of Solomon: "Among His signs are the night and the day, the sun and the moon. Do not prostrate to the sun or the moon, but prostrate to God who created them" (41:37). Both texts insist that creation points to the Creator but must never be confused with Him.

## The Signs of God in Creation

The Wisdom of Solomons argument that the beauty of created things should lead to knowledge of the Creator closely parallels the Quranic concept of *ayat* (signs). The Quran repeatedly invites reflection on natural phenomena — the alternation of day and night, the growth of plants, the movement of clouds — as signs pointing to God. The Wisdom of Solomon employs the same logic: from the grandeur of creation, rational beings should infer the existence and power of the Creator.

## The Idol-Maker Critiqued

The satirical portrait of the craftsman who makes a god from leftover wood resonates with Quranic critiques of idolatry. Ibrahim (Abraham) famously challenged his fathers idol-making (6:74, 21:52-67), and the Quran mocks idols as objects that "cannot create anything, but are themselves created" (16:20). The Wisdom of Solomons ironic treatment of the idol-maker participates in the same prophetic tradition of exposing the absurdity of worshipping human-made objects.
