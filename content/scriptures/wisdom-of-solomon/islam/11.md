## The Mercy of God

The theological excursus on divine mercy in chapter 11 resonates profoundly with Islamic theology, where *rahmah* (mercy) is among Gods most emphasized attributes. Every surah of the Quran (except one) begins with "In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful" (*Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim*). The Wisdom of Solomons declaration that God loves all things He has made and overlooks sins to allow repentance mirrors the hadith qudsi: "My mercy prevails over My wrath."

## Divine Power and Restraint

The assertion that God could have destroyed the wicked instantly but chose restraint reflects the Islamic understanding of divine *hilm* (forbearance). The Quran repeatedly notes that if God punished people immediately for their sins, no creature would be left on earth (16:61, 35:45). Gods power is expressed not through instant punishment but through patient opportunity for repentance — a theology shared between the Wisdom of Solomon and the Quran.

## Gods Love for Creation

The declaration that God loves all that exists (11:24) finds resonance in the Islamic divine name *Al-Wadud* (the All-Loving). While Islamic theology carefully distinguishes between Gods general mercy toward all creation and His special love for the righteous, the underlying conviction that Gods fundamental disposition toward His creation is benevolent aligns with the Wisdom of Solomons portrait of a God who delights in existence itself.
