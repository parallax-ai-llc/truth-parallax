## The Crossing of the Sea

The final chapter recounts the Red Sea crossing, which is also a pivotal event in Islamic sacred history. The Quran describes how God parted the sea for Musa and the Israelites and drowned Pharaoh and his armies (26:63-66). The Wisdom of Solomons interpretation of this event as a cosmic transformation — with creation itself being rearranged — goes beyond the Quranic account in literary elaboration but shares the fundamental conviction that God controls the natural order and reshapes it for His purposes.

## Creation Responsive to Divine Command

The image of the elements being re-tuned to serve Gods salvific purpose resonates with the Quranic teaching that all of creation is in a state of submission (*islam*) to God: "To God submits whatever is in the heavens and on earth, willingly or unwillingly" (3:83). Fire, water, earth, and air serve the Creator — a conviction that the Wisdom of Solomon dramatizes through the Exodus and that the Quran affirms as a universal principle.

## The Faithfulness of God

The books closing affirmation that God glorified His people and stood by them at all times and in all places echoes the Quranic assurance that "God does not fail in His promise" (3:9). Islamic theology holds that Gods commitment to guide, protect, and ultimately judge humanity is unbreakable. While Islam sees this faithfulness as extending to all who submit to God rather than to a single nation, the underlying conviction of divine reliability is shared. The Wisdom of Solomon closes as the Quran teaches: God is the faithful guardian of those who trust in Him.
