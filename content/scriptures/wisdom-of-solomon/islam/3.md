## The Righteous in Divine Protection

The declaration that the souls of the righteous are in Gods hand resonates with the Islamic teaching that the souls of martyrs (*shuhada*) are alive with their Lord, provided for and rejoicing (Quran 3:169-170). While Islamic theology does not adopt the specific framework of the Wisdom of Solomon, the underlying conviction — that death does not separate the righteous from divine care — is shared between the traditions.

## Testing as Divine Wisdom

The metaphor of the righteous tested like gold in a furnace corresponds to the Quranic teaching on *ibtila* (divine testing): "Do people think they will be left alone because they say we believe and will not be tested?" (29:2). Islamic theology holds that trials are a sign of divine love and a means of spiritual purification, closely paralleling the Wisdom of Solomons interpretation of righteous suffering as preparation for greater glory.

## Beyond Earthly Measures of Blessing

The chapters affirmation that the childless but virtuous are blessed challenges conventional measures of success. Islamic ethics similarly teaches that ultimate worth is measured by *taqwa* and righteous deeds, not by wealth, offspring, or worldly status. The Prophet Muhammad taught that God may test a person by withholding worldly blessings, and that patience (*sabr*) in such trials earns divine reward in the hereafter.
