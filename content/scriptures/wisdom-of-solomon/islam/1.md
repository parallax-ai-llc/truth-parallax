## The Call to Righteousness and Taqwa

The opening exhortation to love righteousness resonates with the Quranic concept of *taqwa* — God-consciousness and pious reverence that governs all moral action. Islamic scholars engaging in comparative scripture study note that the Wisdom of Solomons insistence on sincerity of heart mirrors the Quranic teaching that God examines the inner disposition, not outward appearances: "God does not look at your forms or your wealth, but He looks at your hearts and your deeds" (Sahih Muslim).

## The Spirit and Divine Presence

The chapters reference to a spirit of wisdom that departs from the deceitful finds a parallel in the Islamic concept of *nur* (divine light) and *barakah* (blessing) that attend the righteous and withdraw from the sinful. While Islamic theology does not personify divine wisdom in the same way, the underlying principle — that persistent sin distances a person from divine guidance — is central to Quranic ethics.

## Death and Divine Design

The assertion that God did not make death aligns with the Islamic teaching that death is a transition rather than an annihilation. The Quran teaches that every soul shall taste death (3:185) but that death is not the final word — resurrection and judgment follow. The Wisdom of Solomons resistance to seeing death as Gods intention finds resonance in the Islamic emphasis on the afterlife as the true and intended destination of human existence.
