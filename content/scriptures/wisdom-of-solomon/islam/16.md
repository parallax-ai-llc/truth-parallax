## Contrasting Divine Judgments

Chapter 16 continues the comparison between Egypts plagues and Israels blessings. Islamic tradition recognizes the plagues of Egypt as divine signs sent to Pharaoh through Musa (Moses), attested in the Quran (7:130-136). The Wisdom of Solomons theological interpretation — that the same elements served opposite purposes for Egypt and Israel — resonates with the Quranic principle that God uses creation to test, punish, and bless as He wills.

## The Sign of Salvation

The interpretation of the bronze serpent as a sign pointing to God rather than an object of worship is deeply consonant with Islamic theology. Islam rejects any attribution of power to created objects — healing, protection, and salvation come from God alone. The Wisdom of Solomons insistence that "it was not the thing that was seen that healed them, but you, O Lord, the savior of all" (16:7) articulates a principle at the heart of Islamic monotheism.

## Sustenance from Gods Word

The declaration that "it is not the production of crops that nourishes a person, but your word" (16:26) resonates with the Islamic understanding that all provision (*rizq*) ultimately comes from God. The Quran teaches, "There is no creature on earth but that upon God is its provision" (11:6). Both texts point beyond material causation to divine sustenance, cultivating the attitude of trust (*tawakkul*) that is central to Islamic spirituality.
