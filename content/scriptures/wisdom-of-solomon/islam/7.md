## Sulaymans Humility

Chapter 7 opens with Solomon emphasizing his common humanity. Islamic tradition similarly presents Sulayman as a prophet who, despite his extraordinary gifts — dominion over winds, jinn, and animals — remained humble before God. The Quran records Sulayans prayer, "My Lord, inspire me to be grateful for Your favor" (27:19). Both texts emphasize that wisdom does not elevate one above human nature but deepens gratitude and humility.

## Wisdom above All Possessions

Solomons preference for wisdom over riches resonates with the Islamic valuation of *hikmah* (wisdom) as one of the greatest divine gifts. The Quran states, "He gives wisdom to whom He wills, and whoever has been given wisdom has certainly been given much good" (2:269). Islamic scholars regard this as confirming that true wealth lies in knowledge of God and righteous action, not in material accumulation.

## The Attributes of Divine Wisdom

The elaborate description of Wisdoms qualities (7:22-30) — as a breath of divine power, pure emanation, and image of divine goodness — resonates cautiously with Islamic theology. While Islam strictly avoids any suggestion of emanation that might compromise divine unity (*tawhid*), Muslim scholars appreciate the underlying insight that all genuine wisdom derives from and reflects God. The divine names *Al-Hakim* (the All-Wise) and *Al-Alim* (the All-Knowing) express in Islamic terms what the Wisdom of Solomon explores through philosophical personification.
