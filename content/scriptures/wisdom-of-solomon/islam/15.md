## Knowledge of the True God

Chapter 15 opens with a confession of faith in God who is kind, true, patient, and merciful. Islamic theology recognizes these as divine attributes expressed in the Quranic names of God: *Al-Latif* (the Subtle/Kind), *Al-Haqq* (the True), *As-Sabur* (the Patient), and *Ar-Rahman* (the Merciful). The Wisdom of Solomons portrait of the true God stands in sharp contrast to the idols critiqued throughout these chapters and aligns with the Islamic affirmation of *tawhid*.

## The Potter and Human Presumption

The image of the mortal potter fashioning a god from clay carries particular resonance in Islamic thought. The Quran emphasizes that God created humanity from clay (6:2, 23:12) and breathed His spirit into Adam. For a creature made of clay to fashion a god from the same material represents a profound inversion of the proper order. Islamic theology insists on the absolute distinction between Creator (*al-Khaliq*) and creature (*makhluq*), and the Wisdom of Solomon dramatizes the absurdity of confusing the two.

## Animal Worship as the Depths of Folly

The chapters concluding critique of Egyptian animal worship targets what the Wisdom of Solomon considers the most degraded form of false religion. Islamic theology concurs: the worship of animals inverts the Quranic hierarchy in which God appointed humanity as His vicegerent (*khalifah*) over creation (2:30). To worship what one was meant to steward represents a catastrophic abdication of human dignity and responsibility.
