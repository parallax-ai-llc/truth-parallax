## Gods Patience and Gradual Judgment

Chapter 12 explains why God punished the Canaanites gradually rather than destroying them at once. This resonates with the Quranic principle of *imhal* (divine respite): God gives sinners time and opportunity to repent before final judgment. The Quran warns, "Let not the disbelievers think that Our postponing their punishment is good for them" (3:178), while also affirming that the purpose of delay is to allow repentance.

## Sovereignty and Mercy United

The chapters teaching that Gods sovereignty is expressed through mercy rather than tyranny aligns with Islamic political ethics. The caliph or ruler is expected to model divine attributes of justice tempered by mercy. The hadith records that the Prophet Muhammad said, "God is gentle and loves gentleness in all things" (Bukhari and Muslim). The Wisdom of Solomons portrait of divine governance as merciful authority provides a model that Islamic thought embraces.

## The Pedagogy of Judgment

The insight that God judges gradually in order to teach resonates with the Islamic concept of divine *hikmah* (wisdom) operative in all of Gods dealings with humanity. Nothing in Gods creation is purposeless or arbitrary. Even suffering and punishment serve pedagogical purposes — they call sinners to reflection and repentance. The Wisdom of Solomon and the Quran share this conviction that divine judgment is always wise, measured, and purposeful.
