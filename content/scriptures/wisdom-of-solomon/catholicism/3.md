## In the Hand of God

The opening of chapter 3 — "The souls of the righteous are in the hand of God, and no torment will ever touch them" (3:1) — is one of the most frequently read passages in Catholic funeral liturgy. The Church reads this as scriptural support for the doctrine of the intermediate state, the communion of saints, and the hope of resurrection. The passage affirms that death is not annihilation but transition to divine protection.

## Suffering and Purification

The image of the righteous being tested like gold in a furnace (3:5-6) supports Catholic teaching on purification, including the doctrine of purgatory. While the text does not explicitly describe purgatory, the theological logic — that suffering can be redemptive and preparatory for glory — undergirds the Catholic understanding of post-mortem purification. The Council of Trent appealed to this broader theological framework.

## The Barren Woman and the Eunuch

Verses 13-14 bless the barren woman who is undefiled and the eunuch who has committed no lawless deed, promising them special grace. Catholic interpretation sees here a scriptural foundation for the honor given to consecrated virginity and celibacy. The passage transcends the Old Testament assumption that childlessness is a curse, pointing toward the New Testament valuation of chosen celibacy for the sake of the Kingdom (Matthew 19:12).
