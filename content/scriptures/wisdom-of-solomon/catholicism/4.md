## Virtue over Length of Days

Chapter 4 teaches that a short life lived in virtue surpasses a long life spent in wickedness. Catholic moral theology finds here a corrective to the assumption that earthly prosperity signals divine favor. The chapter supports the Churchs teaching that holiness, not longevity or worldly success, is the measure of a life well lived — a principle embodied in the many young saints canonized throughout Catholic history.

## The Translation of the Righteous

The passage describing the righteous one who is "caught up" lest evil change his understanding (4:10-14) is read by Catholic tradition in light of Enochs translation (Genesis 5:24) and Elijahs assumption (2 Kings 2:11). The Churchs doctrine of the Assumption of Mary, while based primarily on Tradition, finds consonance with this texts logic that God may take the holy to himself before corruption can touch them.

## The Judgment to Come

The chapters closing verses anticipate the final judgment, when the wicked will see the righteous exalted and realize their error too late. Catholic eschatology draws on this passage to affirm the reality of particular judgment at death and general judgment at the end of time. The reversal of fortunes depicted here reinforces the Churchs teaching on the last things — death, judgment, heaven, and hell.
