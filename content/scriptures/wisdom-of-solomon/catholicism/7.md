## Solomons Common Humanity

Chapter 7 opens with Solomon affirming his ordinary human origin — formed in the womb, born crying, nursed like every mortal. Catholic tradition reads this as underscoring the accessibility of wisdom: it is not reserved for an elite but offered to all who seek it in humility and prayer. This democratic quality of wisdom corresponds to the universal call to holiness emphasized by the Second Vatican Council in Lumen Gentium.

## Wisdom above All Riches

Solomons declaration that he chose wisdom over wealth, power, and even health (7:7-12) provides scriptural grounding for the religious vow of poverty and the Catholic tradition of renunciation. The saints who abandoned worldly goods — Francis of Assisi, Clare, Ignatius — lived out Solomons insight that wisdom is an inexhaustible treasure whose friendship with God surpasses all material possessions.

## The Twenty-One Attributes of Sophia

The great hymn to Wisdom (7:22-30), listing twenty-one divine attributes, is one of the most theologically significant passages in the deuterocanonical literature. Catholic Christology reads these verses as a prefiguration of the Logos: Wisdom is "a breath of the power of God, a pure emanation of the glory of the Almighty" (7:25), language echoed in the Nicene Creed ("Light from Light"). The author of Hebrews drew on this passage to describe Christ as "the radiance of Gods glory and the exact representation of his being" (Hebrews 1:3).
