## The Call to Righteousness

The Wisdom of Solomon opens with an exhortation to love justice, directed to the rulers of the earth. Catholic interpretation, guided by the Magisterium and the patristic tradition, reads this opening as a universal call to holiness. The Catechism of the Catholic Church draws on the books teaching that God created humanity for incorruption (cf. 1:13-14; 2:23) to support the doctrine that death was not part of Gods original plan for creation but entered through sin.

## The Holy Spirit of Discipline

Verses 4-5 describe a "holy spirit of discipline" that withdraws from the deceitful. Catholic theology finds here an early witness to the Spirits sanctifying role. The language anticipates the sacramental theology of Confirmation, in which the Holy Spirit strengthens believers for lives of disciplined virtue. The Church Fathers, particularly Ambrose, cited this passage in catechetical instruction.

## Death and the Devils Envy

The chapters closing declaration that "through the devils envy death entered the world" (1:13-16, developed in 2:24) is foundational for Catholic teaching on original sin. The Council of Trent cited this understanding in its decree on original sin, and the passage remains central to Catholic anthropology — death is an intruder, not part of Gods design.
