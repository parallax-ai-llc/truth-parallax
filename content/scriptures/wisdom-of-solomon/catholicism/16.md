## The Quail and the Manna

Chapter 16 contrasts Egypts punishment through loathsome animals with Israels nourishment through quail and manna. Catholic sacramental theology reads the manna as a type of the Eucharist — bread from heaven that sustains Gods people on their pilgrimage. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 1094) includes the manna among the Old Testament types of the Eucharist, and the Wisdom of Solomons theological interpretation of manna enriches this typological reading.

## The Bronze Serpent as Sign

The bronze serpent episode (16:5-7) is interpreted as a "sign of salvation" that pointed beyond itself to God as the true healer. Catholic exegesis follows Jesus own interpretation in John 3:14-15, reading the bronze serpent as a type of Christ lifted up on the cross. The passage also supports the Catholic theology of sacramental signs: physical objects can mediate divine grace, but only because Gods power works through them, not because of any inherent magical quality.

## "Not Crops but Your Word"

The declaration that it is Gods word, not the production of crops, that preserves the faithful (16:26) connects with Catholic Eucharistic theology at a deep level. In the Mass, the words of consecration transform bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ. The Wisdom of Solomon anticipates this sacramental logic: material sustenance is ultimately secondary to the word of God that gives and sustains life.
