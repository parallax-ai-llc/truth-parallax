## The Prison of Darkness

Chapter 17 describes the plague of darkness in Egypt with extraordinary literary power. The Egyptians were bound not by physical chains but by darkness and the terror of their own guilty conscience. Catholic moral theology finds here a vivid illustration of the effects of mortal sin: it darkens the intellect, weakens the will, and imprisons the soul in fear. The chapter functions as a dramatic meditation on the spiritual reality behind the physical plague.

## Conscience as Inner Light

The chapters emphasis on the terrors of guilty conscience resonates with Catholic teaching on the natural moral law. The Catechism (CCC 1776-1802) teaches that conscience is the voice of God within, enabling every person to recognize good and evil. When conscience is violated, it does not fall silent but becomes a source of torment — exactly the experience described in this chapter. The Egyptians darkness is ultimately interior: the absence of moral truth.

## Light and Darkness in Catholic Spirituality

Catholic mystical tradition has drawn on light-darkness symbolism from many biblical sources, and the Wisdom of Solomons extended darkness narrative contributes to this tradition. John of the Crosss "dark night of the soul" involves a purifying darkness that differs fundamentally from the punitive darkness of Egypt — yet both traditions affirm that only divine light can ultimately dispel the darkness of human ignorance and sin.
