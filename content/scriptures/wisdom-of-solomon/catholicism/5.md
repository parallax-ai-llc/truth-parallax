## The Confession of the Wicked

Chapter 5 portrays the wicked at the final judgment, confessing in anguish that they were fools to mock the righteous. Catholic liturgy and preaching have drawn extensively on this dramatic scene, which gives literary form to the Churchs teaching on the four last things. The wicked discover that their wealth and arrogance have vanished like a shadow, while the righteous live forever.

## The Divine Warrior

The depiction of God arming himself with creation as his weaponry (5:17-23) — wearing righteousness as a breastplate and taking holiness as a shield — is read by Catholic tradition as both a theophany and a type of Christs victory. The passage influenced Pauls description of the armor of God (Ephesians 6:10-17), and Catholic exegetes note the transformation from a warrior God to the spiritual armor given to believers.

## Cosmic Eschatology

The chapters vision of creation joining Gods battle — with thunderbolts, hailstones, and raging waters — supports Catholic teaching on the cosmic scope of redemption. The material world is not irrelevant to salvation but participates in divine justice. This resonates with Catholic sacramental theology, which holds that material realities can be instruments of grace and agents of Gods purposes.
