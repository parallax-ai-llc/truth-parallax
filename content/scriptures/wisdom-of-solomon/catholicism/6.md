## The Accountability of Rulers

Chapter 6 warns kings and judges that their authority comes from the Most High and that they will be judged more strictly than ordinary people (6:1-8). Catholic social teaching draws on this principle: political authority is not absolute but delegated, and rulers are accountable to God for how they exercise power. This passage has informed papal encyclicals on justice and governance from Leo XIII to Francis.

## Wisdom Seeks the Worthy

The personification of Wisdom as one who "graciously appears to them in their paths and meets them in every thought" (6:16) resonates with Catholic teaching on prevenient grace — the divine initiative that precedes and enables human response. The Catechism teaches that God gives every person the grace necessary for salvation, and this chapter portrays Wisdom as actively pursuing those who are open to receiving her.

## The Sorites of Wisdom

The chain argument of verses 17-20 — from desire to discipline to love to law-keeping to immortality to nearness to God — illustrates the Catholic understanding of the spiritual life as progressive growth in holiness. This graduated ascent echoes the mystical tradition from Pseudo-Dionysius through John of the Cross, in which the soul advances through stages of purification, illumination, and union with God.
