## Overview

The Catholic Church recognizes the Wisdom of Solomon as deuterocanonical Scripture, affirmed at the Councils of Hippo (393), Carthage (397), and definitively at the Council of Trent (1546). It holds the same canonical authority as any other book of the Old Testament and is read regularly in the liturgy, especially in contexts addressing death, judgment, and the communion of saints.

## Theological Significance

Catholic theology draws heavily on the Wisdom of Solomon for several doctrinal areas:

- **Immortality of the soul**: The book's teaching that "the souls of the righteous are in the hand of God" (3:1) supports Catholic doctrine on the intermediate state and purgatory
- **Sophia Christology**: The personification of Wisdom in chapters 7–9 is read typologically as prefiguring Christ, the Wisdom of God (1 Corinthians 1:24)
- **Natural theology**: The argument in chapter 13 that God can be known through creation anticipates the First Vatican Council's teaching on natural revelation
- **Critique of idolatry**: Chapters 13–15 provide a philosophical framework for understanding false worship that influenced scholastic theology

## Liturgical Use

The Wisdom of Solomon appears frequently in the Roman Lectionary, particularly in readings for feasts of martyrs, the commemoration of the faithful departed, and the Common of Holy Men and Women. The passage "the souls of the righteous are in the hand of God" (3:1-9) is among the most commonly read at Catholic funeral liturgies.
