## The Folly of Nature Worship

Chapter 13 opens the books extended critique of idolatry (chapters 13-15) by addressing those who worship the elements of nature — fire, wind, stars, water, or the luminaries of heaven — as gods. While the author considers these worshippers less culpable than those who worship handmade idols, he still finds them blameworthy: from the beauty and grandeur of created things, they should have reasoned to the existence of the Creator.

## Natural Theology

Verses 1-9 present one of the earliest sustained arguments for natural theology in Jewish or Christian literature. The reasoning parallels Pauls argument in Romans 1:19-20 that Gods invisible qualities are clearly seen from what has been made. Protestant scholars have long debated the relationship between these passages, with many concluding that Paul was directly influenced by the Wisdom of Solomon. The passage also anticipates the natural theology arguments of Thomas Aquinas and later Christian philosophy.

## From Admiration to Error

The chapter then turns to the craftsman who carves an idol from wood, the same material he uses for fuel and cooking. The irony is mordant: the artisan prays for health and life to a thing more lifeless than himself. Protestant interpreters connect this polemic to Isaiahs satire on idol-making (Isaiah 44:9-20) and note that the Wisdom of Solomon provides a more philosophically developed version of the same critique.
