## Wisdom in Israels History

Chapter 10 transitions from Solomons prayer to a survey of salvation history, tracing Wisdoms role in guiding and delivering the righteous from Adam through Moses. Notably, the chapter never names these figures directly, referring instead to "the first-formed father," "a righteous man" (Noah), and "a nation of oppressors" (Egypt). Protestant interpreters see this as a literary technique that universalizes the narrative, making it about the pattern of divine wisdom rescuing the faithful rather than merely retelling familiar stories.

## Individual Portraits

Each biblical figure is presented as one whom Wisdom protected, guided, or delivered: she guarded Adam after his transgression, steered Noah through the flood, strengthened Abraham during his trial, rescued Lot from Sodom, accompanied Jacob in his wanderings, and vindicated Joseph in Egypt. Protestant commentators note how this recasting places Wisdom — rather than direct divine intervention — at the center of the narrative, reflecting the authors sophisticated theological vision.

## The Exodus as Wisdoms Greatest Act

The chapter climaxes with Wisdoms role in the Exodus, where she entered the soul of Moses, withstood kings with wonders and signs, led the people through the Red Sea, and drowned their enemies. This sets up the extended meditation on the Exodus in chapters 11-19. Protestant scholars observe that the chapter functions as a bridge between the philosophical first half of the book and the historical second half.
