## Virtue over Longevity

Chapter 4 develops the theme that a virtuous life, even if cut short, is superior to a long life lived in wickedness. This inverts conventional Old Testament wisdom, which typically associated long life with divine blessing. Protestant commentators note the theological development here — the author is working through the problem of righteous suffering more radically than the canonical wisdom books.

## The Righteous Taken Early

The striking claim that God sometimes takes the righteous early to spare them from corruption (4:10-14) echoes the story of Enochs translation in Genesis 5:24. Protestant interpreters read this as an expression of divine providence, though they are cautious about building doctrine on this apocryphal text. The passage has nevertheless provided comfort in pastoral contexts of untimely death.

## The Reversal at Judgment

The chapter concludes by looking forward to a final judgment where the wicked will see the exaltation of those they despised and will be filled with remorse. This eschatological perspective connects with Jesus parable of the rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19-31) and with broader New Testament teaching about the reversal of earthly fortunes.
