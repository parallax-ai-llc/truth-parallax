## Overview

Protestant tradition classifies the Wisdom of Solomon among the Apocrypha — books considered useful for edification but not part of the inspired canonical Scriptures. This position was formalized during the Reformation, though Luther included the Apocrypha in his Bible translation and the original 1611 King James Version printed it between the Testaments.

## Canonical Status

The Westminster Confession (1646) states that the apocryphal books "are of no authority in the Church of God, nor to be any otherwise approved, or made use of, than other human writings." The Anglican Thirty-Nine Articles (1563) take a softer position, affirming they may be read "for example of life and instruction of manners" but not used "to establish any doctrine."

## Interpretive Approach

Despite its non-canonical status, Protestant scholars recognize the Wisdom of Solomon's theological significance:

- **Bridge literature**: It connects Old Testament wisdom traditions with New Testament theology, particularly Paul's language about righteousness, immortality, and the knowledge of God
- **Hellenistic Jewish thought**: The book illustrates how Judaism engaged with Greek philosophy before the Christian era
- **New Testament background**: Passages in Romans 1, Hebrews 1, and Ephesians 6 show possible literary dependence on Wisdom of Solomon
