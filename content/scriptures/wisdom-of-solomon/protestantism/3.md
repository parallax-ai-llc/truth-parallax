## The Souls of the Righteous

Chapter 3 opens with one of the most beloved passages in the book: "The souls of the righteous are in the hand of God, and no torment will ever touch them" (3:1). Though Protestants do not accord this canonical authority, the passage has deeply shaped Christian funeral liturgy and theological reflection on the afterlife. The assurance that apparent death is in fact peace resonates with Pauls confidence in Philippians 1:21-23.

## Suffering as Divine Testing

The righteous who suffer are compared to gold tested in a furnace (3:5-6). Protestant interpreters connect this imagery to 1 Peter 1:7 and James 1:2-4, noting that the Wisdom of Solomon articulates a theology of suffering that the New Testament writers may have drawn upon. Suffering is not meaningless but disciplinary and preparatory.

## The Fate of the Ungodly

The second half of the chapter contrasts the hope of the righteous with the futility of the wicked, whose offspring are cursed and whose achievements come to nothing. Protestant readers appreciate the moral clarity while noting that the books strong emphasis on immortality goes beyond what is explicitly taught in the Hebrew canonical texts.
