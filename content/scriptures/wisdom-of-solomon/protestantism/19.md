## The Red Sea and New Creation

The final chapter recounts the crossing of the Red Sea and the drowning of the Egyptian army, interpreting these events as a transformation of creation itself. The author describes the elements being "re-tuned" like a harp, with earth becoming sea and sea becoming dry land, as God rearranges the natural order to save His people. Protestant interpreters find in this a striking theology of new creation — God is not bound by the fixed order of nature but can reshape it for redemptive purposes.

## Creation Transformed

The most theologically distinctive feature of the chapter is its interpretation of the plagues and the Exodus as a cosmic re-harmonization. Fire burns in water, water forgets its quenching nature, animals change their behavior — the entire created order is reconfigured. Protestant commentators connect this to Pauls vision of creation groaning and awaiting liberation (Romans 8:19-22) and to the new heavens and new earth promised in Revelation 21. The Wisdom of Solomon presents the Exodus not merely as a historical deliverance but as a foretaste of eschatological renewal.

## Conclusion

The book closes abruptly with the affirmation that God glorified and did not neglect His people, standing by them in every time and place (19:22). Protestant readers note the absence of a formal conclusion, which some scholars suggest indicates the work is unfinished. Nevertheless, the final note of divine faithfulness provides an appropriate ending: the God who guided His people through the Exodus continues to guide them, and Wisdom remains the agent of His providential care throughout all of history.
