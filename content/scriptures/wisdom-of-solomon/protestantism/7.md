## Solomon as Everyman

Chapter 7 begins with Solomon emphasizing his common humanity — he was born like all mortals, breathed the same air, and uttered the same first cry. Protestant interpreters note this rhetorical strategy: by stressing his ordinariness, the author underscores that wisdom is not a birthright of kings but a divine gift available to all who pray for it. This democratizing impulse resonates with the Protestant emphasis on the priesthood of all believers.

## The Incomparable Value of Wisdom

Solomon declares that he preferred Wisdom above scepters, thrones, riches, and precious gems (7:7-12). Protestant commentators connect this to Jesus parables of the pearl of great price (Matthew 13:45-46) and the treasure hidden in a field. The passage illustrates the biblical priority of spiritual goods over material wealth, a theme central to Protestant preaching from the Reformation onward.

## The Hymn to Sophia

Verses 22-30 contain the books most elevated description of Wisdom, attributing twenty-one qualities to her — intelligent, holy, unique, subtle, mobile, penetrating, and more. She is described as a breath of the power of God, a pure emanation of his glory, and an image of his goodness. Protestant scholars recognize the christological potential of this language, which the author of Hebrews may have drawn upon in describing Christ as "the radiance of Gods glory" (Hebrews 1:3).
