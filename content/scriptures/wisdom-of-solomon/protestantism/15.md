## Israels Fidelity Contrasted

Chapter 15 begins with a prayer of thanksgiving that Israel knows the true God: "But you, our God, are kind and true, patient, and ruling all things in mercy" (15:1). Even when Israel sins, they belong to God and know His power, and this knowledge itself restrains them. Protestant interpreters note the covenantal logic here — knowledge of God is not merely intellectual but relational, creating moral obligation and providing the basis for repentance.

## The Potter and the Clay

The chapter returns to the idol-maker, now focusing on the potter who fashions clay into both useful vessels and false gods from the same material. The author observes the absurdity: the potter himself was recently formed from earth and will soon return to it, yet he presumes to fashion gods from clay. Protestant commentators connect this imagery to Pauls use of the potter-and-clay metaphor in Romans 9:20-21, though Paul applies it to divine sovereignty rather than the critique of idolatry.

## Egyptian Animal Worship

The chapter concludes with the sharpest polemic, directed at those who worship animals — a clear reference to Egyptian religion. These worshippers are the most foolish of all, for they venerate creatures that are the least attractive of the animal kingdom. Protestant readers note that this critique sets up the final section of the book, where the plagues of Egypt are reinterpreted as divine judgments precisely fitted to Egypts sins.
