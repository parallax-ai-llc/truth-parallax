## The Exodus Retold

Chapter 11 begins the extended comparison between Gods treatment of the Israelites and His punishment of the Egyptians, a literary structure that will continue through the end of the book. The opening section recounts how God provided water from the rock for the thirsty Israelites, while the Egyptians suffered when the Nile was turned to blood. Protestant interpreters note the "measure for measure" principle at work: the very elements that punished Egypt became instruments of blessing for Israel.

## Divine Mercy and Restraint

The chapter contains a remarkable theological excursus on Gods mercy (11:17-26). God could have destroyed the wicked with bears, lions, or by a single breath, but He exercises restraint because He loves all things that exist and hates nothing He has made. Protestant readers find in these verses a profound statement of divine compassion that complements the canonical witness — God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked (Ezekiel 33:11).

## Gods Omnipotent Mercy

The closing verse — "You spare all things, for they are yours, O Lord, you who love the living" (11:26) — has resonated across Christian traditions. Protestant commentators value this as a powerful expression of Gods benevolent sovereignty, even while noting that it must be balanced with the biblical witness to divine judgment.
