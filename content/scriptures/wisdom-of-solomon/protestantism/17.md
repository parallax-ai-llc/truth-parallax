## The Plague of Darkness

Chapter 17 provides an extended, atmospheric description of the ninth plague — the three days of darkness over Egypt (Exodus 10:21-23). The author transforms the brief biblical account into a vivid literary portrait of psychological terror. The Egyptians were imprisoned not merely by physical darkness but by the darkness of their own guilty conscience. Protestant interpreters note the sophisticated literary artistry and the theological depth of equating external darkness with internal spiritual blindness.

## The Terror of Conscience

The chapter develops the theme that the wicked carry their own punishment within them. The Egyptians heard terrifying sounds, saw fearful apparitions, and found that neither fire nor starlight could penetrate the supernatural darkness. The author suggests that those who thought they could hide their sins under the cover of darkness discovered that darkness itself became their prison. Protestant commentators connect this to the New Testament teaching that judgment begins with the revelation of hidden things (Luke 12:2-3).

## Darkness as Theological Symbol

Throughout the chapter, darkness functions as a multivalent symbol: it represents ignorance of God, the bondage of sin, and the withdrawal of divine presence. Protestant readers note that this symbolic framework anticipates Johns Gospel, where light and darkness serve as primary metaphors for the encounter with Christ. The Wisdom of Solomons darkness narrative may have contributed to the early Christian vocabulary of spiritual illumination.
