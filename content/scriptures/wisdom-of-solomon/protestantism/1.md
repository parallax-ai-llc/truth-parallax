## Exhortation to Justice

The Wisdom of Solomon opens with a direct appeal to the rulers of the earth to love righteousness and seek the Lord with sincerity of heart. Protestant interpreters, while noting the books apocryphal status, recognize the theological power of this opening exhortation, which echoes the canonical Proverbs in its insistence that the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom.

## The Holy Spirit and Sin

Verses 4-5 introduce the concept that the Holy Spirit of discipline will flee from the deceitful and withdraw from foolish thoughts. Protestant commentators note the sophisticated pneumatology here, which anticipates New Testament teaching about grieving the Spirit (Ephesians 4:30). The chapter establishes a moral framework in which sin is fundamentally self-destructive.

## Death as the Consequence of Ungodliness

The closing verses (12-16) present a striking teaching: God did not make death, and destruction is not the destiny He intended for humanity. Death entered through the envy of the devil — a passage that influenced early Christian reflection on the Fall. Protestant readers value this as theological background for Pauls argument in Romans 5:12, even while maintaining the texts non-canonical status.
