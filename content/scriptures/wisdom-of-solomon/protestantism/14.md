## The Origin of Idolatry

Chapter 14 offers a remarkable etiological account of how idol worship began. The author proposes two origins: a grieving father who made an image of his dead child and eventually treated it as a god, and rulers who demanded worship of their images across their empires. Protestant commentators recognize this as an early attempt at what modern scholars would call the sociology of religion — explaining religious phenomena through human psychological and political dynamics.

## The Moral Consequences of Idolatry

Verses 22-31 present a devastating catalog of the moral corruption that follows from idolatry: murder, adultery, theft, deceit, disorder, perjury, and ingratitude. The author argues that idol worship is both the beginning and the end of every evil — it initiates moral decline and compounds it. Protestant interpreters connect this passage closely with Romans 1:21-32, where Paul presents a strikingly similar argument about the link between false worship and moral degradation.

## The Sailors Prayer

The chapter opens with an evocative scene of a sailor praying to a wooden idol for safe passage while traveling on a ship made of wood — the ship, crafted by Wisdom, is more reliable than the idol. Protestant readers note the sharp irony: human skill guided by Wisdom produces a seaworthy vessel, while the same material shaped into an idol produces only delusion.
