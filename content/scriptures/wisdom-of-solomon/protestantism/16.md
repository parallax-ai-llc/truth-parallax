## Contrasting Divine Judgments

Chapter 16 resumes the synkrisis (comparison) between Egypts punishments and Israels blessings. While the Egyptians were tormented by loathsome creatures and lost their appetite, Israel received quail as a delicacy and manna as heavenly bread. Protestant interpreters observe the careful literary structure: each Egyptian plague corresponds to a contrasting act of divine provision for Israel, demonstrating Gods discriminating justice.

## The Bronze Serpent

The chapter recounts the episode of the fiery serpents in the wilderness (Numbers 21:4-9), noting that the bronze serpent was a "sign of salvation" to remind Israel that God alone is the savior (16:5-7). Protestant commentators value this passage for its connection to Jesus own interpretation of the bronze serpent in John 3:14-15. The author insists it was not the symbol itself but Gods mercy that healed — an anti-idolatry point consistent with the books broader argument.

## Manna and the Word of God

The description of manna reaches theological heights in verse 26: "It is not the production of crops that nourishes a person, but your word that preserves those who trust in you." Protestant readers recognize the direct parallel to Deuteronomy 8:3, which Jesus quoted in His temptation (Matthew 4:4). The Wisdom of Solomon thus interprets the manna miracle as a lesson about dependence on Gods word rather than material sustenance.
