## The Philosophy of the Wicked

Chapter 2 presents a vivid speech placed in the mouths of the ungodly, who reason that life is short, death is final, and therefore pleasure should be pursued without restraint. Protestant scholars note the resemblance to Epicurean philosophy and to the skepticism of Ecclesiastes, though here the reasoning is presented as morally bankrupt rather than as genuine philosophical inquiry.

## Persecution of the Righteous

The ungodly resolve to oppress the poor, the widow, and the elderly, and to persecute the righteous man who reproaches them for their sins. Verses 12-20 contain language strikingly similar to the Passion narratives — the just one calls God his Father, claims to be Gods child, and is tested with torture and death. Many Protestant commentators acknowledge this as possible background for the New Testament portrayal of Christs suffering.

## Blindness and Self-Deception

The chapter concludes by diagnosing the root problem: the wicked are blinded by their own malice. They fail to understand Gods plan for immortality because they have chosen a covenant with death. Protestant interpreters highlight the pastoral relevance of this diagnosis — that sin distorts perception and leads to increasingly destructive choices.
