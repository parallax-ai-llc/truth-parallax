## Solomons Ordinariness

Chapter 7 opens with Solomons emphasis on his common humanity, establishing that wisdom is accessible to all who seek it. Orthodox theology affirms the universal offer of grace through the divine energies: theosis is the vocation of every human person, not a privilege of the elite. This democratic quality of wisdom resonates with the Orthodox practice of reading the Wisdom of Solomon at Vespers for major feasts, making its teaching available to the entire worshipping community.

## Sophia and the Divine Energies

The great hymn to Wisdom (7:22-30), describing her as a breath of Gods power, a pure emanation of divine glory, and an image of divine goodness, is central to Orthodox Sophiology. Sergius Bulgakov and Pavel Florensky developed elaborate Sophiological theologies drawing on this passage, though their work remains controversial in some Orthodox circles. More broadly, the attributes ascribed to Sophia correspond to what Palamite theology calls the uncreated divine energies — the means by which God is genuinely present to and active in creation without compromising divine transcendence.

## Christological and Pneumatological Readings

Orthodox Christology reads the twenty-one attributes of Sophia in light of the Logos theology of Johns prologue and the cosmic Christ of Colossians 1:15-20. Sophia is the eternal Wisdom through whom all things were made and in whom all things hold together. Simultaneously, the Spirits role is implied: Wisdom as "holy spirit" (7:22) points toward the pneumatological dimension of Gods creative and redemptive action. Orthodox trinitarian theology holds both readings together without reducing one to the other.
