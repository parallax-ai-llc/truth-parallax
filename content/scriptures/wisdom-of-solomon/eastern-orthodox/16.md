## Contrasting Judgments

Chapter 16 continues the synkrisis between Egypts plagues and Israels blessings, demonstrating how the same creation serves opposite purposes. Orthodox theology sees here an expression of the principle that creation is inherently responsive to its Creator: nature is not an autonomous system but a medium through which God acts. The same water that drowns can baptize; the same fire that destroys can illuminate.

## The Bronze Serpent and the Cross

The interpretation of the bronze serpent as a "sign of salvation" (16:6) that pointed to God as the true healer is central to Orthodox typology. Following Jesus own interpretation in John 3:14, Orthodox iconography and theology see the bronze serpent as a type of the Cross. The Feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross celebrates precisely this reality: the instrument of death becomes the instrument of salvation, just as the bronze serpent brought healing rather than harm.

## Manna and the Eucharist

The theological interpretation of manna — that "it is not the production of crops that nourishes a person, but your word" (16:26) — resonates with the Orthodox understanding of the Divine Liturgy. In the Eucharist, ordinary bread and wine become the Body and Blood of Christ through the power of the Holy Spirit invoked in the epiclesis. The Wisdom of Solomon anticipates this sacramental logic: true sustenance comes from Gods word, not from material processes alone.
