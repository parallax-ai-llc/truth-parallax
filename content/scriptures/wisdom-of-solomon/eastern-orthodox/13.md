## Knowledge of God through Creation

Chapter 13 argues that the beauty of created things should lead rational beings to knowledge of the Creator. Orthodox theology embraces this natural theology while insisting that it is completed and perfected by divine revelation. The Fathers, especially Basil the Great in his Hexaemeron, taught that creation is a book that speaks of its Author — but it is the Holy Spirit who gives eyes to read it.

## The Tragedy of Misdirected Worship

The author presents nature worshippers as less culpable than idol worshippers but still blameworthy. Orthodox theology, while affirming the universal offer of grace, teaches that failure to recognize the Creator behind creation represents a fundamental spiritual error. The theology of icons provides a constructive alternative: rather than worshipping created beauty directly, the faithful venerate icons that point through the visible image to the invisible prototype.

## The Irony of the Idol-Maker

The satirical portrait of the craftsman who carves a god from leftover wood continues the prophetic tradition and resonates with the Orthodox iconoclastic controversies. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787) distinguished between the veneration of icons (which honor the person depicted) and idolatry (which attributes divine power to the material object). The Wisdom of Solomons critique targets precisely the confusion that the Council sought to resolve.
