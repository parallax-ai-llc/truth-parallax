## Progressive Judgment

Chapter 12 explains that God punished the Canaanites gradually, sending plagues little by little, to give them time for repentance. Orthodox theology reads this as an expression of Gods pedagogical love — even judgment serves an educative purpose. The canon of Saint Andrew of Crete, chanted during Great Lent, similarly presents Gods dealings with sinners as patient, measured, and oriented toward conversion rather than destruction.

## Gods Sovereignty and Mercy

The declaration that Gods mastery over all makes Him merciful to all (12:16) is foundational for the Orthodox understanding of divine power. True sovereignty is expressed not through domination but through compassion. This theological insight informs the Orthodox critique of any political or ecclesiastical authority that confuses power with coercion — authentic authority, modeled on Gods own governance, is always merciful.

## Hope for Israel

The author concludes that Gods measured dealings with the Canaanites should give Israel hope for divine mercy toward themselves (12:19-22). Orthodox pastoral theology applies this principle in the sacrament of Confession: if God shows patience even to those outside the covenant, how much more will He extend mercy to the baptized who return in repentance. The Wisdom of Solomon supports the Orthodox conviction that no sin is beyond the reach of divine forgiveness.
