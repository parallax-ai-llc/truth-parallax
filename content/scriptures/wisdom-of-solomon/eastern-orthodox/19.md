## Creation Transformed

The final chapter describes the Red Sea crossing as a cosmic transformation: the elements are re-tuned, land becomes sea and sea becomes dry land, fire burns in water. Orthodox theology reads this as an anticipation of the eschatological renewal of creation — the transfiguration of the cosmos that begins with Christs Resurrection and will be completed at the Second Coming. The icon of the Transfiguration shows creation itself illuminated by divine light, a foretaste of the cosmic re-harmonization described here.

## Sacramental Cosmology

The image of creation being reshaped while maintaining its essential harmony supports the Orthodox sacramental understanding of the world. In the sacraments, natural elements — water, bread, wine, oil — are taken up into a new order of grace without ceasing to be themselves. The Wisdom of Solomons portrayal of the elements rearranged at the Exodus provides a scriptural foundation for this sacramental cosmology: matter is not opposed to spirit but is capable of being transfigured by divine action.

## The Enduring Faithfulness of God

The book closes with the affirmation that God exalted and glorified His people, standing by them at all times and in all places (19:22). Orthodox theology reads this as a statement of divine faithfulness that extends from Israel to the Church. The Wisdom of Solomon, read liturgically throughout the Orthodox calendar, serves as a continuous reminder of this faithfulness — the same Wisdom who delivered Israel continues to guide, protect, and illumine the Church in every generation.
