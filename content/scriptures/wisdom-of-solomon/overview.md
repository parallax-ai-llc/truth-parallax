## Overview

The Wisdom of Solomon is a deuterocanonical book composed in Greek, likely in Alexandria, Egypt, during the late first century BCE or early first century CE. Attributed pseudepigraphically to King Solomon, the work represents one of the finest examples of Jewish wisdom literature composed in the Hellenistic period, blending Hebrew theological traditions with Greek philosophical concepts.

## Structure

The book is traditionally divided into three major sections:

1. **The Book of Eschatology** (Chapters 1–5): The destinies of the righteous and the wicked, introducing the concept of immortality
2. **The Book of Wisdom** (Chapters 6–9): Solomon's pursuit of Wisdom, personified as a divine figure, culminating in his great prayer
3. **The Book of History** (Chapters 10–19): A retelling of Israel's salvation history, especially the Exodus, with an extended critique of idolatry

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Wisdom of Solomon occupies a distinctive position across religious traditions:

- **Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy**: Accepted as deuterocanonical Scripture, read liturgically and cited authoritatively
- **Protestantism**: Classified among the Apocrypha; valued for edification but not considered canonical
- **Judaism**: Not included in the Tanakh, though it reflects Jewish wisdom traditions and Alexandrian Jewish thought
- **Islam**: Resonates with Quranic themes of divine wisdom and the folly of idol worship
- **Gnosticism**: The personification of Sophia (Wisdom) became central to Gnostic cosmological systems

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Immortality | The righteous soul endures beyond death; a groundbreaking development in Jewish theology |
| Personified Wisdom | Sophia as a divine attribute, agent of creation, and companion of God |
| Righteousness vs. Wickedness | The contrasting fates of the just and the ungodly |
| Idolatry | An extended philosophical critique of image worship and its origins |
| Salvation History | God's providential care for Israel through the Exodus and wilderness |
| Divine Providence | God's governance of history for the benefit of the faithful |
