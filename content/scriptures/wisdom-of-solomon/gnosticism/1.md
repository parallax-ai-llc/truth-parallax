## Righteousness as Gnosis

Gnostic interpreters read the opening exhortation to love righteousness as an invitation to seek *gnosis* — salvific knowledge of ones true divine origin. The call to sincerity of heart is understood not as moral instruction but as an appeal to awaken from the ignorance imposed by the material world and its rulers. True righteousness, in the Gnostic reading, consists in recognizing the divine spark within and orienting ones life toward the Pleroma (divine fullness).

## The Spirit of Wisdom

The chapters reference to a holy spirit of discipline that withdraws from the deceitful is appropriated by Gnostic systems as evidence of the divine pneuma (spirit) that dwells within the spiritual person (*pneumatikos*). Valentinian Gnosticism taught that the spiritual seed planted by Sophia in certain human beings could be nurtured or quenched. The withdrawal of the spirit described here mirrors the Gnostic fear that the divine spark can be dimmed through attachment to material existence.

## Death and the Demiurge

The assertion that God did not make death takes on radical significance in Gnostic reading. If the transcendent God did not create death, then death belongs to the realm of the Demiurge — the ignorant creator of the material world. Gnostic interpreters seized on this passage to support their fundamental claim that the material world, including mortality, is the product of a lower, flawed deity, not the true God of the Pleroma.
