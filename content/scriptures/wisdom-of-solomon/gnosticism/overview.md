## Overview

The Wisdom of Solomon's personification of Sophia (Wisdom) as a cosmic, quasi-divine figure profoundly influenced Gnostic thought. Gnostic systems appropriated and radically transformed the book's portrait of Wisdom as God's companion, agent of creation, and savior of the righteous, recasting Sophia as a central figure in elaborate cosmological dramas of fall, exile, and redemption.

## Sophia in Gnostic Mythology

In the Wisdom of Solomon, Sophia is a radiant emanation of God's glory who orders the cosmos and guides the righteous (7:22–8:1). Gnostic traditions took this personification further:

- **Valentinian Gnosticism**: Sophia is the lowest Aeon of the Pleroma whose desire to know the Father produces the Demiurge and, ultimately, the flawed material world. Her redemption mirrors the soul's return to the divine fullness.
- **Sethian Gnosticism**: Sophia's fall generates the ignorant creator Yaldabaoth, and her repentance sets in motion the plan to rescue the divine sparks trapped in matter.
- **The Pistis Sophia**: An extended narrative of Sophia's descent through the archons and her eventual rescue by Christ.

## Key Appropriations

The Wisdom of Solomon's language provided Gnostic authors with ready-made theological vocabulary:

- **"Breath of the power of God"** (7:25): Reinterpreted as the emanation process within the Pleroma
- **"Image of his goodness"** (7:26): Applied to the Aeons as reflections of the transcendent Father
- **Light-darkness dualism** (chapters 17–18): Mapped onto the Gnostic opposition between the realm of light (Pleroma) and the darkness of material existence
- **Critique of idolatry** (chapters 13–15): Extended to include the worship of the Demiurge himself as a form of ignorance

## Significance

The Wisdom of Solomon stands as one of the most important pre-Gnostic texts, demonstrating how Jewish wisdom speculation about a feminine divine mediator could be developed into full-scale Gnostic mythology. The trajectory from biblical Sophia to Gnostic Sophia remains one of the most studied developments in the history of early Christianity.
