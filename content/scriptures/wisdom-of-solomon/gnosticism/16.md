## The Serpent as Ambiguous Symbol

Chapter 16s treatment of the bronze serpent (16:5-7) carries particular Gnostic resonance. In Ophite and Naassene Gnostic traditions, the serpent was a positive figure — the bringer of *gnosis* in the Garden of Eden. The Wisdom of Solomons acknowledgment that the bronze serpent was a "sign of salvation" is read by these groups as confirming the serpents salvific role. The insistence that healing came from God rather than the object itself is accepted, but the serpent symbol itself is embraced as pointing to the liberating knowledge that the Demiurge sought to withhold.

## Manna as Spiritual Nourishment

The declaration that "it is not the production of crops that nourishes a person, but your word" (16:26) is read by Gnostic interpreters as affirming the superiority of spiritual over material sustenance. The "word" that truly nourishes is *gnosis* — knowledge of the true God and ones own divine origin. Material food sustains the body, which belongs to the Demiurge; the divine word nourishes the pneumatic spark, which belongs to the Pleroma. The Wisdom of Solomon here articulates the fundamental Gnostic distinction between carnal and spiritual sustenance.

## Creation as Instrument

The broader structure of chapter 16 — showing how the same elements serve opposite purposes — is read by Gnostic interpreters as evidence that the material world is fundamentally unstable and contingent. The elements have no fixed nature but respond to divine command. This instability confirms the Gnostic view that the material world lacks ultimate reality and will eventually dissolve when the divine sparks have been gathered back to the Pleroma.
