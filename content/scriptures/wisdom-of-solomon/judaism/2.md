## The Denial of Afterlife

Chapter 2 puts in the mouths of the ungodly a philosophy that life is short, death is final, and pleasure is the only rational pursuit. Jewish scholars recognize this as a caricature of Epicurean philosophy, adapted to serve the authors polemical purposes. The speech also echoes the pessimism of Ecclesiastes (Qohelet), but whereas Qohelet struggled honestly with lifes absurdity, the ungodly here use their nihilism to justify oppression and violence.

## The Persecution of the Righteous

The depiction of the righteous man persecuted for his piety (2:12-20) reflects real experiences of the Jewish community in Alexandria, where relations between Jews and Greeks were often tense. Jewish readers see here not a messianic prophecy but a portrait of the faithful Jew who is mocked and threatened for adherence to Torah. The experience of the righteous sufferer resonates with the broader Jewish tradition from the Psalms of lament through the martyrologies of the Maccabean period.

## Through the Devils Envy

The concluding verse attributes deaths entry into the world to "the devils envy" (2:24), reflecting the development of angelology and demonology in Second Temple Judaism. This interpretation of the Genesis narrative — identifying the serpent with a supernatural adversary — is found also in 1 Enoch and the Life of Adam and Eve. Jewish scholarship treats this as evidence of the diversity of theological opinion in the late Second Temple period.
