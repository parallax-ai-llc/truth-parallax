## The Contrasting Fates

Chapter 16 continues the comparison between Egypts plagues and Israels blessings. Jewish scholars recognize the midreshic character of this retelling: the author does not simply reproduce the Exodus narrative but interprets it theologically, drawing out lessons about divine justice and mercy that are implicit in the original text. This creative reinterpretation is characteristic of Jewish engagement with Scripture.

## The Bronze Serpent

The interpretation of the bronze serpent as a "sign of salvation" that pointed to God rather than possessing healing power in itself (16:5-7) is significant for Jewish theology. The author preempts any suggestion that the bronze serpent was a magical object — healing came from God alone. The rabbis similarly interpreted the Mishnah (Rosh Hashanah 3:8): "Does the serpent kill or heal? Rather, when Israel looked upward and submitted their hearts to their Father in heaven, they were healed."

## Manna and Divine Sustenance

The declaration that it is Gods word, not agricultural production, that sustains the faithful (16:26) echoes Deuteronomy 8:3. Jewish scholars note that this theological interpretation of the manna reflects the broader Jewish conviction that Torah — the word of God — is the ultimate source of life. The rabbinic tradition of Torah study as the highest human activity finds an early precedent in this passage.
