## The Value of a Short Virtuous Life

Chapter 4 continues the argument that virtue surpasses longevity. Jewish wisdom tradition typically associated long life with divine favor (Proverbs 3:16), but the Wisdom of Solomon inverts this expectation. Jewish scholars see this as a response to the problem of righteous Jews who died young, particularly during periods of persecution. The Maccabean martyrs, who chose death over apostasy, loom in the background.

## The Translation of Enoch

The passage about the righteous one taken early by God (4:10-14), alluding to Enochs translation in Genesis 5:24, reflects the rich tradition of Enoch speculation in Second Temple Judaism. The Enochic literature (1 Enoch, 2 Enoch) developed elaborate traditions about Enochs heavenly journey and transformation. The Wisdom of Solomon draws on this tradition more soberly, using Enochs story to illustrate the principle that God may remove the righteous from a corrupt world.

## Eschatological Reversal

The chapter closes with the wicked recognizing their error at judgment. Jewish scholars note that this eschatological vision reflects the development of judgment and afterlife beliefs in the late Second Temple period. While mainstream rabbinic Judaism would later develop its own doctrines of *Olam Ha-Ba* (the World to Come) and *Gehinnom*, the Wisdom of Solomons contribution to this trajectory demonstrates the diversity and creativity of Jewish theological thought.
