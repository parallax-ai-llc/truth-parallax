## Solomons Humanity

Chapter 7 begins with Solomon emphasizing his ordinary mortal origin. Jewish scholars read this as a literary strategy within the pseudepigraphic convention: by attributing the work to Solomon while insisting on his ordinariness, the author makes the pursuit of wisdom universally accessible. The rhetorical message is that wisdom is not innate but acquired through prayer and devotion, available to any person regardless of status.

## Wisdom above All Treasures

Solomons preference for wisdom over wealth (7:7-12) extends the tradition of 1 Kings 3, where Solomon chose wisdom over long life, riches, or victory. Jewish readers connect this to the rabbinic valuation of Torah study above all other pursuits. The Mishnah (Avot 6:9) teaches that Torah is more precious than gold and pearls — a sentiment the Wisdom of Solomon anticipates in Hellenistic Jewish terms.

## The Hymn to Sophia

The twenty-one attributes of Wisdom (7:22-30) represent the most elaborate personification of divine Wisdom in Jewish literature. Jewish scholars debate whether this represents a hypostasis (a distinct divine entity) or a literary personification of divine attributes. The description of Wisdom as a "breath of the power of God" and "pure emanation of the glory of the Almighty" pushed the boundaries of Jewish monotheism and would later be appropriated by both Christian and Gnostic theologians in ways the author likely did not intend.
