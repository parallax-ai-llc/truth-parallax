## Midrashic Retelling

Chapter 10 retells Israelite history from Adam through Moses, identifying Wisdom as the hidden agent behind each act of deliverance. Jewish scholars recognize this as an early form of midrash — creative reinterpretation of biblical narrative for theological purposes. The technique of not naming the patriarchs is itself interpretively significant: it abstracts from specific historical figures to reveal a universal pattern of divine wisdom at work in human history.

## The Patriarchs and Wisdom

Each figure — Adam, Noah, Abraham, Lot, Jacob, Joseph, Moses — is recast as one whom Wisdom protected, guided, or vindicated. Jewish scholars note how the author selectively highlights episodes that illustrate Wisdoms salvific role while passing over others. This selective retelling is characteristic of Jewish interpretive tradition, where the goal is not comprehensive history but theological illumination.

## The Exodus as Wisdoms Culminating Act

The chapter climaxes with the Exodus, where Wisdom enters Moses soul and performs wonders. Jewish scholars observe that this represents a distinctive theology of the Exodus: rather than attributing deliverance directly to God or to Moses leadership, the author introduces Wisdom as the mediating agent. This mediated theology reflects the Hellenistic Jewish interest in divine intermediaries that would become a major theme in Philos writings.
