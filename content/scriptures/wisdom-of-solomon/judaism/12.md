## The Problem of the Canaanite Conquest

Chapter 12 addresses a theological difficulty: why did God not destroy the Canaanites immediately? The author argues that God acted gradually, giving the inhabitants time for repentance. Jewish scholars see this as a sophisticated theological response to a problem that troubled Jewish thinkers: the morality of the conquest narratives. The Wisdom of Solomon reframes divine judgment as patient, measured, and pedagogical.

## Gods Sovereignty and Mercy

The declaration that Gods strength is the source of justice and His sovereignty over all leads Him to spare all (12:16) anticipates rabbinic discussions of the relationship between divine justice (*middat ha-din*) and divine mercy (*middat ha-rachamim*). The rabbis taught that God created the world with both attributes, and the Wisdom of Solomon reflects this dual emphasis in a Hellenistic philosophical idiom.

## A Lesson in Hope

The chapter concludes that Gods patience with the Canaanites teaches Israel to hope in divine mercy for themselves (12:19-22). Jewish scholars note the pastoral wisdom here: the authors goal is not to explain the Canaanite conquest but to reassure his Jewish audience that the God who showed patience even to the wicked will surely show mercy to His covenant people. This pastoral orientation is characteristic of Jewish wisdom literature.
