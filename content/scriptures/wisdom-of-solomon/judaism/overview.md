## Overview

The Wisdom of Solomon is not part of the Jewish canon (Tanakh) and is classified among the external books (*sefarim hitsonim*). Nevertheless, it is a product of Jewish authorship — almost certainly composed by an Alexandrian Jew writing in Greek during the late Second Temple period. The book represents a remarkable synthesis of biblical wisdom traditions with Greek philosophical concepts, particularly Platonic ideas about the soul and Stoic notions of divine reason pervading the cosmos.

## Historical Context

The work emerged from the vibrant Jewish community of Alexandria, Egypt, which produced other significant texts including the Septuagint translation and the writings of Philo. Alexandrian Jews faced the challenge of maintaining their identity while engaging with the dominant Hellenistic culture, and the Wisdom of Solomon reflects this dual orientation — defending Jewish faith in language that educated Greeks could appreciate.

## Relationship to Canonical Wisdom Literature

While not canonical, the Wisdom of Solomon extends themes found in Proverbs, Job, and Ecclesiastes:

- **Proverbs**: The personification of Wisdom as a feminine divine figure (Proverbs 8) is developed further
- **Ecclesiastes**: The skepticism of Qohelet about death and justice is answered with the doctrine of immortality
- **Job**: The problem of righteous suffering receives a new resolution through the promise of eternal reward

## Scholarly Significance

Modern Jewish scholars study the Wisdom of Solomon as a window into Second Temple Judaism, Diaspora identity, and the development of ideas — particularly the afterlife and divine intermediaries — that would shape both rabbinic Judaism and early Christianity.
