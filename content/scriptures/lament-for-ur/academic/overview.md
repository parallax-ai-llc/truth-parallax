## Overview

The Lament for the Destruction of Ur is one of five extant Sumerian city laments, composed during the Isin-Larsa period (c. 2000–1900 BCE) in response to the fall of the Third Dynasty of Ur (c. 2004 BCE). The text is known from multiple cuneiform manuscripts, with the most complete editions assembled by Samuel Noah Kramer and later refined by Piotr Michalowski. The lament comprises 436 lines organized into eleven *kirugu* (stanzas), each terminated by a liturgical rubric.

## Key Scholarly Issues

### Historical Context
The Ur III dynasty ended when Elamite and Shimashkian forces overran Ur, capturing its last king, Ibbi-Sin. The lament was likely composed under the patronage of the succeeding Isin dynasty, which sought to legitimize its rule by presenting itself as the restorer of Ur's cult.

### Genre: The City Lament
The five Sumerian city laments (Ur, Sumer and Ur, Nippur, Eridu, Uruk) constitute a distinct literary genre characterized by:
- Attribution of destruction to deliberate divine decision
- Intercession by a goddess (typically the city's patron's consort)
- Vivid descriptions of physical destruction
- Concluding appeal for restoration

### Liturgical Function
The city laments appear to have been performed during ceremonies of temple restoration, ritually processing the trauma of destruction before consecrating rebuilt sacred spaces.
