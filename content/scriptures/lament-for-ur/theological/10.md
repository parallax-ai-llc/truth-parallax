The tenth kirugu introduces the theology of restoration — the hope that what has been destroyed can be rebuilt, that absent gods can return, that divine favor can be renewed. This theology is not based on evidence (the city is still in ruins) but on faith — the conviction that the gods who decreed destruction can also decree restoration. The appeal is both humble (acknowledging that the decree was just) and bold (insisting that mercy is also within the gods' power).
