Ningal's lament over the ruins represents a shift from theology to testimony. She is no longer arguing before the divine council but simply witnessing the consequences of its decree. This act of witness — seeing and naming the destruction — has its own theological significance: it refuses to let the catastrophe pass unnamed and unremembered. In a tradition where the gods decreed destruction, the act of mourning is itself a form of theological resistance: insisting that the suffering matters.
