## Overview

The Lament for Ur is uniquely well served by archaeological evidence. Leonard Woolley's extensive excavations at Ur (1922–1934) revealed destruction layers dating to the fall of the Ur III dynasty (c. 2004 BCE), providing material confirmation of the devastation the poem describes. The tablets preserving the text were found at multiple sites, indicating the lament's widespread liturgical use.

## Archaeological Evidence at Ur

### Destruction Layers
Woolley identified clear evidence of violent destruction dating to the end of the Ur III period:
- Burned buildings and collapsed walls in the residential quarters
- The Ekishnugal (temple of Nanna) showing evidence of damage and subsequent rebuilding
- Administrative tablets that abruptly cease, marking the end of bureaucratic activity

### The Ziggurat of Ur-Nammu
The great ziggurat, begun by Ur-Nammu and completed by Shulgi, survived the destruction — its massive mud-brick core was too substantial to be demolished entirely. The poem's references to the temple's desolation rather than destruction are consistent with the archaeological record.

### Residential Areas
The well-preserved residential quarter (dubbed "Abraham's neighborhood" by Woolley, though the connection is apocryphal) shows evidence of the dense urban settlement the poem mourns.

## Material Evidence and Textual Description

The poem's descriptions of destruction align remarkably well with archaeological findings:
- References to breached walls correspond to gaps in the excavated fortifications
- Descriptions of bodies in the streets correlate with unburied remains in destruction levels
- The desolation of canals matches evidence of disrupted irrigation systems
