The third kirugu is the poem's first extended passage of vivid, concrete imagery. The storm is described in terms of overwhelming sensory experience: howling wind, crumbling walls, scattered debris. The poet deploys onomatopoeia and kinetic imagery to convey the chaos of destruction, contrasting sharply with the measured, catalogic style of the opening kiruguS. This shift from order to chaos at the literary level mirrors the shift from civilization to destruction at the narrative level.
