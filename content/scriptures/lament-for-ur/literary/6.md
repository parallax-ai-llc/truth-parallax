The sixth kirugu creates a powerful literary counterpoint to the fifth. Ningal's passionate, expansive plea is met with terse, implacable refusal. The contrast between her lengthy, emotional speech and the assembly's brief, formal rejection generates dramatic irony: the reader knows from the poem's opening that the plea will fail, yet hopes against hope. The literary structure of plea-rejection enacts the theological structure of compassion-overruled-by-decree.
