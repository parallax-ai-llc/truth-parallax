The second kirugu shifts the poem's register from catalogue to narration, describing the deliberations of the divine council. The formal, repetitive language — 'Anu changed his command, Enlil altered his decree' — creates a sense of judicial solemnity appropriate to the pronouncement of cosmic judgment. The use of parallelism between Anu (sky god, ultimate authority) and Enlil (god of earth, executor of decrees) establishes the decree's finality through doubled divine authority.
