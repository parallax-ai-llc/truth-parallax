The ninth kirugu is the poem's most intimate section. Ningal speaks not to the gods but to the city itself, addressing the ruins in second person: 'My city which no longer exists...' This technique of apostrophe — addressing an absent or inanimate entity — creates an unbearable emotional intimacy. The goddess who once inhabited the city now walks through its ruins as a stranger, recognizing familiar places rendered unrecognizable by destruction. The literary effect is one of ghostly presence: Ningal is there, but the city she knew is not.
