Orthodox exegesis reads the death of Antiochus IV in chapter 9 as a demonstration of God's righteous judgment against the enemies of the faith. The detailed description of his physical decomposition is understood not as gratuitous but as symbolizing the spiritual corruption that underlies tyrannical power. Orthodox hymnography frequently celebrates the downfall of persecutors as manifestations of God's justice and vindication of the faithful.

The deathbed repentance of Antiochus is read with cautious hope by Orthodox interpreters, who maintain that the mercy of God extends to all who sincerely repent, even at the last moment. The Orthodox practice of praying for the departed — including those who may have died in a state of sin — reflects the conviction that God's mercy is deeper than human judgment and that the Church's prayers may benefit even those whose final spiritual state is uncertain.
