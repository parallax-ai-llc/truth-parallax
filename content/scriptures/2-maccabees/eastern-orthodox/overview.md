## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church includes 2 Maccabees in its Old Testament canon and draws deeply on its theological themes. The book's emphasis on martyrdom, resurrection, prayer for the departed, and divine intervention aligns closely with Orthodox theological and liturgical sensibilities. The Holy Maccabean Martyrs — Eleazar, the seven brothers, and their mother Solomonia — are commemorated on August 1 and held as exemplars of faithfulness unto death.

## Interpretive Approach

Orthodox exegesis reads 2 Maccabees through the patristic tradition, with the Church Fathers frequently citing the martyrdom narratives as models of Christian virtue. St. Gregory Nazianzen's homily on the Maccabees and St. John Chrysostom's sermons on their mother demonstrate the centrality of these figures in Orthodox moral theology. The book's teaching on prayer for the dead is received without controversy in Orthodoxy, where commemoration of the departed is a regular feature of liturgical life through memorial services (panikhida) and the kollyva offerings.

## Key Themes in Orthodox Reading

Orthodox interpreters highlight the theology of the body implicit in the martyrdom accounts — the brothers' willingness to suffer bodily torture in hope of resurrection reflects the Orthodox conviction that the body participates in salvation and is destined for glorification. The vision of Onias and Jeremiah interceding in heaven (15:12-16) supports the Orthodox doctrine of the communion of saints and the ongoing relationship between the Church Militant and the Church Triumphant. The book's emphasis on God's direct intervention in battle resonates with Orthodox theology of divine energies active in the created world.
