Protestant commentators read the gruesome death of Antiochus IV in chapter 9 as a vivid illustration of divine retribution. The detailed description of his physical suffering — worms, stench, and agony — reflects the biblical pattern of God's judgment against blasphemous rulers, from Pharaoh to Herod (Acts 12:23). The deathbed letter expressing remorse and seeking reconciliation with the Jews is treated by Protestant scholars as likely historical fiction serving a theological purpose.

The chapter's theological message — that God punishes those who persecute his people — resonates with Protestant convictions about divine sovereignty and judgment. However, Protestant interpreters are careful to note that such retribution narratives should not be used to justify human vengeance, as God alone reserves the right to repay evil.
