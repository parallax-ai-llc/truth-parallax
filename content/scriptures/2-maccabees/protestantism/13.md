Protestant commentators read chapter 13's account of the expedition of Antiochus V Eupator as another episode in the ongoing struggle for Jewish religious freedom. The narrative of military conflicts and divine interventions follows the pattern established throughout 2 Maccabees, with human action accompanied and interpreted through supernatural assistance.

The execution of Menelaus, the corrupt high priest, is noted by Protestant interpreters as an instance of poetic justice — the man who had schemed to control the high priesthood through bribery and murder meets his own violent end. Protestant readers see in this narrative a confirmation of the biblical principle that those who sow corruption will reap destruction.
