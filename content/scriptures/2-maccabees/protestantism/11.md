Protestant interpreters approach chapter 11's account of diplomatic negotiations and peace letters with historical interest, noting the complex political maneuvering required to secure Jewish religious freedom within the Seleucid Empire. The various letters reproduced in the text are valued as evidence of Hellenistic diplomatic practice and the legal framework within which Jewish communities negotiated their rights.

The chapter's depiction of a heavenly rider appearing before Lysias's army illustrates 2 Maccabees' characteristic emphasis on divine intervention, which Protestant scholars evaluate as theological interpretation layered over historical events. The peace agreements, however temporary, demonstrate the principle that negotiation and diplomacy are legitimate means of pursuing justice and religious liberty.
