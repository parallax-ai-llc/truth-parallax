Protestant interpreters read the opening letter of 2 Maccabees as a document of Hellenistic Jewish piety addressed to the Egyptian Diaspora. While Protestants do not regard this text as canonical, they recognize its value for understanding the development of Jewish liturgical practice and the relationship between Palestinian and Diaspora communities in the late Second Temple period.

The letter's concern with the proper observance of Hanukkah and the sacred fire of the Temple illustrates the importance of cultic continuity in Second Temple Judaism. Protestant scholars note the letter's emphasis on communal solidarity across geographical boundaries, a theme that resonates with the Reformation principle of the invisible Church uniting believers across all nations and traditions.
