Protestant readers are deeply moved by the account of Eleazar's martyrdom in chapter 6, recognizing in his refusal to eat forbidden food — even in pretense — a model of uncompromising integrity. Eleazar's declaration that he would rather die than set a bad example for the young resonates with Protestant convictions about the responsibility of Christian witness and the refusal to practice even outward conformity to falsehood.

While Protestants do not venerate Eleazar as a saint in the Catholic or Orthodox sense, they regard him as an exemplary figure whose courage under persecution parallels that of Reformation-era martyrs. His willingness to die for a matter that might seem trivial — dietary law — illustrates the Protestant principle that faithfulness in small things is inseparable from faithfulness in great ones.
