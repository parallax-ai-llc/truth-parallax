Protestant commentators approach the second letter in chapter 2 with scholarly interest, noting its account of Jeremiah hiding the ark of the covenant, the tent of meeting, and the altar of incense on Mount Nebo. While Protestants regard these traditions as legendary rather than historical, they recognize their theological significance as expressions of the hope that God's presence would not be permanently lost to Israel.

The chapter's description of Jason of Cyrene's five-volume history, of which 2 Maccabees claims to be an abridgment, is valued by Protestant scholars as evidence of the rich literary activity of Hellenistic Judaism. The author's self-conscious reflection on his method of composition provides rare insight into ancient historiographical practice.
