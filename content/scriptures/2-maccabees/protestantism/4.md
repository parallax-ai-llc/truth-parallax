Protestant interpreters read chapter 4's account of the Hellenization of Jerusalem under Jason and Menelaus as a cautionary tale about the corruption of religious leadership. The willingness of Jewish high priests to purchase their office from a pagan king and to introduce Greek customs into the holy city illustrates the dangers of compromising faith for worldly advancement — a theme central to Reformation polemic.

The chapter's detailed account of institutional corruption, simony, and betrayal resonates with Protestant criticisms of ecclesiastical abuses. The murder of Onias III, the legitimate high priest, by agents of the corrupt Menelaus parallels the persecution of faithful ministers by established religious authorities throughout history.
