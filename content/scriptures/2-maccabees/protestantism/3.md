Protestant readers appreciate chapter 3's dramatic account of Heliodorus's attempt to plunder the Temple treasury and his supernatural punishment as a vivid narrative of divine protection. While Protestants do not draw sacramental conclusions from the episode, they recognize it as an illustration of God's sovereignty over earthly powers and his capacity to intervene on behalf of his people.

The chapter's emphasis on prayer and communal supplication as the means through which divine intervention is secured resonates with Protestant theology of prayer. The high priest Onias's distress and the people's corporate pleading illustrate the Reformed principle that God responds to the earnest prayers of the faithful, even apart from any mediating institution.
