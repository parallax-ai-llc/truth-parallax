Protestant readers approach chapter 14 with interest in the figure of Razis, the elder who commits suicide rather than fall into enemy hands. This episode has been debated throughout Christian history, with some interpreters praising Razis's courage and others condemning self-destruction. Protestant ethics generally regards the act as misguided, however sympathetically the text presents it, distinguishing between the courage of martyrdom (accepting death at the hands of others) and the sin of suicide.

Nicanor's threat to destroy the Temple and build a temple to Dionysus in its place illustrates the extremity of the Seleucid persecution and the existential stakes of the Maccabean resistance. Protestant interpreters note this as evidence of the comprehensive nature of Hellenistic religious imperialism, which sought not merely political submission but the eradication of Jewish worship entirely.
