## Overview

Protestant tradition classifies 2 Maccabees among the Apocrypha and denies it canonical authority. This book has been especially contested in Protestant-Catholic debate because of its explicit support for prayer for the dead (12:43-46), which Catholic theology uses to support the doctrine of purgatory. The Reformers, particularly Martin Luther and John Calvin, rejected the book's canonical status partly on these grounds, arguing that doctrine must be established from the Hebrew canon alone.

## Interpretive Approach

Protestant scholars acknowledge 2 Maccabees as a valuable historical and literary document from the intertestamental period. They note its more rhetorical and theological character compared to 1 Maccabees, reading it as a work of religious propaganda rather than sober history. The martyrdom narratives in chapters 6-7 are widely admired for their moral power, and Protestant commentators have drawn parallels between the Maccabean martyrs and the Reformation-era martyrs documented in Foxe's *Book of Martyrs*.

## Key Themes in Protestant Reading

While rejecting the doctrinal conclusions Catholics draw from 2 Maccabees, Protestant interpreters value the book's emphasis on divine sovereignty, the cost of faithfulness, and the reality of bodily resurrection. The explicit resurrection hope in chapter 7 is recognized as an important witness to the development of this doctrine in Second Temple Judaism. The theology of divine retribution and the sovereignty of God over pagan empires resonate with Reformed theological commitments.
