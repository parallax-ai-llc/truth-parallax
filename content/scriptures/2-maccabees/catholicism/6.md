The martyrdom of Eleazar in chapter 6 is venerated in the Catholic tradition as a model of heroic virtue and faithful witness. The aged scribe's refusal to eat pork or even to pretend to do so illustrates the Catholic moral principle that one must never cooperate in evil, even under threat of death, and that scandal — giving the appearance of compromising the faith — is itself a grave sin.

Catholic moral theology draws from Eleazar's example the teaching that the end does not justify the means. His friends' well-meaning suggestion that he merely pretend to eat forbidden food represents the consequentialist reasoning that Catholic ethics consistently rejects. Eleazar's choice of death over deception demonstrates the absolute nature of moral truth and the priority of conscience over expediency.
