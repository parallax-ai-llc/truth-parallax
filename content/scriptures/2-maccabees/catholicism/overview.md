## Overview

2 Maccabees holds particular doctrinal significance for the Catholic Church as a deuterocanonical book affirmed at the Council of Trent. It provides key scriptural support for several distinctively Catholic doctrines, including prayer for the dead, purgatory, the intercession of saints, and the meritorious nature of martyrdom. The book is read liturgically, and the martyrdom narratives of chapters 6-7 are proclaimed on feast days honoring the Holy Maccabees.

## Interpretive Approach

Catholic exegesis reads 2 Maccabees as inspired Scripture that reveals important truths about the communion of saints and the state of the dead. The passage in 12:43-46, where Judas Maccabeus sends a sin offering to Jerusalem on behalf of fallen soldiers, is understood as biblical evidence for the efficacy of prayer for the deceased and, by extension, the existence of a purifying state after death. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 958, 1032) explicitly cites this passage in its teaching on purgatory.

## Key Themes in Catholic Reading

Catholic interpreters emphasize the book's theology of creation *ex nihilo* (7:28), one of the earliest explicit statements of this doctrine. The mother of the seven martyred brothers is venerated as a model of faith and has been compared to the Blessed Virgin Mary at the foot of the Cross. The book's vision of the heavenly intercession of the saints — with Onias and Jeremiah praying for the people (15:12-16) — provides direct scriptural grounding for the Catholic practice of invoking the saints in prayer.
