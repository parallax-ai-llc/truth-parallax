Catholic interpreters read chapter 14 with particular attention to Nicanor's threat to destroy the Temple and build a shrine to Dionysus — a blasphemous intention that illustrates the demonic nature of religious persecution. The episode reinforces the Catholic teaching on the sanctity of consecrated spaces and the gravity of sacrilege as a sin against God himself.

The case of Razis, who takes his own life rather than submit to capture, has been discussed extensively in Catholic moral theology. While the text presents Razis sympathetically, Catholic teaching has consistently distinguished his action from authentic martyrdom. St. Augustine addressed similar cases in *The City of God*, arguing that suicide is prohibited even in extreme circumstances, as one's life belongs ultimately to God. The Catechism (CCC 2281-2283) maintains this position while acknowledging mitigating circumstances.
