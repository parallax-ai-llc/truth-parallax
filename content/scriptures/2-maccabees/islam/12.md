Muslim scholars approach chapter 12's passage on prayer for the dead (12:39-45) with interest, noting parallels to Islamic practices of praying for the deceased. The du'a for the departed, the funeral prayer (salat al-janazah), and the giving of charity (sadaqah) on behalf of the dead are all Islamic practices that reflect a conviction similar to that expressed in this text — that the living can benefit the dead through their prayers and righteous deeds.

The hadith literature contains numerous traditions about the Prophet Muhammad praying for the dead and encouraging his companions to do so. The Maccabean practice described in this chapter, while arising from a different theological context, illustrates a broader Abrahamic conviction about the solidarity between the living and the dead that Islam shares with the tradition reflected in 2 Maccabees.
