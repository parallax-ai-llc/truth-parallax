## Overview

2 Maccabees is not part of the Islamic scriptural tradition, and the Quran does not reference its specific narratives. However, Muslim scholars engaging with the Isra'iliyyat tradition have found the book's themes deeply consonant with Quranic values. The martyrdom accounts in particular resonate with the Islamic concept of shahada (bearing witness to faith through suffering and death), and some scholars have suggested that the story of the seven brothers may be reflected in the Quranic narrative of the People of the Ditch (Surah al-Buruj, 85:4-8).

## Interpretive Approach

Islamic engagement with 2 Maccabees tends to focus on its moral and theological themes rather than its historical details. The book's insistence on creation *ex nihilo* (7:28) accords perfectly with the Quranic doctrine that God creates by divine command ("Be, and it is" — kun fa-yakun). The theology of divine retribution — where the persecutor Antiochus meets a fitting end — aligns with the Quranic principle that God repays tyrants for their oppression. Muslim readers also find resonance in the book's depiction of a faithful community maintaining its identity under severe imperial pressure.

## Key Themes in Islamic Reading

The martyrdom narratives are the most compelling aspect of 2 Maccabees from an Islamic perspective. The mother who encourages her sons to die rather than abandon their faith embodies the Quranic virtue of sabr (patient endurance in the face of trial). The brothers' confident declaration that God will raise them from the dead parallels the Quranic teaching on resurrection (al-ba'th) and the Day of Judgment. The broader theme of divine justice vindicating the faithful and punishing the oppressor resonates throughout the Quran and hadith literature.
