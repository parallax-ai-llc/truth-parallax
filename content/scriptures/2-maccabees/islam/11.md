Muslim scholars read chapter 11's diplomatic negotiations through the lens of Islamic jurisprudence on treaties and agreements with non-Muslim powers. The principle of sulh (peace agreement) in Islamic law permits the conclusion of binding treaties when they serve the interests of the Muslim community, and the Maccabean peace negotiations with Seleucid authorities illustrate a similar pragmatism in the service of religious freedom.

The heavenly rider who appears before Lysias's army illustrates the Islamic understanding of God's direct intervention in human affairs through angelic agency. The Quran describes angels as God's instruments in the natural world and in human history, and Muslim readers recognize in the Maccabean narratives a pattern of divine intervention consistent with Quranic teaching.
