The martyrdom of Eleazar resonates deeply with Islamic tradition, as his refusal to consume forbidden food (haram) and his willingness to die rather than violate God's law exemplify the Islamic concept of shahada — bearing witness to the truth through faithful action even unto death. The Quranic emphasis on dietary law (halal and haram) provides a framework within which Muslim readers appreciate Eleazar's conviction that obedience to God's commands extends to the most intimate aspects of bodily practice.

Eleazar's refusal to participate even in the pretense of eating forbidden food illustrates the Islamic principle that taqiyyah (concealment of faith under duress), while permitted in extremis, should not extend to active participation in forbidden acts. His concern for the example he sets for the young reflects the Islamic emphasis on the elder's responsibility for the moral formation of the community.
