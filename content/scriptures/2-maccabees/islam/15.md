Muslim scholars read the final chapter of 2 Maccabees with interest in its vision of the heavenly intercession of righteous figures on behalf of the living. While Islamic theology is more cautious about the intercession (shafa'a) of the deceased, the tradition does recognize the special status of the prophets, martyrs, and righteous saints (awliya) in God's presence, and the Quranic affirmation that the martyrs are "alive with their Lord" (3:169) resonates with the Maccabean vision.

The book's conclusion, with its emphasis on the mingling of divine purpose and human effort in the narration of sacred history, reflects a concern shared by Islamic historiography. The Islamic tradition of historical writing (ta'rikh) likewise combines factual record with theological interpretation, understanding human history as the arena in which God's will is made manifest.
