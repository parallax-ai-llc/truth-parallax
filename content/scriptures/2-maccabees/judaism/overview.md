## Overview

Like 1 Maccabees, 2 Maccabees is not part of the Jewish biblical canon, but its narratives have profoundly shaped Jewish religious consciousness. The martyrdom accounts in chapters 6-7 became foundational texts for the Jewish concept of kiddush Hashem — the sanctification of God's name through willingness to die rather than violate the commandments. These stories were retold and expanded in rabbinic literature, particularly in midrashic collections and the Babylonian Talmud (Gittin 57b).

## Interpretive Approach

Jewish scholars approach 2 Maccabees as an important document of Hellenistic Judaism, written in Greek and reflecting a Diaspora perspective distinct from the Palestinian outlook of 1 Maccabees. The book's explicit theology of resurrection and afterlife is recognized as reflecting Pharisaic beliefs that would later become normative in rabbinic Judaism. Modern Jewish historians value the book for its account of the Hellenistic crisis and the internal Jewish debates over assimilation and cultural identity that remain relevant to contemporary Jewish life.

## Key Themes in Jewish Reading

Jewish interpreters give special attention to the mother and her seven sons (chapter 7), a narrative that became a paradigm for Jewish martyrdom through the centuries — from the Roman persecutions to the medieval Rhineland massacres to the Shoah. The theme of bodily resurrection articulated by the brothers influenced the development of this doctrine in Pharisaic and later rabbinic theology. The book's concern with the sanctity of the Temple and the danger of Hellenistic assimilation continues to inform Jewish discussions about maintaining distinctive identity in pluralistic societies.
