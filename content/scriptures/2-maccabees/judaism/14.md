Jewish interpreters approach the figure of Razis in chapter 14 with the complex ethical sensibilities that characterize Jewish discussion of suicide and martyrdom. While the text presents Razis's self-destruction sympathetically — describing him as one who loved his city and was called "father of the Jews" — rabbinic tradition generally distinguishes between kiddush Hashem (sanctification of God's name through accepting death) and active self-destruction.

Nicanor's threat to destroy the Temple and build a pagan shrine illustrates the existential nature of the Maccabean struggle — not merely a political conflict but a battle for the survival of Jewish worship and identity. Jewish readers recognize in this threat a pattern that would repeat throughout Jewish history, as successive powers attempted to replace Jewish sacred spaces with their own religious structures.
