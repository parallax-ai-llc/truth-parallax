Jewish readers interpret the death of Antiochus IV in chapter 9 as an instance of divine measure-for-measure justice — the principle that God repays the wicked according to the nature of their crimes. The detailed description of Antiochus's physical suffering, while perhaps exaggerated for literary effect, serves the theological purpose of demonstrating that the persecutor of Israel cannot escape divine retribution.

The various accounts of Antiochus's death in different sources (1 Maccabees, 2 Maccabees, Polybius, and later rabbinic tradition) illustrate the ways in which historical events are shaped by theological interpretation. Jewish historians have noted that the Antiochus tradition became a paradigm for all subsequent Jewish reflections on the fate of persecutors, from the Roman emperors to Haman in the Purim tradition.
