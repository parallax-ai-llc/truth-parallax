Jewish interpreters approach the passage on prayer for the dead (12:39-45) with interest, noting that it reflects a belief in the efficacy of vicarious atonement that, while not normative in rabbinic Judaism, has parallels in certain Jewish practices. The custom of reciting Kaddish for the departed, the practice of giving charity in memory of the dead (matnat yad), and the Yizkor memorial prayers all reflect a conviction that the living can benefit the dead through their prayers and good deeds.

The discovery of idolatrous amulets on the fallen soldiers raises important questions about the boundaries of Jewish practice and the degree to which syncretism penetrated even the ranks of the Maccabean fighters. Jewish historians see in this episode evidence of the pervasive influence of Hellenistic religious culture on Jewish society, even among those who ostensibly fought against it.
