Jewish interpreters read chapter 10's account of the Temple purification as a parallel and complementary account to 1 Maccabees 4, emphasizing the theological rather than military dimensions of the event. The eight-day celebration and its connection to the Feast of Tabernacles illuminate the liturgical context within which Hanukkah was originally understood — not as an independent festival but as a belated observance of Sukkot, which could not be celebrated during the period of persecution.

The Talmudic transformation of Hanukkah — from a military and political commemoration to a celebration of the miracle of the oil — represents a significant reinterpretation that shifted emphasis from human achievement to divine intervention. Jewish scholars have debated the motivations for this transformation, suggesting that the rabbis may have wished to de-emphasize the Hasmonean military legacy in the aftermath of the disastrous revolts against Rome.
