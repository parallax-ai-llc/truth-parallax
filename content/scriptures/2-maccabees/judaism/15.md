Jewish scholars read the final chapter of 2 Maccabees with particular attention to the vision of Onias and Jeremiah praying for the people (15:12-16), which reflects Second Temple beliefs about the ongoing role of the righteous dead in the affairs of the living. While rabbinic Judaism would develop different frameworks for understanding the relationship between the living and the dead, the tradition of invoking the merit of the ancestors (zekhut avot) maintains a conceptual connection to this passage.

The book's conclusion, with its author's modest assessment of his literary achievement, provides a rare glimpse into the self-consciousness of Hellenistic Jewish writers. Jewish literary scholars have noted the sophistication of this epilogue, which combines conventional Greek modesty with a genuine theological conviction about the importance of commemorating God's acts in history.
