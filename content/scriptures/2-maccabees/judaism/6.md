The martyrdom of Eleazar is one of the foundational narratives of Jewish martyrology, establishing the principle that faithfulness to Torah is worth more than life itself. Jewish tradition has celebrated Eleazar as a model of kiddush Hashem — the sanctification of God's name through willingness to die rather than transgress the commandments. His refusal to eat pork, even in pretense, illustrates the Jewish conviction that Jewish identity is enacted through bodily practice, not merely internal belief.

Rabbinic literature expanded and reinterpreted Eleazar's story, and it became a paradigm for Jewish martyrdom throughout the centuries. During the medieval Rhineland massacres and other persecutions, Jewish communities drew explicitly on the Maccabean martyrdom narratives as models for their own response to forced conversion and violence.
