Jewish interpreters read chapter 5 as a harrowing account of the violence unleashed upon Jerusalem by Antiochus IV — an assault that aimed at the destruction not merely of Jewish political autonomy but of Jewish religious life itself. The massacre of civilians, the plundering of the Temple, and the establishment of foreign garrisons represent one of the darkest moments in Jewish history prior to the Roman destruction of 70 CE.

The brief mention of Judas's withdrawal to the wilderness (5:27) is noted by Jewish historians as evidence of the origins of the resistance movement in small, dedicated groups operating outside established urban centers. This pattern of withdrawal, organization, and return would characterize Jewish resistance movements throughout subsequent history.
