The opening of the Hypostasis of the Archons has attracted scholarly attention for its explicit citation of Ephesians 6:12, making it one of the few Nag Hammadi texts to begin with a direct appeal to a canonical New Testament passage. Bentley Layton and others have analyzed this citation strategy as an attempt to ground the Gnostic cosmological narrative in apostolic authority — claiming Paul as a witness to the reality of archontic powers. This strategy of appropriation reflects the competitive textual environment of second-century Christianity.

The demiurgic myth in chapter 1 — Samael's arrogant self-declaration and the corrective heavenly voice — is a widely attested motif across Gnostic literature. Scholars have identified parallels in the Apocryphon of John, On the Origin of the World, and the Second Treatise of the Great Seth, among others. Comparative analysis suggests a shared mythological tradition that individual texts adapted to their own literary and theological purposes. The Hypostasis of the Archons' version is notable for its narrative economy and dramatic clarity.
