Chapter 3 marks a significant generic shift within the text: from the rewritten-Bible genre of chapters 1-2 to an apocalyptic revelation dialogue. Scholars including Roger Bullard have argued that this shift may indicate the combination of two originally independent compositions. The Norea-Eleleth dialogue adopts the conventions of Jewish apocalyptic literature — a righteous figure receives heavenly revelation through an interpreting angel — while filling these conventional forms with Gnostic theological content.

The Norea tradition extends beyond the Hypostasis of the Archons. She appears in several other ancient texts, including the Thought of Norea (NHC IX,2), and is mentioned by Epiphanius in his catalog of heresies. Birger Pearson and others have traced the development of the Norea tradition across these sources, attempting to reconstruct its history and significance. The figure's possible connection to the biblical Naamah (Genesis 4:22) or to the tradition of Noah's wife has been debated, with no scholarly consensus emerging. The Norea tradition represents an important example of how Gnostic communities developed their own hagiographic and mythological traditions around figures derived from, but transformed beyond recognition from, the Jewish scriptural heritage.
