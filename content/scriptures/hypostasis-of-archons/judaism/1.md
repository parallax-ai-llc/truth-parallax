Chapter 1 of the Hypostasis of the Archons presents the most direct affront to Jewish theology in the Nag Hammadi library. The chief archon's declaration "I am God; there is none besides me" directly quotes the monotheistic affirmations of Deutero-Isaiah (45:5, 46:9), which form the theological bedrock of Jewish faith. By placing these words in the mouth of a monstrous, blind being and having a heavenly voice immediately refute them, the text effectively turns Judaism's highest theological claim into evidence of cosmic delusion.

Jewish scholars studying this text emphasize its polemical character. The name Samael — which in Jewish tradition refers to a specific angelic or demonic figure, not to God — is here applied to the Creator, representing a deliberate act of theological subversion. The text appropriates the sacred language of Jewish monotheism and deploys it against itself, a hermeneutical strategy that reveals deep hostility toward the Jewish theological tradition. For Jewish readers, this chapter illustrates the extreme conclusions to which anti-cosmic theology can lead when it targets the God of Israel.
