## Overview

The Hypostasis of the Archons holds no place in the Jewish tradition and represents one of the most antagonistic rereadings of the Torah in ancient literature. The text takes the God of Genesis — the Creator whom Judaism worships as the one true God — and reinterprets this figure as Samael, a blind, arrogant archon whose very name means "blind god." For Jewish scholars, the text exemplifies the radical anti-Jewish hermeneutics that characterized certain Gnostic movements.

## Inversion of Jewish Scripture

The text systematically inverts the moral valuations of Genesis. The serpent becomes a wisdom teacher, Eve becomes a spiritual awakener, and God's commands become instruments of oppression. From a Jewish perspective, this wholesale reversal represents not merely a different interpretation of Torah but a fundamental assault on its theological framework. The text appropriates Jewish narrative, characters, and concepts while deploying them in the service of a theology that is deeply hostile to the God of Israel.

## Scholarly Analysis

Jewish scholars who study the Hypostasis of the Archons note its dependence on Jewish exegetical traditions even as it subverts them. The figure of Norea, for instance, may be connected to the rabbinic tradition of Naamah, the sister of Tubal-Cain. The text's angelology draws on Jewish sources, and its structure of revelation through angelic instruction follows patterns found in Jewish apocalyptic literature. These connections illustrate the complex and often adversarial relationship between Gnostic and Jewish traditions in late antiquity.
