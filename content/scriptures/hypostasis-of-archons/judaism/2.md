The reversal of the Eden narrative in chapter 2 constitutes a wholesale inversion of Torah. Jewish tradition, expressed in rabbinic midrash and commentary, reads the Garden narrative as a story about the consequences of disobedience to divine command. The serpent (nachash) is understood as a cunning tempter, and the eating of the fruit leads to expulsion from paradise and the introduction of mortality. The Hypostasis of the Archons reverses every element: the serpent is wise, the fruit is liberating, and God's command is oppressive.

From a Jewish hermeneutical perspective, this kind of reading represents what might be called "protest exegesis" — a deliberate strategy of reading the dominant tradition's scripture against its own grain. Jewish scholars recognize the intellectual sophistication of this approach while firmly rejecting its conclusions. The rabbinic tradition developed its own rich and multivocal interpretive traditions around the Garden narrative, but even the most creative midrashim never approach the wholesale reversal found here. The Hypostasis's reading is not merely a different interpretation of Genesis but a fundamental rejection of its theological framework.
