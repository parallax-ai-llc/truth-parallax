Jewish scholars have noted the possible connection between the Gnostic figure of Norea and the biblical Naamah, sister of Tubal-Cain (Genesis 4:22). In some rabbinic traditions, Naamah is associated with seduction and demonic activity, while in others she is Noah's wife. The Hypostasis of the Archons transforms this marginal biblical figure into a heroine of spiritual resistance — a move that illustrates the Gnostic strategy of appropriating and reinterpreting minor characters from Jewish scripture.

The angelic revelation in chapter 3 follows patterns found in Jewish apocalyptic literature — the angelus interpres (interpreting angel) motif is well established in texts like Daniel, Zechariah, and 1 Enoch. However, the content of Eleleth's revelation subverts the Jewish theological framework that the apocalyptic genre originally served. In Jewish apocalypticism, angelic revelation confirms God's sovereignty and promises the vindication of the righteous within the framework of creation. In the Hypostasis of the Archons, angelic revelation exposes the creator as a fraud and promises liberation from creation itself. The form is Jewish; the content is anti-Jewish.
