The reversal of the Garden of Eden narrative in chapter 2 represents, from a Protestant perspective, the most egregious misreading of Scripture in the entire text. Protestant theology, following the canonical narrative and its interpretation by Paul in Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15, understands the serpent's temptation as the occasion of the fall — the catastrophic entry of sin and death into God's good creation. The Hypostasis of the Archons' transformation of the serpent into a liberating instructor inverts the entire biblical storyline.

The elevation of Eve as the spiritual awakener also conflicts with the Protestant reading of Genesis 3, in which both Adam and Eve bear responsibility for disobedience. Protestant theology does not demonize Eve — many Reformed interpreters emphasize the shared nature of the fall — but it does not divinize her either. The Gnostic portrayal of Eve as a bearer of salvific knowledge who transcends the archons' power is seen as projecting an alien theological framework onto the biblical text, replacing the narrative of sin and grace with a narrative of ignorance and enlightenment.
