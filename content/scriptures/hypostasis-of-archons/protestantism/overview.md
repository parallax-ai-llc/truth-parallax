## Overview

Protestantism does not accept the Hypostasis of the Archons as canonical scripture. The text's radical reinterpretation of Genesis — transforming the Creator into a blind archon and the serpent into a bringer of salvation — represents a fundamental departure from the Protestant understanding of Scripture. Protestant engagement with the text is primarily scholarly, treating it as evidence for the diversity of early Christian interpretation and the challenges faced by the apostolic church.

## Pauline Connection

Protestant scholars note the text's explicit appeal to Ephesians 6:12 ("our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities") as its theological starting point. This appropriation of a Pauline passage illustrates how Gnostic interpreters could build elaborate theological systems by radically expanding upon brief scriptural references. Protestant hermeneutics insists on reading such passages within the broader canonical context, where the cosmic powers are understood as subordinate to the sovereign Creator God.

## Theological Assessment

The text's inversion of the Genesis narrative — making God the villain and the serpent the hero — is seen by Protestant theologians as a cautionary example of hermeneutical distortion. The Hypostasis of the Archons demonstrates what can happen when readers approach Scripture with a predetermined philosophical framework (in this case, anti-cosmic dualism) that overrides the text's own theological commitments. It serves as a reminder of the importance of the Reformation principle of letting Scripture interpret Scripture.
