The narrative of Norea's encounter with the angel Eleleth bears a superficial resemblance to Orthodox accounts of angelic visitation and divine revelation, but the theological content differs fundamentally. In Orthodox tradition, angelic encounters serve to confirm and deepen the faith delivered to the apostles; in the Hypostasis of the Archons, Eleleth reveals an entirely alternative cosmology that contradicts the apostolic witness. The Orthodox principle of testing revelation against the consensus of the Fathers (consensus patrum) would exclude Eleleth's teaching.

Orthodox eschatology envisions not the destruction of the archontic order but the transfiguration of all creation at Christ's second coming. The Orthodox liturgy anticipates "the life of the age to come" — a renewed cosmos in which all things participate in the divine glory. The Hypostasis of the Archons' eschatological vision, centered on the elimination of the archons and the liberation of trapped light, lacks the christological and sacramental dimensions that characterize Orthodox hope. For the Orthodox tradition, the future is not about escape from creation but about creation's fulfillment in Christ.
