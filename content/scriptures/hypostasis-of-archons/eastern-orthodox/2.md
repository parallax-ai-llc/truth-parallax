Orthodox theology speaks of "ancestral sin" rather than "original sin" in the Augustinian sense, but it nonetheless maintains that the events in the Garden of Eden represent a genuine fall from grace — not an awakening. The serpent's role in Orthodox exegesis, following the Fathers, is that of the deceiver — the one who leads humanity away from communion with God through the promise of false autonomy. The Hypostasis of the Archons' reversal, in which the serpent is the Instructor bringing salvific knowledge, is incompatible with this patristic consensus.

The text's treatment of Eve is equally problematic for Orthodox theology. While the Orthodox tradition does not demonize Eve — and indeed draws rich typological connections between Eve and the Theotokos (Mary, Mother of God) — it does not divinize her in the manner of the Gnostic text. Orthodox patristic exegesis presents Eve as a fully human person whose free choice, together with Adam's, resulted in humanity's alienation from God. The Hypostasis's presentation of Eve as a spiritual being who transcends the archons' power introduces a docetic anthropology that Orthodox thought rejects.
