The reversal of the Eden narrative in chapter 2 contradicts one of Catholic theology's foundational doctrines: original sin. The Council of Trent (1546) definitively taught that Adam's transgression in the Garden resulted in the loss of holiness and justice, affecting the entire human race. The Hypostasis of the Archons' reinterpretation — in which the fruit of knowledge brings liberation and the serpent is a divine emissary — inverts this teaching entirely, treating the fall as an awakening rather than a catastrophe.

Catholic theology also rejects the text's divinization of Eve. While the Catholic tradition honors Eve's role in salvation history and draws typological connections between Eve and Mary (the "New Eve"), it does not present Eve as a spiritual being who transcends the archons or serves as the bearer of salvific gnosis. The Catholic reading of Genesis 3 maintains that both Adam and Eve are responsible for their disobedience and that their act had real consequences for human nature — consequences remedied only by the incarnation, death, and resurrection of Christ.
