Catholic doctrine categorically rejects the identification of the Creator with a blind, arrogant archon. The First Vatican Council (1870) affirmed that "the one true God, our Creator and Lord, can be known with certainty from the things that have been made." The Hypostasis of the Archons' claim that the creator is a monstrous being named Samael who falsely claims divine sovereignty contradicts the Church's teaching that the one God — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — is the sole creator of all visible and invisible things.

The heavenly voice that corrects Samael's arrogant declaration has no analogue in Catholic theology. While the Catholic tradition acknowledges the reality of divine rebuke (as when God confronts Job or corrects the prophets), the framework here presupposes a fundamental split between the creator and the true God — a position that the Church condemned in its anti-Gnostic and anti-Marcionite teaching. Catholic creation theology insists on the unity of the God who creates and the God who redeems.
