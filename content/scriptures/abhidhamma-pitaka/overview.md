## Overview

The Abhidhamma Pitaka ("Basket of Higher Doctrine") is the third of the three pitakas of the Pali Canon and represents the Buddhist tradition's most systematic and analytical treatment of mind, matter, and their interrelationships. While the Sutta Pitaka presents the Buddha's teachings in conventional language, the Abhidhamma reorganizes and analyzes the same teachings in precise philosophical and psychological terms, using a technical vocabulary that classifies all phenomena into ultimate categories (paramattha dhamma).

## Structure

The Abhidhamma Pitaka comprises 7 books of increasing complexity:

1. **Dhammasangani**: Enumeration of phenomena — the fundamental classification of all mental and material states
2. **Vibhanga**: Book of analysis — detailed examination of the aggregates, sense bases, elements, and other key doctrinal categories
3. **Dhatukatha**: Discussion of elements — how the elements relate to the aggregates and sense bases
4. **Puggalapannatti**: Designation of persons — classification of individuals by their spiritual attainments
5. **Kathavatthu**: Points of controversy — debates between Buddhist schools on disputed doctrinal questions
6. **Yamaka**: Book of pairs — analysis using paired questions to clarify logical relationships
7. **Patthana**: Book of conditional relations — the monumental analysis of 24 types of conditionality

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Theravada Abhidhamma Pitaka is one of several Abhidharma traditions. The Sarvastivada Abhidharma, preserved in Chinese translation, represents an independent systematic analysis used in Mahayana scholastic traditions. The study of Abhidhamma/Abhidharma has profoundly influenced all Buddhist philosophical traditions.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Ultimate Realities | Classification of all phenomena into 82 (or 89/121) categories |
| Mind Analysis | Detailed mapping of consciousness and mental factors |
| Conditionality | The 24 modes by which phenomena condition one another |
| Process Philosophy | How mental and physical events arise and pass away in sequences |
| Classification | Rigorous taxonomic analysis of all aspects of experience |
