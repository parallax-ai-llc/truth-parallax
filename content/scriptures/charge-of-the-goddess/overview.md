## Overview

The Charge of the Goddess is the central liturgical text of Wicca and one of the most widely recognized texts in modern Paganism. In its best-known form, it was composed by Doreen Valiente in the 1950s, reworking an earlier prose version by Gerald Gardner (which itself drew on material from Charles Leland's Aradia, or the Gospel of the Witches and Aleister Crowley's writings). The Charge is typically recited during the ritual of Drawing Down the Moon, in which the High Priestess invokes the Goddess and speaks her words to the assembled coven.

## Structure

The Charge of the Goddess is divided into three sections:

1. **The Invocation and Descent**: The ritual invocation calling the Goddess to descend into the High Priestess, establishing the sacred context
2. **The Words of the Great Mother**: The Goddess speaks directly, proclaiming her nature, her gifts, and her requirements — freedom, ecstasy, reverence for nature, and the sacredness of the body
3. **The Promise and Blessing**: The Goddess's concluding promise of reunion after death and her eternal blessing upon her children

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Wiccan**: The most important liturgical text in Wiccan practice, recited in virtually every tradition and coven
- **Academic**: A key document for the study of modern Paganism, liturgical creativity, and the construction of sacred texts in new religious movements
- **Comparative**: Invites comparison with goddess hymns from ancient Sumer, Egypt, and Greece, as well as Marian devotion in Christianity
- **Philosophical**: Raises questions about immanent divinity, the relationship between body and spirit, and nature-based ethics
- **Feminist**: A landmark text in women's spirituality, affirming the divine feminine and the sacredness of women's bodies and experience
- **Interfaith**: Offers a distinctive voice in interfaith dialogue, representing earth-based spirituality and the worship of the divine as feminine

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Goddess | The divine feminine as the source and sustainer of all life |
| Sacred Nature | The natural world as the body of the Goddess, to be revered and protected |
| Freedom and Ecstasy | True worship as joyful, embodied, and free from shame |
| The Sacred Body | The human body as holy, a vessel of divine presence |
| Death and Return | The promise of reunion with the Goddess after death and rebirth |
