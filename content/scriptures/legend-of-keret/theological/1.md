The first tablet establishes the theological relationship at the heart of the narrative: Keret is 'son of El,' and El's appearance in the dream is an act of paternal compassion. The theology here is one of divine intimacy — El does not merely command but asks, consoles, and provides specific guidance. This image of the compassionate father-god stands in contrast to the distant, authoritative El of the Baal Cycle.

Keret's initial grief also raises the theological question of divine responsibility: if Keret is El's son, why did El allow his family to be destroyed? The text does not address this question directly, focusing instead on restoration rather than explanation — a theological strategy shared with the book of Job.
