## Overview

The Legend of Keret provides the most detailed surviving portrait of the theology of sacred kingship in the ancient Levant. Keret is not merely a human king who worships the gods — he is explicitly called "son of El," "servant of El," and "gracious one of El." This divine-human relationship raises profound theological questions about the nature of kingship, the vulnerability of the divinely chosen, and the obligations between gods and their human agents.

## Key Theological Themes

### The King as Son of El
Keret's designation as *bn il* ("son of El") establishes a special relationship between the supreme god and the human king. This is not merely honorific but carries theological weight: if the king is El's son, his suffering and potential death implicate El himself. El's intervention to heal Keret is thus not mere favor but paternal obligation.

### Theodicy and Royal Suffering
When Keret falls ill, a theological crisis erupts: can the son of El die a mortal death? Keret's son Yassib uses the illness to challenge his father's fitness, arguing that a king who cannot fulfill his duties should abdicate. The text thus addresses the tension between the ideal of sacred kingship and the reality of human frailty.

### The Vow and Its Consequences
Keret makes a vow to the goddess Athirat during his campaign but fails to fulfill it. His illness has been interpreted as divine punishment for this broken vow — suggesting a theology of strict reciprocity between humans and gods.

### Divine Healing
El's healing of Keret involves the creation of a special being (Sha'tiqatu) to drive away the illness. This divine medical intervention illustrates a theology in which the gods are directly involved in healing — a concept with parallels across ancient Near Eastern and biblical traditions.
