The third tablet narrates the crisis that follows Keret's apparent success: the king falls gravely ill, and a succession crisis erupts. Keret's son Yassib challenges his father, accusing him of failing to fulfill the duties of kingship — judging the widow's case, protecting the weak, defending the orphan. El intervenes, creating a healing figure (Sha'tiqatu) who cures Keret. The tablet is damaged at the end, and the conclusion of the Yassib episode is uncertain.

Scholarship has debated whether Keret's illness is punishment for his unfulfilled vow to Athirat or an independent narrative development. Greenstein favors the connection; Parker is more cautious. The Yassib challenge has been analyzed as reflecting actual tensions in Ugaritic royal succession.
