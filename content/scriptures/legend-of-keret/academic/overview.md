## Overview

The Legend of Keret (KTU 1.14–1.16) is one of three major narrative compositions from Ugarit, preserved on three large tablets written by the scribe Ilimilku. Unlike the mythological Baal Cycle, Keret is classified as a royal epic — its protagonist is a human king rather than a deity, though divine beings play crucial roles. The text has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate regarding its genre, its relationship to historical events, and its theological implications for Ugaritic concepts of sacred kingship.

## Key Scholarly Issues

### Genre Classification
Scholars have classified Keret variously as epic, legend, fairy tale, and ritual text. Simon Parker argued that it belongs to the genre of "pre-biblical narrative tradition" — a form of prose-poetry narrative about ancestral figures that also underlies the patriarchal narratives of Genesis.

### Historical Referent
Attempts to connect Keret to a historical Ugaritic king have been inconclusive. The name Keret does not appear in the known king lists of Ugarit, suggesting the text may preserve older (perhaps Hurrian) traditions adapted to the Ugaritic literary context.

### The Illness Episode
Keret's illness and near-death raise questions about the Ugaritic concept of kingship. If the king is "son of El," how can he fall ill? The text seems to address this theological problem directly, with Keret's son Yassib challenging his fitness to rule.
