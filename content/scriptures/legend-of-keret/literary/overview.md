## Overview

The Legend of Keret is a distinctive literary achievement within the Ugaritic corpus, combining elements of epic, folktale, and ritual text into a narrative centered on a human protagonist. Unlike the Baal Cycle's cosmic scope, Keret operates on a more intimate scale — domestic loss, personal piety, military campaign, marriage, illness, and family conflict — rendered through the conventions of Ugaritic poetic art.

## Key Literary Features

### Dream Theophany
El's appearance to the grieving Keret in a dream is one of the most fully developed dream-vision scenes in ancient Near Eastern literature. The scene moves from Keret's tearful sleep to El's sympathetic question ("Why does Keret weep?") to detailed instructions for obtaining a new wife — a narrative structure that combines divine revelation with practical guidance.

### Extended Repetition
Like the Baal Cycle, the Legend of Keret employs extensive verbatim repetition — El's instructions are repeated in full when Keret carries them out. This repetition serves both the conventions of oral-performative poetry and the theological purpose of showing human obedience to divine command.

### The March to Udum
The description of Keret's army marching to Udum is one of the finest passages of Ugaritic narrative poetry, employing hyperbolic imagery: the army is so vast that the solitary man finds a wife, the widow finds provisions, and the sick man takes up his bed and marches.

### Yassib's Challenge
The confrontation between the ill Keret and his ambitious son Yassib provides the text's dramatic climax. Yassib's speech — accusing his father of failing to judge the widow's case and protect the weak — introduces a theme of social justice into the royal epic.

## Narrative Structure

| Section | Literary Function |
|---------|------------------|
| Keret's grief | Establishes the protagonist's crisis |
| El's dream | Divine intervention and instruction |
| The campaign | Epic narrative of military action |
| Marriage and blessing | Fulfillment of divine promise |
| Illness | Crisis and theological complication |
| Yassib's challenge | Dramatic confrontation and climax |
