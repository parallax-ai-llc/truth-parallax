The third tablet is the most dramatically complex section of the Keret narrative. Yassib's speech — accusing his father of injustice — is one of the most powerful passages in Ugaritic literature: 'You do not judge the case of the widow, you do not try the case of the wretched... You do not drive out those who prey on the poor.' The literary effect is shattering: the son turns the language of royal responsibility against his own father.

Keret's response — a curse upon his son — is equally powerful and ambiguous. The text breaks off before the resolution, leaving the reader to wonder: is Keret justified in cursing Yassib, or has Yassib spoken a painful truth? This literary ambiguity is characteristic of the finest ancient narrative.
