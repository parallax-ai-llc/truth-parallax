The second tablet combines three literary modes: military narrative (the march to Udum), romantic narrative (the obtaining of Huraya), and blessing narrative (El's promise of progeny). The shift between these modes demonstrates the text's literary sophistication — it is simultaneously a war story, a love story, and a theological narrative.

The description of Keret's army is one of the finest passages of Ugaritic hyperbolic poetry: 'The solitary man closes up his house; the widow hires herself out for the campaign; the sick man carries his bed and marches.' The image of a society so completely mobilized that even the sick join the march is a literary tour de force.
