The first tablet demonstrates sophisticated literary technique in its handling of the dream-theophany. Keret's grief is described with physical specificity — his tears flowing like shekels of silver, his bed soaked with weeping — creating an image of overwhelming sorrow. The transition from grief to sleep to divine encounter is seamless, blurring the boundary between waking despair and dream revelation.

The extended repetition of El's instructions — first given by El, then executed by Keret — is the text's dominant literary device, occupying much of the tablet. This verbatim repetition serves both the oral-performative tradition and the theological purpose of demonstrating Keret's exact obedience to divine command.
