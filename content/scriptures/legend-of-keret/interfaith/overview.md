## Overview

The Legend of Keret touches on themes that resonate across religious traditions: the righteous person who suffers devastating loss, the divine promise of restoration, the fragility of human life even under divine protection, and the obligations of power. These themes invite interfaith reflection on the nature of faith, the meaning of suffering, and the relationship between divine favor and human responsibility.

## Interfaith Resonances

### The Suffering Righteous
Keret's loss of his entire family and subsequent illness parallel:
- **Judaism**: Job's sufferings and the theology of *tzadik v'ra lo* ("the righteous who suffers")
- **Christianity**: The suffering of Christ as the righteous one, and the theology of redemptive suffering
- **Islam**: The trials of Ayyub (Job) and the patience (*sabr*) of the prophets
- **Buddhism**: The universal reality of suffering (*dukkha*) as the First Noble Truth

### Divine Promise and Progeny
El's promise of children to the childless Keret resonates with:
- **Judaism**: God's covenant with Abraham (Genesis 15, 17)
- **Christianity**: The Annunciation to Mary
- **Islam**: The promise of Isaac and Ishmael to Ibrahim
- **Hinduism**: Dasharatha's fire sacrifice to obtain sons (Ramayana)

### The King's Obligation
Yassib's accusation that his father has failed to protect the widow and the orphan touches a nerve shared across traditions: the ruler's obligation to justice.

## Shared Themes

| Theme | Cross-Traditional Expression |
|-------|----------------------------|
| Righteous suffering | Job, Christ, Ayyub, the bodhisattva |
| Childlessness and promise | Abraham, Hannah, Zachariah, Dasharatha |
| Royal obligation | Justice for widows and orphans (Psalm 72, Quran 4:36) |
| Divine healing | El heals Keret; God heals Hezekiah; the Prophet's healing prayers |
