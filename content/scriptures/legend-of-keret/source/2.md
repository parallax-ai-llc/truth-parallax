---
title: "Legend of Keret - Chapter 2"
scripture: "legend-of-keret"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Keret awoke, and lo, it was a dream. The Gracious One, the Lad of El, awoke, and lo, it was a vision.

**2** He washed himself and made himself ruddy. He washed his hands to the elbow, his fingers up to the shoulder.

**3** He entered into the shade of the pavilion. He took a lamb of sacrifice in his hand, a kid of sacrifice in his right hand. He took his food, all of it. He took a sacrificial bird.

**4** He poured wine from a silver cup, honey from a golden cup. He went up to the top of the tower, he mounted the shoulder of the wall.

**5** He raised his hands to heaven. He sacrificed to the Bull, his father El. He honored Baal with his sacrifice, the Son of Dagan with his offering.

**6** Keret descended from the housetop. He prepared food for the city, wheat for the town. He baked bread for five months, provisions for six months.

**7** The host was provisioned and went forth. The vast host was provisioned. The host was three hundred myriads.

**8** They marched in thousands like the rain, and in myriads like the spring showers. After two they marched by the third, after three by the fourth. The lone man closed his house, the widow locked herself in.

**9** The sick man carried his bed, the blind man was led forth. Even the newly wed went forth. He led his wife to another, his beloved to a stranger.

**10** They went forth like locusts to the field, like grasshoppers to the border of the desert. They marched a day and a second, a third and a fourth day.

**11** At sunset on the fourth day they reached the shrine of Athirat of the Tyrians, even of the Goddess of the Sidonians. There Keret the Noble made a vow.

**12** "As Athirat of the Tyrians lives, as the Goddess of the Sidonians lives, if I take the Lady Hurriya into my house, if I bring the maiden into my court, two-thirds of her price I will give in gold, and a third in silver."

**13** They marched a fifth and a sixth day. Lo, at sunrise on the seventh day they came to Udm the Great, even to Udm the Well-Watered.

**14** He stayed in the fields and encamped. He sent a messenger to Pabil the King. "The message of King Keret: Thus says Keret the Gracious One:"

**15** "Give me the Lady Hurriya, the fairest of thy first-born. Whose grace is as the grace of Anat, whose beauty is as the beauty of Astarte. Give her to me."

**16** And Pabil the King replied: "Take silver and gold, a holding of land and servants forever. Take three horses and a chariot from the stable of a handmaid's son. Take, O Keret, take all these, but give me not the Lady Hurriya."

**17** But Keret the Noble sent again, saying: "What need have I of silver and gold, of a holding of land and servants? Nay, but what is not in my house thou must give. Give me the Lady Hurriya."

**18** And Pabil saw the vast host of Keret, the three hundred myriads encamped against him. He saw there was no withstanding the army, no repelling the host.

**19** So Pabil the King gave the Lady Hurriya to Keret. He gave the fairest of his first-born. The gods blessed Keret and departed.

**20** El the Bull, his father, blessed him: "The woman thou hast taken, O Keret, the woman thou hast taken into thy house, the maiden thou hast brought into thy court, shall bear thee seven sons, yea, eight she shall produce."

**21** "She shall bear the lad Yassib, who shall suck the milk of Athirat, who shall drain the breast of the Virgin Anat, the two wet-nurses of the gods."

**22** And it came to pass that what El had promised was fulfilled. The Lady Hurriya bore him sons. In seven years the sons of Keret were as El had promised, and the daughters of Hurriya were as Baal had granted.

**23** And Athirat remembered his vow, even the Goddess of the Sidonians his promise. And she lifted her voice and cried: "Look now, does Keret the Noble break his vow? Then shall I break my promise."

**24** And Keret fell sick. He was stricken upon his bed. His throne was empty in the assembly. Keret the king was ill, and the land mourned.
