## Overview

The Legend of Keret invites comparison with a wide range of ancient Near Eastern and biblical texts that address the themes of royal suffering, divine promise, dynastic crisis, and the intersection of human vulnerability with sacred authority. Its most significant comparative connections are with the biblical traditions of Job, the patriarchal narratives, and ancient Near Eastern royal ideology.

## Key Comparative Connections

### Biblical Parallels
- **Job**: Both Keret and Job are righteous figures who suffer devastating loss, appeal to the divine, and are ultimately restored. The parallel extends to the literary structure of loss-complaint-divine response-restoration
- **Abraham**: El's promise of progeny to the childless Keret parallels God's covenant with Abraham (Genesis 15, 17)
- **David**: A king favored by God who nonetheless falls into crisis through illness and family conflict (2 Samuel 11–19)
- **2 Kings 20**: Hezekiah's illness and divine healing closely parallel Keret's experience

### Ancient Near Eastern Royal Ideology
Keret's designation as "son of El" reflects the widespread ancient Near Eastern concept of the king as the adopted or biological son of a deity (compare Egyptian pharaonic ideology and Mesopotamian royal hymns).

### Fairy Tale Motifs
The narrative includes motifs common to folkloristic analysis: the childless king, the dream vision, the quest for a bride, the impossible demand, and the magical healing.
