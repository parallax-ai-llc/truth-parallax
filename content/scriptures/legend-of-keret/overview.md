## Overview

The Legend of Keret (KTU 1.14–1.16) is one of three major narrative poems recovered from ancient Ugarit, inscribed on three clay tablets in Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform and dating to approximately 1400–1200 BCE. Unlike the mythological Baal Cycle, Keret is a royal epic centered on a human king who loses his entire family, receives divine guidance from El to obtain a new wife, leads a military campaign, and subsequently falls gravely ill. The text explores the intersection of human kingship and divine favor, the fragility of dynasty, and the obligations of the king to both gods and people.

## Structure

The three tablets narrate Keret's loss, restoration, and crisis:

1. **Tablet 1 (KTU 1.14)**: Keret's grief over his lost family, El's appearance in a dream, and instructions for the military campaign to obtain Huraya of Udum
2. **Tablet 2 (KTU 1.15)**: The march to Udum, the siege, Keret's marriage to Huraya, and the banquet blessed by El with promises of progeny
3. **Tablet 3 (KTU 1.16)**: Keret's grave illness, the failure of his son Yassib, El's intervention to heal the king, and the succession crisis

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Legend of Keret provides unique insight into ancient conceptions of sacred kingship:

- **Academic**: Essential for understanding Ugaritic literary conventions and royal ideology
- **Comparative**: Parallels with biblical narratives of Job, David, and the patriarchal promises
- **Theological**: Addresses theodicy, divine healing, and the king's sacral role as mediator between gods and people
- **Literary**: A rare example of ancient Near Eastern royal epic focused on domestic and personal themes
- **Archaeological**: The tablets illuminate the scribal culture of Late Bronze Age Ugarit
- **Interfaith**: The themes of suffering, faith, and restoration resonate across religious traditions
