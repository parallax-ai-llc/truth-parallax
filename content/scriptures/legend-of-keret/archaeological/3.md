The social obligations of kingship described in Yassib's accusation — protecting widows, orphans, and the poor — are abundantly attested in Ugaritic and ancient Near Eastern royal inscriptions. The 'ideal king' texts from Ugarit and the broader Mesopotamian royal ideology describe the king's primary duty as maintaining justice for the vulnerable members of society.

Archaeological evidence of Ugarit's legal system — court records, legal contracts, and administrative texts — confirms the king's active role in judicial proceedings. The gate complex excavated at Ugarit may have served as the judicial venue where cases like those Yassib describes were heard.
