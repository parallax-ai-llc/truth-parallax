## Overview

The Legend of Keret was discovered at Ras Shamra (ancient Ugarit) during the systematic French excavations that began in 1929. The three tablets (KTU 1.14, 1.15, 1.16) were found in the area between the great temples of Baal and Dagan, in the building identified as the house of the high priest — the same archival context as the Baal Cycle and the Tale of Aqhat.

## Archaeological Context

### The Priestly Library
The concentration of literary, mythological, and ritual texts in the house of the high priest indicates that these texts served cultic and educational functions within Ugarit's religious establishment. The Legend of Keret may have been performed during royal rituals or used in scribal training.

### The Tablets
The three tablets are large multi-column texts inscribed on both sides in the Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform script. Colophons identify the scribe as Ilimilku, the same scribe who copied the Baal Cycle. The tablets date to the 14th–13th centuries BCE.

### Ugarit as a Royal City
The Legend of Keret's focus on sacred kingship reflects the political reality of Ugarit as a prosperous Late Bronze Age city-state governed by a dynasty of kings who served as intermediaries between the gods and their people. The royal palace at Ugarit — one of the largest in the ancient Near East — testifies to the importance of kingship in Ugaritic society.
