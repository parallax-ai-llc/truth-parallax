The Eastern Orthodox Church does not accept Thunder, Perfect Mind as part of the biblical canon. The text is recognized as a product of Gnostic spirituality, which the Orthodox Fathers consistently opposed as a distortion of authentic Christian teaching. Orthodox theology maintains that divine revelation is mediated through Scripture, the Fathers, the councils, and the liturgical life of the Church — not through extra-canonical Gnostic writings.

Orthodox scholars have observed that the paradoxical structure of Thunder, Perfect Mind bears a formal resemblance to apophatic theology, the tradition of speaking about God through negation and paradox. The speaker's simultaneous affirmation and denial of attributes echoes the mystical theology of Dionysius the Areopagite. However, Orthodox interpreters insist that apophatic theology operates within the Trinitarian framework, whereas the Gnostic text lacks this doctrinal grounding.

The figure of Holy Wisdom (Hagia Sophia) holds a revered place in Orthodox theology, architecture, and liturgy. Orthodox scholars are therefore attentive to Gnostic texts that invoke Sophia but redirect her significance away from orthodox Christology and ecclesiology. Thunder, Perfect Mind represents a trajectory that the Fathers deliberately excluded from the Church's canonical and theological patrimony.
