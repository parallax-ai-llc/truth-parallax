Eastern Orthodox interpreters approach Thunder, Perfect Mind chapter 1 with the discernment inherited from the patristic tradition. The text's paradoxical self-declarations by a feminine divine figure are recognized as formally powerful but theologically unmoored from the Trinitarian framework that gives paradox its proper meaning in Orthodox mystical theology. The apophatic tradition of Dionysius the Areopagite and Maximus the Confessor also employs paradox, but always within the ecclesial and sacramental context of the Church's life.

The chapter's insistence on self-knowledge as the path to the divine — "Know me" — contrasts with the Orthodox emphasis on theosis through participation in the sacramental life, prayer, and ascetic practice. Orthodox theology holds that knowledge of God is not a private, intellectual achievement but a communal, liturgical, and transformative process mediated by the Holy Spirit within the Body of Christ.

Orthodox scholars note the resonance between the speaker's claim to universal presence and the Orthodox theology of the Logos, who is present in all creation. However, the absence of any incarnational theology in Thunder, Perfect Mind marks a decisive departure from Orthodox Christology. The Word made flesh, the central mystery of Orthodox faith, finds no echo in this Gnostic text.
