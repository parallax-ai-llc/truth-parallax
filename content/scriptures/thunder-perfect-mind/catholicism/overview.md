The Catholic Church does not recognize Thunder, Perfect Mind as canonical Scripture. The text belongs to the Nag Hammadi library and reflects Gnostic theological perspectives that were rejected by the early Church Fathers. Catholic scholarship treats the text as a significant historical document from the margins of early Christianity, valuable for understanding the religious landscape in which orthodoxy was defined.

Some Catholic scholars have noted intriguing resonances between the paradoxical feminine speaker of Thunder, Perfect Mind and Catholic traditions of Marian devotion and Sophiology. The figure who is simultaneously exalted and humble, honored and scorned, evokes certain aspects of the Virgin Mary as celebrated in Catholic theology — the humble handmaid who becomes Queen of Heaven. These parallels are noted cautiously, without granting the Gnostic text theological authority.

The Church Fathers, including Irenaeus and Hippolytus, opposed Gnostic movements that elevated feminine divine figures outside orthodox Trinitarian theology. Thunder, Perfect Mind exemplifies the kind of Gnostic speculation the Fathers rejected. Catholic scholars study it to understand the theological challenges the early Church confronted and the reasons particular boundaries of canon and doctrine were drawn.
