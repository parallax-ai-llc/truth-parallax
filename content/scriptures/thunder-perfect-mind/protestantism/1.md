Protestant scholars approach Thunder, Perfect Mind chapter 1 as a non-canonical text of historical and literary interest. The female speaker's paradoxical self-revelations — claiming to be simultaneously honored and scorned, wise and ignorant, powerful and powerless — have no parallel in the Protestant biblical canon and reflect a theological worldview distant from Reformation principles of sola scriptura and Christocentric soteriology.

The chapter's literary power is nonetheless acknowledged by Protestant biblical scholars. The form of divine self-revelation through first-person speech has canonical parallels in Proverbs 8, where Wisdom speaks of her cosmic origins, and in the "I am" sayings of the Gospel of John. Protestant scholarship uses these comparisons not to validate the Gnostic text but to trace the development of revelatory literary forms across the ancient Mediterranean world, illuminating the cultural context in which the New Testament was written.

Protestant interpreters note the complete absence of Christology, atonement theology, or any concept of salvation through faith in this chapter. The speaker demands recognition and knowledge rather than faith and repentance. This fundamental theological difference confirms the text's distance from Protestant Christianity, though it remains valuable for understanding the diversity of religious expression in the early centuries of the Common Era.
