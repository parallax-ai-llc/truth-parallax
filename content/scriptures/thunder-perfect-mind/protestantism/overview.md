Protestant scholars do not consider Thunder, Perfect Mind canonical or authoritative for Christian doctrine. The text is classified among the Nag Hammadi writings that reflect Gnostic spirituality rather than orthodox Christianity. Its paradoxical self-declarations by a feminine divine figure stand outside the theological framework of Protestant orthodoxy, which locates divine revelation in the canonical scriptures.

Nonetheless, Protestant biblical scholars have engaged with Thunder, Perfect Mind as an important witness to the diversity of early Christian and near-Christian movements. The text's literary form — a divine figure speaking in paradoxical self-praise — has parallels in canonical Wisdom literature (Proverbs 8, Sirach 24), and Protestant scholars study these connections to better understand how wisdom traditions developed in the Second Temple and early Christian periods.

Protestant interpreters note that the text's theology diverges sharply from biblical monotheism. The speaker's claim to be both "the whore and the holy one" and the absence of any christological or soteriological framework rooted in Scripture place Thunder, Perfect Mind firmly outside Protestant doctrinal boundaries. It remains valuable as a historical document rather than a source of theological authority.
