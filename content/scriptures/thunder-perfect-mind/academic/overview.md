Academic scholarship on Thunder, Perfect Mind (NHC VI,2) has focused on its remarkable literary form, its possible antecedents, and its theological implications. The text is typically dated to the second or third century CE, though its original language (Greek or Coptic) and precise community of origin remain debated. Scholars have classified it as an aretalogy — a self-revelatory discourse in which a deity proclaims their attributes — drawing parallels with Isis aretalogies from the Greco-Roman world.

Bentley Layton, George MacRae, and other scholars have analyzed the paradoxical structure of the text, noting that it does not fit neatly into any single Gnostic system. Some scholars argue it is pre-Gnostic or non-Gnostic, a Jewish wisdom text that was later adopted by Gnostic communities. Others see it as thoroughly Gnostic, with the paradoxes reflecting the Sophia myth of fall and restoration. The lack of explicit mythological narrative makes classification difficult.

Thunder, Perfect Mind has become one of the most widely read Nag Hammadi texts outside academia, partly due to its use in Ridley Scott's Blade Runner: The Final Cut. Feminist scholars including Karen King and Anne McGuire have explored the text as evidence for feminine divine discourse in antiquity. Its reception history demonstrates how ancient texts can acquire new cultural significance in modern contexts.
