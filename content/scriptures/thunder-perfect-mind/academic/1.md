Academic analysis of Thunder, Perfect Mind chapter 1 centers on the text's literary genre, its relationship to Greco-Roman aretalogies, and its place within the Nag Hammadi corpus. The paradoxical self-declarations have been compared to Isis aretalogies, in which the goddess proclaims her powers and attributes in first person. George MacRae's foundational study argued that the text draws on this Hellenistic form while adapting it for a Gnostic or proto-Gnostic context.

The question of whether the text is properly "Gnostic" remains debated. The chapter contains no explicit references to the Demiurge, the Pleroma, archons, or other standard Gnostic mythological elements. Some scholars, including Bentley Layton, classify it as a Gnostic text on the basis of its inclusion in the Nag Hammadi library and its thematic affinities with Sophia mythology. Others argue it may represent an independent tradition of feminine divine discourse that was adopted by Gnostic communities without originating among them.

Linguistic analysis of the Coptic text suggests a Greek original, likely composed in Egypt during the second or third century CE. The text's rhetorical sophistication — its use of antithesis, anaphora, and chiasmus — indicates a literate author familiar with both Jewish wisdom traditions and Hellenistic rhetorical conventions. The chapter functions as a complete literary unit, suggesting that the text may originally have been a single continuous composition rather than an excerpt from a longer work.
