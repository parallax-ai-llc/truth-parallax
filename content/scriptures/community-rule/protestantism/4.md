## Purity and the New Covenant

Protestant scholars read Chapter 4's purity laws in light of the New Testament's complex relationship with Jewish purity traditions. Jesus' declaration that "nothing outside a person can defile them" (Mark 7:15) and Peter's vision in Acts 10 represent a fundamental transformation of the purity categories that the Community Rule so carefully codified. Protestant interpretation sees this transformation as the fulfillment of the prophetic tradition that prioritized inner holiness over external ritual.

## Inner Transformation

The chapter's insistence that external purification requires internal repentance finds resonance in Protestant theology. The Community Rule's recognition that water washing is spiritually meaningless without a transformed heart anticipates the Protestant emphasis on the priority of faith over ritual performance. Protestant scholars note this as evidence that the best of Second Temple Jewish piety already recognized the limitations of purely external observance.

## The Holy Spirit and Purification

The chapter's language about God purifying the righteous through the "Spirit of Truth" is of particular interest to Protestant interpreters, who see here a precursor to the New Testament doctrine of sanctification by the Holy Spirit. While the Community Rule's "Spirit of Truth" is an angelic being rather than a divine person, the functional parallel — a divine agent that purifies believers from within — illuminates the theological environment from which Christian pneumatology emerged.
