## Covenant Theology and the New Testament

Protestant scholars read Chapter 1's covenant ceremony in light of New Testament covenant theology. The solemn oath of entry, the renunciation of evil, and the public commitment to obedience invite comparison with early Christian baptismal liturgies, which similarly required a renunciation of Satan and a profession of faith. The chapter demonstrates that the covenantal framework central to Protestant theology was already well developed in pre-Christian Judaism.

## Grace and Law

The relationship between divine initiative and human response in the covenant ceremony is of particular interest to Protestant interpreters. While the ceremony emphasizes the candidate's commitment to obey, it is framed within the context of God's prior election of the community. This interplay between divine grace and human obligation resonates with the Reformation debate over justification and the relationship between faith and works.

## Implications for Ecclesiology

Protestant scholars also note the implications of the chapter for understanding early church organization. The structured admission process, the communal commitment, and the annual renewal of vows suggest that the earliest Christian communities inherited from Judaism a well-developed model of covenant community that shaped their own institutional development.
