## Genre and Function

Academic scholars classify the Maskil's hymn as a *hodayah* (thanksgiving psalm), related in genre to the Hodayot scroll (1QH) found at Qumran. The hymn's first-person voice, its alternation between confession of human weakness and praise of divine power, and its use of wisdom vocabulary situate it within a broader Qumran literary tradition of devotional composition. Scholars debate whether the hymn was composed specifically for the Community Rule or was an independent composition incorporated into the text at a later redactional stage.

## Theological Anthropology

The hymn provides crucial evidence for the theological anthropology of the Qumran community. Its stark confession of human frailty — "what is one born of woman among all your awesome works?" — has been compared with similar expressions in Job, Ecclesiastes, and the Hodayot. Scholars analyze the hymn's language of flesh (*basar*), spirit (*ruach*), and righteousness (*tsedaqah*) to reconstruct the community's understanding of the human condition and its relationship to divine grace.

## Liturgical Calendar

The hymn's detailed enumeration of prayer times — connected to solar, lunar, and seasonal transitions — provides evidence for the Qumran community's liturgical calendar, which followed a 364-day solar reckoning rather than the lunisolar calendar used in the Jerusalem Temple. This calendrical commitment was one of the fundamental points of disagreement between the Qumran community and the Temple establishment, and the hymn's insistence on praising God "at the times He has ordained" carries a polemical edge.
