## Conciliar Governance

Eastern Orthodox scholars read Chapter 5's description of the community council in light of the Orthodox principle of conciliarity (*sobornost*). The Qumran council's structure, in which priests hold authority but decisions are made through communal deliberation, anticipates the Orthodox model of governance in which the bishop presides but the council of the Church — clergy and laity together — participates in discernment and decision-making.

## The Spiritual Father

The figure of the Maskil resonates with the Orthodox tradition of the *staretz* (spiritual elder or father), who guides others in the spiritual life through wisdom, discernment, and personal holiness. The Maskil's role as examiner of candidates, teacher of doctrine, and spiritual guide anticipates the Orthodox understanding of spiritual direction as essential for growth in holiness.

## The Community as Temple

The chapter's vision of the community council as the spiritual foundation of the world — a "holy of holies" composed of living persons — resonates with the Orthodox theology of the Church as the living Temple of God. Orthodox ecclesiology understands the local eucharistic assembly, gathered around the bishop, as the full manifestation of the Church, the place where heaven and earth meet in the celebration of the Divine Liturgy.
