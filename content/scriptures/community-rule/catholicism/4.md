## Sacramental Prefiguration

Catholic scholars read Chapter 4's purity laws as anticipations of the Church's sacramental system. The ritual washings described in the chapter are seen as "types" or prefigurations of baptism, while the chapter's insistence on inner disposition as a prerequisite for effective ritual resonates with the Catholic teaching that sacraments require proper disposition in the recipient to bear fruit.

## The Body-Soul Unity

The chapter's integration of physical and spiritual purification reflects a holistic anthropology that Catholic theology shares. The conviction that the body's purity and the soul's purity are inseparable anticipates the Catholic understanding of the human person as a unity of body and soul, and the sacramental principle that spiritual grace is communicated through material signs.

## The Community as Temple

The chapter's vision of the purified community as a substitute for the Temple resonates with Catholic ecclesiology, in which the Church is understood as the Body of Christ and the Temple of the Holy Spirit. Catholic interpreters see in the Qumran community's self-understanding a partial anticipation of the fuller reality that the Church would embody: a community of the purified and consecrated in which God truly dwells.
