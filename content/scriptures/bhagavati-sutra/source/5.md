---
title: "Bhagavati Sutra (Viyahapannatti) - Chapter 5: On Karma"
scripture: "bhagavati-sutra"
chapter: 5
---

**1** The venerable Gautama asked: "Lord, what is karma?" The Blessed One said: "O Gautama, karma is the subtle matter that clings to the soul due to its activities of body, speech, and mind. It is the cause of bondage and the obstacle to liberation."

**2** "Karma is of eight kinds: knowledge-obscuring karma, perception-obscuring karma, feeling-producing karma, deluding karma, life-determining karma, body-determining karma, status-determining karma, and obstructive karma."

**3** "Knowledge-obscuring karma covers the soul's natural capacity for infinite knowledge, just as a veil covers the eyes and prevents one from seeing. There are five sub-types, covering the five kinds of knowledge."

**4** "Perception-obscuring karma covers the soul's natural capacity for infinite perception. Feeling-producing karma causes the soul to experience pleasure and pain. These feelings arise not from the Self but from the karmic matter attached to the Self."

**5** "Deluding karma is the most dangerous of all karmas, for it deludes the soul regarding its own true nature. It is of two kinds: faith-deluding and conduct-deluding. Faith-deluding karma prevents right belief; conduct-deluding karma prevents right conduct."

**6** "Life-determining karma determines the span of life in a particular existence. Body-determining karma determines the type of body—whether human, animal, celestial, or infernal. Status-determining karma determines one's station and circumstances."

**7** "Obstructive karma obstructs the soul's natural infinite energy, just as a treasurer may obstruct the king from giving charity even when the king desires to give."

**8** "The influx of karma is caused by the activities of body, speech, and mind. When these activities are performed with passion—with attachment, aversion, or delusion—karma binds the soul tightly, like wet clay sticking to a pot."

**9** "When activities are performed without passion—without attachment, aversion, or delusion—karma touches the soul but does not bind, like dry sand thrown against a pot, which falls away immediately."

**10** "The stoppage of karmic influx is called Samvara. It is achieved through the practice of the five vows, the five kinds of carefulness, the three guards, the ten dharmas, the twelve reflections, and the endurance of hardships."

**11** "The shedding of accumulated karma is called Nirjara. It is of two kinds: natural shedding, which occurs with the passage of time, and deliberate shedding, which is achieved through austerities and meditation."

**12** "When all karma is destroyed, the soul attains liberation—Moksha. It rises to the summit of the universe, where it abides forever in its pure nature of infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, and infinite energy."
