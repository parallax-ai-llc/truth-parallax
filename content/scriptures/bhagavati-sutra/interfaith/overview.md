## Overview

Interfaith interpretation of the Bhagavati Sutra finds in its dialogues a model for the kind of earnest questioning and honest answering that inter-religious dialogue requires. The text's willingness to engage with fundamental questions about life, death, karma, and liberation speaks to concerns shared across all religious traditions.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Universal Questions

The questions Indrabhuti Gautama poses to Mahavira -- about the nature of the soul, the mechanism of suffering, and the possibility of liberation -- are questions every major religious tradition addresses. The Bhagavati Sutra offers distinctive Jain answers to universal human concerns.

### Dialogue as Model

The respectful, rigorous exchange between teacher and student in the Bhagavati Sutra models the kind of honest engagement needed for productive inter-religious dialogue. Truth emerges through sincere questioning and patient explanation.

### Shared Ethical Concerns

The text's ethical teachings -- on non-violence, truthfulness, and compassion -- identify common ground with virtually all major religious traditions.

## Interfaith Connections

| Jain Teaching | Parallel in Other Traditions |
|---------------|------------------------------|
| Mahavira-Gautama dialogue | Socratic dialogue, Buddhist suttas, Upanishadic discussion |
| Karma theory | Buddhist kamma, Hindu karma, Christian "you reap what you sow" |
| Soul liberation | Buddhist nirvana, Hindu moksha, Christian salvation |
| Non-violence | Buddhist ahimsa, Christian pacifism, Gandhian satyagraha |
