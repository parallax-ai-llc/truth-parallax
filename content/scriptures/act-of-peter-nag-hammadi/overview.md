## Overview

The Act of Peter (NHC VI,1) is a short narrative fragment — unique among the Nag Hammadi texts for being a narrative rather than a revelation dialogue, treatise, or prayer. The surviving text describes Peter performing a miraculous healing of his own paralyzed daughter before a crowd, then immediately reversing the healing and returning her to her paralyzed state. Peter explains that the paralysis was ordained by God to protect the girl's virginity — a wealthy man named Ptolemy had been driven to obsession by her beauty, and the paralysis prevented him from corrupting her. The text thus affirms both the reality of apostolic miracle-working and the providential value of suffering, particularly in the service of preserving sexual purity. A Latin parallel (the Actus Vercellenses) preserves a related version of the story.

| 3 | The Lesson of Providence | The story of Ptolemy's desire and repentance, illustrating the providential meaning of suffering |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: The text's emphasis on sexual purity and bodily renunciation connects it to the encratite tendencies of some Gnostic groups
- **Academic**: Studied for its genre (narrative rather than discourse), its relationship to the Acts of Peter tradition, and its theology of suffering
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to hagiographic miracle stories across religious traditions
- **Christian**: Raises theological questions about suffering, providence, and the value of virginity in early Christianity
- **Jewish**: Engages with Jewish concepts of divine providence and the meaning of affliction
- **Interfaith**: Addresses the universal question of why the righteous suffer and whether affliction can serve a spiritual purpose

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Apostolic Miracle | Peter's healing power demonstrates his authority, yet he subordinates it to God's will |
| Providential Suffering | Affliction is not punishment but protection — God uses suffering for spiritual purposes |
| Virginity | Sexual purity is presented as a value worth preserving even at the cost of physical wholeness |
| Reversed Miracle | The reversal of the healing challenges assumptions about what constitutes divine blessing |
