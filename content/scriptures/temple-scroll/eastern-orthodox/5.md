## Symphonia

Eastern Orthodox scholars read Chapter 5's Law of the King in light of the Byzantine principle of *symphonia* — the harmonious cooperation of church and state, in which the emperor and the patriarch work together under God for the welfare of the Christian commonwealth. The Temple Scroll's vision of a king subordinate to priestly authority represents one model of this relationship, anticipating the broader Orthodox conviction that political power must be exercised in accordance with divine law and under the spiritual guidance of the Church.

## Kenotic Authority

The chapter's limitations on royal power resonate with the Orthodox understanding of authority as fundamentally kenotic (self-emptying), following the pattern of Christ who "emptied himself, taking the form of a servant" (Philippians 2:7). Orthodox theology teaches that legitimate authority is exercised through service, humility, and obedience to God, not through the accumulation of power. The Temple Scroll's constrained monarch anticipates this vision of authority shaped by divine law.

## The Christian Emperor

The chapter's vision of a Torah-formed king finds its Christian counterpart in the Orthodox ideal of the Christian emperor, who governs as God's representative on earth and is expected to model his rule on divine justice and mercy. The Temple Scroll's requirement that the king keep a personal Torah scroll and consult it regularly anticipates the Orthodox expectation that rulers will be formed by Scripture and guided by the counsel of the Church.
