## The Iconostasis and Sacred Boundaries

Eastern Orthodox scholars read Chapter 2's concentric courts of graded holiness in light of the iconostasis — the screen of icons that separates the nave from the sanctuary in Orthodox churches. Like the Temple Scroll's graduated courts, the iconostasis creates distinct zones of holiness while simultaneously connecting the worshipper to the mystery beyond. The Royal Doors, opened during specific moments of the liturgy, mediate between the accessible and the hidden, just as the Temple Scroll's court walls regulated movement between zones of sanctity.

## Mystical Ascent

The chapter's progressive approach to the divine center — from outer court to inner court to Holy of Holies — mirrors the Orthodox mystical tradition of progressive ascent toward God. The three stages of the spiritual life described by the Fathers — purification, illumination, and theosis — correspond to the Temple Scroll's spatial progression from the periphery to the sacred center, where God's presence is most intensely experienced.

## The Cosmic Temple

Orthodox scholars connect the chapter's temple design to the patristic understanding of the cosmos itself as a temple. Maximus the Confessor described the universe as a great temple with heaven as its sanctuary and earth as its nave. The Temple Scroll's concentric architecture, radiating holiness outward from the center, reflects this cosmic temple theology in architectural form.
