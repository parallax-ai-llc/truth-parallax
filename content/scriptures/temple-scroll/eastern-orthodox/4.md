## Preparation for Communion

Eastern Orthodox scholars read Chapter 4's purity laws for the holy city in light of the Orthodox practice of preparing for Holy Communion. The Orthodox faithful are expected to prepare through fasting, prayer, confession, and self-examination before receiving the Eucharist — practices that echo the Temple Scroll's insistence on purification before approaching the sacred center. The underlying conviction that holiness requires intentional preparation is shared.

## The Holy Mountain

The chapter's vision of the holy city as a zone of escalating sanctity resonates with the Orthodox veneration of holy places, particularly Mount Athos — the "Holy Mountain" — where an entire peninsula is consecrated to monastic prayer and strict standards of conduct are maintained for all who enter. The Temple Scroll's holy city, with its exclusion of sources of impurity, finds a contemporary echo in the Athonite regulations governing the sacred mountain.

## Deification of the Body

The chapter's concern for bodily purity reflects an understanding of the body as a participant in holiness that Orthodox theology fully embraces. The Orthodox doctrine of theosis includes the body — the physical person is destined for transfiguration, not merely the soul. The Temple Scroll's integration of physical and spiritual purity, in which the body's condition is inseparable from the person's spiritual state, anticipates the Orthodox vision of embodied holiness.
