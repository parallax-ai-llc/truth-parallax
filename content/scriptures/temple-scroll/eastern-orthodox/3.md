## The Liturgical Calendar

Eastern Orthodox scholars read Chapter 3's expanded festival calendar in light of the rich liturgical cycle of the Orthodox Church, which commemorates the mysteries of Christ, the Theotokos, and the saints through an elaborate annual round of feasts and fasts. The Temple Scroll's conviction that sacred time must be comprehensively structured resonates with the Orthodox understanding that the liturgical calendar sanctifies every moment of the year, transforming chronological time into kairos — time filled with divine significance.

## The Offering of Creation

The chapter's festivals of firstfruits — including the additional festivals of New Wine and New Oil — reflect a theology of offering that the Orthodox tradition develops in the Eucharist. The Prothesis (preparation of the gifts) and the Great Entrance of the Divine Liturgy present the bread and wine as the offering of creation back to its Creator. The Temple Scroll's agricultural festivals, in which the produce of the land is consecrated to God, anticipate this eucharistic transformation of the material world.

## The Solar Calendar and Cosmic Order

The chapter's insistence on a solar calendar reflects a conviction that liturgical time must correspond to cosmic order. Orthodox scholars note that while the Orthodox Church ultimately adopted a lunisolar calendar, the underlying principle — that worship must be aligned with the rhythms God established in creation — is shared. The Orthodox computation of Pascha (Easter), with its complex rules involving solar and lunar cycles, reflects the same concern for harmonizing human worship with divine cosmic order.
