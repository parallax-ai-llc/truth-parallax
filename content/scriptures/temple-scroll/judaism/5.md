## The Torah of the King

Jewish scholars read Chapter 5's Law of the King alongside the biblical law of the king in Deuteronomy 17:14-20, which the Temple Scroll significantly expands. The chapter's constitutional limitations on royal authority — restrictions on military power, wealth accumulation, and foreign marriages — represent an interpretation of Deuteronomy that is more restrictive than the historical practice of Israelite and Judean kings, reflecting a priestly critique of monarchical excess.

## Theocracy and Governance

The chapter's subordination of royal to priestly authority is analyzed within broader Jewish debates about the proper form of governance. Jewish political thought has oscillated between theocratic ideals (expressed in the Torah's priestly legislation and the Temple Scroll), monarchical pragmatism (expressed in the Deuteronomistic History), and democratic elements (expressed in synagogue governance and communal decision-making). The Temple Scroll represents the most fully developed statement of the theocratic position.

## The King's Torah Scroll

The chapter's requirement that the king keep a personal copy of the Torah, written under priestly supervision and consulted before all major decisions, expands the similar requirement in Deuteronomy 17:18-19. Jewish scholars note that this provision anticipates the rabbinic ideal of the Torah-observant ruler and reflects the broader Jewish conviction that legitimate authority must be grounded in divine law rather than personal power.
