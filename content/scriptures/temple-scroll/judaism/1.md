## Rewritten Torah

Jewish scholars read Chapter 1's temple instructions as a prime example of "Rewritten Torah," a genre in which biblical legislation is expanded, harmonized, and recontextualized. The Temple Scroll's method of presenting its innovations as direct divine speech — transforming third-person Mosaic narration into first-person divine command — represents a bold interpretive strategy that illuminates the broader phenomenon of scriptural interpretation in Second Temple Judaism.

## Temple Theology

The chapter's vision of the ideal temple is analyzed within the rich Jewish tradition of temple theology, from the Tabernacle instructions in Exodus to Solomon's Temple, Ezekiel's visionary temple (chapters 40-48), and the Second Temple. Jewish scholars note that the impulse to envision the perfect sanctuary — always more glorious and more holy than the existing one — runs throughout Jewish literature and finds expression in rabbinic traditions about the heavenly temple and the messianic rebuilding.

## Authority and Interpretation

The chapter raises fundamental questions about scriptural authority and interpretation. By presenting its novel regulations as direct divine speech, the Temple Scroll claims an authority that potentially rivals the canonical Torah. Jewish scholars analyze this claim in the context of broader debates about the scope and limits of scriptural authority, the legitimacy of post-Mosaic revelation, and the mechanisms by which new legal traditions gained acceptance in ancient Judaism.
