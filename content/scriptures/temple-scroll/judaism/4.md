## The Sanctity of Jerusalem

Jewish scholarship approaches Chapter 4's purity regulations for the holy city within the broader context of Jewish traditions about the sanctity of Jerusalem. The Mishnah (Kelim 1:6-9) also assigns special holiness to the walled city of Jerusalem, requiring certain purity standards of those who enter. The Temple Scroll's more stringent requirements demonstrate that the principle of urban sanctity was widely accepted, even as the specific standards were debated.

## Impurity and Exclusion

The chapter's detailed catalog of impurities that disqualify individuals from entering the holy city is compared by Jewish scholars with the parallel lists in Leviticus, Numbers, and the Mishnah. The Temple Scroll's unique contributions — such as the specific distance required for latrines and the prohibition of sexual intercourse within the city — reveal the distinctive halakhic positions of the Qumran community and the intensity of their concern for spatial holiness.

## The Holiness of the Land

Jewish scholars connect the chapter's purity regulations to the broader Jewish theology of the holiness of the Land of Israel, which the rabbis would develop extensively. The conviction that the land itself possesses sanctity, that this sanctity radiates outward from the Temple in concentric zones, and that human impurity can contaminate the sacred geography is shared by the Temple Scroll and rabbinic tradition, even though the specific boundaries and requirements differ.
