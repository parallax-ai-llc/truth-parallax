## Sacred Architecture

Jewish scholars examine Chapter 2's architectural prescriptions within the long tradition of Jewish concern for sacred space. The chapter's concentric court design, with its escalating purity requirements, reflects a theology of graded holiness attested in the Mishnah's tractate Kelim, which lists ten degrees of sanctity from the land of Israel to the Holy of Holies. While the specific dimensions differ, the underlying principle — that proximity to the divine presence requires greater holiness — is shared.

## Comparison with Ezekiel 40-48

The chapter's temple plan is extensively compared by Jewish scholars with Ezekiel's visionary temple, which served as a major source and model. Both texts envision a concentric temple complex with precise measurements, but the Temple Scroll's design is significantly larger and more elaborate. The differences between the two plans reveal how the Temple Scroll's author engaged in creative interpretation, using Ezekiel as a starting point for an independent architectural vision.

## The Temple in Jewish Memory

The detailed measurements and specifications of Chapter 2 contribute to the rich Jewish tradition of remembering and reimagining the Temple after its destruction. From the Mishnah's tractate Middot (which preserves measurements of the Second Temple) to Maimonides' Laws of the Temple to modern architectural reconstructions, the Jewish engagement with temple architecture has served as both historical memory and messianic hope.
