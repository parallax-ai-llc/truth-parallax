## Calendar Controversies

Jewish scholars analyze Chapter 3's festival calendar within the context of the intense calendar disputes that divided Second Temple Judaism. The 364-day solar calendar advocated by the Temple Scroll stood in opposition to the lunisolar calendar that was eventually adopted by rabbinic Judaism. The existence of this alternative calendar tradition demonstrates that the Jewish liturgical calendar was not fixed in the Second Temple period but was the subject of vigorous debate with significant theological implications.

## Additional Festivals

The chapter's prescription of festivals not found in the canonical Torah — New Wine, New Oil, and Wood Offering — is analyzed as evidence for the diversity of Jewish liturgical practice before the rabbinic standardization. Jewish scholars note that while these specific festivals did not survive into rabbinic Judaism, the impulse to expand the liturgical calendar with additional celebrations of divine provision continued in post-biblical Judaism, as evidenced by Hanukkah and Purim.

## Sacrificial Halakhah

The chapter's detailed sacrificial prescriptions are compared with the rabbinic laws of sacrifice codified in the Mishnah's order of Kodashim. While the specific requirements often differ — reflecting the sectarian community's distinctive interpretive traditions — the categories and concerns are recognizably continuous with the broader Jewish sacrificial tradition. Jewish scholars use these comparisons to trace the development of halakhic reasoning from the Second Temple period to the Mishnah.
