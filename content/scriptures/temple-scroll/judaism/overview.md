## Overview

Jewish scholarship views the Temple Scroll as a remarkable example of "Rewritten Torah," a genre in which biblical laws are reinterpreted, harmonized, and expanded. The scroll demonstrates that creative legal interpretation of the Torah was a widespread practice in the Second Temple period, predating and in some ways anticipating the methods that would later characterize rabbinic midrash and halakhah.

## Interpretive Approach

Jewish scholars analyze the Temple Scroll's legal innovations in comparison with both the biblical source texts and later rabbinic halakhah. The scroll's method of harmonizing contradictory biblical laws, filling gaps in biblical legislation, and extending Torah regulations to new situations represents an early form of the interpretive work that the rabbis would systematize. The text is also studied for what it reveals about pre-rabbinic debates over calendar, purity, and temple practice.

## Key Emphases

Jewish interpretation highlights the Temple Scroll's sophisticated legal reasoning, which often resolves tensions between Exodus, Leviticus, and Deuteronomy by creating synthesized regulations. The scroll's festival calendar, which includes celebrations not found in the canonical Torah such as the festivals of New Wine and New Oil, demonstrates the creative expansion of biblical law. The purity regulations for Jerusalem reveal a concern for the holiness of the sacred city that would continue in rabbinic literature, though with different specific requirements.

## Significance

The Temple Scroll enriches the Jewish understanding of how Torah interpretation developed during the Second Temple period. It demonstrates that the impulse to interpret, expand, and apply biblical law to changing circumstances — the very essence of the halakhic enterprise — was already vigorous centuries before the codification of the Mishnah and Talmud.
