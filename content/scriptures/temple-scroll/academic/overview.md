## Overview

Academic study of the Temple Scroll centers on its status as the longest Dead Sea Scroll and one of the most important examples of "Rewritten Bible" in antiquity. Yigael Yadin's monumental three-volume publication in 1983 established the framework for scholarly analysis, which has since expanded to include studies of its legal hermeneutics, architectural descriptions, and relationship to other Qumran texts.

## Interpretive Approach

Scholars apply source-critical, redaction-critical, and comparative methods to analyze the Temple Scroll's composition and purpose. The scroll's legal material is compared with the biblical source texts, other Second Temple legal traditions, and later rabbinic halakhah to understand its distinctive interpretive methods. Its architectural descriptions are studied in light of ancient Near Eastern temple plans and Hellenistic architectural practices.

## Key Emphases

Academic research has debated whether the Temple Scroll was composed by the Qumran community or predates it, with some scholars arguing it reflects a broader pre-Qumran priestly tradition. The scroll's use of divine first-person speech — transforming Moses' reported legislation into direct divine commands — represents a bold hermeneutical move that scholars continue to debate. The relationship between the Temple Scroll's ideal temple and Ezekiel 40-48 has generated extensive discussion, as has the question of whether the scroll describes a temple intended for the present age or the eschatological future.

## Significance

The Temple Scroll remains essential for the academic study of Second Temple Judaism, biblical interpretation, and ancient legal thought. It provides unique evidence for how Jewish communities engaged in creative rewriting of scripture, developed alternative legal traditions, and envisioned ideal forms of worship that challenged the religious establishment of their day.
