## The Temple Scroll as Rewritten Bible

Academic scholars classify Chapter 1 within the genre of "Rewritten Bible" (or "Rewritten Scripture"), a category that includes Jubilees, the Genesis Apocryphon, and Pseudo-Philo's *Biblical Antiquities*. The Temple Scroll's distinctive contribution to this genre is its transformation of Mosaic narrative into divine first-person speech, eliminating Moses as mediator and presenting the legal material as unmediated divine command. Scholars debate whether this rhetorical strategy represents a claim to canonical authority or a literary convention understood by ancient readers.

## Relationship to Ezekiel 40-48

The chapter's temple plan draws heavily on Ezekiel's visionary temple (chapters 40-48) while making significant modifications. Academic analysis has identified specific points of dependence and divergence, revealing the Temple Scroll author's interpretive method: Ezekiel provides the basic architectural concept, but the scroll adds new features, adjusts measurements, and resolves ambiguities in the prophetic text. This relationship provides a case study in ancient Jewish scriptural interpretation.

## Provenance and Dating

Scholars debate whether the Temple Scroll was composed by the Qumran community or predates it. Arguments for a pre-Qumran origin include the scroll's lack of specific sectarian terminology, its differences from other Qumran texts on certain halakhic points, and its independent literary character. If the scroll predates the community, it may represent a broader priestly tradition that the Qumran sect adopted and adapted. Most scholars date the composition to the second century BCE, though the extant manuscript (11QT) dates to the late first century BCE or early first century CE.
