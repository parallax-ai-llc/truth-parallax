## The 364-Day Calendar

Chapter 3's festival calendar has been the subject of extensive academic study, particularly its relationship to the 364-day solar calendar attested in Jubilees, 1 Enoch, and other Qumran texts. Scholars have reconstructed this calendar in detail, demonstrating that its fixed 52-week year ensures that festivals always fall on the same day of the week — an arrangement that the scroll's advocates regarded as divinely ordained. The calendar's mathematical elegance and practical convenience contrast with its astronomical inaccuracy (the solar year is actually 365.25 days), raising questions about how the community handled the inevitable drift.

## Extra-Biblical Festivals

The chapter's prescription of festivals not found in the Pentateuch — New Wine (approximately 50 days after Shavuot), New Oil (approximately 50 days after New Wine), and the Wood Offering — has attracted significant scholarly attention. These festivals follow the same 50-day interval pattern as the period between Passover and Shavuot, creating a symmetrical festival structure. Scholars analyze these additions as evidence for the community's creative legal reasoning and its claim to possess authoritative traditions not preserved in the canonical Torah.

## Sacrificial Regulations and Their Sources

Academic scholars have traced the chapter's sacrificial prescriptions to their biblical sources, particularly Numbers 28-29, and analyzed the modifications the Temple Scroll introduces. The scroll frequently harmonizes conflicting biblical passages, supplements sparse instructions with additional details, and occasionally introduces entirely new requirements. This comparative work reveals the interpretive methods employed by the scroll's author and contributes to understanding how pre-rabbinic Judaism engaged in legal exegesis.
