## The Law of the King and Deuteronomy 17

Academic scholars analyze Chapter 5 as an exegetical expansion of the Law of the King in Deuteronomy 17:14-20. The Temple Scroll significantly extends the biblical text, adding detailed regulations about the royal bodyguard, the war council, military conduct, and the king's personal Torah scroll. Scholars use this expansion to study the Temple Scroll author's interpretive methods, particularly the techniques of harmonization (combining passages from different biblical books), supplementation (adding new regulations to fill perceived gaps), and contemporization (applying ancient laws to current political situations).

## Historical Context

The chapter's political regulations are analyzed in relation to the historical circumstances of the Hasmonean and early Herodian periods. Scholars debate whether the Law of the King was composed in direct response to the Hasmonean combination of royal and priestly offices, which the Qumran community opposed. The prohibition against the king holding the high priesthood (implied by the subordination of royal to priestly authority) may represent a coded critique of Hasmonean kingship.

## Comparative Political Thought

The chapter's constitutional framework is compared by scholars with other ancient political documents, including Hellenistic kingship treatises, Roman constitutional law, and Near Eastern royal ideology. These comparisons reveal that the Temple Scroll participates in a broader ancient discourse about the nature and limits of political authority, even as its specific theological grounding in Torah sets it apart from secular political theory.
