## The Temple and the Church

Catholic scholars read Chapter 1's divine blueprint for the ideal temple in light of the Catholic understanding of the Church as the new Temple of God. The chapter's vision of a sanctuary where God dwells among his people finds its fulfillment, in Catholic theology, in the Church — particularly in the Eucharistic assembly, where Christ is truly present under the sacramental signs of bread and wine. The Temple Scroll's longing for a perfect dwelling place of God anticipates the Catholic doctrine of Real Presence.

## Sacred Architecture

The chapter's detailed architectural prescriptions resonate with the Catholic tradition of sacred architecture, in which the church building is designed to express and facilitate the encounter between God and his people. From the great medieval cathedrals to the prescriptions of the Second Vatican Council's *Sacrosanctum Concilium*, Catholic tradition has understood that the physical form of the worship space carries theological significance. The Temple Scroll demonstrates that this conviction has deep roots in the biblical tradition.

## Divine Revelation and Tradition

The chapter's presentation of the temple plan as direct divine revelation raises questions about the nature and scope of revelation that Catholic theology addresses through the concept of Sacred Tradition. The Catholic Church teaches that divine revelation is transmitted through both Scripture and Tradition, and the Temple Scroll's claim to represent authentic Mosaic revelation — preserved outside the canonical text — provides a historical analogue for the Catholic understanding of tradition as a channel of divine truth alongside Scripture.
