## Authority Under God

Catholic scholars read Chapter 5's Law of the King within the context of Catholic social teaching on political authority. The chapter's insistence that the king is subject to divine law, dependent on priestly counsel, and limited in the exercise of power resonates with the Catholic tradition of natural law, in which all human authority is understood as delegated and accountable to God. The medieval contest between popes and emperors over ultimate authority finds a distant echo in the Temple Scroll's subordination of royal to priestly power.

## The Common Good

The chapter's constitutional limitations on royal wealth and military power reflect a concern for the common good that Catholic social teaching has developed extensively. The principle that political authority exists to serve the community rather than to aggrandize the ruler is central to Catholic social doctrine, from Thomas Aquinas through Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* to Francis's *Fratelli Tutti*. The Temple Scroll's vision of a constrained monarchy anticipates this tradition.

## The King's Spiritual Formation

The chapter's requirement that the king maintain a personal Torah scroll and consult it regularly resonates with the Catholic emphasis on the spiritual formation of leaders. Catholic tradition insists that those who exercise authority — whether political, ecclesial, or familial — must ground their leadership in prayer, study, and obedience to God's will. The Temple Scroll's vision of a Torah-formed king anticipates this ideal.
