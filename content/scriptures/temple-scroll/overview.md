## Overview

The Temple Scroll (11QT) is the longest of the Dead Sea Scrolls, measuring over eight meters. Discovered in Cave 11 at Qumran, it presents itself as a direct divine revelation to Moses, rewriting and expanding portions of Deuteronomy, Leviticus, and Exodus. The scroll provides a detailed blueprint for an ideal temple, a comprehensive festival calendar, purity regulations for the holy city, and a distinctive "Law of the King."

## Structure

The Temple Scroll is organized into six major sections:

1. **The Ideal Temple** (Chapter 1): God's instructions for the design of the perfect sanctuary
2. **The Temple Courts** (Chapter 2): Precise measurements and layout of the temple complex
3. **The Festival Calendar** (Chapter 3): A solar calendar with expanded festival observances and sacrifices
4. **Purity Laws** (Chapter 4): Regulations for maintaining the holiness of Jerusalem
5. **The Law of the King** (Chapter 5): Constitutional law governing the Israelite monarch
6. **Statutes of Justice** (Chapter 6): Communal laws and judicial procedures

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Temple Scroll speaks to multiple religious and scholarly communities:

- **Essene**: Represents the community's vision of a purified worship system rejected by the Jerusalem priesthood
- **Judaism**: Demonstrates how Second Temple Jews engaged in creative reinterpretation of Torah
- **Christianity**: Illuminates debates about temple theology that shaped early Christian thought
- **Academic**: A landmark text for understanding ancient legal interpretation and rewritten scripture
