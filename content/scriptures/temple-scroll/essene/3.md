## The Solar Calendar

Chapter 3 presents a festival calendar based on a 364-day solar year, which the Essene community regarded as the only legitimate reckoning of sacred time. This calendar, which ensured that festivals always fell on the same day of the week, stood in sharp contrast to the lunisolar calendar used by the Jerusalem Temple. For the Essenes, the calendar question was not merely procedural but theological: celebrating festivals on the wrong days was an act of cosmic desecration that invalidated the sacrifices and provoked divine wrath.

## Expanded Festivals

The chapter prescribes festivals not found in the canonical Torah, including the Festival of New Wine, the Festival of New Oil, and the Festival of Wood Offering. These additional celebrations filled out the annual liturgical cycle according to the community's solar calendar, ensuring that every season of the agricultural year was sanctified through proper ritual observance. The community regarded these festivals as part of the original divine revelation to Moses, preserved in their tradition but lost or suppressed by the Jerusalem establishment.

## Sacrificial Prescriptions

The detailed sacrificial requirements for each festival — specifying the number and type of animals, the accompanying grain offerings, and the libations — demonstrate the community's intense engagement with the practical details of Temple worship, even though they were separated from the actual Temple. These prescriptions served both as a guide for the eschatological restoration and as a standard by which the community judged the inadequacy of current Temple practice.
