## Limiting Royal Power

Chapter 5 presents the "Law of the King," a constitutional document that significantly constrains the authority of the Israelite monarch. For the Essene community, this chapter addressed the historical corruption of kingship that had plagued Israel since the days of Solomon. The king was to be subordinate to priestly authority, bound by specific Torah regulations, and limited in his ability to wage war, accumulate wealth, or contract foreign marriages.

## Priestly Supremacy

The chapter's subordination of royal to priestly authority expressed the Essene ideal of a theocratic society governed by Torah as interpreted by the legitimate priesthood — that is, by the community itself. The king could not go to war, render major legal judgments, or undertake significant policy decisions without the approval of the priestly council. This vision directly challenged the Hasmonean combination of royal and priestly offices, which the Essene community regarded as a fundamental violation of the divine order.

## The Military Regulations

The chapter includes detailed regulations for the king's military conduct, including the composition of a royal bodyguard, the requirements for a defensive war council, and the prohibition against offensive wars without priestly authorization. These regulations reflect both the Essene commitment to regulated warfare (elaborated in the War Scroll) and their conviction that military power must always be subordinated to spiritual authority.
