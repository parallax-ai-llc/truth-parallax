## Overview

For the Essene community, the Temple Scroll represented God's own blueprint for the sanctuary that should have been built but was corrupted by the illegitimate Jerusalem priesthood. The scroll's vision of an ideal temple, governed by a solar calendar and stringent purity laws, stood as a permanent indictment of the existing Temple establishment and a promise of the purified worship that God would one day restore.

## Interpretive Approach

The Essenes regarded the Temple Scroll as possessing an authority equal to or even surpassing the canonical Torah, since it presented God speaking directly in the first person rather than through Moses. This direct divine speech gave the scroll's regulations an immediacy and force that reinforced the community's conviction that their calendar, purity practices, and temple theology were divinely mandated.

## Key Emphases

The Essene reading emphasizes the scroll's solar calendar as the only legitimate reckoning of sacred time, in contrast to the lunar calendar used in the Jerusalem Temple. The detailed architectural plans for concentric temple courts with escalating purity requirements reflect the community's conviction that holiness must be spatially organized and rigorously maintained. The Law of the King, which limits royal power and subordinates the monarch to priestly authority, expresses the Essene ideal of a theocratic society governed by Torah rather than political expedience.

## Significance

The Temple Scroll was central to the Essene community's self-understanding as the guardians of true worship. In the absence of a pure temple, the community itself functioned as a spiritual temple, offering prayers and righteous living as substitutes for animal sacrifice, while awaiting the day when God would establish the ideal sanctuary described in the scroll.
