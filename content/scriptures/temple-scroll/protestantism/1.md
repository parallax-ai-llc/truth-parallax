## The Temple and Christ

Protestant scholars read Chapter 1's vision of the ideal temple in light of the New Testament's Christological temple theology. Jesus' declaration "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" (John 2:19) — interpreted by the evangelist as referring to his body — represents a radical reinterpretation of temple theology that the Temple Scroll helps illuminate. The longing for a perfect sanctuary, which the Temple Scroll expresses through architectural blueprints, finds its fulfillment in Protestant theology through the person of Christ.

## Hebrews and the Heavenly Temple

The chapter's detailed divine instructions for building the ideal temple gain new significance when read alongside Hebrews 8-9, which argues that the earthly sanctuary is a "copy and shadow of the heavenly things" (Hebrews 8:5). Protestant scholars note that both the Temple Scroll and Hebrews recognize the inadequacy of the existing Temple, but they reach opposite conclusions: the Temple Scroll proposes a better earthly temple, while Hebrews points to a heavenly one made accessible through Christ.

## Divine Presence

The chapter's theology of divine dwelling — God's presence concentrated in the sanctuary — invites comparison with the New Testament's expanded understanding of where God dwells. Protestant theology, following John 1:14 ("The Word became flesh and dwelt among us"), understands the incarnation as the ultimate act of divine indwelling, superseding and fulfilling the temple tradition that texts like the Temple Scroll sought to perfect.
