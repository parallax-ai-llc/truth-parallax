## Overview

Protestant scholars engage the Temple Scroll primarily for the light it sheds on Second Temple Jewish temple theology, which forms the essential background for understanding the New Testament's treatment of the temple, particularly the Epistle to the Hebrews. The scroll's vision of an ideal sanctuary and its elaborate purity legislation illuminate the theological world from which early Christian temple imagery emerged.

## Interpretive Approach

Protestant interpretation reads the Temple Scroll comparatively, examining how its temple theology relates to New Testament concepts of Christ as the true temple, the Church as God's dwelling place, and the superiority of the new covenant over the old. The scroll's legal expansions also illustrate the kind of Torah interpretation that Jesus and Paul engaged with and sometimes challenged.

## Key Emphases

The Temple Scroll's detailed blueprint for a perfect temple gains new significance when read alongside Hebrews' argument that the earthly sanctuary is a "copy and shadow" of the heavenly one. Protestant scholars note that the Qumran community, like the early Christians, recognized the inadequacy of the existing Temple — though they reached opposite conclusions, with Qumran proposing a better earthly temple and Christianity proclaiming a heavenly one. The Law of the King invites comparison with New Testament teaching on the relationship between political authority and divine sovereignty.

## Significance

For Protestantism, the Temple Scroll demonstrates the intensity of Jewish longing for pure worship and perfect obedience to God's law — a longing that Protestant theology sees as ultimately fulfilled not in a rebuilt temple but in the person of Christ, "the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world."
