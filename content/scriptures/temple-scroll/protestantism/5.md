## Christ the True King

Protestant scholars read Chapter 5's Law of the King in light of the New Testament's presentation of Jesus as the ultimate fulfillment of Israelite kingship. The Temple Scroll's ideal king — subordinate to Torah, limited in power, dependent on priestly counsel — represents the highest aspiration of the old covenant political order. Protestant theology sees this aspiration fulfilled in Christ, who embodies perfect obedience to the Father's will and exercises authority through sacrificial service rather than coercive power.

## Church and State

The chapter's subordination of royal to priestly authority raises questions about the relationship between religious and political power that have been central to Protestant thought since the Reformation. Luther's doctrine of the two kingdoms, Calvin's vision of the Christian commonwealth, and the Baptist tradition of church-state separation all engage with the same fundamental issue that the Temple Scroll addresses: how should divine authority relate to human political power?

## The Limits of Political Power

The chapter's constitutional constraints on the king — limitations on military adventures, wealth accumulation, and foreign alliances — resonate with the Protestant tradition of limited government. The conviction that all human authority is delegated and accountable to a higher law is shared by the Temple Scroll and the Reformed political tradition, even as the specific mechanisms of accountability differ. The chapter's vision of a ruler bound by Torah anticipates the Protestant emphasis on the rule of law as a check on tyranny.
