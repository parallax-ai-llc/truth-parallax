## Justice and the Kingdom of God

Protestant scholars read Chapter 6's statutes of justice in light of the New Testament's vision of the Kingdom of God, in which justice and mercy are united. Jesus' teaching in the Sermon on the Mount — "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness" (Matthew 5:6) — expresses a longing for justice that the Temple Scroll shares, even as the New Testament transforms the concept of justice from strict legal retribution to the reconciling justice of the cross.

## Law and Gospel

The chapter's detailed legal prescriptions illustrate the character of the law that Protestant theology contrasts with the gospel. The law, in Reformation theology, serves to reveal human sinfulness and drive the sinner to Christ for grace. The Temple Scroll's comprehensive legal code — covering criminal justice, family law, and judicial procedure — represents the law at its most developed, demonstrating both the beauty of God's righteous standards and the impossibility of achieving perfect compliance through human effort.

## The Witness Requirement

The chapter's strict requirements for witness testimony resonate with the New Testament's insistence on truthful speech and reliable witness. Jesus' teaching about truthfulness (Matthew 5:37) and the early church's concern for credible testimony (Acts 1:21-22) build on the same legal tradition that the Temple Scroll codifies. Protestant scholars see continuity between the Temple Scroll's judicial standards and the New Testament's ethical demands, even as the framework of grace transforms the function of the law.
