## Calendar and Christian Worship

Protestant scholars read Chapter 3's festival calendar in light of Paul's teaching about the observance of days and seasons. Paul's declaration that "one person considers one day more sacred than another; another considers every day alike" (Romans 14:5) and his warning against judging others "by a religious festival, a New Moon celebration or a Sabbath day" (Colossians 2:16) represent a significant departure from the Temple Scroll's rigid calendrical requirements.

## Christ as the Fulfillment of the Festivals

The chapter's detailed festival prescriptions are read by Protestant scholars as shadows of the reality found in Christ. The Passover sacrifice points to "Christ, our Passover lamb" (1 Corinthians 5:7); the Feast of Weeks anticipates Pentecost; the Day of Atonement foreshadows Christ's once-for-all sacrifice (Hebrews 9:12). The Temple Scroll's additional festivals — New Wine, New Oil — are seen as expressing a yearning for abundance and blessing that Christ fulfills in the new covenant.

## Sacrifice and Atonement

The chapter's elaborate sacrificial requirements highlight the contrast between the old covenant's repeated sacrifices and the new covenant's once-for-all atonement. Protestant theology, following Hebrews 10:1-18, understands that "it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins" — the very sacrifices that the Temple Scroll prescribes in such meticulous detail. This contrast underscores the sufficiency and finality of Christ's sacrifice, a central tenet of Protestant faith.
