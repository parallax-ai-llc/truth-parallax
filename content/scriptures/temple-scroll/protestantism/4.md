## Purity and the Gospel

Protestant scholars read Chapter 4's purity laws for the holy city in light of Jesus' radical reinterpretation of purity categories. Jesus' association with the "unclean" — lepers, menstruating women, tax collectors, sinners — challenged the exclusionary logic that the Temple Scroll systematizes. Protestant interpretation sees in Jesus' ministry a reversal of the purity system: rather than impurity contaminating holiness, holiness flows outward to purify the impure.

## The Holy City in Revelation

The chapter's vision of a purified holy city invites comparison with the New Jerusalem described in Revelation 21-22, which also features precise measurements and conditions of entry. However, the New Jerusalem has "no temple in it, because the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are its temple" (Revelation 21:22), representing a radical transformation of the Temple Scroll's vision. Purity is no longer maintained through exclusion but through the direct presence of God and the Lamb.

## Inner Holiness

The Temple Scroll's concern for external purity — the physical removal of sources of contamination from the holy city — is contrasted by Protestant scholars with the New Testament's emphasis on inner transformation. Jesus' teaching that "out of the heart come evil thoughts" (Matthew 15:19) and Paul's exhortation to be "transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12:2) relocate the locus of holiness from external space to the human heart, while not denying the importance of the community's visible witness.
