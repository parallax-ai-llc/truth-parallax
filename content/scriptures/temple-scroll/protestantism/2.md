## Sacred Space and the Church

Protestant scholars read Chapter 2's concentric courts of graduated holiness in light of the New Testament's democratization of sacred space. The temple veil, torn at Christ's crucifixion (Matthew 27:51), symbolizes the end of spatial restrictions on access to God's presence. Where the Temple Scroll creates elaborate barriers between different categories of worshippers and the divine presence, the gospel proclaims that all believers have "boldness and access through faith in Christ" (Ephesians 3:12).

## The Priesthood of All Believers

The chapter's strict segregation of priestly, Levitical, and lay access to the temple courts contrasts sharply with the Protestant doctrine of the priesthood of all believers. Protestant theology, grounded in 1 Peter 2:9 ("a royal priesthood, a holy nation"), understands that Christ's sacrifice has abolished the mediatorial priesthood and granted every believer direct access to God. The Temple Scroll's elaborate access restrictions serve as a foil that highlights the revolutionary character of this New Testament teaching.

## Order and Freedom

While rejecting the Temple Scroll's spatial restrictions, Protestant scholars acknowledge the legitimate principle underlying them: that approaching the holy God requires preparation and reverence. The Protestant emphasis on orderly worship (1 Corinthians 14:40), the preaching of the Word, and proper administration of the sacraments reflects a continuing conviction that worship requires structure, even when the specific structures of the old covenant have been superseded.
