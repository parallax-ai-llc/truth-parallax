## Overview

1 Corinthians holds a distinguished place in Catholic theology, particularly for its eucharistic teaching. Paul's account of the Lord's Supper (11:23–26) — "This is my body" — is among the earliest written witnesses to the institution narrative and a cornerstone of Catholic sacramental theology. The letter's vision of the church as Christ's body (chapter 12) also undergirds Catholic ecclesiology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic and Liturgical
The Church Fathers — especially Chrysostom, Augustine, and Clement of Rome (who wrote his own letter to Corinth) — read 1 Corinthians as addressing perennial problems of church unity and discipline. The liturgical use of 1 Corinthians 11:23–26 in the Mass ensures that Pauline eucharistic theology is encountered by every Catholic at worship.

### Thomistic Synthesis
Aquinas's commentary on 1 Corinthians integrates Paul's teaching with sacramental theology. The "discerning the body" of 11:29 grounds the Catholic practice of requiring proper disposition (including confession of mortal sin) before receiving communion. Paul's resurrection argument in chapter 15 is read in light of Aristotelian metaphysics of body and soul.

### Modern Catholic Scholarship
Catholic exegetes like Jerome Murphy-O'Connor and Raymond Collins bring archaeological and social-scientific methods to bear on the Corinthian context, illuminating the social dynamics of patron-client relationships and house churches that shaped the community's conflicts.

## Theological Emphases

- **Real Presence**: 1 Corinthians 11:23–29 as warrant for transubstantiation
- **The Church as Body of Christ**: Chapter 12 as the foundation for Catholic ecclesiology (*Mystici Corporis Christi*, 1943)
- **Purgatory**: 1 Corinthians 3:13–15 ("saved, but only as through fire") cited in support of purification after death
- **Bodily Resurrection**: Chapter 15 affirming the resurrection of the flesh, not merely the survival of the soul
