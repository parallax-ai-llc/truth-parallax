## Idol Food and Avodah Zarah

Paul's discussion of food offered to idols (8:1-13) engages directly with Jewish law concerning avodah zarah (idolatry). The Mishnah tractate Avodah Zarah provides detailed regulations about food, wine, and objects associated with idol worship. Jewish scholars note that Paul's reasoning about idol food follows recognizably halakhic patterns, weighing competing obligations.

## Knowledge and Humility

Paul's warning that "knowledge puffs up" (8:1) echoes rabbinic teachings about the dangers of intellectual arrogance. The Talmud warns: "Whoever is arrogant, the Holy One says, 'I and he cannot dwell together'" (Sotah 5a). Both Paul and the rabbis subordinate knowledge to humility and communal responsibility.

## The Stumbling Block

Paul's concern about causing a brother to stumble by eating idol food (8:9-13) parallels the rabbinic injunction of lifnei iver, "Do not place a stumbling block before the blind" (Leviticus 19:14), which the rabbis extended to any action that leads another into transgression. The principle of communal responsibility governs both Paul's and the rabbis' ethical reasoning.
