## Love and Hesed

Paul's hymn to love (13:1-13) resonates with the Jewish concept of hesed (steadfast love, lovingkindness), which is among the most fundamental divine attributes in Jewish theology. The qualities Paul ascribes to love — patience, kindness, not keeping a record of wrongs — echo rabbinic descriptions of God's hesed and the human imitation of divine attributes (imitatio Dei).

## Prophecy and Torah

Paul's statement that "prophecies will pass away" and "knowledge will pass away" (13:8) raises questions from a Jewish perspective. Judaism affirms that prophecy has largely ceased but insists that Torah is eternal. The suggestion that knowledge itself is temporary contrasts with the rabbinic teaching that Torah study continues even in the world to come.

## Love as the Greatest

Paul's declaration that "the greatest of these is love" (13:13) aligns with Rabbi Akiva's teaching that "Love your neighbor as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18) is the "great principle of the Torah" (Sifra Kedoshim). Jewish and Pauline ethics converge on the centrality of love, though they differ on the broader theological framework.
