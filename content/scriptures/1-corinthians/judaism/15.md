## Resurrection in Jewish Context

Paul's argument for bodily resurrection (15:1-58) sits squarely within a Second Temple Jewish debate. The Pharisees affirmed resurrection; the Sadducees denied it. Paul argues as a Pharisee (cf. Acts 23:6): "If the dead are not raised, then Christ has not been raised" (15:16). Jewish scholars note that Paul's resurrection logic is recognizably Pharisaic.

## The World to Come

Paul's teaching about the resurrection body (15:35-49) invites comparison with rabbinic discussions of olam haba (the world to come). The Talmud (Sanhedrin 90a-92b) contains extensive debate about the nature of resurrection. While details differ, both Paul and the rabbis affirm a transformed bodily existence in the age to come.

## Victory Over Death

Paul's triumph over death (15:54-57) echoes Isaiah 25:8 ("He will swallow up death forever") and Hosea 13:14, both cited by Paul. Jewish liturgy similarly anticipates the defeat of death in the messianic age. The conviction that death is not the final word is shared by Pauline Christianity and Pharisaic Judaism.
