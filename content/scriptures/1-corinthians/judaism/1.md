## Wisdom in Jewish Context

Paul's contrast between divine and worldly wisdom (1:18-25) operates within a thoroughly Jewish framework. The wisdom tradition of Proverbs, Job, and Ben Sira provided the categories Paul employs. Jewish scholars note that Paul's argument parallels the critique of self-sufficient human reasoning found in Ecclesiastes and the Wisdom of Solomon, though he applies it in a distinctly Christological direction.

## The Scandal of the Cross

Paul's acknowledgment that the cross is "a stumbling block to Jews" (1:23) reflects a genuine theological issue. The Torah declares that "a hanged man is cursed by God" (Deuteronomy 21:23), making a crucified messiah scandalous within Jewish categories. Jewish scholars note that this was not mere prejudice but a principled theological objection based on Scripture itself.

## God's Calling and Election

Paul's teaching that God chose the foolish and weak (1:27-29) echoes the prophetic tradition of divine reversal found in Hannah's song (1 Samuel 2) and throughout Isaiah. Jewish readers recognize this rhetoric as thoroughly Jewish, even as Paul applies it to a community that includes Gentiles.
