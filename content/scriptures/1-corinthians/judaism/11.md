## The Communal Meal

Paul's critique of the Corinthian meal practice (11:17-22) addresses social divisions at a shared supper. Jewish communal meals, including the haburah (fellowship meals) of Pharisaic practice, similarly emphasized equality and solidarity among participants. Paul's concern that "one goes hungry while another gets drunk" (11:21) echoes rabbinic ideals of communal justice at shared meals.

## Passover Echoes

Paul's institution narrative (11:23-26) carries unmistakable Passover resonance. The breaking of bread, the cup "after supper," and the language of remembrance (anamnesis) parallel the Passover haggadah's command to retell and relive the Exodus. Jewish scholars note that the Last Supper tradition is deeply embedded in Jewish liturgical practice.

## Head Coverings and Jewish Custom

Paul's instructions about head coverings (11:2-16) invite comparison with Jewish practice. Jewish men traditionally cover their heads during prayer (the kippah/yarmulke), while Paul's instructions seem to require the opposite for men. Jewish scholars note the cultural complexity and the divergence between Paul's Corinthian instructions and Jewish liturgical custom.
