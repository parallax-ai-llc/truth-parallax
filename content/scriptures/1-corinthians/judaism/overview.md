## Overview

Jewish scholarly engagement with 1 Corinthians focuses on recovering Paul's Jewish context and examining how the letter's ethical and theological arguments relate to contemporaneous Jewish thought. The Corinthian correspondence reveals a Paul deeply engaged with questions familiar to Second Temple Judaism: the propriety of food offered to idols, the nature of the resurrection body, communal discipline, and the boundaries of the covenant community.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Paul within Judaism
Scholars such as Mark Nanos and Paula Fredriksen argue that Paul's instructions in 1 Corinthians reflect the concerns of a Jewish apostle navigating the inclusion of Gentiles into a Jewish messianic movement. His rulings on idol food (chapters 8–10) parallel halakhic reasoning about *avodah zarah* (idolatry), and his appeal to "the law" in chapter 14 may reference Torah directly.

### Resurrection in Jewish Context
Paul's extended argument for bodily resurrection in chapter 15 sits within a lively Second Temple debate. The Pharisees affirmed resurrection; the Sadducees denied it. Paul's argument that "if the dead are not raised, then Christ has not been raised" (15:16) reflects Pharisaic logic, and his concept of a "spiritual body" (*soma pneumatikon*) has parallels in apocalyptic literature.

### Comparative Ethics
Jewish scholars note parallels between Paul's communal ethics and rabbinic teachings: the body-as-temple metaphor (6:19) echoes rabbinic notions of human dignity; the principle of not causing a brother to stumble (8:13) parallels *lifnei iver* ("before the blind," Leviticus 19:14).

## Theological Emphases

- **Idol Food and Jewish Law**: Paul's reasoning on idol meat as an extension of Jewish purity and idolatry concerns
- **Resurrection as Jewish Doctrine**: Recovering the Jewish roots of Pauline resurrection theology
- **Community Discipline**: 1 Corinthians 5 and the practice of communal expulsion as paralleling *herem* and *niddui*
- **Wisdom Tradition**: Paul's "wisdom" language (chapters 1–2) in dialogue with Jewish *hokhmah* traditions
