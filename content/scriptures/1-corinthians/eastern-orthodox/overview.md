## Overview

In Eastern Orthodoxy, 1 Corinthians is read above all as a Paschal text. Chapter 15's proclamation of Christ's resurrection — "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death" — resonates with the central liturgical celebration of Orthodoxy: Pascha (Easter). Chrysostom's Paschal Homily, which draws directly on 1 Corinthians 15:55 ("O death, where is thy sting?"), is read at every Orthodox Easter service worldwide.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Chrysostom's Homilies
John Chrysostom delivered forty-four homilies on 1 Corinthians, making it one of his most extensively treated texts. His approach is characteristically pastoral: he addresses the Corinthians' divisions as a failure of love and humility, not merely a doctrinal error. His eucharistic theology in the homilies on chapter 11 emphasizes the awesome mystery (*phrikton mysterion*) of communion.

### Liturgical Reading
1 Corinthians is read extensively in the Orthodox lectionary. The eucharistic institution narrative (11:23–26) is embedded in the anaphora of the Divine Liturgy. Chapter 13's hymn to love is read at weddings. Chapter 15 is the epistle reading at funeral services and at Pascha.

### Patristic Consensus
Orthodox interpretation privileges the consensus of the Fathers (*consensus patrum*). Basil the Great, Gregory of Nyssa, and Maximus the Confessor all engaged with 1 Corinthians, reading the spiritual gifts of chapters 12–14 as manifestations of the Holy Spirit's energies rather than individual possessions.

## Theological Emphases

- **Resurrection as Cosmic Victory**: Chapter 15 as the defeat of death and the transfiguration of all creation
- **Eucharist as Mystery**: Chapter 11 as revealing the awesome, transformative reality of communion
- **Unity in the Body**: Chapter 12's body metaphor as the theological basis for conciliar ecclesiology
- **Wisdom of the Cross**: Chapters 1–2 as the inversion of worldly philosophy through divine foolishness
