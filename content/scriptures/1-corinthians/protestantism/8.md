## Knowledge and Love

Paul's warning that "knowledge puffs up, but love builds up" (8:1) sets the framework for the entire discussion of idol food. Protestant ethics emphasizes that theological knowledge without love is spiritually dangerous. The "strong" who know that idols are nothing must exercise their freedom with care for those whose conscience is weaker.

## The Weak Conscience

Paul's concern for the believer whose conscience is "weak" (8:7-12) — who might be led to eat idol food against their conviction — establishes a principle central to Protestant ethics: Christian freedom must be voluntarily limited by love. The Reformers applied this to adiaphora (things indifferent), insisting that liberty in non-essential matters must never become a stumbling block.

## Sinning Against Christ

Paul's stark conclusion — "by sinning against your brothers and wounding their weak conscience, you sin against Christ" (8:12) — elevates the stakes of the discussion from personal preference to Christological significance. Protestant pastors regularly invoke this principle in disputes about practices that are lawful but potentially harmful.
