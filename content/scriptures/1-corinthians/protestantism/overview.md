## Overview

1 Corinthians has been central to Protestant ecclesiology and ethics since the Reformation. Luther's emphasis on the priesthood of all believers finds support in Paul's vision of the church as a body with many gifted members (chapter 12). The letter also became a battleground in Protestant debates over the Lord's Supper, with Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin each claiming Paul's words in chapter 11 for their respective positions.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Reformation Ecclesiology
The Reformers read 1 Corinthians as Paul's correction of a church that had elevated human wisdom and human leaders over Christ. This resonated with their critique of papal authority and the medieval clergy-laity distinction. Paul's refusal to baptize in his own name (1:14–17) was cited against any cult of personality in church leadership.

### Charismatic and Pentecostal
Chapters 12–14 on spiritual gifts became foundational texts for the Pentecostal and charismatic movements. The gifts of tongues, prophecy, and healing are read as normative for the church today, though cessationist Protestants argue these gifts ended with the apostolic age.

### Evangelical Ethics
Conservative Protestant interpreters draw on 1 Corinthians for guidance on sexuality (chapters 5–7), lawsuits (chapter 6), and gender roles in worship (chapter 11, 14), though these passages generate significant internal debate.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Cross as God's Wisdom**: 1:18–2:16 as the inversion of worldly values
- **The Lord's Supper**: Chapter 11 as the basis for Protestant sacramental theology (memorial, spiritual presence, or real presence)
- **Bodily Resurrection**: Chapter 15 as the definitive Pauline defense of physical resurrection
- **Love as the Greatest Gift**: Chapter 13 as the ethical standard for all spiritual exercise
