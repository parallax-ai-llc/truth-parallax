## The Problem of Division

Paul opens by addressing factions within the Corinthian church: "I follow Paul," "I follow Apollos," "I follow Cephas," "I follow Christ" (1:12). Protestant interpreters see this as a warning against any cult of personality in church leadership. The Reformers cited this passage against papal authority, arguing that no human leader should supplant Christ as the church's head.

## The Foolishness of the Cross

Paul's declaration that "the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God" (1:18) establishes the theology of the cross (theologia crucis) that Luther would make central to Protestant thought. God's power is revealed in weakness, overturning all human expectations of wisdom and strength.

## God's Calling

Paul reminds the Corinthians that "not many of you were wise according to worldly standards" (1:26). Protestant theology reads this as demonstrating that salvation is entirely God's initiative, not a reward for human merit or social status. God chose the foolish, weak, and despised to shame the wise and strong.
