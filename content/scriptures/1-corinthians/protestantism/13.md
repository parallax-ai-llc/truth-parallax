## The Hymn to Love

1 Corinthians 13 is the most famous passage in Paul's letters and one of the most beloved texts in Protestant devotion. Its description of love as "patient and kind" (13:4), never envious or boastful, bears all things and endures all things (13:7), sets the standard for Christian character. Protestant ethicists treat this chapter as the definitive portrait of Christlike character.

## Love and Spiritual Gifts

The chapter's placement between discussions of spiritual gifts (12 and 14) is theologically deliberate. Love is not one gift among many but the essential context in which all gifts must operate. Protestant charismatic theology emphasizes that spectacular gifts without love are "a noisy gong or a clanging cymbal" (13:1).

## The Eschatological Horizon

Paul's statement that prophecies, tongues, and knowledge will "pass away" but love will remain (13:8-13) grounds the cessationist argument that certain gifts were temporary. Whether or not one accepts cessationism, the passage establishes love's permanence: "So now faith, hope, and love abide, these three; but the greatest of these is love" (13:13).
