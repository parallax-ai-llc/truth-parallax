## Marriage in Islamic Perspective

Paul's treatment of marriage (7:1-16) invites comparison with Islamic teaching. Islam regards marriage as a sunnah (prophetic tradition) and a social obligation. The Prophet Muhammad said: "Marriage is part of my sunnah; whoever turns away from my sunnah is not of me" (Ibn Majah). Paul's personal preference for celibacy (7:7-8) contrasts sharply with the Islamic ideal.

## Celibacy Critique

Paul's statement that "it is good for a man not to have sexual relations with a woman" (7:1) represents a position Islam explicitly rejects. The Quran criticizes monasticism: "Monasticism, which they invented for themselves — We did not prescribe it for them" (57:27). Islam sees marriage and family as the natural, divinely intended state for most human beings.

## Divorce

Paul's instruction against divorce (7:10-11) is more restrictive than Islamic law, which permits divorce (talaq) as a last resort. The Prophet Muhammad said: "The most disliked of permissible things to Allah is divorce" (Abu Dawud). Islam allows divorce as a mercy to prevent greater harm, while seeking reconciliation first.
