## Overview

Islamic engagement with 1 Corinthians centers on two main areas: a theological critique of Pauline Christology and soteriology, and a recognition of ethical parallels between Paul's communal instructions and Islamic moral teaching. Muslim scholars generally regard Paul as the architect of a Christianity that diverged from the original monotheistic message of Jesus, and 1 Corinthians — with its teachings on the cross, resurrection, and the Lord's Supper — exemplifies this perceived departure.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Critique of the Cross
Paul's declaration that "we preach Christ crucified" (1:23) and that "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is in vain" (15:17) stands in direct tension with Quranic teaching. Quran 4:157 states that Jesus was not crucified but that "it was made to appear so." The entire soteriological framework of 1 Corinthians — salvation through the cross and resurrection — is therefore rejected as built on a false premise.

### Ethical Common Ground
Muslim scholars recognize substantial overlap between Paul's ethical instructions and Islamic moral teaching: the condemnation of sexual immorality (chapters 5–6), the importance of communal order and modesty (chapter 11), and the priority of love and justice in community life (chapter 13). These parallels are seen as remnants of original prophetic teaching.

### Resurrection Compared
While Islam affirms bodily resurrection (*ba'th*) on the Day of Judgment, it rejects the Christian framework that ties human resurrection to Christ's resurrection. In Islamic theology, resurrection is an act of God's power (*qudrah*), not contingent on any mediating event. Paul's argument in chapter 15 is thus seen as theologically unnecessary.

## Theological Emphases

- **Rejection of Vicarious Atonement**: The cross as salvific event contradicts individual accountability in Islam
- **Bodily Resurrection Affirmed, Christological Framework Rejected**: Islam affirms resurrection but not through Christ
- **Ethical Parallels**: Modesty, sexual ethics, and communal responsibility as shared values
- **Critique of Sacramentalism**: The Lord's Supper as an innovation absent from Jesus's original teaching
