## The Mountain of the Pleroma

Isaiah 2:2 envisions the LORD's mountain "established as the highest of the mountains." Gnostic interpreters read this mountain not as an earthly location but as the Pleroma itself — the fullness of divine reality that transcends the material cosmos. The nations "streaming" to the mountain represent the divine sparks returning to their source, ascending through the archonic realms to rejoin the transcendent God.

## Beyond Swords and Plowshares

"They will beat their swords into plowshares" (2:4). Gnostic reading transcends the political: the transformation of weapons into tools represents the end of the cosmic conflict between the forces of ignorance and the forces of light. When *gnosis* is fully realized, the struggle against the archons ceases — not because the archons are reconciled but because the pneumatic beings have ascended beyond their reach.

## The Humbling of the Demiurge

The "day of the LORD" against everything proud and exalted (2:12-17) is read by Gnostics as describing not God's judgment on human pride but the ultimate humiliation of the Demiurge and his archons. The "cedars of Lebanon" and "lofty mountains" represent the archonic powers that will be brought low when the true God is revealed. The Demiurge's entire creation will be exposed as a shadow of the Pleroma's reality.
