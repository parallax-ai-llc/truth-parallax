Isaiah 66 concludes the prophetic book with themes that Gnostic theology reads as pointing beyond the Demiurge's cosmos to the transcendent Pleroma. The declaration "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool" (66:1) is, in Gnostic reading, either the Demiurge's arrogant self-description (claiming the cosmos as his domain) or the true God's statement that transcends all material structures -- even the heavens the Demiurge claims as his own. The question "Where is the house you will build for me?" is answered in Gnostic theology: no material structure can house the true God, whose dwelling is the Pleroma alone.

The image of sudden, painless birth (66:7-9) is connected to the Gnostic understanding of pneumatic awakening as an instantaneous event. Unlike the gradual process of moral improvement that the psychic traditions describe, gnosis arrives in a flash of recognition -- the moment when the pneumatic perceives, all at once, its true origin, its present condition, and its ultimate destiny. The Valentinian Formula, preserved by Clement of Alexandria, describes gnosis as knowing "who we were, what we became, where we were, whereinto we have been thrown, whereto we speed, wherefrom we are redeemed, what birth is, and what rebirth."

The book's closing vision juxtaposes universal worship (66:23) with eternal judgment (66:24). Gnostic theology reads this duality as the two final destinations of pneumatic and hylic humanity. The pneumatics who have received gnosis enter the Pleroma's eternal worship -- not the repetitive rituals of the Demiurge's temple but the spontaneous self-expression of beings fully united with the divine. The hylics, whose entire existence is material, dissolve with the material cosmos. The fire that "shall not be quenched" (66:24) is not punitive but descriptive: the dissolution of matter is final and irreversible. Isaiah's closing tableau thus depicts, in Gnostic reading, the ultimate separation of spirit from matter and the restoration of all pneumatic light to its original source.
