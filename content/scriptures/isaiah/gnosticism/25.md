## The Plerotic Feast

Isaiah 25's vision of the feast on God's mountain (25:6) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the celebration of return to the Pleroma. The "rich food" and "aged wine" represent the fullness of divine knowledge and the intoxication of *gnosis* — the spiritual nourishment that the divine sparks have been denied throughout their imprisonment in matter. The feast is "for all peoples" — for all the scattered pneumatic elements, regardless of their earthly identities.

## Death Swallowed by the Pleroma

"He will swallow up death forever" (25:8). Gnostic soteriology identifies death not merely as physical mortality but as the condition of separation from the Pleroma. To be incarnate in matter is already to be "dead" in the spiritual sense. The abolition of death is the abolition of material existence itself — the return of all divine elements to the living fullness from which they fell.

## The Reunion with the True God

"Surely this is our God; we trusted in him, and he saved us" (25:9). Gnostic interpreters read this as the ecstatic cry of recognition — the moment when the pneumatic being finally sees the true God face to face, beyond all archonic veils and material illusions. This is the *gnosis* that has been sought throughout the entire cosmic drama: direct knowledge of the God who was hidden behind the Demiurge's pretensions.
