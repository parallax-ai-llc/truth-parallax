The third Servant Song (50:4-9) is interpreted in Gnostic tradition as describing the Revealer's passage through the archonic realm, where he endures persecution from the ruling powers. "I offered my back to those who beat me" (50:6) is understood not as physical suffering but as the Revealer's voluntary submission to the conditions of material existence -- a descent that costs the divine being something, even though his essential nature remains untouched. The Acts of John presents a docetic Christology in which the suffering is real in one sense but does not affect the divine nature of the Revealer.

The morning-by-morning listening (50:4) is connected to the Gnostic practice of interior receptivity -- the cultivation of the "hearing ear" that can perceive the Pleroma's communication through the noise of the archonic world. The Gospel of Thomas opens with the injunction: "Whoever finds the interpretation of these sayings will not experience death" (saying 1). The discipline of spiritual listening, receiving instruction from the transcendent realm, is the fundamental practice of the pneumatic life.

The confidence "He who vindicates me is near" (50:8) expresses the Gnostic certainty that the archons' power is temporary and the Pleroma's vindication certain. The suffering Revealer knows that his mission will succeed because the divine plan encompasses and transcends the archonic resistance. The closing warning against those who "light fires for themselves" (50:11) is read as a critique of all attempts to generate spiritual illumination through material means -- ritual, sacrifice, ascetic practices -- rather than through the direct reception of gnosis from the Pleroma.
