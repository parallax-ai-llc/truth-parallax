Isaiah 62's imagery of new names is deeply significant in Gnostic theology, where naming carries ontological weight. To receive a new name is to receive a new identity -- the pneumatic identity that the archonic world conceals. The Apocryphon of John describes Adam receiving his true name from the Pleroma, a name the archons cannot pronounce or comprehend. The names "Hephzibah" and "Beulah" (62:4) represent the pneumatic's restored identity: no longer "Deserted" (abandoned in the material cosmos) but "My delight is in her" (recognized and claimed by the Pleroma).

The bridal imagery -- "As a bridegroom rejoices over his bride" (62:5) -- is connected to the Gnostic "bridal chamber" mystery, the highest sacrament in Valentinian practice. The Gospel of Philip describes the bridal chamber as the place where the separated pairs (syzygies) are reunited: "If anyone becomes a son of the bridal chamber, he will receive the light." The divine "marriage" is not a metaphor but a real spiritual event in which the pneumatic soul is reunited with its heavenly counterpart, restoring the unity that existed before the fall into matter.

The watchmen who pray without ceasing (62:6) are identified with the pneumatic community whose continuous practice of contemplation maintains the connection between the material world and the Pleroma. In Gnostic communities, practices of meditation, chanting of vowel sequences, and ritual prayer served this function -- keeping the channel open through which pleromatic light could enter the archonic cosmos. The injunction to "give God no rest" (62:7) is read not as presumptuous demand but as the necessary work of maintaining the cosmic link between the two realms.
