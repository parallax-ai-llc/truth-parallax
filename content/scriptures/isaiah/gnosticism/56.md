Isaiah 56's inclusion of foreigners and eunuchs in God's community resonates with the Gnostic principle that pneumatic identity transcends all social, ethnic, and bodily categories. The Gospel of Thomas declares: "When you make the male and the female one and the same, so that the male not be male nor the female female... then will you enter the kingdom" (saying 22). The eunuch, who transcends the binary of male-female, represents in Gnostic reading the ideal pneumatic -- one who has moved beyond the bodily distinctions that the archons use to maintain control.

The declaration that God's house is "for all nations" (56:7) is extended in Gnostic interpretation to mean that the Pleroma transcends the ethnic and national boundaries that the Demiurge established to keep humanity divided. The archonic strategy of creating nations, languages, and races (cf. the Tower of Babel narrative) is designed to prevent the pneumatic seeds from recognizing their common origin and destiny. Isaiah's universal vision undermines this strategy and points toward the Pleroma's fundamental unity.

The condemnation of negligent watchmen (56:10-12) is applied by Gnostic interpreters to all religious leaders -- including those of the emerging proto-orthodox church -- who substitute external ritual for genuine gnosis. The "blind watchmen" who cannot see represent the psychic clergy who possess organizational authority but lack pneumatic insight. The Apocalypse of Peter (NHC VII,3) contains a fierce polemic against church leaders who "call themselves bishop and deacon, as if they had received their authority from God" but who in reality serve the archonic system.
