The designation of Cyrus as "anointed" (45:1) takes on special resonance in Gnostic Christology, where "anointing" (chrism) is understood as the bestowal of pneumatic knowledge. In the Gospel of Philip (NHC II,3), chrismation is considered superior even to baptism: "The chrism is superior to baptism, for it is from the word 'chrism' that we have been called 'Christians.'" Cyrus's anointing is thus read as a type of the pneumatic anointing that every Gnostic initiate receives -- the knowledge of one's true divine origin.

The declaration "I form the light and create darkness" (45:7) is a deeply problematic verse for Gnostic interpreters. If the speaker is the Demiurge, this is a confession of creating the darkness (ignorance) that entraps souls -- a damning self-revelation. If the speaker is the true God, Gnostic theology must account for how the transcendent deity relates to darkness at all. Most Gnostic interpreters resolve this by identifying the speaker as the Demiurge, whose claim to create both light and darkness reveals his confused nature: he cannot distinguish between the true Light of the Pleroma and the derivative light he produces.

The hidden God motif -- "Truly you are a God who has been hiding himself" (45:15) -- is enthusiastically claimed by Gnostic theology. The true God is indeed hidden -- hidden from the Demiurge, hidden from the archons, hidden from all who lack gnosis. The Sethian texts describe the supreme deity as the Invisible Spirit, the Unknown Father, the Silence before all speech. Isaiah inadvertently (or, in the Gnostic reading, deliberately) testifies to the existence of a God beyond the God the biblical tradition normally describes.
