## Overview

Gnostic engagement with Isaiah is distinctive among the prophetic books because of the *Ascension of Isaiah*, a composite Jewish-Christian-Gnostic text (2nd century CE) that became one of the most important extra-canonical works in late antiquity. Isaiah's throne vision (chapter 6), his language of hidden wisdom, and his sharp critique of worldly powers provided fertile ground for Gnostic reinterpretation. In Gnostic readings, the prophet becomes a visionary who ascended through the heavens, encountering the archons and receiving secret knowledge (*gnosis*) of the true God beyond the Demiurge.

## Key Gnostic Texts on Isaiah

### The Ascension of Isaiah
This text, surviving in Ethiopic and fragmentary Latin and Slavonic versions, describes:
1. **The martyrdom of Isaiah** (chapters 1-5): Isaiah is sawn in two by King Manasseh at the instigation of the demon Beliar
2. **The heavenly ascent** (chapters 6-11): Isaiah travels through seven heavens, each ruled by an angel, culminating in a vision of the Trinity and the secret plan for Christ's descent and incarnation

### Nag Hammadi Connections
- **The Apocryphon of John**: Yaldabaoth's declaration "I am God and there is no other" echoes and inverts Isaiah 45:5
- **The Hypostasis of the Archons**: The archons' ignorant boasting parodies Isaiah's proclamations of divine uniqueness
- **Trimorphic Protennoia**: The descent of divine Thought through multiple heavens reverses Isaiah's ascent pattern

## The Gnostic Inversion of Isaiah

| Orthodox Reading | Gnostic Reading |
|-----------------|----------------|
| "Holy, Holy, Holy" — worship of the supreme God (6:3) | The angels may serve the Demiurge unknowingly |
| Isaiah's vision reveals God's glory | Isaiah's ascent reveals the Demiurge's limitations |
| "I am the LORD, there is no other" (45:5) | Yaldabaoth's ignorant boast — other gods exist above him |
| The Suffering Servant redeems through obedience | The revealer-savior descends in disguise to outwit the archons |
| New heavens and new earth — cosmic restoration | Return to the Pleroma — escape from the material cosmos |

## Theological Emphases

- **Heavenly Ascent**: Isaiah as prototype of the Gnostic visionary who penetrates the archonic barriers
- **Secret Knowledge**: Isaiah received hidden wisdom about the true divine nature, unavailable to ordinary prophets
- **Critique of Worldly Power**: Isaiah's denunciation of corrupt kings and rulers maps onto the Gnostic rejection of archonic governance
- **The Disguised Descent**: In the *Ascension of Isaiah*, Christ descends through the heavens in disguise, eluding the archons — a distinctively Gnostic Christological motif

## Major Gnostic Schools and Isaiah

| School | Era | Distinctive Use of Isaiah |
|--------|-----|--------------------------|
| Sethian | 2nd-3rd century | Yaldabaoth's false claim of sole divinity (cf. Isa 45:5) |
| Valentinian | 2nd century | Isaiah's hidden wisdom as analogy for Pleroma theology |
| Ascension tradition | 2nd century | Isaiah's martyrdom and heavenly journey |
| Mandaean | Pre-3rd century | Prophetic ascent through heavenly spheres |
