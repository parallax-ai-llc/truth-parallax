The divine warrior of Isaiah 63:1-6 is read in Gnostic theology as an image of the Pleroma's ultimate assault on the archonic powers. The blood-stained garments of the warrior (63:1-3) represent not human blood but the dissolution of the archonic rulers themselves, whose existence is "blood" in the sense of materiality. The Paraphrase of Shem (NHC VII,1) describes a cosmic battle in which the forces of light overthrow the forces of darkness, freeing the imprisoned sparks. The warrior who has "trodden the winepress alone" (63:3) is the pleromatic power that accomplishes this liberation without assistance from within the material cosmos.

The communal lament (63:7-19) is interpreted as the collective cry of the pneumatic community, remembering past moments of gnosis ("your wonders of old") and lamenting present darkness. The anguished question "Why do you make us wander from your ways?" (63:17) is directed not at the true God but at the Demiurge, whose governance creates the conditions of wandering and confusion. The distinction between the speaker's Father (63:16) and the power responsible for their suffering illustrates the Gnostic two-God theology: the true Father above and the Demiurge below.

The remarkable claim "Abraham does not know us" (63:16) is given a characteristically Gnostic inversion: the pneumatic's true lineage is not biological (descending from Abraham) but spiritual (descending from the Pleroma). Abraham, a figure of the Demiurge's covenant, cannot recognize those whose origin lies in the transcendent realm. The pneumatic belongs to a lineage that precedes Abraham, precedes creation, precedes even the Demiurge -- stretching back to the Pleroma's original emanation.
