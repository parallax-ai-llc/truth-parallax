Isaiah 40 opens with a divine call -- "Comfort, comfort my people" (40:1) -- that Gnostic readers understand as the voice of the unknown Father penetrating the Demiurge's realm. The "comfort" is not material prosperity but gnosis: the knowledge that the pneumatic's true home is the Pleroma, not the material cosmos. The Gospel of Truth (NHC I,3) describes a similar moment of divine communication: "This is the gospel of the one who is searched for, which was revealed to those who are perfect through the mercies of the Father."

The "voice crying in the wilderness" (40:3) is reinterpreted as the call of the divine Messenger -- the Revealer who descends into the material "wilderness" (the Kenoma) to awaken sleeping pneumatics. In Valentinian theology, this Revealer is the aeon Christ or the Savior, who passes through the archonic spheres without being detected and delivers the message of gnosis to those who have ears to hear. The wilderness is not a geographical location but the ontological condition of existence outside the Pleroma.

The meditation on divine transcendence (40:12-26) is enthusiastically affirmed by Gnostic interpreters, who see it as a description of the true God's radical otherness from all created reality -- including the Demiurge himself. The rhetorical question "To whom will you compare God?" (40:18) is read as proving that the Demiurge, who can be described and compared, cannot be the true God. The authentic divine reality is beyond all categories, beyond all comparison, beyond all images -- the Ineffable One of the Sethian tradition. The Demiurge's claim "I am God, and there is no other" (cf. 45:5) is thus exposed as the boast of a being who does not know what lies above him.
