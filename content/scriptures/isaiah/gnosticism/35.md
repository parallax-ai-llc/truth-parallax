Isaiah 35's vision of restoration is read in Gnostic theology as describing the return of the divine sparks to the Pleroma -- the fullness from which they fell. The desert blooming (35:1-2) represents the Kenoma (realm of deficiency) being infused with pleromatic light. This is not the improvement of the material world but its transformation into something qualitatively different -- the restoration of spiritual reality that the Demiurge's creation obscured.

The healings of verses 5-6 -- blind eyes opened, deaf ears unstopped -- are understood in Gnostic exegesis as the awakening of gnosis. The blind are those trapped in ignorance (agnoia), unable to perceive the truth of their divine origin. The deaf are those who cannot hear the call of the divine Messenger. The lame are those immobilized by attachment to material existence. These are not physical conditions but spiritual states, and their healing is the bestowal of saving knowledge. The Testimony of Truth (NHC IX,3) makes similar connections between physical healing and spiritual awakening.

The "Way of Holiness" (35:8) on which "no unclean person may travel" is interpreted as the path of gnosis, accessible only to pneumatics (spirit-bearers) who have received the divine spark. The distinction between those who can walk this way and those who cannot reflects the Gnostic classification of humanity into pneumatics (spiritual), psychics (soul-governed), and hylics (material). The "everlasting joy" (35:10) is the joy of reunion with the Pleroma, where sorrow and sighing -- products of the material condition -- simply cannot exist.
