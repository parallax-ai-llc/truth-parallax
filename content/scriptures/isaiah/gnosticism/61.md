Isaiah 61's description of an anointed figure proclaiming liberation is read in Gnostic theology as the Revealer's self-declaration. The anointing (61:1) corresponds to the bestowal of the divine pneuma that empowers the Gnostic messenger. In the Gospel of Philip, chrismation (anointing) is the supreme sacrament: "It is from the word 'chrism' that we are called 'Christians,' and it is because of the chrism that 'Christ' was called Christ." The anointing is not a metaphor but a genuine transmission of pneumatic power.

The "year of the LORD's favor" (61:2) is interpreted as the aeon (cosmic age) in which the Revealer's mission operates -- the present age of gathering, in which pneumatic seeds are being awakened and collected before the material cosmos is dissolved. The Valentinian myth describes three ages: the pre-cosmic Pleroma, the age of the material cosmos, and the post-cosmic restoration. Isaiah's "year of favor" is the second age, understood not as punishment but as opportunity -- the window during which gnosis is available.

The transformation described -- "beauty for ashes, gladness for mourning" (61:3) -- is the experiential effect of gnosis on the pneumatic. Before gnosis, the pneumatic exists in a state of mourning (pentheo) -- an unspecified sadness that arises from the dimly perceived awareness of being separated from one's true home. After gnosis, this mourning is replaced by the joy (chara) of recognition. The Gospel of Truth describes this transformation: "When he had received knowledge, he did the will of the One who called him... He found himself and He found the One who belongs to him." The "oaks of righteousness" (61:3) are the fully realized pneumatics, rooted in gnosis and bearing the fruit of spiritual knowledge.
