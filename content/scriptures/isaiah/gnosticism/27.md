## The Serpent and the Dragon

"In that day the LORD will punish Leviathan the gliding serpent... and the dragon in the sea" (27:1). Gnostic interpretation of the serpent is characteristically ambivalent. In some Gnostic traditions (Ophite, Naassene), the serpent is a positive figure — the bringer of *gnosis* in Eden. In others, Leviathan represents the archonic power that must be overcome. Isaiah 27 is generally read through the latter lens: the defeat of the cosmic powers that keep the divine sparks imprisoned.

## The Vineyard of the Pleroma

"I, the LORD, watch over it; I water it continually" (27:3). The restored vineyard is the Pleroma in its healed state — the divine fullness tended by the true God. In contrast to chapter 5's vineyard (the Demiurge's failed creation), this vineyard receives genuine divine care and produces authentic spiritual fruit. The restoration of the vineyard represents the healing of the breach that Sophia's fall created.

## The Trumpet of Awakening

"In that day a great trumpet will sound" (27:13). Gnostic soteriology identifies this trumpet with the call of *gnosis* — the revelatory sound that awakens the sleeping sparks and summons them to return to the Pleroma. The *Hymn of the Pearl* in the *Acts of Thomas* describes a similar "call" or "voice" that reaches the prince in Egypt and wakes him from his forgetful sleep, reminding him of his true identity and mission.
