Isaiah 57's observation that the righteous perish unnoticed (57:1-2) is read in Gnostic theology as describing the pneumatic's death -- an event that the material world misunderstands. The archonic world sees death as annihilation, but the Gnostic understands it as liberation. The righteous are "taken away to be spared from evil" (57:1) because death frees the pneumatic soul from the material prison and allows its return to the Pleroma. The Valentinian Letter of Ptolemy to Flora describes death as the dissolution of the body's archonic chains, releasing the divine spark to ascend through the celestial spheres.

The condemnation of idolatrous practices in verses 3-10 is given its most radical Gnostic extension: all worship directed at the Demiurge is, in the deepest sense, idolatry. The Demiurge demands sacrifice, obedience, and worship, but he is himself a created being who does not deserve any of these. The true God, the Unknown Father, does not demand worship but only recognition -- the acknowledgment by the pneumatic of its own divine origin. The distinction between idol worship and Demiurge worship collapses in the Gnostic reading: both involve directing devotion toward a being that is not the ultimate reality.

The paradox of verse 15 -- God dwelling in the "high and holy place" yet with the "contrite and lowly" -- is read as describing the true God's presence within the pneumatic who has been broken by the archonic world. The Pleroma's representative dwells not in temples or heavens but within the human being who has been crushed by material existence and thereby rendered open to transcendent reality. The Gospel of Thomas expresses this: "The kingdom is inside of you, and it is outside of you" (saying 3).
