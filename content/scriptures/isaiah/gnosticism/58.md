Isaiah 58's rejection of external fasting in favor of justice resonates with the Gnostic suspicion of ritual practices that substitute bodily discipline for genuine gnosis. While some Gnostic communities practiced rigorous asceticism (the Manichaeans and certain Sethian groups), others rejected it entirely, arguing that bodily practices are irrelevant to the pneumatic's spiritual condition. The Gospel of Thomas records: "If you fast, you will give rise to sin for yourselves" (saying 14), suggesting that fasting reinforces rather than liberates from material identification.

The "true fast" Isaiah describes -- loosing chains, feeding the hungry, sheltering the homeless (58:6-7) -- is reinterpreted in Gnostic terms as the work of liberating trapped pneumatic sparks. The "chains" are archonic bonds of ignorance; the "hungry" are those starving for gnosis; the "homeless" are pneumatics who have forgotten their pleromatic home. Material acts of charity, while not condemned, are secondary to the fundamental liberation that only gnosis provides. The Letter to Rheginos (Treatise on the Resurrection, NHC I,4) makes the point that spiritual transformation, not physical modification, is the true work of salvation.

The promise "Then your light will break forth like the dawn" (58:8) is interpreted as the illumination that follows the reception of gnosis. The "light" is not metaphorical but describes the actual experience of the pneumatic who perceives, for the first time, the divine light within. The Gnostic texts frequently describe the experience of gnosis in terms of sudden illumination -- a "breaking forth" that transforms the recipient's entire existence. Allogenes (NHC XI,3) describes the visionary's experience: "I saw a light-place, and I was filled with revelation.
