Isaiah 55's invitation -- "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters" (55:1) -- is read in Gnostic theology as the Pleroma's call to all who carry the divine spark. The "waters" are the living waters of gnosis, frequently referenced in Gnostic texts. The Gospel of Thomas records Jesus saying: "Whoever drinks from my mouth will become like me" (saying 108). The "thirst" is the pneumatic's innate longing for the Pleroma -- a longing that material satisfactions cannot quench. The offer "without money" (55:1) indicates that gnosis is not earned or purchased but freely given to those capable of receiving it.

The assurance that God's word "will not return empty" (55:11) is understood through the Gnostic concept of the divine Logos as a creative and redemptive force. In the Trimorphic Protennoia, the divine Word (Logos) descends through three stages -- as Voice, as Speech, and as Word incarnate -- and at each stage accomplishes a specific aspect of the pneumatic's liberation. Isaiah's declaration that the word accomplishes its purpose confirms the Gnostic confidence that the pleromatic mission will succeed despite archonic resistance.

The vision of nature rejoicing -- mountains singing, trees clapping (55:12) -- is interpreted as the material creation's participation in the pneumatic's liberation. In some Gnostic systems, the material world itself contains traces of divine light (since Sophia's fall scattered sparks throughout creation). The cosmic rejoicing of Isaiah 55 anticipates the moment when all trapped light is freed and the material cosmos, having served its purpose, is dissolved or transformed. The "thorn" replaced by the "juniper" (55:13) symbolizes the transformation of archonic constraints into pleromatic freedom.
