## The Watchman Between Worlds

Isaiah 21's watchman, stationed between heaven and earth, is read by Gnostic interpreters as the prototype of the Gnostic seer — the pneumatic being who sees what others cannot. "Day after day, my lord, I stand on the watchtower" (21:8). The watchman occupies the liminal space between the archonic world and the Pleroma, reporting on the movements of cosmic powers.

## "Babylon Has Fallen!"

"Babylon has fallen, has fallen! All the images of its gods lie shattered!" (21:9). Gnostic eschatology sees this as the ultimate denouement of cosmic history: the material world-system ("Babylon") collapses, and all the archonic powers ("its gods") are destroyed. The Revelation of John adopts this Isaianic language for the same purpose — the announcement of the archonic world's end.

## Morning and Night

"Morning is coming, but also the night" (21:12). This enigmatic answer reflects the Gnostic understanding that illumination and darkness coexist in the present age. For those who receive *gnosis*, the morning is already dawning; for those who remain in ignorance, the night deepens. The watchman cannot offer a simple answer because the two conditions are simultaneous.
