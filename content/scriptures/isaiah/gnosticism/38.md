Hezekiah's illness in Isaiah 38 is read in Gnostic theology as a metaphor for the pneumatic soul's bondage to the material body. The body, fashioned by the archons as a prison for the divine spark, is inherently subject to decay and death. Hezekiah's cry -- "I shall not see the LORD in the land of the living" (38:11) -- expresses the existential anguish of the pneumatic trapped in matter, unable to perceive the Pleroma through the veil of flesh.

The extension of Hezekiah's life by fifteen years (38:5) is interpreted in Gnostic terms as the prolongation of the soul's sojourn in the material realm for the purpose of completing its gnosis. The Valentinian system taught that each pneumatic soul must fully develop its spiritual seed before returning to the Pleroma. Death before the completion of gnosis would require another incarnation (some Gnostic systems accepted reincarnation). The additional years represent the time needed for spiritual maturation -- not a blessing from the Demiurge but a provision of the true God.

The sign of the shadow retreating (38:8) is interpreted symbolically: the shadow represents ignorance (skotia), which retreats before the advance of gnosis. The "steps of Ahaz" represent the descending stages by which the soul entered the material world, and the shadow's reversal indicates the soul's ascent back toward the Pleroma. This reading transforms a physical miracle into a spiritual allegory, consistent with the Gnostic hermeneutical method that privileges inner meaning over historical narrative.
