Isaiah 64's prayer "Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down" (64:1) is the quintessential Gnostic cry: the plea for the transcendent God to break through the archonic barriers (the heavens are the archons' sphere of control) and reveal Himself directly. The multiple "heavens" that must be rent correspond to the celestial spheres through which the Revealer must descend, passing through each archonic level. The Ascension of Isaiah describes seven heavens, each ruled by an archon, through which Christ must pass in disguise to reach the material world.

The confession "All our righteous acts are like filthy rags" (64:6) receives a characteristically Gnostic reading: all activities performed within the archonic system -- including religious observance, moral striving, and ritual practice -- are worthless from the perspective of the Pleroma. The archonic world's "righteousness" is not genuine righteousness but a counterfeit virtue that reinforces rather than liberates from material bondage. Only gnosis -- direct knowledge of the divine -- has salvific value. The Valentinian teacher Heracleon, as reported by Origen, argued that worship "in spirit and truth" (John 4:24) transcends all material forms of religious practice.

The potter-clay metaphor (64:8) is given a double reading. On one level, the Demiurge is the potter who shapes the material body -- but his work is inferior, a clumsy imitation of the Pleroma's perfect forms. On another level, the true God is the potter who shaped the pneumatic seed and planted it within the Demiurge's creation. The human being is thus the work of two potters: the body fashioned by the Demiurge, the spirit breathed in by the Pleroma. The prayer "We are all the work of your hand" (64:8) is, from the Gnostic perspective, only half true -- only the pneumatic element is the work of the true God's hand.
