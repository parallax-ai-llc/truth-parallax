The second Servant Song (49:1-7) is read in Gnostic theology as the self-revelation of the Luminous Messenger. The declaration "from the womb he called me" (49:1) is understood as describing the pre-cosmic origin of the Revealer within the Pleroma. The "womb" is Barbelo or the divine Mother -- the first emanation of the Unknown Father in Sethian theology. The Messenger's name is "hidden" (49:2), reflecting the Gnostic emphasis on the secret character of the divine mission.

The maternal imagery of verses 14-16 -- God's love surpassing a mother's love -- is connected by Gnostic interpreters to the divine feminine principle (Sophia, Barbelo, the Mother) that pervades their theological systems. The assurance "Can a mother forget her nursing child?" (49:15) is read as the voice of the divine Mother addressing the scattered pneumatic seeds, assuring them that they have not been abandoned despite the appearance of cosmic separation. The Trimorphic Protennoia (NHC XIII,1) presents a similar divine speech: "I am the Mother of the Voice, speaking in many ways, completing the All."

The Servant's sense of having "labored in vain" (49:4) resonates with the Gnostic theme of the Revealer's apparently fruitless mission in the material world. The archonic rulers resist the message of gnosis, most human beings remain asleep, and the Messenger experiences the futility of working within a system designed to suppress truth. Yet the Father's response is to expand the mission (49:6) -- a pattern that Gnostic communities applied to their own experience of marginalization: rejection by the world confirms rather than negates the authenticity of their message.
