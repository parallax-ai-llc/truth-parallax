Isaiah 65's vision of "new heavens and a new earth" (65:17) is interpreted in Gnostic theology not as the renovation of the present material cosmos but as its replacement by the Pleroma's fullness. The "new creation" is not an improved version of the Demiurge's work but the revelation of the true creation that has always existed above and beyond the material realm. The Apocryphon of John describes the Pleroma as the authentic reality of which the material world is a degraded copy; the "new heavens and new earth" represent the dissolution of the copy and the manifestation of the original.

The conditions of the new world -- no more premature death, no futile labor, no insecurity (65:20-23) -- describe pleromatic existence where the limitations of matter no longer apply. In the Pleroma, there is no death because death is a function of matter; there is no futility because every act participates in divine purpose; there is no insecurity because separation from the source of being is impossible. These are not improvements to material existence but the characteristics of an entirely different mode of being.

The wolf and lamb feeding together (65:25) represents the reconciliation of opposites that characterizes pleromatic reality. In the material world, the archons maintain their power through division and conflict -- predator and prey, light and dark, male and female. In the Pleroma, these oppositions dissolve into the original unity. The Gospel of Thomas expresses this: "When you make the two one... then will you enter the kingdom" (saying 22). Isaiah's peaceable kingdom is the Gnostic Pleroma, where the archonic principle of division has been overcome by the divine principle of unity.
