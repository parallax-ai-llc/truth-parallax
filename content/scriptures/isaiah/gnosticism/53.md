Isaiah 53 receives a distinctive interpretation in Gnostic theology that differs fundamentally from both Jewish and mainstream Christian readings. The Suffering Servant is identified not as Israel collectively nor as a sin-bearing sacrifice, but as the Luminous Revealer who descends into the material world and suffers the consequences of archonic hostility. "He was despised and rejected" (53:3) describes the world's reception of gnosis: the message of liberation is rejected by those who are invested in the archonic system.

The critical verses "he bore our infirmities" and "by his wounds we are healed" (53:4-5) are reinterpreted through a non-substitutionary framework. The Revealer does not bear sin in a legal or transactional sense but takes on the condition of material existence -- with all its suffering and limitation -- in order to communicate gnosis from within. The "healing" accomplished is not the removal of guilt but the bestowal of knowledge. The Gnostic understanding of salvation is ontological, not juridical: the problem is not guilt but ignorance, and the solution is not punishment absorbed but truth revealed.

The Servant's silence before his accusers (53:7) is read through docetic lenses in some Gnostic traditions. In the Second Treatise of the Great Seth (NHC VII,2), the Revealer describes his crucifixion as an illusion: "I did not die in reality but in appearance... It was another who drank the gall and the vinegar; it was not I." The silence of the Servant may indicate not passive submission to suffering but the inner detachment of one whose true nature is untouched by material events. The "offspring" he sees (53:10) are the pneumatic seeds whom his gnosis has awakened -- the spiritual children born not of flesh but of knowledge.
