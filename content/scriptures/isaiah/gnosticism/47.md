Babylon's fall in Isaiah 47 is read in Gnostic theology as a type of Sophia's reversal. Babylon personified as a queen who is brought low (47:1) mirrors the Valentinian narrative of Sophia -- the lowest aeon of the Pleroma -- who fell from her exalted position through presumptuous desire. Both figures claim autonomy: Babylon says "I am, and there is none besides me" (47:8), and Sophia acted without her consort, attempting to comprehend the Father without the Pleroma's mediation.

However, the parallel diverges significantly. In the Gnostic myth, Sophia's fall is not permanent punishment but the beginning of a redemptive process: her error produces the Demiurge and the material world, but it also scatters the divine sparks that will eventually be collected and returned to the Pleroma. Babylon's fall is purely destructive; Sophia's fall is tragically creative. The Gnostic reader sees both the judgment and the redemption in a single cosmic movement.

The condemnation of Babylon's sorcery (47:12-13) is read as a critique of the archonic manipulation of the material world through what appear to be supernatural powers but are actually techniques for maintaining ignorance. The archons use what the Letter of Peter to Philip (NHC VIII,2) calls "powers and principalities" to keep souls trapped in material existence. Astrology, divination, and sorcery are archonic technologies designed to reinforce the illusion that the material cosmos is the ultimate reality and that its rulers control human destiny.
