Isaiah 39's account of Hezekiah displaying his treasures to Babylonian envoys is read in Gnostic interpretation as a warning about revealing sacred knowledge to those unprepared to receive it. The "treasures" represent gnosis -- the hidden knowledge of the pneumatic's divine origin and destiny. Displaying these treasures to representatives of Babylon (a cipher for the archonic world-system) is a violation of the Gnostic discipline of secrecy (disciplina arcani) that many communities practiced.

The prophecy that everything will be carried off to Babylon (39:6) represents the loss of gnosis that occurs when it is shared indiscriminately. The Gospel of Thomas preserves the saying: "Do not give what is holy to dogs, lest they throw it on the dung-heap" (saying 93). Gnostic communities were characteristically secretive, sharing their deepest teachings only with initiated members. Hezekiah's error is a narrative illustration of why this secrecy is necessary: the archonic powers will seize and corrupt any gnosis that falls into their domain.

The transition from chapter 39 to 40 -- from loss to consolation -- mirrors the Gnostic drama of fall and redemption. The soul's descent into matter (represented by the exile to Babylon) is followed by the divine call to return (the "comfort" of chapter 40). This pattern structures the entire Gnostic myth: from the Pleroma's fullness, through Sophia's fall and the creation of the deficient world, to the eventual restoration of all spiritual seeds to their source.
