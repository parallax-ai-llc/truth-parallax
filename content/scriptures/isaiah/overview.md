## Overview

Isaiah is one of the most theologically significant books in the Hebrew Bible and Christian Old Testament. Its Hebrew name, *Yeshayahu* ("YHWH is salvation"), captures the central tension of the book: God's judgment upon unfaithfulness and the promise of ultimate redemption. Spanning 66 chapters, Isaiah addresses the political crises of the 8th-century BCE Judahite kingdom while projecting a vision of cosmic renewal that has shaped Jewish, Christian, and Islamic eschatology for millennia.

## Structure

Isaiah is traditionally divided into three major sections, though scholarship debates their unity:

1. **First Isaiah** (Chapters 1–39): Oracles of judgment and hope set during the Assyrian crisis of the 8th century BCE. Includes the call narrative (ch. 6), Immanuel prophecies (chs. 7–12), oracles against the nations (chs. 13–23), the "Isaiah Apocalypse" (chs. 24–27), and woe oracles (chs. 28–33).
2. **Second Isaiah / Deutero-Isaiah** (Chapters 40–55): Messages of comfort and restoration addressed to Judean exiles in Babylon. Contains the Servant Songs (42:1-4, 49:1-6, 50:4-9, 52:13–53:12) and proclamations of God's incomparability.
3. **Third Isaiah / Trito-Isaiah** (Chapters 56–66): Post-exilic oracles addressing the restored community, culminating in visions of new heavens and a new earth.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Isaiah occupies a unique position across religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: A major prophetic book (Nevi'im), with extensive haftarah readings and central importance in rabbinic and mystical interpretation
- **Christianity**: The most frequently quoted Old Testament prophet in the New Testament, foundational for Christology and soteriology
- **Islam**: Isaiah (*Sha'ya*) is referenced in Islamic tradition as a prophet who foretold the coming of Muhammad
- **Gnosticism**: Isaiah's visionary and apocalyptic material was adapted in texts like the *Ascension of Isaiah*
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding prophetic literature, redaction criticism, and the development of monotheism

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Holy One of Israel | God's transcendence and moral purity — a title unique to Isaiah |
| Judgment and Justice | God's condemnation of social oppression, idolatry, and false worship |
| The Remnant | A faithful minority preserved through judgment |
| Messianic Hope | Oracles of a coming king and servant who will establish justice |
| The Suffering Servant | The enigmatic figure of Isaiah 53 who bears the sins of others |
| New Creation | The eschatological vision of renewed heavens and earth (65:17) |
| Universal Salvation | The nations streaming to Zion (2:2-4) and God's purpose for all peoples |
