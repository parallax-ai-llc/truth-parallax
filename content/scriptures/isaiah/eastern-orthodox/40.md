Isaiah 40 is one of the most frequently read prophetic texts in Orthodox worship, appearing prominently in the Advent/Nativity preparation period and at Theophany (the Baptism of Christ). The "voice crying in the wilderness" (40:3) is identified with John the Forerunner (Prodromos), whose role in Orthodox theology extends beyond mere announcement to active participation in the divine economy. The Forerunner prepares the way not only historically but liturgically: his memory is invoked at every baptism, and his icon stands to the right of Christ on the iconostasis.

The meditation on divine transcendence (40:12-26) resonates profoundly with the apophatic (negative) theology that characterizes the Orthodox intellectual tradition. Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, Gregory of Nyssa, and Maximus the Confessor all emphasize that God transcends every human concept and category. Isaiah's rhetorical questions -- "To whom will you compare God?" (40:18) -- express the fundamental Orthodox conviction that God is beyond all comparison, beyond all likeness, known not through concepts but through direct experience of His uncreated energies.

The eagle imagery of verse 31 -- "they will soar on wings like eagles" -- is interpreted in Orthodox mystical theology as describing the soul's ascent toward God through prayer and contemplation. The great hesychast tradition, from Evagrius through Gregory Palamas, understands this soaring not as escape from the body but as the body-soul unity being lifted into participation in divine life. The Taboric light that the disciples saw at the Transfiguration is the same divine energy that empowers the believer to "run and not grow weary" (40:31).
