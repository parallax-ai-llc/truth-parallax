## The Heavenly Liturgy

Isaiah 6 is the single most liturgically significant passage in the entire Old Testament for Orthodox worship. The seraphim's cry — "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of His glory" (6:3) — is the *Sanctus* sung at every Divine Liturgy. Orthodox theology understands the earthly liturgy as a participation in the heavenly liturgy that Isaiah witnessed: when the Church sings "Holy, Holy, Holy," it joins the angelic worship that never ceases before God's throne.

## The Vision of the Uncreated Light

Isaiah's theophany — "I saw the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up" (6:1) — is understood through the Palamite distinction between God's essence (unknowable) and God's energies (experienced). Isaiah did not see God's essence but was granted a vision of the divine glory — the same uncreated light that the hesychasts seek through contemplative prayer. The vision both reveals and conceals: God makes Himself known while remaining infinitely beyond human comprehension.

## Cleansing by Fire

The coal from the altar touching Isaiah's lips (6:6-7) is a central image in Orthodox sacramental theology. St. Symeon the New Theologian compared Eucharistic communion to the burning coal of Isaiah: "This fire that I receive is God Himself, the very fire that purified the lips of the prophet." The communion hymn in the Liturgy of St. Basil explicitly references this Isaianic image.

## The Commission and the Mystery of Hardening

The paradoxical commission — "Make the heart of this people calloused" (6:10) — is interpreted by the Orthodox Fathers not as God creating unbelief but as the inevitable effect of divine light on eyes accustomed to darkness. Just as physical light blinds those emerging from a dark cave, the brilliance of prophetic truth overwhelms those unprepared to receive it. The remedy is *metanoia* — the gradual reorientation of the heart toward the light.
