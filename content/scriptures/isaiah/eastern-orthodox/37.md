Hezekiah's prayer in the temple (37:14-20) is a paradigmatic act of worship in Orthodox theology. The king enters the sacred space, spreads the letter before the LORD, and prays -- an act that the Orthodox liturgical tradition sees as the proper posture of the Church before every crisis. The temple is not merely a building but the place where heaven and earth intersect, a concept fundamental to Orthodox architecture and iconography. Every Orthodox church, with its dome representing heaven and its nave representing earth, replicates this sacred meeting point.

God's deliverance through the angel who strikes the Assyrian camp (37:36) is celebrated in Orthodox angelology. The Church teaches that angelic powers are actively involved in the defense of the faithful and the Church. The Synaxis of the Archangel Michael and All the Bodiless Powers (November 8/21) honors this angelic ministry. Icons of the archangel with sword drawn echo the imagery of Isaiah 37 and remind the faithful that the battle against evil is waged on both visible and invisible planes.

The declaration "I planned it long ago" (37:26) is read through the Orthodox understanding of divine economy -- God's unified plan of salvation encompasses all of history. The Fathers insisted that nothing catches God by surprise: Assyria's rise and fall, Israel's exile and return, and ultimately the Incarnation, Cross, and Resurrection are all comprehended in a single divine act of love. This is not fatalism but the confidence that history has a direction and a goal -- the recapitulation of all things in Christ (Ephesians 1:10).
