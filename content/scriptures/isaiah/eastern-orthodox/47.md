Orthodox interpretation of Isaiah 47 reads Babylon's fall as a spiritual lesson about the nature of pride and its inevitable consequences. The patristic tradition, from John Chrysostom to Symeon the New Theologian, consistently identifies pride (hyperephania) as the root passion from which all other passions grow. Babylon's self-deification -- "I am, and there is none besides me" (47:8) -- is the paradigmatic expression of this root sin, echoing the Fathers' account of Satan's original rebellion.

The condemnation of Babylon's sorcery and divination (47:12-13) reinforces the Orthodox Church's canonical prohibition of occult practices. The Rudder (Pedalion), the collection of canons governing Orthodox life, includes strict penalties for Christians who engage in fortune-telling, astrology, or other forms of divination. This prohibition is not arbitrary but theological: to seek knowledge of the future through occult means is to deny God's providential governance and to seek power apart from grace -- precisely Babylon's error.

The taunt song's literary form -- poetry of mocking directed at fallen power -- finds its liturgical counterpart in the Orthodox Paschal celebration, where Christ's victory over death is proclaimed with triumphant irony: "Where, O death, is your sting? Where, O Hades, is your victory?" (1 Corinthians 15:55). The downfall of every Babylon, every pretension to ultimate power apart from God, is already accomplished in Christ's Resurrection. Orthodox eschatology holds that the final manifestation of this victory is certain because its ground has already been established in the Paschal event.
