Isaiah 55's invitation -- "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters" (55:1) -- is a baptismal and eucharistic text in Orthodox liturgical tradition. It is read at the Great Blessing of Water at Theophany (January 6/19), when the priest blesses the waters in commemoration of Christ's baptism. The "waters" of Isaiah become the sanctified waters of baptism, and the invitation to "come, buy wine and milk without money" (55:1) anticipates the Eucharistic banquet where the baptized receive Christ's Body and Blood freely.

The word of God that "will not return to me empty" (55:11) is foundational to the Orthodox theology of the divine Logos. In Orthodox understanding, the "word" is not merely spoken language but the creative and sustaining power of the Logos -- the Second Person of the Trinity through whom all things were made (John 1:3). When Scripture is proclaimed in the liturgy, it is not merely read but enacted: the Logos Himself speaks through the sacred text, and His word accomplishes the salvation it announces. This is why the Gospel book is enthroned on the altar and processed through the nave during the Little Entrance -- it is an icon of Christ the Word.

The vision of creation rejoicing -- "the mountains and hills will burst into song" (55:12) -- resonates with the Orthodox theology of cosmic transfiguration. The Fathers taught that creation itself groans for liberation (Romans 8:22) and that the final consummation will involve not the destruction but the transformation of the material world. The Orthodox blessing of water, food, homes, and fields expresses this conviction: matter is destined for glorification, and every blessing anticipates the final transfiguration when all creation will sing the praise of its Creator.
