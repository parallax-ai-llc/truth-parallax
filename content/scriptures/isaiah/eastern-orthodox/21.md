## The Prophet's Anguish

Isaiah 21 reveals the emotional cost of prophetic vision. "My body is racked with pain, pangs seize me like those of a woman in labor" (21:3). Orthodox theology of prophecy sees this suffering as participation in God's own grief over human sin — the same *sympatheia* that led to the Incarnation. The prophet does not stand outside the situation he describes but enters into it with his whole being.

## The Watchman of the Night

"Watchman, what is left of the night?" (21:11). Orthodox liturgical theology hears this question in every dawn vigil (*pannychis*). The Church watches through the night of history, waiting for the dawn of Christ's return. The ambiguous answer — "Morning is coming, but also the night" — reflects the Orthodox understanding that the Church lives in the tension between the light that has already come (the Incarnation) and the full dawn that is yet to arrive.

## The Fall of Babylon Announced

"Babylon has fallen, has fallen!" (21:9). Orthodox eschatology reads every historical fall of oppressive power as a partial fulfillment of this prophetic pattern. The icon of the Last Judgment, prominently displayed in Orthodox churches, depicts the final fall of all worldly power before the throne of Christ.
