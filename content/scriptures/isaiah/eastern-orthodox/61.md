Isaiah 61's anointing by the Spirit (61:1) is connected in Orthodox theology to the Mystery of Chrismation, the anointing with holy myron that immediately follows baptism. In this sacrament, the newly baptized receives the "seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit" -- a participation in the same anointing that Christ claims in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4:18-19). The myron is prepared by the Ecumenical Patriarch (or head of each autocephalous church) with elaborate ceremony, signifying the anointing's apostolic origin and ecclesial significance.

The "year of the LORD's favor" (61:2) is understood in Orthodox theology as the entire present age -- the time between Christ's first and second comings, during which grace is available through the sacraments and the preaching of the gospel. This is the era of the Church, the period of opportunity for repentance and transformation. The Orthodox liturgical year enacts this "year of favor" cyclically, with each annual round of feasts and fasts offering renewed encounters with the mysteries of salvation.

The transformation from mourning to joy (61:3) -- "a crown of beauty instead of ashes" -- is liturgically enacted in the Orthodox Paschal transition. The somber vestments of Holy Week give way to the brilliant white and gold of Pascha. The faithful who have journeyed through the "ashes" of Great Lent receive the "oil of joy" of the Resurrection. The "oaks of righteousness" (61:3) are understood as the saints, who through centuries of spiritual growth have become mighty witnesses, their lives rooted in Christ and bearing fruit for the life of the world.
