## Jerusalem: The Valley of Vision

Isaiah 22 turns judgment upon Jerusalem itself. Orthodox exegesis emphasizes the irony: the "Valley of Vision" should be the place of clearest spiritual sight, yet its inhabitants are blind to the approaching judgment. Orthodox self-understanding includes the prophetic capacity for self-critique — the recognition that the Church itself can fail to live up to its calling.

## The Key of David

"I will place on his shoulder the key of the house of David; what he opens no one can shut, and what he shuts no one can open" (22:22). Orthodox Christology applies this to Christ as the one who holds all authority — the same language used in Revelation 3:7. Orthodox ecclesiology sees this authority exercised through the episcopal office, but always as delegated from Christ and never as autonomous human power.

## The Feast in the Face of Judgment

"Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!" (22:13). Orthodox moral theology identifies this attitude as *apognosis* — despair that abandons hope and seeks refuge in pleasure. The Orthodox response is not grim asceticism but joyful hope grounded in the resurrection. The Paschal greeting — "Christ is risen!" — is the definitive answer to the fatalism of 22:13.
