## The Fall of the Morning Star

"How you have fallen from heaven, morning star, son of the dawn!" (14:12). Orthodox patristic tradition, following Origen and the Cappadocians, reads this as describing the fall of Satan — the angelic being whose pride led to rebellion against God. While acknowledging the primary historical referent as the Babylonian king, Orthodox theology sees the spiritual dimension as inseparable from the historical: behind every tyrannical king stands the archetype of diabolical pride.

## The Five Aspirations of Pride

The king's fivefold "I will" (14:13-14) is analyzed by the Fathers as the anatomy of pride (*hyperephania*): the desire to ascend, to elevate oneself, to claim divine prerogatives, to tower above others, and to make oneself equal to God. Orthodox ascetic theology, from John Climacus's *Ladder of Divine Ascent* to modern spiritual guides, identifies these movements as the fundamental temptation that underlies all others.

## Sheol and the Mockery of the Dead

The dramatic scene in Sheol (14:9-11) serves in Orthodox theology as a meditation on the consequences of pride. The departed kings who rise to mock the fallen tyrant embody the truth that death strips away all pretension. Orthodox funeral services emphasize this theme: earthly glory is temporary, and only the soul's relationship with God endures. The kontakion of the funeral service — "Give rest, O Christ, to your servant" — stands in deliberate contrast to the tyrant's boast.
