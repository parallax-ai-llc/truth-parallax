Isaiah 59's diagnosis of sin -- "Your iniquities have separated you from your God" (59:2) -- is foundational to the Orthodox understanding of sin as separation from God rather than primarily as legal transgression. The Fathers, especially Maximus the Confessor, understood sin not as breaking rules but as turning away from the source of life. The consequence of sin is not primarily punishment but spiritual death -- the loss of communion with God that is the soul's true life. Orthodox confession (the Mystery of Repentance) aims at restoring this communion, not merely at obtaining legal absolution.

The corporate confession (59:9-15) is valued in Orthodox liturgical practice. The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete, chanted during the first and fifth weeks of Great Lent, is a sustained act of corporate penitence that echoes Isaiah's tone: "Where shall I begin to lament the deeds of my wretched life?" The shift from accusation to confession -- from "they" to "we" -- is essential to Orthodox spiritual practice, which insists that the penitent must identify with sinners rather than separate from them.

The divine warrior passage (59:16-20) is read christologically in Orthodox theology. When no human intercessor could be found (59:16), God Himself intervened -- the Incarnation is the ultimate divine intervention. The "Redeemer" who comes to Zion (59:20) is Christ, and the armor of God (59:17) that Paul applies to believers (Ephesians 6) is, in Orthodox understanding, the grace received through the sacraments. The baptismal garment, the chrism of anointing, the shield of faith communicated through the Eucharist -- these are the concrete means by which believers participate in the divine warrior's victory.
