Isaiah 51's call to "look to the rock from which you were cut" (51:1) is interpreted in Orthodox tradition as an invitation to remember the Fathers -- the patriarchs, prophets, apostles, and saints whose faith constitutes the living Tradition of the Church. Orthodox theology distinguishes between Tradition (capital T, the living transmission of the faith) and traditions (lowercase t, local customs). Isaiah's exhortation grounds the former: the community's identity is rooted in a chain of faithful witness stretching back to Abraham.

The creation-combat imagery of verse 9 -- "Was it not you who cut Rahab to pieces?" -- is read through the Paschal lens in Orthodox theology. Christ's descent into Hades is the ultimate divine combat, in which the Logos defeats death from within. The Resurrection icon (Anastasis) depicts Christ standing on the shattered gates of Hades, crushing the power of death -- a visual fulfillment of Isaiah's prayer for the "arm of the LORD" to awake and act as in days of old.

The "cup of wrath" (51:17, 22) carries deep liturgical significance in Orthodox Eucharistic theology. The chalice of the Eucharist transforms the cup of judgment into the cup of salvation. In every Divine Liturgy, the priest prays, "Your own of Your own we offer unto You," acknowledging that what was a cup of death becomes, through Christ's sacrifice, the cup of life. This transformation is not merely symbolic but real: Orthodox theology teaches that the Eucharistic elements are truly the Body and Blood of Christ, and the cup of wrath has been definitively replaced by the cup of blessing.
