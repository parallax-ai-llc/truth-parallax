## The Slaying of Leviathan

"In that day the LORD will punish Leviathan the gliding serpent" (27:1). Orthodox theology reads this as the eschatological defeat of Satan, accomplished in principle through Christ's death, descent into Hades, and resurrection. The Paschal icon of the Anastasis (Resurrection) depicts Christ standing on the broken gates of Hades, pulling Adam and Eve from their tombs — the visual fulfillment of Isaiah's promise of Leviathan's destruction.

## The Tended Vineyard

"I, the LORD, watch over it; I water it continually" (27:3). In contrast to the condemned vineyard of chapter 5, this vineyard receives divine care and protection. Orthodox ecclesiology reads this as a description of God's relationship with the Church: Christ, the true vine (John 15:1), tends His people through the sacraments, Scripture, and the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

## The Sound of the Great Trumpet

"In that day a great trumpet will sound" (27:13). Orthodox eschatology connects this to the final gathering of the elect at Christ's return. The trumpet call in Orthodox liturgy — the bells that summon the faithful to worship — is understood as a present echo of the eschatological trumpet. Every call to worship is a rehearsal for the final ingathering.
