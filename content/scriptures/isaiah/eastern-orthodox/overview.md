## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Isaiah is anchored in the patristic tradition and the liturgical life of the Church. Isaiah's vision of God's holiness (chapter 6), the cosmic scope of redemption, and the language of divine glory resonate deeply with Orthodox theology's emphasis on *theosis* (deification) and the transfiguration of all creation. The Orthodox Church reads Isaiah extensively in liturgical services, particularly during Great Lent and Holy Week, where the Suffering Servant passages are central.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Authority
Orthodox reading of Isaiah is guided by the Greek and Syriac Fathers:
- **St. Basil the Great**: Applied Isaiah's social justice oracles to the responsibilities of the wealthy
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Preached extensively on Isaiah's moral demands and messianic promises
- **St. Cyril of Alexandria**: Wrote a comprehensive *Commentary on Isaiah* emphasizing Christological fulfillment
- **St. Ephrem the Syrian**: Poetic-typological interpretation rich in paradox and symbol

### Liturgical Context
Isaiah permeates Orthodox worship. The Trisagion ("Holy, Holy, Holy") from Isaiah 6:3 is sung at every Divine Liturgy. The prophecies of the Suffering Servant are chanted during Holy Week. The vision of cosmic peace (11:6-9) shapes the Orthodox understanding of the eschaton.

### Theophanic Reading
Isaiah's temple vision (chapter 6) is understood as a genuine theophany — an encounter with the uncreated divine energies — that prefigures the Incarnation and is participated in sacramentally through the Divine Liturgy.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: Isaiah's vision of humanity and creation being transformed by divine glory is the prototype for Orthodox soteriology
- **The Holy One of Israel**: God's holiness is not merely moral purity but ontological transcendence — the uncreated God who nevertheless draws near
- **Cosmic Redemption**: Isaiah's new heavens and new earth (65:17) is not destruction but transfiguration of the cosmos
- **Ancestral Sin**: Isaiah's diagnosis of human corruption is read through the lens of inherited mortality rather than inherited guilt
