## The Woe Against Intoxication

Isaiah 28 opens with a condemnation of Ephraim's drunken leadership. Orthodox ascetic theology reads literal drunkenness as a symptom of deeper spiritual intoxication — the inability to perceive reality clearly due to attachment to pleasure and worldly comfort. The monastic tradition's emphasis on *nepsis* (watchfulness, sobriety) is a direct response to the prophetic warning against spiritual drunkenness.

## The Tested Cornerstone

"See, I lay a stone in Zion, a tested stone, a precious cornerstone" (28:16). Orthodox Christology identifies this stone with Christ, the foundation upon which the Church is built. The Orthodox practice of placing a relic in the antimension (the cloth on which the Eucharist is celebrated) symbolizes the Church's foundation on Christ and the apostles — the living stones built upon the cornerstone.

## The Covenant with Death Annulled

"Your covenant with death will be annulled" (28:18). Orthodox theology reads this as Christ's destruction of death's power through His own death and resurrection. The Paschal homily of St. John Chrysostom proclaims: "He that was taken by death has annihilated it... Hades took a body and encountered God!" — a direct echo of Isaiah's language.

## The Farmer Parable

The agricultural parable (28:23-29) is read by Orthodox theology as illustrating God's *oikonomia* — the divine economy that applies different measures to different situations with perfect wisdom. The Church's pastoral approach, which adapts to different cultural and personal contexts while maintaining doctrinal fidelity, embodies this principle.
