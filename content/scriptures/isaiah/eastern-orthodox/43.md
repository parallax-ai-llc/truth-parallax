Isaiah 43's declaration "I have called you by name, you are mine" (43:1) is central to Orthodox baptismal theology. In the Orthodox baptismal rite, the candidate is called by name three times as they are immersed and raised from the water -- a ritual enactment of the divine calling Isaiah describes. The passage through water and fire (43:2) is liturgically present in the baptismal immersion and in the chrismation (anointing with holy oil) that immediately follows, symbolized by candle flame and the fragrance of myrrh.

The "new thing" of verse 19 is read in Orthodox theology as the Incarnation itself -- the radically new act by which God enters creation from within rather than acting upon it from without. The Fathers insisted that the Incarnation is not merely new in degree but new in kind: "God became man that man might become God" (Athanasius). This pattern of divine self-giving that transforms creation is the "new thing" that supersedes even the exodus, though it fulfills its deepest meaning.

God's self-identification as the one who blots out transgressions "for my own sake" (43:25) is connected in Orthodox theology to the divine philanthropy -- God's love for humanity that requires no external motivation. The Orthodox Liturgy of St. Basil prays, "You did not turn away from Your creature whom You had made, O Good One, nor did You forget the work of Your hands." God forgives not because humanity merits it but because love is God's nature -- a conviction that grounds the Orthodox emphasis on divine mercy in the Jesus Prayer: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner."
