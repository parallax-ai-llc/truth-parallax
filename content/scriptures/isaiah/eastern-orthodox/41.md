Orthodox interpretation of Isaiah 41 reads the "fear not" declarations (41:10, 13, 14) through the lens of the Resurrection, which is the definitive divine act that overcomes all fear. The Paschal troparion -- "Let us call brothers even those that hate us, and forgive all by the Resurrection" -- expresses the fearlessness that flows from Christ's victory over death. The command "Do not fear" is not mere consolation but a participation in the Resurrection reality that transforms all circumstances.

The trial speech against the idols (41:21-29) is connected in patristic exegesis to the Church's struggle against idolatry in all its forms. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787), while defending the veneration of icons, carefully distinguished between the honor given to the prototype through the image and the worship due to God alone. John of Damascus's three treatises on icons drew on the prophetic polemic against idols to articulate the positive theology of sacred images: icons are not idols because they represent real persons who participate in divine grace, not fabricated deities.

The "worm Jacob" (41:14) whom God promises to make into a "threshing sledge" is read in Orthodox theology as an image of theosis -- the transformation of weakness into strength through participation in divine life. This is not mere empowerment but ontological change: the worm does not simply receive help but is made into something qualitatively different. The Fathers saw here a microcosm of the entire divine economy: God takes what is lowly and transforms it into an instrument of His glory.
