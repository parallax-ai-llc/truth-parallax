## The City of Salvation

Isaiah 26 sings of a "strong city" whose walls are salvation itself (26:1). Orthodox ecclesiology reads this as a type of the Church — the city of God that endures when all earthly cities fall. The Orthodox understanding of the Church as the mystical Body of Christ, protected by the gates of Hades, draws on this prophetic image.

## Peace, Peace

"You will keep in perfect peace those whose minds are steadfast" (26:3). The doubled *shalom* resonates with the Orthodox liturgical greeting "Peace be to all" and the frequent petitions for peace in the Divine Liturgy. Orthodox hesychast practice — the cultivation of inner stillness through the Jesus Prayer — seeks the *shalom shalom* that Isaiah describes: a peace that transcends circumstances and rests in God alone.

## The Resurrection of the Dead

"Your dead will live, LORD; their bodies will rise" (26:19). Orthodox resurrection theology finds in this verse one of the earliest and clearest Old Testament anticipations of bodily resurrection. The Easter matins canon celebrates the fulfillment of this promise: "Yesterday I was buried with Thee, O Christ; today I rise with Thee in Thy rising." The verse grounds the Orthodox insistence that salvation encompasses the body, not just the soul.

## The Shelter Until Wrath Passes

"Go, my people, enter your rooms and shut the doors behind you" (26:20). Orthodox typology connects this to the Passover, to Noah's ark, and to the Church as the ark of salvation. The faithful are sheltered within the sacramental community while God's judgment passes through the world — a theme powerfully expressed in the Orthodox baptismal liturgy, where the newly baptized enters the sanctuary for the first time.
