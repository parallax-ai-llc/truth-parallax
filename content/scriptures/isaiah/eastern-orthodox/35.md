Isaiah 35 is a cherished text in Orthodox liturgical life, read during Great Lent and the Paschal season as an image of the transformation Christ accomplishes. The desert blooming (35:1-2) is understood through the theology of theosis (deification): the barren wilderness of fallen human nature is transformed by divine grace into a garden of spiritual fruitfulness. Gregory Palamas taught that this transformation is not metaphorical but ontological -- humanity genuinely participates in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4).

The healing miracles described in verses 5-6 -- blind eyes opened, deaf ears unstopped, the lame leaping -- are read christologically but also ecclesially. In the Orthodox understanding, these healings continue in the sacramental life of the Church, particularly in the Mystery of Holy Unction (anointing of the sick) and in the healing ministries associated with holy relics, wonder-working icons, and the prayers of the saints. The Church is the ongoing location of Isaiah's promised restoration.

The "Way of Holiness" (35:8) is understood in Orthodox ascetic theology as the path of purification (katharsis), illumination (photismos), and deification (theosis) that the Church Fathers describe. Only the redeemed walk this road -- not because God excludes others, but because the Way itself requires a transformation that only grace can accomplish. The "everlasting joy" (35:10) of the chapter's conclusion is the joy of the Paschal celebration: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life."
