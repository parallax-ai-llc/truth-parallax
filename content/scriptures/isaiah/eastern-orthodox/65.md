Isaiah 65's "new heavens and a new earth" (65:17) is foundational to Orthodox eschatology, which teaches not the destruction of creation but its transfiguration. The Fathers consistently distinguished between annihilation and transformation: the fire of the eschaton purifies rather than destroys, just as the burning bush was aflame but not consumed (Exodus 3:2). Maximus the Confessor taught that the "new creation" is the present creation revealed in its true form -- permeated by divine glory and freed from the distortions introduced by the fall.

The description of blessed life in the renewed world (65:20-23) -- longevity, security, fruitful labor -- is understood in Orthodox theology as pointing toward the age of theosis when human existence will be transformed by participation in divine life. The conditions described are not merely improved versions of present existence but qualitatively different: death, futility, and alienation are overcome not by better technology or politics but by the presence of God, who "will be all in all" (1 Corinthians 15:28).

The wolf and lamb feeding together (65:25) echoes Isaiah 11 and envisions the restoration of the original harmony between humanity and creation. Orthodox ecology, informed by the Ecumenical Patriarch Bartholomew's environmental initiatives, draws on this prophetic vision. The "Green Patriarch" has called environmental destruction a sin precisely because it violates the divine intention for creation's harmony. The Orthodox tradition of blessing animals, fields, and waters expresses the conviction that all creation is destined for the peace Isaiah describes.
