Isaiah 53 is the liturgical and theological heart of Great and Holy Friday in the Orthodox Church. The entire Matins service (sung on Holy Thursday evening) and the Royal Hours of Holy Friday are saturated with Isaiah 53's imagery. The Servant who is "despised and rejected" (53:3) and who "bore our infirmities" (53:4) is identified with Christ in His Passion, but Orthodox theology reads this chapter through a distinctive theological framework that emphasizes victory through suffering rather than penal substitution.

The Orthodox understanding of atonement, shaped by the Fathers -- particularly Athanasius, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Maximus the Confessor -- sees the Servant's suffering as the means by which God enters death from within and destroys it. "By his wounds we are healed" (53:5) is understood through the lens of the Incarnation: the Logos assumes human nature in its fullness, including the experience of death, and by uniting it to His divine life, transforms and deifies it. This is Christus Victor soteriology: the cross is Christ's battle against death, sin, and the devil, and the Resurrection is His victory.

The Servant's silence before his accusers (53:7) is a central motif in Orthodox Holy Week hymnography: "He who wraps the heavens in clouds is wrapped in the purple of mockery." The paradox of the Creator submitting to creation's judgment, the Life accepting death, defines the Orthodox theology of kenosis. The "offspring" the Servant sees (53:10) is understood as the Church -- the fruit of Christ's self-offering -- which continues His presence in the world through the Eucharist, where His sacrificed and risen Body is perpetually offered and received.
