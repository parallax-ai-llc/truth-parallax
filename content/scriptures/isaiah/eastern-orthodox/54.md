Orthodox interpretation of Isaiah 54 reads the "barren woman" (54:1) as a figure of the Church, following Paul's exegesis in Galatians 4:27 but extending it through patristic ecclesiology. The Church is "barren" in the sense that she does not produce children through natural generation but through baptism -- a spiritual birth that is no less real than physical birth. Chrysostom's commentary on this passage celebrates the Church's miraculous fruitfulness: from a handful of Galilean fishermen, an assembly spanning every nation and language has grown.

The covenant of peace (54:10) is understood in Orthodox theology as grounded in the Incarnation itself. When God assumes human nature, the breach between Creator and creation is healed at the ontological level. The "unfailing love" that "will not be shaken" is not merely an attitude of God but a new reality established in the hypostatic union. The Orthodox Eucharistic liturgy celebrates this covenant at every celebration: "Take, eat; this is my Body... Drink of it, all of you; this is my Blood of the new covenant."

The Marian dimension is also present in Orthodox reading. The "barren woman" echoes Hannah and Sarah, and ultimately finds its fulfillment in the Theotokos, who conceives without human agency and gives birth to the One through whom all spiritual children are born. The promise "No weapon forged against you will prevail" (54:17) is applied both to the Church and to the Theotokos, who is honored as the "Invincible Champion" (Hypermacho Stratego) in the Akathist hymn, protectress of the faithful against all spiritual enemies.
