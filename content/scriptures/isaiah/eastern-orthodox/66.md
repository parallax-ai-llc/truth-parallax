Isaiah 66 brings the prophetic book to its climax with themes that pervade Orthodox theology. The declaration "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool" (66:1), quoted by Stephen in Acts 7, does not negate sacred space in Orthodox understanding but universalizes it. The Orthodox temple, with its dome representing heaven and its iconography representing the communion of saints, is a microcosm of the universe as God's dwelling -- not a container of the divine but a point of intense meeting between Creator and creation.

The image of Zion giving birth (66:7-9) is applied in Orthodox theology to the Theotokos and to the Church. Mary's painless birth of Christ (as Orthodox tradition teaches) and the Church's birth of new members through baptism both fulfill this prophecy. The maternal comfort of verse 13 -- "As a mother comforts her child, so will I comfort you" -- grounds the Orthodox tradition of maternal divine imagery, expressed most fully in the Theotokos who mediates divine comfort to the faithful. The Paraklesis (Supplication Canon) to the Theotokos is among the most frequently served Orthodox devotions, directly embodying this motherly consolation.

The book's final vision -- universal worship (66:23) alongside irrevocable judgment (66:24) -- encapsulates Orthodox eschatology's teaching on the two destinies. Orthodox theology, following Gregory of Nyssa's universalist tendencies while also affirming the reality of hell as taught by most Fathers, holds these two visions in tension. The prayer "for the peace of the whole world" that opens every liturgy expresses the Church's hope; the sobering reality of verse 24, quoted by Christ Himself (Mark 9:48), expresses the urgency of repentance. The Orthodox Church prays for all and hopes for all while taking with full seriousness the possibility of eternal loss.
