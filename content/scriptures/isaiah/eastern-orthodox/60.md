Isaiah 60's vision of Zion illuminated by divine glory -- "The LORD rises upon you and his glory appears over you" (60:2) -- is read in Orthodox theology through the lens of the Transfiguration. The uncreated light that shone on Mount Tabor and that the hesychast saints experience in prayer is the same glory Isaiah describes. Gregory Palamas taught that this light is not created but is the eternal radiance of God Himself, accessible to human experience through prayer and purification. Every Orthodox church, filled with icons and candlelight, is designed as a space where this divine illumination is symbolically and sacramentally present.

The nations streaming to Zion (60:3-7) with gifts of gold and frankincense is read at the feast of Theophany alongside the narrative of the Magi. Orthodox theology sees in the Magi's journey the first fruits of the Gentile nations' incorporation into God's people -- a process that continues through the Church's missionary expansion. The iconographic tradition of the Nativity icon always includes the Magi's journey, and the gifts they bear correspond to Isaiah's prophecy, connecting Old Testament vision to New Testament fulfillment.

The promise "The LORD will be your everlasting light" (60:19) is the ultimate statement of what Orthodox theology calls "the age to come" -- the eighth day that transcends the weekly cycle and inaugurates eternity. Orthodox churches are often octagonal or include octagonal elements to symbolize this eighth day. The everlasting light that replaces sun and moon is the uncreated light of God's own glory, which the saints already perceive in foretaste and which will be the universal reality of the consummated Kingdom.
