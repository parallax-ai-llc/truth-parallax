The second Servant Song (49:1-7) is read in Orthodox theology with both christological and ecclesiological significance. The Servant called "from the womb" (49:1) is Christ, but Orthodox interpretation also applies this to the Church, which is formed by the Holy Spirit in the "womb" of the Old Covenant and brought forth at Pentecost. The Theotokos herself is the living link between these two readings: she bears the Servant in her womb and is also the type of the Church that bears Christ to the world.

The maternal imagery of verses 14-16 -- God's love surpassing a mother's love -- is deeply integrated into Orthodox devotional life through the theology of the Theotokos. The Akathist hymn to the Mother of God, one of the most beloved Orthodox prayers, celebrates Mary as the one through whom divine maternal love becomes tangibly present. The engraving of names on God's palms (49:16) is read in connection with the Resurrection icon, where Christ's hands, marked with nail wounds, reach out to grasp Adam and Eve and pull them from Hades.

The promise that the Servant will be "a light for the Gentiles, that my salvation may reach to the ends of the earth" (49:6) is fulfilled in Orthodox understanding not merely through verbal proclamation but through the liturgy itself. The Divine Liturgy is the ongoing event of salvation reaching the ends of the earth. Every celebration of the Eucharist is a participation in the Servant's universal mission, and the dismissal -- "Let us go forth in peace" -- sends the faithful into the world as bearers of the light Isaiah describes.
