## The Mountain of Transfiguration

Isaiah 2:2-4 envisions the nations streaming to God's holy mountain. Orthodox theology connects this to the Transfiguration of Christ on Mount Tabor — the revelation of divine glory that draws all creation toward *theosis*. The mountain is not merely a geographical location but a symbol of the ascent toward God. The liturgical celebration of the Transfiguration (August 6) is one of the Great Feasts and embodies the eschatological hope of Isaiah 2.

## Peace as Cosmic Harmony

"They will beat their swords into plowshares" (2:4). Orthodox peace theology is grounded not in political activism alone but in the conviction that true peace is the restoration of cosmic harmony — the original order of creation disrupted by the Fall. The prayers for peace in the Divine Liturgy ("For the peace from above and for the salvation of our souls, let us pray to the Lord") draw on this Isaianic vision.

## The Humbling of Human Pride

The "day of the LORD" against everything proud and exalted (2:12-17) is central to Orthodox spiritual theology. The ascetic tradition — from the Desert Fathers to contemporary monasticism — understands humility as the foundation of all virtue and pride as the root of all sin. Isaiah's vision of the proud being brought low is not merely future prophecy but a description of the spiritual law that operates in every human life.
