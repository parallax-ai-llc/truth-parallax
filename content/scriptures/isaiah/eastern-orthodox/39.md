The Orthodox reading of Isaiah 39 emphasizes Hezekiah's pride as a spiritual lesson about the dangers of vainglory (kenodoxia), one of the eight principal passions identified by Evagrius of Pontus and adopted by John Cassian. The king who trusted God against Sennacherib now displays his treasures to pagan ambassadors (39:2), illustrating the patristic teaching that spiritual victory often precedes spiritual fall. The Desert Fathers warned that the moment after a great spiritual achievement is the moment of greatest vulnerability.

Isaiah's prophecy of exile to Babylon (39:6-7) is read as both historical prediction and spiritual teaching. The loss of the temple treasures to a pagan empire symbolizes what happens when the soul loses its inner treasures -- the virtues cultivated through ascetic practice -- to worldly attachment. Maximus the Confessor taught that the true "Babylonian captivity" is the captivity of the nous (spiritual intellect) to the passions, and liberation comes through repentance and the practice of the virtues.

The transition from chapter 39 to chapter 40 -- from judgment to comfort -- mirrors the Paschal movement that structures Orthodox liturgical life. Great Lent's penitential character gives way to the explosive joy of Pascha, and this passage from darkness to light is the fundamental rhythm of Orthodox Christianity. The entire book of Isaiah, in the Orthodox lectionary, is read during Great Lent precisely because it enacts this movement from exile to restoration, from cross to resurrection.
