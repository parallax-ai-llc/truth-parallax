## Tyre and the Judgment of Commerce

Isaiah 23 pronounces judgment on Tyre, the commercial capital of the ancient world. Orthodox social theology sees this as a warning against the idolatry of wealth. The Fathers — particularly St. John Chrysostom and St. Basil — preached extensively against the accumulation of wealth at the expense of the poor, drawing on prophetic texts like this one.

## The Sanctification of Wealth

The chapter's conclusion — Tyre's profits "set apart for the LORD" (23:18) — points toward the Orthodox understanding that all material goods are ultimately God's and must be used for His purposes. The Orthodox practice of offering (*prosphora*) — bringing bread to be consecrated in the Eucharist — embodies this principle: material goods are sanctified when offered back to God.

## The Seventy-Year Pattern

Tyre's seventy-year period of desolation followed by restoration (23:15-17) illustrates the Orthodox conviction that God's judgment is temporary and purposeful, aimed not at destruction but at purification and renewal. Even commercial civilizations can be restored and their wealth redirected toward divine service.
