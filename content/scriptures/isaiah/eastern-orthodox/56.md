Isaiah 56's inclusion of foreigners and eunuchs challenges the Orthodox Church's own history of ethnic particularism. While the theology is emphatically universal -- the Liturgy prays "for the peace of the whole world" -- the Church's organizational structure along national lines (autocephalous churches) has sometimes led to the heresy of phyletism (the reduction of Church to ethnic identity), condemned at the Council of Constantinople in 1872. Isaiah 56 provides a prophetic corrective: God's house is "for all nations" (56:7).

The eunuch's inclusion (56:3-5) is particularly significant in Orthodox theology because the Ethiopian eunuch of Acts 8, whom Philip baptizes, becomes one of the earliest witnesses to the universality of Christian initiation. Orthodox tradition holds that baptism confers full membership in the Body of Christ regardless of any prior exclusion. The "memorial" and "name better than sons and daughters" (56:5) promised to eunuchs is fulfilled in the monastic tradition, where celibate men and women receive new names at tonsure and become spiritual fathers and mothers of many.

The denunciation of negligent watchmen (56:10-12) has been applied throughout Orthodox history to bishops and priests who neglect their pastoral duties. The canons of the Ecumenical Councils include provisions for the removal of negligent clergy, and the prophetic indictment serves as a perpetual reminder that ordination confers responsibility, not merely status. Saints like John Chrysostom, who was exiled for criticizing the imperial court's luxury while the poor suffered, embodied Isaiah's prophetic standard.
