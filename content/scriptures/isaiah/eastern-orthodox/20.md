## The Prophet's Body as Oracle

Isaiah's three-year sign-act — walking "stripped and barefoot" (20:2) — is read by Orthodox theology through the lens of the Incarnation. The prophet's body becomes the medium of divine communication, anticipating the ultimate sign-act: the Word becoming flesh. Orthodox theology of the body affirms that the material can bear the divine — a principle articulated in the defense of icons against the iconoclasts.

## The Exposure of False Hope

The sign reveals the fate awaiting those who trust in Egypt and Cush: "stripped and barefoot, with buttocks bared" (20:4). Orthodox ascetic theology sees this as a parable of spiritual exposure: false securities will eventually be stripped away, revealing the nakedness of those who refused the clothing of divine grace. Baptismal typology is relevant here: the catechumen strips off old garments before being clothed in the white robe of Christ.

## The Lament of the Coastland

"How then can we escape?" (20:6). Orthodox preaching draws on this cry to emphasize the impossibility of escape from divine judgment through human means. The only true escape is *metanoia* — repentance and turning to the God who alone can save.
