The Orthodox reading of Isaiah 34 emphasizes the theophanic dimensions of divine judgment. The heavens dissolving and rolling up like a scroll (34:4) is understood through the lens of the Transfiguration theology central to Eastern Christianity: creation, which veils the uncreated glory of God, will one day be penetrated and transformed by that glory. The Fathers, particularly Basil the Great and Gregory of Nyssa, understood cosmic upheaval not as destruction but as the painful birth pangs of a transfigured creation.

The judgment on Edom (34:5-17) is read in the patristic tradition as a type of the Last Judgment, which Orthodox theology presents not as an external punishment imposed by God but as the experience of God's uncreated light by those unprepared to receive it. Isaac the Syrian taught that the "fire" of judgment is the fire of God's love itself, which purifies the righteous and torments those who have closed themselves to it. The burning pitch and sulfur (34:9) thus describe the subjective experience of encountering divine love in a state of spiritual resistance.

The "scroll of the LORD" (34:16) that records every decree is connected in Orthodox theology to the Logos -- the divine Word through whom all things were made. Nothing in creation exists outside the Logos's creative intention, and the fulfillment of every prophetic word demonstrates the coherence of God's economy (oikonomia) -- the unified plan of salvation that runs from creation through incarnation to the eschaton.
