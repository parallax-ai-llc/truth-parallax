Hezekiah's canticle (38:9-20) holds a liturgical place in Orthodox worship as one of the nine biblical odes that form the basis of the canon -- the complex hymn structure sung at Matins. The sixth ode of the canon is drawn from this passage, and its themes of descent into death and divine rescue shape Orthodox prayer throughout the liturgical year. The cry "I shall not see the LORD in the land of the living" (38:11) expresses the pre-Paschal understanding of death that Christ's Resurrection definitively transforms.

Orthodox theology reads Hezekiah's healing in connection with the Mystery of Holy Unction. The application of figs (38:21) is understood typologically: God uses material means as vehicles of healing grace, a principle that grounds the Orthodox sacramental use of oil, water, bread, and wine. The material world is not bypassed in salvation but is itself sanctified and becomes a channel of divine energy (energeia). This conviction distinguishes Orthodox theology from any form of spiritual dualism.

The fifteen additional years (38:5) prompt reflection on God's will and human prayer. The Fathers taught that prayer does not change God's eternal counsel but that God's counsel eternally includes human prayer as a genuine factor. John of Damascus explained that God's providence is not a fixed script that ignores human freedom but a living dialogue in which divine initiative and human response are interwoven. Hezekiah's prayer and God's response exemplify this synergy (synergia) that is central to Orthodox soteriology.
