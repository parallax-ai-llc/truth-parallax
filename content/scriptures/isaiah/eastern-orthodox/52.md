The proclamation "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news" (52:7) is celebrated in Orthodox tradition through the theology of evangelization that characterizes the Church's missionary history. The equal-to-the-apostles missionaries -- Cyril and Methodius, Nina of Georgia, Innocent of Alaska -- are honored for carrying the "beautiful feet" of the gospel to new peoples. The Orthodox liturgy commemorates these missionaries with a rank equal to the apostles, recognizing that their work continues Isaiah's prophetic vision.

The call "Depart! Go out from there!" (52:11) is understood in Orthodox monastic tradition as the foundational call of the ascetic life. Anthony the Great's departure to the desert, Basil the Great's establishment of cenobitic communities, and the entire tradition of Orthodox monasticism from Sinai to Athos to Valaam enacts this prophetic command. The monastic departure is not world-denial but world-transfiguration: by withdrawing to pray, the monk creates a space where heaven touches earth.

The Servant Song's opening (52:13-15) is read in Orthodox theology through the lens of the paradox of glory-through-suffering. The Servant who is "raised and lifted up" (52:13) while simultaneously "disfigured beyond recognition" (52:14) embodies the paschal mystery: Christ is most glorified at the moment of His greatest abasement. The Orthodox cross always depicts the corpus (Christ's body) -- not as a symbol of defeat but of triumphant love. The "sprinkling of many nations" (52:15) is connected to the baptismal rite, where the newly illumined are sprinkled with holy water as they enter the eucharistic community.
