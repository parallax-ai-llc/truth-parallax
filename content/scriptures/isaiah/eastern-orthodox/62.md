Isaiah 62's imagery of Zion receiving a new name (62:2) is connected in Orthodox theology to the practice of renaming at monastic tonsure and, more broadly, to the theology of transformation that baptism and chrismation accomplish. The old name -- associated with the old life -- gives way to a new name that expresses the new identity in Christ. Orthodox monastics receive their names from the abbot, often learning them only at the tonsure ceremony, signifying that their new identity comes from God through the community, not from their own choosing.

The bridal imagery -- "As a bridegroom rejoices over his bride, so will your God rejoice over you" (62:5) -- is central to Orthodox nuptial theology. The Orthodox wedding ceremony, or "crowning," presents marriage as a participation in the Kingdom of God, a foretaste of the eschatological wedding feast of the Lamb (Revelation 19:7-9). The crowns placed on the couple's heads are crowns of both glory and martyrdom, signifying that marital love, like Christ's love for the Church, involves self-sacrifice.

The watchmen who pray without ceasing (62:6) are identified with the monastic communities, especially the monasteries of Mount Athos, where the unceasing prayer of the Psalter and the Jesus Prayer has been maintained for over a millennium. The Orthodox conviction that ceaseless prayer sustains the world is rooted in passages like this one. The watchmen "give the LORD no rest" (62:7) -- a remarkable image of human persistence that the monastic tradition takes literally, organizing liturgical prayer to cover every hour of the day and night.
