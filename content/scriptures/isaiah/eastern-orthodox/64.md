Isaiah 64's prayer "Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down" (64:1) is read in Orthodox theology as fulfilled in the Incarnation and, definitively, at the Theophany (Baptism of Christ), when the heavens are "torn open" (Mark 1:10) and the Spirit descends. The Orthodox feast of Theophany is one of the most important in the liturgical calendar, second only to Pascha, precisely because it celebrates this tearing open of the boundary between heaven and earth. The Great Blessing of Water enacted at this feast is the liturgical realization of Isaiah's prayer: the heavens are opened, and divine grace sanctifies the material world.

The confession "All our righteous acts are like filthy rags" (64:6) is read in Orthodox theology with characteristic nuance. While affirming that unaided human effort cannot achieve salvation, the Orthodox tradition -- following the Fathers against Pelagians and semi-Pelagians -- also insists on the reality of synergy (synergia): divine grace and human effort cooperate in the process of theosis. The "filthy rags" describe the condition of those separated from God by sin, not the inherent worthlessness of human goodness. Grace does not replace human effort but transforms and elevates it.

The potter-clay image (64:8) is central to Orthodox anthropology. Human beings are indeed clay in God's hands, but the Incarnation reveals that this clay is destined for divine glory. The Logos took clay -- human nature -- and united it to divinity, demonstrating that the potter's intention was always theosis. The prayer "We are all the work of your hand" (64:8) is an act of trust that the potter's design, however mysterious, is oriented toward the creature's ultimate good.
