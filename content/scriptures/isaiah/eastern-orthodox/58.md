Isaiah 58 is one of the most important texts in the Orthodox Great Lenten tradition. It is read during the first week of Great Lent, establishing the proper understanding of fasting that governs the entire season. Orthodox fasting -- which includes abstaining from meat, dairy, oil, and wine -- is extensive, but Isaiah 58 insists that external discipline without interior transformation and social justice is meaningless. The Fathers consistently taught that fasting from food must be accompanied by fasting from sin.

The works of mercy enumerated in verse 7 -- feeding the hungry, sheltering the homeless, clothing the naked -- correspond to the Orthodox emphasis on almsgiving as an essential companion to fasting and prayer. The Lenten triad of prayer, fasting, and almsgiving derives from Isaiah's insistence that true worship encompasses both vertical (God-directed) and horizontal (neighbor-directed) dimensions. Chrysostom's homilies on almsgiving draw extensively on this chapter, arguing that the person who fasts but ignores the poor is worse than the person who does neither.

The promise "Then your light will break forth like the dawn" (58:8) is interpreted through the Orthodox theology of uncreated light. The light that breaks forth from a life of prayer, fasting, and mercy is not metaphorical but a participation in the divine light that shone on Mount Tabor. Seraphim of Sarov, who appeared transfigured by light during his conversation with Motovilov, demonstrated that Isaiah's promise is experientially verifiable: the holy life genuinely radiates divine glory. The connection between ethical conduct and spiritual illumination is not incidental but essential -- a principle that defines Orthodox spirituality.
