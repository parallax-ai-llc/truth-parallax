The first Servant Song (42:1-9) is read in the Orthodox Church at the Vesperal Liturgy of Holy Saturday, connecting the Servant's mission to Christ's descent into Hades. "Here is my servant, whom I uphold" (42:1) is understood christologically: the Father upholds the Son even in the depths of death. The Servant who "will not falter or be discouraged" (42:4) is Christ who descends into death and emerges victorious, pulling Adam and Eve from the grave -- the scene depicted in the quintessential Orthodox icon of the Resurrection (the Anastasis).

The promise that the Servant will be "a covenant for the people and a light for the Gentiles" (42:6) is fulfilled in the Orthodox understanding of the Church as the Body of Christ that radiates divine light to all nations. The theology of uncreated light, developed by Gregory Palamas, teaches that the light the Servant brings is not created illumination but the very energy of God -- the same light that shone on Mount Tabor and that illumines the hearts of the saints. Every Orthodox church is oriented eastward toward the rising sun as a liturgical expression of this orientation toward the divine light.

The "bruised reed" and "smoldering wick" (42:3) that the Servant will not break or extinguish are understood in Orthodox pastoral theology as images of the divine compassion (philanthropia) that characterizes Christ's approach to sinners. The principle of oikonomia -- pastoral flexibility in the application of canonical strictness -- reflects this Servant-like gentleness. Orthodox canon law distinguishes between akribeia (strict application) and oikonomia (merciful adaptation), and the Servant Song grounds the latter in the very character of Christ.
