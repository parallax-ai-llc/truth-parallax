Isaiah 46's contrast between idols that must be carried (46:1-2) and the God who carries His people (46:3-4) resonates with the Orthodox understanding of the Theotokos (Mother of God). The icon of the Theotokos holding the Christ child -- the Hodegetria ("She who shows the Way") -- illustrates this paradox: Mary carries the One who carries all creation. Orthodox hymnography celebrates this: "He whom the whole world cannot contain was contained in your womb, O Theotokos." The idol that must be borne on beasts of burden contrasts utterly with the God who, in the Incarnation, allows Himself to be borne by a human mother.

The declaration "My purpose will stand, and I will do all that I please" (46:10) is understood in Orthodox theology through the concept of God's providential economy. The Fathers distinguished between God's "antecedent will" (that all be saved) and God's "consequent will" (which takes human freedom into account). God's purpose unfailingly stands, but it includes rather than overrides human choice. John of Damascus articulated this most carefully: God wills all things that are good, permits all things that happen, but causes only what is good.

The "bird of prey from the east" (46:11) -- Cyrus -- illustrates what the Orthodox tradition calls the "pedagogy of God": the divine method of teaching humanity through historical events, using unexpected instruments to accomplish unexpected purposes. This pedagogy includes all of salvation history and culminates in the most unexpected act of all -- the Incarnation and the Cross, where divine power is revealed in what appears to be utter weakness.
