The designation of Cyrus as "anointed" (45:1) is interpreted in Orthodox Christology as a type that finds its antitype in Christ, the ultimate Anointed One. Orthodox theology emphasizes that Christ's anointing is not external (like Cyrus's) but ontological: the divine and human natures are united in the one Person of the Logos, and this hypostatic union is the source of all other anointings. The Mystery of Chrismation, where the newly baptized is anointed with holy myron, communicates a participation in Christ's own anointing.

The declaration "I form the light and create darkness" (45:7) is read through the Palamite distinction between God's essence (unknowable) and God's energies (experienceable). The "light" God creates is created light -- sun, stars, physical illumination. But the uncreated light of God's own glory is not created but eternally radiates from the divine essence. Gregory Palamas taught that this distinction preserves both divine transcendence and the reality of human experience of God. The darkness is not evil but the absence of created light; evil has no ontological existence but is a privation.

The universal call "Turn to me and be saved, all you ends of the earth" (45:22) grounds the Orthodox Church's missionary consciousness. While the Orthodox tradition has sometimes been characterized as ethnically insular, its theological self-understanding is emphatically universal. The great missionary saints -- Cyril and Methodius among the Slavs, Nicholas of Japan, Innocent of Alaska -- carried this Isaianic vision to diverse peoples. The Orthodox liturgy is celebrated in hundreds of languages, embodying the prophetic promise that all nations will acknowledge the one God.
