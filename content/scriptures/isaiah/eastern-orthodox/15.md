## Compassion in Judgment

Isaiah 15 is distinguished by the prophet's genuine grief over Moab's destruction. "My heart cries out over Moab" (15:5). Orthodox theology of compassion (*sympatheia*) sees in Isaiah's tears a reflection of God's own grief. The Fathers teach that God does not delight in the destruction of the wicked (Ezekiel 33:11) but grieves over the suffering that sin makes necessary.

## The Devastation of Places

The detailed catalog of Moabite cities in ruins grounds the prophecy in concrete human suffering. Orthodox theology insists that judgment is never abstract — it falls on real people in real places, with real consequences. The Orthodox prayer tradition includes petitions for all the suffering, regardless of faith or nationality, reflecting the universal compassion that Isaiah models.

## Flight and Exile

The image of Moabite refugees fleeing with their possessions (15:7) resonates with the Orthodox Church's own historical experience of exile and displacement — from the fall of Constantinople to the sufferings of Christians in the Middle East. Orthodox pastoral practice emphasizes hospitality to strangers and refugees as a fundamental expression of Christian love.
