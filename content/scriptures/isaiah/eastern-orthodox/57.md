The opening of Isaiah 57 -- the righteous perishing without anyone caring (57:1-2) -- is read in Orthodox theology in connection with the veneration of martyrs and saints. The Church teaches that the death of the righteous, far from being a tragedy, is a "precious death" (Psalm 116:15) -- a passage into the fuller life of God's presence. The Orthodox funeral service proclaims, "Blessed is the way in which you walk today, for a place of rest has been prepared for you." The righteous are "taken away to be spared from evil" (57:1), and their entry into eternal life is a cause for both sorrow and hope.

The condemnation of idolatrous practices in verses 3-10 is read in the Orthodox tradition through the ascetic theology of the passions. While literal child sacrifice and fertility worship may be historical phenomena, the Fathers understood them as extreme expressions of the universal human tendency to worship created things rather than the Creator. The eight principal passions identified by the Desert Fathers -- gluttony, lust, avarice, anger, sadness, acedia, vainglory, and pride -- are all forms of idolatry in which a created good is elevated to ultimate status.

The paradox of verse 15 -- God dwelling "in a high and holy place, but also with the contrite and lowly in spirit" -- is central to Orthodox mystical theology. Gregory Palamas's distinction between God's essence (forever transcendent) and God's energies (genuinely present in creation) provides the theological framework: God is simultaneously beyond all creation and intimately present to the humble heart. The hesychast tradition of interior prayer -- the Jesus Prayer repeated in the stillness of the heart -- is the practical appropriation of this paradox. The monk who prays "Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me" enters the space where the Most High dwells with the most lowly.
