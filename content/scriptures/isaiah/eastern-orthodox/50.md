The third Servant Song (50:4-9) is read in the Orthodox Church during Holy Week, specifically on Holy Monday and Tuesday, connecting the Servant's suffering to Christ's Passion. "I offered my back to those who beat me" (50:6) is understood not merely as prediction but as revelation of the divine kenosis -- God's self-emptying love that accepts suffering rather than impose it. Cyril of Alexandria's commentary on this passage emphasizes that the Logos Himself suffers in His human nature, not through weakness but through the free exercise of divine love.

The Servant's morning-by-morning listening (50:4) is connected to the Orthodox practice of morning prayer (Orthros/Matins), which begins each day with attentive listening to God's word. The entire structure of the Horologion (the book of the hours) reflects this rhythm: the day begins and ends with prayer, punctuated by hours of prayer throughout. The Servant's "well-instructed tongue" (50:4) is the fruit of this disciplined listening, and Orthodox homiletical tradition insists that preaching must flow from prayer rather than mere study.

The confidence "He who vindicates me is near" (50:8) is echoed in the Paschal proclamation. The Orthodox Paschal liturgy is an extended celebration of vindication -- Christ's vindication through Resurrection, and through Him, the vindication of all who suffer unjustly. The closing warning against those who "walk by the light of your own fires" (50:11) is applied by the Fathers to all forms of self-will and spiritual pride that substitute human reasoning for the illumination of the Holy Spirit -- the fundamental temptation of heresy.
