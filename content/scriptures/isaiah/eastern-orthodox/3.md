## The Withdrawal of Divine Grace

Isaiah 3 describes God removing competent leadership from Jerusalem. Orthodox theology reads this through the lens of divine *energies*: God does not punish arbitrarily but withdraws the sustaining grace that holds society together. When a community persistently rejects God's ways, it experiences the natural consequences of that rejection — not because God actively destroys but because He respects human freedom and allows the results of sin to unfold.

## Social Collapse as Spiritual Symptom

The social chaos described in 3:4-5 — youths ruling, inferiors oppressing superiors — is understood in Orthodox theology as the external symptom of internal spiritual disorder. When the soul is disordered, society reflects that disorder. The patristic principle that the *microcosm* (individual human being) and *macrocosm* (society and creation) mirror each other is fundamental to Orthodox social theology.

## The Stripping of Ornaments

The critique of Jerusalem's women (3:16-26) is read typologically by the Fathers as a description of the soul that has adorned itself with worldly attachments rather than virtues. St. John Chrysostom applied this passage to the wealthy of Constantinople, arguing that excessive luxury is not merely bad taste but spiritual blindness — the substitution of external adornment for the inner beauty that comes from communion with God.
