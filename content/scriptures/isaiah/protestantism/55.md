Isaiah 55 is one of the great evangelical chapters of the Old Testament, containing the free offer of the gospel that Protestant theology holds dear. "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat" (55:1) is the paradigmatic text for the Protestant doctrine of free grace — salvation cannot be purchased by human effort but is offered freely by God.

The "everlasting covenant" of verse 3, identified with "the sure mercies of David" (KJV), is interpreted christologically: the Davidic promises find their fulfillment in Christ, who establishes an eternal kingdom. Paul quotes this verse in Acts 13:34 in connection with Christ's resurrection, linking the covenant promises to the defeat of death. Protestant covenant theology builds extensively on this connection between Davidic promise and New Covenant fulfillment.

Verses 8-9 — "My thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways" — are among the most quoted in Protestant theology, used to affirm divine transcendence and the limits of human understanding. Yet the context is not abstract philosophy but the surprising generosity of God's pardon (55:7). The chapter closes with the assurance that God's word "will not return to me empty" (55:11), a text that grounds Protestant confidence in the power of preaching and Scripture.
