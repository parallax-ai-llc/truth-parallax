## The Burden of Moab

Isaiah 15 is a lament over the destruction of Moab, Israel's neighbor and frequent adversary east of the Dead Sea. What distinguishes this oracle from others in the collection is its tone of genuine grief. "My heart cries out over Moab" (15:5). Protestant interpreters note that Isaiah does not gloat over Moab's suffering but mourns it, revealing a prophetic compassion that transcends national boundaries.

## Cities of Devastation

The oracle catalogs the destruction of Moabite cities — Ar, Kir, Dibon, Nebo, Medeba — with vivid imagery of weeping, baldness, and sackcloth. Protestant commentators observe that the specificity of these place-names grounds the prophecy in concrete historical reality rather than abstract theology. The judgment of nations is not a theoretical principle but something that falls on real cities, real people, and real suffering.

## Flight and Desolation

The chapter depicts refugees fleeing southward, carrying their possessions across the Brook of the Willows (15:7). "The waters of Nimrim are dried up and the grass is withered" (15:6). Protestant exegesis sees in Moab's desolation a warning to all nations that pride and self-sufficiency — Moab's characteristic sins according to Isaiah 16:6 — lead inevitably to ruin. Even nations outside the covenant are subject to God's moral governance of the world.
