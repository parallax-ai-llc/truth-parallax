## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Isaiah is shaped by the Reformation conviction that Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) is the ultimate authority for faith and practice. Isaiah holds a privileged place in Protestant theology because its messianic prophecies — particularly the Suffering Servant passages — are understood as direct predictions fulfilled in Jesus Christ. The book has been called "the Fifth Gospel" within Protestant tradition for its extensive Christological content.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Christological-Prophetic
The dominant Protestant reading treats Isaiah's oracles as forward-looking prophecy fulfilled in the New Testament. The virgin birth (7:14), the child born as "Wonderful Counselor" (9:6), the shoot from Jesse's stump (11:1), and above all the Suffering Servant (52:13–53:12) are read as direct messianic predictions.

### Historical-Grammatical
Protestant scholars emphasize understanding Isaiah in its original historical context — the Assyrian crisis of Ahaz's and Hezekiah's reigns — while maintaining that the text's fuller meaning points beyond its immediate setting to Christ.

### Redemptive-Historical
Reformed interpreters situate Isaiah within the overarching covenant narrative of Scripture, tracing how the themes of judgment, exile, and restoration fit within God's unfolding plan of redemption from creation to consummation.

## Theological Emphases

- **Penal Substitutionary Atonement**: Isaiah 53 is the Old Testament foundation for the doctrine that Christ bore the penalty for human sin
- **Justification by Faith**: Isaiah's call to trust God rather than human alliances (7:9; 28:16) prefigures the Reformation doctrine of *sola fide*
- **The Sovereignty of God**: God's control over nations (chs. 13–23) and history demonstrates absolute divine sovereignty
- **The Remnant**: The faithful minority in Isaiah foreshadows the true church within visible Christendom
