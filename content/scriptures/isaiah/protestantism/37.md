Isaiah 37 records Hezekiah's prayer and God's dramatic deliverance of Jerusalem from Sennacherib's army. Protestant theology treats this chapter as a paradigmatic example of answered prayer. Hezekiah's act of spreading Sennacherib's threatening letter before the LORD in the temple (37:14) is frequently cited in devotional literature as a model for bringing impossible situations to God.

God's response through Isaiah (37:21-35) contains one of the most striking declarations of divine sovereignty in the prophets: "I have planned it long ago" (37:26). Reformed theologians find in this verse strong support for divine foreordination — Assyria's conquests were not outside God's plan but were themselves instruments of His purpose. Yet Assyria is still held morally accountable for its arrogance (37:28-29).

The angel of the LORD striking 185,000 Assyrian soldiers (37:36) and Sennacherib's subsequent assassination (37:38) demonstrate what Protestant interpreters call the "already/not yet" pattern of divine judgment: God acts decisively within history as a foretaste of final eschatological judgment.
