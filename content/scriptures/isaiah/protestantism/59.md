Isaiah 59 presents a devastating analysis of sin's consequences: "Your iniquities have separated you from your God" (59:2). Protestant hamartiology (doctrine of sin) draws heavily on this chapter's comprehensive catalog of human fallenness. Paul quotes verses 7-8 in Romans 3:15-17 as part of his indictment of universal human sinfulness, demonstrating that Isaiah's diagnosis applies not just to ancient Israel but to all humanity.

The confession in verses 9-15 — "we look for light, but all is darkness" — is notable because it shifts from third person to first person plural. Protestant interpreters value this corporate confession as a model: the prophet identifies with the sin of his people rather than standing apart in judgment. This pattern is echoed in Daniel 9 and is recommended in Protestant pastoral theology as the appropriate posture for spiritual leaders.

The divine warrior imagery in verses 16-20 — God putting on righteousness as a breastplate and salvation as a helmet — is directly echoed in Paul's "armor of God" passage (Ephesians 6:14-17). Protestant interpreters see God Himself acting because no human intercessor could be found (59:16). This becomes a christological text: Christ is the one who intervenes when all human resources fail. The Redeemer coming to Zion (59:20), quoted in Romans 11:26, grounds Protestant hope for Israel's future salvation.
