Isaiah 50 presents the third Servant Song (50:4-9), depicting a figure who faces suffering with resolute trust in God. "I offered my back to those who beat me, my cheeks to those who pulled out my beard" (50:6) is read by Protestant interpreters as a direct prophecy of Christ's passion, fulfilled in the mockery and abuse described in the Gospel accounts (Matthew 26:67, 27:30).

The Servant's declaration "The Sovereign LORD has given me a well-instructed tongue" (50:4) establishes the Servant as both learner and teacher — one who listens to God each morning and speaks words that sustain the weary. Protestant homiletical theology draws on this as a model for preaching: the minister must first be taught by God before teaching others.

The Servant's defiant confidence — "He who vindicates me is near; who then will bring charges against me?" (50:8) — is echoed in Paul's triumphant declaration in Romans 8:33-34. Protestant interpreters see this as evidence that Paul read Isaiah 50 christologically and understood justification through the lens of the Servant's vindication. The closing warning in verse 11, against those who "walk by the light of your own fires," cautions against self-generated righteousness — a theme central to Reformation soteriology.
