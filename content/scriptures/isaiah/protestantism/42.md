Isaiah 42 contains the first of the four Servant Songs (42:1-9), which are central to Protestant Christology. "Here is my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen one in whom I delight" (42:1) is quoted in Matthew 12:18-21 as fulfilled in Jesus. Protestant interpreters universally identify the Servant in this passage as Christ, in contrast to Jewish readings that identify the servant as Israel collectively.

The Servant's manner — "He will not shout or cry out, or raise his voice in the streets" (42:2) — is read as a prophecy of Christ's gentle ministry. "A bruised reed he will not break" (42:3) has become a beloved Protestant image of Christ's compassion toward the weak and struggling. Calvin saw here a rebuke to all who exercise spiritual authority through coercion rather than gentleness.

The chapter shifts dramatically at verse 10 to a "new song" of praise and then at verse 18 to a rebuke of blind and deaf Israel. Protestant interpreters see this juxtaposition as intentional: the Servant's faithfulness (42:1-9) stands in sharp contrast to Israel's failure (42:18-25). This contrast sets the theological framework for understanding why a faithful Servant must come to accomplish what unfaithful Israel could not.
