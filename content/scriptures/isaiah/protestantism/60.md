Isaiah 60 envisions Zion transformed by divine glory: "Arise, shine, for your light has come, and the glory of the LORD rises upon you" (60:1). Protestant interpreters read this as describing the Church in the messianic age, illuminated by Christ who is "the light of the world" (John 8:12). The nations streaming to Zion's light (60:3) is connected to the Magi's journey in Matthew 2 and to the Great Commission's fulfillment.

The wealth of nations flowing to Zion — gold, frankincense, flocks (60:5-7) — is interpreted differently across Protestant traditions. Postmillennialists see this as a literal transformation of human civilization under the gospel's influence. Premillennialists locate its fulfillment in the millennial kingdom. Amillennialists read it as symbolic of the spiritual riches the nations bring to the Church. All agree that the passage envisions a reversal of the plunder and poverty that characterized exile.

The closing vision — "The sun will no more be your light by day... the LORD will be your everlasting light" (60:19-20) — is directly echoed in Revelation 21:23-25. Protestant eschatology sees in Isaiah 60 a preview of the new creation where God's unmediated presence replaces all created sources of light. The promise "your days of sorrow will end" (60:20) grounds the Christian hope of a world where death and suffering are no more.
