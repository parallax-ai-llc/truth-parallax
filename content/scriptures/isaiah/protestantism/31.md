## Woe to Those Who Go Down to Egypt

Isaiah 31 returns to the central theme of chapters 28-33: Judah's fatal attraction to Egyptian military power. "Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help, who rely on horses, who trust in the multitude of their chariots" (31:1). Protestant interpreters note the theological contrast stated with maximal clarity: "But the Egyptians are mere mortals and not God; their horses are flesh and not spirit" (31:3). The passage articulates one of the sharpest distinctions in biblical theology — Creator versus creature, spirit versus flesh — and insists that confusing them is the essence of idolatry.

## The Lion That Will Not Be Frightened

God's protection of Jerusalem is compared to a lion that cannot be driven from its prey by a band of shepherds (31:4). Protestant commentators note the unusual ferocity of the image: God protects Zion not like a gentle shepherd but like a lion defending its kill. "So the LORD Almighty will come down to do battle on Mount Zion and on its heights." Divine protection is not passive but aggressively active.

## The Return and the Burning of Idols

"Return, you Israelites, to the One you have so greatly revolted against. For in that day every one of you will reject the idols of silver and gold your sinful hands have made" (31:6-7). Protestant theology sees repentance and the renunciation of idols as inseparable. True turning to God necessarily involves turning away from false substitutes. The chapter closes with Assyria's fall — "Assyria will fall by no human sword" (31:8) — confirming that salvation is entirely God's work, not the result of military strategy or political alliance.
