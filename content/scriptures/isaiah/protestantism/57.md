Isaiah 57 contrasts the fate of the righteous, who find peace in death (57:1-2), with the idolatrous practices of those who sacrifice children and engage in fertility cults (57:3-10). Protestant interpreters read the opening verses as a correction to the assumption that premature death indicates divine disfavor — sometimes God takes the righteous away from evil to come. This has been a source of pastoral comfort in Protestant bereavement ministry.

The indictment of idolatry in verses 3-10 uses sexually charged language to describe Israel's unfaithfulness, continuing the prophetic tradition of portraying covenant-breaking as spiritual adultery. Protestant interpreters apply this broadly to any attachment that displaces God from the center of devotion, extending the critique beyond literal idol worship to materialism, power-seeking, and cultural accommodation.

The chapter concludes with one of Isaiah's most beautiful statements of divine condescension: "I live in a high and holy place, but also with the one who is contrite and lowly in spirit" (57:15). Protestant spirituality draws deeply on this paradox — the transcendent God dwelling with the humble. This verse grounds the Protestant emphasis on contrition and humility as prerequisites for experiencing God's presence, while the closing refrain "There is no peace for the wicked" (57:21) reiterates that peace is reserved for the repentant.
