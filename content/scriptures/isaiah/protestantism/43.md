Isaiah 43 opens with one of the most powerful declarations of divine redemption in the prophets: "Fear not, for I have redeemed you; I have called you by name, you are mine" (43:1). Protestant theology reads this as a paradigmatic statement of election and covenant relationship. The promise of passing through water and fire unharmed (43:2) is connected typologically to baptism and trial.

The "new thing" God declares in verse 19 — "I am doing a new thing; now it springs up, do you not perceive it?" — is a key text for Protestant eschatology and missiology. It establishes that God is not bound to repeat past patterns of salvation but works creatively in history. Many Protestant interpreters apply this to the New Covenant inaugurated by Christ, which surpasses even the exodus.

Verses 22-28 contain a startling accusation: Israel has not called upon God but has burdened Him with sins. Yet verse 25 declares, "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake." Protestant Reformation theology found in this verse a proof text for grace apart from works — God forgives not because of Israel's merit but for the sake of His own name and character. Luther cited this passage as evidence that justification is entirely God's initiative.
