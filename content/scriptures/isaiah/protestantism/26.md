## A Song of Trust

Isaiah 26 is a psalm of confidence sung by the redeemed community in "that day." "We have a strong city; God makes salvation its walls and ramparts" (26:1). Protestant interpreters contrast this city with the "city of chaos" destroyed in chapter 24. The true city's walls are not made of stone but of salvation itself — divine protection that no enemy can breach. The passage anticipates the New Jerusalem of Revelation 21, whose walls are the glory of God.

## Perfect Peace

"You will keep in perfect peace those whose minds are steadfast, because they trust in you" (26:3). This verse has become one of the most cherished texts in Protestant devotional life. The Hebrew *shalom shalom* (peace peace) conveys completeness and wholeness. Protestant interpreters see here the subjective experience of justification — the peace that comes from trusting God rather than circumstances. The verse grounds inner tranquility in theological reality: peace is not a psychological technique but a consequence of faith.

## Resurrection Hope

"Your dead will live, LORD; their bodies will rise — let those who dwell in the dust wake up and shout for joy" (26:19). Protestant interpreters debate whether this refers to the literal resurrection of individuals or to the national restoration of Israel. Most evangelical commentators see at minimum a seed of bodily resurrection hope that develops through Daniel 12:2 into the full New Testament doctrine. The verse stands as one of the earliest clear articulations of life beyond death in the Hebrew Bible.

## Waiting Behind Closed Doors

The chapter concludes with counsel to hide until God's wrath passes: "Go, my people, enter your rooms and shut the doors behind you; hide yourselves for a little while until his wrath has passed by" (26:20). Protestant commentators connect this to the Passover imagery of Exodus 12 — God's people sheltered while judgment falls on the world — and to the Christian hope of divine protection through the final tribulation.
