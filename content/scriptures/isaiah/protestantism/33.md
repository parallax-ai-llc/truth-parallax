## The Destroyer Will Be Destroyed

Isaiah 33 opens with a woe against an unnamed destroyer: "Woe to you, destroyer, you who have not been destroyed! Woe to you, betrayer, you who have not been betrayed!" (33:1). Protestant interpreters generally identify this as Assyria, but the anonymity gives the oracle universal application: every oppressor will eventually face the judgment they have inflicted on others. The principle of retributive justice — "When you stop destroying, you will be destroyed" — is stated with epigrammatic force.

## The Prayer for Grace

"LORD, be gracious to us; we long for you. Be our strength every morning, our salvation in time of distress" (33:2). Protestant commentators note the shift from prophetic oracle to communal prayer, modeling the appropriate response to crisis. The prayer does not ask for military victory or political advantage but for grace and divine presence. This is the prayer of faith that Isaiah has been calling for throughout the book — trusting God rather than human resources.

## Who Can Dwell with Consuming Fire?

"The sinners in Zion are terrified; trembling grips the godless: 'Who of us can dwell with the consuming fire? Who of us can dwell with everlasting burning?'" (33:14). The answer describes the righteous person in terms reminiscent of Psalms 15 and 24: one who walks righteously, speaks honestly, rejects gain from extortion, and refuses bribes (33:15). Protestant interpreters see this as confirming that God's holiness demands moral transformation, not merely correct doctrine.

## The King in His Beauty

The chapter — and the woe oracle cycle (chapters 28-33) — concludes with a glorious vision: "Your eyes will see the king in his beauty and view a land that stretches afar" (33:17). Zion is depicted as a place of safety, abundance, and divine forgiveness: "No one living in Zion will say, 'I am ill'; and the sins of those who dwell there will be forgiven" (33:24). Protestant theology sees here the consummation of Isaiah's double theme: the holiness that terrifies sinners and the grace that heals and forgives them. Both meet in the person of Christ, the king in His beauty.
