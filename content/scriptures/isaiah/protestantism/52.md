Isaiah 52 opens with a call for Zion to awaken and put on strength, then transitions into the beginning of the fourth Servant Song (52:13-15). The declaration "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news" (52:7) is quoted by Paul in Romans 10:15 as the basis for Christian proclamation. Protestant missiology considers this verse foundational: the gospel must be carried by messengers.

The command "Depart, depart, go out from there! Touch no unclean thing!" (52:11) is applied in 2 Corinthians 6:17 to the call for spiritual separation. Protestant ecclesiology, particularly in the Free Church tradition, has drawn on this text to justify separation from what is perceived as corrupt religious establishment. The Puritans, Separatists, and Anabaptists all found warrant here for their distinctive church polity.

The Servant Song's opening (52:13-15) introduces the theme of exaltation through suffering: the Servant will be "raised and lifted up and highly exalted" (52:13), yet his appearance was "disfigured beyond that of any human being" (52:14). Protestant Christology sees here the paradox of the cross: Christ's deepest humiliation is simultaneously his greatest glory. The "sprinkling of many nations" (52:15) is debated — does it mean ritual purification or startled astonishment? — but either reading points to the Servant's universal impact.
