Isaiah 39 records Hezekiah's reception of Babylonian envoys and Isaiah's prophecy of the coming exile — a pivotal transition in the book. Protestant interpreters note the irony that Hezekiah, who trusted God against Assyria, now proudly displays his treasures to representatives of the very power that will eventually destroy Jerusalem (39:2).

Isaiah's oracle in verses 5-7 — that everything in the palace will be carried to Babylon and Hezekiah's descendants will serve as eunuchs — is treated as a watershed moment. This prophecy provides the historical bridge between "First Isaiah" (chapters 1-39) and "Second Isaiah" (chapters 40-66). Conservative Protestant scholars who maintain single authorship see this as predictive prophecy; those who accept the documentary hypothesis view it as a literary transition composed with knowledge of the exile.

Hezekiah's response — "The word of the LORD you have spoken is good" (39:8), because peace would last his lifetime — has troubled Protestant commentators. Some, like Matthew Henry, see genuine submission to God's will; others detect selfish complacency. Either way, the chapter serves as a sober warning about the dangers of pride and shortsightedness even among the faithful.
