Isaiah 56 opens the final section of Isaiah with a remarkable promise of inclusion for those previously excluded from the covenant community. Foreigners and eunuchs — categories explicitly barred in Deuteronomy 23:1-8 — are welcomed into God's house (56:3-7). Protestant interpreters see this as anticipating the New Covenant's radical inclusivity, fulfilled in passages like Acts 8 (the Ethiopian eunuch) and Ephesians 2 (Gentile inclusion).

The declaration that God's house "will be called a house of prayer for all nations" (56:7) is quoted by Jesus when cleansing the temple (Mark 11:17). Protestant interpreters see Jesus invoking Isaiah to condemn the temple establishment for failing to realize Isaiah's inclusive vision. The Reformers applied this critique to ecclesiastical structures that created barriers between ordinary believers and God.

The chapter shifts abruptly in verses 9-12 to condemn Israel's watchmen as blind and greedy, more interested in personal comfort than their duties. Protestant preachers have applied this critique to negligent clergy throughout church history. The drunken shepherds who say "tomorrow will be like today" (56:12) represent spiritual complacency that the Reformation sought to overturn.
