Isaiah 65 presents God's response to the lament of chapters 63-64, beginning with the startling declaration "I was found by those who did not seek me" (65:1) — quoted by Paul in Romans 10:20 as referring to Gentile inclusion. Protestant interpreters see this as one of the clearest Old Testament anticipations of the gospel going to the nations, a reversal that redefines the boundaries of God's people.

The vision of "new heavens and a new earth" (65:17) is foundational to Protestant eschatology and is directly cited in Revelation 21:1 and 2 Peter 3:13. The description of life in the new creation — long life, secure homes, fruitful labor (65:20-23) — is debated among Protestant interpreters. Premillennialists see this as describing the millennial kingdom because death still exists (65:20), while amillennialists read the language as accommodated imagery for the eternal state.

The closing image of the wolf and lamb feeding together (65:25) recalls Isaiah 11:6-9 and envisions the complete restoration of the created order. Protestant environmental theology, though a relatively recent development, draws on this passage to argue that God's redemptive purposes include the renewal of nature, not just human souls. The vision of shalom encompassing all creation challenges any reduction of salvation to purely spiritual or individual categories.
