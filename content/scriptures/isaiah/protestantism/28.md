## Woe to the Crown of Ephraim

Isaiah 28 opens a new section of woe oracles (chapters 28-33) with a pronouncement against the northern kingdom. "Woe to that wreath, the pride of Ephraim's drunkards, to the fading flower, his glorious beauty" (28:1). Protestant interpreters see the drunkenness as both literal and metaphorical — Ephraim is intoxicated with pride and self-deception, unable to perceive the approaching Assyrian threat.

## The Precious Cornerstone

"So this is what the Sovereign LORD says: 'See, I lay a stone in Zion, a tested stone, a precious cornerstone for a sure foundation; the one who relies on it will never be stricken with panic'" (28:16). This is one of the most important messianic texts in Protestant theology, quoted by Paul (Romans 9:33; 10:11), Peter (1 Peter 2:6), and applied to Christ as the foundation of faith. The stone stands in contrast to the "covenant with death" that Judah's leaders have made with worldly powers (28:15).

## The Covenant with Death

"We have made a lie our refuge and falsehood our hiding place" (28:15). Protestant interpreters identify this as Judah's alliance with Egypt or Assyria — a political arrangement that the leaders treat as their security. God declares He will annul this covenant (28:18). The passage warns against every substitute for genuine trust in God: political alliances, military strength, economic security — all the "covenants with death" that nations make when they refuse to trust the cornerstone.

## The Farmer's Wisdom

The chapter concludes with a surprising agricultural parable (28:23-29). A farmer knows when to plow, what to plant where, and how to thresh each crop appropriately. "All this also comes from the LORD Almighty, whose plan is wonderful, whose wisdom is magnificent" (28:29). Protestant commentators see this as a defense of God's varied methods: just as a wise farmer applies different techniques to different crops, God applies different measures — judgment, mercy, discipline, restoration — to different situations.
