## The Valley of Vision

Isaiah 22 turns from foreign nations to Jerusalem itself — "the Valley of Vision" — with a startling oracle of judgment against God's own city. Protestant interpreters note the irony of the title: the city that should see most clearly is spiritually blind. The people are feasting when they should be mourning, celebrating when they should be repenting: "But see, there is joy and revelry, slaughtering of cattle and killing of sheep, eating of meat and drinking of wine! 'Let us eat and drink,' you say, 'for tomorrow we die!'" (22:13). Paul quotes this phrase in 1 Corinthians 15:32.

## Shebna and Eliakim

The chapter's second half focuses on two officials: Shebna, the self-aggrandizing steward who is carving out an elaborate tomb for himself (22:15-19), and Eliakim, who will replace him and receive "the key of the house of David" (22:22). Protestant interpreters see Eliakim as a type of Christ — the one who opens and no one shuts, who shuts and no one opens (cf. Revelation 3:7). The transfer of authority from the unworthy Shebna to the faithful Eliakim illustrates God's freedom to remove and install leaders according to His purposes.

## The Peg in a Firm Place

Eliakim is described as "a peg driven into a firm place" on which the entire household's weight can hang (22:23-24). Yet even this promise ends ominously: "In that day the peg driven into the firm place will give way" (22:25). Protestant commentators take this as a sober reminder that even the best human leaders are insufficient — only Christ, the ultimate Eliakim, is a peg that will never give way.
