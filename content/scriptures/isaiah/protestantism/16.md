## Moab's Appeal for Refuge

Isaiah 16 continues the Moab oracle with a remarkable plea: "Send lambs as tribute to the ruler of the land, from Sela, across the desert, to the mount of Daughter Zion" (16:1). Moab is counseled to seek shelter under Judah's protection. Protestant interpreters see here an anticipation of the universal call: even ancient enemies can find refuge under God's appointed king. Verse 5 — "In love a throne will be established; in faithfulness a man will sit on it" — is read as a messianic allusion embedded within an oracle against a foreign nation.

## The Pride of Moab

"We have heard of Moab's pride — how great is her arrogance! — of her conceit, her pride and her insolence" (16:6). Protestant commentators consistently identify pride as the root sin that brings nations under judgment. Moab's pride is not merely political arrogance but a fundamental refusal to acknowledge God's sovereignty. The passage provides a case study in the biblical principle that "God opposes the proud but shows favor to the humble" (James 4:6, echoing Proverbs 3:34).

## The Prophet's Grief

"Therefore I weep with Jazer for the vines of Sibmah. My tears drench you, Heshbon and Elealeh" (16:9). Once again, Isaiah shares in the grief of the judged nation. Protestant interpreters note that prophetic proclamation of judgment does not preclude — and in fact requires — genuine compassion. This combination of truthfulness and tenderness characterizes the prophetic office as Protestantism understands it.
