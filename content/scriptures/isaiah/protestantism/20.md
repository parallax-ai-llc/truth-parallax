## The Prophetic Sign-Act

Isaiah 20 is a brief but arresting narrative. God commands Isaiah to walk "stripped and barefoot" for three years as a prophetic sign against Egypt and Cush (20:2-3). Protestant interpreters emphasize the costliness of prophetic obedience: Isaiah's public humiliation embodies the message he proclaims. The sign-act tradition (found also in Jeremiah and Ezekiel) demonstrates that the prophetic word is not merely spoken but enacted — truth is performed, not just declared.

## Egypt and Cush Exposed

The meaning of the sign is explicit: "Just as my servant Isaiah has gone stripped and barefoot for three years, as a sign and portent against Egypt and Cush, so the king of Assyria will lead away stripped and barefoot the Egyptian captives and Cushite exiles" (20:3-4). Protestant commentators note the contrast between the voluntary humiliation of the prophet and the forced humiliation of the conquered. Those who refuse God's word willingly will experience its truth involuntarily.

## The Failure of False Hope

The chapter concludes with the inhabitants of the coastland lamenting: "See what has happened to those we relied on, those we fled to for help and deliverance from the king of Assyria! How then can we escape?" (20:6). Protestant theology finds here a recurring Isaianic principle applied with devastating clarity: trusting in human powers rather than God leads to disappointment and ruin. The passage warns against every form of political messianism that looks to worldly powers for ultimate security.
