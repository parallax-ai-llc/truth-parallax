Isaiah 58 is the prophetic gold standard for Protestant social ethics. God rejects Israel's fasting because it is accompanied by exploitation of workers and quarreling (58:3-4). True fasting, God declares, is "to loose the chains of injustice" and "to set the oppressed free" (58:6). This chapter has been central to Protestant social justice movements from the abolitionists to the civil rights movement.

The specific acts of justice enumerated — sharing food with the hungry, providing shelter for the homeless, clothing the naked (58:7) — anticipate Jesus's teaching in Matthew 25:35-36 about the sheep and goats. Protestant interpreters see this as evidence that genuine faith necessarily produces works of mercy, a theme that bridges the Reformation's insistence on faith alone with James's insistence that faith without works is dead (James 2:17).

The conditional promises of verses 8-12 — "Then your light will break forth like the dawn" — tie spiritual vitality to social righteousness. The Sabbath teaching in verses 13-14 is interpreted variously: some Protestant traditions maintain strict Sabbath observance based on this passage, while others see it as pointing to the spiritual rest found in Christ (Hebrews 4). In either case, the chapter insists that worship divorced from justice is unacceptable to God.
