## Leviathan Slain

Isaiah 27 opens with the eschatological defeat of Leviathan, "the gliding serpent... the monster of the sea" (27:1). Protestant interpreters understand this as God's final victory over the forces of chaos and evil, drawing on ancient Near Eastern mythological imagery to express a theological truth: evil is not an eternal dualism but a created power that God will definitively destroy. The New Testament applies this pattern to Christ's victory over Satan (Revelation 12; 20:2).

## The Pleasant Vineyard

In contrast to the vineyard of chapter 5 that yielded wild grapes and was destroyed, chapter 27 presents a vineyard that God Himself tends and protects: "I, the LORD, watch over it; I water it continually. I guard it day and night" (27:3). Protestant interpreters see this as the restoration of what was lost — the unfaithful people judged in chapter 5 will be replanted and renewed. The shift from judgment to grace within the vineyard metaphor encapsulates the movement of the entire book.

## The Ingathering of Israel

The chapter — and the "Isaiah Apocalypse" (chapters 24-27) as a whole — concludes with a vision of exiles returning at the sound of a great trumpet: "In that day a great trumpet will sound. Those who were perishing in Assyria and those who were exiled in Egypt will come and worship the LORD on the holy mountain in Jerusalem" (27:13). Protestant eschatology connects this trumpet to Jesus's teaching about the gathering of the elect (Matthew 24:31) and Paul's "last trumpet" (1 Corinthians 15:52). The scattered people of God will be reunited — the final answer to the exile that dominates so much of Isaiah.
