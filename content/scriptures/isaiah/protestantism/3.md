## Judgment on Jerusalem's Leadership

Isaiah 3 details the consequences of Judah's unfaithfulness through the removal of competent leadership. God will take away "the mighty man and the soldier, the judge and the prophet" (3:2). Protestant commentators see this as a covenantal curse: when a nation abandons God, He withdraws the gift of wise governance. Calvin applied this principle broadly, arguing that good government is a sign of divine favor and bad government a mark of divine judgment.

## Social Collapse and Moral Inversion

Verses 4-5 describe a society turned upside down: "I will make mere youths their officials; children will rule over them." Protestant exegesis reads this as a warning against the moral relativism that follows when divine authority is rejected. The passage illustrates the Reformed principle that social order depends upon the fear of God — when it is absent, anarchy follows.

## The Women of Jerusalem

The chapter's extended critique of the "daughters of Zion" and their elaborate finery (3:16-26) has been interpreted variously. Some Protestant commentators read it as a specific condemnation of luxury and vanity; others see it as a prophetic symbol for the pride of the entire nation. The detailed inventory of jewelry and clothing serves to expose the superficiality of a culture that adorns its exterior while its interior life decays.
