Isaiah 47 is a taunt song against Babylon, personified as a pampered queen who will be reduced to slavery. Protestant interpreters read this as both a historical prophecy of Babylon's fall to Persia in 539 BC and a typological preview of the fall of all arrogant worldly powers. The echoes in Revelation 17-18, where "Babylon the Great" falls, are extensively noted by Protestant commentators.

The accusation "You said, 'I am, and there is none besides me'" (47:8, 10) directly parodies the LORD's own self-declarations (cf. 45:5-6). Protestant interpreters see this as the essence of human sin: claiming for oneself the prerogatives that belong to God alone. Babylon's sorceries and enchantments (47:12-13) are condemned as attempts to control destiny apart from God, a critique that Protestant theology extends to all forms of occultism and divination.

Verse 15 — Babylon's counselors cannot save her — serves as a warning that human wisdom apart from God is ultimately futile. Protestant preachers from the Puritans onward have used this chapter to warn against cultural complacency and national pride, drawing parallels between ancient Babylon and contemporary societies that trust in wealth and military power rather than in God.
