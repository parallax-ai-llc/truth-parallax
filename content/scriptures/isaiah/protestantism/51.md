Isaiah 51 calls the faithful remnant to "look to the rock from which you were cut" (51:1), pointing back to Abraham and Sarah as paradigms of faith. Protestant interpreters connect this to Paul's argument in Romans 4 that Abraham's faith, not his works, was the basis of his righteousness. The chapter thus grounds the community's hope in the same faith that characterized their ancestor.

The creation language of verse 9 — "Was it not you who cut Rahab to pieces, who pierced that monster through?" — draws on ancient Near Eastern combat mythology while subordinating it to the LORD's sovereignty. Protestant interpreters debate whether "Rahab" refers to Egypt (as in Psalm 87:4) or to a primordial chaos creature, but agree that the point is God's demonstrated power to save. The exodus imagery merges with the return from exile to create a composite picture of redemption.

The "cup of wrath" imagery (51:17, 22) is theologically significant for Protestant atonement theology. The cup that Jerusalem drank is taken from her hand and given to her tormentors. In the Gospels, Jesus prays that "this cup" might pass from Him (Matthew 26:39), which Protestant interpreters understand as Christ drinking the cup of divine wrath on behalf of His people — the heart of penal substitutionary atonement.
