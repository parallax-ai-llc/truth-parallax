## Isaiah's Vision of the Holy God

Isaiah 6 records the prophet's throne-room vision — one of the most theologically significant passages in the Old Testament. "I saw the Lord, high and exalted, seated on a throne" (6:1). Protestant interpreters note that this vision comes "in the year that King Uzziah died," marking the transition from human kingship to divine sovereignty. Calvin argued that God revealed Himself precisely when earthly security was removed, so that Isaiah would depend solely on the heavenly King.

## The Trisagion and Divine Holiness

The seraphim's cry — "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty; the whole earth is full of his glory" (6:3) — establishes the foundational attribute of God in Isaiah's theology. Protestant theology sees the threefold "holy" as both a superlative intensifier and, for many interpreters, a Trinitarian intimation. The vision of holiness provokes Isaiah's confession of uncleanness (6:5), demonstrating the Reformed principle that genuine knowledge of God always produces awareness of human sinfulness.

## Cleansing and Commission

The coal from the altar touching Isaiah's lips (6:6-7) prefigures the gospel pattern: God initiates cleansing before sending the prophet. The commission itself is paradoxical — "Go and tell this people: Be ever hearing, but never understanding" (6:9). Jesus quotes this passage to explain the purpose of parables (Matthew 13:14-15), and Paul cites it at the climax of Acts (28:26-27). Protestant interpreters see here the mystery of divine sovereignty in both revelation and hardening.

## "How Long, O Lord?"

Isaiah's anguished question "How long, Lord?" (6:11) receives a devastating answer: until the land is utterly desolated. Yet the final verse preserves a seed of hope — "the holy seed is its stump" (6:13). This remnant theology is central to Protestant readings of both Old and New Testaments: judgment is never God's final word; a stump of grace survives every catastrophe.
