## Maher-shalal-hash-baz

Isaiah 8 opens with another sign-child whose ominous name — "Quick to the plunder, swift to the spoil" — warns of Assyria's imminent conquest of Damascus and Samaria. Protestant interpreters see the naming of the child, witnessed by reliable witnesses and recorded on a large scroll, as establishing the prophetic word with legal certainty. God's word is public, verified, and will be vindicated by events.

## The Overwhelming Flood

The Assyrian invasion is compared to the flooding of the Euphrates: "It will overflow all its channels, run over all its banks, and sweep on into Judah, swirling over it, passing through it and reaching up to the neck" (8:7-8). Protestant commentators note that Judah is not fully submerged — the waters reach "the neck" but do not drown the nation. This is judgment restrained by mercy, a theme central to the Reformed understanding of God's dealings with His covenant people.

## Conspiracy and Fear

Isaiah is warned not to share the people's panic: "Do not call conspiracy everything this people calls a conspiracy; do not fear what they fear" (8:12). Protestant exegesis applies this broadly: believers are called to a fundamentally different posture toward political and social crises. The fear of God displaces all other fears, a principle that Luther articulated memorably in his explanation of the First Commandment.

## The Testimony Sealed

The chapter concludes with Isaiah binding up his testimony among his disciples (8:16) and committing himself to wait for the Lord. "To the law and to the testimony! If they do not speak according to this word, it is because there is no dawn for them" (8:20). Protestant theology sees here the principle of *sola scriptura* in embryonic form — God's written word as the ultimate standard of truth.
