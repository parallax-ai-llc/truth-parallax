Isaiah 66, the book's grand finale, weaves together themes of judgment and salvation, temple and creation, Israel and the nations. The opening declaration — "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool. Where is the house you will build for me?" (66:1) — is quoted by Stephen in Acts 7:49-50 to argue that God cannot be confined to a human-made temple. Protestant theology draws on this to emphasize spiritual worship over institutional religion.

The image of Zion giving birth to a nation in a single day (66:7-8) is applied by Protestant interpreters to various historical moments: the birth of the Church at Pentecost, the sudden spread of the gospel, or the future restoration of Israel. The maternal comfort imagery — "As a mother comforts her child, so will I comfort you" (66:13) — again uses female metaphor for God, which Protestant theology acknowledges while maintaining that God transcends gender categories.

The book closes with a universal vision: "all mankind will come and bow down before me" (66:23) alongside the sobering image of unending judgment — "the worms that eat them will not die, the fire that burns them will not be quenched" (66:24), language Jesus applies to Gehenna in Mark 9:48. Protestant eschatology finds in this dual ending both the hope of universal worship and the reality of final judgment, refusing to resolve the tension. The book of Isaiah thus ends as it began — with a call to decision between faithfulness and rebellion.
