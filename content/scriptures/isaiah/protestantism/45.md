Isaiah 45 expands the Cyrus oracle with the astonishing designation "his anointed" (mashiach, 45:1) — the only time in the Hebrew Bible that a Gentile ruler receives this title. Protestant interpreters grapple with the theological implications: God's purposes transcend ethnic and religious boundaries. Calvin noted that this shattered any assumption that God works only through His covenant people.

The declaration "I form the light and create darkness, I bring prosperity and create disaster" (45:7) is one of the strongest statements of divine sovereignty in Scripture. Protestant theology, especially in its Reformed expression, uses this verse to ground the doctrine of God's comprehensive providence. Arminius and his followers pushed back, arguing the verse addresses God's role in national destiny rather than individual salvation.

Verses 22-25 — "Turn to me and be saved, all you ends of the earth, for I am God, and there is no other" — constitute a universal call to salvation that Protestant evangelicalism considers foundational. The scope extends beyond Israel to encompass every nation. This universalism of invitation, paired with the particularity of God's identity, shapes the Protestant missionary imperative: one God, one salvation, offered to all.
