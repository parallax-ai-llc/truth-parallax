## The Shoot from Jesse's Stump

Isaiah 11 follows the image of felled trees at the end of chapter 10 with a contrasting vision: "A shoot will come up from the stump of Jesse; from his roots a Branch will bear fruit" (11:1). Protestant interpreters see this as one of the clearest messianic prophecies in the Old Testament. The Davidic dynasty, seemingly cut down, will produce a new growth — not from the royal court in its glory but from the humblest origins, the root system of Jesse (David's father, not David himself).

## The Sevenfold Spirit

"The Spirit of the LORD will rest on him — the Spirit of wisdom and of understanding, the Spirit of counsel and of might, the Spirit of the knowledge and fear of the LORD" (11:2). Protestant theology sees here a comprehensive description of the Spirit's anointing upon the Messiah. The seven qualities (counting "the Spirit of the LORD" as the first) have been linked to the seven spirits before God's throne in Revelation 1:4 and to the fullness of the Spirit's work in the believer.

## The Peaceable Kingdom

"The wolf will live with the lamb" (11:6) — this vision of cosmic peace has captivated Protestant imagination from the Puritans to contemporary evangelicalism. Debate continues over whether this describes a literal millennial kingdom, the spiritual transformation brought by the gospel, or the final consummation. All Protestant interpreters agree, however, that it envisions the comprehensive reversal of the curse: nature itself will be healed when the messianic king reigns.

## The Second Exodus

The chapter's closing vision of God gathering the remnant "a second time" (11:11) and drying up the Euphrates to create a highway connects messianic redemption to the Exodus pattern. Protestant commentators see this as typology operating at the grandest scale: the new redemption surpasses the old, encompassing not just Israel but people "from the four quarters of the earth" (11:12).
