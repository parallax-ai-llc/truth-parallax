Protestant interpreters read Isaiah 34 as a sweeping oracle of divine judgment against the nations, with Edom singled out as representative of all who oppose God's purposes. Verses 1-4 summon the entire earth to hear the verdict: "The LORD is angry with all nations" (34:2). Reformed commentators such as Calvin see this as a demonstration of God's sovereign justice extending beyond Israel to encompass universal history.

The desolation described in verses 5-15, where Edom becomes a haunt of jackals and wild birds, is understood typologically. Many Protestant exegetes, following the pattern set by the book of Revelation (cf. Revelation 34:8's echoes in Revelation 14:10-11), treat Edom as a figure for all worldly powers that set themselves against God. The "scroll of the LORD" mentioned in verse 16 has generated discussion about whether Isaiah refers to his own prophetic book or to a heavenly register of God's decrees.

The chapter's vivid apocalyptic imagery — stars falling, heavens rolling up like a scroll (34:4) — is frequently connected to New Testament eschatology, particularly Matthew 24:29 and Revelation 6:13-14. Protestant dispensationalists tend to read these as literal end-times events, while amillennial interpreters understand them as prophetic hyperbole expressing the totality of divine judgment.
