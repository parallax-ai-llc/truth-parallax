Isaiah 46 contrasts Babylon's gods Bel and Nebo, who must be carried by their worshippers (46:1-2), with the LORD who carries Israel "from the womb" to "gray hairs" (46:3-4). Protestant interpreters find this reversal theologically profound: false religion burdens its adherents, while the true God bears His people. Luther used this contrast to critique what he saw as the burdensome religious system of medieval Catholicism.

The declaration "I make known the end from the beginning" (46:10) is a cornerstone text for the Protestant doctrine of divine foreknowledge and predestination. Reformed theology reads this as affirming not merely God's knowledge of future events but His active determination of them: "My purpose will stand, and I will do all that I please." Arminian Protestants interpret it as foreknowledge without foreordination, but all agree it establishes God's absolute distinction from finite beings.

The "bird of prey from the east" (46:11), again referencing Cyrus, reinforces the theme that God commands history. Protestant interpreters emphasize that even pagan conquerors serve God's redemptive purposes without knowing it. The chapter's concluding promise of salvation and splendor for Israel (46:13) points forward to the ultimate deliverance that the New Testament identifies with Christ.
