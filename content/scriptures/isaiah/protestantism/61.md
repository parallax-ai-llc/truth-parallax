Isaiah 61 is the chapter Jesus reads in the Nazareth synagogue to inaugurate His public ministry (Luke 4:16-21), making it one of the most christologically significant passages in the Protestant canon. "The Spirit of the Sovereign LORD is on me, because the LORD has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor" (61:1) defines the Messiah's mission in terms of liberation, healing, and proclamation. Jesus's declaration "Today this scripture is fulfilled in your hearing" is the paradigmatic moment of messianic self-identification.

Protestant interpreters note that Jesus stops reading mid-sentence in Luke 4, omitting "the day of vengeance of our God" (61:2b). This is taken as evidence of a two-stage fulfillment: Christ's first coming inaugurates the "year of the LORD's favor," while the "day of vengeance" awaits His second coming. This hermeneutical move is foundational to the Protestant understanding of the "already/not yet" kingdom.

The transformation described in verses 3-7 — beauty for ashes, gladness for mourning, a garment of praise for a spirit of despair — encapsulates the Protestant understanding of conversion and sanctification. The "oaks of righteousness" (61:3) planted by the LORD for His glory provide an organic metaphor for spiritual growth that contrasts with works-based religion. Believers are not self-made but divinely planted and cultivated.
