Isaiah 62 continues the vision of Zion's restoration with the imagery of new names and covenant renewal. The declaration "You will be called by a new name that the mouth of the LORD will bestow" (62:2) is connected in Protestant interpretation to Revelation 2:17 and the believer's new identity in Christ. The old names "Deserted" and "Desolate" give way to "Hephzibah" (my delight is in her) and "Beulah" (married) — expressing the intimacy of the divine-human relationship (62:4).

The marital imagery — "As a bridegroom rejoices over his bride, so will your God rejoice over you" (62:5) — feeds into Protestant ecclesiology's understanding of the Church as the bride of Christ (Ephesians 5:25-32). This is not mere metaphor but expresses the covenantal union that defines the community's identity. Puritan devotional writers were particularly drawn to this bridal mysticism, seeing in it the deepest expression of the believer's relationship with God.

The watchmen on Jerusalem's walls who "will never be silent day or night" (62:6) are interpreted as intercessors. Protestant prayer movements have drawn on this imagery to establish continuous prayer initiatives. The command to "give him no rest till he establishes Jerusalem" (62:7) is a remarkable text that presents persistent prayer as the appropriate human response to divine promises — not replacing God's sovereignty but cooperating with it.
