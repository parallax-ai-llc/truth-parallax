## The Isaiah Apocalypse Begins

Isaiah 24 marks a dramatic shift from the specific national oracles of chapters 13-23 to a vision of universal, cosmic judgment. "See, the LORD is going to lay waste the earth and devastate it" (24:1). Protestant interpreters recognize this as one of the Old Testament's most explicitly apocalyptic chapters, foreshadowing the eschatological visions of Daniel and Revelation. The scope is comprehensive: priest and people, master and servant, buyer and seller all face the same judgment (24:2).

## The Earth Polluted by Its Inhabitants

"The earth is defiled by its people; they have disobeyed the laws, violated the statutes and broken the everlasting covenant" (24:5). Protestant theology identifies the "everlasting covenant" with either the Noahic covenant or a broader moral order inscribed in creation. The passage teaches that human sin has cosmic consequences — the earth itself groans under the weight of moral corruption, a theme Paul develops in Romans 8:19-22.

## The City of Chaos

An unnamed "city of chaos" is described as ruined and desolate (24:10-12). Protestant commentators see this as the archetype of human civilization built apart from God — Babel/Babylon in its essence. Every city, culture, and empire that organizes itself in opposition to God's purposes shares in this judgment. The contrast with the "city of God" that will emerge in Isaiah's later chapters is deliberate.

## The Song from the Ends of the Earth

Amid the devastation, songs of praise are heard "from the ends of the earth: Glory to the Righteous One!" (24:16). Yet Isaiah himself cries, "But I said, 'I waste away, I waste away! Woe to me!'" — aware that the path to universal praise passes through universal judgment. Protestant interpreters find this tension between eschatological hope and present suffering characteristic of the Christian life between the ascension and the return of Christ.
