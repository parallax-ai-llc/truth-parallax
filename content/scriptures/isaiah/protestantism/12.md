## Songs of Salvation

Isaiah 12 functions as a doxological conclusion to the first major section of the book (chapters 1-12). Its six verses contain two short psalms of thanksgiving that respond to the preceding cycle of judgment and messianic promise. Protestant interpreters note the structural parallel with the Song of the Sea in Exodus 15: just as Israel sang after deliverance from Egypt, the redeemed community sings after the promised deliverance described in chapters 9-11.

## "I Will Trust and Not Be Afraid"

"Surely God is my salvation; I will trust and not be afraid. The LORD, the LORD himself, is my strength and my defense; he has become my salvation" (12:2). Protestant theology finds in this verse the essence of *sola fide*: salvation is attributed entirely to God, and the human response is trust rather than achievement. The echoes of Exodus 15:2 ("The LORD is my strength and my song; he has become my salvation") deliberately connect Isaiah's promised redemption to the foundational act of deliverance in Israel's history.

## Drawing Water from the Wells of Salvation

"With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation" (12:3). This verse is liturgically significant in multiple traditions, but Protestant interpreters connect it especially to Jesus's words at the Feast of Tabernacles: "Let anyone who is thirsty come to me and drink" (John 7:37). The image of abundant, accessible water for salvation encapsulates the gospel offer as Protestantism understands it — grace freely available to all who come in faith.
