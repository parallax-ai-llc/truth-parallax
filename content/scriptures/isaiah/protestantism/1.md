## The Great Arraignment

Isaiah opens with a courtroom scene in which God summons heaven and earth as witnesses against His covenant people. Protestant interpreters emphasize the covenantal framework: Israel stands accused not of minor infractions but of fundamental rebellion against the God who "reared and brought up" children (1:2). Calvin saw this as establishing the pattern of all prophetic preaching — calling God's people back from apostasy to covenant fidelity.

## Ritual Without Righteousness

Verses 11-17 contain one of Scripture's sharpest critiques of empty religious observance. God declares that He takes no delight in sacrifices, assemblies, or prayers when they are offered by hands "full of blood." Protestant exegesis connects this to the Reformation principle that external religion without inward faith is worthless. Luther pointed to this passage as evidence that works-righteousness is condemned not only in the New Testament but already in the prophets.

## The Call to Repentance

"Come now, let us reason together" (1:18) stands as one of the most beloved Protestant texts on grace. The imagery of sins as scarlet being made white as snow is read as an Old Testament anticipation of justification — God's gracious offer to cleanse the guilty. Yet the passage is not unconditional: "If you are willing and obedient, you shall eat the good of the land; but if you refuse and rebel, you shall be eaten by the sword" (1:19-20). Protestant commentators note the tension between divine initiative and human response that runs throughout Isaiah.

## The Faithful City Restored

The chapter closes with a vision of Zion purified through judgment (1:25-27). "Zion shall be redeemed by justice, and those in her who repent, by righteousness." Reformed interpreters see this as the first statement of a theme that will crescendo through the entire book: God's unwavering commitment to purify and restore His people, even through severe means.
