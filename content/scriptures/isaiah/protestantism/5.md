## The Song of the Vineyard

Isaiah 5 contains one of the Old Testament's most artful parables. God is depicted as a vinedresser who has done everything possible for his vineyard — clearing stones, planting choice vines, building a watchtower — yet the vineyard yields only wild grapes (5:1-7). Protestant interpreters emphasize that the parable indicts Israel not for lack of divine provision but for willful unfruitfulness. Jesus's parable of the tenants (Matthew 21:33-44) deliberately echoes this passage, extending its judgment.

## The Six Woe Oracles

Verses 8-23 pronounce six "woes" against specific sins: land-grabbing (5:8), drunken revelry (5:11), cynical mockery of God (5:18-19), moral inversion (5:20), self-conceit (5:21), and corruption of justice (5:22-23). Protestant preachers from the Puritans to the present have used these woes as a template for social criticism. The progression reveals a society that has systematically abandoned every dimension of covenant faithfulness.

## The Outstretched Hand

The chapter concludes with a terrifying image of God summoning a distant nation to execute judgment. "He lifts up a banner for the distant nations" (5:26), and they come with devastating speed. The refrain "Yet for all this, his anger is not turned away, his hand is still outstretched" (5:25) recurs through chapters 9-10, forming a structural thread of escalating judgment. Protestant commentators identify Assyria as the immediate referent while recognizing the pattern applies to all nations that defy God.
