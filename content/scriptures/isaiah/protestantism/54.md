Isaiah 54 follows the Suffering Servant passage with a song of restored relationship, which Protestant interpreters read as describing the fruits of the atonement accomplished in chapter 53. The barren woman who now has more children than the married woman (54:1) is quoted by Paul in Galatians 4:27 as an allegory of the New Covenant community — the Church — which surpasses the old covenant in fruitfulness.

The covenant of peace (54:10) — "Though the mountains be shaken and the hills be removed, yet my unfailing love for you will not be shaken" — is a foundational text for the Protestant doctrine of the perseverance of the saints. God's commitment to His people is grounded not in their faithfulness but in His own unchanging character. Reformed theology cites this alongside Romans 8:38-39 as assurance that nothing can separate believers from God's love.

The promise that "no weapon forged against you will prevail" (54:17) and that God will vindicate His servants is read as applying to the Church in every age. Protestant interpreters from the Reformation onward have claimed this promise during periods of persecution, seeing in it divine assurance that the gates of hell will not prevail against Christ's Church (Matthew 16:18).
