## The Oracle Against Tyre

Isaiah 23 concludes the oracles against the nations (chapters 13-23) with a prophecy against Tyre, the great Phoenician trading city. "Wail, you ships of Tarshish! For Tyre is destroyed" (23:1). Protestant interpreters see Tyre as representing the pinnacle of commercial civilization — wealth, sophistication, and international trade networks — all of which are subject to divine judgment. The chapter serves as a warning against the idolatry of economic power.

## The Lord's Purpose Against Tyre

"The LORD Almighty planned it, to bring down her pride in all her splendor" (23:9). Protestant theology emphasizes that Tyre's downfall is not accidental but purposeful. God's governance extends to the commercial sphere: no economic system is autonomous or exempt from moral accountability. The passage challenges modern assumptions about the neutrality of markets and the self-sufficiency of wealth.

## Tyre's Seventy-Year Sabbath and Restoration

Remarkably, the oracle concludes with Tyre's restoration after seventy years (23:15-18). Even more remarkably, the restored Tyre's commercial profits "will be set apart for the LORD" and "will supply abundant food and fine clothes for those who live before the LORD" (23:18). Protestant commentators see here an anticipation of the sanctification of all human activity — even commerce and trade — under God's kingdom. The nations' wealth, previously used for self-glorification, will be redirected toward the service of God and His people.
