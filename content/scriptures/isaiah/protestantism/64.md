Isaiah 64 continues the communal lament begun in chapter 63, with one of the most memorable prayers in Scripture: "Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down" (64:1). Protestant interpreters see this as the cry of humanity before the Incarnation — a longing fulfilled when "the Word became flesh" (John 1:14). The tearing of the heavens at Christ's baptism (Mark 1:10) may echo this petition.

The confession "All our righteous acts are like filthy rags" (64:6) is one of the most frequently cited verses in Protestant soteriology. It undergirds the Reformation doctrine that human righteousness is insufficient for salvation and that only Christ's imputed righteousness can stand before God. Critics have argued this verse is taken out of context — it describes Israel in exile, not a universal theological principle — but Protestant systematic theology has consistently applied it broadly as expressing the inadequacy of human moral effort before a holy God.

The potter-clay metaphor (64:8) — "We are the clay, you are the potter" — is echoed in Paul's argument about divine sovereignty in Romans 9:20-21. Protestant interpreters see this as establishing God's absolute right to shape His people according to His purposes. The chapter's closing plea — "Will you keep silent and punish us beyond measure?" (64:12) — leaves the reader in suspense, awaiting God's answer in chapters 65-66.
