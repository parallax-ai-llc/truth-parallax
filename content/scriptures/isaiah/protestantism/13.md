## The Oracle Against Babylon

Isaiah 13 opens the section of oracles against foreign nations (chapters 13-23) with a sweeping prophecy against Babylon. Protestant interpreters note the difficulty that Babylon was not yet a major power in Isaiah's time (8th century BCE), which some take as evidence of prophetic foreknowledge and others as an indication of later composition. The chapter's apocalyptic imagery — darkened stars, shaking heavens — elevates Babylon's fall into a type of universal judgment.

## The Day of the Lord

"The day of the LORD is coming — cruel, with wrath and fierce anger — to make the land desolate and destroy the sinners within it" (13:9). Protestant eschatology draws heavily on Isaiah's "day of the LORD" language. The cosmic upheaval described in 13:10-13 — sun darkened, moon refusing to shine, stars falling — is echoed in Jesus's Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24:29) and in Revelation. The passage establishes that God's judgment is not limited to individual nations but encompasses the entire created order.

## The Medes as God's Instrument

God declares, "I have commanded those I prepared for battle; I have summoned my warriors" (13:3). As with Assyria in chapter 10, pagan armies serve God's purposes without knowing it. Protestant commentators see this as further evidence of divine sovereignty over history: God orchestrates the rise and fall of empires to accomplish His redemptive purposes. The specific mention of the Medes (13:17) anticipates the historical coalition that conquered Babylon in 539 BCE.
