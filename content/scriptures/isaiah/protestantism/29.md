## Woe to Ariel

Isaiah 29 addresses Jerusalem as "Ariel" — a name meaning either "altar hearth" or "lion of God." "Woe to you, Ariel, Ariel, the city where David settled!" (29:1). Protestant interpreters see the double meaning as deliberate: Jerusalem is both the place of sacrifice and the place of God's fierce presence. The oracle warns that God will besiege His own city, yet ultimately deliver it — purifying through fire as an altar purifies sacrifice.

## The Sealed Book

"For you this whole vision is nothing but words sealed in a scroll" (29:11). The people's spiritual blindness is described as an inability to read God's revelation — whether literate or illiterate, they cannot comprehend. Protestant theology connects this to the theme of spiritual hardening introduced in chapter 6. Without the illuminating work of the Spirit, Scripture remains a sealed book. The Reformation emphasis on the Spirit's internal testimony (*testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum*) draws on passages like this one.

## Worship with Lips, Not Hearts

"The Lord says: 'These people come near to me with their mouth and honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me'" (29:13). Jesus quotes this passage directly in His critique of the Pharisees (Matthew 15:8-9). Protestant theology has consistently used this text to distinguish between external religiosity and genuine faith — a distinction central to the Reformation's critique of medieval Catholicism and equally applicable as self-critique within Protestant churches.

## The Reversal to Come

The chapter ends with a stunning reversal: "In a very short time, will not Lebanon be turned into a fertile field and the fertile field seem like a forest?" (29:17). The deaf will hear, the blind will see, the humble will rejoice, and the ruthless will vanish. Protestant interpreters see this as anticipating the signs of the messianic age that Jesus points to when John the Baptist sends his disciples to ask, "Are you the one who is to come?" (Matthew 11:3-5).
