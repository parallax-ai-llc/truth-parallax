Isaiah 63 opens with the striking image of a divine warrior returning from Edom with garments stained crimson (63:1-6). Protestant interpreters have long debated whether this is a christological text. Revelation 19:13-15 depicts Christ returning in blood-stained robes, suggesting the apostle John read Isaiah 63 messianically. The warrior's declaration "I have trodden the winepress alone" (63:3) is understood as Christ's solitary work of judgment and redemption.

The chapter transitions to a tender communal lament (63:7-19) that recalls God's past mercies and pleads for renewed intervention. The affirmation "In all their distress he too was distressed, and the angel of his presence saved them" (63:9) is a key text for the Protestant doctrine of divine compassion — God does not observe suffering from a distance but enters into it. Some interpreters identify "the angel of his presence" as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ.

The prayer "You, LORD, are our Father" (63:16) is notable because fatherhood language for God is relatively rare in the Old Testament. Protestant interpreters see this as anticipating the intimate "Abba" relationship that Jesus makes possible (Romans 8:15). The anguished question "Why, LORD, do you make us wander from your ways and harden our hearts?" (63:17) raises the problem of divine hardening that Protestant theology, especially in its Reformed expression, addresses through the doctrine of reprobation — though Arminian Protestants read this as lament rather than theological statement.
