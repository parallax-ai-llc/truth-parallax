## The Mountain of the Lord

Isaiah 2 opens with one of the most exalted visions in prophetic literature: "In the last days the mountain of the LORD's temple will be established as the highest of the mountains" (2:2). Protestant interpreters debate whether this refers to the millennial kingdom, the church age, or the new creation. Dispensationalist readers see a literal future fulfillment in a restored Jerusalem, while Reformed interpreters often understand it as the spiritual exaltation of the Church among the nations.

## Swords into Plowshares

The vision of nations beating swords into plowshares (2:4) has become one of Scripture's most iconic images of peace. Protestant commentators note that this peace is not achieved through human effort but through divine adjudication — "He will judge between the nations." The passage grounds Christian hope for peace not in political programs but in the coming reign of God.

## The Day of the Lord Against Pride

The second half of the chapter shifts dramatically from eschatological hope to present warning. The "day of the LORD" will bring low everything proud and exalted — the cedars of Lebanon, the high towers, the trading ships of Tarshish (2:12-16). Protestant theology sees here the principle that God opposes the proud, a theme that Paul develops in Romans 1-3 and that Luther placed at the center of his theology of the cross.
