## The Desert by the Sea — Oracle on Babylon

Isaiah 21 contains three cryptic oracles. The first, "A prophecy against the Desert by the Sea" (21:1), is generally understood as referring to Babylon. The prophet describes a terrifying vision: "A dire vision has been shown to me — the traitor betrays, the looter takes loot" (21:2). Protestant interpreters note the intensity of Isaiah's emotional response — "My body is racked with pain, pangs seize me" (21:3) — indicating that prophetic insight into divine judgment is not delivered dispassionately.

## The Watchman on the Tower

The image of the watchman stationed to report what he sees (21:6-9) has become a defining metaphor for the prophetic office in Protestant thought. "Day after day, my lord, I stand on the watchtower; every night I stay at my post" (21:8). The watchman's announcement — "Babylon has fallen, has fallen!" (21:9) — is echoed directly in Revelation 14:8 and 18:2. Protestant eschatology connects Isaiah's original oracle about historical Babylon to the fall of "Babylon the Great" in the apocalyptic vision.

## Oracles Against Edom and Arabia

The brief oracles on Dumah/Edom (21:11-12) and Arabia (21:13-17) carry their own power. The Edom oracle is hauntingly ambiguous: "Watchman, what is left of the night?" The answer — "Morning is coming, but also the night" — resists easy resolution. Protestant commentators see this as an honest acknowledgment that history contains both hope and darkness, and that the prophetic word does not always offer simple comfort.
