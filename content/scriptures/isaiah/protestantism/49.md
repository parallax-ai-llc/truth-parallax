Isaiah 49 contains the second Servant Song (49:1-7), which deepens the portrait of the Servant's mission. The Servant is called "from the womb" (49:1) and designated "a light for the Gentiles" (49:6) — language that Luke's Gospel applies to both Jesus (Luke 2:32) and Paul's apostolic mission (Acts 13:47). Protestant interpreters see this dual application as revealing the Servant's work extending through His body, the Church.

The Servant's initial sense of futility — "I have labored in vain" (49:4) — resonates deeply in Protestant pastoral theology. Even the Messiah's ministry appeared unsuccessful by worldly measures, a comfort to ministers and missionaries who labor without visible results. Yet God's response is to enlarge the Servant's mission from Israel alone to the nations (49:6), turning apparent failure into expanded purpose.

The maternal imagery of verses 14-16 — "Can a mother forget the baby at her breast? Though she may forget, I will not forget you" — is among the most tender passages in the prophets. Protestant interpreters note that God here uses female imagery for Himself, affirming that divine love surpasses even the strongest human bonds. The image of names engraved on God's palms (49:16) is sometimes connected to Christ's nail-scarred hands in post-resurrection appearances.
