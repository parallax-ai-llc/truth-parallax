Isaiah 53 is the theological summit of the book for Protestant interpretation and arguably the single most important Old Testament passage for Christian soteriology. The Suffering Servant who is "pierced for our transgressions" and "crushed for our iniquities" (53:5) is identified without qualification as Jesus Christ. The entire structure of penal substitutionary atonement — the dominant Protestant atonement theory — finds its Old Testament foundation here.

The chapter's language of substitution is exhaustive: "he bore the sin of many" (53:12), "the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all" (53:6), "by his wounds we are healed" (53:5). Luther called this chapter the "foremost passage on the suffering and resurrection of Christ" and argued that it leaves no room for works-righteousness because salvation is accomplished entirely by another. Calvin likewise saw here the clearest Old Testament expression of imputed righteousness: the Servant's righteousness credited to those who deserved only punishment.

The Servant's silence before his accusers (53:7), his death with the wicked and burial with the rich (53:9), and his vindication when he "sees his offspring and prolongs his days" (53:10) are mapped onto the Gospel narrative with striking precision. Philip's encounter with the Ethiopian eunuch in Acts 8:32-35, where this passage becomes the springboard for explaining the gospel, provides the New Testament warrant for this christological reading. Protestant interpreters acknowledge the Jewish reading of the servant as Israel but consider it inadequate to account for the specificity of the suffering described.
