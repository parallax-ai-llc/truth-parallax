## The Valley of Vision

Isaiah 22 pronounces judgment on Jerusalem itself. Rashi identifies the historical context as the Assyrian siege under Sennacherib or the later Babylonian campaigns. The ironic title "Valley of Vision" highlights the city's spiritual blindness: the place that should see most clearly has become the most spiritually obtuse.

## Shebna and Eliakim

The transfer of authority from Shebna to Eliakim (22:20-24) is read by Jewish commentators as an illustration of God's freedom to remove and install leaders. Rashi identifies Shebna as a high official who had secretly negotiated with the enemy, while Eliakim was a faithful servant of the king. The "key of the house of David" (22:22) represents administrative authority over the royal household.

## The Peg That Gives Way

Even Eliakim, the faithful steward, is warned that the "peg driven into a firm place will give way" (22:25). Jewish interpretation takes this as a sober reminder that all human authority is limited and temporary. Radak sees this as a warning against nepotism — even a good leader can be brought down by the excessive demands of family members and dependents who "hang on" him.
