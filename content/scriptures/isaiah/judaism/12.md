## Songs of Thanksgiving

Isaiah 12 functions as a liturgical conclusion to the first section of the book. Its two brief psalms of praise are incorporated into Jewish liturgical life, particularly in the *Havdalah* service that concludes Shabbat, where 12:2 is recited: "Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust and not be afraid."

## Drawing Water with Joy

"With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation" (12:3). The Talmud (Sukkah 48b) associates this verse with the water-drawing ceremony (*simchat beit ha-sho'evah*) during the festival of Sukkot, which was described as the greatest joy in the Temple. The Mishnah (Sukkah 5:1) records: "One who has not seen the joy of the water-drawing has never seen joy in his life." The verse connects salvation to tangible, celebratory experience.

## Making Known Among the Nations

"Make known among the nations what he has done; proclaim that his name is exalted" (12:4). Jewish interpretation reads this as Israel's vocation to be a witness to God's deeds among the nations — a form of *kiddush Hashem* (sanctification of God's name). The chapter's universal scope — calling on the nations to hear — reflects the tension in Jewish theology between particularism (God's special covenant with Israel) and universalism (God's sovereignty over all nations).
