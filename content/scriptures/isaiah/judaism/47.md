Isaiah 47's taunt song against Babylon personified as a fallen queen is read in Jewish tradition through the prism of exile and eventual redemption. The command for Babylon to "sit in the dust" (47:1) reverses the relationship between captor and captive, oppressor and oppressed. Midrash Eichah Rabbah connects Babylon's downfall to the measure-for-measure (middah k'neged middah) principle: just as Babylon humiliated Jerusalem, so Babylon will be humiliated.

Babylon's claim "I am, and there is none besides me" (47:8, 10) -- a blasphemous parody of God's own self-declaration -- is identified by Jewish commentators as the quintessential expression of human arrogance (ga'avah). Rabbeinu Yonah of Gerona, in his Sha'arei Teshuvah (Gates of Repentance), lists self-deification as the highest degree of pride and connects it to the fall of every empire that has oppressed Israel. The pattern of rise and fall -- Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Rome -- demonstrates the truth of Isaiah's oracle.

The condemnation of Babylon's astrologers and sorcerers (47:12-13) reinforces the Torah's prohibition against divination and occult practices (Deuteronomy 18:10-12). Maimonides, in his Laws of Idolatry (11:16), cites this passage alongside the Deuteronomic prohibition, arguing that astrology and divination are not merely forbidden but actually fraudulent -- they have no genuine predictive power. Isaiah's mockery of Babylon's stargazers who "cannot save themselves" (47:14) supports Maimonides' rationalist critique.
