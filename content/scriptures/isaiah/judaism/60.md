Isaiah 60 presents one of the most expansive visions of messianic glory in the prophetic literature, and Jewish tradition reads it as describing the ultimate restoration of Jerusalem and the ingathering of exiles. "Arise, shine, for your light has come" (60:1) is incorporated into Friday evening liturgy and is understood as referring to the Shekhinah (divine presence) returning to Zion. The Zohar interprets this light as the or ha-ganuz -- the primordial light of creation that God hid away for the righteous in the World to Come.

The nations bringing their wealth to Jerusalem (60:5-7, 11) is understood by Maimonides (Laws of Kings 12:5) as describing the economic and cultural transformation of the messianic age: the nations will voluntarily acknowledge Jerusalem's centrality and contribute to its restoration. This is not imperialism but the natural gravitational pull of divine truth. Abarbanel envisions this as a reversal of exile's humiliations: the wealth that was plundered from Israel will be returned with interest, and the nations that oppressed Israel will serve its rebuilding.

The promise "The LORD will be your everlasting light" (60:19) is connected in Jewish mystical tradition to the concept of or ein sof -- the infinite light of God that transcends all created luminaries. The Sabbath and festival candles lit in Jewish homes are understood as small reflections of this ultimate light. The verse's eschatological vision -- a world where God's unmediated presence replaces sun and moon -- corresponds to the rabbinic description of the World to Come, where "the righteous sit with their crowns on their heads and enjoy the radiance of the Shekhinah" (Berakhot 17a).
