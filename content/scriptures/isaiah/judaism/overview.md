## Overview

In Judaism, Isaiah (*Yeshayahu*) is the first and longest of the Latter Prophets (Nevi'im Acharonim) in the Hebrew Bible. The book holds immense liturgical and theological importance: seven consecutive haftarah readings from Isaiah ("Seven of Consolation") follow Tisha B'Av, and Isaiah's visions of justice, redemption, and universal peace have profoundly shaped Jewish messianic thought, ethical teaching, and liturgical life.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
**Rashi** and **Ibn Ezra** ground their readings in the historical context of the Assyrian and Babylonian periods. Rashi consistently reads messianic passages as referring to the people of Israel collectively or to historical figures like Hezekiah, pushing back against Christological interpretations.

### Derash (Homiletical)
The Midrash and Talmud draw extensively on Isaiah. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) records that Isaiah reduced the Torah's commandments to six principles (Isaiah 33:15-16), and that Habakkuk further reduced them to one: "The righteous shall live by faith."

### Remez and Sod (Allegorical and Mystical)
Kabbalistic tradition reads Isaiah's throne vision (chapter 6) as a description of the *merkavah* (divine chariot) and the inner structure of the *sefirot*. The Zohar interprets the Suffering Servant as the soul of the *Shekhinah* in exile.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Suffering Servant as Israel**: Jewish interpretation identifies the Servant of Isaiah 53 with the nation of Israel suffering among the nations, not an individual messiah
- **Messianic Age, Not Messiah**: The focus is on the age of universal peace and justice (2:2-4; 11:1-9) rather than a single messianic figure
- **Teshuvah (Repentance)**: Isaiah's call to repentance (1:18-20) is central to the liturgy of the High Holy Days
- **Social Justice**: Isaiah's demand for ethical conduct over empty ritual (1:11-17; 58:1-12) is foundational to Jewish prophetic ethics

## Liturgical Context

Isaiah provides more haftarah readings than any other prophetic book:

| Occasion | Passage | Theme |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Shabbat Chazon | 1:1-27 | Rebuke before Tisha B'Av |
| Shabbat Nachamu | 40:1-26 | First consolation after Tisha B'Av |
| Seven of Consolation | Various (40-66) | Seven weeks of comfort |
| Yom Kippur Morning | 57:14–58:14 | True fasting and justice |

## Major Commentators

| Commentator | Era | Approach |
|-------------|-----|----------|
| Rashi | 11th century | Peshat with midrashic synthesis |
| Ibn Ezra | 12th century | Grammatical-rational, hints at dual authorship |
| Radak (David Kimchi) | 12th-13th century | Systematic anti-Christian polemic |
| Abarbanel | 15th century | Political-historical, messianic analysis |
| Malbim | 19th century | Linguistic precision, anti-Reform apologetics |
