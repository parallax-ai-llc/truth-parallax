Isaiah 43's opening declaration -- "Fear not, for I have redeemed you; I have called you by name, you are mine" (43:1) -- is one of the most powerful expressions of the covenant relationship in Jewish theology. The naming language connects to the profound Jewish understanding of the name (shem) as expressing essential identity. God knows Israel not as an abstraction but as a particular, named reality -- a theological conviction that grounds the Jewish emphasis on the concreteness and specificity of revelation.

The promise of passing through water and fire (43:2) is read by Jewish commentators as referring to the historical experiences of the exodus (water) and the Babylonian furnace (fire, cf. Daniel 3), with application to all future persecutions. Midrash Tanchuma extends this to every generation: whenever Israel faces existential threat, God accompanies them through it. This interpretation has been profoundly meaningful in post-Holocaust Jewish theology, where the question of divine presence in suffering is central.

The "new thing" (43:19) is interpreted by most Jewish commentators as the return from exile, which will surpass even the exodus in its wonders. The Talmud (Berakhot 12b-13a) records a debate about whether the future redemption will eclipse the exodus in liturgical memory, concluding that both will be remembered but the final redemption will take precedence. God's declaration that He blots out transgressions "for my own sake" (43:25) is foundational to the Jewish understanding of divine mercy: God forgives not because Israel deserves it but because mercy is intrinsic to the divine nature, as expressed in the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy (Exodus 34:6-7).
