Isaiah 55 is read as the concluding address of Deutero-Isaiah's message of consolation, offering a universal invitation that encompasses both Israel and the nations. The call "Ho, everyone who thirsts, come to the waters" (55:1) is interpreted by the Talmud (Taanit 7a) as referring to Torah: "There is no water except Torah." The free offer -- "without money and without cost" -- expresses the Jewish conviction that Torah is accessible to all who seek it, regardless of social status or wealth. This democratization of learning is one of Judaism's most distinctive contributions.

The "everlasting covenant, the sure mercies of David" (55:3) is understood as God's renewal of the Davidic promise, pointing toward the messianic era when a descendant of David will restore the kingdom. Radak connects this to the broader prophetic expectation of a messianic king who will gather the exiles, rebuild the Temple, and inaugurate an age of peace. The covenant's description as "everlasting" (olam) reinforces Jewish theology's insistence that God's promises to Israel are permanent and unconditional.

The declaration that God's word "will not return to me empty" (55:11) is foundational to Jewish theology of Torah. Every word of Scripture accomplishes its purpose -- an idea that grounds the rabbinic practice of deriving meaning from every detail of the text, including spelling variations, numerical values (gematria), and juxtapositions. The celebrated closing image -- "the mountains and hills will burst into song before you, and all the trees of the field will clap their hands" (55:12) -- is read as a vision of cosmic joy at Israel's redemption, when creation itself will celebrate the fulfillment of God's promises.
