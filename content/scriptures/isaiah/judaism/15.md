## The Burden of Moab

Isaiah 15 laments the destruction of Moab with notable compassion. Rashi identifies the historical context as an Assyrian invasion of Moab. Jewish interpretation notes that despite Moab's history of enmity toward Israel (Numbers 22-25), the prophet grieves over Moab's suffering — modeling the principle that compassion transcends national boundaries.

## "My Heart Cries Out Over Moab"

The phrase "my heart cries out over Moab" (15:5) is striking in its emotional intensity. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 39b) records God's response when the angels wished to sing at the drowning of the Egyptians: "My creatures are drowning in the sea, and you want to sing songs?" Jewish theology insists that compassion for the suffering of all people — even enemies — is a divine attribute that humans are called to emulate.

## The Cities of Destruction

The detailed catalog of Moabite cities in ruins serves, in Jewish reading, as a warning about the fragility of human civilization. Radak notes that Moab's prosperity and apparent security did not protect it from divine judgment. The specificity of the place-names prevents the reader from spiritualizing the suffering into abstraction.
