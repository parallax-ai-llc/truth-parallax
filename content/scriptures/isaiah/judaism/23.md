## The Oracle Against Tyre

Isaiah 23 pronounces judgment on Tyre, the great Phoenician trading city. Rashi and Radak connect this to the Assyrian campaigns against Phoenician cities, while noting that the complete fulfillment came with Alexander the Great's siege of Tyre in 332 BCE. Jewish commentators see Tyre as representing the pinnacle of commercial civilization — brilliant, sophisticated, and ultimately vulnerable to divine judgment.

## The Lord's Purpose

"The LORD Almighty planned it, to bring down her pride" (23:9). Jewish theology of history affirms that God's governance extends to the commercial realm. The Talmud (Megillah 6a) discusses the relationship between Tyre and Jerusalem, noting a tradition that "when one is full, the other is empty" — suggesting an inverse relationship between commercial and spiritual prosperity.

## The Seventy Years and Restoration

Tyre's seventy-year desolation (23:15) parallels Jerusalem's seventy-year exile. Jewish commentators see the parallel as deliberate: the same God who judges Jerusalem also judges Tyre, and the same God who restores Jerusalem can restore even a pagan commercial city. The conclusion — Tyre's profits "set apart for the LORD" (23:18) — envisions the sanctification of commerce in the messianic age.
