Isaiah 62's imagery of Zion receiving new names -- "Hephzibah" (my delight is in her) and "Beulah" (married) (62:4) -- is interpreted in Jewish mystical tradition through the metaphor of the divine marriage between God and Israel. The Song of Songs, read allegorically as the love story between God and His people, provides the interpretive framework. The Zohar sees in the Shabbat the weekly renewal of this marriage, when the Shekhinah is reunited with the Holy One, blessed be He, and creation experiences the harmony intended from the beginning.

The watchmen on Jerusalem's walls (62:6) who "never be silent day or night" are identified by Jewish commentators with those who pray for Jerusalem's restoration -- a practice that continues in Jewish life through the daily Amidah prayer, which includes petitions for the rebuilding of Jerusalem and the return of God's presence to Zion. The Western Wall (Kotel) tradition of continuous prayer embodies this prophetic injunction, and the custom of placing written prayers in the Wall's crevices reflects the command to "give the LORD no rest till he establishes Jerusalem" (62:7).

The command to "prepare the way for the people" and "build up the highway" (62:10) is connected in Jewish tradition to the messianic preparation. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 97b-98a) discusses whether the Messiah will come only when Israel repents or whether redemption will come regardless of merit at an appointed time. The highway imagery suggests active preparation -- teshuvah, mitzvot, and acts of justice -- as humanity's contribution to the messianic process. This tension between divine initiative and human effort is central to Jewish messianic theology.
