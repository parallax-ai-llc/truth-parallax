## The Syro-Ephraimite Crisis

Isaiah 7 is set during the alliance of Syria and Ephraim against Judah (735 BCE). Rashi and the classical commentators read this chapter entirely within its historical context, without the Christological overlay that characterizes Christian interpretation. The crisis tests Ahaz's faith: will he trust God's promise that the alliance will fail, or will he seek Assyrian protection?

## The Almah and Immanuel

"The *almah* will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel" (7:14). Jewish interpretation consistently reads *almah* as "young woman" rather than "virgin," and identifies the child as a sign for Ahaz's immediate situation — most likely Ahaz's own son Hezekiah, or Isaiah's son, or another contemporary child. Rashi states explicitly that this refers to a young woman of Isaiah's own time. The Radak devotes considerable attention to refuting the Christian reading, arguing that the Hebrew *almah* never means "virgin" in the technical sense; the Hebrew word for that is *betulah*.

## The Sign's Timeframe

"Before the boy knows enough to reject the wrong and choose the right, the land of the two kings you dread will be laid waste" (7:16). Jewish commentators emphasize the temporal specificity: the sign is meant to reassure Ahaz about an imminent threat, not to predict an event seven centuries later. The destruction of Syria and Ephraim by Assyria (within a few years of this prophecy) constitutes the sign's fulfillment.

## Butter and Honey

The diet of "curds and honey" (7:15) is read by Rashi as indicating a time of desolation when the land supports only pastoral and wild food — not as a sign of abundance but of depopulation. Ibn Ezra concurs, seeing the entire passage as describing the Assyrian devastation that Ahaz's policies will bring upon Judah.
