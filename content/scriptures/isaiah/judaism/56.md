Isaiah 56 is notable in Jewish interpretation for its inclusion of foreigners (gerim) and eunuchs (sarisim) in the covenant community -- categories that Deuteronomy 23 appeared to exclude. The Talmud (Yevamot 76a) discusses at length how these Isaianic promises modify or supersede the Deuteronomic restrictions, concluding that the prophetic expansion reflects a development within the divine plan. The convert (ger tzedek) who "holds fast to the covenant" (56:4, 6) is granted full membership in the community of Israel -- a principle codified in halakhah.

The declaration that God's house "will be called a house of prayer for all nations" (56:7) is understood as defining the Temple's ultimate purpose. The Mishnah (Middot 2:3) notes that the Temple's outer courtyard was designated for Gentile worship, reflecting Isaiah's vision. The destruction of the Temple by Rome was thus doubly tragic: it ended not only Israel's worship but the one institution that concretely embodied the universal accessibility of the one God. Jewish prayers for the Temple's rebuilding include the hope that it will fulfill this universal function.

The condemnation of negligent leaders (56:10-12) -- watchmen who are "blind" and "dogs that love to slumber" -- is applied by Jewish commentators to both prophets and community leaders who fail in their responsibilities. The mussar (ethical) tradition, from Rabbeinu Yonah through the Vilna Gaon to Rabbi Israel Salanter, has consistently held community leaders to the highest standards of diligence and accountability, drawing on passages like this one to ground its critique of complacency among the rabbinate.
