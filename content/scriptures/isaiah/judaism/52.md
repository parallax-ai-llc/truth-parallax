Isaiah 52 opens with a call for Zion to "awake" and "put on strength" (52:1), which Jewish liturgy has incorporated into the Lekha Dodi hymn sung on Friday evening to welcome the Sabbath. The imagery of Jerusalem casting off the chains of exile is understood as anticipating the messianic redemption, and the Shabbat itself is experienced in Jewish mysticism as a foretaste of that redemption -- the "Sabbath Queen" who brings a taste of the World to Come.

The declaration "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news" (52:7) is interpreted in Jewish tradition as referring to the messenger who will announce the arrival of the Messiah and the final redemption. The Talmud (Pesachim 68a and Makkot 24a) records that Rabbi Akiva wept upon seeing the desolation of the Temple Mount but was comforted by the certainty that just as the prophecies of destruction had been fulfilled, so too would the prophecies of restoration. The "good news" (mevaser) is specifically the news of God's return to Zion (52:8), which the watchmen see "eye to eye."

The Servant Song beginning at 52:13 is perhaps the most contested passage in Jewish-Christian interpretation. Jewish exegesis identifies the servant who is "raised and lifted up and highly exalted" (52:13) while also "disfigured beyond recognition" (52:14) as the people of Israel -- despised by the nations during exile but destined for vindication. This reading is strongly defended by Rashi, Radak, and Ibn Ezra, who argue that the context of chapters 40-55 consistently identifies the servant as Israel. The shift to chapter 53 continues this corporate interpretation.
