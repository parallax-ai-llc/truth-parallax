The opening of Isaiah 57 -- the righteous perishing unnoticed (57:1-2) -- is interpreted in the Talmud (Moed Katan 28a) as teaching that the death of the righteous is as grievous to God as the destruction of the Temple. The Midrash explains that God sometimes takes the righteous from the world to spare them from witnessing the calamities about to befall the generation -- a concept that has provided comfort in Jewish mourning practices and is echoed in the burial liturgy's prayer that the deceased "go in peace."

The condemnation of idolatrous practices (57:3-10) -- including child sacrifice and fertility worship -- is read by Jewish commentators as referring to actual practices that persisted in Israelite society despite the prophets' objections. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 64a) discusses the historical reality of child sacrifice to Molech, and Maimonides in the Guide for the Perplexed (III:37) provides an anthropological explanation of how these practices arose and why the Torah prohibits them so severely. The prophetic denunciation reinforces the absolute prohibition that halakhah maintains.

The paradox of verse 15 -- God dwelling in the "high and holy place" yet with the "contrite and lowly in spirit" -- is central to Jewish theology's understanding of divine immanence and transcendence. The kabbalistic concept of tzimtzum (divine contraction), developed by Isaac Luria, addresses this paradox: the infinite God contracts Himself to create space for finite beings and dwells with those who empty themselves in humility. The verse is quoted in Hasidic literature extensively, particularly by Rabbi Nachman of Breslov, who taught that the broken heart (lev nishbar) is the truest dwelling place of the divine.
