## Moab Seeks Shelter

Isaiah 16 envisions Moab seeking refuge under Judah's protection. Jewish commentators see the reference to a throne "established in love" and a ruler who "seeks justice" (16:5) as an allusion to the Davidic dynasty — specifically to the righteous rule that the Messiah will establish. The Targum Jonathan renders this verse with explicit messianic language.

## The Pride of Moab

"We have heard of Moab's pride — how great is her arrogance" (16:6). Jewish moral theology identifies pride as the characteristic sin that brings nations under judgment. The Mishnah (Avot 4:21) teaches that "jealousy, desire, and honor-seeking drive a person from the world" — and Moab's pride exemplifies the last of these vices on a national scale.

## The Prophet's Tears

"So I weep, as Jazer weeps" (16:9). Jewish tradition values the capacity for empathetic grief as a prophetic virtue. The midrash on Lamentations (Eichah Rabbah) records that Jeremiah also wept over the nations — not only over Israel. The ability to mourn the suffering of others, even adversaries, distinguishes the true prophet from the false.
