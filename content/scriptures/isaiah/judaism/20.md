## The Prophetic Sign-Act

Isaiah 20 describes the prophet walking "stripped and barefoot" for three years as a sign against Egypt and Cush. Jewish interpretation emphasizes the costliness of prophetic obedience: the prophet's personal dignity is sacrificed to communicate God's message. The Talmud (Yevamot 49b) discusses the hardships endured by the prophets, recognizing that prophetic service demands total self-surrender.

## Egypt and Cush Exposed

The sign's meaning is explicit: Egypt and Cush will be led away as captives by Assyria. Rashi dates this to the period of Sargon II's campaigns (c. 711 BCE). Jewish commentators note the practical lesson: those who rely on Egypt and Cush for protection against Assyria will be disappointed, because these supposed protectors will themselves be conquered.

## The Vanity of False Hope

"How then can we escape?" (20:6). Jewish interpretation applies this cry to every instance of misplaced trust. The inhabitants of the Philistine coast, who had relied on Egypt, discover too late that their protector has fallen. The passage reinforces the prophetic demand for trust in God alone — a theme that runs through every chapter of Isaiah.
