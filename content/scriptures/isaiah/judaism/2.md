## The Mountain of the Lord

Isaiah 2:2-4 presents the vision of nations streaming to God's holy mountain. Jewish interpretation reads this as a description of the messianic age (*yemot ha-mashiach*) when all nations will acknowledge the God of Israel and seek Torah instruction from Jerusalem. Rashi connects this to the ingathering of exiles and the universal recognition of God's sovereignty. The passage is the basis for the Jewish conviction that the messianic age brings universal peace.

## Torah from Zion

"The law (*Torah*) will go out from Zion, the word of the LORD from Jerusalem" (2:3). Jewish interpretation emphasizes that the messianic age does not abolish Torah but universalizes it. The nations will seek Torah instruction willingly, recognizing its divine wisdom. Radak explains that in the messianic age, the truth of Torah will be so self-evident that the nations will abandon their false religions and come to learn from Israel.

## Swords into Plowshares

"Nation will not take up sword against nation, nor will they train for war anymore" (2:4). This vision of universal peace is the hallmark of Jewish messianic expectation. The Rambam (Maimonides) includes the cessation of warfare among the signs of the messianic age (*Hilkhot Melakhim* 12:5). Jewish theology insists that this prophecy has never been fulfilled — its unfulfillment is part of the Jewish argument that the Messiah has not yet come.

## The Day Against Pride

The "day of the LORD" against human arrogance (2:12-22) is read by Jewish commentators as a warning that applies to all peoples, including Israel. Ibn Ezra notes that the catalog of proud objects — cedars, oaks, mountains, towers, ships — represents every form of human self-exaltation. The Talmud (Sotah 5a) teaches that God says of the arrogant person: "He and I cannot dwell together in the world."
