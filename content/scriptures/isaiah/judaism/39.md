Jewish interpretation of Isaiah 39 focuses on Hezekiah's failure and its consequences as a lesson in humility and divine judgment. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 96a) explains that the Babylonian envoys were sent because of the astronomical sign (38:8), and Hezekiah's proud display of his treasures (39:2) was the sin that ultimately led to the Babylonian exile. Rashi notes the irony: the very wealth displayed to impress Babylon would later be carried off to Babylon.

Isaiah's prophecy of exile (39:5-7) is particularly poignant in Jewish reading because it establishes the inevitability of the galut (exile) that would define Jewish history for millennia. The transition from chapters 39 to 40 -- from the announcement of exile to the promise of comfort -- mirrors the liturgical structure of the Jewish calendar, where the three haftarot of rebuke (preceding Tisha B'Av) are followed by seven haftarot of consolation (the "shiva d'nechemta" beginning with Isaiah 40).

Hezekiah's response -- "The word of the LORD is good" (39:8) -- is interpreted critically by most commentators. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) records a debate about whether Hezekiah was expressing genuine submission to God's decree or selfish relief that the calamity would not occur in his lifetime. Radak takes the more charitable view, seeing it as acceptance of divine justice. Either way, the narrative serves as a warning that even the most righteous are not immune to spiritual failure, a theme that pervades Jewish ethical literature (mussar).
