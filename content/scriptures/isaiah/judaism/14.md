## The Mashal Against the King of Babylon

Isaiah 14 presents a taunt-song (*mashal*) over the fallen Babylonian king. Jewish interpretation, unlike Christian tradition, does not read this passage as describing Satan's fall. Rashi identifies the subject as the king of Babylon (likely Nebuchadnezzar or his dynasty), and the "morning star" (*helel ben shachar*) as a poetic metaphor for the king's former glory, not an angelic being.

## The Arrogance of Self-Deification

"I will ascend above the tops of the clouds; I will make myself like the Most High" (14:14). Jewish commentators see this as the ultimate expression of human hubris — the attempt to claim divine status. Radak connects this to the broader prophetic critique of kings who forget that their authority is delegated by God. The midrash notes that Nebuchadnezzar's madness (Daniel 4) was the direct consequence of precisely this kind of self-deification.

## Sheol Receives the King

The scene in Sheol (14:9-11) is notable for its vivid depiction of the afterlife. Jewish theology of Sheol in the prophetic period conceived of it as a shadowy underworld where the dead exist in a diminished state. The mockery of the fallen king by the other shades illustrates the Talmudic principle that "measure for measure" (*middah k'neged middah*) governs divine justice: the one who exalted himself above all is brought lower than all.
