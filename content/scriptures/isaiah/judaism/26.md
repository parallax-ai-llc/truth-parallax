## The Strong City

Isaiah 26 sings of a "strong city" that God protects with salvation (26:1). Jewish interpretation identifies this with Jerusalem in its messianic glory — the restored city that will serve as the center of God's kingdom on earth. The contrast with the "city of chaos" destroyed in chapter 24 is deliberate: the city built on justice endures; the city built on pride falls.

## Shalom, Shalom

"You will keep in perfect peace (*shalom shalom*) those whose minds are steadfast" (26:3). This verse is central to Jewish devotional life and theology of trust (*bitachon*). The doubled *shalom* indicates completeness — peace in this world and peace in the world to come, according to the midrash. The Talmud (Berakhot 64a) concludes with the principle that Torah scholars increase peace (*shalom*) in the world, citing Isaiah.

## Resurrection

"Your dead will live; their bodies will rise" (26:19). This is one of the key proof-texts for the doctrine of bodily resurrection (*techiyat ha-metim*) in rabbinic Judaism. The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 10:1) lists denial of resurrection as grounds for exclusion from the world to come, and the Talmud (Sanhedrin 90b) cites Isaiah 26:19 among the biblical evidence for this belief. The second blessing of the *Amidah* praises God as the one who "gives life to the dead."

## Hiding Until the Wrath Passes

"Enter your rooms and shut the doors behind you" (26:20). Jewish commentators connect this to the Passover in Egypt, when the Israelites remained indoors while the Angel of Death passed over. The image of divine protection amid judgment has sustained Jewish communities through centuries of persecution.
