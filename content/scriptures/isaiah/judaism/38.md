Hezekiah's illness and recovery (Isaiah 38) is one of the most discussed narratives in rabbinic literature. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) provides a famous explanation for Hezekiah's illness: he was punished because he had not married and had children. When Isaiah came to announce his death, Hezekiah responded that he had refrained from marriage because he foresaw through the Holy Spirit that his children would be wicked (as indeed Manasseh proved to be). Isaiah rebuked him: "What business have you with the secrets of the Merciful One? You should have done what you were commanded!"

Hezekiah's psalm (38:9-20) is studied as a model of how the righteous respond to suffering. The honest expression of fear -- "I shall not see the LORD (Yah) in the land of the living" (38:11) -- reflects a pre-rabbinic understanding of death that later Jewish theology modified significantly. The rabbinic concept of Olam Ha-Ba (the World to Come) and the resurrection of the dead transforms the despair of this verse into a stage of understanding that awaits fuller revelation.

The sign of the shadow retreating on the stairway of Ahaz (38:8) is discussed in the Talmud (Sanhedrin 96a) as having cosmic significance: the day was supernaturally shortened, an event noticed by the Babylonian king Merodach-Baladan, which prompted him to send envoys to Hezekiah (chapter 39). This connection between chapters illustrates the rabbinic approach to Scripture as an intricately connected web where every detail has significance.
