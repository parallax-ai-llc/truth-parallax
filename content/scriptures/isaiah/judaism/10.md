## Assyria as God's Rod

"Woe to Assyria, the rod of my anger" (10:5). Rashi emphasizes the paradox: Assyria is both God's instrument and a sinner punished for its cruelty. Jewish theology of divine providence holds that God's use of nations as instruments does not remove their moral responsibility. The Talmud (Makkot 10b) applies the principle: "Through the means of one who is guilty, guilt comes."

## The Boasting of the Axe

"Does the axe raise itself above the person who swings it?" (10:15). Jewish interpretation uses this rhetorical question to establish a fundamental principle of theology: no creature is autonomous. Radak sees this as the definitive refutation of imperial arrogance — every empire that claims self-generated power is as absurd as an axe that boasts of cutting wood by itself.

## Shear-jashub: A Remnant Shall Return

"A remnant will return, a remnant of Jacob will return to the Mighty God" (10:21). The name of Isaiah's son Shear-jashub embodies this promise. Jewish theology of the remnant has sustained the Jewish people through centuries of persecution: even in the worst catastrophe, God preserves a faithful core. The concept grounds the Jewish conviction that the covenant is unbreakable — God will never completely abandon His people.

## The Destruction of Assyria

The chapter's concluding vision of God cutting down Assyria (10:33-34) demonstrates the principle that empires are temporary. Rashi connects this to the destruction of Sennacherib's army (2 Kings 19:35). Jewish historiography, shaped by prophetic theology, reads the rise and fall of empires as evidence of divine governance rather than blind chance.
