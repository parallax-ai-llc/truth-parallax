## Chazon Yeshayahu — The Vision of Isaiah

Isaiah 1 is read as the haftarah for Shabbat Chazon, the Sabbath before Tisha B'Av (the fast commemorating the destruction of the Temple). This liturgical placement shapes Jewish reading profoundly: the chapter is heard as a warning about the consequences of national sin that led to the Temple's destruction. Rashi reads the opening verse as indicating that Isaiah prophesied during the reigns of four kings, making his ministry span the better part of a century.

## The Ox and the Donkey

"The ox knows its master, the donkey its owner's manger, but Israel does not know, my people do not understand" (1:3). The Talmud (Avodah Zarah 5b) uses this verse to illustrate the severity of Israel's ingratitude: even animals recognize their provider, but Israel fails to recognize God. Rashi notes the ascending scale of shame: the ox knows its master from birth, the donkey only recognizes the food trough — yet Israel fails to match even the donkey's level of awareness.

## The Rejection of Sacrifices

"What to me is the multitude of your sacrifices?" (1:11). Jewish interpretation does not read this as abolishing the sacrificial system but as insisting that sacrifices without ethical conduct are meaningless. The Talmud (Yoma 86b) teaches that teshuvah (repentance) is the essential precondition for sacrifice to be effective. Radak emphasizes that God rejects not the institution but the hypocrisy of those who offer sacrifices while continuing to oppress the poor.

## Scarlet and Snow

"Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow" (1:18). This verse is central to the theology of teshuvah. The Talmud (Shabbat 89b) records that this promise applies even to the most grievous sins, and that God's offer of cleansing is always available to the penitent. The verse is invoked during the High Holy Days as evidence that no sin is beyond the reach of repentance and divine forgiveness.
