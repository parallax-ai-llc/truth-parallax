The second Servant passage (49:1-7) is read in Jewish tradition as continuing the identification of the servant with Israel. The servant called "from the womb" (49:1) is the Jewish people, chosen before birth for a divine mission. The name "Israel" (49:3) makes this identification explicit, despite Christian attempts to read it as an individual figure. Rashi and Radak both insist that the servant is the collective people of Israel, while acknowledging that the servant can also be understood as the righteous remnant within Israel or as the prophet himself.

The servant's lament "I have labored in vain" (49:4) resonates deeply with Jewish historical experience. Centuries of exile, persecution, and martyrdom seemed to contradict the promise of divine election. Yet the passage insists that God's purpose will be vindicated: the servant will not only restore Israel but become "a light for the Gentiles" (49:6). This universal dimension of Israel's mission is emphasized by modern Jewish thinkers like Martin Buber and Abraham Joshua Heschel, who saw Jewish existence as inherently oriented toward the redemption of all humanity.

The maternal imagery (49:14-16) -- Zion as a mother who feels abandoned but is assured of God's unfailing love -- speaks powerfully to the Jewish experience of exile (galut). The engraving of Jerusalem on God's palms (49:16) is interpreted in Midrash as meaning that God carries the memory of the destroyed Temple perpetually, just as the Jewish people carry it in their daily prayers, turning toward Jerusalem three times a day. The verse "Can a mother forget her nursing child?" (49:15) is echoed in the liturgy of the High Holy Days, where God's parental love for Israel is a recurring theme.
