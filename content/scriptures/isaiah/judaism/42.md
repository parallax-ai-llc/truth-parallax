Jewish interpretation of the Servant passages in Isaiah is one of the most significant areas of Jewish-Christian exegetical disagreement. In Isaiah 42:1-9, the "servant" is understood by virtually all Jewish commentators as referring to the people of Israel collectively, not to an individual messiah. Rashi, following the Targum Jonathan, identifies the servant as Israel: "Here is my servant" is God presenting Israel to the nations as His chosen instrument of justice and light.

The declaration that the servant will be "a light to the nations" (42:6) is understood as Israel's vocation to bring knowledge of the one God to all peoples -- a mission accomplished not through political conquest or religious proselytism but through the witness of Torah-faithful living. This concept of or la-goyim (light to the nations) has been central to Jewish self-understanding, particularly in the modern period when thinkers like Rabbi Abraham Isaac Kook and Rabbi Joseph Soloveitchik reflected on Israel's universal mission.

The chapter's shift to rebuke (42:18-25) -- "Who is blind but my servant?" -- is read by Jewish commentators as an honest acknowledgment that Israel has often failed in its mission. Radak interprets this blindness as Israel's inability to perceive God's purpose in exile, while Rashi sees it as the moral and spiritual failures that led to exile in the first place. This dual portrait -- the servant as both chosen and failing -- reflects the Jewish theological conviction that election does not guarantee perfection but imposes responsibility.
