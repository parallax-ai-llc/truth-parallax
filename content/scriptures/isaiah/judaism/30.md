## Rebellious Children

Isaiah 30 condemns Judah's embassy to Egypt. Rashi identifies the specific historical mission and notes that the prophetic objection is not to diplomacy per se but to diplomacy conducted without consulting God. Jewish theology of divine guidance insists that significant communal decisions should be made in light of Torah and prophetic wisdom, not solely on the basis of political calculation.

## Rahab the Do-Nothing

"Egypt's help is utterly useless. Therefore I call her Rahab the Do-Nothing" (30:7). Rashi explains the wordplay: *Rahab* (the mythological chaos monster) is reduced to *shevet* (sitting still, doing nothing). The great power that Israel feared and courted is exposed as impotent. Jewish humor and irony, which flourish in prophetic literature, serve a theological purpose: deflating pretension and redirecting trust to God alone.

## The Teacher and the Way

"Your eyes will see your Teacher... your ears will hear a voice behind you, saying, 'This is the way; walk in it'" (30:20-21). Jewish theology of Torah study finds in this verse the promise that God will guide those who seek His way. Rashi reads the "Teacher" as God Himself, who will no longer be hidden but will be directly accessible. The verse grounds the Jewish conviction that God's guidance is available to those who study Torah and seek righteousness.

## The Night of Song

"You will sing as on the night you celebrate a holy festival" (30:29). Rashi identifies this festival night as the night of Passover. Jewish liturgical life echoes this connection: the Seder night is both a memorial of past deliverance and an anticipation of future redemption. The singing in the face of judgment reflects the Jewish capacity for joy even in the midst of suffering — a quality rooted in trust in God's ultimate faithfulness.
