## The Crown of Pride

Isaiah 28 opens with a woe against Ephraim's "crown of pride" and drunken leadership. Rashi connects this to the final years of the northern kingdom before the Assyrian conquest. Jewish moral theology sees drunkenness — both literal and metaphorical — as a failure of *da'at* (knowledge/awareness), the capacity for clear perception that distinguishes responsible leadership.

## The Cornerstone in Zion

"See, I lay a stone in Zion, a tested stone, a precious cornerstone" (28:16). Jewish interpretation identifies this cornerstone variously as the Temple, the Torah, the Davidic king, or the righteous remnant. Rashi reads it as referring to the Davidic dynasty. The Targum applies it to the king/Messiah. Unlike Christian interpretation, Jewish reading does not see a single messianic figure here but rather the enduring foundation of God's covenant with Israel.

## The Covenant with Death

"We have made a covenant with death" (28:15). Rashi reads this as Judah's alliance with Egypt — a "covenant with death" because Egypt cannot ultimately protect against the Assyrian threat. Jewish political theology draws from this the principle that alliances based on deception and self-deception are ultimately self-destructive.

## The Farmer's Wisdom

The agricultural parable (28:23-29) is read by Jewish commentators as an illustration of God's varied methods. Rashi explains that just as a wise farmer knows to treat each crop differently, God applies different measures — discipline, mercy, exile, restoration — to different situations. "This also comes from the LORD of hosts; wonderful is his counsel, and great his wisdom" (28:29).
