## The Massa Against Babylon

Isaiah 13 opens the section of oracles against foreign nations with a *massa* (burden/oracle) against Babylon. Jewish commentators note that Babylon was not yet a dominant power in Isaiah's time, which raised questions even among classical interpreters. Ibn Ezra hints that this section may derive from a later period, while traditional commentators like Rashi maintain that prophetic foreknowledge accounts for the anachronism.

## The Day of the Lord

"The day of the LORD is coming — cruel, with wrath and fierce anger" (13:9). Jewish eschatology distinguishes between the "day of the LORD" as it applies to individual nations (Babylon's fall to the Medes) and the universal "day" that inaugurates the messianic age. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98b) records debates about whether the messianic age will come through catastrophic judgment or gradual transformation.

## The Medes as God's Instrument

"See, I will stir up against them the Medes" (13:17). Rashi connects this to the historical Medo-Persian conquest of Babylon in 539 BCE. Jewish theology of history sees in the rise and fall of empires evidence of divine governance: God orchestrates the movements of nations to accomplish His purposes, ultimately for the benefit of Israel and the fulfillment of the covenant promises.
