Isaiah 64's prayer "Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down" (64:1) expresses the raw longing for divine intervention that has characterized Jewish prayer throughout the exile. This verse is particularly associated with the kabbalistic concept of "arousal from below" (itaruta d'letata) -- human prayer that stimulates a corresponding "arousal from above" (itaruta d'le'ela). The Zohar teaches that heaven and earth are interconnected, and human yearning has the power to precipitate divine response.

The confession "All our righteous acts are like a filthy garment" (64:6) is read in Jewish theology not as a statement about the inherent worthlessness of human goodness but as a confession of the specific community's failure during the exile. Radak explains that the verse describes Israel's condition when separated from God by sin -- their previously righteous deeds have been corrupted. This is not a universal statement about human nature but a particular acknowledgment of this generation's spiritual state. Jewish theology maintains that human beings are capable of genuine righteousness through free choice and divine assistance.

The potter-clay metaphor (64:8) -- "We are the clay, you are the potter" -- is echoed in the Unetaneh Tokef prayer of the High Holy Days: "Like clay in the hand of the potter, who expands and contracts it at will, so are we in Your hand." Yet Jewish theology insists that the potter's design includes the clay's freedom: human beings are not inert material but responsive agents. The Talmud's teaching "Everything is in the hands of Heaven except the fear of Heaven" (Berakhot 33b) preserves this balance between divine sovereignty and human responsibility.
