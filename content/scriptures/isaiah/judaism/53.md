Isaiah 53 is the single most disputed chapter between Jewish and Christian exegesis. Jewish interpretation, from the earliest rabbinic sources to modern commentators, overwhelmingly identifies the "suffering servant" as the people of Israel, not as an individual messiah. Rashi's commentary is definitive: the servant is "the congregation of Israel and their righteous ones," who suffer vicariously among the nations. The speakers in 53:1-6 who confess "we esteemed him stricken by God" are the nations who finally realize that Israel's suffering was not punishment but a consequence of bearing witness to God in a hostile world.

This reading is grounded in several textual observations. The servant in surrounding chapters (41:8, 44:1, 44:21, 45:4, 48:20, 49:3) is explicitly called "Israel" or "Jacob." The singular grammatical form is explained by the well-established Hebrew convention of treating a nation as a collective singular (e.g., "Israel is my firstborn son," Exodus 4:22). The servant's suffering -- despised, rejected, bearing the sins of many -- corresponds precisely to Israel's historical experience: expelled from its land, persecuted among the nations, yet maintaining its covenant identity.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98b) does preserve an individual messianic reading, identifying the suffering servant with a messianic figure who bears the afflictions of Israel. This minority tradition was developed in some midrashic sources and appears in certain kabbalistic texts. However, the mainstream of Jewish exegesis from medieval times onward has firmly rejected the christological reading and maintained the collective interpretation. Abarbanel devoted extensive argumentation to this point in his commentary, systematically countering Christian interpretations verse by verse. The chapter remains a living site of Jewish-Christian dialogue and disagreement.
