## The Destroyer Destroyed

Isaiah 33 opens with a woe against an unnamed destroyer. Rashi identifies this as Sennacherib, whose campaign against Judah was followed by his own army's mysterious destruction (2 Kings 19:35). Jewish theology of retributive justice sees here the principle of *middah k'neged middah* (measure for measure): the destroyer is destroyed, the betrayer is betrayed.

## The Prayer of the Community

"LORD, be gracious to us; we long for you" (33:2). Jewish liturgical prayer — from the daily *Amidah* to the confessional prayers of Yom Kippur — echoes this cry for grace. The longing (*kivinu lakh*) expressed here is understood as the fundamental posture of the Jewish soul before God: waiting, hoping, trusting in divine mercy.

## Who Can Endure the Fire?

"The sinners in Zion are terrified: 'Who of us can dwell with the consuming fire?'" (33:14). Jewish moral theology reads the answer (33:15-16) as a description of the righteous life: walking in integrity, speaking truthfully, rejecting unjust gain, refusing bribes. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) cites this passage in its famous discussion of reducing the 613 commandments to core principles, noting that Isaiah identified six essential qualities.

## The King in His Beauty

"Your eyes will see the king in his beauty" (33:17). Jewish messianic hope finds in this verse the promise of seeing the Messiah — the king from David's line who will rule in justice and establish peace. The concluding promise that "the sins of those who dwell there will be forgiven" (33:24) connects the messianic age to the complete atonement of Israel's sins — a promise that sustains Jewish hope through every trial.
