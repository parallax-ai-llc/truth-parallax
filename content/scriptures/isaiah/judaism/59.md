Isaiah 59 presents a devastating analysis of sin's consequences that Jewish theology reads through the lens of collective responsibility. "Your iniquities have separated you from your God" (59:2) is understood not merely as individual sin but as communal corruption that has national consequences. The Talmudic principle "kol Yisrael arevim zeh ba-zeh" (all Israel are responsible for one another, Sanhedrin 27b) means that individual sin affects the entire community, and the community bears responsibility for failing to prevent it.

The corporate confession (59:9-15) provides a model for the Vidui (confessional prayers) recited on Yom Kippur. The shift from accusation ("they") to confession ("we") is precisely the movement the Yom Kippur liturgy demands: "We have sinned, we have transgressed, we have acted wickedly." The alphabetical acrostic of the Al Chet prayer mirrors the comprehensiveness of Isaiah's catalog of sins. Significantly, the confession is always in the first person plural -- the individual identifies with the community's failures, even those they have not personally committed.

The divine warrior passage (59:16-20) is interpreted in Jewish eschatology as describing God's direct intervention at the end of days when human efforts at teshuvah (repentance) prove insufficient. The Redeemer who comes to Zion (59:20) is identified with the Messiah, and Paul's quotation of this verse in Romans 11:26 represents a point where Jewish and Christian readings momentarily converge, though they diverge on the identity of the Redeemer. The covenant promise in verse 21 -- God's Spirit and words will not depart from Israel "from now on and forever" -- grounds the Jewish conviction that Torah is eternal and irrevocable.
