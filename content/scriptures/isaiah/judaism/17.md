## The Oracle Against Damascus

Isaiah 17 pronounces judgment on Damascus (Syria/Aram) and its ally Ephraim. Rashi connects this oracle to the specific historical events of the Syro-Ephraimite war and Tiglath-Pileser III's conquest of Damascus in 732 BCE. Jewish interpretation emphasizes the immediate historical fulfillment as evidence of prophetic authenticity.

## The Glory of Jacob Fading

"The glory of Jacob will fade" (17:4). Jewish commentators read this as a warning specifically to the northern kingdom, whose alliance with Damascus sealed its fate. The Targum applies the metaphor of wasting flesh to the progressive loss of territory and population that Ephraim suffered in the decades before the Assyrian conquest of Samaria in 722 BCE.

## Turning to the Holy One of Israel

"In that day people will look to their Maker" (17:7). Jewish theology of suffering holds that one of God's purposes in judgment is to redirect human attention from idols to the living God. Rashi notes that the abandonment of the Asherah poles and incense altars (17:8) represents a genuine turning — not merely the absence of idols but the positive reorientation of worship toward the One God.
