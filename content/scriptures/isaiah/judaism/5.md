## Shirat HaKerem — The Song of the Vineyard

Isaiah 5:1-7 is one of the most artful *mashalim* (parables) in the Tanakh. Rashi identifies the vineyard as the people of Israel and the vinedresser as God, noting that the parable leads the listener to pronounce judgment on themselves — a technique also used by Nathan's parable to David (2 Samuel 12). The pun in 5:7 — *mishpat/mispach* (justice/bloodshed), *tzedakah/tze'akah* (righteousness/outcry) — is considered one of the finest examples of prophetic wordplay.

## The Six Woes

The woe oracles (5:8-23) are read in Jewish tradition as a comprehensive catalog of social sins. The Talmud draws on several of these woes in its legal discussions: the prohibition of land-grabbing (5:8) informs halakhic discussions of property law; the condemnation of those who "call evil good and good evil" (5:20) is cited in discussions of judicial corruption. Abarbanel reads the woes as describing the progressive moral decline that leads to national catastrophe.

## The Distant Nation Summoned

"He lifts up a banner for the distant nations" (5:26). Jewish interpretation identifies the "distant nation" with Assyria (and later Babylon), understanding God's use of foreign powers as instruments of judgment as a painful but consistent feature of divine governance. The midrash notes that God's sovereignty extends even to nations that do not acknowledge Him — a principle that comforts Israel in exile by affirming that persecution does not indicate divine absence.
