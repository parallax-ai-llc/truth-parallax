## Leviathan Destroyed

"In that day the LORD will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent" (27:1). Jewish interpretation identifies Leviathan with the primordial sea monster of chaos (cf. Job 41; Psalm 74:14). Rashi and the Talmud (Bava Batra 75a) describe God's eschatological slaying of Leviathan, whose flesh will be served at the messianic banquet (see chapter 25). The destruction of Leviathan symbolizes God's final victory over all forces of chaos and evil.

## The Restored Vineyard

"In that day — 'a fruitful vineyard, sing about it!'" (27:2). Jewish interpretation sees this as the reversal of chapter 5's condemned vineyard. The restored vineyard is Israel in the messianic age — purified, protected, and fruitful. God's anger is removed (27:4), and the vineyard is "watered continually" (27:3). The passage expresses the Jewish conviction that God's commitment to Israel is permanent despite temporary judgment.

## The Great Shofar

"In that day a great *shofar* will sound" (27:13). Jewish liturgical life resonates deeply with this verse. The *shofar* blown on Rosh Hashanah anticipates the eschatological *shofar* that will signal the ingathering of exiles and the inauguration of the messianic age. The daily *Amidah* includes the prayer "Sound the great *shofar* for our freedom, and raise a banner to gather our exiles," drawing directly on this Isaianic promise.
