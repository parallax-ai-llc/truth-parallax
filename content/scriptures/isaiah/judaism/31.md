## Flesh and Not God

"The Egyptians are human, and not God; their horses are flesh, and not spirit" (31:3). Jewish theology identifies this as one of the sharpest statements of the Creator-creature distinction in the Tanakh. Rashi emphasizes that the verse is not merely political advice but a theological declaration: no created power — however impressive — is comparable to God. The verse grounds the first of the Rambam's Thirteen Principles of Faith: the absolute unity and uniqueness of God.

## The Lion Over Its Prey

"As a lion growls, a great lion over its prey" (31:4). Rashi reads this as a simile for God's fierce determination to protect Jerusalem: just as a lion cannot be driven from its kill by shepherds' shouts, God will not be deterred from His purpose by any opposing force. The image conveys not gentleness but fierce, protective love.

## Teshuvah and the Destruction of Idols

"Return to Him from whom the Israelites have so greatly revolted" (31:6). Jewish theology of *teshuvah* (repentance) sees this as a direct call to return to God accompanied by concrete action — the destruction of idols (31:7). The Rambam teaches (*Hilkhot Teshuvah* 2:2) that genuine repentance requires not only regret and confession but the abandonment of the sinful behavior — precisely the pattern Isaiah describes.

## The Sword Not of Mortals

"Assyria will fall by no human sword" (31:8). Rashi connects this to the miraculous destruction of Sennacherib's army (2 Kings 19:35). Jewish theology of salvation emphasizes that the decisive acts of deliverance in Israel's history are accomplished by God, not by human military prowess — from the Exodus to the fall of Sennacherib.
