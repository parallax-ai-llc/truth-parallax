Isaiah 40 is the most liturgically prominent chapter of the entire book in Jewish life, serving as the first of the seven haftarot of consolation (shiva d'nechemta) read on the Shabbat following Tisha B'Av (Shabbat Nachamu). The opening words "Nachamu, nachamu ami" ("Comfort, comfort my people," 40:1) give this Shabbat its name and mark the transition from the mourning of Tisha B'Av to the anticipation of redemption. The doubled imperative is explained by the Midrash (Pesikta de-Rav Kahana) as corresponding to the double portion of suffering Israel has endured.

The "voice crying in the wilderness" (40:3) is interpreted in Jewish exegesis as referring to the prophetic call to prepare for the return from exile, not as a prediction of any individual forerunner. Rashi reads the verse as "A voice calls: In the wilderness, prepare the way of the LORD" -- the preparation takes place in the wilderness, not the voice. Ibn Ezra similarly connects it to the historical return from Babylon. This reading differs fundamentally from the Christian interpretation and represents one of the classic points of Jewish-Christian exegetical divergence.

The meditation on God's incomparability (40:12-26) is foundational to Jewish theology's understanding of divine transcendence. Maimonides built his entire negative theology on passages like "To whom will you compare God?" (40:18), arguing in the Guide for the Perplexed that God can only be described by what He is not. The closing promise (40:31) -- "those who hope in the LORD (kovei Hashem) will renew their strength" -- uses the Hebrew root k-v-h, which connotes not passive waiting but active, tense expectation, like a rope pulled taut. Jewish commentators see here the spiritual posture appropriate to exile: alert, expectant, trusting.
