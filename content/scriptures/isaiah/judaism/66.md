Isaiah 66 brings the prophetic book to its conclusion with themes that span the entire work: judgment and salvation, Temple and cosmos, Israel and the nations. The opening declaration -- "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool. Where is the house you will build for me?" (66:1) -- is interpreted by Jewish commentators not as rejecting the Temple but as relativizing it. The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 34:1) teaches that God does not need a physical dwelling but commanded the Temple's construction so that the divine presence (Shekhinah) would have a designated meeting place with Israel. Solomon expressed the same insight at the Temple's dedication: "The heavens, even the highest heaven, cannot contain you" (1 Kings 8:27).

The image of Zion giving birth to a nation in a day (66:7-8) is applied in Jewish eschatological thought to the sudden and miraculous nature of the final redemption. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) debates whether the Messiah will come suddenly or gradually, and this verse supports the "sudden" view. The birth metaphor also carries the connotation of labor pains (chevlei mashiach) -- the suffering that precedes redemption, a concept that has been tragically relevant throughout Jewish history.

The book's closing vision -- universal worship (66:23) alongside the fate of the rebellious (66:24) -- is interpreted in Jewish liturgical practice with a characteristic modification. When Isaiah 66 is read as a haftarah, verse 23 is repeated after verse 24 so that the reading ends on a note of hope rather than judgment. This practice (attested in the Talmud, Megillah 31a, for other prophetic books as well) reflects the rabbinic conviction that the final word of prophecy should be consolation, not condemnation -- a principle that expresses Judaism's fundamental orientation toward hope and redemption.
