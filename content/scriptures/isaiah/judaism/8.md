## Maher-shalal-hash-baz

Isaiah names his son "Quick to the plunder, swift to the spoil" (8:1) as a public sign of Assyria's imminent conquest of Syria and Samaria. Rashi notes that the name is recorded on a large scroll and witnessed by reliable witnesses — Uriah the priest and Zechariah — establishing legal evidence that the prophecy preceded the event. Jewish interpretation emphasizes the evidentiary function: the prophet's word is publicly verifiable.

## The Gentle Waters of Shiloah

"Because this people has rejected the gently flowing waters of Shiloah" (8:6). Rashi interprets the "waters of Shiloah" as the quiet, unassuming sovereignty of the Davidic dynasty — rejected in favor of flashy alliances with foreign powers. The midrash reads this as a parable of Torah: the gentle, life-giving waters of Torah study are rejected in favor of the thundering torrents of worldly philosophy and power.

## Conspiracy and Fear

"Do not call conspiracy everything this people calls a conspiracy" (8:12). Jewish interpretation applies this to the prophet's refusal to participate in popular panic. Rashi reads this as God's instruction not to fear the Syro-Ephraimite alliance. The principle extends beyond the immediate situation: the person of faith is called to a different assessment of threats than the surrounding culture.

## To the Torah and the Testimony

"To the law (*Torah*) and to the testimony!" (8:20). Jewish interpretation reads this as the definitive criterion for evaluating all claims: does it accord with Torah? Rashi sees this as a rebuke against those who consult mediums and spiritists (8:19) rather than the written and oral Torah. The verse grounds the rabbinic principle that all authentic teaching must ultimately be rooted in Torah.
