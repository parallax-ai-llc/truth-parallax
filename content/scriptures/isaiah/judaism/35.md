Isaiah 35 is read in Jewish tradition as a vision of the messianic redemption (geulah), when the exiles will return to the Land of Israel in joy. The desert blooming (35:1-2) is understood literally as referring to the transformation of the Land of Israel, which rabbinic literature describes as becoming desolate during exile and flourishing upon Israel's return. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) cites the land's agricultural productivity as one of the signs of the approaching messianic age.

The healing miracles of verses 5-6 -- blind eyes opened, deaf ears unstopped, the lame leaping -- are interpreted in mainstream Jewish exegesis as describing the physical restoration that will accompany the messianic era, not as christological predictions. Maimonides, in his Laws of Kings (12:1), describes the messianic age as a time when the natural order is perfected but not fundamentally altered. Other authorities, particularly those influenced by kabbalistic thought, read these healings as both physical and spiritual: the blindness and deafness of exile will give way to clear perception of divine truth.

The "Way of Holiness" (35:8) is connected by Jewish commentators to the concept of the messianic highway on which the exiles will travel home. Radak interprets this as the miraculous road God will prepare through the wilderness, free from predators and dangers (35:9), recalling the original exodus route. The "ransomed of the LORD" (35:10) who return to Zion with "everlasting joy" describes the ingathering of exiles (kibbutz galuyot) that remains a central element of Jewish messianic hope and daily liturgical prayer.
