Isaiah 54's image of the "barren woman" who rejoices with many children (54:1) is read in Jewish tradition as referring to Jerusalem/Zion, desolate during exile but destined for restoration. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) connects the barren woman to several matriarchs -- Sarah, Rebecca, Rachel -- whose initial barrenness was reversed by divine intervention. This pattern of barrenness-to-fertility functions in Jewish theology as a paradigm for exile and redemption: what appears permanently desolate is precisely the site of God's surprising intervention.

The covenant of peace (54:10) -- "Though the mountains be shaken and the hills be removed, yet my unfailing love (chesed) for you will not be shaken" -- is one of the most important statements of covenantal permanence in the Hebrew Bible. Jewish theology draws on this verse to affirm that the covenant with Israel is irrevocable, regardless of Israel's failures or the length of exile. Ramban develops this extensively: God's chesed is not conditional on Israel's merit but is rooted in God's own faithfulness to His promises. The verse is recited in times of crisis as an affirmation of unbreakable divine commitment.

The promise that "no weapon forged against you will prevail" (54:17) has been a source of strength throughout Jewish history. During the Crusades, the Inquisition, and the pogroms, Jewish communities drew on this verse as assurance that the covenant people would survive every attempt at their destruction. The verse's liturgical use in various Jewish prayer traditions reflects the ongoing experience of threat and the corresponding trust in divine protection.
