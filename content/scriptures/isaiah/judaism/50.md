Isaiah 50's third Servant passage (50:4-9) is read in Jewish tradition as the voice of the faithful remnant that endures suffering for its commitment to Torah and the covenant. The Servant who "offered my back to those who beat me" (50:6) is understood as Israel enduring persecution from the nations -- a reading that gained tremendous poignancy during the Crusades, the Inquisition, the pogroms, and the Shoah. The medieval piyyut (liturgical poetry) tradition frequently echoes this verse in compositions commemorating martyrdom.

The Servant's morning-by-morning attentiveness -- "He wakens my ear to listen like one being instructed" (50:4) -- is connected by Jewish commentators to the daily practice of Torah study. The Shema's command to "teach them diligently to your children and speak of them when you sit in your house, when you walk by the way, when you lie down, and when you rise up" (Deuteronomy 6:7) establishes the rhythm of learning that the Servant embodies. Rashi interprets "morning by morning" as the regularity of prophetic instruction that Israel receives through the ongoing study of Torah.

The Servant's confidence -- "He who vindicates me is near" (50:8) -- expresses the Jewish theological conviction that ultimate vindication is certain even when present circumstances suggest otherwise. Nahmanides (Ramban), in his account of the Barcelona Disputation (1263), argued that Jewish suffering does not disprove Jewish election but confirms it: the servant suffers precisely because of his fidelity to God. The closing warning against those who "walk by the light of your own fires" (50:11) is interpreted as a caution against replacing divine Torah with human-devised systems of meaning.
