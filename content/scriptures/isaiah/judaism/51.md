Isaiah 51's call to "look to the rock from which you were cut" (51:1) is understood in Jewish tradition as a call to remember Abraham and Sarah (mentioned by name in 51:2), the founding parents whose faith established the covenant. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 14:6) draws on this verse to teach that God "quarried" Abraham from the mass of humanity as one quarries stone from a mountain -- selecting him not because of inherent superiority but because of his responsiveness to the divine call.

The creation-combat imagery of verse 9 -- "Was it not you who cut Rahab to pieces?" -- is interpreted by Jewish commentators through the lens of the exodus, where "Rahab" represents Egypt (as in Psalm 87:4 and 89:10). The plea for God to "awake" and act "as in days of old" expresses the Jewish hope that the future redemption will recapitulate and surpass the exodus. The Passover Haggadah's extended meditation on the exodus serves this very function: by remembering past redemption, the community cultivates hope for future deliverance.

The "cup of wrath" (51:17, 22) that God takes from Jerusalem's hand and gives to her tormentors expresses the measure-for-measure principle (middah k'neged middah) that Jewish theology sees operating throughout history. The nations that made Israel drink the cup of suffering will themselves drink it. This is not understood as vindictiveness but as divine justice (din) operating within history. The Midrash connects this to the prophetic principle that God uses nations as instruments of judgment but holds them accountable for exceeding their mandate.
