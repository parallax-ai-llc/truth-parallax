Jewish interpretation of Isaiah 34 reads this oracle of judgment against Edom through the lens of Israel's long and bitter relationship with Esau's descendants. In rabbinic literature, "Edom" became a code name for Rome and, by extension, for the Christian empires that persecuted the Jewish people. The Talmud (Megillah 6a) and various midrashim read references to Edom's destruction as prophecies of Rome's downfall, providing eschatological hope during centuries of exile and persecution.

Rashi interprets the cosmic imagery of verse 4 -- stars falling and heavens rolling up -- as poetic expressions of national catastrophe rather than literal astronomical events. Ibn Ezra similarly reads the chapter as referring to the historical destruction of Edom's territory, noting the geographical accuracy of the desolate landscape described in verses 11-15. The mention of specific animals inhabiting the ruins (34:11-15) demonstrates the prophet's familiarity with the fauna of the Arabah and Negev regions.

The "scroll of the LORD" (34:16) is interpreted by Radak (Rabbi David Kimchi) as referring to the prophetic record itself -- Isaiah invites future generations to verify that his predictions have come true. This hermeneutical move is characteristic of Jewish exegesis: prophecy is not abstract theology but testable prediction rooted in historical events. The chapter's placement before chapter 35's vision of restoration follows the rabbinic principle that prophecies of consolation follow prophecies of judgment (Talmud, Berakhot 31a).
