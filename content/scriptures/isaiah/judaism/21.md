## The Desert by the Sea

Isaiah 21 contains three enigmatic oracles. Jewish commentators generally identify the "Desert by the Sea" with Babylon, following the Targum. Rashi notes the prophet's intense suffering — "my body is racked with pain" (21:3) — as evidence that prophetic vision is not comfortable revelation but agonizing participation in divine judgment.

## The Watchman's Report

"Day after day, my lord, I stand on the watchtower" (21:8). Jewish interpretation sees the watchman as a metaphor for the prophet himself, stationed by God to observe and report on the movements of history. The midrash extends this to the role of the sage and teacher in every generation: those who watch over the community must be vigilant and honest in reporting what they see.

## Morning and Night

"Watchman, what is left of the night?" The answer — "Morning is coming, but also the night" (21:12) — is one of the most enigmatic statements in Isaiah. Jewish commentators read this as a characteristically prophetic refusal to offer simple comfort: redemption is coming, but further suffering lies ahead first. Radak interprets this as a response to Edom/Seir, promising that while morning (deliverance) will come for Israel, night (judgment) awaits Edom.
