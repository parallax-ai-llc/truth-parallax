Isaiah 65 opens with God declaring "I was found by those who did not seek me" (65:1), which Jewish commentators read as referring to God's gracious availability to Israel even when they had not sought Him wholeheartedly. Rashi identifies the seekers as the returning exiles whom God met with favor despite their imperfect repentance. This contrasts with the Christian reading (via Romans 10:20) that applies the verse to Gentile inclusion -- a classic point of exegetical divergence between the traditions.

The vision of "new heavens and a new earth" (65:17) is foundational to the Jewish concept of Olam Ha-Ba (the World to Come). Maimonides (Laws of Repentance 8:1-8) distinguishes between the messianic age (a historical period of peace and Torah observance) and the World to Come (a transcendent reality beyond physical description). The "new creation" of Isaiah 65 may describe either or both, depending on how literally the life conditions of verses 20-23 are interpreted. The description of long life and productive labor (65:20-22) fits the messianic age better than the purely spiritual World to Come.

The wolf and lamb feeding together (65:25) echoes the peaceable kingdom of Isaiah 11:6-9. Maimonides (Laws of Kings 12:1) takes this metaphorically: in the messianic age, Israel will live in peace with the nations that previously oppressed them, "the wolf" representing previously hostile peoples. Other authorities, including some kabbalists and Ramban, read it literally: the messianic age will involve a genuine transformation of the natural order, restoring the harmony that existed before Adam's sin. This debate reflects the broader Jewish discussion about the degree of supernatural change the messianic age will bring.
