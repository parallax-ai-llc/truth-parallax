Isaiah 61 describes an anointed figure who proclaims liberty to captives and comfort to mourners. Jewish interpretation identifies this figure as the prophet Isaiah himself or as a representative of Israel's prophetic tradition, rather than as the Messiah specifically. Rashi reads the anointing (61:1) as the prophetic gift that enables Isaiah to bring God's message of consolation to the exiles. Ibn Ezra similarly sees the speaker as the prophet commissioned for a specific historical mission.

The "year of the LORD's favor" (61:2) is connected by Jewish commentators to the Jubilee (yovel) legislation of Leviticus 25, when debts were canceled, slaves freed, and land returned to its original owners. This socioeconomic legislation, unique in the ancient world, enshrined the principle that ultimate ownership of the land belongs to God, not to human proprietors. The messianic age is understood as the ultimate Jubilee -- the restoration of all things to their proper order. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 8b-9a) discusses the Jubilee's halakhic details, connecting its liberation theology to Isaiah's prophetic vision.

The promise that Israel will be called "priests of the LORD" and "ministers of our God" (61:6) extends to the entire nation the priestly vocation originally assigned to the tribe of Levi. Jewish theology understands this as describing Israel's ultimate purpose: to serve as a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6) in the messianic age. The rebuilding of "ancient ruins" (61:4) is read literally as the restoration of the Land of Israel and figuratively as the renewal of Torah study and observance after the devastation of exile.
