## Light in the Land of Darkness

"The people walking in darkness have seen a great light" (9:1). Jewish interpretation identifies the "people walking in darkness" as the northern tribes of Zebulun and Naphtali, who were the first to suffer Assyrian conquest (2 Kings 15:29). Rashi reads the "great light" as the deliverance experienced during Hezekiah's reign, when Sennacherib's army was miraculously destroyed. The light is thus historical rather than messianic in the primary Jewish reading.

## The Child Born — Hezekiah

"For to us a child is born, to us a son is given" (9:5, Hebrew numbering). Jewish interpretation consistently identifies this child as King Hezekiah, one of Judah's most righteous kings. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 94a) records a tradition that God wished to make Hezekiah the Messiah and Sennacherib the Gog and Magog, but the attribute of Justice argued against it because Hezekiah did not compose songs of praise despite the miracles performed for him.

## The Throne Names

The four names — "Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace" — are read by Jewish commentators as describing God's actions on behalf of the king, not the king's own nature. Rashi interprets: "The Wonderful Counselor, the Mighty God, the Everlasting Father, has called his name 'Prince of Peace.'" The names are thus attributed to God as subject, with only "Prince of Peace" applying to the human king. This interpretation directly counters the Christian reading that ascribes divine titles to the child.

## The Outstretched Hand

The judgment section (9:7-20) with its refrain "his hand is still outstretched" describes God's escalating punishment of the northern kingdom. Rashi reads the historical sequence carefully: foreign invasion, civil war, famine, and ultimately the total destruction of the northern kingdom. The refrain indicates that each judgment was meant to provoke repentance, but the people refused to turn.
