The designation of Cyrus as God's "anointed" (mashiach, 45:1) is unique in the Hebrew Bible -- no other Gentile receives this title. Jewish commentators grapple with its implications carefully. Rashi explains that "anointed" here means simply "appointed for a task," not that Cyrus receives the same sanctity as a Jewish king or the future Messiah. Radak similarly minimizes the term's theological weight, emphasizing that God's choice of Cyrus demonstrates sovereignty over all nations rather than any special spiritual status for the Persian emperor.

The declaration "I form the light and create darkness, I make peace and create evil (ra)" (45:7) has generated extensive discussion in Jewish theology. This verse is incorporated into the daily morning liturgy (Birkat Yotzer Or) in modified form: "Who forms light and creates darkness, who makes peace and creates everything (ha-kol)" -- the rabbis substituted "everything" for "evil" to avoid suggesting that God actively creates moral evil. The Talmud (Berakhot 11b) explains this liturgical modification while acknowledging the verse's original meaning: God's sovereignty encompasses all of reality, including the conditions that humans experience as negative.

The universal call to salvation (45:22) is read by Jewish commentators as an invitation to all nations to acknowledge the one God of Israel. This is not understood as a call to convert to Judaism but to abandon idolatry and embrace the Noachide laws -- the seven universal commandments that Jewish tradition holds binding on all humanity. Maimonides (Laws of Kings 8:10-11) teaches that righteous Gentiles who observe these laws have a share in the World to Come.
