Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 62 emphasizes the bridal and covenantal imagery as expressing the relationship between Christ and the Church. The name "Hephzibah" — "my delight is in her" (62:4) — is applied to the Church as the Bride of Christ, a theme developed extensively in Catholic ecclesiology and mystical theology. The Song of Solomon, Isaiah's bridal imagery, and Ephesians 5 form a continuous tradition that the Catholic mystical writers — Bernard, John of the Cross, Teresa of Avila — explore in their commentaries on the soul's union with God.

The watchmen on the walls (62:6-7) who pray without ceasing are understood as a type of the contemplative religious orders whose primary vocation is intercessory prayer. The Carmelite, Carthusian, and Poor Clare traditions embody this continuous intercession that Isaiah describes. Catholic teaching holds that the prayers of contemplatives sustain the Church's active mission — a conviction expressed in Therese of Lisieux's designation as co-patron of missions despite never leaving her Carmelite convent.

The command to "prepare the way for the people" and "build up the highway" (62:10) is applied in Catholic pastoral theology to the work of catechesis and evangelization. Just as roads must be built for the people to travel, so the Church must provide the catechetical and sacramental infrastructure that enables people to encounter Christ. The RCIA (Rite of Christian Initiation of Adults) process embodies this highway-building: a structured path of formation that prepares catechumens to enter the Church through Baptism, Confirmation, and Eucharist.
