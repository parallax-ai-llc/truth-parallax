## The Vision of the Holy God

Isaiah 6 describes the prophet's encounter with God enthroned in the Temple. Catholic liturgical theology recognizes this as the foundational theophany behind the *Sanctus* of the Mass: "Holy, holy, holy Lord, God of hosts." The threefold acclamation of holiness has been sung in every Eucharistic liturgy since the earliest centuries. St. Jerome and St. Ambrose both interpreted the Trisagion as a Trinitarian confession — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit each addressed as "Holy."

## Confession and Cleansing

Isaiah's response — "Woe to me! I am ruined! For I am a man of unclean lips" (6:5) — models the penitential act that precedes Eucharistic participation. Catholic sacramental theology sees the burning coal from the altar as a type of the Eucharist: the body and blood of Christ, taken from the altar of sacrifice, purifies the communicant. St. Cyril of Jerusalem used this image in his *Mystagogical Catecheses* to explain the transforming power of Holy Communion.

## The Commission and Its Paradox

God's commission — "Go and tell this people: Be ever hearing, but never understanding" (6:9-10) — presents the mystery of divine hardening. Catholic theology, following Aquinas, distinguishes between God's permissive will (allowing hearts to harden through their own resistance) and God's active will. The passage does not teach that God creates unbelief but that persistent refusal of grace produces a judicial blindness that the prophet is sent to expose.

## The Holy Seed

"The holy seed is its stump" (6:13). Catholic ecclesiology finds here the principle of indefectibility: the Church, like the stump, may be reduced to apparent insignificance but can never be fully destroyed. The holy seed that survives judgment becomes the foundation of renewed life — a pattern fulfilled in the death and resurrection of Christ and in the Church's survival through every persecution.
