The second Servant Song (49:1-7) occupies a central place in Catholic theology, particularly in its Marian dimensions. The Servant's call "from the womb" (49:1) is connected to Catholic teaching on vocation and predestination — the belief that God calls each person to a specific mission before birth (cf. Jeremiah 1:5). The Catechism teaches that every human life has a divine purpose discernible through prayer, spiritual direction, and attentiveness to the signs of the times (CCC 2567).

Catholic Marian theology finds in 49:1 a type of Mary's own vocation. Just as the Servant is named and called from the womb, so Mary was prepared by the Immaculate Conception for her role as Theotokos (Mother of God). The declaration "You are my servant, Israel, in whom I will display my splendor" (49:3) is applied to the Church and, by extension, to Mary as the Church's exemplary member and type (*Lumen Gentium* 53).

The maternal imagery of verses 14-16 — "Can a mother forget her nursing child?" — is among the most important texts in Catholic theology for understanding divine love. Catholic tradition has never hesitated to apply feminine and maternal images to God, as seen in Julian of Norwich's theology and in the writings of Clement of Alexandria. Pope John Paul I famously stated that "God is Father but even more so God is Mother," drawing on precisely this Isaianic tradition. The promise "I have engraved you on the palms of my hands" (49:16) is read in Catholic devotion as anticipating Christ's wounds, which become signs of eternal love.
