Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 34 reads this oracle of judgment against the nations through the lens of the Church's eschatological teaching. The Fathers of the Church, particularly Jerome, who lived near Edom and knew the landscape firsthand, understood the desolation described in verses 5-15 as both a historical prophecy against Edom and a typological foreshadowing of the Last Judgment. The Catechism's teaching on the "four last things" — death, judgment, heaven, and hell — finds resonance in this chapter's imagery of blood-soaked earth and burning pitch (34:9).

The summons "Come near, you nations, to hear" (34:1) is read in Catholic liturgical tradition as a call to universal accountability before God. Vatican II's *Lumen Gentium* affirms that all peoples stand under God's judgment and mercy alike. The "scroll of the LORD" in verse 16, which guarantees that every word will be fulfilled, is connected to Catholic teaching on the inerrancy and authority of Sacred Scripture as articulated in *Dei Verbum*.

The ecological devastation described — thorns, nettles, wild animals inhabiting ruins — is understood by patristic commentators as the consequence of sin's corrosive effect on creation itself. This resonates with Pope Francis's *Laudato Si'*, which teaches that environmental degradation is linked to moral and spiritual disorder. The chapter thus speaks to the Catholic understanding of integral ecology rooted in prophetic tradition.
