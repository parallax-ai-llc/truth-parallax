Isaiah 40 holds a privileged place in Catholic liturgy as the great Advent text. "Comfort, comfort my people" (40:1) is proclaimed during the Advent season as the Church prepares to celebrate the Incarnation. The "voice crying in the wilderness" (40:3) is identified with John the Baptist, whose role as precursor to Christ is central to Catholic understanding of salvation history. The Catechism teaches that John completes the cycle of prophets begun by Elijah (CCC 523).

The chapter's meditation on God's transcendence — "Who has measured the waters in the hollow of his hand?" (40:12) — resonates with the Catholic intellectual tradition's emphasis on the via negativa: God is known partly through recognizing what He is not. The Fourth Lateran Council (1215) taught that between Creator and creature, no similarity can be noted without noting an even greater dissimilarity. Isaiah 40's rhetorical questions embody this principle, pushing human understanding toward the ineffable mystery of God.

The closing promise that "those who hope in the LORD will renew their strength" (40:31) is understood in Catholic spirituality through the theological virtue of hope. Unlike mere optimism, hope in the Catholic sense is a supernatural virtue infused at baptism, directing the soul toward God as its final end. The eagle imagery (40:31) appears in Catholic mystical writers like John of the Cross, who used it to describe the soul's ascent toward divine union. The Catechism lists hope among the three theological virtues (CCC 1817-1821), and Isaiah 40 provides its Old Testament foundation.
