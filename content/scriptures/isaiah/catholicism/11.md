## The Shoot from Jesse

"A shoot will come up from the stump of Jesse" (11:1). Catholic Christology sees this as a key messianic prophecy fulfilled in Jesus of Nazareth, who comes not from royal splendor but from humble origins. The choice of Jesse rather than David emphasizes that the messianic line has been cut back to its roots — a humiliation that prefigures the kenosis (self-emptying) of the Incarnation. Catholic art has represented this passage in the "Tree of Jesse" motif found in medieval manuscripts and cathedral windows.

## The Seven Gifts of the Spirit

Catholic theology derives from Isaiah 11:2-3 the doctrine of the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit: wisdom, understanding, counsel, fortitude, knowledge, piety, and fear of the Lord. The Catechism (CCC 1831) teaches that these gifts complete and perfect the virtues of those who receive them, making the faithful "docile in readily obeying divine inspirations." The sacrament of Confirmation confers these gifts in a special way, and the prayer for the sevenfold Spirit is drawn directly from this passage.

## The Peaceable Kingdom

"The wolf will live with the lamb" (11:6). Catholic eschatology reads this as a description of the final restoration of all creation. *Laudato Si'* (2015) draws on Isaianic cosmic peace to articulate the integral ecology that connects care for creation with care for the poor. The peaceable kingdom is not merely a spiritual metaphor but envisions the actual reconciliation of the created order.

## The Second Exodus

The ingathering of the remnant from "the four quarters of the earth" (11:12) is read by Catholic missiology as a type of the Church's universal mission. The second exodus surpasses the first: God gathers not just Israel but people from every nation into the new covenant community.
