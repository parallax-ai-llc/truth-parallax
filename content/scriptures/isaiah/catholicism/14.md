## The Taunt Song Against Babylon's King

Isaiah 14 presents a *mashal* (taunt) over the fallen Babylonian monarch. "How you have fallen from heaven, morning star, son of the dawn!" (14:12). Catholic tradition, following the Fathers — particularly Origen, Tertullian, and Augustine — has long read this as a veiled description of Satan's fall from glory. While modern Catholic scholarship acknowledges the primary historical referent as the Babylonian king, the typological reading remains valid within Catholic hermeneutics: the king embodies the same pride that caused Lucifer's fall.

## The Five "I Wills" of Pride

"I will ascend to the heavens; I will raise my throne above the stars of God; I will sit enthroned on the mount of assembly" (14:13-14). Catholic moral theology identifies five assertions of autonomous will as the essence of *superbia*: the attempt to usurp God's place. This passage has shaped Catholic understanding of the first and greatest sin — the sin that precedes and generates all others.

## Sheol and the Limits of Power

The depiction of the king's arrival in Sheol, where former rulers rise to mock him (14:9-11), serves in Catholic teaching as a meditation on death as the great equalizer. Catholic funeral liturgy's reminder — "dust you are, and to dust you shall return" — echoes the prophetic mockery of earthly pretension. No power survives the encounter with mortality except the power of God.
