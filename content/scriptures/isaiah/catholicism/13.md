## The Day of the Lord Against Babylon

Isaiah 13 opens the oracles against the nations with a sweeping prophecy of Babylon's destruction. Catholic eschatological interpretation, following the patristic tradition, reads Babylon at two levels: as a historical empire and as a type of every civilization that sets itself against God. Augustine's *De Civitate Dei* makes Babylon the archetype of the "earthly city" governed by self-love rather than love of God.

## Cosmic Upheaval

The cosmic imagery — darkened stars, shaking heavens, trembling earth (13:10-13) — is read by Catholic theology as pointing beyond any single historical event to the final judgment. The *Dies Irae* hymn of the medieval Latin liturgy draws on this Isaianic imagery. Catholic eschatology maintains that these signs will accompany Christ's second coming, as Jesus Himself teaches in the Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24:29).

## The Sanctified Warriors

"I have commanded those I prepared for battle; I have summoned my warriors to carry out my wrath" (13:3). Catholic theology of providence understands this as God directing the course of history through secondary causes. The Medes and Persians who overthrow Babylon serve God's purposes without knowing it — a principle that extends to all of history. The Church reads all events, including catastrophic ones, within the framework of divine providence.
