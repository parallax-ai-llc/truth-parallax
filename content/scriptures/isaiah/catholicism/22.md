## The Valley of Vision — Jerusalem Judged

Isaiah 22 turns from foreign nations to Jerusalem itself. Catholic exegesis notes the ironic title "Valley of Vision" — the city that should see most clearly is blind to its own spiritual condition. The people feast while judgment approaches: "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!" (22:13). Catholic moral theology identifies this attitude as *desperatio* — despair masked as hedonism, the abandonment of hope that leads to moral dissolution.

## Shebna and Eliakim

The transfer of authority from the unfaithful steward Shebna to the faithful Eliakim (22:20-24) is of immense importance in Catholic theology. Jesus's words to Peter — "I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 16:19) — deliberately echo Isaiah 22:22: "I will place on his shoulder the key to the house of David." Catholic ecclesiology sees Eliakim as a type of Peter and of the papal office: the steward who holds the master's keys in his absence, with authority to open and shut.

## The Key of David

"What he opens no one can shut, and what he shuts no one can open" (22:22). The application of this verse to Christ in Revelation 3:7 and to Peter in Matthew 16:19 creates a chain of authority central to Catholic understanding: Christ possesses supreme authority, which He delegates to Peter and his successors as stewards of the kingdom until His return.
