Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 57 begins with the consoling observation that "the righteous perish, and no one takes it to heart... the righteous are taken away to be spared from evil" (57:1-2). This passage has been important in Catholic teaching on the mystery of death, particularly the deaths of the young or innocent. The Catechism teaches that God may in His providence call the righteous home before their time as an act of mercy, shielding them from greater suffering to come (cf. Wisdom 4:7-14, a deuterocanonical text that expands this theme).

The denunciation of idolatrous practices in verses 3-10, including child sacrifice and fertility worship, is read by Catholic moral theology as condemning not only ancient practices but their contemporary equivalents. The Magisterium's consistent teaching against abortion draws on the prophetic tradition's horror at the sacrifice of children. Pope John Paul II's *Evangelium Vitae* explicitly connects modern threats to life with the ancient idolatrous practices the prophets condemned.

The magnificent paradox of verse 15 — "I live in a high and holy place, but also with the one who is contrite and lowly in spirit" — is a cornerstone of Catholic mystical theology. The tradition from Augustine through Bernard of Clairvaux to Teresa of Avila teaches that God's transcendence does not preclude intimacy; rather, the humble heart becomes the dwelling place of the Almighty. This verse grounds the Catholic emphasis on humility as the foundational virtue, and the promise of peace to "the one who is far off and near" (57:19) anticipates the reconciliation effected by Christ (Ephesians 2:17).
