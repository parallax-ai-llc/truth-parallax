Isaiah 61 is the messianic manifesto that Jesus claims in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4:16-21), and Catholic Christology reads the entire chapter as a program for Christ's mission that continues through the Church. The anointing of the Spirit (61:1) is understood as the prototype of the sacramental anointings — Baptism, Confirmation, Holy Orders, and Anointing of the Sick — through which Christ's mission is communicated to the faithful. The Catechism identifies these as the "sacraments of initiation" and "sacraments of service" that configure the recipient to Christ's priestly, prophetic, and kingly offices.

The "year of the LORD's favor" (61:2) is connected in Catholic tradition to the Jubilee — a practice Pope Boniface VIII inaugurated in 1300 and which continues to mark the Church's life in regular intervals. The Holy Year of Mercy proclaimed by Pope Francis in 2015-2016 explicitly drew on Isaiah 61's language of liberation, debt-forgiveness, and new beginnings. The Jubilee tradition enacts Isaiah's vision liturgically, offering plenary indulgences as concrete expressions of God's liberating mercy.

The transformation of mourning into joy (61:3) is understood in Catholic spirituality through the theology of consolation developed by Ignatius of Loyola in his *Spiritual Exercises*. Consolation is not merely emotional relief but a movement of the soul toward God, marked by "increase of faith, hope, and love." The "garment of praise instead of a spirit of despair" corresponds to the white baptismal garment and to the vestments worn in the liturgy, which symbolize the new identity conferred by grace. The "oaks of righteousness" (61:3) represent the saints whose lives, rooted in Christ, bear fruit for the world.
