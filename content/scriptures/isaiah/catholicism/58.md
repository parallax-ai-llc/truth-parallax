Isaiah 58 is the definitive Old Testament text for Catholic social teaching's integration of worship and justice. The chapter is read on the Friday after Ash Wednesday, immediately orienting the Lenten fast toward its proper ends. God's rejection of a fast that ignores social injustice (58:3-5) and His definition of true fasting — "to loose the chains of injustice, to untie the cords of the yoke" (58:6) — is the scriptural foundation for the Catholic principle that liturgy without charity is empty formalism.

The specific works of mercy enumerated — feeding the hungry, sheltering the homeless, clothing the naked (58:7) — correspond precisely to the corporal works of mercy that Catholic tradition has codified: feed the hungry, give drink to the thirsty, clothe the naked, shelter the homeless, visit the sick, visit the imprisoned, bury the dead. These are not optional for Catholics but constitute an integral dimension of the faith. Pope Francis has added "care for creation" as an extension of this tradition, rooted in the prophetic insistence that right worship encompasses all of life.

The conditional promises of verses 8-12 — "Then your light will break forth like the dawn, and your healing will quickly appear" — are understood in Catholic theology as describing the fruits of authentic conversion. The light imagery connects to baptism (traditionally called "illumination" or *photismos* in the Eastern Catholic tradition) and to the role of the Church as "light of the world" (Matthew 5:14). Catholic social encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* to *Fratelli Tutti* all build on the prophetic conviction that social justice is not peripheral to faith but essential to it.
