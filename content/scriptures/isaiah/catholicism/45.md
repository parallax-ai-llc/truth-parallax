The designation of Cyrus as God's "anointed" (mashiach, 45:1) prompts extensive Catholic reflection on the nature of anointing and its sacramental significance. While Cyrus's anointing is metaphorical, the Catholic tradition of sacramental anointing — in Baptism, Confirmation, Holy Orders, and Anointing of the Sick — traces its theological roots to the biblical practice of anointing as a sign of divine commissioning. Christ, the ultimate "Anointed One," fulfills what Cyrus merely prefigures.

The declaration "I form the light and create darkness, I bring prosperity and create disaster" (45:7) engages Catholic theology at the point of theodicy. The Catechism teaches that God permits evil without being its author (CCC 311-314), drawing on Augustine and Aquinas who argued that evil is a privation of good rather than a positive creation. Catholic interpretation reads this verse as affirming God's sovereignty over all events while maintaining the moral distinction between what God directly wills and what He permits within His providential plan.

The universal scope of salvation — "Turn to me and be saved, all you ends of the earth" (45:22) — is foundational to the Catholic understanding of the Church's universal mission. *Ad Gentes*, Vatican II's decree on missionary activity, grounds the Church's obligation to evangelize in precisely this kind of Old Testament universalism. The Catholic Church understands itself as the sacrament of this universal salvation, not in the sense that all must be formally enrolled but that Christ's saving work, mediated through the Church, extends to all humanity.
