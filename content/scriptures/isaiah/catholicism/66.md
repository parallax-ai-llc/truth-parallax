Isaiah 66 brings the entire prophetic book to its climax with themes that pervade Catholic theology: the inadequacy of any temple to contain God (66:1), the birth of a new community (66:7-9), divine maternal comfort (66:13), and universal worship combined with final judgment (66:18-24). Stephen's quotation of 66:1 in Acts 7:49 does not, in Catholic interpretation, negate sacred space but rather universalizes it: the Church's many temples throughout the world are not containers of God but meeting places where heaven and earth intersect.

The image of Zion giving birth painlessly to a nation (66:7-8) is applied in Catholic Marian theology to Mary's role as Mother of the Church. Just as Zion brings forth children, so Mary, at the foot of the cross (John 19:26-27), becomes the spiritual mother of all believers. The Catechism teaches that Mary's motherhood extends to all members of Christ's body (CCC 963-975). The maternal comfort of verse 13 — "As a mother comforts her child, so will I comfort you" — grounds the Catholic practice of seeking Mary's intercession and maternal care.

The book's final vision — "all mankind will come and bow down before me" (66:23) juxtaposed with the unending punishment of the rebellious (66:24) — encapsulates Catholic eschatology's dual teaching on universal hope and the reality of hell. The Catechism affirms both that God "predestines no one to go to hell" (CCC 1037) and that hell is a real possibility for those who persist in mortal sin. The Church prays for the salvation of all (as in the Good Friday liturgy) while acknowledging the freedom to refuse God's mercy. Isaiah 66 thus closes with the same call to decision that runs through the entire Catholic reading of the prophets.
