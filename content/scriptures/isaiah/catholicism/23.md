## The Judgment of Commercial Power

Isaiah 23 concludes the oracles against the nations with a prophecy against Tyre, the great Phoenician commercial center. Catholic social teaching reads this oracle as a warning against the absolutization of economic power. Pope Francis's *Evangelii Gaudium* (2013) echoes Isaiah's critique when it warns against an "economy of exclusion" that idolizes profit and treats human beings as disposable.

## Tyre's Pride and God's Purpose

"The LORD Almighty planned it, to bring down her pride in all her splendor" (23:9). Catholic theology affirms that God's providence governs the economic sphere as much as the political. No market is autonomous, no wealth self-generated, and no commercial system exempt from moral judgment. The prophetic critique of Tyre anticipates the Church's consistent teaching that economic activity must serve the common good.

## The Sanctification of Commerce

The chapter's conclusion — Tyre's profits "set apart for the LORD" (23:18) — points toward the sanctification of all legitimate human activity. Catholic social teaching, particularly in *Laborem Exercens* (1981) and *Caritas in Veritate* (2009), affirms that work and commerce can be forms of participation in God's creative activity when directed toward justice and the common good. Tyre's wealth, once used for self-glorification, will be redirected to serve God's purposes.
