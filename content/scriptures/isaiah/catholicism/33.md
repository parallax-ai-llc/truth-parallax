## The Destroyer Destroyed

Isaiah 33 opens the final woe oracle with a principle of divine retribution: "When you stop destroying, you will be destroyed" (33:1). Catholic theology of divine justice affirms that God's justice is not arbitrary but corresponds to moral reality. The principle of proportionate justice — that agents receive what they have dealt to others — is articulated throughout Catholic moral teaching, from the Catechism to papal encyclicals on social justice.

## The Prayer for Grace

"LORD, be gracious to us; we long for you" (33:2). Catholic liturgical prayer is saturated with this Isaianic cry. The *Kyrie eleison* ("Lord, have mercy") that opens every Mass echoes the prophet's plea. Catholic spirituality of longing — expressed in Augustine's "Our hearts are restless until they rest in you" — finds its prophetic foundation in passages like this.

## Who Can Dwell with Consuming Fire?

"Who of us can dwell with the consuming fire?" (33:14). Catholic theology of purgatory draws on the biblical association of divine holiness with purifying fire. The answer — those who walk righteously and speak honestly (33:15) — is not a prescription for earning salvation but a description of the transformed character that results from grace. The passage corresponds to the Beatitudes: those who are pure in heart shall see God (Matthew 5:8).

## The King in His Beauty

"Your eyes will see the king in his beauty" (33:17). Catholic mystical theology reads this as an anticipation of the beatific vision — the direct, face-to-face encounter with God that is the ultimate destiny of the redeemed. The chapter — and the entire woe cycle — concludes with forgiveness: "the sins of those who dwell there will be forgiven" (33:24). Catholic soteriology holds together holiness and mercy: the God whose consuming fire terrifies sinners is the same God who forgives and heals, and both meet in the king whose beauty the redeemed will finally see.
