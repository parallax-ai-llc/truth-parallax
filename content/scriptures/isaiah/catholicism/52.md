Isaiah 52's proclamation "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news" (52:7) is foundational to the Catholic theology of evangelization. Pope Paul VI's apostolic exhortation *Evangelii Nuntiandi* (1975) and Pope Francis's *Evangelii Gaudium* (2013) both draw on this verse to articulate the Church's missionary identity. Every baptized Catholic is called to be a bearer of good news — this is not optional but constitutive of Christian identity.

The command "Depart, depart, go out from there!" (52:11) is read in Catholic tradition as the call to holiness that Vatican II extended to all the faithful, not just clergy and religious. *Lumen Gentium* chapter 5, "The Universal Call to Holiness," teaches that every Christian is called to depart from sin and pursue perfection. The "vessels of the LORD" (52:11) that must be carried in purity are connected to the sacred vessels of the liturgy and, by extension, to the bodies of the faithful as temples of the Holy Spirit.

The opening of the fourth Servant Song (52:13-15) — "He will be raised and lifted up and highly exalted" — is given a threefold christological reading in Catholic tradition: raised on the cross, lifted up in resurrection, and exalted in ascension. This corresponds to the Paschal Mystery that the Catechism presents as the center of Catholic faith (CCC 571-655). The "sprinkling of many nations" (52:15) is connected to baptismal aspersion and to the sprinkling rite (*Asperges me*) that opens the solemn Eucharistic liturgy.
