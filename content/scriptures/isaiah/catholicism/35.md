Isaiah 35's vision of the desert blooming and the ransomed returning to Zion occupies a significant place in the Catholic liturgical year. This chapter is read during Advent, when the Church prepares for both the commemoration of Christ's first coming and the anticipation of His return. The imagery of eyes opened and ears unstopped (35:5-6) is understood as fulfilled in Jesus's healing miracles, which the Church reads as signs of the Kingdom's irruption into history.

The "Way of Holiness" (35:8) is interpreted in Catholic tradition as a figure for the sacramental life of the Church, the path God provides for His pilgrim people. The Council of Trent's teaching on sanctification as a real transformation — not merely a forensic declaration — finds support in this passage where the redeemed walk a road specially prepared by God. The unclean shall not journey on it, but the redeemed shall walk there (35:8-9), reflecting the Church's understanding of baptismal regeneration as the entry point to the way of salvation.

The concluding promise of "everlasting joy" (35:10) where "sorrow and sighing will flee away" anticipates the beatific vision that Catholic theology describes as the ultimate destiny of the blessed. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 1023-1029) teaches that this joy is not merely emotional but ontological — the soul's participation in the very life of God. Patristic writers like Augustine saw in Isaiah 35 a preview of the heavenly Jerusalem.
