Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 47's taunt against Babylon reads it through the lens of the spiritual combat tradition. Babylon personified as a "virgin daughter" (47:1) who is brought low parallels the Church's teaching on the fate of pride in all its forms. The *Magnificat* — Mary's canticle in Luke 1:51-52, "He has brought down the powerful from their thrones and lifted up the lowly" — echoes precisely this Isaianic theme and is prayed daily at Vespers in the Liturgy of the Hours.

The condemnation of Babylon's sorcery and enchantments (47:12-13) reinforces the Catholic Church's consistent prohibition of occult practices. The Catechism explicitly condemns divination, magic, sorcery, and astrology (CCC 2115-2117), tracing this prohibition through Scripture from Deuteronomy to Isaiah to the Acts of the Apostles. Babylon's reliance on stargazers and astrologers (47:13) exemplifies the futility that Catholic teaching identifies in all attempts to manipulate supernatural powers outside the sacramental order.

The claim "I am, and there is none besides me" (47:8) represents for Catholic moral theology the sin of pride in its most radical form — the creature claiming divine prerogatives. The tradition of the Seven Deadly Sins places pride (*superbia*) first because it is the root of all other sins. Augustine identified this self-exaltation as the original sin of the angels and the fundamental disorder of the human will. Isaiah 47 dramatizes its consequences on a civilizational scale, providing scriptural warrant for the Church's persistent teaching that pride precedes destruction.
