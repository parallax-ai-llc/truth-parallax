Isaiah 51's call to "look to the rock from which you were cut" (51:1) is interpreted in Catholic tradition as an invitation to remember the foundations of faith — Abraham's obedience, the patriarchal covenant, and the continuous chain of tradition connecting the present community to its origins. Catholic ecclesiology emphasizes apostolic succession as precisely this kind of rock: the unbroken link between the contemporary Church and the apostles whom Christ commissioned.

The creation imagery in verse 9 — "Awake, awake, arm of the LORD" — draws on mythological language that Catholic exegesis, following the Pontifical Biblical Commission's guidelines, recognizes as literary convention rather than literal cosmogony. The Church's sophisticated approach to biblical genres, affirmed in *Dei Verbum* 12, allows Catholic interpreters to appreciate the poetic force of this passage without reducing it to historical reportage or dismissing it as mere myth.

The "cup of wrath" (51:17, 22) is deeply significant for Catholic Eucharistic theology. The cup that brings judgment is transformed, in Christ's institution of the Eucharist, into the cup of salvation. Jesus's words "This cup is the new covenant in my blood" (Luke 22:20) deliberately invert the prophetic imagery. Catholic liturgy celebrates this transformation at every Mass: the cup of wrath becomes the chalice of blessing, and what Isaiah described as judgment is re-presented as mercy. This typological reading is central to the Catholic understanding of how the Old Testament is fulfilled, not abrogated, in the New.
