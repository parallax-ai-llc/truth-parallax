Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 39 reads Hezekiah's reception of Babylonian envoys as a cautionary tale about the dangers of vainglory. The king's display of all his treasures (39:2) is understood through the moral theology of pride: showing everything "in his house" reveals an attachment to material possessions that compromises spiritual discernment. The Fathers saw in Hezekiah's behavior a warning that even those who have experienced great divine favor remain vulnerable to sin.

Isaiah's prophecy that everything will be carried to Babylon (39:6) functions in Catholic interpretation as both historical prediction and spiritual lesson. The loss of sacred treasures to a pagan empire prefigures the despoilment that occurs when the Church loses its spiritual patrimony through worldliness. Catholic social teaching, beginning with Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* and continuing through Francis's *Evangelii Gaudium*, warns against the same temptation: allowing material wealth to define the community's identity.

The transition from chapters 39 to 40 — from the announcement of exile to the proclamation of comfort — mirrors the paschal mystery at the heart of Catholic faith. Suffering and loss precede consolation and restoration. The liturgical year enacts this pattern annually: Lent's penitence gives way to Easter's joy, and the same movement from judgment to mercy structures the entire book of Isaiah as Catholic lectionary readings present it.
