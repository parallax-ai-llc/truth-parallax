Isaiah 55 is one of the most important chapters for Catholic sacramental theology. The invitation "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters" (55:1) is read in the Easter Vigil liturgy as the catechumens prepare for baptism. The "waters" are simultaneously the living water of baptismal regeneration and the Eucharistic banquet — "come, buy wine and milk without money" (55:1) — which the Church offers freely to all who believe. The gratuity of salvation ("without money and without cost") is central to Catholic teaching on grace.

The "everlasting covenant" (55:3) is understood as the New Covenant established by Christ, which the Catholic Church teaches is mediated through the seven sacraments. The Second Vatican Council's *Sacrosanctum Concilium* describes the liturgy as the summit and source of the Church's life — the place where the covenant invitation of Isaiah 55 is perpetually extended. The Eucharist, in particular, is the "covenant meal" that nourishes the faithful for their earthly pilgrimage.

God's word that "will not return to me empty" (55:11) is foundational to the Catholic doctrine of the efficacy of the proclaimed Word. The Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy gives Scripture a place of honor in every liturgical celebration, and Catholic homiletic tradition insists that the Word of God is not merely informational but performative — it accomplishes what it announces. This principle also grounds the Church's teaching on the power of sacramental formulas: the words of consecration, absolution, and baptism effect real change because God's word does not return void.
