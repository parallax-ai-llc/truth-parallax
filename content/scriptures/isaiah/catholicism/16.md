## Seeking Shelter Under David's Throne

Isaiah 16 opens with Moab seeking refuge under Judah's protection. "Let the Moabite fugitives stay with you; be their shelter from the destroyer" (16:4). Catholic theology reads this as an anticipation of the universal offer of salvation: even ancient enemies can find refuge under the messianic king's authority. The mention of a throne "established in love" and a ruler who "in judging seeks justice" (16:5) carries messianic resonance.

## The Pride That Destroys

"We have heard of Moab's pride — how great is her arrogance!" (16:6). Catholic spiritual directors from the Desert Fathers to Ignatius of Loyola have identified pride as the fundamental obstacle to grace. Moab's pride is not merely political arrogance but the spiritual disposition that refuses to acknowledge dependence on God. The passage illustrates the Catholic teaching that pride is the deadliest of the capital sins because it prevents the humility necessary for repentance.

## Prophetic Weeping

"So I weep, as Jazer weeps, for the vines of Sibmah" (16:9). Catholic pastoral theology draws on Isaiah's example to affirm that the proclamation of hard truths must be accompanied by genuine love for those who hear them. The Church's prophetic mission — speaking truth about sin and its consequences — is authenticated by compassion, not by the detachment or hostility that would turn prophecy into mere denunciation.
