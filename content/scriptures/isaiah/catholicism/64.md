Isaiah 64's anguished prayer "Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down" (64:1) is understood in Catholic theology as the cry of humanity before the Incarnation — a prayer answered definitively in the birth of Christ. The Advent liturgy draws on this longing, and the *Rorate Caeli* chant ("Drop down dew, you heavens, from above") is directly inspired by this Isaianic petition. The tearing of the heavens finds its New Testament fulfillment at Christ's baptism (Mark 1:10) and at the moment of His death, when the temple veil is torn (Matthew 27:51).

The confession "All our righteous acts are like filthy rags" (64:6) is read in Catholic theology with important nuance. While affirming that unaided human effort cannot merit salvation, Catholic teaching — following the Council of Trent — maintains that human works, when animated by grace, are genuinely meritorious. The verse describes the condition of Israel in exile, separated from God by sin, not the inherent worthlessness of all human goodness. The Catholic position holds that grace perfects nature rather than replacing it: works done in the state of grace are truly good and pleasing to God (CCC 2006-2011).

The potter-clay metaphor (64:8) appears prominently in Catholic catechesis about the relationship between divine sovereignty and human freedom. Unlike some Reformed interpretations that emphasize the clay's complete passivity, Catholic theology, following Aquinas, teaches that God's sovereign action includes the activation of human freedom. Grace does not override the will but empowers it. The prayer's closing plea for mercy (64:12) exemplifies the Catholic understanding of prayer as cooperation with God's initiative: God moves the heart to ask for what He already desires to give.
