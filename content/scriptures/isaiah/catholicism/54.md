Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 54 reads the "barren woman" who rejoices with many children (54:1) as a figure of the Church. Patristic writers including Augustine and Cyprian saw in this passage the Church's unexpected fruitfulness: born from the side of Christ on the cross (John 19:34), the Church produces spiritual children through baptism far exceeding the Old Covenant community in number and universality. Galatians 4:27 confirms this ecclesiological reading.

The covenant of peace (54:10) is connected to Catholic sacramental theology. The "unfailing love" (*hesed*) that will not be shaken is understood as the foundation of the sacramental economy: God's commitment to save through visible, tangible signs. The Council of Trent taught that the sacraments effect what they signify — they are not mere symbols but channels of the grace Isaiah describes. The "covenant of peace" finds its definitive expression in the Eucharist, which the Church calls the "sacrament of sacraments."

Catholic Marian theology also finds resonance in this chapter. The woman who was "storm-tossed and not comforted" (54:11) but is then adorned with precious stones reflects the Church's teaching on Mary's journey from the sorrows of the Passion (the *Stabat Mater*) to her glorious assumption. Just as Zion is promised that "no weapon forged against you will prevail" (54:17), so Catholic dogma teaches that Mary was preserved from the stain of original sin and that the Church, though persecuted, is indefectible.
