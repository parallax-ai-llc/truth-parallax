Isaiah 43 opens with words of redemptive intimacy: "I have called you by name, you are mine" (43:1). Catholic baptismal theology finds here a prefiguration of the sacrament in which each person is called by name and claimed for Christ. The imagery of passing through water and fire (43:2) is explicitly baptismal in the patristic reading: Cyril of Jerusalem, in his *Mystagogical Catecheses*, connected this passage to the baptismal font where the catechumen passes through water into new life.

The declaration "I am doing a new thing" (43:19) is read by Catholic interpreters as anticipating the New Covenant that does not abolish but fulfills the Old. Vatican II's *Nostra Aetate* affirms the continuing validity of God's covenant with Israel while teaching that the Church is the new People of God. This "new thing" includes the sacramental economy, the outpouring of the Holy Spirit, and the universal mission of the Church — all of which surpass while presupposing the exodus experience.

God's self-identification as the one who "blots out your transgressions for my own sake" (43:25) is connected to the Sacrament of Reconciliation (Confession). Catholic teaching holds that forgiveness is not merely a divine attitude but an effective act communicated through sacramental channels. The priest's words of absolution — "I absolve you from your sins" — participate in the same divine action Isaiah describes. The chapter's insistence that forgiveness comes from God's initiative, not human merit, aligns with the Catholic teaching on grace as entirely unmerited (CCC 1996-2005).
