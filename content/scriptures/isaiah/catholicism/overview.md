## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Isaiah draws on the interplay of Scripture, Sacred Tradition, and the Magisterium. Isaiah is among the most liturgically prominent Old Testament books in Catholicism, read extensively during Advent, Lent, and the Easter Vigil. The Church Fathers — particularly Jerome, who produced the Vulgate translation — and medieval theologians like Thomas Aquinas established interpretive traditions that remain authoritative alongside modern biblical scholarship encouraged since *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943).

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Four Senses of Scripture
Catholic exegesis reads Isaiah at multiple levels:
1. **Literal**: The prophet's message to 8th-century Judah during the Assyrian crisis
2. **Allegorical**: Messianic and ecclesiological prefigurations — Christ, Mary, and the Church
3. **Moral**: Calls to social justice, care for the poor, and authentic worship
4. **Anagogical**: The new heavens and new earth as eschatological hope

### Marian Typology
Isaiah 7:14 ("the virgin shall conceive") holds special importance in Catholic theology as a prophecy of the Virgin Birth. The Marian reading is affirmed by Matthew 1:23 and by the dogmatic tradition stretching from the Council of Ephesus (431) through *Lumen Gentium* (1964).

## Theological Emphases

- **Christ and the Church**: The Suffering Servant is Christ, and the restored Zion is the Church
- **Social Justice**: Isaiah's condemnation of oppression (1:17; 10:1-2; 58:6-7) is foundational for Catholic Social Teaching
- **Liturgical Integration**: The Advent readings from Isaiah shape Catholic eschatological and Christological piety
- **Universal Salvation**: Isaiah's vision of all nations coming to God (2:2-4; 49:6) supports the Catholic understanding of the Church's universal mission
