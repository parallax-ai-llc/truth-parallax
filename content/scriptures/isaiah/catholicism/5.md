## The Vineyard of the Lord

Isaiah's vineyard parable (5:1-7) is one of the most carefully constructed allegories in prophetic literature. Catholic exegesis, following the Fathers, reads the vineyard as Israel and the Church in succession. Jesus's parable of the wicked tenants (Matthew 21:33-44) explicitly builds on Isaiah 5, transferring the vineyard's care to new tenants — which Catholic theology understands as the establishment of the Church as the new people of God, while maintaining continuity with Israel.

## The Six Woes

The woe oracles (5:8-23) address land-grabbing, drunkenness, cynicism, moral inversion, self-conceit, and judicial corruption. Catholic social teaching has drawn extensively on these woes. Pope Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) echoes the condemnation of exploitative land practices (5:8). The Catechism cites Isaiah's critique of those who "call evil good and good evil" (5:20) as a warning against the moral relativism that distorts conscience (CCC 1751).

## The Outstretched Hand of Judgment

The closing image of God summoning a distant nation as an instrument of judgment (5:26-30) establishes the theological principle that God's providence governs even the movements of pagan armies. Catholic theology of history, developed by Augustine in *De Civitate Dei* and refined by later theologians, draws on prophetic texts like this to affirm that all of history unfolds under divine governance.
