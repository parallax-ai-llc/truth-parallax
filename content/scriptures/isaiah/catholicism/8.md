## The Two Signs: Judgment and Hope

Isaiah 8 presents the sign-child Maher-shalal-hash-baz alongside the continuing Immanuel theme. Catholic interpreters see the juxtaposition as deliberate: the child of judgment (Maher-shalal-hash-baz) and the child of promise (Immanuel) represent two dimensions of God's dealing with His people. Judgment and salvation are not alternatives but complementary aspects of divine providence.

## The Waters of Shiloah Rejected

"Because this people has rejected the gently flowing waters of Shiloah" (8:6), God will send the overwhelming flood of Assyrian invasion. Catholic sacramental theology reads the "waters of Shiloah" (the pool of Siloam) as a baptismal type — the quiet, humble waters of divine grace rejected in favor of worldly power. Jesus's healing of the blind man at the Pool of Siloam (John 9:7) deepens this typological connection.

## The Sanctuary and the Stumbling Stone

"He will be a holy place; for both Israel and Judah he will be a stone that causes people to stumble and a rock that makes them fall" (8:14). Catholic Christology sees here a prophecy of the dual effect of Christ's coming: sanctuary for believers, stumbling block for those who refuse faith. The Catechism (CCC 587-591) uses the stone imagery from Isaiah to explain how Jesus simultaneously fulfilled and challenged Israel's expectations.

## To the Law and the Testimony

Isaiah's instruction to "bind up the testimony" and to test all claims by "the law and the testimony" (8:16, 20) is read by Catholic theology not as an endorsement of Scripture alone but as an affirmation of the prophetic-revelatory tradition that the Church continues to steward. The testimony is entrusted to disciples — a community of interpretation, not individual readers.
