## Ariel Besieged and Delivered

Jerusalem, addressed as "Ariel" (altar hearth), is warned of coming siege — but also promised deliverance. "Suddenly, in an instant, the LORD Almighty will come with thunder and earthquake and great noise" (29:6). Catholic theology reads the dual movement — siege followed by divine intervention — as a type of the Paschal Mystery: the death that precedes resurrection, the cross that precedes glory.

## The Sealed Book and Spiritual Blindness

"The whole vision is nothing but words sealed in a scroll" (29:11). Catholic theology of revelation emphasizes that Scripture requires the interpretive key of Sacred Tradition and the Magisterium. Without the Church's living teaching authority, the Bible remains a "sealed book" — intelligible at the surface level but closed in its deepest meaning. The Second Vatican Council's *Dei Verbum* affirms that Scripture and Tradition together constitute a single deposit of faith.

## Lip Service Condemned

"These people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me" (29:13). Catholic liturgical reform, particularly after Vatican II, has been concerned with precisely this danger: that liturgical participation might become merely external. *Sacrosanctum Concilium* calls for "full, conscious, and active participation" — the engagement of the heart that Isaiah demands.

## The Reversal of Creation

The chapter concludes with transformation imagery: Lebanon becomes a fertile field, the deaf hear, the blind see, the humble rejoice (29:17-19). Catholic liberation theology reads this as God's preferential option for the marginalized made manifest. The reversal anticipates Mary's *Magnificat* (Luke 1:46-55) — itself deeply shaped by Isaianic language — and the Beatitudes of Jesus's Sermon on the Mount.
