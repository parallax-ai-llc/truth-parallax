## Canticles of Salvation

Isaiah 12 provides two brief psalms of thanksgiving that close the first major section of the book. Catholic liturgical practice incorporates these canticles into the Liturgy of the Hours, where they function as the community's grateful response to God's saving acts. The chapter's placement after the messianic promises of chapters 9-11 models the liturgical rhythm of proclamation followed by praise.

## The Wells of Salvation

"With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation" (12:3). Catholic sacramental theology connects this image to the baptismal font — the well of living water from which the faithful draw the grace of new life. The Easter Vigil liturgy uses water imagery extensively, and this verse from Isaiah contributes to the rich baptismal typology that links Old Testament water motifs to Christian initiation.

## "Make Known Among the Nations What He Has Done"

The missionary imperative of 12:4-5 — "proclaim that his name is exalted" — anticipates the Church's evangelizing mission. Catholic missiology, articulated in *Ad Gentes* (1965) and *Evangelii Nuntiandi* (1975), understands the proclamation of God's deeds among the nations as intrinsic to the Church's identity, not an optional activity. Isaiah's doxology is not private devotion but public witness.
