Hezekiah's illness and recovery in Isaiah 38 is read in Catholic tradition through the lens of the Sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick. While the formal sacrament is instituted in the New Testament (James 5:14-15), Catholic theology sees its roots in Old Testament practices of prayer and physical healing. Hezekiah's turning to God in illness, God's response through the prophet, and the application of a fig poultice (38:21) combine spiritual and physical dimensions of healing that the sacrament unites.

Hezekiah's canticle (38:9-20) is incorporated into the Liturgy of the Hours and is valued as a prayer of thanksgiving after deliverance from death. The cry "I shall not see the LORD in the land of the living" (38:11) reflects a pre-resurrection understanding of death that Catholic theology sees as transformed by Christ's victory. The Church teaches that death, while still an enemy, has been conquered — a development that gives Hezekiah's anguish its full pathos only in retrospect.

The fifteen additional years granted to Hezekiah (38:5) raise questions about God's will and human prayer that Catholic theology addresses through the distinction between God's antecedent and consequent will. Thomas Aquinas taught that God's providence includes the prayers of the faithful as genuine secondary causes. Hezekiah's prayer does not change God's eternal plan but is itself part of that plan — a teaching that preserves both divine sovereignty and the efficacy of human intercession.
