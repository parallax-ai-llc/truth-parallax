Isaiah 65's opening — "I revealed myself to those who did not ask for me" (65:1) — is a critical text for Catholic theology of mission and revelation. Paul quotes it in Romans 10:20 as evidence that God's saving purposes have always extended beyond Israel. Catholic teaching, particularly *Lumen Gentium* 16, holds that those who through no fault of their own do not know the gospel can attain salvation if they seek God with a sincere heart — a principle rooted in the divine initiative Isaiah describes.

The vision of "new heavens and a new earth" (65:17) is foundational to Catholic eschatology. The Catechism teaches that at the end of time, God will transform the entire cosmos, not merely save individual souls (CCC 1042-1050). This passage grounds the Catholic principle that redemption encompasses material creation — a conviction expressed in the sacramental system (which uses material elements as vehicles of grace) and in Catholic environmental teaching. *Laudato Si'* explicitly connects care for creation to the eschatological hope of cosmic renewal.

The description of life in the renewed world — long life, secure dwelling, fruitful labor (65:20-23) — resonates with Catholic social teaching's emphasis on the common good. The conditions Isaiah describes — no premature death, no exploited labor, no insecurity — are the very goals that Catholic social encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* to *Fratelli Tutti* pursue. Catholic theology holds that while the fullness of this vision awaits eschatological fulfillment, Christians are called to approximate it in the present through works of justice and mercy.
