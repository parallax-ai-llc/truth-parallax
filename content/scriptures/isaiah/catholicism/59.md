Isaiah 59 presents a comprehensive analysis of sin and its effects that Catholic theology uses to develop its understanding of personal and structural sin. The declaration "Your iniquities have separated you from your God" (59:2) is foundational to the Catholic doctrine of mortal sin, which the Catechism defines as a grave violation that destroys charity in the heart and turns one away from God (CCC 1855-1861). The separation Isaiah describes is not merely metaphorical but ontological — sin genuinely ruptures the soul's relationship with God.

The corporate confession (59:9-15) models what Catholic theology calls "examination of conscience," a practice recommended before the Sacrament of Reconciliation. The honest acknowledgment of transgression — "Our offenses are many in your sight, and our sins testify against us" (59:12) — reflects the Confiteor's "through my fault, through my fault, through my most grievous fault." Catholic moral theology insists that genuine conversion requires this kind of unflinching self-knowledge, aided by the grace of the Holy Spirit.

The divine warrior passage (59:16-20) introduces the motif of God Himself intervening because no human mediator could be found. Catholic Christology reads this as pointing to the Incarnation: when no human could bridge the gap between God and sinful humanity, God became human. The Redeemer who comes to Zion (59:20) is Christ, and his "armor" — righteousness, salvation, zeal — is shared with the Church through the sacraments and the gifts of the Holy Spirit (cf. Ephesians 6:14-17, which the Catechism interprets sacramentally).
