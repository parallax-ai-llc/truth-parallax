Isaiah 53 is the single most important Old Testament chapter for Catholic soteriology and is read in its entirety on Good Friday, the most solemn day of the liturgical year. The Catechism dedicates extensive attention to this passage, identifying the Suffering Servant with Christ and interpreting his sufferings as vicarious atonement: "He was pierced for our transgressions" (53:5) is fulfilled on Calvary (CCC 601-618).

Catholic interpretation, while affirming the substitutionary dimension, is broader than penal substitution alone. The Servant's suffering is understood through multiple atonement models: satisfaction (Anselm), sacrifice (Aquinas), solidarity with sinners (modern Catholic theology), and the revelation of divine love (von Balthasar). The Eucharist is the sacramental re-presentation of the sacrifice described in Isaiah 53: "He poured out his life unto death" (53:12) corresponds to the words of institution, "This is my body, given for you."

The detail that the Servant "was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death" (53:9) is fulfilled in Christ's burial in the tomb of Joseph of Arimathea (Matthew 27:57-60). Catholic theology sees significance in the Servant's silence (53:7) — an expression of what Hans Urs von Balthasar called "Holy Saturday theology," the descent into the silence of death where Christ encounters the dead and brings them liberation. The "offspring" the Servant sees after his suffering (53:10) is understood as the Church, born from Christ's self-offering, constituting the new People of God.
