## The Fading Crown of Ephraim

Isaiah 28 opens a new series of woe oracles with a condemnation of Ephraim's drunken leadership. Catholic moral theology reads the literal drunkenness as a symbol of spiritual intoxication — the inability to perceive reality clearly due to self-indulgence and sin. The passage warns that leadership corrupted by vice endangers not only the leaders themselves but the entire community entrusted to their care.

## The Tested Cornerstone

"See, I lay a stone in Zion, a tested stone, a precious cornerstone for a sure foundation" (28:16). Catholic Christology identifies this cornerstone as Christ, following Peter's explicit application in 1 Peter 2:6. The Catechism (CCC 756) teaches that Christ is the foundation of the Church: "Built upon this foundation, the Church is never overthrown." The cornerstone stands in contrast to the "covenant with death" (28:15) — every false foundation that human beings substitute for Christ.

## The Strange Work of God

"The LORD will rise up... to do his work, his strange work, and perform his task, his alien task" (28:21). Catholic theology of divine action recognizes that judgment is God's "strange" or "alien" work — not His characteristic activity but something imposed by human rebellion. God's proper work is salvation, mercy, and restoration. This distinction, developed by Luther but rooted in Catholic patristic tradition, prevents the reduction of God to either sentimental mercy or terrifying wrath.

## The Parable of the Farmer

The agricultural parable (28:23-29) illustrates God's wisdom in applying different measures to different situations. Catholic pastoral theology has drawn on this image to justify the principle of pastoral accommodation: different spiritual conditions require different pastoral approaches, just as different crops require different methods of cultivation.
