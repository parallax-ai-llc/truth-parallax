The first Servant Song (42:1-9) is central to Catholic Christology and is read at the liturgy on the feast of the Baptism of the Lord, connecting "Here is my servant, whom I uphold" (42:1) with the Father's voice at Jesus's baptism: "This is my beloved Son" (Matthew 3:17). Catholic theology identifies the Servant as Christ while also recognizing a corporate dimension: the Church, as the Body of Christ, participates in the Servant's mission to bring "justice to the nations" (42:1).

The Servant's gentleness — "A bruised reed he will not break" (42:3) — is a touchstone for Catholic pastoral practice. Pope Francis has repeatedly invoked this image to describe the Church's mission as a "field hospital" that tends to the wounded rather than demanding perfection. The Second Vatican Council's *Gaudium et Spes* reflects this spirit: the Church reads the signs of the times with pastoral sensitivity rather than juridical rigidity. The Servant's method is persuasion, not coercion.

The "covenant for the people and a light for the Gentiles" (42:6) is understood in Catholic theology as fulfilled in the New Covenant established at the Last Supper. The Eucharistic prayers explicitly echo this covenant language. Catholic ecclesiology sees the Church as the universal sacrament of salvation (*Lumen Gentium* 1), extending the Servant's light to all peoples. The "new song" (42:10) is liturgical: the Church's worship is the ongoing proclamation of God's saving deeds, reaching "from the ends of the earth" through the universal Church's celebration.
