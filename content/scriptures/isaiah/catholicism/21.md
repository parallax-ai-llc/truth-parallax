## The Burden of the Desert by the Sea

Isaiah 21 contains three cryptic oracles that Catholic exegesis reads as prophetic visions of judgment against Babylon, Edom, and Arabia. The prophet's intense emotional reaction — "My body is racked with pain, pangs seize me" (21:3) — reveals that prophetic insight is not dispassionate observation but participated suffering. Catholic theology of prophecy sees the prophet as one who enters into God's own grief over the world's corruption.

## The Watchman's Vigil

"Day after day, my lord, I stand on the watchtower" (21:8). Catholic ecclesiology has applied the watchman image to the episcopal office: bishops are watchmen set over the Church to observe and warn. The *Liturgy of the Hours* — the Church's daily cycle of prayer — embodies the watchman's unceasing vigilance, keeping watch through the night until the dawn of Christ's return.

## "Babylon Has Fallen, Has Fallen"

The watchman's announcement (21:9) reverberates through Scripture to Revelation 14:8 and 18:2. Catholic eschatology sees every historical fall of Babylon — the Assyrian conquest, the Persian conquest, the fall of Rome — as partial fulfillments of a pattern that will reach its consummation in the final judgment. The Book of Revelation's use of Isaianic language confirms the typological depth of the original oracle.
