## The Prophet's Body as Sign

Isaiah 20 records a three-year prophetic sign-act in which Isaiah walks "stripped and barefoot" as a warning against relying on Egypt and Cush (20:2-3). Catholic theology of the body, developed by St. John Paul II in his Theology of the Body, recognizes that the human body can be a vehicle of divine communication. The prophetic sign-act tradition — in which the body enacts the prophetic message — anticipates the Incarnation itself, in which God's definitive Word is spoken through a human body.

## The Humiliation of False Hopes

The sign's meaning is explicit: as Isaiah is exposed, so will Egypt and Cush be led away naked and ashamed by Assyria. Catholic pastoral theology draws from this the principle that false securities are eventually exposed. The Church's prophetic mission includes warning against placing ultimate trust in any human power, institution, or ideology — a warning addressed to the Church itself as much as to the world.

## The Cry of the Coastland

"See what has happened to those we relied on!" (20:6). Catholic theology of hope, articulated in Benedict XVI's *Spe Salvi* (2007), distinguishes between the false hopes of worldly power and the authentic hope grounded in God's promises. Isaiah 20 serves as a cautionary tale about the former, clearing the ground for the latter.
