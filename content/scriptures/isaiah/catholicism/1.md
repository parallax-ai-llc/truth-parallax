## The Prophetic Indictment

Isaiah opens as a divine lawsuit (*rib*) against Judah, summoning heaven and earth as witnesses (1:2). Catholic exegesis, following the patristic tradition, reads this courtroom scene within the framework of covenant theology. St. Jerome's *Commentary on Isaiah* establishes the tone: Israel's sin is not mere transgression but ingratitude — the rebellion of children against a loving father. The Church Fathers universally applied this indictment to the spiritual condition of all humanity before grace.

## Sacrifice and Justice

"What to me is the multitude of your sacrifices?" (1:11). Catholic interpretation does not read this as a rejection of ritual worship itself but as a condemnation of ritual divorced from moral life. The Second Vatican Council's *Sacrosanctum Concilium* echoes Isaiah's insistence that authentic worship must transform the worshipper. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 2100) cites this passage to teach that outward sacrifice must express interior devotion.

## "Come Now, Let Us Reason Together"

The offer of cleansing in 1:18 — "though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow" — is understood in Catholic theology as an anticipation of the sacrament of Reconciliation. God initiates the dialogue of conversion, but human cooperation (*synergeia*) is required: "If you are willing and obedient" (1:19). This passage illustrates the Catholic understanding of grace as both prevenient and cooperative.

## Zion Redeemed by Justice

The chapter's conclusion (1:25-31) envisions purification through divine judgment. Catholic social teaching draws on Isaiah 1:17 — "learn to do right; seek justice; defend the oppressed" — as a foundational text for the Church's commitment to social justice, cited in numerous papal encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* to *Fratelli Tutti*.
