## The Defeat of Leviathan

"In that day, the LORD will punish with his sword — his fierce, great and powerful sword — Leviathan the gliding serpent" (27:1). Catholic eschatology reads this as the definitive defeat of evil, accomplished by Christ's death and resurrection and consummated at His return. The serpent imagery connects Genesis 3:15 (the *protoevangelium*) to Isaiah 27 to Revelation 12 and 20, creating a salvation-historical arc that Catholic theology traces as the progressive victory over the ancient enemy.

## The Restored Vineyard

In contrast to the condemned vineyard of chapter 5, God now tends a vineyard with protective care: "I, the LORD, watch over it; I water it continually" (27:3). Catholic ecclesiology reads this as the transition from old Israel to the Church — the new vineyard that God Himself guards and nurtures. The sacraments, in Catholic understanding, are the means by which God "waters continually" — providing grace for growth and protection against spiritual threats.

## The Great Trumpet

"In that day a great trumpet will sound" (27:13). Catholic liturgical eschatology connects this trumpet to the final gathering of the elect at Christ's return, as described in 1 Thessalonians 4:16 and 1 Corinthians 15:52. The Eucharistic liturgy itself, in Catholic understanding, is an anticipation of this final ingathering — a foretaste of the heavenly liturgy in which all the scattered children of God will be reunited.
