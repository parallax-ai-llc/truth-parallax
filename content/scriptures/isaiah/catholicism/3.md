## The Collapse of Social Order

Isaiah 3 describes God's removal of competent leadership from Jerusalem as a covenantal punishment. Catholic social teaching sees this as confirmation that good governance is a gift of divine providence. When a society systematically violates justice, God permits the natural consequences to unfold — including the collapse of social institutions. The *Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church* echoes Isaiah's concern for just leadership.

## The Oppression of the Poor

"The LORD enters into judgment against the elders and leaders of his people: It is you who have ruined my vineyard; the plunder from the poor is in your houses" (3:14). Catholic liberation theology and Catholic social teaching converge on this passage. The "preferential option for the poor" articulated at Medellin (1968) and Puebla (1979) finds its prophetic roots in texts like this one: God holds leaders accountable for the condition of the vulnerable.

## The Daughters of Zion

The extended critique of Jerusalem's women (3:16-26) has been read by Catholic moral theologians as a warning against vanity and materialism. The passage is not misogynistic in its intent — the women function as symbols of the entire city's self-indulgent culture. The stripping away of ornaments represents the removal of false securities that prevent genuine encounter with God.
