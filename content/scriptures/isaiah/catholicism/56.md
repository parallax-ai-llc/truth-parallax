Isaiah 56's inclusion of foreigners and eunuchs in God's covenant community is a pivotal text for Catholic ecclesiology. The declaration that God's house "will be called a house of prayer for all nations" (56:7), quoted by Jesus in Mark 11:17, validates the Catholic Church's self-understanding as a universal (*katholike*) community that transcends ethnic, cultural, and social boundaries. Vatican II's *Nostra Aetate* and *Ad Gentes* ground the Church's outreach to all peoples in this prophetic vision.

The eunuch's inclusion (56:3-5) is read as anticipating the New Covenant's radical expansion of covenant membership beyond the categories established in the Mosaic Law. Catholic theology sees here a principle of progressive revelation: God's saving purposes, always universal in intention, become increasingly explicit through the prophets and reach definitive expression in Christ. The Ethiopian eunuch's baptism in Acts 8 is the narrative fulfillment of Isaiah's promise, and Catholic missiology draws on this connection.

The condemnation of irresponsible leaders (56:10-12) — watchmen who are "blind" and "dogs that love to sleep" — has been applied throughout Catholic history to negligent clergy. The Council of Trent's reforms of clerical discipline, Vatican II's renewal of priestly formation (*Optatam Totius*), and Pope Francis's emphasis on pastoral accountability all echo Isaiah's insistence that those charged with spiritual oversight must fulfill their duties. The Catholic Church's canonical structures of accountability — episcopal visitations, ad limina reports, and diocesan reviews — are institutional expressions of this prophetic concern.
