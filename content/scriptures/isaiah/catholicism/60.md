Isaiah 60's vision of Zion radiant with divine glory is read in Catholic liturgy on the Solemnity of the Epiphany, when the Church celebrates the manifestation of Christ to the Gentile nations. "Nations will come to your light, and kings to the brightness of your dawn" (60:3) is fulfilled in the Magi's journey (Matthew 2:1-12). The gifts of "gold and incense" (60:6) correspond directly to the Magi's offerings, and Catholic tradition sees in this the first fruits of the universal Church.

The vision of the nations' wealth streaming into Zion (60:5-13) is interpreted by Catholic social teaching not as a justification for ecclesial wealth but as a prophecy of the cultural contributions all peoples bring to the Church. Vatican II's *Gaudium et Spes* teaches that the Church enriches and is enriched by the cultures it encounters. The Catholic principle of inculturation — adapting the gospel to local cultures while preserving its essence — finds its prophetic warrant in Isaiah's vision of diverse nations bringing their treasures to the one God.

The promise "The LORD will be your everlasting light" (60:19) finds its liturgical expression in the Easter Vigil's *Exsultet* and in the Paschal Candle that burns throughout the Easter season. The Catechism teaches that Christ is the "Light of the World" whose light the Church reflects and transmits (CCC 748). Catholic architecture expresses this theology through the centrality of the tabernacle lamp — the eternal flame signifying Christ's eucharistic presence — which makes every Catholic church a place where Isaiah's promise is perpetually realized.
