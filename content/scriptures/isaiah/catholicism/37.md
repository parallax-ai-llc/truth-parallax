Catholic interpretation of Isaiah 37 highlights Hezekiah's prayer in the temple (37:14-20) as a model of liturgical intercession. The king does not merely pray privately but brings his petition into the sacred space, spreading the threatening letter before the LORD — an act that Catholic commentators connect to the presentation of intentions during the Eucharistic liturgy. The temple as the locus of divine encounter is essential to Catholic sacramental theology.

God's response through Isaiah, declaring that He will defend Jerusalem "for my own sake and for the sake of David my servant" (37:35), is read through the lens of the communion of saints. God acts not only in response to present prayer but in fidelity to covenants made with those who have gone before. Catholic theology sees here a precursor to the Church's practice of invoking the intercession of the saints, whose merits, united to Christ's, continue to benefit the living.

The miraculous destruction of the Assyrian army (37:36) is commemorated in Catholic tradition as an act of divine providence. The angel of the LORD who strikes the camp is connected to the angelic ministry that Catholic theology teaches continues to operate in the Church and in individual believers' lives. The Catechism affirms that angels serve as God's messengers and agents (CCC 328-336), and this passage provides one of the most dramatic Old Testament demonstrations of that ministry.
