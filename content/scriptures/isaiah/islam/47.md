Isaiah 47's taunt against Babylon personified as a fallen queen resonates with the Quran's repeated warnings against civilizational arrogance. Babylon's claim "I am, and there is none besides me" (47:8) is the quintessential expression of kibr (arrogance) that the Quran identifies as the sin of Iblis (Satan) himself: "He refused and was arrogant and became of the disbelievers" (Quran 2:34). Muslim commentators see in Babylon's fall a confirmation of the Quranic principle that "every self-deluding boaster" faces divine humbling.

The condemnation of Babylon's sorcery and astrology (47:12-13) aligns with the Islamic prohibition against sihr (sorcery) and kahanah (divination). The Quran treats sorcery as a grave sin: "And they followed instead what the devils had recited during the reign of Solomon... but Solomon did not disbelieve; the devils disbelieved, teaching people magic" (Quran 2:102). Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) classifies sorcery and fortune-telling among the major sins, and Isaiah's mockery of Babylon's astrologers supports this prohibition.

The pattern of civilizational rise and fall -- Babylon's wealth, confidence, and sudden destruction -- is a central theme in Quranic historiography. The Quran instructs believers to learn from the destruction of past civilizations: "Have they not traveled through the earth and observed how was the end of those before them? They were greater than them in power" (Quran 35:44). Isaiah 47 provides one such lesson, and Muslim commentators include it among the examples that confirm the Quran's universal principle: no earthly power, however great, endures when it defies the Creator.
