## Ariel Under Judgment

Isaiah 29 pronounces woe on Jerusalem ("Ariel"). Islamic theology affirms the principle that sacred cities are not immune from divine judgment. The Quran records God's destruction of cities that rejected their prophets: "And how many a city did We destroy while it was committing wrongdoing" (Al-Anbiya 21:11). No earthly place is sacred in itself — only obedience to God confers divine protection.

## The Sealed Revelation

"The whole vision is nothing but words sealed in a scroll" (29:11). Islamic theology recognizes that revelation can be "sealed" from those whose hearts are hardened. The Quran describes this condition: "Allah has sealed their hearts and their hearing, and over their vision is a veil" (Al-Baqarah 2:7). Both traditions identify spiritual blindness as the result of persistent refusal to accept divine guidance.

## Lip Service Condemned

"These people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me" (29:13). Islamic theology strongly condemns *nifaq* (hypocrisy) — the outward display of faith without inner sincerity. The Quran devotes extensive attention to the *munafiqun* (hypocrites): "And of the people are some who say, 'We believe in Allah and the Last Day,' but they are not believers" (Al-Baqarah 2:8). Isaiah's critique of lip-service worship is echoed precisely in the Quranic condemnation of hypocrisy.

## The Reversal of Conditions

The chapter's concluding vision of reversal — the deaf hear, the blind see, the humble rejoice (29:18-19) — aligns with the Quranic promise that God will ultimately restore justice and reward the faithful. "So verily, with hardship, there is ease. Verily, with hardship, there is ease" (Al-Sharh 94:5-6).
