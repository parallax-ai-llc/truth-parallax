## Jerusalem and Accountability

Isaiah 22 pronounces judgment on Jerusalem itself — God's own chosen city. Islamic theology affirms the principle that proximity to divine revelation increases accountability. The Quran teaches: "You are the best nation produced for mankind — you enjoin what is right, forbid what is wrong, and believe in Allah" (Al Imran 3:110) — but this status carries responsibility rather than immunity from judgment.

## The Key of David

The transfer of the "key of the house of David" from Shebna to Eliakim (22:22) illustrates the Islamic principle that God grants and withdraws authority according to His will. The Quran declares: "You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will" (Al Imran 3:26). No human holds authority permanently or unconditionally.

## Eating and Drinking Before Death

"Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die" (22:13). Islamic moral theology condemns this attitude as *ghaflah* — heedlessness, the failure to remember death and prepare for the hereafter. The Quran repeatedly warns against this spiritual torpor: "The mutual rivalry [for worldly increase] diverts you, until you visit the graves" (Al-Takathur 102:1-2).
