## The Absolute Distinction

"The Egyptians are mere mortals and not God; their horses are flesh and not spirit" (31:3). This verse articulates with perfect clarity the principle of *tawhid* — the absolute distinction between Creator and creation that is the foundation of Islamic theology. The Quran proclaims: "There is nothing like unto Him" (Al-Shura 42:11). Isaiah's insistence on this distinction — between mortal and God, flesh and spirit — is the essence of Islamic monotheism.

## Divine Protection

God's protection of Jerusalem, compared to a lion and to hovering birds (31:4-5), illustrates the divine attributes of *al-Muhaymin* (the Guardian) and *al-Hafiz* (the Protector). Islamic theology affirms that God protects the believing community not passively but actively — fighting on behalf of those who trust in Him.

## The Call to Return

"Return to the One from whom you have deeply revolted" (31:6). Islamic *tawbah* (repentance) is understood as a return to God from whom all people have wandered through sin. The Quran promises: "Say, 'O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins'" (Al-Zumar 39:53). The prophetic call to return is the same across all divine dispensations.

## Victory by Divine Power Alone

"Assyria will fall by no human sword" (31:8). Islamic theology affirms that ultimate victory belongs to God, not to human military power. The Quran describes the Battle of Badr: "You did not kill them, but it was Allah who killed them; and you did not throw when you threw, but it was Allah who threw" (Al-Anfal 8:17). Both traditions insist that salvation comes from God, not from human strength.
