## The Destroyer Judged

Isaiah 33 pronounces woe on the unnamed destroyer. Islamic theology affirms the principle of divine retribution (*jaza'*): those who oppress others will themselves face judgment. The Quran declares: "And the retribution for an evil act is an evil one like it" (Al-Shura 42:40). The prophetic warning that the destroyer will be destroyed is consistent with the Quranic principle of proportionate justice.

## The Cry for Mercy

"LORD, be gracious to us; we long for you" (33:2). Islamic prayer (*du'a*) centers on the same cry for divine mercy (*rahmah*). Every surah of the Quran (except one) begins with "In the Name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful" (*Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim*). The prophetic longing for God expressed in Isaiah 33 is the same longing that Islam cultivates through the five daily prayers and the constant invocation of God's mercy.

## Dwelling with the Consuming Fire

"Who can dwell with the consuming fire?" (33:14). Islamic theology describes the Day of Judgment as a time when God's presence will be experienced as either mercy or fire, depending on the state of the soul. The Quran describes both the fire of Jahannam for the wicked and the gardens of Jannah for the righteous. Isaiah's question and answer — the righteous person who walks in integrity — aligns with the Quranic description of those who will be saved.

## The Vision of the King

"Your eyes will see the king in his beauty" (33:17). Islamic eschatology includes the *ru'yah* — the vision of God that the righteous will be granted in Paradise. The Quran hints at this: "Some faces that Day will be radiant, looking at their Lord" (Al-Qiyamah 75:22-23). Isaiah's promise of seeing the king in his beauty resonates with this most exalted of Islamic hopes — the direct vision of the Divine that constitutes the ultimate reward of faith.
