Isaiah 62's imagery of Zion receiving new names is read in Islamic tradition through the concept of divine naming (tasmiyah), which carries theological weight. In the Quran, Allah teaches Adam "the names of all things" (Quran 2:31), establishing naming as a divine prerogative. The new names "Hephzibah" (my delight is in her) and "Beulah" (married) (62:4) express a renewed relationship between God and His community -- a relationship that Islam understands as always available to those who submit to divine will.

The watchmen who pray unceasingly (62:6-7) correspond to the Islamic ideal of continuous dhikr (remembrance of God). The Quran commands, "O you who have believed, remember Allah with much remembrance" (Quran 33:41). The Sufi tradition developed the practice of perpetual dhikr circles, where groups of practitioners maintain unbroken chains of divine remembrance, echoing the watchmen Isaiah describes. The injunction to "give the LORD no rest" (62:7) is understood as reflecting the Islamic belief that persistent prayer (dua with ilhah, insistence) is beloved by Allah.

The command to "prepare the way for the people" (62:10) resonates with the Islamic concept of da'wah -- the invitation to the straight path. Every Muslim bears responsibility for conveying the message of Islam, and the preparation of the "highway" is understood as removing obstacles that prevent people from recognizing divine truth. The Quran instructs, "Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good instruction" (Quran 16:125), and the prophetic mission of building roads to God defines the Muslim community's role in the world.
