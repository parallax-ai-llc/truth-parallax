Isaiah 54's imagery of the "barren woman" who rejoices with many children (54:1) is read in Islamic tradition through the lens of divine provision (rizq). Allah's power to reverse barrenness -- physical and spiritual -- is a recurring Quranic theme. The stories of Ibrahim and Sarah (Quran 11:71-72), Zakariyya and his wife (Quran 19:2-10), and Maryam's miraculous conception (Quran 19:16-22) all demonstrate that God can bring life where human expectation sees only impossibility. Isaiah's barren woman joins this prophetic pattern.

The covenant of peace (54:10) -- "my unfailing love for you will not be shaken" -- corresponds to the Islamic understanding of God's mercy (rahma) as His most fundamental attribute. A hadith qudsi (sacred hadith in which God speaks) records Allah saying: "My mercy prevails over My wrath." The permanence of divine love that Isaiah describes is affirmed in the Quran: "He has decreed upon Himself mercy" (Quran 6:12). This divine commitment to mercy provides the theological foundation for Islamic hope and trust.

The promise "No weapon forged against you will prevail" (54:17) is read by Muslim scholars as a general promise to all communities that maintain faith and obedience to God. The Quran offers similar assurance: "If you are patient and fear Allah, their plot will not harm you at all" (Quran 3:120). Islamic history draws on this principle in its narrative of Muslim communities that endured persecution -- from the early Muslim community's trials in Mecca to subsequent periods of foreign invasion -- and survived through reliance on divine protection.
