The third Servant passage (50:4-9) describes a figure who endures persecution with steadfast trust in God -- a profile that Islamic scholars connect to the experience of prophets throughout history. The Quran teaches that every prophet faced rejection and suffering: "Messengers were denied before you, but they were patient over the denial and were harmed until Our victory came to them" (Quran 6:34). The Servant who "offered my back to those who beat me" (50:6) exemplifies the prophetic quality of sabr (patient endurance) that the Quran prizes above all others.

The Servant's morning-by-morning attentiveness (50:4) is connected by some Muslim commentators to the practice of tahajjud -- the voluntary night/early morning prayer that the Quran recommends to the Prophet Muhammad: "And from part of the night, pray with it as additional worship for you; it is expected that your Lord will resurrect you to a praised station" (Quran 17:79). The discipline of daily spiritual attunement, receiving instruction from God before speaking to others, is a prophetic characteristic that Islam values highly.

The Servant's confidence that "He who vindicates me is near" (50:8) corresponds to the Quranic assurance that Allah is always near: "And when My servants ask you concerning Me -- indeed I am near" (Quran 2:186). Islamic theology holds that vindication (nasr) comes from Allah alone and in His timing. The warning against those who "light fires for themselves" (50:11) is read as a condemnation of those who substitute human-devised guidance for divine revelation -- a critique Islam directs at any religious system that departs from pure monotheism.
