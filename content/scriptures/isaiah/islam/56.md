Isaiah 56's inclusion of foreigners and eunuchs in God's covenant community (56:3-7) is read in Islamic perspective as confirming the universalism that Islam sees as intrinsic to authentic monotheism. The Quran declares, "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another. Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (Quran 49:13). Isaiah's expansion of covenant membership beyond ethnic Israel anticipates the Islamic ummah's principle of universal inclusion based on faith rather than lineage.

The declaration that God's house "will be called a house of prayer for all nations" (56:7) aligns with the Islamic understanding of the mosque (masjid) as a universal space of worship. The Quran describes the Kaaba as "the first house of worship established for mankind" (Quran 3:96), open to all who worship the one God. Islamic law holds that any clean space can serve as a place of prayer, reflecting the principle that God's accessibility is not confined to a single location or people.

The condemnation of negligent leaders (56:10-12) resonates with the Quranic warnings to religious scholars (ulama) who fail in their responsibilities. The Quran condemns "those who conceal what We sent down of clear proofs and guidance after We made it clear for the people in the Scripture" (Quran 2:159). Islamic tradition holds the ulama to high standards of diligence and sincerity, and the prophetic hadith warns that "the worst of scholars is the one who approaches rulers, and the worst of rulers is the one who approaches scholars" (for corrupt purposes).
