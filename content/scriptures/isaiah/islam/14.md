## The Fall of the Tyrant

Isaiah 14 presents a taunt-song over the fallen king of Babylon. Islamic interpretation reads this as a moral lesson about the consequences of pride and self-deification. The Quran recounts the fall of Pharaoh — another tyrant who claimed divine status — as a paradigmatic warning: "He [Pharaoh] said, 'I am your most exalted lord!' So Allah seized him with the punishment of the last and the first life" (Al-Nazi'at 79:24-25).

## "I Will Make Myself Like the Most High"

The king's aspiration to divine status (14:13-14) represents the ultimate sin in Islamic theology: *shirk* in its most extreme form — not merely associating partners with God but claiming to be God. The Quran identifies this as Pharaoh's sin and as the defining characteristic of Iblis, who refused to acknowledge a being above himself. Isaiah's prophetic condemnation of this pretension is fully consistent with Quranic teaching.

## The Humbling of the Proud

The scene in Sheol — the mighty king reduced to weakness among the shades (14:10) — illustrates the Quranic teaching that death is the great equalizer. "Every soul will taste death" (Al Imran 3:185). Islamic funeral practices emphasize the same truth: all human beings, regardless of status, return to God equally dependent on His mercy.
