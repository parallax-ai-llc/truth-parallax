Isaiah 51's call to remember the "rock from which you were cut" (51:1) -- Abraham and Sarah (51:2) -- connects to the Islamic veneration of Ibrahim (Abraham) as the father of monotheism. The Quran identifies Ibrahim as a hanif -- one who turned away from polytheism to worship God alone: "Indeed, Ibrahim was a community unto himself, devoutly obedient to Allah, inclining toward truth, and he was not of the polytheists" (Quran 16:120). Muslim commentators see Isaiah's call to remember Abraham as confirming the centrality of Ibrahim's legacy in all Abrahamic traditions.

The creation-combat imagery (51:9) is read by Islamic scholars with theological caution. The Quran's creation account involves no battle with primordial chaos or sea monsters; Allah creates by His word alone: "Indeed, Our word to a thing when We intend it is but that We say to it, 'Be,' and it is" (Quran 16:40). Muslim commentators interpret Isaiah's mythological language either as poetic license or as evidence of textual corruption that introduced non-monotheistic elements into the biblical text.

The "cup of wrath" imagery (51:17, 22) is connected to the Quranic description of the bitter drink of the damned on the Day of Judgment: "They will be given to drink boiling water that will sever their intestines" (Quran 47:15). The transfer of the cup from Jerusalem to her oppressors (51:23) illustrates the Islamic principle of divine justice operating in history -- oppressors will taste the suffering they inflicted. The Quran promises, "And never think that Allah is unaware of what the wrongdoers do" (Quran 14:42).
