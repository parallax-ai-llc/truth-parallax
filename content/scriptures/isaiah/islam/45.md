The designation of Cyrus as "anointed" (mashiach, 45:1) is read in Islamic perspective as demonstrating that God's instruments of salvation are not limited to any ethnic or religious group. This universalism is central to Islamic theology: "And We certainly sent into every nation a messenger" (Quran 16:36). The fact that a Persian emperor could serve God's purposes without knowing Him (45:4-5) illustrates the breadth of divine sovereignty that the Quran affirms.

The declaration "I form the light and create darkness, I bring prosperity and create disaster" (45:7) is affirmed in Islamic theology as an expression of divine sovereignty over all things, both pleasant and unpleasant. The Quran teaches, "Say, 'All things are from Allah'" (Quran 4:78), and Islamic theology holds that both good and evil occur by divine permission, though moral evil originates in human choice. The theological school of Ash'arism emphasizes divine omnipotence over all events, while the Maturidi school preserves more space for human agency, but both affirm the sovereignty Isaiah describes.

The universal call "Turn to me and be saved, all you ends of the earth" (45:22) is read by Muslim scholars as confirming the universal scope of prophetic mission that finds its culmination in Islam. While each prophet was sent to a specific people, the final messenger -- Muhammad -- was sent to all of humanity: "We have not sent you except as a mercy to the worlds" (Quran 21:107). Isaiah's universal invitation is thus seen as anticipating the universalism that Islam fully realizes.
