Islamic interpretation of the second Servant passage (49:1-7) continues the discussion of whether the servant refers to a prophet. Some Muslim scholars identify the servant called "from the womb" (49:1) and designated "a light for the Gentiles" (49:6) as Muhammad, whose mission extended beyond any single nation to encompass all of humanity. The Quran describes Muhammad as "a witness, a bringer of good tidings, and a warner, and one who invites to Allah by His permission, and an illuminating lamp" (Quran 33:45-46) -- language that parallels Isaiah's description of the servant as a light to the nations.

The maternal imagery of verses 14-16 -- God's love surpassing a mother's love -- resonates with the Quranic emphasis on divine mercy (rahma). The Arabic root r-h-m, from which "mercy" (rahma) derives, is etymologically connected to rahim (womb), and Islamic theology sees in this linguistic connection a deliberate divine self-description: Allah's mercy is womb-like in its encompassing, nurturing quality. The Quran opens every surah (except one) with "In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious (al-Rahman), the Most Merciful (al-Rahim)" -- two names derived from this same root.

The promise "I have engraved you on the palms of my hands" (49:16) is read by Muslim commentators with caution regarding anthropomorphism (tajsim). Islamic theology, particularly in its Ash'ari and Maturidi expressions, affirms God's attributes as described in revelation but without likening them to human qualities: "There is nothing like unto Him" (Quran 42:11). Such verses in the biblical text are understood as metaphorical expressions of divine attention and care, not as suggesting that God has physical hands.
