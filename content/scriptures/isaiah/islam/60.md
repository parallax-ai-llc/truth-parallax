Isaiah 60's vision of divine light transforming Zion is connected in Islamic theology to the Quranic concept of Allah as the Light of the heavens and the earth. The famous "Light Verse" (Ayat al-Nur, Quran 24:35) describes Allah's light as "a niche within which is a lamp, the lamp is within glass, the glass as if it were a pearly white star lit from the oil of a blessed olive tree." Isaiah's "Arise, shine, for your light has come" (60:1) is understood as describing the same divine illumination that the Quran celebrates.

The nations streaming to Zion's light (60:3) is interpreted by some Muslim scholars as prefiguring the universal ummah that would eventually gather around the light of Islam. While the specific identification with Jerusalem is acknowledged, Islamic theology sees the principle of divine light attracting all peoples as fulfilled most completely in the Prophet Muhammad's mission and the global spread of Islam. The wealth of nations flowing to the center of worship (60:5-7) parallels the annual Hajj pilgrimage, where millions gather at the Kaaba from every nation.

The promise of "the LORD will be your everlasting light" (60:19) corresponds to the Islamic vision of the Hereafter, where the righteous dwell in the light of Allah's countenance. The Quran describes the righteous on the Day of Judgment: "Their light will proceed before them and on their right, and they will say, 'Our Lord, perfect for us our light'" (Quran 66:8). The eschatological vision of divine light replacing created luminaries is shared between Isaiah and the Quran, confirming for Muslim scholars the unity of authentic prophetic revelation.
