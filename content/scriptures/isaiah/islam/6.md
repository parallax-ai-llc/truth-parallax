## The Vision of Divine Holiness

Isaiah 6 describes the prophet's encounter with God enthroned in the Temple. Islamic theology resonates with the depiction of divine transcendence and the angelic worship surrounding God's throne. The Quran describes angels who "glorify Him night and day and do not slacken" (Al-Anbiya 21:20), paralleling the seraphim's unceasing praise. Both traditions affirm that worship of God is the fundamental activity of the heavenly realm.

## The Prophet's Unworthiness

Isaiah's cry — "I am a man of unclean lips" (6:5) — reflects the prophetic humility that Islam celebrates. The Prophet Muhammad was known for his profound humility before God, often weeping in prayer. Islamic theology teaches that awareness of one's own inadequacy before God is the beginning of true spiritual knowledge — a principle exemplified in Isaiah's response to the divine vision.

## Cleansing and Commission

The burning coal that purifies Isaiah's lips (6:6-7) represents divine preparation for prophetic service. Islamic theology affirms that God prepares His prophets for their missions — the Quran describes how God "opened" Muhammad's breast (*sharh al-sadr*, Al-Sharh 94:1) to prepare him for revelation. Both traditions insist that prophetic authority derives from divine initiative, not human qualification.
