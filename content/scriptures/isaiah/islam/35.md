Isaiah 35's vision of healing and restoration is read in Islamic tradition as a description of the conditions that will prevail in Jannah (Paradise) or in the messianic age that precedes the final resurrection. The blind seeing, the deaf hearing, and the lame leaping (35:5-6) parallel Quranic descriptions of the perfection awaiting the righteous in the Hereafter. The Quran describes Paradise as a place where "they will have whatever they wish therein, and with Us is more" (Quran 50:35) -- a perfection that Isaiah's healings anticipate.

The desert blooming with water in the wilderness (35:6-7) echoes the Quranic theme of divine provision. Allah's power to bring life from barren land is one of the Quran's recurring proofs of divine sovereignty and resurrection: "And you see the earth barren, but when We send down upon it rain, it quivers and swells and grows [something] of every beautiful kind" (Quran 22:5). Muslim commentators see Isaiah's desert imagery as expressing the same theological point: the God who can transform a wasteland into a garden can surely raise the dead.

The "Way of Holiness" (35:8) is read by Muslim scholars as anticipating the Sirat -- the straight path that the Quran describes and that Muslims pray for in every recitation of Al-Fatiha: "Guide us to the straight path" (Quran 1:6). The concept of a divinely prepared road reserved for the righteous, from which the unclean are excluded, resonates with the Islamic understanding of the Sirat al-Mustaqim as both an ethical path in this life and an eschatological bridge in the Hereafter.
