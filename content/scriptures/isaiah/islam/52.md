The declaration "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news" (52:7) is understood in Islamic tradition as describing the universal prophetic mission of conveying divine guidance. The Arabic term for "bringing good news" (tabshir) is the same root used for the Quran's description of prophets as mubashirun (bearers of glad tidings): "And We send not the messengers except as bringers of good tidings and warners" (Quran 18:56). Muslim commentators see in Isaiah's messenger a type of the prophetic vocation that culminates in Muhammad.

The call to "Depart! Go out from there!" (52:11) resonates with the concept of hijrah in Islamic tradition -- the duty to leave a land of oppression for one where faith can be practiced freely. The Prophet Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina established this principle as a permanent feature of Islamic law and spirituality. The Quran states, "Was not the earth of Allah spacious enough for you to emigrate therein?" (Quran 4:97), establishing migration as a religious obligation when circumstances require it.

The Servant passage beginning at 52:13-15 is read by Islamic scholars with a distinctive perspective. The Servant who is "disfigured beyond recognition" (52:14) yet destined for exaltation does not, in Islamic reading, refer to a divine figure who atones for sin through his death. Islam rejects the concept of vicarious atonement: "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (Quran 6:164). The passage may describe the suffering of prophetic communities generally, or it may contain elements of tahrif that obscure the original meaning.
