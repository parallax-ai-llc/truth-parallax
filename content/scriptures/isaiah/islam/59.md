Isaiah 59's diagnosis -- "Your iniquities have separated you from your God" (59:2) -- expresses the Islamic understanding that sin creates a barrier between the human being and Allah, though Islamic theology rejects the concept of original sin. Each person is born in a state of fitrah (natural disposition toward God), and sin represents a departure from this original purity. The Quran teaches, "Whatever strikes you of disaster -- it is for what your hands have earned; but He pardons much" (Quran 42:30). Sin's consequences are real but are remedied through individual repentance, not through vicarious atonement.

The corporate confession (59:9-15) models what Islamic theology calls tawbah (repentance), which involves acknowledging sin, feeling genuine remorse, and resolving not to repeat the offense. The Quran promises, "And He it is who accepts repentance from His servants and pardons misdeeds, and He knows what you do" (Quran 42:25). The shift from accusation to confession in Isaiah mirrors the movement from heedlessness (ghaflah) to awareness that Islamic spiritual practice seeks to cultivate.

The divine warrior passage (59:16-20) -- God acting when no human intercessor could be found -- is read in Islamic theology as expressing divine sovereignty over salvation. While Islam acknowledges the intercession (shafa'ah) of prophets on the Day of Judgment, this intercession operates only by Allah's permission: "Who is it that can intercede with Him except by His permission?" (Quran 2:255). The fundamental Islamic conviction is that Allah alone saves, using whatever means He chooses -- a principle Isaiah's passage supports.
