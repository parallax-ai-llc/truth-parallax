The deliverance of Jerusalem recorded in Isaiah 37 is recognized in Islamic tradition as an authentic act of divine intervention. The angel who destroys the Assyrian army (37:36) parallels Quranic accounts of angelic intervention on behalf of believers. At the Battle of Badr, Allah sent angels to fight alongside the Muslims: "When you asked help of your Lord, and He answered you, 'Indeed, I will reinforce you with a thousand from the angels, following one another'" (Quran 8:9). The pattern of divine rescue through angelic agency connects the biblical and Quranic narratives.

Hezekiah's prayer in the temple (37:14-20) is evaluated positively in Islamic perspective as an example of sincere dua (supplication). The Quran encourages believers to call upon Allah in times of distress: "And your Lord says, 'Call upon Me; I will respond to you'" (Quran 40:60). Hezekiah's prayer exhibits the qualities Islamic theology values in supplication: sincerity (ikhlas), dependence on God alone (tawakkul), and acknowledgment of God's unique sovereignty (tawhid). His affirmation that God alone is God "of all the kingdoms of the earth" (37:16) aligns perfectly with the Islamic confession.

The declaration "I planned it long ago" (37:26) resonates with the Islamic doctrine of qadr (divine decree). Everything that occurs happens by Allah's will and according to His eternal plan: "No disaster strikes except by permission of Allah" (Quran 64:11). Muslim commentators see in this verse confirmation that the biblical prophets taught the same understanding of divine sovereignty that the Quran affirms, even though later textual corruption may have obscured other aspects of the original message.
