## Disobedient Children

Isaiah 30 condemns Judah's leaders for seeking Egyptian help without consulting God. Islamic theology identifies this as *istikhlaaf* — seeking substitutes for God's guidance. The Quran warns: "And whoever takes Satan as an ally instead of Allah has certainly sustained a clear loss" (Al-Nisa 4:119). The prophetic insistence on consulting God before making major decisions is affirmed in the Islamic practice of *istikhara* (the prayer for guidance).

## The Uselessness of Egypt

"Egypt's help is utterly useless" (30:7). Islamic theology affirms that all power belongs to God alone. The Quran states: "Whatever Allah grants to people of mercy — none can withhold it; and whatever He withholds — none can release it thereafter" (Fatir 35:2). The prophetic mockery of worldly powers that claim to offer what only God can provide is consistent across the biblical and Quranic traditions.

## The Voice of Divine Guidance

"Your ears will hear a voice behind you, saying, 'This is the way; walk in it'" (30:21). Islamic theology identifies this "voice" with the guidance that comes through the Quran, the Sunnah, and the inner witness of *fitrah* (the innate human disposition toward God). The Prophet Muhammad said: "I have left among you two things; you will never go astray as long as you hold fast to them: the Book of God and my Sunnah" (Muwatta of Malik).

## Judgment and Celebration

The juxtaposition of divine judgment on the oppressor and song among the faithful (30:29-33) resonates with the Islamic understanding that God's judgment and God's mercy operate simultaneously. The Day of Judgment is a day of terror for the wicked and joy for the righteous — a duality present in both Isaiah and the Quran.
