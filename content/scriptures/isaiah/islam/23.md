## The Judgment of Commercial Power

Isaiah 23 pronounces judgment on Tyre, the great commercial city. Islamic economics — with its prohibition of *riba* (usury), emphasis on *zakat* (obligatory charity), and insistence on fair trade — echoes Isaiah's critique of commercial power divorced from moral accountability. The Quran warns: "And do not consume one another's wealth unjustly" (Al-Baqarah 2:188).

## The Lord's Purpose Against Tyre

"The LORD Almighty planned it, to bring down her pride" (23:9). Islamic theology affirms that God's governance extends to the economic sphere. No commercial system operates independently of divine oversight. The Quran teaches that wealth is a trust (*amanah*) from God, to be used in accordance with His guidance.

## The Sanctification of Commerce

The chapter's conclusion — Tyre's profits "set apart for the LORD" (23:18) — points toward the Islamic ideal of economic activity conducted in accordance with *shari'ah* (divine law). Islam does not condemn commerce but insists that all economic activity must serve divine purposes and the common good. The concept of *halal* (permissible) commerce sanctifies economic life within the framework of divine guidance.
