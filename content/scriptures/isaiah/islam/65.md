Isaiah 65's vision of "new heavens and a new earth" (65:17) resonates with Quranic eschatology, which describes the transformation of creation at the end of time: "The Day the earth will be replaced by another earth, and the heavens as well, and all creatures will come out before Allah" (Quran 14:48). Muslim scholars see in Isaiah's vision confirmation that the biblical prophets taught the same eschatological transformation that the Quran reveals. The "new creation" is understood in Islamic theology as the reconstitution of reality for the eternal life that follows the Day of Judgment.

The conditions described in the renewed world -- long life, secure homes, fruitful labor (65:20-23) -- are compared by Muslim commentators to Quranic descriptions of Paradise (Jannah). The Quran describes Jannah as a place of eternal habitation where "they will not hear therein any ill speech -- only greetings of peace -- and they will have their provision therein, morning and afternoon" (Quran 19:62). The absence of premature death, futile labor, and insecurity corresponds to the Quranic promise of a world purified of all suffering.

The wolf and lamb feeding together (65:25) is read in Islamic tradition as describing the harmony of Paradise, where all enmity ceases. A hadith recorded by Muslim describes the Day of Judgment as a time when "security will prevail on earth so that lions will graze with camels, tigers with cattle, and wolves with sheep." The prophetic vision of interspecies peace reflects the Islamic conviction that the violence and predation of the present world are consequences of the fall that the Hereafter will reverse.
