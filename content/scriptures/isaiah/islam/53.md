Isaiah 53 is one of the most significant points of divergence between Islamic interpretation and Christian theology. Islam categorically rejects the Christian reading that identifies the Suffering Servant as a divine figure whose death atones for the sins of humanity. The Quran's fundamental theological principles oppose this interpretation on multiple grounds: "No bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (Quran 53:38); Allah forgives sins directly without need for an intermediary sacrifice; and the Quran explicitly denies that Jesus (Isa) was crucified: "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but another was made to resemble him to them" (Quran 4:157).

Muslim scholars have offered alternative readings of Isaiah 53. Some identify the suffering servant as the people of Israel, aligning with the Jewish interpretation. Others see the passage as describing the collective suffering of the prophets and righteous servants of God throughout history. Still others regard the chapter as one of the biblical passages most affected by tahrif, where the original meaning has been distorted to support later theological developments that the Quran corrects. Al-Qurtubi and other classical commentators addressed this passage when responding to Christian missionary use of the Hebrew Bible.

The description of vicarious suffering -- "he bore the sin of many" (53:12) -- is particularly problematic for Islamic theology. While Islam acknowledges that prophets suffer, their suffering is understood as a test (ibtila) and a means of elevating their status, not as a mechanism for transferring guilt. The Islamic concept of tawbah (repentance) is direct: the sinner turns to Allah, and Allah forgives without requiring payment or substitution. Each person is responsible for their own deeds: "Every soul earns not blame except against itself" (Quran 6:164). This principle makes the Christian doctrine of substitutionary atonement theologically untenable from the Islamic perspective.
