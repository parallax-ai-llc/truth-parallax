Isaiah 46's contrast between idols carried by their worshippers (46:1-2) and the God who carries His people (46:3-4) is a powerful theological image that Islamic commentators frequently reference. The Quran makes the same point with characteristic directness: the idols "cannot help them, nor can they help themselves" (Quran 7:192). The imagery of Bel and Nebo being loaded onto pack animals as they flee Babylon's fall demonstrates what the Quran calls the "weakness" (da'f) of all that is worshipped besides Allah.

The declaration "I make known the end from the beginning" (46:10) is understood in Islamic theology as expressing ilm al-ghayb -- God's exclusive knowledge of the unseen, past, present, and future. The Quran reserves this knowledge for Allah alone: "And with Him are the keys of the unseen; none knows them except Him" (Quran 6:59). This exclusivity is a defining attribute of divinity in Islamic theology, and any claim by created beings to possess such knowledge is rejected as a form of shirk.

The prediction that God will summon a "bird of prey from the east" (46:11) to accomplish His purposes demonstrates what Islamic theology calls qadr (divine decree) operating through historical causation. Allah does not act randomly but through a chain of causes that He ordains. The Quran's narrative of past peoples -- how civilizations rise and fall according to divine law -- provides the framework for understanding how specific historical figures like Cyrus serve purposes within God's overarching plan.
