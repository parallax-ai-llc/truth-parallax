## The Judgment of Babylon

Isaiah 13 pronounces divine judgment on Babylon. Islamic historical theology recognizes this pattern: God destroys civilizations that persist in oppression and idolatry. The Quran recounts the destruction of numerous ancient peoples as warnings (*'ibrah*) for later generations: "Have they not traveled through the land and seen how was the end of those before them?" (Yusuf 12:109).

## The Day of the Lord

"The day of the LORD is coming — cruel, with wrath and fierce anger" (13:9). Islamic eschatology devotes extensive attention to *Yawm al-Qiyamah* (the Day of Resurrection/Judgment). The cosmic imagery in Isaiah 13 — darkened stars, shaking earth — parallels Quranic descriptions: "When the sun is wrapped up, when the stars fall and lose their luster, when the mountains are moved" (Al-Takwir 81:1-3). Both traditions describe the final judgment in terms of cosmic upheaval.

## Divine Warriors

God summons warriors to execute judgment (13:3). Islamic theology affirms that God uses various means — natural, human, and supernatural — to accomplish His purposes. The concept of divine armies (*junud*) appears in the Quran: "To Allah belong the soldiers of the heavens and the earth" (Al-Fath 48:7). God's sovereignty extends to every realm and every instrument of His will.
