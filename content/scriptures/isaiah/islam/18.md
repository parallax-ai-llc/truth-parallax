## The Distant Nation of Cush

Isaiah 18 addresses Cush with notable respect. Islamic tradition has long valued the Ethiopian connection: the first *hijrah* (emigration) of early Muslims was to the Christian kingdom of Abyssinia (Ethiopia), where the Negus offered them protection. The Prophet Muhammad instructed his companions: "Leave the Abyssinians alone as long as they leave you alone." Isaiah's respectful oracle toward Cush resonates with Islam's historical appreciation for this African civilization.

## God's Patient Sovereignty

"I will remain quiet and will look on from my dwelling place" (18:4). Islamic theology of divine attributes includes *al-Sabur* (the Patient) and *al-Halim* (the Forbearing). God's silence is not indifference but patient sovereignty — waiting for the right moment to act. The Quran teaches: "And Allah gives respite to the wrongdoer until, when He seizes him, He does not let him escape" (Hud 11:102).

## Gifts to the Lord of Hosts

The chapter concludes with Cush bringing gifts to God's dwelling place (18:7). Islamic eschatology envisions a similar universal acknowledgment of God. The submission of all nations to divine sovereignty is the ultimate goal of prophetic history according to Islam.
