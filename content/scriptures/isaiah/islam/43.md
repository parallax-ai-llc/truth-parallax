Isaiah 43's declaration of divine uniqueness -- "Before me no god was formed, nor will there be one after me. I, even I, am the LORD, and apart from me there is no savior" (43:10-11) -- is one of the passages Muslim scholars most readily affirm in the biblical text. These verses express pure tawhid without any trinitarian qualification, and Muslim polemicists from the medieval period onward have cited them as evidence that the original biblical message was strictly monotheistic. The Islamic shahada -- "There is no god but Allah" -- is seen as the same truth Isaiah declares.

The promise of passing through water and fire (43:2) is read in Islamic theology as expressing the general principle that Allah protects those who trust in Him through trials: "And whoever fears Allah -- He will make for him a way out, and will provide for him from where he does not expect" (Quran 65:2-3). The Quranic narrative of Ibrahim (Abraham) being thrown into fire yet emerging unharmed (Quran 21:69) provides a parallel: divine protection operates for all prophets and righteous communities who maintain faith under persecution.

God's declaration that He blots out transgressions "for my own sake" (43:25) resonates with the Quranic teaching on divine forgiveness. The Quran emphasizes that Allah forgives because mercy (rahma) is intrinsic to His nature: "Your Lord has decreed upon Himself mercy" (Quran 6:54). Muslim theology holds that Allah's forgiveness is unconditional in the sense that it requires only sincere repentance (tawbah), not any mediating sacrifice or intercessor -- a reading that differs from Christian atonement theology and that Islamic scholars see supported by Isaiah's own language of direct divine forgiveness.
