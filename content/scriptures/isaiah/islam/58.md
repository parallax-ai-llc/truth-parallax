Isaiah 58 is one of the prophetic passages most valued in Islamic ethical teaching for its insistence that ritual worship must be accompanied by social justice. The Quran makes the same point with equal force: "It is not righteousness that you turn your faces toward the east or the west, but true righteousness is in one who believes in Allah... and gives wealth, in spite of love for it, to relatives, orphans, the needy, the traveler, those who ask, and for freeing slaves" (Quran 2:177). Isaiah's rejection of fasting divorced from justice perfectly mirrors the Quranic integration of ibadah (worship) and muamalat (social dealings).

The specific acts of justice Isaiah demands -- feeding the hungry, sheltering the homeless, clothing the naked (58:7) -- correspond to the Islamic obligations of sadaqah (charitable giving) and zakat (obligatory alms). The Quran repeatedly pairs salat (prayer) with zakat (charity), making clear that worship without social responsibility is incomplete: "And establish prayer and give zakat" (Quran 2:43). The Prophet Muhammad taught that "whoever goes to sleep with a full stomach while his neighbor is hungry is not truly a believer" (al-Hakim) -- a sentiment Isaiah would fully endorse.

The connection between true fasting and spiritual illumination -- "Then your light will break forth like the dawn" (58:8) -- resonates with the Islamic understanding of Ramadan. Fasting in Islam is not merely abstinence from food but a comprehensive discipline that includes controlling the tongue, the eyes, and the heart. The Quran explains the purpose of fasting: "that you may become righteous" (Quran 2:183). Isaiah's teaching that the fast God desires involves social transformation aligns with the Islamic view that Ramadan should produce lasting ethical change, not just temporary physical deprivation.
