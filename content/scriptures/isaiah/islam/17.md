## The Oracle Against Damascus

Isaiah 17 pronounces judgment on Damascus and its alliance with Ephraim. Islamic interpretation reads this as an instance of God's judgment on nations that form alliances in opposition to divine guidance. The Quran warns against alliances that substitute for trust in God: "O you who believe, do not take the disbelievers as allies instead of the believers" (Al-Nisa 4:144) — though the principle applies to all forms of misplaced trust.

## Turning to the Maker

"In that day people will look to their Maker" (17:7). This verse expresses the core Islamic conviction of *tawhid*: all human beings are called to recognize and worship their Creator alone. The Quran declares: "And your god is one God. There is no deity except Him" (Al-Baqarah 2:163). Isaiah's vision of people abandoning idols to turn to their Maker anticipates the universal monotheism that Islam proclaims.

## The Sovereignty of God Over Nations

The chapter's closing image of nations rebuked and scattered by God (17:13) affirms what the Quran declares repeatedly: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth" (Al-Ma'idah 5:120). No nation, however powerful, can resist God's command.
