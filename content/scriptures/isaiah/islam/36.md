Islamic interpretation of Isaiah 36 reads the Assyrian threat to Jerusalem as an illustration of the Quranic principle that oppressive powers, no matter how mighty, are subject to Allah's decree (qadr). The Rabshakeh's arrogance -- claiming divine authorization for Assyria's campaign (36:10) -- exemplifies what the Quran condemns as speaking about Allah without knowledge: "Say, 'My Lord has only forbidden immoralities... and that you say about Allah that which you do not know'" (Quran 7:33).

The theological manipulation in the Rabshakeh's speech is analyzed by Muslim commentators as a form of deception that the Quran warns against. His demand that the people abandon trust in God and submit to superior military force (36:4-10) contradicts the Islamic virtue of tawakkul (reliance on Allah). The Quran teaches, "And upon Allah let the believers rely" (Quran 3:122), and the example of the Muslim community at the Battle of Badr -- outnumbered but victorious through divine aid -- provides a parallel to Jerusalem's situation.

The historical details of the Assyrian campaign are valued in Islamic historiography, which sees the Israelite prophets as genuine messengers whose narratives, while subject to textual corruption, preserve authentic accounts of God's dealings with earlier communities. Ibn Kathir in Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah includes the Sennacherib narrative as part of the continuous history of prophetic communities, connecting it to the Quranic principle that Allah protects those who trust in Him and destroys the arrogant.
