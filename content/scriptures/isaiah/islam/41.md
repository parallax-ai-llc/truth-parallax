Isaiah 41's trial speech, in which God challenges the idols to demonstrate their power, is read in Islamic theology as a powerful expression of tawhid -- the absolute oneness and uniqueness of God. The challenge "Tell us what the future holds" (41:22) establishes a criterion that the Quran also applies: true deity is demonstrated by knowledge of the unseen (ilm al-ghayb), which belongs to Allah alone: "Say, 'None in the heavens and earth knows the unseen except Allah'" (Quran 27:65). The idols' silence (41:28-29) confirms what the Quran declares about false gods.

The "fear not" declarations (41:10, 13, 14) correspond to the Quranic assurance given to believers: "Indeed, those who have said, 'Our Lord is Allah,' and then remained on a right course -- the angels will descend upon them, saying, 'Do not fear and do not grieve'" (Quran 41:30). Islamic spirituality understands fear of anything other than Allah as a form of shirk (associating partners with God), because it attributes ultimate power to a created thing. The repeated "fear not" in Isaiah is thus read as a call to pure tawakkul.

The idol polemic (41:21-29) aligns with one of the Quran's most persistent themes: the condemnation of shirk as the greatest sin. "Indeed, Allah does not forgive association with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills" (Quran 4:48). Muslim commentators see Isaiah's vigorous monotheism as evidence of the original purity of the biblical message, before what Islam regards as later theological corruptions introduced concepts that compromise strict divine unity.
