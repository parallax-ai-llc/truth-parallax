Islamic interpretation of Hezekiah's illness (Isaiah 38) connects it to the broader Quranic theme of illness and healing as tests from Allah. The Quran teaches that suffering is a means of spiritual purification: "And We will surely test you with something of fear and hunger and a loss of wealth and lives and fruits, but give good tidings to the patient" (Quran 2:155). Hezekiah's response -- turning to God in prayer rather than despairing -- models the Islamic virtue of sabr (patient endurance) combined with active supplication.

The healing that follows Hezekiah's prayer illustrates the Quranic teaching that Allah is Ash-Shafi (the Healer). The Quran attributes Ibrahim's (Abraham's) declaration to this divine attribute: "And when I am ill, it is He who cures me" (Quran 26:80). Muslim tradition holds that all healing ultimately comes from Allah, whether through natural means (like the fig poultice of 38:21), prayer, or miraculous intervention. The combination of prayer and material remedy in Hezekiah's case corresponds to the Islamic approach to medicine, which values both medical treatment and spiritual supplication.

The additional fifteen years granted to Hezekiah (38:5) raises questions about ajal (the appointed term of life) in Islamic theology. The Quran states, "When their term has come, they will not remain behind an hour, nor will they precede it" (Quran 7:34). Muslim theologians reconcile apparent changes in lifespan through the concept of the "Preserved Tablet" (al-Lawh al-Mahfuz), which contains the final decree, while intermediate records may be altered through dua and good deeds. This theological framework accommodates Hezekiah's extension without contradicting the fixity of divine decree.
