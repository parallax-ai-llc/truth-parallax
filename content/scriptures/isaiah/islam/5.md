## The Parable of the Vineyard

Isaiah 5:1-7 presents God's care for His people as a vinedresser's care for a vineyard. Islamic interpretation recognizes the parable form as a prophetic teaching method used throughout scripture. The Quran itself employs agricultural parables: "The example of those who spend their wealth in the way of Allah is like a seed of grain that sprouts seven ears" (Al-Baqarah 2:261). Both traditions use creation imagery to teach spiritual truths.

## The Woes Against Injustice

The six woes (5:8-23) condemn sins that the Quran also explicitly prohibits: exploitation of the poor, drunkenness, moral inversion, and corruption of justice. The Quranic prohibition of *khamr* (intoxicants, Al-Ma'idah 5:90) echoes Isaiah's woe against those who "rise early in the morning to run after their drinks" (5:11). The prophetic critique of social injustice is remarkably consistent across the biblical and Quranic traditions.

## Divine Judgment Through Nations

God's summoning of a distant nation to execute judgment (5:26) illustrates the Islamic understanding of God's sovereignty (*qadr*) over all nations and events. The Quran affirms that God raises and destroys civilizations according to His will: "How many a town We have destroyed while it was committing wrongdoing" (Al-Anbiya 21:11). The prophetic insight that God uses nations as instruments of judgment is shared by both traditions.
