The opening of Isaiah 57 -- the righteous perishing while the wicked prosper (57:1-2) -- addresses a theological problem that the Quran also engages. The Quran teaches that worldly prosperity is not a measure of divine approval: "And let not those who disbelieve ever think that because We extend their time of enjoyment it is better for them. We only extend it for them so that they may increase in sin" (Quran 3:178). The righteous may suffer and die while the wicked flourish temporarily, but the ultimate accounting occurs on the Day of Judgment.

The condemnation of idolatrous practices (57:3-10) -- including child sacrifice and illicit worship -- is read through the Quranic prohibition of shirk and its associated practices. The pre-Islamic Arabs engaged in practices similar to those Isaiah condemns: burying daughters alive, worshipping at sacred stones and trees, and practicing divination. The Quran abolished these practices and identified them as remnants of the jahiliyyah (age of ignorance) that prophetic revelation overcomes.

The paradox of verse 15 -- God dwelling in the "high and holy place" yet with the "contrite and lowly in spirit" -- expresses a truth that Islamic theology affirms through the concept of divine nearness (qurb). The Quran states, "And We have already created man and know what his soul whispers to him, and We are closer to him than his jugular vein" (Quran 50:16). Islamic mystical tradition (tasawwuf) explores this divine intimacy extensively, teaching that God's transcendence and immanence are both real and non-contradictory -- a theological insight that Isaiah's paradox beautifully captures.
