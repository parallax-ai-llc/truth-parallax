## The Swift Spoil

Isaiah 8 presents the sign-child Maher-shalal-hash-baz as evidence that prophetic warnings will be fulfilled. Islamic theology affirms that God always fulfills His promises and warnings — "The promise of Allah is true" (Luqman 31:33). The prophetic sign functions as evidence of divine truthfulness, establishing the prophet's credentials.

## The Overwhelming Flood

The image of Assyrian invasion as a flood (8:7-8) resonates with Quranic descriptions of divine punishment falling on disobedient peoples. The Quran recounts the destruction of numerous ancient civilizations — 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lut — through natural catastrophes that serve as divine signs. Isaiah's flood imagery belongs to the same prophetic tradition of warning through dramatic natural metaphors.

## Seeking Guidance from God Alone

"Should not a people inquire of their God?" (8:19). This rhetorical question aligns with the Quranic condemnation of seeking guidance from any source other than God. The Quran prohibits *sihr* (sorcery) and divination, insisting that guidance comes from God alone through His prophets and His Book. Isaiah's polemic against necromancy and spiritism echoes the Islamic insistence on *tawhid* in all matters of guidance.
