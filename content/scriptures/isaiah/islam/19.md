## The Lord Comes to Egypt

Isaiah 19 describes God's intervention in Egypt — judgment followed by remarkable restoration. Islamic historical consciousness notes that Egypt became one of the earliest and most important centers of Islam after the Arab conquest. The transformation of Egypt from a land of idolatry to a center of monotheistic worship can be read as a fulfillment of Isaiah's prophetic vision.

## The Failure of Idolatry

"The idols of Egypt tremble before him" (19:1). Islamic theology of *tawhid* affirms that all idols are powerless before God. The Quran recounts how Ibrahim (Abraham) destroyed his people's idols and challenged them: "Do you worship that which you yourselves carve?" (Al-Saffat 37:95). Isaiah's depiction of trembling idols anticipates the universal triumph of monotheism.

## The Altar in Egypt

"In that day there will be an altar to the LORD in the heart of Egypt" (19:19). Islamic interpretation sees this as fulfilled in the establishment of mosques throughout Egypt — places of worship dedicated to the One God. The great mosques of Cairo — Al-Azhar, Amr ibn al-As, Ibn Tulun — represent the transformation of Egypt into a center of divine worship.

## Egypt, Assyria, and Israel Blessed Together

"Blessed be Egypt my people, Assyria my handiwork, and Israel my inheritance" (19:25). This remarkable verse envisions the reconciliation of ancient enemies under God's blessing. Islamic universalism — "We have not sent you [Muhammad] except as a mercy to the worlds" (Al-Anbiya 21:107) — echoes this vision of divine blessing encompassing all peoples.
