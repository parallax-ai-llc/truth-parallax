Isaiah 55's free invitation -- "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat" (55:1) -- resonates with the Islamic understanding of divine generosity (karam). Allah's provision is not earned but freely given: "And there is no creature on earth but that upon Allah is its provision" (Quran 11:6). The gratuity of God's offer in Isaiah -- "without money and without cost" -- corresponds to the Islamic rejection of any transactional understanding of the God-human relationship. Salvation is not purchased but received through sincere submission (islam) to the divine will.

The assurance that God's word "will not return to me empty" (55:11) is understood in Islamic theology through the concept of the effective divine word (kalimah). The Quran itself is understood as the eternal speech (kalam) of Allah, uncreated and all-powerful: "Indeed, when He intends a thing, His command is only that He says to it, 'Be,' and it is" (Quran 36:82). Every divine utterance accomplishes its purpose, and the prophetic messages throughout history -- including those delivered through Isaiah -- participate in this creative and sustaining power.

The imagery of nature rejoicing -- "the mountains and hills will burst into song" (55:12) -- is connected by Muslim commentators to the Quranic teaching that all creation engages in divine praise (tasbih): "The seven heavens and the earth and whatever is in them exalt Him. And there is not a thing except that it exalts His praise, but you do not understand their way of exalting" (Quran 17:44). Isaiah's vision of a singing creation is thus not mere poetry but a description of the cosmic reality that the Quran reveals: every element of creation glorifies its Creator.
