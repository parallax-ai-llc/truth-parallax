Isaiah 39's account of Hezekiah displaying his treasures to Babylonian envoys is read in Islamic interpretation as a cautionary tale about the dangers of pride (kibr) and showing off (riya). The Quran warns repeatedly against ostentation: "And do not walk upon the earth exultantly. Indeed, you will never tear the earth apart, and you will never reach the mountains in height" (Quran 17:37). Hezekiah's display of wealth (39:2) contradicts the Islamic virtue of humility that should characterize those whom Allah has blessed.

The prophecy of Babylonian exile (39:5-7) demonstrates the Islamic principle that divine blessings misused lead to divine retribution. The Quran describes this pattern in the story of Qarun (Korah), who boasted of his wealth and was swallowed by the earth (Quran 28:76-82). Muslim commentators draw parallel lessons: material prosperity is a trust (amanah) from Allah, not a personal achievement, and displaying it pridefully invites divine correction.

The transition from chapters 39 to 40 -- from judgment to consolation -- illustrates what Muslim theologians call the balance between divine justice (adl) and divine mercy (rahma). The Quran teaches that Allah's mercy encompasses all things (Quran 7:156), and even His punishments serve a merciful purpose: to awaken the heedless, purify the faithful, and ultimately guide creation toward its proper end. Isaiah's shift from the announcement of exile to the promise of comfort reflects this Quranic balance.
