Isaiah 66 brings the prophetic book to its conclusion with themes that intersect significantly with Quranic theology. The declaration "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool" (66:1) expresses divine transcendence in language that Islamic theology affirms while carefully avoiding anthropomorphism. The Quran describes Allah's Throne (Arsh) as encompassing the heavens and earth: "His Throne extends over the heavens and the earth, and their preservation tires Him not" (Quran 2:255). Islamic scholars interpret both the biblical and Quranic "throne" language as expressing sovereignty rather than physical location.

The image of sudden birth (66:7-9) is connected by Muslim scholars to the Quranic teaching about the suddenness of the Hour (al-Sa'ah): "They ask you about the Hour: when is its arrival? Say, 'Its knowledge is only with my Lord. None will reveal its time except Him. It lays heavily upon the heavens and the earth. It will not come upon you except unexpectedly'" (Quran 7:187). The transformative events of the end times occur with startling speed, catching the unprepared off guard.

The book's closing vision of universal worship (66:23) aligns with the Islamic eschatological hope that all creation will ultimately acknowledge Allah's sovereignty. The Quran teaches that on the Day of Judgment, "every knee shall bow" and every tongue confess the divine reality. The sobering image of unending judgment (66:24) -- "the worms that eat them will not die, the fire that burns them will not be quenched" -- corresponds closely to Quranic descriptions of Jahannam (Hell): "Every time their skins are roasted through, We will replace them with other skins so they may taste the punishment" (Quran 4:56). Islamic eschatology takes the reality of eternal punishment seriously, using it as motivation for repentance in the present life.
