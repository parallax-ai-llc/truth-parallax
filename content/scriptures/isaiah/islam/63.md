The divine warrior imagery of Isaiah 63:1-6 is read in Islamic theology through the lens of divine judgment (hisab) and retribution (iqab). Allah's power to defeat oppressive nations is a recurring Quranic theme: "And how many a city did We destroy while it was committing wrong -- so it is now fallen into ruin -- and how many an abandoned well and lofty palace" (Quran 22:45). The bloodstained garments of the warrior returning from Edom represent divine justice executed against those who oppress God's servants -- a justice that Islamic eschatology places ultimately at the Day of Judgment.

The communal lament (63:7-19) models the kind of honest prayer that Islam values. The Quran presents prophets who questioned, pleaded, and even expressed frustration in their dialogue with Allah -- from Ibrahim's question "My Lord, show me how You give life to the dead" (Quran 2:260) to Musa's (Moses') persistent petitions. The declaration "You, LORD, are our Father" (63:16) is, however, problematic for Islamic theology. While the verse expresses divine care, Islam does not use parental language for God. Allah is Rabb (Lord/Sustainer) and Wali (Protector/Friend), but not "Father," as this could imply begetting, which the Quran explicitly denies: "He neither begets nor is born" (Quran 112:3).

The anguished question "Why do you make us wander from your ways?" (63:17) engages the Islamic debate about divine guidance and human responsibility. The Quran teaches that Allah guides whom He wills and leads astray whom He wills (Quran 14:4), but Islamic theology generally maintains that this "leading astray" follows the person's own choice to reject guidance: "Allah does not wrong the people at all, but it is the people who are wronging themselves" (Quran 10:44).
