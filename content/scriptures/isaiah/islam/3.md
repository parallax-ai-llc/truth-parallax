## The Collapse of Unjust Leadership

Isaiah 3 describes God's removal of corrupt leaders. Islamic political theology affirms that just governance is a divine trust (*amanah*) that can be withdrawn when leaders fail their responsibilities. The Quran warns: "O you who believe, do not betray God and the Messenger, nor knowingly betray your trusts" (Al-Anfal 8:27). The prophetic critique of unjust leadership is consistent across the biblical and Quranic traditions.

## Social Justice and the Poor

"The LORD enters into judgment against the elders and leaders: it is you who have devoured the vineyard; the plunder from the poor is in your houses" (3:14). Islamic social ethics, grounded in the Quranic mandate of *zakat* (obligatory charity) and the prohibition of *riba* (usury), echoes Isaiah's denunciation of those who exploit the vulnerable. The Prophet Muhammad declared: "He is not a believer whose stomach is filled while his neighbor goes hungry."

## Vanity and Superficiality

The extended critique of the "daughters of Zion" (3:16-26) carries a message that Islamic moral theology affirms: the pursuit of outward adornment at the expense of inner virtue is a form of spiritual blindness. The Quran teaches: "O children of Adam, We have bestowed upon you clothing to conceal your private parts and as adornment. But the clothing of righteousness — that is best" (Al-A'raf 7:26).
