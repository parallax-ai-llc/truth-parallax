Isaiah 64's prayer "Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down" (64:1) is read by Muslim scholars with reservations about its theological implications. Islam emphasizes divine transcendence (tanzih): Allah does not literally "come down" or dwell within creation. The Quran describes God's relationship to creation through His attributes and acts, not through physical descent. This prayer is understood either as poetic language expressing the longing for divine intervention or as an example of the theological imprecision that Islam sees in the current biblical text.

The confession "All our righteous acts are like filthy rags" (64:6) is not accepted in Islamic theology as a statement about the inherent worthlessness of human goodness. Islam teaches that righteous deeds (amal salih) have genuine value before Allah: "Whoever does righteousness, whether male or female, while being a believer -- those will enter Paradise and will not be wronged, even as much as the speck on a date seed" (Quran 4:124). The Islamic view is that human beings, created in the best form (ahsan taqwim, Quran 95:4), are capable of genuine goodness when guided by revelation and divine assistance (tawfiq).

The potter-clay metaphor (64:8) is affirmed in Islamic theology to the extent that it expresses divine sovereignty over creation. Allah is al-Musawwir (the Fashioner) and al-Khaliq (the Creator) who shapes humanity according to His will: "He is Allah, the Creator, the Inventor, the Fashioner; to Him belong the best names" (Quran 59:24). However, Islamic theology insists that the clay is not merely passive: human beings bear the amanah (trust) of moral responsibility (Quran 33:72) and are accountable for their choices within the parameters God has established.
