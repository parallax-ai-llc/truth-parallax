Isaiah 61 describes an anointed figure proclaiming liberation and restoration. Islamic interpretation reads this through the lens of the prophetic mission: every prophet was anointed in the sense of being chosen and empowered by Allah for a specific task. The Spirit's role in the anointing (61:1) is understood in Islamic theology as referring to the Ruh (divine Spirit/breath) that empowers the prophets, not as a distinct divine person (as in Trinitarian theology). The Quran describes the Ruh as Allah's instrument: "Say, 'The Holy Spirit has brought it down from your Lord in truth'" (Quran 16:102).

The "year of the LORD's favor" (61:2) is connected by Muslim scholars to the concept of Jubilee and, more broadly, to the Islamic principle of social justice that requires periodic redistribution of wealth. The Quran's zakat system and its prohibition of riba (usury) serve similar functions to the Jubilee: preventing the permanent concentration of wealth in few hands. The Quranic principle "so that wealth does not circulate only among the rich of you" (Quran 59:7) echoes Isaiah's vision of liberation and restoration for the dispossessed.

The transformation described -- "beauty for ashes, gladness for mourning" (61:3) -- is read in Islamic spiritual tradition as describing the effects of tawbah (repentance) and divine forgiveness. When a person turns sincerely to Allah, the burden of sin is replaced by the lightness of divine acceptance. The hadith records the Prophet saying, "He who repents from sin is like one who has no sin" (Ibn Majah). Isaiah's imagery of radical transformation captures the Islamic conviction that God's mercy can completely renew the human condition.
