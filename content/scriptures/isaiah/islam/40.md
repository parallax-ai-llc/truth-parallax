Isaiah 40's declaration of divine transcendence and sovereignty is one of the passages Islamic scholars have most appreciated in the biblical text, finding it closely aligned with Quranic theology. The rhetorical questions -- "Who has measured the waters in the hollow of his hand?" (40:12) and "To whom will you compare God?" (40:18) -- echo the Quran's insistence on divine incomparability: "There is nothing like unto Him" (Quran 42:11). Muslim commentators see in Isaiah 40 evidence that the original biblical revelation contained a pure monotheism consistent with tawhid.

The "voice crying in the wilderness" (40:3) is read differently in Islamic tradition than in Christian exegesis. Muslim scholars do not identify this voice with John the Baptist (Yahya) in any special sense, though Yahya is honored as a prophet in the Quran (Quran 19:12-15). Rather, the "voice" represents the prophetic call that has been heard throughout history: every prophet, from Adam to Muhammad, has called people to prepare for divine encounter by straightening their ways and submitting to God's will.

The passage "All people are like grass... but the word of our God endures forever" (40:6-8) resonates with the Quranic theme of the transience of worldly life (dunya) and the permanence of divine reality. The Quran states, "Whatever you have will end, but what Allah has is lasting" (Quran 16:96). Islamic spirituality (tasawwuf) draws on this contrast between temporal and eternal to cultivate zuhd (detachment from worldly things) -- not as world-rejection but as proper ordering of priorities, placing the eternal above the ephemeral.
