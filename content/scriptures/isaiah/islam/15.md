## Compassion for the Suffering

Isaiah 15 laments Moab's destruction with genuine grief. Islamic ethics (*akhlaq*) values compassion for all people, including those outside the Muslim community. The Prophet Muhammad said: "The merciful are shown mercy by the Most Merciful. Be merciful to those on earth, and the One above the heavens will be merciful to you" (Sunan al-Tirmidhi). Isaiah's compassion for Moab models the universal mercy that Islam teaches.

## The Fragility of Nations

The detailed description of Moab's devastated cities illustrates the Quranic theme that no civilization is permanent. "How many a town We have destroyed that was insolent in its way of living, and those are their dwellings which have not been inhabited after them except briefly" (Al-Qasas 28:58). The prophetic warning against false security applies to all peoples and all eras.

## Flight and Displacement

The image of Moabite refugees fleeing destruction resonates with the Islamic value of hospitality to travelers and displaced persons. The Prophet Muhammad himself was a refugee (*muhajir*), and the Quranic concept of *hijrah* (migration for God's sake) dignifies the experience of displacement while affirming God's sovereignty over all circumstances.
