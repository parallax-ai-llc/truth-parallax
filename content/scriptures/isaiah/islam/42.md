Islamic interpretation of the "servant" in Isaiah 42:1-9 diverges significantly from both Jewish and Christian readings. Some Muslim scholars, following a tradition attributed to Ibn Abbas, have identified the servant as the Prophet Muhammad, pointing to the description of one who brings "justice to the nations" (42:1) and serves as "a light for the Gentiles" (42:6). This reading sees Isaiah as prophesying the coming of the final messenger who would establish a universal community (ummah) transcending ethnic boundaries.

The servant's gentleness -- "A bruised reed he will not break" (42:3) -- is compared by Muslim commentators to the Quranic description of Muhammad's character: "And indeed, you are of a great moral character" (Quran 68:4) and "It was by the mercy of Allah that you were lenient with them" (Quran 3:159). The pattern of a gentle prophet who establishes justice not through coercion but through moral example is central to the Islamic understanding of Muhammad's mission.

However, mainstream Islamic scholarship treats all such identifications cautiously, given the doctrine of tahrif (textual corruption). The principle is that biblical passages may preserve authentic prophetic content but cannot be relied upon with certainty in their current form. The Quran itself is the criterion (furqan) by which all prior scriptures are evaluated. Where Isaiah's servant passages align with Quranic teaching about prophethood and justice, Muslim scholars affirm them; where they diverge, the Quran takes precedence.
