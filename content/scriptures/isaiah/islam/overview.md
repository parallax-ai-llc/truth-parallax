## Overview

In Islamic tradition, Isaiah (*Sha'ya* in Arabic) is regarded as one of the prophets of the Children of Israel who preached monotheism and warned against idolatry. While the book of Isaiah is not part of the Quran, Islamic scholars have engaged with it extensively — particularly passages understood to foretell the coming of Prophet Muhammad. Islamic interpretation operates through the principle that the original revelations given to Israelite prophets were authentic but that the transmitted biblical text has been subject to *tahrif* (alteration or corruption).

## Relationship to the Quran

While Isaiah is not directly named in the Quran, several Isaianic themes have strong Quranic resonance:

| Isaiah Theme | Quranic Parallel | Key Surahs |
|-------------|-----------------|------------|
| God's absolute oneness | Tawhid | Al-Ikhlas 112:1-4 |
| Condemnation of idolatry | Rejection of shirk | Al-An'am 6:74-82 |
| Universal judgment | Day of Judgment (Yawm al-Qiyamah) | Al-Qari'ah 101 |
| Light to the nations | Islam as mercy to the worlds | Al-Anbiya 21:107 |
| The servant of God | Muhammad as 'abd Allah | Al-Jinn 72:19 |

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Muhammad in Isaiah
Classical Muslim scholars — including **Ibn Kathir**, **Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya**, and **Ahmad Deedat** — have identified passages in Isaiah as foretelling Prophet Muhammad. Isaiah 42:1-4 (the servant who brings justice to the nations) and Isaiah 21:7 (the rider on a camel) are among the most frequently cited.

### Tahrif (Textual Corruption)
Islamic theology holds that the *Tawrat* and *Injil* were genuine divine revelations that were subsequently altered. Isaiah's text is therefore read selectively: passages affirming pure monotheism and prophetic ethics are accepted, while those attributed to theological corruption are set aside.

### Tawhid as Hermeneutical Key
All prophetic scripture is read through the lens of strict divine unity. Isaiah's proclamations of God's uniqueness — "I am the LORD, and there is no other" (45:5) — are embraced as authentic prophetic monotheism consistent with the Quranic message.

## Theological Emphases

- **Prophetic Continuity**: Isaiah is one link in the chain of prophets culminating in Muhammad
- **Social Justice**: Isaiah's denunciation of oppression resonates with Quranic emphasis on justice (*'adl*) and care for the vulnerable
- **Eschatology**: Isaiah's apocalyptic imagery parallels Quranic accounts of the Last Day
- **Anti-Idolatry**: Isaiah's mockery of idol-making (44:9-20) aligns with the Quranic rejection of *shirk*

## Major Commentators

| Scholar | Era | Contribution |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| Ibn Kathir | 14th century | *Al-Bidaya wa'l-Nihaya* — references to Isaiah in prophetic history |
| Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya | 14th century | *Hidayat al-Hayara* — biblical prophecies of Muhammad |
| Al-Tabari | 9th-10th century | Historical references to Sha'ya in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa'l-Muluk* |
| Ahmad Deedat | 20th century | Popular apologetics on biblical prophecies of Muhammad |
| Rahmatullah Kairanawi | 19th century | *Izhar al-Haqq* — systematic comparison of Bible and Quran |
