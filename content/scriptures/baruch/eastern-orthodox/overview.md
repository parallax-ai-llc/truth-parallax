## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church receives Baruch as canonical scripture within the Septuagint tradition, closely linked to the prophecy of Jeremiah. Orthodox interpretation reads the book as a continuation of Jeremiah's prophetic ministry through his scribe, addressing the exiled community with a call to repentance and a promise of restoration.

## Theological Emphases

Orthodox exegesis highlights the book's theology of corporate repentance. The confession in chapters 1-3, where the exiled community acknowledges its collective sin, resonates with the Orthodox understanding of the Church as a body that repents and prays together. The wisdom poem (3:9-4:4) is read in patristic tradition as a witness to the pre-incarnate Logos. The verse "She appeared on earth and lived among men" (3:38) is interpreted by some Church Fathers as a prophecy of the Incarnation.

## Liturgical Context

Baruch is read in Orthodox lectionary cycles, and its themes of exile, repentance, and return inform the theology of Great Lent. The personification of Jerusalem as a mourning and then consoled mother (chapters 4-5) connects to the Orthodox veneration of the Theotokos and the theology of the Church as mother of the faithful. The Letter of Jeremiah (chapter 6) provides a sustained argument against idolatry that Orthodox tradition applies to all forms of false worship.
