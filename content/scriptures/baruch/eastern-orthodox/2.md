Orthodox exegesis of Baruch 2 emphasizes the chapter's theology of corporate repentance. The confession acknowledges that the entire community bears responsibility for the sins that led to exile, reflecting the Orthodox understanding of sin as communal as well as personal. The Orthodox penitential services, particularly the Canon of St. Andrew of Crete read during Great Lent, share this chapter's sense of the community confessing together before God.

The chapter's appeal to God's mercy amid acknowledged sin reflects the Orthodox theology of divine philanthropia (love of humanity). God's justice in sending exile is affirmed, but the prayer's ultimate confidence rests on God's nature as merciful and compassionate. This dialectic between justice and mercy, judgment and hope, is central to Orthodox soteriology and is expressed liturgically in the repeated petition: 'Lord, have mercy.'
