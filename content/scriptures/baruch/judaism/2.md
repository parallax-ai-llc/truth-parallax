Jewish scholarly analysis of Baruch 2 notes the prayer's close parallels with Daniel 9 and Nehemiah 9, suggesting a shared liturgical tradition of communal confession in the Second Temple period. This tradition of corporate repentance, acknowledging national sin as the cause of exile, became foundational in Jewish liturgy. The Yom Kippur Vidui (confession) carries forward this pattern of communal acknowledgment of sin.

The chapter's Deuteronomistic theology, in which exile is the direct consequence of covenant violation, represents one strand of Jewish thinking about suffering that has coexisted with other perspectives throughout Jewish history. The book of Job challenges this framework, and rabbinic literature develops a more nuanced understanding of divine justice. Jewish readers engage Baruch 2 as a significant example of one important theological tradition while recognizing its limitations as a complete account of God's ways.
