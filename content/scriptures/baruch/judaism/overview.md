## Overview

Baruch is not part of the Jewish biblical canon (Tanakh), and no Hebrew original has survived. The book is nonetheless significant for Jewish studies as a product of Second Temple Judaism that reflects the theological concerns of Jews grappling with the experience of exile and the question of how to maintain faithfulness far from Jerusalem and the Temple.

## Historical Significance

The penitential prayer in Baruch closely parallels the prayer of Daniel 9, suggesting a shared liturgical tradition of communal confession in the Second Temple period. This tradition of corporate repentance, acknowledging national sin as the cause of exile and appealing to God's mercy, became foundational in Jewish liturgy, particularly in the Yom Kippur services. Baruch thus provides important evidence for the development of penitential prayer in Judaism.

## The Wisdom Poem and Torah

The identification of wisdom with Torah in Baruch 3:9-4:4 reflects a theological development also visible in Sirach (Ben Sira) 24. This equation of cosmic wisdom with the specific revelation given to Israel at Sinai became a central tenet of rabbinic Judaism. While Baruch itself was not received into the canon, its theology of Torah as the unique possession and privilege of Israel is thoroughly Jewish and deeply influential.
