Islamic reading of Baruch 2 would affirm the chapter's theology of divine punishment followed by mercy for the repentant. The Quran presents this as a universal pattern: 'Whatever misfortune happens to you is because of what your hands have earned, and He pardons much' (Quran 42:30). The communal confession in Baruch 2, acknowledging that exile results from disobedience, is consistent with this Quranic principle.

Muslim scholars would note the parallels between Baruch's prayer and the Quranic stories of past nations. The Quran describes numerous communities that were punished for rejecting their prophets but were offered mercy when they repented. The chapter's movement from confession to appeal for mercy reflects the Islamic understanding of tawbah as a return to God that is always possible and always welcomed: 'Say: O My servants who have transgressed against their souls, do not despair of the mercy of Allah' (Quran 39:53).
