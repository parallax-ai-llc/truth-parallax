Islamic engagement with Baruch 3 would find strong resonance in the chapter's teaching that true wisdom comes only from God. The Quran presents knowledge (ilm) as a divine attribute that God shares with humanity through revelation: 'He taught Adam the names of all things' (Quran 2:31). The chapter's assertion that no nation has found wisdom through its own efforts aligns with the Islamic teaching that human reason alone is insufficient for guidance.

The identification of wisdom with divine law (Torah in Baruch, Sharia in Islam) reflects a shared theological conviction that God's revealed guidance is the supreme form of wisdom. Muslim scholars would note the parallel between Baruch's claim that wisdom is uniquely given to Israel through Torah and the Islamic teaching that the Quran is the final, perfected revelation of divine wisdom to all humanity. Both traditions affirm that wisdom is not a human achievement but a divine gift.
