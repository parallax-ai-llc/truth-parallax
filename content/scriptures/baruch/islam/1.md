Islamic engagement with Baruch 1 would read the chapter through the Quranic lens of prophetic history. The exile of the Israelites as a consequence of disobedience is a pattern the Quran recognizes repeatedly: nations that reject God's guidance face divine punishment, but the door of repentance (tawbah) remains open. The exiles' weeping and fasting in response to Baruch's reading models the proper response to awareness of sin.

The chapter's portrait of a community maintaining its worship and spiritual discipline in exile resonates with the early Muslim community's experience of hijra (migration) from Mecca to Medina. The principle that believers can maintain their faith and communal worship even far from their sacred center is affirmed in Islamic theology. Muslim readers would recognize in the exiles' devotion to Jerusalem a parallel to their own attachment to Mecca and the Ka'ba.
