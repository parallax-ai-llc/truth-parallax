## Overview

Baruch is not recognized as scripture in Islam and has no direct parallel in the Quran or Hadith. The book is attributed to the scribe of Jeremiah (Irmiya in Islamic tradition), a figure recognized in some Islamic sources as a prophet or righteous person associated with the destruction of Jerusalem. Islamic engagement with Baruch would fall under the category of Isra'iliyyat.

## Parallels and Resonances

The book's central themes resonate strongly with Quranic theology. The penitential prayer, confessing that exile is a consequence of disobedience to God, parallels the Quranic pattern in which nations are punished for rejecting their prophets and then offered mercy upon repentance. The Letter of Jeremiah's polemic against idolatry (chapter 6) is closely aligned with the Quran's sustained critique of idol worship (shirk), which is considered the gravest sin in Islam.

## Islamic Assessment

Muslim scholars would find Baruch's monotheistic theology and its condemnation of idolatry fully consistent with Islamic principles. The book's insistence that suffering results from disobedience to God and that mercy follows repentance mirrors the Quranic formula found throughout the stories of past nations. The identification of wisdom with divine law also resonates with the Islamic understanding of Sharia as God's guidance for humanity.
