Islamic reading of Baruch 4 would appreciate the chapter's message of hope amid suffering. The personification of Jerusalem as a mother encouraging her children to trust in God's promise resonates with the Quranic assurance that divine help is always near: 'Indeed, with hardship comes ease' (Quran 94:5-6). The chapter's movement from lament to hope mirrors the Quranic pattern of testing followed by divine relief.

Muslim scholars would note the parallels between the exiles' experience and the trials faced by various prophetic communities in the Quran. The promise that God will restore what was lost and punish the oppressors aligns with the Quranic vision of divine justice. The chapter's insistence that the oppressors will face divine judgment resonates with the Islamic eschatological expectation of a Day when all wrongs will be addressed.
