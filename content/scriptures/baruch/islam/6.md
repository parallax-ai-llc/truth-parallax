Islamic engagement with Baruch 6 (the Letter of Jeremiah) finds the strongest resonance of any chapter in the book, as the letter's sustained polemic against idolatry closely parallels the Quran's most central theological concern. The Quran devotes extensive attention to refuting idol worship: 'Do they worship that which does not benefit them or harm them?' (Quran 21:66). The Letter of Jeremiah's arguments, point by point, mirror this Quranic logic.

Muslim scholars would affirm the letter's every argument: idols cannot see, hear, speak, move, save, or judge. They are the work of human hands, subject to decay, dependent on human care, and utterly powerless. The Quran makes identical points about the idols of the Quraysh and other polytheists. The Letter of Jeremiah represents a moment of profound theological convergence between the biblical and Quranic traditions, both insisting with passionate urgency that worship belongs to the one true God alone.
