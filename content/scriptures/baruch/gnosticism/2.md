Gnostic interpretation of Baruch 2 would read the confession of sin not as an acknowledgment of moral failure before a just God but as the recognition of the soul's entanglement in the material world. The 'sins' that led to exile, in a Gnostic reading, are the soul's progressive involvement with matter, its forgetfulness of its true nature, and its submission to the Demiurge's law. Exile is not a punishment imposed from above but the natural consequence of spiritual amnesia.

The prayer's appeal to God's mercy would be reinterpreted as an appeal to the true transcendent God beyond the Demiurge. The Gnostic reader would distinguish between the God who commands obedience to law (the Demiurge) and the God who responds to the soul's deepest cry for liberation (the true God of the Pleroma). The chapter's desperation, its sense of being trapped in consequences from which there is no escape, mirrors the Gnostic experience of material existence as a prison from which only divine intervention can provide release.
