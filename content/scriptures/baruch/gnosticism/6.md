A Gnostic reading of Baruch 6 (the Letter of Jeremiah) would extend the critique of idolatry beyond physical statues to the worship of the Demiurge himself. In Gnostic theology, the Demiurge is the ultimate idol: a created being who claims to be God and demands worship that belongs only to the transcendent, unknowable God of the Pleroma. The letter's detailed arguments against idol worship, showing that these gods cannot see, hear, save, or judge, would be applied to the Demiurge and his archons.

The letter's refrain, 'they are not gods, do not fear them,' would be read by the Gnostic as liberation from the Demiurge's false claim to divinity. The Gnostic pneumatic, armed with this knowledge, need not fear the cosmic rulers who demand worship and obedience. The Letter of Jeremiah thus becomes, in a Gnostic reading, a manifesto of liberation from all false gods, including the creator god of the material world. The true God is beyond all images, beyond all material manifestation, accessible only through the direct experience of gnosis.
