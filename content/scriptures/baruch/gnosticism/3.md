Gnostic reading of Baruch 3 would interpret the search for wisdom as the search for gnosis, the saving knowledge that liberates the soul from material imprisonment. The chapter's assertion that no nation has found wisdom through its own efforts aligns with the Gnostic teaching that gnosis cannot be achieved through human reason alone but must be received as divine revelation from beyond the material cosmos.

The verse 'She appeared on earth and lived among men' (3:38) holds particular significance for Gnostic interpretation. Rather than reading this as a prophecy of the Incarnation in the orthodox sense, a Gnostic reader might identify this wisdom figure with Sophia herself, the divine Aeon who descended into the material world and whose presence provides the possibility of gnosis for those who can perceive her. The identification of wisdom with Torah would be reinterpreted: the true 'law' is not the Demiurge's commandments but the hidden knowledge of the soul's divine origin and destiny.
