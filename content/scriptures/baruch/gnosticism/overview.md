## Overview

No surviving Gnostic text directly interprets Baruch, though a Gnostic work titled the Book of Baruch by the Gnostic teacher Justin is described by Hippolytus in his Refutation of All Heresies. This Gnostic Baruch is a distinct composition that uses the name of Jeremiah's scribe for a radically different cosmological and soteriological narrative. The canonical Baruch's themes of exile, hidden wisdom, and liberation nonetheless invite Gnostic-resonant readings.

## Gnostic-Resonant Themes

The experience of exile in Baruch, alienation from the homeland, suffering in a foreign land, longing for return, maps directly onto the Gnostic narrative of the soul's exile in the material world. The people of Israel weeping in Babylon become, in a Gnostic reading, the divine sparks trapped in matter, mourning their separation from the Pleroma. The wisdom poem's question "Who has found the place of wisdom?" (3:15) resonates with the Gnostic emphasis on the hiddenness of true knowledge, accessible only through revelation.

## Justin's Book of Baruch

Hippolytus describes Justin's Gnostic Book of Baruch as containing a myth involving three principles: the Good (the transcendent God), Elohim (a father figure), and Eden (a female earth-mother). Elohim ascends and abandons Eden, causing the suffering of creation. An angel named Baruch is sent to deliver humanity from the material world's misery. This text demonstrates how the name and legacy of Baruch could be appropriated for Gnostic cosmological purposes, reframing exile as a cosmic rather than historical condition.
