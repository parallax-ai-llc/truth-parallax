Gnostic interpretation of Baruch 5 would read the vision of Jerusalem's glorification as a description of the Pleroma in its restored fullness. The robe of righteousness and diadem of glory that Jerusalem is told to wear represent the luminous garments of the Aeons in their transcendent state. The return of the scattered children from east and west symbolizes the gathering of all divine sparks back to their source.

The transformation of the landscape, mountains leveled and valleys filled, would be read as the dissolution of the material cosmos's obstacles to the soul's return. The archonic barriers that separate the soul from the Pleroma will be removed, and the path of return will be made smooth. The chapter's joyful vision provides the Gnostic reader with a narrative of hope: the material world's imprisonment is temporary, and the ultimate destiny of the pneumatic is return to the realm of light.
