A Gnostic reading of Baruch 1 would interpret the Babylonian exile as a metaphor for the cosmic exile of divine sparks in the material world. The weeping of the exiles represents the mourning of pneumatic souls who dimly remember their true homeland in the Pleroma but find themselves trapped in the Demiurge's creation. The sending of money and letters to Jerusalem symbolizes the attempt to maintain connection with the transcendent realm through prayer and ritual.

The historical Baruch's role as a scribe, one who transmits secret knowledge, would resonate with the Gnostic emphasis on written revelation as the vehicle of salvific knowledge. The Gnostic text known as Justin's Book of Baruch, described by Hippolytus, appropriates Baruch's name precisely because of his association with hidden knowledge and prophetic tradition. The canonical Baruch's setting in exile provides the perfect narrative framework for the Gnostic theme of spiritual alienation.
