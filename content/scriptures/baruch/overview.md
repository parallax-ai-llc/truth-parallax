## Overview

Baruch is a deuterocanonical book attributed to Baruch ben Neriah, the scribe and companion of the prophet Jeremiah. Set during the Babylonian exile, the book is a composite work containing a prose prayer of confession, a wisdom poem, a prophetic consolation, and the Letter of Jeremiah (chapter 6) warning against idolatry.

## Structure

The book comprises several distinct literary units unified by the theme of exile and hope:

1. **Historical Introduction** (1:1–14): Baruch reads his book in Babylon and sends it to Jerusalem
2. **Confession and Prayer** (1:15–3:8): A communal prayer acknowledging Israel's sin and seeking mercy
3. **Wisdom Poem** (3:9–4:4): Wisdom identified with the Torah, given uniquely to Israel
4. **Prophetic Consolation** (4:5–5:9): Jerusalem addressed as a mother mourning her children, promised restoration
5. **Letter of Jeremiah** (Chapter 6): A satirical polemic against idol worship

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Baruch's reception varies significantly across traditions:

- **Catholicism / Eastern Orthodoxy**: Canonical, often appended to Jeremiah
- **Protestantism**: Apocryphal; not considered authoritative for doctrine
- **Judaism**: Not part of the Tanakh, though its themes of exile and repentance resonate deeply with Jewish liturgy
- **Historical**: Important witness to Second Temple Judaism's theology of exile

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Exile | The central experience shaping the book's theology and prayers |
| Repentance | Communal confession as the necessary response to exile |
| Wisdom | Personified wisdom identified with Torah, exclusive to Israel |
| Consolation | Prophetic promises of restoration and return |
| Idolatry | The Letter of Jeremiah's sustained argument against worship of false gods |
