Catholic exegesis of Baruch 2 highlights the prayer of confession as a model for the Church's sacramental practice of reconciliation. The prayer's structure, acknowledging specific sins, recognizing their consequences, and appealing to divine mercy, mirrors the form of sacramental confession. The Catechism's teaching on the conditions for genuine repentance (contrition, confession, and satisfaction) finds narrative illustration in this chapter.

The chapter's theology of exile as a consequence of sin, followed by the promise of restoration for the repentant, aligns with the Catholic understanding of the relationship between sin, penance, and divine mercy. The prayer's corporate dimension, confessing the sins of the nation rather than individual transgressions alone, reflects the Catholic teaching that sin has both personal and communal dimensions. The Lenten liturgy draws on this theology of communal repentance.
