## Overview

The Catholic Church recognizes Baruch as canonical scripture, confirmed at the Council of Trent (1546). In Catholic Bibles, Baruch follows the Book of Lamentations and is closely associated with the Jeremiah tradition. The Letter of Jeremiah is included as chapter 6. Catholic interpretation values Baruch for its theology of repentance, its identification of wisdom with the Law of God, and its prophetic message of consolation.

## Theological Emphases

Catholic exegesis highlights the penitential prayer of Baruch (1:15-3:8) as a model of communal confession that resonates with the Church's sacramental practice of reconciliation. The acknowledgment that suffering in exile is a consequence of sin, and that God's mercy is available to the repentant, aligns with Catholic teaching on contrition and divine forgiveness. The wisdom poem's identification of wisdom with the Torah (3:9-4:4) is read in light of Catholic theology as pointing toward Christ, the Wisdom of God incarnate.

## Liturgical and Pastoral Use

Baruch appears in the Catholic Lectionary, particularly during Lent and the Easter Vigil, where its themes of repentance and promised restoration complement the liturgical season. The consolation passages (4:5-5:9), in which Jerusalem is personified as a mother calling her children home, are used in the context of Advent and eschatological hope. The Letter of Jeremiah's polemic against idolatry remains pastorally relevant as a call to worship the one true God.
