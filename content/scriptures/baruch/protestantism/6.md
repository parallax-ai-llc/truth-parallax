Baruch 6, known as the Letter of Jeremiah, is a sustained satirical polemic against idol worship. The letter systematically mocks the powerlessness of idols: they cannot save themselves from rust and moths, they cannot see or speak, they are carried because they cannot walk, and they are utterly helpless before natural forces. The argument draws on Isaiah 44:9-20 and Jeremiah 10:1-16.

Protestant interpreters recognize the Letter as a vigorous application of the canonical prophetic critique of idolatry. Its arguments are not original but rather amplify and extend the satire found in Isaiah and Jeremiah. The letter's relevance extends beyond ancient idol worship to any misplaced ultimate devotion, a point that Calvin made in his discussion of the 'perpetual factory of idols' that is the human heart. While not canonical, the Letter of Jeremiah provides a useful catechetical resource for teaching the folly of false worship.
