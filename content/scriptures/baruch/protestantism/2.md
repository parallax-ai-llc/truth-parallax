Baruch 2 continues the communal prayer of confession, detailing Israel's sins and acknowledging that exile is the just consequence of disobedience to God's commands. The prayer closely parallels Daniel 9 and Nehemiah 9, leading Protestant scholars to identify a shared tradition of penitential prayer in Second Temple Judaism.

Protestant interpreters note that the chapter's theology is thoroughly Deuteronomistic: obedience brings blessing, disobedience brings curse, and repentance opens the way to restoration. While this theology is affirmed in the canonical tradition, Protestant commentators also note that it is complicated by the wisdom literature (Job, Ecclesiastes) and ultimately by the cross of Christ, which reveals a deeper dimension of divine justice and mercy than the retributive framework alone can capture.
