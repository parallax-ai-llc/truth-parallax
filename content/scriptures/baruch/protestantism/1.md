Protestant readers of Baruch 1 encounter the book's historical setting: Baruch, identified as Jeremiah's scribe, reads his composition to the Jewish exiles in Babylon, who respond with weeping, fasting, and the collection of money to send to Jerusalem. Protestant scholars have questioned whether the historical Baruch authored this text, noting chronological difficulties and the book's absence from the Hebrew canon.

Despite these reservations, Protestant interpreters recognize the chapter's theological significance as a portrait of diaspora Jewish piety. The exiles' response to Baruch's reading, their desire to maintain connection with Jerusalem's Temple worship even in exile, resonates with canonical passages like Psalm 137 and Daniel 6. The chapter illustrates the tension between physical exile and spiritual fidelity that is a central theme in both testaments.
