Baruch 3 transitions from penitential prayer to a wisdom poem that asks the fundamental question: where is wisdom to be found? The chapter surveys the nations and finds that none of them, neither the wealthy nor the powerful, have discovered wisdom. Only God knows the way to wisdom, and God has given it to Israel in the form of the Torah.

Protestant readers recognize this chapter's strong parallel with Job 28, which asks the same question and concludes that wisdom belongs to God alone. The identification of wisdom with Torah anticipates the theology of Sirach 24, and both texts contribute to the Jewish wisdom tradition that Paul engages in 1 Corinthians 1-2, where Christ is identified as the wisdom of God. While Protestants do not derive doctrine from Baruch, this chapter offers valuable context for understanding the wisdom christology of the New Testament.
