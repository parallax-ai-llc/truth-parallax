## Overview

Protestantism does not include Baruch in the biblical canon. The book is classified as apocryphal, grouped with the other deuterocanonical writings excluded by the Reformers on the basis that it was not part of the Hebrew Bible. No Hebrew original of Baruch has survived, and the book's attribution to Jeremiah's scribe was questioned by Protestant scholars who noted apparent historical and chronological difficulties.

## Reasons for Exclusion

The Westminster Confession and other Reformed confessions explicitly exclude Baruch from the canon. Protestant scholars have observed that the book appears to be a composite work from different periods, making the attribution to the historical Baruch problematic. The Letter of Jeremiah (chapter 6), which forms part of Baruch in Catholic Bibles, was sometimes treated as a separate work and was likewise excluded.

## Engagement with the Content

Protestant interpreters nonetheless find value in Baruch as a witness to Second Temple Jewish piety. The penitential prayer (1:15-3:8) closely parallels Daniel 9 and offers a moving model of communal confession. The wisdom poem (3:9-4:4) provides a significant example of Jewish wisdom theology that identifies wisdom with Torah. Many Protestant scholars study Baruch for its contribution to understanding the development of Jewish theology between the testaments.
