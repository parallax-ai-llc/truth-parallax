## Overview

The Popol Vuh offers a profound contribution to interfaith dialogue, presenting a non-Western creation narrative and theological vision that enriches global understanding of humanity's diverse spiritual heritage. Its themes — the divine creation of the world, the purpose of human existence, the relationship between the living and the dead, the role of sacrifice in sustaining cosmic order — resonate with the central concerns of the world's major religious traditions while offering distinctively Maya perspectives on these universal questions.

Interfaith engagement with the Popol Vuh highlights both shared patterns and illuminating differences: like Genesis, it narrates creation through divine speech, but it also describes multiple failed attempts at creating humanity, suggesting a more experimental divine creativity. Like Buddhist and Hindu traditions, it envisions cosmic cycles of creation and destruction. Like many indigenous traditions, it grounds human identity in relationship with the natural world — specifically, in the sacred substance of maize. The Popol Vuh thus expands the conversation about creation, purpose, and the sacred beyond the boundaries of the Abrahamic and Asian traditions that have dominated interfaith discourse.
