## Overview

The Popol Vuh ("Book of the Community" or "Book of Counsel") is the most important surviving text of Maya mythology and cosmogony. Originally composed in the K'iche' (Quiché) Maya language and transcribed in Latin script in the mid-sixteenth century, the Popol Vuh preserves the creation mythology, heroic narratives, and dynastic history of the K'iche' Maya of highland Guatemala. It was first recorded by K'iche' Maya nobles shortly after the Spanish conquest and later copied by the Dominican friar Francisco Ximénez around 1701-1715.

The Popol Vuh recounts the creation of the world and humanity by the divine pair Heart of Sky and Sovereign Plumed Serpent, the adventures of the Hero Twins Hunahpu and Xbalanque in their defeat of the Lords of the Underworld (Xibalba), and the subsequent creation of the current race of humans from maize. The text concludes with the genealogical history of the K'iche' ruling lineages, connecting mythological origins to historical political authority. It is a work of extraordinary literary power, theological sophistication, and cultural significance.

## Structure

The Popol Vuh can be divided into the following parts:

| Part | Content |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Preamble — The creation of the earth, the first attempts to create beings who will worship the gods |
| 2 | The deeds of the vainglorious Seven Macaw and his sons Zipacna and Earthquake |
| 3 | The story of the father and uncle of the Hero Twins — One Hunahpu and Seven Hunahpu — and their defeat in Xibalba |
| 4 | The miraculous conception and birth of the Hero Twins Hunahpu and Xbalanque |
| 5 | The Hero Twins' youth — their conflicts with their elder half-brothers and their preparation for the journey to Xibalba |
| 6 | The Hero Twins' descent to Xibalba and their triumph over the Lords of Death |
| 7 | The successful creation of humans from maize — the first four men and their wives |
| 8 | The migrations and settlements of the K'iche' people |
| 9 | The genealogies and history of the K'iche' ruling houses |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: A foundational text in Mesoamerican studies, linguistics, and comparative mythology
- **Comparative**: Rich parallels with creation narratives, hero myths, and underworld journeys worldwide
- **Indigenous**: A living sacred text for contemporary K'iche' Maya communities
- **Theological**: Presents a sophisticated cosmogony and theology of creation, sacrifice, and renewal
- **Archaeological**: Corroborated and illuminated by Classic Maya archaeological and epigraphic evidence
- **Interfaith**: Offers a non-Western creation narrative of universal significance and profound spiritual depth

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Creation | Multiple attempts to create beings capable of worshipping the gods |
| Sacrifice | The necessity of sacrifice — especially self-sacrifice — for cosmic renewal |
| Duality | The pervasive role of paired and complementary forces (Hero Twins, dual creators) |
| Maize | The centrality of maize as the substance of human flesh and symbol of life |
| Xibalba | The underworld as a place of trial, death, and eventual transformation |
| Lineage | The connection between cosmic origins and historical political authority |
