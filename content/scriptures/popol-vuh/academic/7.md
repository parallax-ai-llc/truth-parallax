Part 7 of the Popol Vuh — The Creation of Humans from Maize — has been the subject of extensive academic analysis across multiple disciplines: Mesoamerican anthropology, K'iche' Maya linguistics, comparative mythology, literary criticism, and ethnohistory. Scholars examine this section's narrative structure, its K'iche' rhetorical features, its relationship to pre-Columbian Maya textual and iconographic traditions, and its evidence for ancient Maya religious belief and practice.

Key scholarly questions concerning this part include the degree to which the sixteenth-century alphabetic text preserves pre-Columbian narrative traditions, the relationship between its content and Classic Maya (250-900 CE) art and inscriptions, the literary and rhetorical sophistication of its K'iche' prose, and its significance for understanding the broader Mesoamerican cultural sphere. Academic engagement with this section contributes to the ongoing project of recognizing the Popol Vuh as a major work of world literature deserving of the same scholarly attention given to comparable texts from other civilizations.
