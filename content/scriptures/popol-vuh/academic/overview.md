## Overview

Academic study of the Popol Vuh encompasses multiple disciplines: Mesoamerican anthropology and archaeology, K'iche' Maya linguistics, ethnohistory, literary criticism, and comparative mythology. Scholars analyze the text's complex history of composition and transmission — from its pre-Columbian oral and possibly hieroglyphic antecedents, through its sixteenth-century transcription in the Latin alphabet by K'iche' nobles, to its eighteenth-century copy by Francisco Ximénez and its subsequent publication and translation history.

Key scholarly questions include the relationship between the alphabetic Popol Vuh and pre-Columbian Maya texts and iconography, the degree to which the sixteenth-century transcription was influenced by Christian concepts and narrative patterns, the text's value as a source for reconstructing pre-Columbian Maya religion and worldview, and its literary and rhetorical sophistication as a work of K'iche' oral literature committed to writing.
