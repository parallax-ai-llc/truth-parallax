Part 3 of the Popol Vuh — One Hunahpu and Seven Hunahpu in Xibalba — can be illuminated by archaeological and epigraphic evidence from Classic Maya civilization (250-900 CE). The themes, figures, and narrative patterns of this section appear in various forms in Classic Maya art — on painted pottery, carved stone monuments, and architectural facades — as well as in hieroglyphic inscriptions, demonstrating the deep antiquity of these traditions.

Archaeological analysis examines the correspondence between the narrative content of this section and the material record, considering how Classic Maya visual and textual representations of related themes confirm, supplement, or diverge from the Popol Vuh's account. This evidence demonstrates that the Popol Vuh preserves genuinely ancient Maya traditions, transmitted orally for centuries before their sixteenth-century transcription, and provides crucial context for interpreting the archaeological record.
