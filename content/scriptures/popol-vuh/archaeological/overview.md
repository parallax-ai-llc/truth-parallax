## Overview

Archaeological and epigraphic evidence from Classic Maya civilization (250-900 CE) provides crucial context for understanding the Popol Vuh, confirming that many of its narratives were known centuries before the sixteenth-century transcription. The Hero Twins, the Lords of Xibalba, the Maize God, and other figures from the Popol Vuh appear frequently in Classic Maya art — on painted pottery, carved stone monuments, and architectural facades — demonstrating the deep antiquity and wide distribution of these mythological traditions.

Archaeological analysis of the Popol Vuh examines the correspondence between its narratives and the material record: the depiction of the Hero Twins on Late Classic polychrome pottery, the Maize God's resurrection scenes in architectural sculpture, references to mythological events in hieroglyphic inscriptions, and the relationship between the text's cosmological geography and actual Maya landscape and settlement patterns. This evidence confirms the Popol Vuh as a genuine preservation of pre-Columbian Maya tradition, not a colonial-era invention.
