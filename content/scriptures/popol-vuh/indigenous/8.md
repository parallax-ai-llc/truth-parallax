The migration narratives trace the K'iche' ancestors' journey from a place called Tulan Zuyua — "Seven Caves, Seven Canyons" — through hardship and darkness to the highlands of Guatemala. The first ancestors carry their patron deity Tohil (associated with fire and sacrifice) wrapped in a bundle on their backs, and they endure cold, hunger, and the separation of languages as the various peoples disperse. This section functions simultaneously as sacred history, charter of territorial legitimacy, and model for understanding displacement and resilience.

For contemporary K'iche' communities, many of whom experienced forced displacement during Guatemala's internal armed conflict (1960-1996), the migration narratives carry acute resonance. The ancestors' journey through darkness and privation, their dependence on the fire given by Tohil, and their persistent movement toward a promised homeland mirror the experience of refugee communities who maintained cultural identity through ceremonies, language, and collective memory even in exile. The Popol Vuh's migration account validates the experience of displacement while affirming that cultural continuity does not require remaining in a single place.

The pivotal role of fire in the migration section connects to the ceremonial fire (*k'ak'*) that remains central to K'iche' spiritual practice. Tohil grants fire to the K'iche' ancestors, and other peoples who have lost their own fires must come to the K'iche' to receive it. This establishes both the K'iche' people's spiritual authority and the principle of interdependence among peoples. Contemporary fire ceremonies conducted by ajq'ijab' at mountain shrines recall this original gift, and the maintenance of ceremonial fire is understood as a continuation of the covenant between Tohil and the K'iche' ancestors.

The arrival at the mountain Chi Q'ij, where the ancestors await the first sunrise, is one of the most emotionally charged passages in the Popol Vuh. The people pray and weep as they watch for the morning star (Venus), and when the sun finally rises, all the animals and people rejoice. The first sunrise is also the moment when the patron deities — Tohil, Auilix, and Jacawitz — are turned to stone, establishing the sacred sites that anchor K'iche' territorial identity. These petrified deities are the huacas (sacred stones) at specific mountain locations, and their veneration connects K'iche' communities to both their ancestors and the landscape itself.
