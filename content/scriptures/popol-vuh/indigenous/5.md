The youth of Hunahpu and Xbalanque is characterized by their growing mastery of the skills they will need in Xibalba and by their relationship with the natural world. The twins are hunters, using blowguns to bring down birds from the trees, and this practice connects them to the forests of the K'iche' highlands where hunting remains both a subsistence activity and a ceremonial practice. The blowgun (*pub'*) requires patience, silence, and precise knowledge of animal behavior — qualities that will prove essential in their confrontation with the death lords.

A pivotal episode involves the transformation of their jealous half-brothers, One Monkey and One Artisan, into spider monkeys. The twins trick them into climbing a tree that grows impossibly tall, then instruct them to loosen their loincloths and use them as tails to descend. The half-brothers become howler monkeys, forever associated with art, music, and writing. K'iche' tradition holds that artisans and scribes carry something of One Monkey and One Artisan's spirit, and their transformation is not purely a punishment but a form of cosmic reassignment. Contemporary K'iche' artists and musicians sometimes invoke One Monkey as a patron figure.

The twins' discovery of their father's ballgame equipment — hidden in the rafters of their grandmother Xmucane's house — marks their assumption of their inherited destiny. The rubber ball, yokes, and arm guards are not merely sporting equipment but ceremonial instruments. The K'iche' ballgame (*pitz*) enacts cosmic struggle between opposing forces, and the court itself represents the threshold between the surface world and Xibalba. When the twins begin to play, they deliberately provoke the Lords of Death just as their father and uncle did — but this time with full awareness of what they are initiating.

The relationship between the twins and the animals of the forest — the rat who reveals the hidden equipment, the birds they hunt, the insects they will later employ as spies — reflects the K'iche' understanding that human beings exist within a web of reciprocal relationships with other species. Animals are not resources to be exploited but allies, teachers, and sometimes adversaries within a shared cosmos. The milpa farmer's respect for the animals who share the cornfield stems from this same understanding of ecological kinship.
