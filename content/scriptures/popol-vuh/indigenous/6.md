The Hero Twins' journey into Xibalba and their triumph over the death lords constitutes the spiritual and dramatic center of the Popol Vuh. Unlike their father and uncle, Hunahpu and Xbalanque descend prepared. They send a mosquito ahead as a scout, which bites each lord in turn, causing them to call out each other's names — knowledge that proves decisive, since naming confers power in K'iche' metaphysics. The twins thus enter Xibalba already knowing their adversaries, a reversal of the ignorance that doomed the previous generation.

The trials of the houses — Darkness, Cold, Jaguars, Fire, and the House of Bats — are overcome through intelligence and cooperation rather than brute strength. In the House of Darkness, the twins place macaw feathers on their torches and fireflies on their cigars, giving the appearance of burning without consuming the objects. This trickster strategy is deeply valued in K'iche' culture, where *na'oj* (wisdom-intelligence) is considered a higher virtue than physical power. Each house of trial corresponds to a specific aspect of death and suffering, and the twins' solutions model how human beings can navigate adversity through creativity and mutual support.

The twins' ultimate strategy involves their own death and resurrection. They allow themselves to be killed on Xibalba's ball court, their bones ground into the river, where they regenerate first as catfish, then as wandering performers. In disguise, they perform miracles — burning a house and restoring it, sacrificing a person and bringing them back to life — until the fascinated death lords request the same treatment. The twins sacrifice One Death and Seven Death but do not resurrect them, breaking the power of Xibalba forever. This sequence teaches that the defeat of death requires passage through death itself, a principle that informs K'iche' funerary practice and ancestral veneration.

Following their victory, the twins rise into the sky as the Sun and Moon. This celestial transformation connects the mythic narrative to the observable cosmos: the daily journey of the sun across the sky and into the underworld at night reenacts the Hero Twins' descent and return. Contemporary ajq'ijab' honor the sunrise as a moment of triumph recapitulated, and ceremonies timed to the solar cycle invoke the twins' victory over death. The story thus provides the cosmological foundation for K'iche' time-keeping, agricultural planning, and the ceremonial calendar that structures community life.
