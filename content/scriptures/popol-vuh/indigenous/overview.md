## Overview

For contemporary K'iche' Maya communities in Guatemala, the Popol Vuh is not merely an ancient text but a living expression of cultural identity, spiritual wisdom, and historical consciousness. Indigenous engagement with the Popol Vuh emphasizes continuity with pre-Columbian Maya civilization, the ongoing relevance of its spiritual and moral teachings, and its role as a charter of K'iche' identity and sovereignty.

K'iche' Maya scholars and spiritual leaders (*ajq'ijab'*, daykeepers) read the Popol Vuh in relation to contemporary Maya ceremonial practice, the 260-day sacred calendar (*Cholq'ij*), agricultural cycles, and community governance. The text's creation narrative continues to inform Maya understandings of the relationship between humans, the natural world, and the divine. Indigenous readings of the Popol Vuh resist its reduction to "mythology" in the dismissive sense, insisting on its status as sacred scripture — a revealed text containing truths essential to human flourishing.
