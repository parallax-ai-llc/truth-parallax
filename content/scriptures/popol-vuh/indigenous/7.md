The creation of humanity from maize represents the theological climax of the Popol Vuh. After the failures of mud and wood, the gods discover the mountain of sustenance (*Paxil*) where white and yellow corn grow in abundance. The animals — fox, coyote, parrot, and crow — lead the gods to this hidden place. Xmucane grinds the corn nine times, and from this ground maize mixed with water, the first four human beings are formed: Jaguar Quitze, Jaguar Night, Not Right Now, and Dark Jaguar. These are the K'iche' ancestors, and their creation from corn establishes the fundamental identity between the Maya people and the maize plant.

This is not metaphor but ontology in K'iche' thought. Contemporary K'iche' communities understand themselves as literally constituted of corn — their flesh is masa, their blood is corn gruel (*atol*). The milpa (corn field) is therefore not merely a food source but the site of ongoing human creation. Planting corn is a sacred act that continues the work the gods began at Paxil, and the harvest is a form of communion with one's own substance. The devastating effects of industrial monoculture and GMO corn on traditional milpa farming are experienced not simply as economic disruption but as an assault on Maya identity and the sacred covenant between people and maize.

The first humans are so perfect that they can see everything — the four corners of the sky, the round face of the earth. Their vision disturbs the gods, who fear that beings with unlimited knowledge will not depend on their creators. The gods therefore cloud human sight, "as when breath is blown on the face of a mirror," limiting human perception. This diminishment is understood not as punishment but as the condition that makes relationship possible. If humans knew everything, they would not need to pray, consult the calendar, seek counsel from Elders, or perform ceremony. Limited vision creates the space for spiritual practice, community interdependence, and the ongoing dialogue between humans and the divine that the ajq'ijab' facilitate.

The creation of the first four women — who appear while the men sleep — establishes gender complementarity as a foundational principle of K'iche' society. The women are not created from the men (as in some other traditions) but appear independently as the men's partners. K'iche' communities traditionally organize through complementary gender roles in agriculture, weaving, ceremony, and governance, and this narrative grounds those practices in cosmic precedent. The pairing of the first ancestors also establishes the lineage system that structures K'iche' social organization to the present day.
