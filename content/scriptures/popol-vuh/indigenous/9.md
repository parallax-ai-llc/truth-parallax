The genealogies that close the Popol Vuh list the generations of K'iche' lords from the first ancestors to the colonial period, establishing an unbroken chain of authority connecting mythic origins to historical governance. For K'iche' communities, this section transforms the entire preceding narrative from sacred story into political charter. The right to govern, to hold land, and to conduct ceremonies derives from descent traced through these genealogies back to Jaguar Quitze and the other first humans created from maize.

The three great K'iche' lineage houses — the Kaweq, Nijaib, and Ajaw K'iche' — each trace their descent through specific lords named in this section. The genealogies thus function as a living constitutional document, and their recitation in ceremony reinforces the social and political structure of K'iche' society. Contemporary K'iche' communities in Chichicastenango, Quetzaltenango, and other highland centers still organize around lineage principles rooted in these ancestral records, even as they navigate the pressures of Guatemalan national politics and global economic forces.

The final entries in the genealogy reference the arrival of the Spanish and the disruption of the indigenous political order. The text acknowledges its own moment of composition — written down in alphabetic script because the original hieroglyphic book (*ilb'al*, "the instrument of sight") has been lost or hidden. This metanarrative gesture is crucial for indigenous readings: the Popol Vuh presents itself as a recovery project, an attempt to preserve essential knowledge in a time of cultural crisis. Contemporary K'iche' scholars like Sam Colop have argued that this self-awareness makes the Popol Vuh not merely a pre-Columbian survival but an early colonial resistance text — a deliberate act of cultural preservation in the face of destruction.

The genealogies also encode astronomical knowledge. Certain lord-names correspond to calendar positions, and the succession of generations maps onto cycles of the Cholq'ij and the Haab' (365-day solar calendar). Ajq'ijab' who maintain the calendar tradition read the genealogies as a temporal map, connecting specific ancestors to specific positions in the great calendar round. This intersection of genealogy and astronomy reflects the K'iche' understanding that human history unfolds within cosmic time, and that the events of the family, the community, and the cosmos are expressions of a single interconnected order.
