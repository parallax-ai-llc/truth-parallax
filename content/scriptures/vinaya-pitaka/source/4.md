---
title: "Vinaya Pitaka Section 4: Pacittiya (Rules of Expiation)"
scripture: "vinaya-pitaka"
chapter: 4
---

**1** Whatever monk should intentionally speak a falsehood, this is a Pacittiya offence requiring confession and expiation. The Blessed One laid down this rule because truthful speech is the foundation of the holy life.

**2** Whatever monk should speak insulting words to another monk, this is a Pacittiya offence. The Blessed One said: "Monks, you should not speak insulting words to one another. You are companions in the holy life, fellow wayfarers on the path."

**3** Whatever monk should eat food at the wrong time — that is, after midday and before dawn — this is a Pacittiya offence. The Blessed One allowed eating only between dawn and midday, for the sake of health, moderation, and ease in meditation.

**4** Whatever monk should drink fermented liquor or spirits, this is a Pacittiya offence. The Blessed One said: "Monks, fermented liquor and spirits are not to be drunk. They are the cause of heedlessness, and heedlessness is the cause of all unwholesome states."

**5** Whatever monk should intentionally destroy the life of a living being — an animal or insect — this is a Pacittiya offence. The Blessed One said: "A monk should be mindful in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, so as not to harm any living being."

**6** Whatever monk should dig the earth or have it dug, this is a Pacittiya offence. This rule was laid down because the earth was considered by some to contain living organisms, and because the act of digging can destroy plant and insect life.

**7** Whatever monk should eat food that he has stored overnight, this is a Pacittiya offence. The Blessed One said: "Monks, food should not be stored. A monk should eat what he receives on that day, maintaining contentment with what is given."

**8** Whatever monk should lie down in the same lodging as a woman, even for one night, this is a Pacittiya offence. This rule was laid down to protect the monk's purity and to guard against the arising of desire and suspicion.

**9** Whatever monk should teach the Dhamma in more than five or six sentences to a woman without a competent man being present, this is a Pacittiya offence, unless it is done with a knowledgeable companion present.

**10** There are ninety-two Pacittiya rules in all. When a monk commits a Pacittiya offence, he should confess it to another monk, saying: "Venerable sir, I have committed a Pacittiya offence. I confess it." And the other monk should say: "Do you see it?" "I see it." "Guard against it in the future." "I shall guard against it."
