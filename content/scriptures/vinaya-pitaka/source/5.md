---
title: "Vinaya Pitaka Section 5: Parivara (The Epitome of the Vinaya)"
scripture: "vinaya-pitaka"
chapter: 5
---

**1** How many categories of offences are there? There are seven categories: Parajika (defeat), Sanghadisesa (requiring formal meetings), Aniyata (undetermined), Nissaggiya Pacittiya (expiation with forfeiture), Pacittiya (expiation), Patidesaniya (acknowledgement), and Sekhiya (training rules), together with the rules for settling disputes.

**2** How many Parajika rules are there? Four. How many Sanghadisesa rules? Thirteen. How many Aniyata rules? Two. How many Nissaggiya Pacittiya rules? Thirty. How many Pacittiya rules? Ninety-two. How many Patidesaniya rules? Four. How many Sekhiya rules? Seventy-five. How many rules for settling disputes? Seven.

**3** What is the origin of the Vinaya? When the Blessed One was dwelling at Vesali, certain monks of the Vajjian clan committed offences. The Blessed One, seeing the necessity, laid down rules for the guidance of the Sangha and for the long endurance of the true Dhamma.

**4** The Blessed One said: "Monks, I lay down these training rules for ten reasons: for the excellence of the Sangha, for the comfort of the Sangha, for the restraint of ill-disposed persons, for the ease of well-disposed monks, for the control of present defilements, for the prevention of future defilements."

**5** "For the benefit of non-believers, for the increase of believers, for the establishment of the true Dhamma, and for the support of the Vinaya. For these ten reasons I lay down these training rules."

**6** A monk who has committed an offence should not conceal it. Concealment is itself an offence. Just as a man bitten by a poisonous snake should quickly seek the physician, so a monk who has committed an offence should quickly confess it and undertake the prescribed remedy.

**7** The Sangha acts by consensus. When a formal act of the Sangha is to be carried out — ordination, penance, rehabilitation, or any other act — the motion is announced and the Sangha gives its consent through silence. If any member objects, the act does not proceed.

**8** The Bhikkhuni Vinaya contains eight heavy rules (Garudhamma) in addition to the rules shared with monks. The Blessed One established the Bhikkhuni Sangha at the request of Mahapajapati Gotami, the Buddha's foster mother, through the intercession of Ananda.

**9** The seven methods for settling disputes are: settlement in the presence of both parties, settlement by the recollection of the accused, settlement by the verdict of a committee, settlement by majority decision, settlement by the acknowledgement of the accused, settlement for one who is insane, and settlement by covering over as with grass.

**10** The Blessed One, at the time of his final passing, said to Ananda: "It may be, Ananda, that the Sangha may, if it wishes, abolish the lesser and minor training rules after my passing." But when the monks could not agree on which rules were minor, the Elder Mahakassapa declared: "Let us not abolish any rule that the Blessed One has laid down, and let us not introduce any rule that the Blessed One has not laid down. Let us proceed in conformity with the training rules as laid down."
