The final tablet's fragmentary condition limits archaeological correlations, but Teshub's established kingship is abundantly attested in the material record. The great temple of Teshub at Aleppo, excavated by Kay Kohlmeyer, reveals a cult tradition stretching from the third millennium BCE into the Iron Age. At Hattusa, the storm god is the most frequently depicted deity in both temple sculpture and seal impressions.

The treaty traditions of the Hittite Empire regularly invoke Teshub as the primary divine witness, confirming his position at the head of the pantheon as described in the Song of Kumarbi's conclusion. International treaties between the Hittites and Egypt (notably the Egyptian-Hittite peace treaty of Ramesses II and Hattusili III) list Teshub first among the divine witnesses.
