## Overview

The Song of Kumarbi was discovered among the vast cuneiform archives at Hattusa (modern Bogazkoy, Turkey), the capital of the Hittite Empire. The site has yielded over 30,000 cuneiform tablets and fragments since excavations began under Hugo Winckler in 1906. The mythological text was found in the temple archive area and reflects the multilingual, multicultural character of the Hittite scribal establishment.

## Archaeological Context

### Hattusa (Bogazkoy)
The Hittite capital was a massive fortified city covering approximately 1.8 square kilometers, with monumental temples, a royal citadel (Buyukkale), and extensive scribal facilities. The cuneiform archives were concentrated in several locations:
- **Temple I**: The main archive, containing religious, mythological, and ritual texts
- **Buyukkale**: The royal citadel, housing administrative and diplomatic texts
- **Building A on Buyukkale**: Where many literary and mythological texts were found

### The Tablet
The Song of Kumarbi (CTH 344) survives in fragments, primarily from 13th-century BCE copies. The tablets are written in Hittite cuneiform — an adaptation of the Mesopotamian cuneiform script to the Hittite language. The mythological content, however, is Hurrian in origin, reflecting the deep Hurrian cultural influence on the Hittite court, particularly from the 15th century BCE onward.

### Hurrian Archaeological Evidence
Hurrian culture, from which the Kumarbi mythology derives, is attested archaeologically at sites including:
- **Urkesh (Tell Mozan)**: A major Hurrian center in northeastern Syria
- **Nuzi (Yorghan Tepe)**: Hurrian administrative center in Iraq
- **Alalakh (Tell Atchana)**: Evidence of Hurrian religion in the Amuq Valley

## Key Archaeological Sites

| Site | Relevance |
|------|-----------|
| Hattusa (Bogazkoy) | Provenance of the tablets |
| Urkesh (Tell Mozan) | Hurrian religious center; Kumarbi worship attested |
| Yazilikaya | Rock sanctuary near Hattusa with Hurrian divine procession |
| Sapinuwa (Ortakoy) | Secondary Hittite archive with mythological texts |
