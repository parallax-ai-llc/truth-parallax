The violence at the heart of the Song of Kumarbi — a god attacking another god's body to seize power — raises uncomfortable questions that different traditions handle differently. Monotheistic traditions generally reject the idea of violence within the divine realm. Hindu tradition includes divine combat but within a framework of cosmic order (*dharma*). Buddhist teaching regards all violence as rooted in ignorance.

Yet the myth speaks to a truth that resonates across traditions: transformation often requires a rupture with what came before. The new order (Teshub's just kingship) emerges from the violence of succession. This pattern — destruction as the precondition for creation — appears in traditions from the Hindu concept of Shiva as destroyer-creator to the Christian theology of the cross.
