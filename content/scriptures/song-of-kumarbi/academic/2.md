The second tablet narrates the poem's central event: Kumarbi attacks the fleeing Anu and bites off his genitals, swallowing them. Anu's seed impregnates Kumarbi with several deities, including the storm god Teshub. Guterbock's analysis identified this as the mythological core of the text, paralleling the castration of Ouranos by Kronos in Hesiod. Scholarly debate has focused on the precise number and identity of the gods conceived within Kumarbi — the text is damaged at key points, and reconstructions vary.

The act of swallowing as a means of absorbing divine power is attested in other Hurrian myths and may reflect a broader ancient Near Eastern concept of power transfer through ingestion.
