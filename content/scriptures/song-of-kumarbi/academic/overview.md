## Overview

The Song of Kumarbi (CTH 344) is a Hittite-language mythological text with Hurrian religious content, first published by Hans Gustav Guterbock in 1946. The text was discovered among the vast cuneiform archives at Hattusa (modern Bogazkoy), the Hittite capital, and dates to the 13th century BCE, though the mythological traditions it preserves are likely older. The composition belongs to a broader "Kumarbi Cycle" that includes the Song of LAMMA, the Song of Silver, and the Song of Hedammu.

## Key Scholarly Issues

### Hurrian Origins
Although written in Hittite, the divine names, mythological framework, and theological concepts are Hurrian. Scholars including Guterbock, Hoffner, and Beckman have traced the text's origins to the Hurrian religious tradition of southeastern Anatolia and northern Mesopotamia, later adopted and adapted by the Hittite scribal establishment.

### The Hesiod Connection
The most discussed aspect of the text is its structural parallel with Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE):
- Succession of divine rulers through violence
- The swallowing motif (Kumarbi swallows Anu's genitals; Kronos swallows his children)
- The final establishment of the storm god's rule (Teshub / Zeus)

Scholars debate whether this represents direct transmission (via Phoenician intermediaries?), indirect cultural contact, or independent development of a common Indo-European or Near Eastern pattern.

### Textual Condition
The text is significantly damaged, with major lacunae. The precise sequence of events, especially in the later portions, remains uncertain and subject to scholarly reconstruction.
