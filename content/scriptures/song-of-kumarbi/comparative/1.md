Alalu's reign and overthrow establish the pattern of divine succession that parallels Hesiod's Theogony so remarkably. Alalu corresponds to no clear Greek figure, though some scholars have suggested he reflects a pre-Olympian deity lost from the Greek tradition. The pattern of the first king being overthrown by his cupbearer or servant parallels mythological traditions from Mesopotamia (Erra and Ishum) and reflects a widespread ancient understanding that power is inherently contested.

The nine-year reign period may connect to the Greek tradition of nine-year cultic cycles (Minos ruling Crete in nine-year periods, according to Homer).
