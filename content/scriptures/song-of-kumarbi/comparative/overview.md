## Overview

The Song of Kumarbi is one of the most important texts in comparative mythology, providing the strongest evidence for direct or indirect transmission of mythological narratives from the ancient Near East to archaic Greece. The structural parallels between the Hurro-Hittite succession myth and Hesiod's Theogony have generated a vast scholarly literature on cultural contact in the Bronze and early Iron Ages.

## Key Comparative Parallels

### Hesiod's Theogony
The parallels are remarkably specific:

| Hurro-Hittite | Greek |
|---------------|-------|
| Alalu (first king) | Ouranos (first ruler) |
| Anu overthrows Alalu | Kronos overthrows Ouranos |
| Kumarbi bites off Anu's genitals | Kronos cuts off Ouranos's genitals |
| Kumarbi becomes pregnant with gods | Kronos swallows his children |
| Teshub (storm god) takes kingship | Zeus (storm god) takes kingship |

### Phoenician Intermediaries
The Greek writer Philo of Byblos (c. 100 CE) preserves a Phoenician cosmogony attributed to Sanchuniathon that includes a divine succession myth, suggesting a Levantine channel of transmission between Anatolia/Mesopotamia and Greece.

### Mesopotamian Parallels
The Babylonian Enuma Elish shares the theme of a young storm god (Marduk) defeating older cosmic powers, though it lacks the specific succession-by-castration motif. The Sumerian poem "Enlil and Namzitarra" contains related motifs of divine conflict.

### Indo-European Perspectives
Some scholars (e.g., Jaan Puhvel) have argued that the succession myth reflects a Proto-Indo-European pattern, though the specificity of the Hurro-Hittite/Greek parallels suggests cultural contact rather than common inheritance.

## Transmission Models

| Model | Proponents | Evidence |
