The fifth tablet, despite its fragmentary condition, provides the narrative's resolution. The pattern of succession — each king overthrown by the next — reaches its end with Teshub, whose rule is not challenged within this text (though subsequent poems in the Kumarbi Cycle will test it). The literary effect is one of arrival: after the turbulence of the succession narrative, the poem concludes with stability.

The poem's overall structure thus traces an arc from order (Alalu's unchallenged reign) through chaos (the succession conflicts) to a new order (Teshub's legitimate kingship) — a narrative pattern that mirrors the cosmogonic pattern of order-chaos-new order found across ancient Near Eastern literature.
