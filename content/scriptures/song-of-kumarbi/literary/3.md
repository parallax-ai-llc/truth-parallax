The third tablet explores the grotesque and the comic in equal measure. Kumarbi's distress as the gods grow within him — his conversations with Anu about what he has swallowed, his attempts to manage his impossible condition — introduces an element of dark humor rare in ancient mythological texts. The literary effect is disorienting: the audience is asked to sympathize with a character who achieved his position through an act of appalling violence and is now suffering the consequences.

The dialogue between Kumarbi and Anu — antagonist-become-victim and victim-become-adviser — is narratively innovative, creating a relationship of grudging mutual dependence.
