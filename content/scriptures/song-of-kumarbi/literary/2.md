The castration scene is the poem's literary climax, described with startling vividness: 'He bit his loins and his manhood united with Kumarbi's insides like bronze.' The simile 'like bronze' — comparing the fusion of divine seed with divine body to the smelting of metal — is one of the most striking images in ancient literature. It transforms the grotesque act into a metallurgical metaphor, suggesting that what is happening is not merely violence but transformation — the forging of new gods from old power.

The narrative pace accelerates dramatically in this section, moving from the formulaic pattern of the first tablet to rapid, violent action.
