## Overview

The Song of Kumarbi is a remarkable example of mythological narrative poetry, distinguished by its vivid bodily imagery, its compact narrative structure, and its blend of the cosmic and the grotesque. Though composed in the relatively austere style characteristic of Hittite literary texts, the poem achieves moments of striking dramatic power, particularly in the scene of Kumarbi's assault on Anu.

## Key Literary Features

### Narrative Economy
The poem moves through the succession of divine kings with remarkable speed. Alalu's reign, Anu's coup, and Kumarbi's rise are narrated in quick succession, creating a sense of accelerating cosmic instability until the climactic confrontation.

### The Castration Scene
The central literary moment is Kumarbi's attack on the fleeing Anu: "He bit his loins, and his manhood united with Kumarbi's insides like bronze." The image is simultaneously violent, sexual, and cosmogonic — a single act that is at once an attack, an impregnation, and the origin of new gods.

### Speech and Dialogue
The poem employs direct speech to dramatize the relationships between the gods:
- Anu's warning to Kumarbi about what he has swallowed
- Kumarbi's reactions to the revelation of his pregnancy
- The negotiations over the birth of the new gods

### The Body as Landscape
Kumarbi's body becomes the terrain of the narrative — his insides the womb of new gods, his skull the exit point for divine birth. This conflation of body and cosmos is a distinctive literary feature with few parallels in ancient Near Eastern literature.

## Structural Overview

| Section | Narrative Function |
|---------|-------------------|
| Alalu's reign | Establishes the pattern of kingship |
| Anu's coup | First succession — sets the precedent |
| Kumarbi's assault | Climactic act of violence and transformation |
| Pregnancy and birth | Resolution — new gods emerge |
| Teshub's kingship | Establishment of final order |
