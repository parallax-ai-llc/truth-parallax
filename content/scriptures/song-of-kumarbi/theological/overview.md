## Overview

The Song of Kumarbi presents a theology in which cosmic order is achieved not through primordial harmony but through successive acts of violence — each generation of gods overthrowing the previous through physical force. This vision of divine governance raises profound theological questions about the nature of sovereignty, the relationship between power and legitimacy, and whether violence is constitutive of cosmic order or merely the means of its establishment.

## Key Theological Themes

### Kingship Through Violence
Unlike traditions that present divine rule as eternal and unchallenged, the Song of Kumarbi depicts kingship as contested and won through physical dominance. Each ruler serves for a numbered period ("for nine years Alalu was king in heaven") before being overthrown.

### The Body as Cosmic Site
Kumarbi's act of biting off and swallowing Anu's genitals — and his subsequent pregnancy with the gods — presents the divine body as the site of cosmic transformation. Creation here emerges not from speech or thought but from ingestion, gestation, and violent birth.

### The Storm God's Legitimacy
Teshub's rise to power represents the final establishment of legitimate divine order. As the storm god, Teshub's sovereignty is linked to his control of weather, fertility, and the natural order — suggesting that legitimate kingship is defined by its benefits to the cosmos rather than merely by the power to seize it.

### Hurrian-Hittite Theological Synthesis
The text reflects a theological synthesis between Hurrian deities (Kumarbi, Teshub) and Mesopotamian cosmic concepts (Anu as sky god, the divine assembly), mediated through Hittite literary and ritual traditions.

## Theological Tensions

| Tension | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Power vs. legitimacy | Each ruler is king but is overthrown |
| Violence vs. order | Cosmic order emerges through violent succession |
| Body vs. spirit | Creation through bodily ingestion rather than word or will |
| Succession vs. eternity | Does Teshub's rule represent a final order or another stage? |
