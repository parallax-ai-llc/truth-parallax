Teshub's kingship represents the theological concept of legitimate sovereignty — rule that is not merely powerful but right. Unlike Alalu and Kumarbi, whose reigns were temporary and contested, Teshub's rule is presented as the final, stable order. This theology of culminating sovereignty — the idea that the current divine order is the best and final one — serves to legitimate both the divine hierarchy and, by extension, the human political order that mirrors it.

The Hittite king, who served as Teshub's earthly representative, derived his authority from this theological framework: just as Teshub rules the gods through legitimate succession, the Hittite king rules humanity through divine appointment.
