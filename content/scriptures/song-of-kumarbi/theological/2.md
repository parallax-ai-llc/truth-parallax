The castration and swallowing scene is theologically extraordinary: cosmic power is transferred through an act of bodily violence. Kumarbi's attack on Anu is simultaneously an act of rebellion, an act of consumption, and an act of conception. The theological implications are profound: divine power is not spiritual but physical — it can be bitten off, swallowed, and gestated. The body of a god is the site of cosmic transformation.

This theology of embodied divine power contrasts with later philosophical traditions (both Greek and Abrahamic) that emphasize the incorporeality of the divine. In the Song of Kumarbi, the gods are radically embodied — their power is in their flesh.
