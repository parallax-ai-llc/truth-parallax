## Overview

The Song of Kumarbi (also known as "Kingship in Heaven") is a Hittite mythological text composed in the Hittite language with strong Hurrian religious influence, dating to approximately the 14th–13th centuries BCE. Discovered among the cuneiform archives at Hattusa (modern Bogazkoy, Turkey), the poem narrates a succession of divine kings — Alalu, Anu, Kumarbi, and Teshub — each overthrowing the previous ruler through violence. The text is the first in a cycle of related Hurro-Hittite myths sometimes called the Kumarbi Cycle.

## Structure

The five tablets of the Song of Kumarbi trace the violent succession of divine kingship:

1. **Tablet 1**: Alalu's reign in heaven and his overthrow by Anu
2. **Tablet 2**: Kumarbi's attack on Anu — the biting and swallowing of Anu's genitals
3. **Tablet 3**: Kumarbi's pregnancy with the gods born from Anu's seed
4. **Tablet 4**: The birth of Teshub and the other gods from Kumarbi's body
5. **Tablet 5**: Teshub's rise to kingship and the establishment of the new divine order

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Song of Kumarbi holds extraordinary importance for comparative mythology:

- **Academic**: A key text in Hittitology and the study of Hurrian religion within the Hittite empire
- **Comparative**: The succession myth closely parallels Hesiod's *Theogony* (Ouranos–Kronos–Zeus), suggesting direct or indirect transmission
- **Theological**: Explores the nature of divine sovereignty, legitimacy, and cosmic order
- **Literary**: A striking example of mythological narrative centered on bodily violence and transformation
- **Archaeological**: The Bogazkoy archives preserve a multilingual scribal culture that blended Hittite, Hurrian, and Mesopotamian traditions
- **Interfaith**: The theme of divine succession resonates with myths from Canaan, Greece, and Mesopotamia

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Divine Succession | Violent overthrow as the mechanism of cosmic governance |
| Swallowing and Birth | The grotesque act of Kumarbi ingesting Anu's power and bearing gods |
| Kingship in Heaven | The question of who rightfully rules the divine realm |
| Hurrian-Hittite Synthesis | Blending of Hurrian deities with Hittite literary tradition |
| Cosmic Order | The final establishment of Teshub's legitimate sovereignty |
