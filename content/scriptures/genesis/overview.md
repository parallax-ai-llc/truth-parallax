## Overview

Genesis is the first book of the Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament. Its Hebrew name, *Bereshit* ("In the beginning"), reflects its opening words and central concern: the origins of the world, humanity, and the people of Israel.

## Structure

Genesis is traditionally divided into two main sections:

1. **Primeval History** (Chapters 1–11): Creation, the Fall, Cain and Abel, the Flood, and the Tower of Babel
2. **Patriarchal Narratives** (Chapters 12–50): The stories of Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Genesis holds a unique position as a shared foundational text across multiple religious traditions:

- **Judaism**: The first book of the Torah, read annually in synagogue liturgy
- **Christianity**: The theological foundation for doctrines of creation, fall, and redemption
- **Islam**: Many narratives parallel Quranic accounts (Adam, Noah, Abraham/Ibrahim)
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding ancient Near Eastern literature and religion

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Creation | God's sovereign act of bringing the world into existence |
| Covenant | God's binding promises to Noah, Abraham, and their descendants |
| Fall | Humanity's disobedience and its consequences |
| Election | God's choice of particular individuals and peoples |
| Providence | God's ongoing care and direction of human affairs |
