## Binyamin Arrives in Misr

Genesis 43 narrates the brothers' second journey to Egypt, this time with Binyamin, as demanded by Yusuf. Ya'qub reluctantly allows Binyamin to go. The brothers are received warmly by Yusuf, who is overcome with emotion upon seeing his full brother. The Quranic parallel in Surah Yusuf 12:63-69 covers these events with characteristic focus on Ya'qub's prophetic insight and Yusuf's restraint.

## Quranic Parallels

| Yahudha pledges surety for Binyamin (43:8-10) | 12:66: Ya'qub demands a solemn oath (*mawthiq*) from Allah before sending Binyamin | Quran elevates the pledge to a divine covenant, not merely a brother's promise |

## Yusuf's Private Revelation to Binyamin

The most significant Quranic addition is **Quran 12:69**: Yusuf privately tells Binyamin, "Indeed, I am your brother, so do not despair over what they used to do." This detail is absent from Genesis, where Yusuf maintains his disguise throughout. The Quranic version shows:

1. **Prophetic compassion**: Yusuf does not leave his full brother in anguish unnecessarily
2. **Strategic disclosure**: He tells Binyamin but instructs him to keep the secret, as the larger plan is not yet complete
3. **Comfort**: The phrase "do not despair" (*la tabta'is*) carries deep emotional weight — Binyamin had presumably mourned Yusuf for years

## Ya'qub's Instructions About the Gates

Ya'qub's instruction to enter Egypt by different gates (12:67) merits attention. Islamic commentators offer several explanations:

- **Protection from the evil eye (*al-'ayn*)**: A group of handsome young men entering together might attract envy. The hadith confirms the reality of the evil eye: "The evil eye is real" (Sahih Muslim 2188)
- **Practical caution**: Reducing their visibility as a foreign group in a time of famine
- **Spiritual lesson**: Ya'qub immediately qualifies his practical advice with theological submission: "I cannot avail you against Allah at all" — worldly precautions do not replace divine decree

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** focuses on the emotional dynamics of the reunion. He notes that Yusuf's self-control — weeping privately (Genesis) and revealing himself only to Binyamin (Quran) — exemplifies prophetic composure. The ability to restrain powerful emotions while serving a divine purpose is a hallmark of prophetic character.

**Al-Tabari** provides extensive detail from Isra'iliyyat sources about the banquet, the seating arrangements (Yusuf seats the brothers in pairs, leaving Binyamin alone, then takes him as his own companion), and the emotional tension of the encounter.

**Al-Qurtubi** examines Ya'qub's demand for a divine covenant (*mawthiq min Allah*) rather than a mere human promise. This elevates the brothers' obligation: breaking this oath would be a sin against Allah, not merely against their father.

**Al-Razi** discusses the permissibility of strategic concealment (*tawriyah*) — speaking truth that is understood differently by the listener. Yusuf's behaviour throughout this chapter involves calculated ambiguity rather than outright falsehood, which Islamic jurisprudence permits under certain conditions.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter illustrates several Islamic virtues working in concert: Ya'qub's **tawakkul** (trust in Allah despite fear), Yusuf's **hilm** (forbearance and emotional control), and the brothers' grudging honesty (they fulfil the oath). The narrative demonstrates that divine plans unfold through human actions — but the controlling agent is always Allah.
