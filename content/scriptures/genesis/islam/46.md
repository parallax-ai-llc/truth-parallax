## Ya'qub's Journey to Misr

Genesis 46 records Ya'qub's journey to Egypt with his entire household, a genealogical list of those who went, and the emotional reunion between Ya'qub and Yusuf. The Quranic parallel in Surah Yusuf 12:93-100 covers the journey and reunion with its characteristic focus on the miraculous restoration of Ya'qub's sight and the fulfilment of Yusuf's childhood dream.

## Quranic Parallels

| Genesis 46 | Quran 12:93-100 | Key Differences |
|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Ya'qub sets out for Egypt (46:1-7) | 12:93: Brothers told to "bring me your family, all together" | Same migration |
| God speaks to Ya'qub at Beersheba: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt" (46:2-4) | Not explicitly in Quran, but Ya'qub's spiritual state is addressed through the shirt miracle | Genesis adds a theophany; Quran relies on the shirt narrative |
| Genealogical list of 70 persons (46:8-27) | Not in Quran | Genesis provides detailed genealogy absent from Quranic account |
| Ya'qub and Yusuf reunite (46:29-30) | 12:99-100: "When they entered upon Yusuf, he took his parents to himself and said, 'Enter Egypt, if Allah wills, safe and secure'" | Both record the reunion; Quran adds the phrase "if Allah wills" |
| Ya'qub: "Now let me die, since I have seen your face" (46:30) | Not in Quran | Genesis expresses readiness for death; Quran moves to the dream fulfilment |
| Not in Genesis 46 | 12:100: Parents and brothers prostrate before Yusuf; Yusuf says, "This is the interpretation of my dream of before. My Lord has made it reality" | Quran places the dream fulfilment here — the climactic moment |

## The Shirt and the Restoration of Sight

The Quranic account adds a detail that bridges chapters 45 and 46. When the caravan carrying Yusuf's shirt departs Egypt:

> **"And when the caravan departed [from Egypt], their father said, 'Indeed, I find the smell of Yusuf, if only you would not think me feeble-minded.'"** (12:94)

Those around him respond dismissively: "By Allah, indeed you are in your old error" (12:95). But when the shirt arrives:

> **"And when the bearer of good tidings arrived, he cast it over his face, and he returned to seeing. He said, 'Did I not tell you that I know from Allah that which you do not know?'"** (12:96)

This miraculous restoration confirms Ya'qub's prophetic knowledge — he had sensed Yusuf's presence across the distance, and his faith was vindicated.

## The Dream Fulfilled (Quran 12:100)

The climactic moment of the entire surah occurs when Ya'qub's family arrives in Egypt:

> **"And he raised his parents upon the throne, and they fell down before him in prostration. And he said, 'O my father, this is the interpretation (*ta'wil*) of my dream of before. My Lord has made it reality.'"** (12:100)

The eleven stars, the sun, and the moon from Yusuf's childhood dream (12:4) — his eleven brothers, his father, and his mother (or aunt, as Rahil had died) — now prostrate before him. The circle is complete.

**Ibn Kathir** notes that this prostration was one of honour (*sujud tahiyyah*), permitted in the Sharia of Ya'qub but abrogated in the Sharia of Muhammad. The dream's fulfilment after decades of trial demonstrates that Allah's promises are always fulfilled, though the timing belongs to Him alone.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** emphasises that the entire Yusuf narrative — from dream to fulfilment — illustrates **qadar** (divine decree). Every apparent setback (the well, slavery, prison) was a step toward the dream's realisation. He quotes the final verses of Surah Yusuf: "Indeed, it is a lesson for those of understanding. It is not a narration invented, but a confirmation of what was before it" (12:111).

**Al-Tabari** supplements the Quranic account with Isra'iliyyat details about the journey, identifying specific family members and elaborating on the reunion scene. He treats the genealogical information from Genesis 46 as historical data.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the legal status of migration (*hijrah*) for family reunification, noting that Ya'qub's journey was both spiritually and practically motivated — a principle that Islamic jurisprudence applies to family obligations.

**Sayyid Qutb** reads the reunion as the resolution of the "best of stories": patient faith is always rewarded, divine plans always unfold perfectly, and the righteous are vindicated in this life or the next. He notes that the Quran does not describe Ya'qub's readiness to die (as Genesis does) but instead focuses on thanksgiving and the acknowledgment of divine wisdom.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter embodies the culmination of **sabr** (patience). Ya'qub waited decades, wept until blind, yet never lost faith in Allah's plan. His vindication — the restoration of his sight, the reunion with Yusuf, the fulfilment of the dream — teaches that patient faith is never wasted. As the Quran states: **"Indeed, Allah does not allow to be lost the reward of those who do good"** (12:90).
