## Life in Misr and Ya'qub's Final Days

Genesis 47 describes the settlement of Ya'qub's family in Goshen, Ya'qub's audience with Pharaoh, Yusuf's land-consolidation policies during the famine, and Ya'qub's request to be buried in Canaan. The Quran does not cover this post-reunion period of Yusuf's story — Surah Yusuf effectively concludes with the dream's fulfilment (12:100-101). The events of Genesis 47 are therefore treated as Isra'iliyyat.

## Quranic Parallels

Direct Quranic parallels are absent for this chapter. However, several Quranic principles apply:

- **Yusuf's governance**: Quran 12:55 records Yusuf's request: "Appoint me over the storehouses of the land. Indeed, I am a knowing guardian (*hafiz 'alim*)." Genesis 47 shows the outworking of this appointment through specific economic policies.
- **Ya'qub's faith until death**: Quran 2:132-133 records Ya'qub's deathbed scene (which Genesis places in chapter 49): "Ya'qub said to his sons, 'What will you worship after me?' They said, 'We will worship your God and the God of your fathers — Ibrahim, Isma'il, and Ishaq — one God. And to Him we submit.'"
- **Stewardship of land**: The Quranic principle that "the earth belongs to Allah; He causes to inherit it whom He wills" (7:128) frames any discussion of land policy.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Ya'qub blesses Pharaoh (47:7, 10) | Ya'qub pronounces blessing on Pharaoh | A prophet blessing a non-Muslim ruler is not objectionable; prophets showed respect to temporal authorities |
| Yusuf's land policy (47:13-26) | Yusuf acquires all land for Pharaoh; Egyptians become tenants owing 20% of produce | Potentially oppressive; Islamic land law (*ahkam al-aradi*) does not endorse state confiscation of private land |
| Priestly land exemption (47:22, 26) | Priests keep their land because Pharaoh feeds them | Preferential treatment for pagan priests is problematic from an Islamic perspective |
| Ya'qub's age: 130 years (47:9) | Ya'qub tells Pharaoh his age and calls his years "few and difficult" | The longevity is accepted in Islamic tradition; prophets of earlier ages lived longer |
| Burial request (47:29-31) | Ya'qub asks to be buried in Canaan, not Egypt | Unproblematic; the desire to be buried in ancestral/holy land is honoured in Islamic tradition |

## Yusuf's Economic Policies

Genesis 47:13-26 describes Yusuf acquiring all Egyptian land for the crown during the famine — first taking money, then livestock, then land itself, reducing the population to serfdom. Islamic commentators engage with this cautiously:

**Al-Tabari** records these economic details from Isra'iliyyat sources without strong endorsement. The policies raise questions about justice and the proper role of government in crisis.

Islamic economic principles would evaluate Yusuf's policies against several standards:
- **Prohibition of exploitation**: Taking advantage of famine to consolidate land could be seen as exploitative
- **Public interest (*maslaha*)**: If the policies prevented mass starvation, they served the greater good
- **Prophetic dispensation**: Yusuf operated under a different Sharia; Islamic economic regulations came later

Most Islamic scholars resolve this tension by noting that the Quran praises Yusuf's governance without specifying its details — suggesting that the specific policies recorded in Genesis may not be historically accurate, or that they were appropriate for that context.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** does not elaborate on Yusuf's economic policies, as the Quran does not address them. He focuses instead on the theological conclusion of the Yusuf narrative: **"My Lord, You have given me authority and taught me the interpretation of dreams. Creator of the heavens and earth, You are my protector in this world and in the Hereafter"** (12:101).

**Al-Tabari** treats the economic details as historical information, noting Yusuf's administrative competence without making theological judgments about specific policies.

**Modern scholars** like **Muhammad Abduh** have used Yusuf's governance as a model for Islamic economic planning — emphasising the principles of competence, foresight, and public service rather than the specific mechanisms described in Genesis.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter's primary Islamic lesson concerns **preparation and governance** — the prophetic responsibility to use authority wisely for public benefit. Yusuf's governance, whatever its specific form, is understood as service to Allah through service to people. The chapter also teaches that prophetic figures engage fully with worldly governance while maintaining their spiritual orientation, as expressed in Yusuf's prayer: "Cause me to die a Muslim and join me with the righteous" (12:101).
