## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Ibrahim and the King

Genesis 20 narrates a second "wife-sister" episode, in which Abraham tells King Abimelech of Gerar that Sarah is his sister, Abimelech takes Sarah, God warns Abimelech in a dream, and Sarah is returned. This narrative presents challenges for Islamic theology due to the apparent deception by a prophet, and it receives relatively limited attention compared to the major Ibrahim episodes. The Quran does not reference this specific event.

Islamic scholars who address this narrative apply the concept of *tawriyah* — a form of permissible equivocation where a statement has a true meaning different from the apparent meaning. Ibrahim's reference to Sarah as his "sister" is interpreted as "sister in faith" — technically true and not a lie. The hadith literature does reference Ibrahim's three "lies" (in fact, three instances of *tawriyah*), acknowledging these episodes while reinterpreting them to preserve prophetic integrity.

## Quranic Parallels

There are no direct Quranic parallels to Genesis 20. However, several Quranic principles are relevant:

- **Quran 4:125** — "And who is better in religion than one who submits his face to Allah while being a doer of good and follows the religion of Abraham, inclining toward truth? And Allah took Abraham as an intimate friend." Ibrahim's status as *Khalil Allah* means his character must be understood in the most favorable light.
- **Quran 21:51** — "And We had certainly given Abraham his sound judgment before, and We were of him well-knowing." God's testimony to Ibrahim's good judgment precludes attributing moral failure to him.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 20 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Abraham's deception | Tells Abimelech Sarah is his sister | *Tawriyah* — permissible equivocation, not a lie |
| Abraham as half-truth | "She is indeed my sister, the daughter of my father" (20:12) | Not accepted as literal; "sister in faith" interpretation preferred |
| God warns Abimelech | God comes to Abimelech in a dream | Consistent with Islamic belief that God can communicate with non-prophets through dreams |
| Abraham as prophet | God calls Abraham a prophet (20:7) | Fully affirmed; Ibrahim is a prophet and *Khalil Allah* |
| Sarah taken by a king | Abimelech takes Sarah | God's protection of Sarah is emphasized over any failure of Ibrahim |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** addresses the "wife-sister" episodes in the context of the hadith about Ibrahim's three instances of *tawriyah*. The hadith (found in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim) lists three statements of Ibrahim that appear to be untrue: calling Sarah his sister, saying the "biggest idol did it" when he smashed the idols (Quran 21:63), and claiming illness to avoid an idol festival (37:89). Al-Tabari explains that each statement had a true underlying meaning and therefore did not constitute lying in the moral sense. The emphasis is on Ibrahim's wisdom and his use of language to navigate dangerous situations.

**Ibn Kathir** treats this episode with characteristic brevity regarding Isra'iliyyat details. He acknowledges the hadith about Ibrahim's *tawriyah* but focuses on the theological principle: prophets do not lie. The distinction between *tawriyah* (equivocation with a true meaning) and *kadhib* (lying) is critical in Islamic jurisprudence and theology. Ibn Kathir stresses that God's protection of Sarah — preventing Abimelech from touching her — demonstrates divine providence over the prophetic family. The episode, in his reading, is ultimately about God's faithfulness, not Ibrahim's weakness.

**Al-Tha'labi** integrates this episode into his broader narrative of Ibrahim's life, treating it as one of several trials through which Ibrahim's faith was refined. He describes Sarah's composure and prayer during her captivity, her trust in God, and the divine intervention that protected her. Al-Tha'labi's treatment elevates both Ibrahim and Sarah as models of faith under duress.
