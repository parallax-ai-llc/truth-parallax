## Ishaq and the Wells of Gerar

Genesis 26 presents the only chapter focused primarily on Isaac — known in Islamic tradition as **Ishaq** (إسحاق). The chapter records the renewal of divine promises to Ishaq, his sojourn among the Philistines, disputes over wells, and a peace agreement with Abimelech. Islamic tradition acknowledges Ishaq as a prophet and righteous servant of Allah, though his narrative receives far less attention than that of his father Ibrahim or his brother Isma'il.

In the Quran, Ishaq is mentioned with honour but briefly. The focus of the Abrahamic covenant in Islamic theology flows through Isma'il and his descendants — a fundamental point of divergence from the biblical account that shapes how this chapter is read.

## Quranic Parallels

Ishaq is named in several Quranic passages, always in contexts of divine blessing:

- **Quran 37:112-113**: "And We gave him the good tidings of Ishaq, a prophet among the righteous. And We blessed him and Ishaq."
- **Quran 21:72**: "And We bestowed upon him Ishaq and Ya'qub as an additional gift, and each of them We made righteous."
- **Quran 11:71**: The announcement of Ishaq's birth to Ibrahim and Sarah.

There is no direct Quranic parallel to the events of Genesis 26 — the wells, the deception about Rebekah, or the covenant with Abimelech. These narratives fall into the category of **Isra'iliyyat** — traditions from Jewish and Christian sources.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Ishaq's deception about his wife | He calls Rebekah his "sister" (26:7) | Rejected — prophets do not lie for self-preservation; *ismah* (prophetic infallibility) precludes deliberate deception |
| Covenant primacy | Ishaq inherits the Abrahamic covenant as primary heir | The covenant line runs through Isma'il to Muhammad; Ishaq is blessed but not the primary covenant bearer |
| Wells as symbols | Prosperity and divine favour | Acknowledged, but the well of Zamzam associated with Isma'il holds greater significance |
| Famine response | Ishaq told not to go to Egypt (26:2) | No Quranic comment; treated as neutral Isra'iliyyat |

## Isra'iliyyat Evaluation

Since Genesis 26 has no direct Quranic or hadith parallel, its contents are evaluated according to classical Isra'iliyyat principles:

- **Neutral material**: The well-digging narratives, disputes with herdsmen, and covenant with Abimelech neither contradict nor confirm Islamic sources. Following the principle attributed to the Prophet Muhammad — *"Neither believe nor disbelieve the People of the Book"* (Sahih al-Bukhari 4485) — these may be narrated without endorsement.
- **Rejected material**: The deception about Rebekah (26:7-11) contradicts the Islamic doctrine of **ismah**. **Ibn Kathir** omits this episode in his *Qisas al-Anbiya'*, consistent with his practice of filtering out narratives that compromise prophetic dignity.

## Commentators' Views

**Al-Tabari** includes some Genesis 26 material in his *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk*, drawing on the transmissions of **Wahb ibn Munabbih** and **Ka'b al-Ahbar**, early converts from Judaism who brought extensive biblical knowledge into Islamic scholarship. Al-Tabari presents these without strong endorsement, following his encyclopedic method.

**Ibn Kathir** takes a more critical approach, restricting his Ishaq narrative to what is confirmed by Quran and hadith. He emphasises Ishaq's status as a prophet and the father of Ya'qub, without elaborating on the well disputes or Abimelech episodes.

**Al-Tha'labi** in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* provides a fuller account, drawing on Isra'iliyyat traditions more freely, while noting their origins.

The Islamic moral reading of this chapter centres on **sabr** (patience) and **tawakkul** (reliance on Allah). Ishaq's persistence in re-digging wells and seeking peace rather than conflict reflects prophetic character — gentle, persevering, and trusting in divine provision.
