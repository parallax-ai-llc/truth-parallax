## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Qabil and Habil

Genesis 4 recounts the story of Cain and Abel — known in Islamic tradition as Qabil and Habil — including the first murder in human history. This narrative has one of the most direct Quranic parallels of any Genesis passage, appearing in Surah al-Ma'idah 5:27-32. The Quranic version is theologically dense and includes the famous declaration that killing one person unjustly is as if one killed all of humanity (5:32), a statement with profound implications for Islamic law and ethics.

The Islamic reading diverges from Genesis in several respects. The Quran does not mention the specific offerings (grain vs. animal) but focuses on the inner disposition: one brother's offering was accepted because of *taqwa* (God-consciousness), and the other's was rejected for lack of it. The aftermath also differs — the Quran includes the episode of the crow sent by God to teach Qabil how to bury his brother, a detail absent from Genesis.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 5:27-31** — "And recite to them the story of Adam's two sons, in truth, when they both offered a sacrifice, and it was accepted from one of them but was not accepted from the other. Said [the latter], 'I will surely kill you.' Said [the former], 'Indeed, Allah only accepts from the God-fearing.' ... Then Allah sent a crow searching in the ground to show him how to hide the disgrace of his brother. He said, 'O woe to me! Have I failed to be like this crow and hide the body of my brother?' And he became of the regretful."
- **Quran 5:32** — "Because of that, We decreed upon the Children of Israel that whoever kills a soul unless for a soul or for corruption in the land — it is as if he had slain mankind entirely. And whoever saves one — it is as if he had saved mankind entirely."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 4 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Names | Cain and Abel | Qabil and Habil (from Arabic tradition) |
| Offerings | Grain vs. firstborn of flock | Not specified in Quran; focus is on *taqwa* |
| Reason for rejection | Not clearly stated in Genesis | Lack of God-consciousness (*taqwa*) |
| Abel's response | Not recorded | "I will not stretch out my hand against you" — a model of patience |
| Burial | Not described | Crow sent by God to teach burial |
| Mark of Cain | God places a protective mark on Cain | Not mentioned in Islamic sources |
| Cain's descendants | Genealogy through Lamech (4:17-24) | Largely treated as Isra'iliyyat |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides an extensive retelling, incorporating Isra'iliyyat traditions about the cause of the dispute — often involving marriage arrangements between the brothers and their twin sisters. He records traditions from Ibn Abbas and others suggesting that each brother was born with a twin sister, and that the dispute arose over who would marry whom. While these details are not in the Quran, Al-Tabari includes them as part of the historical narrative tradition without fully endorsing them.

**Ibn Kathir** narrates the twin-sister tradition but notes it comes from earlier People of the Book sources. He focuses his analysis on the Quranic text itself, drawing out the ethical lessons: Habil's refusal to fight back demonstrates the Islamic virtue of *sabr* (patience) and trust in divine justice. Ibn Kathir emphasizes that the verse "I will not stretch out my hand against you to kill you" (5:28) establishes Habil as a righteous model. He also elaborates on Quran 5:32, connecting the story to the sanctity of human life in Islamic jurisprudence.

**Al-Tha'labi** in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* gives the most elaborate account, describing the emotional dimensions of the story — Qabil's envy, Habil's piety, and the parents' grief. His narrative includes the crow episode in vivid detail and uses the story to illustrate the destructive nature of *hasad* (envy), one of the major spiritual diseases in Islamic ethics.

The genealogy of Cain's descendants in Genesis 4:17-24, including Lamech's boast of violence, receives little attention in Islamic sources. This material is considered Isra'iliyyat and is neither confirmed nor denied by the Quran.
