## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Death of Sarah

Genesis 23 narrates the death of Sarah at age 127 in Hebron, Abraham's mourning, and his purchase of the cave of Machpelah from Ephron the Hittite as a burial site. This chapter has no direct Quranic parallel. The Quran does not mention Sarah's death, her age, the purchase of land, or the cave of Machpelah. Islamic engagement with this material is therefore entirely through the Isra'iliyyat tradition and the general principles of Islamic interpretation.

The narrative is significant for Jewish and Christian traditions as it establishes a legal claim to land in Canaan. In Islamic perspective, this land-purchase narrative does not carry the same weight, as the Quranic covenant with Ibrahim is spiritual rather than territorial. Nonetheless, Muslim historians incorporated the basic facts of Sarah's death and burial into their universal histories.

## Handling of Isra'iliyyat Material

Genesis 23 is a straightforward example of Isra'iliyyat material — historical narrative from the biblical tradition that neither contradicts nor confirms Islamic scripture. The chapter contains no theological claims that would conflict with Islamic principles, making it part of the "neutral" category that may be narrated without endorsement.

**Al-Tabari** includes Sarah's death in his historical narrative, noting her age and the location of burial. He draws on Isra'iliyyat sources for these details, as the Quran is silent. Al-Tabari treats the information as historically useful without investing it with theological significance. The legal negotiations between Ibrahim and the Hittites over the burial cave are mentioned as historical background, not as precedent for land claims.

**Ibn Kathir** mentions Sarah's death briefly in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah*. His treatment is characteristically concise on matters not attested in the Quran or hadith. He notes her death and burial but does not elaborate on the purchase negotiation, which is a distinctly biblical legal concern. Ibn Kathir's primary interest is in what Sarah represents theologically — the mother of Ishaq, a righteous woman who received good tidings from the angels (11:71), and a partner in Ibrahim's prophetic mission.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides a more narrative account, describing Ibrahim's grief and his honorable treatment of his wife's burial. Al-Tha'labi uses the episode to illustrate Ibrahim's character — even in grief, he conducted himself with dignity and justice, paying full price for the burial site rather than accepting it as a gift. This characterization is consistent with the Quranic image of Ibrahim as a model of ethical conduct.

## Sarah in Islamic Tradition

Though the Quran does not name Sarah explicitly, she is identified in tafsir as the wife of Ibrahim who received the angelic good tidings of Ishaq's birth (11:71-73). Traditions describe her as a woman of great beauty, faith, and patience. Her life alongside Ibrahim through his trials — emigration, the separation from Hajar and Isma'il, and the long wait for a child — is seen as exemplifying the virtue of *sabr* (patient endurance). Her death, while not a Quranic event, is treated with respect by Muslim historians as part of the prophetic family's biography.
