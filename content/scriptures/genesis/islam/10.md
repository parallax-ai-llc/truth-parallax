## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Table of Nations

Genesis 10, the "Table of Nations," traces the descendants of Noah's three sons — Shem, Ham, and Japheth — and their spread across the ancient world. This chapter has no direct Quranic parallel. The Quran does not provide ethnographic genealogies or trace the origins of specific peoples to Noah's sons. Islamic engagement with this material is therefore almost entirely through the Isra'iliyyat tradition, where Muslim historians and commentators incorporated biblical genealogies into their universal histories.

The Islamic approach to this material is guided by a key Quranic principle: human diversity is a sign of God's creative power, not a basis for hierarchy. "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another. Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (Quran 49:13).

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 49:13** — "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another." This verse, while not directly referencing Genesis 10, provides the Islamic framework for understanding human diversity.
- **Quran 11:48** — Nations descending from those on the ark with Nuh, some blessed and some destined for punishment.
- **Quran 30:22** — "And of His signs is the creation of the heavens and the earth and the diversity of your languages and your colors. Indeed in that are signs for those of knowledge."

## Handling of Isra'iliyyat Material

Genesis 10 is a prime example of material that falls squarely in the "neutral" Isra'iliyyat category — neither confirmed nor denied by Islamic revelation. Muslim scholars engaged with it primarily in the genre of universal history (*tarikh*), not tafsir (Quranic commentary).

**Al-Tabari** in his *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* (History of the Prophets and Kings) provides one of the most extensive Islamic treatments of the Table of Nations. He reproduces much of the genealogical information, mapping Noah's descendants to known peoples and regions. Shem's descendants are associated with the Arabs, Persians, and other peoples of the East; Ham's with Africans and some peoples of the South; Japheth's with Turks, Slavs, and Europeans. Al-Tabari treats this as historical information useful for understanding the spread of humanity, without attaching theological significance to the divisions.

**Ibn Kathir** addresses the Table of Nations more briefly, acknowledging the basic framework of three branches from Nuh's sons but cautioning against reliance on specific details from biblical sources. He stresses that in Islam, lineage confers no spiritual superiority — a principle established by the Prophet Muhammad's hadith: "There is no superiority of an Arab over a non-Arab, nor of a non-Arab over an Arab... except by piety and good action" (Musnad Ahmad). Ibn Kathir uses this principle to counter any reading of Genesis 10 that might establish racial or ethnic hierarchies.

**Al-Tha'labi** incorporates the genealogical material into his narrative framework, using it to set the stage for the stories of later prophets. His interest is in the narrative flow from Nuh to Ibrahim, and the Table of Nations provides the connective tissue. He does not invest the genealogies with theological weight but treats them as useful historical background.

## Islamic Principles Applied

The Islamic approach to Genesis 10 is governed by several principles:

1. **No racial hierarchy**: The Quran explicitly rejects the notion that any ethnic group is inherently superior (49:13). The "curse of Ham" tradition, sometimes derived from Genesis 9-10, has no standing in Islamic theology.
2. **Diversity as divine sign**: Human ethnic and linguistic diversity is presented as evidence of God's creative power (30:22), not as a result of sin or punishment.
3. **Genealogy serves providence**: The spread of nations is understood as part of God's plan to establish prophets among all peoples (10:47, 16:36).
