## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Great Sacrifice

Genesis 22, the Aqedah or "Binding of Isaac," is one of the most theologically charged chapters in the entire Bible. It narrates God's command to Abraham to sacrifice his son Isaac, Abraham's obedience, and God's provision of a ram as a substitute. Islam has a parallel narrative that is equally central to its theology and practice — the sacrifice is commemorated annually during Eid al-Adha and ritually reenacted by millions of pilgrims at Mina during Hajj. However, the Islamic tradition identifies the son of sacrifice as Isma'il, not Ishaq, and the Quranic account differs in several important ways.

This is arguably the single most important point of divergence between the biblical and Quranic Ibrahim narratives, with profound implications for how each tradition understands covenant, election, and the sacred geography of worship.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 37:99-111** — The complete sacrifice narrative: "And he said, 'Indeed, I will go to [where I am ordered by] my Lord; He will guide me. My Lord, grant me [a child] from among the righteous.' So We gave him good tidings of a forbearing boy. And when he reached with him [the age of] exertion, he said, 'O my son, indeed I have seen in a dream that I [must] sacrifice you, so see what you think.' He said, 'O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, of the steadfast.' And when they had both submitted and he put him down upon his forehead, We called to him, 'O Abraham, you have fulfilled the vision.' Indeed, We thus reward the doers of good. Indeed, this was the clear trial. And We ransomed him with a great sacrifice. And We left for him [favorable mention] among later generations: 'Peace upon Abraham.'"
- **Quran 37:112** — Immediately after, God gives "good tidings of Isaac" — scholars use this sequence to argue the first son mentioned was Isma'il.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 22 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Son to be sacrificed | Isaac (22:2) — "your only son Isaac" | Isma'il — the majority Islamic position based on Quran 37:99-113 |
| "Your only son" | Specification of Isaac | Islam notes Isaac was never the "only" son — Isma'il was firstborn |
| Location | Mount Moriah (22:2) — later identified with the Temple Mount | Mina, near Mecca — the site of the Hajj sacrifice |
| Son's knowledge | Isaac appears unaware until the binding | Isma'il is told and consents: "Do as you are commanded" (37:102) |
| Son's response | Isaac does not speak during the binding | Active consent — Isma'il demonstrates *islam* (submission) alongside his father |
| The command | Direct divine command | Through a dream (*ru'ya*), which for prophets carries the force of revelation |
| Substitutionary ram | God provides a ram caught in a thicket (22:13) | "We ransomed him with a great sacrifice" (*dhibh 'azim*, 37:107) — commemorated in Eid al-Adha |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** records both positions — that the son was Isma'il or Ishaq — reflecting genuine scholarly disagreement in early Islam. He presents traditions from Companions and Successors supporting each identification. However, he leans toward the view that it was Ishaq, based on certain Isra'iliyyat traditions. This is a notable exception where Al-Tabari diverges from what became the majority position. His intellectual honesty in recording both views is characteristic of his methodology.

**Ibn Kathir** firmly identifies the son as Isma'il. His primary textual argument is based on the sequence in Quran 37: the prayer for a son (37:100), the birth of a "forbearing boy" (37:101), the sacrifice narrative (37:102-107), and then *after* the sacrifice, "We gave him good tidings of Isaac" (37:112). Since the good news of Ishaq comes after the sacrifice story, the son in the sacrifice must be someone else — namely Isma'il, the firstborn. Ibn Kathir also argues that Genesis 22:2's "your only son" makes more sense if applied to Isma'il, who was the only son before Ishaq's birth. He considers the identification of Isaac in the biblical text to be *tahrif*.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides the most emotional and detailed narrative of the sacrifice. He describes Ibrahim's dream over three consecutive nights, Isma'il's willing preparation, Iblis's attempts to dissuade the family (the origin of the stone-throwing ritual at Mina during Hajj), and the dramatic moment when the knife would not cut. His account emphasizes the *mutual* submission of father and son — "when they had both submitted" (37:103) — as the theological climax. This mutual submission is the essence of *islam* itself.

## Eid al-Adha Connection

The Quranic sacrifice narrative is the theological foundation for Eid al-Adha, one of the two major Islamic festivals. Every year, Muslims worldwide sacrifice an animal in commemoration of Ibrahim's obedience, distributing the meat to family, friends, and the poor. During Hajj, pilgrims perform the sacrifice at Mina and throw stones at three pillars representing the points where Iblis tempted Ibrahim, Hajar, and Isma'il.
