## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Hajar and Isma'il

Genesis 16 tells of Sarai giving her servant Hagar to Abram, Hagar's pregnancy, the conflict between the two women, Hagar's flight to the desert, and the angel's promise about Ishmael. This chapter is of enormous significance for Islam, as it introduces two figures who are absolutely central to Islamic sacred history: Hajar (Hagar) and Isma'il (Ishmael). In Islam, Isma'il is not the "other" son — he is the firstborn of Ibrahim, a prophet in his own right, and the ancestor of the Prophet Muhammad. Hajar is honored as a woman of extraordinary faith whose desperate search for water in the desert is ritually commemorated by every Muslim who performs Hajj.

The Islamic narrative diverges from Genesis 16 in tone, emphasis, and theological significance. Where Genesis portrays Hagar as a servant caught between her mistress and her master, Islamic tradition elevates her as a model of trust in God (*tawakkul*).

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 14:37** — "Our Lord, I have settled some of my descendants in an uncultivated valley near Your sacred House." Ibrahim places Hajar and Isma'il near the future site of the Ka'bah — this is the Islamic version of Hagar's journey into the wilderness.
- **Quran 37:100-101** — "My Lord, grant me [a child] from among the righteous. So We gave him good tidings of a forbearing boy." The birth of Isma'il as an answer to Ibrahim's prayer.
- **Quran 2:158** — "Indeed, al-Safa and al-Marwah are among the symbols of Allah." The ritual of *sa'y* (running between the hills of Safa and Marwah during Hajj) commemorates Hajar's search for water.
- **Quran 19:54** — "And mention in the Book, Ishmael. Indeed, he was true to his promise, and he was a messenger and a prophet."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 16 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Hagar's status | Sarai's Egyptian servant/slave | Honored mother of Isma'il; some traditions identify her as an Egyptian princess |
| Conflict with Sarah | Hagar despises Sarai; Sarai mistreats Hagar | Acknowledged but not central; focus is on divine plan |
| Location of the wilderness | Wilderness of Shur, near Egypt | Near Mecca, in the valley of Bakkah (Quran 3:96) |
| Ishmael's destiny | "Wild donkey of a man" (16:12) | Prophet, ancestor of Muhammad, co-builder of the Ka'bah |
| The well | Beer-lahai-roi (16:14) | Zamzam — the miraculous well that still flows in Mecca |
| Angel's message | Promise of many descendants (16:10) | Expanded in Islamic tradition: Isma'il's line leads to the Prophet Muhammad |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides an extensive account of Hajar and Isma'il's journey to the wilderness near Mecca. Drawing on hadith traditions (particularly from Sahih al-Bukhari, narrated by Ibn Abbas), he describes Ibrahim bringing Hajar and the infant Isma'il to the barren valley, leaving them with dates and water, and Hajar's desperate search for water. She runs between the hills of Safa and Marwah seven times — the origin of the Hajj ritual of *sa'y*. The angel Jibril (Gabriel) then strikes the ground, and the well of Zamzam springs forth. Al-Tabari connects this narrative directly to the establishment of Mecca as a sacred site.

**Ibn Kathir** elevates the Hajar narrative to one of the most important episodes in sacred history. He cites the long hadith in al-Bukhari about Ibrahim's journey with Hajar and Isma'il in detail. Ibn Kathir emphasizes that Ibrahim's placement of his family in the desert was a divine command, not an act of abandonment — and Hajar's response when she learned this ("Then God will not neglect us") exemplifies perfect *tawakkul*. He notes that the characterization of Ishmael as a "wild donkey of a man" in Genesis 16:12 is rejected as *tahrif*; the Quran describes Isma'il as "true to his promise" and "a messenger and a prophet" (19:54).

**Al-Tha'labi** provides the richest narrative treatment, describing Hajar's emotions, her trust in God, and the miraculous appearance of Zamzam. He connects the story to the later settlement of the Jurhum tribe near the well, Isma'il's marriage into that tribe, and the eventual building of the Ka'bah — establishing the unbroken chain from Hajar's journey to the sacred geography of Islam.
