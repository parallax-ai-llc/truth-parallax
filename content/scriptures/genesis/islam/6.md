## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Corruption Before the Flood

Genesis 6 describes the corruption of the earth, the mysterious "sons of God" marrying "daughters of men," God's regret over creating humanity, and the command to Noah to build an ark. The Islamic reading of this chapter intersects with one of the Quran's most extensively treated prophetic narratives — the story of Nuh (Noah), who has an entire surah (Surah 71, *Nuh*) dedicated to him and appears in numerous other passages.

Islam reshapes this narrative in several critical ways. The "sons of God" passage (6:1-4) is treated with great suspicion, as it implies divine beings mating with humans — a concept incompatible with Islamic theology. God's "regret" (6:6) is rejected as anthropomorphism. And Nuh is elevated from a passive righteous man to an active prophet-preacher who spent centuries calling his people to *tawhid*.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 71:1-28** — The entire Surah Nuh details his prophetic mission: "Indeed, We sent Noah to his people, saying, 'Warn your people before there comes to them a painful punishment.'" He preaches day and night for 950 years (29:14), but his people reject him.
- **Quran 11:25-35** — Nuh's mission as a warner, the people's mockery, and God's command to build the ark.
- **Quran 23:23-30** — Nuh's preaching and the flood.
- **Quran 29:14** — "And We certainly sent Noah to his people, and he remained among them a thousand years minus fifty years."
- **Quran 7:59-64** — Nuh calls his people to worship God alone; they reject him and are drowned.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 6 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| "Sons of God" (6:1-4) | Angels/divine beings marry human women | Rejected entirely; incompatible with Islamic angelology (angels have no free will to sin) |
| Nephilim/giants | Offspring of divine-human unions | Not recognized in standard Islamic theology |
| God's "regret" (6:6) | "The Lord regretted that He had made man" | Rejected as anthropomorphism; God does not change His mind |
| Noah's role | Righteous man who "walked with God" | Active prophet who preached for centuries; a model of *da'wah* (calling to God) |
| Reason for flood | General wickedness and violence | Specifically *shirk* (polytheism) and rejection of the prophet |
| Duration of preaching | Not specified | 950 years (Quran 29:14) |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** addresses the "sons of God" passage by recording Isra'iliyyat traditions, some of which describe the "sons of God" as the descendants of Seth and the "daughters of men" as descendants of Cain. He presents multiple interpretations without endorsing the angel-human intermarriage reading, which is theologically untenable in Islam. Regarding God's regret, Al-Tabari follows standard Islamic hermeneutics: such language must be understood metaphorically or attributed to textual corruption, as God is not subject to emotional changes.

**Ibn Kathir** firmly rejects the angel-marriage interpretation and criticizes those who transmitted it uncritically. He focuses instead on the Quranic narrative, where the cause of the flood is explicitly *shirk* — the people's refusal to worship God alone despite Nuh's centuries of preaching. Ibn Kathir's account emphasizes Nuh as a model of patience (*sabr*) and perseverance in prophetic mission, noting that only a small number believed despite 950 years of effort.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides extensive detail about the corruption of Nuh's people, drawing on both Quranic and extra-Quranic sources. He describes the spread of idol worship beginning with the veneration of righteous predecessors, a tradition also found in Sahih al-Bukhari. The names Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr — mentioned in Quran 71:23 — were originally pious people whose images were later worshipped as idols.
