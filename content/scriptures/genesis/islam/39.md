## Yusuf and the Wife of al-'Aziz

Genesis 39 narrates Yusuf's service in the house of Potiphar, the attempted seduction by Potiphar's wife, Yusuf's refusal, and his subsequent imprisonment on false charges. This chapter has one of the most extensive and detailed Quranic parallels in all of Genesis. In Islamic tradition, the woman is known as **Zulaykha**, and her husband is called **al-'Aziz** (the mighty one) — a title, not a name. The episode is central to Yusuf's characterisation as a model of **'iffah** (chastity) and **sabr** (patience).

## Quranic Parallels

Genesis 39 corresponds to Quran 12:21-35. The Quranic account is significantly more detailed than Genesis in several respects:

| Genesis 39 | Quran 12 | Key Differences |
|------------|----------|----------------|
| Potiphar's wife attempts seduction (39:7) | 12:23: "She closed the doors and said, 'Come, you'" | Quran adds dramatic detail: bolted doors |
| Yusuf refuses (39:8-9) | 12:23-24: "He said, 'I seek refuge in Allah'... And she certainly determined [to seduce] him, and he would have inclined to her had he not seen the proof of his Lord" | Quran adds the "proof of his Lord" (*burhan*) — a divine intervention |
| She grabs his garment (39:12) | 12:25: "She tore his shirt from the back" | Quran specifies: torn from behind, proving he was fleeing |
| She accuses Yusuf (39:14-18) | 12:25-26: Similar accusation | Quran adds the witness/judge episode (see below) |
| Not in Genesis | 12:26-28: A witness testifies: "If his shirt is torn from the front, she has told the truth; if torn from the back, she has lied" | Entirely absent from Genesis — the "shirt test" |
| Not in Genesis | 12:30-32: The women of the city gossip; Zulaykha invites them to a banquet; they cut their hands upon seeing Yusuf's beauty | The famous "banquet scene" has no Genesis parallel |
| Yusuf imprisoned (39:20) | 12:33-35: Yusuf prays to Allah to prefer prison over sin; Allah "averted from him their plan" | Quran presents imprisonment as Yusuf's own choice, guided by Allah |

## The "Proof of His Lord" (*Burhan Rabbi-hi*)

The phrase **"had he not seen the proof of his Lord"** (12:24) has generated extensive commentary. What was this proof?

- **Ibn Abbas**: A vision of Ya'qub appearing to warn him
- **Al-Tabari**: Records multiple traditions — some say Ya'qub's image, others say a direct divine warning
- **Al-Razi**: Argues it was an internal spiritual conviction (*ma'rifah*) — Yusuf's knowledge of Allah's presence prevented him from sinning
- **Ibn Kathir**: Favours the view that it was Allah's protection (*ismah*) — the divine proof was the very quality of prophetic infallibility that kept Yusuf chaste

This detail is crucial because it transforms the narrative from mere human willpower to divine intervention — Yusuf was protected by Allah, not simply strong-willed.

## The Banquet Scene (Quran 12:30-32)

One of the most famous episodes in Islamic literature, entirely absent from Genesis: the women of the city gossip about Zulaykha's infatuation. She invites them to a banquet, gives each a knife, and brings Yusuf before them. **"When they saw him, they greatly admired him and cut their hands and said, 'Perfect is Allah! This is not a man; this is none but a noble angel!'"** (12:31).

Zulaykha then publicly admits her desire and declares that if Yusuf does not comply, he will be imprisoned (12:32). This scene:

1. Demonstrates Yusuf's extraordinary beauty — a divine gift
2. Shifts sympathy partially toward Zulaykha, showing her desire was humanly understandable
3. Shows Yusuf's response: he turns to Allah in prayer rather than to human reasoning

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** provides extensive commentary on this chapter, emphasising Yusuf's chastity as the defining quality of his prophetic character. He cites the hadith in which seven categories of people will be shaded by Allah's throne on the Day of Judgment, including "a man whom a beautiful woman of high status invited to sin, and he said, 'I fear Allah'" (Sahih al-Bukhari 1423) — widely understood as referring to Yusuf.

**Al-Qurtubi** discusses the legal implications: Yusuf's imprisonment on false charges raises questions about justice and the treatment of the falsely accused. He notes that Yusuf chose prison over sin — **"My Lord, prison is more beloved to me than that to which they invite me"** (12:33) — establishing a precedent that preserving one's faith takes priority over physical freedom.

**Al-Zamakhshari** in *Al-Kashshaf* analyses the literary artistry of the Quranic account, noting how the "shirt test," the banquet scene, and Yusuf's prayer create a morally and dramatically richer narrative than the Genesis version.
