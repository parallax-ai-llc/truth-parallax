## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Tower and Ibrahim's Lineage

Genesis 11 contains two sections: the Tower of Babel narrative (11:1-9) and the genealogy from Shem to Abram (11:10-32). Islamic tradition engages with both, though in distinctive ways. The Tower of Babel story does not appear explicitly in the Quran, but Islamic tradition contains parallel narratives about Nimrod (Namrud), a tyrannical king who challenged God and is often associated with the tower-building enterprise. The genealogy section transitions from the post-flood world to the era of Ibrahim — and this transition marks the beginning of what is arguably the most important prophetic narrative in the Quran after that of Muhammad himself.

Ibrahim is mentioned by name in the Quran approximately 69 times, more than any other prophet except Moses. His lineage, his call, and his struggles form the backbone of Quranic salvation history. Genesis 11's genealogy serves as the bridge to this central narrative.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 2:258** — "Have you not considered the one who argued with Abraham about his Lord because Allah had given him kingship? When Abraham said, 'My Lord is the one who gives life and causes death,' he said, 'I give life and cause death.'" This tyrant is traditionally identified as Nimrod (Namrud), associated with the Babel era.
- **Quran 21:68-69** — Nimrod orders Ibrahim thrown into a fire, but God commands: "O fire, be coolness and safety upon Abraham."
- **Quran 37:83-98** — Ibrahim's early life and mission among idol-worshippers, providing context for the transition in Genesis 11 from the tower episode to Ibrahim's call.
- **Quran 6:74** — "And when Abraham said to his father Azar, 'Do you take idols as deities?'" — identifying Ibrahim's father, corresponding to Terah in Genesis 11:26.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 11 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Tower of Babel | Humanity builds tower to reach heaven; God confuses languages | No direct Quranic parallel; Nimrod traditions serve a similar function |
| Confusion of languages | God's act to scatter humanity | Quran 30:22 sees linguistic diversity as a divine sign, not punishment |
| Nimrod | Not named in Genesis (later tradition) | Central antagonist figure in Islamic tradition; argued with Ibrahim |
| Terah/Azar | Terah, father of Abram (11:26) | Azar (Quran 6:74) — debate over whether Azar is the same as Terah |
| Abram's origin | Ur of the Chaldeans (11:31) | Various traditions; emphasis on the polytheistic environment |
| Sarai's barrenness | "Sarai was barren" (11:30) | Acknowledged but not emphasized until later narratives |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides extensive material on Nimrod, drawing on both Isra'iliyyat and Islamic traditions. In his account, Nimrod is the archetypal tyrannical king who claimed divinity — the ultimate expression of *shirk*. Al-Tabari connects Nimrod to the tower-building enterprise, recording traditions that describe Nimrod's attempt to ascend to heaven to wage war against God. This narrative serves the same theological function as Genesis 11's tower story: human arrogance against divine authority. Al-Tabari also reproduces the genealogy from Nuh to Ibrahim, largely following the biblical framework while noting the transition to the era of prophetic monotheism.

**Ibn Kathir** focuses on the theological significance of the Ibrahim narrative beginning in this chapter. He identifies Azar as Ibrahim's father (following Quran 6:74) but notes scholarly debate about whether Azar was Ibrahim's biological father or his uncle — a discussion arising from the need to reconcile the Quranic name with the biblical Terah. Ibn Kathir emphasizes that Ibrahim was raised in a deeply polytheistic environment, making his recognition of *tawhid* all the more remarkable and divinely guided.

**Al-Tha'labi** dedicates significant space to the Nimrod narrative in *Qisas al-Anbiya'*, portraying him as a foil to Ibrahim. The tower episode appears in his retelling as one of several expressions of Nimrod's hubris, alongside his claim to give life and death and his casting Ibrahim into the fire. Al-Tha'labi's narrative skillfully weaves the transition from the post-flood corruption (symbolized by Nimrod) to the renewal of monotheism through Ibrahim's prophetic mission.
