## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Post-Flood Covenant

Genesis 9 contains God's covenant with Noah — including permission to eat meat, the prohibition of blood, and the rainbow as a sign — followed by the troubling episode of Noah's drunkenness and the curse of Canaan. Islamic engagement with this chapter is selective and critical. The covenant elements find some resonance in Islamic law, but the narrative of Noah's drunkenness is firmly rejected as incompatible with the doctrine of prophetic infallibility (*'ismah*).

Islam holds that all prophets were protected from major sins and moral degradation. A prophet of God becoming drunk and lying naked is considered inconceivable in Islamic theology. This episode is treated as either a fabrication introduced through *tahrif* or a misunderstanding of the original text. The curse of Canaan — often historically misused to justify racial hierarchies — also finds no support in Islamic tradition.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 11:48** — "It was said, 'O Noah, disembark in security from Us and blessings upon you and upon nations [descending] from those with you. But other nations [of them] — We will grant them enjoyment; then there will touch them from Us a painful punishment.'" This post-flood address serves a function similar to the covenant in Genesis 9 but without the specific terms.
- **Quran 5:3** — The prohibition of blood and dead animals in Islamic dietary law echoes Genesis 9:4, though derived from Quranic revelation independently.
- **Quran 57:26** — "And We have already sent Noah and Abraham and placed in their descendants prophethood and scripture." This affirms Nuh's role as progenitor of future prophets.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 9 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Covenant sign (rainbow) | Rainbow as sign of God's promise | Not mentioned in the Quran; treated as Isra'iliyyat |
| Permission to eat meat | Granted after the flood (9:3) | Islam has its own dietary laws derived from the Quran |
| Blood prohibition | "You must not eat meat with blood" (9:4) | Paralleled in Islamic law (Quran 5:3) but independently revealed |
| Noah's drunkenness | Noah plants vineyard, gets drunk, lies naked (9:20-21) | Categorically rejected under *'ismah*; prophets do not commit such acts |
| Curse of Canaan | Ham sees nakedness; Canaan is cursed (9:22-25) | Rejected; no racial or genealogical curses in Islamic theology |
| "Be fruitful and multiply" | Divine command to repopulate (9:1) | Implied in Quran 11:48 but not as a specific command |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** records the post-flood narrative and mentions traditions about Nuh's descendants — Shem, Ham, and Japheth — and their settlements. He includes Isra'iliyyat material about the division of the earth among them. However, he does not include the drunkenness episode in his authoritative account. Where he encounters such traditions, they are clearly marked as coming from the People of the Book and are not endorsed. Al-Tabari's treatment reflects the standard Islamic hermeneutical approach: prophetic narratives from biblical sources are accepted only insofar as they do not contradict Islamic principles.

**Ibn Kathir** is explicit in his rejection of the drunkenness narrative. He argues that attributing such behavior to a prophet of God's stature — one who spent 950 years in patient da'wah — is an insult to prophethood and a mark of textual corruption. He notes that the Quran's final word on Nuh is one of honor: "Peace upon Noah among the worlds" (37:79). A prophet greeted with divine peace cannot be one who degraded himself through intoxication. Ibn Kathir also dismisses the curse of Canaan, noting that in Islam, no person bears the sin of another (Quran 6:164).

**Al-Tha'labi** focuses on the positive legacy of Nuh, narrating traditions about his righteousness, his long life, and his status as the "second Adam" — the ancestor of all post-flood humanity. He treats the covenant themes in Genesis 9 as broadly consistent with Islamic principles (sanctity of life, dietary laws) while firmly excluding the drunkenness episode from the prophetic biography.
