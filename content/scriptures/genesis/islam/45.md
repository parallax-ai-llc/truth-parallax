## Yusuf Reveals Himself to His Brothers

Genesis 45 records the climactic moment of the Yusuf narrative: he reveals his identity to his brothers, weeping aloud, forgives them, and sends them to bring Ya'qub to Egypt. This chapter corresponds to one of the most celebrated passages in the Quran — Surah Yusuf 12:89-93 — and represents the theological heart of the entire narrative: **forgiveness, divine providence, and the fulfilment of prophetic dreams**.

## Quranic Parallels

| Genesis 45 | Quran 12:89-93 | Key Differences |
|------------|----------------|----------------|
| Yusuf reveals himself: "I am Joseph!" (45:3) | 12:90: "They said, 'Are you indeed Yusuf?' He said, 'I am Yusuf, and this is my brother'" | Both accounts share the dramatic revelation |
| Brothers are terrified (45:3) | 12:89: "He said, 'Do you know what you did with Yusuf and his brother when you were ignorant?'" | Quran frames it as a question, not an accusation |
| Yusuf forgives: "Do not be distressed" (45:5) | 12:92: "He said, 'No blame upon you today. Allah will forgive you, and He is the most merciful of the merciful'" | Quran's forgiveness is more explicit and theologically framed |
| "God sent me before you to preserve life" (45:5-8) | 12:90: "Indeed, he who fears Allah and is patient — Allah does not allow to be lost the reward of those who do good" | Genesis emphasises providence; Quran emphasises taqwa and sabr |
| Yusuf sends his shirt to Ya'qub (not in Gen 45 explicitly) | 12:93: "Go with this shirt of mine and cast it over the face of my father; he will become seeing" | Miraculous element: the shirt restores Ya'qub's sight |
| Invitation to come to Egypt (45:9-13) | 12:93: "And bring me your family, all together" | Same invitation |

## "No Blame Upon You Today" (*La Tathrib 'Alaykum al-Yawm*)

Yusuf's words of forgiveness in **Quran 12:92** — **"No blame upon you today"** — are among the most celebrated in Islamic ethical tradition. The phrase carries multiple layers:

1. **Complete absolution**: Not partial forgiveness but total release from blame
2. **Present-tense mercy**: "Today" (*al-yawm*) marks a new beginning; the past is closed
3. **Divine model**: "Allah will forgive you" — Yusuf does not merely exercise his own prerogative but invokes Allah's mercy

The Prophet Muhammad repeated these exact words when he conquered Makkah and addressed the Quraysh who had persecuted him for two decades: **"Go, for you are free. No blame upon you today."** This parallel establishes Yusuf as a prophetic model for Muhammad himself.

## The Shirt That Restores Sight

The Quran adds a miraculous element absent from Genesis: Yusuf sends his shirt (*qamis*) to Ya'qub, instructing the brothers to cast it over their father's face, whereupon **"he will become seeing"** (12:93). This detail:

- Creates narrative symmetry: the shirt torn in Zulaykha's chamber (12:25) and the shirt bloodied by the brothers (12:18) is now the shirt of healing
- Confirms Ya'qub's blindness from weeping (12:84), itself a Quranic detail
- Demonstrates Yusuf's prophetic knowledge: he knows the shirt will heal

**Ibn Kathir** notes that this miracle is attributed to Allah's power, not to the shirt itself — preventing any suggestion of talismanic properties. The shirt is the means (*sabab*); Allah is the healer.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** identifies this chapter as the culmination of the sabr-reward paradigm: Yusuf endured the well, slavery, temptation, and prison with patience, and his reward is not only personal vindication but the ability to forgive those who wronged him. This is the highest expression of prophetic character.

**Al-Qurtubi** examines the ethics of forgiveness in Islamic law. He notes that Yusuf had the right (*haqq*) to seek retribution — he was wronged grievously. His choice to forgive illustrates that while justice permits retaliation, mercy (*rahma*) is superior. This is reinforced by **Quran 42:40**: "But whoever pardons and makes reconciliation — his reward is from Allah."

**Sayyid Qutb** reads the revelation scene as the climax of the Quran's "best of stories." He emphasises that every preceding trial was preparation for this moment — the moment when human forgiveness mirrors divine mercy. Qutb argues that this is the Quran's answer to the problem of suffering: suffering serves divine purpose, and those who endure with faith are ultimately vindicated.

**Al-Tabari** records Isra'iliyyat traditions that elaborate on the emotional scene — Yusuf's weeping, the brothers' shame, and their gradual recognition. He notes that the reconciliation fulfils Ya'qub's original hope, expressed decades earlier, that Allah would "bring them all back to me" (a sentiment found in 12:83).

## Islamic Moral Principles

This chapter embodies the Islamic virtues of **'afw** (pardon), **sabr** (patience), and **tawakkul** (trust in Allah's plan). Yusuf's forgiveness is not weakness but the ultimate expression of strength — the strength of one who has been vindicated by Allah and therefore has no need for human vengeance.
