## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Covenant of Circumcision

Genesis 17 introduces the covenant of circumcision — God establishes an "everlasting covenant" with Abraham, changes his name from Abram to Abraham, commands circumcision as the sign of the covenant, promises Isaac through Sarah, and reaffirms blessings for Ishmael. Islamic tradition engages extensively with this chapter, particularly regarding circumcision and the relative status of Isma'il and Ishaq.

Circumcision (*khitan*) in Islam is not a sign of a specific covenant with one people but a practice of *fitrah* — the natural disposition toward purity that God instilled in all humanity. It is traced to Ibrahim as a *sunnah* (prophetic practice) and is considered either obligatory (*wajib*) or strongly recommended (*sunnah mu'akkadah*) depending on the school of jurisprudence. The Quranic emphasis, however, falls not on the physical sign but on the inward submission: "the religion of your father, Ibrahim. It is He who named you 'Muslims'" (Quran 22:78).

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 2:124** — "And when Abraham was tried by his Lord with commands and he fulfilled them." Many scholars identify circumcision as one of the "commands" (*kalimat*) with which Ibrahim was tested.
- **Quran 2:128-129** — "Our Lord, and make us Muslims [in submission] to You and from our descendants a Muslim nation." Ibrahim's prayer encompasses both Isma'il's and Ishaq's lines.
- **Quran 37:112** — "And We gave him good tidings of Isaac, a prophet from among the righteous." The promise of Ishaq is confirmed.
- **Quran 21:72** — "And We gave him Isaac and Jacob in addition, and all [of them] We made righteous."
- **Quran 30:30** — "The *fitrah* of Allah upon which He has created [all] people." Circumcision is considered part of this natural disposition.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 17 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Circumcision as covenant sign | Sign of the covenant between God and Abraham's descendants | Practice of *fitrah*; not exclusive to one lineage |
| Name change | Abram to Abraham; Sarai to Sarah | Not emphasized in the Quran; Ibrahim is always "Ibrahim" |
| Ishmael's status | Blessed but not the covenant heir (17:20-21) | Equal prophet; ancestor of Muhammad; covenant includes his line |
| "I will establish my covenant with Isaac" (17:21) | Covenant specifically through Isaac's line | Rejected as exclusive; Quran's covenant is through righteousness, not lineage |
| Abraham falls on his face laughing (17:17) | Abraham laughs at the promise | Prophetic *'ismah* — interpreted cautiously; joy rather than disbelief |
| Ishmael circumcised at 13 (17:25) | Included in circumcision but not in the covenant | Isma'il's circumcision confirms his participation in Ibrahim's legacy |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** discusses circumcision within the context of Ibrahim's *kalimat* (commands) in Quran 2:124. He records traditions identifying these commands as including circumcision, along with other practices of cleanliness and worship. Al-Tabari presents the circumcision of both Isma'il and Ibrahim together as evidence that both lines are equally included in Ibrahim's religious legacy. He incorporates Isra'iliyyat material about the ages of circumcision but subordinates it to the Quranic framework of *fitrah*.

**Ibn Kathir** addresses the theological implications of Genesis 17:21 directly. He argues that the biblical text's restriction of the covenant to Isaac's line reflects *tahrif* — a deliberate alteration to exclude Isma'il. The Quran makes no such distinction; both sons are praised as prophets, and Ibrahim's prayer in Quran 2:128 explicitly asks for a "Muslim nation" from his descendants — a prayer fulfilled through both the Israelite prophets and the Prophet Muhammad. Ibn Kathir cites the hadith: "Ibrahim was circumcised when he was eighty years old, with an adze" (Sahih al-Bukhari), confirming the practice's prophetic origin.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides extensive narrative detail about the circumcision and its significance, connecting it to Ibrahim's complete submission to God's commands. He notes that each trial Ibrahim faced — fire, emigration, circumcision, the sacrifice — represented a deepening of his *islam* (submission). The circumcision of Isma'il at thirteen (as in Genesis 17:25) is recorded and becomes the basis for traditions about the appropriate age for circumcision in some Islamic legal discussions, though most scholars recommend it be performed in infancy.
