## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Flood

Genesis 7 describes the onset of the Great Flood — Noah enters the ark with his family and the animals, the rain falls for forty days and nights, and all life outside the ark perishes. The Quranic flood narrative overlaps substantially with this chapter but includes one dramatic episode entirely absent from Genesis: the drowning of Nuh's own son, who refuses to board the ark. This addition transforms the story from one of simple obedience and survival into a profound meditation on the limits of prophetic intercession and the sovereignty of God's judgment.

Islamic tradition also differs on the flood's scope. While Genesis presents a universal flood destroying all life on earth, many Muslim scholars hold that the flood was regional, limited to the territory of Nuh's people. This interpretation is based on Quranic phrasing that emphasizes the destruction of Nuh's *people* rather than all of humanity.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 11:40-43** — "Until when Our command came and the oven overflowed, We said, 'Load upon the ship of each [creature] two mates and your family, except those about whom the word has preceded, and [include] whoever has believed.' But none had believed with him, except a few." The oven (*tannur*) bursting is a distinctive Quranic detail.
- **Quran 11:42-43** — The drowning of Nuh's son: "And Noah called to his son who was apart [from them], 'O my son, come aboard with us and be not with the disbelievers.' [But] he said, 'I will take refuge on a mountain to protect me from the water.' [Noah] said, 'There is no protector today from the decree of Allah, except for whom He gives mercy.' And the waves came between them, and he was among the drowned."
- **Quran 11:44** — "And it was said, 'O earth, swallow your water, and O sky, withhold [your rain].' And the water subsided, and the matter was accomplished."
- **Quran 54:11-15** — "So We opened the gates of the heaven with rain pouring down and caused the earth to burst with springs, and the waters met for a matter already predestined. And We carried him on a [construction of] planks and nails."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 7 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Who boards the ark | Noah, his wife, three sons and their wives (8 people) | Nuh, believers (a few), pairs of animals; one son and possibly his wife excluded |
| Nuh's son | All sons board the ark | One son refuses and drowns (11:42-43) |
| Scope of flood | Universal, covering all earth | Debated; many scholars hold it was regional |
| Duration | 40 days of rain; 150 days of flooding | Duration not specified in the Quran |
| Clean/unclean animals | Seven pairs of clean, two of unclean | No clean/unclean distinction; pairs of each kind |
| The "tannur" | Not mentioned | The *tannur* (oven/furnace) overflows as a sign (11:40) |
| Nuh's wife | Saved with the family | Described as a disbeliever in Quran 66:10; her fate is debated |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides one of the most detailed accounts of the flood in Islamic literature. He records traditions about the ark's construction, its dimensions, and the loading of the animals, drawing on Isra'iliyyat sources. He discusses the *tannur* extensively — some traditions identify it as a specific oven in Kufa (Iraq), others as a metaphor for the beginning of the flood. Al-Tabari presents the drowning of Nuh's son as one of the most emotionally powerful episodes in prophetic history.

**Ibn Kathir** focuses on the theological lessons. The drowning of Nuh's son demonstrates that blood relationship to a prophet provides no spiritual protection — a principle reinforced by the Quran's discussion of Nuh's wife and Lut's wife as examples of disbelieving spouses of prophets (66:10). Ibn Kathir notes Quran 11:45-47, where Nuh appeals to God for his son, and God rebukes him: "He is not of your family; indeed, his work was unrighteous."

**Al-Tha'labi** elaborates on the emotional texture of the story, describing Nuh's anguish as he watches his son drown. He also discusses traditions about who else was on the ark and the conditions of the voyage. His account emphasizes God's mercy alongside His justice — the flood destroyed the wicked, but the ark preserved the seeds of future human civilization.
