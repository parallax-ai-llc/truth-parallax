## Yusuf's Dreams and His Brothers' Betrayal

Genesis 37 opens the Yusuf (Joseph) narrative — the most extensively paralleled biblical story in the Quran. Surah Yusuf (12) covers the entire arc from dream to reunion in 111 verses, and the Quran introduces it as **ahsan al-qasas** — "the best of stories" (12:3). This chapter records Yusuf's two dreams, his brothers' jealousy, their plot to kill him, his casting into a well, and his sale into slavery in Egypt (*Misr*).

## Quranic Parallels

Genesis 37 corresponds to Quran 12:4-20. The parallels are extensive and detailed:

| Genesis 37 | Quran 12 | Key Differences |
|------------|----------|----------------|
| Yusuf dreams of sheaves bowing (37:5-7) | Not mentioned separately | Quran combines into one dream |
| Yusuf dreams of sun, moon, and eleven stars bowing (37:9) | 12:4: "I saw eleven stars and the sun and the moon; I saw them prostrating to me" | Nearly identical imagery |
| Ya'qub favours Yusuf (37:3-4) | 12:8: "Yusuf and his brother [Binyamin] are more beloved to our father than we" | Brothers acknowledge the favouritism |
| Brothers plot to kill him (37:18-20) | 12:9-10: "Kill Yusuf or cast him out... Do not kill Yusuf but throw him into the bottom of the well" | Quran specifies one brother intervened to save his life |
| Cast into a pit (37:24) | 12:15: "They put him into the bottom of the well" | Same event; Quran adds divine reassurance (12:15) |
| Coat dipped in blood (37:31-32) | 12:18: "And they brought upon his shirt false blood" | Same deception; Quran adds Ya'qub's response (see below) |
| Sold to traders (37:28) | 12:19-20: "A traveller sent down his bucket... and they sold him for a paltry price" | Similar; Quran emphasises the low price |

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

### Ya'qub's Foreknowledge
In the Quran, Ya'qub immediately warns Yusuf: **"O my son, do not relate your vision to your brothers, lest they contrive a plan against you"** (12:5). This detail — absent from Genesis — demonstrates Ya'qub's prophetic discernment and explains his awareness of the brothers' jealousy.

### Divine Reassurance in the Well
The Quran adds a detail absent from Genesis: while in the well, Allah reassures Yusuf directly — **"And We inspired to him: You will surely inform them about this affair of theirs while they do not perceive"** (12:15). This transforms the pit from a moment of pure abandonment into one of divine communication.

### Ya'qub's Response to the Bloody Coat
In Genesis, Ya'qub mourns and "refuses to be comforted" (37:35). The Quran gives him a more perceptive response: **"Rather, your souls have enticed you to something. So patience is most fitting. And Allah is the one sought for help against what you describe"** (12:18). Ya'qub suspects deception — consistent with his prophetic insight — and turns to sabr and tawakkul.

### The "Coat of Many Colours"
Genesis's famous "coat of many colours" (37:3) has no equivalent in the Quran, where the shirt (*qamis*) appears only when bloodied. The ornate coat as a symbol of favouritism is a biblical detail not found in the Quranic account.

## Prophetic Infallibility and the Brothers

A critical distinction: in Islamic tradition, Yusuf is a **prophet** (*nabi*) and his brothers are not. Their jealousy and betrayal do not violate *ismah* because they are not prophets. The Quran presents them as ordinary men driven by envy — **"In Yusuf and his brothers there are signs for those who ask"** (12:7).

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** provides one of the most detailed treatments of the Yusuf narrative in Islamic literature, closely comparing the Quranic account with Isra'iliyyat traditions. On Genesis 37, he notes that Ya'qub's love for Yusuf was rooted in recognising his prophetic potential, not mere parental favouritism.

**Al-Tabari** draws extensively on both Quranic tafsir and Isra'iliyyat sources. He identifies the brother who saved Yusuf from death as Yahudha (Judah) in some traditions and Rubil (Reuben) in others — corresponding to the biblical Reuben (37:21-22).

**Al-Qurtubi** emphasises that the chapter teaches the danger of **hasad** (envy), which the Prophet Muhammad called one of the destructive sins (Sahih Muslim 2564).
