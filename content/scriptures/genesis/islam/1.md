## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Creation

Genesis 1 describes God creating the heavens and the earth in six days, culminating in the creation of humanity. The Islamic reading of this chapter is shaped profoundly by the Quran's own creation narratives, which affirm the six-day structure while diverging on critical theological points. Islam holds that the original Torah (*Tawrat*) contained authentic revelation, but that its present text has undergone *tahrif* (alteration). The Quranic account therefore serves as the corrective standard.

In Islamic theology, the creation narrative is fundamentally an expression of *tawhid* — God's absolute oneness and sovereignty. The plural "Let us make man" (Genesis 1:26) is categorically rejected as any hint of plurality in the divine nature. Muslim commentators interpret such phrasing either as a royal "we" (a feature also found in the Quran, where Allah uses the majestic plural) or as evidence of textual corruption.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran affirms that God created the heavens and the earth in six periods (*ayyam*):

- **Quran 7:54** — "Indeed, your Lord is Allah, who created the heavens and the earth in six days and then established Himself above the Throne."
- **Quran 41:9-12** — A detailed sequence: two days for the earth, four days for sustaining provisions, and two days for the heavens, often discussed in tafsir regarding whether these overlap or are sequential.
- **Quran 50:38** — "And We did certainly create the heavens and earth and what is between them in six days, and there touched Us no weariness." This directly counters the Genesis notion of God "resting" on the seventh day.
- **Quran 2:29** — "It is He who created for you all of that which is on the earth. Then He directed Himself to the heaven and made them seven heavens."

The creation of Adam is addressed separately in Quran 2:30-34, 15:26-29, and 38:71-85, where Adam is designated as *khalifah* (vicegerent) on earth — a role of honor, not merely the last item in a sequential list.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 1 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| "Let us make man" | Understood variously (Trinity, divine council) | Rejected as implying plurality; royal "we" or *tahrif* |
| God "resting" on the 7th day | Sabbath rest after creation | Explicitly denied — Quran 50:38 states no weariness touched God |
| "Image of God" (*imago Dei*) | Humanity made in God's image | Reinterpreted; some hadith reference "image" but scholars reject anthropomorphism |
| Order of creation | Light, sky, land, vegetation, stars, animals, humans | Quran gives a different arrangement in 41:9-12; earth provisions before heavens |
| Six "days" | Literal or figurative 24-hour days | *Ayyam* understood as periods or epochs; not necessarily solar days |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** (d. 923) in *Jami' al-Bayan* records multiple traditions about the sequence of creation, including Isra'iliyyat material from Ka'b al-Ahbar and Wahb ibn Munabbih. He presents the creation of the earth on Sunday and Monday, mountains and vegetation on Tuesday and Wednesday, and the heavens on Thursday and Friday. He narrates these without always adjudicating between them, preserving the diversity of early reports.

**Ibn Kathir** (d. 1373) in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* takes a more critical approach. He affirms the six-day creation but cautions against accepting Isra'iliyyat details that contradict the Quran. He emphasizes that the "days" of creation are cosmic periods, not earthly days, since the sun was not yet created. He firmly rejects the concept of divine rest, calling it incompatible with God's nature.

**Al-Tha'labi** (d. 1035) in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* provides an expansive retelling, drawing on biblical and rabbinic material. He is more permissive in including Isra'iliyyat but frames them within an Islamic theological context. His account emphasizes the angels' question about Adam's appointment as khalifah — a narrative found in Quran 2:30 — as the theological climax of the creation story.
