## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Birth of Ishaq

Genesis 21 describes the birth of Isaac, the expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael, God's rescue of Hagar in the wilderness, and Abraham's covenant with Abimelech at Beersheba. This chapter is of great significance for Islamic tradition because the expulsion of Hajar and Isma'il corresponds to one of the most sacred narratives in Islam — their journey to the valley of Mecca and the miraculous appearance of the well of Zamzam. What Genesis presents as an act of family conflict, Islam frames as a divinely orchestrated event that established the sacred geography of Islam.

The birth of Ishaq is acknowledged and celebrated in the Quran. Islamic tradition does not diminish Ishaq; he is honored as a prophet. But the Islamic reading contests the Genesis implication that Isma'il was expelled and marginalized while Isaac inherited the covenant. In Islamic perspective, both sons were blessed, and Isma'il's journey to Arabia was the fulfillment of a separate but equally important divine plan.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 37:112-113** — "And We gave him good tidings of Isaac, a prophet from among the righteous. And We blessed him and Isaac." Ishaq's birth as a divine gift to Ibrahim.
- **Quran 21:72** — "And We gave him Isaac and Jacob in addition, and all [of them] We made righteous."
- **Quran 14:37** — "Our Lord, I have settled some of my descendants in an uncultivated valley near Your sacred House." The Quranic parallel to the Hagar-Ishmael expulsion, reframed as Ibrahim's deliberate act of obedience to God.
- **Quran 2:158** — The *sa'y* ritual between Safa and Marwah during Hajj, commemorating Hajar's search for water.
- **Quran 11:71** — "And his wife was standing, and she smiled. Then We gave her good tidings of Isaac."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 21 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Sarah demands expulsion (21:10) | Sarah says "cast out this slave woman and her son" | The separation was God's command, not Sarah's jealousy; Quran 14:37 |
| Abraham distressed (21:11) | Abraham is troubled by the demand | Ibrahim was fulfilling a divine command; his trust in God was complete |
| Hagar wanders in Beersheba (21:14) | Desert of Beersheba near Canaan | The valley of Mecca (Bakkah) — a completely different geography |
| The well | God opens Hagar's eyes to see a well (21:19) | Zamzam — Jibril strikes the ground; the well still flows today |
| Ishmael's future | "I will make him into a great nation" (21:18) | Ancestor of the Prophet Muhammad; the "great nation" is the Muslim *ummah* |
| Isaac's superiority implied | Covenant continues through Isaac (21:12) | Both sons are prophets; no hierarchy in prophetic status |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides the definitive Islamic account of Hajar and Isma'il's journey to Mecca, primarily based on the long hadith narrated by Ibn Abbas in Sahih al-Bukhari. In this account, Ibrahim brings Hajar and Isma'il to the barren valley near the future site of the Ka'bah and leaves them with a leather bag of dates and a water skin. When Hajar asks if God commanded this, Ibrahim confirms, and she responds with exemplary faith: "Then He will not neglect us." When the water runs out, Hajar runs between Safa and Marwah seven times, searching for water or travelers. The angel Jibril then causes Zamzam to spring from the ground. Al-Tabari treats this as the founding narrative of Mecca.

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes the theological reframing. In Genesis 21, the expulsion is driven by Sarah's jealousy and reluctantly permitted by God. In Islamic tradition, the entire event is a divine command, and Ibrahim's obedience is an act of supreme faith — comparable in significance to the sacrifice of his son. Ibn Kathir connects the Zamzam well to the eventual settlement of the Jurhum tribe, Isma'il's marriage, and the building of the Ka'bah. He also notes that Ishaq's birth is a source of joy and divine blessing (11:71-72), not a replacement for Isma'il.

**Al-Tha'labi** weaves the two narratives — Ishaq's birth and Isma'il's journey — into a unified story of God's providence. Both sons receive divine gifts: Ishaq receives the covenant of prophethood through the Israelite line, while Isma'il receives the sacred trust of the Ka'bah and the ancestral honor of the final prophet. Al-Tha'labi's narrative is irenic, avoiding the competition between the two sons that characterizes some biblical and post-biblical traditions.
