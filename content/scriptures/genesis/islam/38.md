## Yahudha and Tamar

Genesis 38 interrupts the Yusuf narrative to tell the story of Yahudha (Judah) and his daughter-in-law Tamar. After Yahudha's sons Er and Onan die, he fails to give Tamar his youngest son Shelah as husband. Tamar then disguises herself as a prostitute, sleeps with Yahudha, and conceives twins — Perez and Zerah. This chapter is among the most rejected passages in Islamic engagement with Genesis.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not mention this episode in any form. There is no reference to Tamar, and Yahudha (Judah) is not named individually in the Quran — he appears only as one of Yusuf's brothers collectively.

The Quran's treatment of sexual morality provides the framework for Islamic rejection of this narrative:

- **Quran 17:32**: "And do not approach unlawful sexual intercourse (*zina*). Indeed, it is an immorality and an evil way."
- **Quran 24:2-3**: The Quran prescribes severe punishment for zina and emphasises the gravity of the offence.
- **Quran 25:68**: Among the qualities of the servants of the Most Merciful: "those who do not invoke with Allah another deity, nor kill the soul which Allah has forbidden except by right, nor commit unlawful sexual intercourse."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Yahudha sleeps with a disguised prostitute | He thinks Tamar is a shrine prostitute and sleeps with her (38:15-18) | Rejected entirely; a son of Ya'qub would not engage in zina |
| Onan's sin | Onan "spills his seed" to avoid giving offspring to his brother's line (38:9-10) | The sexual detail is considered inappropriate for scripture; the episode is ignored |
| Tamar's deception | She poses as a prostitute to secure offspring (38:14) | While her cause may be just (denied her right to Shelah), the means are impermissible |
| Yahudha's verdict | He declares Tamar "more righteous than I" (38:26) | A morally compromised man judging sexual morality is considered incongruent with prophetic family standards |
| Lineage significance | Perez is ancestor of David and, in Christian tradition, of Jesus | Islamic genealogy of 'Isa (Jesus) does not depend on this line; 'Isa is born miraculously without a father |

## Isra'iliyyat Evaluation: Categorical Rejection

This chapter represents one of the clearest cases of **rejected Isra'iliyyat** in Islamic scholarship. Multiple principles are violated:

1. **Prophetic family honour**: The sons of Ya'qub, while not prophets themselves, belong to a prophetic household. Attributing zina to Yahudha degrades the entire family.
2. **Sexual morality**: Zina is among the gravest sins in Islam. Narrating it without clear condemnation in a sacred text is viewed as evidence of *tahrif*.
3. **Narrative placement**: The chapter interrupts the Yusuf story — which the Quran presents as a coherent, morally instructive whole. Islamic scholars view the insertion as an editorial choice that disrupts the prophetic narrative.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** does not include this episode in his prophetic histories. His silence is deliberate and consistent with his methodology of excluding Isra'iliyyat that contradict Islamic moral principles.

**Al-Tabari** records some elements of the Yahudha-Tamar narrative from Isra'iliyyat transmitters but treats them with evident reservation. He does not endorse the narrative as historically reliable.

**Modern scholars** like **Muhammad Husayn Haykal** and **Sayyid Qutb** cite this chapter as evidence of textual corruption in the Torah. Qutb in *Fi Zilal al-Quran* argues that the purity of the Quranic Yusuf narrative — uninterrupted by such episodes — demonstrates the Quran's superiority as a preserved revelation.

The contrast between Genesis 38 and the Quran's handling of the same narrative period is striking. Where Genesis inserts a story of sexual transgression, the Quran continues directly from Yusuf's sale into slavery to his encounter with the wife of al-'Aziz (Quran 12:21-29) — and even in that episode of sexual temptation, Yusuf's chastity is the central message.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter serves, in Islamic reading, as a negative example — illustrating what happens when sacred texts are altered by human editors who introduce material inconsistent with prophetic standards. The Islamic emphasis on **'iffah** (chastity) and the protection of prophetic family honour makes this chapter incompatible with the Quranic worldview.
