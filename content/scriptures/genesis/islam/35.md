## Return to Bethel and Purification

Genesis 35 records Ya'qub's return to Bethel, his command that his household put away foreign gods, the death of Rahil during Binyamin's birth, Reuben's transgression with Bilhah, and the death of Ishaq. Islamic tradition finds significant themes here — particularly the purging of idols from a prophetic household and the affirmation of tawhid.

## Quranic Parallels

Several themes in Genesis 35 have Quranic resonance:

- **Purging idols**: Ya'qub commands his household to "put away the foreign gods that are among you" (35:2). This directly parallels Ibrahim's campaign against idolatry — **Quran 21:57-58**: "By Allah, I will surely plan against your idols." The prophetic imperative to establish tawhid in one's own household is confirmed in **Quran 66:6**: "Protect yourselves and your families from a Fire."
- **Rahil's death**: Though the Quran does not mention Rahil's death, Yusuf's separation from his mother is part of the background grief that permeates Surah Yusuf.
- **Binyamin's birth**: Binyamin plays a crucial role in the Yusuf narrative (Quran 12:59-82), and his birth is recorded here.
- **Ya'qub's twelve sons listed** (35:22-26): The twelve correspond to the "eleven stars" of Yusuf's dream (Quran 12:4), plus Yusuf himself.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Foreign gods in Ya'qub's household | Household members possess idols that Ya'qub buries (35:2-4) | The existence of idols is troubling but Ya'qub's forceful removal is viewed positively; confirms his prophetic role as enforcer of tawhid |
| Reuben sleeps with Bilhah | Reuben lies with his father's concubine (35:22) | Categorically rejected; such an act in a prophetic household is considered *tahrif* of the text |
| Name change to Israel | God renames Ya'qub to "Israel" (35:10) | Accepted as an alternative name; the wrestling etymology (ch. 32) is rejected but the name itself is Quranically confirmed (3:93) |
| Ishaq's death | Ishaq dies at 180 years, buried by both sons (35:28-29) | Accepted; the peaceful death and burial by both Ya'qub and Esau is unproblematic |
| God identifies as "El Shaddai" | God says "I am El Shaddai" (35:11) | The divine name is understood differently; Islam uses the 99 Names of Allah but does not recognise "El Shaddai" as a revealed name |

## The Purification Episode

Ya'qub's command to remove foreign gods (35:2-4) is one of the most positively received episodes in this section of Genesis from an Islamic perspective. It demonstrates:

1. **Prophetic authority**: Ya'qub exercises spiritual leadership over his household
2. **Tawhid enforcement**: The removal and burial of idols mirrors Ibrahim's iconoclasm
3. **Ritual purification**: The command to "purify yourselves and change your garments" (35:2) resonates with Islamic *taharah* (ritual purification) practices

This episode confirms Ya'qub's status as a true prophet in Islamic eyes — one who actively combated shirk (associating partners with Allah).

## Commentators' Views

**Al-Tabari** treats the Bethel return and purification positively, noting Ya'qub's authority in commanding the destruction of idols. He records Rahil's death and Binyamin's birth as important genealogical markers for the Yusuf narrative.

**Ibn Kathir** mentions the deaths of Rahil and Ishaq as part of the prophetic history. He notably omits the Reuben-Bilhah episode entirely, consistent with his filtering of morally problematic Isra'iliyyat. He emphasises Ya'qub's role as a righteous patriarch who maintained monotheism in his family.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides a more detailed account of Rahil's death, noting the pathos of Yusuf losing his mother in childhood — a detail that deepens the emotional resonance of his later story of separation and reunion.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter's central Islamic lesson concerns **tawhid in the household** — the prophetic responsibility to ensure that one's family is free from idolatry and committed to the worship of Allah alone. This duty is enshrined in Quran 20:132: "And enjoin prayer upon your family and be steadfast therein."
