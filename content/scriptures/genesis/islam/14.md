## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the War of the Kings

Genesis 14 describes the war of four kings against five, the capture of Lot, Abram's military rescue of Lot with 318 trained men, and the encounter with Melchizedek — the priest-king of Salem who blesses Abram and receives a tithe. This chapter has no direct Quranic parallel. The war, the military campaign, and the Melchizedek encounter are entirely absent from Islamic scripture. Islamic engagement with this material is therefore limited to Isra'iliyyat traditions preserved by historians and commentators.

The Melchizedek figure, of immense theological significance in Jewish and especially Christian tradition (Hebrews 7), holds virtually no place in Islamic theology. There is no Islamic doctrine of priestly kingship or pre-Levitical priesthood, and the concept of tithing to a priest-king has no parallel in Islamic practice, where *zakat* is given to the poor and needy rather than to a priestly intermediary.

## Handling of Isra'iliyyat Material

**Al-Tabari** includes elements of the Genesis 14 narrative in his universal history, recording the war of the kings and Ibrahim's rescue of Lut. His treatment is straightforward historical narration without significant theological commentary. The military episode is presented as evidence of Ibrahim's courage and loyalty to family — virtues consistent with Islamic portrayal of prophets. Al-Tabari does not give the Melchizedek encounter significant attention, as it has no Islamic theological framework to support it.

**Ibn Kathir** barely addresses Genesis 14's content. In *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah*, he mentions Ibrahim's concern for Lut but focuses on the Quranic relationship between the two prophets rather than the military expedition. The absence of this material from the Quran signals to Ibn Kathir that its details are not essential to understanding Ibrahim's prophetic mission. He applies his characteristic skepticism toward Isra'iliyyat narratives that add military or political dimensions to prophetic biographies without Quranic support.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides a somewhat fuller account in *Qisas al-Anbiya'*, including the rescue narrative as part of Ibrahim's biography. His interest is in the narrative continuity — demonstrating Ibrahim's protective care for Lut — rather than in the theological dimensions that Jewish and Christian interpreters draw from this chapter (Melchizedek typology, tithing, etc.).

## Islamic Principles Applied

While this chapter receives minimal Islamic attention, several principles guide how scholars approach it:

| Principle | Application to Genesis 14 |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| *'Ismah* (prophetic infallibility) | Ibrahim's military action is not seen as morally problematic; defending family is righteous |
| Isra'iliyyat framework | Material neither confirmed nor denied; narrated without endorsement |
| No priesthood in Islam | Melchizedek as priest-king has no Islamic theological parallel |
| *Zakat* vs. tithing | Ibrahim's tithe to Melchizedek is a foreign concept; *zakat* goes to the needy, not clergy |
| Prophets and power | Islam recognizes prophets who wielded political/military power (Dawud, Sulayman), so Ibrahim's military role is not inherently problematic |

## Broader Context

The absence of Genesis 14's content from the Quran is itself instructive. The Quran's presentation of Ibrahim is focused on his theological arguments against polytheism, his willingness to sacrifice his son, his building of the Ka'bah, and his role as the father of monotheistic faith. Military campaigns, political alliances, and priestly encounters are not part of the Quranic Ibrahim. This selective emphasis reflects the Quran's consistent focus on spiritual and moral lessons rather than historical or political narrative.
