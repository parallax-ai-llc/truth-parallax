## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Angelic Visit

Genesis 18 narrates three visitors coming to Abraham, the announcement that Sarah will bear a son, Sarah's laughter, and Abraham's intercession for Sodom. This chapter has one of the most direct and detailed Quranic parallels in the Ibrahim narrative. The Quran devotes significant attention to the angelic visit in multiple surahs, making it one of the best-attested shared narratives between Genesis and the Quran.

However, the Islamic version contains critical differences. In Genesis 18, one of the visitors appears to be God Himself ("the Lord appeared to Abraham," 18:1), and Abraham seemingly bargains with God over the fate of Sodom. In the Quran, the visitors are angels (*mala'ikah*), and there is no appearance of God in human form — such anthropomorphism is incompatible with *tawhid*. Ibrahim's concern for Lut's people reflects his compassion, but the Quran makes clear that God's decree cannot be changed through negotiation.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 11:69-76** — "And certainly did Our messengers come to Abraham with good tidings; they said, 'Peace.' He said, 'Peace,' and did not delay in bringing [them] a roasted calf. But when he saw their hands not reaching for it, he distrusted them and felt from them apprehension. They said, 'Fear not. We have been sent to the people of Lot.' And his wife was standing, and she smiled. Then We gave her good tidings of Isaac, and after Isaac, Jacob."
- **Quran 15:51-60** — "And inform them about the guests of Abraham. When they entered upon him and said, 'Peace.' [Abraham] said, 'Indeed, we are fearful of you.' They said, 'Fear not. Indeed, we give you good tidings of a learned boy.'"
- **Quran 51:24-34** — "Has there reached you the story of the honored guests of Abraham? When they entered upon him and said, 'Peace.' He said, 'Peace, [you are] a people unknown.' Then he went to his family and came with a fattened calf and placed it near them; he said, 'Will you not eat?'"
- **Quran 29:31-32** — "And when Our messengers came to Abraham with the good tidings, they said, 'Indeed, we will destroy the people of that city.'"

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 18 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Identity of visitors | "The Lord" and two angels (18:1-2) | Angels only; God does not appear in human form |
| Number of visitors | Three men | Not specified in Quran; traditions say three or more angels |
| The meal | Abraham prepares calf; visitors eat | Ibrahim prepares calf; visitors do NOT eat — this causes his fear (11:70) |
| Sarah laughs (18:12) | Sarah laughs in disbelief | She "smiled" (*dahikat*, 11:71) — interpreted as joy, not disbelief |
| Bargaining over Sodom | Abraham negotiates from 50 down to 10 righteous | No bargaining; Ibrahim argues (*yujadiluna*) for Lut's people but is told the decree is final (11:74-76) |
| God's appearance | Theophany — God appears as a man | Impossible in Islam; *tawhid* forbids God taking human form |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides a detailed account of the angelic visit, noting the key Quranic detail that the angels did not eat the food Ibrahim prepared. In Arab culture, a guest who refuses food may be an enemy, which explains Ibrahim's fear. Al-Tabari records traditions identifying the angels as Jibril (Gabriel), Mika'il (Michael), and Israfil. He addresses Ibrahim's advocacy for Lut's people, interpreting Quran 11:74-76 — "Indeed, Abraham was forbearing, grieving and [frequently] returning [to Allah]. [The angels said], 'O Abraham, give up this [plea]. Indeed, the command of your Lord has come, and indeed, there will reach them a punishment that cannot be repelled.'"

**Ibn Kathir** focuses on the theological implications. He stresses that the visitors were exclusively angels — not God in any form. The Genesis presentation of a divine visitor is attributed to *tahrif*. Ibn Kathir also defends Sarah's response as joyful rather than skeptical. The Quran uses *dahikat* (she smiled/laughed), and Ibn Kathir interprets this as an expression of happiness upon hearing the destruction of the wicked people of Lut, or upon receiving the good news of Ishaq. He rejects any reading that portrays Sarah as doubting God's power.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides rich narrative detail about Ibrahim's hospitality, describing his rushing to prepare the finest calf for his guests — a model of *ikram al-dayf* (honoring the guest), one of Ibrahim's most celebrated virtues in Islamic tradition. The angels' refusal to eat and Ibrahim's resulting anxiety are described with emotional depth. Al-Tha'labi uses this episode to demonstrate that even prophets experience human fears, but their faith ultimately prevails.
