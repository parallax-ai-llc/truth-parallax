## The Incident at Shechem

Genesis 34 recounts the violation of Dinah, daughter of Ya'qub, by Shechem son of Hamor, and the violent retribution carried out by her brothers Simeon and Levi, who slaughter the men of Shechem after deceiving them into circumcision. This chapter has no Quranic parallel and is treated as Isra'iliyyat. Islamic engagement focuses on questions of justice, proportionality, and the conduct expected of a prophetic family.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not mention Dinah or the Shechem incident. However, relevant principles include:

- **Quran 5:8**: "O you who believe, be persistently standing firm for Allah as witnesses in justice, and do not let the hatred of a people prevent you from being just. Be just; that is nearer to righteousness."
- **Quran 16:126**: "And if you punish, punish with an equivalent of that with which you were harmed. But if you are patient — it is better for those who are patient."
- **Quran 42:40-42**: "The retribution for an evil act is an evil one like it, but whoever pardons and makes reconciliation — his reward is from Allah... The cause is only against the ones who wrong the people and tyrannise upon the earth without right."

These verses establish the Islamic framework for understanding collective punishment and proportionality — principles directly relevant to evaluating Simeon and Levi's actions.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Dinah's violation | Shechem assaults Dinah (34:2) | The crime itself is not disputed; sexual violence is condemned in Islamic law |
| Deceptive use of circumcision | Simeon and Levi propose circumcision as a peace condition, then attack (34:13-29) | Using a religious covenant as a weapon of deception is morally reprehensible; violates the Quranic command to fulfill covenants (5:1) |
| Collective punishment | All males of Shechem are killed for one man's crime (34:25-26) | Contradicts Islamic legal principles: *"No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another"* (Quran 6:164) |
| Ya'qub's response | Ya'qub rebukes Simeon and Levi for practical reasons — endangering the family (34:30) | Islamic reading would expect a stronger moral rebuke; the response seems inadequate for a prophet |

## Isra'iliyyat Evaluation

This chapter falls into the category of **problematic Isra'iliyyat** for several reasons:

1. **Collective punishment**: The massacre of an entire city for one man's crime violates the Quranic principle of individual responsibility.
2. **Deceptive covenant**: Using circumcision — a religious rite — as a tactical weapon violates the sanctity of covenants.
3. **Prophetic passivity**: Ya'qub's apparent inability to prevent or adequately condemn the massacre raises questions about his authority and moral leadership.

**Ibn Kathir** omits this episode entirely from his *Qisas al-Anbiya'*. **Al-Tabari** includes it but without endorsement, as part of his comprehensive historical method.

## Commentators' Views

**Al-Tabari** records the Shechem narrative from Isra'iliyyat transmitters as historical background. He does not offer extensive theological commentary but presents it within the broader context of Ya'qub's family history.

**Al-Tha'labi** in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* provides a more detailed account, treating the episode as an example of how human passion can lead to excess, even in prophetic families. He distinguishes between Ya'qub's prophetic character and his sons' pre-prophetic conduct.

Modern Islamic scholars tend to use this chapter as evidence of **tahrif** — arguing that the original Torah would not have attributed such conduct to the sons of a prophet without clear divine condemnation. The narrative's moral ambiguity (Ya'qub rebukes for pragmatic rather than ethical reasons) is seen as reflecting editorial perspective rather than prophetic teaching.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter raises enduring questions about **justice and proportionality** in Islam. While defending family honour is valued, the means must be just. The Quran's emphasis on proportional response (16:126) and individual responsibility (6:164) provides the ethical framework that Islamic readers bring to this difficult narrative.
