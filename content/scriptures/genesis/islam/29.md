## Ya'qub's Marriage to Leah and Rahil

Genesis 29 narrates Ya'qub's arrival in Haran, his meeting with Rahil (Rachel) at the well, his seven years of service to Laban for her hand, the substitution of Leah on the wedding night, and a further seven years of labour for Rahil. This chapter has no direct Quranic parallel and is treated entirely as Isra'iliyyat material in Islamic scholarship.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not mention Rahil, Leah, or Laban by name. Ya'qub's wives are referenced only indirectly through their children — particularly Yusuf, whose mother is understood in Islamic tradition to be Rahil.

- **Quran 12:6**: Allah addresses Yusuf: "And thus your Lord will choose you and teach you the interpretation of narratives and complete His favour upon you and upon the family of Ya'qub." The "family of Ya'qub" encompasses the lineage established through these marriages.
- **Quran 12:68**: Ya'qub is described as possessing *ilm* (knowledge) — "he was a possessor of knowledge because of what We had taught him."

The well scene — Ya'qub meeting Rahil at a well and rolling away the stone — has thematic parallels with Musa's (Moses') encounter at the well in Midian (Quran 28:23-26), where he also meets his future wife. This well-encounter motif runs through prophetic narratives in both biblical and Quranic literature.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Laban's deception | Laban substitutes Leah for Rahil on the wedding night (29:23-25) | Problematic: a prophet being deceived challenges *ismah*; some scholars view it as part of divine testing |
| Polygamy | Ya'qub marries both sisters | Polygamy itself is not objectionable in Islamic law; marrying two sisters simultaneously was later prohibited in Sharia (Quran 4:23) but pre-Islamic prophets followed earlier dispensations |
| Service for marriage | Ya'qub works 14 years for his wives | Not objectionable; *mahr* (bridal gift) can take various forms, and the Quran records Musa serving a similar term (Quran 28:27) |
| "Hated" wife | God sees that Leah is "hated" (29:31) | The term is understood as "less preferred" rather than actual hatred; a prophet would not hate a wife |

## Isra'iliyyat Evaluation

This chapter falls into the category of **neutral Isra'iliyyat** — material that neither contradicts nor confirms the Quran. The basic narrative of Ya'qub's marriages is accepted as historical background for the twelve tribes of Israel (*Banu Isra'il*), whose existence is confirmed in the Quran.

The substitution of Leah raises *ismah* concerns but is generally treated not as Ya'qub's moral failure but as Laban's deception, which the prophet bore with patience. **Al-Tabari** records this narrative from Wahb ibn Munabbih and presents Ya'qub's response as a model of **sabr** (patience) — enduring injustice without abandoning trust in Allah.

## Commentators' Views

**Al-Tabari** provides a detailed account of Ya'qub's time with Laban, drawn heavily from Isra'iliyyat sources. He treats the narrative as legitimate historical background, noting that Ya'qub's patience and perseverance reflect prophetic character.

**Ibn Kathir** briefly mentions Ya'qub's marriages in the context of establishing the genealogy of Banu Isra'il, without dwelling on the deception narrative. His focus remains on Ya'qub's status as a prophet and the father of the twelve tribes.

**Al-Tha'labi** gives the most elaborate treatment, including details about Ya'qub's love for Rahil and his grief at being deceived. He frames the episode as a divine test that ultimately served Allah's plan for the prophetic lineage.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter illustrates **sabr** in the face of injustice and **tawakkul** in trusting Allah's plan even when human schemes cause suffering. Ya'qub's willingness to serve an additional seven years demonstrates the prophetic qualities of perseverance and commitment to honourable conduct.
