## Ya'qub Blesses Yusuf's Sons

Genesis 48 records Ya'qub's adoption and blessing of Yusuf's two sons, Ephraim and Manasseh, elevating them to the status of his own sons and giving the younger Ephraim precedence over the elder Manasseh. This chapter has no Quranic parallel and is treated as Isra'iliyyat in Islamic scholarship.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not mention Ephraim or Manasseh by name, nor does it record the blessing episode. However, the broader theme of Ya'qub's deathbed instructions is addressed:

- **Quran 2:132-133**: "And Ibrahim instructed his sons and Ya'qub: 'O my sons, indeed Allah has chosen for you this religion, so do not die except while you are Muslims.' Or were you witnesses when death approached Ya'qub, when he said to his sons, 'What will you worship after me?' They said, 'We will worship your God and the God of your fathers — Ibrahim, Isma'il, and Ishaq — one God. And we are Muslims submitting to Him.'"

This Quranic passage presents Ya'qub's deathbed concern as purely theological — ensuring his descendants maintain **tawhid**. There is no mention of tribal blessings, material inheritance, or the preferment of one grandson over another.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Adoption of grandsons | Ya'qub adopts Ephraim and Manasseh as his own (48:5) | Islamic law does not recognise full adoption (*tabanni*) in the same way; the pre-Islamic practice was abrogated (Quran 33:4-5) |
| Crossing hands to bless | Ya'qub crosses his hands to place the right on Ephraim (48:13-14) | No Islamic parallel; the significance of the right hand exists in Islamic ritual but not in this context |
| Younger over elder | Ya'qub deliberately favours the younger (48:17-19) | The pattern of the younger being chosen over the elder (Ishaq/Isma'il debate, Ya'qub/Esau) is a biblical theme; Islamic tradition reverses it for Isma'il |
| Tribal territory promises | Ya'qub assigns territory to Yusuf's sons (48:21-22) | Land promises specific to Israelite settlement in Canaan are not part of Islamic theology |
| "The Angel who has redeemed me" | Ya'qub invokes an angel of redemption (48:16) | The concept of angelic redemption has no Islamic parallel; redemption comes from Allah alone |

## Isra'iliyyat Evaluation

This chapter falls into **neutral Isra'iliyyat** territory. The basic narrative — an elderly patriarch blessing his grandchildren — is not theologically objectionable. However, several elements require Islamic qualification:

1. **The adoption language** (48:5) conflicts with Islamic family law, which maintains biological lineage distinctions (Quran 33:4-5)
2. **The angelic redeemer** (48:16) introduces a mediating figure between God and humanity that Islamic theology rejects
3. **The tribal-territorial dimension** is specific to Israelite history and has no bearing on Islamic salvation history

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** does not engage with this chapter, as it falls entirely outside the Quranic narrative. His treatment of Ya'qub's final days focuses exclusively on the Quranic deathbed scene (2:132-133), which addresses theological legacy rather than tribal blessing.

**Al-Tabari** includes some material about Yusuf's sons from Isra'iliyyat transmitters, treating it as genealogical background for understanding the development of Banu Isra'il. He does not offer theological commentary on the blessing episode.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides a fuller account in *Qisas al-Anbiya'*, noting the reversal of the elder-younger dynamic as part of a pattern in prophetic history. He does not, however, attach the same theological significance to it that Jewish and Christian traditions do.

The Islamic reading of Ya'qub's final days consistently redirects attention from tribal blessings to the tawhid-focused deathbed scene of Quran 2:133 — the question is not "which tribe will be greatest?" but "what will you worship after me?"

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter, read Islamically, reinforces the principle that **spiritual inheritance matters more than material inheritance**. Ya'qub's true legacy is not tribal territory or political primacy but the religion of Ibrahim — Islam (*submission to Allah*). This reframing is characteristic of the Quran's approach to patriarchal narratives: stripping away ethnic-national concerns and centring pure monotheism.
