## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Destruction of the People of Lut

Genesis 19 describes the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah, the rescue of Lot and his daughters, Lot's wife looking back and becoming a pillar of salt, and the deeply troubling episode of Lot's daughters making their father drunk and committing incest. The Quran addresses the destruction of Lut's people extensively — it is one of the most frequently referenced divine punishments in the Quran, appearing in over a dozen surahs. However, the Islamic version differs sharply from Genesis on key points, most critically in its absolute rejection of the incest episode, which is considered a grave insult to a prophet of God.

In the Quran, Lut is a prophet sent to call his people away from their unprecedented sin (*fahishah*). His prophetic mission, their rejection, and their destruction serve as a paradigmatic warning against moral corruption and the rejection of God's messengers.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 7:80-84** — "And [We sent] Lot when he said to his people, 'Do you commit such immorality as no one has preceded you with from among the worlds? Indeed, you approach men with desire, instead of women. Rather, you are a transgressing people.' ... And We rained upon them a rain [of stones]. Then see how was the end of the criminals."
- **Quran 11:77-83** — The angels arrive at Lut's house, the townspeople come demanding the guests, Lut's distress, the angels reveal their identity: "They said, 'O Lot, indeed we are messengers of your Lord; they will never reach you.'"
- **Quran 15:61-77** — Lut's interaction with the angels and the destruction.
- **Quran 26:160-175** — Lut's preaching to his people and their destruction.
- **Quran 54:33-39** — "The people of Lot denied the warning. Indeed, We sent upon them a storm of stones."
- **Quran 66:10** — Lut's wife is cited as an example of disbelief alongside Nuh's wife: "Allah presents an example of those who disbelieved: the wife of Noah and the wife of Lot."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 19 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Lot offers his daughters (19:8) | Lot offers his daughters to the mob | Quran 11:78 — "these are my daughters" interpreted by scholars as meaning the women of his nation (marriage as an alternative), not offering daughters to a mob |
| Lot's wife | Turns to a pillar of salt (19:26) | Lut's wife was a disbeliever who stayed behind and perished (66:10); the "pillar of salt" detail is Isra'iliyyat |
| Incest with daughters (19:30-38) | Lot's daughters get him drunk and conceive | Categorically rejected; absolutely incompatible with *'ismah*. Considered *tahrif* of the worst kind |
| Moab and Ammon | Nations descended from the incest | No Islamic recognition of this origin story |
| Mechanism of destruction | Fire and brimstone from heaven | Stones of baked clay (*hijarat min sijjil*) — Quran 11:82, 15:74 |
| Lut's character | Righteous but passive | An active prophet who preached for years; his failure to convert his people is not his fault |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** provides one of the most detailed Islamic accounts of Sodom's destruction. He records traditions about the angels' visit, the townspeople's attempt to assault the guests, and Lut's anguish. Al-Tabari notes that the Quran has Lut say "these are my daughters" (11:78) and interprets this as an offer of lawful marriage — not a sexual offer to a violent mob. The destruction is described vividly: the angels lifted the towns into the sky and overturned them, then rained upon them stones of marked clay. Crucially, Al-Tabari does not include the incest narrative in his account of Lut. The episode is entirely absent from his prophetic biography.

**Ibn Kathir** is emphatic in rejecting the incest story. He calls it one of the clearest examples of *tahrif* in the biblical text — an accusation against a prophet that no Muslim should accept or transmit. He notes that the Quran describes Lut as "of the righteous" (Quran 21:75) and a prophet who "was given judgment and knowledge" (21:74). Such a figure could not commit incest, even unknowingly. Ibn Kathir also addresses Lut's wife, noting that her disbelief demonstrates the Islamic principle that family ties to a prophet do not guarantee salvation — each person is judged by their own faith and deeds.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides the most dramatic retelling, emphasizing the contrast between Lut's perseverance and his people's depravity. He describes the destruction with literary vividness, drawing on both Quranic imagery and supplementary traditions. The story serves in his work as one of the most powerful warnings (*'ibrah*) in prophetic history — a reminder that no community is safe from God's punishment if it persists in rejecting His messengers and transgressing His laws.
