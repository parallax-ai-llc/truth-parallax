## Ya'qub's Vision at Bethel

Genesis 28 records Ya'qub's departure from his family, his famous dream of a ladder reaching to heaven with angels ascending and descending, and Allah's renewal of the Abrahamic covenant with him. Islamic tradition engages cautiously with this chapter: the vision itself has some resonance with Islamic angelology, but the theological framing — particularly the idea of God "standing above" the ladder and binding Himself to a specific place — raises concerns about anthropomorphism.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not narrate Ya'qub's dream or his journey to Haran. However, several themes find indirect parallels:

- **Angels ascending and descending**: Quran 70:4 speaks of angels ascending to Allah "in a Day the measure of which is fifty thousand years." The concept of angels as intermediaries between heaven and earth is well established in Islamic cosmology.
- **Quran 97:4**: On the Night of Decree (*Laylat al-Qadr*), "The angels and the Spirit descend therein by permission of their Lord for every matter."
- **Divine covenant renewal**: Quran 2:132-133 confirms that the covenant of monotheism passed through Ibrahim, Isma'il, Ishaq, and Ya'qub.

The dream of the ladder (*sullam*) has no specific Quranic equivalent, though the imagery of vertical connection between heaven and earth appears in Islamic mystical literature (*mi'raj* traditions).

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| The ladder vision | God stands "above" the ladder and speaks (28:13) | Anthropomorphic language is rejected; Allah is not spatially located |
| Bethel as holy place | Ya'qub consecrates the site: "This is the house of God" (28:17) | Islam does not recognise Bethel as a sacred site; the sacred houses are in Makkah and Madinah |
| Anointing a stone pillar | Ya'qub sets up and anoints a stone (28:18) | Potentially idolatrous in Islamic reading; only the Black Stone (*al-Hajar al-Aswad*) holds ritual significance |
| Tithing vow | Ya'qub promises a tenth to God (28:22) | No parallel; *zakat* obligations are later Sharia prescriptions |

## Theological Concerns

### Anthropomorphism (*Tashbih*)

The statement that God "stood above" the ladder (28:13) directly conflicts with the Islamic principle of **tanzih** — divine transcendence beyond spatial location. **Al-Ash'ari** and subsequent theologians insist that Allah cannot be described as occupying a place or having physical position relative to created things. This verse is therefore considered an instance of *tahrif* or at minimum a metaphorical expression that has been misread literally.

### Sacred Geography

Ya'qub's declaration that Bethel is "the gate of heaven" (28:17) conflicts with Islamic sacred geography, which centres on the **Kaaba** in Makkah as the original house of worship built by Ibrahim and Isma'il (Quran 3:96). Islamic tradition does acknowledge **al-Masjid al-Aqsa** in Jerusalem, but Bethel holds no sacred status.

## Commentators' Views

**Al-Tabari** records the Bethel vision from Isra'iliyyat sources, presenting it as part of the patriarchal history without strong theological endorsement. He focuses on Ya'qub's journey and the continuation of the prophetic line.

**Ibn Kathir** mentions Ya'qub's departure but does not elaborate on the ladder vision, consistent with his general avoidance of Isra'iliyyat that lack Quranic support.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides a fuller narrative in *Qisas al-Anbiya'*, noting the dream tradition while framing it within the broader context of prophetic experiences (*ru'ya*) — true dreams being one-forty-sixth of prophethood according to hadith (Sahih al-Bukhari 6989).
