## Ya'qub's Wrestling and the Name "Israel"

Genesis 32 contains one of the most theologically charged episodes in the entire Bible: Ya'qub wrestles with a mysterious figure through the night, receives a hip injury, and is renamed "Israel" — traditionally interpreted as "one who strives with God." This chapter presents extraordinary difficulties for Islamic interpretation, as the concept of a human physically wrestling with God or an angel and prevailing fundamentally conflicts with Islamic theology.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran does not narrate the wrestling episode. The name "Israel" (*Isra'il*) appears in the Quran as a designation for Ya'qub:

- **Quran 3:93**: "All food was lawful to the Children of Israel except what Israel [Ya'qub] had made unlawful to himself before the Torah was revealed."
- **Quran 19:58**: The prophets are described as "from the descendants of Adam and from those We carried with Nuh, and from the descendants of Ibrahim and Israel."

The Quran uses the name "Israel" without explaining its etymology or connecting it to a wrestling episode. Islamic tradition generally treats "Israel" as meaning "servant of God" (*abd Allah*) rather than "one who strives with God."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Biblical Account | Islamic Perspective |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Wrestling with God/angel | Ya'qub wrestles a mysterious figure all night (32:24-25) | Rejected outright; no creature can physically contend with Allah, and overcoming an angel contradicts angelic nature |
| "You have striven with God" | The figure says Ya'qub has "striven with God and with men and prevailed" (32:28) | Contradicts **tanzih** (divine transcendence); God is beyond physical interaction |
| Hip injury | Ya'qub's hip is dislocated (32:25) | A prophet being permanently injured by a divine being is theologically incoherent in Islamic terms |
| Name change to Israel | Name given as result of the wrestling (32:28) | "Isra'il" is accepted as Ya'qub's alternative name but its etymology is understood differently |
| Face of God (*Peniel*) | Ya'qub names the place "Peniel" — "I have seen God face to face" (32:30) | Directly contradicts **Quran 6:103**: "Vision perceives Him not, but He perceives all vision" |

## Theological Rejection

This chapter represents perhaps the clearest case where Islamic theology mandates rejection of a biblical narrative. Multiple core principles are at stake:

### Tanzih (Divine Transcendence)
Allah is beyond physical form and spatial presence. **Quran 42:11**: "There is nothing like unto Him." The idea of God engaging in physical combat with a human is categorically impossible.

### Ismah (Prophetic Protection)
A prophet being attacked and injured — whether by God or an angel — contradicts the Islamic understanding of prophets as divinely protected.

### Angelic Nature
Angels in Islamic theology are beings of light (*nur*) who obey Allah absolutely (Quran 66:6). An angel attacking a prophet or being overpowered by a human is inconsistent with their nature.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** does not include the wrestling narrative in his prophetic histories. He mentions Ya'qub's name "Israel" without the wrestling etymology, treating it simply as an alternative proper name.

**Al-Tabari** records versions of the story from Isra'iliyyat transmitters but with evident discomfort. Some of his sources identify the figure as an angel rather than God, which mitigates but does not resolve the theological problems.

**Al-Razi** in *Mafatih al-Ghayb* addresses anthropomorphic passages systematically, arguing that any text attributing physical form or spatial limitation to Allah reflects corruption of the original revelation.

Modern scholars like **Muhammad Abduh** and **Rashid Rida** cite this episode as evidence of *tahrif* in the biblical text, noting that such anthropomorphism is precisely what the Quran corrects.

## Islamic Moral Reading

Setting aside the wrestling episode, the chapter's broader theme of Ya'qub's fear before meeting Esau and his turning to prayer (32:9-12) does resonate with Islamic values. A prophet's response to danger is **du'a** (supplication) and trust in Allah — the model followed by Muhammad before Badr and in other moments of trial.
