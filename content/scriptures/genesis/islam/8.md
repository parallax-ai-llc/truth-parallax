## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Waters Receding

Genesis 8 describes the subsiding of the flood waters, the ark resting on the mountains of Ararat, Noah sending out birds to test for dry land, and his offering of sacrifice upon disembarking. The Quranic treatment of this phase is brief but contains a significant geographical divergence: the Quran states the ark came to rest on al-Judi, not Ararat. This difference, along with the Islamic reinterpretation of the sacrifice narrative, marks the key points of divergence between the traditions.

The Quran's account of the flood's end is characterized by its literary power rather than narrative detail. The famous verse "O earth, swallow your water, and O sky, withhold your rain" (11:44) is considered one of the most rhetorically magnificent verses in the Quran, praised by Arabic literary critics as a supreme example of *i'jaz* (inimitability).

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 11:44** — "And it was said, 'O earth, swallow your water, and O sky, withhold [your rain].' And the water subsided, and the matter was accomplished, and the ship came to rest on al-Judi. And it was said, 'Away with the wrongdoing people.'"
- **Quran 11:48** — "It was said, 'O Noah, disembark in security from Us and blessings upon you and upon nations [descending] from those with you.'"
- **Quran 23:28-29** — "And when you have boarded the ship, you and those with you, then say, 'Praise to Allah who has saved us from the wrongdoing people.' And say, 'My Lord, let me land at a blessed landing place, and You are the best to accommodate [us].'"

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 8 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Landing site | Mountains of Ararat (8:4) | Al-Judi (11:44) — identified variously as a mountain in southeastern Turkey or northern Iraq |
| Birds sent out | Raven, then dove with olive branch | Not mentioned in the Quran |
| Noah's sacrifice | Burnt offering; God smells "pleasing aroma" (8:21) | Not in the Quran; the anthropomorphic "smelling" is rejected |
| God's response | "Never again will I curse the ground" (8:21) | Quran focuses on blessings and security upon disembarking (11:48) |
| Seasons restored | "While the earth remains, seedtime and harvest..." (8:22) | Not addressed in Quranic parallel |
| Disembarkation prayer | Not present | "Praise to Allah who has saved us" (23:28) — gratitude emphasized |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** devotes attention to identifying al-Judi, recording traditions that place it near Mosul in Iraq or in the Cizre region of southeastern Turkey. He notes that earlier Muslim scholars debated the exact location but agreed it was distinct from the biblical Ararat. Al-Tabari also includes Isra'iliyyat material about the raven and dove episodes but does not treat them as authoritative. He emphasizes the Quranic command to disembark "in security and with blessings" as the theological climax — the flood's resolution is framed as divine mercy, not merely the end of punishment.

**Ibn Kathir** focuses on the theological significance of the flood's end. He stresses that Nuh's prayer of praise upon landing (23:28-29) models the Islamic virtue of gratitude (*shukr*) to God after deliverance. He does not engage extensively with the sacrificial scene of Genesis 8, which has no Quranic parallel, and rejects the notion that God could "smell" a pleasing aroma — such language, he argues, is either metaphorical or reflects *tahrif*. Ibn Kathir also discusses the post-flood world as a new beginning, with the descendants of those on the ark forming the nations of the earth.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides the most detailed post-flood narrative, describing the conditions on the ark, the gradual appearance of dry land, and the settlement of Nuh's descendants. He includes traditions about where Nuh and his family initially settled and how they began to rebuild human civilization. His account, while embellished, emphasizes the theme of divine providence — God's destruction of the wicked is always followed by the establishment of the righteous.
