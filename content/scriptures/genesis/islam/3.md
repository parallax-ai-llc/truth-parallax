## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Adam's Slip

Genesis 3 narrates the temptation, disobedience, and expulsion from the Garden. This is arguably the chapter where Islamic and Christian readings diverge most sharply. In Christian theology, this event constitutes "the Fall" — a catastrophic rupture introducing original sin into the human condition. Islam categorically rejects this framework. The Quran presents Adam's disobedience as a *zallah* (slip) or *ma'siyah* (act of disobedience) from which Adam fully repented and was completely forgiven. There is no inherited guilt, no corrupted human nature, and no need for a redemptive sacrifice to undo Adam's error.

The role of Iblis (Satan) is also recast. In Genesis, a serpent tempts Eve. In the Quran, Iblis — a jinn who refused to prostrate before Adam out of arrogance — directly whispers to both Adam and Hawwa. There is no special blame placed on Eve. The Quran describes the temptation as directed at the pair together, and both repent together.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 2:36-37** — "But Satan caused them to slip out of it and removed them from that in which they had been. And We said, 'Go down, all of you, as enemies to one another.' ... Then Adam received from his Lord words, and He accepted his repentance. Indeed, it is He who is the Accepting of Repentance, the Merciful."
- **Quran 7:20-25** — Iblis whispers to both of them, swearing he is a sincere adviser. Their nakedness is exposed, and they cover themselves with leaves. God addresses them both, and they descend to earth.
- **Quran 20:115-123** — "And We had already taken a promise from Adam before, but he forgot; and We found not in him determination." Adam forgot rather than willfully rebelled — a key nuance for *'ismah* (prophetic infallibility).
- **Quran 7:23** — The prayer of repentance: "Our Lord, we have wronged ourselves, and if You do not forgive us and have mercy upon us, we will surely be among the losers."

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 3 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| The tempter | A serpent tempts Eve specifically | Iblis (a jinn, not a serpent) tempts both Adam and Hawwa |
| Eve's blame | Eve singled out as first to eat | Both tempted equally; no special blame on Hawwa |
| Original sin | Foundational to Christian soteriology | Explicitly denied; Adam repented and was forgiven (2:37) |
| Nature of the act | Willful rebellion, "the Fall" | A slip (*zallah*), forgetting (*nasiya*), not willful rebellion |
| Consequence | Cursed ground, painful childbirth, death enters | Descent to earth was part of God's plan from the start (2:30) |
| Serpent cursed | Serpent cursed to crawl | No serpent in the Quranic narrative |
| "Seed of the woman" | Proto-evangelium (3:15) | Not recognized in Islamic exegesis |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** narrates the story at length, incorporating Isra'iliyyat traditions about the serpent, but always subordinating them to the Quranic framework. He records traditions about Iblis entering the garden through the serpent, possibly drawn from Jewish sources, but his theological analysis centers on the Quranic emphasis on repentance and forgiveness. He stresses that Adam's descent to earth was part of God's pre-ordained plan, not a punishment that altered human nature.

**Ibn Kathir** firmly rejects any narrative that places greater blame on Hawwa. He cites Quran 20:121 — "And Adam disobeyed his Lord and erred" — noting that Adam is named as the one who disobeyed, not Hawwa. He argues that traditions blaming women for the Fall are Isra'iliyyat corruptions that contradict the Quran's balanced presentation. He also emphasizes that the verse "Adam received words from his Lord" (2:37) demonstrates complete divine forgiveness, leaving no residual guilt.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides extensive narrative detail, describing the conversation between Iblis and the pair, their covering themselves with fig leaves, and their descent. While his account is embellished with storytelling elements, his theological conclusion aligns with standard Islamic doctrine: the descent was a transition, not a fall, and Adam emerged as the first prophet, guided and forgiven.
