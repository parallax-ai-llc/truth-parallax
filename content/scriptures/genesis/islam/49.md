## Ya'qub's Final Words

Genesis 49 records Ya'qub's deathbed blessings and prophecies over each of his twelve sons, ranging from curses (Simeon, Levi) to exalted praise (Judah, Joseph). The chapter concludes with Ya'qub's death and his instruction to be buried in the Cave of Machpelah. Islamic tradition has a profoundly different account of Ya'qub's deathbed — the Quran transforms the scene from tribal prophecy into a testament of pure monotheism.

## Quranic Parallels

The Quran's account of Ya'qub's deathbed is found in **Surah al-Baqarah 2:132-133**, and it stands in sharp contrast to Genesis 49:

**Genesis 49**: Ya'qub delivers individual prophecies about each tribe's future — political, military, and territorial.

**Quran 2:133**: "Or were you witnesses when death approached Ya'qub, when he said to his sons, **'What will you worship after me?'** They said, 'We will worship your God and the God of your fathers — Ibrahim, Isma'il, and Ishaq — one God. And to Him we are Muslims (*muslimun*).'"

| Genesis 49 | Quran 2:132-133 | Key Differences |
|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Individual tribal prophecies (49:3-27) | Single question about worship | Quran replaces tribal destiny with theological testament |
| Curses on Simeon and Levi (49:5-7) | No condemnation of specific sons | A prophet does not curse his own children in Islamic tradition |
| Judah as lion/ruler (49:8-12) | No special status for Yahudha | Islamic tradition does not assign messianic significance to the Judah lineage |
| Joseph's lengthy blessing (49:22-26) | No individual blessing | Yusuf's status is established through Surah 12, not through Ya'qub's deathbed words |
| Burial instruction (49:29-32) | Not in this Quranic passage | Burial details are Isra'iliyyat |
| Ya'qub dies (49:33) | Death implied but focus is on the theological exchange | Quran centres the moment on tawhid, not on death |

## The Quranic Deathbed: Tawhid as Legacy

The Quran's version of Ya'qub's deathbed is one of the most theologically significant passages in Islamic engagement with Genesis. Several principles are at work:

### Monotheism over Tribalism
Where Genesis is concerned with the political future of twelve tribes, the Quran asks only one question: "What will you worship?" The entire tribal-political dimension is replaced by a single theological imperative.

### Ibrahim's Legacy
The Quran places Ya'qub's deathbed in the context of Ibrahim's instruction (2:132): "Ibrahim instructed his sons and Ya'qub: 'O my sons, indeed Allah has chosen for you this religion, so do not die except while you are Muslims.'" This makes the deathbed scene part of a continuous chain of prophetic monotheism stretching from Ibrahim through Isma'il, Ishaq, and Ya'qub.

### The Term "Muslim"
The sons declare themselves **muslimun** — "those who submit." This is not anachronistic in Islamic theology; it reflects the Quranic teaching that all prophets and their true followers were "Muslim" in the sense of submitting to Allah. Islam is not viewed as a new religion but as the restoration of the original faith of Ibrahim.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** treats Quran 2:132-133 as the definitive account of Ya'qub's final moments. He notes that the Quran's silence on tribal prophecies indicates either that they were not part of the original revelation or that they are irrelevant to the theological message. Ya'qub's legacy is Islam, not tribal destiny.

**Al-Tabari** records some of the tribal blessings from Isra'iliyyat sources as historical-genealogical data, while clearly prioritising the Quranic account as theologically authoritative. He notes the contrast between the two versions without attempting harmonisation.

**Al-Razi** in *Mafatih al-Ghayb* argues that the Quranic version corrects the biblical account: the original scene must have been a profession of tawhid, and the tribal prophecies were later additions reflecting Israelite political concerns.

**Sayyid Qutb** reads the Quranic deathbed scene as the defining statement of Islamic identity: true religion is not tribal, ethnic, or national. It is submission to Allah. He argues that this single verse dismantles the ethnic theology that pervades the Genesis tribal blessings.

## The Shiloh Prophecy (49:10)

The famous verse "until Shiloh comes" (49:10) has been interpreted by some Islamic scholars — including **Ibn Kathir** in certain passages — as a reference to the Prophet Muhammad. The "sceptre" departing from Judah "until Shiloh comes" is read as prophecy that Judah's religious authority would end with the advent of Muhammad. This interpretation is disputed but forms part of the Islamic tradition of finding Muhammadan prophecies in earlier scriptures (cf. Quran 7:157).
