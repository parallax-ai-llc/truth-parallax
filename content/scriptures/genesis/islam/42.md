## The Brothers Come to Misr

Genesis 42 narrates the first journey of Yusuf's brothers to Egypt (*Misr* in Arabic) to buy grain during the famine. Yusuf recognises them but they do not recognise him. He accuses them of being spies, imprisons Simeon as hostage, and demands they bring Binyamin (Benjamin). The Quranic parallel in Surah Yusuf 12:58-68 covers the same events with characteristic theological additions.

## Quranic Parallels

| Genesis 42 | Quran 12:58-68 | Key Differences |
|------------|----------------|----------------|
| Brothers come to buy grain (42:3-5) | 12:58: "And the brothers of Yusuf came and entered upon him, and he recognised them but they did not recognise him" | Same basic event |
| Ya'qub keeps Binyamin home (42:4) | 12:63-64: Brothers later ask Ya'qub to send Binyamin; he fears for him as he feared for Yusuf | Same protectiveness but detailed later |
| Yusuf accuses them of spying (42:9-14) | Not directly paralleled in Quran | Genesis adds the spying accusation |
| Yusuf demands Binyamin (42:15-20) | 12:59-60: "Bring me a brother of yours from your father... If you do not bring him, there will be no measure for you" | Same demand |
| Money returned in sacks (42:25-28) | 12:62: "And he said to his servants, 'Put their merchandise into their saddlebags so that they will recognise it when they return to their family'" | Same event; Quran shows Yusuf's deliberate instruction |
| Brothers distressed by returned money (42:28, 35) | 12:65: "When they opened their baggage, they found their merchandise returned to them" | Same discovery |
| Ya'qub's grief (42:36-38) | 12:63-67: Ya'qub resists sending Binyamin; eventually agrees with conditions | Quran provides more detail on Ya'qub's internal state |

## Ya'qub's Response

The Quranic treatment of Ya'qub in this chapter is notably different from Genesis. In Genesis, Ya'qub appears as a passive, grief-stricken old man. In the Quran, he is an active prophetic figure exercising discernment:

**Genesis 42:36**: "Everything is against me!" — a statement of despair.

**Quran 12:67**: Ya'qub instructs his sons to enter Egypt by different gates, "but I cannot avail you against Allah at all. The decision is only with Allah. Upon Him I have relied, and upon Him let those who rely put their trust."

This transformation is significant: the Quran's Ya'qub never despairs. Even in grief, he maintains **tawakkul** (trust in Allah) and **sabr** (patience). His precaution about entering by different gates shows practical wisdom combined with spiritual submission.

## The Motif of Non-Recognition

Both accounts agree that the brothers did not recognise Yusuf. Islamic commentators explain this through multiple factors:

1. **Time elapsed**: Decades had passed since they threw him in the well as a youth
2. **Changed status**: Yusuf was now dressed as an Egyptian official, speaking Egyptian
3. **Divine concealment**: Allah veiled Yusuf's identity as part of the test — **"He recognised them but they did not recognise him"** (12:58)

**Al-Razi** emphasises the third factor: the non-recognition was divinely orchestrated. It served Allah's plan to test the brothers, purify them through trial, and ultimately bring about family reconciliation.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** treats this chapter as the beginning of the brothers' moral reckoning. Their journey to Egypt is not merely economic but part of Allah's plan to confront them with their past sin. He notes that Yusuf's treatment of them — stern but not cruel — reflects prophetic justice: testing without vengeance.

**Al-Tabari** supplements the Quranic account with Isra'iliyyat details: the brothers' conversation among themselves, their suspicion of being punished for what they did to Yusuf, and the emotional dynamics of their interaction with the unrecognised brother.

**Al-Qurtubi** draws a legal principle from Yusuf's demand for Binyamin: it is permissible in Islamic governance to use strategic conditions and tests when serving a higher purpose, provided the ultimate goal is just.

**Sayyid Qutb** reads the brothers' journey as an illustration of **qadar** (divine decree): the very famine that brought suffering was the means by which Allah reunited Yusuf with his family. What appeared as hardship was in reality divine mercy unfolding through time.
