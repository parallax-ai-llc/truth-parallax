## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Covenant with Ibrahim

Genesis 15 describes God's covenant with Abram — the promise of innumerable descendants, the vision of future bondage and liberation, and the dramatic covenant ceremony involving halved animals and a smoking fire pot passing between them. The Quran confirms the essential theme of God's covenant with Ibrahim but presents it in fundamentally different terms. In the Quran, the covenant is about Ibrahim's appointment as an *imam* (spiritual leader) for humanity, contingent on obedience and submission — not primarily about land or biological descendants.

The covenant ceremony with halved animals (Genesis 15:9-17) has no Quranic parallel. The Quran's covenant with Ibrahim is established through trials and fulfilled commands (Quran 2:124), not through ritual sacrifice. The promise of descendants is acknowledged, but Islamic tradition emphasizes that the fulfilled promise includes Isma'il's line (leading to the Arabs and ultimately to Prophet Muhammad) alongside Ishaq's line.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 2:124** — "And when Abraham was tried by his Lord with commands and he fulfilled them. [Allah] said, 'Indeed, I will make you a leader for the people.' [Abraham] said, 'And of my descendants?' [Allah] said, 'My covenant does not include the wrongdoers.'" This is the central Quranic covenant passage — the promise of leadership conditioned on righteousness.
- **Quran 2:260** — "And when Abraham said, 'My Lord, show me how You give life to the dead.' [Allah] said, 'Have you not believed?' He said, 'Yes, but [I ask] only that my heart may be satisfied.'" Ibrahim's request for a sign parallels the Genesis 15 context of seeking assurance.
- **Quran 6:75-79** — Ibrahim's contemplation of the heavens, arriving at certainty about God — thematically connected to God showing Abram the stars in Genesis 15:5.
- **Quran 14:35-41** — Ibrahim's prayer for his descendants in both Mecca and the broader world.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 15 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Promise of descendants | "Count the stars" — innumerable offspring | Acknowledged but includes both Isma'il's and Ishaq's lines |
| Covenant ceremony | Animal halves, fire pot, divine passage | No parallel; Islamic covenant is through obedience and trial |
| Land promise | "To your offspring I give this land" (15:18) | Not emphasized in the Quran; Ibrahim's legacy is spiritual, not territorial |
| Abram "believed" (15:6) | Credited as righteousness | Ibrahim's faith is central to the Quran, but "righteousness by faith" as a theological category is not developed as in Paul's theology |
| Future bondage (15:13) | 400 years in a foreign land | The Bani Isra'il narrative in Egypt is Quranic but not linked to this specific covenant passage |
| Boundaries (15:18-21) | From the River of Egypt to the Euphrates | No Quranic support for specific territorial boundaries promised to Ibrahim's offspring |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** records traditions about Ibrahim's covenant with God but focuses on the Quranic presentation in 2:124. He interprets the "trials" (*kalimat*) through which Ibrahim was tested as including various commands — some traditions enumerate them as the practices of *fitrah* (natural disposition), such as circumcision; others identify them as the major tests of Ibrahim's life (fire, emigration, sacrifice of his son). Al-Tabari does not engage extensively with the halved-animal ceremony of Genesis 15, which has no Islamic analogue.

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes that the Quranic covenant is conditional: "My covenant does not include the wrongdoers" (2:124). This fundamentally distinguishes it from readings of Genesis 15 that emphasize unconditional land promises. Ibrahim's covenant in Islam is a covenant of *tawhid* and righteous action, available to all who follow his path — not an ethnic or territorial entitlement. Ibn Kathir cites Quran 2:135: "They say, 'Be Jews or Christians [so] you will be guided.' Say, 'Rather, [we follow] the religion of Abraham, inclining toward truth, and he was not of the polytheists.'"

**Al-Tha'labi** connects the Genesis 15 themes to Ibrahim's broader life narrative, noting that the desire for descendants and the concern about legacy are deeply human emotions that even the greatest prophet experienced. He presents Ibrahim's prayers for offspring (echoed in Quran 14:35-41 and 37:100) as expressions of faith — asking God for what He has already promised.
