## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Call of Ibrahim

Genesis 12 narrates God's call to Abram to leave his homeland, the promise of a great nation, his journey to Canaan, and the episode in Egypt where he presents Sarai as his sister. Islamic tradition deeply engages with the first part — Ibrahim's call and migration — which corresponds to a major Quranic theme. The Egypt episode, however, is treated with significant reservation due to its implications for prophetic character.

In Islamic theology, Ibrahim holds the unique title *Khalil Allah* — the Friend of God (Quran 4:125). He is the archetypal *hanif*, the pure monotheist who rejected the idolatry of his people and submitted entirely to God. His migration (*hijrah*) from his homeland is seen as a prototype for the Prophet Muhammad's later migration from Mecca to Medina, establishing *hijrah* as a fundamental Islamic concept: leaving a land of disbelief for the sake of one's faith.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 21:51-71** — Ibrahim's early mission: confronting his father and people about their idols, smashing the idols, being thrown into fire, and being saved by God. This provides the background for his departure.
- **Quran 29:26** — "And Lot believed him. [Abraham] said, 'Indeed, I will emigrate to [the service of] my Lord. Indeed, He is the Exalted in Might, the Wise.'" This is the Quranic equivalent of the call to leave.
- **Quran 37:99** — "And [Abraham] said, 'Indeed, I will go to [where I am ordered by] my Lord; He will guide me.'"
- **Quran 6:75-79** — Ibrahim's contemplation of the stars, moon, and sun, arriving at *tawhid* — "I have turned my face toward He who created the heavens and the earth, inclining toward truth, and I am not of those who associate others with Allah."
- **Quran 2:124** — "And when Abraham was tried by his Lord with commands and he fulfilled them, [Allah] said, 'Indeed, I will make you a leader for the people.'"

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 12 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Nature of the call | God calls Abram to leave (12:1) | Ibrahim's departure follows confrontation with idolaters and persecution |
| Promise of land | Specific land promise to Abram's descendants | Quran does not emphasize a land promise; focus is on spiritual leadership |
| "Great nation" promise | Abram's descendants will be a great nation | Ibrahim as leader (*imam*) of all people (2:124), not one nation |
| Sarai as sister (12:10-20) | Abram tells Sarai to say she is his sister | Problematic for *'ismah*; treated cautiously or reinterpreted |
| Pharaoh takes Sarai | Pharaoh takes Sarai into his house | Some hadith traditions acknowledge this but emphasize Sarah's piety and God's protection |
| Wealth gained | Abram acquires wealth through the deception | Not emphasized; focus remains on Ibrahim's spiritual mission |

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** narrates Ibrahim's departure from his homeland within the broader context of his confrontation with Nimrod and the idolaters. The call to leave is not presented as a sudden divine command (as in Genesis 12:1) but as the culmination of a prophetic mission that met rejection. Al-Tabari includes traditions about Ibrahim's journey through various lands, including Egypt. Regarding the "sister" episode, Al-Tabari records hadith traditions that reference this event (found in Sahih al-Bukhari), but notes that Ibrahim's statement was a form of *tawriyah* (equivocation with a true underlying meaning) — Sarah was his "sister in faith," not a lie in the strict sense.

**Ibn Kathir** emphasizes Ibrahim's migration as an act of supreme faith — leaving everything familiar for the sake of God. He connects this to the Quranic concept of *hijrah*, noting that Ibrahim's journey established a pattern followed by subsequent prophets and ultimately by Muhammad. On the Egypt episode, Ibn Kathir follows the hadith narration but stresses that Ibrahim used permissible *tawriyah*, not deception. God protected Sarah through divine intervention, and Ibrahim's faith never wavered.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides the most narrative-rich account, detailing Ibrahim's travels, his encounters, and the divine signs that accompanied him. He treats Ibrahim's journey as a pilgrimage of faith, each station representing a deepening of his relationship with God. The Egypt episode is presented briefly, with emphasis on God's protection of Sarah and Ibrahim's trust in divine providence.
