## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on Finding a Wife for Ishaq

Genesis 24, the longest chapter in Genesis, narrates Abraham sending his servant to find a wife for Isaac from his kinfolk in Mesopotamia. The servant's journey, his prayer at the well, his encounter with Rebekah, the marriage negotiations, and Rebekah's journey to Canaan form an extended narrative about divine providence in marriage. This chapter has no Quranic parallel. The Quran does not mention Rebekah (Rifqah) by name, nor does it narrate the circumstances of Ishaq's marriage.

Islamic engagement with this material is limited to Isra'iliyyat traditions recorded by Muslim historians. The chapter's theological content — God's providential guidance in arranging a righteous marriage — is consistent with Islamic values but not specifically addressed in Islamic scripture.

## Handling of Isra'iliyyat Material

This chapter falls entirely within the neutral Isra'iliyyat category. Its narrative elements do not contradict Islamic principles, and the values it portrays — seeking a righteous spouse, trusting in God's guidance, maintaining family connections — are compatible with Islamic ethics. However, the detailed narrative is not part of the Quranic worldview, which is selective about which prophetic episodes it includes.

**Al-Tabari** mentions Ishaq's marriage to Rifqah (Rebekah) in his historical narrative, noting her lineage and the birth of their sons Ya'qub (Jacob) and Esau. His treatment is brief — a historical notation rather than a developed narrative. The servant's prayer at the well and the elaborate negotiation scene are not reproduced in detail, as they serve the biblical narrative's purposes (establishing covenantal lineage) more than Islamic ones.

**Ibn Kathir** addresses Ishaq's marriage with characteristic brevity regarding non-Quranic material. In *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah*, he records that Ishaq married Rifqah and that she bore him two sons. His interest is in the genealogical chain from Ibrahim through Ishaq to Ya'qub — a lineage that leads to the Israelite prophets — rather than in the marriage narrative itself. Ibn Kathir notes that Ishaq was a prophet (Quran 37:112) and that his marriage was part of God's plan for the continuation of prophetic lineage.

**Al-Tha'labi** provides a somewhat fuller account in *Qisas al-Anbiya'*, incorporating elements of the marriage story as part of his continuous narrative of the prophets. He treats the episode as illustrating Ibrahim's care for his son's spiritual welfare — seeking a wife from a family that maintained some connection to monotheistic tradition rather than from the idol-worshippers of Canaan. This detail, drawn from the biblical narrative, is consonant with the Islamic emphasis on marrying within the faith.

## Islamic Principles Applied

| Principle | Relevance to Genesis 24 |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Marriage in Islam | Seeking a righteous spouse is a prophetic sunnah; Ibrahim's concern for Ishaq's marriage reflects this value |
| Divine guidance (*istikharah*) | The servant's prayer for God's guidance parallels the Islamic practice of seeking God's guidance in major decisions |
| Family involvement | Ibrahim's role in arranging the marriage is consistent with Islamic practice of family involvement in marriage |
| Endogamy | Ibrahim's preference for a wife from his kinfolk resonates with Islamic encouragement to marry within the community of faith |
| Trust in God (*tawakkul*) | The servant's reliance on God's signs echoes the Islamic virtue of trusting God's plan |

## Broader Context

The absence of this chapter from the Quran is itself notable. The Quran's selective retelling of prophetic history focuses on moments of theological crisis, moral testing, and divine intervention — not on domestic arrangements or marriage negotiations. Genesis 24's narrative, while beautifully crafted and theologically meaningful in its biblical context, addresses concerns (covenantal lineage through Isaac, the promised land, and endogamy within Abraham's clan) that are not central to the Quran's presentation of Ibrahim's legacy.
