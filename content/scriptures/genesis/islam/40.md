## Dreams in Prison: Yusuf as Interpreter and Da'i

Genesis 40 recounts Yusuf's time in prison with two fellow inmates — the king's cupbearer and baker — and his interpretation of their dreams. This chapter has a close Quranic parallel in Surah Yusuf 12:36-42, but the Quranic version adds a crucial dimension: Yusuf uses the occasion to call his fellow prisoners to **tawhid** (monotheism) before interpreting their dreams.

## Quranic Parallels

| Genesis 40 | Quran 12:36-42 | Key Differences |
|------------|----------------|----------------|
| Two prisoners have dreams (40:5-8) | 12:36: "Two young men entered prison with him. One said, 'I saw myself pressing wine.' The other said, 'I saw myself carrying bread on my head, from which birds were eating'" | Substantially identical; Quran omits that one was cupbearer and one baker but the dreams convey the same roles |
| Yusuf says interpretation belongs to God (40:8) | 12:37: "No food will come to you as provision but that I will inform you of its interpretation before it comes to you. That is from what my Lord has taught me" | Quran expands: Yusuf attributes his gift to Allah's teaching |
| Not in Genesis | 12:38-40: Yusuf delivers a da'wah sermon on tawhid before interpreting | Entirely absent from Genesis (see below) |
| Cupbearer: restored in three days (40:12-13) | 12:41: "One of you will give drink to his master" | Same interpretation, less detail |
| Baker: executed in three days (40:18-19) | 12:41: "The other will be crucified and birds will eat from his head" | Same outcome; Quran is more blunt |
| Cupbearer forgets Yusuf (40:23) | 12:42: "But Satan made him forget the mention to his master, and Yusuf remained in prison several years" | Quran attributes the forgetting to Satan's influence |

## Yusuf's Da'wah in Prison (Quran 12:37-40)

The most significant addition in the Quranic account is Yusuf's monotheistic sermon, which has no Genesis equivalent. Before interpreting the dreams, Yusuf declares:

> **"I have left the religion of a people who do not believe in Allah and are disbelievers in the Hereafter. And I follow the religion of my fathers — Ibrahim and Ishaq and Ya'qub. It was not for us to associate anything with Allah. That is from the favour of Allah upon us and upon mankind, but most people are not grateful."** (12:37-38)

He then directly challenges the prisoners:

> **"O my two companions of prison, are diverse lords better, or Allah, the One, the Prevailing? You worship not besides Him except mere names you have named — you and your fathers — for which Allah has sent down no authority. Judgement is not but for Allah. He has commanded that you worship not except Him. That is the correct religion, but most people do not know."** (12:39-40)

This passage transforms Yusuf from a dream-interpreter into a **da'i** (caller to faith). Islamic commentators view this as the chapter's central message: every situation — even imprisonment — is an opportunity to proclaim tawhid.

## Commentators' Views

**Ibn Kathir** emphasises that Yusuf's dream interpretation was not fortune-telling (*kahanah*) — which is prohibited in Islam — but a divinely granted gift (*ilm min Allah*). He notes the hadith: "True dreams are one-forty-sixth of prophethood" (Sahih al-Bukhari 6989), establishing dream interpretation as a legitimate prophetic function.

**Al-Qurtubi** draws legal principles from the chapter: the permissibility of a Muslim offering services (interpretation) to non-Muslims, the obligation of da'wah even in adverse circumstances, and the prophetic model of establishing credibility before delivering a message.

**Sayyid Qutb** in *Fi Zilal al-Quran* highlights the contrast between Yusuf's approach and the Genesis account. In Genesis, Yusuf simply interprets dreams. In the Quran, he first establishes his prophetic credentials, delivers the message of tawhid, and only then addresses the dreams. This ordering — faith before worldly matters — reflects the Quranic priority structure.

**Al-Tabari** records traditions identifying the two prisoners: one as the king's cupbearer (*saqi*) and the other as his baker (*khabbaz*), consistent with Genesis. He notes that Yusuf's request to be remembered to the king (12:42) was a permissible use of worldly means (*asbab*), though some scholars view it as a momentary lapse in *tawakkul*.

## Islamic Moral Principles

The chapter illustrates **da'wah in adversity** — the prophetic obligation to call people to Allah regardless of personal circumstances. Yusuf does not wait for freedom or comfort; he uses the prison itself as a pulpit. This models the behaviour expected of every Muslim: to convey the message of Islam wherever one finds oneself.

The attribution of the cupbearer's forgetfulness to Satan (12:42) also teaches that even divinely ordained plans unfold through natural means — and that setbacks are part of Allah's timing, not evidence of His absence.
