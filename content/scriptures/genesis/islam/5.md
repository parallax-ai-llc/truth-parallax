## Summary: The Islamic Perspective on the Genealogies

Genesis 5 presents the genealogy from Adam to Noah, listing ten generations with extraordinarily long lifespans. This chapter has minimal direct Quranic engagement — the Quran does not provide genealogical lists in this manner. Islamic scholars therefore treat this material primarily as Isra'iliyyat: traditions from the People of the Book that are neither confirmed nor denied by Islamic revelation, and are to be evaluated cautiously.

The one figure in this chapter who does appear prominently in Islamic tradition is Enoch (Genesis 5:21-24), widely identified with the Quranic prophet Idris. The Quran's brief but elevated mention of Idris — "We raised him to a high station" (19:57) — has generated extensive commentary about his identity, his knowledge, and his mysterious departure from the world.

## Quranic Parallels

- **Quran 19:56-57** — "And mention in the Book, Idris. Indeed, he was a man of truth and a prophet. And We raised him to a high station."
- **Quran 21:85** — "And [mention] Isma'il and Idris and Dhul-Kifl; all were of the patient."

The identification of Idris with Enoch is based on the parallel between "God took him" (Genesis 5:24) and "We raised him to a high station" (Quran 19:57). This identification is widespread but not unanimous among scholars.

## Key Differences from the Biblical Account

| Topic | Genesis 5 | Islamic Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Genealogical lists | Central to the biblical narrative structure | Not a Quranic genre; treated as Isra'iliyyat |
| Extreme lifespans | 900+ years for early patriarchs | Neither confirmed nor denied; some scholars accept, others are cautious |
| Enoch/Idris | "Walked with God; God took him" (5:24) | Idris was a prophet raised to a high station; possibly taken to heaven alive |
| "In the image of God" (5:1) | Humanity created in God's image | Anthropomorphism reinterpreted; divine attributes not shared literally |
| Seth's line | Emphasis on a righteous lineage | Seth (*Shith*) recognized in tradition as a prophet after Adam |

## Handling of Isra'iliyyat Material

Islamic scholars approach Genesis 5 with the standard Isra'iliyyat framework:

**Al-Tabari** freely incorporates the genealogical information from Genesis 5 into his *Tarikh* (History), listing the generations from Adam to Nuh with their lifespans largely matching the biblical record. He draws on authorities such as Wahb ibn Munabbih and Ka'b al-Ahbar, who transmitted traditions from Jewish sources. Al-Tabari does not typically challenge the specific numbers but presents them as historical reports.

**Ibn Kathir** is more selective. In *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah*, he mentions the line from Adam to Nuh but focuses on the figures that have Islamic theological significance — particularly Shith (Seth), whom he identifies as a prophet who received scriptures, and Idris. He cautions against relying too heavily on biblical genealogies, noting that the exact numbers may have been corrupted during transmission. He devotes significant attention to Idris, discussing traditions about his knowledge of astronomy, writing, and sewing, and his ascension to the heavens.

**Al-Tha'labi** includes the genealogy in *Qisas al-Anbiya'* and uses the long lifespans as evidence of God's blessing upon the early generations. He does not challenge the numbers but embeds them within a narrative framework emphasizing divine providence and the gradual unfolding of human history toward prophethood.

## Islamic Principles Applied

The extreme longevity reported in this chapter is generally accepted as possible within Islamic cosmology — God has power over all things, and the early generations may have been granted longer lives as part of God's plan. The key Islamic principle applied to this chapter is that genealogies serve a purpose only insofar as they establish prophetic lineages and demonstrate God's providential guidance of history toward His chosen messengers.
