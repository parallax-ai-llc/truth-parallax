## The Covenant of Circumcision

Genesis 17 records God's appearance to Abram at age ninety-nine — thirteen years after Ishmael's birth — to establish circumcision as the sign of the covenant, to change Abram's and Sarai's names, and to promise the birth of Isaac. This chapter is foundational for Protestant covenant theology and the debate over the relationship between the sign of the covenant and the reality it represents.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "I am God Almighty; walk before me, and be blameless" (17:1)

God reveals himself as *El Shaddai* — "God Almighty." After thirteen years of silence following the Hagar episode, God reappears with a command: "Walk before me, and be blameless."

Protestant interpreters note:

- "Blameless" (*tamim*) means wholehearted integrity, not sinless perfection
- The command follows the covenant promise — obedience is the response to grace, not its condition
- *El Shaddai* emphasizes God's power to do what is humanly impossible — relevant for the promise that a ninety-year-old barren woman will bear a son

### The Covenant Elaborated (17:2-8)

God expands the promises made in chapters 12 and 15:

| Promise | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Multiplication | "I will make you exceedingly fruitful" |
| Nations | "Kings shall come from you" |
| Everlasting covenant | "Between me and you and your offspring... throughout their generations" |
| Land | "All the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession" |
| Relationship | "I will be their God" — the covenant formula |

The phrase "I will be their God" is the heart of covenant theology. **Robertson** identifies this formula as the central thread uniting all biblical covenants, from Abraham to the New Covenant (Jeremiah 31:33; Revelation 21:3).

### Name Changes (17:5, 15)

| Old Name | New Name | Meaning |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Abram ("exalted father") | Abraham ("father of a multitude") | God inscribes the promise into his identity |
| Sarai ("my princess") | Sarah ("princess") | Her royal status is universalized — "mother of nations" |

**Calvin** interpreted the name changes as seals of God's promise: just as God's word creates what it declares, the new names make Abraham and Sarah what they are called to be. The name change is performative — it constitutes a new identity grounded in divine promise.

### Circumcision as Covenant Sign (17:9-14)

"This is my covenant, which you shall keep... Every male among you shall be circumcised... It shall be a sign of the covenant between me and you."

Circumcision is the most debated element of Genesis 17 among Protestant traditions:

| Tradition | View of Circumcision | Implication for Baptism |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Reformed/Presbyterian | Sign of the covenant of grace; replaced by baptism in the New Covenant | Infant baptism is warranted as the New Covenant counterpart |
| Baptist/Evangelical | Sign specific to the Abrahamic-Mosaic economy; not directly replaced by baptism | Believer's baptism only; the New Covenant sign requires faith |
| Lutheran | Primarily an Old Testament type fulfilled in Christ; baptism is a means of grace | Infant baptism, but grounded differently than Reformed theology |

**Calvin** argued that circumcision and baptism are essentially the same sign pointing to the same reality — incorporation into the covenant community. Since infants were included in the Old Covenant through circumcision, they should be included in the New Covenant through baptism.

**Baptist theologians** counter that the New Covenant is fundamentally different: Jeremiah 31:34 promises that all members of the New Covenant will "know the Lord," which implies that only believers should receive the covenant sign.

Paul's interpretation is crucial: Romans 4:11 calls circumcision "a sign" and "a seal of the righteousness that [Abraham] had by faith." The sign confirmed what was already true — it did not create the reality. This supports the Protestant insistence that outward signs do not save; faith does.

### Abraham's Laughter and the Promise of Isaac (17:17-19)

"Then Abraham fell on his face and laughed and said to himself, 'Shall a child be born to a man who is a hundred years old? Shall Sarah, who is ninety years old, bear a child?'"

Abraham's laughter has been variously interpreted:

- **Calvin**: It was laughter of joyful astonishment, mixed with human weakness — not unbelief
- **Luther**: A natural human reaction that did not negate Abraham's faith
- **Some interpreters**: A moment of genuine doubt, quickly corrected by God's reaffirmation

Abraham also intercedes for Ishmael: "Oh that Ishmael might live before you!" God responds with a dual promise:

| Isaac | Ishmael |
|-------|---------|
| "I will establish my covenant with him" | "I will bless him and make him fruitful" |
| The covenant line | Twelve princes, a great nation |
| Born of promise | Born of the flesh |
| Sarah's son | Hagar's son |

The distinction between Isaac and Ishmael is central to Paul's argument in Galatians 4 and Romans 9: God's covenant purposes run through the child of promise, not the child of human effort. Election is God's sovereign prerogative.

### Abraham's Obedience (17:23-27)

"That very day Abraham... circumcised" every male in his household, including Ishmael and all servants. The immediacy of obedience — "that very day" — demonstrates the faith that takes God at his word without delay.

## Theological Significance

1. **Covenant theology**: Genesis 17 is the structural center of Reformed covenant theology, establishing the pattern of promise, sign, and obligation that shapes the entire biblical narrative
2. **Sign and reality**: Circumcision as a sign that seals but does not create righteousness anticipates the Protestant understanding of sacraments
3. **Election**: The Isaac/Ishmael distinction demonstrates that God's covenant purposes are determined by sovereign choice, not human lineage or effort
4. **Christology**: Circumcision of the heart (Deuteronomy 30:6; Romans 2:29; Colossians 2:11) finds its fulfillment in Christ, who accomplishes the inward reality that the outward sign could only symbolize
