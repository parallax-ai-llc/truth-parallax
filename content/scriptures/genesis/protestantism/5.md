## Genealogy from Adam to Noah

Genesis 5 presents the ten-generation genealogy from Adam to Noah, structured by a repetitive formula: "he lived... he fathered... he died." This chapter bridges the primeval narrative from Creation and Fall to the Flood, demonstrating both the persistence of God's blessing and the relentless reign of death.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "In the likeness of God" and "in his own likeness" (5:1-3)

The chapter opens by recalling that God made Adam "in the likeness of God," then states that Adam "fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image." Protestant theology finds here a crucial distinction:

- **The image of God persists**: Humanity retains the *imago Dei* even after the Fall
- **The image is now marred**: Adam's son bears Adam's fallen likeness, not the original perfection

**Reformed federal theology** sees this as foundational: Adam as the covenant head transmits both the image of God and the corruption of sin to his descendants. This dual reality — dignity and depravity — defines the Protestant understanding of human nature.

### The Refrain of Death: "and he died"

The most prominent literary feature of Genesis 5 is the sevenfold repetition of "and he died." Protestant interpreters emphasize that this refrain fulfills God's warning in Genesis 2:17. Death reigns from Adam onward (Romans 5:14).

### Enoch: "He walked with God, and he was not" (5:24)

The exception to the refrain of death is Enoch. "Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him." This verse has generated significant Protestant reflection:

- **Luther** saw Enoch's translation as a testimony that God can deliver from death, a foretaste of resurrection
- **Calvin** interpreted the phrase "walked with God" as describing a life of faith and holiness in the midst of a corrupt generation
- **Hebrews 11:5** confirms: "By faith Enoch was taken up so that he should not see death"

Protestant theology sees Enoch as a type of the believer's hope: death is not the final word. His translation points forward to Christ's resurrection and the promise that believers will be raised or "caught up" (1 Thessalonians 4:17).

### The Extraordinary Lifespans

The ages recorded in Genesis 5 — ranging from 365 to 969 years — have been debated among Protestant scholars:

| View | Description |
|------|------------|
| Literal ages | The patriarchs lived these exact years; pre-Flood conditions differed |
| Symbolic/schematic | Numbers carry theological or literary significance |
| Dynastic | Names represent family lines, not individuals |

Most evangelical and Reformed scholars accept the numbers at face value while acknowledging the ages decline dramatically after the Flood, which they connect to the cumulative effects of sin and the changed post-Flood environment.

### Lamech's Prophecy about Noah (5:29)

"Out of the ground that the Lord has cursed, this one shall bring us relief from our work and from the painful toil of our hands."

Lamech names his son Noah (linked to the Hebrew word for "rest" or "comfort") with a prophetic expectation of relief from the curse of Genesis 3:17-19. Protestant interpreters see this as an expression of messianic hope — though Noah would bring partial relief through the covenant after the Flood, ultimate rest comes only through Christ (Matthew 11:28-30).

## Theological Significance

1. **Death as the wage of sin**: The genealogy demonstrates that the penalty of Genesis 2:17 is carried out universally — "and he died" is the human condition apart from grace
2. **Covenant continuity**: God preserves a faithful line from Adam through Seth to Noah, fulfilling the promise of 3:15
3. **Grace amidst judgment**: Enoch's translation and Lamech's hope show that even under death's reign, God offers glimpses of redemption
4. **Federal headship**: Adam's likeness — both dignified and fallen — passes to all his descendants
