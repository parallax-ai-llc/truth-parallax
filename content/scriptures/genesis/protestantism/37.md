## Joseph Sold into Slavery

Genesis 37 opens the Joseph cycle, one of the most masterfully constructed narratives in all of Scripture. Joseph, the favored son of Jacob and Rachel, receives a special robe from his father and dreams two dreams in which his family bows before him. His brothers, already jealous of his favored status, despise him further. When Joseph is sent to check on his brothers tending flocks near Shechem, they conspire to kill him. Reuben intervenes, persuading them to cast Joseph into a pit instead. In Reuben's absence, Judah suggests selling Joseph to a passing caravan of Ishmaelite traders, who take him to Egypt. The brothers dip Joseph's robe in goat's blood and present it to Jacob, who mourns his son as dead.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Israel loved Joseph more than any other of his sons" (37:3)

Protestant interpreters see Jacob's favoritism as a tragic repetition of the dysfunction that plagued his own upbringing (Isaac favored Esau, Rebekah favored Jacob). The cycle of parental favoritism produces envy and division. Yet God's sovereignty works through and despite these human failures.

### The Two Dreams (37:5-11)

Joseph's dreams — sheaves bowing and sun, moon, and stars bowing — are prophetic revelations, not mere fantasy. Protestant theology affirms that God communicates through dreams in the patriarchal period, and that these dreams are ultimately fulfilled in Genesis 42-44.

| Dream | Imagery | Fulfillment |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| First dream (37:7) | Sheaves of grain bowing | Brothers bow before Joseph in Egypt seeking grain (42:6) |
| Second dream (37:9) | Sun, moon, eleven stars bowing | Jacob and the whole family come to Egypt and honor Joseph (46-47) |

**Luther** observed that God often reveals His purposes long before their fulfillment, testing faith through prolonged waiting and apparent contradiction.

### "Come, let us sell him to the Ishmaelites" (37:27)

Judah's proposal to sell Joseph rather than kill him is morally ambiguous. While it spares Joseph's life, it commodifies a brother as merchandise. Protestant commentators note the irony: Judah, who here trades his brother for twenty pieces of silver, will later undergo profound moral transformation in Genesis 44 when he offers himself in Benjamin's place.

### Joseph as a Type of Christ

Protestant typological interpretation has long recognized Joseph as one of the most complete Old Testament types of Christ:

- Beloved son sent by his father (37:13-14; cf. John 3:16)
- Rejected and betrayed by his own brothers (37:18-28; cf. Matthew 26:14-16)
- Sold for pieces of silver (37:28; cf. Matthew 26:15)
- Stripped of his robe (37:23; cf. John 19:23-24)
- Descended into a pit/prison before exaltation (37:24; cf. Philippians 2:8-9)

## Theological Significance

Genesis 37 is foundational for the Protestant doctrine of divine providence:

- **Sovereignty over evil**: What the brothers intend for harm, God intends for good (a principle stated explicitly in 50:20). Protestant theology insists that God's sovereign purposes are not thwarted by human sin but operate through it.
- **The hiddenness of God's plan**: At the close of chapter 37, everything appears hopeless — Joseph is enslaved, Jacob is grief-stricken, the covenant family is fractured. Yet God is silently at work, positioning Joseph exactly where he needs to be.
- **The cost of favoritism**: The narrative does not excuse Jacob's partiality or Joseph's apparent tactlessness in reporting his dreams. Grace works through flawed people, not because of their flaws.
