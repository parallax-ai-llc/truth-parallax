## Jacob's Journey to Egypt and the Vision at Beersheba

Genesis 46 narrates Jacob's departure from Canaan to Egypt. On the way, he stops at Beersheba — the place associated with his father Isaac and grandfather Abraham — and offers sacrifices to God. In a night vision, God speaks to Jacob: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for there I will make you into a great nation. I myself will go down with you to Egypt, and I will also bring you up again" (46:3-4). Reassured, Jacob continues with his entire family. The chapter includes a genealogical list of seventy persons who went down to Egypt. Upon arrival, Joseph meets Jacob in Goshen, and father and son are reunited after more than twenty years.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt" (46:3)

Jacob has every reason to be apprehensive. Egypt represents exile from the promised land, and both Abraham's and Isaac's sojourns outside Canaan were fraught with danger (12:10-20; 26:1-11). God's reassurance addresses a covenantal concern: is leaving the land a departure from the promise?

Protestant interpreters draw out the significance:

| Patriarch | Relation to Egypt | Outcome |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| Abraham | Went down during famine (12:10) | Encountered danger; no divine command to go |
| Isaac | Told NOT to go to Egypt (26:2) | Stayed in the land; covenant reaffirmed |
| Jacob | Told to go with divine assurance (46:3-4) | Goes with God's promise of return and nationhood |

**Calvin** observed that God meets Jacob precisely at the boundary — Beersheba, the southernmost point of the promised land — to assure him that leaving Canaan does not mean leaving God's purposes. The promise travels with the people, not the geography.

### "I myself will go down with you... and I will also bring you up again" (46:4)

This promise operates on multiple levels:

- **Immediate**: Jacob's family will be sustained in Egypt
- **Generational**: Israel will eventually return to Canaan (the Exodus)
- **Personal**: Jacob himself will be "brought up" — his body carried back to Canaan for burial (50:13)

Protestant theology reads this as an affirmation that God's presence with His people is not location-dependent. The Reformation's emphasis on the priesthood of all believers and the accessibility of God's presence finds an Old Testament root here.

### The Seventy Souls (46:27)

The number seventy is symbolically significant in Scripture. Protestant scholars note parallels with the seventy nations of Genesis 10 and the seventy elders of Israel (Exodus 24:1; Numbers 11:16). The covenant family that enters Egypt in miniature will emerge as a nation — from seventy to perhaps two million at the Exodus.

**Luther** reflected that this small family carried within it the promise of the Messiah. The preservation of these seventy souls was the preservation of the gospel itself.

### The Reunion (46:29-30)

"Joseph made ready his chariot and went up to meet Israel his father in Goshen. He presented himself to him and fell on his neck and wept on his neck a good while." Jacob's words upon seeing Joseph — "Now let me die, since I have seen your face and know that you are still alive" — echo Simeon's Nunc Dimittis in Luke 2:29-30. Protestant interpreters see in Jacob's satisfied peace a foreshadowing of the believer's rest upon seeing the fulfillment of God's promises.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 46 addresses core Protestant convictions:

- **God's promise transcends geography**: Leaving the promised land does not forfeit the promise. Protestant mission theology builds on this principle — God's purposes advance globally, not only in holy places.
- **Divine accompaniment**: "I myself will go down with you" echoes the great promise of Immanuel — God with us. Believers facing exile, displacement, or unknown futures can rely on God's committed presence.
- **Covenant continuity**: The genealogical list, though seemingly tedious, is theologically vital. It establishes that the entire covenant community enters Egypt intact, ready to become the nation God promised Abraham.
- **Providence in famine**: What looked like disaster (the famine) becomes the means of preservation. Protestant theology sees in this a paradigm: God uses adversity as His instrument, not His obstacle.
