## Jacob's Crossed-Hands Blessing

Genesis 48 records Jacob's adoption and blessing of Joseph's two sons, Ephraim and Manasseh. When Joseph brings his sons to the ailing Jacob, Jacob first recounts God's appearance at Luz (Bethel) and the covenant promises given there. He then formally adopts Ephraim and Manasseh as his own sons, giving them equal standing with Reuben and Simeon — effectively granting Joseph a double portion. When Joseph positions Manasseh, the firstborn, at Jacob's right hand and Ephraim at his left, Jacob deliberately crosses his hands, placing his right hand on the younger Ephraim's head. Joseph objects, but Jacob insists: "I know, my son, I know. He also shall become a people, and he also shall be great. Nevertheless, his younger brother shall be greater than he" (48:19).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Ephraim and Manasseh shall be mine, as Reuben and Simeon are" (48:5)

Jacob's adoption of Joseph's sons is legally and theologically significant. Protestant interpreters note:

| Action | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| Adoption as sons | Elevates Joseph's children to tribal status |
| "As Reuben and Simeon" | Joseph receives the double portion normally due the firstborn |
| Reuben's displacement | Consequences of Reuben's sin (35:22) now formalized |
| Two tribes from Joseph | Joseph's line becomes Ephraim and Manasseh in the tribal allotment |

**Calvin** observed that this adoption demonstrates God's freedom to reorder human hierarchies according to His purposes. Birthright and inheritance are not determined by natural order but by divine election.

### The Crossed Hands (48:13-20)

The crossing of Jacob's hands is one of the most striking images in Genesis. Despite Joseph's objection, Jacob intentionally places the greater blessing on the younger son. Protestant interpreters identify a recurring pattern:

- **Isaac over Ishmael** (the younger receives the promise)
- **Jacob over Esau** (the younger supplants the elder)
- **Ephraim over Manasseh** (the younger receives the right-hand blessing)
- **David, the youngest son** (chosen over his elder brothers)

This pattern illustrates the Protestant doctrine of unconditional election: God's choice is sovereign, not based on human convention, merit, or birth order. Paul appeals to this principle explicitly in Romans 9:10-13.

**Luther** noted the irony that Jacob, who himself received the blessing intended for the firstborn, now consciously enacts the same reversal. What was obtained by deception in chapter 27 is here performed openly and deliberately by divine direction.

### "The Angel who has redeemed me from all evil, bless the boys" (48:16)

Jacob's blessing invokes "the Angel" (*ha-mal'ak*) who has redeemed him — the same figure he wrestled at Peniel (32:24-30) and who appeared at Bethel. Protestant interpreters, following the Reformers, identify this as a Christophany — a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ. Jacob invokes three persons: "God, before whom my fathers Abraham and Isaac walked" (the Father), "the God who has been my shepherd all my life long" (the Son as shepherd, cf. John 10:11), and "the Angel who has redeemed me" (the redemptive work of Christ).

**Calvin** was cautious about reading full Trinitarian theology into this passage but acknowledged that the Angel is distinguished from other angels and equated with God Himself — pointing toward the incarnation.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 48 is rich in Protestant doctrinal significance:

- **Sovereign election**: The crossed-hands blessing is a visual parable of God's freedom to choose apart from human expectation. Election is God's prerogative, not earned by birth order or merit.
- **Adoption and grace**: Jacob's adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh models the believer's adoption into God's family (Ephesians 1:5). Those who were "not my people" become full heirs.
- **The double portion**: Joseph's double portion through his two sons anticipates the principle that God rewards faithfulness abundantly. Yet even this reward is distributed according to grace, not human arrangement.
- **Christophany**: Jacob's invocation of the redeeming Angel connects the patriarchal promises to their fulfillment in Christ, reinforcing Protestant Christological reading of the Old Testament.
