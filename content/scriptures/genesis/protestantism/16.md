## Hagar and Ishmael

Genesis 16 narrates Sarai's plan to obtain an heir through her servant Hagar, the resulting conflict, Hagar's flight, and the first appearance of the "angel of the Lord" in Scripture. The chapter explores the tension between human impatience and divine promise, a theme that Paul will develop allegorically in Galatians 4.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Sarai's Plan (16:1-3)

"Now Sarai, Abram's wife, had borne him no children. She had a female Egyptian servant whose name was Hagar. And Sarai said to Abram, 'Behold now, the Lord has prevented me from bearing children. Go in to my servant; it may be that I shall obtain children by her.'"

The plan was legally and culturally acceptable in the ancient Near East — surrogate motherhood through a servant was a well-attested practice. Yet Protestant interpreters consistently view it as a failure of faith:

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Culturally | Acceptable — following Mesopotamian custom |
| Theologically | A lapse of faith — trying to fulfill God's promise by human means |
| Narratively | Creates conflict that persists for generations |

**Calvin** was direct: Sarai and Abram sinned by taking matters into their own hands rather than waiting for God's timing. The promise of Genesis 15 was clear, yet ten years of barrenness (16:3) eroded their patience.

**Luther** noted the irony: the very faith celebrated in chapter 15 falters in chapter 16. The patriarch who believed God for offspring as numerous as the stars now resorts to human engineering. This pattern — great faith followed by significant failure — is characteristic of biblical saints and demonstrates that faith is sustained by grace, not human consistency.

### "And Abram listened to the voice of Sarai" (16:2)

Protestant commentators frequently draw a parallel to Genesis 3:17: "Because you have listened to the voice of your wife" — Adam and Abram both yield to their wives' counsel against God's revealed will. The echo is intentional: the narrator signals that this decision, like Eve's, will bring painful consequences.

### Conflict Between Sarai and Hagar (16:4-6)

When Hagar conceives, she "looked with contempt on her mistress." Sarai responds harshly, and Abram passively permits it: "Your servant is in your power; do to her as you please." Hagar flees into the wilderness.

Protestant theology observes:

- Sin begets more sin — the ill-conceived plan produces pride, jealousy, and cruelty
- Abram's passivity recalls Adam's silence in Eden — the patriarch fails to lead
- Hagar, though a secondary figure, is not invisible to God

### The Angel of the Lord (16:7-14)

"The angel of the Lord found her by a spring of water in the wilderness."

This is the first appearance of the "angel of the Lord" (*malak YHWH*) in Scripture. Protestant theology has given this figure significant attention:

- **Many Reformed interpreters** (including Calvin) identify the angel of the Lord as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (a Christophany), since the angel speaks as God and is identified with God (16:13)
- **Lutheran tradition** similarly tends toward the Christophanic interpretation
- **Some evangelical scholars** distinguish the angel from God while acknowledging the angel bears divine authority

The angel's message to Hagar contains both command and promise:

- **Command**: "Return to your mistress and submit to her" (16:9) — God does not endorse flight from suffering but provides purpose within it
- **Promise**: "I will surely multiply your offspring so that they cannot be numbered" (16:10) — Hagar receives her own promise of descendants
- **Prophecy**: Ishmael ("God hears") will be "a wild donkey of a man" (16:12) — independent, restless, in conflict

### "You are a God of seeing" (16:13)

Hagar's response is remarkable — she names God: *El Roi*, "the God who sees me." She is the only person in Genesis who gives God a name. Protestant interpreters highlight:

- God sees the outcast and the afflicted
- Divine compassion extends beyond the covenant line
- Hagar's encounter foreshadows the gospel's reach to all peoples

### The Birth of Ishmael (16:15-16)

Abram is eighty-six years old when Ishmael is born. Thirteen years will pass before God speaks again (chapter 17). Protestant commentators see the silence as significant: the years with Ishmael represent the consequences of acting "according to the flesh" — a long detour, though not outside God's providential plan.

## Theological Significance

1. **Flesh versus Spirit**: Paul's allegory in Galatians 4:21-31 interprets Hagar and Sarah as representing two covenants — law and grace, flesh and Spirit. Ishmael, born "according to the flesh," contrasts with Isaac, born "through promise"
2. **God's sovereignty over human failure**: Ishmael's birth is not God's plan A, yet God incorporates it into his purposes and blesses Ishmael in his own right
3. **The God who sees**: *El Roi* reveals a God who is attentive to suffering, even the suffering of those on the margins
4. **Patience and faith**: The chapter warns against attempting to achieve God's purposes through human scheming — a failure of the *sola fide* principle: trusting God alone for what he has promised
