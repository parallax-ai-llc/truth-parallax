## The Brothers Return with Benjamin

Genesis 43 narrates the second journey to Egypt. As famine worsens, Jacob reluctantly agrees to send Benjamin, persuaded by Judah's pledge to serve as personal surety for the boy's safety. The brothers bring double money (to return what was mysteriously placed in their sacks) along with gifts of balm, honey, spices, myrrh, pistachios, and almonds. Upon arrival, Joseph sees Benjamin and orders a feast prepared at his house. The brothers fear they are being lured into a trap because of the returned money, but Joseph's steward reassures them: "Your God and the God of your father has put treasure in your sacks" (43:23). Simeon is restored to them. When Joseph enters and sees Benjamin, he is overcome with emotion and weeps privately. At the feast, the brothers are seated in birth order — to their astonishment — and Benjamin receives five times the portion of the others.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "I will be a pledge of his safety... let me bear the blame forever" (43:9)

Judah's offer to serve as surety for Benjamin marks a decisive moment in his moral transformation. Protestant interpreters trace Judah's arc:

| Chapter | Judah's Action | Moral Direction |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| 37 | Proposes selling Joseph | Self-serving — profits from a brother's suffering |
| 38 | Withholds Shelah, visits a prostitute | Failure of responsibility and integrity |
| 38:26 | Confesses "She is more righteous than I" | Awakening of conscience |
| 43 | Pledges himself as surety for Benjamin | Self-sacrifice — risks himself for another |
| 44 | Offers himself as slave in Benjamin's place | Complete transformation — substitutionary love |

**Calvin** saw in Judah's pledge a foreshadowing of Christ's suretyship — the one from whose tribe the Messiah would come offers himself in another's place.

### "Your God and the God of your father has put treasure in your sacks" (43:23)

The steward's words are theologically loaded. A pagan household servant attributes the mysterious money to the God of Israel. Protestant interpreters see this as evidence that God's providence is discernible even to those outside the covenant community. The steward's statement also reassures the brothers that they are not under judgment — the money was a gift, not a trap.

### Joseph's Emotion (43:30)

"His heart yearned for his brother, and he sought a place to weep." Joseph's private tears over Benjamin reveal that his harsh treatment of his brothers in chapter 42 was not motivated by cruelty. Protestant commentators compare Joseph's hidden compassion with God's own heart: the Lord sometimes disciplines those He loves while His ultimate purpose is restoration and reconciliation.

### Benjamin's Five Portions (43:34)

Benjamin receives five times the food of his brothers. Protestant interpreters note several possible meanings:

- A test of whether the brothers will be jealous of a favored younger son (as they were of Joseph)
- A sign of Joseph's special love for his only full brother
- The number five as a recurring motif in the Joseph narrative (cf. 45:22; 47:2)

The brothers' response is telling: "they drank and were merry with him." There is no record of jealousy — suggesting genuine change.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 43 deepens the narrative's Protestant theological themes:

- **Substitutionary surety**: Judah's pledge anticipates the biblical theme of substitution that culminates in Christ. The one who will bear another's punishment in his place comes from the line that produces the ultimate Substitute.
- **Providence in details**: The seating in birth order, the returned money, the precise timing of Benjamin's journey — every detail is ordered by a providence that governs even dining arrangements. Reformed theology affirms that God's sovereignty extends to the smallest particulars.
- **Reconciliation as process**: Healing takes time. Joseph does not reveal himself yet because the brothers' transformation is not yet proven. Protestant pastoral wisdom recognizes that genuine reconciliation requires evidence of repentance, not merely the desire for restored fellowship.
- **Joy amid testing**: The chapter ends with a feast — an unexpected moment of joy in the midst of famine and anxiety. Protestant theology holds that God grants foretastes of restoration even before the full resolution is revealed.
