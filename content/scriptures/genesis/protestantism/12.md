## The Call of Abram

Genesis 12 marks the great turning point of the biblical narrative. After eleven chapters of universal history — creation, fall, flood, and Babel — God narrows his focus to one man and one family through whom he will bless all nations. The call of Abram inaugurates the patriarchal period and establishes the covenant promises that drive the rest of Scripture.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Call and Promise (12:1-3)

"Now the Lord said to Abram, 'Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you. And I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing.'"

Protestant theology identifies this as one of the most important passages in Scripture. The promise contains seven elements:

| Promise | Content | Fulfillment |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Great nation | Numerous descendants | Israel, and spiritually the church (Galatians 3:29) |
| Blessing | God's favor upon Abram | Material and spiritual prosperity |
| Great name | Renown and significance | Contrast with Babel's "make a name for ourselves" (11:4) |
| Be a blessing | Channel of blessing to others | The missional calling of God's people |
| Bless those who bless | Protection and favor | God's covenant loyalty to Abram's allies |
| Curse those who curse | Divine protection | God identifies with his people |
| All families blessed | Universal scope of redemption | The gospel of Christ (Galatians 3:8) |

**Calvin** emphasized that Abram was called out of idolatry (Joshua 24:2) — grace precedes faith, and God's initiative precedes human response. Abram did not seek God; God sought Abram.

**Luther** saw in this call the foundation of the gospel itself. Galatians 3:8 says that Scripture "preached the gospel beforehand to Abraham, saying, 'In you shall all the nations be blessed.'" The Abrahamic promise is thus the Old Testament form of the New Testament gospel.

### "So Abram went, as the Lord had told him" (12:4)

Abram's response is simple obedience. Hebrews 11:8 interprets this: "By faith Abraham obeyed when he was called to go out to a place that he was to receive as an inheritance. And he went out, not knowing where he was going."

Protestant theology highlights the relationship between faith and obedience here:

- Faith is trust in God's word and promises
- Obedience flows from faith as its necessary consequence
- Abram left everything familiar — country, kindred, father's house — at God's command
- This is not meritorious obedience but responsive trust

### Arrival in Canaan (12:5-9)

Abram passes through the land, and at Shechem God appears: "To your offspring I will give this land." Abram builds an altar and "called upon the name of the Lord."

Protestant interpreters note:

- The Canaanites were "in the land" — Abram is a stranger in the promised territory
- Altar-building marks Abram as a worshiper; he claims the land by faith, not by force
- The pattern of pilgrimage and worship defines the life of faith: Abram is simultaneously heir and sojourner

### Abram in Egypt (12:10-20)

A famine drives Abram to Egypt, where he asks Sarai to say she is his sister, fearing the Egyptians will kill him. Pharaoh takes Sarai into his household, God strikes Pharaoh with plagues, and Abram is sent away with great wealth.

This episode has troubled Protestant commentators:

- **Calvin** refused to excuse Abram's deception, calling it a "pious fraud" that was nevertheless sinful
- **Luther** was more sympathetic, seeing Abram as weak in faith but not abandoned by God
- **Evangelical interpreters** generally view it as an honest portrayal of a flawed patriarch, demonstrating that God's promises depend on God's faithfulness, not human perfection

The episode also foreshadows the Exodus: God's people go to Egypt because of famine, a foreign ruler oppresses them, God sends plagues, and they leave with great wealth.

## Theological Significance

1. **Sola gratia**: God's call is sovereign and unmerited — Abram was an idolater before God chose him
2. **Justification by faith**: Abram's response of faith (developed further in chapter 15) becomes the paradigm for how sinners are made right with God
3. **Covenant theology**: The Abrahamic promise is the seedbed from which all subsequent covenants grow
4. **Missional purpose**: God's particular calling of one man serves a universal aim — "all the families of the earth" are in view
5. **Typology**: Abram's journey from Ur to Canaan via Egypt foreshadows Israel's larger story and ultimately the believer's pilgrimage of faith
