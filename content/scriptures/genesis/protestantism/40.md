## Joseph Interprets Dreams in Prison

Genesis 40 takes place entirely within the royal prison. Pharaoh's cupbearer and baker are imprisoned and each has a dream on the same night. Joseph notices their troubled faces and offers to interpret, declaring, "Do not interpretations belong to God?" (40:8). The cupbearer dreamed of a vine with three branches producing grapes that he pressed into Pharaoh's cup; Joseph interprets this as restoration to his position within three days. The baker dreamed of three baskets of bread on his head, with birds eating from the top basket; Joseph interprets this as execution within three days. Both interpretations are fulfilled exactly. Yet the cupbearer forgets Joseph, and he remains in prison.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Do not interpretations belong to God?" (40:8)

This declaration is foundational for Protestant theology of revelation. Joseph disclaims any personal ability to interpret dreams, attributing all such knowledge to God alone. Protestant interpreters contrast this with the magicians and wise men of Egypt who will fail in chapter 41, highlighting that true wisdom comes only from God.

**Calvin** noted that Joseph's statement is a confession of faith: he directs the prisoners away from himself and toward the sovereign God who alone reveals hidden things (cf. Daniel 2:28).

### The Two Dreams and Their Outcomes (40:9-22)

| Element | Cupbearer's Dream | Baker's Dream |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Symbol | Vine with three branches | Three baskets of bread |
| Number "three" | Three days until restoration | Three days until execution |
| Action | Pressed grapes into Pharaoh's cup | Birds ate from the basket |
| Outcome | Restored to his position | Hanged on a tree |

Protestant commentators observe the stark contrast: two men in the same prison, on the same night, with structurally similar dreams, yet radically different fates. This serves as a sober reminder of God's sovereign freedom in judgment and mercy.

### "Remember me... and mention me to Pharaoh" (40:14)

Joseph's plea to the cupbearer is entirely reasonable — he seeks legitimate advocacy for his unjust imprisonment. Yet the cupbearer forgets him for two full years (40:23; 41:1). Protestant interpreters draw two lessons:

- **Human helpers fail**: Placing hope in human agents often leads to disappointment. Psalm 146:3 — "Put not your trust in princes."
- **God's timing is sovereign**: The cupbearer's forgetfulness is not a failure of providence but part of it. Joseph must remain in prison until God's appointed time.

**Luther** preached that these two forgotten years were among Joseph's greatest trials, testing whether his faith could endure not just suffering but the silence of God.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 40, though brief, advances crucial Protestant themes:

- **God as the source of all wisdom**: Joseph's declaration in 40:8 establishes a principle that runs through the entire Joseph narrative — human wisdom is derivative; God alone reveals truth. This resonates with the Reformation emphasis on *sola Scriptura* and the sufficiency of divine revelation.
- **Providence in delay**: The cupbearer's forgetfulness seems like a cruel twist, but it is precisely calibrated. Joseph will be remembered at exactly the right moment — when Pharaoh needs a dream interpreter (41:9-13). What looks like neglect is actually divine timing.
- **Faithful service in obscurity**: Joseph serves his fellow prisoners with care, noticing their distress and offering help. Protestant theology of vocation teaches that faithfulness in hidden places prepares one for greater responsibility.
- **Life and death in God's hands**: The contrasting fates of the cupbearer and baker illustrate that outcomes belong to God. Joseph interprets truly, but he does not determine the outcome.
