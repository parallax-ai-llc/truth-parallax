## Wickedness of Humanity and Noah's Commission

Genesis 6 marks a turning point in the primeval narrative. Humanity's corruption has reached a climax, God expresses grief over creation, and judgment is decreed — yet one man, Noah, finds grace. The chapter introduces the Flood narrative that will dominate chapters 6 through 9 and establishes themes of judgment and salvation that echo throughout Scripture.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The sons of God saw the daughters of man" (6:1-4)

This passage has generated three major Protestant interpretations:

| View | Description | Proponents |
|------|------------|-----------|
| Angelic beings | Fallen angels cohabited with human women | Luther, many early Protestants, some modern evangelicals |
| Sethite line | The godly line of Seth intermarried with Cain's ungodly line | Calvin, Augustine, most Reformed commentators |
| Royal tyrants | Ancient kings or rulers took women by force | Kline, some ANE scholarship |

**Calvin** strongly favored the Sethite interpretation, arguing that angels are incorporeal and cannot procreate. He saw the passage as a warning against the covenant community compromising through ungodly marriages.

**Luther** took the angelic view but focused on the theological point: however one identifies the "sons of God," the result is an escalation of evil that provokes divine judgment.

The Nephilim mentioned in 6:4 — "the mighty men of old, the men of renown" — remain enigmatic. Protestant scholarship generally cautions against speculative elaboration and focuses on the text's function: demonstrating the corruption that warranted the Flood.

### "The Lord regretted that he had made man" (6:6)

This anthropopathic language has been carefully treated in Protestant theology:

- **Calvin**: God accommodates himself to human understanding; the language expresses the intensity of divine displeasure, not a change in God's eternal decree
- **Reformed scholastics**: God is impassible in his being but relates to creation in real, responsive ways
- **Luther**: Took the grief more literally as genuine divine sorrow, emphasizing God's personal engagement with creation

### "But Noah found grace in the eyes of the Lord" (6:8)

This is the first occurrence of the word "grace" (*chen*) in Scripture. Protestant theology highlights:

- Grace precedes obedience: Noah found grace *before* he is described as righteous (6:9)
- Noah's righteousness was relative ("blameless in his generation"), not absolute
- The pattern of grace-then-obedience is the same structure that defines the gospel

**Evangelical interpreters** emphasize that Noah's salvation was by grace through faith, exactly as in the New Testament. Noah did not earn deliverance; he received it.

### "Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation" (6:9)

Protestant commentators are careful to qualify Noah's righteousness:

- It was a righteousness of faith, not sinless perfection (as Genesis 9:20-21 will demonstrate)
- "In his generation" may indicate he stood out against a wicked backdrop
- His walking "with God" echoes Enoch (5:24) and marks him as part of the faithful remnant

### The Ark Instructions (6:14-22)

God's detailed instructions for the ark demonstrate that salvation comes on God's terms. Protestant interpreters draw several points:

1. **Obedience to the word**: Noah built according to God's specifications — a principle underlying *sola scriptura*
2. **Typology**: The ark is a type of Christ, the only means of salvation from judgment (1 Peter 3:20-21)
3. **Covenant faithfulness**: God preserves a remnant even in universal judgment

## Theological Significance

1. **Total depravity confirmed**: "Every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (6:5) — this verse is foundational for the Reformed doctrine of total depravity
2. **Sovereign grace**: God takes the initiative to save; Noah did not devise his own rescue
3. **Judgment and salvation**: The Flood establishes the biblical pattern — God judges sin but provides a way of escape for those who trust him
4. **Christological typology**: The ark foreshadows salvation in Christ; as only those in the ark survived, only those "in Christ" are saved (2 Corinthians 5:17)
