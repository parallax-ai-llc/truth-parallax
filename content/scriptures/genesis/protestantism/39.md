## Joseph's Faithfulness and False Accusation

Genesis 39 follows Joseph into Egypt, where he is purchased by Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh and captain of the guard. The LORD is with Joseph, and everything he touches prospers. Potiphar recognizes Joseph's ability and places him in charge of his entire household. However, Potiphar's wife repeatedly attempts to seduce Joseph. He refuses, appealing to his loyalty to Potiphar and to God: "How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (39:9). When she seizes his garment and he flees, she accuses him of attempted assault. Potiphar has Joseph thrown into prison, but even there the LORD is with Joseph, and the prison keeper entrusts him with authority over the other prisoners.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The LORD was with Joseph" (39:2, 21)

This phrase appears as a refrain throughout the chapter (vv. 2, 3, 21, 23), forming a theological frame. Protestant interpreters see this as the key to understanding the entire Joseph narrative: God's presence is not measured by outward circumstances. Joseph is a slave and then a prisoner, yet the LORD is with him in both settings.

| Setting | Joseph's Status | Evidence of God's Presence |
|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Potiphar's house | Slave | Prospers in everything; entrusted with all Potiphar owns |
| Prison | Falsely accused prisoner | Finds favor with the keeper; entrusted with all prisoners |

**Calvin** emphasized that God's providence does not exempt believers from suffering but accompanies them through it, bringing fruit even in the darkest circumstances.

### "How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (39:9)

This is one of the most celebrated verses in Protestant moral theology. Joseph's refusal is grounded not in pragmatic fear of consequences but in theological conviction — sin is ultimately an offense against God.

**Luther** commended Joseph as a model of the *coram Deo* life — living before the face of God. When temptation comes, the decisive question is not "Will I get caught?" but "How does this stand before God?"

Protestant interpreters also note the contrast with Judah in the preceding chapter: where Judah yielded to sexual temptation, Joseph resists. The juxtaposition is intentional, highlighting Joseph's exceptional integrity.

### Joseph Flees (39:12)

Joseph's physical flight from temptation is taken as a model for 1 Corinthians 6:18 — "Flee from sexual immorality." Protestant pastoral teaching has long cited this episode as evidence that the godly response to certain temptations is not to stand and fight but to run.

## Joseph as a Type of Christ

The typological parallels deepen in this chapter:

- Falsely accused by those he served (39:14-18; cf. Matthew 26:59-60)
- Suffered unjustly without protest (39:20; cf. Isaiah 53:7)
- Brought blessing to everyone around him (39:5; cf. Ephesians 1:3)
- Descended further before exaltation (39:20; cf. Philippians 2:8)

## Theological Significance

Genesis 39 is central to several Protestant doctrines:

- **Providence in adversity**: God's sovereign plan advances through Joseph's unjust imprisonment. What appears to be a setback places Joseph exactly where he needs to be to interpret dreams (chapter 40) and eventually stand before Pharaoh (chapter 41).
- **Moral integrity as witness**: Joseph's faithfulness in Potiphar's house is not merely private virtue; it becomes a public testimony to the God of Israel. Even Potiphar sees that "the LORD was with him" (39:3).
- **The cost of righteousness**: Doing the right thing leads to prison. Protestant theology affirms that faithfulness does not guarantee immediate reward, but it does guarantee God's presence.
- **Sanctification in vocation**: Joseph serves with excellence whether as slave or prisoner, embodying the Reformation principle that all work done faithfully before God is sacred.
