## Abram and Lot Separate

Genesis 13 records the peaceful separation of Abram and Lot due to the abundance of their possessions, and God's reaffirmation of his promise to Abram after the separation. The chapter draws a sharp contrast between the way of faith (Abram) and the way of sight (Lot), establishing themes that will shape the narrative through Genesis 19.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Return from Egypt (13:1-4)

Abram returns from Egypt "very rich in livestock, in silver, and in gold" and journeys back to the altar he had built between Bethel and Ai, where he again "called upon the name of the Lord."

Protestant interpreters note the spiritual restoration: after the lapse of faith in Egypt (chapter 12), Abram returns to the place of worship. **Calvin** observed that this return to the altar signals repentance and renewed devotion — Abram's faith, though imperfect, is resilient because it rests on God's promises rather than on Abram's performance.

### The Dispute and Abram's Generosity (13:5-9)

"Then Abram said to Lot, 'Let there be no strife between you and me... for we are kinsmen. Is not the whole land before you? Separate yourself from me.'"

Abram, as the senior patriarch and the recipient of God's promise, had every right to choose first. Instead, he defers to Lot. Protestant theology draws important lessons:

| Abram's Action | Theological Principle |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Yields his right to choose | Faith enables generosity; those who trust God's provision need not grasp |
| Pursues peace | Romans 12:18 — "live peaceably with all" |
| Trusts God's promise | If God has promised the land, Abram need not seize it himself |

**Luther** praised Abram's magnanimity as the fruit of faith: "He who believes God's promise can afford to be generous." This stands in contrast to Babel's grasping ambition and the spirit of self-preservation displayed in Egypt.

### Lot's Choice (13:10-13)

"And Lot lifted up his eyes and saw that the Jordan Valley was well watered everywhere like the garden of the Lord, like the land of Egypt... So Lot chose for himself all the Jordan Valley."

Lot's decision is presented as reasonable by worldly standards but spiritually disastrous:

- He chose "by sight" — the land looked like Eden and Egypt (both loaded terms in Genesis)
- He moved progressively toward Sodom: first he "saw," then he "chose," then he "moved his tent as far as Sodom" (13:12), and eventually "sat in the gate of Sodom" (19:1)
- The narrator immediately adds: "Now the men of Sodom were wicked, great sinners against the Lord" (13:13)

**Reformed interpreters** have identified Lot's choice as a case study in worldliness: evaluating circumstances by material advantage rather than spiritual wisdom. Lot is not an unbeliever (2 Peter 2:7-8 calls him "righteous"), but he is a compromised believer whose choices bring grief.

### God's Reaffirmation to Abram (13:14-17)

"Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are, northward and southward and eastward and westward, for all the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring forever."

The parallels with Lot's looking are intentional:

| Lot | Abram |
|-----|-------|
| "Lifted up his eyes" to choose for himself | "Lift up your eyes" at God's command |
| Saw the Jordan Valley | Sees all the land in every direction |
| Chose a portion | Receives the whole |
| Relied on his own judgment | Receives God's promise |

God also promises descendants "as the dust of the earth" — an impossibility for the childless Abram, requiring faith. And God commands Abram to "walk through the length and the breadth of the land" — a symbolic act of possession by faith.

### Abram at Hebron (13:18)

Abram settles at the oaks of Mamre in Hebron and builds another altar. The pattern continues: wherever Abram goes, he worships. Hebron will become the burial site of the patriarchs (chapter 23) and later a significant city in Israel's history.

## Theological Significance

1. **Faith versus sight**: Abram trusts God's unseen promise; Lot trusts his own eyes — a contrast Paul echoes in 2 Corinthians 5:7
2. **Generosity born of faith**: Those who trust God's sovereignty can afford to yield their rights — a principle applied in Philippians 2:3-4
3. **Progressive compromise**: Lot's gradual drift toward Sodom warns against the incremental nature of worldly accommodation
4. **God's faithfulness**: After Abram acts in faith, God reaffirms and expands his promises — grace responds to faith
