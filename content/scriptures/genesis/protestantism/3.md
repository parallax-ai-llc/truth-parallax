## The Fall of Humanity

Genesis 3 describes the pivotal moment of human disobedience — the eating of the forbidden fruit and the cascade of consequences that follows. For Protestant theology, this chapter is arguably the most doctrinally significant passage in Genesis, serving as the foundation for the doctrines of original sin, total depravity, and the need for redemption.

## The Serpent's Temptation (3:1-5)

"Now the serpent was more crafty than any other beast of the field."

### Identity of the Serpent

- **Traditional Protestant view**: The serpent is Satan in disguise or his instrument (cf. Revelation 12:9, 20:2)
- **Luther**: Identified the serpent explicitly with the devil
- **Calvin**: The serpent was Satan's "tool," a real animal used as a mask

### The Structure of Temptation

Protestant interpreters often identify a three-fold pattern:

1. **Doubt**: "Did God actually say...?" — questioning God's word
2. **Denial**: "You will not surely die" — contradicting God's warning
3. **Desire**: "You will be like God" — appealing to pride and autonomy

## The Act of Disobedience (3:6-7)

"She took of its fruit and ate, and she also gave some to her husband who was with her, and he ate."

### Key Protestant Observations

- **Adam's presence**: "Who was with her" — Adam was present and passive
- **Sensory appeal**: The fruit appealed to sight, taste, and wisdom
- **Immediate consequence**: "The eyes of both were opened" — shame and self-consciousness

## The Consequences (3:8-24)

### God's Response

Protestant theology emphasizes both God's justice and mercy in the aftermath:

| Consequence | Recipient | Significance |
|------------|-----------|--------------|
| Crawling on belly | Serpent | Degradation of Satan's instrument |
| Enmity with woman's seed | Serpent | The *protoevangelium* (first gospel promise) |
| Pain in childbearing | Eve | Suffering enters the created order |
| Toil and thorns | Adam | Work becomes laborious, ground is cursed |
| Death | All humanity | "Dust you are and to dust you shall return" |

### The Protoevangelium (3:15)

"He shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel."

This verse is central to Protestant Christology:

- **Luther**: Called this the "first gospel" — a promise of Christ's victory over Satan
- **Calvin**: Saw the "seed of the woman" as pointing to Christ
- **Reformed theology**: The beginning of the "covenant of grace"

## Doctrine of Original Sin

Protestant theology, following Augustine and the Reformers, sees Genesis 3 as establishing:

- **Total depravity**: All aspects of human nature are affected by the Fall
- **Original guilt**: Adam acted as the representative head of humanity (Federal Theology)
- **Inherited corruption**: Sin is passed to all descendants (Romans 5:12-21)

## Theological Significance

Genesis 3 in Protestant theology:

1. Explains the origin of evil and suffering in God's good creation
2. Establishes humanity's need for a savior
3. Provides the first promise of redemption (protoevangelium)
4. Reveals God's character: just yet merciful (clothing Adam and Eve in 3:21)
