## Jacob before Pharaoh and Life in Goshen

Genesis 47 covers three major scenes. First, Joseph presents five of his brothers and then his father Jacob to Pharaoh. Jacob blesses Pharaoh and describes his life as a pilgrimage: "The days of the years of my sojourning are 130 years. Few and evil have been the days of the years of my life" (47:9). Pharaoh grants the family the best land in Goshen. Second, as the famine intensifies, Joseph administers the crisis by collecting all Egypt's money, then livestock, and finally the people's land in exchange for grain, making Pharaoh owner of virtually everything except the priests' holdings. Third, Jacob lives in Egypt seventeen years and, approaching death, makes Joseph swear to bury him not in Egypt but in Canaan with his fathers.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The days of the years of my sojourning are 130 years. Few and evil have been the days of the years of my life" (47:9)

Jacob's self-assessment before Pharaoh is one of the most poignant statements in Genesis. Protestant interpreters read it as a theology of pilgrimage:

| Patriarch | Years | Self-Assessment |
|-----------|-------|----------------|
| Abraham | 175 | "Died in a good old age, old and full of years" (25:8) |
| Isaac | 180 | Quiet blessing in old age |
| Jacob | 147 | "Few and evil" — marked by conflict, exile, grief |

**Calvin** noted that Jacob's description reflects the Reformed understanding of the Christian life as a *peregrinatio* — a journey through a world that is not our home. Hebrews 11:13-16 explicitly cites the patriarchs as pilgrims seeking a better country.

**Luther** saw in Jacob's honest lament a model of faith that does not pretend life is easy. Jacob acknowledges his suffering while still standing in God's promise. Authentic faith is compatible with honest sorrow.

### Jacob Blesses Pharaoh (47:7, 10)

The text notes twice that Jacob "blessed" Pharaoh. Protestant commentators observe the remarkable reversal: a wandering shepherd-patriarch blesses the most powerful monarch on earth. Hebrews 7:7 cites the principle that "the inferior is blessed by the superior." Jacob's blessing of Pharaoh signals that covenant status outranks worldly power.

### Joseph's Famine Administration (47:13-26)

Joseph's economic policies — acquiring all money, livestock, land, and labor for Pharaoh — have generated significant debate among Protestant interpreters:

- **Pragmatic reading**: Joseph saved lives. In an extreme crisis, centralized control was necessary for survival.
- **Critical reading**: Joseph's policies created a system of economic servitude that would later be turned against his own descendants.
- **Providential reading**: The text neither praises nor condemns the policies; it simply narrates God's preservation of life through imperfect means.

### "Do not bury me in Egypt" (47:29-31)

Jacob's insistence on burial in Canaan is an act of faith. He claims the promised land as his true home even though he dies in a foreign country. Protestant interpreters connect this to Hebrews 11:21-22 — the patriarchs' burial instructions are counted as acts of faith because they affirm confidence in God's promise of the land.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 47 contributes several themes to Protestant theology:

- **Life as pilgrimage**: Jacob's description of his life as "sojourning" grounds the Reformation understanding of the *vita Christiana* as a journey toward a heavenly homeland. Believers are aliens and strangers in this world (1 Peter 2:11).
- **Covenant superiority over worldly power**: Jacob blessing Pharaoh inverts the expected power dynamic. In God's economy, the bearer of the promise outranks the bearer of the scepter.
- **Stewardship in crisis**: Joseph's administration, whatever its moral complexity, demonstrates that believers are called to use wisdom and planning in the face of disaster. Stewardship is a vocational calling.
- **Faith in death**: Jacob's burial request demonstrates that faith extends beyond death. The promise of land — and ultimately of resurrection — shapes how believers face their mortality.
