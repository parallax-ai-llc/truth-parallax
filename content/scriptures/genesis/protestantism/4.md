## Cain and Abel

Genesis 4 narrates the first generation born after the Fall and demonstrates sin's rapid intensification. The chapter moves from worship and fratricide to the building of a civilization in rebellion against God, concluding with a glimmer of hope in the birth of Seth.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Offerings of Cain and Abel (4:3-5)

"Abel brought of the firstborn of his flock and of their fat portions. And the Lord had regard for Abel and his offering, but for Cain and his offering he had no regard."

Protestant interpreters have offered several explanations for why God accepted Abel's offering and rejected Cain's:

| Interpretation | Proponents | Reasoning |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Faith, not works | Luther, Calvin | Hebrews 11:4 — Abel offered "by faith" |
| Blood sacrifice required | Many Evangelicals | Foreshadows substitutionary atonement |
| Heart disposition | Reformed broadly | Cain's offering reflected a proud, self-righteous heart |
| Firstfruits principle | Various | Abel gave the "firstborn" and "fat portions"; Cain merely brought "an offering" |

**Calvin** insisted the decisive factor was not the material of the offering but the faith of the offerer. Abel's sacrifice was acceptable because it flowed from genuine trust in God's promise, while Cain approached God on his own terms.

**Luther** interpreted the passage as the first instance of the distinction between true and false worship — a theme that would become central to Reformation theology and the principle of *sola scriptura*: worship must be ordered by God's word, not human invention.

### "Sin is crouching at the door" (4:7)

This verse personifies sin as a predatory force. Protestant theology sees here a confirmation that after the Fall, sin is not merely an occasional failure but an indwelling power. The language anticipates Paul's discussion in Romans 7 of sin that "dwells in me."

Reformed theology emphasizes that God's warning to Cain demonstrates both common grace (God counseling even the unrighteous) and human responsibility — Cain was warned yet chose to act.

### The First Murder (4:8)

"Cain rose up against his brother Abel and killed him." The brevity of the narrative is striking. Protestant commentators note that this is the immediate fruit of the Fall: the protoevangelium of Genesis 3:15 spoke of enmity, and that enmity now manifests within the first family. **1 John 3:12** interprets Cain's act as flowing from being "of the evil one."

### "Am I my brother's keeper?" (4:9)

Cain's defiant question to God reveals the depth of sin's effect on conscience and community. Protestant interpreters see in this exchange a pattern: sin produces not only violence but evasion and self-justification. God's response — "The voice of your brother's blood is crying to me from the ground" — establishes that God hears the cry of the innocent, a theme running through all of Scripture.

## The Line of Cain (4:17-24)

The genealogy of Cain presents the development of culture alongside the escalation of sin:

- **Enoch** — city-building (civilization without God)
- **Jabal** — livestock (nomadic culture)
- **Jubal** — music (arts)
- **Tubal-Cain** — metalworking (technology)
- **Lamech** — polygamy and the "Song of the Sword" (4:23-24), boasting of disproportionate violence

Protestant theologians observe a pattern of common grace: God allows cultural advancement even among the ungodly. Yet Lamech's song shows that apart from redemption, human achievement accompanies moral deterioration.

## The Birth of Seth (4:25-26)

"To Seth also a son was born, and he called his name Enosh. At that time people began to call upon the name of the Lord."

The chapter ends with hope. The line of Seth replaces Abel as the covenant line through which the "seed of the woman" (3:15) will come. Protestant covenant theology sees this as the beginning of the visible community of faith — the church in embryonic form.

## Theological Significance

1. **Justification by faith**: Abel's accepted offering foreshadows the principle that right standing before God comes through faith, not human merit
2. **Total depravity's progression**: Sin moves from disobedience (ch. 3) to murder (ch. 4) in a single generation
3. **Typology**: Abel is a type of Christ — the righteous sufferer whose blood speaks (Hebrews 12:24), though Christ's blood "speaks a better word"
4. **Two lines of humanity**: The Cain/Seth division foreshadows the biblical theme of the city of man versus the city of God
