## Judgment Falls on the Cities of the Plain

Genesis 19 narrates one of the most dramatic episodes of divine judgment in Scripture. The two angels who accompanied the LORD in chapter 18 arrive at Sodom in the evening, where Lot receives them with hospitality. The men of Sodom surround the house demanding access to the visitors, revealing the depth of the city's wickedness. The angels strike the mob with blindness, then urgently warn Lot to flee with his family. God rains sulfur and fire on Sodom and Gomorrah, destroying the cities and all their inhabitants. Lot's wife looks back and becomes a pillar of salt. The chapter closes with the troubling account of Lot's daughters and their incestuous offspring, Moab and Ammon.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah is so great" (19:13; cf. 18:20)

Protestant interpreters emphasize that God's judgment does not fall arbitrarily. The "outcry" language suggests the suffering of victims — the oppressed, the violated, the stranger. **Ezekiel 16:49** broadens the sin of Sodom to include pride, excess of food, and neglect of the poor, and Reformed commentators regularly cite this cross-reference to show that Sodom's wickedness was comprehensive, not reducible to a single category.

### Lot's Wife (19:26)

Jesus references Lot's wife directly in Luke 17:32: "Remember Lot's wife." Protestant preachers from Bunyan to Spurgeon have treated her as a warning against spiritual hesitation:

| Theme | Application |
|-------|------------|
| Looking back | Attachment to the world one is called to leave |
| Disobedience | Direct violation of the angel's command (19:17) |
| Judgment on half-heartedness | Outward departure without inward conviction |
| Pillar of salt | A lasting memorial of the cost of unbelief |

**Calvin** warned that Lot's wife was physically delivered but spiritually remained in Sodom — her heart never left the city. This became a standard illustration in Puritan preaching about the danger of professing faith without possessing it.

### The Rescue of Lot (19:16, 29)

Verse 16 is striking: "When he hesitated, the men grasped his hand and the hands of his wife and of his two daughters and led them safely out of the city, for the LORD was merciful to them." Protestant theology draws several conclusions:

- **Sovereign mercy**: Lot is saved not by his own resolve but by God's gracious intervention. He literally has to be dragged to safety.
- **Abraham's intercession answered**: Verse 29 explicitly states God "remembered Abraham" when He rescued Lot, connecting intercessory prayer to divine action.
- **Righteous but weak**: 2 Peter 2:7-8 calls Lot "righteous," yet his conduct in this chapter reveals deep compromise. Protestant commentators use this tension to illustrate justification by faith apart from moral perfection.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 19 functions in Protestant theology as a primary text on divine judgment and sovereign rescue:

- **Pattern of judgment**: Jesus and the apostles repeatedly point to Sodom as the archetype of catastrophic judgment on sin (Matt. 10:15; 2 Pet. 2:6; Jude 7). Protestant eschatology often parallels Sodom's destruction with the final judgment.
- **Typology of rescue**: Just as Lot was pulled from Sodom before fire fell, Protestant interpreters see a type of God's deliverance of believers before or through judgment — a pattern invoked in debates about eschatological sequences.
- **The limits of proximity**: Lot chose Sodom for its prosperity (13:10-11) and rose to sit in its gate (19:1), yet he could not reform it. Reformed pastors frequently draw the lesson that cultural assimilation without transformation leads to spiritual erosion.
- **Common grace withdrawn**: The destruction illustrates what happens when God's patience reaches its limit and restraining grace is removed from a society.
