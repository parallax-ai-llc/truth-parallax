## The Child of Promise Arrives

Genesis 21 marks the long-awaited fulfillment of God's promise: Isaac is born to Abraham and Sarah. The chapter then records the painful expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael at Sarah's insistence, God's preservation of Hagar in the wilderness, and concludes with Abraham's treaty with Abimelech at Beersheba. The chapter weaves together themes of joy, domestic conflict, divine faithfulness, and covenant diplomacy.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The LORD visited Sarah as he had said" (21:1)

Protestant theology places enormous emphasis on the precision of this language. God did exactly what He promised, at exactly the time He specified (cf. 18:10). **Calvin** treated this verse as a model for how all divine promises operate — they may be delayed beyond human patience, but they are never abandoned. The birth of Isaac after twenty-five years of waiting (from the initial promise in 12:2) became a standard Puritan illustration of patient faith.

### Sarah's Laughter Transformed (21:6)

Sarah declares, "God has made laughter for me; everyone who hears will laugh over me." The laughter of disbelief (18:12) has become the laughter of joy. Protestant interpreters see a pattern:

| Stage | Reference | Type of Laughter |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Abraham's laughter | 17:17 | Wonder mixed with doubt |
| Sarah's laughter | 18:12 | Skeptical disbelief |
| Isaac's name ("he laughs") | 21:3 | Embodied promise |
| Sarah's laughter | 21:6 | Joy and testimony |

This progression illustrates the Reformation theme that God transforms doubt into praise, working through human weakness rather than bypassing it.

### The Expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael (21:9-21)

This passage is theologically loaded. Paul explicitly allegorizes it in Galatians 4:21-31, reading Hagar as the old covenant of law and Sarah as the new covenant of grace. Protestant interpreters have consistently followed Paul's typological reading:

- **Ishmael** represents those born "according to the flesh" — human effort to fulfill divine promises
- **Isaac** represents those born "according to the Spirit" — God's sovereign initiative
- **The expulsion** represents the incompatibility of law-based righteousness with grace-based righteousness

Yet Protestant commentators also attend to the pastoral dimension. God hears Ishmael's cry (21:17) and provides for him, demonstrating that those outside the covenant line are not outside God's care. **Matthew Henry** noted that God's compassion extends beyond the boundaries of election.

### Treaty at Beersheba (21:22-34)

Abraham's treaty with Abimelech demonstrates the patriarch's growing stature and the blessing that flows through him to surrounding nations — a partial fulfillment of "in you all families of the earth shall be blessed" (12:3). The planting of a tamarisk tree and calling on "the LORD, the Everlasting God" (El Olam) establishes worship in the land of promise.

## Theological Significance

- **Fulfillment and faith**: Isaac's birth validates the entire structure of Abrahamic faith. Protestant soteriology anchors itself in this pattern: God promises, faith receives, God fulfills. Romans 4:18-22 builds its argument for justification by faith directly on this narrative.
- **Election and grace**: The Isaac-Ishmael distinction is foundational to Reformed theology of election. Paul's use of this narrative (Rom. 9:7-8; Gal. 4:28) establishes that covenant membership is determined by divine promise, not natural descent or human effort.
- **God hears the outcast**: Hagar's rescue in the wilderness shows that God's sovereign election of Isaac does not entail cruelty toward Ishmael. Protestant pastoral theology draws comfort from this — God's particular purposes do not exhaust His general compassion.
