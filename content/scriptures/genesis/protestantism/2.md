## The Second Creation Account

Genesis 2 provides a more intimate and detailed account of creation, focusing on the formation of the first human and the establishment of the Garden of Eden. Protestant interpreters have long debated the relationship between this chapter and Genesis 1.

## Relationship to Genesis 1

### Complementary View
Most conservative Protestant scholars see Genesis 2 as a complementary, not contradictory, account — a "zoom in" on Day 6 of Genesis 1:

- **Calvin**: Chapter 2 gives "a more detailed narrative of the creation of man"
- **Waltke**: The two accounts are "complementary perspectives on the same event"

### Source-Critical View
Some Protestant scholars accept the Documentary Hypothesis, seeing Genesis 1 (Priestly source) and Genesis 2 (Yahwist source) as independent accounts woven together by later editors.

## The Formation of Adam (2:7)

"Then the LORD God formed man of dust from the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life."

Protestant interpretation emphasizes:

- **Humanity's creatureliness**: Formed from dust, humans are part of creation
- **Divine intimacy**: The breath of life comes directly from God
- **Body-soul unity**: The human person is an integrated whole, not a soul trapped in a body

## The Garden of Eden (2:8-17)

The Garden represents God's ideal environment for human flourishing:

- **Provision**: "Every tree that is pleasant to the sight and good for food" (2:9)
- **Vocation**: "To work it and keep it" (2:15) — meaningful labor before the Fall
- **Probation**: The tree of knowledge as a test of obedience

### The Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil

| Interpretation | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| Moral autonomy | The tree represents the temptation to define good and evil apart from God |
| Wisdom/maturity | Knowledge reserved for divine prerogative |
| Experiential knowledge | Knowledge gained through disobedience rather than trust |

## The Creation of Eve (2:18-25)

"It is not good that the man should be alone; I will make him a helper fit for him."

### Protestant Views on Gender and Marriage

- **Complementarian**: Eve as "helper" indicates a distinct, complementary role
- **Egalitarian**: "Helper" (*ezer*) is used of God elsewhere (Psalm 121:1-2), implying no subordination
- **Marriage**: The "one flesh" union (2:24) is foundational for Protestant marriage theology

## Theological Significance

- **Covenant of Works**: Reformed theology often sees 2:16-17 as establishing a covenant with Adam
- **Sabbath institution**: 2:1-3 grounds the practice of weekly rest
- **Marriage**: 2:24 is cited by Jesus (Matthew 19:5) as the divine norm for marriage
