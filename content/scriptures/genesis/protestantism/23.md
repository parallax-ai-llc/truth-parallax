## The First Purchase of Promised Land

Genesis 23 records the death of Sarah at age 127 in Kiriath-arba (Hebron) and Abraham's careful negotiation to purchase a burial site. Abraham approaches the Hittites, identifies himself as "a stranger and sojourner" among them, and negotiates with Ephron the Hittite for the cave of Machpelah. Despite Ephron's initial offer to give the land freely, Abraham insists on paying the full price of four hundred shekels of silver. The cave becomes the first permanent foothold of the covenant family in the promised land.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "A stranger and a sojourner among you" (23:4)

Abraham's self-description captures a defining posture of faith that reverberates through the entire biblical narrative. Hebrews 11:13-16 explicitly connects this language to the faith of the patriarchs, who "acknowledged that they were strangers and exiles on the earth" and were seeking "a better country, that is, a heavenly one." Protestant theology draws two key applications:

- **Pilgrim identity**: Believers are citizens of heaven living as resident aliens in the world. This shaped Puritan self-understanding profoundly, from Bunyan's *Pilgrim's Progress* to the New England settlers who described themselves as "pilgrims."
- **Already and not yet**: Abraham possessed the promise of the land but not its reality. This tension between present promise and future fulfillment is central to Protestant eschatology.

### The Negotiation with Ephron (23:7-16)

The detailed commercial transaction follows well-attested ancient Near Eastern patterns of land purchase:

| Stage | Verse | Action |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Public request | 23:3-4 | Abraham addresses the assembly |
| Offer of gift | 23:6 | Hittites offer any tomb |
| Specific request | 23:9 | Abraham names the cave of Machpelah |
| Ephron offers freely | 23:11 | Land offered as gift |
| Abraham insists on payment | 23:13 | Full price demanded |
| Price named and paid | 23:15-16 | 400 shekels weighed publicly |

**Calvin** observed that Abraham refused the gift to ensure an incontestable legal claim. Protestant commentators see this as an act of faith: Abraham treated the land as genuinely valuable because God had promised it to his descendants, and he would not receive it under ambiguous terms.

### Abraham's Insistence on Full Payment

Abraham's determination to pay full market value — even an inflated price, as some scholars suggest — has drawn attention from Protestant interpreters for several reasons:

1. **Integrity in dealings**: Abraham's conduct establishes a model of honest commercial ethics, a theme the Reformers applied to Christian vocation and marketplace behavior.
2. **Tangible claim**: By paying publicly, Abraham secured legal title that no one could later contest. The deed was witnessed by the entire community at the city gate.
3. **Faith made concrete**: Purchasing a burial plot in the promised land was an act of confidence that God's promise would outlast Abraham's own lifetime.

## Theological Significance

Though Genesis 23 lacks the dramatic revelations or theophanies of surrounding chapters, Protestant theology finds it deeply significant:

- **The down payment of promise**: Machpelah is the first parcel of the promised land owned by Abraham's family. Protestant interpreters see it as an earnest — a down payment guaranteeing the full inheritance to come. Paul uses similar language about the Holy Spirit as a "guarantee" (arrabon) of the believer's inheritance (Eph. 1:14).
- **Death within the covenant**: Sarah's death does not nullify the covenant. Abraham buries her in the promised land, expressing faith that death is not the final word. This anticipates the Protestant conviction that believers die "in Christ" and await resurrection.
- **Ordinary faithfulness**: Not every chapter of the life of faith involves dramatic testing or divine encounter. Sometimes faithfulness looks like negotiating honestly, grieving properly, and making practical provisions — all done in trust that God's promises endure beyond the grave.
