## Jacob's Return to Bethel

Genesis 35 marks a pivotal transition in the Jacob narrative. God commands Jacob to return to Bethel, the place where God first appeared to him during his flight from Esau (Genesis 28). Jacob orders his household to put away foreign gods and purify themselves. At Bethel, God reaffirms the covenant, formally changing Jacob's name to Israel and repeating the promises of land and descendants. The chapter also records two deaths — Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin near Bethlehem, and Isaac dies at Hebron — and one grievous sin, Reuben's violation of Bilhah, his father's concubine.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Put away the foreign gods that are among you" (35:2)

Jacob's command to purify the household before returning to Bethel is read by Protestant interpreters as a paradigm for covenant renewal. Just as Jacob's family had to cleanse themselves of idolatrous influences accumulated during their time in Shechem, believers must continually repent and remove idols from their lives.

**Calvin** saw this episode as proof that even the covenant family was not immune to idolatry, requiring deliberate acts of repentance and rededication.

### "Your name shall be Israel" (35:10)

Although Jacob was already renamed in Genesis 32:28 during his wrestling at Peniel, here God formally reaffirms the name change at Bethel. Protestant scholars note the literary significance:

| Event | Location | Significance |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| First naming (32:28) | Peniel — struggle with God | Name given in the context of personal transformation |
| Reaffirmation (35:10) | Bethel — house of God | Name confirmed in the context of covenant worship |

The dual naming emphasizes that identity in God is both personally experienced and publicly confirmed within the worshipping community.

### Rachel's Death and Benjamin's Birth (35:16-20)

Rachel names her dying son *Ben-oni* ("son of my sorrow"), but Jacob renames him *Benjamin* ("son of the right hand"). Protestant interpreters find deep typological significance:

- **Luther** noted that sorrow and glory are intertwined: Benjamin's birth through Rachel's suffering prefigures how God brings life through death.
- **Matthew Henry** observed that the father's renaming reflects faith — refusing to let grief define the child's identity, instead claiming God's promise.

Rachel's burial near Bethlehem later becomes significant in Jeremiah 31:15 and Matthew 2:18, connecting this moment to the messianic narrative.

### Reuben's Sin (35:22)

"Reuben went and lay with Bilhah his father's concubine. And Israel heard of it." The terse narration, with no recorded response from Jacob, is striking. Protestant commentators understand Jacob's silence here as a deliberate literary device — his judgment is deferred until the deathbed blessing in Genesis 49:3-4, where Reuben loses the birthright. This demonstrates the principle that sin has consequences even when immediate judgment is withheld.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 35 is rich in Protestant theological themes:

- **Covenant renewal**: The return to Bethel models the pattern of repentance, purification, and recommitment that marks the Christian life. Believers are called to return continually to the place of their first encounter with God.
- **God's faithfulness**: Despite the moral failures recorded in chapters 34-35, God reaffirms His covenant with Jacob. Grace is not contingent on human performance.
- **Providence through suffering**: Rachel's death in childbirth, while heartbreaking, produces the twelfth son, completing the tribal structure of Israel. God's purposes advance even through grief.
- **The twelve sons complete**: With Benjamin's birth, all twelve sons of Jacob are accounted for, setting the stage for the Joseph narrative and the formation of the twelve tribes.
