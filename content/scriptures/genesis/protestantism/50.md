## The End of Genesis and the Persistence of the Promise

Genesis 50 brings the first book of Scripture to its close. Joseph mourns his father, and Jacob is embalmed in the Egyptian manner — a process lasting forty days, with seventy days of mourning. A great procession accompanies Jacob's body to Canaan, where he is buried in the cave of Machpelah with Abraham, Sarah, Isaac, Rebekah, and Leah. After the burial, the brothers fear that Joseph will now take revenge. They send a message claiming that Jacob instructed Joseph to forgive them. Joseph weeps and delivers his definitive theological statement: "As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring it about that many people should be kept alive" (50:20). He reassures them and provides for their families. Joseph lives to 110 years, sees his great-grandchildren, and before dying makes the sons of Israel swear to carry his bones out of Egypt when God visits them. His body is embalmed and placed in a coffin in Egypt.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (50:20)

This is arguably the single most important verse for the Protestant doctrine of divine providence. It articulates what theologians call "compatibilism" — the coexistence of genuine human moral responsibility and absolute divine sovereignty:

| Agent | Intention | Action | Moral Status |
|-------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| The brothers | Evil — jealousy, hatred, greed | Sold Joseph into slavery | Culpable, sinful |
| God | Good — preservation of many lives | Directed the same events toward salvation | Righteous, sovereign |

**Calvin** regarded this verse as the definitive biblical statement on providence: "Joseph indicates that the wickedness of his brothers was overruled by the secret providence of God... God has wonderful methods of accomplishing what He has decreed."

**Luther** emphasized that this verse does not excuse sin but reframes it: "The brothers' sin remains sin, but God's governance is greater. He takes the worst that men can do and bends it toward life."

The Westminster Confession of Faith (V.4) reflects this theology: "The almighty power, unsearchable wisdom, and infinite goodness of God so far manifest themselves in his providence, that it extendeth itself even to the first fall, and all other sins of angels and men; and that not by a bare permission, but such as hath joined with it a most wise and powerful bounding."

### "Am I in the place of God?" (50:19)

Joseph's rhetorical question renounces the right to judge or avenge. Protestant interpreters see this as the practical outworking of a robust theology of sovereignty: if God is the ultimate judge, then human vengeance is both unnecessary and presumptuous. This connects directly to Paul's instruction in Romans 12:19 — "Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God."

### "God will surely visit you" (50:24-25)

Joseph's deathbed prophecy anticipates the Exodus. The Hebrew verb *paqad* ("visit") carries the sense of divine intervention — God will actively come to deliver His people. Joseph's request that his bones be carried out of Egypt is an act of faith (Hebrews 11:22), expressing confidence that the covenant promise of land will be fulfilled.

This request is honored centuries later when Moses carries Joseph's bones out of Egypt (Exodus 13:19), and they are finally buried at Shechem (Joshua 24:32) — completing a journey that spans the entire Pentateuch.

## The Book Ends with a Coffin

Protestant commentators have long noted the striking final image of Genesis: "So Joseph died, being 110 years old. They embalmed him, and he was put in a coffin in Egypt." The book that begins with creation, light, and life ends with death, embalming, and a coffin in a foreign land. This creates a profound narrative tension:

- Genesis 1: "In the beginning God created..." — fullness of life
- Genesis 50: "...a coffin in Egypt" — death in exile

The unresolved ending points forward. The promise is not abandoned, but it is not yet fulfilled. Protestant theology reads this as a reminder that the Old Testament is incomplete without the New — the story of redemption stretches from Genesis to Revelation, from coffin to resurrection.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 50 crystallizes the book's central Protestant themes:

- **Providence over evil**: The definitive statement of 50:20 anchors Protestant confidence that God's sovereign purpose cannot be defeated by human sin. This verse has sustained believers through persecution, injustice, and inexplicable suffering across centuries.
- **Forgiveness rooted in theology**: Joseph's forgiveness is not sentimental but theological. He forgives because he sees God's hand. Protestant teaching holds that the deepest motivation for forgiving others is the recognition that God governs all things for good (Romans 8:28).
- **Covenant hope beyond death**: Both Jacob's burial in Canaan and Joseph's bones-oath express faith in promises not yet seen. The book of Genesis ends in hope — not yet fulfillment — modeling the posture of faith that characterizes the entire biblical narrative.
- **The forward-looking word**: "God will surely visit you" is a prophetic declaration that the story is not over. Protestant theology sees in this the persistent rhythm of promise and fulfillment that culminates in Christ.
