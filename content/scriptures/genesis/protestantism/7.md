## The Flood Begins

Genesis 7 records the execution of divine judgment announced in chapter 6. Noah enters the ark with his family and the animals, the floodwaters come, and every living thing on dry land perishes. The chapter is structured around Noah's obedience, God's sovereign action, and the totality of destruction.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Come into the ark" (7:1)

God invites Noah to enter the ark. Protestant interpreters have noted the significance of the word "come" rather than "go" — suggesting that God himself is present within the ark, welcoming Noah into safety. **Matthew Henry** saw this as an expression of God's hospitable grace: the Lord does not merely command obedience but draws his people into refuge.

### Clean and Unclean Animals (7:2-3)

"Take with you seven pairs of all clean animals... and a pair of the animals that are not clean."

The distinction between clean and unclean animals before the Mosaic law raises interpretive questions:

| Question | Protestant Response |
|----------|-------------------|
| How did Noah know which animals were clean? | God revealed this distinction before Moses; the categories are not arbitrary but reflect divine order |
| Why seven pairs of clean animals? | To provide for sacrifice after the Flood (8:20) without extinguishing any species |
| Does this contradict 6:19 ("two of every sort")? | No — 6:19 gives the general instruction; 7:2-3 adds the specific detail for clean animals |

**Calvin** argued that the clean/unclean distinction was part of God's ongoing instruction to his people and anticipated the sacrificial system — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

### "And the Lord shut him in" (7:16)

This brief statement is theologically rich in Protestant interpretation:

- **Divine initiative**: God himself seals the ark — salvation is secured by God, not by human effort
- **Irrevocable judgment**: Once God shuts the door, the opportunity for escape is past
- **Security of the believer**: Those whom God places in the ark are kept safe by his hand

**Luther** saw in this verse a picture of justification: God covers and protects the believer, enclosing them in Christ as Noah was enclosed in the ark. The shutting of the door also anticipates Jesus's parable of the closed door (Luke 13:25) and the wise and foolish virgins (Matthew 25:10).

### The Universality of the Flood (7:19-23)

"All the high mountains under the whole heaven were covered... Everything on the dry land in whose nostrils was the breath of life died."

The scope of the Flood has been debated among Protestants:

| View | Description | Proponents |
|------|------------|-----------|
| Global flood | The Flood covered the entire earth | Luther, Calvin, most conservative evangelicals |
| Local/regional flood | The Flood covered the known inhabited world | Some evangelical scholars (e.g., John Walton, Hugh Ross) |
| Theological narrative | The account uses universal language for theological emphasis | Some mainline Protestant scholars |

Most Reformed and evangelical interpreters hold to a universal flood, reading the language of totality ("all flesh," "everything," "under the whole heaven") as deliberately comprehensive. The New Testament references to the Flood as a type of final judgment (2 Peter 3:5-7, Matthew 24:37-39) reinforce the global reading.

### Noah's Obedience (7:5, 9, 16)

Three times the text states that Noah did "as the Lord had commanded him." Protestant theology sees this as the pattern of faith working through obedience:

- Noah's obedience did not earn salvation — he had already "found grace" (6:8)
- His obedience was the fruit of faith, not its root
- This pattern anticipates the Reformation principle: saved by grace alone, through faith alone, but genuine faith is never alone — it produces works (James 2:22)

## Theological Significance

1. **Judgment is real**: The Flood demonstrates that God's warnings of judgment are not empty threats; sin has catastrophic consequences
2. **Salvation is particular**: Not all were saved — only those in the ark; Protestant theology draws a line from here to the doctrine of election
3. **Christ as the Ark**: As the ark was the sole means of rescue from divine judgment, so Christ is the only mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5)
4. **Typology of baptism**: 1 Peter 3:20-21 connects the Flood waters to baptism, a connection Protestant theology interprets as pointing to identification with Christ's death and resurrection
