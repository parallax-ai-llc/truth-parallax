## Jacob's Journey to Egypt

Jacob sets out for Egypt with all his household. At Beersheba, God appears to him in a night vision and reassures him: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for I will make of you a great nation there. I myself will go down with you to Egypt, and I will also bring you up again" (46:3-4). The chapter then provides a genealogical list of the seventy members of Jacob's family who entered Egypt. The narrative concludes with the emotional reunion of Jacob and Joseph in Goshen.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt" (46:3)

God's reassurance addresses a real fear: Egypt was associated with danger in the patriarchal narrative. Abraham's sojourn in Egypt involved deception and near-disaster (Genesis 12:10-20), and Isaac had been explicitly forbidden to go there (Genesis 26:2). Catholic theology sees in God's words a pattern of divine accompaniment — God does not merely permit the journey but promises to be present within it. The CCC (2575) describes how God "calls Moses from the midst of a burning bush" with similar words of assurance, establishing a pattern of divine commissioning.

### "I myself will go down with you... and I will also bring you up again" (46:4)

This promise operates on multiple levels. Literally, it assures Jacob of his return — at least in burial (Genesis 50:13). Prophetically, it points to the Exodus, when God will bring Jacob's descendants out of Egypt. Catholic typological reading sees in this descent and ascent the pattern of the Paschal Mystery: Christ descends into death and brings His people up with Him.

| Descent and Ascent | Old Testament | New Testament Fulfillment |
|---|---|---|
| Jacob goes down to Egypt | Genesis 46:3-4 | Christ's incarnation and descent |
| God accompanies His people | "I will go down with you" | Emmanuel — "God with us" (Matthew 1:23) |
| Promise of return | "I will bring you up" | Resurrection and ascension |

### The Seventy Souls (46:27)

The number seventy carries deep symbolic significance in biblical and Catholic tradition. Seventy represents the fullness of the nations (cf. the Table of Nations in Genesis 10, which lists seventy peoples). The CCC (765) notes that Jesus sent out seventy(-two) disciples (Luke 10:1), corresponding to the number of nations in Genesis 10. The seventy souls entering Egypt thus represent, in seed form, the universal scope of God's saving plan.

### The Reunion of Jacob and Joseph (46:29-30)

"Joseph made ready his chariot and went up to meet Israel his father in Goshen. He presented himself to him and fell on his neck and wept on his neck a good while" (46:29). Jacob's response — "Now let me die, since I have seen your face and know that you are still alive" (46:30) — echoes Simeon's *Nunc Dimittis* in Luke 2:29-30: "Now you are dismissing your servant in peace... for my eyes have seen your salvation." Catholic liturgical tradition incorporates the *Nunc Dimittis* into Night Prayer (Compline), and its Old Testament root in Jacob's words deepens its meaning.

## Theological Significance

**God's presence in transitions.** Jacob's journey from Canaan to Egypt marks a decisive transition in salvation history — the chosen family leaves the promised land. Catholic theology recognizes that God often calls His people into unfamiliar and even threatening situations. The CCC (145) describes Abraham's faith as a model of obedient trust in God's promises, and Jacob's journey continues this pattern. God's promise of accompaniment — "I will go down with you" — is the foundation of the Catholic understanding of divine providence in times of upheaval and exile.

**Genealogy and ecclesiology.** The detailed list of Jacob's descendants (46:8-27) serves a theological purpose beyond mere record-keeping. It establishes the identity of the people of God — naming each member of the community that will become Israel. Catholic ecclesiology similarly emphasizes that the Church is not an abstraction but a communion of concrete persons, each known by name to God. The CCC (2158) teaches that "God calls each one by name," affirming the dignity and irreplaceability of every person within the community of faith.

**The holy family in exile.** The migration of Jacob's family to Egypt has been read by Catholic tradition as a prefiguration of the Holy Family's flight to Egypt (Matthew 2:13-15). In both cases, God preserves His chosen ones by sending them into a foreign land. The connection reinforces Matthew's theological point that Jesus recapitulates the history of Israel in His own life.
