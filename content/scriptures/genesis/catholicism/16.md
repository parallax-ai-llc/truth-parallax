## Hagar and Ishmael

Genesis 16 recounts Sarai's plan to obtain offspring through her Egyptian servant Hagar, the resulting pregnancy and conflict, Hagar's flight into the wilderness, and the angel's appearance to her with promises about her son Ishmael. Catholic theology reads this chapter as a lesson in the consequences of impatience with God's timing, the dignity of the marginalised, and God's mercy toward all people — including those outside the primary covenant line.

## Sarai's Plan (16:1-4)

### Human Initiative and God's Providence

Sarai's proposal that Abram take Hagar as a concubine follows the custom of the ancient Near East (attested in the Code of Hammurabi and Nuzi tablets). Catholic moral theology evaluates this action carefully:

- **St. Augustine** (*City of God* XVI.25): Abram did not act from lust but from the desire for offspring; nevertheless, the plan represents a failure to trust fully in God's promise
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Sarai acted out of genuine suffering — her barrenness was a source of profound grief in that culture — but the plan substituted human calculation for divine providence
- **CCC 2374-2379**: The Church teaches that the desire for children is natural and good, but the means used to fulfil that desire must be morally ordered. Catholic bioethics draws on principles visible already in this narrative: the consequences of circumventing God's plan

### Consequences of the Plan

The immediate result is domestic conflict: Hagar "looked with contempt on her mistress" (16:4), and Sarai responds with harsh treatment. Catholic moral theology sees a cautionary pattern:

| Action | Consequence | Theological Lesson |
|--------|------------|-------------------|
| Sarai's plan to use Hagar | Hagar conceives and despises Sarai | Human schemes to fulfil God's promises create unforeseen suffering |
| Sarai blames Abram (16:5) | Abram abdicates responsibility | Failure to lead spiritually compounds the problem |
| Sarai treats Hagar harshly (16:6) | Hagar flees into the wilderness | Injustice toward the vulnerable has real human costs |

## The Angel and Hagar (16:7-14)

### God Sees the Marginalised

The angel of the LORD finds Hagar "by a spring of water in the wilderness" (16:7). This is the first appearance of the angel of the LORD in Scripture, and it is directed not to a patriarch but to a foreign slave woman.

- **CCC 332**: Angels serve God's providential plan for all people, not only for the chosen line
- **St. Ambrose**: God's care for Hagar demonstrates that divine mercy extends beyond the boundaries of covenant privilege — a principle that will reach its fullness in the universal mission of the Church
- **Pope Francis** (*Evangelii Gaudium* 209): Catholic social teaching insists that God shows special concern for those who are excluded and marginalised

### "El Roi — The God Who Sees Me" (16:13)

Hagar names God *El Roi*, "the God who sees." This is the only instance in Genesis where a human being gives God a name:

- **CCC 2129**: God is known through his acts — Hagar knows God because he has seen her in her distress
- **St. John Chrysostom**: Hagar's naming of God is an act of genuine faith — she recognises the personal, caring nature of the divine
- Catholic Marian theology draws a parallel: as God "looked upon the lowliness of his handmaiden" (Luke 1:48, the *Magnificat*), so he looked upon Hagar in her affliction

## The Promise Concerning Ishmael (16:10-12)

The angel promises Hagar that her descendants will be "too many to count" and that her son will be named Ishmael ("God hears"):

- **CCC 841**: The Church acknowledges that Muslims "profess to hold the faith of Abraham, and together with us they adore the one, merciful God." Islamic tradition traces descent from Ishmael, and Catholic teaching treats this connection with respect
- The description of Ishmael as "a wild donkey of a man" (16:12) is not pejorative in context but describes the free, independent life of the desert nomad
- God's care for Ishmael and Hagar shows that divine blessing is not limited to the covenant line, even if the specific promises to Abram will be fulfilled through another son

## The Return and Ishmael's Birth (16:15-16)

Hagar returns to Abram and Sarai and bears Ishmael when Abram is eighty-six years old. Catholic exegesis notes that obedience to God's instruction (the angel told Hagar to return) requires humility and courage — Hagar submits to a difficult situation in trust that God will fulfill his promises.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 16 in Catholic theology:

1. Reveals God's mercy toward those outside the primary covenant line — the marginalised and the foreigner
2. Demonstrates the consequences of impatience with God's timing and the use of morally disordered means to achieve good ends
3. Introduces the first angelic appearance in Genesis, directed to the most vulnerable person in the narrative
4. Establishes a theological basis for Catholic engagement with Islam through the shared Abrahamic heritage
