## The Fall of Humanity

Genesis 3 is foundational for Catholic doctrine. The Council of Trent (1546) and the *Catechism of the Catholic Church* draw extensively on this chapter to articulate the doctrines of original sin, the promise of redemption, and the role of Mary in salvation history.

## The Serpent's Temptation (3:1-5)

### Catholic Teaching on the Serpent

- **CCC 391-395**: The serpent represents Satan, a fallen angel who rebelled against God
- **CCC 2852**: Satan is "a liar and the father of lies" (John 8:44)
- The Church teaches that the devil is a personal being, not merely a symbol of evil

### Structure of the Temptation

Catholic moral theology identifies in the serpent's strategy:

1. **Distortion of God's command**: Making God appear restrictive
2. **Denial of consequences**: "You will not die"
3. **Promise of autonomy**: "You will be like God, knowing good and evil"

The *Catechism* notes: "Man, tempted by the devil, let his trust in his Creator die in his heart" (CCC 397).

## Original Sin (3:6-7)

### The Council of Trent on Original Sin (1546)

The definitive Catholic teaching:

| Trent Decree | Teaching |
|-------------|----------|
| Canon 1 | Adam lost holiness and justice by his transgression |
| Canon 2 | Adam's sin is transmitted to all descendants by propagation, not imitation |
| Canon 3 | Original sin is remitted by baptism |
| Canon 4 | Infant baptism is necessary because of original sin |
| Canon 5 | Concupiscence remains after baptism but is not itself sin |

### Nature of Original Sin

Catholic theology distinguishes:

- **Original sin in Adam** (*peccatum originale originans*): The actual sin of the first parents
- **Original sin in us** (*peccatum originale originatum*): The inherited state — loss of original holiness and justice, not personal guilt
- **Concupiscence**: The disordered desire that inclines toward sin, a consequence but not identical with original sin

## The Protoevangelium (3:15)

"I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel."

### Catholic Marian Reading

Catholic tradition reads this verse with a specifically Marian emphasis:

- **"The woman"**: Identified with Mary, the New Eve
- **"Her offspring"**: Christ, born of the Virgin Mary
- **Immaculate Conception**: Mary's freedom from original sin is seen as fulfilling the "enmity" between the woman and the serpent — she was never under Satan's dominion
- **CCC 411**: "Many Fathers and Doctors of the Church have seen the woman announced in the *Protoevangelium* as Mary"

### Typology: Eve and Mary

| Eve | Mary |
|-----|------|
| Disobedience | Obedience ("Let it be done to me") |
| Brought death | Brought the Author of Life |
| "Mother of all living" | Mother of the Church |
| Tempted by the angel (Satan) | Visited by the angel (Gabriel) |

## The Consequences (3:8-24)

### God's Justice and Mercy

Catholic theology sees in the punishments both justice and the seeds of mercy:

- **Pain in childbearing**: Suffering becomes redemptive when united with Christ's suffering
- **Toil**: Work is not a punishment per se (Adam worked in Eden) but becomes laborious
- **Death**: Physical death is a consequence; spiritual death is remedied by grace
- **Clothing** (3:21): God's care for His fallen creatures — a sign of continuing love

### Expulsion from Eden

The expulsion is not merely punishment but also protection:
- Preventing access to the Tree of Life in a fallen state
- Setting the stage for the long journey of salvation history
- The cherubim and flaming sword point to the sacred space later replicated in the Temple

## Theological Significance

Genesis 3 in Catholic theology:

1. Establishes the doctrine of original sin as defined by Trent
2. Reveals Mary's role in salvation through the *Protoevangelium*
3. Shows that God's mercy is present even in judgment
4. Grounds the necessity of baptism for the remission of original sin
5. Points forward to Christ's redemptive work on the Cross
