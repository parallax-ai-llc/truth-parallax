## Return to Bethel and Rachel's Death

Genesis 35 records God's command that Jacob return to Bethel, the place where God had first appeared to him (Genesis 28). Jacob orders his household to purify themselves and abandon their foreign gods. At Bethel, God reaffirms the covenant promises, renaming Jacob "Israel." Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin on the road to Ephrath (Bethlehem), and is buried there. The chapter closes with a list of Jacob's twelve sons and the notice of Isaac's death and burial.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Put away the foreign gods that are among you and purify yourselves" (35:2)

Jacob's call to purification before approaching the holy place at Bethel is read by Catholic tradition as a prefigurement of sacramental preparation. St. Cyril of Alexandria saw in this command an image of the catechumen's renunciation of idolatry before baptism. The *Catechism* (CCC 2113) teaches that idolatry is not limited to pagan statues: "Idolatry consists in divinizing what is not God" — wealth, power, pleasure, or any created thing placed above the Creator.

### God's Reaffirmation of the Covenant (35:9-12)

God appears to Jacob and confirms both his new name, Israel, and the promises of land and descendants. Catholic theology sees this as a pivotal moment in the divine economy of salvation: God freely renews His promises despite the sins recounted in the preceding chapters. The CCC (762) teaches that "the gathering together of the People of God began at the moment when sin destroyed the communion of men with God," and this renewal at Bethel is part of that gradual gathering.

### The Death of Rachel (35:16-20)

Rachel's death while giving birth to Benjamin has been a subject of deep reflection in Catholic tradition. The name she gives — Ben-oni ("son of my sorrow") — is changed by Jacob to Benjamin ("son of the right hand"). Church Fathers such as St. Ephrem the Syrian saw in this a foreshadowing of how suffering and death give way to glory: sorrow is transformed into honor.

### Rachel's Tomb and Bethlehem

St. Jerome, who lived near Bethlehem, noted the profound significance of Rachel's burial there. Catholic tradition sees a providential connection between Rachel weeping for her children (Jeremiah 31:15, quoted in Matthew 2:18) and the massacre of the Holy Innocents. The grief of Rachel becomes an image of all innocent suffering that awaits the redemption brought by Christ, born in that same Bethlehem.

## Theological Significance

This chapter marks a turning point in the patriarchal narrative. The return to Bethel represents a renewal of the covenant relationship, echoing the Church's understanding that God continually calls His people back to fidelity. The Catholic liturgical tradition of pilgrimage — from medieval journeys to Compostela and Jerusalem to modern pilgrimages to Lourdes and Fatima — finds a scriptural root in Jacob's journey back to the place of encounter with God.

The listing of Jacob's twelve sons (35:23-26) is significant for Catholic ecclesiology. The CCC (765) draws a direct parallel between the twelve tribes and the twelve apostles, seeing in both the structure of God's people. The twelve sons born of struggle, rivalry, and suffering become the foundation of Israel, just as the twelve apostles — with all their human weakness — became the foundation of the Church.

Isaac's death and burial by both Esau and Jacob (35:29) demonstrates reconciliation between brothers, a theme dear to Catholic social teaching. The works of mercy include burying the dead, and the joint burial honors both filial duty and fraternal peace.
