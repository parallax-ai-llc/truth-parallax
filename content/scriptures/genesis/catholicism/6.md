## The Corruption of Humanity and God's Plan of Salvation through Noah

Genesis 6 marks a pivotal turning point in salvation history. Humanity's wickedness has become so pervasive that God determines to cleanse the earth by flood — yet in his mercy, he preserves Noah and his family. Catholic theology reads this chapter as a profound meditation on the relationship between divine justice and divine mercy, and as a rich source of typological prefigurations of baptism and the Church.

## The "Sons of God" and the Nephilim (6:1-4)

### Catholic Interpretations

This enigmatic passage has generated multiple Catholic interpretations:

| Interpretation | Proponents | Description |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Fallen angels | Justin Martyr, Clement of Alexandria | Angelic beings mated with human women |
| Sethite/Cainite intermarriage | Augustine, Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria | The righteous line of Seth mingled with the wicked line of Cain |
| Rulers/tyrants | Ephrem the Syrian | Powerful men who abused their authority |

- **St. Augustine** (*City of God* XV.23) favored the Sethite interpretation, which became the dominant view in the Latin tradition
- **CCC 391-395**: The Church affirms the reality of fallen angels but does not require any specific reading of Genesis 6:1-4
- The Nephilim are described as mighty men of old; their significance in Catholic reading lies in the escalation of human pride and violence

## God's Grief and Judgment (6:5-7)

### "The LORD was sorry that he had made man" (6:6)

Catholic theology handles this anthropomorphic language with care:

- **St. Thomas Aquinas** (*Summa Theologiae* I, q.3): God is impassible — he does not experience emotion as creatures do; the text uses human language to convey God's just response to sin
- **CCC 1431**: God's "grief" is his holy will reacting against the disorder of sin
- The universality of wickedness — "every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" — confirms Catholic teaching on the depth of sin's corruption, while affirming (against total depravity doctrines) that Noah remained capable of righteousness through grace

## "Noah Found Favor in the Eyes of the LORD" (6:8-10)

### Grace and Righteousness

The statement that Noah "found favor" (*chen*, grace) is the first explicit mention of grace in Scripture. Catholic theology notes:

- **CCC 2569**: Noah's righteousness is a response to divine grace, not autonomous achievement
- **St. Ambrose** (*De Noe*): Noah is righteous because God's grace enables him to be so — a teaching consistent with the Catholic understanding of justification defined at the Council of Trent
- Noah as "blameless in his generation" does not imply sinlessness but faithfulness to God amid universal corruption

## The Ark Instructions (6:11-22)

### Typological Significance

The ark has been one of the most richly developed types in Catholic tradition:

- **St. Peter** (1 Peter 3:20-21): The ark prefigures baptism — "eight persons were saved through water"
- **CCC 1219**: "The ark of Noah prefigures salvation by Baptism"
- **St. Augustine** (*City of God* XV.26): The dimensions of the ark correspond to the proportions of the human body, symbolizing Christ — and the door in the side recalls the wound in Christ's side from which blood and water flowed (John 19:34)
- **St. Cyprian** (*De Unitate Ecclesiae*): The ark as a type of the Church — outside the ark there is no salvation, just as outside the Church there is no ordinary means of salvation

### Noah's Obedience

"Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him" (6:22). Catholic teaching holds Noah as a model of obedient faith:

- He acted on God's word without visible evidence of the coming flood
- His obedience stands in contrast to the disobedience of Adam (Genesis 3) and Cain (Genesis 4)
- **Hebrews 11:7**: "By faith Noah, being warned by God concerning events as yet unseen, in reverent fear constructed an ark for the saving of his household"

## Theological Significance

Genesis 6 in Catholic theology:

1. Demonstrates that sin has consequences not only for individuals but for the entire created order
2. Establishes the principle that God always preserves a righteous remnant
3. Provides the foundational type for baptism and for the Church as the ark of salvation
4. Shows the interplay of justice and mercy that characterises all of God's dealings with humanity
