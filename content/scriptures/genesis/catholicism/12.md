## The Call of Abram

Genesis 12 marks the decisive turning point from primeval history to patriarchal history — from the universal crisis of sin to God's particular plan of salvation through one man and his descendants. Catholic theology regards the call of Abram as one of the most important moments in all of Scripture, inaugurating the covenant relationship that will culminate in Christ and the Church.

## The Call and the Promise (12:1-3)

### "Go from Your Country" (12:1)

God commands Abram to leave everything — country, kindred, father's house — for a land that God will show him. Catholic interpretation emphasises:

- **CCC 145**: "Abraham is the model of... obedience of faith... he obeyed and went out, not knowing where he was going (Heb 11:8)"
- **St. John Chrysostom** (*Homilies on Genesis* 31): The threefold leaving (country, kindred, father's house) represents increasing levels of detachment — a progressive stripping that Catholic spiritual theology connects to the mystical journey
- **CCC 2570**: "As soon as God calls him, Abraham leaves... Abraham is wholly 'attentive to the will of God'"

### The Threefold Promise (12:2-3)

| Promise | Content | Catholic Fulfilment |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| "I will make of you a great nation" | Nationhood, numerous descendants | Israel, and through Christ, the Church — the new people of God (CCC 762) |
| "I will bless you and make your name great" | Personal blessing and renown | Abraham becomes "our father in faith" (Roman Canon of the Mass) |
| "In you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" | Universal blessing | Christ, descendant of Abraham, through whom all nations receive salvation (Galatians 3:8) |

### Universal Blessing through Particular Election

Catholic theology sees in 12:3 the key to understanding Israel's election:

- **CCC 59-60**: God chose Abraham not to exclude other nations but as the instrument through which all nations would be blessed
- **Vatican II** (*Lumen Gentium* 9): "God... willed to make men holy and save them, not as individuals without any bond between them, but rather to make them into a people"
- **Benedict XVI** (*Deus Caritas Est* 9): Abraham's calling reveals that God's love is both particular and universal — he chooses one to reach all

## Abram's Obedience (12:4-9)

### Journey to Canaan

Abram travels with Sarai and Lot to Canaan, where God appears and says, "To your offspring I will give this land" (12:7). Abram responds by building altars at Shechem and between Bethel and Ai.

- **CCC 2570**: Abram's altars express his faith as response to God's self-revelation
- **St. Augustine** (*City of God* XVI.12): Abram's journey through the land, building altars, is a liturgical act — he consecrates the land to God before possessing it
- Catholic lectionary tradition reads Abraham's journey as a paradigm for the life of faith: called, trusting without seeing, worshipping along the way

### The Significance of Altar-Building

Each altar Abram builds marks a place of divine encounter. Catholic sacramental theology sees a pattern:

- God reveals himself → the human person responds in worship → God deepens the relationship
- This pattern reaches its fullness in the Eucharistic liturgy, where God's Word is proclaimed and the community responds with the sacrificial offering

## The Sojourn in Egypt (12:10-20)

### Abram's Deception

Famine drives Abram to Egypt, where he asks Sarai to say she is his sister, fearing for his life. Catholic moral theology treats this episode honestly:

- **St. Augustine** (*Contra Mendacium*): Abram's deception was a sin, though mitigated by fear; it cannot be justified as a righteous stratagem
- **St. John Chrysostom**: God protects Sarai despite Abram's failure — God's plan does not depend on human perfection
- Catholic exegesis notes the pattern: Abram's faith coexists with genuine human weakness. The Church teaches that the patriarchs are models of faith, not of moral perfection in every act

### God's Protection of Sarai

God strikes Pharaoh's household with plagues to protect Sarai. Catholic typological readings see in this episode a foreshadowing of the Exodus: a patriarch's family goes to Egypt, Pharaoh is plagued, and God's people emerge with great possessions.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 12 in Catholic theology:

1. Inaugurates covenant history — God's definitive strategy for redeeming the world through a chosen people
2. Establishes Abraham as the "father of faith" for Jews, Christians, and Muslims (CCC 841)
3. Reveals that God's particular election is ordered toward universal blessing
4. Demonstrates that God's faithfulness persists despite human failure
