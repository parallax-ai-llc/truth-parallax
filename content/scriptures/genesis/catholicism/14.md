## The War of Kings and the Blessing of Melchizedek

Genesis 14 recounts a war among Mesopotamian and Canaanite kings, Abram's military rescue of Lot, and the mysterious encounter with Melchizedek, king of Salem and priest of God Most High. For Catholic theology, this chapter is of extraordinary importance: Melchizedek's offering of bread and wine is the preeminent Old Testament type of the Eucharist, and his priestly order is the basis for understanding Christ's eternal priesthood.

## The War and Abram's Rescue of Lot (14:1-16)

### Historical Context

Four eastern kings led by Chedorlaomer defeat five kings of the Jordan plain, sacking Sodom and Gomorrah and taking Lot captive. Abram musters 318 trained men from his household and pursues the invaders, defeating them in a night raid.

Catholic exegesis notes:

- **St. Ambrose** (*De Abraham* I.3): Abram's rescue of Lot demonstrates that the just man does not abandon family even when they have chosen poorly
- **CCC 2302-2310**: Catholic teaching on legitimate defence finds an early scriptural precedent — Abram uses force proportionally to rescue the innocent
- **St. Augustine**: The number 318 was given symbolic significance by the Fathers; in Greek numerals, 318 = IHT, seen as an allusion to the name of Jesus (IH) and the Cross (T) — though modern Catholic exegesis treats this as an example of patristic allegory rather than intended meaning

## Melchizedek (14:17-20)

### The Encounter

"And Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine. He was priest of God Most High. And he blessed him..." (14:18-19)

This brief passage has generated an enormous body of Catholic theological reflection.

### Melchizedek as Type of Christ

| Melchizedek | Christ | Source |
|------------|--------|--------|
| King of Salem ("peace") | Prince of Peace (Isaiah 9:6) | Hebrews 7:2 |
| Priest of God Most High | Eternal High Priest | Hebrews 5:6, 6:20 |
| Offers bread and wine | Institutes the Eucharist with bread and wine | CCC 1333 |
| Without recorded genealogy | Eternal, without beginning or end | Hebrews 7:3 |
| Blesses Abraham | Greater than Abraham | Hebrews 7:7 |
| Receives tithes from Abraham | Receives the worship of all believers | Hebrews 7:4-10 |

### The Eucharistic Reading

Catholic tradition has consistently read Melchizedek's offering of bread and wine as a prefiguration of the Eucharist:

- **CCC 1333**: "Melchizedek, king of Salem, brought out bread and wine... a prefiguring of his own offering"
- **CCC 1544**: "The Christian tradition considers Melchizedek... as a prefiguration of the priesthood of Christ"
- **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I): At Mass, the priest prays that God will accept the Eucharistic offering "as once you were pleased to accept... the holy sacrifice, the spotless victim, which your high priest Melchizedek offered you, a holy sacrifice, a spotless victim"
- **Council of Trent**, Session XXII: Explicitly teaches that Christ's priesthood is "according to the order of Melchizedek" and that the Mass fulfils what Melchizedek prefigured

### Church Fathers on Melchizedek

- **St. Clement of Alexandria** (*Stromateis* IV.25): Melchizedek is "a king of peace... who gave bread and wine, sanctified food, as a type of the Eucharist"
- **St. Cyprian** (*Epistle 63*): Used the Melchizedek passage to defend the use of wine (not water) in the Eucharistic chalice against certain heretical groups
- **St. Jerome**: Salem is identified with Jerusalem — Melchizedek reigned where Christ would later offer his sacrifice
- **St. Augustine** (*City of God* XVI.22): Melchizedek's sacrifice is the first public appearance of the sacrifice that the whole world now offers to God

## Abram's Refusal of the King of Sodom (14:21-24)

After the encounter with Melchizedek, the king of Sodom offers Abram all the recovered goods. Abram refuses:

"I have lifted my hand to the LORD, God Most High... that I would not take a thread or a sandal strap or anything that is yours" (14:22-23).

Catholic moral theology sees in this refusal:

- A contrast between two responses to Abram: Melchizedek offers a priestly blessing and Abram receives it; the king of Sodom offers material wealth and Abram refuses it
- The principle that the person of faith is not indebted to the powers of this world
- **St. Ambrose**: Abram's refusal teaches that righteousness cannot be bought, and that the just man must not allow the wicked to claim credit for his prosperity

## Theological Significance

Genesis 14 in Catholic theology:

1. Provides the foundational Old Testament type of the Eucharist in Melchizedek's offering of bread and wine
2. Establishes the priestly order "according to Melchizedek" that Christ fulfils (Psalm 110:4; Hebrews 5-7)
3. Is directly referenced in the Roman Canon of the Mass, making it liturgically present at every celebration of the Eucharist
4. Demonstrates that true blessing comes from God's priest, not from the powers of the world
