## The Destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah

Genesis 19 recounts the dramatic destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah, the rescue of Lot and his daughters, and the transformation of Lot's wife into a pillar of salt. Catholic interpretation reads this chapter as a revelation of divine justice against grave sin, while also drawing typological connections to eschatological judgment and the necessity of obedience to God's commands.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Two Angels at Sodom (19:1-3)

Lot receives two visitors at the gate of Sodom. Catholic tradition contrasts Lot's hospitality with the hostility of the Sodomites:

- **St. Ambrose**: Lot's welcome mirrors Abraham's hospitality in chapter 18, showing that the virtues of the patriarch extended to his household
- **Moral teaching**: Hospitality to strangers remains a touchstone of Catholic social doctrine (CCC 2447)

### The Sin of Sodom (19:4-11)

The Catholic Church has consistently taught that the sins of Sodom were grave:

| Interpretive Tradition | Emphasis | Key Source |
|----------------------|----------|------------|
| Sexual sin | Homosexual acts and sexual violence | Augustine, *City of God* XVI.30 |
| Inhospitality and injustice | Cruelty to strangers, pride | Ezekiel 16:49-50 |
| Comprehensive sinfulness | Multiple sins including arrogance and neglect of the poor | CCC 1867 (sins crying to heaven) |

- **CCC 1867**: Lists "the sin of the Sodomites" among sins that cry to heaven for vengeance
- **Ezekiel 16:49**: The prophet identifies pride, excess of food, and neglect of the poor as Sodom's sins — Catholic tradition holds both the sexual and social dimensions together
- **Jude 1:7**: References Sodom's "unnatural desire" as a warning

### Lot's Wife (19:26)

"But his wife, behind him, looked back, and she became a pillar of salt."

- **Jesus' warning** (Luke 17:32): "Remember Lot's wife" — looking back signifies attachment to a sinful world
- **St. Irenaeus**: Her transformation is a sign of the consequences of disobedience and divided loyalty
- **Moral theology**: The Church reads this as a warning against nostalgia for sin during the journey of conversion

### Lot and His Daughters (19:30-38)

Catholic commentators treat this episode with sobriety:

- The incestuous origins of Moab and Ammon demonstrate the consequences of moral degradation in Sodom's proximity
- **St. Chrysostom**: Even the righteous can fall when isolated from a community of faith
- The narrative serves as a cautionary account rather than an endorsement

## Typological Significance

The Church Fathers read the destruction of Sodom typologically:

- **Fire from heaven**: Prefigures the final judgment (2 Peter 2:6-9)
- **Lot's rescue**: A type of God's deliverance of the righteous from judgment
- **The angel's command to flee**: Echoes Christ's instruction to flee when tribulation comes (Matthew 24:16-18)

## Theological Significance

Genesis 19 in Catholic theology:

- **Divine justice is real**: God does not ignore grave sin indefinitely
- **Mercy within judgment**: Even in destruction, God rescues Lot for Abraham's sake (19:29)
- **The intercession of the righteous**: Abraham's prayer from chapter 18 bears fruit in Lot's deliverance
- **Warning against attachment to sin**: Lot's wife stands as a permanent admonition
