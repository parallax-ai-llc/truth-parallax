## Jacob Blesses Joseph's Sons

When Jacob falls ill, Joseph brings his two sons, Manasseh (the firstborn) and Ephraim, to receive their grandfather's blessing. Jacob first adopts them as his own: "Ephraim and Manasseh shall be mine, as Reuben and Simeon are" (48:5). When Joseph positions the boys so that Jacob's right hand will rest on Manasseh's head, Jacob deliberately crosses his hands, placing his right hand on Ephraim, the younger. Despite Joseph's objection, Jacob insists: "His younger brother shall be greater than he" (48:19). Jacob blesses them, invoking the God of Abraham and Isaac.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The angel who has redeemed me from all evil, bless the boys" (48:16)

Jacob's invocation of a redeeming angel (*ha-malakh ha-goel*) is significant in Catholic theology. The Church Fathers saw in this verse an early intimation of the Trinity: Jacob invokes God (48:15) and an angel-redeemer in parallel, suggesting a distinction within the divine identity. Catholic commentators, while cautious about reading full Trinitarian doctrine into the Old Testament, recognize such passages as seeds of later revelation (CCC 238).

### The Crossed Hands (48:14)

Jacob's crossing of his hands is one of the most celebrated typological images in patristic exegesis. The Church Fathers — including St. Ephrem, St. Ambrose, St. Augustine, and St. Caesarius of Arles — unanimously saw in this gesture a prefiguration of the Cross. The crossing of hands to bless forms the shape of the Cross, and through the Cross, God's blessing passes from the "elder" (Israel/the Synagogue) to the "younger" (the Church drawn from the nations).

| Element | Typological Meaning |
|---|---|
| Crossed hands | The Cross of Christ |
| Right hand on the younger son | Greater blessing passes to the Church |
| Left hand on the firstborn | Israel retains blessing, but is surpassed |
| Joseph's objection | The mystery of election is hidden from human wisdom |
| Jacob's insistence | God's sovereign choice transcends human expectation |

St. Ambrose wrote: "Jacob crossed his hands, not by error but by mystery. He foresaw in spirit that the grace of Christ's cross would bless the peoples of the world" (*On the Patriarchs* 2.9).

### "In you shall Israel bless, saying, 'God make you as Ephraim and as Manasseh'" (48:20)

This blessing became a standard Jewish blessing for children and remains in use today. Catholic liturgical tradition draws on the patriarchal blessings in its own rites of blessing, particularly in the baptismal liturgy, where parents and godparents invoke God's blessing upon children. The CCC (1669) teaches that "sacramentals derive from the baptismal priesthood" and that blessings "consecrate persons to God."

### The Adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh (48:5)

Jacob's adoption of Joseph's sons — elevating them to the status of his own children — has been read by Catholic theologians as a type of divine adoption. The CCC (1) teaches that God "calls man to seek him, to know him, to love him" and that through baptism, Christians become "adoptive sons" of God (CCC 1265). Just as Ephraim and Manasseh, born in Egypt to a Gentile mother, are incorporated into the people of Israel by Jacob's declaration, so believers from all nations are incorporated into the Church by God's grace.

## Theological Significance

**The reversal of primogeniture.** Throughout Genesis, God consistently chooses the younger over the elder: Abel over Cain, Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau, Joseph over his brothers, and now Ephraim over Manasseh. Catholic theology interprets this pattern as a revelation of the nature of grace: God's election is free and unmerited, not based on natural right or human expectation. The CCC (312) affirms that God's ways are not bound by human conventions.

**The Cross as source of blessing.** The patristic reading of Jacob's crossed hands as a sign of the Cross is not mere allegorizing but reflects a deep theological conviction: the Cross is the instrument through which God's blessing reaches all nations. The CCC (1067) teaches that "the saving work of his holy and sanctifying Cross is celebrated in the liturgy" — and the Fathers saw this mystery already prefigured in Jacob's gesture.

**Grace and nature.** Jacob blesses with "dimmed eyes" (48:10) — his natural vision fails, but his spiritual vision is sharpened. Catholic theology recognizes that grace perfects and elevates nature (*gratia perficit naturam*). Jacob cannot see, yet he sees more truly than Joseph, whose eyes are clear but whose understanding of God's plan is incomplete.

**The continuity of covenant.** Jacob explicitly connects his blessing to "the God before whom my fathers Abraham and Isaac walked" (48:15). Catholic theology emphasizes the continuity of God's covenant from Abraham through Christ and the Church (CCC 59-64). Each generation receives and transmits the blessing, forming a living chain of faith.
