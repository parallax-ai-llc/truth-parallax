## The Second Creation Account

Catholic interpretation of Genesis 2 sees this chapter as a complementary narrative that reveals deeper truths about human nature, vocation, and the divine plan for marriage. The Pontifical Biblical Commission and *Dei Verbum* guide Catholic scholars to attend to the literary genre while affirming the theological truth conveyed.

## The Formation of Adam (2:7)

"The LORD God formed man of dust from the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life."

### Catholic Teaching on Human Origins

- **CCC 362-368**: The human person is a unity of body and soul, with the soul directly created by God
- **Humani Generis (1950)**: The body may have developed through evolution, but the soul is immediately created by God for each person
- **Monogenism**: The traditional Catholic position holds that all humans descend from a single pair, though contemporary theologians debate this

### Body-Soul Unity

Catholic anthropology, following Aristotle and Aquinas, teaches that:
- The soul is the "form" of the body — they are not separate substances
- Death (separation of soul and body) is unnatural, a consequence of sin
- The resurrection of the body completes human salvation

## The Garden of Eden (2:8-17)

### Typological Reading

The Church Fathers read the Garden typologically:

| Garden Symbol | Typological Meaning |
|--------------|-------------------|
| The Garden | The Church / Paradise |
| The Tree of Life | The Cross / The Eucharist |
| The four rivers | The four Gospels |
| Adam's sleep | Christ's death on the Cross |
| Eve from Adam's side | The Church born from Christ's pierced side |

### The Tree of Knowledge

Catholic interpretation emphasizes:
- **Moral testing**: God gave humans genuine freedom, including the freedom to reject Him
- **Natural law**: The prohibition reveals a moral order inscribed in creation
- **Trust**: The test is fundamentally about trusting God's goodness

## The Creation of Eve (2:18-25)

"It is not good that the man should be alone; I will make him a helper fit for him."

### Catholic Teaching on Marriage

The *Catechism* (CCC 1601-1666) draws heavily on Genesis 2:

- **Sacramentality**: Marriage is a sacrament instituted by God in creation
- **Indissolubility**: "What God has joined together, let no man separate" — rooted in the one-flesh union
- **Complementarity**: Man and woman are made for each other — equal in dignity, different in nature
- **Procreation**: Marriage is ordered toward the generation of children and their education

### *Theology of the Body*

John Paul II's extensive catechesis on Genesis 2 includes:

- **Original solitude**: Adam's awareness of being different from animals — the basis of personhood
- **Original unity**: The recognition of Eve as "bone of my bones" — the communion of persons
- **Original nakedness**: "They were not ashamed" — the pre-fallen state of transparency and trust

## Theological Significance

Genesis 2 in Catholic theology:

1. Reveals the sacramental nature of marriage
2. Establishes the dignity of human labor as cooperation with God's creative work
3. Shows the relational nature of the human person (reflecting the Trinity)
4. Provides the foundation for the Church's teaching on human sexuality
