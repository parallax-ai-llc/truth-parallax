## The Abrahamic Covenant

Genesis 15 records the formal establishment of God's covenant with Abram through a solemn ritual. It contains the foundational verse on faith and righteousness (15:6), the promise of innumerable descendants, the prophecy of Israel's future slavery and liberation, and the mysterious covenant ceremony with divided animals. Catholic theology regards this chapter as central to the doctrines of faith, justification, and covenant.

## "Do Not Be Afraid, Abram" (15:1-6)

### The Dialogue of Faith

God appears to Abram in a vision, promising to be his "shield" and "great reward." Abram responds with a bold question: "O Lord GOD, what will you give me, for I continue childless?" (15:2). Catholic exegesis admires Abram's honesty:

- **CCC 2571**: "Abraham's faith does not weaken... even though God seems to delay his promise"
- **St. John Chrysostom** (*Homilies on Genesis* 36): Abram's questioning is not doubt but the prayer of one who trusts God enough to be honest with him

### "He Believed the LORD, and He Counted It to Him as Righteousness" (15:6)

This is one of the most theologically significant verses in all of Scripture, central to Catholic teaching on justification:

| Theological Question | Catholic Teaching | Key Source |
|---------------------|-------------------|------------|
| What is Abram's faith? | Personal trust in God's promise, not mere intellectual assent | CCC 144-146 |
| Is faith alone sufficient? | Faith is the beginning of justification, but must be completed by love and works | Council of Trent, Session VI, Canons 9-12 |
| What does "counted as righteousness" mean? | God truly makes the believer righteous, not merely declares them so | Trent, Session VI, Chapter 7 |
| Does this contradict James 2:21-24? | No — Paul and James address different aspects of the same reality | CCC 1815-1816 |

- **Council of Trent**, Session VI, Chapter 8: "We are said to be justified by faith because faith is the beginning of human salvation, the foundation and root of all justification"
- **St. Augustine** (*De Spiritu et Littera* 32): Abraham's faith was a living faith animated by love — he obeyed God's call (chapter 12), interceded for Sodom (chapter 18), and was willing to sacrifice Isaac (chapter 22)
- **CCC 146**: "Abraham is the model of such obedience offered us by Sacred Scripture. The Virgin Mary is its most perfect embodiment"

### Catholic vs. Protestant Readings

The Catholic interpretation of Genesis 15:6 differs from the Reformation reading:

- Catholic theology understands justification as transformation (God makes the person righteous), not merely imputation (God declares the person righteous)
- Faith and works are not opposed but organically united — Abram's faith in 15:6 is the same faith that moved him to obey in 12:1 and will move him to offer Isaac in 22:1
- **CCC 1989**: "Justification is not only the remission of sins, but also the sanctification and renewal of the interior man"

## The Covenant Ceremony (15:7-21)

### The Divided Animals (15:9-11)

God instructs Abram to bring a heifer, goat, ram, turtledove, and young pigeon, and to cut them in half. This is the ancient Near Eastern covenant ritual (*berith* — "to cut a covenant"):

- The contracting parties would walk between the divided animals, invoking upon themselves the fate of the animals if they broke the covenant
- **CCC 2571**: God alone passes through the pieces (as a smoking fire pot and flaming torch) — signifying that God takes the covenant obligation entirely upon himself
- **St. Irenaeus**: God's unilateral passage between the pieces means the covenant depends on divine faithfulness, not human performance

### The Prophecy of Slavery and Exodus (15:13-16)

God reveals that Abram's descendants will be enslaved for four hundred years but will come out "with great possessions." Catholic exegesis notes:

- God's foreknowledge does not eliminate human freedom — the enslavement is foreseen but not caused by God
- The promise of liberation foreshadows the Exodus, the central saving event of the Old Testament
- **CCC 62**: "God formed his people Israel... to prepare the coming of Christ"

### "Deep Sleep" and "Dreadful Darkness" (15:12)

The *tardemah* (deep sleep) that falls on Abram recalls Adam's deep sleep in Genesis 2:21. Catholic typological reading connects the two:

- From Adam's sleep came Eve; from the covenant ratified during Abram's sleep comes Israel
- Both moments involve God acting while the human recipient is passive — emphasising divine initiative

## Theological Significance

Genesis 15 in Catholic theology:

1. Provides the foundational text for the Catholic understanding of faith, justification, and the relationship between faith and works
2. Establishes the covenant form that will characterise God's relationship with his people throughout salvation history
3. Demonstrates that God's covenant depends on divine faithfulness, not human merit
4. Prophetically anticipates the Exodus and the entire arc of Israel's history
