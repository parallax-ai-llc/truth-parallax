## Joseph Interprets the Dreams of Two Officials

In prison, Joseph encounters two officials of Pharaoh: the chief cupbearer and the chief baker, both imprisoned for offending the king. Each has a dream on the same night and is troubled because there is no one to interpret them. Joseph tells them, "Do not interpretations belong to God?" (40:8) and proceeds to interpret both dreams. The cupbearer will be restored to his position in three days; the baker will be executed. Both predictions come true exactly as Joseph said, but the cupbearer forgets Joseph.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Do not interpretations belong to God?" (40:8)

This declaration is central to the Catholic theology of charisms and prophecy. Joseph disclaims personal credit for his gift, attributing it entirely to God. The CCC (799-801) teaches that charisms — special gifts of the Holy Spirit — are given "for the common good" and must be received with gratitude and humility. Joseph models the proper disposition toward divine gifts: they are from God, for others, and not for self-aggrandizement.

### The Two Prisoners: Life and Death (40:12-19)

The Church Fathers drew a powerful typological reading from the two prisoners. St. Augustine and St. Ephrem both noted the parallel to the two thieves crucified alongside Christ:

| Genesis 40 | The Crucifixion |
|---|---|
| Joseph in prison between two men | Christ on the Cross between two thieves |
| The cupbearer is restored (saved) | The good thief enters paradise (Luke 23:43) |
| The baker is condemned (executed) | The unrepentant thief is lost |
| Both receive judgment from Joseph | Both receive judgment from Christ |
| Resolution in three days (40:12-13) | Resurrection on the third day |

The "three days" motif appears for both the cupbearer and the baker, and Catholic exegetes have consistently linked this to the three days between Christ's death and resurrection. St. Caesarius of Arles developed this parallel at length in his sermons.

### "Remember me when it is well with you" (40:14)

Joseph's plea to the cupbearer expresses a deeply human need for solidarity and remembrance. Catholic tradition sees in this an image of the prayer of souls in purgatory, who rely on the intercession and remembrance of the living. The CCC (1032) affirms the practice of praying for the dead and recalls 2 Maccabees 12:46: "It is a holy and wholesome thought to pray for the dead."

### "Yet the chief cupbearer did not remember Joseph, but forgot him" (40:23)

The cupbearer's forgetfulness extends Joseph's suffering for two more years. Catholic spiritual writers see in this human ingratitude a reminder that we must place our ultimate trust not in human benefactors but in God alone. St. Teresa of Avila wrote, "Let nothing disturb you... God alone suffices." The passage also invites an examination of conscience: how often do we forget those who have helped us, or fail to advocate for the unjustly imprisoned?

## Theological Significance

**God as the source of all wisdom.** Joseph's insistence that interpretation belongs to God establishes a principle that runs through all of Scripture: human wisdom is a participation in divine wisdom. St. Thomas Aquinas taught that the gift of prophecy is a transient illumination of the mind by God, not a permanent personal possession (*Summa Theologiae* II-II, q. 171-174).

**The prison as school of sanctity.** Catholic hagiography is filled with saints whose most profound spiritual growth occurred in prison: St. Paul, St. John of the Cross, St. Maximilian Kolbe, St. Thomas More. Joseph's prison becomes the place where his charism of dream interpretation is exercised and refined. Providence uses the lowest point of his life as the preparation for his greatest service.

**Solidarity with prisoners.** Catholic social teaching, rooted in Matthew 25:36 ("I was in prison and you came to me"), calls for attention to the incarcerated. Joseph's compassion toward his fellow prisoners — noticing their distress, offering help — embodies the corporal work of mercy of visiting the imprisoned (CCC 2447).
