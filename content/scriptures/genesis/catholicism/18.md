## The Visit of the Three at Mamre

Genesis 18 holds a privileged place in Catholic theology because the appearance of three visitors to Abraham at Mamre has been read by the Church Fathers as a veiled revelation of the Holy Trinity. Abraham sees three men yet addresses them as "Lord" (singular), prompting a rich tradition of Trinitarian exegesis that finds visual expression in Andrei Rublev's famous icon of the Trinity.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The LORD appeared to him by the oaks of Mamre" (18:1-3)

The opening verse states that the LORD appeared, yet Abraham sees three men. The interplay between singular and plural has been central to patristic commentary:

- **St. Augustine** (*De Trinitate* II.10): "Abraham saw three and worshipped one" — the three visitors manifest the triune God
- **St. Hilary of Poitiers**: The passage reveals the mystery of plurality in the Godhead
- **CCC 237-248**: The Trinity is the central mystery of the Christian faith, progressively revealed through Old Testament theophanies

| Patristic Reading | Proponent | Interpretation |
|-------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Trinitarian theophany | Augustine, Hilary | Three visitors = Father, Son, Holy Spirit |
| Christ with two angels | Justin Martyr, Origen | The LORD is the pre-incarnate Word |
| Angels as messengers | Chrysostom | Three angels sent by God |

### The Promise of Isaac (18:9-15)

Sarah laughs at the promise of a son in her old age. Catholic interpretation emphasizes:

- **Divine omnipotence**: "Is anything too hard for the LORD?" (18:14) — cited in CCC 269 on God's almighty power
- **Marian typology**: The Church Fathers compare Sarah's miraculous conception in old age with the Virgin Mary's conception by the Holy Spirit. Both demonstrate that God's power transcends natural limitations
- **Faith and doubt**: Sarah's laughter becomes joy — a pattern the Church sees fulfilled in the Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55)

### Abraham's Intercession for Sodom (18:22-33)

Abraham's bold negotiation with God to spare Sodom for the sake of the righteous is one of the great Old Testament passages on intercessory prayer.

- **CCC 2571**: "Abraham's faith does not weaken; he intercedes for the inhabitants of Sodom"
- **Model of prayer**: Abraham approaches God with humility yet persistence, setting the pattern for Christian intercession
- **Justice and mercy**: The dialogue reveals that God's justice is not opposed to mercy — He will spare a city for even ten righteous persons

### Theological Implications of the Intercession

Catholic moral theology draws several principles from Abraham's bargaining:

1. **Solidarity in grace**: The righteous can benefit others — the theological basis for the communion of saints
2. **Progressive revelation of mercy**: God's willingness to reduce the number from fifty to ten reveals the depth of divine mercy
3. **Priestly intercession**: Abraham acts as a mediator between God and the people, prefiguring Christ's priestly intercession (Hebrews 7:25)

## Hospitality as Sacred Duty

The Catholic tradition has consistently read Abraham's reception of the visitors as a model of Christian hospitality:

- **St. Benedict** (Rule of St. Benedict, Chapter 53): "All guests who present themselves are to be welcomed as Christ"
- **Hebrews 13:2**: "Do not neglect to show hospitality to strangers, for thereby some have entertained angels unawares"
- **CCC 2447**: The works of mercy include welcoming the stranger

## Theological Significance

Genesis 18 in Catholic theology establishes:

- **Trinitarian foreshadowing**: The deepest Old Testament anticipation of the Trinity
- **The power of intercession**: Prayer can avert divine judgment
- **Divine fidelity**: God's promises are certain despite human doubt
- **The vocation of hospitality**: Openness to the stranger as openness to God
