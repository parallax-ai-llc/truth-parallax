## The Genealogy from Adam to Noah

Genesis 5 presents the ten-generation genealogy from Adam to Noah, structured by a solemn refrain: each patriarch is born, lives, begets children, and dies. Catholic interpretation reads this chapter as far more than a historical record; it is a theological meditation on the consequences of the Fall, the persistence of the *imago Dei*, and God's providential guidance of salvation history.

## Structure and Significance

### The Repeated Formula

The chapter follows a remarkably consistent pattern for each patriarch:

| Element | Meaning in Catholic Theology |
|---------|------------------------------|
| "When X had lived Y years, he fathered Z" | God's blessing of fruitfulness (Gen 1:28) endures after the Fall |
| "He had other sons and daughters" | Humanity continues to multiply by divine providence |
| "And he died" | The consequence of original sin (Gen 2:17; CCC 1008) |

The refrain "and he died" echoes through the chapter like a funeral bell. Catholic theology sees this as the fulfillment of God's warning in Genesis 2:17 and the confirmation of the Council of Trent's teaching that death entered the world through Adam's sin (Session V, Canon 1).

### The Exception: Enoch (5:21-24)

"Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him." This extraordinary verse breaks the pattern of death. Catholic tradition interprets Enoch as:

- **A sign of hope**: Even under the regime of death, God can deliver the righteous
- **CCC 2569**: Enoch is cited as an example of one who "walked with God" — a model of prayer and communion
- **Patristic tradition**: Many Fathers, including Tertullian and Irenaeus, connected Enoch with Elijah as witnesses reserved for the end times (cf. Revelation 11:3)
- **Hebrews 11:5**: "By faith Enoch was taken up so that he should not see death" — the Catholic lectionary uses this to teach that faith transcends mortality

## The Long Lifespans

### Catholic Approaches to Interpretation

The extraordinary ages recorded in Genesis 5 (Adam at 930, Methuselah at 969) have been the subject of varied Catholic interpretation:

- **St. Augustine** (*City of God* XV.9): Insisted the ages should be taken literally, arguing that the human constitution before the Flood differed from later periods
- **Modern Catholic exegesis**: The Pontifical Biblical Commission (1948 and subsequent documents) permits the recognition of literary conventions in the primeval history, including symbolic or schematic uses of numbers
- **Theological reading**: The gradual decline in lifespan from Adam to the patriarchs after the Flood reflects the increasing distance from original grace

The Church does not require a single interpretation of the numbers but insists that the text conveys the truth that humanity was created for life, not death, and that death is an intruder resulting from sin.

## "In the Likeness of God" and "In His Own Likeness" (5:1-3)

### The Transmission of the *Imago Dei*

A crucial detail: Genesis 5:1 recalls that God made Adam "in the likeness of God," while 5:3 says Adam fathered Seth "in his own likeness, after his image." Catholic theology draws a distinction:

- **CCC 1701-1702**: Every human person retains the image of God, even after the Fall
- **Council of Trent**: Original sin damages but does not destroy the *imago Dei*; human nature is "wounded, weakened" but not annihilated
- **St. Irenaeus** (*Against Heresies* V.16.2): The image is retained; the likeness (understood as supernatural grace) is lost and must be restored by Christ

## Theological Significance

Genesis 5 in Catholic theology:

1. Demonstrates that death is universal because of original sin — the relentless "and he died"
2. Preserves the *imago Dei* through every generation despite the Fall
3. Enoch's translation offers a sign that God's plan includes victory over death
4. The genealogy connects Adam to Noah, showing God's providential preservation of the righteous line that will carry the promise of Genesis 3:15
