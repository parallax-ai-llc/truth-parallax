## The Covenant of Circumcision

Genesis 17 records the deepening of God's covenant with Abram through the institution of circumcision, the renaming of Abram and Sarai to Abraham and Sarah, and the promise that Sarah herself will bear a son — Isaac — through whom the covenant will be established. Catholic theology reads this chapter as a pivotal moment in salvation history and the primary Old Testament basis for understanding baptism as the sacrament of covenant initiation.

## God Appears to Abram (17:1-2)

### "I Am God Almighty; Walk Before Me, and Be Blameless"

God reveals himself as *El Shaddai* (God Almighty) and calls Abram to covenant fidelity:

- **CCC 2571**: God's command to "walk before me" establishes the covenantal pattern of ongoing relationship, not merely a one-time act of faith
- **St. Augustine**: "Walk before me" means living in God's presence with integrity — the whole of the moral life summarised in one command
- **Council of Trent**, Session VI: The call to be "blameless" (*tamim*) illustrates the Catholic teaching that justification includes genuine interior transformation, not only forensic declaration

## The Name Changes (17:5, 15)

### From Abram to Abraham, Sarai to Sarah

| Original Name | New Name | Meaning | Theological Significance |
|--------------|----------|---------|-------------------------|
| Abram ("exalted father") | Abraham ("father of a multitude") | The covenant changes his identity | God's promise is inscribed in his very name |
| Sarai ("my princess") | Sarah ("princess") | From particular to universal | She is princess not just of one household but of nations and kings |

Catholic sacramental theology sees in these name changes a precedent for the practice of taking a new name at baptism and confirmation:

- **CCC 2156-2159**: A Christian name signifies the person's new identity in Christ
- The change of name at religious profession and papal election also echoes this pattern
- **St. John Chrysostom**: God renames Abram to seal the covenant identity — just as baptism makes us "a new creation" (2 Corinthians 5:17)

## The Covenant of Circumcision (17:9-14)

### Circumcision as Covenant Sign

God commands circumcision as "a sign of the covenant between me and you" (17:11), to be performed on every male at eight days old:

- **CCC 527**: Jesus himself was circumcised on the eighth day (Luke 2:21), "the sign of his incorporation into Abraham's descendants, into the people of the covenant"
- **St. Paul** (Romans 4:11): Circumcision was "a sign or seal of the righteousness that he had by faith" — the sign confirmed what faith had already established
- **Council of Florence** (1442): Circumcision was efficacious for the remission of original sin under the Old Covenant, in the same way that baptism is under the New

### Circumcision and Baptism

Catholic theology draws a direct line from circumcision to baptism:

| Circumcision (Old Covenant) | Baptism (New Covenant) | Source |
|----------------------------|----------------------|--------|
| Sign of covenant membership | Sacrament of covenant initiation | CCC 1214 |
| Performed on the 8th day | The "eighth day" — day of resurrection | Colossians 2:11-12 |
| Applied to infants | Infant baptism practiced from apostolic times | CCC 1250-1252 |
| Marked the flesh | Marks the soul with indelible character | CCC 1272-1274 |
| Required for covenant participation | "Unless one is born of water and the Spirit" (John 3:5) | CCC 1257 |

- **St. Augustine** (*De Baptismo Contra Donatistas*): Just as Abraham circumcised Isaac before Isaac could choose, the Church baptises infants, incorporating them into the covenant by the faith of the community
- **CCC 1250**: "Born with a fallen human nature and tainted by original sin, children also have need of the new birth in Baptism"
- This passage is a primary Catholic argument for infant baptism: God himself commands covenant initiation for those too young to make a personal choice

## The Promise of Isaac (17:15-22)

### Abraham's Laughter (17:17)

Abraham falls on his face and laughs: "Shall a child be born to a man who is a hundred years old?" Catholic interpretation:

- **St. John Chrysostom**: Abraham's laughter is not unbelief but overwhelmed joy — the reaction of one who grasps the sheer improbability of God's grace
- **CCC 489**: The pattern of God working through humanly impossible births (Sarah, Rebekah, Hannah, Elizabeth) reaches its climax in the Virgin Mary
- The name Isaac (*Yitzhak*, "he laughs") memorialises God's power to bring life from apparent death — a theme that will culminate in the Resurrection

### Ishmael Not Excluded (17:18-21)

Abraham intercedes for Ishmael: "Oh that Ishmael might live before you!" God responds by blessing Ishmael but specifying that the covenant will be established through Isaac. Catholic teaching:

- God's blessing is broader than the covenant — Ishmael is blessed but Isaac carries the line of promise
- **CCC 839-841**: The Church recognises God's ongoing relationship with peoples outside the visible covenant community

## Abraham's Obedience (17:23-27)

"That very day Abraham... circumcised" every male in his household. Catholic exegesis emphasises the immediacy and completeness of Abraham's obedience:

- He does not delay or negotiate
- He includes his entire household — the covenant is not individualistic but communal
- **CCC 2570**: Abraham's obedience is the model for the Church's response to God's commands

## Theological Significance

Genesis 17 in Catholic theology:

1. Establishes circumcision as the Old Covenant sign that baptism fulfils and supersedes
2. Provides the primary scriptural basis for Catholic infant baptism
3. Reveals the covenant as a relationship that transforms identity (name changes) and is inscribed on the body
4. Demonstrates God's faithfulness in fulfilling promises that exceed all human possibility
