## The Birth of Isaac and the Expulsion of Hagar

Genesis 21 records the fulfillment of God's promise in the birth of Isaac, followed by the painful expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael. Catholic interpretation celebrates the fidelity of God in fulfilling His covenant promises while also addressing the difficult moral and theological questions raised by Hagar's dismissal.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Birth of Isaac (21:1-7)

"The LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did to Sarah as he had promised."

- **CCC 489**: The birth of Isaac from aged parents prefigures the greater miracle of the Incarnation — God's power transcends natural impossibility
- **St. Paul's allegory** (Galatians 4:21-31): Isaac, the child of promise, represents the new covenant and the freedom of grace, while Ishmael represents the old covenant and the flesh
- **Marian typology**: Sarah's miraculous motherhood anticipates Mary's virginal conception. Both women received a child through divine intervention that exceeded natural expectation

| Old Testament Figure | New Testament Fulfillment | Theological Connection |
|---------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Sarah, barren yet conceiving | Mary, virgin yet conceiving | God's power over nature |
| Isaac, child of promise | Jesus, Son of the eternal promise | Covenant fulfillment |
| Sarah's laughter becomes joy | Mary's Magnificat | Faith transformed by grace |

### Sarah's Laughter (21:6)

"God has made laughter for me; everyone who hears will laugh over me."

- **St. Augustine**: The name Isaac (*yitzhak*, "he laughs") transforms Sarah's doubt (18:12) into joy — a pattern the Church recognizes in the paschal mystery, where sorrow becomes Easter joy
- **Liturgical connection**: The Easter Vigil readings include the Abraham-Isaac narratives, linking the laughter of promise to the joy of the Resurrection

### The Expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael (21:8-21)

This episode presents a genuine moral difficulty. Catholic interpretation offers several perspectives:

- **Pauline allegory**: St. Paul interprets the expulsion typologically (Galatians 4:30), seeing it as the necessary separation between the covenant of grace and the covenant of law
- **God's care for Hagar**: Despite the expulsion, God hears Ishmael's cry and provides water — demonstrating that divine providence extends to all people, not only the covenant line
- **CCC 2575**: God hears the cry of the suffering. Hagar's experience in the wilderness anticipates Israel's experience in the desert, where God provides water from rock

### The Covenant at Beersheba (21:22-34)

Abraham's treaty with Abimelech establishes peace and demonstrates Abraham's growing influence:

- **Catholic social teaching**: The treaty models just relations between peoples, rooted in mutual respect and sworn oaths
- **Abraham as patriarch**: His ability to negotiate as an equal with kings shows that God's blessing has tangible effects in the social order
- **The tamarisk tree and worship**: Abraham plants a tree and calls on the name of the LORD — worship sanctifies the land

## Theological Significance

Genesis 21 in Catholic theology:

- **God keeps His promises**: Isaac's birth is the definitive proof of divine fidelity
- **Grace and nature**: The child of promise comes through supernatural intervention, not human effort alone
- **Universal providence**: God cares for Hagar and Ishmael even outside the covenant line
- **Joy as a fruit of faith**: Sarah's laughter becomes a sign of the joy that comes from trusting God
