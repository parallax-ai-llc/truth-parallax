## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Genesis is guided by the principle that Scripture must be read within the living Tradition of the Church and under the authority of the Magisterium. Unlike *sola scriptura* approaches, Catholic exegesis draws on the Church Fathers, conciliar documents, and papal encyclicals to inform its reading.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Four Senses of Scripture
Following the medieval tradition, Catholic exegesis recognizes multiple layers of meaning:

1. **Literal**: What the text says about historical events
2. **Allegorical**: What the text reveals about Christ and the Church
3. **Moral (Tropological)**: What the text teaches about how to live
4. **Anagogical**: What the text points to regarding eternal destiny

### *Dei Verbum* (1965)
The Second Vatican Council's document on divine revelation established that:
- Scripture must be interpreted in light of the "content and unity of the whole of Scripture"
- The living Tradition of the Church is essential for proper interpretation
- Scientific findings and literary genres must be considered

## Theological Emphases

- **Creation as gift**: The world is gratuitous, not necessary
- **Natural law**: Genesis reveals a moral order inscribed in creation
- **Typology**: Adam as type of Christ, Eve as type of Mary, the Garden as type of the Church
- **Monogenism debate**: The historical reality of a first human pair

## Key Magisterial Documents

| Document | Year | Key Teaching |
|----------|------|-------------|
| *Providentissimus Deus* | 1893 | Scripture cannot err; proper role of science |
| *Divino Afflante Spiritu* | 1943 | Encouraged use of literary genres in interpretation |
| *Humani Generis* | 1950 | Evolution compatible with faith; monogenism required |
| *Dei Verbum* | 1965 | Scripture, Tradition, and Magisterium as one source |

## Notable Catholic Commentators

| Commentator | Era | Contribution |
|-------------|-----|-------------|
| St. Augustine | 4th-5th century | *De Genesi ad Litteram* — non-literal creation days |
| St. Thomas Aquinas | 13th century | Synthesis of faith and reason on creation |
| Henri de Lubac | 20th century | Recovery of the four senses of Scripture |
| Joseph Ratzinger | Contemporary | *In the Beginning* — theological reading of Genesis |
