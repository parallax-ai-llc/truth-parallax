## The Corruption of Humanity and the Command to Build

Genesis 6 marks a turning point in the primeval history: humanity's wickedness reaches such a degree that God resolves to cleanse the earth through a flood, while preserving the righteous Noah and his family. Orthodox tradition reads this chapter as a profound meditation on the consequences of unchecked passion, the patience of God, and the typological significance of the Ark as an image of the Church.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The sons of God saw the daughters of men that they were fair" (6:2)

The identity of the "sons of God" has been debated since antiquity. Two principal Orthodox interpretations exist:

| Interpretation | Proponents | Explanation |
|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Angelic beings | Book of Enoch tradition; some early Fathers | Fallen angels cohabited with human women |
| Sethite line | St. John Chrysostom, St. Augustine, St. Ephrem | The righteous descendants of Seth intermarried with the godless line of Cain |

St. John Chrysostom strongly favored the Sethite interpretation, arguing that angels, being incorporeal, cannot engage in physical union. This reading emphasizes a moral rather than mythological lesson: the mingling of the righteous with the unrighteous leads to universal corruption. The Orthodox marriage canons, which discourage marriages that would compromise the faith of the faithful, find partial grounding in this reading.

### "My Spirit shall not strive with man forever, for he is indeed flesh; yet his days shall be one hundred and twenty years" (6:3)

The Fathers understood the 120 years as a period of repentance granted by God before the judgment of the Flood. St. John Chrysostom emphasized divine patience (*makrothymia*) — God does not act in haste but provides abundant time for repentance. This theme echoes in the Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete: "The Lord waited long for me to turn to Him, but I refused." The liturgical structure of Great Lent itself mirrors this divine patience, providing a sustained period for the faithful to return to God.

### "The Lord was sorry that He had made man on the earth, and He was grieved in His heart" (6:6)

Does God experience regret? The Fathers insisted on the principle of divine impassibility while acknowledging the anthropopathic language of Scripture. St. John Chrysostom explained that Moses uses human language to convey the intensity of God's response to human wickedness — this is *synkatabasis*, divine condescension in speech. God does not change or experience surprise, but Scripture describes His response in terms we can grasp. St. John of Damascus later systematized this approach in *On the Orthodox Faith*, distinguishing between what is properly said of God and what is said according to human analogy.

### "Noah found grace in the eyes of the Lord" (6:8)

Noah is the first person in Scripture explicitly said to have found grace (*charis*). Orthodox theology reads this as a confirmation that righteousness is always a synergy between human effort and divine grace. Noah was righteous not by his own power alone but because he cooperated with God's grace. This synergistic understanding distinguishes Orthodoxy from both Pelagian self-reliance and certain Western formulations of irresistible grace.

### "Make yourself an ark of gopherwood" (6:14)

The Ark is one of the most important typological images in Orthodox theology. The Fathers developed extensive parallels:

- **The Ark = The Church**: Just as the Ark preserved the faithful through the waters of judgment, so the Church preserves the faithful through the waters of baptism (1 Peter 3:20-21)
- **The Door = Christ**: There is one door into the Ark (6:16), and Christ says "I am the door" (John 10:9)
- **Noah = A type of Christ**: The righteous one who saves his household through obedience
- **The Flood = Baptism**: The waters that destroy the old world and inaugurate a new creation

These typological connections are woven into the Orthodox baptismal prayers, which explicitly reference Noah and the Ark.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 6 demonstrates the Orthodox understanding that human freedom can lead to catastrophic corruption when severed from communion with God. Yet even in judgment, God provides a way of salvation. The 120-year period, the preservation of Noah, and the detailed instructions for the Ark all reveal a God who judges with reluctance and saves with intention. The faithful hear this chapter read during the Great Lent parimiae, where it serves as a call to repentance before the Paschal renewal.
