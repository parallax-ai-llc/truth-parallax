## Paradise and the Formation of Humanity

Genesis 2 presents a more intimate account of creation, focusing on the formation of Adam, the planting of Paradise (Eden), and the creation of Eve. Orthodox theology reads this chapter as the establishment of the original human vocation: to cultivate communion with God, grow toward theosis, and exercise priestly stewardship over creation. St. John Chrysostom devoted several of his *Homilies on Genesis* to this chapter, emphasizing God's extraordinary care for humanity and the dignity of the conjugal union.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The Lord God formed man of the dust of the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life" (2:7)

The Fathers see two dimensions here. The formation from dust indicates humanity's kinship with the material creation — a point that grounds the Orthodox affirmation of the body's goodness and its destiny for resurrection. The "breath of life" (*pnoe zoes*) indicates the spiritual soul, the faculty by which humanity is oriented toward God. St. Gregory of Nyssa, in *On the Making of Man*, taught that the human person is uniquely situated at the boundary of the visible and invisible worlds, a mediator called to unite all things in God.

St. Irenaeus used this verse to illustrate his understanding of the complete human person: body formed by God's hands (the Son and the Spirit), animated by divine breath. Humanity is not a soul trapped in a body but an integral union of matter and spirit.

### "The tree of life also in the midst of the garden, and the tree of knowledge of good and evil" (2:9)

The two trees represent the two paths before humanity. Orthodox tradition, following St. Ephrem the Syrian, understands the Tree of Life as a sacramental reality — a genuine source of ongoing communion with God. Some Fathers identify it typologically with the Cross and with the Eucharist. The Tree of Knowledge was not evil in itself; rather, the commandment not to eat from it was pedagogical, a means by which Adam and Eve could exercise their free will and grow into the divine likeness through obedience.

| Symbol | Orthodox Interpretation | Liturgical Echo |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Tree of Life | Communion with God; eternal life | The Cross; the Eucharist |
| Tree of Knowledge | Test of obedience; premature grasping | The fruit of disobedience in Lenten hymnography |
| The Garden | Participation in uncreated grace | Paradise regained through baptism |
| The River | Grace flowing from God's presence | Baptismal waters |

### "It is not good that the man should be alone" (2:18)

St. John Chrysostom emphasized that this verse reveals the social nature of the human person, which reflects the communion of the Holy Trinity. The creation of Eve from Adam's side (2:21-22) is read as the foundation of the mystery of marriage. In the Orthodox wedding service (*Crowning*), the priest explicitly invokes Adam and Eve as the archetype of conjugal union. Marriage is understood not merely as a social contract but as a path of mutual theosis — the spouses help one another grow into the divine likeness.

### "Therefore shall a man leave his father and his mother, and shall cleave unto his wife: and they shall be one flesh" (2:24)

St. Paul quotes this verse in Ephesians 5:31-32 to reveal the mystery of Christ and the Church. Orthodox theology reads the original marriage in Eden as a prophetic icon of the union between Christ (the New Adam) and His Bride, the Church. The nakedness without shame (2:25) signifies the original state of grace — transparency before God, the absence of the passions that arose after the Fall.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 2 establishes several foundational Orthodox doctrines: the psychosomatic unity of the human person, the sacramental character of marriage, and the vocation of humanity as priest of creation. The command to "tend and keep" the garden (2:15) is understood in priestly terms — the Greek *ergazesthai kai phylassein* echoes the Levitical language of liturgical service. Adam is the first priest, and Eden is the first temple.

The chapter also establishes that humanity was created in a state of innocence but not yet in a state of perfection. The Fathers, particularly St. Irenaeus and St. Theophilus of Antioch, taught that Adam and Eve were like children who needed to mature into the fullness of the divine likeness. This is crucial for the Orthodox understanding of ancestral sin: the Fall was not the corruption of a perfected nature but the tragic derailing of an unfinished journey.
