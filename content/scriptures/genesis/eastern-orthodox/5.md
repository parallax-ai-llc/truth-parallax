## The Book of the Generations of Adam

Genesis 5 presents the genealogy from Adam to Noah, a ten-generation line that bridges the narrative from creation to the Flood. Orthodox tradition reads this chapter through the Septuagint (LXX), which is the authoritative Old Testament text for the Orthodox Church. The chronological figures in the LXX differ significantly from the Masoretic text used in most Western Bibles, yielding a longer chronology. The Fathers treated these genealogies not as incidental data but as theologically meaningful records of God's providential care for the messianic line.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "This is the book of the generations of Adam. In the day that God created man, in the likeness of God made he him" (5:1)

The chapter opens by reaffirming the *imago Dei* — even after the Fall, humanity retains the image of God. St. John Chrysostom noted that the genealogy begins with a reminder of human dignity precisely because the preceding chapter ended with the degradation of Cain's line. The image of God, though darkened by sin, is never destroyed. This is the theological basis for the Orthodox insistence on the inherent dignity of every human person and the possibility of repentance at any stage of life.

### "And he died" — The Refrain of Mortality

The most striking feature of Genesis 5 is the repeated refrain: "and he died." Despite the extraordinary longevity of the antediluvian patriarchs, each entry concludes with death. The Fathers read this as a sober meditation on the consequence of ancestral sin. Death is not natural to human beings — it is an intruder, a consequence of the separation from the Tree of Life. The liturgical hymns of the funeral service echo this understanding: "I weep and I wail when I think upon death."

### "Seth begat a son in his own likeness, after his image" (5:3)

A subtle but important distinction: Adam was made in God's image, but Seth is born in Adam's likeness. The Fathers noted that the transmission of life after the Fall includes the transmission of mortality and a darkened condition. This is the Orthodox understanding of ancestral sin — not inherited guilt, but inherited mortality and the inclination toward passion. St. Cyril of Alexandria wrote that death, having entered through Adam, passed to all his descendants as a kind of contagion.

### "Enoch walked with God; and he was not, for God took him" (5:24)

Enoch's translation is one of the most remarkable verses in Genesis. He did not die but was taken by God — a sign that death is not the final word. The Orthodox Church reads Enoch's translation as a foreshadowing of Christ's Ascension and of the general resurrection. In the hymnography of the Ascension, the faithful sing of Christ opening the way to heaven that had been closed since Adam's expulsion. Enoch and Elijah (who was also taken up) are traditionally identified as the two witnesses of Revelation 11.

St. Maximus the Confessor interpreted Enoch's "walking with God" as a description of the contemplative life — the nous turned perpetually toward God, undistracted by the passions. This is the goal of hesychastic prayer: to "walk with God" unceasingly.

## Theological Significance

The genealogy of Genesis 5 serves as a bridge between the theology of creation and the theology of salvation. It establishes that God preserves a righteous line through which His purposes will be fulfilled. The faithful are not anonymous — each name matters, each generation carries forward the promise. The Orthodox liturgical calendar commemorates the righteous forefathers (the Sunday before Christmas, the Sunday of the Holy Forefathers), reading these genealogies as part of the preparation for the Incarnation.

The chapter also introduces the paradox that defines the entire Old Testament: life and death coexist, and humanity's extraordinary gifts (longevity, the image of God) are shadowed by the reality of mortality. The resolution will come only in Christ, the New Adam, who tramples down death by death.
