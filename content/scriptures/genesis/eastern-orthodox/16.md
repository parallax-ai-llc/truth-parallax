## Hagar, Ishmael, and God Who Sees

Genesis 16 introduces the complex narrative of Sarai's barrenness leading to the surrogate arrangement with Hagar, the resulting conflict, Hagar's flight into the wilderness, and her encounter with the Angel of the Lord. Orthodox theology reads this chapter with attention to the human suffering involved, the mercy of God toward all persons regardless of status, and the typological significance of the two covenants that St. Paul develops in Galatians 4.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Sarai said to Abram, 'The Lord has restrained me from bearing children. Please, go in to my maid'" (16:2)

St. John Chrysostom treated Sarai's proposal with nuance. He acknowledged the cultural legitimacy of surrogate arrangements in the ancient Near East while noting that Sarai's plan represented a failure of patience — an attempt to fulfill God's promise through human contrivance rather than waiting for divine action. The Fathers saw this as a recurring pattern in salvation history: the human temptation to force God's hand rather than endure the waiting that faith requires.

Abram's compliance is also instructive. St. John Chrysostom noted that Abram, though a man of great faith, was not immune to the pressure of immediate circumstances. The spiritual life is not a linear ascent but a path marked by stumbles that God incorporates into His larger plan.

### "When she saw that she had conceived, her mistress became despised in her eyes" (16:4)

The shift in Hagar's attitude and Sarai's subsequent harshness reveal the corrosive effects of sin on human relationships. St. Ephrem the Syrian observed that the arrangement created a situation of structural injustice: Hagar was used as a means to an end, and the resulting power dynamics produced resentment on all sides. Orthodox social teaching, rooted in patristic commentary, recognizes that unjust structures generate cycles of pain that entangle everyone involved.

| Character | Situation | Patristic Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Sarai | Proposes the surrogate arrangement | Impatience; attempting to control God's plan |
| Abram | Complies without consulting God | Passivity; failure to exercise discernment |
| Hagar | Despises Sarai after conceiving | Pride arising from new status |
| All three | Entangled in mutual suffering | The consequences of acting outside God's timing |

### "The Angel of the Lord found her by a spring of water in the wilderness" (16:7)

Hagar, fleeing into the desert, is met by the Angel of the Lord — whom many Fathers identified as a pre-incarnate manifestation of the Son of God (a Christophany). The significance is profound: God seeks out a foreign slave woman in the wilderness. She is not Abram's covenant partner; she has no standing in the promise. Yet God cares for her, speaks to her, and makes promises about her son.

St. John Chrysostom emphasized this as evidence of God's universal love. The Orthodox Church insists that divine care extends to all persons, not only to the elect. The Angel's question — "Where have you come from, and where are you going?" (16:8) — echoes the divine questions addressed to Adam ("Where are you?") and to Cain ("Where is Abel?"). God initiates conversation with the suffering person, drawing them out of isolation and into relationship.

### "You shall call his name Ishmael, because the Lord has heard your affliction" (16:11)

The name Ishmael means "God hears." Despite the fact that Ishmael is not the child of promise, God names him and promises him a future. The Orthodox tradition does not demonize Ishmael or Hagar; rather, it recognizes that God's providence encompasses all peoples. The blessing given to Ishmael — "he will be a wild man; his hand shall be against every man, and every man's hand against him" (16:12) — is read as a prophetic description rather than a curse.

### "She called the name of the Lord who spoke to her, You-Are-the-God-Who-Sees" (16:13)

Hagar gives God a name — *El Roi*, "the God who sees." She is the first person in Scripture to name God. This is remarkable: a foreign slave woman has a direct encounter with the living God and responds with theological insight. St. Gregory the Theologian reflected on the paradox of divine vision: God sees all things, yet to be seen by God is not surveillance but salvation. To be seen by God is to be known, loved, and preserved.

The well is named Beer-lahai-roi — "the well of the Living One who sees me." The Fathers connected this to the Orthodox understanding of theophany: God makes Himself present at particular places, sanctifying them. This principle underlies the veneration of holy places, icons, and relics in Orthodox practice.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 16 demonstrates that God's mercy is not limited to the righteous or the chosen. Hagar, outside the covenant, receives a theophany, a promise, and a naming. Orthodox theology holds that God's salvific will extends to all humanity, even when His economy works through particular persons and peoples. The chapter also warns against the attempt to achieve God's purposes through human manipulation rather than patient faith — a theme that will recur throughout Genesis.
