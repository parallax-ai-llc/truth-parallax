## The Death and Burial of Sarah

Genesis 23 records the death of Sarah at the age of 127, Abraham's mourning for her, and his purchase of the cave of Machpelah from Ephron the Hittite as a burial site. Though seemingly a simple narrative of real estate negotiation, Orthodox theology finds in this chapter rich theological reflection on death, mourning, the dignity of the body, and the faith that clings to God's promise even in the face of loss.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Sarah lived one hundred and twenty-seven years; these were the years of the life of Sarah" (23:1)

Sarah is the only woman in the Old Testament whose age at death is recorded, signaling her unique importance in salvation history. The Orthodox Church honors Sarah as a righteous foremother, commemorated on the Sunday of the Holy Forefathers (two Sundays before Christmas). St. John Chrysostom observed that Sarah's entire life was marked by the interplay of promise and patience — decades of barrenness, followed by the miraculous birth of Isaac. Her life is a model of the faith that endures.

### "Abraham came to mourn for Sarah and to weep for her" (23:2)

The Fathers noted that Abraham mourns — genuinely, openly — and then rises to attend to the practical necessities of burial. Orthodox pastoral theology draws on this pattern: grief is legitimate, even holy, but it does not paralyze the person of faith. The funeral service of the Orthodox Church balances lamentation with hope, sorrow with the assurance of resurrection. The kontakion of the funeral service captures this: "With the saints give rest, O Christ, to the soul of your servant, where there is neither sickness nor sorrow nor sighing, but life everlasting."

The Orthodox tradition of mourning includes specific periods (the third day, the ninth day, the fortieth day, and the annual memorial), acknowledging that grief requires time and communal support. Abraham's public mourning for Sarah established the paradigm for honoring the dead with both tears and dignity.

### The Negotiation for the Cave of Machpelah (23:3-18)

The extended negotiation between Abraham and the Hittites is far more than a real estate transaction. It is Abraham's first act of land ownership in Canaan — the promised land. The Fathers saw deep significance in this:

| Detail | Surface Meaning | Theological Significance |
|--------|----------------|-------------------------|
| Abraham owns no land | He is a sojourner | The faithful are "strangers and pilgrims" on earth |
| He buys a burial cave | Practical necessity | Faith in the promise extends beyond death |
| Full price paid publicly | Fair transaction | The land is acquired by justice, not conquest |
| A cave — hidden, underground | Burial space | The body rests in hope of resurrection |

St. Ephrem the Syrian noted that Abraham, who had been promised "all the land," begins by purchasing a single cave for the dead. This is the paradox of faith: the promise is vast, but its present realization is small. Abraham does not complain or doubt; he acts within the limitations of the present while trusting in the fullness of the future. This is the posture of the Orthodox Christian during Great Lent and in all earthly life — living within the "already" of God's grace while awaiting the "not yet" of the fullness of the Kingdom.

### "Abraham buried Sarah his wife in the cave of the field of Machpelah" (23:19)

The careful burial of Sarah's body reflects the Orthodox conviction that the human body is sacred. The body is not a prison to be discarded but a temple of the Holy Spirit destined for resurrection. The Orthodox funeral rite includes the anointing of the body with oil, the placement of an icon on the chest, and the covering with a funeral shroud — all expressing reverence for the body that will be raised.

St. John of Damascus defended the veneration of the relics of saints on precisely this basis: if the body is the dwelling place of the Holy Spirit, then even after death it remains worthy of honor. Abraham's careful purchase of a permanent burial site — rather than a temporary or improvised arrangement — demonstrates this reverence. The cave of Machpelah would become the burial place of Abraham, Isaac, Rebekah, Jacob, and Leah, making it a site of profound pilgrimage significance.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 23 teaches the Orthodox faithful how to face death with faith. Abraham mourns because death is an enemy (1 Corinthians 15:26) — it is not natural or good. But he acts with purpose because death is not the final word. The purchase of Machpelah is an act of hope: Abraham buries Sarah in the promised land because he believes that the promise of God transcends the grave. The Epistle to the Hebrews confirms this: the patriarchs "died in faith, not having received the promises, but having seen them afar off ... they desired a better country, that is, a heavenly one" (Hebrews 11:13-16).

The chapter also teaches the integration of the spiritual and the practical. Faith does not exempt the believer from the ordinary work of negotiation, commerce, and social interaction. Abraham deals honestly and generously with the Hittites, modeling the Orthodox conviction that every aspect of life — including business — is to be conducted in the light of God's presence.
