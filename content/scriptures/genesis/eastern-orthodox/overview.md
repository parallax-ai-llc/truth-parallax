## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Genesis is rooted in the consensus of the Church Fathers (*consensus patrum*) and the liturgical life of the Church. Unlike Western approaches that may emphasize juridical categories (guilt, punishment, satisfaction), Orthodoxy reads Genesis through the lens of *theosis* — humanity's calling to participate in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4).

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Exegesis
The Orthodox Church privileges the readings of the Greek Fathers:
- **St. Basil the Great**: *Hexaemeron* (Nine Homilies on the Six Days of Creation)
- **St. Gregory of Nyssa**: *On the Making of Man*
- **St. John Chrysostom**: *Homilies on Genesis* (67 homilies)
- **St. Maximus the Confessor**: Cosmic Christology and the *logoi* of creation

### Liturgical Reading
Genesis is read extensively during Great Lent, particularly in the *parimiae* (Old Testament readings at Vespers). The liturgical context shapes how the text is understood — always pointing toward Christ and the Paschal mystery.

### The Four Senses
Like the Catholic tradition, Orthodoxy recognizes multiple senses of Scripture, but with distinctive emphasis:
1. **Historical**: What happened
2. **Typological**: How it prefigures Christ and the Church
3. **Moral/Tropological**: How it applies to the spiritual life
4. **Anagogical**: How it points to the eschaton and theosis

## Theological Emphases

- **Creation as theophany**: The world reveals God's uncreated energies (Palamite theology)
- **The Fall as ontological catastrophe**: Not primarily juridical guilt but a distortion of human nature
- **Ancestral sin vs. original sin**: Orthodox theology distinguishes from the Augustinian/Western formulation
- **Restoration in Christ**: The Second Adam restores what the first Adam lost — the path to deification
