## The Ancestral Transgression

Genesis 3 recounts the temptation by the serpent, the disobedience of Adam and Eve, and the consequences that follow. Orthodox theology refers to this event as the *ancestral sin* (*propatirikon hamartima*), deliberately distinguishing it from the Western Augustinian concept of "original sin." The difference is not merely terminological: Orthodoxy teaches that humanity inherited the consequences of Adam's sin — mortality, corruption, a darkened nous — but not personal guilt for his act. St. John Chrysostom and St. Maximus the Confessor are the principal patristic voices on this chapter.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "The serpent was more cunning than any beast of the field" (3:1)

The Fathers identify the serpent with the devil, a fallen angelic being who used the animal as an instrument. St. John Chrysostom emphasized that the serpent approached Eve with a question — "Did God really say?" — thereby introducing doubt about God's goodness. The strategy of the enemy is not frontal assault but subtle distortion of truth. This patristic insight informs the Orthodox understanding of spiritual warfare (*nepsis*) and the need for watchfulness of the heart.

### "You will be like gods, knowing good and evil" (3:5)

The supreme irony: humanity was already called to become like God through obedience, growth, and grace. The serpent's lie offered a counterfeit theosis — deification through autonomy rather than communion. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that Adam chose the way of self-will (*autexousia* misused) over the way of love, grasping at the end while rejecting the means. This is the fundamental structure of all sin: seeking a genuine good through a disordered path.

### "The eyes of them both were opened, and they knew that they were naked" (3:7)

The "opening of eyes" was not illumination but the dawning of shame. The nakedness that had been a sign of grace and transparency now became a source of fear. St. Gregory of Nyssa interpreted the coverings of fig leaves as humanity's attempt to clothe itself in its own righteousness — a self-help project doomed to fail. Only God can provide adequate covering.

### "The Lord God made garments of skins, and clothed them" (3:21)

This verse has profound significance in Orthodox theology. The "garments of skin" (*dermatinous chitonas*) have been interpreted in multiple ways:

| Interpretation | Proponent | Significance |
|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Mortality and bodily corruptibility | St. Gregory of Nyssa | The body became subject to death after the Fall |
| God's merciful provision | St. John Chrysostom | Even in judgment, God clothes and cares for His creatures |
| Coarsened materiality | St. Maximus the Confessor | The cosmos itself was affected by human sin |
| Typology of baptism | Liturgical tradition | The baptismal garment replaces the garments of skin |

In the baptismal rite, the newly illumined is clothed in a white garment, symbolizing the restoration of the original robe of glory that was lost at the Fall. The Lenten hymn "I have stripped off the robe of glory" (from the Canon of St. Andrew of Crete) directly references this theology.

### "I will put enmity between you and the woman" (3:15) — The Protoevangelion

Orthodox tradition reads this as the first proclamation of the Gospel. The "seed of the woman" who will crush the serpent's head is Christ. The Theotokos (Mother of God) is the New Eve who reverses the disobedience of the first Eve through her obedient "Let it be to me according to your word" (Luke 1:38). This typology is central to Orthodox Mariology and is celebrated in the hymnography of the Annunciation.

## Theological Significance

The Orthodox understanding of the Fall centers on mortality rather than guilt. When God said "you shall surely die," He described not a judicial punishment but an ontological consequence: separation from the Source of life brings death. Humanity fell into a condition of mortality, and this mortality gave rise to fear, selfishness, and all the passions. As St. Athanasius wrote in *On the Incarnation*, humanity was "returning to nothingness" — and it was to reverse this trajectory that the Word became flesh.

The expulsion from Paradise is read during the Vespers of Forgiveness Sunday, which opens Great Lent. The entire Lenten journey is structured as a return to Paradise, culminating in Pascha, when Christ descends into Hades and leads Adam and Eve out by the hand — an image depicted in the iconic Anastasis (Resurrection) icon.
