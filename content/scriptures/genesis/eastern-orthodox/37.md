## Joseph Sold into Slavery

Genesis 37 opens the Joseph cycle, the great concluding narrative of Genesis. Joseph, favored son of Jacob and Rachel, receives a special robe from his father and is given two prophetic dreams in which his family bows before him. His brothers, already consumed by jealousy over his favored status, plot to kill him. Reuben intervenes, persuading them to cast Joseph into a pit. In Reuben's absence, Judah proposes selling Joseph to Ishmaelite traders bound for Egypt. The brothers dip Joseph's robe in goat's blood and present it to Jacob, who mourns his son as dead. Joseph is sold in Egypt to Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "Israel loved Joseph more than any other of his sons" (37:3)

St. John Chrysostom addresses Jacob's favoritism with characteristic directness: parental partiality is a recurring pattern of ancestral sin in this family — Isaac and Rebekah each favored a different son, and now Jacob repeats the cycle. Yet Chrysostom also notes that Joseph's special status has a providential dimension: the beloved son must go down into a kind of death so that he might rise and save his family. The pattern is Christological through and through.

### The Two Dreams (37:5-11)

| Dream | Imagery | Fulfillment | Typological Meaning |
|-------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| First (37:7) | Sheaves of grain bowing | Brothers bow before Joseph in Egypt (42:6) | The nations bow before Christ, the Bread of Life |
| Second (37:9) | Sun, moon, eleven stars | Entire family comes to Egypt (46-47) | All creation worships Christ at the Parousia |

St. Ephrem the Syrian reads Joseph's dreams as prophetic revelations given by the Holy Spirit. The imagery of cosmic obeisance — sun, moon, and stars — points beyond the immediate fulfillment to the eschatological worship described in Revelation. In Orthodox iconography, the Pantocrator icon depicts Christ as the one before whom all creation bows, fulfilling what Joseph's dreams prefigured.

### "Come, let us sell him to the Ishmaelites" (37:27)

The selling of Joseph for twenty pieces of silver is one of the most explicit Old Testament types of Christ's betrayal. The Fathers elaborate the parallels with exacting detail:

- The beloved son is sent by his father to his brothers (cf. John 3:16)
- He is rejected by his own (cf. John 1:11)
- He is stripped of his garment (cf. John 19:23-24)
- He is sold for silver (cf. Matthew 26:15)
- He descends into a pit before his exaltation (cf. Philippians 2:8-9)
- His "death" is reported to his father through a blood-stained garment (cf. the Passion)

The Holy Week hymns of the Orthodox Church draw heavily on these parallels. On Great Wednesday, the Bridegroom Matins service includes hymns that explicitly link Judas's betrayal to the brothers' sale of Joseph.

### Jacob's Mourning (37:34-35)

Jacob tears his garments and mourns, refusing to be comforted. St. Maximus the Confessor sees in Jacob's grief an image of the human condition after separation from God — inconsolable sorrow that can only be healed by reunion with the beloved. Jacob will not know comfort until he sees Joseph again, just as humanity cannot find rest until reunited with Christ.

## Theological Significance

- **Joseph as supreme Old Testament type of Christ**: The Orthodox liturgical tradition treats Joseph as the most complete prefiguration of Christ in the Old Testament. The kontakion for Great Monday makes this explicit: "Joseph, cast into a pit by his envious brothers, prefigured Thee, O Christ, sold by Thy disciple." The entire Joseph narrative is read through this Christological lens.
- **Providence and the mystery of evil**: How does God accomplish good through evil actions? The Fathers answer: not by causing evil but by weaving it into His redemptive plan. The brothers' sin is real and culpable; God's use of it is sovereign and good. This paradox lies at the heart of Orthodox theodicy.
- **The passions as source of sin**: The brothers' jealousy (*phthonos*) is the root passion driving the narrative. St. Basil the Great identifies envy as among the most destructive of the passions — it attacks the good itself, resenting another's blessing. Orthodox ascetical theology teaches that the passions must be purified, not by suppression but by transformation through grace.
