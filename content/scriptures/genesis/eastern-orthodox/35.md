## Purification and Return

Genesis 35 marks a turning point in Jacob's life. God commands him to return to Bethel — the place of his ladder vision — and build an altar there. Before departing, Jacob orders his household to put away all foreign gods and purify themselves. They bury the idols under the oak at Shechem. At Bethel, God appears to Jacob again, confirms his new name Israel, and renews the covenant promises. On the journey south, Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin near Bethlehem and is buried there. Reuben sleeps with Bilhah, Jacob's concubine. The chapter concludes with a list of Jacob's twelve sons and the death of Isaac at Hebron, where Esau and Jacob bury him together.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "Put away the foreign gods that are among you and purify yourselves" (35:2)

St. John Chrysostom sees Jacob's command as a model of repentance (*metanoia*) — a decisive break with idolatry that must precede encounter with God. The burial of the idols under the oak at Shechem is a liturgical act of renunciation. In the Orthodox baptismal rite, the catechumen faces west and spits upon Satan, then turns east toward Christ. Jacob's household enacts this same turning — from false gods to the living God.

### "God appeared to Jacob again... and blessed him" (35:9)

The second theophany at Bethel completes what began in Genesis 28. The Fathers read the two Bethel encounters as a frame around Jacob's exile — departure and return, promise and fulfillment. St. Maximus the Confessor teaches that the spiritual life follows this same rhythm: God gives a vision that initiates the journey, the soul passes through long struggle, and God confirms the vision upon return.

| Event | Spiritual Meaning |
|-------|------------------|
| Putting away foreign gods | Renunciation of the passions; first step of purification |
| Purification and changing garments | Baptismal imagery; putting on the new self |
| Building the altar at Bethel | Liturgical worship as fulfillment of repentance |
| God's reappearance | Theophany as reward for faithfulness |
| Renewal of the name "Israel" | Confirmation of the transformed identity |
| Rachel's death in childbirth | The cost of bringing new life; suffering and glory intertwined |

### Rachel's Death and Benjamin's Birth (35:16-20)

Rachel's death giving birth to Benjamin is one of the most poignant moments in Genesis. She names the child Ben-oni ("son of my sorrow"), but Jacob renames him Benjamin ("son of the right hand"). St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in this double naming the paradox of the Christian life: what appears as sorrow to the world is revealed as glory from God's perspective. The Fathers also note that Rachel's tomb near Bethlehem becomes a site of prophetic weeping (Jeremiah 31:15; Matthew 2:18), linking Rachel's grief to the suffering of the Holy Innocents — and ultimately to the Theotokos standing at the Cross.

### Reuben's Sin (35:22)

Reuben's transgression with Bilhah receives only a single verse, but its consequences echo through the rest of Genesis. Jacob "heard of it" — and the silence that follows is ominous. St. Chrysostom reads this silence as a father's grief too deep for words. Reuben's sin will cost him the firstborn's blessing (49:3-4) and illustrates the Orthodox teaching that sin always has consequences, even when forgiven.

## Theological Significance

- **Repentance as return**: The structure of Genesis 35 — putting away idols, purifying, returning to the place of original encounter — maps the Orthodox understanding of repentance. *Metanoia* is not merely feeling sorry but a comprehensive reorientation: renouncing what is false, purifying the heart, and returning to communion with God. The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete, sung during Lent, follows exactly this pattern.
- **Death and new life**: Rachel's death and Benjamin's birth, occurring together, embody the Paschal mystery. Life comes through death; joy through sorrow. The Orthodox funeral service proclaims this truth: "In the midst of life we are in death," yet death itself is the passage to resurrection.
- **The gathering of the twelve**: With Benjamin's birth, the twelve sons are complete. The Fathers see the number twelve as signifying cosmic completeness — twelve tribes, twelve apostles, the fullness of God's people. The foundation of the Church is laid in the loins of Jacob-Israel.
