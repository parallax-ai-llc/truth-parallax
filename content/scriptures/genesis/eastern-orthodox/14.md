## The War of the Kings and the Blessing of Melchizedek

Genesis 14 recounts a military campaign of four eastern kings against five kings of the Jordan plain, the capture of Lot, Abram's armed rescue, and the mysterious encounter with Melchizedek, king of Salem and priest of God Most High. For Orthodox theology, the central significance of this chapter lies in the figure of Melchizedek, whose offering of bread and wine and whose priesthood "without genealogy" are read as a profound type of Christ and the Eucharist.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### Abram the Warrior (14:13-16)

Abram, called here "the Hebrew" for the first time, gathers 318 trained servants and pursues the kings who captured Lot. The Fathers noted Abram's courage and loyalty: he did not abandon his kinsman despite Lot's earlier choice to separate. St. John Chrysostom praised Abram's bravery while emphasizing that the victory was God's — a small band defeating a coalition of kings is clearly a divine act. The number 318 was noted by some early Christian writers (such as the Epistle of Barnabas) as having symbolic significance: in Greek numerals, 318 can be represented as TIH, where T resembles the cross and IH are the first two letters of Jesus' name.

### "Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine; he was the priest of God Most High" (14:18)

This is one of the most theologically charged verses in the Old Testament for Orthodox Christianity. Melchizedek appears without introduction, without genealogy, without recorded birth or death. The Epistle to the Hebrews (7:1-3) interprets this silence as typologically significant: Melchizedek is "made like the Son of God" in his mysterious, eternal priesthood.

The Orthodox Church reads Melchizedek's offering of bread and wine as the first Eucharistic type in Scripture:

| Element | Old Testament Reality | New Testament Fulfillment |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Bread and wine | Melchizedek's offering | The Eucharistic gifts |
| Priest of God Most High | Melchizedek's title | Christ's eternal priesthood |
| King of Salem (peace) | Earthly kingship | Christ, the Prince of Peace |
| Without genealogy | No recorded ancestry | The eternal, uncreated Son |
| Blessing of Abram | Priest blesses patriarch | Christ blesses His Church |

In the Liturgy of St. Basil the Great, the anaphora explicitly references Melchizedek: the bread and wine offered by the faithful are placed in continuity with the offering of this ancient priest-king. The Orthodox priesthood is understood as participating in the eternal priesthood of Christ, which is "according to the order of Melchizedek" (Psalm 109/110:4), not according to the Levitical order that came later through Moses.

### "Blessed be Abram of God Most High, Possessor of heaven and earth" (14:19)

Melchizedek blesses Abram in the name of "God Most High" (*El Elyon*). The Fathers identified El Elyon with the one true God worshipped by the Church. St. Clement of Alexandria understood Melchizedek's priesthood as evidence that true knowledge and worship of God existed outside the line of Abraham — a point relevant to the Orthodox understanding of the "seeds of the Word" (*logoi spermatikoi*) present in all cultures.

### "He gave him a tithe of all" (14:20)

Abram offers a tithe — a tenth of the spoils — to Melchizedek. The Epistle to the Hebrews argues that this demonstrates Melchizedek's superiority to Abraham and, by extension, to the Levitical priesthood descended from Abraham. Orthodox theology uses this argument to establish the supremacy of Christ's priesthood over the Old Testament sacrificial system. The practice of tithing in the Orthodox Church, while not legalistic, finds its first scriptural precedent here.

### Abram Refuses the King of Sodom (14:21-24)

After the encounter with Melchizedek, the king of Sodom offers Abram the spoils of war. Abram refuses: "I will not take a thread or a sandal strap ... lest you should say, 'I have made Abram rich.'" St. John Chrysostom contrasted the two kings: from the priest-king of Salem, Abram receives a blessing; from the king of Sodom, Abram takes nothing. The faithful must discern the source of their gifts — what comes from God should be received with gratitude; what comes from the world's corruption should be refused.

## Theological Significance

The encounter with Melchizedek is read in every Orthodox Liturgy, not as text but as lived reality. When the priest offers bread and wine at the altar, he stands in the line of Melchizedek and, through him, in the eternal priesthood of Christ. The chapter demonstrates that God's salvific work is not confined to one lineage — Melchizedek precedes Abraham, operates outside the covenant people, and yet serves the Most High God. This universality of grace is central to the Orthodox understanding of God's economy.
