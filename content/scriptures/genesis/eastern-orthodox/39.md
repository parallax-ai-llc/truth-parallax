## Joseph in Potiphar's House

Genesis 39 follows Joseph into Egypt, where he is sold to Potiphar, captain of Pharaoh's guard. The Lord is with Joseph, and he prospers in everything he does. Potiphar places him in charge of his entire household. Potiphar's wife repeatedly attempts to seduce Joseph, but he refuses, declaring, "How can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" One day she seizes his garment; he flees, leaving it in her hand. She accuses him of assault. Potiphar imprisons Joseph, but even in prison the Lord is with him, and the keeper of the prison entrusts him with all the prisoners.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "The Lord was with Joseph, and he became a successful man" (39:2)

St. John Chrysostom emphasizes that Joseph's success is not worldly luck but the fruit of divine presence. The phrase "the Lord was with Joseph" recurs throughout the chapter (vv. 2, 3, 21, 23) like a liturgical refrain. Chrysostom teaches that when God is with a person, no external circumstance — slavery, false accusation, imprisonment — can destroy them. This is the foundation of the Orthodox understanding of martyrdom: the faithful may suffer outwardly while being inwardly sustained by divine grace.

### "How can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (39:9)

Joseph's response to temptation is among the most celebrated examples of virtue in patristic literature. The Fathers note that Joseph appeals not to social consequences or personal honor but to God — "sin against God." This reveals a conscience formed by divine awareness. St. Maximus the Confessor would identify Joseph as one who has achieved *apatheia* — not the absence of feeling but the mastery of the passions through love of God.

| Element | Joseph's Temptation | Christ's Temptation |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Setting | Foreign land, separated from family | Wilderness, alone |
| Tempter | Potiphar's wife — appeals to desire | Satan — appeals to power, glory, appetite |
| Response | "How can I sin against God?" | "It is written..." |
| Consequence | False accusation and imprisonment | Angels minister to Him |
| Outcome | Exaltation from prison to throne | Exaltation from Cross to Resurrection |

### Joseph Flees (39:12)

The Fathers consistently praise Joseph's decision to flee rather than fight. St. Basil the Great teaches that flight from temptation is not cowardice but wisdom. The ascetical tradition of the Desert Fathers follows this principle: when confronted with overwhelming temptation, the monk is counseled to flee — physically if necessary. St. John Climacus lists flight from occasions of sin as one of the essential strategies of spiritual combat.

St. Ephrem the Syrian draws attention to the garment left behind: Joseph loses his outer garment but preserves his inner virtue. This echoes the loss of the special robe in chapter 37. In both cases, Joseph is stripped of external coverings, yet his essential identity — the beloved son, the faithful servant — remains intact. The Fathers see here a type of Christian martyrdom: the body may be stripped and destroyed, but the soul clothed in Christ cannot be harmed.

### "The keeper of the prison committed to Joseph's care all the prisoners" (39:22)

Even in prison, Joseph's character produces authority. Chrysostom marvels at how God transforms every location into a place of ministry for Joseph — Potiphar's house, the prison, and eventually all of Egypt. This illustrates the Orthodox teaching that the saint sanctifies the place, not the place the saint. Wherever a person filled with the Holy Spirit is present, that space becomes a site of divine activity.

## Theological Significance

- **Chastity as spiritual combat**: Joseph's resistance to seduction is a central text in Orthodox ascetical theology. The monastic tradition holds up Joseph as the supreme Old Testament example of *sophrosyne* (chastity, temperance, self-mastery). His story demonstrates that virtue requires both grace and effort — God is with Joseph, yet Joseph must choose to flee.
- **Suffering of the righteous**: Joseph's imprisonment despite his innocence raises the perennial question of righteous suffering. The Fathers answer: suffering is not always punishment; it can be the crucible in which virtue is perfected. Joseph's prison becomes the antechamber of his exaltation, just as Christ's tomb becomes the site of Resurrection.
- **Joseph as type of Christ descending**: Joseph's descent — from favored son to slave to prisoner — mirrors the *kenosis* (self-emptying) of Christ described in Philippians 2. The Orthodox Paschal hymn proclaims, "Christ descended into the tomb" — and in Joseph's story, the Fathers see this descent prefigured. The deeper the descent, the more glorious the ascent.
