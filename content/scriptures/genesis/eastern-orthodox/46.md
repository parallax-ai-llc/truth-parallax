## The Journey to Egypt

Genesis 46 recounts Jacob's departure for Egypt to be reunited with Joseph. At Beersheba, God appears to Jacob in a night vision, reassuring him: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for I will make you a great nation there. I will go down with you and I will also bring you up again." The chapter includes a genealogy listing the seventy members of Jacob's household who enter Egypt. Upon arrival, Judah is sent ahead to Goshen, and Joseph rides out to meet his father. They embrace, and Jacob declares, "Now let me die, since I have seen your face."

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt... I will go down with you" (46:3-4)

St. John Chrysostom identifies this theophany as one of the most tender moments of divine reassurance in Scripture. Jacob's fear is understandable — Egypt is outside the promised land, and the family's descent there seems to contradict the covenant promise. Yet God accompanies His people even into exile. Chrysostom teaches that God's presence is not tied to geography but to relationship. Wherever the faithful go, God goes with them.

The Fathers see in Jacob's descent to Egypt a type of Christ's descent into Hades. Just as Jacob goes down to find his son alive among the "dead," so Christ descends to the realm of death to liberate the captives. The promise "I will bring you up again" prefigures the Resurrection — the great *anabasis* (ascent) that reverses every descent.

### The Seventy Souls (46:27)

| Number | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| 70 souls enter Egypt | Corresponds to the 70 nations of Genesis 10 |
| 70 elders appointed by Moses | Continuation of the pattern (Numbers 11:16) |
| 70 disciples sent by Christ | The universal mission (Luke 10:1) |
| The Septuagint (LXX) | Translated by 70 (or 72) elders |

St. Ephrem the Syrian notes that the number seventy signifies universality. The seventy souls who enter Egypt represent the whole of Israel — a microcosm of all humanity. The Fathers see a deliberate correspondence with the seventy nations descended from Noah (Genesis 10): Israel enters Egypt as a condensed image of the human race, destined to be redeemed by God and brought up to the promised land. The Orthodox Church's use of the Septuagint (LXX) — the Greek translation of the Old Testament traditionally attributed to seventy translators — reinforces this numerical theology.

### The Genealogy (46:8-27)

Orthodox theology reads genealogies not as antiquarian records but as testimonies to God's faithfulness across generations. Each name represents a person known to God, held in divine memory. The liturgical commemoration of saints follows this same logic: the Church remembers, generation by generation, those who have gone before in faith. The *Synaxarion* (the daily reading of saints' lives) is the Church's ongoing genealogy of grace.

### "Now let me die, since I have seen your face" (46:30)

Jacob's words upon embracing Joseph echo Simeon's *Nunc Dimittis*: "Lord, now lettest thou thy servant depart in peace, for mine eyes have seen thy salvation" (Luke 2:29-30). St. Maximus the Confessor would read both statements as expressions of *theoria* — the vision of God's saving purpose made manifest. For Jacob, seeing Joseph alive is seeing God's faithfulness incarnated. For Simeon, holding the Christ child is seeing the same faithfulness in its fullness.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine accompaniment**: The promise "I will go down with you" is the foundation of the Orthodox theology of divine presence. God does not observe from a distance but enters into the depths of human experience. The Incarnation is the ultimate fulfillment of this promise — God descending not merely to Egypt but into human nature itself, into death and Hades.
- **Exile as preparation**: The descent into Egypt, while seemingly a departure from the promise, is actually its necessary preparation. The family must grow into a nation, and this can only happen in Egypt. The Fathers see here a principle of spiritual growth: periods of apparent exile — times of spiritual dryness, suffering, or displacement — often serve a divine purpose that is revealed only in retrospect.
- **The family as church**: The seventy souls entering Egypt are not merely individuals but a community of faith bound by covenant. Orthodox ecclesiology understands the family (*oikos*) as the "domestic church" (*kat' oikon ekklesia*). Jacob's household, with its complex and imperfect members, is nevertheless the vessel of salvation — just as the empirical Church, with all its human weakness, remains the Body of Christ.
