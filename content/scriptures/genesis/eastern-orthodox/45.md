## Joseph Makes Himself Known

Genesis 45 is the great revelation scene of the Joseph narrative. Unable to restrain himself any longer, Joseph orders all Egyptians to leave and reveals his identity to his brothers: "I am Joseph! Is my father still alive?" The brothers are stunned and terrified. Joseph weeps aloud, embraces Benjamin, kisses all his brothers, and speaks the theological key to the entire narrative: "God sent me before you to preserve life... it was not you who sent me here, but God." Pharaoh hears and offers the best of Egypt to Joseph's family. Joseph sends wagons, provisions, and gifts for Jacob, urging his brothers to bring their father to Egypt.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "I am Joseph" (45:3)

St. John Chrysostom compares this moment to the Resurrection appearances of Christ. Just as the disciples were terrified and disbelieving when the risen Lord appeared, so Joseph's brothers are dismayed. The one they believed dead stands before them in glory. Chrysostom sees Joseph's self-revelation as a type of the Paschal mystery: the crucified and buried one is revealed as alive and reigning. The words "I am Joseph" echo the divine "I AM" — a subtle link that the Fathers do not miss.

In Orthodox Paschal hymnography, the troparion proclaims: "Christ is risen from the dead!" This announcement, like Joseph's, transforms terror into joy, despair into hope, death into life.

### "It was not you who sent me here, but God" (45:8)

This verse is the theological center of the entire Joseph cycle. St. Maximus the Confessor would read it as the supreme illustration of divine providence working through human freedom without negating it. The brothers' act was genuinely evil; God's purpose was genuinely good. Both are real. The mystery of providence is that God does not cause evil but incorporates it into a larger narrative of redemption.

| Human Action | Divine Purpose |
|-------------|---------------|
| Brothers sell Joseph (37:28) | God sends Joseph to Egypt (45:5) |
| Potiphar's wife accuses him (39:14-18) | God positions Joseph in prison (39:21) |
| Cupbearer forgets Joseph (40:23) | God delays until Pharaoh's dream (41:1) |
| Famine drives the family to Egypt (42:1-2) | God preserves the covenant family (45:7) |
| Brothers intend evil | God intends good — "to preserve a remnant" (45:7) |

### "To preserve for you a remnant on earth" (45:7)

The language of "remnant" (*she'erit*) anticipates one of the most important concepts in prophetic theology. The Fathers connect Joseph's words to the prophetic promise of a faithful remnant that survives judgment (Isaiah 10:20-22). The Orthodox Church understands itself as the continuation of this remnant — the faithful community preserved through history by divine providence.

### Joseph Weeps on Benjamin's Neck (45:14-15)

Joseph's embrace of Benjamin and then all his brothers is the most physically intimate moment in the patriarchal narratives. St. Ephrem the Syrian sees in Joseph's tears the overflow of divine compassion. Joseph does not forgive grudgingly or conditionally but with total, embodied love. The kiss of peace exchanged in the Orthodox Divine Liturgy before the anaphora reflects this same reality: reconciliation must be complete and embodied before the community approaches the Eucharistic table.

## Theological Significance

- **Revelation as Paschal event**: Joseph's self-revelation follows the pattern that the Orthodox Church celebrates at Pascha. The one who descended into death (slavery, prison) now appears in glory. The one who was rejected by his own is revealed as their savior. The entire Joseph cycle is, in patristic reading, a narrative icon of the Paschal mystery.
- **Forgiveness as divine act**: Joseph's forgiveness is not merely a human decision but a participation in divine mercy. He forgives because he sees God's hand in the entire story. Orthodox theology teaches that genuine forgiveness — the forgiveness that heals rather than merely tolerates — flows from the vision of providence. When we see that God is working all things together for good, we can release our grievances.
- **The unity of the family as image of the Church**: The reunification of Joseph and his brothers prefigures the gathering of the scattered children of God into one (John 11:52). The Orthodox understanding of the Church as *sobornost* (conciliarity, organic unity) finds an Old Testament archetype in this family reunion. Division, caused by sin, is overcome by forgiveness, made possible by providence.
- **Cosmic transfiguration**: Joseph's declaration that God's purpose was "to preserve life" (*lehayyot*) resonates with the Orthodox vision of cosmic transfiguration. God's saving work is not limited to individual souls but encompasses the whole of creation. Joseph preserves not only his family but entire nations from death.
