## Life in Egypt

Genesis 47 describes the settlement of Jacob's family in Goshen and the administration of Egypt during the famine. Joseph presents his father and five brothers to Pharaoh. Jacob blesses Pharaoh and describes his life as a pilgrimage: "Few and evil have been the days of the years of my life." Joseph manages the famine with wisdom, acquiring all of Egypt's money, livestock, and land for Pharaoh in exchange for grain. The people become Pharaoh's tenants. Only the priests' land is exempt. As Jacob nears death, he makes Joseph swear to bury him not in Egypt but in the ancestral tomb in Canaan.

## Key Verses and Orthodox Interpretation

### "The days of the years of my pilgrimage are a hundred and thirty years; few and evil have been the days" (47:9)

St. John Chrysostom finds in Jacob's words a profound theology of earthly life. Jacob, standing before the most powerful ruler on earth, describes his entire existence as a pilgrimage (*paroikia*) — a temporary sojourn, not a permanent dwelling. Chrysostom teaches that this is the authentic Christian posture: we are resident aliens in this world, citizens of the heavenly Kingdom. The Epistle to the Hebrews (11:13-16) develops this theme explicitly, citing the patriarchs as examples of those who "confessed that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth."

The word Jacob uses — which the Septuagint renders as *paroikia* — gives us the English word "parish." Every Orthodox parish (*paroikia*) is, by definition, a community of pilgrims, a gathering of those whose true home is elsewhere.

### "Jacob blessed Pharaoh" (47:7, 10)

The Fathers note the striking reversal: the patriarch — elderly, poor, a refugee — blesses the emperor. St. Ephrem the Syrian observes that spiritual authority transcends political power. The one who carries the covenant blessing has something that all the wealth and power of Egypt cannot provide. In Orthodox tradition, the relationship between Church and state is modeled on this dynamic: the Church blesses temporal authority but is not subordinated to it.

| Actor | Worldly Status | Spiritual Status |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Pharaoh | Emperor of Egypt | Recipient of blessing |
| Jacob | Elderly refugee and pilgrim | Bearer of the covenant; blesses Pharaoh |
| Joseph | Pharaoh's vizier | Steward of divine providence |
| Priests of Egypt | Exempt from land seizure | Prefigure the clergy's distinct calling |

### Joseph's Administration (47:13-26)

Joseph's management of the famine raises complex ethical questions. He acquires all of Egypt's resources for Pharaoh, reducing the population to servitude. The Fathers generally read this positively — Joseph saves the people from starvation, and the arrangement (a 20% tax) is comparatively mild. St. Basil the Great, however, would remind readers that wealth concentrated in few hands always carries the danger of injustice. Orthodox social teaching emphasizes that the earth's goods are given by God for all people and that stewardship (*oikonomia*) must serve the common good.

### "Bury me not in Egypt... but carry me out of Egypt and bury me in their burial place" (47:29-30)

Jacob's insistence on burial in Canaan is not mere sentiment but a confession of faith. The Fathers interpret it as Jacob's testimony that the promise of the land is still active, even in death. The dead patriarch will "dwell" in the promised land, awaiting the fulfillment of God's purposes. Orthodox funeral theology resonates: the body of the departed is treated with reverence because it will participate in the resurrection. Burial in a particular place is an eschatological statement.

## Theological Significance

- **Life as pilgrimage**: Jacob's self-description as a pilgrim is foundational for Orthodox spirituality. The monastic tradition, from the Desert Fathers onward, structures the entire spiritual life around the concept of *xeniteia* — voluntary exile, the deliberate embrace of pilgrim status. The monk leaves home, family, and familiar surroundings to journey toward God. Every Christian, in the Orthodox understanding, is called to a form of this same pilgrimage.
- **Blessing across boundaries**: Jacob's blessing of Pharaoh demonstrates that the covenant is not a closed system. The blessing given to Abraham — "in you all families of the earth shall be blessed" — begins to be fulfilled in small ways throughout the patriarchal narratives. The Orthodox Church's missionary vocation flows from this same impulse: the Church exists not for itself but to bless all nations.
- **Stewardship and justice**: Joseph's administrative role raises the perennial question of how power should be exercised. Orthodox social teaching, articulated in the encyclicals of the Ecumenical Patriarchate and in theologians like Christos Yannaras, insists that economic life must be ordered toward human dignity and the common good, reflecting the *koinonia* (communion) that characterizes the life of the Trinity.
