## The Covenant of Circumcision and the New Names

Genesis 17 records the establishment of circumcision as the sign of God's covenant with Abraham, the changing of Abram and Sarai's names to Abraham and Sarah, and the specific promise that Sarah herself — not Hagar — will bear the son of the covenant. Orthodox theology reads this chapter as a pivotal moment in the unfolding of God's economy, while also reading circumcision typologically as a foreshadowing of baptism.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "I am Almighty God; walk before Me and be blameless" (17:1)

God reveals Himself as *El Shaddai* — rendered in the LXX as *Theos sou*, "your God." The command to "walk before Me" echoes the description of Enoch (5:24) and Noah (6:9) and establishes the pattern of covenant life: perpetual awareness of God's presence. St. Maximus the Confessor connected this "walking before God" to the practice of unceasing prayer (*proseuche adialeiptos*), the goal of the hesychastic tradition. To "be blameless" (*teleios*) means not sinless perfection but wholehearted devotion — an undivided heart.

### "No longer shall your name be called Abram, but your name shall be Abraham" (17:5)

The change from Abram ("exalted father") to Abraham ("father of many nations") is more than nominal. In Orthodox understanding, a name change signifies a transformation of identity and mission. This is reflected in the Orthodox practice of giving a new name at monastic tonsure — the monk dies to the old self and receives a new identity in Christ. The patriarch's new name carries his vocation: he is no longer merely an individual but the father of a multitude.

Similarly, Sarai becomes Sarah — both meaning "princess," but the change marks God's explicit inclusion of Sarah in the covenant promise. The Fathers noted that God's covenant is with both husband and wife, not with the man alone.

### "Every male child among you shall be circumcised ... it shall be a sign of the covenant" (17:10-11)

Circumcision functions as a physical seal of the covenant relationship. Orthodox theology maintains a clear typological reading: circumcision in the flesh foreshadows baptism in the Spirit. St. Paul explicitly makes this connection in Colossians 2:11-12: "In Him you were also circumcised with the circumcision made without hands ... buried with Him in baptism."

| Old Covenant Sign | New Covenant Fulfillment |
|-------------------|-------------------------|
| Circumcision of the flesh | Baptism — circumcision of the heart |
| On the eighth day | Christ rose on the eighth day (the day after the Sabbath) |
| Removes flesh | Removes the "old man" of sin |
| Sign of belonging to Israel | Seal of belonging to Christ |
| Males only | All persons — male and female, slave and free |

The eighth day is theologically significant in Orthodox tradition. The week has seven days, corresponding to this present age; the eighth day transcends the cycle and represents the age to come. Christ's Resurrection on the "eighth day" inaugurates the new creation. Orthodox baptisteries were traditionally octagonal, reflecting this symbolism. The fact that circumcision was performed on the eighth day (17:12) is read as a prophetic anticipation of baptism into the Resurrection.

### Abraham's Laughter (17:17)

"Abraham fell on his face and laughed, and said in his heart, 'Shall a child be born to a man who is one hundred years old?'" The Fathers interpreted Abraham's laughter in varying ways. St. John Chrysostom understood it as an expression of amazement rather than disbelief — Abraham was overwhelmed by the magnitude of the promise, not doubting God's ability to fulfill it. This stands in contrast to Sarah's later laughter (18:12), which the text presents more ambiguously. The name Isaac (*Yitzhak*, "he laughs") memorializes this moment: the child of promise is born from holy astonishment.

### "As for Ishmael, I have heard you. Behold, I have blessed him" (17:20)

Abraham's intercession for Ishmael reveals his fatherly love. God responds graciously: Ishmael will be blessed, will father twelve princes, and will become a great nation. Yet the covenant passes through Isaac. The Orthodox Fathers did not read this as a rejection of Ishmael but as a specification of the covenantal line through which the Messiah would come. God's blessing is wider than His covenant — all peoples benefit from God's providence, even when the particular economy of salvation runs through a specific lineage.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 17 reveals the sacramental structure of the covenant: an invisible spiritual reality (God's promise and commitment) is given a visible, bodily sign (circumcision). This principle — that God works through material means to communicate spiritual grace — is foundational to the Orthodox understanding of the sacraments (Holy Mysteries). Baptism, Chrismation, the Eucharist, and all the sacraments embody this same logic: the invisible grace of God is communicated through visible, tangible elements.

The chapter also demonstrates that God's promises unfold slowly and require patient faith. Thirteen years pass between the birth of Ishmael (chapter 16) and the covenant of chapter 17. The spiritual life requires endurance — a theme central to the Lenten journey.
