## Hagar and Ishmael: Complexity in the Patriarchal Household

Genesis 16 introduces the triangle of Sarai, Abram, and Hagar — a narrative of frustrated hopes, domestic conflict, and divine compassion for the marginalized. Within parashat Lech Lecha, this chapter addresses the human impatience that arises when divine promises are delayed. Jewish commentators engage honestly with the moral tensions in the text, neither whitewashing Sarai's harshness nor dismissing Hagar's experience.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Sarai, Abram's wife, had borne him no children" (16:1)

The emphasis on Sarai's barrenness frames the entire chapter. The Talmud (Yevamot 64a) asks why the matriarchs were barren and answers: because God desires the prayers of the righteous. Barrenness was not punishment but provocation to deeper relationship with God. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 45:2) adds that the barrenness of the matriarchs also ensured that the birth of each child would be recognized as miraculous rather than natural.

### "She had an Egyptian maidservant named Hagar" (16:1)

Rashi, citing Genesis Rabbah (45:1), identifies Hagar as a daughter of Pharaoh. When Pharaoh witnessed the miracles God performed for Sarai in Egypt (chapter 12), he said, "Better for my daughter to be a maidservant in this household than a mistress in another." The name *Hagar* is connected to *ha-agar* ("this is the reward") — her servitude in Abraham's house was her reward for proximity to divine blessing.

### Sarai's Proposal (16:2-3)

Sarai suggests that Abram take Hagar as a secondary wife to produce an heir. This practice is well-attested in ancient Near Eastern law (cf. the Code of Hammurabi). The Ramban explains that Sarai acted from genuine self-sacrifice — giving her own maidservant to her husband so that she could "be built up through her" (*ibbaneh*), a wordplay on *ben* (son) and *binyan* (building).

The text notes that this occurred "after Abram had dwelt ten years in the land of Canaan" — Rashi derives from this the halakhic principle (codified in the Talmud, Yevamot 64a) that if a couple has been married ten years without children, the husband should take steps to fulfill the commandment to be fruitful and multiply.

### Hagar's Contempt and Sarai's Response (16:4-6)

When Hagar conceives, "her mistress became light in her eyes." Rashi explains that Hagar began telling people: "Sarai appears righteous but is not, for she has not conceived in all these years, while I conceived immediately." Sarai responds harshly (*va-te'anneha*) — the same root used for affliction/oppression.

| Commentator | View of Sarai's Treatment of Hagar |
|---|---|
| Rashi | Sarai imposed heavy labor and harsh conditions |
| Ramban | "Our mother sinned in this affliction, and Abraham too by allowing it. God heard Hagar's affliction and gave her a son who would be a wild man, afflicting Abraham's descendants" |
| Radak | Sarai's actions, while understandable, were excessive |

The Ramban's criticism is notably sharp: he directly links Sarai's mistreatment of Hagar to the future enmity between Ishmael's descendants and Israel. This is a remarkable example of Jewish tradition holding its own heroes morally accountable.

### The Angel at the Spring (16:7-14)

An angel finds Hagar at a spring in the wilderness and instructs her to return and submit to Sarai. The angel also promises that her son Ishmael ("God hears") will be the father of a multitude. Hagar is the first person in the Torah to receive an angelic visitation and the first to name God: *El Ro'i* — "God of Seeing" or "God Who Sees Me."

Rashi notes that Hagar's familiarity with angels — she was not frightened — reflects her upbringing in Abraham's household, where angelic visitations were commonplace. The well is named *Be'er Lachai Ro'i* ("Well of the Living One Who Sees Me"), which reappears in Isaac's story (24:62, 25:11).

### Ishmael's Character (16:12)

The angel describes Ishmael: "He shall be a wild donkey of a man (*pere adam*); his hand against everyone, and everyone's hand against him." Rashi reads this as describing a desert-dwelling lifestyle — a hunter and raider. The Targum Onkelos softens it: "He shall be a man among men" — a strong, independent figure.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 16 in Jewish thought teaches:

- **God sees the oppressed**: Hagar's experience establishes that divine compassion extends beyond the covenant family
- **Moral accountability of the righteous**: Even Sarai and Abram are criticized for their treatment of Hagar
- **Patience in waiting for God's promises**: Human attempts to force divine timing create complications
- **Naming as theology**: Hagar's naming of God (*El Ro'i*) demonstrates that even a non-Israelite can have genuine encounter with the divine
