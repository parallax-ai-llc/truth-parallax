## The Birth of Yitzchak: Laughter Fulfilled

Genesis 21 narrates the miraculous birth of Isaac (*Yitzchak*), the expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael, and Abraham's treaty with Abimelech at Beer-sheba. This chapter, within parashat Vayera, marks the fulfillment of the long-awaited promise and introduces the painful dynamics of succession — which child will carry the covenant forward. The Torah reading of Genesis 21 is assigned to the first day of Rosh Hashanah, underscoring its liturgical centrality.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "And YHVH remembered (*pakad*) Sarah" (21:1)

The verb *pakad* ("visited" or "remembered") carries rich theological meaning. This verse is read as the Haftarah introduction on Rosh Hashanah because divine "remembrance" — God turning attention toward the barren and forgotten — is the central theme of the holiday. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 11a) teaches that Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were all "remembered" on Rosh Hashanah.

Rashi notes that the juxtaposition with Abraham's prayer for Abimelech's household (20:17) is deliberate: because Abraham prayed for another's fertility, God granted his own. This reinforces the Talmudic principle (Bava Kamma 92a) of reciprocal blessing through intercessory prayer.

### "Sarah said: God has made laughter (*tzechok*) for me" (21:6)

The name *Yitzchak* ("he will laugh") transforms the earlier laughter of disbelief (17:17, 18:12) into laughter of joy. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 53:8) teaches that on the day Isaac was born, many barren women were remembered, many sick were healed, and many prayers were answered — the birth of one righteous person brings blessing to the entire world.

### The Feast and Ishmael's Behavior (21:8-9)

Abraham makes a great feast on the day Isaac is weaned. Sarah sees Ishmael *metzachek* — a word deliberately echoing *Yitzchak*. Rashi, drawing on Genesis Rabbah (53:11), interprets *metzachek* as encompassing three grave sins: idolatry, sexual immorality, and murder — all derived from other biblical uses of the root *tz-ch-k*.

| Meaning of *metzachek* | Proof-Text | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Idolatry | "The people rose to make merry (*le-tzachek*)" — Golden Calf (Exodus 32:6) | Genesis Rabbah 53:11 |
| Sexual immorality | "He came to make sport (*le-tzachek*) of us" — Potiphar's wife (Genesis 39:17) | Genesis Rabbah 53:11 |
| Murder | "Let the young men arise and play (*vi-sachaku*)" — Abner's warriors (2 Samuel 2:14) | Genesis Rabbah 53:11 |

Other commentators take a less severe view. Ibn Ezra suggests Ishmael was simply mocking Isaac. The Rambam reads the conflict as primarily about inheritance rather than moral transgression.

### The Expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael (21:10-14)

Sarah demands: "Cast out this slave woman and her son, for the son of this slave woman shall not inherit with my son, with Isaac." Abraham is distressed, but God tells him: "Whatever Sarah tells you, listen to her voice" (*shema be-kolah*).

Rashi derives from "listen to her voice" that Sarah was a greater prophet than Abraham. The Talmud (Megillah 14a) counts Sarah among the seven prophetesses of Israel. Her insistence on Ishmael's removal is not jealousy but prophetic discernment about the covenantal future.

The scene of Hagar wandering in the wilderness of Beer-sheba, placing Ishmael under a bush and weeping, is one of the Torah's most poignant passages. Rashi explains that Ishmael was ill — the "evil eye" cast by those who gazed at him enviously at the feast had made him sick. The angel calls to Hagar "from heaven" (21:17) and opens her eyes to see a well — the water was there all along, but she could not see it until God revealed it.

### "God heard the voice of the lad where he is" (21:17)

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 53:14) derives a crucial principle: God judges a person according to their present state (*ba-asher hu sham*), not according to their future deeds. The angels protested: "Will You save someone whose descendants will cause Israel to die of thirst?" God replied: "What is he now? Righteous or wicked?" "Righteous." "Then I judge him as he is now." This principle has profound implications for Jewish jurisprudence and theology — judgment is based on the present, not on predetermined fate.

### Abraham and Abimelech at Beer-sheba (21:22-34)

The chapter concludes with a treaty between Abraham and Abimelech, sealed by Abraham's gift of seven ewes — giving Beer-sheba (*Be'er Sheva*, "Well of the Oath" or "Well of Seven") its name. Abraham plants a tamarisk tree (*eshel*) and calls on the name of YHVH. Rashi, citing Sotah 10a, reads *eshel* as an acronym: *Akhilah* (food), *Shetiyah* (drink), *Linah* (lodging) — or alternatively *Levayah* (escort) — the essential components of hospitality.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 21 in Jewish thought conveys:

- **Divine faithfulness**: The promise of Isaac's birth, however long delayed, is fulfilled
- **Judgment by present merit**: The principle of *ba-asher hu sham* rejects determinism and affirms individual agency
- **Prophetic authority of the matriarchs**: Sarah's voice carries divine weight
- **Hospitality as worship**: Abraham's *eshel* at Beer-sheba combines human kindness with proclamation of God's name
