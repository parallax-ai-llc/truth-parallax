## Brit Noach: The Covenant with Noah

Genesis 9 records God's covenant with Noah and all living creatures — the first explicit *brit* (covenant) in the Torah. This chapter is of immense importance in Jewish law and thought because it establishes the Noahide laws (*sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*), the universal moral code binding on all humanity. The chapter also introduces the permission to eat meat, the prohibition against consuming blood, and the troubling episode of Noah's drunkenness.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Seven Noahide Laws (9:1-7)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 56a-60a) derives from the post-flood commandments — combined with the earlier command in Genesis 2:16 — seven universal laws obligating all descendants of Noah:

| Noahide Law | Source in Rabbinic Derivation |
|---|---|
| Prohibition of idolatry | "YHVH" (2:16) — acknowledgment of God |
| Prohibition of blasphemy | "Elohim" (2:16) — reverence for God's name |
| Prohibition of murder | "Whoever sheds the blood of man" (9:6) |
| Prohibition of sexual immorality | "And He commanded" (2:16) — interpreted as sexual boundaries |
| Prohibition of theft | "The man" (2:16) — respecting what belongs to others |
| Prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal | "But flesh with its life-blood you shall not eat" (9:4) |
| Establishment of courts of justice | "Elohim" as judges (2:16) — legal order |

The Rambam (Maimonides) in *Mishneh Torah* (Hilkhot Melakhim 8:10-11) codifies these laws and declares that any non-Jew who observes them on the basis of divine revelation has a share in the World to Come. This framework represents Judaism's universalist ethics — a moral order for all humanity that does not require conversion to Judaism.

### Permission to Eat Meat (9:3)

"Every moving thing that lives shall be food for you; as I gave you the green plants, I now give you everything." Rashi explains that Adam was not permitted to eat meat — only vegetation (1:29). After the flood, God expanded the dietary permission. The Ramban connects this to a shift in the cosmic order: before the flood, animals and humans shared a more intimate relationship; the flood shattered this, and dominion now includes consumption.

However, one restriction remains: "Flesh with its life (*nafsho*), its blood, you shall not eat" (9:4). This prohibition — *ever min ha-chai* (a limb from a living animal) — is considered binding on all humanity and reflects the Jewish insistence that even permitted killing must be bounded by moral restraint.

### "Whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed" (9:6)

This verse establishes the principle of capital jurisdiction and human responsibility for justice. Rashi reads the Hebrew structure *shofekh dam ha-adam ba-adam* to yield an additional meaning: "whoever sheds the blood of a person within a person" — referring to killing a fetus. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 57b) uses this to establish that the Noahide code prohibits abortion, though the halakhic implications for Jews are derived from other sources.

The Ramban explains that capital punishment after the flood reflects the fact that before the flood, there was no enforcement mechanism — Cain murdered Abel without human consequence — and this lawlessness contributed to the world's destruction.

### The Rainbow Covenant (9:12-17)

The rainbow (*keshet*) is the sign of God's covenant never to destroy the earth by flood again. The Mishnah (Avot 5:6) lists the rainbow among ten things created at twilight on the eve of the first Shabbat — suggesting it was built into creation's design from the beginning.

The Talmud (Chagigah 16a) warns against staring at a rainbow, as it represents a manifestation of divine glory (cf. Ezekiel 1:28). When one sees a rainbow, the blessing recited is: "Blessed are You, YHVH our God, King of the universe, who remembers the covenant, is faithful to His covenant, and keeps His word."

### Noah's Drunkenness and Ham's Sin (9:20-27)

The nature of Ham's transgression is debated. Rashi, citing the Talmud (Sanhedrin 70a), presents two opinions: Ham either castrated Noah or committed a sexual violation against him. The severity of Noah's curse upon Canaan (Ham's son) suggests the offense was far graver than merely "seeing nakedness."

## Theological Significance

Genesis 9 establishes:

- **Universal moral law**: The Noahide code provides a framework for righteous living applicable to all humanity
- **Sanctity of blood/life**: The blood prohibition connects physical sustenance to moral awareness
- **Covenant as divine commitment**: God binds Himself — the rainbow is a reminder to God, not only to humanity
- **Justice requires institutions**: The post-flood world mandates courts and accountability
