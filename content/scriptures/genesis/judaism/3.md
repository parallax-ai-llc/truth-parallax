## The Expulsion from Gan Eden

Genesis 3 recounts the encounter with the serpent (*nachash*), the eating of the forbidden fruit, and the subsequent expulsion from the Garden of Eden. Jewish interpretation of this chapter differs fundamentally from Christian readings: there is no doctrine of "original sin" in Judaism, no concept of a permanent fallen human nature inherited by all descendants. Instead, the rabbis see this narrative as illustrating the dynamics of temptation, moral choice, and the possibility of return (*teshuvah*).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Serpent (3:1)

Rashi explains that the serpent was the most cunning (*arum*) of all creatures, noting the wordplay with *arummim* ("naked") at the end of chapter 2. The serpent's cunning is juxtaposed with human vulnerability. Genesis Rabbah (19:1) presents the serpent as motivated by jealousy — it saw Adam and Eve in their glory and desired Eve for itself.

Jewish tradition emphatically does not identify the serpent with Satan in the Christian sense. While later midrashic literature sometimes associates the serpent with the *Samael* (an angelic adversary), the plain meaning (*peshat*) presents it as a crafty animal. The Rambam (Maimonides) in the *Guide for the Perplexed* (2:30) reads the serpent allegorically as representing the imagination and physical desires.

### The Dialogue of Temptation (3:1-6)

The rabbis note that Eve added to God's command. God said not to *eat* from the tree; Eve told the serpent they were also forbidden to *touch* it (3:3). The Midrash (Avot de-Rabbi Natan 1:5) teaches that the serpent pushed her against the tree, and when she did not die from touching it, she concluded the prohibition against eating was equally exaggerated. This yields the halakhic principle: "Whoever adds to the Torah ultimately diminishes it."

| Actor | Midrashic Motivation |
|---|---|
| Serpent | Jealousy of Adam; desire for Eve (Genesis Rabbah 18:6) |
| Eve | Added to God's command, creating vulnerability |
| Adam | Listened passively; failed to intervene (Rashi on 3:6 — "he was with her") |

### "Your eyes will be opened" (3:5)

The Ramban explains that the serpent spoke a half-truth. Their eyes were indeed opened, but what they gained was not divine wisdom but the subjective experience of shame and moral confusion. Before the sin, they knew good and evil intellectually; afterward, they experienced them as internal conflict — the perpetual struggle of the *yetzer hara* and *yetzer hatov*.

### Consequences, Not Curses (3:14-19)

Jewish reading carefully distinguishes: the serpent and the ground are cursed (*arur*), but Adam and Eve themselves are never cursed. The consequences they receive — painful labor, difficult childbirth, mortality — are described as natural results rather than punitive condemnation. The Talmud (Eruvin 100b) derives from the punishments of Eve insights into the nature of female experience, though these are descriptive rather than prescriptive.

Rashi on "By the sweat of your brow you shall eat bread" (3:19) notes that this is actually a kindness compared to the initial declaration that Adam would eat "thorns and thistles" — Adam wept at that prospect, and God relented to give him the dignity of earning bread through labor.

### "Garments of skin" (3:21)

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 20:12) records a variant reading: *kotnot or* — "garments of light" rather than "garments of skin" (*kotnot or* with an *aleph* vs. an *ayin*). Rabbi Meir's Torah scroll reportedly contained this reading, suggesting that God clothed Adam and Eve in luminous garments. This becomes significant in kabbalistic thought, where the original garments of light represent the soul's pristine state.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 3 in Jewish theology establishes:

- **Moral agency**: Humans choose sin; it is not inevitable or inherited. Each person stands as Adam stood, facing the choice between obedience and desire
- **Teshuvah (repentance)**: The Midrash notes that Adam eventually did teshuvah, composing Psalm 92 (a psalm for Shabbat) upon recognizing the power of repentance (Genesis Rabbah 22:13)
- **No permanent fall**: Humanity is diminished but not corrupted in essence. The divine image remains intact
- **Divine compassion**: God clothes the naked, establishing the model for *gemilut chasadim* (acts of lovingkindness) — the Talmud (Sotah 14a) teaches: "As God clothed the naked, so should you"
