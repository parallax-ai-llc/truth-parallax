## Summary

Genesis 40 concludes Parashat Vayeshev. Pharaoh's chief cupbearer (*sar ha-mashkim*) and chief baker (*sar ha-ofim*) are imprisoned and placed under Joseph's care. Each has a dream on the same night. Joseph interprets the dreams: the cupbearer will be restored in three days, the baker will be executed. Both interpretations prove accurate. Joseph asks the cupbearer to remember him before Pharaoh, but the cupbearer forgets.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Do not interpretations belong to God?" (40:8)

Joseph's declaration before interpreting the dreams is a theological statement of the first order. He does not claim personal power but attributes all interpretive ability to God. Rashi notes the contrast with pagan dream interpreters (*chartumim*), who claimed their own skill. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 89:6) sees this as Joseph's perpetual habit: in every circumstance, he directs attention to God rather than himself — in Potiphar's house (39:9), in prison (40:8), and before Pharaoh (41:16).

### The Cupbearer's Dream (40:9-13)

| Symbol | Joseph's Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Vine with three branches | Three days until restoration |
| Budding, blossoming, grapes | The process will unfold naturally, step by step |
| Squeezing grapes into Pharaoh's cup | Restoration to his former office |

The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 88:5) adds an allegorical layer: the vine represents Israel, the three branches represent Moses, Aaron, and Miriam (or alternatively the three pilgrimage festivals), and the cup represents the cup of salvation. This allegorical reading treats the prison dreams as encoded prophecies about Israel's future redemption from Egypt — the very Egypt in which Joseph now languishes.

### The Baker's Dream (40:16-19)

The baker, encouraged by the favorable interpretation given to the cupbearer, shares his dream. Joseph's interpretation is devastating: the three baskets represent three days, and "Pharaoh will lift your head *from upon you*" (40:19) — a grim wordplay on the cupbearer's positive "Pharaoh will lift your head" (40:13). Rashi notes the linguistic precision: the same phrase, with one preposition added (*me-alekha*, "from upon you"), transforms restoration into execution.

The Midrash observes that the baker's dream contained its own ominous sign that a skilled interpreter would recognize: birds were eating from the baskets on his head, and a competent royal baker would never permit birds near the food. The dream reflected the baker's own guilt.

### "The chief cupbearer did not remember Joseph; he forgot him" (40:23)

The double expression — "did not remember... forgot" — seems redundant. Rashi explains: he did not remember him *then* (immediately), and he forgot him *afterward* (when he returned to his duties). The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 89:3) teaches that Joseph was punished with two additional years of imprisonment for placing his trust in human agency rather than God — one year for "remember me" and one year for "mention me" (40:14). This teaching reflects the rabbinic ambivalence about *hishtadlut* (human effort): while practical action is permitted and even required, excessive reliance on human beings rather than God is a spiritual failing.

## Theological Significance

The chapter explores the relationship between divine revelation and human agency. Joseph interprets dreams — a prophetic function — yet he does so for a pagan cupbearer and baker. God's communication is not limited to the covenant community; even Egyptians receive meaningful dreams. However, only Joseph, rooted in Abrahamic faith, can interpret them. This establishes a pattern central to Jewish theology: God communicates universally but is understood through the lens of Torah and covenantal wisdom.

The theme of memory and forgetting runs deep. The cupbearer's forgetting parallels the larger human tendency to forget God's agents once the crisis has passed. The Midrash connects this to Israel's eventual enslavement: Pharaoh "did not know Joseph" (Exodus 1:8) echoes the cupbearer's forgetting — ingratitude toward those who serve the divine purpose.

Joseph's extra two years in prison, in the Midrashic reading, illustrate the exacting standard applied to the righteous (*tzaddikim*). God judges the righteous "to a hair's breadth" (*medakdek... k'chut ha-sa'arah*), not to punish them but to refine them. Joseph needed those additional years to reach the spiritual readiness required for his elevation to viceroy.
