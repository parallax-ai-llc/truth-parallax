## Summary

Genesis 41 opens Parashat Miketz (41:1-44:17). Two years after the cupbearer's release, Pharaoh dreams of seven fat cows devoured by seven lean cows, and seven full ears of grain consumed by seven thin ears. Egypt's magicians cannot interpret the dreams. The cupbearer finally remembers Joseph, who is summoned from prison. Joseph interprets both dreams as one: seven years of abundance followed by seven years of devastating famine. He advises Pharaoh to appoint an overseer to store grain. Pharaoh appoints Joseph himself, giving him the Egyptian name Zaphenath-Paneah and a wife, Asenath. During the years of plenty, Joseph stores vast quantities of grain. The famine arrives, and all nations come to Egypt to buy food.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "It is not in me; God will answer Pharaoh's welfare" (41:16)

Joseph's self-effacement before Pharaoh repeats his prison declaration (40:8). Rashi emphasizes: "The wisdom is not mine." The Midrash contrasts Joseph with the Egyptian *chartumim* (magicians), who offered interpretations but could not satisfy Pharaoh because their readings were fragmented — they could not see the two dreams as one unified message. Joseph's interpretive superiority derives not from technique but from prophetic insight granted by God.

### The Dreams as One (41:25-32)

Joseph's key insight — "Pharaoh's dream is one" (*chalom Paro echad hu*) — is profoundly significant. Where the magicians saw two separate dreams, Joseph perceived unity. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 89:7) relates that the magicians offered interpretations such as "seven daughters will be born to you and seven will die," reading the dreams about Pharaoh's personal life. Joseph recognized that dreams from God address the public welfare, not private matters.

| Dream Element | Joseph's Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Seven fat cows / seven full ears | Seven years of abundance |
| Seven lean cows / seven thin ears | Seven years of famine |
| The dream was repeated twice | The matter is established by God and will happen soon (41:32) |

The principle that repetition confirms certainty (*shanah... ki nakhon ha-davar*) became a hermeneutical rule applied elsewhere in Torah interpretation.

### Joseph's Appointment (41:39-44)

Pharaoh's words — "Since God has made all this known to you, there is no one so discerning and wise as you" (41:39) — are read by the Midrash as an acknowledgment that Joseph's God is superior to Egypt's gods. Rashi notes the irony: Joseph, a Hebrew slave and prisoner, ascends to second-in-command because he credits God rather than claiming personal ability. The Torah repeatedly demonstrates that humility before God is the path to genuine authority.

The ring, fine garments, and gold chain given to Joseph recall — and reverse — the stripping of his coat in Chapter 37. What was taken by human malice is restored with interest by divine providence.

### Joseph's Egyptian Name and Wife (41:45)

"Pharaoh called Joseph's name *Zaphenath-Paneah*." The Talmud (Sotah 36b) translates this as "the one who explains hidden things" (*tsofnat* = reveals, *pa'aneach* = secrets). The Targum Onkelos renders it similarly. Joseph's new identity does not replace his Israelite identity but adds a public dimension to it.

Asenath daughter of Poti-phera is identified by the Midrash (Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer 38) as the daughter of Dinah, adopted by Potiphera. This tradition resolves the difficulty of Joseph marrying an Egyptian woman: Asenath was actually of Israelite descent. Her sons Ephraim and Manasseh are thereby fully Israelite, which matters for Jacob's later blessing (Chapter 48).

### "Joseph named the firstborn Manasseh... the second Ephraim" (41:51-52)

| Son | Name Meaning | Joseph's Statement |
|---|---|---|
| Manasseh | "Causing to forget" | "God has made me forget all my hardship and all my father's house" |
| Ephraim | "Fruitfulness" | "God has made me fruitful in the land of my affliction" |

Rashi and the Midrash note a tension: can it be righteous to "forget" one's father's house? The answer is that Joseph did not forget his family — as the rest of the narrative proves — but rather God healed the trauma of his suffering so that it no longer tormented him. *Nasshani* (caused me to forget) refers to the pain, not the people.

## Theological Significance

The chapter is the fullest demonstration of divine providence (*hashgachah pratit*) in Genesis. Every detail of Joseph's story — the sale, the imprisonment, the cupbearer's forgetting, the timing of Pharaoh's dreams — converges at exactly the right moment. The Midrash teaches that God prepared the cure before the disease: Joseph was sent to Egypt years before the famine so that the mechanism of salvation would already be in place.

This theological pattern — *refuah kodem la-makkah* ("the remedy precedes the affliction") — became foundational in Jewish thought about suffering and history. The exile always contains within it the seeds of redemption, even when those seeds are invisible to those experiencing the suffering.

Parashat Miketz is traditionally read on Shabbat Chanukah, and the connection is thematic: just as Joseph's light emerged from the darkness of prison, the Chanukah lights emerge from the darkness of winter — light in exile, hope in oppression.
