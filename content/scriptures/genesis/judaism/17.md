## Brit Milah: The Covenant of Circumcision

Genesis 17 records one of the most defining moments in Jewish identity: the institution of circumcision (*brit milah*) as the physical sign of the covenant between God and Abraham. This chapter, still within parashat Lech Lecha, also narrates the change of names from Abram to Abraham and from Sarai to Sarah, and announces that the covenant will continue specifically through Isaac, the son yet to be born to Sarah.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Walk before Me and be perfect (*tamim*)" (17:1)

God appears to Abram at age 99 and commands: "I am El Shaddai; walk before Me and be perfect." Rashi connects *tamim* to circumcision: as long as Abram was uncircumcised, he was considered physically "incomplete." The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 46:1) lists other interpretations — *tamim* means wholehearted obedience, or walking with integrity before God.

The name *El Shaddai* is interpreted by Rashi as "the One who said to His world, 'Enough!' (*dai*)" — God who sets limits on creation. The Ramban connects *Shaddai* to divine power that transcends natural limitations, appropriate for the promise of a child to a couple past childbearing age.

### The Name Change: Abram to Abraham (17:5)

The addition of the letter *heh* transforms *Avram* ("exalted father") into *Avraham* ("father of a multitude of nations"). Rashi explains that the numerical value of the *heh* is 5, and the letter *resh* (value 200) that was split from "Avram" was redistributed — previously he was father of Aram alone; now he is father of all nations.

The Talmud (Berakhot 13a) rules that calling Abraham by his former name "Abram" violates a positive commandment, since God renamed him. This has practical halakhic implications — liturgical and legal texts must use the name Abraham.

### Brit Milah: The Commandment (17:10-14)

"This is My covenant which you shall keep, between Me and you and your descendants after you: every male among you shall be circumcised."

| Aspect | Halakhic Detail |
|---|---|
| Timing | Eighth day of life (17:12), overrides Shabbat (Shabbat 132a) |
| Obligation | Father's duty; if father fails, the *beit din* acts; if they fail, the man himself must arrange it as an adult |
| Penalty for non-compliance | *Karet* (spiritual excision) — 17:14 |
| Scope | Household members and purchased servants included (17:12-13) |
| Abraham's age at circumcision | 99 years old — demonstrating that the mitzvah applies at any age |

The Talmud (Shabbat 137b) records that brit milah is so beloved that it overrides the Shabbat prohibitions against wounding and blood-letting. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 46:4) teaches that Abraham performed the circumcision on Yom Kippur, and God looks upon the blood of the brit each Yom Kippur as atonement for Israel.

Rashi on "uncircumcised male who does not circumcise the flesh of his foreskin — that soul shall be cut off" (17:14) emphasizes that the covenant is inscribed in the body. Unlike other commandments that are performed through action, brit milah creates a permanent physical transformation — the body itself becomes a sign of the covenant.

### Sarah's Name Change and Promise (17:15-19)

Sarai becomes Sarah — both names mean "princess," but Rashi explains: *Sarai* meant "my princess" (limited to her own people); *Sarah* means "princess" universally. The Talmud (Berakhot 13a), however, rules that the prohibition against using the old name applies only to Abraham, not to Sarah, since her name change was not accompanied by the same explicit prohibition.

Abraham's response to the promise that Sarah will bear a son is laughter (17:17) — the verb *tzachak*, root of the name *Yitzchak* (Isaac). Rashi distinguishes Abraham's laughter from Sarah's laughter in chapter 18: Abraham's was joyful and believing; Sarah's expressed doubt. Other commentators debate whether Abraham's laughter was entirely free of skepticism.

### Ishmael's Blessing (17:20)

God promises that Ishmael too will be blessed: "I will make him fruitful and exceedingly numerous; he shall become the father of twelve princes, and I will make him a great nation." However, the covenant (*brit*) will be established specifically through Isaac (17:21). This distinction — blessing versus covenant — is crucial for understanding Jewish theology of election: Ishmael receives material blessing, but the covenantal relationship passes through Isaac.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 17 in Jewish thought establishes:

- **Covenant inscribed in flesh**: Brit milah is the most visceral expression of Jewish identity — a commandment that marks the body permanently
- **Names shape destiny**: The addition of *heh* (a letter of God's name) to Abraham and Sarah signifies their elevation to new spiritual status
- **Particularity within universalism**: God blesses Ishmael but covenants with Isaac — election does not mean others are cursed
- **Obedience at any age**: Abraham's circumcision at 99 demonstrates that no one is too old for transformation
