## Summary

Genesis 35 marks Jacob's return to Bethel as God commanded. He instructs his household to purify themselves and remove all foreign gods. God appears to Jacob at Bethel, reaffirms the name Israel and the covenant promise of land and progeny. The chapter then records three deaths — Deborah (Rebekah's nurse), Rachel (in childbirth with Benjamin), and Isaac — and one grave sin: Reuben's violation of his father's bed. The list of Jacob's twelve sons concludes this section of Parashat Vayishlach.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Put away the foreign gods that are among you" (35:2)

Jacob commands his household to surrender their idols before ascending to Bethel. Rashi identifies these as the spoils taken from Shechem (Chapter 34), which included idolatrous objects. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 81:3) adds that Jacob buried the idols under the oak (*elah*) at Shechem — a permanent burial, not merely a hiding. This act became a model for the halakhic requirement to destroy idols rather than merely store them (*bitul avodah zarah*).

The command to "change your garments" (35:2) is understood by the Zohar as referring to spiritual transformation — shedding not only physical garments contaminated by idolatry but the inner dispositions that permitted attachment to foreign worship.

### Rachel's Death and Benjamin's Birth (35:16-20)

Rachel dies giving birth to her second son. With her last breath, she names him *Ben-Oni* — "son of my sorrow/strength." Jacob renames him *Binyamin* — "son of the right hand" (i.e., son of strength, son of the south).

| Name | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Ben-Oni (Rachel's name) | Son of my sorrow | Reflects the mother's dying agony |
| Binyamin (Jacob's name) | Son of the right hand / Son of the south | Rashi: "right hand" = strength; born in the Land of Israel (south of Aram), unlike the other eleven |

The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 82:9) notes that Benjamin was the only son born in the Land of Israel, which is why his territory later included Jerusalem and the Temple. Rachel's burial "on the road to Ephrath" rather than in the Cave of Machpelah is explained by Rashi (citing Jeremiah 31:15): God placed her tomb on the road so that when her descendants would pass by on the way to Babylonian exile, Rachel would pray for them — "Rachel weeps for her children."

### Reuben's Sin (35:22)

"Reuben went and lay with Bilhah, his father's concubine." The Talmud (Shabbat 55b) insists: "Whoever says Reuben sinned is simply mistaken." The rabbis reinterpret the verse: after Rachel's death, Jacob moved his bed to Bilhah's tent (Rachel's handmaid). Reuben, defending his mother Leah's honor, moved the bed to Leah's tent. The Torah calls this "lying with Bilhah" because disturbing a father's conjugal arrangements is treated *as if* it were a sexual violation.

This protective re-reading demonstrates the rabbinic principle of *limud zekhut* — judging others favorably — especially regarding the patriarchs and tribal ancestors. Nevertheless, the act cost Reuben the firstborn's rights (see 1 Chronicles 5:1 and Jacob's testament in Genesis 49:3-4).

### Isaac's Death (35:28-29)

Isaac dies at 180 and is buried by both sons, Esau and Jacob, together. Rashi notes that Esau's presence at the burial is one of the few acts for which tradition credits him. The Midrash observes that Isaac outlived the years of sorrow — he lived to learn that Jacob returned safely and prospered — and died in peace, unlike Abraham who died before the full consequences of Ishmael's path became clear.

## Theological Significance

The chapter traces a pattern of purification and loss. Jacob's spiritual ascent to Bethel requires removing idols, changing garments, and confronting death. Jewish theology understands this as a paradigm: spiritual elevation (*aliyah*) is not painless. The deaths of Deborah, Rachel, and Isaac strip Jacob of his maternal, spousal, and paternal connections, leaving him to stand as Israel — defined not by family bonds but by covenantal identity.

The Bethel theophany (35:9-13) is notable for its formal, almost liturgical quality: God recites the name change, the promise of nationhood, and the land grant. The rabbis read this as a "second confirmation" of the covenant, necessary because Jacob had been abroad in an impure land. Return to the Land requires covenant renewal — a principle later applied to the generation that entered Canaan under Joshua.

Rachel's roadside tomb becomes one of the most powerful symbols in Jewish consciousness: the mother who sacrifices herself for her children and continues to advocate for them even after death. This image directly counters despair in exile: if Rachel is praying, return is assured.
