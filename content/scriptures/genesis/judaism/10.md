## Shivim Umot Ha-Olam: The Seventy Nations

Genesis 10, known as the Table of Nations, catalogues the descendants of Noah's three sons — Shem, Ham, and Japheth — as they spread across the earth after the flood. Jewish tradition counts seventy nations emerging from this genealogy, a number that carries deep symbolic significance throughout rabbinic literature. This chapter concludes the section within parashat Noach that precedes the Tower of Babel narrative.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Seventy Nations

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 37:1) connects the seventy nations listed here to numerous other "seventies" in the Torah: the seventy members of Jacob's family who descended to Egypt (Genesis 46:27), the seventy elders appointed by Moses (Numbers 11:16), and the seventy bulls offered during Sukkot (Numbers 29:12-34), which the Talmud (Sukkah 55b) explains were offered on behalf of the seventy nations of the world.

| Son of Noah | Descendants | Traditional Geographic Region |
|---|---|---|
| Japheth | 14 nations (Gomer, Magog, Madai, Javan, Tubal, Meshech, Tiras, and sub-groups) | Asia Minor, Europe, northern regions |
| Ham | 30 nations (Cush, Mizraim, Put, Canaan, and sub-groups) | Africa, Canaan, Mesopotamia (partially) |
| Shem | 26 nations (Elam, Asshur, Arpachshad, Lud, Aram, and sub-groups) | Near East, Mesopotamia |

Rashi and the Targumim identify many of these names with known peoples: *Yavan* is Greece, *Madai* is Media (Persia), *Ashkenaz* is Germany (hence "Ashkenazi" Jews), *Mizraim* is Egypt, *Kena'an* is Canaan. These identifications shaped Jewish geographical consciousness for centuries.

### Nimrod (10:8-12)

Nimrod receives special attention as "a mighty hunter before YHVH." Rashi interprets this negatively: Nimrod was a hunter of human minds, ensnaring people through rebellion against God. The phrase "before YHVH" means "in defiance of YHVH." Genesis Rabbah (37:2) identifies Nimrod as the instigator of the Tower of Babel project, making him the archetype of human arrogance and organized rebellion against divine authority.

The Rambam (*Hilkhot Avodah Zarah* 1:1) places Nimrod in the historical progression of idolatry: in the generation of Enosh, people began worshipping the stars as God's intermediaries; by Nimrod's time, idolatry had become a full system of political and religious control.

Ibn Ezra takes a more neutral view: *gibbor tzayid* ("mighty hunter") may simply describe Nimrod's prowess, and "before YHVH" may be a superlative expression meaning "extraordinarily mighty." This rationalist reading demonstrates the diversity of Jewish interpretive approaches.

### "Japheth will dwell in the tents of Shem" (from 9:27, echoed in ch. 10)

The geographical and cultural interplay between the lines of Shem and Japheth is reflected in a Talmudic teaching (Megillah 9b): the beauty of Japheth (associated with Greek culture and aesthetics) shall dwell in the tents of Shem (associated with Torah learning). This was invoked to justify translating the Torah into Greek — the beauty of the Greek language serving the sacred content of Shem's heritage.

### The Purpose of Genealogy

The Ramban explains that the Table of Nations serves multiple purposes: it demonstrates that the entire human family descends from one ancestor (Noah), establishing universal human kinship; it provides the historical backdrop for the calling of Abraham (chapter 12), showing from what context he was chosen; and it fulfills the blessing "be fruitful and multiply" given in 9:1.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 10 in Jewish thought conveys:

- **Universal human family**: All nations share a common ancestor, grounding Jewish universalism alongside Jewish particularism
- **Israel among the nations**: The seventy nations provide the backdrop against which Israel's unique calling is understood — not as superiority but as specific covenantal mission
- **Providence over history**: The dispersion of nations is not random but divinely guided
- **Names carry destiny**: Rabbinic tradition treats each name as encoding information about the character and fate of the people it represents
