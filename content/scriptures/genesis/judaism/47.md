## Summary

Genesis 47 describes the settlement of Jacob's family in Goshen. Joseph presents five brothers and then Jacob himself to Pharaoh. Jacob blesses Pharaoh and, when asked his age, responds that his 130 years have been "few and evil" compared to his ancestors. As the famine worsens, Joseph administers Egypt's grain supply — the people pay with money, livestock, land, and finally their own labor, making Pharaoh owner of all Egypt except the priests' land. Jacob lives seventeen years in Egypt and, as death approaches, makes Joseph swear to bury him in the Land of Israel.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Jacob blessed Pharaoh" (47:7, 10)

Jacob blesses Pharaoh both upon arrival and departure. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 95:4) specifies the blessing: Jacob prayed that the Nile's waters would rise to meet Pharaoh's feet — that is, the famine would end. And indeed, Rashi notes, from the time Jacob entered Egypt the famine's severity diminished. The Midrash derives a principle: the righteous bring blessing wherever they go. Jacob's presence in Egypt was not merely beneficial to his family but to the entire land.

### "Few and evil have been the days of the years of my life" (47:9)

Jacob's self-assessment is remarkably bleak. His 130 years have been marked by flight from Esau, Laban's deception, Dinah's violation, Joseph's disappearance, and chronic grief. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 95:1) records a startling divine response: God said, "I saved you from Esau and Laban, restored Dinah and Joseph, and you complain that your days are few and evil? Your life will be shortened by the number of words in your complaint." This Midrash counts 33 words in Jacob's answer, and notes that Jacob lived to 147 — exactly 33 years fewer than his father Isaac's 180.

| Patriarch | Lifespan | Midrashic Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Abraham | 175 years | Died before his time so he would not see Esau's wickedness |
| Isaac | 180 years | Full measure for the *olah temimah* (perfect offering) |
| Jacob | 147 years | Shortened by 33 years for complaining about his lot |

Whether this Midrash is taken literally or homiletically, it conveys a powerful message: gratitude is a covenantal obligation, and complaining about divinely ordained circumstances carries consequences.

### Joseph's Administration (47:13-26)

Joseph's economic policy — buying all of Egypt's land and labor for Pharaoh in exchange for food — has generated both admiration and discomfort among commentators:

Rashi presents Joseph's actions without judgment, as practical administration during crisis. Nachmanides views the policy as wise governance that preserved life at the cost of freedom — an acceptable trade during existential famine. The Midrash, however, notes that Joseph's consolidation of all land under Pharaoh sowed the seeds of the later enslavement: the infrastructure of total state control that Joseph built was eventually turned against his own descendants.

The Sforno offers a remarkable reading: Joseph deliberately moved the Egyptian population from place to place (47:21) so that the Israelites would not be perceived as aliens — everyone was displaced. This act of social engineering was designed to protect his family.

### The Oath to Bury in the Land (47:29-31)

Jacob makes Joseph swear to bury him in the Cave of Machpelah, not in Egypt. He demands the oath be sworn with a hand placed "under my thigh" (*tachat yerekhi*) — the same gesture Abraham required of his servant (24:2). Rashi explains this involves swearing on the sign of circumcision, the covenant mark.

"Israel bowed at the head of the bed" (47:31). The Talmud (Megillah 16b) explains: Jacob thanked God that his descendants (*mittato*) were all worthy — none had strayed from the covenant. Alternatively, he bowed in gratitude that he would be buried in the Land of Israel despite dying in exile.

## Theological Significance

The chapter raises the theological problem of exile and rootedness. Jacob's family settles prosperously in Egypt — "they acquired property in it and were fruitful and multiplied greatly" (47:27) — yet Jacob insists on burial in Canaan. The rabbinic message is clear: material prosperity in exile must never substitute for attachment to the Land of Israel. Goshen is a temporary dwelling (*dirah ara'it*), not a permanent home.

Jacob's insistence on Canaan burial also carries eschatological significance. The Talmud (Ketubot 111a) teaches that the dead buried in the Land of Israel will be the first to be resurrected in the messianic age. Jacob's request reflects this belief: to be buried in the Land is to affirm the promise of ultimate return and restoration.

The seventeen years Jacob lives in Egypt (47:28) are described by the Zohar as the best years of his life — finally reunited with Joseph, surrounded by all his children, living in peace. The Hebrew word *vayechi* ("and he lived") that opens this section (also the name of the final parashah) implies that only now did Jacob truly *live*. This paradox — the truest life experienced in exile — anticipates the complex Jewish relationship with diaspora existence.
