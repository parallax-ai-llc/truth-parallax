## Abraham and Abimelech: Ethics Among the Nations

Genesis 20 recounts Abraham's sojourn in Gerar, where he again presents Sarah as his sister, leading Abimelech king of Gerar to take her. God intervenes through a dream, Abimelech rebukes Abraham, and Abraham prays for Abimelech's household to be healed. This chapter, within parashat Vayera, raises challenging questions about Abraham's moral judgment and the surprising righteousness of a gentile king.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Why This Story Appears Here

Rashi addresses the placement of this episode immediately after the destruction of Sodom: Abraham moved from the oaks of Mamre because, after Sodom's destruction, travelers no longer passed through the region and there were no guests to host. Having lost the opportunity for hospitality — his defining virtue — Abraham relocated to Gerar where he could resume welcoming wayfarers.

The Ramban sees a different connection: juxtaposed with Lot's daughters' episode, the narrative about Sarah and Abimelech establishes Sarah's purity. Despite being taken into a royal household, she remained untouched — in contrast to Lot's compromised family.

### Abraham's Deception (20:2, 11-13)

Abraham repeats the sister-wife stratagem used in Egypt (chapter 12). His justification to Abimelech is revealing: "I said to myself, 'There is surely no fear of God (*yir'at Elohim*) in this place, and they will kill me on account of my wife'" (20:11).

| Commentator | Assessment of Abraham's Conduct |
|---|---|
| Rashi | Abraham reasoned from the absence of moral infrastructure — no fear of God means no restraint on violence |
| Ramban | Criticizes Abraham again (as in ch. 12) for endangering Sarah rather than trusting God |
| Radak | Abraham's assessment was correct about the general culture but wrong about Abimelech personally |
| Sforno | Abraham's repeated use of deception reflects an ongoing character test he had not yet mastered |

Abraham also reveals that the statement "she is my sister" contains a partial truth: Sarah is the daughter of his father (though not his mother), or alternatively his niece (Rashi identifies Sarah with Iscah, daughter of Haran). The Talmud (Megillah 15a) discusses the relationship between partial truth and deception.

### Abimelech's Integrity (20:4-7)

Abimelech's response is striking: "Lord, will You slay a righteous nation?" He protests his innocence, and God acknowledges it — "I also knew that you did this in the integrity of your heart." A gentile king is described as acting with moral integrity (*tom levav*), and God Himself validates it.

This passage is significant for Jewish theology of gentile righteousness. The Rambam (Hilkhot Melakhim 8:11) rules that a non-Jew who observes the Noahide laws out of moral conviction is considered among the righteous of the nations. Abimelech exemplifies this category — he fears God, acts justly, and is responsive to divine communication.

### God's Protection of Sarah (20:6)

"I prevented you from sinning against Me" — God tells Abimelech He did not allow him to touch Sarah. Rashi explains that Abimelech was physically restrained: his body was sealed (*nitam*), afflicted so that he could not approach Sarah. This divine intervention ensures that Isaac's paternity is unquestionable — a matter of halakhic significance for the legitimacy of Abraham's covenantal line.

### Abraham's Prayer (20:17)

"Abraham prayed to God, and God healed Abimelech and his wife and his female servants, and they bore children." The Talmud (Bava Kamma 92a) derives from this verse a principle: "Whoever prays on behalf of another, when he himself needs that same thing, he is answered first." Abraham prayed for Abimelech's household to bear children, and in the very next chapter (21:1-2), Sarah conceives Isaac. Abraham's intercessory prayer for a gentile king brought blessing upon himself.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 20 in Jewish thought conveys:

- **Gentile righteousness is real**: Abimelech's integrity challenges any assumption that those outside the covenant lack moral capacity
- **Fear of God (*yir'at Elohim*) is the foundation of ethics**: Without it, Abraham believed no moral restraint would operate — yet Abimelech proved him wrong
- **Intercessory prayer for others brings personal blessing**: The principle of *zekhut* (merit) accrued through selfless prayer
- **Divine protection of the covenantal line**: God intervenes to ensure the integrity of the promise regarding Isaac
