## Summary

Genesis 28 opens with Isaac formally blessing Jacob and sending him to Paddan-Aram to find a wife from Rebekah's family. Esau, seeing that Canaanite wives displease his parents, takes a wife from Ishmael's line. Jacob departs and, at a place he later names Bethel, has the famous vision of a ladder (*sulam*) reaching heaven, with angels ascending and descending. God stands above and reaffirms the Abrahamic covenant. Jacob awakens, anoints the stone as a pillar, and vows to tithe all that God gives him.

This chapter begins Parashat Vayetze (28:10-32:3), one of the rare Torah portions with no paragraph breaks — read as a single continuous narrative of exile and return.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "He encountered the place" (28:11) — *Vayifga ba-makom*

The verb *vayifga* (encountered/struck upon) is understood by the Talmud (Berakhot 26b) as referring to prayer. From this verse the rabbis derive that Jacob instituted the evening prayer (*Arvit/Ma'ariv*), just as Abraham instituted the morning prayer (*Shacharit*, Gen. 19:27) and Isaac the afternoon prayer (*Minchah*, Gen. 24:63). The word *makom* ("the place") is also one of God's names (*Ha-Makom*), suggesting Jacob encountered the Divine Presence itself.

Rashi, citing Chullin 91b, teaches that the "place" was Mount Moriah — the future site of the Temple — which "jumped" toward Jacob. This miraculous compression of geography (*kefitzat ha-derekh*) signals that sacred space operates beyond ordinary physics.

### The Ladder — *Sulam* (28:12)

The ladder (*sulam*, occurring only here in the entire Torah) has generated extraordinary commentary:

| Interpreter | Meaning of the Ladder |
|---|---|
| Rashi / Midrash | The ramp of the Temple altar, whose numerical value (*gematria*) equals *sulam*: *sulam* = 130 = *Sinai* |
| Nachmanides | A vision of divine providence — angels represent God's governance of nations |
| Zohar | The ladder connects the lower and upper worlds, the sefirot from Malkhut to Keter |
| Sforno | Human prayer ascends and divine blessing descends |
| Maimonides (*Guide* I:15) | A parable of prophetic ascent through intellectual perfection |

The gematria equivalence of *sulam* (samekh-lamed-mem = 60+30+40 = 130) and *Sinai* (samekh-yod-nun-yod = 60+10+50+10 = 130) links Jacob's personal revelation to the national revelation at Sinai — the private anticipates the communal.

### "Angels ascending and descending" (28:12)

The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 68:12) asks: why do the angels ascend *first* if they are heavenly beings? Because these are the angels assigned to the Land of Israel, who accompanied Jacob thus far and now ascend, while the angels of the Diaspora descend to escort him abroad. This teaches that different angels govern the Land and the exile — a concept with profound implications for the theology of *galut* (exile) and *geulah* (redemption).

### Jacob's Vow and the Tithe (28:20-22)

Jacob's vow — "If God will be with me... then the Lord shall be my God... and I will surely tithe" — is the Torah's first explicit mention of tithing by a patriarch (Abraham's tithe to Melchizedek in Genesis 14 being to a priest). The doubled verb *aser a'asrenu* ("I will surely tithe") is read by the Talmud (Taanit 9a) as a wordplay: *aser bishvil she-titasher* — "tithe so that you will become rich." This is one of the rare cases where the rabbis permit performing a mitzvah with the expectation of material reward.

## Theological Significance

Bethel represents the nexus of heaven and earth in Jewish thought. The Midrash identifies it with the *Even ha-Shetiyah*, the Foundation Stone from which the world was created and upon which the Holy of Holies would later stand. Jacob's experience demonstrates that divine revelation can occur unexpectedly — "Surely the Lord is in this place, and I did not know it" (28:16) — a verse that has informed Jewish mystical theology about the hidden presence of God in all places and circumstances.

The chapter also establishes the covenantal pattern of exile and promised return. God's assurance "I will bring you back to this land" (28:15) becomes the template for every subsequent Jewish exile. The principle *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim* is explicitly at work: Jacob's departure and eventual return foreshadow the Egyptian exile, the Babylonian exile, and the present dispersion.
