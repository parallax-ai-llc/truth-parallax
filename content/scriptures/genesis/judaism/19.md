## The Destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah

Genesis 19 narrates the angels' visit to Sodom, the depravity of its inhabitants, the rescue of Lot and his daughters, the destruction of the cities of the plain, the transformation of Lot's wife into a pillar of salt, and the incestuous origin of Moab and Ammon. This chapter, in parashat Vayera, is read in Jewish tradition as a case study in institutional wickedness and the limits of divine patience.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Sin of Sodom

Jewish tradition emphatically interprets the sin of Sodom as more than sexual immorality. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 109a-b) and Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 49:6, Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer 25) describe Sodom as a society that institutionalized cruelty, particularly toward strangers and the poor:

| Sin of Sodom | Source |
|---|---|
| Refusing hospitality to travelers | Sanhedrin 109a |
| Punishing anyone who gave charity | Genesis Rabbah 49:6 — the story of the maiden executed for feeding a poor man |
| Perverting justice — judges who legalized theft | Sanhedrin 109b — the "judges of Sodom" (*dayyanei Sedom*) |
| Procrustes-like cruelty to guests | Sanhedrin 109b — stretching or cutting visitors to fit a standard bed |
| Economic exploitation and prohibition of commerce with outsiders | Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer 25 |

Ezekiel 16:49 supports this reading: "This was the sin of Sodom: arrogance, surfeit of bread, and careless ease, and she did not strengthen the hand of the poor and needy." The prophetic and rabbinic consensus is that Sodom's defining wickedness was social injustice, not exclusively sexual transgression.

The expression *middat Sedom* ("the quality of Sodom") becomes a legal term in the Talmud (Eruvin 49a; Bava Batra 12b) for someone who refuses to share what costs them nothing — "what's mine is mine, and what's yours is yours" when taken to an extreme becomes the ethic of Sodom (Avot 5:10).

### Lot's Hospitality (19:1-3)

Lot sits at the gate of Sodom — the place of judges and civic leaders — and invites the angels to his home. Rashi notes that Lot had learned hospitality from Abraham, but his execution is inferior: Abraham offered fine flour and choice meat; Lot offered matzot (unleavened bread). The Midrash connects this detail to the timing: the destruction occurred on Passover, so Lot was baking matzot.

### Lot's Offer of His Daughters (19:8)

Lot's shocking offer to surrender his daughters to the mob in place of his guests is deeply troubling. The Ramban views it as a moral failure — no circumstance justifies sacrificing one's children. Rashi takes a more measured approach, suggesting Lot calculated that he had a greater obligation to his guests (whom he had invited under his protection) than to his daughters (whom he hoped the mob would not actually harm). Neither commentator excuses the act.

### "Do not look behind you" (19:17)

The angel's instruction not to look back is violated by Lot's wife, who becomes a pillar of salt. Rashi explains that she "sinned through salt" — when the guests arrived, she went to the neighbors asking for salt, deliberately alerting them to the presence of strangers. Her punishment matched her crime (*middah ke-neged middah*).

The Talmud (Berakhot 54b) teaches that one who sees the pillar of Lot's wife should recite two blessings: one acknowledging God as the true Judge, and one thanking God for remembering the righteous (who were saved).

### The Daughters of Lot (19:30-38)

The origin of Moab ("from father") and Ben-Ammi ("son of my people") through Lot's daughters' incestuous acts generates complex rabbinic discussion. The Midrash notes that the elder daughter named her son brazenly — *Mo'av* transparently declares the incest — while the younger used a euphemism (*Ben-Ammi*). As a result, the Torah later treats their descendants differently: Ammon is treated somewhat more favorably than Moab.

Yet the rabbis ultimately find redemption in this sordid episode: from Moab descends Ruth the Moabite, and from Ruth descends King David, ancestor of the Messiah. The Midrash (Ruth Rabbah 2:1) teaches that the daughter's intention was for the sake of perpetuating humanity (they believed the world had been destroyed), and from impure origins God brings forth the purest royal line.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 19 in Jewish thought teaches:

- **Institutional evil surpasses individual sin**: Sodom's crime was systematizing cruelty — making injustice the law
- **Hospitality as life and death**: The contrast between Abraham's hospitality (ch. 18) and Sodom's hostility defines the moral spectrum
- **Divine justice is proportional**: Punishments match crimes; the wicked bring destruction upon themselves
- **Redemption from impurity**: The Davidic line emerging from Lot's daughters demonstrates that God's providence operates through the most unlikely and morally compromised circumstances
