## Brit Bein HaBetarim: The Covenant Between the Parts

Genesis 15 records one of the most theologically significant events in the Torah: the Covenant Between the Parts (*Brit Bein HaBetarim*), in which God formally promises Abram descendants, land, and prophetic knowledge of Israel's future exile and redemption. This chapter, within parashat Lech Lecha, introduces the concept of *emunah* (faith/trust) as a defining quality of the covenantal relationship.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Fear not, Abram; I am a shield for you" (15:1)

The chapter opens with God's assurance to Abram after the battle with the four kings. Rashi explains that Abram feared two things: that he had exhausted his merit by the miracle of the military victory, and that relatives of the slain kings would seek revenge. God addresses both concerns — "I am a shield" (protection) and "your reward is very great" (merit remains).

The Ramban adds that the vision came "after these things (*achar ha-devarim ha-eleh*)" — after Abram's inner deliberation (*devarim* can mean "words" or "things") about whether he had acted rightly in going to war.

### "What will You give me, seeing I go childless?" (15:2-3)

Abram's complaint is remarkable for its directness. He tells God that without an heir, all promises of greatness are meaningless — his servant Eliezer of Damascus will inherit everything. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 44:6) notes that Abram's willingness to challenge God models the legitimacy of honest prayer — questioning God is not impiety but relationship.

### "He believed in YHVH, and He reckoned it to him as righteousness" (15:6)

This verse is interpreted very differently in Jewish and Christian traditions. In Pauline theology (Romans 4, Galatians 3), it becomes the proof-text for justification by faith alone. Jewish reading resists this:

| Jewish Commentator | Interpretation of *tzedakah* |
|---|---|
| Rashi | God reckoned Abraham's faith as a merit (*zekhut*) — not as a substitute for deeds but as itself a righteous act |
| Ramban | The verse describes Abraham's trust that God would give him a biological heir despite his advanced age |
| Sforno | Abraham did not ask for a sign regarding the promise of children — his trust was the righteous act |
| Rambam | Faith (*emunah*) in Judaism is always linked to action; belief without obedience is incomplete |

The key distinction: in Jewish thought, *emunah* is not mere intellectual assent but active trust expressed through living according to God's commandments. Abraham's faith was counted as righteousness because it was the kind of faith that leads to righteous action — as demonstrated throughout his life.

### The Covenant Ceremony (15:9-11)

God instructs Abram to take a heifer, a goat, a ram, a turtledove, and a young pigeon — each three years old — and split them (except the birds). This ancient covenant ritual, where parties walked between the halves of slaughtered animals, signified a self-imprecatory oath: "May I become like these animals if I break this covenant."

Rashi notes that the three animals correspond to the three types of sacrifices that Israel would later offer in the Temple. The birds represent Israel itself — as the dove was not split, Israel will never be destroyed.

The birds of prey (*ayit*) that descend upon the carcasses (15:11) are interpreted by the midrash as representing the nations that will attempt to devour Israel. Abram drives them away — foreshadowing Israel's preservation despite exile and persecution.

### The Prophecy of Exile (15:13-16)

God reveals to Abram that his descendants will be strangers in a land not theirs, enslaved for 400 years, but will emerge with great wealth. This prophecy is foundational to the Passover Haggadah, which recounts *Brit Bein HaBetarim* as the origin of both the exile and the redemption.

The Talmud (Megillah 9a) debates when the 400-year count begins — from Isaac's birth, not from the actual entry into Egypt — yielding 210 years of actual Egyptian residence rather than 400.

### "A smoking furnace and a flaming torch passed between the pieces" (15:17)

Only God — represented by fire — passes between the animal halves. Abram does not pass through. The Ramban explains the profound implication: this is a unilateral covenant. God binds Himself unconditionally. The covenant with Abraham is not contingent on human performance in the way that later covenants at Sinai involve mutual obligations.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 15 establishes:

- **Emunah as relational trust**: Faith in Judaism is active confidence in God's promises, inseparable from righteous living
- **Unilateral divine commitment**: God alone walks between the parts — the covenant is guaranteed by divine faithfulness
- **Exile and redemption as paired realities**: Jewish suffering is not random but part of a providential plan that culminates in deliverance
- **Prophecy as burden**: Abram experiences a "great dark dread" (15:12) — knowing the future brings suffering, not comfort
