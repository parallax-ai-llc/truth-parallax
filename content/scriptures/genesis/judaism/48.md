## Summary

Genesis 48 records Jacob's adoption and blessing of Joseph's two sons, Ephraim and Manasseh. Joseph brings the boys to his ailing father, and Jacob elevates them to the status of his own sons — "Ephraim and Manasseh shall be mine like Reuben and Simeon" (48:5). When Joseph positions the boys so that the elder Manasseh is at Jacob's right hand, Jacob deliberately crosses his hands, placing his right hand on the younger Ephraim. Joseph protests, but Jacob insists: the younger will surpass the elder. Jacob blesses both boys and declares that all Israel will invoke their names in blessing.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Ephraim and Manasseh shall be mine like Reuben and Simeon" (48:5)

Jacob's adoption of Joseph's sons has profound legal and tribal consequences: Joseph effectively receives a double portion — the share of the firstborn (*bechor*) — through his two sons becoming independent tribes. Rashi notes this is the right of the firstborn that was transferred from Reuben to Joseph (see 1 Chronicles 5:1). The birthright blessing (*berakhah*) went to Judah (kingship), but the material double portion (*pi shenayim*) went to Joseph.

The Midrash asks why Jacob adopted Ephraim and Manasseh specifically: because these boys were born and raised in Egypt yet remained faithful to the covenant. Their merit was that they maintained Israelite identity amid Egyptian culture — a model for all diaspora Jews.

### Jacob's Crossed Hands (48:14)

"Israel stretched out his right hand and placed it on Ephraim's head — he was the younger — and his left on Manasseh's head. He *sikkel* his hands, for Manasseh was the firstborn."

The word *sikkel* is variously translated. Rashi reads it as "he acted wisely/deliberately" (*sekhel* = wisdom). The Targum renders it "he crossed his hands." Ibn Ezra emphasizes that Jacob acted with prophetic knowledge, not mere preference.

| Element | Significance |
|---|---|
| Right hand on Ephraim | The right hand conveys the greater blessing; Ephraim's tribe will produce Joshua |
| Crossed hands (*sikkel*) | Deliberate inversion of the natural order — the younger surpasses the elder |
| Joseph's protest (48:17-18) | Even the righteous cannot always understand God's choices |
| Jacob's insistence (48:19) | Prophetic certainty overrides human convention |

This scene recapitulates the entire Genesis theme of the displaced firstborn: Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau, Joseph over Reuben, and now Ephraim over Manasseh. The Midrash explains this recurring pattern as God's demonstration that election follows divine wisdom, not human primogeniture.

### "By you shall Israel bless, saying: May God make you like Ephraim and Manasseh" (48:20)

This verse is the basis for the traditional Shabbat blessing given by Jewish parents to their sons (*birkat banim*) every Friday evening: *Yesimkha Elohim k'Ephraim v'khi-Menasheh* — "May God make you like Ephraim and Manasseh."

Why bless with these names rather than with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob? The Midrash offers several explanations: (1) Ephraim and Manasseh were the first brothers in Genesis who did not quarrel — breaking the pattern of Cain/Abel, Isaac/Ishmael, Jacob/Esau, Joseph/brothers; (2) they maintained their identity in exile; (3) the younger accepted the elder's precedence, and the elder accepted the younger's elevation, without jealousy. The blessing thus asks that children live without sibling rivalry — the resolution of Genesis's central family trauma.

### "I am about to die, but God will be with you and bring you back to the land of your fathers" (48:21)

Jacob's dying assurance to Joseph mirrors God's assurance to Jacob himself (46:4). The pattern of promise-transmission continues: each generation reassures the next that exile is temporary and return is guaranteed. Rashi adds that Jacob foresaw specifically the exodus under Moses — *pakod yifkod* ("God will surely remember you"), using the language that Moses will later use (Exodus 3:16).

### "I give you Shechem — one portion beyond your brothers" (48:22)

The word *shekhem* can mean "Shechem" (the city) or "one portion" (*shekhm echad*). Rashi reads it literally: Jacob gives Joseph the city of Shechem, which Jacob "took from the hand of the Amorite with my sword and my bow." The Targum Onkelos translates "sword and bow" as "my prayer and my supplication" — spiritual rather than military weapons. This interpretive move transforms conquest into prayer, reflecting the rabbinic discomfort with patriarchal violence.

## Theological Significance

The chapter establishes the theology of blessing as transmission — not merely words spoken but spiritual reality conveyed across generations. Jacob's crossed hands demonstrate that blessing follows God's plan, not human expectation. The patriarchal order repeatedly subverts primogeniture to teach that divine election is free and sovereign.

The Shabbat blessing of children with Ephraim and Manasseh's names carries the deepest aspiration of Jewish family life: children who maintain identity in exile, who live without destructive rivalry, and who accept their respective roles with grace. Every Friday evening, Jewish parents re-enact Jacob's blessing, connecting the family table to the patriarchal narrative.

The adoption formula also has halakhic resonance: it models the integration of converts and adopted children into the Jewish community. Just as Ephraim and Manasseh, born to an Egyptian mother, became fully Israelite through Jacob's declaration, so too can those from outside the community be fully incorporated through proper procedure.
