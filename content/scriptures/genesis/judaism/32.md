## Summary

Genesis 32 opens Parashat Vayishlach (32:4-36:43). Jacob sends messengers ahead to Esau and learns that Esau approaches with 400 men. In fear, Jacob divides his camp, prays to God, and prepares an elaborate gift. That night, alone by the Jabbok ford, Jacob wrestles with a mysterious figure until dawn. The figure injures Jacob's hip but cannot prevail; Jacob demands and receives a blessing — a new name, Israel (*Yisrael*), "for you have striven with God and with men, and have prevailed." Jacob names the place Peniel ("face of God") and limps away at sunrise.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Jacob was greatly afraid and distressed" (32:8)

Rashi distinguishes the two emotions: "afraid" that he might be killed, "distressed" that he might have to kill. This double fear reveals Jacob's moral character — even facing an enemy who swore to murder him, Jacob dreads the prospect of shedding blood. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 76:2) presents this as a model for Jewish military ethics: legitimate self-defense coupled with anguish over its necessity.

### Jacob's Prayer (32:10-13)

This is the first extended personal prayer in the Torah and became a model for Jewish liturgical prayer. The rabbis identify three elements that correspond to the structure of the *Amidah*:

| Element | Verse | Liturgical Parallel |
|---|---|---|
| Acknowledging God's past kindness | "I am unworthy of all the mercies" (32:11) | *Avot* — invoking the merit of ancestors |
| Stating the present need | "Deliver me from the hand of my brother" (32:12) | Middle blessings — petitions |
| Invoking God's promises | "You said, 'I will surely do you good'" (32:13) | Expressions of trust and hope |

Rashi notes that Jacob does not rely on his own merit but says *katonti* — "I have become small/unworthy." The Talmud (Berakhot 32a) derives from this that one should always approach God with humility, not claiming entitlement to divine protection.

### The Wrestling (32:25-30)

The identity of Jacob's opponent is debated extensively:

The Talmud (Chullin 91a) identifies him as the *sar shel Esav* — the guardian angel of Esau, the spiritual archetype of Esau's power. Rashi follows this reading. The struggle is thus not merely physical but cosmic: Jacob contends with the spiritual force behind Esau's dominion, and by prevailing, secures Israel's ultimate destiny to overcome the nations represented by Edom/Esau.

Nachmanides reads the encounter as prophetic allegory: throughout history, the descendants of Esau will oppress Israel and wound them, but never destroy them. Israel will limp but endure until the dawn of redemption, when the angel of Esau will finally "bless" Israel — acknowledge their supremacy.

The Zohar interprets the wrestling as Jacob's integration of the attribute of *Gevurah* (severity/judgment), previously associated with Isaac and Esau. By absorbing this blow and demanding blessing, Jacob becomes complete — *shalem* (33:18) — uniting *Chesed* (Abraham), *Gevurah* (Isaac), and *Tiferet* (beauty/harmony), the central pillar of the *sefirot*.

### "Your name shall no longer be Jacob, but Israel" (32:29)

The name *Yisrael* is parsed as *sar-El* — "prince of God" or *yashar-El* — "upright with God." Rashi follows the first: "you have become a prince and ruler." The Midrash Tanchuma adds that the name change signifies that Jacob's destiny will no longer be defined by the *akev* (heel) — grasping, supplanting — but by direct, face-to-face encounter with the divine.

### The Sinew of the Hip — *Gid Ha-Nasheh* (32:33)

From this verse derives the dietary prohibition against eating the sciatic nerve (*gid ha-nasheh*), one of the 613 commandments. The Talmud (Chullin 89b-103b) devotes extensive discussion to its laws. Nachmanides explains the prohibition's deeper meaning: Israel must remember that even in exile and suffering — limping from the wound Esau's angel inflicted — they endure and the sun eventually rises upon them.

## Theological Significance

This chapter is the theological hinge of the Jacob narrative. The man who began as a "heel-grabber" and deceiver becomes Israel — one who confronts God and prevails. Jewish theology reads this transformation as paradigmatic: *teshuvah* (repentance/return) is not merely remorse but a wrestling with one's deepest self that results in a new identity. The wound Jacob carries is itself significant — transformation leaves marks, and wholeness in Jewish thought is not the absence of struggle but its fruit.
