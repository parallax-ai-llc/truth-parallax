## Summary

Genesis 34 recounts the assault on Dinah, daughter of Jacob and Leah, by Shechem son of Hamor the Hivite. Shechem then seeks to marry Dinah, and Hamor proposes intermarriage and commercial union between the two peoples. Jacob's sons deceitfully stipulate that all Shechemite males must be circumcised. On the third day after the circumcision, when the men are in greatest pain, Simeon and Levi attack the city, kill every male, and rescue Dinah. The other brothers plunder the city. Jacob rebukes Simeon and Levi for endangering the family, and they respond: "Shall our sister be treated as a harlot?"

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Dinah went out" (34:1)

The verse says *vatetzei Dinah bat Leah* — "Dinah the daughter of Leah went out." Rashi (citing Bereshit Rabbah 80:1) connects her "going out" to Leah's own character — "like mother, like daughter," since Leah is described as going out to meet Jacob (30:16). However, Nachmanides objects to blaming Dinah and insists the verse simply narrates her movements without moral judgment.

The Midrash's tendency to scrutinize Dinah's "going out" has been critiqued by modern Jewish commentators, particularly Nechama Leibowitz, who emphasized that the Torah places blame squarely on Shechem: "he took her, lay with her, and humiliated her" (34:2) — three verbs indicating escalating violence.

### The Misuse of Circumcision (34:14-17)

The sons of Jacob demand circumcision as a precondition for intermarriage, but their intention is deception. This is deeply problematic in Jewish law: circumcision (*brit milah*) is the sign of the Abrahamic covenant, the most sacred physical mark of Jewish identity. Using it as a weapon fundamentally desecrates its meaning.

| Commentator | Moral Assessment |
|---|---|
| Maimonides (*Mishneh Torah*, Laws of Kings 9:14) | Shechem deserved death under Noahide law for failing to establish justice; the city was complicit |
| Nachmanides | Disagrees with Maimonides; only Shechem was guilty; the brothers acted disproportionately |
| Rashi | Simeon and Levi acted as brothers, from genuine fraternal obligation |
| Sforno | The deception itself was justified to rescue Dinah, who was still held captive |

### The Debate Between Jacob and His Sons (34:30-31)

Jacob's rebuke — "You have troubled me, making me odious to the inhabitants of the land" — seems to focus on pragmatic consequences rather than moral principle. Simeon and Levi's response — "Shall our sister be treated as a harlot?" — appeals to honor and justice. The Midrash notes that Jacob does not answer them, suggesting their argument carried force.

However, Jacob reserves his definitive judgment for his deathbed (49:5-7), where he curses Simeon and Levi's anger and divides them in Israel. Nachmanides explains that Jacob agreed with the motive but condemned the method: righteous anger that leads to indiscriminate killing crosses a moral boundary.

### "He had committed an outrage in Israel" (34:7) — *Nevalah b'Yisrael*

This phrase — *nevalah b'Yisrael* — becomes a technical legal term in later biblical and rabbinic usage for the most grievous sexual transgressions. Remarkably, the text calls the act an outrage "in Israel" even before the nation of Israel formally exists. Rashi explains: the phrase is proleptic — the Torah uses the name that will endure. The Midrash reads it as establishing a permanent principle: such acts are never acceptable among the people of Israel.

## Theological Significance

Jewish tradition does not offer a single moral verdict on this chapter; it preserves the argument. Maimonides justifies the brothers on the grounds of Noahide law: the Shechemites were complicit in failing to bring Shechem to justice, and thus all bore guilt. Nachmanides strongly dissents, arguing that collective punishment violates justice. This unresolved halakhic debate across centuries illustrates the Jewish commitment to moral complexity.

The chapter also speaks to the tension between assimilation and distinctiveness. Hamor's offer — "intermarry with us... dwell and trade with us" (34:9-10) — represents the perpetual temptation of cultural absorption. The brothers' refusal, however morally complicated in its execution, reflects the Jewish imperative to maintain covenantal identity. The Torah presents the dilemma without easy resolution: isolation risks vulnerability (Dinah's situation), but integration risks dissolution.

The desecration of circumcision as a weapon resonates through halakhic tradition as a cautionary principle: sacred instruments must never be instrumentalized for profane purposes.
