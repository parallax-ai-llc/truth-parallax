## Summary

Genesis 26 is one of the few chapters devoted almost exclusively to Isaac. A famine drives him to Gerar, where God reaffirms the Abrahamic covenant. Isaac passes off Rebekah as his sister before Abimelech, prospers greatly, and then faces conflict over wells dug by his father Abraham that the Philistines had stopped up. He re-digs them and finally finds peace at Rehoboth and Beer-sheba, where Abimelech seeks a treaty with him. The chapter closes with Esau's Hittite marriages, which bring grief to his parents.

This chapter falls within Parashat Toldot (25:19-28:9), which traces the generational unfolding of the Abrahamic promise through Isaac and his sons.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Do not go down to Egypt" (26:2)

Rashi explains that God specifically warned Isaac not to descend to Egypt because Isaac, having been bound on the altar (*akedat Yitzchak*), possessed the status of a perfect burnt offering (*olah temimah*). Just as a burnt offering may not leave the sacred precincts, Isaac was forbidden to leave the Land of Israel. This extraordinary midrashic comment (Bereshit Rabbah 64:3) elevates Isaac's sanctity beyond that of Abraham and Jacob, both of whom went to Egypt.

### "Isaac sowed in that land and reaped a hundredfold" (26:12)

The Talmud (Taanit 8b) derives from this verse that even estimating crop yields for purposes of tithing (*ma'aser*) was practiced by the patriarchs. Rashi notes the hundredfold return was measured against an estimate the soil had been assessed at — a poor soil in a famine year — making the blessing even more extraordinary. Nachmanides sees this agricultural success as a sign that the patriarchal blessings operate within the natural order yet transcend it.

### The Wells: Esek, Sitnah, Rehoboth (26:20-22)

The re-digging of Abraham's wells is a paradigm of the rabbinic principle *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim* — the deeds of the fathers are a sign for the children. Nachmanides (Ramban) provides a celebrated interpretation: the three wells correspond to the three Temples. Esek ("contention") represents the First Temple, destroyed amid strife. Sitnah ("enmity") represents the Second Temple, destroyed by hatred. Rehoboth ("spaciousness") represents the future Third Temple, which will be built without conflict, when God "makes room" for Israel.

| Well | Meaning | Ramban's Temple Allegory |
|------|---------|--------------------------|
| Esek | Contention | First Temple — contested and destroyed |
| Sitnah | Enmity | Second Temple — destroyed through *sinat chinam* (baseless hatred) |
| Rehoboth | Spaciousness | Third Temple — built in peace and expansion |

### "She is my sister" (26:7)

Ibn Ezra observes that Isaac's deception mirrors Abraham's earlier ruse (Gen. 12, 20), reinforcing the pattern of *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim*. The Midrash, however, is more lenient toward Isaac than toward Abraham, noting that Isaac technically said "she is my sister" (*achoti hi*), and the Hebrew word *achot* can mean "kinswoman." The rabbis debated whether the patriarchs' deceptions constituted permissible speech in situations of mortal danger (*pikuach nefesh*).

## Theological Significance

Jewish tradition regards Isaac as the most hidden of the patriarchs — the one whose inner life is least narrated. The Zohar associates Isaac with the sefirah of *Gevurah* (strength/judgment), and his characteristic mode is restraint and endurance rather than initiative. He does not discover new wells; he re-digs his father's wells. He does not journey to Mesopotamia; he remains in the Land. This "repetition with fidelity" is itself a profound spiritual quality in Jewish thought: the ability to preserve and restore what previous generations built.

The chapter also introduces a halakhic theme: the obligation to honor agreements. Abimelech's treaty with Isaac at Beer-sheba (26:26-31) establishes the principle that covenants between nations carry moral weight. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 105a) derives from such interactions that *derekh eretz* (ethical conduct) applies universally, not only among Israelites.

Esau's marriages to Hittite women (26:34-35) foreshadow the conflict in Chapter 27. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 65:4) compares the grief these marriages caused Isaac and Rebekah to "the smoke of idolatrous incense" that filled their home — a vivid image of cultural and spiritual incompatibility.
