## Gan Eden: The Garden and the First Humans

Genesis 2 continues the Bereshit parashah with a second, more intimate account of creation, focusing on the formation of the first human (*adam*) and the planting of the Garden of Eden. Jewish tradition does not read this chapter as contradicting Genesis 1 but as elaborating on the sixth day's events from a different literary perspective.

Rashi notes the shift from the divine name *Elohim* in chapter 1 to *YHVH Elohim* in chapter 2. As Genesis Rabbah (12:15) explains, this combination signifies that God united the attribute of mercy (YHVH) with the attribute of justice (Elohim) in governing the created world.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "YHVH Elohim formed the man, dust from the ground" (2:7)

The Hebrew wordplay is crucial: *adam* (human) is formed from *adamah* (earth/ground). Rashi highlights that God gathered dust from the four corners of the earth so that wherever a person dies, the earth will receive them for burial. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 38a) describes Adam's creation in twelve hours of the sixth day, detailing each stage from dust-gathering to receiving the soul.

The double verb *vayyitzer* ("and He formed") is spelled with two *yods*, which the Talmud (Berakhot 61a) interprets as indicating two inclinations: the *yetzer hatov* (good inclination) and the *yetzer hara* (evil inclination). This is a foundational departure from Christian "original sin" theology — in Judaism, both inclinations are created by God and the struggle between them is the essence of moral life.

### The Two Trees (2:9)

The Tree of Life (*Etz HaChayyim*) and the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil (*Etz HaDa'at Tov VaRa*) generate extensive rabbinic discussion:

| Tree | Rabbinic Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Tree of Life | Associated with Torah study (Proverbs 3:18); eternal life through divine wisdom |
| Tree of Knowledge | Debate over its species: grape vine, wheat, fig, or etrog (Berakhot 40a, Sanhedrin 70a) |

The Ramban explains that before eating from the Tree of Knowledge, Adam perceived truth and falsehood objectively. Afterward, moral knowledge became subjective — humans now experience good and evil as competing desires rather than clear categories.

### The Command Not to Eat (2:16-17)

Rashi, drawing on the Talmud (Sanhedrin 56b), explains that this prohibition is the basis for the Noahide laws. The Talmud derives all seven Noahide commandments from this single verse through midrashic exegesis: "And YHVH Elohim commanded the man" — each word yielding a separate obligation.

### "It is not good for the man to be alone" (2:18)

This is the first time God declares something "not good" (*lo tov*) in the creation narrative. The Talmud (Yevamot 62b-63a) uses this verse extensively to discuss the obligation of marriage. Rabbi Elazar states: "Any man who has no wife is not a man, as it says, 'Male and female He created them and called their name Adam'" (Genesis 5:2).

### Creation of Woman (2:21-23)

Rashi explains that God deliberated from which part of the body to create woman. Not from the head, lest she be domineering; not from the eye, lest she be curious; not from the ear, lest she be an eavesdropper — but from the rib, a modest and hidden part (Genesis Rabbah 18:2). Despite this care, the midrash notes with gentle humor, all these traits appeared anyway.

The Hebrew *tzela* is translated as "rib" but can also mean "side." The Talmud (Berakhot 61a) preserves an opinion that Adam was originally created with two sides or faces (*du-partzufin*), which God then separated into two beings.

### "Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother" (2:24)

This verse establishes the halakhic basis for marriage as an independent household. The Ramban sees it as a prophetic statement embedded in the narrative — a divine ordinance about the nature of human bonding.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 2 establishes foundational Jewish concepts:

- **Human partnership with God**: Adam is placed in the garden *le'ovdah u-leshomrah* ("to work it and guard it"), which the Midrash reads as referring to Torah commandments
- **Marriage as divine institution**: The first human relationship reflects God's design
- **Dual inclination**: The *yetzer hara* is not demonic but part of God's creation — without it, the Talmud says, no one would build a house, marry, or engage in commerce (Genesis Rabbah 9:7)
