## The Corruption Before the Flood

Genesis 6 begins parashat Noach and describes humanity's moral deterioration to the point where God resolves to destroy the world through a flood. This chapter raises profound theological questions about divine emotion, justice, and the nature of evil — all of which Jewish commentators address with characteristic depth and diversity of opinion.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The sons of God (*bnei ha-elohim*) saw the daughters of men" (6:2)

The identity of the *bnei ha-elohim* is vigorously debated:

| Commentator | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Rashi | Sons of princes and judges (authority figures) |
| Ramban | Descendants of Seth (the righteous line) |
| Ibn Ezra | Noblemen, those of high social rank |
| Targum Onkelos | "Sons of the great ones" — avoids any angelic reading |

Jewish tradition largely resists the interpretation — found in 1 Enoch and adopted by some Church Fathers — that these were fallen angels. Rashi explicitly states *bnei ha-elohim* means sons of rulers and judges. The focus remains on human moral failure rather than angelic transgression.

### The Nephilim (6:4)

Rashi derives *nephilim* from the root *n-ph-l* ("to fall"): they fell and caused the world to fall. They are identified with mighty warriors whose size and strength led them to arrogance rather than righteousness. The Talmud (Yoma 67b) and targumim consistently interpret them as powerful human beings, not as angelic-human hybrids.

### "And YHVH regretted (*vayyinnachem*) that He had made the man" (6:6)

This verse presents a major theological challenge: can the omniscient God "regret"? Jewish commentators offer multiple approaches:

- **Rashi**: God was consoled (*nechama*) about His decision, having already determined to create humanity even knowing they would sin, because righteous people would also emerge
- **Ramban**: The word expresses God's shift from the attribute of mercy to the attribute of justice — not a change of mind but a change of mode
- **Rambam (Maimonides)**: In the *Guide for the Perplexed* (1:29), he explains that the Torah speaks in "the language of human beings" (*dibrah Torah bi-leshon bnei adam*). God does not literally experience regret; the text uses anthropopathic language to convey the gravity of the situation

### "Noah found favor (*chen*) in the eyes of YHVH" (6:8)

Rashi notes the wordplay: *Noach* reversed spells *chen* (grace/favor). This literary device suggests that Noah's name itself prophesied his special status. The Zohar elaborates this into a mystical teaching about the reciprocal relationship between the righteous person and divine grace.

### "Noah was a righteous man, perfect in his generations" (6:9)

The qualifying phrase "in his generations" (*be-dorotav*) sparks one of the most famous rabbinic debates (Sanhedrin 108a):

- **Favorable view** (Rabbi Yochanan): If Noah was righteous even in a corrupt generation, how much more so would he have been in a righteous one
- **Unfavorable view** (Resh Lakish): Only by the low standards of his generation was he considered righteous; compared to Abraham, he would have been unremarkable

This interpretive tension is itself theologically instructive: Jewish tradition preserves both readings, refusing to flatten the ambiguity of the text.

### The Ark's Dimensions (6:14-16)

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 108b) notes that the ark's construction took 120 years, during which Noah served as a preacher of repentance. When passersby asked what he was doing, he warned them of the coming flood, giving them opportunity to repent. The midrash emphasizes that God's desire was always for teshuvah, not destruction.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 6 in Jewish thought establishes:

- **Human corruption is human responsibility**: The text attributes moral failure to human choices, not to cosmic forces or angelic rebellion
- **Divine justice is tempered by patience**: God waits 120 years, giving humanity time to repent
- **Righteousness is possible in any era**: Noah demonstrates that environment does not determine moral destiny
- **God's "regret" reveals relationship**: The Torah's anthropomorphic language reflects the covenantal bond between God and humanity
