## Summary

Genesis 43 resumes as the famine intensifies and Jacob's family exhausts their grain. Jacob resists sending Benjamin, but Judah pledges himself as personal surety (*arev*). The brothers return to Egypt with double money and gifts. Joseph, seeing Benjamin, orders a feast prepared. The brothers are seated in birth order — to their astonishment — and Benjamin receives a portion five times larger than the others. They drink and celebrate with Joseph, still unaware of his identity.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### Judah's Guarantee (43:9)

"I will be surety for him (*anokhi e'ervenu*); from my hand you shall require him. If I do not bring him back to you and set him before you, then I shall bear the blame forever (*v'chatati l'kha kol ha-yamim*)."

Rashi notes the force of *e'ervenu* — from the root *arev* (guarantor/surety) — which implies not merely a promise but a binding legal obligation. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 91:6) teaches that Judah accepted responsibility in both this world and the next. This pledge marks Judah's full transformation: the man who proposed selling Joseph (37:26) now offers himself in place of Rachel's remaining son.

| Aspect | Reuben's Offer (42:37) | Judah's Offer (43:9) |
|---|---|---|
| Pledge | "Kill my two sons" | "I will bear the blame forever" |
| Nature | Sacrifices others | Sacrifices himself |
| Effectiveness | Jacob refuses | Jacob accepts |
| Leadership quality | Impulsive | Responsible |

This contrast sealed Judah's claim to leadership and kingship. The Talmud (Makkot 11b) connects Judah's *arvut* (surety) to the broader principle of *kol Yisrael arevim zeh ba-zeh* — "all Israel are guarantors for one another" — the foundational concept of collective Jewish responsibility.

### "May God Almighty grant you mercy" (43:14) — Jacob's Prayer

Jacob invokes *El Shaddai* — the divine name associated with patriarchal covenant and with setting limits (*dai* = enough). Rashi reads Jacob's prayer as: "The One who said *dai* (enough) to creation at the beginning — may He say *dai* to my sufferings." The Midrash adds that Jacob prayed not only for Benjamin's safe return but for Simeon's release, showing that even in his grief he remembered all his children.

Jacob's closing words — "If I am bereaved, I am bereaved" (*ka'asher shakholti shakhalti*) — are interpreted by the Targum as an expression of resigned faith, not fatalism. The doubled verb suggests: "as I have already been bereaved [of Joseph], so I accept the possibility of further bereavement." Rashi reads it as implicit prayer: Jacob was hinting at a fast he undertook for his sons' safety.

### The Brothers Seated in Birth Order (43:33)

"They sat before him, the firstborn according to his birthright and the youngest according to his youth, and the men looked at one another in astonishment." The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 93:7) describes Joseph striking his goblet (*gavi'a*) and pretending to read their birth order through divination. The brothers were astounded — how could this Egyptian know? This detail heightens the dramatic tension while demonstrating Joseph's control over the situation.

### Benjamin's Fivefold Portion (43:34)

Benjamin receives five times the food of any brother. Rashi asks: Joseph had already learned from his own experience that favoritism breeds jealousy — why repeat the pattern? The answer is that this is part of the *teshuvah* test: will the brothers become jealous of Benjamin, Rachel's son, receiving preferential treatment? Their willingness to celebrate rather than resent this favoritism is evidence of inner change.

The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 93:8) adds that Joseph gave Benjamin five garments, again echoing the favoritism theme. The number five foreshadows the five changes of clothing Benjamin will receive (45:22) and connects to the five books of Torah that will be given through the tribes descended from Rachel's line.

## Theological Significance

The chapter centers on the theme of *arvut* — surety, mutual responsibility, and vicarious bearing of another's burden. Judah's self-pledge transforms him from a passive participant in Joseph's sale into an active agent of redemption. Jewish theology sees in Judah's evolution the paradigm of *ba'al teshuvah* — the penitent who rises higher than one who never sinned. The Talmud (Berakhot 34b) teaches that "in the place where the penitent stands, even the perfectly righteous cannot stand."

The feast scene also prefigures the ultimate reconciliation: enemies eating together in peace. The Midrash notes that the brothers "drank and became intoxicated with him" (43:34) — for the first time since Joseph's sale, the family shares joy. This motif of shared meals as signs of covenant restoration appears throughout the Torah (e.g., the covenant meal at Sinai, Exodus 24:11).
