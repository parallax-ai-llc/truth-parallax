## Summary

Genesis 50, the final chapter of Bereshit, brings the patriarchal era to its close. Joseph mourns Jacob and has him embalmed according to Egyptian custom. A great funeral procession carries Jacob to the Cave of Machpelah in Canaan for burial. After the burial, the brothers fear that Joseph will now take revenge, but he reassures them with the theological declaration: "You meant it for evil, but God meant it for good." Joseph lives to see three generations of descendants, and before dying at 110, he makes his brothers swear to carry his bones out of Egypt when God redeems them. The chapter — and Genesis — ends with Joseph embalmed and placed in a coffin in Egypt.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Burial Procession (50:7-13)

The funeral of Jacob was a grand affair attended by Egyptian officials, elders, and the entire household. Rashi (citing Sotah 13a) relates that when the procession reached the Cave of Machpelah, Esau's descendants attempted to block the burial, claiming the remaining burial plot belonged to Esau. Naphtali, renowned for his swiftness, ran back to Egypt to fetch the deed of sale. Meanwhile, Hushim son of Dan, who was deaf and did not hear the argument, asked what was happening and, upon learning that his grandfather's burial was being delayed, struck Esau with a club and killed him. Esau's head rolled into the cave and was buried there — the Midrash's way of acknowledging that Esau, despite everything, was Isaac's son and deserved burial alongside his ancestors.

### "Am I in the place of God?" (50:19)

When the brothers prostrate themselves and offer to be Joseph's slaves, Joseph refuses with words that echo and transform his earlier statement to Rachel (30:2). There, "Am I in God's place?" was spoken in frustration. Here, it expresses profound humility: "It is not my role to judge or punish you — that belongs to God alone."

The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 100:8) teaches that Joseph was saying: "Even if I wanted to harm you, I could not — you are ten, and I am one. If ten candles could not extinguish one, how shall one extinguish ten?" This image of light — Joseph as an inextinguishable candle — became a metaphor for the righteous person whom no conspiracy can destroy.

### "You meant it for evil, but God meant it for good" (50:20)

This verse — *atem chashavtem alai ra'ah, Elohim chashavah l'tovah* — is the theological summary of the entire Joseph narrative and, in many ways, of Genesis itself.

| Dimension | Content |
|---|---|
| Human intention | The brothers acted from jealousy and hatred — real sin with real consequences |
| Divine intention | God redirected the sin toward preserving life (*l'ma'an... l'hachayot am rav*) |
| Moral outcome | The sin is not excused; the brothers still needed *teshuvah* |
| Theological principle | Providence does not cancel free will; both coexist |

Nachmanides reads this as Joseph's definitive statement of *hashgachah pratit* (individual divine providence): God governs not merely the grand sweep of history but the specific actions of individuals, weaving even sinful choices into the fabric of redemption. This does not make the sin good — it makes God's governance unimaginably wise.

The Sforno adds that Joseph's refusal to punish also reflects a legal principle: since the sale ultimately resulted in benefit, Joseph could not retroactively claim damages. The divine outcome transformed the legal status of the original act.

### "God will surely remember you" — *Pakod Yifkod* (50:24-25)

Joseph's deathbed prophecy — "God will surely remember you and bring you up from this land to the land He swore to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob" — became one of the most important phrases in Jewish redemption theology. The doubled verb *pakod yifkod* ("will surely remember/visit") became the code word by which the future redeemer would authenticate himself. The Talmud (Sotah 13a) records that this phrase was transmitted from Joseph through the tribal elders and the midwives Shifrah and Puah, until Moses used it (Exodus 3:16) — and the people believed him because they recognized the password of redemption.

### "Carry my bones up from here" (50:25)

Joseph makes his brothers swear to transport his remains when the exodus occurs. Unlike Jacob, who demanded immediate burial in Canaan, Joseph asks to wait — his bones will travel with the nation. The Midrash (Mekhilta, Beshalach) praises Moses for personally fulfilling this oath: while all Israel was gathering Egyptian spoils at the exodus, Moses was searching for Joseph's coffin. The Talmud (Sotah 13a) relates that Joseph's coffin traveled through the wilderness alongside the Ark of the Covenant, and passersby would ask, "What are those two arks?" The answer: "One is the coffin of a dead man, and the other is the Ark of the Living God — and one fulfilled what is written in the other."

### Genesis Ends in a Coffin

The Midrash notes the stark ending: the first book of the Torah — which began with creation, light, and life — concludes with the word *b'Mitzrayim* ("in Egypt") and the image of a coffin (*aron*). This is deliberate: Genesis does not end with resolution but with anticipation. The coffin is a promise — Joseph's bones are waiting for redemption. The book that began with "In the beginning" ends in exile, setting the stage for Exodus and the larger drama of liberation.

## Theological Significance

The final chapter of Genesis establishes the theological framework that sustains Jewish consciousness through every subsequent exile: the certainty of redemption. *Pakod yifkod* — God will remember. This is not a vague hope but a sworn covenant, transmitted from generation to generation, confirmed by the deeds of the fathers as signs for the children.

Joseph's final act — requesting that his bones accompany the nation — demonstrates that the dead and the living share a common destiny in Jewish thought. The Midrash's image of Joseph's coffin alongside the Ark crystallizes this: the moral life of the righteous (*tzaddik*) is itself Torah. Joseph, who fulfilled the commandments before they were given, is a living Torah scroll whose presence sanctifies and instructs.

Genesis as a whole, read through the Jewish lens, is the story of covenant formation: from creation to family to the threshold of nationhood. It ends not with triumph but with trust — the patriarchal generation has planted seeds that will germinate in Exodus. The coffin in Egypt is simultaneously an ending and a beginning, a death and a promise, exile and the guarantee of return.

*Chazak, chazak, v'nitchazek* — "Be strong, be strong, and let us be strengthened." This traditional declaration, recited at the conclusion of each book of the Torah, affirms that ending a book is not a completion but a strengthening for what comes next.
