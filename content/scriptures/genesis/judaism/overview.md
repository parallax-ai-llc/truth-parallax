## Overview

In Judaism, Genesis (*Bereshit*, "In the beginning") is the first of the five books of the Torah, the most sacred section of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh). Jewish interpretation of Genesis spans millennia — from the Targumim (Aramaic translations) and Midrash, through the Talmud, the medieval commentators (Rishonim), and into modern scholarship.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Peshat (Plain Meaning)
The straightforward, contextual meaning of the text. Champion: **Rashi** (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040-1105), whose commentary is printed alongside the Torah in most editions.

### Derash (Homiletical)
Interpretive elaboration, often found in Midrash collections:
- **Midrash Rabbah** (Genesis Rabbah) — verse-by-verse rabbinic commentary
- **Midrash Tanchuma** — homiletical midrash

### Remez (Allegorical/Hint)
Hidden or symbolic meanings. Used extensively by **Nachmanides** (Ramban, 1194-1270) and kabbalistic interpreters.

### Sod (Mystical)
The deepest, esoteric meaning. **Zohar** and Kabbalistic tradition read Genesis as encoding the structure of the divine *sefirot*.

## Theological Emphases

- **Creation as ongoing**: God continually sustains the world (*mehadesh b'tuvo b'khol yom tamid*)
- **No "original sin"**: Judaism does not hold the Augustinian doctrine; the *yetzer hara* (evil inclination) is part of human nature, not a fallen state
- **Covenant**: The Abrahamic covenant (*brit*) as the foundation of Jewish identity
- **Torah as blueprint**: The Midrash teaches that God looked into the Torah and created the world
- **Election of Israel**: The patriarchal narratives establish Israel's unique relationship with God
