## Vayera: The Three Visitors and Abraham's Intercession

Genesis 18 opens parashat Vayera with one of the Torah's most vivid narratives: God appears to Abraham at the oaks of Mamre, three visitors arrive, Sarah laughs at the promise of a son, and Abraham intercedes for Sodom. This chapter is foundational for Jewish teachings on hospitality, prayer, and the relationship between divine justice and human advocacy.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "And YHVH appeared to him at the oaks of Mamre" (18:1)

Rashi explains that this visitation occurred on the third day after Abraham's circumcision (chapter 17), the most painful day of recovery. God came to visit the sick (*bikkur cholim*) — the Talmud (Sotah 14a) derives from this the obligation to visit the sick, as one of the acts by which we imitate God. Furthermore, Rashi notes that God made the day unusually hot (removing the sun from its sheath) so that no travelers would disturb Abraham — yet when Abraham was distressed at having no guests, God sent the three angels.

### Hospitality (*Hakhnasat Orchim*) (18:2-8)

Abraham runs to greet the three strangers, bows, prepares a lavish meal, and stands over them while they eat. The Talmud (Shabbat 127a) declares: "Hospitality to wayfarers is greater than receiving the Divine Presence (*Shekhinah*)" — derived from the fact that Abraham interrupted his encounter with God (18:1) to attend to the travelers (18:2).

| Abraham's Act | Halakhic/Ethical Derivation |
|---|---|
| Ran to greet them (18:2) | Eagerness in performing mitzvot (*zerizut*) |
| "Let a little water be brought" (18:4) | Abraham delegated water through a servant; God later provided Israel water through a servant (Moses). Abraham served bread personally; God provided manna directly (Bava Metzia 86b) |
| Stood over them while they ate (18:8) | The host serves the guests — the ideal of personal attention |
| Prepared tongue with mustard, three measures of fine flour (18:6-7) | Generosity beyond what was promised — he offered "a morsel of bread" but served a feast |

The Talmud (Bava Metzia 86b) systematically compares what Abraham did personally versus through intermediaries, teaching that what he did himself, God later did for his descendants personally; what he delegated, God later delegated.

### Sarah's Laughter (18:12-15)

Sarah laughs internally (*va-titzhak Sarah be-kirbah*) upon hearing she will bear a child at age 90 with Abraham at 99. God reports her laughter to Abraham but subtly changes her words: Sarah had said "and my lord is old" (18:12), but God told Abraham she said "Shall I indeed bear a child, old as I am?" (18:13) — omitting the reference to Abraham's age.

Rashi explains that God altered Sarah's words for the sake of peace (*shalom bayit*) between husband and wife. The Talmud (Yevamot 65b) derives from this the principle that one may modify the truth to preserve domestic harmony — a halakhic ruling with far-reaching implications.

### Abraham's Intercession for Sodom (18:23-33)

Abraham's negotiation with God — from fifty righteous to ten — is one of the Torah's most remarkable passages. Abraham challenges God: "Shall the Judge of all the earth not do justice?" (*ha-shofet kol ha-aretz lo ya'aseh mishpat?*)

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 49:9) notes Abraham's boldness: he said "dust and ashes" (*afar va-efer*) to acknowledge his lowliness while simultaneously pressing his case. The Talmud comments that as reward for this humility, his descendants received the mitzvah of the ashes of the red heifer (*efer parah*) and the dust used in the sotah ritual (*afar sotah*).

The descent from fifty to ten — and the abrupt stop at ten — raises the question: why not continue to one? The Ramban suggests that fewer than ten righteous could not constitute a community (*edah*) capable of sustaining a city's moral fabric. The Mishnah (Megillah 4:3) defines a *minyan* — the quorum for communal prayer — as ten, and this passage is one of the proof-texts.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 18 in Jewish thought teaches:

- **Hospitality is supreme**: Greater even than receiving the Shekhinah — this prioritization of human need is characteristically Jewish
- **Prayer as advocacy**: Abraham models the righteous person who argues with God on behalf of others, a tradition continued by Moses, the prophets, and the Hasidic *tzaddikim*
- **Peace justifies modifying truth**: The divine example of editing Sarah's words establishes that harmony takes precedence over literal accuracy
- **Justice requires a community**: The righteous cannot save a city alone — moral life requires collective commitment
