## The Akedah: The Binding of Isaac

Genesis 22 contains the *Akedah* ("Binding"), the supreme test of Abraham's faith and one of the most theologically significant narratives in all of Jewish thought. The chapter recounts God's command to sacrifice Isaac, the journey to Mount Moriah, the last-moment substitution of a ram, and the reaffirmation of God's covenant. Read on the second day of Rosh Hashanah, the Akedah is invoked daily in the morning liturgy (*Shacharit*) and permeates Jewish theology, philosophy, and ethical reflection.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "God tested (*nissah*) Abraham" (22:1)

The Torah explicitly frames this as a test (*nisayon*), not a command intended to be carried out. The Mishnah (Avot 5:3) counts the Akedah as the tenth and greatest of Abraham's ten trials. Rashi explains that *nissah* comes from *nes* (banner/standard): "He raised him like a banner for the world to see" — the test was designed to publicly demonstrate Abraham's devotion.

The Rambam (*Guide for the Perplexed* 3:24) interprets the Akedah philosophically: it demonstrates the ultimate extent to which love and fear of God can go. Abraham acted not from expectation of reward but from pure devotion — the model of *avodat Hashem* (service of God) for its own sake.

### "Take your son, your only one, whom you love — Isaac" (22:2)

Rashi famously unpacks the progressive narrowing of God's command:

| God's Words | Abraham's Response (Midrash) | God's Clarification |
|---|---|---|
| "Your son" | "I have two sons" | "Your only one" |
| "Your only one" | "Each is the only one of his mother" | "Whom you love" |
| "Whom you love" | "I love them both" | "Isaac" |

This midrashic dialogue (Genesis Rabbah 55:7) portrays God as gently increasing the emotional weight of the command, making Abraham's obedience progressively more agonizing — and more meritorious.

### "The two of them walked together (*yachdav*)" (22:6, 8)

The word *yachdav* ("together") appears twice: once before Isaac asks about the lamb, and once after Abraham's devastating answer ("God will provide the lamb for the burnt offering, my son"). Rashi comments on the second occurrence: "Even though Isaac understood that he was going to be slaughtered, they walked together — with one heart (*be-lev echad*)." Father and son are united in purpose. This transforms the Akedah from a story of blind obedience into one of shared, conscious devotion.

### The Binding, Not the Sacrifice (22:9-12)

The Jewish name for this event is crucial: the *Akedah* — the *Binding* — not the sacrifice. Isaac is bound, placed on the altar, but not killed. The angel's cry "Do not stretch your hand against the lad" (22:12) is the climax. Jewish theology insists that God never wanted human sacrifice — the entire point was to demonstrate willingness while prohibiting the act.

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 56:7) records that Abraham said: "Let me at least draw a drop of blood from him." God replied: "Do not do anything (*me'umah*) to him" — not even a blemish (*mum*), reading *me'umah* as connected to *mum*. This emphatic prohibition of harming Isaac is Judaism's definitive rejection of human sacrifice.

The Ramban notes the theological distinction from the Christian reading: in Christianity, the Akedah prefigures God's offering of His Son. In Judaism, the message is precisely the opposite — God stops the sacrifice. The binding demonstrates devotion; the stopping demonstrates that God does not desire such offerings.

### "Abraham called that place YHVH-Yireh" (22:14)

Rashi identifies Mount Moriah with the future site of the Temple in Jerusalem. The name *YHVH Yireh* ("God will see/provide") merges with *Shalem* (the name given by Shem/Melchizedek in 14:18) to form *Yeru-shalem* — Jerusalem. The Temple mount is thus eternally associated with Abraham's willingness and God's provision.

### The Ram (22:13)

The ram caught in the thicket by its horns is offered "in place of (*tachat*) his son." The Midrash (Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer 31) teaches that this ram was created at twilight on the sixth day of creation, prepared since the beginning of the world for this moment. Every part of the ram was used: its ashes became the foundation of the Temple altar, its sinews became the strings of David's harp, its skin became Elijah's belt, its left horn was blown at Sinai, and its right horn — larger — will be blown to herald the messianic era.

The shofar blown on Rosh Hashanah recalls the ram of the Akedah, invoking the merit of Abraham and Isaac (*zekhut avot*) before God.

## Theological Significance

The Akedah in Jewish thought establishes:

- **Faith tested through action**: *Emunah* is not intellectual but existential — lived out in the most extreme circumstances
- **Rejection of human sacrifice**: God's intervention definitively prohibits offering human life
- **Zekhut Avot (merit of the ancestors)**: The Akedah generates enduring merit invoked by all subsequent generations in prayer
- **Moriah and Jerusalem**: The binding sanctifies the site of the future Temple, linking patriarchal devotion to communal worship
- **Isaac as willing participant**: Jewish tradition emphasizes Isaac's agency — he was 37 years old (according to rabbinic chronology) and went willingly
