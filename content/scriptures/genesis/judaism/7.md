## The Mabbul: Destruction and Preservation

Genesis 7 describes the onset of the great flood (*mabbul*), the entry into the ark, and the rising of the waters. The chapter is part of parashat Noach and is read in Jewish communities worldwide in the autumn Torah reading cycle. Rabbinic interpretation attends to every detail — from the distinction between clean and unclean animals to the precise timing of the flood — extracting halakhic, ethical, and theological meaning.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Come into the ark, you and all your household" (7:1)

Rashi notes that God says "come" (*bo*) rather than "go" (*lekh*), implying that God's presence would be with Noah inside the ark. This detail carries theological weight: divine accompaniment persists even in catastrophe.

God also tells Noah: "For you I have seen as righteous before Me in this generation." Rashi highlights "before Me" (*lefanai*), teaching that Scripture uses restrained praise when speaking in someone's presence but fuller praise in their absence (in 6:9, when Noah is merely introduced, he is called "righteous and perfect").

### Clean and Unclean Animals (7:2-3)

Noah is instructed to take seven pairs of clean (*tahor*) animals and one pair of unclean (*tamei*). This raises the question: how did Noah know which were clean before the Torah was given at Sinai?

| Approach | Commentator |
|---|---|
| Noah studied Torah before Sinai | Rashi, citing Genesis Rabbah 26:3 — the patriarchs observed the Torah |
| God instructed him directly | Ramban — specific divine revelation |
| "Clean" here means suitable for sacrifice, not kashrut | Ibn Ezra — practical criterion |

The Talmud (Zevachim 116a) debates whether the extra clean animals were for post-flood sacrifices (which Noah indeed offers in 8:20) or for food consumption after God permits meat-eating in 9:3.

### "In the six hundredth year of Noah's life, in the second month" (7:11)

The precise dating of the flood generates halakhic-style chronological debate. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 11b-12a) records a dispute:

- **Rabbi Eliezer**: The "second month" is Marcheshvan (counting from Tishrei, the creation of the world). The flood came in autumn.
- **Rabbi Yehoshua**: The "second month" is Iyyar (counting from Nisan, the month of redemption). The flood came in spring.

This dispute connects to a broader disagreement about whether the world was created in Tishrei or Nisan — a debate preserved in the liturgy to this day.

### "The fountains of the great deep burst open and the windows of heaven were opened" (7:11)

Rashi explains the dual source of the flood — from below and above — as corresponding to the dual nature of the generation's sin. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 108a) teaches that because the generation sinned with heat (sexual immorality), they were punished with hot water. The midrash adds that the waters from below were boiling, intensifying the punishment to match the transgression — the principle of *middah ke-neged middah* (measure for measure).

### "And YHVH shut him in" (7:16)

God personally closes the door of the ark. Rashi explains this was to protect Noah from the mockers who might attack him and to shield the ark from the violent waters. Genesis Rabbah (32:8) elaborates that God sealed the ark with divine protection.

The Zohar reads this closing mystically: God sealed Noah in a spiritual enclosure (*tevah*, which also means "word"), suggesting that the righteous person finds refuge within the sacred words of prayer and Torah.

### The Duration of the Flood (7:17-24)

The waters prevail for 150 days. The Ramban carefully calculates the timeline, harmonizing the various numbers given throughout chapters 7 and 8. He notes that the Torah's precision with dates reflects its character as a historical testimony, not myth — the account reads like a log or chronicle.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 7 in Jewish thought conveys:

- **Divine judgment is real but reluctant**: God waits until the last moment and accompanies the righteous even through destruction
- **Measure for measure**: The nature of the punishment reflects the nature of the sin
- **Preservation within destruction**: The ark (*tevah*) represents the principle that God always preserves a remnant — a theme recurring throughout Jewish history
- **Torah predates Sinai**: The concept of clean and unclean animals before the formal giving of the Torah supports the rabbinic idea that Torah is woven into the fabric of creation
