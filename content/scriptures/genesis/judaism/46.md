## Summary

Genesis 46 narrates Jacob's journey to Egypt. He stops at Beer-sheba to offer sacrifices, and God appears to him in a night vision, reassuring him: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for I will make you into a great nation there. I will go down with you, and I will surely bring you up again." The chapter then provides a genealogy of Jacob's family — seventy souls who descended to Egypt. Jacob sends Judah ahead to Goshen to prepare the way, and father and son are reunited in an emotional encounter.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt" (46:3)

Why was Jacob afraid? Rashi explains: Jacob was distressed (*mitztaer*) at being compelled to leave the Land of Israel. He knew from God's covenant with Abraham (Genesis 15:13) that his descendants would be enslaved in a foreign land, and he feared this descent was the beginning of that exile. God's reassurance addresses both fears: the exile will produce a great nation, and God will personally accompany Jacob.

The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 94:5) adds that Jacob feared he was unworthy of divine protection abroad, since Isaac had been explicitly forbidden from leaving the Land (26:2). God's response — "I will go down with you" (*anokhi ered imkha*) — establishes the profound doctrine of *galut ha-Shekhinah*: the Divine Presence itself goes into exile with Israel. The Talmud (Megillah 29a) universalizes this: wherever Israel is exiled, the *Shekhinah* is exiled with them, and when they are redeemed, the *Shekhinah* is redeemed with them.

### The Seventy Souls (46:26-27)

The genealogy lists seventy persons (*shiv'im nefesh*) who descended to Egypt. The count is difficult: the list yields sixty-nine names by direct enumeration. Various solutions are offered:

| Solution | Source |
|---|---|
| Jochebed (Moses' mother) was born at the gates of Egypt, completing the count | Rashi, citing Sotah 12a |
| God Himself is included as the seventieth, fulfilling "I will go down with you" | Midrash Tanchuma |
| Jacob himself is the seventieth | Ibn Ezra |
| The number is typological, not exact — seventy represents the nations of the world (Genesis 10) | Nachmanides |

The number seventy is laden with symbolism: seventy elders of Israel (Numbers 11:16), seventy members of the Sanhedrin, seventy faces of Torah. The descent of seventy souls to Egypt parallels the seventy nations of the Table of Nations — Israel among the nations, a microcosm of humanity within exile.

### "He sent Judah ahead to direct the way to Goshen" (46:28)

Rashi, citing Bereshit Rabbah 95:3, translates *l'horot* not as "to direct" but "to teach" — Judah was sent ahead to establish a house of study (*beit midrash*) so that Torah instruction would be available immediately upon arrival. This extraordinary interpretation — prioritizing Torah study even before physical settlement — encapsulates the rabbinic conviction that Torah is the spiritual oxygen of Jewish existence. Without Torah, mere physical survival in exile is meaningless.

### The Reunion (46:29-30)

"Joseph harnessed his chariot and went up to meet Israel his father in Goshen. He appeared before him, fell on his neck, and wept on his neck for a long time" (46:29). Rashi notes that *Joseph* wept on *Jacob's* neck, but Jacob did not weep on Joseph's. Why? Because Jacob was reciting the *Shema* — "Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One." At the moment of his greatest human joy, Jacob channeled that emotion into divine worship, fulfilling the commandment to love God "with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your might."

This teaching became paradigmatic in Jewish spirituality: the highest human emotions are not suppressed but directed toward God. Joy, love, and relief are not diminished by being sanctified — they are amplified.

## Theological Significance

The chapter marks the formal beginning of Israel's Egyptian sojourn — the event that will define Jewish historical consciousness forever. The descent to Egypt is the archetype of all subsequent exiles (*galuyot*), and God's promises to Jacob become the template for all assurances of eventual return: "I will go down with you... I will bring you up."

The principle *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim* reaches its fullest expression: Jacob's descent foreshadows the nation's descent; his fear mirrors their fear; his reassurance prefigures the reassurances given through Moses. The Midrash notes that God's word *anokhi* ("I") — "I will go down with you" — uses the same word that opens the Ten Commandments at Sinai: *Anokhi Hashem Elohekha*. The God who descends into exile is the same God who liberates from it.

The genealogy serves as a theological bridge: it transforms a family into a nation. The seventy souls who enter Egypt are the seed from which *am Yisrael* (the people of Israel) will grow — fulfilling the covenantal promise of innumerable progeny that stretches back to Abraham.
