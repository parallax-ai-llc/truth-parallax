## Bereshit: The Creation Account

Genesis 1 opens the Torah with the word *Bereshit* ("In the beginning"), which also names the first weekly Torah portion (parashah). Jewish interpretation of this chapter is shaped by millennia of rabbinic commentary, from the Mishnah's caution about creation speculation to the intricate discussions of the medieval commentators.

Rashi famously opens his Torah commentary by asking why the Torah begins with creation at all, rather than with the first commandment given to Israel (Exodus 12:2). He answers, citing Rabbi Yitzchak, that it establishes God's sovereignty over the earth so that no nation can accuse Israel of stealing the land — "The whole earth belongs to the Holy One, blessed be He; He created it and gave it to whom He saw fit" (Rashi on Genesis 1:1).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Bereshit bara Elohim" (1:1)

The grammatical structure of the opening phrase has been debated extensively:

- **Rashi**: Reads *bereshit* as a construct state — "In the beginning of God's creating," rather than an absolute statement. This follows the interpretation found in Genesis Rabbah 1:1.
- **Ibn Ezra**: Defends the absolute reading — "In the beginning, God created" — and critiques those who render it otherwise on grammatical grounds.
- **Ramban (Nachmanides)**: Sees the verse as describing *creatio ex nihilo* (*yesh me-ayin*), the creation of primordial matter from absolute nothingness. He connects this to kabbalistic teaching about the initial divine contraction (*tzimtzum*) that made space for creation.

### The Name "Elohim" (1:1)

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 12:15) explains why the creation account uses the divine name *Elohim* (associated with strict justice) rather than the Tetragrammaton YHVH (associated with mercy): God initially intended to create the world with strict justice alone, but saw it could not endure and therefore partnered mercy with justice — which is why Genesis 2:4 introduces YHVH alongside Elohim.

### "Let us make man" (1:26)

This plural phrasing is a classic point of Jewish-Christian divergence. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 38b) records that the rabbis were well aware heretics (*minim*) used this verse to argue for plurality in the godhead. Rashi explains that God consulted the angels as a matter of humility and courtesy, even though this risked misinterpretation, to teach that the greater should consult the lesser.

| Commentator | Interpretation of "Let us make" |
|---|---|
| Rashi | God consulted the ministering angels out of humility |
| Ibn Ezra | Plural of majesty, or God addressing the earth |
| Ramban | God spoke to the earth to contribute the body while He contributed the soul |
| Midrash Rabbah | God consulted the Torah as a blueprint |

### *Tzelem Elohim* — The Image of God (1:27)

Jewish teaching understands the *tzelem* (image) not as a physical likeness but as the capacity for moral reasoning, free will, and relationship with God. The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 4:5) derives from the creation of a single human being the principle that whoever destroys a single life is as if they destroyed an entire world, and whoever saves a single life is as if they saved an entire world.

### The Sabbath (1:31–2:3)

The culmination of creation in Shabbat is foundational to Jewish life. The Talmud (Shabbat 119b) teaches that whoever recites the *Vayechulu* passage (Genesis 2:1-3) on Friday evening becomes a partner with God in the work of creation. Shabbat is not merely rest but the purpose toward which all creation moves.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 1 establishes core Jewish theological principles:

- **Monotheism**: One God alone creates — no competing deities, no primordial battle
- **Goodness of the material world**: Each day's work is pronounced *tov* (good), affirming the physical world's inherent value
- **Human dignity and responsibility**: Bearing the divine image confers both sanctity and obligation
- **Shabbat as cosmic principle**: Rest is woven into the fabric of reality itself
