## Migdal Bavel: The Tower and the Scattering

Genesis 11 contains two distinct sections: the Tower of Babel narrative (11:1-9) and the genealogy from Shem to Abram (11:10-32). Together they conclude parashat Noach and set the stage for the calling of Abraham in parashat Lech Lecha. The Tower story addresses the danger of collective human arrogance, while the genealogy narrows the universal scope of Genesis 1-10 toward the particular family through whom God will work.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The whole earth had one language and uniform words" (11:1)

Rashi explains *devarim achadim* ("uniform words") as meaning they had a unified purpose — specifically, a conspiracy against God. Genesis Rabbah (38:6) identifies this single language as Hebrew, the *leshon ha-kodesh* (holy tongue), which was the original language of creation. The multiplying of languages at Babel was thus a diminishment, fragmenting the primal unity of human speech.

The Ramban sees the "one language" as an historical statement: all of post-flood humanity spoke a common tongue until this divine intervention scattered them. Ibn Ezra agrees that the language was Hebrew but focuses on the sociological implications — shared language enabled dangerous unanimity of purpose.

### "Let us build a city and a tower with its top in the heavens" (11:4)

The generation's sin is debated among the commentators:

| Commentator | Nature of the Sin |
|---|---|
| Rashi | Rebellion against God — "Let us wage war against Him" |
| Ramban | Defying God's command to disperse and fill the earth (cf. 9:1) |
| Rambam (*Guide* 3:29) | Idolatry — building a center of star worship |
| Sforno | Establishing tyrannical centralized government |
| Rabbi S.R. Hirsch | Suppressing individual freedom in favor of collectivism |

The phrase "let us make a name for ourselves" (*na'aseh lanu shem*) is contrasted by the midrash with God's later promise to Abraham, "I will make your name great" (12:2). The builders sought fame through self-aggrandizement; Abraham received it through obedience.

### "YHVH descended to see the city and the tower" (11:5)

Rashi explains that God did not literally need to descend — He is omniscient. The Torah records this to teach judges and courts that they must investigate personally before passing judgment, not relying on hearsay. This derives a halakhic principle from a narrative detail, a characteristic rabbinic interpretive move.

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 38:8) adds a devastating irony: God "descended to see" a tower that the builders boasted would reach heaven — the gap between human pretension and divine perspective could not be greater.

### "Let us descend and confuse their language" (11:7)

The plural "let us" echoes Genesis 1:26 ("let us make man"). Rashi again explains that God consulted His heavenly court out of courtesy and humility. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 109a) describes the confusion vividly: one person would ask for bricks and receive mortar; another would ask for mortar and receive bricks. The inability to communicate led to frustration and violence.

### The Genealogy of Shem to Abram (11:10-26)

The Mishnah (Avot 5:2) counts ten generations from Noah to Abraham, paralleling the ten from Adam to Noah: "To make known how great is God's patience, for all those generations continued to provoke Him until Abraham our father came and received the reward of them all."

The declining lifespans from Shem (600 years) to Nahor (148 years) reflect a gradual diminishment that the Ramban attributes to changing environmental conditions after the flood and the dispersion.

### Abram, Nahor, and Haran (11:27-32)

The chapter introduces the family of Terah, Abram's father, in Ur of the Chaldeans. The death of Haran "before his father Terah" (11:28) is elaborated by the Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 38:13) in one of its most famous stories: Terah was an idol-maker, and young Abram smashed his father's idols, claiming the largest idol had done it. When Terah protested that idols cannot act, Abram replied, "Then why do you worship them?" Haran, forced to choose sides, chose ambiguously and was killed by fire — the meaning of "Ur" (fire) of the Chaldeans.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 11 in Jewish thought establishes:

- **The danger of uniformity without God**: Unity in rebellion is worse than diversity in peace
- **Language as divine gift**: The multiplying of languages both punishes and protects humanity from collective evil
- **Abraham's emergence from idolatry**: The genealogy contextualizes Abraham's radical monotheism against a world of idol worship
- **Divine patience has limits but also purpose**: The ten generations before Abraham are not wasted but prepare the ground for covenant
