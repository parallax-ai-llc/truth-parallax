## The War of Kings and Malki-Tzedek

Genesis 14 presents Abram in an unexpected role: military commander. When a coalition of four Mesopotamian kings defeats the five kings of the Jordan plain and captures Lot, Abram musters 318 trained men, pursues the invaders, and rescues his nephew. The chapter concludes with the enigmatic encounter with Melchizedek (*Malki-Tzedek*), king of Salem, and Abram's refusal of the king of Sodom's spoils. Jewish interpretation of this chapter engages questions of duty to family, the ethics of war, and the nature of tithing.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### The Four Kings Against Five (14:1-12)

The war pits the eastern coalition led by Chedorlaomer of Elam against the rebellious kings of the Jordan plain, including the kings of Sodom and Gomorrah. Rashi identifies Amraphel, king of Shinar, with Nimrod — the same tyrant who, according to midrash, cast Abraham into a furnace in Ur of the Chaldeans (Genesis Rabbah 42:4). If this identification holds, the war becomes a clash between Abram's old adversary and the corrupt cities that sheltered his nephew.

### Abram's 318 Men (14:14)

The number 318 is given special attention. Rashi, citing the Talmud (Nedarim 32a), identifies the "318 trained men born in his house" as a single person: Eliezer, Abram's servant. The numerical value (*gematria*) of the name Eliezer (aleph-lamed-yod-ayin-zayin-resh) equals 318. This reading transforms the military victory from a conventional battle into a miraculous triumph achieved with minimal forces — emphasizing divine aid over human armies.

| Reading | Source | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Literally 318 trained men | Peshat (plain meaning) | Abram maintained a significant household militia |
| Eliezer alone (gematria = 318) | Rashi, Nedarim 32a | Miracle; divine aid |
| Abram divided forces for night attack | Rashi on 14:15, Genesis Rabbah 43:2 | Military strategy inspired by God |

### The Rescue of Lot (14:14-16)

Abram's willingness to risk his life for Lot — who had voluntarily separated and moved to Sodom — illustrates the principle of *pidyon shvuyim* (redeeming captives), which the Rambam (Hilkhot Matanot Aniyyim 8:10) calls one of the greatest commandments. The Midrash praises Abram for not holding a grudge against Lot despite their separation.

### Malki-Tzedek, King of Salem (14:18-20)

The encounter with Melchizedek is interpreted very differently in Jewish and Christian traditions. Malki-Tzedek brings out bread and wine and blesses Abram as a "priest of God Most High (*El Elyon*)."

Jewish tradition (Talmud, Nedarim 32b) identifies Malki-Tzedek with Shem, son of Noah, who was still alive according to the biblical chronology. As the eldest patriarch and a righteous survivor of the flood, Shem served as priest of the God of heaven. The name *Malki-Tzedek* ("my king is righteousness") and his title as king of *Shalem* (identified with Jerusalem) connect him to the future site of the Temple.

The Talmud (Nedarim 32b) adds that the priesthood was subsequently taken from Malki-Tzedek's line and given to Abraham's descendants because Malki-Tzedek blessed Abram before blessing God (verse 19 before verse 20) — a lapse in propriety. This is the rabbinic counter to the Christian reading (Hebrews 7) that uses Melchizedek to argue for a priesthood superseding the Levitical order.

### Abram's Refusal of Sodom's Wealth (14:21-24)

The king of Sodom offers Abram all the recovered property. Abram refuses: "I have lifted my hand to YHVH, God Most High, Possessor of heaven and earth, that I will not take a thread or a sandal strap." Rashi explains that Abram refused so that the king of Sodom could never claim, "I made Abram rich." All wealth must be attributed to God alone.

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 43:9) teaches that this act of renunciation was rewarded: the "thread" became the thread of blue (*tekhelet*) in the tzitzit; the "sandal strap" became the strap of the tefillin. By refusing earthly rewards, Abram merited the commandments — spiritual wealth that endures eternally.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 14 in Jewish thought establishes:

- **Duty to rescue**: The obligation to save captives (*pidyon shvuyim*) overrides personal grievance
- **Humility before God**: True wealth comes from divine blessing, not human patronage
- **Jerusalem's sanctity**: Malki-Tzedek's Salem prefigures the Temple city
- **Mitzvot as reward**: Spiritual commandments are the true compensation for righteous action
