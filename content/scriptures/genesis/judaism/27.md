## Summary

Genesis 27 narrates one of the Torah's most dramatic episodes: the aged and blind Isaac prepares to bless Esau, his firstborn, but Rebekah orchestrates a deception in which Jacob, disguised in Esau's garments and goatskins, receives the blessing instead. When Esau discovers the ruse, he weeps bitterly and receives a lesser blessing. Esau then resolves to kill Jacob, and Rebekah arranges for Jacob to flee to her brother Laban in Haran.

This chapter concludes the main narrative of Parashat Toldot and sets the stage for Jacob's long exile.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The voice is the voice of Jacob, but the hands are the hands of Esau" (27:22)

This verse became one of the most frequently cited in rabbinic literature. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 65:20) reads it as a permanent principle: when Jacob's voice — the voice of Torah study and prayer — is strong, Esau's hands have no power. When that voice falters, Esau's hands prevail. The Talmud (Gittin 57b) applies this to the relationship between Israel and the nations throughout history.

Rashi emphasizes the Hebrew wordplay: *ha-kol kol Ya'akov* — "the voice is the voice of Jacob." The word *kol* (voice) appears without the letter *vav*, making it read *kal* (light/diminished), hinting that Jacob spoke softly, almost giving himself away.

### Was Jacob's Deception Justified?

This is among the most debated questions in Jewish exegesis. Several approaches exist:

| Commentator | Position |
|---|---|
| Rashi | Jacob was entitled to the blessing because he had purchased the birthright (25:33) |
| Nachmanides | Rebekah acted on prophetic authority — "the elder shall serve the younger" (25:23) |
| Sforno | Jacob acted reluctantly and only at his mother's insistence |
| Radak | The deception was necessary because Isaac was mistaken about Esau's character |
| Malbim | Jacob spoke in technically true double-meanings (*I am Esau your firstborn* = "I am [Jacob]; Esau is your firstborn") |

The Malbim's linguistic reading is particularly ingenious. When Jacob says *anokhi Esav bekhorekha* (27:19), the clause can be parsed as: "I am [who I am]; Esau is your firstborn" — technically avoiding an outright lie. This reflects the rabbinic sensitivity to the prohibition against falsehood (*midvar sheker tirchak*, Exodus 23:7) even in extreme circumstances.

### Isaac's Trembling (27:33)

"Isaac trembled exceedingly" (*vayecherad Yitzchak charadah gedolah ad me'od*). Rashi explains Isaac saw Gehinnom (hell) open beneath Esau. The Zohar teaches that at this moment Isaac recognized, through prophetic clarity, that the divine plan had always intended the blessing for Jacob. His trembling was not anger at being deceived but shock at having nearly thwarted God's will.

### Esau's Blessing (27:39-40)

Esau receives a blessing "of the sword" — by military power he will live, and he will serve his brother, but will eventually "break his yoke." The Targum Onkelos renders this conditional: when Israel sins, Esau will dominate; when Israel is faithful, Esau will serve. This conditional reading became foundational for the rabbinic understanding of Jewish-Roman (and later Jewish-Christian) relations, since Edom/Esau was identified with Rome in Talmudic literature.

## Theological Significance

The chapter raises profound questions about divine providence and human agency. Jewish theology does not resolve the tension neatly. The rabbis affirmed both that Rebekah and Jacob acted rightly *in this specific situation* and that deception is generally forbidden. The Talmud's principle that one may not do evil that good may come (*ein omrim la-adam chatei k'dei she-yizkeh chaverkha*) stands in tension with the narrative — a tension the tradition preserves rather than eliminates.

The concept of *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim* applies powerfully here: Jacob's deception before Isaac foreshadows his own deception by Laban (Chapter 29) and later by his sons with Joseph's bloodied coat (Chapter 37). The Midrash explicitly links these events as measure-for-measure (*middah k'neged middah*): the one who deceived through goatskins is deceived through a goat's blood.

Isaac's blessings also establish a typology of two modes of existence — the spiritual (Jacob/Israel) and the temporal-military (Esau/Edom) — that runs through all of Jewish historical consciousness.
