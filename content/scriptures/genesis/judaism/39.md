## Summary

Genesis 39 follows Joseph's rise and fall in Potiphar's household. Purchased by the Egyptian officer, Joseph prospers in everything he does, and Potiphar entrusts him with full authority over his estate. Potiphar's wife repeatedly attempts to seduce Joseph, but he refuses on both moral and theological grounds. When she seizes his garment and he flees, she accuses him of attempted assault. Potiphar imprisons Joseph, but even in prison God is with him, and he gains the warden's trust.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The Lord was with Joseph" (39:2, 21)

This phrase appears twice — once in Potiphar's house, once in prison — forming an inclusio that brackets the entire chapter. Rashi explains that Joseph constantly had God's name on his lips; even as a slave, he prayed and attributed his success to divine providence. The Midrash (Bereshit Rabbah 86:6) adds that Joseph's appearance (*yefeh to'ar vi-yefeh mar'eh*, "handsome in form and appearance," 39:6) was a deliberate echo of his mother Rachel (29:17), signaling that his beauty was a test, not merely a trait.

### Joseph's Refusal (39:8-9)

Joseph's speech of refusal is the Torah's most explicit statement of sexual ethics rooted in theological reasoning:

"How then could I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (39:9)

The Talmud (Sotah 36b) elaborates on the moment of temptation: when Joseph was on the verge of yielding, he saw the image of his father Jacob's face (*d'mut d'yukno shel aviv*) in the window, and this vision gave him strength to resist. The mystical interpretation is that the *tzelem Elohim* (divine image) transmitted through the patriarchal line intervened at the critical moment.

| Element of Joseph's Refusal | Moral Principle |
|---|---|
| "My master has withheld nothing from me except you" (39:9) | Betrayal of trust compounds the sin |
| "You are his wife" (39:9) | Respect for the marital bond — a Noahide obligation |
| "Sin against God" (39:9) | Sexual transgression is ultimately a sin against the divine, not merely against another person |

Nachmanides notes that Joseph's reasoning operates on multiple levels: legal (adultery violates Noahide law), interpersonal (breach of trust), and theological (offense against God). This layered ethical reasoning became a model for Jewish moral analysis.

### "She caught him by his garment" (39:12)

Joseph's garment is seized — for the second time in the narrative, a garment is used against him (the first being the bloodied coat). The Midrash sees a pattern: Joseph's garments are repeatedly instruments of his downfall but ultimately of his elevation (the royal robes of Chapter 41). Clothing in the Joseph narrative functions as a symbol of identity imposed by others, which Joseph must transcend.

Rashi, citing Sotah 36b, records the tradition that Joseph entered the house "to do his work" — the Talmud debates whether this means literal work or whether he had briefly considered yielding to temptation. Either way, he "fled and went outside" — the Torah's recommended response to sexual temptation is not debate but flight.

### Joseph as *Yosef Ha-Tzaddik* (39:7-12)

This chapter is the primary basis for Joseph's title *Ha-Tzaddik* — "the Righteous One." The Zohar explains that Joseph's resistance to sexual temptation (*shmirat ha-brit*, guarding the covenant) is the defining act of the *tzaddik*. In Kabbalistic thought, Joseph corresponds to the sefirah of *Yesod* (foundation), which channels divine blessing into the world through the proper use of generative power. Sexual purity is thus not merely moral restraint but a cosmic function.

## Theological Significance

The chapter establishes a theology of divine presence in adversity. God is "with Joseph" not by preventing suffering but by ensuring that suffering becomes productive. Joseph prospers in Potiphar's house, then prospers in prison — the location changes, but God's presence is constant. This understanding profoundly influenced Jewish thought about exile: God accompanies Israel into exile (*galut ha-Shekhinah*), and suffering does not indicate divine abandonment.

Joseph's resistance to temptation became the paradigmatic example of self-mastery (*kibbush ha-yetzer*) in rabbinic ethics. The Talmud (Yoma 35b) lists Joseph among those figures who will serve as testimony against sinners at the final judgment: "If Joseph, a young man, alone, in a foreign land, a slave with no social standing, could resist — what is your excuse?"

The false accusation and imprisonment also establish a pattern that resonates through Jewish experience: the righteous suffer unjustly, but God ultimately vindicates them. The Midrash notes that Joseph's imprisonment lasted twelve years — a measure-for-measure consequence for slandering his brothers in Chapter 37, one year for each brother he maligned.
