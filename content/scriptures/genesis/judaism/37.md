## Summary

Genesis 37 opens Parashat Vayeshev (37:1-40:23) and begins the Joseph cycle, the Torah's longest continuous narrative. Jacob favors Joseph, the son of his beloved Rachel, and gives him a special garment (*ketonet passim*). Joseph reports two dreams of dominion — sheaves bowing and celestial bodies prostrating — which intensify his brothers' hatred. When Joseph is sent to check on his brothers in the field, they conspire to kill him. Reuben intervenes, and Joseph is thrown into a pit. Judah then suggests selling him to Ishmaelite/Midianite merchants, who bring Joseph to Egypt. The brothers dip the coat in goat's blood and present it to Jacob, who mourns inconsolably. The chapter closes with Joseph sold to Potiphar in Egypt.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Jacob dwelt" — *Vayeshev Ya'akov* (37:1)

Rashi's celebrated comment: "Jacob sought to dwell in tranquility (*shalvah*), and the trouble of Joseph sprang upon him. The righteous seek to dwell in peace, but the Holy One says: 'Is it not enough for the righteous that which awaits them in the world to come, that they also seek tranquility in this world?'" This teaching (Bereshit Rabbah 84:3) establishes a foundational principle: the righteous are tested precisely at the moment they believe their trials are over.

### The Coat — *Ketonet Passim* (37:3)

The meaning of *passim* is debated:

| Interpretation | Source |
|---|---|
| A coat of many colors | Targum, based on *pas* = stripe/segment |
| A long-sleeved or full-length garment | Rashi, Ibn Ezra — a sign of aristocratic status |
| A fine wool tunic | Modern scholarship, based on cognate languages |
| A coat reaching the palms (*pas* = palm) and soles | Bereshit Rabbah 84:8 |

The Talmud (Shabbat 10b) derives a practical lesson: "A person should never single out one child from among the others, for on account of two *sela'im* worth of fine wool that Jacob gave Joseph beyond his brothers, they became jealous, and the matter unfolded until our ancestors descended to Egypt." Parental favoritism has national consequences.

### Joseph's Dreams (37:5-11)

The Midrash debates whether Joseph was right to share his dreams. Nachmanides argues the dreams were prophetic and Joseph was obligated to report them. Rashi suggests Joseph spoke from youthful naivety. The Zohar teaches that true prophetic dreams must be told — an untold dream cannot be fulfilled, just as an uninterpreted dream is like an unread letter.

Jacob's response is notable: he "rebuked" Joseph publicly but "kept the matter in mind" (37:11) — he recognized the prophetic potential but did not want to further inflame the brothers.

### The Pit — "The pit was empty; there was no water in it" (37:24)

Rashi, citing the Talmud (Shabbat 22a), offers a chilling comment: "There was no water in it — but there *were* snakes and scorpions in it." The seemingly redundant phrase "the pit was empty, there was no water" implies the pit was not truly empty. This becomes a model of midrashic reading: every word in the Torah carries meaning, and apparent redundancy signals hidden content.

### The Goat's Blood (37:31-33)

Jacob, who deceived his father Isaac with goatskins, is now deceived by goat's blood. The *middah k'neged middah* (measure for measure) principle operates across decades. The Midrash explicitly connects the two goats: "The voice that said *haker na* ('please identify') to Isaac now hears *haker na* from his own sons." The identical phrase binds the two deceptions inextricably.

### "He refused to be comforted" (37:35)

Rashi explains: one cannot be comforted for a person who is actually alive, because the divine decree of forgetfulness that accompanies death does not apply to the living. Jacob's inability to find comfort is itself an unconscious recognition that Joseph still lives. This remarkable psychological-theological insight suggests that grief that cannot resolve points to an unfinished story.

## Theological Significance

Joseph is the *tzaddik* (righteous one) par excellence in Jewish tradition. His narrative is the Torah's fullest exploration of how divine providence operates through human evil. The brothers' sin — born of jealousy and hatred (*sinat chinam*) — becomes the mechanism of God's plan to save the family from famine. This paradox does not excuse the sin; it reveals the depth of divine wisdom.

The Joseph story also inaugurates the theme of exile as purposeful suffering. The descent to Egypt begins not with a divine command but with human sin — yet God weaves it into redemptive history. The principle *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim* applies to the national level: just as Joseph descends to Egypt through betrayal and rises to save his family, so Israel will descend through enslavement and rise through exodus.
