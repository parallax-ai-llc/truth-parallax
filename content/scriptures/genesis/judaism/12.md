## Lech Lecha: The Calling of Abraham

Genesis 12 opens parashat Lech Lecha, one of the most consequential chapters in the Torah. With God's command to Abram — "Go forth from your land, your birthplace, and your father's house" — the narrative pivots from universal history to the particular story of one family through whom all nations will be blessed. This moment is the foundation of Jewish identity, and the rabbis treat it as the beginning of the covenantal relationship between God and Israel.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Lech lecha — Go forth" (12:1)

The doubled expression *lech lecha* (literally "go to yourself" or "go for yourself") is richly interpreted. Rashi explains: "Go for your own benefit and for your own good." The journey is not merely geographic but spiritual — Abram must leave behind not only his physical home but the idolatrous culture that shaped him.

The Zohar reads *lech lecha* mystically: "Go to yourself" — discover your true essence, your soul's root. This interpretation has been central to Hasidic teaching, where the phrase becomes a paradigm for every person's spiritual journey.

The Mishnah (Avot 5:3) lists this as the first of Abraham's ten trials (*nisyonot*), though the Rambam and other authorities disagree on the precise enumeration. Leaving one's homeland, family, and culture was itself an act of extraordinary faith — Abram had no map, no destination specified, only "the land that I will show you."

### The Five-Fold Blessing (12:2-3)

God promises Abram:

| Promise | Rabbinic Interpretation |
|---|---|
| "I will make you a great nation" | Refers to the formula "God of Abraham" in prayer (Rashi) |
| "I will bless you" | Material prosperity — wealth (Rashi) |
| "I will make your name great" | Refers to "God of Isaac" in prayer (Rashi) |
| "You shall be a blessing" | The blessings conclude with Abraham — "God of Jacob" and "Shield of Abraham" (Rashi) |
| "All the families of the earth shall be blessed through you" | Universal blessing mediated through Abraham's descendants |

The Ramban explains that these blessings were necessary because travel in the ancient world was dangerous, reducing a person's wealth, reputation, and fertility. God's promises directly counter these three risks.

### Abram's Journey (12:4-9)

Abram departs at age 75, taking Sarai, Lot, and "the souls they had made in Haran" (12:5). Rashi, following the Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 39:14), explains "the souls they had made" as converts — people Abram and Sarai had brought under the wings of the Shekhinah. Abram converted the men, Sarai the women. This establishes the paradigm of Abraham and Sarah as the first practitioners of outreach and hospitality.

Upon arriving in Canaan, Abram builds altars at Shechem and between Bethel and Ai, "calling on the name of YHVH" (12:8). Rashi explains this means he proclaimed God's name publicly — not merely personal prayer but public teaching of monotheism.

### The Descent to Egypt (12:10-20)

Famine drives Abram to Egypt, where he asks Sarai to identify herself as his sister, fearing the Egyptians will kill him to take her. This episode troubles commentators:

- **Ramban**: Criticizes Abram sharply — "Know that Abraham our father sinned a great sin unintentionally, for he brought his righteous wife into a stumbling block of sin." The Ramban sees this as a moral failure with consequences: the Egyptian exile was partly a result of this misstep.
- **Rashi**: Takes a more sympathetic view, noting that Sarai was indeed Abram's half-sister (or niece, following the identification of Sarai with Iscah in 11:29), so the statement was technically true.
- **Radak**: Explains Abram's reasoning pragmatically — better a living husband who might be set aside than a dead one who cannot protect his wife at all.

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 40:5) draws a typological parallel: Abram's descent to Egypt, the plague upon Pharaoh, and his departure with wealth foreshadow Israel's later experience — descent, plagues, and exodus with Egyptian wealth.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 12 in Jewish thought establishes:

- **Election as vocation**: Abraham is chosen not for privilege but for mission — to be a blessing to all families of the earth
- **Faith as action**: *Lech lecha* demands movement, not merely belief. Abraham's faith is expressed through leaving, building, and proclaiming
- **The Land of Israel**: The first divine promise of the land to Abraham's descendants grounds the Jewish relationship to Eretz Yisrael
- **Imperfect patriarchs**: The Ramban's willingness to critique Abraham demonstrates that Jewish tradition does not idealize its heroes but learns from their moral complexity
