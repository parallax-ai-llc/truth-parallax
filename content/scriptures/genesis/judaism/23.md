## Chayei Sarah: The Life of Sarah

Genesis 23 opens parashat Chayei Sarah — paradoxically named "Life of Sarah" though it begins with her death. The chapter records Sarah's death at age 127, Abraham's mourning, and his purchase of the Cave of Machpelah in Hebron as a burial site. Jewish tradition reads this chapter as foundational for land law, burial customs, and the enduring Jewish connection to the Land of Israel.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "The life of Sarah was one hundred years and twenty years and seven years" (23:1)

The unusual phrasing — breaking the age into three segments — invites interpretation. Rashi explains: at 100 she was like 20 in beauty (free of sin, since a person under 20 is not subject to heavenly punishment); at 20 she was like 7 in beauty. The repetition of "years" (*shanah*) after each number teaches that "all of them were equal for good."

The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 58:1) connects Sarah's death to the Akedah: when Satan told Sarah that Abraham had nearly sacrificed Isaac, her soul departed from the shock. This juxtaposition — the Akedah immediately followed by Sarah's death — implies a tragic consequence of the binding. Some commentators see Abraham's test as extracting a cost not only from him but from his family.

### Abraham's Mourning (23:2)

"Abraham came to eulogize Sarah and to weep for her." Rashi notes the small letter *kaf* in the Hebrew word *ve-livkotah* ("and to weep for her"), suggesting that Abraham's weeping was restrained. The Ramban explains that Abraham's mourning was genuine but measured because he had faith in the World to Come — grief tempered by theological confidence.

The sequence — eulogy (*hesped*) before weeping (*bekhiyah*) — establishes the Jewish mourning order. The eulogy speaks of the deceased's virtues publicly; the weeping is personal and follows.

### The Negotiation for Machpelah (23:3-18)

Abraham's purchase of the Cave of Machpelah from Ephron the Hittite is recorded with extraordinary legal precision — the most detailed commercial transaction in the Torah. The rabbis see this meticulous documentation as establishing permanent Jewish ownership of the site.

| Stage | Verse | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Abraham requests burial rights | 23:4 | Identifies himself as "a stranger and a sojourner" — humble negotiation |
| Hittites offer freely | 23:5-6 | Public offer of generosity |
| Abraham insists on purchase | 23:9 | Full price (*kesef male*) — no gift that could be reclaimed |
| Ephron offers the entire field | 23:11 | Appears generous but inflates the price |
| Abraham pays 400 silver shekels | 23:16 | "Current among merchants" — top commercial rate |
| Legal transfer recorded | 23:17-18 | Field, cave, trees, borders — all specified before witnesses |

Rashi, drawing on the Midrash, contrasts Ephron's character with Abraham's: Ephron "said much and did little" — he offered the field for free but ultimately charged an exorbitant price. Abraham "said little and did much" — he offered a morsel of bread to the angels but served a feast (chapter 18).

The Ramban explains that Abraham insisted on purchasing rather than accepting a gift to ensure that no future claim could be made against the burial site. The legal language (*kinyan*, acquisition) ensures permanence.

### The Cave of Machpelah (23:19)

The name *Machpelah* means "doubled" or "folded." Rashi offers two explanations: the cave had an upper and lower chamber (doubled structure), or it contained couples (*zugot*) — Adam and Eve, Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebekah, Jacob and Leah. The Zohar adds a mystical dimension: the cave is the entrance to the Garden of Eden, and the patriarchs and matriarchs rest at the threshold between this world and the next.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 23 in Jewish thought establishes:

- **Dignity in death**: The elaborate mourning and burial customs reflect the sanctity of the human body, created in God's image
- **Legal acquisition of the Land**: Abraham's insistence on a legitimate purchase — not a gift — establishes the principle that Jewish connection to the land is both divinely promised and legally acquired
- **Hebron's sanctity**: Machpelah becomes one of the four holy cities of the Land of Israel, the burial place of the patriarchs and matriarchs
- **Integrity in commerce**: The transaction models transparent, witnessed, and fair dealing
