## Finding Rivkah: The First Shidduch

Genesis 24 is the longest chapter in Genesis, narrating Abraham's commission to his servant to find a wife for Isaac from among his kinsmen in Mesopotamia. The servant's journey, his prayer at the well, his encounter with Rebekah (*Rivkah*), the negotiation with her family, and her departure to marry Isaac constitute a richly detailed story that the rabbis regard as a paradigm of divine providence (*hashgachah pratit*) and the matchmaking process (*shidduch*).

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Abraham was old, advanced in days, and YHVH had blessed Abraham with everything" (24:1)

Rashi notes *ba-kol* ("with everything"): the numerical value (*gematria*) of *ba-kol* (52) equals that of *ben* (son) — Abraham's ultimate blessing was his son Isaac. Others in the Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 59:7) interpret *ba-kol* as meaning Abraham had a daughter named *Ba-kol*, or that he lacked nothing materially.

The chapter's opening also establishes Abraham's urgency: at advanced age, he must secure the covenantal future by finding Isaac a suitable wife.

### The Servant's Oath (24:2-9)

Abraham makes his servant swear not to take a wife for Isaac from among the Canaanites. Rashi identifies the servant as Eliezer (though the chapter never names him), noting a tension: Eliezer himself had a daughter he wished to marry to Isaac. Abraham replies, "My son is blessed; you are cursed" — referring to Eliezer's descent from Canaan, who was cursed by Noah. The accursed cannot join with the blessed.

Abraham also insists that Isaac must not return to Mesopotamia: "Beware that you do not take my son back there" (24:6). Rashi explains that the Land of Israel had been promised to Abraham's descendants, and Isaac — who had been consecrated as an offering on Mount Moriah — could not leave the holy land. Isaac is the only patriarch who never leaves Eretz Yisrael.

### The Prayer at the Well (24:12-14)

The servant's prayer is a model of practical faith. Rather than asking for a miraculous sign, he requests that the woman who offers water to him and his camels be the one designated for Isaac. The Ramban explains that this was not divination (*nichush*, which is forbidden) but a prayer for guidance based on character: the woman who would offer water to a stranger's camels — an extraordinary act of generosity — would demonstrate the *chesed* (lovingkindness) essential for the matriarch of Israel.

| Quality Tested | Action | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Initiative | Offers water unprompted | Leadership and awareness |
| Generosity | Extends offer to camels — enormous labor (ten camels drink vast quantities) | Chesed beyond obligation |
| Energy | "She hurried" and "she ran" — repeated verbs | Eagerness in kindness (*zerizut*) |
| Modesty | Offers hospitality without impropriety | Moral refinement |

### Rebekah at the Well (24:15-21)

The servant watches in silence as Rebekah performs exactly what he had prayed for. Rashi emphasizes the miraculous timing: "Before he had finished speaking" (24:15) — she appeared before the prayer was complete. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 60:5) notes that the water rose to meet her, just as it had risen for Rachel at the well — a sign of divine favor.

### The Negotiation with Bethuel and Laban (24:29-60)

Laban's eagerness to welcome the servant is attributed by Rashi to his noticing the gold jewelry on Rebekah: "When he saw the ring and the bracelets on his sister's hands" (24:30) — Laban was motivated by greed, not hospitality. This characterization of Laban as mercenary foreshadows his later dealings with Jacob in Genesis 29-31.

The servant recounts the entire story to Rebekah's family, and the Torah repeats the narrative almost verbatim. The Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 60:8) explains this apparent redundancy: "The ordinary conversation of the patriarchs' servants is more beloved before God than the Torah of their descendants" — even the repeated details of this story contain more spiritual substance than one might expect.

### "Will you go with this man?" (24:58)

Rebekah's family asks her directly, and she replies, "I will go" (*elekh*). Rashi derives from this the halakhic principle that a woman cannot be married without her consent — her verbal agreement is required. This becomes a cornerstone of Jewish marriage law (Kiddushin 2a-b).

### The Meeting of Isaac and Rebekah (24:63-67)

Isaac was "going out to meditate (*la-su'ach*) in the field toward evening" when he sees the approaching caravan. Rashi identifies *la-su'ach* with *tefillah* (prayer) — the Talmud (Berakhot 26b) attributes the institution of the afternoon prayer (*Minchah*) to Isaac based on this verse.

Rebekah sees Isaac and "falls from the camel" — Rashi explains she was overwhelmed by his majestic appearance, the radiance of a man sanctified by the Akedah.

## Theological Significance

Genesis 24 teaches:

- **Providence operates through human action**: The servant prays, but he also devises a practical test. Faith and effort work together.
- **Chesed as the supreme character trait**: The defining quality of a matriarch of Israel is lovingkindness
- **Consent in marriage**: Rebekah's "I will go" establishes women's agency in the marriage bond
- **The sanctity of narrative**: Even "ordinary" conversation, when rooted in devotion, is Torah
