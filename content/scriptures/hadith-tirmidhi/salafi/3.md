Salafi scholars engage with at-Tirmidhi's sections on commercial transactions and judicial proceedings as essential sources for establishing the prophetic norms of economic and legal life. The emphasis falls on deriving clear rulings from authenticated hadiths: the prohibition of usury in all its forms, the requirement of transparent dealings, and the impermissibility of transactions involving excessive uncertainty (*gharar*) are treated as binding prophetic legislation.

The judgment traditions are applied to contemporary questions of Islamic governance and legal procedure. Salafi scholars use at-Tirmidhi's narrations to argue that legitimate judgment must be based on divine law rather than human legal systems, and that the qualifications for judges include knowledge of the Quran and Sunnah as primary requirements. At-Tirmidhi's own commentary on judicial differences among early scholars is read as evidence that even where opinions vary, the methodological commitment to evidence-based ruling from prophetic sources remained constant in the earliest period of Islam.
