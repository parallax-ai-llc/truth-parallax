Salafi scholars approach at-Tirmidhi's opening section on faith and purification with particular attention to the creedal implications of the traditions recorded. The hadiths defining faith and its branches are used to establish the Salafi understanding of *iman* as comprising belief, speech, and action — a position that Salafi scholars trace to the earliest Muslim community and defend against later theological formulations that they consider innovations.

The purification traditions are engaged with focus on the strongest narrations — those at-Tirmidhi grades as *sahih* — as the basis for correct practice. Salafi scholars cross-reference at-Tirmidhi's gradings with the evaluations of later hadith critics, particularly Shaykh al-Albani, whose comprehensive re-assessment of at-Tirmidhi's collection has become a standard reference in Salafi circles. Where at-Tirmidhi's grading differs from al-Albani's, Salafi scholars generally follow the later scholar's more rigorous assessment, reflecting the Salafi commitment to ever-stricter standards of authentication in matters of worship.
