Academic study of at-Tirmidhi's prayer and fasting sections yields insights into the standardization of Islamic ritual practice in the third Islamic century. The variant narrations preserved in these chapters reveal that prayer practices were not yet fully uniform across the Muslim world, and at-Tirmidhi's recording of these differences, along with his notes on which scholars favored which position, provides a valuable snapshot of legal debate in real time.

Scholars also examine these sections for evidence of the relationship between at-Tirmidhi's collection and earlier compilations. Parallel traditions found in al-Bukhari and Muslim can be compared in their chains of transmission and textual variants, illuminating the processes of hadith transmission and editorial selection. The fasting chapters are of particular interest for studying how seasonal and calendrical practices were codified through hadith literature, with academic researchers tracing how specific fasting traditions were received, transmitted, and applied across different regions of the early Islamic world.
