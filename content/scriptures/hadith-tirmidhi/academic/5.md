Academic scholarship examines at-Tirmidhi's tafsir section as an important witness to the early development of hadith-based Quranic commentary. Unlike standalone tafsir works that proceed verse by verse through the Quran, at-Tirmidhi's compilation selects traditions linked to specific verses and subjects them to the same grading methodology applied throughout his collection. This approach reveals how the disciplines of hadith criticism and Quranic exegesis intersected in ninth-century Islamic scholarship.

The supplication chapters are studied for their contribution to the history of Islamic devotional practice and liturgical language. Scholars analyze the formulaic structure of prophetic prayers, their thematic range, and their relationship to Quranic language, examining how these texts were compiled, transmitted, and incorporated into the daily devotional routines of Muslim communities. The material also provides evidence for the study of early Arabic prose style, the theology embedded in devotional language, and the processes by which specific prayers became canonical parts of Islamic worship.
