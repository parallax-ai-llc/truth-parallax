Academic scholarship examines at-Tirmidhi's sections on trade and judgment as sources for understanding the development of Islamic commercial and procedural law. The hadiths on prohibited transactions, contract forms, and judicial qualifications reveal the legal concerns of the early Muslim community and the prophetic precedents invoked to address them. Scholars analyze how these traditions were mobilized in the formation of the various schools of Islamic jurisprudence.

At-Tirmidhi's practice of noting jurisprudential opinions after individual hadiths is of particular academic interest, as it provides evidence of the state of legal debate in the late third Islamic century. Researchers examine which scholars and schools he cites, how he characterizes their positions, and whether his presentation suggests a preference for any particular legal methodology. These editorial interventions transform the collection from a simple hadith repository into a document of comparative jurisprudence, offering modern historians a window into the dynamic interplay between hadith transmission and legal reasoning.
