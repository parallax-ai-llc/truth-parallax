Academic scholars study at-Tirmidhi's virtues and manners sections as valuable sources for the social history of the early Muslim community and the cultural construction of prophetic exemplarity. The traditions describing the Prophet's appearance, habits, and character traits are examined not only for their historical content but also for what they reveal about the values and ideals of the communities that transmitted them.

The *fada'il* (virtues) genre, well represented in at-Tirmidhi, has attracted considerable academic attention as a site of ideological contestation. Scholars analyze how traditions praising specific companions reflect the political and sectarian dynamics of early Islam, with different groups promoting hadiths that elevate their preferred figures. At-Tirmidhi's decisions about which virtues traditions to include and how to grade them provide evidence of his own positioning within these debates. The manners sections, meanwhile, offer rich material for the study of early Islamic social norms, etiquette, and material culture.
