A comparative analysis of at-Tirmidhi's virtues and manners sections reveals the universal phenomenon of the exemplary founder figure whose character traits serve as models for followers. The detailed prophetic portrait in at-Tirmidhi parallels the descriptions of the Buddha in the *Lalitavistara*, the depictions of Jesus in the Gospels, the characterization of Confucius in the *Analects*, and the accounts of Guru Nanak in the *Janam Sakhis*. Each tradition constructs a comprehensive portrait of its founder's conduct as normative for the community.

The manners traditions recorded by at-Tirmidhi address topics — hospitality, table etiquette, dress, speech, and social interaction — that find parallels in virtually every religious tradition's ethical literature. The emphasis on greeting others with peace echoes the Hebrew *shalom* greeting and the Buddhist *metta* (loving-kindness) practice. The prophetic guidance on generous hospitality parallels the Abrahamic hospitality traditions in Judaism and Christianity. These shared ethical concerns, expressed through tradition-specific forms, illustrate how religious communities translate universal moral intuitions into concrete behavioral norms.
