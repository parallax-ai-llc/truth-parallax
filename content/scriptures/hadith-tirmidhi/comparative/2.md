A comparative reading of at-Tirmidhi's prayer and fasting sections situates Islamic devotional practice within the broader spectrum of structured prayer and ascetic discipline found across world religions. The five daily prayers, with their prescribed times and physical postures, can be compared with the Liturgy of the Hours in Catholic and Orthodox Christianity, the three daily prayer services in Judaism, and the regular meditation periods observed in Buddhist monastic life.

Fasting traditions in at-Tirmidhi parallel ascetic disciplines in other traditions: the Ramadan fast resonates with Christian Lent, the Jewish fasts of Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av, the Hindu *vrata* practices, and the Buddhist *uposatha* observances. What is distinctive about at-Tirmidhi's presentation is his systematic grading of each fasting tradition by reliability, a methodological feature that finds its closest parallel in the Rabbinic practice of distinguishing between universally accepted and disputed practices. These cross-traditional parallels illuminate both shared patterns and distinctive features of Islamic devotional discipline.
