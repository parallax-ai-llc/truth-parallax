A comparative reading of at-Tirmidhi's tafsir and supplication chapters situates them within the broader phenomenon of authoritative scriptural commentary and liturgical prayer across religious traditions. The concept of prophetic commentary on revealed scripture parallels the role of oral Torah in Judaism (where the written Torah is understood through rabbinic explanation), the function of patristic exegesis in Christianity, and the role of Bhashya commentaries on the Vedas in Hindu tradition. Each tradition develops mechanisms for authoritative interpretation that complement its primary scripture.

The supplication genre represented in at-Tirmidhi finds parallels in the Jewish *siddur* (prayer book), the Christian *Book of Common Prayer*, the Buddhist *paritta* (protective chants), and the Hindu *stotras* (hymns of praise). What distinguishes the Islamic approach is the direct attribution of these prayers to the Prophet himself, grounded in authenticated chains of transmission. This combination of devotional content and critical authentication represents a distinctive contribution to the comparative study of liturgical texts, illustrating how a tradition can simultaneously cultivate heartfelt prayer and rigorous scholarly methodology.
