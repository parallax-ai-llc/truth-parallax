A comparative reading of at-Tirmidhi's opening section on faith and purification reveals structural parallels with the introductory frameworks of other religious traditions. The definition of faith through its component articles resembles the creedal formulations found in Christian catechisms, the Thirteen Principles of Maimonides in Judaism, and the foundational doctrines articulated in Buddhist abhidharma literature. Each tradition faces the challenge of distilling complex theological systems into accessible summaries for practitioners.

The purification traditions invite cross-traditional comparison with ritual purity systems in other religions. The Islamic emphasis on ablution before prayer parallels Jewish *netilat yadayim* (ritual handwashing), Hindu *snanam* (ritual bathing), and Shinto *misogi* (purification under water). Within Islam itself, at-Tirmidhi's recording of divergent purification practices among the legal schools mirrors the pluralism found within other religious traditions — such as the variation between Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jewish practice, or the liturgical differences among Eastern and Western Christian traditions.
