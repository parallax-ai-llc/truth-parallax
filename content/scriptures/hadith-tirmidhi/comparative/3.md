A comparative reading of at-Tirmidhi's sections on trade and judgment reveals shared concerns with economic ethics and judicial fairness across religious traditions. The prohibition of usury in Islamic law, documented through at-Tirmidhi's hadiths, parallels similar prohibitions in the Hebrew Bible, medieval Christian canon law, and certain Hindu legal texts. Each tradition grapples with the tension between commercial activity and the exploitation of need, arriving at similar prohibitions through distinct theological reasoning.

The judicial traditions recorded by at-Tirmidhi invite comparison with legal frameworks in other religious systems. The emphasis on the qualifications of judges — knowledge, impartiality, and piety — resonates with the Rabbinic requirements for members of the Sanhedrin, the canonical qualifications for ecclesiastical judges in Christian law, and the Confucian ideal of the virtuous magistrate. At-Tirmidhi's recording of multiple jurisprudential opinions on a single issue mirrors the Talmudic practice of preserving minority opinions alongside majority rulings, suggesting a shared recognition that legal pluralism within a tradition can be a source of strength.
