The virtues and manners sections represent the heart of Sufi engagement with at-Tirmidhi's collection. These chapters describe the Prophet's character in vivid detail — his humility, compassion, generosity, patience, and constant remembrance of God — providing the model that every Sufi seeker aspires to embody. At-Tirmidhi's related masterpiece, *al-Shama'il al-Muhammadiyyah*, expands on these themes, and together these works constitute one of the most important textual foundations of Sufi devotion to the Prophet.

Sufi tradition understands prophetic virtues not merely as moral qualities to be imitated externally but as spiritual states to be realized inwardly through divine grace and sustained practice. When at-Tirmidhi records that the Prophet was the most generous of people, Sufis read this as indicating a station of absolute detachment from the world. When he reports the Prophet's constant remembrance of God, they understand this as describing the state of perpetual awareness (*muraqabah*) that is the goal of Sufi practice. These chapters thus serve simultaneously as historical reports and spiritual aspirations.
