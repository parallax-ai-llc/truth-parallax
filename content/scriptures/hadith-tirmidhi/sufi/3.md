Sufi engagement with at-Tirmidhi's sections on trade and judgment emphasizes the spiritual dimensions of worldly transactions. While Sufi masters do not reject commercial activity, they insist that all dealings must be conducted with awareness of God's presence and accountability before the divine tribunal. The hadiths prohibiting dishonesty and exploitation are read not merely as legal rules but as descriptions of spiritual diseases — greed, deception, and injustice — that corrupt the heart.

The judgment traditions resonate with the Sufi concept of *muhasabah* — the practice of holding oneself to account before God's final accounting. At-Tirmidhi's hadiths on the qualities of a just judge are understood as describing the spiritual discernment that comes from inner purification: only one whose heart is free from selfish desire can judge truly. Sufi commentators draw connections between worldly justice and the divine justice that governs the spiritual realm, seeing in these traditions a call to integrity in all dimensions of life — outer and inner, material and spiritual.
