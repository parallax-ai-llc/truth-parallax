Sufi interpreters approach at-Tirmidhi's opening section on faith and purification as an invitation to explore the inner dimensions of these foundational categories. Faith is understood not merely as intellectual assent to a set of doctrines but as a living, dynamic relationship with God that grows and deepens through spiritual practice. The hadith on intention (*niyyah*) is read as establishing the primacy of the heart's orientation over outward performance.

The purification traditions hold particular significance for Sufi readers, who understand ritual cleanliness as a symbol and prerequisite of inner purification. The ablution is not merely a physical washing but a spiritual preparation in which the believer consciously turns toward God. Sufi masters such as al-Ghazali developed elaborate teachings on the inner meanings of each stage of purification, and at-Tirmidhi's hadiths on these subjects provide the textual foundation for such contemplative practice. The progression from faith to purification mirrors the Sufi path itself — beginning with the heart's turning and proceeding through the cleansing of the soul.
