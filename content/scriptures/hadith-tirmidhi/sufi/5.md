Sufi interpreters find in at-Tirmidhi's tafsir and supplication chapters the intimate voice of the Prophet in communion with God. The prophetic commentary on the Quran reveals layers of meaning that Sufi hermeneutics seeks to penetrate — moving from the outward (*zahir*) meaning to the inner (*batin*) significance that speaks to the heart. At-Tirmidhi's traditions on specific Quranic verses are read alongside the interpretations of great Sufi exegetes such as Ibn Arabi and al-Qushayri.

The supplication chapters hold extraordinary value for Sufi devotional life. Prophetic prayers are understood as models of perfect communion with God — each word reflecting the Prophet's intimate knowledge of divine reality. Sufi practitioners memorize and recite these supplications as part of their regular spiritual exercises (*awrad*), believing that the prophetic words carry a transformative power (*barakah*) that opens the heart to God's presence. The combination of Quranic commentary and supplication in this final section embodies the Sufi ideal: understanding God's word and then responding with a prayer that flows from the deepest center of the heart.
