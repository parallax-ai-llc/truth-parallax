Sufi tradition regards prayer and fasting as the primary vehicles of spiritual transformation, and at-Tirmidhi's chapters on these subjects are read as guides to the inner life of worship. The hadith "Prayer is the mi'raj (ascension) of the believer" captures the Sufi understanding that each prayer is an opportunity for the worshipper's spirit to ascend toward divine presence, not merely a legal obligation to be fulfilled.

The fasting traditions are cherished by Sufis for their emphasis on disciplining the lower self (*nafs*). Fasting is understood as a practice that weakens the ego's hold and creates space for spiritual awareness. At-Tirmidhi's hadiths on voluntary fasting — particularly the Prophet's practice of fasting on Mondays and Thursdays — are adopted by Sufi orders as part of their regular spiritual discipline. The combination of prayer and fasting represents the ascetic foundation upon which the more advanced stages of the Sufi path are built, and at-Tirmidhi's careful recording of these prophetic practices provides the normative basis for Sufi devotional life.
