## Overview

The Sunni tradition holds Jami at-Tirmidhi in high esteem as one of the six canonical hadith collections, with particular appreciation for at-Tirmidhi's innovative system of grading traditions. Unlike al-Bukhari and Muslim, who included only hadiths they deemed fully authentic, at-Tirmidhi deliberately included traditions of varying strength while transparently labeling each, creating an invaluable pedagogical resource for students of hadith science.

## Interpretive Approach

Sunni scholars value at-Tirmidhi's practice of noting which legal school holds which position on a given issue, effectively providing a survey of early Islamic jurisprudential thought after many hadiths. His classification system — distinguishing *sahih*, *hasan*, *da'if*, and *gharib* — refined the vocabulary of hadith criticism and remains foundational to Sunni hadith methodology. The four legal schools draw upon his collection extensively, appreciating both the hadiths themselves and his editorial commentary.

## Theological Significance

For Sunni Muslims, at-Tirmidhi's collection serves as a bridge between hadith scholarship and comparative jurisprudence. His work preserves not only the words and actions of the Prophet but also the interpretive landscape of the early Muslim scholarly community. His *Kitab al-Shama'il* (a separate work on the Prophet's character and appearance) is among the most beloved devotional texts in Sunni Islam, and the traditions it draws upon are well represented in the Jami.
