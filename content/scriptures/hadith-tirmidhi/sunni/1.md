At-Tirmidhi opens his collection with traditions on faith and purification, establishing the twin foundations of Islamic religious life: correct belief and ritual cleanliness. Sunni scholars note that at-Tirmidhi follows al-Bukhari's precedent in beginning with the inner dimension of religion before proceeding to its outward forms, grounding the entire enterprise in sincerity of intention and sound creed.

The section on faith includes at-Tirmidhi's narration of the Hadith of Gabriel, which defines Islam, Iman, and Ihsan as the three dimensions of the religion. Sunni commentators highlight at-Tirmidhi's grading of these traditions, observing that he classifies the core creedal hadiths as *sahih* or *hasan sahih*, lending them the highest levels of authority. The purification chapters detail the regulations of ablution, ritual bathing, and dry ablution, with at-Tirmidhi carefully noting where the four legal schools diverge on specific procedures — a characteristic feature that makes his collection uniquely useful for comparative fiqh within the Sunni tradition.
