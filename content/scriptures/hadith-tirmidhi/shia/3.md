Shia scholars approach at-Tirmidhi's commercial and judicial traditions with attention to how prophetic guidance on justice and governance relates to the Imamate. The hadiths on judgment are of particular interest, as Shia theology holds that the legitimate authority to adjudicate belongs to the Imams as divinely appointed successors to the Prophet. Traditions about Ali's judicial wisdom, which at-Tirmidhi records in various places, are highlighted as evidence of his special qualification for leadership.

The trade sections are engaged more on their legal substance, with Shia jurists comparing at-Tirmidhi's narrations on commercial transactions with the rulings preserved in Shia legal texts. Where there is agreement — such as the prohibition of usury and the requirement of honest dealing — Shia scholars acknowledge a shared prophetic foundation. Where there is divergence, they attribute the difference to the corruption of transmission chains or the exclusion of Ahl al-Bayt narrators from certain lines of transmission in the Sunni tradition.
