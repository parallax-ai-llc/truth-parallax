Shia scholars engage with at-Tirmidhi's Book of Faith and Purification by examining how the foundational traditions on creed relate to the question of legitimate authority after the Prophet. While the hadith on intention is universally accepted, Shia interpreters expand its application to encompass the political sphere — arguing that the intention behind allegiance to a leader is itself a matter of faith that determines the validity of one's religious standing.

The creedal hadiths in this section are read alongside parallel traditions from the Shia canon, particularly al-Kafi. Where at-Tirmidhi records traditions defining faith through its articles and branches, Shia scholars note the absence or marginalization of *wilayah* (allegiance to Ali and the Imams) as a pillar of faith. The purification chapters are engaged more neutrally, though Shia jurisprudence differs from Sunni practice on specific points such as the manner of wiping in ablution, and Shia scholars note where at-Tirmidhi's narrations support or contradict their own legal positions.
