Shia engagement with at-Tirmidhi's chapters on prayer and fasting centers on identifying points of convergence and divergence with Twelver Shia jurisprudence. Shia practice differs from Sunni practice in several aspects of prayer — including the combination of prayers, the form of the *adhan*, and specific postures — and Shia scholars examine at-Tirmidhi's narrations for evidence that may support their own positions from within the Sunni canon.

The fasting chapters are engaged with particular attention to traditions narrated through members of the Prophet's household, whose authority Shia Islam recognizes as binding. Where at-Tirmidhi records a hadith through Ahl al-Bayt narrators on fasting regulations, Shia scholars note this as corroboration of their own legal tradition. At-Tirmidhi's grading of these traditions is itself a point of interest — when he authenticates a hadith narrated by Ali or Ja'far al-Sadiq, Shia scholars use this as evidence that even Sunni authorities affirm the reliability of Imami narrators on matters of practical worship.
