The virtues section of at-Tirmidhi is of great significance for Shia scholars because it contains numerous traditions praising the Prophet's family and companions. Shia interpreters focus on hadiths about the merits of Ali, Fatimah, al-Hasan, and al-Husayn, which they cite as Sunni canonical evidence for the special status of Ahl al-Bayt. At-Tirmidhi's recording of traditions such as "Ali is to me as Aaron was to Moses" and his narrations about the Hadith of the Cloak are treated as particularly important.

At the same time, Shia scholars scrutinize traditions praising companions whom Shia historiography views critically, noting the chains of narration and the political contexts that may have motivated the circulation of such hadiths. The manners section is engaged more harmoniously, as the ethical teachings of the Prophet are broadly shared across Islamic traditions. Shia commentators read these traditions through the lens of the Imams' own ethical teachings, finding continuity between prophetic manners and the conduct exemplified by the twelve Imams.
