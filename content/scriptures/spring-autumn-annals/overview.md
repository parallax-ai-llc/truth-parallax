## Overview

The Spring and Autumn Annals (Chunqiu) is a chronicle of the state of Lu covering 242 years from 722 to 481 BCE, traditionally attributed to Confucius himself. As the last of the Five Classics, it holds a unique position as a work of historical record imbued with moral judgment. The Confucian tradition maintains that through subtle choices of language — a single word of praise or blame — Confucius embedded ethical verdicts on the actions of rulers, ministers, and states, creating a model of moral historiography that profoundly influenced Chinese civilization.

## Structure

The annals are organized according to the twelve reigning dukes of Lu:

1. **Duke Yin (722–712 BCE)** — Early entries establishing the chronicle's moral framework
2. **Duke Huan (711–694 BCE)** — Fratricidal succession and interstate conflicts
3. **Duke Zhuang (693–662 BCE)** — Military campaigns and ritual violations
4. **Duke Min (661–660 BCE)** — The briefest reign, marked by political instability
5. **Duke Xi (659–627 BCE)** — The era of the first hegemons and interstate alliances
6. **Duke Wen (626–609 BCE)** — Consolidation and diplomatic maneuvering
7. **Duke Xuan (608–591 BCE)** — Internal factional struggles within Lu
8. **Duke Cheng (590–573 BCE)** — Declining ducal authority and rising ministerial power
9. **Duke Xiang (572–542 BCE)** — The longest reign, spanning thirty years of political complexity
10. **Duke Zhao (541–510 BCE)** — Exile of the duke and the dominance of the Three Huan families
11. **Duke Ding (509–495 BCE)** — Confucius's brief period of political service in Lu
12. **Duke Ai (494–481 BCE)** — Final entries and the Master's twilight years

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Chunqiu has been interpreted through diverse lenses:

- **Confucian**: The supreme example of moral historiography, where naming conventions encode ethical judgments
- **Neo-Confucian**: Studied for its demonstration that historical events manifest cosmic moral principles
- **Daoist**: Engaged critically as an example of Confucian attachment to human institutions and hierarchical order
- **Buddhist-Chinese**: Read for its portrayal of impermanence and the karmic consequences of political actions
- **Academic**: Subject of intensive debate regarding Confucius's actual authorship and the text's relationship to its three commentarial traditions
