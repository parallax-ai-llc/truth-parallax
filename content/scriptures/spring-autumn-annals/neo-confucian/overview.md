## Overview

Neo-Confucian interpreters read the Spring and Autumn Annals as demonstrating that historical events manifest cosmic moral principles (tianli). The Song dynasty commentator Hu Anguo produced the most influential Neo-Confucian treatment, arguing that Confucius's chronicle reveals the operation of Heaven's moral order within the political realm. For Neo-Confucians, the Chunqiu is not simply a record of what happened but a revelation of why events unfolded as they did, governed by the inexorable logic of moral cause and consequence.

## Interpretive Approach

The Neo-Confucian reading emphasizes the Chunqiu's relevance to the theory of orthodox succession (zhengtong), which became a central concern in Song dynasty political thought. Hu Anguo argued that the Annals establish criteria for distinguishing legitimate from illegitimate political authority — criteria grounded in moral principle rather than mere military power or hereditary claim. The Cheng brothers connected the Chunqiu to their broader philosophical project of investigating principle (qiongli), treating historical events as instances of principle working itself out in the human world. This approach made the study of history a form of moral philosophy.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Neo-Confucian insistence that history is governed by moral logic rather than mere contingency continues to provoke philosophical debate. Its vision of historiography as an enterprise inseparable from ethical judgment speaks to contemporary discussions about objectivity, values, and the purpose of historical knowledge.
