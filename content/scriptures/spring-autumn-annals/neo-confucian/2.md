Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Huan section reads the fratricidal succession and subsequent interstate conflicts as demonstrating how departures from principle (li) at the most fundamental level — the taking of political power through murder — corrupt all subsequent political action. Hu Anguo's commentary traces the chain of moral causation from Duke Huan's illegitimate rise to the disorders that characterized his reign, showing that every political event recorded in these entries can be understood as a consequence of the original violation of principle.

The interstate conflicts documented during Duke Huan's reign are analyzed by Neo-Confucian readers through the framework of heavenly principle versus selfish desire (renyu). Military campaigns motivated by genuine concern for the interstate moral order reflect the operation of principle, while those driven by territorial ambition or revenge express selfish desire. The Annals' linguistic distinctions between different types of military action are read as encoding this metaphysical judgment, with Confucius's word choices revealing whether each action aligned with or departed from cosmic moral principle.

The cyclical violence of Duke Huan's reign — gaining power through assassination and losing his life to assassination — confirms, for Neo-Confucian readers, the thesis that moral principle operates with the regularity of natural law. This pattern demonstrates that history is not random or contingent but governed by principles as reliable as those governing the physical world, a conviction central to the Neo-Confucian understanding of the unity of moral and natural orders.
