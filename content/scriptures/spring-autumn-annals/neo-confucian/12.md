Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Ai section, the chronicle's conclusion, reads the final entries and the capture of the qilin as the culmination of the Annals' demonstration of moral principle operating through history. The ending of the chronicle is understood not as arbitrary but as philosophically determined: Confucius ceased writing at the precise moment when the moral significance of the historical record had been fully articulated. The Annals' conclusion is therefore a statement about the completeness of the moral teaching rather than merely a biographical fact about the Master's death.

The capture of the qilin, interpreted by the Confucian tradition as an inauspicious portent, is read by Neo-Confucian interpreters as a cosmic confirmation of the moral analysis that the Annals have conducted. The appearance of a mythical creature at this moment in history represents, in Neo-Confucian terms, the response of the cosmos to the moral condition of human civilization — a manifestation of the unity of principle that connects natural phenomena with human affairs. The qilin's capture signals that the moral disorder documented in the chronicle has reached cosmic proportions.

Hu Anguo's commentary treats the ending of the Annals as establishing the point at which the orthodox transmission of the Way passes from direct political engagement to textual and philosophical transmission. The Neo-Confucian claim to have recovered the Way after a millennium of obscurity rests on the conviction that principle, preserved in texts like the Chunqiu, awaits rediscovery by minds capable of apprehending it. The Annals' conclusion thus marks not an ending but a transition in the mode of principle's transmission through human history.
