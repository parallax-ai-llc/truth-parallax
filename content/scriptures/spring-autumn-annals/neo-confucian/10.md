Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Zhao section treats the exile of the duke as the most dramatic illustration of the moral metaphysics that undergirds the entire chronicle. Duke Zhao's expulsion from his own state represents, in Neo-Confucian analysis, the complete triumph of selfish desire over principle in the political life of Lu — a moment when the moral decay traced throughout the Annals reaches its most extreme and visible expression.

Hu Anguo's commentary reads the exile as demonstrating the Neo-Confucian principle that political authority without moral substance is ultimately illusory. Duke Zhao possessed the name and formal status of ruler but lacked the moral authority that genuine governance requires. The Three Huan families possessed the actual power but lacked the legitimacy that only principle can confer. This bifurcation of form and substance, name and reality, represents the ultimate failure of the rectification of names — and its political consequences validate the Neo-Confucian insistence that moral and political orders are inseparable.

The Duke Zhao period is also significant for Neo-Confucian interpreters because it coincides with the formative years of Confucius's intellectual development. The young Confucius's observation of the political disorders documented in these entries is understood as a crucial influence on his moral philosophy. Neo-Confucian readers see in the Master's later teachings a direct response to the political and moral crisis he witnessed during Duke Zhao's reign, making these entries a key context for understanding the origins of the Confucian tradition itself.
