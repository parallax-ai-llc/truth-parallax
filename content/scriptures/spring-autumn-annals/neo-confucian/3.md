Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Zhuang section reads the extensive military campaigns and ritual violations of this period through the lens of the investigation of principle (qiongli). Each event recorded in the Annals becomes an occasion for examining how principle operates or is obscured in specific historical circumstances. The thirty-two years of Duke Zhuang's reign provide a rich field for this philosophical investigation, as the interplay between principled and unprincipled action becomes increasingly complex.

The Battle of Changshao and other military engagements are analyzed by Neo-Confucian readers for their demonstration of the practical consequences of aligning action with principle versus acting from selfish desire. Cao Gui's strategy of patient waiting, which led to Lu's victory over the larger Qi army, is understood as an instance of governance aligned with principle — a recognition that effective action requires attunement to the underlying pattern of circumstances rather than impulsive response to surface appearances.

The ritual violations recorded during Duke Zhuang's reign receive particularly careful Neo-Confucian analysis. Each departure from proper ceremonial practice represents, in Neo-Confucian terms, a failure to express principle through the forms that ritual provides. Neo-Confucian philosophy understands ritual not as arbitrary convention but as the structured expression of cosmic principle in human relationships. The erosion of ritual documented in these entries therefore represents not merely social disorder but a metaphysical rupture between human practice and the moral order of the universe.
