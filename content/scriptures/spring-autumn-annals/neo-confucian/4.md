Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Min section, covering only two years, treats this brief and violent reign as a concentrated demonstration of what happens when heavenly principle is completely obscured by selfish human desire in the political realm. The rapidity of Duke Min's fall from power illustrates the Neo-Confucian teaching that political authority divorced from moral principle is inherently unstable, collapsing as soon as the forces of selfish desire that sustain it turn against each other.

The extreme brevity of this section carries metaphysical significance for Neo-Confucian readers. In a chronicle where the length of each section naturally corresponds to the duration of each reign, the stark compression of the Duke Min entries formally mirrors the instability and impermanence of power built on unprincipled foundations. The contrast between this brief section and the extended entries for longer reigns provides a visual demonstration of the Neo-Confucian thesis that moral substance gives duration and stability to political achievements.

Hu Anguo's commentary uses the Duke Min period to illustrate the concept of "accumulated disorder" — the Neo-Confucian teaching that moral decline is not sudden but results from the gradual accumulation of departures from principle over time. The violence that ended Duke Min's reign did not arise from nowhere but was the predictable consequence of moral deterioration traced through the preceding reigns. Neo-Confucian readers thus understand the Duke Min section not as an isolated crisis but as the logical culmination of a process governed by moral causation.
