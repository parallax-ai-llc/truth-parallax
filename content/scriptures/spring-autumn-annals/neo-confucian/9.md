Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Xiang section, spanning thirty-one years and representing the most complex portion of the Annals, provides the fullest demonstration of the Neo-Confucian approach to moral historiography. The extraordinary density and variety of events recorded during this reign challenge the interpreter to trace the operation of principle through an increasingly tangled web of political relationships, military conflicts, and institutional transformations.

Hu Anguo's commentary on the Duke Xiang period represents his most sustained and ambitious philosophical analysis. The rise of new political formations, the transformation of interstate relations, and the emergence of new forms of political authority are all analyzed as expressions of the same underlying moral dynamics that govern the entire chronicle. The Neo-Confucian interpreter's task is to demonstrate that beneath the apparent chaos of events, principle continues to operate with unwavering consistency.

The debates surrounding Zichan's legal reforms in Zheng, which fall within the Duke Xiang period, received particular Neo-Confucian attention. The question of whether codified law represents a legitimate expression of principle or a degraded substitute for moral governance touched directly on Neo-Confucian concerns about the relationship between external regulation and inner cultivation. Zhu Xi and other Neo-Confucians acknowledged the practical necessity of law while maintaining that genuine moral order must ultimately rest on the transformation of hearts and minds through education and self-cultivation.
