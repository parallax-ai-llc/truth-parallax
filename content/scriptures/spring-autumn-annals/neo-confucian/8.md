Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Cheng section reads the continued decline of ducal authority as demonstrating the inexorable consequences of failing to align governance with heavenly principle. The transfer of effective power from the duke to ministerial families represents, in Neo-Confucian analysis, a fundamental inversion of the proper order — the triumph of private interest over public good, of material force (qi) over principle (li) in the political realm.

Hu Anguo's commentary on the Duke Cheng period applies the Neo-Confucian concept of "reverent seriousness" (jing) to political analysis. The erosion of ducal authority is traced not to any single dramatic failure but to the cumulative effect of insufficient moral vigilance — the gradual relaxation of the spiritual discipline that must sustain political authority. This analysis mirrors the Neo-Confucian teaching about individual moral cultivation: just as personal virtue requires constant attentiveness to principle, political legitimacy requires ongoing moral effort.

The military and diplomatic events of this period are read through the Neo-Confucian lens as further evidence that moral decline in one domain inevitably produces disorder in others. The unity of principle ensures that political, military, diplomatic, and ritual affairs are all interconnected expressions of the same underlying moral reality. Neo-Confucian readers therefore understand each entry in the Duke Cheng section not as an isolated event but as a manifestation of the general moral condition of the age.
