Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Xi section engages with the hegemonic system through the framework of principle (li) and its partial or distorted expression in political institutions. The rise of the great hegemons represents, for Neo-Confucian readers, a complex moral phenomenon: the hegemons maintained a degree of interstate order that reflected principle, but their authority rested on power rather than moral legitimacy, representing a degraded substitute for the genuine moral order of the sage-kings.

Hu Anguo's treatment of the hegemons applies the Neo-Confucian distinction between the "kingly way" (wangdao) and the "hegemon's way" (badao) that became central to Song dynasty political thought. The kingly way governs through moral influence and the spontaneous attraction of virtue; the hegemon's way maintains order through strategic alliances and the threat of force. While both produce stability, only the kingly way reflects genuine principle. The hegemons' achievements are therefore understood as approximations of true governance — better than chaos but fundamentally inferior to rule by moral authority.

The interstate conferences of the Duke Xi period are analyzed by Neo-Confucian readers for their illustration of how principle can be partially embodied in imperfect political arrangements. The conventions of hegemonic diplomacy — formal oaths, mutual defense obligations, agreed-upon norms of interstate conduct — represent attempts to institutionalize moral constraints on political behavior. Neo-Confucian interpreters value these arrangements as evidence that even in times of moral decline, the human recognition of principle cannot be entirely suppressed.
