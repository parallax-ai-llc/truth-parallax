Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Ding section carries unique weight because it documents Confucius's own attempt to put principle into practice through direct political action. The Master's brief period of governmental service in Lu represents, for Neo-Confucian readers, the supreme test of the thesis that moral cultivation and political governance are united expressions of heavenly principle. Confucius's effort to dismantle the power of the Three Huan families is understood as an attempt to restore principle to its rightful place in the political order.

The failure of Confucius's political program is interpreted by Neo-Confucian readers not as evidence that principle is powerless in the political realm but as demonstrating the depth of moral corruption that had accumulated over generations. The forces of selfish desire (renyu) had become so entrenched in Lu's political institutions that even the sage could not overcome them through immediate political action. This reading preserves the Neo-Confucian conviction that principle is ultimately sovereign while accounting for the historical reality of Confucius's political failure.

Hu Anguo's commentary treats Confucius's departure from Lu as a pivotal moment in the orthodox transmission of the Way (daotong). Unable to realize his vision through direct political action, the Master redirected his efforts toward teaching and textual compilation — producing, among other works, the Spring and Autumn Annals itself. Neo-Confucian readers understand this redirection as demonstrating that the transmission of principle through education and scholarship can ultimately achieve what direct political action cannot.
