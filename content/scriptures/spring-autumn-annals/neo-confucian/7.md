Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Xuan section traces the deepening factional struggles within Lu as a case study in how the failure to cultivate principle at the individual level produces political disorder at the institutional level. The Three Huan families' consolidation of power is understood through the Neo-Confucian framework as the political consequence of accumulated selfish desire (renyu) overcoming the restraining influence of heavenly principle (tianli) in the conduct of those who hold power.

The Neo-Confucian reading connects the internal politics of the Duke Xuan period to the broader philosophical question of the relationship between the mind and governance. The Cheng brothers taught that "regulating the family, governing the state, and bringing peace to the world" all begin with the rectification of the mind (zhengxin). The failure of Lu's ducal house to maintain its authority is therefore understood as originating in a failure of moral cultivation — a diagnosis that extends the Neo-Confucian analysis from political history to moral psychology.

The interstate events of this period are analyzed by Hu Anguo for their revelation of the increasing disconnect between political rhetoric and moral reality. Neo-Confucian readers note that states continued to invoke the language of righteousness and loyalty while pursuing nakedly self-interested policies. This gap between word and deed represents, in Neo-Confucian terms, the ultimate corruption of the rectification of names — the very principle that the Annals were composed to uphold.
