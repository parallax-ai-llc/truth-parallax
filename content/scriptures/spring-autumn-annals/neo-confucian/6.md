Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Wen section reads the post-hegemonic diplomatic maneuvering as illustrating the progressive obscuring of principle by selfish desire in interstate relations. With the decline of the hegemonic system, states increasingly pursued their interests through strategic calculation rather than moral commitment, and Neo-Confucian readers trace in the Annals' entries the widening gap between political practice and the requirements of principle.

Hu Anguo's commentary on the Duke Wen entries emphasizes the theory of orthodox succession (zhengtong), arguing that the Annals distinguish between political actors who represent legitimate moral authority and those who exercise power without genuine moral standing. The diplomatic negotiations of the Duke Wen period are analyzed for evidence of Confucius's assessment of which states and leaders retained some connection to principle and which had abandoned it entirely.

The internal politics of Lu during Duke Wen's reign are read by Neo-Confucian interpreters as a microcosm of the broader metaphysical drama between principle and desire. The growing power of the ministerial families represents the triumph of private interest over public duty — a political expression of the same selfish desire (renyu) that Neo-Confucian self-cultivation seeks to overcome at the individual level. The parallel between personal moral cultivation and political governance is central to the Neo-Confucian reading of the Chunqiu.
