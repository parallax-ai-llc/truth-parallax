Neo-Confucian interpretation of the Duke Yin section reads the opening entries of the Spring and Autumn Annals as establishing the metaphysical foundations for the chronicle's moral historiography. Hu Anguo's Song dynasty commentary treats Duke Yin's reign as demonstrating the operation of heavenly principle (tianli) in the political realm from the very first entry. The question of Duke Yin's legitimacy — whether he was rightful ruler or mere regent — is understood as a case study in how principle manifests in the specific circumstances of political succession.

The Neo-Confucian reading of the opening entry connects the Annals' chronological framework to the cosmic moral order. The reference to the "king's first month" is understood not merely as a dating convention but as an assertion of the unity of time, moral order, and political legitimacy. The calendar, properly observed, reflects the alignment of human institutions with heavenly principle; the Annals' meticulous dating thus enacts, at a formal level, the Neo-Confucian conviction that all phenomena are governed by principle.

Duke Yin's assassination is read by Neo-Confucian interpreters as demonstrating the inexorable operation of moral causation in political life. The connection between Duke Yin's ambiguous legitimacy and his violent death illustrates the Neo-Confucian thesis that departures from principle produce consequences as predictable as the natural phenomena that result from violations of physical law. Historical events are thus understood as manifestations of moral law.
