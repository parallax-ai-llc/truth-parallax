Daoist interpretation of the Duke Xi section and its documentation of the hegemonic era engages critically with the Confucian ambivalence toward the hegemons. From a Daoist perspective, the debate between the "kingly way" and the "hegemon's way" is ultimately irrelevant, since both represent forms of organized domination that depart from the spontaneous order of the Dao. The hegemons' attempts to impose interstate stability through alliances and military deterrence are understood as sophisticated elaborations of the same impulse toward control that Daoist philosophy identifies as the root of political dysfunction.

The diplomatic conferences and interstate oaths recorded during the Duke Xi period are read by Daoist interpreters as rituals of mutual coercion dressed in the language of cooperation. The elaborate protocols of hegemonic diplomacy — the formal meetings, the sworn agreements, the coordinated military campaigns — represent precisely the kind of artificial social mechanisms that the Daodejing identifies as symptoms of the loss of genuine harmony. True harmony, in the Daoist understanding, does not require oaths and alliances because it arises naturally from the attunement of communities to the Dao.

Yet Daoist readers acknowledge that the hegemonic era, for all its artificiality, represented a relatively stable period compared to what followed. This observation is consistent with the Daoist teaching that even imperfect order is preferable to the chaos of unconstrained competition, while maintaining that genuinely stable order can arise only from alignment with the natural Dao rather than from institutional design.
