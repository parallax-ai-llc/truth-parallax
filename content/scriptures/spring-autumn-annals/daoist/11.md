Daoist interpretation of the Duke Ding section, documenting Confucius's own political career, provides the most pointed Daoist critique of the Confucian project. The Master's attempt to implement his moral vision through direct political action — including his effort to dismantle the fortified cities of the Three Huan families — is read by Daoist interpreters as a prime example of the futility of trying to impose moral order on inherently resistant political systems. The failure of Confucius's political program confirms the Daoist teaching that governance through force of will, however well-intentioned, contradicts the principle of wu-wei.

The Zhuangzi's own treatment of Confucius's political career is deeply ironic, and Daoist readers of the Annals bring this ironic perspective to the Duke Ding entries. The greatest sage of the Confucian tradition, armed with profound moral insight and driven by passionate commitment to justice, proved unable to change the political order of a single small state. Daoist interpreters read this failure not as a reflection on Confucius's personal inadequacy but as a demonstration that the political approach itself — the attempt to transform society through institutional reform and moral authority — is fundamentally misconceived.

Confucius's departure from Lu following his political failure is understood by Daoist readers as an involuntary approximation of the Daoist ideal of withdrawal from political engagement. While Confucius left Lu reluctantly and continued to seek political employment elsewhere, Daoist interpreters note that his years of wandering, free from the burdens of office, produced his most enduring intellectual achievements — suggesting that creative flourishing requires freedom from political entanglement.
