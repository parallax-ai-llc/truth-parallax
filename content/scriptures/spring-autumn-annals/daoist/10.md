Daoist interpretation of the Duke Zhao section reads the duke's exile as the definitive exposure of the illusory nature of political authority within hierarchical systems. Duke Zhao possessed the title, the lineage, and the formal status of ruler but was powerless against the accumulated practical power of the ministerial families. From a Daoist perspective, this gap between nominal authority and actual power reveals the fundamental emptiness of the political categories — ruler, minister, legitimate, illegitimate — upon which the Confucian moral system depends.

The Daoist reading finds in Duke Zhao's exile an inadvertent illustration of the Daodejing's teaching about the relationship between form and emptiness. The duke's formal authority, hollow and unsupported by real power, is like the "thirty spokes that share one hub" — useful only insofar as the empty center functions. When the center loses its function, the wheel collapses. Political authority, like all human constructions, depends on conditions that it cannot ultimately control, and the Daoist response is not to seek better conditions but to recognize the impermanence of all constructed order.

The fact that Confucius witnessed these events during his youth gives them particular resonance for Daoist readers. The young Confucius's response to Duke Zhao's exile — his commitment to restoring legitimate political order through moral cultivation — is understood by Daoist interpreters as a characteristic but ultimately misguided reaction. Rather than recognizing the fundamental instability of political power, Confucius doubled down on the project of moral governance, creating a tradition that Daoist philosophy sees as perpetuating the very illusions that the duke's exile should have dispelled.
