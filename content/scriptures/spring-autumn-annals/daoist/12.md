Daoist interpretation of the Duke Ai section, the chronicle's final portion, reads the ending of the Annals as an acknowledgment — however unconscious — that the Confucian project of moral historiography has reached its natural limit. The capture of the qilin, which Confucian tradition interprets as a portent of the Way's decline, is understood by Daoist readers differently: as a symbol of the impossibility of domesticating wild, natural reality within the categories of human moral judgment. The qilin, a creature of the wild, captured and misidentified, represents the Dao itself — present everywhere but distorted when grasped by human conceptual systems.

Confucius's reported weeping at the capture of the qilin is read by Daoist interpreters with a mixture of sympathy and critique. The Master's grief reflects a genuine recognition that something precious and irreplaceable has been lost — a recognition that resonates with Daoist sensitivity to the beauty and fragility of the natural world. However, the Confucian interpretation of this grief as mourning for the decline of the political order misses what Daoist readers see as its deeper significance: a glimpse of the awareness that the moral categories imposed on reality by the Confucian tradition cannot contain the fullness of existence.

The ending of the Annals is understood by Daoist readers as the most eloquent moment in the entire chronicle — the moment when the compulsive recording of human political affairs finally ceases, and silence prevails. In Daoist philosophy, this silence is not emptiness but fullness: the space in which the Dao, unburdened by human interpretation, can speak for itself.
