Daoist interpretation of the Duke Xiang section, the longest in the chronicle, finds in the sheer complexity and density of events confirmation of the Daoist principle that overelaboration of political systems leads to unmanageable complexity. The thirty-one years of Duke Xiang's reign document an interstate system of such intricacy that no single actor — however wise or virtuous — could comprehend its dynamics, let alone control its outcomes. This observation supports the Daoist argument that complex governance is inherently self-defeating.

The emergence of new political formations and the transformation of old relationships during this period are read by Daoist interpreters as evidence of the inherent impermanence and unpredictability of political arrangements. The Daodejing's teaching that "reversal is the movement of the Dao" applies to political systems as much as to natural phenomena: every alliance generates its counter-alliance, every consolidation of power provokes resistance, and every attempt at stability sows the seeds of its own dissolution.

Zichan's legal reforms in Zheng, which receive extensive attention from both Confucian and Neo-Confucian interpreters, are viewed by Daoist readers as an attempt to address the symptoms of political dysfunction rather than its underlying cause. From a Daoist perspective, the need for codified law arises precisely because the spontaneous order of the Dao has been disrupted by the artificial structures of civilized governance. More laws represent more complexity, which generates more opportunities for conflict and evasion, in an ever-escalating cycle that the Daoist tradition recommends breaking through simplification rather than further regulation.
