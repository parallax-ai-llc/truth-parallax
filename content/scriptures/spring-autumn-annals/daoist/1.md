Daoist interpretation of the Duke Yin section approaches the opening entries of the Spring and Autumn Annals with skepticism toward the Confucian claim that subtle word choices encode definitive moral judgments. From a Daoist perspective, the question of Duke Yin's legitimacy — which generates elaborate Confucian commentary — illustrates the futility of attempting to impose fixed moral categories on inherently fluid political situations. The Zhuangzi's teaching that right and wrong are relative rather than absolute applies directly to the succession disputes that open the chronicle.

The Daoist reading questions whether the meticulous recording of political events serves any genuinely useful purpose. The entries documenting diplomatic meetings, ritual observances, and interstate communications represent, from a Daoist standpoint, the kind of detailed attention to human conventions that distracts from awareness of the larger natural order. The energy devoted to determining proper succession, correct ritual performance, and legitimate political authority reflects the Confucian attachment to human institutions that Daoist philosophy identifies as a source of suffering.

Duke Yin's assassination, rather than illustrating the operation of moral law as Confucian readers claim, is understood by Daoist interpreters as demonstrating the inherent violence of political life. The Daoist conclusion is not that Duke Yin failed to be virtuous enough but that political power itself, by its nature, generates the conflicts that destroy those who hold it. The only genuine security lies in withdrawing from the pursuit of power altogether.
