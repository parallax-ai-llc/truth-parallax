Daoist interpretation of the Duke Zhuang section reads the military campaigns and ritual violations of this period as further evidence of the inherent instability of the political order that the Confucian tradition seeks to preserve and perfect. The extensive warfare documented during these thirty-two years demonstrates, from a Daoist perspective, that the elaborate system of interstate relations, diplomatic protocols, and military conventions described in the Annals fails to prevent the very conflicts it is designed to manage.

The Daoist reading of the ritual violations recorded during Duke Zhuang's reign differs fundamentally from the Confucian interpretation. Where Confucian commentators see each departure from proper ceremony as a step in moral decline, Daoist readers question whether the rituals themselves serve a genuinely useful purpose. The Daodejing teaches that "when ritual is insisted upon and there is no response, people are forced to comply with rolled-up sleeves." From this perspective, ritual violations may represent not moral failure but a natural rejection of imposed formalities that have lost their connection to authentic human experience.

The Battle of Changshao, which Confucian readers celebrate as a triumph of wisdom over brute force, receives a more ambivalent Daoist reception. While the strategic patience of Cao Gui resonates with the Daoist appreciation of waiting and non-forcing, the context remains military conflict — an arena that Daoist philosophy regards as the ultimate failure of governance. The Daoist ideal is not winning wars wisely but creating conditions in which wars do not arise.
