Daoist interpretation of the Duke Xuan section reads the deepening factional struggles within Lu as a natural and predictable consequence of the hierarchical political system that the Confucian tradition venerates. The Three Huan families' accumulation of power at the expense of the ducal house is understood by Daoist readers not as an anomaly or a moral failure but as the inevitable dynamic of any system built on competition for status and authority. The Daodejing's teaching that "the more prohibitions there are, the poorer the people become" extends to the political observation that the more elaborate the system of ranks and offices, the more intensely people compete for them.

The Daoist reading emphasizes the futility of the moral rhetoric that surrounds the factional struggles. Both the ducal house and the ministerial families claim to act in the interests of Lu and in accordance with ritual propriety, but from a Daoist perspective these claims merely dress up the pursuit of power in moral language. The Zhuangzi's skepticism about the possibility of distinguishing genuine virtue from its counterfeit applies directly to the political actors documented in the Duke Xuan entries.

Daoist interpreters find in the increasing complexity and instability of Lu's internal politics confirmation of the Daoist principle that simplicity is the key to sustainable order. The multiplication of political factions, competing interests, and strategic calculations produces a system that is too complex to govern effectively, vindicating the Daoist preference for small communities organized around natural relationships rather than elaborate institutional hierarchies.
