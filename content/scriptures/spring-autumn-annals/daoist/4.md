Daoist interpretation of the Duke Min section finds in this extremely brief reign a stark illustration of the Daoist teaching that political power is inherently precarious. The two-year duration of Duke Min's rule, ending in violent death, demonstrates what the Daodejing expresses as the principle that "what is hard and stiff will be broken; what is soft and yielding will endure." Duke Min's position at the apex of a rigid hierarchical structure made him the most visible target for political violence rather than the most protected member of the community.

The Daoist reading contrasts sharply with the Confucian interpretation of this section. Where Confucian commentators find in Duke Min's fall a moral lesson about the consequences of political disorder, Daoist readers see confirmation that the political system itself — with its fixed hierarchies, hereditary offices, and competition for power — generates the instability that destroys those caught within it. The remedy is not better governance within the system but a fundamental transformation of the relationship between human communities and the exercise of authority.

The brevity of this section also resonates with the Daoist appreciation of silence and emptiness. In a chronicle where every entry is claimed by Confucian interpreters to carry moral weight, the near-absence of entries for Duke Min's reign speaks more eloquently than elaborate narrative. Daoist readers find in this textual silence an inadvertent acknowledgment that the political events of these two years resist the kind of moral categorization that the Confucian project demands.
