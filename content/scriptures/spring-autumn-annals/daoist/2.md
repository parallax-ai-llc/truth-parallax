Daoist interpretation of the Duke Huan section reads the cycle of fratricidal violence and interstate conflict as confirming the Daoist diagnosis that competition for political power is inherently destructive. The pattern of assassination and counter-assassination that characterizes the transition from Duke Yin to Duke Huan is understood not as a moral lesson about the consequences of particular virtues or vices but as evidence of the structural violence built into hierarchical political systems.

The interstate conflicts of Duke Huan's reign are viewed by Daoist readers as the predictable outcome of a political order organized around competing claims to authority. From a Daoist perspective, the Confucian attempt to distinguish between just and unjust wars through terminological precision misses the fundamental point: all military conflict reflects a failure of the spontaneous harmony that would prevail if human societies were organized in accordance with the Dao rather than in competition for power and status.

The cyclical violence documented in this section — Duke Huan gaining power through murder and losing his life to murder — is read by Daoist interpreters as an illustration of the Daodejing's teaching that violence turns back upon the violent. Unlike the Confucian reading, which treats this pattern as evidence of moral causation, the Daoist reading understands it as demonstrating the natural tendency of force to generate opposing force. The solution lies not in more virtuous governance but in the abandonment of the systems of power that generate conflict.
