Daoist interpretation of the Duke Wen section reads the diplomatic maneuvering of the post-hegemonic era as confirming the Daoist analysis that political complexity generates its own disorders. The increasingly intricate web of alliances, rivalries, and strategic calculations described in these entries demonstrates, from a Daoist perspective, that the attempt to manage interstate relations through deliberate design produces an ever-expanding apparatus of negotiation and coercion that ultimately collapses under its own weight.

The Daoist reading finds in the growing power of ministerial families within Lu a natural consequence of the hierarchical political system. When authority is organized in fixed structures of command, those closest to the mechanisms of power inevitably accumulate it at the expense of those at the nominal top. This dynamic is understood not as a moral failure of particular individuals but as a structural inevitability of the kind of governance that the Confucian tradition promotes. The Daoist alternative — governance through non-interference and the cultivation of natural harmony — avoids this structural problem by refusing to concentrate power in the first place.

The entries recording diplomatic exchanges during Duke Wen's reign are approached by Daoist readers with attention to what they reveal about the gap between political language and political reality. The formal courtesies and ritual formulas that cloak strategic maneuvering illustrate the Daodejing's observation that "fine words are not trustworthy." Daoist interpretation thus reads the Annals against the grain of their Confucian purpose, finding in the chronicle's own evidence the materials for a critique of the political order it documents.
