Daoist interpretation of the Duke Cheng section views the continued transfer of real power from the duke to his ministers as the natural entropy of political systems. From a Daoist perspective, the distinction between legitimate ducal authority and illegitimate ministerial usurpation is less meaningful than it appears to Confucian readers. Both forms of power operate through the same mechanisms of coercion, patronage, and institutional control; the shift from one to the other is merely a redistribution within a fundamentally flawed system.

The Daoist reading of the Duke Cheng entries draws particular attention to the human costs of political competition documented in the chronicle. Behind the terse annalistic entries recording battles, diplomatic conferences, and succession events lie stories of ordinary people conscripted into military service, communities disrupted by political conflicts, and families torn apart by factional rivalries. Daoist interpreters emphasize these costs as evidence that the political order celebrated by the Confucian tradition exacts an unacceptable toll on those who live under it.

The entries recording Lu's diminished interstate standing during this period are understood by Daoist readers as demonstrating the paradox of political ambition. The more intensely states pursue power, influence, and strategic advantage, the more they undermine the conditions for genuine flourishing. The Daodejing's teaching that "those who would gain the world by doing are bound to fail" applies with particular force to the interstate competition documented in these entries.
