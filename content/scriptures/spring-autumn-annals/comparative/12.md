Comparative study of the Duke Ai section examines the ending of the Spring and Autumn Annals alongside the conclusions of other ancient historical works. Thucydides's unfinished history of the Peloponnesian War, the abrupt ending of Xenophon's Hellenica, and the final entries of the Babylonian chronicles all raise comparable questions about why ancient historical records terminate where they do. Comparative analysis reveals that the endings of ancient chronicles are rarely natural stopping points but reflect the circumstances of their authors, the conditions of their transmission, or deliberate compositional decisions.

The capture of the qilin that concludes the Annals invites comparison with portentous events recorded at the conclusions of other ancient narratives. The Hebrew Bible's final historical books end with prophetic warnings and promises; the Roman annalistic tradition frequently concludes annual records with notices of prodigies and portents; and Indian historical chronicles often mark transitions between eras with cosmic events. These comparative parallels suggest that ancient civilizations shared a tendency to mark significant historical transitions with accounts of extraordinary natural phenomena, interpreting the non-human world as responsive to the moral condition of human civilization.

The Spring and Autumn Annals as a whole, viewed in comparative perspective, represents a distinctive contribution to the global tradition of historical writing. Its combination of terse annalistic form with embedded moral judgment, its organization around the reigns of successive rulers, and its integration into a canonical textual tradition distinguish it from other ancient approaches to history. Comparative study reveals both the universal human impulse to record and moralize the past and the culturally specific forms that this impulse has taken across civilizations, making the Chunqiu an indispensable reference point for understanding the diversity of ancient historiography.
