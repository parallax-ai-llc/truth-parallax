Comparative study of the Duke Xiang section, the most extensive portion of the chronicle, provides the richest material for cross-civilizational comparison of late Bronze Age and early Iron Age political systems. The complex interstate dynamics documented over thirty-one years are compared with the equally intricate political relationships of the contemporary Greek world, the Neo-Assyrian Empire's management of its multiethnic territories, and the emerging political formations of the Indian subcontinent.

The peace conference of 546 BCE between Jin and Chu, documented in the Annals and elaborated in the Zuozhuan, invites comparison with other ancient peace settlements. The Peace of Callias between Athens and Persia, the various peace agreements of the Peloponnesian War, and the treaties documented in Near Eastern diplomatic archives all represent attempts by ancient civilizations to negotiate stable frameworks for coexistence among competing powers. Comparative analysis reveals different approaches to peace-making — from the Chinese emphasis on hierarchical ordering to the Greek preference for bilateral agreements.

Zichan's legal reforms are placed in comparative context with the broader ancient phenomenon of legal codification. The publication of laws in Zheng in 536 BCE occurred within decades of Solon's legal reforms in Athens (594 BCE) and within the broader ancient Near Eastern tradition of published law codes extending back to Hammurabi. This remarkable temporal convergence of codification movements across Eurasia has generated scholarly debate about whether these developments reflect common structural causes or independent responses to analogous political and social challenges.
