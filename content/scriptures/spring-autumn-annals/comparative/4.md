Comparative study of the Duke Min section places this extremely brief reign alongside other instances of rapid succession and political instability in the ancient world. The phenomenon of short-lived rulers — deposed, assassinated, or overthrown within months or years of assuming power — recurs across ancient civilizations, from the Roman "Year of the Four Emperors" to the rapid succession of Assyrian kings during periods of political crisis. These comparative cases illuminate the structural vulnerabilities common to hereditary monarchies and the conditions under which political systems become susceptible to rapid regime change.

The brevity of Duke Min's reign raises comparative questions about the relationship between political stability and institutional development. Scholars compare the conditions that produced instability in Lu with those that generated political crises in other ancient states, identifying common factors such as the absence of clear succession rules, the presence of powerful competing factions, and the weakness of institutional mechanisms for resolving political disputes peacefully.

The recording of Duke Min's brief reign within the annalistic format also invites formal comparison with how other ancient chronicles handle periods of rapid political change. The terse Chinese annalistic style, which records even a two-year reign with the same formal apparatus used for thirty-year reigns, is contrasted with the more elaborative narrative approaches of Greek historiography and the evaluative summaries found in the Hebrew Books of Kings, revealing different conventions for representing political discontinuity in historical writing.
