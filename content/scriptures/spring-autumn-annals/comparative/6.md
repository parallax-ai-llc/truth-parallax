Comparative study of the Duke Wen section examines the post-hegemonic transition alongside similar periods of systemic transformation in other ancient interstate systems. The decline of single-state hegemony and the emergence of more fluid, multipolar competition parallels developments in other ancient multistate systems, including the post-Athenian period in Greece after 404 BCE and the fragmentation of Alexander's empire among the Diadochi. These comparative cases illuminate the dynamics of power transition in multistate systems and the strategies employed by smaller states navigating between competing powers.

The growing power of ministerial families within Lu is compared with the rise of powerful aristocratic factions in other ancient polities. The Roman Republic's struggle with senatorial oligarchy, the Athenian contest between aristocratic factions and democratic movements, and the Indian kingdoms' negotiations between royal authority and Brahmanical power all represent parallel cases of tension between central authority and elite faction. Comparative analysis reveals that the challenge of preventing the concentration of power in the hands of subordinate elites is a universal problem of premodern governance.

The diplomatic strategies employed by Lu during this period — balancing between major powers, seeking protective alliances, and maintaining autonomy through skillful negotiation — are compared with the strategies of small states in other multistate systems. The parallels with Greek minor states navigating between Athens and Sparta, or Italian city-states maneuvering between major European powers, reveal common strategic patterns that transcend cultural and historical boundaries.
