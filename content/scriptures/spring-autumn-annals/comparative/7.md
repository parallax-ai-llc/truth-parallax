Comparative study of the Duke Xuan section examines the intensifying factional struggles within Lu alongside comparable developments in other ancient polities. The contest between the ducal house and the Three Huan families finds parallels in the late Roman Republic's struggles between populares and optimates, the Athenian conflicts between aristocratic and democratic factions, and the Indian Magadha kingdom's internal power struggles. These comparisons illuminate the universal dynamics of factional competition within states that have outgrown the ability of a single ruler to maintain effective personal control.

The relationship between internal factional conflict and interstate behavior documented during the Duke Xuan period invites comparison with similar dynamics in other ancient multistate systems. Scholars have noted that internal political divisions often influenced external policy in ancient Greece, Rome, and India, just as the Three Huan families' internal position affected Lu's diplomatic and military posture. Comparative analysis of these cases reveals patterns in how domestic political structures shape foreign policy behavior across diverse civilizations.

The changing norms of political conduct documented during this period are compared with parallel transformations in the conventions of political behavior in other ancient societies. The erosion of traditional constraints on political competition — the increasing willingness to use violence, deception, and institutional manipulation — parallels the breakdown of the mos maiorum in the late Roman Republic and the eclipse of aristocratic honor codes in Renaissance Italy, suggesting that the deterioration of informal norms is a recurrent feature of political systems under stress.
