Comparative study of the Duke Xi section and the hegemonic system draws extensive parallels with interstate systems in other ancient civilizations. The Chinese institution of hegemony (ba) is compared with the Greek institution of hegemonia, the Roman concept of imperium, and the Indian concept of samraja (overlordship). All represent attempts by dominant states within multistate systems to exercise leadership without fully absorbing the sovereignty of subordinate states, and comparative analysis reveals different balances between coercive and consensual elements in each system.

The interstate conferences documented during the Duke Xi period invite comparison with the diplomatic assemblies of ancient Greece, including the amphictyonic councils that managed shared religious sites and mediated disputes among member states. Both the Chinese and Greek systems developed conventions for multilateral diplomacy, including formal meeting protocols, oath-taking ceremonies, and mechanisms for enforcing agreements. Comparative analysis reveals that the Chinese system placed greater emphasis on hierarchical relationships among participants, while the Greek system maintained a more egalitarian framework among its members.

The role of military deterrence in maintaining the hegemonic order is compared with the function of military power in other ancient interstate systems. The hegemon's ability to organize collective military action against threats to the system parallels the Roman practice of leading allied campaigns and the modern concept of collective security. Comparative analysis illuminates the conditions under which dominant states can maintain order through credible military commitments and the circumstances that lead to the breakdown of such arrangements.
