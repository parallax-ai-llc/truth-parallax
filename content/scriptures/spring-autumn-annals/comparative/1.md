Comparative study of the Duke Yin section places the opening entries of the Spring and Autumn Annals alongside the beginnings of other ancient chronicles to examine how different civilizations initiate the act of historical recording. The Annals' first entry — marking the year, season, and month — is compared with the dating formulas of Mesopotamian king lists, Egyptian pharaonic records, and Roman annalistic tradition. Each civilization developed distinctive conventions for organizing temporal experience, and these conventions reveal fundamental assumptions about the relationship between political authority, cosmic order, and the passage of time.

The question of Duke Yin's legitimacy, which generates the Annals' first moral problem, is compared with succession disputes documented in other ancient chronicles. The Hebrew Books of Kings' treatment of contested successions, the Greek accounts of tyrannical seizures of power, and the Mesopotamian practice of recording the legitimacy claims of new rulers all provide comparative contexts for understanding how ancient civilizations used historical narrative to address questions of political legitimacy.

The assassination of Duke Yin finds parallels in the violent political transitions recorded in chronicles across the ancient world. Comparative analysis of how different historical traditions record political violence — whether they moralize, justify, or simply document — illuminates the distinctive features of the Chinese annalistic approach and its relationship to broader patterns of historical writing in premodern civilizations.
