Academic study of the Duke Yin section examines the opening entries of the Spring and Autumn Annals for evidence about the composition, dating, and authorship of the chronicle. Scholars analyze whether the formulaic language of the first entries reflects the conventions of an established annalistic genre or represents innovations specific to the Lu state chronicle. The question of whether the Annals began as a court record later attributed to Confucius or were composed by the Master himself remains central to academic debate.

Linguistic analysis of the Duke Yin entries reveals standardized formulaic patterns for recording events such as accessions, deaths, battles, diplomatic meetings, and natural phenomena. Scholars have catalogued these formulas and analyzed their consistency across the entire chronicle, finding both regular patterns and meaningful variations. The debate over whether variations encode intentional moral judgments (as the Confucian commentarial tradition claims) or simply reflect the conventions of different scribal traditions or different periods of composition remains unresolved.

Historical reconstruction of the Duke Yin period draws on the Annals in conjunction with the Zuozhuan's narrative expansions and archaeological evidence. The political situation in Lu during 722-712 BCE — including the question of Duke Yin's actual political status — has been investigated using bronze inscriptions and comparative evidence from other states' records. Scholars debate the reliability of the Zuozhuan's narrative elaborations, questioning whether they represent independent historical traditions or later literary constructions built upon the Annals' terse framework.
