Academic study of the Duke Ai section examines the final entries of the Spring and Autumn Annals for evidence about the composition and purpose of the chronicle as a whole. The Annals end in 481 BCE with the capture of the qilin, an event whose significance has been debated by scholars for centuries. Modern academic analysis questions whether the qilin entry represents a genuine historical record (perhaps the capture of an unusual animal), a symbolic statement by the chronicle's compiler, or a later addition to the text.

The question of why the Annals end in 481 BCE rather than at Duke Ai's death in 468 BCE has generated extensive scholarly debate. If Confucius was the compiler, his death in 479 BCE would explain why the chronicle ends before the duke's reign concluded, but the 481 BCE terminus predates even Confucius's death by two years. Scholars have proposed various explanations, including the possibility that Confucius ceased compiling before his death, that later editors truncated the text, or that the Annals were not compiled by Confucius and the terminus reflects other factors.

The Duke Ai entries provide the last chronological data in the Annals, marking the transition between the Spring and Autumn period and the Warring States era. Academic scholars study these final entries in conjunction with other sources to understand the political, social, and cultural changes that characterized this transitional moment. The end of the Annals thus marks not only the conclusion of a textual tradition but a turning point in Chinese history that would lead to the eventual unification of China under the Qin dynasty.
