Academic study of the Duke Cheng section examines the continued erosion of ducal authority in Lu as part of a broader investigation into the transformation of political institutions during the late Spring and Autumn period. Scholars analyze the entries for evidence about the specific mechanisms through which ministerial families displaced ducal authority, including their control of military forces, management of state revenues, and domination of ritual activities that had previously been ducal prerogatives.

The military entries for Duke Cheng's reign have been studied by scholars of ancient Chinese warfare for evidence about changes in military organization, technology, and strategy during the sixth century BCE. The increasing scale and frequency of military campaigns documented in these entries reflects broader transformations in Chinese warfare — including the transition from chariot-based aristocratic warfare to mass infantry mobilization — that would culminate in the large-scale conflicts of the Warring States period.

The diplomatic entries for this period provide evidence for understanding the evolving relationship between the major states of Jin and Chu and the smaller states that navigated between them. Scholars have analyzed the patterns of conference attendance, alliance formation, and military coordination documented in these entries to reconstruct the dynamics of what has been termed the "bipolar" interstate system of the mid-Spring and Autumn period, in which Jin and Chu competed for dominance over the central and southern Chinese states.
