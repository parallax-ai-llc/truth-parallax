Academic study of the Duke Zhuang section provides rich material for analyzing the political and military history of the early seventh century BCE. The thirty-two years of entries document a transitional period in Spring and Autumn interstate relations, as the state of Qi began its rise to hegemonic dominance under Duke Huan of Qi and the balance of power among the major states shifted significantly. Scholars use these entries in conjunction with the Zuozhuan and bronze inscriptions to reconstruct the complex diplomatic and military dynamics of this era.

The Battle of Changshao (684 BCE), recorded in the Annals and elaborated in the Zuozhuan, has been extensively studied as a source for understanding early Chinese military practices. Archaeological evidence from the battlefield region and comparative analysis of military terminology in bronze inscriptions and other texts have been used to evaluate the historicity and accuracy of the traditional account. The strategy attributed to Cao Gui has been analyzed by military historians as evidence for the sophistication of early Chinese strategic thought.

The "ritual violations" recorded during Duke Zhuang's reign have been studied by scholars of Chinese religion for evidence about the evolution of sacrificial and ceremonial practices during the Spring and Autumn period. Rather than assuming, as traditional commentators do, that these entries record departures from a fixed ritual standard, academic scholars investigate whether they might instead document the dynamic process through which ritual practices were transformed, adapted, and contested as political and social conditions changed.
