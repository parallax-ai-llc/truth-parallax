Academic study of the Duke Xuan section analyzes the intensifying factional dynamics within Lu and their relationship to broader patterns of political transformation across the Spring and Autumn states. Scholars examine the entries documenting the activities of ministerial families for evidence about the structural causes of the shift from ducal to ministerial governance — a transformation that occurred in multiple states during this period and represents one of the most significant political developments of the Spring and Autumn era.

The internal political entries for Duke Xuan's reign have been studied using prosopographical methods, reconstructing the kinship networks, marriage alliances, and patronage relationships that connected political actors within and across state boundaries. This approach reveals the degree to which factional politics was driven by structural factors — including patterns of landholding, military command, and ritual prerogative — rather than by the individual moral qualities emphasized by the traditional commentarial tradition.

Interstate relations during the Duke Xuan period are analyzed by historians for evidence of the changing norms of interstate conduct. Scholars have identified patterns suggesting that the conventions governing diplomatic exchange, military engagement, and treaty-making were evolving during this period, with older norms of ritual propriety gradually giving way to more pragmatic calculations of strategic advantage. This transformation has been compared to similar shifts in interstate norms in ancient Greece and Renaissance Italy.
