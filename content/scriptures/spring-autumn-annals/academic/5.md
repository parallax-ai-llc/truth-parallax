Academic study of the Duke Xi section provides the primary textual evidence for understanding the hegemonic system that defined the mid-Spring and Autumn period. Scholars analyze the entries documenting interstate conferences, alliance formations, and military campaigns led by hegemons to reconstruct the institutional structure of hegemonic authority. Key questions include whether hegemony was a formally recognized institution with defined powers and obligations or an informal arrangement maintained by a shifting combination of military strength, diplomatic skill, and moral prestige.

The extensive diplomatic activity recorded during Duke Xi's thirty-three-year reign has been used by historians to reconstruct the evolving system of interstate relations in this period. Scholars have mapped the patterns of alliance and rivalry, identifying shifts in the balance of power as the hegemony passed from Qi to Jin. The terminology used to describe interstate meetings — whether they are recorded as hui (conference), meng (covenant), or other terms — has been studied for evidence about the formality and binding nature of these diplomatic events.

The relationship between the hegemonic system and the Zhou royal authority is a central concern of academic analysis. Scholars examine entries recording interactions between hegemons and the Zhou king to assess the degree to which hegemony operated within or against the framework of royal legitimacy. The evidence suggests a complex and evolving relationship in which hegemons simultaneously claimed to act on behalf of the Zhou king and exercised authority that effectively superseded royal power.
