Academic study of the Duke Wen section examines the post-hegemonic transition period and its implications for understanding the evolution of the interstate system. The eighteen years of entries document how states adapted their diplomatic and military strategies as the hegemonic system established under Duke Huan of Qi and Duke Wen of Jin gave way to more fluid and competitive patterns of interstate relations. Scholars analyze these entries for evidence of the institutional changes that accompanied this political transition.

The internal politics of Lu during Duke Wen's reign provide important evidence for understanding the growing power of ministerial lineages, a development that would define the late Spring and Autumn period across multiple states. Academic analysis examines the entries recording the activities of the Three Huan families for evidence about the institutional mechanisms through which ministerial families accumulated power. Scholars study patterns of land control, military command, and access to state rituals to reconstruct the material bases of ministerial authority.

The diplomatic entries for the Duke Wen period have been studied by scholars of ancient Chinese international relations as evidence for the emergence of a more multipolar interstate system. With the decline of single-state hegemony, smaller states like Lu faced the challenge of navigating between competing major powers. The strategies employed — alliance-switching, balancing, and bandwagoning — have been analyzed using frameworks from modern international relations theory, providing comparative insights into the dynamics of multipolar political systems.
