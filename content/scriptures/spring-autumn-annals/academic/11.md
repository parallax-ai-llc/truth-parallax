Academic study of the Duke Ding section addresses the historically contentious question of Confucius's actual political career in Lu. The traditional account of the Master's service as Minister of Crime and his effort to demolish the fortified cities of the Three Huan families has been examined critically by modern scholars, who debate the reliability, scope, and significance of the traditional narrative. The Annals' own entries for this period are characteristically terse and do not directly mention Confucius's official activities.

The discrepancy between the Annals' silence about Confucius's political role and the Zuozhuan's detailed narrative of his official career raises important questions about the authorship of the chronicle. If Confucius composed the Annals during this period, his decision not to record his own activities might reflect modesty, convention, or a deliberate separation between the chronicler's public role and his literary enterprise. Alternatively, the absence of references to Confucius may suggest that his political career was less prominent than later tradition claimed or that the Annals were not composed by the Master himself.

The fifteen years of Duke Ding's reign document the final period of the Spring and Autumn interstate system and the emergence of conditions that would define the Warring States era. Scholars study the entries for evidence of the military, economic, and institutional changes — including the expansion of warfare, the rise of professional armies, and the growing irrelevance of the Zhou royal house — that marked this transitional period.
