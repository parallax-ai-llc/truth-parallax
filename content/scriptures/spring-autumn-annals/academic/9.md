Academic study of the Duke Xiang section, the most extensive in the chronicle, provides the richest material for reconstructing the political, social, and institutional history of the late Spring and Autumn period. The thirty-one years of entries document an era of transformative change, including the peace conference of 546 BCE between Jin and Chu, the emergence of new centers of power such as Wu and Yue, and the increasing sophistication of diplomatic practice across the interstate system.

The Duke Xiang entries have been analyzed by scholars using quantitative methods to identify trends in the frequency and types of events recorded. Statistical analysis of warfare frequency, diplomatic meetings, and ritual observations reveals patterns of change over time that illuminate the structural transformation of the interstate system. Scholars have also employed network analysis to map the patterns of diplomatic interaction documented in these entries, revealing the evolving structure of alliances and rivalries.

Zichan's legal reforms in Zheng, documented in entries correlated with the Zuozhuan's more detailed narrative, have been studied as a watershed moment in Chinese legal history. Scholars debate whether Zichan's inscription of penal laws on bronze vessels represents the first instance of codified law in China or merely the first well-documented case of a more widespread practice. Comparative legal historians have placed Zichan's reforms alongside the codification movements of ancient Athens, Rome, and India to understand the conditions under which societies transition from customary to codified legal systems.
