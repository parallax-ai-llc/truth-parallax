Academic study of the Duke Min section, consisting of only two years of entries, examines this extremely brief reign as evidence for the political instability that characterized certain periods of Spring and Autumn state governance. Scholars analyze the circumstances of Duke Min's accession and assassination for insights into the mechanisms of succession crisis in Zhou-era feudal states, comparing the Lu case with similar episodes of rapid succession and political violence documented in other states.

The brevity of the Duke Min entries raises important methodological questions for historians. With so few annalistic records for this period, scholars are particularly dependent on the Zuozhuan's narrative to reconstruct the political circumstances. This dependence highlights the broader question of the relationship between the Annals and the Zuozhuan: whether the Zuozhuan represents an independent historical source that can fill gaps in the annalistic record or whether its narratives are literary constructions that may not preserve reliable historical information for periods where the Annals provide little data.

The political crisis documented in the Duke Min section has been studied by scholars of comparative political history as an instance of the "succession problem" that afflicted hereditary monarchies across the ancient world. Comparative analysis with succession crises in Mesopotamian city-states, Greek tyrannies, and Roman imperial succession illuminates the structural vulnerabilities of systems that concentrate political authority in a single individual without effective mechanisms for ensuring stable transfer of power.
