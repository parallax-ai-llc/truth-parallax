Academic study of the Duke Huan section analyzes the eighteen years of entries for evidence about early Spring and Autumn period interstate relations, succession practices, and the political dynamics of the Lu ruling house. Scholars examine the entries recording Duke Huan's accession for clues about the actual circumstances of his rise to power, comparing the Annals' terse recording with the more elaborate narrative provided by the Zuozhuan and evaluating both against archaeological and epigraphic evidence.

The interstate conflicts recorded during Duke Huan's reign provide historians with valuable data for reconstructing the political geography and military dynamics of the early eighth century BCE. Scholars analyze the frequency, participants, and outcomes of recorded conflicts to map patterns of alliance and rivalry among the Zhou-era states. The terminology used to describe military actions — whether a campaign is recorded as fa (attack), qin (invade), or zheng (punitive expedition) — has been studied statistically to determine whether variations correlate with moral judgment, as traditional commentators claim, or with the type or scale of military action.

The assassination of Duke Huan in 694 BCE has been investigated by historians for its evidence about the mechanisms of political violence in early Chinese states. Scholars examine the institutional conditions that made assassination a recurring feature of Spring and Autumn political life, including the relationship between the ruling house and powerful ministerial lineages, the absence of effective bodyguard institutions, and the role of personal retainers in court politics.
