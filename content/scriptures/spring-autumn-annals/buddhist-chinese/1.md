Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Yin section reads the opening entries of the Spring and Autumn Annals through the lens of karmic causation and the cycle of suffering that pervades samsaric existence. Duke Yin's ambiguous legitimacy and eventual assassination are understood as illustrating the Buddhist teaching that political power, rooted in craving and attachment, inevitably produces suffering for those who pursue it and those subject to it.

The question of legitimate succession that opens the chronicle resonates with the Buddhist understanding of conditioned arising. Duke Yin's position as regent rather than rightful ruler was itself the product of a chain of prior conditions — family circumstances, political calculations, historical precedents — that Buddhist readers trace as an instance of the interconnected web of causes and effects that constitutes samsaric existence. Nothing in the political world arises independently; every situation is the product of prior conditions and will in turn condition future events.

Duke Yin's assassination is read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as a vivid illustration of the First Noble Truth: that existence in the realm of power and status is inherently marked by suffering. The violence that ended Duke Yin's reign is not understood as a punishment for specific moral failings but as an inevitable feature of a political world governed by the three poisons of greed, hatred, and delusion. Buddhist readers find in this opening section a compelling invitation to reflect on the nature of worldly power and its inability to provide lasting security or genuine peace.
