Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Xuan section reads the deepening internal factional struggles as a manifestation of the Buddhist concept of the "realm of hungry ghosts" operating at the political level. The competing factions within Lu, each seeking to accumulate more power, influence, and wealth, exhibit the insatiable craving that Buddhist cosmology attributes to the hungry ghost realm — a state of being characterized by constant desire that can never be fully satisfied.

The Three Huan families' consolidation of power is understood by Buddhist readers through the framework of karmic momentum. The initial concentration of power by these families created conditions that made further accumulation increasingly easy and the ducal house's resistance increasingly futile. This self-reinforcing dynamic mirrors the Buddhist understanding of how unwholesome habits of mind, once established, generate their own momentum, making it progressively more difficult to reverse course without the radical intervention that Buddhist awakening provides.

Buddhist-Chinese interpreters also find in the Duke Xuan entries opportunities for cultivating compassion (karuna). Rather than simply condemning the political actors for their moral failures, Buddhist readers are encouraged to recognize the suffering inherent in their situation — the anxiety of those who hold power precariously, the frustration of those who feel entitled to more, and the confusion of those who cannot distinguish between genuine well-being and the temporary satisfactions of status and influence.
