Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Wen section reads the post-hegemonic diplomatic maneuvering as illustrating the Buddhist teaching that attachment to outcomes produces suffering. The states of the Spring and Autumn period, anxiously navigating shifting alliances and uncertain power dynamics, exemplify the mental state of beings trapped in samsara — constantly calculating, strategizing, and worrying about conditions that are fundamentally beyond their control.

The growing power of ministerial families within Lu is understood by Buddhist readers through the lens of the three poisons. The ministers' accumulation of power reflects the operation of greed (for influence and wealth), hatred (toward rivals and obstacles), and delusion (the belief that political power can provide lasting satisfaction). Buddhist-Chinese interpreters note that these same forces operate at every level of the political system documented in the Annals, from interstate rivalries to court intrigues, confirming the Buddhist diagnosis that worldly suffering has a common root in the fundamental defilements of the unawakened mind.

The diplomatic exchanges of this period are read by Buddhist interpreters as exercises in what Buddhist psychology calls "proliferation" (papanca) — the mind's tendency to generate increasingly complex conceptual elaborations that obscure rather than clarify reality. The intricacy of post-hegemonic diplomacy represents, in Buddhist terms, the proliferation of strategic thinking that moves ever further from the simple, direct awareness of present reality that Buddhist practice cultivates.
