Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Min section reads this briefest portion of the Annals as a concentrated meditation on the impermanence (anicca) of all conditioned phenomena. Duke Min's two-year reign, ending in violent death, illustrates with stark clarity the Buddhist teaching that no worldly position — however exalted — provides lasting security. The rapidity of his fall demonstrates that political power is among the most impermanent of all conditioned states, subject to dissolution at any moment.

Buddhist readers find in the extreme brevity of this section an invitation to contemplate the preciousness and fragility of human life itself. The young Duke Min, thrust into a position of nominal authority in a politically turbulent state, had no opportunity to develop the wisdom or build the alliances that might have preserved his position. Buddhist interpreters draw from this the teaching that human life, like political power, cannot be taken for granted — a reflection that motivates the Buddhist emphasis on making the most of one's present circumstances for spiritual development.

The violence that ended Duke Min's reign is understood by Buddhist-Chinese readers through the framework of collective karma. The political community of Lu had accumulated karmic conditions — through prior acts of violence, factional rivalry, and moral negligence — that made Duke Min's assassination virtually inevitable. Buddhist interpretation thus moves beyond individual moral responsibility to consider the systemic karmic forces that shape the fate of entire communities, applying the Buddhist analysis of causation to the political realm documented in the Annals.
