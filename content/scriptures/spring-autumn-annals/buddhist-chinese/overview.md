## Overview

Chinese Buddhist readers of the Spring and Autumn Annals find in its chronicle of political upheaval, moral decline, and dynastic instability a vivid illustration of Buddhist teachings on impermanence and karmic causation. The 242-year record of the state of Lu, with its recurring patterns of usurpation, assassination, and the erosion of legitimate authority, resonates with the Buddhist understanding of samsara as a realm of ceaseless change and suffering driven by delusion and craving for power.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Chunqiu draws parallels between the Confucian concept of the Mandate of Heaven and the Buddhist understanding of collective karma shaping the fate of political communities. The rise and fall of ruling families, the moral failures of individual dukes and ministers, and the gradual deterioration of the Zhou political order are read as manifestations of karmic processes. Buddhist commentators also note the text's implicit recognition that no political arrangement is permanent — a truth that aligns with the doctrine of impermanence (anicca). The Chunqiu's record of violence and betrayal becomes an occasion for cultivating compassion and reflecting on the suffering inherent in worldly power.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Buddhist-Chinese perspective on the Chunqiu offers resources for reflecting on political violence, the moral consequences of institutional corruption, and the possibility of cultivating wisdom and compassion within the arena of political life.
