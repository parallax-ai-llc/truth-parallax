Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Ding section approaches Confucius's political career with deep respect while maintaining the Buddhist perspective that worldly engagement, however well-intentioned, cannot by itself resolve the fundamental causes of suffering. Confucius's effort to restore moral governance in Lu is understood by Buddhist readers as a noble expression of compassion for the people's welfare — an impulse consistent with the bodhisattva ideal — even as its ultimate failure demonstrates the limitations of political action as a vehicle for genuine transformation.

The Master's attempt to dismantle the fortified cities of the Three Huan families is read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters through the lens of the bodhisattva's engagement with the world. Like the bodhisattva who acts within samsara for the benefit of sentient beings while maintaining awareness that samsaric conditions cannot be permanently perfected, Confucius engaged with the political world knowing that complete success was unlikely. Buddhist readers find in his persistence a form of the bodhisattva's inexhaustible compassion that continues to act despite the recognition of suffering's depth.

Confucius's departure from Lu is understood by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as a moment of renunciation that parallels, in political terms, the Buddha's own departure from his father's palace. Both figures left positions of relative comfort and influence to pursue a larger vision of human welfare. While Confucius's vision remained oriented toward worldly governance rather than spiritual liberation, Buddhist readers honor his willingness to sacrifice personal security for the sake of his convictions.
