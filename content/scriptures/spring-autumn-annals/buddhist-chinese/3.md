Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Zhuang section reads the military campaigns and ritual violations of this thirty-two-year period as manifestations of the collective delusion that sustains political violence. The extensive warfare documented in these entries illustrates the Buddhist teaching that military conflict arises from the fundamental misperception of separate selfhood — the belief that one state's interests are opposed to another's, when in reality all beings are interconnected within the web of interdependent origination.

The ritual violations noted during Duke Zhuang's reign are approached by Buddhist readers with a perspective distinct from both Confucian and Daoist readings. Buddhist interpreters neither mourn the decline of ritual order (as Confucians do) nor dismiss ritual as artificial (as Daoists might). Instead, they understand ritual as a form of mindful practice that, when performed with genuine attention and sincerity, can cultivate wholesome mental states. The decay of ritual represents not merely a social disorder but a loss of the mindfulness and intentionality that give ceremonial practice its transformative power.

The Battle of Changshao is read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as illustrating the Buddhist teaching that patience (kshanti) is a supreme virtue. Cao Gui's strategy of patient waiting, while applied to military ends that Buddhism cannot endorse, demonstrates the practical power of the mental quality that Buddhist practice cultivates for spiritual purposes. This reading exemplifies the Buddhist-Chinese approach of extracting spiritual lessons from worldly contexts, recognizing partial truths within the limitations of political action.
