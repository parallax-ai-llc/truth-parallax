Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Xiang section, the longest in the chronicle, reads this period's extraordinary political complexity as a meditation on the Buddhist concept of interdependent origination (pratityasamutpada) operating at the scale of interstate relations. The dense web of alliances, conflicts, negotiations, and transformations documented over thirty-one years illustrates the Buddhist teaching that all phenomena arise in dependence upon conditions, are sustained by conditions, and cease when conditions change.

The sheer volume of events recorded during Duke Xiang's reign provides Buddhist interpreters with extensive material for tracing the chains of causation that link political actions to their consequences across time and space. Each entry in the Annals is understood not as an isolated event but as a node in an interconnected web of causes and effects, illustrating the Buddhist analysis of conditioned arising with unprecedented historical detail. This perspective transforms the Annals from a record of discrete events into a map of the causal networks that constitute samsaric existence at the political level.

Zichan's legal reforms are read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters through the concept of skillful means (upaya). While codified law does not represent the ultimate Buddhist ideal of governance through wisdom and compassion, Buddhist readers recognize it as a pragmatic response to conditions of moral decline — a "conventional truth" that serves beneficial purposes within the framework of worldly governance even as it falls short of the "ultimate truth" of awakened awareness.
