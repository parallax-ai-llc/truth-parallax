Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Huan section reads the fratricidal succession and its aftermath as a textbook illustration of how the karma generated by violent action perpetuates cycles of suffering. Duke Huan's rise to power through the assassination of Duke Yin creates what Buddhist philosophy calls "unwholesome karma" — a force that conditions future events in predictable and destructive patterns. His own assassination eighteen years later represents the maturation of this karma, demonstrating the Buddhist teaching that violent actions inevitably return suffering to the actor.

The interstate conflicts recorded during this period are understood by Buddhist readers as collective expressions of the three poisons at the political level. Greed for territory and resources, hatred toward rival states, and delusion about the possibility of achieving security through military force drive the conflicts that the Annals meticulously document. Buddhist-Chinese interpreters note that these collective afflictions mirror the individual mental defilements that Buddhist practice seeks to overcome, suggesting that political transformation requires the same inner work as personal liberation.

The pattern of violence and counter-violence that characterizes the Duke Huan entries provides Buddhist readers with compelling evidence for the Buddhist analysis of samsara as a self-perpetuating cycle. Each act of violence generates the conditions for further violence, creating a chain of suffering that can only be broken through the cultivation of wisdom and compassion rather than through more effective applications of political or military power.
