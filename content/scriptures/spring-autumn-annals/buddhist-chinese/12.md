Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Ai section reads the chronicle's conclusion as a meditation on impermanence, loss, and the limits of worldly accomplishment. The final entries document a political world in advanced decline, and the ending of the Annals with the capture of the qilin is understood by Buddhist readers as a symbol of the fundamental unsatisfactoriness (dukkha) of conditioned existence — a moment when even Confucius, the greatest sage of the Chinese tradition, confronted the inadequacy of his efforts to transform the political world.

Confucius's weeping at the capture of the qilin is read by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters as a moment of profound grief that approaches the Buddhist recognition of universal suffering. Buddhist readers see in the Master's tears not merely mourning for the decline of the political order but a deeper awareness of the suffering inherent in all worldly existence. This moment of compassionate grief connects Confucius, in the Buddhist reading, to the bodhisattva's universal compassion that responds to the suffering of all sentient beings.

The ending of the Annals is understood by Buddhist readers as illustrating the Buddhist teaching that all things come to an end — even the most ambitious projects of documentation and moral instruction. The silence that follows the last entry of the chronicle represents, in Buddhist perspective, the cessation of one form of teaching and the opening of space for new forms of wisdom. Buddhist-Chinese interpreters find in this silence an invitation to move beyond the historical record into the realm of direct spiritual practice, where the suffering documented in 242 years of political history can be addressed at its root through the cultivation of wisdom and compassion.
