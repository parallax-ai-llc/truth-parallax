Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Zhao section reads the exile of the duke as a dramatic illustration of the Buddhist teaching on the reversal of fortune that characterizes samsaric existence. Duke Zhao's transformation from nominal ruler to exile demonstrates the radical impermanence of worldly status and the suffering that accompanies attachment to positions of power. Buddhist readers find in his experience a compelling argument for cultivating the inner equanimity that remains stable regardless of external circumstances.

The dominance of the Three Huan families is understood by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters through the concept of the "wheel of becoming" (bhavachakra). The ministerial families' accumulation of power is driven by the same forces of craving, clinging, and becoming that perpetuate the cycle of samsara at the individual level. Their political dominance, achieved through the displacement of the ducal house, does not represent liberation from the cycle but merely a different position within it — subject to the same forces of impermanence and suffering that afflicted the authority they displaced.

The Buddhist reading of this section emphasizes the universality of suffering across political positions. Duke Zhao suffers in exile, but the Three Huan families suffer the anxiety of maintaining their contested authority. Buddhist interpreters note that neither the powerful nor the powerless escape suffering within the political realm, reinforcing the Buddhist teaching that genuine freedom requires transcending the entire framework of worldly status rather than achieving a more favorable position within it.
