Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Cheng section reads the continued decline of ducal authority as a profound lesson in the Buddhist teaching of non-self (anatta) applied to political institutions. The ducal authority of Lu, which once appeared substantial and enduring, is revealed through the Annals' documentation to be an empty construct — dependent on conditions, lacking inherent existence, and subject to dissolution when its supporting conditions change. Buddhist readers find in this political dissolution a concrete illustration of the teaching that all phenomena, including seemingly permanent institutions, lack inherent self-nature.

The military engagements recorded during Duke Cheng's reign are read by Buddhist interpreters as generating collective unwholesome karma that accumulates across generations. Each battle produces death, injury, and displacement that create conditions of suffering extending far beyond the immediate combatants. Buddhist-Chinese readers emphasize that the Annals' terse recording of military events conceals an ocean of human suffering that should motivate compassion and a commitment to peace.

The entries documenting Lu's diminished interstate standing are understood by Buddhist readers as illustrating the teaching on the eight worldly concerns (gain and loss, fame and disrepute, praise and blame, pleasure and pain). The political actors documented in these entries are trapped in reactive patterns driven by these worldly concerns, unable to find equanimity because their sense of well-being depends entirely on external conditions. Buddhist interpretation finds in this political documentation a powerful argument for the cultivation of inner peace independent of worldly circumstances.
