Buddhist-Chinese interpretation of the Duke Xi section and the hegemonic era reads the rise of interstate alliances and the hegemonic system through the Buddhist understanding of conditioned co-arising. The political stability achieved under the hegemons is understood as a conditioned state — dependent upon specific causes and conditions that are themselves impermanent. Buddhist readers note that the hegemonic order, however impressive, carried within it the seeds of its own dissolution, illustrating the Buddhist principle that all compounded phenomena are inherently subject to decay.

The figure of the hegemon is analyzed by Buddhist-Chinese interpreters through comparison with the Buddhist concept of the cakravartin, the wheel-turning universal monarch. Both the Chinese hegemon and the Buddhist cakravartin maintain order through a combination of moral authority and practical power. However, Buddhist teaching makes clear that even the cakravartin's achievements are impermanent and that worldly sovereignty, however just, cannot substitute for the ultimate liberation that only spiritual awakening provides. The hegemons of the Spring and Autumn period thus represent, in Buddhist terms, the highest achievement possible within the realm of samsaric governance.

The interstate conferences and oaths recorded during this period are read by Buddhist interpreters as attempts to create stability through mutual commitments — a political analogue of the Buddhist practice of taking precepts. Buddhist readers note that political oaths, like spiritual vows, require ongoing cultivation and mindful attention to maintain their effectiveness. The eventual breakdown of hegemonic agreements parallels the Buddhist observation that ethical commitments require constant renewal through practice.
