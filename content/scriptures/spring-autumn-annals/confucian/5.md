Confucian interpretation of the Duke Xi section engages extensively with the rise of the hegemonic system (ba) that defined the political landscape of the mid-Spring and Autumn period. Duke Xi's thirty-three-year reign coincides with the era of the great hegemons, particularly Duke Huan of Qi and Duke Wen of Jin, and Confucian readers find in the Annals' treatment of these figures a nuanced moral assessment of the tension between hegemonic power and legitimate royal authority.

The Confucian tradition is deeply ambivalent about the hegemons. On one hand, figures like Duke Huan of Qi, guided by his minister Guan Zhong, maintained interstate order and defended Chinese civilization against peripheral threats. On the other hand, the hegemonic system represented a de facto usurpation of the Zhou king's authority, substituting the power of individual states for the moral authority of the legitimate sovereign. Confucius's language in the Annals is read as carefully calibrating his recognition of the hegemons' achievements with his disapproval of their implicit challenge to the ritual order.

The interstate conferences and alliances recorded during Duke Xi's reign receive detailed Confucian moral analysis. The Gongyang Commentary pays particular attention to the language used to describe these diplomatic events, arguing that variations in terminology reflect Confucius's judgment on whether each conference served the cause of legitimate order or merely advanced the hegemon's self-interest. These entries establish the Confucian framework for evaluating political alliances according to moral rather than purely strategic criteria.
