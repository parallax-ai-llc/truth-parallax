## Overview

The Confucian tradition regards the Spring and Autumn Annals as Confucius's crowning intellectual achievement — a work that, through deliberate choices of wording, embeds moral judgments on 242 years of history. Mencius famously declared that "Confucius completed the Spring and Autumn and rebellious ministers and villainous sons were struck with terror." The text is understood not as mere chronicle but as a work of moral legislation through naming, demonstrating that the careful use of language constitutes ethical action.

## Interpretive Approach

Confucian interpretation of the Chunqiu has traditionally relied on the three major commentaries: the Zuozhuan, the Gongyang, and the Guliang. The Gongyang Commentary in particular developed elaborate theories about Confucius's hidden meanings, arguing that variations in terminology — such as whether a ruler's death is recorded as "died" or "was killed" — encode precise moral verdicts. The principle of "praise and blame through a single word" (yi zi baobian) became the hallmark of Confucian historical writing. The Zuozhuan provides narrative context that makes the terse annalistic entries comprehensible, while the Guliang offers its own interpretive traditions emphasizing ritual propriety.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Confucian approach to the Chunqiu continues to inform discussions about the ethics of historical writing, the power of naming in public discourse, and the responsibilities of historians and journalists to embed moral clarity in their accounts of events.
