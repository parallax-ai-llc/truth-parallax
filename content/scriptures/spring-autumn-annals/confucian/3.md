Confucian interpretation of the Duke Zhuang section focuses on the moral significance of the military campaigns and ritual violations that characterize this thirty-two-year reign. Duke Zhuang's era coincides with the increasing assertiveness of the state of Qi under Duke Huan of Qi, and Confucian readers find in the Annals' treatment of inter-state relations during this period a careful calibration of praise and blame that reflects Confucius's moral assessment of each state's conduct.

The major military engagements recorded under Duke Zhuang receive detailed Confucian analysis for their moral coding. The famous Battle of Changshao (684 BCE), in which Lu defeated Qi through the strategist Cao Gui's counsel to wait for the enemy's morale to decline, is treated by the Zuozhuan as an example of how wisdom and patience can compensate for inferior military strength — a lesson consistent with the Confucian preference for moral and intellectual resources over brute force.

The ritual violations noted during Duke Zhuang's reign are read by Confucian interpreters as symptoms of the broader erosion of the Zhou ritual order. Confucius's recording of irregularities in sacrificial practice, diplomatic protocol, and court ceremony serves not merely as historical documentation but as moral commentary. Each departure from proper ritual represents, in the Confucian reading, a step in the decline from the idealized order of the early Zhou toward the moral chaos of the later Spring and Autumn period.
