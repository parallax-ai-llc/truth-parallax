Confucian interpretation of the Duke Zhao section reads the dramatic events of this thirty-two-year period — including the duke's exile from his own state — as the culmination of the moral decline traced throughout the Annals. Duke Zhao's forced departure from Lu in 517 BCE, driven out by the Three Huan families who had accumulated effective control of the state, represents for Confucian readers the ultimate consequence of the erosion of legitimate authority that the chronicle has been documenting.

The exile of Duke Zhao provides Confucian commentators with their most pointed illustration of the rectification of names (zhengming). The Annals' treatment of the exile — how it records Duke Zhao's movements, how it refers to the Three Huan families' actions, and how it describes the political status of Lu during the duke's absence — is analyzed with extraordinary care for evidence of Confucius's moral judgment. The Confucian tradition reads these entries as encoding both a condemnation of the usurping families and a nuanced assessment of Duke Zhao's own moral responsibility for losing his position.

The Duke Zhao period is also significant for Confucian readers because it overlaps with Confucius's own life and early career. The entries for this section therefore carry a special weight, as they document events that Confucius witnessed directly rather than reconstructed from earlier records. Confucian commentators debate whether the Annals' moral coding becomes more precise and pointed during this period, reflecting the Master's personal experience of the political disorders he recorded.
