Confucian interpretation of the Duke Xiang section, the longest in the Annals covering thirty-one years, treats this extended reign as the period in which the moral decline of the Spring and Autumn era reached its most complex and consequential phase. Duke Xiang's reign coincides with transformative events across the Chinese world, and the density of the annalistic entries for this period provides Confucian commentators with their richest material for demonstrating the moral logic of the Chunqiu.

The Confucian tradition finds in the Duke Xiang entries a comprehensive display of the Annals' system of moral coding. Interstate conferences, military campaigns, diplomatic marriages, succession disputes, and ritual performances are all recorded with the precise terminological distinctions through which Confucius is understood to have embedded his moral verdicts. The Gongyang and Guliang commentaries offer their most elaborate interpretive arguments for passages in this section, debating the significance of individual word choices and grammatical constructions.

The Duke Xiang period includes events of lasting significance for Confucian political thought, including the rise of the Zichan as the effective ruler of Zheng. Zichan's attempts to reform governance through codified law and rational administration are treated ambivalently by the Confucian tradition, which values his commitment to public welfare while questioning whether legal codification can substitute for the moral influence that proper ritual governance should provide. The debates surrounding Zichan's reforms remain relevant to Confucian discussions of law and morality.
