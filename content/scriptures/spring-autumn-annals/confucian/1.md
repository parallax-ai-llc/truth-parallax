Confucian interpretation of the Duke Yin section treats these opening entries as establishing the moral framework for the entire chronicle. Duke Yin's eleven-year reign is understood as a period of ambiguous legitimacy that allowed Confucius to demonstrate his principles of praise and blame from the very first entry. The Gongyang Commentary explains that Duke Yin was a regent who governed on behalf of his younger brother, the rightful heir, and that Confucius's treatment of this situation establishes the Annals' fundamental concern with legitimacy and proper succession.

The opening entry of the Chunqiu — recording the first year of Duke Yin, spring, the king's first month — is read by Confucian commentators as laden with moral significance. The reference to the "king's first month" affirms the authority of the Zhou royal house even as the chronicle focuses on the state of Lu, establishing the Confucian principle that local political life should be understood in relation to the legitimate universal order. Every subsequent entry is measured against the standards established in this inaugural record.

Duke Yin's assassination in 712 BCE provides the first major instance of the Annals' moral coding. The Confucian tradition reads Confucius's choice of language in recording this event as encoding a precise moral verdict on the assassin and the political circumstances that led to the killing. The pattern of moral judgment through naming conventions established here governs the interpretation of all subsequent entries in the chronicle.
