Confucian interpretation of the Duke Xuan section focuses on the deepening internal factional struggles within the state of Lu and their moral implications. Duke Xuan's reign marks a further stage in the erosion of ducal authority, as the Three Huan families — descended from Duke Huan — consolidated their grip on the real levers of power. Confucian readers find in the Annals' treatment of this period a sustained indictment of the usurpation of legitimate authority by powerful families.

The Confucian tradition reads Confucius's language in the Duke Xuan entries as encoding moral judgments on the factional politics that increasingly dominated Lu's governance. When the Annals record actions taken by ministerial families rather than the duke, Confucian commentators detect in the phrasing a deliberate commentary on the inversion of proper political relationships. The principle of "rectification of names" (zhengming) operates throughout these entries, as Confucius uses nomenclature to distinguish between legitimate authority and its usurpation.

The interstate relations recorded during Duke Xuan's reign reflect the broader disintegration of the moral order that the Confucian tradition traces throughout the Spring and Autumn period. The increasing frequency of treaty violations, surprise attacks, and diplomatic betrayals documented in these entries serves, for Confucian readers, as evidence that the abandonment of ritual propriety in domestic governance inevitably produces disorder in international relations as well. The moral coherence of the political order depends upon consistent adherence to principle at every level.
