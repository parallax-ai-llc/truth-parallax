Confucian interpretation of the Duke Min section, the briefest in the entire chronicle covering only two years, treats this truncated reign as a concentrated expression of the political disorder that results from the breakdown of ritual propriety and moral governance. Duke Min's accession at a young age and his murder after just two years on the throne exemplify the vulnerability of political authority when it lacks the moral foundations that the Confucian tradition insists are essential for stable governance.

The very brevity of the Duke Min section carries moral significance for Confucian readers. The extreme compression of the annalistic record for this period conveys, through its formal properties, the sense of a polity in crisis. The Zuozhuan provides the narrative context that the terse entries merely hint at: factional violence, the machinations of powerful ministers, and the collapse of the norms that should protect the ruler's person. Confucian interpreters read these events as the culmination of the moral decline traced through the preceding reigns.

The Gongyang Commentary draws from the Duke Min entries important principles about the relationship between political stability and moral order. The ease with which Duke Min was deposed and killed demonstrates, for Confucian readers, that political power without moral authority is inherently fragile. This section serves as a cautionary lesson about the consequences of allowing ritual propriety and moral cultivation to decay, reinforcing the Confucian argument that virtue, not force, is the true foundation of political stability.
