Confucian interpretation of the Duke Ai section, the final portion of the Spring and Autumn Annals, reads these entries as the chronicle's somber conclusion, composed during the last years of Confucius's life. The Annals end in 481 BCE with the capture of a qilin (unicorn), an event that the Confucian tradition interprets as a cosmic portent marking the end of an era. Confucius is said to have wept upon hearing of the qilin's capture, recognizing in this inauspicious sign the definitive failure of his mission to restore the Way.

The Duke Ai entries document a world in which the political disorders that the Annals have traced reach their most extreme expression. The erosion of legitimate authority, the dominance of ministerial families, and the disintegration of the interstate moral order established by the early Zhou are all present in these final entries. Confucian readers understand the chronicle's conclusion not as a natural stopping point but as a moral statement: the Master ceased writing because the state of the world had reached a point where the moral categories of the Annals could no longer adequately address the depth of the disorder.

The Gongyang Commentary's interpretive tradition, known as the "three ages" theory, finds in the Annals' overall arc a movement from the age of "what was transmitted" (the earliest entries) through the age of "what was heard" to the age of "what was personally witnessed." This theory suggests that Confucius's moral judgments become increasingly precise and detailed as the chronicle approaches his own time, reaching their fullest expression in these final entries before falling silent.
