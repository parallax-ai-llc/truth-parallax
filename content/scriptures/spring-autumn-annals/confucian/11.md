Confucian interpretation of the Duke Ding section holds special significance because this period includes Confucius's own brief tenure in political office in the state of Lu. According to traditional accounts, Confucius served as Minister of Crime and even as acting prime minister during Duke Ding's reign, giving him direct experience of the political challenges he had previously observed as a scholar and teacher. Confucian readers find in the Annals' entries for this period traces of the Master's own political program and his frustration at its failure.

The events surrounding Confucius's political career are scrutinized by Confucian commentators for evidence of the principles he sought to implement. His attempt to dismantle the fortified cities of the Three Huan families — a direct challenge to the ministerial power that had usurped ducal authority — represents the Confucian program of restoring legitimate political order through the rectification of names and the reassertion of proper hierarchical relationships. The failure of this effort, which led to Confucius's departure from Lu, is understood as a defining moment in the Confucian tradition.

The Confucian tradition reads the Duke Ding section with awareness that Confucius was simultaneously participant in and chronicler of the events he recorded. This dual role intensifies the moral weight of the entries, as each recorded event is understood to reflect not only the Master's historical judgment but also his personal experience of political engagement and disillusionment. The entries for this period thus carry an autobiographical dimension absent from the rest of the chronicle.
