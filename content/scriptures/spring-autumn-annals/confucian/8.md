Confucian interpretation of the Duke Cheng section reads the eighteen years of this reign as documenting the continued decline of ducal authority and the rise of ministerial power to a point where the nominal ruler became increasingly irrelevant to the actual governance of Lu. Confucian readers find in the Annals' treatment of this period a deepening moral tragedy, as the institutional framework of legitimate governance is progressively hollowed out from within.

The military and diplomatic entries during Duke Cheng's reign are analyzed by Confucian commentators for their reflection of Lu's diminished status within the interstate system. Confucius's language in recording Lu's participation in coalitions led by Jin is scrutinized for indications of whether Lu acted as a willing ally or a reluctant subordinate. The Confucian reading finds in these entries evidence of the moral consequences of internal political decay: a state that cannot maintain proper order within its own borders inevitably loses standing and autonomy in its external relations.

The Zuozhuan's narrative for the Duke Cheng period includes several episodes that became canonical examples in Confucian moral discourse. Stories of loyalty and betrayal, proper and improper conduct in battle, and the moral reasoning of statesmen provide the Confucian tradition with a rich gallery of exemplary and cautionary figures. Confucian readers treat these narratives as complementary to the Annals' terse entries, providing the moral context that transforms bare historical facts into ethical instruction.
