Confucian interpretation of the Duke Wen section reads these eighteen years of annalistic entries as documenting a period of political consolidation in which the ducal house of Lu attempted to stabilize its position through diplomatic maneuvering within the evolving interstate system. The post-hegemonic era required states to navigate complex webs of alliance and rivalry, and Confucian readers find in the Annals' treatment of Duke Wen's diplomacy a moral assessment of the strategies employed to maintain political survival.

The Confucian tradition pays particular attention to the entries recording Duke Wen's relationships with the major powers of Jin and Chu. The moral coding of diplomatic exchanges — whether a meeting is recorded as a "conference" or an "audience," whether the duke is described as "going to" or "being summoned by" another state — reveals, according to Confucian interpretation, Confucius's assessment of the relative dignity and moral standing of the parties involved. Each terminological choice carries weight in the Annals' system of moral historiography.

The internal politics of Lu during Duke Wen's reign also receive Confucian moral scrutiny. The growing power of ministerial families at the expense of ducal authority is traced through the Annals' entries and elaborated in the Zuozhuan's narratives. Confucian readers understand this shift as a critical stage in the process of political deterioration that would eventually render the dukes of Lu mere figureheads — a development that confirmed the Confucian diagnosis that political order depends upon the maintenance of proper hierarchical relationships defined by ritual.
