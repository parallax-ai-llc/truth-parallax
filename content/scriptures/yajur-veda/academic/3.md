The Agnichayana chapter has attracted extraordinary scholarly interest since Frits Staal's documentation of the 1975 performance of the ritual in Kerala — the first full Agnichayana in decades and possibly the oldest surviving ritual of humanity performed in its traditional form. Staal's multi-volume *Agni* (1983) provides a comprehensive record of the ritual's geometry, mantras, and performance.

The mathematical knowledge embedded in the Agnichayana — particularly the geometric specifications in the Shulba Sutras — has been studied by historians of mathematics including A. Seidenberg, who controversially argued that Vedic geometry may have influenced Greek mathematics rather than the reverse. The altar's use of the Pythagorean theorem, the construction of squares equal in area to given circles, and the transformation of shapes demonstrate sophisticated spatial reasoning.
