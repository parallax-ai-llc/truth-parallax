## Overview

The Yajur Veda contains the most important liturgical text in all of Shaivism: the Sri Rudram (also called Rudra Prashna or Namakam), found in the fourth chapter of the Krishna Yajur Veda's Taittiriya Samhita. Together with the Chamakam that follows it, the Sri Rudram constitutes the supreme Shaiva liturgical prayer, chanted daily in Shiva temples across India and forming the heart of the Rudra Abhisheka ceremony.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Sri Rudram as Shiva's Self-Revelation
The Sri Rudram's eleven anuvaka (sections) enumerate Rudra-Shiva's presence in every aspect of existence — from the lord of cattle to the indweller of thieves, from the gentle healer to the terrible destroyer. Shaivas read this not as a list of attributes but as a revelation that Shiva pervades all beings and all conditions without exception.

### Chamakam and Divine Blessings
The Chamakam ("I desire") that follows the Rudram is a comprehensive prayer for every form of worldly and spiritual blessing. Together, the Namakam (salutations) and Chamakam (desires) represent the complete Shaiva liturgical cycle of worship and grace.

## Theological Emphases

- **Shiva in All Things**: The Rudram reveals Shiva as immanent in every being, every social role, every natural phenomenon
- **Liturgical Centrality**: No Shaiva temple ritual is complete without the recitation of the Rudram
- **Abhisheka**: The Rudram accompanies the ceremonial bathing of the Shiva Linga, the central act of Shaiva worship
- **Panchakshara**: The five-syllable mantra "Namah Shivaya" has its Vedic root in the Rudram's repeated "Namo" salutations
