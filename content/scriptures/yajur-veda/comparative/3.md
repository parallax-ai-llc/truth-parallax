The Agnichayana fire altar, constructed as a cosmic model in the shape of a bird, compares with sacred architecture traditions that seek to replicate cosmic structure in the built environment. Solomon's Temple as a microcosm, the Buddhist stupa as a model of the cosmos, the Gothic cathedral as an image of the heavenly Jerusalem, and the Islamic mosque oriented toward Mecca all share the Agnichayana's principle that sacred space should mirror sacred reality.

The mathematical knowledge embedded in the fire altar's construction compares with the sacred geometry of other traditions: the Golden Ratio in Greek temple architecture, the geometric patterns of Islamic art, the proportional systems of Gothic cathedrals, and the astronomical alignments of Stonehenge and the Egyptian pyramids. The Vedic contribution to this universal tradition of sacred geometry is among the earliest documented.
