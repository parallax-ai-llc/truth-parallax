The Darsha-Purnamasa new and full moon rites compare with lunar religious observances across cultures: the Jewish calendar's lunar cycle determining festivals, the Islamic determination of Ramadan by moon sighting, the Buddhist observance of *uposatha* on full and new moon days, and Chinese and East Asian lunar festival traditions. The universal human practice of marking lunar phases with religious ceremony reflects the deep connection between celestial observation and sacred time.

The basic structure of the Darsha-Purnamasa — fire, offering, prayer, and communal participation — constitutes a pattern of sacrifice found across ancient religions: the Greek *thusia*, the Roman *sacrificium*, the Israelite *korban*, and the Chinese *ji* all share these fundamental elements. The Yajur Veda's detailed documentation of this basic sacrificial pattern provides a unique comparative baseline for understanding sacrifice as a cross-cultural religious phenomenon.
