The Rajasuya and Ashvamedha royal rituals compare with coronation and sovereignty ceremonies across cultures: the anointing of Israelite kings described in the Hebrew Bible, the coronation rites of European monarchs, the enthronement of Japanese emperors, and the installation of African chiefs all share the principle that political authority requires ritual legitimation. The Yajur Veda's royal formulas provide one of the most detailed ancient records of this universal practice.

The Ashvamedha horse sacrifice invites comparison with animal sacrifice in the assertion of sovereignty across cultures. The Roman *suovetaurilia*, the Norse blot, and the Aztec sacrifice of jaguars all use animal sacrifice as a means of establishing or renewing royal and cosmic power. The comparative study of these rites illuminates the deep structural connections between sacrifice, sovereignty, and the maintenance of cosmic order.
