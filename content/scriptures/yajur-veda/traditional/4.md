The royal ritual formulas of the Yajur Veda cover two of the most spectacular ceremonies in Vedic religion: the Rajasuya (royal consecration) and the Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice). The Rajasuya establishes a king's sovereignty through a year-long series of rituals including symbolic chariot races, cattle raids, and the ritual 'rebirth' of the king as the lord of all directions.

The Ashvamedha, in which a consecrated horse wanders freely for a year before being sacrificed, is the supreme assertion of royal power. The Yajur Veda's formulas for these rites encode a political theology in which the king's authority derives from cosmic order (*rta*) and is maintained through ritual action. Traditional commentators explain that these ceremonies do not merely symbolize sovereignty but actually create and sustain it through the power of the mantras.
