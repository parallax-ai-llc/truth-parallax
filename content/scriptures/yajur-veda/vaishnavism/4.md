The Rajasuya and Ashvamedha royal rites are interpreted by Vaishnavas as demonstrations of Vishnu's supreme sovereignty. The earthly king's authority is derivative, granted by the Lord through the ritual process. The Ashvamedha horse, which wanders freely asserting the king's dominion, symbolizes Vishnu's own unchallenged sovereignty over all the worlds — a sovereignty that the earthly king participates in but does not independently possess.

Vaishnava theology reads the royal rituals as prefiguring the Lord's own royal pastimes: Rama's Ashvamedha in the Ramayana and the Pandavas' Rajasuya in the Mahabharata connect these Vedic rituals to the Lord's avatara activities. The Yajurvedic formulas for royal consecration thus acquire narrative resonance within the larger Vaishnava scriptural universe.
