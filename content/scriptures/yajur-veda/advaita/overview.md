## Overview

The Yajur Veda is of paramount importance to Advaita Vedanta because it is the parent text of two of the most significant Upanishads: the Isha Upanishad (from the Shukla Yajur Veda) and the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad (the largest and among the oldest of all Upanishads). Shankara's commentaries on both are foundational texts of the Advaita school, and the Brihadaranyaka's mahavakya "Aham Brahmasmi" ("I am Brahman") is one of the four great statements of non-dual identity.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Isha Upanishad
The Isha Upanishad's opening verse — "All this is pervaded by the Lord" — establishes the non-dual vision at the very conclusion of the Shukla Yajur Veda Samhita. Shankara reads this as the definitive teaching that transcends and completes the ritual content of the preceding chapters.

### Inner Sacrifice
Advaitins interpret the Yajur Veda's elaborate sacrificial system metaphorically: the true sacrifice is the offering of the ego into the fire of self-knowledge. The external yajna purifies the mind and prepares the aspirant for the internal realization that the sacrificer, the sacrifice, and the deity are all one Brahman.

## Theological Emphases

- **Aham Brahmasmi**: The Brihadaranyaka's great statement is the culmination of Yajurvedic teaching
- **Isha Upanishad**: Bridges the ritual and knowledge sections as the Samhita's final chapter
- **Ritualism Transcended**: The elaborate sacrifices are preparatory; knowledge alone liberates
- **Yajnavalkya**: The greatest teacher of the Brihadaranyaka, a model of the Advaitic sage
