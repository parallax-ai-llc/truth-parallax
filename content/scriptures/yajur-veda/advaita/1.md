Advaitins interpret the Darsha-Purnamasa rites as the entry point of a spiritual discipline that ultimately transcends itself. The regular performance of these twice-monthly sacrifices purifies the mind (*chitta-shuddhi*) and cultivates the dispassion (*vairagya*) necessary for the higher knowledge of Brahman. The formulas' rhythmic recitation itself becomes a meditative practice that stills the mental agitation that obscures non-dual awareness.

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, which concludes the Shukla Yajur Veda, reinterprets the basic sacrificial acts symbolically: the offering into fire becomes the offering of the ego into the fire of knowledge. Advaitins thus read the Darsha-Purnamasa formulas as simultaneously performing their ritual function and pointing toward the higher sacrifice of self-knowledge.
