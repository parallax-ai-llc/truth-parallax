## Chapter 69 — The Names and Deeds of the Fallen Angels

1 Enoch 69 describes a heavenly figure — the "Son of Man" or "Elect One" — who exercises divine judgment and receives the worship of angels. Muslim scholars engage with this material through the lens of Islamic monotheism (*tawhid*), which affirms that judgment belongs to Allah alone and that no created being shares in divine prerogatives.

## Islamic Scholarly Perspective

The exalted Son of Man figure in this chapter raises important theological questions from an Islamic perspective. Islam affirms Isa (Jesus) as a prophet and messiah (*al-Masih*) but rejects the Christian theological development that identified Jesus with a divine, pre-existent Son of Man figure. The Enochic Son of Man traditions illustrate the pre-Christian Jewish speculations that Christianity later developed in directions Islam considers incompatible with pure monotheism.

Muslim scholars note that the Quran's own eschatological passages describe Allah as the sole judge on the Day of Resurrection (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*), with prophets serving as witnesses rather than judges (Surah an-Nisa 4:41).

## Comparative Observations

The theme of divine judgment central to this chapter resonates strongly with Quranic eschatology. Both traditions affirm the reality of final judgment, the vindication of the righteous, and the punishment of the wicked — though they differ on the identity and role of the eschatological judge.
