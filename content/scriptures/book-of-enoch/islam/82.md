## Chapter 82 — The Calendar and the Stars

1 Enoch 82 belongs to the Astronomical Book (chapters 72-82), presenting detailed cosmological teachings about the divinely ordained movements of celestial bodies. Muslim scholars engage with this material through the Quranic affirmation of cosmic order as a sign (*ayah*) of Allah's creative power and wisdom.

## Islamic Scholarly Perspective

The Astronomical Book's vision of celestial bodies governed by divine command resonates with the Quran's frequent references to the sun, moon, and stars as signs of Allah's sovereignty (Surah Ya-Sin 36:38-40). This chapter's cosmological framework, while reflecting ancient astronomical assumptions, embodies the theological principle — shared with Islam — that the natural order testifies to the Creator's wisdom and power. Islamic astronomers of the Golden Age, including al-Biruni and al-Tusi, engaged with cosmological traditions from multiple cultures while maintaining the Quranic insistence on divine creative authority.

## Comparative Observations

Muslim scholars note that while the Quran does not depend on Enochic astronomy, both traditions share the conviction that celestial phenomena are governed by divine decree rather than operating autonomously. The angelic mediation of cosmic forces described in this chapter finds a parallel in the Islamic understanding of angels (*mala'ika*) as servants of Allah who execute divine commands throughout creation.
