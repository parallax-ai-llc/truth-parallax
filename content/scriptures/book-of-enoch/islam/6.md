## The Fall of the Watchers and Harut and Marut

The Watcher narrative of 1 Enoch 6 finds a significant parallel in Quran 2:102, which mentions two angels, Harut and Marut, sent to Babylon. Classical Islamic commentators, particularly al-Tabari, preserve extensive narratives about these angels that closely mirror the Enochic account of the Watchers' descent and their teaching of forbidden knowledge.

## Key Themes

### The Harut-Marut Connection
Al-Tabari's *Tarikh* and *Tafsir* preserve a detailed account remarkably similar to 1 Enoch 6-8: angels descend to earth, become enamored of a beautiful woman (named Zuhra/Venus), and teach humanity forbidden knowledge including magic and sorcery. Islamic scholars debate whether these traditions derive from Jewish *isra'iliyyat* or reflect independent prophetic knowledge.

### Angelic Impeccability
The Watcher narrative raises a fundamental theological question in Islam: can angels sin? The majority Sunni position, following the Ash'ari school, holds that angels are inherently obedient (*ma'sum*) and incapable of sin. This creates a tension with the Harut-Marut tradition that some scholars resolve by arguing the angels were tested but did not actually fall, while others maintain they were given free will as a special divine test.

### Forbidden Knowledge
Both 1 Enoch 8 and the Harut-Marut tradition emphasize the teaching of sorcery (*sihr*) as a primary transgression. Islamic law unanimously condemns sorcery as *haram*, and some scholars connect this prohibition directly to the angelic tradition.

## Theological Significance

For Islamic readers, the parallel between the Watcher narrative and the Harut-Marut tradition illustrates the shared Abrahamic heritage of prophetic narratives while raising important theological questions about angelic nature, divine testing, and the prohibition of occult knowledge.
