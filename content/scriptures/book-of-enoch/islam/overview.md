## Overview

Islamic tradition engages with the Enochic legacy primarily through the figure of Idris, whom the Quran mentions twice (19:56-57; 21:85-86) and whom most classical commentators identify with the biblical Enoch. While 1 Enoch itself is not a recognized text in Islam, numerous Enochic themes — the heavenly ascent, angelic rebellion, and the transmission of forbidden knowledge — appear in Islamic sources through the channels of *isra'iliyyat* (Israelite traditions) and independent prophetic narratives.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Quranic Identification
The Quran describes Idris as "a man of truth, a prophet" who was "raised to a high station" (19:56-57). Classical commentators such as al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir identified this "raising" with Enoch's translation to heaven (Genesis 5:24), connecting it to the elaborate heavenly journeys described in 1 Enoch.

### Harut and Marut Tradition
Quran 2:102 mentions two angels, Harut and Marut, sent to Babylon who taught people magic. Muslim scholars have long noted the parallel with the Watchers of 1 Enoch who descend and teach forbidden arts. Al-Tabari's commentary preserves extensive narratives that closely mirror the Enochic Watcher tradition.

### Prophetic Heavenly Ascent
Muhammad's Night Journey (*Isra' wa Mi'raj*) shares structural elements with Enoch's cosmic travels: guided tours through multiple heavens, encounters with angels, and visions of paradise and punishment.

## Theological Emphases

- **Prophetic Status**: Idris/Enoch is honored as a prophet in Islamic tradition
- **Angelic Nature**: The Harut-Marut tradition raises questions about whether angels can sin
- **Heavenly Knowledge**: Enoch/Idris as a transmitter of divine wisdom, including writing and astronomy
- **Eschatological Visions**: Parallels between Enochic judgment scenes and Quranic descriptions of the Last Day
