## Overview

Protestant interpretation of 1 Enoch is shaped by the tension between the text's exclusion from the biblical canon and its undeniable influence on the New Testament. The Epistle of Jude directly quotes 1 Enoch 1:9, and numerous Enochic themes — angelic rebellion, eschatological judgment, the Son of Man — permeate the Gospels, Pauline epistles, and Revelation. Protestants therefore engage 1 Enoch as a vital witness to Second Temple Jewish thought without granting it canonical authority.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historical-Contextual
Most Protestant scholars treat 1 Enoch as essential background literature for understanding the New Testament world. The text illuminates the Jewish apocalyptic matrix from which Christianity emerged, particularly the development of angelology, demonology, and messianic expectation.

### Conservative Evangelical
Some evangelicals approach 1 Enoch cautiously, acknowledging Jude's quotation while insisting this does not confer canonical status. They often compare Enochic traditions with Genesis 6:1-4 to reconstruct the "sons of God" narrative.

### Reformed Academic
Reformed scholars like Meredith Kline and Michael Heiser have drawn on Enochic traditions to develop the "Divine Council" framework, reading the Watchers narrative alongside Psalm 82 and Deuteronomy 32.

## Theological Emphases

- **Canon and Authority**: 1 Enoch illustrates why Protestants distinguish canonical from useful literature
- **Genesis 6 Interpretation**: The Watchers tradition provides the oldest interpretation of the "sons of God"
- **Christological Titles**: The "Son of Man" in the Parables (chs. 37-71) as background for Jesus' self-designation
- **Spiritual Warfare**: The Watchers and their demonic offspring inform Protestant demonology
