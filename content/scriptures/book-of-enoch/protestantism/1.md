## The Coming Judgment

Protestant interpreters read 1 Enoch's opening chapter as a striking parallel to biblical theophanies, particularly the blessing of Moses in Deuteronomy 33 and the vision of Habakkuk 3. Enoch pronounces a prophetic oracle concerning the "Elect One" and the final judgment — language that Jude 14-15 directly quotes, making this the most canonically significant passage in the entire book.

## Key Themes

### The Jude Quotation
The direct citation of 1 Enoch 1:9 in Jude 14-15 has generated extensive Protestant debate. Calvin and Luther largely ignored the connection, but modern evangelical scholars like Richard Bauckham have argued that Jude treats the Enochic prophecy as genuinely authoritative without necessarily affirming the entire book's inspiration.

### Theophanic Language
The imagery of God "coming forth from his dwelling" with "ten thousand holy ones" echoes Deuteronomy 33:2 and Zechariah 14:5. Protestant scholars note this shared tradition of divine warrior imagery that culminates in Revelation 19.

### Universal Judgment
The chapter establishes the eschatological framework for the entire Book of the Watchers. All creation — mountains melting, hills sinking — responds to God's coming judgment, a theme central to Protestant eschatology.

## Theological Significance

This chapter demonstrates that Jewish apocalyptic expectation of final judgment did not originate with Christianity but was deeply embedded in Second Temple thought, providing essential context for Jesus' own eschatological teaching.
