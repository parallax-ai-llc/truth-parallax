## The Seven Archangels

Chapter 20 provides a catalog of the seven holy angels and their respective domains: Uriel over the world and Tartarus, Raphael over human spirits, Raguel over the luminaries, Michael over Israel, Sariel over spirits that sin, Gabriel over paradise and the cherubim, and Remiel over the resurrected.

## Key Themes

### Angelic Hierarchy
Protestant interpreters approach Enochic angelology with measured interest. While Reformed theology is generally cautious about elaborate angel hierarchies (associated more with Catholic and Orthodox tradition), the text provides important background for NT references to "thrones, dominions, principalities, and powers" (Colossians 1:16).

### Named Angels in Scripture
Only Michael and Gabriel appear by name in the Protestant canon (Daniel 8-12; Luke 1:19-26). The additional named angels in 1 Enoch — Uriel, Raphael, Raguel, Sariel, Remiel — demonstrate that Second Temple Judaism had a far more developed angelology than the canonical texts preserve.

### Functional Theology
Each angel oversees a specific domain, suggesting a theology of delegated divine governance. Protestant commentators note the parallel with the "prince of Persia" and "prince of Greece" in Daniel 10, where angelic beings are assigned territorial responsibilities.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant readers, this chapter illuminates the rich angelic theology that the New Testament authors assumed but rarely explained. Understanding Second Temple angelology helps interpret passages about spiritual warfare, cosmic powers, and Christ's supremacy over all angelic authority.
