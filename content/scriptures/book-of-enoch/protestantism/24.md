## The Mountain of God and the Fragrant Tree

Enoch sees seven magnificent mountains, the central one reaching to heaven like a throne. Near it stands a fragrant tree whose fruit is reserved for the righteous after the great judgment.

## Key Themes

### The Divine Throne
The mountain "like the seat of a throne" evokes Ezekiel 28:14 (the holy mountain of God), Isaiah 14:13 (the mount of assembly), and Psalm 48:2 (Mount Zion). Protestant interpreters see this as evidence that the "cosmic mountain" motif — God's throne atop the highest peak — was a shared concept across Israelite and Jewish apocalyptic tradition.

### The Tree of Life
The fragrant tree reserved for the righteous after judgment recalls the Tree of Life in Genesis 2-3 and anticipates Revelation 2:7 and 22:2. Protestant typological reading connects Eden's lost tree with the eschatological tree of Revelation: what was forfeited in the Fall will be restored in the new creation.

### Eschatological Feast
The tree's fruit, which will be given to "the holy and elect," prefigures the messianic banquet (Isaiah 25:6; Matthew 8:11; Revelation 19:9). Protestant covenant theologians see this as part of a continuous biblical promise of eschatological provision.

## Theological Significance

This chapter demonstrates that the hope of paradise restored — central to Protestant eschatology — was already fully articulated in Second Temple Judaism. The vision of the fragrant tree on God's mountain connects creation, fall, and redemption in a single coherent narrative arc.
