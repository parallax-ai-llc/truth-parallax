## The Garden of Righteousness

Enoch arrives at the Garden of Righteousness and beholds magnificent trees, including the tree of wisdom (the tree of knowledge from Genesis 2-3). The garden's beauty surpasses all previous landscapes.

## Key Themes

### The Tree of Knowledge Revisited
The chapter's description of the tree of wisdom — whose fruit resembles grape clusters and whose fragrance extends far — provides an extra-biblical reflection on the tree of knowledge of good and evil. Protestant interpreters note that 1 Enoch treats the tree itself as good, shifting blame entirely to the act of disobedience.

### Paradise Theology
The Garden of Righteousness in 1 Enoch represents a theological space distinct from both the primordial Eden and the eschatological paradise. Protestant scholars see this intermediate paradise concept as part of the development that led to Jesus' promise of "paradise" to the penitent thief (Luke 23:43) and Paul's "third heaven" (2 Corinthians 12:4).

### Wisdom and Obedience
The tree's identification with wisdom connects the Enochic narrative to the broader Jewish wisdom tradition (Proverbs 3:18, where wisdom is called "a tree of life"). Protestant interpreters note that true wisdom in the Enochic framework comes through obedience, not through illicit knowledge stolen from heaven.

## Theological Significance

This chapter contributes to the Protestant understanding of paradise as a real theological category — not merely a literary device but a genuine expectation of restored communion between God and humanity in a perfected creation.
