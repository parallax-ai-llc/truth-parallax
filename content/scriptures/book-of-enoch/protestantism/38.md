## Chapter 38 — The First Parable: Judgment of the Wicked

1 Enoch 38, within the Parables of Enoch (chapters 37-71), presents visionary material concerning divine judgment, heavenly secrets, and eschatological hope. Protestant scholars approach this section as valuable evidence of Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic thought, while maintaining its non-canonical status.

## Protestant Scholarly Assessment

This chapter reflects the apocalyptic worldview that profoundly influenced early Christianity. The themes of divine judgment, heavenly revelation, and cosmic transformation visible here provide essential context for understanding the apocalyptic dimensions of Jesus' teaching and the Book of Revelation.

Protestant exegetes note that while the Parables of Enoch are not quoted directly in the New Testament, the conceptual framework they represent — including angelic hierarchies, eschatological judgment, and the vindication of the righteous — pervades the New Testament writings. Understanding this chapter helps illuminate the theological environment in which Christianity emerged.

## Historical Context

The Parables of Enoch are notably absent from the Qumran library, leading scholars to debate whether they are a later addition to the Enochic corpus. Protestant scholars have proposed dates ranging from the 1st century BCE to the 1st century CE, with significant implications for their relationship to early Christian thought.
