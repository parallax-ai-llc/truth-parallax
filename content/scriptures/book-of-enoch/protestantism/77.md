## Chapter 77 — The Four Quarters of the Earth

1 Enoch 77 belongs to the Astronomical Book (chapters 72-82), one of the oldest sections of the Enochic corpus. Protestant scholars approach this material with historical-critical interest, recognizing its significance for understanding pre-Christian Jewish cosmology and its relationship to the biblical creation accounts in Genesis 1.

## Protestant Scholarly Assessment

The Astronomical Book presents a solar calendar of 364 days, standing in tension with the lunisolar calendar that became normative in rabbinic Judaism. Protestant scholars note that the Qumran community also adopted a 364-day calendar, suggesting sectarian disputes about liturgical time shaped Second Temple Judaism. This chapter's cosmological framework reflects an ordered creation under divine sovereignty — a theme consonant with Reformed theology's emphasis on God's providential governance of nature.

## Theological Significance

While Protestant interpreters do not grant this text canonical authority, its astronomical teachings illuminate the intellectual world of Second Temple Judaism. The insistence on cosmic order governed by divine law anticipates the natural theology tradition that some Protestant theologians have engaged with cautiously, affirming that creation reveals God's wisdom even as Scripture remains the supreme authority for faith and doctrine.
