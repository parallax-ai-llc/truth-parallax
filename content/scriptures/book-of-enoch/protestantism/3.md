## Nature's Unwavering Course

This brief chapter continues Enoch's meditation on cosmic regularity. Protestant interpreters note its compressed poetic form, in which the unchanging behavior of celestial bodies serves as a foil for the coming narrative of angelic disobedience.

## Key Themes

### Divine Sovereignty
The chapter's insistence that natural phenomena do not "change their courses" reflects the Protestant emphasis on God's sovereign governance of creation. The Westminster Confession similarly affirms that God "upholds, directs, disposes, and governs all creatures, actions, and things."

### Implicit Judgment
By cataloguing nature's obedience, Enoch implicitly indicts those who deviate from their God-given roles. Protestant commentators connect this to the Watchers' abandonment of their heavenly station — a violation of created order that makes their punishment just.

## Theological Significance

Protestant systematic theologians find in these nature passages a pre-philosophical articulation of what later theology calls divine providence and natural law. The chapter demonstrates that Jewish apocalyptic was not merely speculative but grounded in careful theological reflection on the created order.
