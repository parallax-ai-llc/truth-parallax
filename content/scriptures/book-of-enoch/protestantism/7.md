## The Nephilim and Their Violence

Chapter 7 describes the offspring of the Watchers and human women — the Nephilim, giants of three thousand ells who devour the produce of humanity and eventually turn to consuming human beings themselves. Protestant interpreters read this as a theological account of evil's escalation.

## Key Themes

### The Origin of Evil
Protestant theology traditionally locates evil's origin in the Fall of Genesis 3. The Enochic tradition adds a second origin point: the angelic rebellion. Some Protestant scholars, following Heiser, see these as complementary narratives addressing different dimensions of evil — human moral failure and superhuman corruption.

### Violence and Bloodshed
The Nephilim's insatiable appetite — consuming all human provisions, then animals, then humans themselves — parallels the escalating violence of Genesis 6:11-13 that provokes the Flood. Protestant commentators see this as illustrating the principle that sin, unchecked, always escalates.

### Cosmic Disruption
The mingling of angelic and human natures produces monstrous offspring, illustrating the Protestant theological principle that violating God's created boundaries produces not hybrid vitality but destructive chaos.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant readers, this chapter underscores the seriousness of transgressing divinely established order. The Nephilim embody the consequences of angelic rebellion in physical form — violence, consumption, and the corruption of creation itself.
