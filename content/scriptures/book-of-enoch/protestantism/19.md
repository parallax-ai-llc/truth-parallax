## The Place of the Deceiving Angels

This brief chapter describes the final prison of the angels who led women astray, where they await the great judgment. The angel Uriel serves as Enoch's guide, providing authoritative interpretation of the vision.

## Key Themes

### Angelic Guidance
The pattern of an interpreting angel (*angelus interpres*) explaining visions to a human seer is a hallmark of apocalyptic literature. Protestant scholars trace this convention from 1 Enoch through Daniel (8:16; 9:21) to Revelation (17:1; 21:9), recognizing it as a standard literary device for conveying divine revelation.

### Seduction and Deception
The angels' sin is described specifically as deceiving and seducing humanity. Protestant moral theology notes that seduction — leading others into sin — is consistently presented as among the gravest offenses in Scripture (Matthew 18:6-7), worse even than the original sin itself.

### Gendered Violence
Modern Protestant feminist scholars have drawn attention to the chapter's implicit acknowledgment that the Watchers' actions constitute sexual violence. The women are victims, not willing participants, and the text's sympathy lies with those who suffered from angelic transgression.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant theology, this chapter reinforces the principle that spiritual beings who lead others into sin bear responsibility not only for their own rebellion but for its consequences. The severity of their punishment matches the scope of the harm they caused.
