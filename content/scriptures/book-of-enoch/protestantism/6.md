## The Fall of the Watchers

Chapter 6 narrates the fateful decision of 200 angels, led by Shemihazah, to descend to Mount Hermon and take human wives. For Protestant interpreters, this is the interpretive crux of the entire Book of the Watchers, providing the oldest extant reading of Genesis 6:1-4.

## Key Themes

### The "Sons of God" Debate
Protestant scholarship is divided on whether Genesis 6:1-4 describes angelic beings or the Sethite line intermarrying with Cainites. The Enochic interpretation — that "sons of God" means angels — was dominant in early Christianity and has been revived by scholars like Michael Heiser, who argues this was the universal pre-Augustinian reading.

### The Oath on Mount Hermon
The angels bind themselves by oath on Mount Hermon, a detail rich in biblical geography. Protestant scholars note that Hermon was a liminal sacred site at Israel's northern boundary, and the wordplay between *herem* (oath/ban) and Hermon underscores the self-cursing nature of their pact.

### Angelic Free Will
The narrative presupposes that angels possess genuine freedom to rebel — a view consistent with Protestant angelology, which affirms that Satan and the demons chose to fall.

## Theological Significance

This chapter provides essential background for New Testament passages about angels "who did not keep their proper domain" (Jude 6) and spirits in prison (1 Peter 3:19-20), texts that remain enigmatic without the Enochic framework.
