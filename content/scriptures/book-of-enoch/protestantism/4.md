## The Contrast of Nature and Humanity

Chapter 4 sharpens the contrast between the obedient natural order and sinful humanity. Protestant interpreters see here a precursor to Paul's argument in Romans 8:19-22, where creation "groans" under the weight of human fallenness.

## Key Themes

### Total Depravity in Embryo
Reformed interpreters note that Enoch's contrast between nature's faithfulness and human rebellion anticipates the Protestant doctrine of total depravity. While nature fulfills its purpose, humanity alone deviates from its created intention — a theme that runs from Genesis 3 through Romans 3:23.

### The Moral Argument
The chapter employs a *qal wahomer* (lesser to greater) argument: if inanimate creation obeys God's decrees, how much more should rational beings? Protestant preachers from Spurgeon to Lloyd-Jones have used similar reasoning.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant theology, this chapter establishes that moral accountability is woven into the fabric of creation itself. Judgment is not arbitrary divine anger but the natural consequence of violating a moral order as real and as binding as the physical order governing the stars.
