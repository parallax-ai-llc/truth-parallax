## Enoch's Commission as Mediator

Chapter 12 introduces Enoch's unique role: hidden away before the Watchers' descent, he is now commissioned by the holy angels to deliver God's judgment to the fallen Watchers. Protestant interpreters find in this commissioning scene important background for understanding biblical prophetic calls.

## Key Themes

### The Prophetic Calling
Enoch's commission mirrors the call narratives of Isaiah 6, Jeremiah 1, and Ezekiel 1-3. Protestant scholars note that the pattern — divine encounter, commissioning, message of judgment — was already well-established in Israelite tradition and continued into the apocalyptic genre.

### Ironic Reversal
The Watchers, who abandoned heaven, must now receive God's message through a human — a dramatic reversal of the expected cosmic hierarchy. Protestant commentators see this as anticipating the Incarnation's logic, where God chooses the lowly to shame the mighty (1 Corinthians 1:27).

### Enoch's Righteousness
Enoch is described as a "righteous man" and "scribe of righteousness," titles that establish his authority. Protestant interpreters connect this to the biblical emphasis on righteousness as the prerequisite for prophetic ministry.

## Theological Significance

This chapter establishes the pattern of human mediation between heaven and earth that runs through biblical theology. For Protestants, while Christ is the ultimate mediator, Enoch's role illustrates the broader biblical theme of God choosing human agents to communicate divine truth.
