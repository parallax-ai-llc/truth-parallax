## The Order of Nature as Witness

Protestant interpreters connect this chapter's appeal to natural order with Romans 1:19-20, where Paul argues that creation reveals God's eternal power. Enoch observes how the celestial bodies — sun, moon, and stars — faithfully follow their appointed courses, serving as a silent rebuke to human disobedience.

## Key Themes

### Natural Revelation
The argument from nature's regularity to humanity's moral obligation resonates with the Reformed doctrine of general revelation. Calvin's *Institutes* similarly argued that the order of creation leaves humanity "without excuse," echoing both Enoch and Paul.

### Covenant Faithfulness
Protestant covenant theologians note that Enoch presents nature as a covenant-keeping partner with God. The seasons change on schedule, the stars do not deviate — unlike the Watchers who will abandon their appointed stations in chapter 6.

### Wisdom Tradition
The chapter's observational mode connects 1 Enoch to Israel's wisdom tradition (Proverbs 6:6-8; Job 38-41), where careful observation of nature yields theological insight. Protestant scholars see this as evidence of a broad Jewish wisdom movement underlying the apocalyptic genre.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant readers, this chapter establishes a moral cosmology: the universe itself testifies to God's ordering power, making angelic and human rebellion all the more culpable.
