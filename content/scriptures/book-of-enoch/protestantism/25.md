## The Tree of Life and Jerusalem

The archangel Michael explains the fragrant tree to Enoch: its fruit will be given to the righteous and humble after the final judgment, granting long life and joy. The tree will be transplanted to the holy place beside God's temple — identified with Jerusalem.

## Key Themes

### Eschatological Jerusalem
The identification of the tree's destination with Jerusalem connects Enochic eschatology to the broader biblical tradition of Zion as the center of God's final redemption. Protestant scholars note the continuity from this passage through Revelation 21-22, where the New Jerusalem contains the tree of life.

### The Fragrance of Salvation
The tree's incomparable fragrance symbolizes the life-giving presence of God. Protestant interpreters connect this sensory imagery to Paul's metaphor of believers as "the aroma of Christ" (2 Corinthians 2:15) and the incense of Revelation 5:8.

### Longevity and Joy
Michael promises that those who eat the fruit will "live a long life on earth, such as your fathers lived." Protestant interpreters note the deliberately Edenic language — this is a restoration of the pre-Fall condition, not merely an improvement on current human existence.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant eschatology, this chapter confirms that the biblical narrative arc from Eden to New Jerusalem was already understood in Second Temple Judaism. The tree of life, lost through disobedience, will be restored through divine judgment and mercy — a pattern that Protestants see fulfilled in Christ's redemptive work.
