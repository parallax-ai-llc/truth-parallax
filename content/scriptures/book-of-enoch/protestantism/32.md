## The Garden of Eden and the Tree of Knowledge

Raphael identifies the garden as Eden and the tree as the one from which Adam and Eve ate. Enoch learns the full geography of paradise, including its relationship to other cosmic features he has observed.

## Key Themes

### Eden's Continued Existence
The chapter presupposes that Eden still exists as a heavenly or cosmic location, not merely as a past historical site. Protestant interpreters are divided: some (following Calvin) read Eden as historical geography now lost, while others see the Enochic tradition as reflecting a heavenly Eden that persists as a theological reality.

### Raphael as Guide
The archangel Raphael serves as the interpreting angel in this section. Protestant scholars note that Raphael, while prominent in Second Temple literature (also featured in Tobit), does not appear by name in the Protestant canon — illustrating how 1 Enoch preserves a broader angelological tradition.

### Knowledge and Responsibility
Raphael's explanation connects the tree's fruit to the knowledge that brought about the Fall. Protestant commentators note the consistent biblical theme: knowledge divorced from relationship with God leads to destruction, while knowledge received within faithful obedience leads to life.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant readers, this chapter's treatment of the Eden narrative provides important evidence for how Second Temple Judaism understood the Fall. The emphasis on knowledge, obedience, and their consequences anticipates the Pauline theology of sin in Romans 5, where Adam's disobedience introduces death into the world.
