## Aftermath of Judgment

This brief chapter records the lament arising from the destruction wrought by the Nephilim and the subsequent divine judgment. Protestant commentators treat it as a transitional passage connecting the Watcher narrative to Enoch's commissioning.

## Key Themes

### The Cost of Evil
Protestant interpreters emphasize that judgment, while just and necessary, is not celebrated. The desolation described here reflects the theological principle that sin's consequences extend far beyond the sinner — a theme central to Protestant hamartiology.

### Transitional Function
The chapter serves as a literary hinge, closing the narrative of the Watchers' crimes and judgment while preparing for Enoch's role as heavenly mediator. Protestant literary scholars note the careful structural composition that belies the text's reputation as disjointed apocalyptic.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant readers, this chapter illustrates that divine judgment is not vindictive but restorative. The devastation is real, but it clears the way for the renewal promised in chapter 10's conclusion — a pattern repeated throughout Scripture from the Flood through the Cross to the final judgment.
