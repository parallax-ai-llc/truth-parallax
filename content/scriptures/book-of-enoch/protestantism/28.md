## The Desert of Spices in the East

Enoch journeys eastward through desert landscapes and sees trees that exude precious spices, including frankincense and myrrh. The geography shifts from judgment to beauty as Enoch moves toward Eden.

## Key Themes

### Creation's Abundance
The detailed catalog of fragrant trees and spices illustrates God's creative generosity. Protestant natural theologians have long argued that creation's beauty and abundance reveal the character of the Creator — an argument that finds early expression in passages like this.

### Eastward Toward Eden
The journey's eastward direction carries symbolic weight, as Eden was "in the east" (Genesis 2:8). Protestant typological readers see Enoch's eastward journey as a movement toward humanity's lost paradise — a return to the source of creation's abundance.

### Frankincense and Myrrh
The specific mention of frankincense and myrrh resonates with their biblical significance: worship materials (Exodus 30:34), gifts to the Christ child (Matthew 2:11), and burial spices (John 19:39). Protestant Christological interpreters note these connections without pressing them as intentional prophecy.

## Theological Significance

For Protestant readers, this chapter's celebration of natural beauty and abundance serves as a reminder that the created world, despite its corruption, still reflects God's goodness — a theological conviction expressed in the doctrine of common grace.
