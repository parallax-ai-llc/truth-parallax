## Chapter 45 — The Second Parable: The Elect One

1 Enoch 45 contains some of the most christologically significant material in the Parables of Enoch, with its references to the "Son of Man" and "Elect One." Protestant scholars have extensively debated whether Jesus' self-designation as "Son of Man" in the Gospels draws upon the Enochic tradition or the Danielic vision (Daniel 7:13-14) — or both.

## Protestant Scholarly Assessment

The Son of Man/Elect One figure in this chapter functions as a pre-existent, heavenly judge who vindicates the righteous and condemns the wicked. Protestant interpreters note significant parallels with New Testament Christology, particularly the Johannine portrayal of Jesus as the pre-existent Word and the Matthean depiction of the Son of Man on his throne of glory (Matthew 25:31).

Reformed scholars have debated whether the Parables directly influenced the New Testament authors or whether both traditions independently develop Daniel 7. The absence of the Parables from the Qumran library complicates dating and provenance questions.

## Theological Significance

This chapter contributes to Protestant understanding of messianic expectation in Second Temple Judaism. The exalted figure described here represents one strand of Jewish hope that the New Testament authors would transform through the lens of Jesus' life, death, and resurrection.
