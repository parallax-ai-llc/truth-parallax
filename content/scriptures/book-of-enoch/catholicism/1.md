## The Coming Judgment

Catholic interpreters read 1 Enoch 1 through the lens of patristic reception, noting that early Church Fathers such as Tertullian, Clement of Alexandria, and Justin Martyr regarded this opening theophany with great seriousness. The chapter describes God's coming from Sinai with ten thousand holy ones to execute judgment on all — language that Jude 14-15 directly cites.

## Key Themes

### Patristic Authority
Tertullian considered the Book of Enoch authentic scripture, partly because of Jude's citation. While the Church ultimately did not include 1 Enoch in the canon, Catholic tradition maintains that Jude's use of this material reflects the Holy Spirit's guidance, validating the theological truth of the quoted passage without endorsing the entire work.

### Theophanic Tradition
The imagery of divine approach — mountains melting, the earth trembling — stands within the biblical theophanic tradition (Exodus 19; Habakkuk 3; Psalm 97). Catholic liturgical theology recognizes such imagery as expressing the overwhelming holiness of God encountered in the Eucharist.

### Universal Judgment
The chapter's vision of judgment against "all the ungodly" resonates with Catholic eschatology's affirmation of a general judgment (*iudicium universale*) in which all people will be judged by Christ, as affirmed by the Nicene Creed ("He will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead").

## Theological Significance

For Catholic readers, this chapter demonstrates that the early Church's eschatological expectations were deeply rooted in Jewish apocalyptic tradition, providing continuity between the Old and New Testaments that Catholic theology emphasizes.
