## Enoch's Throne Vision

Chapter 14 presents one of the most elaborate theophanic visions in Jewish literature. Enoch ascends through concentric chambers of crystal and fire to behold the divine throne — an experience that profoundly influenced Ezekiel's merkavah vision and the throne scenes of Revelation.

## Key Themes

### The Heavenly Temple
The two-chambered structure Enoch enters — an outer house of crystal and an inner house of fire — mirrors the Jerusalem Temple's architecture. Catholic liturgical theology interprets this as confirming that the earthly sanctuary was a copy of the heavenly original (Hebrews 8:5), a principle that grounds the theology of sacred space in Catholic worship.

### The Great Glory
God is described as the "Great Glory" seated on a throne of fire, surrounded by angelic hosts. Catholic mystical theology, from Pseudo-Dionysius to Teresa of Avila, draws on this tradition of overwhelming divine luminosity that both attracts and overwhelms the human seer.

### Liturgical Parallels
The angelic worship surrounding the throne — with cherubim, seraphim, and countless holy ones — directly informs the Catholic Mass's *Sanctus* ("Holy, holy, holy"), which the Church understands as joining earthly worship to the eternal heavenly liturgy described in passages like this.

## Theological Significance

For Catholic theology, Enoch's throne vision provides the foundational template for understanding the Mass as participation in heavenly worship. The vision's emphasis on God's transcendent glory, mediated through angelic liturgy, shapes Catholic sacramental and mystical theology to this day.
