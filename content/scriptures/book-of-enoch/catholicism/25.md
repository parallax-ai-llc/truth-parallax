## The Tree of Life

Catholic interpreters read this chapter within Enoch's second cosmic journey, finding in its descriptions of heavenly geography and cosmic features a theological reflection on God's creative sovereignty and the moral structure of the universe.

## Key Themes

### Sacred Geography
The Catholic sacramental imagination finds resonance in Enoch's detailed descriptions of cosmic spaces invested with theological meaning. Just as Catholic theology sees the material world as capable of mediating divine grace, so the Enochic tradition sees cosmic geography as revealing God's purposes.

### The Created Order
The chapter's attention to natural features and cosmic structures reflects the Catholic conviction, affirmed by Vatican I, that God can be known through the created order. The beauty and order of creation point beyond themselves to the Creator.

## Theological Significance

For Catholic readers, this chapter contributes to the Enochic vision of a cosmos thoroughly permeated by divine purpose — a perspective consonant with the Catholic sacramental worldview that sees all creation as potentially revelatory of God's glory.
