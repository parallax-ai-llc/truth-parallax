## Overview

Catholic engagement with 1 Enoch reflects a complex historical trajectory. Several early Church Fathers — Tertullian, Clement of Alexandria, and Irenaeus — treated the text with considerable respect, and some regarded it as scripture. However, by the fourth and fifth centuries, influenced by Augustine and Jerome, the Western Church moved decisively toward excluding 1 Enoch from the canon. The text remained outside the Catholic deuterocanonical collection, yet its themes deeply shaped Catholic angelology, demonology, and eschatology.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Reception
Tertullian defended 1 Enoch's authority in *De Cultu Feminarum*, arguing its preservation through the Flood confirmed divine approval. Clement of Alexandria cited it approvingly, while Justin Martyr drew on Watcher traditions in his *Second Apology*.

### Augustinian Rejection
Augustine's *City of God* (Book 15) reinterpreted the "sons of God" in Genesis 6 as the Sethite line rather than fallen angels, effectively undermining the Enochic framework and contributing to the text's marginalization.

### Modern Catholic Scholarship
Contemporary Catholic scholars engage 1 Enoch through the lens of intertestamental studies, recognizing its importance for understanding the development of doctrines on angels, demons, and the afterlife that became central to Catholic theology.

## Theological Emphases

- **Angelology**: The Watcher tradition contributed to the elaborate Catholic hierarchy of angels
- **Demonology**: Fallen angel narratives shaped Catholic understanding of demonic origins
- **Purgatorial Imagery**: Enoch's visions of intermediate states parallel later Catholic eschatology
- **Intercession of Saints**: Enoch as heavenly intercessor prefigures the Catholic doctrine
