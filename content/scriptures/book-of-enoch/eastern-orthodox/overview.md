## Overview

Eastern Orthodox engagement with 1 Enoch is distinguished by a broader canonical sensibility than Western Christianity. While most Orthodox churches do not include 1 Enoch in their canon, the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church — the largest Oriental Orthodox communion — regards it as fully canonical scripture. Within the broader Orthodox tradition, Enochic themes of angelic hierarchy, cosmic order, and divine judgment resonate deeply with Orthodox theology and liturgical life.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Ethiopian Orthodox Canonical Reading
For the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, 1 Enoch is inspired scripture read alongside Genesis, Daniel, and Revelation. The text's survival in Ge'ez reflects Ethiopia's unique role as preserver of this ancient tradition, and Enochic themes permeate Ethiopian liturgy, hymnography, and art.

### Greek Patristic Reception
Early Greek Fathers engaged with Enochic traditions extensively. Irenaeus referenced the Watchers in *Against Heresies*, and the Enochic angel-fall narrative influenced Byzantine angelology and iconography.

### Synaxarion Traditions
Orthodox liturgical calendars commemorate Enoch as a righteous patriarch, and his heavenly translation (Genesis 5:24) is celebrated as a prefigurement of theosis — humanity's participation in divine life.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: Enoch's transformation and heavenly ascent prefigure the Orthodox doctrine of deification
- **Cosmic Liturgy**: The angelic worship scenes in 1 Enoch parallel Orthodox liturgical theology
- **Spiritual Warfare**: The Watcher tradition informs Orthodox ascetic theology about demonic temptation
- **Eschatological Judgment**: Enoch's visions of final judgment align with Orthodox eschatology
