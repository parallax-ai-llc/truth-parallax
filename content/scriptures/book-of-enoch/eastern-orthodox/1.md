## The Coming Judgment and Divine Glory

Eastern Orthodox interpreters approach 1 Enoch 1 through the lens of liturgical theology and patristic tradition. The chapter's theophany — God approaching from Sinai with ten thousand holy ones — resonates with the Orthodox experience of divine glory (*doxa*) encountered in the Divine Liturgy and the theology of divine energies.

## Key Themes

### The Divine Energies
The chapter's description of God's approach — mountains melting, earth consumed by fire — illustrates what Gregory Palamas would later articulate as God's uncreated energies: the real but distinct manifestation of divine power in creation. Orthodox theology insists that God is truly encountered in these manifestations while remaining unknowable in essence.

### Ethiopian Canonical Status
For the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, this chapter is canonical scripture read alongside Jude, confirming that the prophecy of Enoch preserved here carries full scriptural authority. This canonical status reflects the broader Orthodox willingness to recognize a more expansive scriptural tradition than Western Christianity.

### Liturgical Eschatology
The vision of final judgment with angelic hosts directly informs Orthodox liturgical texts, particularly the hymns of the Last Judgment Sunday (*Meatfare Sunday*), where the imagery of divine approach and universal judgment echoes Enochic language.

## Theological Significance

For Orthodox readers, this chapter expresses the eschatological reality that pervades all Orthodox worship: every celebration of the Divine Liturgy anticipates the final theophany, when God's glory will be revealed to all creation.
