## The Coming Judgment

Jewish interpreters of 1 Enoch 1 situate this chapter within the rich tradition of Second Temple apocalyptic literature that flourished alongside — and sometimes in tension with — the traditions that became rabbinic Judaism. The chapter's theophanic language echoes Deuteronomy 33:2 and Habakkuk 3, placing Enoch firmly within Israel's prophetic heritage.

## Key Themes

### Second Temple Context
During the centuries when 1 Enoch was composed and circulated, Judaism was a diverse phenomenon encompassing Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, and various apocalyptic movements. The Qumran community preserved multiple copies of the Book of the Watchers, suggesting that this opening vision of judgment was central to at least one significant Jewish movement.

### Mosaic Typology
Enoch's prophetic oracle mirrors Moses' final blessing (Deuteronomy 33), establishing Enoch as a Mosaic figure — one who receives revelation from God and transmits it to the community. Jewish scholars note that this literary strategy legitimizes the Enochic tradition by connecting it to the Torah's authority.

### Judgment and Theodicy
The chapter's insistence that God will come to judge "all the ungodly" addresses the fundamental Jewish theological question of divine justice: why do the righteous suffer while the wicked prosper? The promise of eschatological judgment provides a future resolution to present inequity.

## Theological Significance

For Jewish readers, this chapter represents a significant moment in the history of Jewish thought — the development of a fully articulated eschatological vision that would influence both rabbinic Judaism and early Christianity, even as the rabbis ultimately excluded the text from the canon.
