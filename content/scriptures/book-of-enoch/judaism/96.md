## Chapter 96 — Comfort for the Righteous, Woes for the Wealthy

1 Enoch 96 belongs to the Epistle of Enoch (chapters 91-105), which contains the Apocalypse of Weeks and prophetic denunciations of injustice. Jewish scholars engage with this section as evidence of the strong tradition of social criticism within Second Temple Judaism.

## Jewish Scholarly Analysis

The Epistle's woes against the wealthy and powerful echo the classical prophetic tradition of Amos, Isaiah, and Micah. This chapter demonstrates the continuity of prophetic social criticism into the Second Temple period, challenging the notion that prophecy simply ceased after Malachi. Jewish scholars note that the Epistle's ethical vision — condemnation of economic exploitation, false weights and measures, and the oppression of the poor — reflects real social conditions in Hellenistic Judea and anticipates rabbinic legal provisions protecting the vulnerable.

## Historical Significance

This chapter contributes to Jewish understanding of the ethical traditions that shaped rabbinic Judaism. The Epistle's concern for justice and its confidence in divine vindication resonate with the prophetic heritage that the rabbis preserved and developed through halakhic legislation. The text demonstrates that the passion for social justice expressed in talmudic discussions of economic fairness (*ona'ah*) and care for the poor had deep roots in pre-rabbinic Jewish literature.
