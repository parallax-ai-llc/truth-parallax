## Chapter 46 — The Head of Days and Son of Man

1 Enoch 46 presents the "Son of Man" — a heavenly figure of pre-existent glory who executes divine judgment. Jewish scholars engage with this chapter as crucial evidence for understanding messianic speculation in Second Temple Judaism, a period of extraordinary theological creativity within Jewish tradition.

## Jewish Scholarly Analysis

The Son of Man figure in the Parables of Enoch represents one stream of Second Temple messianic thought — the transcendent, heavenly redeemer — alongside the more common Davidic political messiah. Jewish scholars note that this chapter develops the "one like a son of man" vision from Daniel 7:13-14 in directions that significantly influenced later Christian Christology.

The pre-existence attributed to the Son of Man in this chapter reflects broader Second Temple speculation about pre-existent entities (Torah, Wisdom, the Name) that would be developed differently in rabbinic Judaism and Christianity. The Parables of Enoch represent a path not taken by rabbinic Judaism, which emphasized the Davidic messiah and downplayed transcendent messianic figures.

## Historical Significance

This chapter is particularly significant for Jewish-Christian dialogue, as it demonstrates that beliefs about a transcendent, pre-existent heavenly savior figure were part of the Jewish theological landscape before Christianity emerged. Understanding this context transforms the conversation about Christological origins.
