## The Fall of the Watchers

Jewish interpretation of the Watchers' descent in chapter 6 represents one of the most consequential exegetical traditions in Second Temple Judaism. The identification of the "sons of God" in Genesis 6:1-4 as angels who rebel against God by taking human wives was the dominant Jewish reading before the rise of rabbinic Judaism.

## Key Themes

### The Pre-Rabbinic Reading
The Qumran community, Philo (partially), Josephus, and the Septuagint translators all understood Genesis 6:1-4 in terms consistent with the Enochic Watcher tradition. This was not a marginal reading but the mainstream Jewish interpretation until the rabbis deliberately reinterpreted the passage.

### Rabbinic Reinterpretation
The early rabbis systematically dismantled the Watcher interpretation. The Targumim rendered "sons of God" as "sons of the judges" or "sons of the mighty," and the Talmud (b. Yoma 67b) reframed Azazel as a place-name rather than a fallen angel. This hermeneutical shift likely reflected concerns about angelolatry and the dangers of speculative cosmology.

### Mount Hermon and Sacred Geography
The oath-taking on Mount Hermon connects the Watcher tradition to Israelite sacred geography. Jewish scholars note the wordplay between *Hermon* and *herem* (oath/ban), and the mountain's location at the northern boundary of Israel marked it as a liminal space where heaven and earth — dangerously — intersect.

## Theological Significance

For Jewish readers, this chapter illuminates a major interpretive tradition within ancient Judaism that the rabbis chose to suppress. Understanding why the rabbis rejected the Watcher reading — concerns about dualism, angelic autonomy, and speculative excess — reveals much about the formation of normative Judaism.
