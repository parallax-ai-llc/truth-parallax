## Overview

The Book of Enoch occupies a paradoxical place in Jewish tradition. It was immensely influential during the Second Temple period — Aramaic fragments of every section except the Parables were found at Qumran — yet the rabbis who shaped normative Judaism after 70 CE decisively excluded it from the Tanakh. Despite this exclusion, Enochic traditions about angels, demons, heavenly ascent, and cosmic judgment continued to echo through later Jewish mystical literature, including the Hekhalot texts and Kabbalistic works.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Second Temple Context
In its original Jewish setting, 1 Enoch addressed pressing concerns: the origin of evil, the justice of God, the fate of the righteous, and the structure of the cosmos. The Qumran community treasured these texts, and the Astronomical Book may be among the oldest Jewish literature outside the Torah.

### Rabbinic Rejection
The Talmud (b. Hagigah 15b) warns against speculation about "what is above, what is below, what is before, and what is after," effectively discouraging the kind of heavenly-ascent mysticism central to 1 Enoch. The rabbis also reinterpreted Genesis 6:1-4, avoiding the angel-marriage reading.

### Mystical Appropriation
Despite rabbinic caution, Enochic motifs resurfaced in the Hekhalot literature (3rd-7th centuries CE) and later in Kabbalah. The figure of Metatron in 3 Enoch (Sefer Hekhalot) is directly derived from Enochic tradition.

## Theological Emphases

- **Origin of Evil**: The Watchers explain evil's entry into the world without blaming God
- **Heavenly Ascent**: Enoch as the archetype of mystical journey to the divine throne
- **Calendar Disputes**: The solar calendar of 1 Enoch vs. the rabbinic lunar calendar
- **Metatron**: Enoch's transformation into the supreme angel in later mysticism
