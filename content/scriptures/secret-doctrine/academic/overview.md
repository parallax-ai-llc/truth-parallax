Academic study of the Secret Doctrine situates it within the context of late 19th-century Western esotericism, Orientalism, and the encounter between European and Asian religious traditions. Scholars examine Blavatsky's claims about the Stanzas of Dzyan — no independent manuscript of the 'Book of Dzyan' has ever been identified — and trace her sources in available Sanskrit, Tibetan, and Western esoteric texts.

Historians of ideas analyze the Secret Doctrine as a creative synthesis of Neoplatonic emanationism, Hindu Vedanta, Buddhist philosophy, Kabbalistic number symbolism, and contemporary evolutionary science. The work's reception history is studied for its influence on subsequent esoteric movements, the visual arts, literature (W.B. Yeats, Kandinsky, Mondrian), and the development of comparative religion as an academic discipline.
