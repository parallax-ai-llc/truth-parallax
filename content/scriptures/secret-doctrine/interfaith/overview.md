The Secret Doctrine holds a distinctive place in interfaith discourse as one of the first modern attempts to argue systematically for the underlying unity of the world's religious traditions. Blavatsky's thesis — that an 'Ancient Wisdom' or 'Secret Doctrine' lies behind all exoteric religions — anticipated many themes that would later become central to interfaith dialogue: the conviction that truth transcends any single tradition, the search for common ground beneath doctrinal differences, and the recognition that mystical and esoteric traditions across religions share more in common than do their exoteric forms.

Interfaith readers may appreciate the Secret Doctrine's universalist aspiration while noting the tension between its claim to transcend all particular traditions and its often polemical engagement with specific religious positions. The work's legacy for interfaith dialogue is ambivalent: inspiring in its vision of religious unity, yet problematic in its tendency to subsume genuine religious diversity into a single, hierarchically organized 'perennial philosophy.'
