## Overview

An interfaith approach to the Book of the Dead seeks to understand these ancient texts as expressions of universal human concerns about death, moral accountability, and the hope for life beyond the grave. Rather than treating them as merely historical artifacts, interfaith study recognizes in them living questions that continue to resonate across religious traditions.

## Key Interfaith Themes

### Moral Judgment After Death
The weighing of the heart against the feather of Ma'at speaks to a concern shared by virtually all religious traditions: the belief that moral conduct in this life has consequences beyond death. This connects to:
- **Judaism and Christianity**: Divine judgment of the righteous and wicked
- **Islam**: The scales of judgment on the Day of Reckoning
- **Hinduism**: Karma and its consequences across lifetimes
- **Buddhism**: The workings of karma in determining rebirth

### The Journey of the Soul
The deceased's passage through the Duat resonates with other traditions' descriptions of the soul's post-mortem journey, from the Tibetan bardo states to the Christian purgatorial tradition.

### Truth and Cosmic Order
Ma'at as the foundation of both cosmic and moral order parallels concepts of dharma, logos, Tao, and natural law in other traditions.
