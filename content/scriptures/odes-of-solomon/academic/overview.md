## Overview

The Odes of Solomon have been the subject of vigorous scholarly debate since their publication by J. Rendel Harris in 1909. The fundamental questions remain open: What is the original language (Syriac or Greek)? What is the date of composition (late first century, early second century, or later)? What is the community of origin (Jewish-Christian, proto-orthodox, Gnostic, or sui generis)? And what is the literary genre (liturgical hymns, baptismal songs, mystical poetry, or some combination)?

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Language of Composition

The debate over the original language centers on whether the Odes were composed in Syriac and later translated into Greek (Harris, Charlesworth) or composed in Greek and translated into Syriac (Lattke, Frankenberg). The Coptic citations in Pistis Sophia and the Greek fragment (P. Bodmer XI) provide evidence for Greek transmission, but the theological vocabulary and imagery are deeply rooted in Syriac literary and theological traditions.

### Dating and Provenance

Most scholars place the composition between 100 and 150 CE, making the Odes roughly contemporary with the later books of the New Testament. The absence of clear references to persecution, developed Christology, or institutional hierarchy suggests an early date, though some scholars have argued for a later provenance based on perceived Gnostic elements.

### Theological Classification

The Odes resist easy classification. They contain elements that can be read as proto-orthodox, Gnostic, Jewish-Christian, and baptismal. Charlesworth has argued that they represent a unique form of early Syrian Christianity that predates later sectarian divisions; Drijvers has emphasized Gnostic elements; Lattke has provided the most comprehensive commentary, treating each ode individually.
