## Ode 2: Syriac Theological Reading

Ode 2 is lost in all surviving manuscripts, leaving a gap at the beginning of the collection. Its absence is itself significant for scholars reconstructing the collection's history and transmission. Ode 2 is read through the Syriac theological lens with attention to the robe of glory, baptismal transformation, the feminine Spirit, and the distinctive vocabulary of early Syriac Christianity. The Syriac interpretation emphasizes the ode's contribution to the broader theme of joy and light that characterizes this section of the collection.

The imagery of Ode 2 — light, garments, joy, the Lord as crown — belongs to the distinctive vocabulary of Syriac baptismal theology. The "robe of glory" (*estla d-shubha*) that the singer receives evokes the Syriac tradition that Adam and Eve were clothed in light before the Fall, and that baptism restores this primordial garment. Ephrem the Syrian would later develop this theology extensively in his *Hymns on Paradise* and *Hymns on the Nativity*, but the Odes provide its earliest poetic expression.

The ode's celebration of joy and transformation reflects the experience of the newly baptized — the neophyte who has passed through the waters, been anointed with oil, and received the white garment that symbolizes their new life in Christ. The Syriac baptismal liturgy, as reconstructed by Sebastian Brock and others, placed the anointing (*rushma*) before the water baptism, making the candidate a "christ" (anointed one) before entering the waters — a sequence that the Odes' imagery may reflect.
