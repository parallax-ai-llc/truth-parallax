## Overview

The Infinite Life Sutra is not part of the Pali canon and plays no direct role in Theravada doctrine or practice. However, elements of its cosmological vision and devotional framework find distant echoes in the Theravada tradition's own descriptions of other world-systems and the practice of buddhanussati (recollection of the Buddha).

## Points of Comparison

The Pali canon describes multiple world-systems and acknowledges the existence of other Buddhas. The Theravada practice of recollecting the Buddha's qualities (buddhanussati) as a concentration exercise shares a structural similarity with Pure Land recitation, though the soteriological frameworks differ fundamentally. Theravada emphasizes self-effort and insight, while the Infinite Life Sutra foregrounds Amitabha's other-power.

## Contemporary Dialogue

In modern inter-Buddhist dialogue, Theravada scholars have engaged with Pure Land thought as a contrasting model of Buddhist soteriology. The tension between self-power (jiriki) and other-power (tariki) provides a productive framework for understanding the diversity of Buddhist approaches to liberation.
