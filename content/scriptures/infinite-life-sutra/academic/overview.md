## Overview

Academic study of the Infinite Life Sutra has focused on its textual history, the development of Pure Land thought, and the social dimensions of Pure Land devotion. The sutra exists in multiple Sanskrit, Chinese, and Tibetan recensions, providing rich material for textual criticism and the study of how Buddhist texts evolve.

## Textual History

Five Chinese translations survive, the most influential being Samghavarman's 252 CE translation (the "standard" version) and the Sanskrit manuscripts discovered in Nepal. Comparison of these versions reveals significant variations in the number and content of Dharmakara's vows, suggesting a complex textual history in which the sutra grew and was modified over centuries.

## Social and Historical Context

Scholars such as James Dobbins and Mark Blum have examined how Pure Land Buddhism addressed the spiritual needs of populations who could not pursue monastic discipline — farmers, merchants, women, and others marginalized by elite Buddhist institutions. The Infinite Life Sutra's promise of universal accessibility made it a vehicle for the democratization of Buddhist soteriology.
