Academic analysis of the description of Sukhavati has examined its literary sources, cosmological assumptions, and social implications. Scholars have traced the imagery to earlier Indian Buddhist descriptions of heavenly realms and to broader Indian literary traditions of idealized landscapes. The Pure Land's description as a realm without social hierarchy, gender distinction, or material want has been interpreted as a utopian vision that reflects and critiques the inequalities of the societies in which the sutra was composed and received. Gregory Schopen and others have examined how Sukhavati functioned as a 'counter-world' offering an alternative to the difficulties of monastic and lay life.
