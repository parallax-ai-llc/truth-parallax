## Overview

The Infinite Life Sutra (無量壽經), also known as the Larger Sukhavativyuha Sutra, is the foundational scripture of Pure Land Buddhism. It narrates the story of the monk Dharmakara, who made 48 great vows before the Buddha Lokeshvararaja, pledging to create a perfect Buddha-land where all beings could be reborn and attain enlightenment. Upon fulfilling these vows, Dharmakara became the Buddha Amitabha (Infinite Light), presiding over the Western Pure Land of Sukhavati.

## Structure

The sutra is traditionally divided into three major sections:

1. **Dharmakara's Vows** (Chapter 1): The origin story of Amitabha and his 48 vows
2. **Description of Sukhavati** (Chapter 2): The splendors of the Pure Land and the qualities of beings born there
3. **Exhortation to Practice** (Chapter 3): The Buddha's encouragement to seek rebirth in Sukhavati

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Infinite Life Sutra has shaped Buddhist devotion across East Asia:

- **Pure Land**: The primary scriptural foundation for Jodo Shu and Jodo Shinshu in Japan, and analogous movements in China, Korea, and Vietnam
- **Mahayana**: One of the "Three Pure Land Sutras" studied throughout East Asian Buddhism
- **Zen**: Engaged through the practice of nembutsu-zen and dual practice traditions
- **Theravada**: Not canonical, but its cosmological vision invites comparison with Pali descriptions of Buddha-fields

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 48 Vows | Dharmakara's comprehensive pledges forming the basis of Pure Land soteriology |
| Other-Power (Tariki) | Salvation through Amitabha's compassionate power rather than self-effort alone |
| Nembutsu/Nianfo | Recitation of Amitabha's name as the primary practice for rebirth |
| Sukhavati | The idealized Buddha-land free from suffering and conducive to enlightenment |
| Universal Accessibility | The teaching that all beings, regardless of capacity, can attain rebirth |
