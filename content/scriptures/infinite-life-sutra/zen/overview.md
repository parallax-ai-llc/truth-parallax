## Overview

The relationship between Zen and Pure Land Buddhism is more complex than the common perception of opposition might suggest. In Chinese Buddhism especially, the dual practice of Chan meditation and nianfo (Buddha-name recitation) has a long history, and the Infinite Life Sutra has been read through a Zen lens that interprets the Pure Land as a metaphor for the awakened mind.

## Mind-Only Pure Land

The Chan master Yongming Yanshou (904–975) famously advocated the unity of Chan and Pure Land practice. In this reading, Sukhavati is not merely an external realm but the pure nature of mind itself. "Self-nature Amitabha, mind-only Pure Land" became a widely quoted phrase expressing this integration. The Infinite Life Sutra's description of Sukhavati is read as a description of the mind's inherent luminosity.

## The Nembutsu as Koan

Some Zen teachers have used the nembutsu as a form of koan practice, asking "Who is it that recites the Buddha's name?" This transforms devotional recitation into a method of self-inquiry, bridging the apparent gap between Zen investigation and Pure Land faith.
