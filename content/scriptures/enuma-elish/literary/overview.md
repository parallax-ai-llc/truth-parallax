## Overview

Literary study of the Enuma Elish examines this Babylonian creation epic as a masterwork of ancient Mesopotamian mythological poetry. Scholars analyze its narrative structure, poetic techniques, rhetorical strategies, and its place within the broader tradition of ancient Near Eastern literature.

## Key Literary Approaches

### Narrative Structure
The seven-tablet structure of the Enuma Elish follows a carefully crafted narrative arc: from primordial chaos through generational conflict to cosmic battle, creation, and the establishment of order. The narrative builds toward a climax in Tablet IV (the battle with Tiamat) before shifting to the ordering of the cosmos and the cataloguing of Marduk's authority.

### Poetic Technique
The composition employs characteristic features of Akkadian poetry:
- **Formulaic repetition**: Repeated passages that convey messages between gods
- **Parallelism**: Synonymous and antithetical parallel lines
- **Epic catalogues**: Lists of divine names, cosmic features, and divine attributes
- **Dramatic speeches**: Extended orations that advance the narrative and develop character

### Rhetorical Purpose
The Enuma Elish is simultaneously literature and propaganda — a poetic composition that serves the political-theological purpose of establishing Marduk's supremacy and Babylon's preeminence.

### Genre and Context
The text belongs to the genre of cosmogonic myth, combining elements of theogony (divine genealogy), theomachy (divine combat), and etiology (explanation of origins).
