## Overview

Archaeological study of the Enuma Elish examines the cuneiform tablets on which the text was inscribed, their findspots, and the broader material context of Babylonian religious practice. The text's archaeological history is intertwined with the early development of Assyriology as a discipline.

## Key Archaeological Discoveries

### Discovery at Nineveh
The primary tablets of the Enuma Elish were discovered in the ruins of the library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh (modern Mosul, Iraq) during excavations by Austen Henry Layard and Hormuzd Rassam in the 1850s. George Smith first published the text in 1876.

### The Akitu Festival Context
Archaeological and textual evidence indicates that the Enuma Elish was recited during the Akitu (New Year) festival in Babylon, connecting the text to specific ritual contexts and architectural spaces.

### Subsequent Finds
Additional fragments and versions have been found at various Mesopotamian sites, allowing scholars to reconstruct damaged portions and understand regional variations.
