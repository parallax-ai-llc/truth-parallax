## Overview

Orthodox interpretation of Philemon, guided by the homilies of John Chrysostom, emphasizes the transfiguration of human relationships through the gospel. The letter illustrates the Orthodox understanding that in Christ all human bonds are elevated and transformed without being destroyed.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Transfiguration of Social Bonds
Orthodox reading emphasizes that the gospel does not abolish natural and social relationships but transfigures them. Onesimus remains Philemon's servant in the social order, but both are now united in a higher bond as brothers in Christ. This reflects the Orthodox understanding that creation is not rejected but transformed.

### Chrysostom's Reading
John Chrysostom's three homilies on Philemon are foundational for Orthodox interpretation. Chrysostom praised Paul's rhetorical delicacy and drew moral lessons about forgiveness, hospitality, and the duty of the powerful toward the vulnerable.

### Kenotic Love
Paul's willingness to bear Onesimus's debt (v.18-19) is read as an icon of Christ's kenotic love — the self-emptying described in Philippians 2:5-8 made visible in pastoral practice.
