## Overview

Islamic engagement with Philemon examines the letter's approach to slavery in comparison with Qur'anic legislation and prophetic *sunnah*. Both traditions inherited the institution of slavery and sought to regulate and humanize it, while ultimately providing theological resources for its abolition.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Slavery in Islamic Perspective
Islamic law (*fiqh*) extensively regulated slavery, mandating humane treatment and establishing multiple pathways to manumission, including the freeing of slaves as expiation for sins (Qur'an 5:89; 58:3). Muslim scholars compare Paul's appeal for Onesimus with the Prophet's teaching: "Your slaves are your brothers whom Allah has placed under your authority" (Sahih al-Bukhari).

### Brotherhood in Faith
Paul's declaration that Onesimus is now "a beloved brother" (v.16) resonates with the Qur'anic principle that "the believers are but brothers" (49:10). Both traditions affirm that faith creates a bond that transcends social distinctions, though the social implications were worked out differently.

### Forgiveness and Mercy
The appeal for Philemon to forgive and receive Onesimus reflects values central to Islam. The Qur'an repeatedly extols forgiveness: "Let them pardon and overlook. Would you not like that Allah should forgive you?" (24:22).
