## Overview

Philemon has been deeply significant in Protestant social ethics, particularly in debates over slavery and abolition. Protestant abolitionists in the 18th and 19th centuries argued that Paul's appeal — treating Onesimus "no longer as a slave, but as a beloved brother" (v.16) — subverts the institution of slavery from within.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Imputed Righteousness as Analogy
Protestants frequently read Paul's offer to "charge that to my account" (v.18) as an analogy for the imputation of Christ's righteousness. Just as Paul takes on Onesimus's debt, so Christ takes on the believer's sin-debt before the Father.

### Appeal Rather Than Command
Paul's rhetorical strategy — appealing on the basis of love rather than commanding by apostolic authority (vv.8-9) — illustrates Protestant ideals of persuasion and voluntary obedience in Christian community.

### Slavery and Freedom
The letter forced Protestant traditions to wrestle with the relationship between spiritual equality and social structures. It remains a test case for how the gospel transforms social relationships.
