---
title: "Majjhima Nikaya - Chapter 2: Sabbāsava Sutta (All the Taints)"
scripture: "majjhima-nikaya"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Thus have I heard. On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling at Savatthi, in Jeta's Grove. There he addressed the monks: "I will teach you the way of restraining all the taints. Listen and attend carefully."

**2** "Monks, I say that the destruction of the taints is for one who knows and sees, not for one who does not know and does not see. Who knows and sees what? Wise attention and unwise attention."

**3** "When a person attends unwisely, unarisen taints arise, and arisen taints increase. When a person attends wisely, unarisen taints do not arise, and arisen taints are abandoned."

**4** "There are taints that should be abandoned by seeing. The untaught ordinary person does not understand what things are fit for attention and what things are unfit for attention. He attends to things unfit for attention and does not attend to things fit for attention."

**5** "He attends unwisely in this way: 'Was I in the past? Was I not in the past? What was I in the past? How was I in the past? Shall I be in the future?' Or he is inwardly perplexed about the present: 'Am I? Am I not? What am I? How am I? Where has this being come from? Where will it go?'"

**6** "When he attends unwisely in this way, one of six views arises in him: 'I have a self,' or 'I have no self,' or 'I perceive self with self,' or 'I perceive not-self with self,' or 'I perceive self with not-self,' or 'This self of mine speaks and feels and experiences the results of good and bad actions.'"

**7** "But the well-taught noble disciple attends wisely: 'This is suffering. This is the origin of suffering. This is the cessation of suffering. This is the way leading to the cessation of suffering.' When he attends wisely in this way, three fetters are abandoned: identity view, doubt, and adherence to rules and observances."

**8** "There are taints that should be abandoned by restraining. A monk, reflecting wisely, dwells with the sense faculties restrained. Taints that might arise through non-restraint of the senses are abandoned."

**9** "There are taints that should be abandoned by using. A monk, reflecting wisely, uses the robe, alms food, lodging, and medicines only for the purpose of warding off cold, heat, hunger, and disease—not for pleasure or adornment."

**10** "There are taints that should be abandoned by enduring. A monk, reflecting wisely, endures cold, heat, hunger, thirst, flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, snakes, harsh speech, and bodily feelings that are painful."
