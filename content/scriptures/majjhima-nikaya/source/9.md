---
title: "Majjhima Nikaya - Chapter 9: Culavedalla Sutta (The Shorter Set of Questions and Answers)"
scripture: "majjhima-nikaya"
chapter: 9
---

**1** Thus have I heard. On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling at Rajagaha. Then the layman Visakha went to the nun Dhammadinna and asked questions.

**2** "Lady, what is identity?" "Friend Visakha, these five aggregates of clinging are called identity by the Blessed One: the aggregate of form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness subject to clinging."

**3** "Lady, what is the origin of identity?" "It is this craving that leads to renewed becoming, accompanied by delight and lust, finding delight here and there—that is, craving for sense-pleasures, craving for becoming, craving for non-becoming."

**4** "Lady, what is the cessation of identity?" "It is the remainderless fading away and cessation of that very craving, the giving up, relinquishing, releasing, and letting go of it."

**5** "Lady, what is the Noble Eightfold Path?" "It is right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration."

**6** "Lady, are the three aggregates—virtue, concentration, wisdom—included in the Noble Eightfold Path, or is the Noble Eightfold Path included in the three aggregates?" "The three aggregates are not included in the Noble Eightfold Path, but the Noble Eightfold Path is included in the three aggregates."

**7** "Right speech, right action, and right livelihood are included in the aggregate of virtue. Right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration are included in the aggregate of concentration. Right view and right intention are included in the aggregate of wisdom."

**8** "Lady, what is feeling?" "There are three kinds of feeling: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling." "What is the underlying tendency of pleasant feeling? The underlying tendency of lust. Of painful feeling? The underlying tendency of aversion. Of neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling? The underlying tendency of ignorance."

**9** "Lady, what is the counterpart of the cessation of perception and feeling?" "Friend Visakha, the cessation of perception and feeling should not be described as pleasant or painful. When a monk enters it, speech has ceased, bodily activity has ceased, and mental activity has ceased, but his life is not exhausted, his vital warmth has not subsided, and his faculties are bright and clear."

**10** Visakha, having heard the answers, went to the Blessed One and reported them. The Blessed One said: "Visakha, the nun Dhammadinna is wise. If you had asked me those questions, I would have answered in exactly the same way."
