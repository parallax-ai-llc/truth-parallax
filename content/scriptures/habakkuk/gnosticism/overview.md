## Overview

Gnostic readers find in the Book of Habakkuk a prophetic articulation of their central complaint against the created order: why does the world reward the wicked and crush the righteous? For Gnostic interpreters, Habakkuk's anguished questions are not merely addressed to God but expose the fundamental injustice of a cosmos governed by the Demiurge. The answer — "the righteous shall live by his faith" — is reread as "the righteous shall live by his *gnosis*," by the saving knowledge that liberates the soul from the prison of the material world.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The Gnostic reading of Habakkuk transforms the book's theodicy into a cosmological complaint. The Babylonians, whom God raises up as instruments of judgment, represent the archontic forces that the Demiurge uses to maintain his control over the material realm. The five woe oracles of chapter 2 become indictments of the material world's systemic corruption. The theophany of chapter 3 is read as a vision of the true God's power piercing through the Demiurge's domain.

## Theological Emphases

- **Cosmic Theodicy**: Habakkuk's complaint exposes the injustice inherent in the Demiurge's cosmos
- **Gnosis as Faith**: The "faithfulness" by which the righteous live is reinterpreted as saving knowledge
- **The Woe Against the World**: The five woes condemn the structures of the material order itself
- **The True God Revealed**: The theophany of chapter 3 glimpses the God beyond the Demiurge
