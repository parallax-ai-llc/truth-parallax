## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Habakkuk is renowned for the verse "the righteous shall live by his faithfulness" (2:4), which the Talmud (Makkot 24a) identifies as the ultimate distillation of all 613 commandments into a single principle. The Hebrew word *emunah* — faithfulness, steadfastness, trust — captures the essence of the covenant relationship. The Dead Sea Scrolls community produced a *Pesher Habakkuk* that reinterpreted the entire book in light of their own historical circumstances, demonstrating the text's enduring power to speak to new crises.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Rabbinic interpretation honors Habakkuk's willingness to question God while affirming the ultimate justice of divine governance. The medieval commentators — Rashi, Radak, and Abarbanel — engaged closely with the historical context of the Babylonian threat while drawing broader theological lessons. The Pesher Habakkuk from Qumran provides a fascinating example of how Second Temple Judaism reread prophetic texts in light of contemporary events.

## Theological Emphases

- **Emunah**: Faithfulness to God is the single principle on which all the commandments stand
- **Theodicy**: Habakkuk's questions about divine justice are honored as legitimate expressions of covenantal relationship
- **Prophetic Freedom**: The prophet may question God — this is not blasphemy but the privilege of intimacy
- **The Pesher Tradition**: The book's openness to reinterpretation reflects the living quality of prophetic scripture
