## Emunah: The Foundation of All Commandments

Jewish interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 — "the righteous shall live by his *emunah*" — reaches its most famous expression in the Talmud (Makkot 24a), where Rabbi Simlai traces a progression from Moses' 613 commandments through David's 11, Isaiah's 6, and Micah's 3, until Habakkuk reduces them all to one: *emunah* — faithfulness, steadfastness, trust in God. This verse is understood not as a replacement of the mitzvot but as their root and foundation. All the commandments flow from and return to the single principle of faithful trust in God.

## The Pesher Habakkuk

The Dead Sea Scrolls community produced a *pesher* (interpretive commentary) on Habakkuk that reinterpreted chapters 1-2 in light of their own historical crisis. The "righteous" became the Teacher of Righteousness; the "wicked" became the Wicked Priest. The Pesher demonstrates the enduring relevance of Habakkuk's text: each generation of Jewish readers has found in it a mirror of their own struggles with injustice and divine delay.

## The Five Woes and Universal Justice

The five woe oracles are read in Jewish interpretation as statements of universal moral law — applicable to all nations, not only to Babylon. Rashi and Radak apply them to the specific historical context of Babylonian imperialism, but the principles they articulate — against greed, violence, exploitation, shame, and idolatry — are understood as binding on all humanity under the Noahide laws.
