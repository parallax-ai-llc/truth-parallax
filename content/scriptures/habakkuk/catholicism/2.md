## The Righteous Shall Live by Faithfulness

Catholic interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 reads the verse within the broader context of Catholic soteriology. The *fides* by which the righteous live is not bare intellectual assent but *fides formata caritate* — faith formed and animated by love. The Council of Trent, while affirming the necessity of faith, insisted that the faith that justifies is the faith that works through love. Habakkuk's vision supports this reading: the righteous person's life of faith is a life of faithful action, not mere passive trust.

## The Watchtower and Contemplation

Habakkuk's decision to station himself on the watchtower (2:1) and wait for God's response has been read in Catholic contemplative tradition as a model of prayerful watchfulness. The monk, the contemplative, the person of prayer takes up a position of attentive waiting — not passive but actively receptive to God's word. The great Carmelite and Ignatian traditions of spiritual discernment echo Habakkuk's posture.

## "The LORD Is in His Holy Temple" (2:20)

This verse is used in Catholic liturgical tradition as a call to reverent silence before the mystery of God. The five woes expose the cacophony of human sin; the closing verse calls for the silence of adoration. Catholic worship moves from the confession of sin to the silence of contemplation, and Habakkuk 2:20 marks that transition.
