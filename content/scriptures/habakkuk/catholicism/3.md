## The Canticle of Habakkuk

Catholic interpretation treasures Habakkuk 3 as one of the great canticles of the Old Testament. It is incorporated into the Liturgy of the Hours and prayed during the Friday morning office of Lent. The theophanic vision of God's power — his glory covering the heavens, his brightness like the sun, his might shaking the nations — is read in the Catholic tradition as a prophetic anticipation of Christ's coming in glory. The canticle moves from vision to trust, modeling the movement of Lenten prayer.

## The Theophany and the Incarnation

The Church Fathers read "God came from Teman, the Holy One from Mount Paran" (3:3) as a prefiguration of the Incarnation. Origen and other patristic commentators saw in God's "coming" from the south a reference to the divine entry into human history. The theophany is not merely a memory of past acts but a prophecy of God's definitive coming in Christ.

## Habakkuk's Act of Faith (3:17-19)

The prophet's declaration of faith in the face of total deprivation is read in Catholic spirituality as a model of *abandonment to divine providence*. The Carmelite tradition of John of the Cross — the "dark night of the soul" in which all consolations are stripped away and only naked faith in God remains — finds a biblical precedent in Habakkuk's closing words.
