## Overview

The Book of Habakkuk is unique among the prophets because its primary dialogue is not between the prophet and the people but between the prophet and God. Habakkuk questions why God tolerates injustice in Judah, and God's answer — that he is raising up the Babylonians as an instrument of judgment — only deepens the prophet's anguish: how can a righteous God use an even more wicked nation to punish his people? The divine response, "the righteous shall live by his faithfulness" (2:4), became one of the most consequential verses in the history of theology, foundational to Paul's theology and to the Protestant Reformation.

## Structure

1. **Chapter 1**: Habakkuk's two complaints and God's two responses; the Babylonian threat
2. **Chapter 2**: The watchtower vision; the five woe oracles; "the righteous shall live by his faithfulness"
3. **Chapter 3**: Habakkuk's prayer — a theophanic hymn of God's power and the prophet's resolved trust

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: The Pesher Habakkuk from the Dead Sea Scrolls shows how the book was reinterpreted for new crises; "the righteous shall live by his faithfulness" is a key rabbinic text
- **Christianity**: Habakkuk 2:4 is quoted three times in the New Testament (Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11, Hebrews 10:38) and was the verse that sparked Luther's Reformation insight
- **Islam**: The prophet's wrestling with divine justice mirrors the Quranic principle that God's wisdom surpasses human understanding
- **Gnosticism**: Habakkuk's complaint against a world where the wicked prosper aligns with Gnostic suspicion of the created order

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Theodicy | The central question: why does God permit evil, and why does he use the wicked to judge the less wicked? |
| Faith/Faithfulness | The righteous survive by trusting in God's ultimate justice even when present circumstances are unjust |
| Divine Sovereignty | God orchestrates the movements of nations according to his purposes |
| The Woe Oracles | Five oracles condemning greed, violence, exploitation, idolatry, and drunkenness |
| Resolved Trust | The book ends not with answers but with the prophet's decision to trust God regardless |
