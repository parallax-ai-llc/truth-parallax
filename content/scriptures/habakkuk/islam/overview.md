## Overview

While the Book of Habakkuk is not part of the Quran, its central themes resonate with core Islamic principles. The prophet's struggle with the problem of evil — why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer? — mirrors questions addressed throughout the Quran and hadith literature. The Islamic response, like Habakkuk's, centers on *tawakkul* (trust in God) and the conviction that God's wisdom surpasses human understanding: "Perhaps you dislike a thing and it is good for you; and perhaps you like a thing and it is bad for you. And Allah knows, while you know not" (Quran 2:216).

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Muslim scholars engage with Habakkuk through the *Isra'iliyyat* framework. Some medieval Muslim scholars also noted Habakkuk 3:3 — "God came from Teman, the Holy One from Mount Paran" — and, following earlier traditions, read the reference to Paran as an allusion to the Arabian Peninsula and the coming of the Prophet Muhammad, since Paran is associated with Ishmael's lineage in Islamic tradition.

## Theological Emphases

- **Tawakkul**: Trust in God's plan even when the present is unjust or incomprehensible
- **Divine Wisdom**: God's use of nations as instruments of judgment reflects a wisdom beyond human grasp
- **Sovereignty over Nations**: God raises and lowers civilizations according to his will
- **Patience in Suffering**: The righteous endure suffering with faith, knowing that God's justice will prevail
