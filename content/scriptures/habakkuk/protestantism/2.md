## "The Righteous Shall Live by His Faith" (2:4)

Protestant interpretation of Habakkuk 2 is dominated by the verse that ignited the Reformation. Luther recounted that it was while meditating on Paul's quotation of Habakkuk 2:4 in Romans 1:17 that the doctrine of justification by faith alone became clear to him. The verse declares that in a world where the wicked seem to prosper, the righteous person survives and thrives by trusting God — not by human works, political alliances, or military strength.

## The Watchtower Vision (2:1-5)

Habakkuk stations himself on the watchtower to await God's response to his complaint. The instruction to "write the vision and make it plain" emphasizes the clarity and permanence of God's answer. Protestant interpreters note that the vision has a temporal dimension — "it awaits its appointed time" — teaching the believer to trust God's timing as well as God's justice.

## The Five Woe Oracles (2:6-20)

The five woes condemn greed, violence, exploitation, drunkenness, and idolatry. Protestant commentators read these not as addressed only to Babylon but as universal moral principles. The final woe — against idolatry — climaxes in the great contrast: "The LORD is in his holy temple; let all the earth keep silence before him" (2:20). The living God stands in judgment over all human substitutes.
