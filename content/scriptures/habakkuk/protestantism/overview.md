## Overview

No book of the Minor Prophets has had a greater impact on Protestant theology than Habakkuk. The verse "the righteous shall live by his faith" (2:4) was the spark that ignited Martin Luther's Reformation insight and became the foundational text for the doctrine of *sola fide* — justification by faith alone. Paul's quotation of this verse in Romans 1:17 and Galatians 3:11 made it central to Protestant soteriology, and the book as a whole models the believer's honest struggle with the problem of evil.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Luther and Calvin both wrote on Habakkuk with great attention, seeing in the prophet a model of faith that trusts God's justice even when present circumstances seem to contradict it. The five woe oracles of chapter 2 are read as God's verdict on all unjust power. Chapter 3's prayer-hymn is read as the summit of the book: a declaration of faith that transcends circumstances.

## Theological Emphases

- **Sola Fide**: Habakkuk 2:4 is the cornerstone text for justification by faith alone
- **Honest Prayer**: The prophet models the freedom to bring complaints and hard questions to God
- **Theodicy**: The book wrestles with the question of why God permits evil and uses wicked instruments
- **Resolved Trust**: Faith means trusting God's character even when his actions seem inscrutable
