## The Prophet's Prayer-Hymn

Protestant interpretation of Habakkuk 3 reads the chapter as the theological resolution of the entire book. After questioning God's justice (chapter 1) and receiving a partial answer (chapter 2), the prophet responds with a prayer-hymn that is simultaneously a vision of God's power and a declaration of faith. The theophanic imagery — God coming from Teman, pestilence going before him, mountains scattering, the sun and moon standing still — evokes the Exodus and Sinai traditions, reminding both prophet and reader that the God who acted in the past will act again.

## "Yet I Will Rejoice in the LORD" (3:17-19)

These closing verses are among the most powerful declarations of faith in all of Scripture. The prophet envisions total economic collapse — no figs, no grapes, no olives, no grain, no flocks, no herds — and declares that even then, "I will rejoice in the LORD." Protestant theology treasures this passage as the ultimate expression of *sola fide*: faith that rests not on circumstances but on God alone.

## The Movement from Complaint to Praise

The arc of the entire book — from desperate complaint to resolute praise — is read as a model of the Christian life. Faith does not mean the absence of doubt or the suppression of hard questions. It means bringing those questions to God and ultimately arriving at a trust that transcends them. Habakkuk does not receive a full answer, but he receives something better: the presence and power of God himself.
