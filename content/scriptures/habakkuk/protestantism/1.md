## The Prophet's Two Complaints

Protestant interpretation of Habakkuk 1 reads the chapter as a model of honest, anguished prayer. Habakkuk's first complaint (1:2-4) asks why God tolerates injustice within Judah — violence, strife, and the paralysis of the law. God's answer (1:5-11) — that he is raising up the Babylonians as an instrument of judgment — provokes a deeper complaint (1:12-17): how can a righteous God use an even more wicked nation to punish his people?

## The Freedom to Question God

Calvin emphasized that Habakkuk's complaints are not sinful but faithful. The prophet does not doubt God's existence or power but struggles with how God exercises his sovereignty. Protestant tradition values this model of prayer: faith that is honest about its struggles is more authentic than faith that suppresses its questions.

## The Babylonian Instrument (1:5-11)

God's declaration that he is raising up the Chaldeans shocks the prophet because it seems to compound injustice rather than resolve it. Protestant commentators see here a recurring biblical pattern: God's ways are not our ways, and the instruments God uses in history often confound human expectations. The chapter establishes the problem that the rest of the book will address.
