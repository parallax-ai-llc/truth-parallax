## The Fourth Ode

In Orthodox liturgical practice, Habakkuk 3 is the basis for the Fourth Ode of the Canon, sung at Matins. The prayer-hymn is woven into the daily worship of the Church, ensuring that Habakkuk's vision of divine power and his declaration of faith are continually before the faithful. The theophanic imagery — God clothed in glory, the mountains trembling, the seas parting — connects the prophetic vision to the great acts of salvation history: the Exodus, the Transfiguration, and the Parousia.

## The Theophany of Glory

Orthodox theology reads Habakkuk's theophany as a vision of the uncreated divine energies — the glory of God that permeates creation without being reduced to it. The statement "His glory covered the heavens, and the earth was full of his praise" (3:3) resonates with the Orthodox understanding of creation as transfigured by divine presence. The theophany is not a display of raw power but a revelation of beauty and holiness.

## Faith as Participation in the Divine Life

Habakkuk's closing declaration — "Yet I will rejoice in the LORD, I will take joy in the God of my salvation" (3:18) — is read in Orthodox theology as a statement of participation in the divine life. Joy in God is not a human emotion produced by favorable circumstances but a fruit of *theosis* — the soul's union with God that transcends all external conditions.
