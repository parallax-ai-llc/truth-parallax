## Overview

In the Eastern Orthodox tradition, the Book of Habakkuk is treasured above all for its magnificent canticle in chapter 3, which is incorporated into the liturgical life of the Church as part of the biblical odes sung at Matins. The theophanic vision of God coming from Teman, shaking the earth, splitting the rivers, and riding on his chariots of salvation is read as a revelation of divine glory that anticipates the Incarnation, the Transfiguration, and the Second Coming.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The Greek Fathers — particularly Cyril of Alexandria and Theodoret — composed homilies and commentaries on Habakkuk. The Orthodox reading emphasizes the movement of the book from complaint to praise, from questioning to adoration — a movement that mirrors the liturgical progression from penitence to doxology. Habakkuk 2:4 is read not as a doctrine of forensic justification but as a call to living faithfulness that participates in God's own life.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theophanic Glory**: The canticle reveals God's majestic power over creation and history
- **Liturgical Prayer**: Chapter 3 is a living part of the Church's worship, not merely a historical text
- **From Complaint to Praise**: The book models the spiritual journey from anguish to trust
- **Living Faithfulness**: The righteous live by a faithfulness that is participation in the divine life
