## The Final Entries and Scholarly Questions

The Copper Scroll's concluding reference to a duplicate document has generated extensive scholarly discussion about the text's purpose and provenance. The reference suggests a systematic archival practice — the creation of multiple copies of important records — that would be more consistent with an institutional origin (such as the Temple administration) than with a private or fictional composition. Judah Lefkovits has argued that this archival feature, combined with the text's prosaic inventory style, strongly supports its authenticity as a genuine treasure record.

The material composition of the scroll itself has been studied using metallurgical analysis. The copper sheets (approximately 99% copper with traces of tin) were produced through a hammering process rather than casting, a technique consistent with metalworking practices of the late Second Temple period. The decision to inscribe the text on copper rather than perishable parchment suggests that the authors intended the record to survive for an extended period — further evidence of a practical rather than literary purpose.

The dating of the Copper Scroll remains debated. Paleographic analysis of the letter forms suggests a date in the mid-first century CE, possibly around 25-75 CE. If this dating is correct, the scroll's composition may be connected to the growing instability in Judea that culminated in the revolt against Rome (66-70 CE). The hypothesis that the Copper Scroll records an emergency concealment of Temple wealth before or during the Roman siege provides a plausible historical context, though definitive proof remains elusive. The scroll stands as one of the most intriguing and unresolved puzzles in Dead Sea Scrolls scholarship.
