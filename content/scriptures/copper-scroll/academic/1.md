## Treasure Locations in the Jerusalem Region: Scholarly Analysis

The Jerusalem treasure locations described in Chapter 1 have been subjected to extensive archaeological and topographic analysis. Scholars have attempted to match the Copper Scroll's descriptions with known features of the Jerusalem landscape, including the Temple Mount, the Kidron Valley, and surrounding areas. The results have been mixed: some locations correspond to known architectural features, while others use terminology that remains obscure or refers to structures no longer extant.

The quantities of treasure listed — hundreds of talents of gold and silver — have been a major point of scholarly debate. Józef Milik calculated the total at approximately 4,630 talents, an amount he considered impossibly large, supporting his view that the text is fictional. Allegro and others have countered that the Temple treasury was known from Josephus and other sources to contain vast wealth, making such quantities plausible if not certain. Al Wolters has proposed that some of the weight measurements may refer to smaller units than the standard talent, potentially reducing the total to more credible levels.

The linguistic analysis of Chapter 1's Hebrew has revealed features characteristic of Mishnaic rather than Biblical Hebrew — the use of the definite article in construct chains, distinctive vocabulary items, and spelling conventions that differ from the literary Hebrew of other Qumran texts. These features support the hypothesis that the Copper Scroll originated in a different social and linguistic milieu from the Qumran community, possibly among Temple personnel in Jerusalem.
