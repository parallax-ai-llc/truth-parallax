## The Final Entries and the Mystery

The Copper Scroll's concluding mystery — hidden treasure, a sealed duplicate, locations that resist identification — resonates with the Orthodox appreciation for the apophatic dimension of divine reality. Orthodox theology, following Pseudo-Dionysius and the Cappadocian Fathers, insists that God is ultimately beyond human comprehension, known truly but never exhaustively. The Copper Scroll's unresolved mystery provides a material analogy for this theological principle: some realities resist our grasp, and the attempt to possess them fully misses their essential nature.

The reference to a duplicate scroll also connects to the Orthodox understanding of tradition as a living transmission — not merely a written record but a dynamic reality communicated through persons, practices, and the life of the community. The Copper Scroll's knowledge could not be preserved through a single written document; it required a network of transmission that included both text and personal knowledge. Similarly, Orthodox theology insists that the deposit of faith is transmitted through Scripture, Sacred Tradition, and the lived experience of the Church — not through any single channel alone.

The Copper Scroll's enduring mystery ultimately serves as a reminder that the deepest treasures lie beyond the reach of human recovery. Orthodox eschatology teaches that the fullness of God's kingdom — the ultimate treasure — is hidden with Christ in God (Colossians 3:3) and will be revealed at the Second Coming. Until then, the faithful live in a world of partial revelation and concealed glory, trusting that what remains hidden is preserved by God for the joy of the age to come.
