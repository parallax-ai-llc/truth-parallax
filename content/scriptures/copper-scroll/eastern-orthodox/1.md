## Treasure Locations in the Jerusalem Region

The Copper Scroll's catalog of treasure hidden around Jerusalem resonates with the Orthodox tradition's deep reverence for the Holy City as the site of salvation's central events. Orthodox pilgrimage to Jerusalem, established from the earliest centuries, reflects the conviction that the physical landscape of the Holy Land bears an enduring sacred significance. The Copper Scroll's detailed topographic knowledge testifies to the same intimate relationship with Jerusalem's sacred geography.

Orthodox interpreters read the Temple treasures described in Chapter 1 typologically. The gold and silver vessels of the Temple worship — hidden to preserve them from desecration — foreshadow the sacred vessels of the Divine Liturgy, which the Orthodox tradition treats with extraordinary reverence. The antimension, the chalice, the diskos, and other liturgical objects are understood as participating in the heavenly worship they make present, just as the Temple vessels participated in the sacrificial system God ordained.

The act of hiding sacred objects to preserve them from desecration also connects to the Orthodox experience of iconoclasm — both the eighth-century Byzantine controversy and later persecutions. Orthodox Christians hid icons, sacred texts, and liturgical objects during periods of persecution, an act of faithful preservation that mirrors the Copper Scroll's concealment of Temple treasure. The Orthodox Church venerates as heroes those who protected sacred things from destruction, seeing in their action a participation in God's own care for what is holy.
