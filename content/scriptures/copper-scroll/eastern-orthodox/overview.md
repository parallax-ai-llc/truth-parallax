## Overview

Eastern Orthodox engagement with the Copper Scroll draws upon the Orthodox tradition's deep appreciation for the sanctity of material things and the theology of icons. The Copper Scroll's implicit conviction that sacred objects possess inherent significance — worth hiding, preserving, and cataloging — resonates with the Orthodox theology of matter as capable of bearing divine presence and grace.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

Orthodox interpreters read the Copper Scroll within the typological framework of the Temple as a foreshadowing of the Church. The hidden treasures of the Temple anticipate the "hidden treasures" of the faith that the Church preserves — the sacramental mysteries, the apostolic tradition, and the deposit of faith that is both openly proclaimed and mysteriously deep. The Seventh Ecumenical Council's defense of icons rested partly on the principle that material things can be vehicles of divine presence — a principle the Copper Scroll's world clearly shared.

The scroll's mystery — its unrecovered treasure, its cryptic locations, its uncertain purpose — also resonates with the Orthodox appreciation for apophatic theology and the conviction that the deepest realities remain partially hidden even in revelation. The Copper Scroll, like the Orthodox liturgy, gestures toward a reality that cannot be fully grasped or possessed but only approached with reverence and wonder.
