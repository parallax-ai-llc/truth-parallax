## Overview

The Copper Scroll (3Q15) is one of the most enigmatic texts among the Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in Cave 3 at Qumran in 1952. Unlike the other scrolls, which are written on parchment or papyrus, this text was inscribed on thin sheets of copper, rolled into two separate scrolls. It catalogs sixty-four locations where vast quantities of gold, silver, and other precious items were allegedly buried throughout the Judean region.

## Structure

The Copper Scroll is organized into three sections based on geographic distribution:

1. **Jerusalem Region** (Chapter 1): Treasure deposits in and around the Temple Mount, the Kidron Valley, and surrounding areas
2. **Judean Desert** (Chapter 2): Caches hidden in wadis, caves, and landmarks of the Judean wilderness
3. **Final Entries** (Chapter 3): The last treasure descriptions and a mysterious reference to a duplicate document with additional details

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Copper Scroll raises questions that interest multiple traditions:

- **Essene**: Its relationship to the Qumran community and possible Essene connections to Temple wealth
- **Judaism**: Its connection to the Temple treasury and the fate of sacred objects after 70 CE
- **Christianity**: Its illumination of the material and economic context of first-century Judea
- **Academic**: Its archaeological, linguistic, and historical puzzles

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Hidden Treasure | Sixty-four caches of gold, silver, and precious items buried across Judea |

| Material Culture | Concrete evidence of economic life and wealth in Second Temple Judea |
| Eschatological Hope | Possible connection to expectations of future Temple restoration |
