## Treasure Locations in the Jerusalem Region

The Copper Scroll's catalog of sacred objects hidden around Jerusalem resonates with the Catholic tradition's profound respect for sacred materiality. The meticulous concealment of Temple vessels and liturgical objects reflects a conviction, shared by Catholic teaching, that consecrated things participate in a sanctity that demands reverent treatment. The Catholic practice of preserving relics, maintaining the reservation of the Blessed Sacrament, and treating sacred vessels with particular care all express the same principle that animated the Copper Scroll's authors.

Catholic interpreters note the continuity between the Temple worship described implicitly in the Copper Scroll and the Church's own liturgical life. The gold and silver vessels of the Temple, used in sacrifice and worship, are typological precursors to the sacred vessels of the Eucharistic liturgy — the chalice, paten, and ciborium that hold the Body and Blood of Christ. The care taken to preserve the Temple's vessels foreshadows the Church's own veneration of its liturgical instruments.

The Jerusalem locations described in Chapter 1 also hold significance for Catholic pilgrimage tradition. The Holy Land, and Jerusalem in particular, has been a central destination for Catholic pilgrims since the fourth century. The Copper Scroll's detailed topographic references to sites around the Temple Mount and the Kidron Valley connect to a landscape that Catholic devotion has continuously venerated as the stage of salvation history.
