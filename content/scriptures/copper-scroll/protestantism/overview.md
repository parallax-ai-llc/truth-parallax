## Overview

Protestant scholars have engaged the Copper Scroll primarily as historical evidence for the material and economic world of first-century Judea, while also drawing theological contrasts between the scroll's concern with earthly treasure and the New Testament's emphasis on spiritual riches. Jesus's teaching to "store up treasures in heaven" (Matthew 6:19-20) and Paul's insistence that "the things that are seen are transient, but the things that are unseen are eternal" (2 Corinthians 4:18) provide a theological lens through which Protestant interpreters read the Copper Scroll.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

Protestant engagement with the Copper Scroll tends to emphasize the contrast between material and spiritual wealth. While recognizing the scroll's historical significance, Protestant interpreters draw attention to the theological irony of meticulously hidden treasure that was never recovered — a concrete illustration of Jesus's warnings about the futility of earthly accumulation. The parable of the treasure hidden in a field (Matthew 13:44) takes on additional resonance when read alongside the Copper Scroll's catalog of literally buried wealth.

The scroll also provides context for understanding the economic dimensions of the Temple system that Jesus confronted. His cleansing of the Temple (Mark 11:15-17), his prediction of its destruction (Mark 13:2), and his claim to replace the Temple with his own body (John 2:19-21) all engage with the material reality to which the Copper Scroll testifies — a Temple establishment of enormous wealth and economic power.
