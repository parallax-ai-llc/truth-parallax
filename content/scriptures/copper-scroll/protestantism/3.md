## The Final Entries and the Mystery

The Copper Scroll's reference to a duplicate scroll and its never-recovered treasure illustrate a theme central to Protestant eschatology: the distinction between earthly things that pass away and heavenly realities that endure. The meticulous effort to catalog and hide vast wealth ultimately proved futile — the treasure was never recovered, the duplicate document was never found, and the Temple whose worship the treasure served was destroyed.

Protestant theology reads this outcome as a parable of sorts. Jesus warned that "where your treasure is, there your heart will be also" (Matthew 6:21), and the Copper Scroll's lost treasure demonstrates the transience of earthly wealth with stark historical concreteness. The contrast with the "imperishable inheritance" promised in 1 Peter 1:4 — "kept in heaven for you" — could not be sharper.

Yet Protestant interpreters also recognize the genuine piety behind the Copper Scroll's concealment of Temple objects. The desire to preserve sacred things from destruction reflects a reverence for God's worship that the New Testament affirms even as it redirects. The Copper Scroll's mystery ultimately points beyond itself to the deeper mystery that Paul proclaims: "Christ in you, the hope of glory" (Colossians 1:27) — a treasure that no earthly power can confiscate or destroy.
