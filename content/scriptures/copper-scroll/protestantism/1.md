## Treasure Locations in the Jerusalem Region

The Copper Scroll's catalog of treasure hidden around Jerusalem provides concrete context for Jesus's ministry in a city dominated by the Temple's enormous wealth. Protestant interpreters note the theological significance of Jesus's teachings about wealth and treasure when read against the backdrop of the material reality described in the Copper Scroll — a Temple system that accumulated and administered vast quantities of gold, silver, and precious objects.

Jesus's command to "store up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where neither moth nor rust destroys" (Matthew 6:20) takes on heightened meaning when one considers that literal treasures were being hidden in the earth around Jerusalem. The Copper Scroll's buried gold provides a concrete illustration of the futility Jesus described — treasure hidden, forgotten, and lost, while the "treasure in heaven" that Jesus offered endures eternally.

The chapter also illuminates the economic context of Jesus's cleansing of the Temple. The wealth described in the Copper Scroll, combined with Josephus's accounts of Temple revenue, reveals an institution of immense economic power. Jesus's confrontation with the money changers and his declaration that the Temple had become a "den of robbers" (Mark 11:17) engage directly with this economic reality, challenging the system's priorities and anticipating the destruction that would render all its accumulated wealth irrelevant.
