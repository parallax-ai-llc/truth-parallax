## Overview

Sufi engagement with the Rehatnamas reveals significant parallels between the Khalsa code of discipline and the Sufi tradition of *adab* (spiritual etiquette and discipline). Both traditions understand ethical conduct not as mere rule-following but as the outer expression of an inner spiritual reality. The Sufi *tariqah* (path) orders the disciple's daily life through practices of prayer, remembrance (*dhikr*), fasting, and service, much as the Rehatnama orders the Khalsa Sikh's life through *Nitnem*, *Nam Simran*, *seva*, and moral conduct.

The historical connections between Sikh and Sufi traditions in Punjab add depth to this comparison. Guru Nanak engaged extensively with Sufi masters, and the Sikh emphasis on the *Guru-Sikh* relationship echoes the Sufi *pir-murid* (master-disciple) bond. The Rehatnamas' concern with the balance between outer form and inner substance resonates with the ongoing Sufi dialectic between *zahir* (outward) and *batin* (inward) dimensions of religious life.
