## Overview

In comparative perspective, the Rehatnamas invite analysis alongside other religious codes of conduct and communal discipline. The Khalsa code shares structural and functional similarities with Jewish *halakhah*, Islamic *sharia*, Christian monastic rules (such as the Rule of St. Benedict), and the Buddhist *vinaya*. Each of these systems seeks to order daily life according to spiritual principles, creating a distinctive communal identity through shared practices, dietary regulations, and ethical norms.

The Rehatnamas are particularly interesting in comparative context because they emerge from a tradition that explicitly rejects caste, priesthood, and empty ritual. The Khalsa code thus represents an alternative model of religious discipline — one that is egalitarian in principle, martial in orientation, and designed for householders rather than monastics. Comparative analysis of the Rehatnamas illuminates universal questions about the relationship between faith and practice, individual conscience and communal authority, and the role of visible identity markers in sustaining religious commitment.
