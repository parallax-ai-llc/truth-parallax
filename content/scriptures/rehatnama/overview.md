## Overview

The Rehatnamas are a collection of codes of conduct (*rehat*) attributed to Guru Gobind Singh (1666-1708) and his close companions, prescribing the ethical, spiritual, and social norms for members of the Khalsa — the community of initiated Sikhs. While several Rehatnamas exist, they collectively articulate the distinctive way of life that Guru Gobind Singh envisioned for the Khalsa, encompassing personal discipline, communal responsibility, martial readiness, and devotional practice.

The Rehatnamas address a wide range of topics, from the maintenance of the five articles of faith (*Panj Kakaar* — Kesh, Kangha, Kara, Kachera, Kirpan) to dietary regulations, prayer obligations, marital conduct, and the ethics of warfare. They represent the codification of Sikh identity as it crystallized in the early eighteenth century, a period of intense persecution and existential struggle for the Sikh community.

## Structure

The eight sections of the Rehatnama tradition address the following domains:

| Section | Theme |
|---------|-------|
| 1 | The Khalsa Identity — Amrit, the Five Ks, and the outward form of the Sikh |
| 2 | Daily Spiritual Practice — Nitnem, meditation, and congregational worship |
| 3 | Moral Conduct — Truthfulness, humility, compassion, and service |
| 4 | Dietary and Bodily Discipline — Food regulations, hygiene, and physical fitness |
| 5 | Social Ethics — Relations with family, community, and broader society |
| 6 | Economic Ethics — Honest livelihood, charity, and the sharing of resources |
| 7 | Martial Ethics — The principles of righteous warfare (*Dharma Yudh*) and self-defense |
| 8 | Violations and Restoration — Transgressions (*kurehats*) and the process of reconciliation |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Sikh**: The foundation of Khalsa discipline and the basis for the modern Sikh Rehat Maryada
- **Academic**: A key source for understanding the formation of Sikh identity, communal boundaries, and ethical norms
- **Comparative**: Offers rich material for comparison with other codes of conduct — monastic rules, halakhah, sharia, vinaya
- **Sufi**: Resonances with Sufi ethical disciplines (*adab*) and the ordering of the spiritual life
- **Bhakti**: Engages with the broader Bhakti tension between interior devotion and external form
- **Interfaith**: Raises universal questions about the relationship between faith, practice, identity, and community

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Khalsa Identity | The distinctive visible identity of the initiated Sikh |
| Five Ks (*Panj Kakaar*) | The five articles of faith as symbols of commitment and belonging |
| *Rehat* (Discipline) | The ordering of daily life according to spiritual principles |
| *Seva* (Service) | Selfless service to community and humanity as spiritual practice |
| *Dharma Yudh* | Righteous warfare as a last resort in defense of justice and the oppressed |
| *Sangat* and *Pangat* | The importance of congregational worship and communal eating |
