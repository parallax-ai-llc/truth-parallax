Section 4 of the Rehatnama addresses dietary and bodily discipline, which the Bhakti tradition relates to the body as temple and instrument of devotion. The Rehatnama's prescriptions raise a question that is central to the Bhakti movement: What is the relationship between inner devotion and outer practice? The Sant-Bhakti tradition from which Sikhism emerged was characterized by a radical emphasis on interior devotion over external form, and the Khalsa code's detailed prescriptions might seem to represent a reversal of this emphasis.

The Bhakti reading of this section, however, understands the Khalsa discipline not as a return to the formalism the Sants rejected but as a distinctive kind of outer form — one chosen in love and commitment rather than imposed by birth or social convention. The Rehatnama's prescriptions are understood as the visible expression of an inner reality — the devotee's total dedication to the divine — rather than as substitutes for that inner reality.
