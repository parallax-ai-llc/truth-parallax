## Overview

The Bhakti tradition's engagement with the Rehatnamas reveals a creative tension at the heart of Sikh self-understanding. The Sant-Bhakti movement from which Sikhism emerged was characterized by a radical rejection of external religious forms — caste marks, pilgrimages, elaborate rituals — in favor of interior devotion to the formless God. The Rehatnamas, by contrast, prescribe a detailed external discipline including specific articles of dress, dietary regulations, and communal rituals. Understanding how the Khalsa tradition reconciles these apparently competing impulses is central to Bhakti-informed readings of the Rehatnamas.

The Bhakti perspective suggests that the Khalsa discipline represents not a return to the formalism the Sants rejected but rather a new kind of religious form — one deliberately chosen as an expression of inner commitment rather than imposed by birth or social convention. The Five Ks are understood not as magical talismans but as visible reminders of spiritual vows, maintaining the Bhakti insistence that external practices are valuable only insofar as they express and sustain genuine interior devotion.
