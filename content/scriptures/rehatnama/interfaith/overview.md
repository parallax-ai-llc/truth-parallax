## Overview

In interfaith contexts, the Rehatnamas raise fundamental questions about the nature of religious identity, the relationship between faith and practice, and the role of visible markers in sustaining a community of faith. The Khalsa code's prescription of a distinctive visible identity — particularly the uncut hair (*Kesh*) and turban — has made Sikhs one of the most visibly identifiable religious communities in the world, inviting both admiration and, in some contexts, discrimination and violence.

Interfaith engagement with the Rehatnamas offers opportunities for dialogue on shared ethical concerns — truthfulness, compassion, justice, service to others — as well as on the distinctive ways different traditions balance individual spiritual experience and communal belonging. The Rehatnamas' emphasis on *seva* (selfless service) and *langar* (communal kitchen open to all regardless of faith, caste, or social status) embodies principles that resonate across traditions and have made the Gurdwara one of the most practically inclusive religious institutions in the world.
