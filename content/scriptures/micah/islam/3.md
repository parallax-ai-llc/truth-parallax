## Leadership Accountability

Islamic interpretation of Micah 3 resonates strongly with the Quran's insistence on the accountability of leaders. The Prophet Muhammad said: "Each of you is a shepherd and each of you is responsible for his flock" (Bukhari and Muslim). Micah's devastating critique of rulers who exploit their people, prophets who mislead them, and priests who sell their services aligns with the Islamic condemnation of leaders who betray their trust (*amanah*).

## The Corruption of Religious Authority

The Islamic tradition is particularly sensitive to the corruption of religious knowledge for material gain. The Quran warns repeatedly against scholars who trade God's revelations for worldly benefit. Micah's indictment of prophets who "divine for money" (3:11) echoes this concern and reinforces the Islamic emphasis on the purity and disinterestedness of religious scholarship.

## The Consequences of Corruption

The prophecy that Zion will be destroyed because of its leaders' corruption illustrates the Quranic principle that civilizations fall not because of external enemies but because of internal moral decay. God's judgment comes when a society's leaders — political, religious, and judicial — collectively abandon justice.
