## God's Case Against His People

Islamic interpretation of Micah 6 resonates with the Quran's repeated emphasis on accountability. God has blessed his people with guidance and deliverance, and he holds them responsible for how they respond. The Quran presents a similar dynamic: "O you who have believed, why do you say what you do not do? Great is hatred in the sight of Allah that you say what you do not do" (61:2-3). Micah's covenant lawsuit echoes this principle of divine accountability.

## Justice, Mercy, and Humility

The three requirements of Micah 6:8 correspond closely to core Islamic virtues: *'adl* (justice), *rahmah* (mercy), and *khushu'* (humble devotion before God). The Quran commands: "Be just; that is nearer to righteousness" (5:8), and the Prophet Muhammad declared: "The merciful are shown mercy by the Most Merciful." Micah's synthesis of prophetic ethics finds a natural home in Islamic moral teaching.

## Worship Without Righteousness

The chapter's rejection of lavish sacrifice without moral reform aligns with the Quran's teaching: "It is not their meat nor their blood that reaches Allah: it is your piety that reaches Him" (22:37). Both Micah and the Quran insist that external religious observance without inner transformation and ethical conduct is worthless before God.
