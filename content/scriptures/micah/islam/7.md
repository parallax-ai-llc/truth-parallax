## Trust in God Amid Moral Collapse

Islamic interpretation of Micah 7 resonates with the Quranic counsel to maintain trust in God (*tawakkul*) even when surrounded by corruption and faithlessness. The prophet's declaration "But as for me, I will look to the LORD" (7:7) mirrors the Quranic imperative: "Put your trust in Allah if you are truly believers" (5:23). The chapter models a faith that does not depend on the righteousness of others but rests entirely on God's faithfulness.

## God's Forgiveness

The closing celebration of God's forgiveness (7:18-20) aligns with the Islamic understanding of God as *al-Ghaffar* (the Oft-Forgiving) and *al-Tawwab* (the Acceptor of Repentance). The Quran declares: "Say, 'O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins'" (39:53). Micah's confidence in divine mercy mirrors this Quranic assurance.

## The Universality of Divine Mercy

The chapter's movement from particular lament to universal praise reflects the Islamic conviction that God's mercy encompasses all things. Micah's concluding doxology — celebrating a God who pardons iniquity and delights in steadfast love — expresses the same divine reality that the Quran names when it declares: "My mercy encompasses all things" (7:156).
