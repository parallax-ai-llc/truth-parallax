## The Three Failures of Leadership

Jewish interpretation of Micah 3 identifies three distinct failures: judges who pervert justice for bribes, prophets who tailor their message to their audience's ability to pay, and priests who teach Torah for money. The Talmud draws on this chapter to discuss the ethics of receiving payment for Torah study and religious leadership. While later halakhah permitted payment for the time spent teaching (as distinct from payment for the Torah itself), Micah's critique remained a powerful check on commercialized religion.

## "Zion Shall Be Plowed as a Field"

This verse (3:12) holds a unique place in Jewish history. When Jeremiah prophesied Jerusalem's destruction a century later, the elders of Judah cited Micah 3:12 to argue that prophecy of doom was a legitimate part of Israel's tradition (Jeremiah 26:18). The preservation of this precedent saved Jeremiah's life and established the principle that true prophecy may include harsh judgment against Israel's most sacred institutions.

## Prophetic Courage

Micah's declaration of Spirit-empowerment (3:8) is honored in Jewish tradition as a model of prophetic integrity. The prophet speaks not what is popular or profitable but what God requires — even when that message is the destruction of the holy city itself.
