## The Theophany and Its Context

Jewish interpretation of Micah 1 situates the prophet in the historical context of the late eighth century BCE, when the Northern Kingdom of Israel (Samaria) was falling to the Assyrian Empire. Micah witnesses the destruction of Samaria (722 BCE) and prophesies that the same judgment will reach Judah and Jerusalem. Rashi and Radak connect the chapter's imagery to the historical Assyrian campaigns, while also drawing broader theological lessons about the consequences of idolatry.

## The Wordplay Lament

The series of puns on town names (1:10-16) is one of the most celebrated passages of Hebrew wordplay in the Tanakh. Jewish commentators — particularly Ibn Ezra and Radak — devote extensive attention to the linguistic artistry, noting that the wordplay is not decorative but prophetic: the very names of these places contain their destinies. This technique reflects the Hebrew understanding that names carry inherent meaning and power.

## Prophetic Grief and National Tragedy

Micah's grief is understood as both personal and national. The prophet mourns not only for the suffering to come but for the spiritual failure that made it inevitable. Jewish interpretation reads this lament in continuity with the broader tradition of national mourning that culminates in the Tisha B'Av liturgy.
