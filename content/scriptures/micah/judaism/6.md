## The Riv: God's Lawsuit Against Israel

Jewish interpretation of Micah 6 reads the chapter as a formal *riv* — a legal proceeding in which God, as the aggrieved covenant partner, states his case against Israel before cosmic witnesses (the mountains). The Midrash notes the poignancy of God's opening: "What have I done to you?" — God speaks not as an angry judge but as a hurt parent. The recitation of God's saving acts (Exodus, wilderness, Balaam's curse overturned) reminds Israel of the covenant's foundation in grace.

## The Three Principles (6:8)

The Talmud (Makkot 24a) records that Micah reduced the 613 commandments to three: justice, mercy, and humility before God. This is not understood as a replacement of the mitzvot but as their distillation — the spirit that animates all 613. Rashi and the later commentators emphasize that Micah's triad encompasses the obligations between humans (justice and mercy) and between humans and God (walking humbly).

## The Priority of Ethics Over Sacrifice

The rhetorical question "Shall I offer my firstborn for my transgression?" (6:7) is read as a rejection of the pagan logic that more expensive offerings produce better results. Jewish theology insists that God desires obedience, not sacrifice (1 Samuel 15:22) — a principle that Micah articulates with devastating clarity.
