## Overview

In Jewish tradition, the Book of Micah holds a special place as the source of one of the most celebrated summaries of the Torah's ethical demands. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) records that Micah reduced the 613 commandments to three principles: "to do justice, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God" (6:8). This verse has become foundational for Jewish ethical teaching and is frequently invoked in discussions of *tikkun olam* — the repair of the world through righteous action.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Rabbinic interpretation engages extensively with Micah's covenant lawsuit (chapter 6), reading it as God's loving plea with Israel to return to the covenant's ethical core. The medieval commentators — Rashi, Radak, and Ibn Ezra — provided detailed philological and theological analysis. Messianic passages (4:1-5, 5:1-3) are read as referring to a future era of peace under a Davidic ruler, not yet fulfilled.

## Theological Emphases

- **Ethical Monotheism**: Micah 6:8 distills the Torah to its moral essence
- **Tikkun Olam**: The prophetic demand for justice inspires the Jewish commitment to repairing the world
- **Covenant Responsibility**: Israel is held accountable not for ritual failure but for moral failure
- **Messianic Hope**: The future ruler from Bethlehem and the age of universal peace remain objects of Jewish expectation
