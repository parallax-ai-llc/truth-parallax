## Overview

The Book of Micah contains the oracles of an eighth-century BCE prophet from the Judean village of Moresheth. A contemporary of Isaiah, Micah directed his message against the corruption of Jerusalem's ruling class — the judges who took bribes, the priests who taught for pay, and the prophets who prophesied for money. His covenant lawsuit against Israel (chapter 6) culminates in one of the Hebrew Bible's most celebrated summaries of true religion: "to do justice, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God" (6:8). The book also contains the prophecy that a ruler would come from Bethlehem (5:2), a passage of immense significance in Christian interpretation.

## Structure

1. **Chapters 1-2**: Judgment against Samaria and Judah; oppression of the poor
2. **Chapters 3-5**: Indictment of leaders; the Bethlehem prophecy; the reign of the remnant
3. **Chapters 6-7**: The covenant lawsuit; lament and hope; God's steadfast love

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: Micah 6:8 is central to Jewish ethical teaching; the book models prophetic advocacy for the poor
- **Christianity**: Micah 5:2 is cited in the Nativity narrative as predicting the birthplace of the Messiah (Matthew 2:6)
- **Islam**: Themes of social justice, divine judgment on oppressors, and God's ultimate mercy resonate with Quranic principles
- **Gnosticism**: The critique of institutional corruption is read as an indictment of the material world's structures of power

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Social Justice | Micah condemns the exploitation of the poor by the wealthy and powerful |
| Covenant Lawsuit | God brings a legal case against his people for their unfaithfulness |
| The Bethlehem Prophecy | A future ruler from a small town will shepherd Israel in God's strength |
| Remnant Theology | God will preserve and restore a faithful remnant from exile |
| True Religion | Not sacrifice but justice, mercy, and humility before God |
