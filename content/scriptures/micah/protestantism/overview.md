## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Micah has centered on the book's twin pillars: the prophetic demand for social justice and the messianic promise of a ruler from Bethlehem. Reformers valued Micah's uncompromising critique of corrupt religious and political leaders as a biblical warrant for confronting institutional abuse. Micah 6:8 — "to do justice, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God" — has become one of the most frequently cited verses in Protestant ethical teaching.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Calvin read Micah as a prophet who demonstrated the true function of the pastoral office: not flattery but faithful confrontation of sin. The Bethlehem prophecy (5:2) is understood as a direct messianic prediction fulfilled in Christ's birth. Dispensationalist interpreters also see future eschatological fulfillment in the restoration promises of chapters 4-5. Evangelical social ethics movements have drawn heavily on Micah's advocacy for the poor.

## Theological Emphases

- **Messianic Fulfillment**: Micah 5:2 predicts Christ's birth in Bethlehem
- **Prophetic Social Ethics**: The book demands justice for the poor and condemns economic exploitation
- **True Worship**: God requires justice, mercy, and humility — not merely ritual observance
- **Covenant Faithfulness**: God holds his people accountable to the covenant's ethical demands
