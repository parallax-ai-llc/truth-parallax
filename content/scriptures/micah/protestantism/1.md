## God's Judgment on Samaria

Protestant interpretation of Micah 1 reads the chapter as a dramatic theophany in which God descends to judge the idolatry and corruption of both Samaria and Judah. The imagery of mountains melting and valleys splitting depicts the cosmic consequences of divine judgment. Calvin emphasized that God's judgment begins with his own people — Samaria falls first, but Judah is not immune. The chapter establishes the prophetic principle that privilege increases accountability.

## The Wordplay of Mourning (1:10-16)

The series of wordplays on the names of Judean towns — untranslatable in English — is a lament in which each city's name becomes a prophecy of its fate. Protestant commentators note that this literary technique serves a theological purpose: the very names of the places God's people inhabit testify against them. The chapter ends with a call to mourning as "they have gone from you into exile" (1:16).

## Theological Themes

Micah's opening theophany establishes that the God who judges is not a distant deity but one who "comes down" (1:3) — who personally engages with human sin and its consequences. This divine descent anticipates, in Protestant reading, the ultimate descent of God in the Incarnation.
