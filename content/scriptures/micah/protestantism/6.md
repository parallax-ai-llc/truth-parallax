## The Covenant Lawsuit

Protestant interpretation of Micah 6 reads the chapter as a dramatic courtroom scene in which God brings a legal case (*riv*) against his people. The mountains and hills serve as witnesses, and God presents his case: "O my people, what have I done to you? How have I wearied you?" (6:3). The form is that of a covenant lawsuit — God is the aggrieved party in a broken covenant relationship. The chapter is one of the most powerful demonstrations of the prophetic understanding that Israel's relationship with God is covenantal, not merely cultic.

## "What Does the LORD Require?" (6:6-8)

This passage — culminating in "to do justice, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God" — is among the most cited verses in Protestant ethics. The Reformers read it as a decisive statement that God requires moral obedience, not ritual performance. Luther and Calvin both emphasized that sacrifices and offerings, however lavish, cannot substitute for lives of justice and compassion.

## The Indictment of Dishonesty (6:9-16)

The chapter closes with specific accusations: dishonest scales, violence, and lying. Protestant interpreters apply these charges as a mirror for every society, arguing that commercial dishonesty and institutional violence are as offensive to God today as they were in eighth-century Judah.
