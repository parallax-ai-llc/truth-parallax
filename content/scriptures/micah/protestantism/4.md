## The Vision of Universal Peace

Protestant interpretation of Micah 4 reads the chapter as a glorious vision of the messianic age, when "the mountain of the house of the LORD shall be established as the highest of the mountains" (4:1) and all nations will stream to it. The parallel passage in Isaiah 2:2-4 has generated extensive discussion about the relationship between the two prophets. Protestant commentators generally see this vision as fulfilled initially in the Church's universal mission and ultimately in the eschatological kingdom of God.

## Swords into Plowshares (4:3)

This verse has become one of the most iconic peace texts in Western civilization. Protestant peace traditions — Mennonite, Quaker, and broader evangelical movements — have drawn on it as a scriptural mandate for peacemaking. However, Protestant interpreters differ on whether the vision describes a present calling or a future reality: some read it as a command to pursue peace now, while others see it as a prophecy of conditions that only Christ's return will establish.

## The Remnant and the Lame (4:6-7)

God's promise to make the lame a remnant and the outcasts a strong nation reverses all human expectations. Protestant theology reads this as a principle of grace: God works through weakness, not strength, and his kingdom is built from those the world discards.
