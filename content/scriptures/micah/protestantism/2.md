## Woe to the Oppressors

Protestant interpretation of Micah 2 reads the chapter as a prophetic indictment of economic injustice. Those who "devise wickedness and work evil on their beds" (2:1) are the wealthy landowners who systematically dispossess the poor of their ancestral land — the inheritance God gave each family in Israel. The chapter demonstrates the prophetic principle that social injustice is not merely a political problem but a theological offense: stealing land from the poor is stealing from God.

## False Prophets and True (2:6-11)

Micah confronts prophets who tell the powerful what they want to hear: "Do not preach" (2:6). The contrast between the false prophets who promise prosperity and Micah who speaks uncomfortable truth is a theme that Protestant interpreters apply to every age. Luther saw in these false prophets the predecessors of every comfortable religion that blesses the status quo.

## The Promise of the Remnant (2:12-13)

The chapter ends with a sudden shift to promise: God will gather the remnant of Israel like sheep in a fold. Protestant interpreters debate whether this conclusion is original to Micah or a later addition, but theologically it establishes the pattern of judgment followed by restoration that characterizes the prophetic message.
