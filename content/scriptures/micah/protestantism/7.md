## Lament and Hope

Protestant interpretation of Micah 7 reads the chapter as a movement from the darkest lament to the brightest hope. Micah surveys his society and finds faithlessness at every level: "The faithful have perished from the earth, and there is no one upright among mankind" (7:2). Family bonds are broken, trust is shattered. Yet the chapter does not end in despair but in one of the most powerful declarations of trust in God's mercy in all of Scripture.

## "But as for Me, I Will Look to the LORD" (7:7)

This verse is a watershed moment in the book. Having catalogued the complete moral collapse of his society, Micah turns from human failure to divine faithfulness. Protestant piety has drawn deeply on this verse as a model of personal faith in the midst of societal decline — when all human supports fail, the believer looks to God alone.

## God's Incomparable Mercy (7:18-20)

"Who is a God like you, pardoning iniquity?" — a play on Micah's own name (*Mikhah* = "Who is like God?"). These closing verses celebrate God's steadfast love (*chesed*) and faithfulness to the promises made to Abraham. Protestant theology reads this as the ultimate assurance that God's mercy will triumph over judgment, grounding the believer's hope not in human goodness but in God's unbreakable covenant.
