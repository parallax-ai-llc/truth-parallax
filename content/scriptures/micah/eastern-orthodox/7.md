## The Dark Night and the Dawn

Orthodox interpretation of Micah 7 reads the chapter as a spiritual journey from darkness to light — a pattern that mirrors the paschal movement from Good Friday to Easter. The prophet's lament over the faithlessness of his society (7:1-6) is read as a description of the human condition without God. The turning point — "But as for me, I will look to the LORD" (7:7) — is the moment of faith that transforms despair into hope.

## The Theophany of Mercy

The closing verses (7:18-20) are read as a theophany of mercy — a revelation of God's deepest nature. "Who is a God like you?" is not merely a rhetorical question but a liturgical acclamation. Orthodox worship is built on the conviction that God is defined by mercy, and Micah's doxology is a precursor to the Great Doxology of the Liturgy.

## Casting Sins into the Sea (7:19)

"You will cast all our sins into the depths of the sea." This image has become one of the most powerful metaphors for divine forgiveness in Christian tradition. Orthodox interpretation reads it as an eschatological promise: God will not merely forgive sin but destroy it utterly, casting it into the abyss so that it can never surface again.
