## Oppression and Prophetic Witness

Orthodox interpretation of Micah 2 reads the prophet's denunciation of economic oppression as a model for the Church's prophetic responsibility. The Greek Fathers emphasized that the exploitation of the poor is not merely an injustice between humans but a sin against God, who is the true owner of all land and the protector of the vulnerable. Chrysostom frequently cited passages like Micah 2 in his sermons against the wealthy classes of Constantinople.

## The Remnant as Flock

The vision of God gathering the remnant "like sheep in a fold" (2:12) is read in Orthodox tradition as a Christological image. Christ is the good shepherd who gathers the scattered flock — a theme central to Orthodox iconography and hymnography. The "one who breaks open the way" (2:13) is identified with Christ, who breaks through death and opens the way to resurrection.

## The Danger of False Comfort

The false prophets who say "Do not preach" represent, in Orthodox reading, the perennial temptation to substitute comfortable spirituality for genuine repentance. True Orthodoxy, the Fathers insisted, does not offer cheap comfort but calls for transformation.
