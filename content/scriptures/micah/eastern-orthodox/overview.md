## Overview

In the Eastern Orthodox tradition, the Book of Micah is most prominently associated with the Feast of the Nativity. The Bethlehem prophecy (5:2) is read during the Royal Hours of Christmas Eve, and Orthodox hymnography celebrates the mystery of God's choice to enter human history through a humble village. The Greek Fathers read Micah's vision of swords beaten into ploughshares (4:3) as an eschatological icon of the peace that Christ's kingdom will ultimately bring to all creation.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Chrysostom and Theodoret of Cyrrhus provided detailed commentaries on Micah, emphasizing both the moral and the Christological dimensions. The Orthodox reading holds together the prophetic demand for justice with the messianic hope: Christ's coming from Bethlehem is the ultimate act of divine justice and mercy. The covenant lawsuit of chapter 6 is read liturgically as God's loving confrontation with his people, calling them to repentance and transformation.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Condescension**: The ruler from Bethlehem reveals God's willingness to enter the humblest circumstances
- **Eschatological Peace**: The vision of universal peace is an icon of the Kingdom to come
- **Prophetic Justice**: Micah calls the Church to embody justice as an essential dimension of its witness
- **Theosis**: God's covenant invitation — to walk humbly with him — is an invitation into communion and transformation
