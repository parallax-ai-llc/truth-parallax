## Shepherds Who Devour the Flock

Orthodox interpretation of Micah 3 connects the prophet's indictment of corrupt leaders to the broader Orthodox teaching on the pastoral responsibility of bishops and clergy. The imagery of leaders who "eat the flesh of my people" (3:3) is used in Orthodox homiletical tradition to warn against any exercise of authority that exploits rather than serves. The true shepherd lays down his life for the sheep; the false shepherd consumes them.

## The Spirit and Prophecy

Micah's claim to Spirit-empowerment (3:8) is understood in Orthodox theology not as a private spiritual experience but as a charism given for the good of the community. The Spirit equips the prophet to speak truth that the community needs to hear, even when — especially when — that truth is unwelcome. Orthodox tradition honors the *holy fool* and the monastic elder as bearers of this prophetic charism.

## Zion's Judgment

The prophecy of Zion's destruction is read eschatologically: every human institution, however sacred its origin, is subject to divine judgment. The Orthodox tradition applies this not with triumphalism but with sobriety, recognizing that the Church itself must continually submit to the purifying judgment of God's word.
