## The Theophany of Judgment

Orthodox interpretation of Micah 1 reads the theophanic opening as a revelation of God's majestic power and holiness. The mountains melt, the valleys split — creation itself cannot withstand the direct presence of its Creator. This imagery is connected liturgically to the hymns of Holy Week, where the cosmic upheaval at Christ's crucifixion echoes the prophetic theophanies. God's descent in judgment is understood as the obverse of God's descent in mercy — both reveal the same divine nature.

## Samaria as Warning

The fall of Samaria serves as a warning to Judah and, by extension, to the Church. Orthodox interpretation applies the prophetic principle that those who have received the most are held to the highest standard. The chapter's stern message is not foreign to Orthodox piety, which maintains a strong sense of the fear of God alongside the assurance of divine love.

## The Prophet's Grief

Micah's personal grief — "I will lament and wail, I will go stripped and naked" (1:8) — is understood as a prophetic sharing in God's own grief over his people's sin. The prophet is not a detached observer but a participant in the divine pathos.
