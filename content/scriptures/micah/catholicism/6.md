## God's Plea with His People

Catholic interpretation of Micah 6 reads the covenant lawsuit as God's loving plea with his people to return to the covenant's moral heart. The chapter is used in Lenten and penitential liturgies, and the *Improperia* (Reproaches) of Good Friday — "O my people, what have I done to you?" — are drawn directly from Micah 6:3-4. In the Catholic reading, Christ on the cross speaks Micah's words, asking his people why they have rejected his love.

## The Synthesis of True Religion (6:8)

Catholic moral theology draws extensively on Micah 6:8 as a summary of the divine law. The verse does not reject sacrifice — the Catholic tradition maintains the centrality of the Eucharistic sacrifice — but insists that external worship without interior conversion and ethical living is empty. The three requirements — justice, mercy, humility — correspond to the Christian's obligations to neighbor, to those in need, and to God.

## Social Sin

The chapter's closing indictment of dishonest business practices, violence, and deceit is read in Catholic Social Teaching as a prophetic exposure of structural sin — the systemic patterns of injustice that corrupt entire societies and institutions.
