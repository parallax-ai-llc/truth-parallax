## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Micah has been deeply shaped by the Bethlehem prophecy (5:2), which is central to the Church's celebration of the Incarnation. The Gospel of Matthew explicitly connects Herod's inquiry about the Messiah's birthplace to Micah's oracle, and the verse features prominently in the Christmas liturgy. Beyond Christology, Catholic Social Teaching has drawn extensively on Micah's advocacy for the poor and his indictment of unjust leaders, reading the prophet as a precursor to the Church's preferential option for the poor.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Patristic commentators — Jerome, Augustine, and Cyril — read Micah typologically, seeing the Bethlehem prophecy as the hinge connecting Old and New Testaments. Medieval commentators developed Marian readings, connecting the mother who gives birth (5:3) to the Virgin Mary. Modern Catholic scholarship integrates historical-critical method with the tradition's theological reading, recognizing Micah as a voice for justice that anticipates the social encyclicals.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Incarnation**: Micah 5:2 prophesies the birthplace of Christ, the Word made flesh
- **Catholic Social Teaching**: Micah's denunciation of exploitation informs the Church's social doctrine
- **Prophetic Authority**: The prophet speaks God's truth to corrupt power, modeling the Church's prophetic vocation
- **The Remnant**: God preserves a faithful people through judgment — a type of the Church
