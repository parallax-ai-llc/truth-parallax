## The Cry Against Economic Injustice

Catholic interpretation of Micah 2 draws on the chapter's condemnation of land seizure and economic exploitation to develop principles of Catholic Social Teaching. The encyclical *Rerum Novarum* and its successors echo Micah's concern for the dispossessed. The prophet's denunciation of those who "covet fields and seize them, and houses, and take them away" (2:2) anticipates the Church's teaching on the universal destination of goods — that the earth's resources are intended for all, not merely for the powerful.

## The Silencing of Prophecy

Micah's confrontation with those who say "Do not preach" illustrates the perennial temptation to silence the prophetic voice. Catholic interpretation sees in this passage a warning to the Church itself: whenever the institution suppresses the voices that speak for the poor, it repeats the sin Micah condemns.

## The Shepherd and the Remnant

The chapter's closing vision of God gathering his remnant like a shepherd gathers sheep (2:12-13) is read as a messianic and ecclesial image. Christ is the shepherd who breaks through obstacles to gather his flock, and the Church is the remnant called out of judgment into hope.
