## Jesus Greater Than Moses

The comparison between Jesus and Moses (3:1-6) articulates a fundamental Gnostic hierarchy of revelation. Moses was "faithful in all God's house as a servant" (3:5), but the "God" he served was the Demiurge, and his law was the Demiurge's instrument of cosmic governance. Moses received the law on Sinai from a being who appeared in fire and smoke, the typical manifestation of an archon wielding power through terror. Jesus, by contrast, is "faithful over God's house as a son" (3:6), where "God" now refers to the transcendent Father, and the "house" is not the material temple but the Pleroma itself. The son comes from within the divine fullness; the servant merely administered the prison from a position of relative privilege.

The urgent warning "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts" (3:7-8) is the call of gnosis breaking through the archontic static. The "voice" is not the thundering commands of the Demiurge but the still, inner whisper of the divine spark recognizing its origin. To "harden the heart" is to retreat into the shell of material consciousness when confronted with the terrifying prospect of liberation. The Israelites in the wilderness (3:16-18) serve as the cautionary example of souls who, having been led out of one form of bondage, lacked the courage to enter the promised land of full gnosis.

The statement that "they were unable to enter because of unbelief" (3:19) is, in Gnostic terms, a diagnosis of spiritual failure. Unbelief is not the absence of intellectual assent but the refusal to trust the pneumatic knowledge that contradicts everything the archontic world presents as real. The wilderness wandering represents the intermediate state of the psychic believer who has left the gross materiality of Egyptian (hylic) existence but has not yet entered the Pleroma. Such souls circle endlessly in the middle realm, sustained by psychic religion but never tasting the fullness of gnosis.
