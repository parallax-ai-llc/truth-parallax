## The Supremacy of God's Son

The opening declaration that God "has spoken to us by his Son" (1:2) after speaking "at many times and in many ways" through prophets establishes a critical Gnostic distinction between partial and complete revelation. The prophets received fragmentary transmissions through the veil of the archontic heavens, often distorted by the Demiurge's interference. The Son, however, is a direct emanation of the transcendent Father, bypassing the entire cosmic hierarchy to deliver unmediated gnosis.

The Son as "the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature" (1:3) describes an aeon proceeding from the Pleroma's fullness, not a creature fashioned by the Demiurge. He is the visible expression of the invisible Bythos, the only being who can make the unknowable Father known. That he "upholds the universe by the word of his power" (1:3) indicates that the true sustaining force of existence is not the Demiurge's clumsy craftsmanship but the Logos emanating from the divine depth. When he "made purification for sins," he dissolved the karmic chains binding divine sparks to the cycle of material incarnation.

The Son's superiority to angels (1:4-14) is of paramount importance in Gnostic cosmology, where angels are identified with the archons, the planetary rulers who govern the material spheres. The Demiurge created the angels as administrators of his prison-cosmos, and they demand worship from the souls trapped within. Hebrews' insistence that the Son is "much superior to angels" places the divine Savior above and beyond the entire archontic hierarchy. The quotation "Let all God's angels worship him" (1:6) inverts the power structure: the archons must bow before the aeon who descends from the true God, recognizing an authority they cannot comprehend. The Son's throne is "forever and ever" (1:8), eternal in a way the archons' temporary rule is not.
