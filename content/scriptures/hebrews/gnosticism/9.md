## The Blood of Christ

The detailed description of the earthly tabernacle's furnishings (9:1-5) presents, from the Gnostic perspective, a map of the archontic cosmos. The outer tent corresponds to the material world accessible to hylic consciousness. The inner tent, the Holy of Holies, separated by the second curtain, represents the boundary between the Demiurge's domain and the Pleroma beyond. The high priest entering the inner tent "once a year" (9:7) symbolizes the rare and fleeting contact between the archontic order and the divine realm beyond it, a contact the Demiurge neither fully understands nor controls.

Christ entering "not through the blood of goats and calves but through his own blood" (9:12) transforms the meaning of sacrifice entirely. In the Demiurge's system, animal blood propitiated a jealous god who demanded material offerings. Christ's sacrifice operates at a different level: his "blood" is not literal gore but the life-substance of a divine aeon poured into the material cosmos, permeating it with the light of gnosis. He entered "not into holy places made with hands, which are copies of the true things, but into heaven itself" (9:24), passing beyond the Demiurge's counterfeit heavens into the authentic presence of the transcendent Father.

The principle that "without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness" (9:22) is read by Gnostic interpreters as a statement about the old covenant's economy, not a universal metaphysical truth. The Demiurge required blood because his system of justice operates through material exchange. The new dispensation transcends this crude transactionalism. Christ was "offered once to bear the sins of many" (9:28) in the sense that the divine aeon's single descent into matter accomplished what endless animal sacrifices never could: the liberation of divine sparks from the guilt and ignorance imposed by the Demiurge's law. His "second appearance" (9:28) will complete the gathering of all pneumatic souls into the Pleroma.
