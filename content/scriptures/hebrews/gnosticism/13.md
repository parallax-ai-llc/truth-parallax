## Final Exhortations

The instruction to "show hospitality to strangers, for thereby some have entertained angels unawares" (13:2) carries a particular charge in Gnostic cosmology. The "strangers" are pneumatic souls wandering through the Demiurge's domain, disguised in material bodies, unrecognized by the hylic world. The "angels" entertained unawares may be divine messengers from the Pleroma, aeons who have descended to aid the scattered sparks. The pneumatic community must remain open to unexpected visitations from the higher realms, recognizing that the transcendent Father sends help through channels the archons cannot anticipate.

The declaration that "Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever" (13:8) affirms the unchanging nature of the divine aeon in contrast to the Demiurge's mutable creation. The material world shifts and decays, the archons' power waxes and wanes, but the Savior exists in the eternal present of the Pleroma. Gnostic readers hear in this verse the timelessness of gnosis itself: the knowledge that liberates is not historically contingent but eternally true, available to every era because it transcends the temporal dimension the Demiurge imposed on his cosmos.

The summons to "go to him outside the camp and bear the reproach he endured" (13:13) is the Gnostic call to leave the institutional boundaries of the Demiurge's religion. The "camp" is the organized religious community that worships the inferior creator. To go "outside" is to step beyond the walls of conventional piety into the dangerous freedom of gnosis, accepting the social stigma that accompanies the rejection of the dominant religious order. "For here we have no lasting city, but we seek the city that is to come" (13:14) is the pneumatic's confession that no earthly structure, religious or political, is home. The "city to come" is the Pleroma, the heavenly Jerusalem beyond the archontic spheres, and the pneumatic lives always as an exile, oriented toward a homeland the material eye cannot see.
