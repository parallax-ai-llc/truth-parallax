## Jesus the Great High Priest

The principle that "every high priest chosen from among men is appointed to act on behalf of men in relation to God" (5:1) is reframed by Gnostic interpreters as a description of the archontic priesthood. The priests of the old covenant were chosen from within the material realm to mediate between humanity and the Demiurge. Their sacrifices and rituals belonged entirely to the inferior god's economy. Christ's priesthood transcends this system entirely because he was not appointed by the Demiurge but designated by the transcendent Father as "a priest forever, after the order of Melchizedek" (5:6).

Melchizedek occupies a singular position in Gnostic thought. As a figure "without father, without mother, without genealogy" (as Hebrews 7 will elaborate), he represents a being untouched by the Demiurge's system of generation and lineage. In some Gnostic texts, Melchizedek is identified as a heavenly being or aeon who prefigures the Savior's transcendence of the material order. Christ's priesthood after this order means his mediating work operates entirely outside the Demiurge's jurisdiction.

The statement that Christ "learned obedience through what he suffered" (5:8) describes the divine aeon's experiential immersion in the conditions of material imprisonment. This is not moral education but salvific identification: the Savior had to know suffering from the inside to effectively liberate those trapped within it. The chapter's closing rebuke that the audience should be "teachers" by now but still need "milk, not solid food" (5:12) employs the classic Gnostic distinction between elementary psychic teaching (milk) and advanced pneumatic gnosis (solid food). Those who remain at the milk stage are psychics incapable of the deeper mysteries, while "the mature" who can handle solid food (5:14) are pneumatics whose "powers of discernment" have been trained to distinguish the Demiurge's deceptions from the Pleroma's truth.
