## Jesus Made Like His Brothers

The statement that humanity was made "a little lower than the angels" (2:7) takes on profound meaning in Gnostic cosmology, where angels are archons who govern the material spheres. Humans were placed beneath the archontic rulers not by the transcendent Father's design but by the Demiurge's arrangement, which stationed humanity at the bottom of the cosmic hierarchy. Yet the passage immediately adds that God "crowned him with glory and honor" and "put everything in subjection under his feet" (2:7-8), revealing the hidden truth that the human being, bearing the divine spark, is destined to surpass the archons who currently dominate.

The description of the Savior as "the founder of salvation made perfect through suffering" (2:10) who brings "many sons to glory" describes the divine aeon's descent into matter. He was "made perfect through suffering" not because the divine is inherently deficient but because the passage through the archontic spheres and incarnation in the Demiurge's material prison constitutes a suffering that completes the Savior's knowledge of the condition he came to remedy. Only by entering the prison can he reveal the escape route to those trapped within.

The pivotal declaration that "through death he might destroy the one who has the power of death, that is, the devil" (2:14) identifies the chief archon as the lord of death, the power who uses mortality as the ultimate tool of enslavement. Death is not a natural condition but the Demiurge's weapon, the threat that keeps souls obedient and fearful. By passing through death and emerging beyond it, the Savior demonstrated that the archon of death has no power over the pneumatic self. Those who "through fear of death were subject to lifelong slavery" (2:15) are humanity under the archontic regime, and the Savior's mission is precisely their liberation from this fear through the gnosis that the divine spark is indestructible.
