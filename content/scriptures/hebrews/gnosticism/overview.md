## Overview

Hebrews was enormously attractive to Gnostic interpreters. Its Platonic-sounding contrast between earthly "shadows" and heavenly "realities" (8:5; 10:1), its mysterious figure of Melchizedek (chapter 7), and its imagery of passing through the veil into the divine presence all resonated with core Gnostic themes of transcendence and spiritual ascent.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Shadow and Reality
The letter's contrast between the earthly tabernacle as a "shadow" and the heavenly reality (8:5; 10:1) aligned with the Gnostic worldview that the material cosmos is an inferior copy of the divine Pleroma. Gnostic readers took this dualism further than the author intended, treating the material world as not merely inferior but actively evil.

### Melchizedek as Cosmic Figure
The mysterious Melchizedek — "without father or mother" and "without beginning of days or end of life" (7:3) — was identified in some Gnostic systems as a heavenly aeon or divine emanation. The Nag Hammadi text *Melchizedek* (NHC IX,1) portrays him as a priestly figure in a Gnostic cosmological drama.

### Passing Through the Veil
The imagery of Jesus passing through the veil into the Holy of Holies (6:19-20; 9:11-12; 10:19-20) was read as the paradigmatic act of spiritual ascent — the prototype for the Gnostic soul's passage through the barriers separating the material world from the Pleroma of divine fullness.
