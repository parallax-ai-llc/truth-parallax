## The New Covenant

Christ as "a minister in the holy places, in the true tent that the Lord set up, not man" (8:2) perfectly articulates the Gnostic distinction between the earthly temple, constructed according to the Demiurge's specifications, and the heavenly sanctuary within the Pleroma. The earthly tabernacle was a "copy and shadow of the heavenly things" (8:5), a phrase Gnostic readers take as confirmation that the entire material world is a degraded imitation of the divine fullness. Moses was shown a "pattern" on the mountain (8:5), but the pattern he perceived was filtered through the Demiurge's limited understanding, producing a copy of a copy, twice removed from the original.

The announcement of the "new covenant" (8:8) that renders the first "obsolete" (8:13) is the definitive Gnostic claim that the Demiurge's dispensation has been superseded. The old covenant, made with Israel "when I took them by the hand to lead them out of Egypt" (8:9), was an arrangement between the Demiurge and his chosen psychic community. They "did not continue in my covenant" (8:9) because the Demiurge's covenant, based on external law and threat of punishment, could never secure genuine transformation. It addressed the soul (psyche) but could not reach the spirit (pneuma).

The new covenant's promise that "I will put my laws into their minds, and write them on their hearts" (8:10) describes the internalization of gnosis. No longer mediated through stone tablets or priestly institutions, the knowledge of the transcendent Father is inscribed directly on the pneumatic self. The climactic promise that "they shall all know me, from the least of them to the greatest" (8:11) envisions the universal awakening of all divine sparks, the eschatological moment when gnosis is no longer the possession of a pneumatic elite but the shared experience of all who carry the light within them. The instruction "shall not teach, each one his neighbor" (8:11) anticipates the dissolution of the teacher-student hierarchy when every spark knows itself as a fragment of the divine.
