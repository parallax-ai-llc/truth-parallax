## Overview

Islamic engagement with Hebrews is complex, as the letter's central claims — Christ's divinity, his atoning sacrifice, and the supersession of the old covenant — all diverge from Islamic theology. However, the letter's rich engagement with shared figures like Ibrahim (Abraham) and Musa (Moses) provides significant points of contact.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### God Has Spoken
The opening declaration that "God has spoken" through various means (1:1-2) resonates with the Islamic understanding of progressive revelation (*wahy*) through a succession of prophets. However, the claim that God's final word came through a divine Son contradicts the Islamic insistence that Muhammad is the seal of the prophets.

### Ibrahim and the Hall of Faith
The heroes of faith in chapter 11, especially Ibrahim, are central figures in Islam. The Qur'anic account of Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son (37:99-111) parallels the faith described in Hebrews 11:17-19, though Islam identifies the son as Isma'il rather than Ishaq.

### Sacrifice and Atonement
Islam rejects the concept of vicarious atonement central to Hebrews. The Qur'an states that "no bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another" (53:38). The Islamic practice of *qurbani* (sacrifice during Eid al-Adha) commemorates Ibrahim's obedience but does not carry atoning significance in the Christian sense.
