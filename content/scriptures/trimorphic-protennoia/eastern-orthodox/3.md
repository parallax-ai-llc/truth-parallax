The third chapter's presentation of the Logos descending and "putting on" Jesus constitutes, from the Orthodox perspective, the most serious theological error in the text. The Orthodox Church celebrates the Incarnation as the central mystery of the faith: the Logos did not merely appear in human form but genuinely became human, uniting divine and human natures without confusion, change, division, or separation, as the Council of Chalcedon defined.

The baptismal rite described in this chapter — the five seals — also diverges from Orthodox baptismal theology. In Orthodox practice, baptism is a threefold immersion in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, followed by chrismation and the Eucharist. The Gnostic five seals appear to be a multi-stage initiatory process aimed at cosmic ascent rather than incorporation into the Body of Christ. Orthodox scholars study these differences to understand the alternative sacramental practices that existed in early Christianity and why the Church settled on the forms preserved in the Orthodox tradition.
