The Eastern Orthodox tradition does not accept Trimorphic Protennoia as Scripture. Orthodox theologians recognize the text as a Sethian Gnostic composition that appropriates and distorts central Christian themes, particularly the Logos theology found in John's Gospel. The patristic Fathers who combated Gnosticism — including Irenaeus, Hippolytus, and Epiphanius — would have recognized this text as exemplifying the kind of speculative theology they opposed.

Orthodox theology is centered on the Incarnation of the Logos, the mystery celebrated in every Divine Liturgy. Trimorphic Protennoia's description of the Logos descending and "putting on" Jesus contradicts the Orthodox insistence, grounded in the Councils of Nicaea and Chalcedon, that the Word truly became flesh and dwelt among us. The Gnostic text presents a docetic savior who passes through the material world without genuinely entering it.

The three forms of Protennoia — Voice, Speech, and Word — are sometimes compared to the Orthodox doctrine of the Trinity. Orthodox scholars reject this comparison as superficial. The three forms are successive manifestations of a single figure, not three co-eternal, co-equal Persons in perichoretic communion.
