The second chapter's description of Protennoia as Speech resonates with the Orthodox theology of the divine Logoi — the creative words or principles through which God sustains all things, as developed by Maximus the Confessor. However, Orthodox scholars note a critical difference: the Maximian Logoi are expressions of God's creative and sustaining love for the material world, while Protennoia's Speech works to dismantle the material world's hold on the divine sparks.

Orthodox interpreters also note the chapter's implicit elitism. The Speech of Protennoia is directed to those who are spiritually capable of receiving it — the seeds of Seth. Orthodox ecclesiology, by contrast, holds that the Church's doors are open to all and that the sacraments are offered to every baptized Christian. The Gnostic distinction between pneumatics, psychics, and hylics finds no place in Orthodox soteriology, which teaches that every human being bears the image of God and is called to theosis.
