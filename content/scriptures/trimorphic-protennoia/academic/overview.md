Academic scholarship on Trimorphic Protennoia (NHC XIII,1) has focused on three major questions: its classification within Sethian Gnostic literature, its relationship to the Gospel of John, and the nature of the baptismal ritual described in its third section. John D. Turner's comprehensive analysis places the text within the broader Sethian literary tradition alongside the Apocryphon of John, the Gospel of the Egyptians, and Zostrianos.

The most debated issue in Trimorphic Protennoia scholarship is its relationship to the Prologue of John (1:1-18). Gesine Schenke Robinson argued that both texts depend on a common hymnic source. Yvonne Janssens proposed that Trimorphic Protennoia preserves an earlier form of the Logos hymn that John adapted. Most scholars now favor a more nuanced view: the two texts share a common theological vocabulary rooted in Jewish Wisdom and Logos traditions, but direct literary dependence in either direction is difficult to demonstrate conclusively.

The third section's description of the "five seals" baptism has attracted attention as possible evidence for actual Gnostic liturgical practice. Scholars debate whether the text describes a real ritual performed by a Sethian community or employs baptismal language metaphorically.
