The third chapter has generated the most extensive academic debate, primarily because of its parallels to John 1:1-18. The question of priority — whether the Gnostic text depends on John or vice versa — has been addressed by numerous scholars. Gesine Schenke Robinson argued for a common source hypothesis, while Craig Evans and others have maintained Johannine priority. The current scholarly consensus, represented by the work of Turner and Einar Thomassen, favors a model of shared traditions in which both texts draw on a common pool of Jewish Logos and Wisdom theology.

The chapter's baptismal content — the rite of the five seals — has been studied by scholars seeking to reconstruct Sethian ritual practice. April DeConick and others have argued that the five seals represent a real initiatory sequence involving ascent, disrobing, immersion, investiture, and enthronement. The ritual terminology shows connections to other Sethian texts (the Gospel of the Egyptians, Zostrianos) and to Mandaean baptismal practices, suggesting a broader ritual tradition within which the Sethian rite can be situated.
