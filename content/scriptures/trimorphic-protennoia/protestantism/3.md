The third chapter of Trimorphic Protennoia draws the most scholarly attention from Protestant interpreters because of its extensive parallels to the Prologue of the Gospel of John. The Logos descends into the world, is not recognized, and brings light to those in darkness — language that echoes John 1:1-18 almost verbatim in places. Protestant New Testament scholars have debated whether this reflects Gnostic dependence on John or a shared hymnic tradition.

The chapter's description of the Logos "putting on" Jesus presents a docetic Christology that Protestant theology firmly rejects. The Incarnation, in Protestant understanding, is not a disguise but a genuine union of divine and human natures. The Apostles' Creed and the Nicene Creed both affirm that Christ was truly born, truly suffered, and truly rose — affirmations that Trimorphic Protennoia either denies or reinterprets. The chapter's baptismal language — the "five seals" — also differs fundamentally from Protestant sacramental theology, in which baptism signifies incorporation into Christ's death and resurrection rather than passage through hostile cosmic spheres.
