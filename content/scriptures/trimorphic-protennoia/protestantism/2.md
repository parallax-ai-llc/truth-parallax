The second chapter, presenting Protennoia's descent as Speech, introduces what Protestant scholars recognize as a theology of progressive revelation — the idea that God reveals truth in stages. Protestant hermeneutics acknowledges progressive revelation within the biblical canon, from the Old Testament to the New. However, the Gnostic version inverts the biblical pattern by presenting revelation as the unveiling of secret knowledge to an elect few rather than the public proclamation of God's covenant faithfulness to all people.

Protestant interpreters note the chapter's emphasis on knowledge as the means of salvation, contrasting this sharply with the Reformation principle of sola fide. In Trimorphic Protennoia, salvation comes through gnosis — esoteric knowledge of one's divine origin. In Protestant theology, salvation comes through faith in Christ's atoning work, available to all through the proclaimed Word. The two frameworks share vocabulary — "word," "knowledge," "salvation" — while meaning fundamentally different things.
