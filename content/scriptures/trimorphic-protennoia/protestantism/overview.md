Protestant scholarship engages with Trimorphic Protennoia primarily through its relationship to the Gospel of John. The text's third section, describing the descent of the Logos into the world, contains striking parallels to John's Prologue — "I am the Word who dwells in the silence," "I descended to their midst," "I put on Jesus" — that have generated extensive scholarly discussion about the origins of Johannine theology.

Protestant New Testament scholars including Raymond Brown and Craig Keener have examined the relationship between Trimorphic Protennoia and John 1:1-18. The parallels are significant: both texts describe a pre-existent divine Word/Logos who descends into a world of darkness, is not recognized, and brings light and life. Protestant scholars generally argue that the Gnostic text depends on and reinterprets John rather than the reverse, though some acknowledge the possibility of shared traditions.

Despite the Johannine parallels, Protestant interpreters maintain that Trimorphic Protennoia's theology is fundamentally incompatible with canonical Christianity. The text presents a docetic Christology — the Logos "puts on" Jesus as a garment rather than becoming incarnate — and lacks any theology of atonement, resurrection, or justification by faith.
