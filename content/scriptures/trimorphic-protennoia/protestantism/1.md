Protestant scholars examine the first chapter of Trimorphic Protennoia as a revelation discourse in which a divine Voice speaks from beyond the material world. The chapter's description of a hidden divine presence moving through creation and awakening chosen individuals reflects the Sethian Gnostic worldview, which Protestant theology regards as fundamentally incompatible with biblical revelation. The Voice that speaks here is not the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob but a Gnostic aeon whose mission is to subvert rather than sustain the created order.

Nonetheless, Protestant scholars note that the concept of a divine Voice that calls and awakens resonates with canonical themes. God's voice calls to Moses from the burning bush, speaks from Sinai, and addresses the prophets. The Gnostic text transforms this motif by placing the Voice in opposition to the creator of the material world, a move that Protestant theology rejects as a fundamental distortion of the biblical doctrine of creation.
