Jewish scholars approach the first chapter of Trimorphic Protennoia with attention to its appropriation of the concept of divine Voice (bat kol). In rabbinic tradition, the bat kol — literally "daughter of a voice" — is a diminished form of prophecy that continues after the cessation of classical prophecy. The Gnostic text transforms this modest, mediated divine communication into a cosmic force that shakes the foundations of reality, reflecting the Gnostic tendency to magnify and mythologize Jewish theological concepts.

The chapter's implicit rejection of the created world as a prison stands in direct conflict with Jewish theological affirmation of creation's goodness. Genesis 1 declares the world "very good" (tov me'od); the Gnostic text treats the material world as an error to be overcome. Jewish scholars view this cosmological reversal as evidence that Gnosticism, despite borrowing extensively from Jewish sources, represents a fundamental departure from Jewish thought.
