The third chapter's Logos theology has deep roots in Jewish thought, particularly the Logos concept developed by Philo of Alexandria. Philo's Logos is the divine intermediary through whom God creates and governs the world — a concept that remains within Jewish monotheism while addressing Hellenistic philosophical concerns. The Gnostic text transforms Philo's Logos from a creative principle into a rescuing agent who liberates souls from the very creation the Logos helped produce.

Jewish scholars view this transformation as characteristic of the Gnostic relationship to Judaism: Gnostic texts borrow Jewish vocabulary, concepts, and narrative structures while inverting their theological meaning. The chapter's baptismal imagery also has Jewish roots in the mikveh tradition, but the Gnostic ritual of the five seals serves a cosmological purpose entirely foreign to Jewish immersion practices, which are oriented toward purity and covenant renewal rather than cosmic escape.
