The second chapter's themes of divine Speech and hidden knowledge engage Jewish concepts that are deeply rooted in Torah and rabbinic tradition. The idea that God speaks and the world is created (dabar as both "word" and "thing") is foundational to Jewish theology. Trimorphic Protennoia appropriates this concept but directs it against the created order: the divine Speech does not create the world but liberates prisoners from it.

Jewish scholars also note the chapter's resemblance to certain Merkavah mystical traditions, in which the adept ascends through heavenly palaces and encounters divine speech and angelic beings. The Gnostic text may have drawn on these Jewish esoteric traditions while radically reinterpreting them. Where Merkavah mysticism remains within the framework of Jewish monotheism and Torah observance, the Gnostic version severs the connection between mystical experience and covenantal obedience.
