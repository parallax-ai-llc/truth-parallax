The third chapter's Logos Christology presents Catholic scholars with the most theologically significant material. The parallels to John's Prologue are striking and have generated extensive Catholic scholarly commentary. Catholic exegetes generally maintain that the Gospel of John represents the authentic apostolic witness to the Incarnation of the Logos, while Trimorphic Protennoia offers a Gnostic reinterpretation that drains the Incarnation of its material reality.

The Catholic Church's dogmatic definition of the Incarnation — that the Word truly became flesh, not merely appeared in flesh — was formulated in direct opposition to docetic Christologies like the one presented in this chapter. The Council of Chalcedon's affirmation that Christ is "truly God and truly man" excludes the Gnostic notion that the divine Logos merely wore a human body as a garment. Catholic scholars study this chapter to understand the theological pressures that necessitated the conciliar definitions, viewing the Gnostic text as evidence for why precision in Christological language was essential for the early Church.
