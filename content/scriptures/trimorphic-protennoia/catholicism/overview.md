Catholic scholarship treats Trimorphic Protennoia as a non-canonical Gnostic text of considerable theological and historical interest. The text's three-fold structure — Voice, Speech, and Word — presents a quasi-trinitarian pattern that Catholic scholars compare with orthodox Trinitarian theology while noting the fundamental differences. The Gnostic "trinity" of Protennoia's three forms lacks the co-equal, co-eternal personhood that Catholic dogma attributes to Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

The text's description of the Logos "putting on" Jesus as a garment raises the specter of docetism, the heresy that Christ only appeared to have a physical body. Catholic Christology, grounded in the Council of Chalcedon, insists on the full reality of the Incarnation — the Word became flesh, not merely clothed itself in flesh. Trimorphic Protennoia exemplifies the christological deviations that the early councils were convened to address.

Catholic scholars value the text as evidence for understanding the theological environment of the first centuries. The parallels with John's Prologue illuminate the intellectual currents in which the Fourth Gospel was composed and received.
