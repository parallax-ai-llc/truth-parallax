Catholic scholars approach the first chapter of Trimorphic Protennoia with attention to its quasi-trinitarian structure. The Voice of Protennoia, descending from the invisible Father, bears a formal resemblance to Catholic pneumatology — the theology of the Holy Spirit, who is often described as the divine breath or voice. However, Catholic theologians insist on the distinction between formal resemblance and theological identity. Protennoia's Voice operates within a dualistic framework that opposes spirit to matter, whereas Catholic theology affirms the unity of creation under a single good Creator.

The chapter's description of the Voice moving through creation without being recognized by the ruling powers has been compared to the Catholic understanding of prevenient grace — the divine initiative that precedes human response. Catholic scholars note the comparison while emphasizing that grace, in Catholic theology, works within and through the material world rather than against it.
