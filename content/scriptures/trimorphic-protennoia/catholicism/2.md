The second chapter's presentation of Protennoia as Speech engages Catholic scholars interested in the theology of divine communication. The Catholic tradition holds that God speaks through Scripture, Tradition, and the Magisterium — a threefold channel of revelation that is public, communal, and ecclesial. Trimorphic Protennoia's Speech, by contrast, is directed to a spiritual elite and reveals secrets hidden from the majority of humanity.

Catholic interpreters note the chapter's emphasis on the transformative power of the divine word, a theme central to Catholic sacramental theology. In the Mass, the words of consecration transform bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ. The Gnostic text similarly attributes transformative power to divine speech, though the transformation envisioned is liberation from matter rather than the sanctification of matter. This difference crystallizes the fundamental divergence between Catholic and Gnostic soteriology.
