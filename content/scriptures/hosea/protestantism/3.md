Hosea 3, the shortest chapter in the book, describes the prophet's redemption of Gomer — buying her back from slavery or prostitution for fifteen shekels of silver and a measure of barley. Protestant interpreters read this as a powerful foreshadowing of Christ's redemptive work: purchasing his people at a cost, restoring them from bondage to covenant relationship.

The price paid — silver and barley — is notably modest, suggesting either Gomer's degraded condition or the humility of redemptive love. Luther saw in this transaction the essence of the gospel: God does not wait for the sinner to return but goes and pays the price for redemption. The command to Gomer — "You must dwell as mine for many days" (3:3) — speaks of the discipline and restoration that follow redemption, a period of purification before full restoration.

Verse 5 looks forward to a time when Israel will "return and seek the LORD their God and David their king." Protestant interpreters, especially in Reformed traditions, read "David their king" as a messianic reference pointing to Christ, the son of David, to whom repentant Israel will ultimately turn.
