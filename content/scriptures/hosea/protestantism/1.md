Hosea 1 opens with one of the most startling divine commands in Scripture: God tells the prophet to marry "a wife of whoredom" (1:2). Protestant interpreters have long debated whether this was a literal marriage or a visionary experience. Calvin favored an allegorical reading, finding it inconceivable that God would command immorality, while most modern evangelical scholars accept the literal reading as a prophetic sign-act — God demonstrating through Hosea's lived experience the scandal of divine grace.

The three children born to Hosea and Gomer receive symbolic names that encode God's message to Israel. Jezreel ("God sows") references the bloodshed at the Jezreel valley. Lo-Ruhamah ("Not pitied") declares the withdrawal of divine mercy. Lo-Ammi ("Not my people") announces the severance of the covenant relationship. Protestant theology reads these names as depicting the consequences of covenant unfaithfulness — a progression from discipline to abandonment.

Yet the chapter concludes with a stunning reversal: the very names of judgment become names of promise. "Not my people" will become "children of the living God" (1:10). Protestant interpreters, following Paul's citation in Romans 9:25-26, read this as a prophecy of Gentile inclusion in the people of God — those who were not God's people becoming God's people through grace.
