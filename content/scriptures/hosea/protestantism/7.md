Hosea 7 uses vivid imagery to portray Israel's moral and political corruption. The nation is compared to a heated oven whose baker has fallen asleep (7:4-7) — passions burning uncontrolled while those responsible have abdicated their duties. Protestant interpreters read this as a picture of a society consumed by intrigue, conspiracy, and moral chaos, where one king after another is assassinated.

The chapter's most memorable image is Ephraim as "a cake not turned" (7:8) — burned on one side, raw on the other. Protestant preachers have applied this metaphor extensively: half-baked religion, partial commitment, the attempt to combine worship of God with worldly compromise. Israel "mixes himself among the peoples" through foreign alliances, losing his distinctive identity as God's covenant people.

Hosea 7:13-16 portrays God's grief over Israel's stubborn refusal to return despite repeated warnings. "Woe to them, for they have strayed from me!" (7:13). Protestant theology reads this as evidence that divine judgment is not capricious but the sorrowful response of a loving God to persistent, willful rebellion. The chapter closes with the grim irony that Israel's princes will "fall by the sword because of the insolence of their tongue" — their arrogant speech bringing its own destruction.
