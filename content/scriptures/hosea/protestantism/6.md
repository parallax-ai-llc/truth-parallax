Hosea 6 opens with one of the most famous calls to repentance in the Old Testament: "Come, let us return to the LORD; for he has torn us, that he may heal us" (6:1). Protestant interpreters debate whether these words represent genuine repentance or superficial religiosity. The verse "After two days he will revive us; on the third day he will raise us up" (6:2) has been read typologically as a prophecy of Christ's resurrection on the third day.

Verse 6 — "For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, and the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings" — is among the most quoted verses in Protestant theology. Jesus himself cites it twice (Matthew 9:13; 12:7). The verse encapsulates the Reformation insistence that external religious performance is worthless without the inward reality of love for God and neighbor. It does not reject ritual entirely but subordinates it to relational faithfulness.

The remainder of the chapter catalogs Israel's failures: their covenant loyalty is "like a morning cloud" that vanishes quickly (6:4). Protestant preachers have long used this image to describe fair-weather faith. The references to violence at Gilead and Shechem (6:8-9) indicate that covenant violation has corrupted every institution — even the priests commit murder along the pilgrim roads.
