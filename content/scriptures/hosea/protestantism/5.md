Hosea 5 announces judgment on both Israel (Ephraim) and Judah for their pride and political scheming. The chapter opens with indictments of priests, the royal house, and the people for setting traps at Mizpah and Tabor — likely references to centers of corrupt worship. Protestant interpreters read this as a warning against the entanglement of religion with political power.

The central crisis is Israel's attempt to solve its problems through foreign alliances rather than repentance. When Ephraim saw his sickness, he went to Assyria (5:13) — a reference to the pro-Assyrian policies of Israel's final kings. Protestant theology draws from this a principle about the futility of worldly solutions to spiritual problems. Neither military alliances nor political maneuvering can substitute for genuine return to God.

The chapter concludes with God withdrawing to "his place" until the people acknowledge their guilt and seek his face (5:15). Protestant interpreters read this as both judgment and invitation — God's absence creates the conditions for genuine repentance, leading directly into the great call of chapter 6.
