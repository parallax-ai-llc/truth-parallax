## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Hosea emphasizes the book's vivid portrayal of God's covenant love (*hesed*) for an unfaithful people. Reformation and post-Reformation commentators have consistently read Hosea as a powerful illustration of the gospel dynamic — divine grace extended to those who deserve only judgment. The marriage metaphor is central: God as the faithful husband, Israel as the adulterous wife.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Protestant scholars have debated whether Hosea's marriage to Gomer was a literal event or a prophetic allegory. **Calvin** argued for a visionary interpretation, finding it unthinkable that God would command a prophet to marry an immoral woman. **Luther**, by contrast, accepted the literal reading as a sign-act demonstrating God's scandalous grace. Modern evangelical scholarship generally favors the literal reading as consistent with other prophetic sign-acts.

## Theological Emphases

The book's theology aligns naturally with Protestant soteriology. Hosea 6:6 ("I desire mercy, not sacrifice") is read as anticipating the Reformation critique of works-righteousness. The cycle of judgment and restoration in chapters 4-14 illustrates the law-gospel dialectic central to Lutheran and Reformed theology. Hosea 11:1 is understood typologically as pointing to Christ's sojourn in Egypt (Matthew 2:15), and the restoration promises of chapter 14 are read as anticipating the new covenant in Christ.
