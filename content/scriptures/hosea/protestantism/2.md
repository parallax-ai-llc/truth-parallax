Hosea 2 develops the marriage metaphor into a full covenant lawsuit. God, the faithful husband, charges Israel, the unfaithful wife, with pursuing other lovers — the Baals — whom she credits for the gifts that actually come from God (2:5-8). Protestant interpreters read this as a paradigm of spiritual adultery: attributing to false gods or worldly sources the blessings that come from the one true God.

The chapter traces a dramatic arc from judgment to restoration. God will strip Israel bare, exposing the futility of her idolatry (2:9-13). But judgment is not the final word. In a passage of breathtaking tenderness, God declares: "I will allure her, bring her into the wilderness, and speak tenderly to her" (2:14). Protestant theology sees here the gospel pattern — law exposing sin, then grace drawing the sinner back through love rather than coercion.

The chapter culminates in a renewed covenant expressed as a new betrothal: "I will betroth you to me forever; I will betroth you to me in righteousness and justice, in steadfast love and mercy" (2:19-20). Reformed theologians read this as anticipating the new covenant in Christ, where the broken relationship between God and humanity is restored not through human effort but through divine initiative.
