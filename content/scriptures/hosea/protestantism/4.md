Hosea 4 transitions from the personal narrative of chapters 1-3 to a series of oracles against Israel. God opens a formal covenant lawsuit (*riv*): "There is no faithfulness or steadfast love, and no knowledge of God in the land" (4:1). Protestant interpreters emphasize "knowledge of God" not as intellectual assent but as relational intimacy — the kind of knowing that transforms conduct.

The chapter delivers a devastating indictment of the priesthood: "My people are destroyed for lack of knowledge; because you have rejected knowledge, I reject you from being a priest to me" (4:6). This verse has been enormously influential in Protestant tradition, grounding the insistence on biblical literacy and sound teaching. The Reformers cited it against clergy who kept Scripture from the laity.

The interweaving of religious and sexual infidelity throughout the chapter reflects the Canaanite fertility religion that Israel had adopted. Protestant commentators note that Hosea is not merely using metaphor — the worship of Baal literally involved ritual prostitution. The chapter's blunt language about Israel "playing the whore" (4:15) serves as a prophetic exposure of the real consequences of syncretism.
