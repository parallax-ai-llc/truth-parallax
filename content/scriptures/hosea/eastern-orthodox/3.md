Hosea 3 narrates the redemption of Gomer, which Orthodox theology reads as a type of Christ's redemptive descent. The Son of God enters the "slave market" of fallen humanity, paying the price not with silver and barley but with his own blood. The patristic theme of Christ's harrowing of Hades — descending to the depths to rescue the captive — finds an Old Testament anticipation in Hosea's descent to reclaim his wife.

The prescribed period of purification (3:3) — dwelling in faithfulness before full restoration — corresponds to the Orthodox understanding of the catechumenate and the process of spiritual formation. Salvation is not a single moment but a journey of transformation, a gradual conforming of the human person to the divine image through ascetical struggle and sacramental participation.

The eschatological promise of verse 5 — seeking "the LORD their God and David their king" — points toward the Orthodox understanding of the parousia, when all of creation will be gathered into the kingdom of the Messiah. The reference to David is read christologically as pointing to Christ, the true King whose reign inaugurates the age of resurrection and cosmic renewal.
