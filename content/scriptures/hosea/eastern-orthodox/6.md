Hosea 6 opens with the penitential cry that has shaped Orthodox worship: "Come, let us return to the LORD" (6:1). The promise "on the third day he will raise us up" (6:2) is read in Orthodox theology as a direct prophecy of Christ's resurrection — the central event of the entire biblical narrative. The paschal reading transforms this verse from a general statement about restoration into a specific anticipation of the empty tomb.

Verse 6 — "I desire mercy and not sacrifice" — is not, in Orthodox reading, a rejection of liturgical worship but a demand that worship be animated by inner transformation. The Divine Liturgy presupposes the worshipper's genuine repentance and charity; without these, the sacred mysteries become occasions of judgment rather than healing. John Chrysostom's homilies repeatedly echo this prophetic insistence.

The lament that Israel's love is "like a morning cloud" (6:4) speaks to the Orthodox understanding of *nepsis* (spiritual watchfulness) — the constant attentiveness required to sustain the spiritual life against the tendency toward distraction and torpor. The Desert Fathers developed practices of prayer and self-examination precisely to combat this ephemeral quality in human devotion.
