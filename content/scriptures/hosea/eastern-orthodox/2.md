Hosea 2 unfolds the covenant lawsuit in its full dramatic scope. Orthodox interpretation emphasizes the ontological dimension of the marriage metaphor — the union between God and his people is not merely contractual but reflects the deep mystery of divine-human communion that reaches its fullness in the Incarnation. Israel's unfaithfulness is not merely a legal violation but a rupture in the fabric of being.

The divine "alluring" into the wilderness (2:14) resonates with the Orthodox monastic tradition, where the desert is the place of encounter with God. The stripping away of false comforts mirrors the ascetic practice of *kenosis* — self-emptying that creates space for divine filling. Maximus the Confessor's theology of desire is relevant here: human desire, misdirected toward created goods, must be purified and redirected toward its true object.

The new betrothal (2:19-20) anticipates what Orthodox theology calls *theosis* — the transformative union between God and humanity. The covenant is renewed not merely as a legal arrangement but as a participation in divine life, grounded in righteousness, justice, steadfast love, and mercy — the very attributes of God communicated to the restored bride.
