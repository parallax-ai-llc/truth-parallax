## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Hosea is shaped by the patristic heritage, liturgical usage, and the theological framework of *theosis* (divinization). The Greek Fathers read Hosea as a profound revelation of God's *philanthropia* — divine love for humanity — expressed through the willingness to pursue and restore an unfaithful people. The marriage metaphor speaks not merely of legal covenant but of ontological union between God and creation.

## Patristic Heritage

**Cyril of Alexandria** produced the most substantial patristic commentary on Hosea, reading the prophet's marriage as an allegory of God's dealings with Israel and, by extension, with the Church. **Theodoret of Cyrus** offered a more historically grounded reading while maintaining the typological dimension. Both Fathers emphasized divine condescension (*synkatabasis*) — that God stoops to use human analogies, even scandalous ones, to communicate the depth of divine love.

## Liturgical and Theological Significance

In Orthodox liturgical life, Hosea's call to repentance and promise of restoration resonate during Great Lent and the preparatory weeks. Hosea 6:1-2 ("After two days he will revive us; on the third day he will raise us up") is read as a prophecy of the Resurrection. The Orthodox reading emphasizes that judgment is not punitive destruction but therapeutic discipline — God's severe mercy aimed at healing and restoring the covenant relationship, drawing humanity toward participation in the divine nature.
