Hosea 9 announces the end of Israel's festivals and the coming exile — a reversal of the Exodus that strips away all the blessings of the promised land. Islamic interpretation draws parallels with the Quranic accounts of nations expelled from their lands as divine punishment. The Quran states: "How many a city We destroyed while it was committing wrong, so it fell into ruin" (22:45).

The rejection of the prophet described in 9:7 — "The prophet is a fool, the man of the spirit is mad" — echoes the treatment of prophets throughout the Quran. Virtually every prophet faces ridicule and rejection: Noah is called a madman (54:9), Muhammad is accused of being possessed (68:2), and the general pattern is stated explicitly: "No messenger came to them except that they ridiculed him" (36:30).

The invocation of past sins — particularly Gibeah (9:9) and Baal-peor (9:10) — illustrates the Quranic principle that unrepented sin accumulates across generations. The Quran warns: "Those are a people who have passed on; they will have what they earned, and you will have what you have earned" (2:134). Each generation is accountable for its own choices, but the patterns of sin established by predecessors create conditions that make repentance increasingly difficult.
