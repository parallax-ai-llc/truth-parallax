Hosea 2 develops the metaphor of Israel's spiritual infidelity through vivid marital imagery. Islamic interpretation engages this chapter cautiously, noting that while the Quran uses the relationship between God and community as a framework, it avoids the marital metaphor for the divine-human relationship. Allah is not described as a spouse but as Lord (*Rabb*), Master (*Mawla*), and Protector (*Wali*).

The chapter's theological substance — the condemnation of idolatry and the promise of restoration through repentance — is fully consistent with Quranic teaching. Hosea's charge that Israel credited the Baals for God's gifts (2:8) parallels the Quranic critique of *shirk*: attributing to created beings what belongs to Allah alone. The Quran repeatedly asks, "Is there a god besides Allah?" (27:60-64), echoing Hosea's insistence on exclusive devotion.

The restoration promises of 2:14-23 describe God drawing Israel back through mercy rather than coercion. This aligns with the Quranic emphasis on *tawbah* (repentance) and *rahmah* (divine mercy). The Quran affirms that God loves those who turn in repentance (2:222) and that divine mercy is extended to all who sincerely return, regardless of the severity of their previous transgressions.
