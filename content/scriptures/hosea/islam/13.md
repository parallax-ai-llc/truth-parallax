Hosea 13 pronounces devastating judgment on Ephraim for idolatry. The statement "I am the LORD your God from the land of Egypt; you know no God but me, and there is no savior besides me" (13:4) is the closest the Hebrew Bible comes to the Islamic *shahada* — the declaration of absolute monotheism. Islamic readers find in this verse a confirmation of the original monotheistic message that all prophets, from Adam through Muhammad, were sent to proclaim.

The descriptions of divine wrath — God as lion, leopard, and bear (13:7-8) — present the severity of judgment against those who persist in *shirk*. The Quran similarly employs vivid imagery of divine punishment: "So each We seized for his sin; among them were those upon whom We sent a storm of stones, and among them were those seized by the blast, and among them were those whom We caused the earth to swallow" (29:40).

The chapter's treatment of death and Sheol (13:14) raises questions that Islamic eschatology addresses directly. The Quran affirms that death is not the end: "Every soul will taste death. Then to Us will you be returned" (29:57). While the Hebrew concept of Sheol differs from the Islamic understanding of *barzakh* (the intermediate state between death and resurrection), both traditions affirm that physical death does not annihilate the person and that ultimate judgment awaits.
