Hosea 12 invokes the patriarch Jacob, known in Islamic tradition as Ya'qub, one of the honored prophets. The Quran mentions Ya'qub as a righteous servant: "And We gave him Isaac and Jacob, all of whom We guided" (6:84). However, the Genesis traditions that Hosea references — Jacob's deception, wrestling with God, and life of struggle — differ significantly from the Quranic portrait of prophets as morally exemplary from birth.

The chapter's emphasis on God communicating through prophets (12:10, 13) aligns with the Islamic understanding of *wahy* (divine revelation) as the primary means by which God guides humanity. The Quran affirms: "And We did not send any messenger except in the language of his people so he could make things clear for them" (14:4). Hosea's insistence that God acts through prophetic mediation supports the Islamic principle of prophethood as God's chosen method of communication.

The charge that Ephraim "feeds on wind and pursues the east wind" (12:1) through futile foreign alliances illustrates the Islamic concept of *tawakkul* (reliance on God) versus *shirk* in its broader sense — placing ultimate trust in anything other than God. The Quran warns: "And whoever takes Satan as an ally instead of Allah has certainly sustained a clear loss" (4:119).
