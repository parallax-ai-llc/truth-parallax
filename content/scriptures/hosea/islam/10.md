Hosea 10 opens with the paradox of a prosperous vine that directs its fruit toward false worship (10:1). Islamic theology identifies this as the danger of *ni'mah* (divine blessing) received without *shukr* (gratitude). The Quran warns: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you in favor; but if you deny, indeed, My punishment is severe" (14:7). Hosea's Israel exemplifies the ingratitude that transforms blessing into curse.

The prophecy that Beth-aven's golden calf will be carried to Assyria as tribute (10:5-6) illustrates the impotence of idols — a central theme in both Hosea and the Quran. The Quran asks: "Do they have legs by which they walk? Or do they have hands by which they strike? Or do they have eyes by which they see?" (7:195). The idol that cannot protect itself certainly cannot protect its worshippers, and its removal exposes the futility of *shirk*.

The exhortation to "sow righteousness and reap steadfast love" (10:12) aligns with the Quranic emphasis on righteous action (*amal salih*) as the necessary companion of faith (*iman*). The Quran consistently pairs belief with righteous deeds: "Those who believe and do righteous deeds — for them is forgiveness and great reward" (35:7). Hosea's agricultural metaphor expresses the same principle: spiritual harvest requires the planting of moral seeds.
