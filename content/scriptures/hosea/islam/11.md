Hosea 11 presents God as a loving parent who nurtured Israel from infancy. The image of God teaching Israel to walk and carrying them in his arms (11:3-4) resonates with the Quranic emphasis on God's *rahmah* (mercy) and *ri'ayah* (care). While the Quran does not use the parental metaphor for God — Allah is not described as "father" — it affirms that God's mercy is greater than a mother's mercy for her child, as recorded in hadith.

The verse "Out of Egypt I called my son" (11:1) uses the title "son" for Israel in a collective sense. Islamic theology firmly rejects any literal filial relationship between God and creation — "He neither begets nor is begotten" (112:3). However, the metaphorical meaning of special care and chosen status for a community is consistent with the Quranic concept of God selecting and guiding certain peoples, as with the children of Israel: "O children of Israel, remember My favor which I bestowed upon you" (2:40).

The divine declaration "I am God and not a man" (11:9) resonates powerfully with Islamic theology's emphasis on divine transcendence (*tanzih*). God's ways are categorically different from human ways — a principle that stands at the heart of *tawhid*. The Quran affirms: "There is nothing like unto Him" (42:11). In Islamic reading, this verse reinforces the fundamental distinction between Creator and creation.
