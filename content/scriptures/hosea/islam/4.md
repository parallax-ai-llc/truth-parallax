Hosea 4 opens a formal divine lawsuit against Israel, cataloguing moral failures that Islamic ethics also condemns: lying, murder, theft, and adultery. The foundational charge — "no knowledge of God in the land" (4:1) — parallels the Quranic concept of *jahl* (ignorance), the pre-Islamic condition of spiritual blindness that the Prophet Muhammad was sent to dispel. In both Hosea and the Quran, moral corruption stems from the failure to know and acknowledge God.

The indictment of the priesthood (4:4-10) resonates with the Quranic critique of religious leaders who mislead their communities. The Quran warns: "O you who believe, indeed many of the scholars and monks consume the wealth of people unjustly and turn them away from the way of Allah" (9:34). Hosea's charge that priests "feed on the sin of my people" (4:8) describes the same corruption — religious authorities who profit from the spiritual ignorance of those they should guide.

The descriptions of idolatrous worship in 4:11-19 illustrate the category of *shirk* in its most blatant form. The Quran declares that Allah "does not forgive association with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills" (4:48). Hosea's portrayal of Israel consulting wooden idols and engaging in fertility rituals represents exactly the kind of spiritual corruption that the Quran identifies as the gravest possible sin.
