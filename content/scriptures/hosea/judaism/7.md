Hosea 7 employs vivid imagery to describe Israel's internal corruption. The oven metaphor (7:4-7) — heated overnight, its baker asleep — depicts a society consumed by political intrigue and assassination. Rashi connects this to the rapid succession of kings in Israel's final decades, when courtiers murdered their sovereigns with disturbing regularity. The Midrash sees the oven as representing unchecked desire (*yetzer hara*) that consumes from within when not governed by Torah.

The image of Ephraim as "a cake not turned" (7:8) is interpreted by Jewish commentators as half-baked commitment — neither fully devoted to God nor fully assimilated among the nations. Ibn Ezra reads this as the essential problem of syncretism: Israel attempted to worship both God and the Baals, succeeding at neither. The Talmudic concept of being "lukewarm" in observance finds its prophetic precursor here.

The pathos of 7:13-16 — "Woe to them, for they have strayed from me! I would redeem them, but they speak lies against me" — reveals the frustration of divine love encountering persistent human resistance. Jewish theology emphasizes that God's desire to redeem is constant, but redemption requires human participation through *teshuvah*. God cannot force repentance without violating the gift of free will.
