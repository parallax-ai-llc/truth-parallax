Hosea 3 records the prophet's repurchase of Gomer for fifteen shekels of silver and some barley. Rashi interprets the modest price as reflecting Gomer's degraded condition — she had sunk so low that her value was less than that of a slave. The Talmud (Pesachim 87b) reads the transaction as parallel to God's redemption of Israel: even in their lowest state, God does not abandon them but pays the cost of restoration.

The period of abstinence prescribed in 3:3 is understood in Jewish tradition as corresponding to the exile — a time when Israel would be without king, sacrifice, sacred pillar, ephod, or teraphim (3:4). This verse has been interpreted as a description of the current Jewish condition in exile: deprived of the Temple and its institutions, yet still belonging to God and awaiting the full restoration of the covenantal relationship.

The concluding promise (3:5) — that Israel will seek the LORD and "David their king" — is a messianic text in Jewish interpretation. "David their king" refers to the Messiah from the Davidic line who will reunify the nation. The Targum explicitly renders this as "the Messiah, son of David." The temporal marker "in the latter days" places this fulfillment in the eschatological future, maintaining the ongoing Jewish expectation of redemption.
