Hosea 11 is one of the most theologically significant chapters in Jewish prophetic literature. God speaks as a parent recalling the early days of Israel's "childhood" — the Exodus, the wilderness, the tender care lavished on a young nation. The verse "Out of Egypt I called my son" (11:1) recalls the foundational moment of Jewish identity: the Exodus as the birth of the nation, God's fatherly act of liberation.

The divine anguish of 11:8 — "How can I give you up, Ephraim?" — reveals a dimension of God's character that rabbinic theology explores extensively. The Midrash describes God as torn between the attribute of justice (*middat ha-din*) and the attribute of mercy (*middat ha-rahamim*). In Hosea 11, mercy prevails: "I will not execute my burning anger... for I am God and not a man" (11:9). This becomes a foundational text for the rabbinic teaching that God's mercy ultimately exceeds his strict justice.

The Targum Jonathan renders this passage with characteristic theological care, emphasizing God's transcendence while preserving the emotional force of the original. The declaration "I am God and not a man" grounds Jewish theology's insistence on divine otherness — God's love is real but not subject to human limitations of patience, consistency, or capacity for forgiveness.
