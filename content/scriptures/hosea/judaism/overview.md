## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Hosea (*Hoshea*) is the first of the Twelve Minor Prophets (*Trei Asar*), which are counted as a single book in the Hebrew Bible. The Talmud (Bava Batra 14b) identifies Hosea as the earliest of the four great eighth-century prophets (Hosea, Isaiah, Amos, Micah), and rabbinic literature regards him as a prophet of exceptional compassion whose intercession for Israel shaped the prophetic tradition.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The Talmud (Pesachim 87a-b) records a remarkable midrash: God initially told Hosea that Israel had sinned, expecting him to plead for mercy as Moses had. When Hosea instead suggested replacing Israel with another nation, God commanded him to marry a promiscuous woman and have children with her — so that when God later told him to abandon his family, Hosea would understand Israel's pain and intercede on their behalf. This reading transforms the marriage narrative into a lesson about prophetic empathy.

## Liturgical and Theological Significance

Hosea 14:2-10, the great call to repentance (*teshuvah*), is read as the *haftarah* for Shabbat Shuvah — the Sabbath between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, the most penitential period of the Jewish year. Hosea 2:21-22 ("I will betroth you to me forever") is recited daily by observant Jews when wrapping the tefillin around their fingers, transforming the covenant marriage metaphor into embodied liturgical practice. The Targum Jonathan renders Hosea's prophecies with careful attention to removing anthropomorphisms, reflecting Jewish theological sensibilities about divine transcendence.
