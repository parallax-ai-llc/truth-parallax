Hosea 6 opens with a call to repentance that has become central to Jewish liturgy: "Come, let us return to the LORD" (6:1). This passage is read as the haftarah for Shabbat Shuvah — the Sabbath between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur — the most penitential moment of the Jewish year. The confidence that God "has torn, and he will heal us" (6:1) embodies the Jewish understanding that divine discipline is always oriented toward restoration.

Verse 6 — "I desire *hesed* and not sacrifice" — has generated extensive rabbinic discussion. The Talmud (Avot de-Rabbi Natan 4) records that after the Temple's destruction, Rabbi Yohanan ben Zakkai consoled his students by citing this verse: acts of lovingkindness can now take the place of sacrificial offerings. This interpretation became foundational for post-Temple Judaism, grounding the practice of *gemilut hasadim* as a legitimate form of divine service.

The lament that Israel's devotion is like a morning cloud (6:4) is read by Jewish commentators as a warning against superficial repentance. The Talmud distinguishes between *teshuvah* motivated by fear (which is accepted but incomplete) and *teshuvah* motivated by love (which transforms transgressions into merits). Hosea's complaint suggests that Israel's repentance was of the former kind — genuine in the moment but lacking the depth to endure.
