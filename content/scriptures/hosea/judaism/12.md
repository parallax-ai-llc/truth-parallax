Hosea 12 revisits the Jacob traditions, holding up the patriarch as both model and mirror for Israel. The references to Jacob's prenatal struggle (12:3), his wrestling at Peniel (12:4), and his encounter at Bethel (12:4) are read in Jewish tradition as revealing the complex character of Israel's ancestor — cunning, persistent, and ultimately transformed by encounter with God.

Rashi and other commentators note that the name "Israel" — derived from Jacob's wrestling — means "one who struggles with God." This etymology defines the Jewish spiritual posture: faith is not passive acceptance but active engagement, questioning, and wrestling. The Talmudic tradition of *mahloket l'shem shamayim* (argument for the sake of heaven) finds its patriarchal precedent in Jacob's refusal to let go until he received a blessing.

The chapter's invocation of the prophetic tradition (12:10, 13) — God speaking through prophets and using visions — establishes the authority of prophetic revelation alongside Torah. Jewish theology holds Torah and Prophecy (*Nevi'im*) in dynamic relationship: Torah provides the foundational law, while the prophets interpret, apply, and call the community back to its covenantal obligations.
