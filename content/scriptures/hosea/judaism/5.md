Hosea 5 announces judgment on both Ephraim and Judah, implicating the entire house of Israel. Jewish commentators note that the chapter addresses three groups — priests, people, and the royal house — holding each accountable for the corruption of the nation. Radak observes that the leaders bear greater responsibility because their influence determines the spiritual direction of the people.

The political dimension — Ephraim seeking Assyria's help (5:13) — is read by Jewish commentators as a failure of *bitahon* (trust in God). The Midrash draws a general principle: when Israel trusts in human alliances rather than divine providence, disaster follows. This theme recurs throughout Jewish history, from the Assyrian and Babylonian crises through the Roman period, and shapes the theological reflection on exile and redemption.

God's withdrawal (5:15) is interpreted by Rashi as conditional: God will wait until Israel acknowledges its guilt and seeks his face. The connection to chapter 6 is crucial — God's absence creates the possibility for genuine *teshuvah*. Jewish theology understands divine "hiding" (*hester panim*) not as abandonment but as a painful but necessary condition for the exercise of human free will and authentic return.
