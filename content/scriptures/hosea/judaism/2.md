Hosea 2 develops the marriage metaphor into a formal *riv* (covenant lawsuit). Jewish interpretation reads God's charges against Israel within the framework of the Sinai covenant — Israel has violated its marriage contract by pursuing the Baals. Rashi notes that the "lovers" Israel pursues are the fertility gods whom she credits for grain, wine, and oil, gifts that actually come from God.

The passage describing God's allurement of Israel into the wilderness (2:14-15) is read in rabbinic tradition as a prophecy of the messianic age. The "Valley of Achor" (trouble) becomes a "door of hope" — the very place of Israel's ancient sin (Joshua 7) becomes the gateway to restoration. The Midrash sees this as a principle of divine transformation: God turns the sites of judgment into places of renewal.

The betrothal formula of 2:21-22 has become part of daily Jewish liturgy. When wrapping the tefillin strap around the finger, observant Jews recite: "I will betroth you to me forever; I will betroth you to me in righteousness and justice, in love and compassion." This practice transforms Hosea's prophecy into embodied covenantal devotion, renewed each morning as an act of spiritual marriage between God and Israel.
