Hosea 13 pronounces what appears to be a death sentence on Ephraim, tracing the trajectory from early faithfulness to terminal idolatry. When Ephraim "spoke trembling, he was exalted in Israel" (13:1) — Rashi interprets this as the early period when the tribe of Ephraim commanded respect. But guilt through Baal worship brought spiritual death. The Midrash reads this as a cautionary tale about how quickly a community can fall from prominence to destruction.

The verse "I am the LORD your God from the land of Egypt; you know no God but me" (13:4) restates the fundamental claim of the first commandment. Jewish theology regards this as the *ikkar* (foundational principle) of the entire Torah: exclusive loyalty to the one God who liberated Israel from bondage. All of Hosea's charges reduce to this: Israel has violated the first and greatest commandment.

Verse 14 — concerning Death and Sheol — is debated among Jewish commentators. Some read it as a rhetorical question implying refusal to redeem ("Shall I ransom them? No!"), while others read it as a promise of ultimate redemption even from death. The latter reading connects to the doctrine of *tehiyyat ha-metim* (resurrection of the dead), which later became a core principle of Jewish faith as articulated by Maimonides.
