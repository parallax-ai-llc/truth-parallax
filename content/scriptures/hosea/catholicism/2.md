Hosea 2 unfolds as a divine covenant lawsuit in which God charges Israel with spiritual adultery. Catholic interpretation emphasizes the sacramental dimension of the marriage metaphor — the covenant between God and Israel is not a mere contract but a sacred bond that cannot be dissolved, even by infidelity. This informs the Catholic theology of marriage as an indissoluble sacrament reflecting Christ's union with the Church.

The passage where God "allures" Israel into the wilderness (2:14) has been read by Catholic mystics as a description of the spiritual life. The wilderness represents the place of stripping away — where false consolations are removed so that the soul can encounter God directly. This reading influenced Carmelite spirituality, particularly the writings of John of the Cross on the "dark night" as a purgative stage leading to deeper union with God.

The betrothal language of 2:19-20 — "I will betroth you to me in righteousness and justice, in steadfast love and mercy" — is central to Catholic nuptial theology. Benedict XVI cited these verses in *Deus Caritas Est* to illustrate how divine love transforms *eros* into *agape*, passionate desire into covenantal faithfulness.
