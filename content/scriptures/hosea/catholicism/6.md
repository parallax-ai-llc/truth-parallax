Hosea 6 opens with the great penitential call: "Come, let us return to the LORD" (6:1). Catholic liturgical practice draws heavily on this chapter, particularly during Lent. The promise "on the third day he will raise us up" (6:2) has been read patristically as a prophecy of Christ's resurrection, connecting Israel's corporate restoration to the paschal mystery.

Verse 6 — "I desire mercy and not sacrifice" — holds a central place in Catholic moral theology. Jesus cites it twice in Matthew's Gospel, and the Catholic tradition interprets it not as a rejection of sacramental worship but as an insistence that the interior disposition must match the exterior act. The Eucharist, as the supreme sacrifice, demands a life of mercy and justice from those who receive it.

The chapter's lament that Israel's devotion is "like a morning cloud, like the dew that goes early away" (6:4) speaks to the Catholic emphasis on perseverance in the spiritual life. Transient emotional fervor must be rooted in habitual virtue and sustained by the sacraments if it is to endure. The spiritual masters warn against mistaking initial consolation for mature faith.
