## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Hosea draws on a rich tradition stretching from the Church Fathers through the medieval commentators to modern papal encyclicals. The marriage metaphor at the heart of Hosea is read not only as a historical account but as a sacramental sign — the union of God and Israel prefiguring both Christ's union with the Church and the sacramental dignity of marriage itself.

## Patristic and Medieval Reading

The Church Fathers, including **Jerome** and **Cyril of Alexandria**, interpreted Hosea's marriage allegorically and typologically. Jerome emphasized that Gomer represents the synagogue that rejected its divine spouse, while the restored wife prefigures the Church. Medieval commentators in the Thomistic tradition read the covenant language of Hosea through the lens of grace and merit, seeing Israel's return to God as enabled by divine initiative rather than human effort alone.

## Modern Catholic Interpretation

Pope Benedict XVI's encyclical *Deus Caritas Est* (2005) draws extensively on Hosea to illustrate the nature of divine love — that God's *eros* for his people transforms into *agape* through covenant faithfulness. Catholic social teaching also draws on Hosea's critique of injustice and empty ritualism (Hosea 6:6) as a call for authentic worship expressed through justice and mercy. The Catechism cites Hosea in its treatment of God's spousal love for his people (CCC 219).
