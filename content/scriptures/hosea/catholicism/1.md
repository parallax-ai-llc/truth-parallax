Hosea 1 introduces the prophetic sign-act that defines the entire book: God commands Hosea to marry Gomer, a woman of unfaithfulness, as a living parable of God's relationship with Israel. Catholic tradition, following Jerome and the patristic consensus, has generally favored reading this marriage as historically real while rich in allegorical significance. The sacramental theology of marriage informs the Catholic reading — the union of Hosea and Gomer, however broken, remains a sign of the covenant between God and his people.

The three children's names — Jezreel, Lo-Ruhamah, and Lo-Ammi — announce escalating judgment. Catholic interpretation reads these not merely as punishments but as stages in a divine pedagogy. God's withdrawal of mercy and covenant identity is therapeutic, aimed at bringing Israel to recognition of its need. The Catechism (CCC 218-221) draws on the spousal love language of the prophets, including Hosea, to describe God's love as both passionate and faithful.

The reversal promised in 1:10-11 is understood in Catholic theology as fulfilled in the Church, where those who were "not my people" — Gentiles — become children of the living God through baptism. This ecclesiological reading connects Hosea's prophecy to the universal scope of salvation.
