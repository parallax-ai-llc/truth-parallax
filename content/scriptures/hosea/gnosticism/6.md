Hosea 6 opens with a penitential call that, in Gnostic reading, represents the voice of those still trapped within the Demiurgic system: "Come, let us return to the LORD" (6:1). The promise "on the third day he will raise us up" takes on cosmic significance — the third day as the moment when the pneumatic spark, buried in matter, finally rises to rejoin the Pleroma. This anticipates the Gnostic reading of Christ's resurrection as the paradigm of spiritual liberation.

Verse 6 — "I desire mercy and not sacrifice" — contains a profound tension when read through Gnostic lenses. The Demiurge, who established the sacrificial system, now claims to despise it. This internal contradiction supports the Gnostic contention that the God of the Hebrew Bible is inconsistent — alternately demanding and rejecting the very institutions he created. The true God beyond the Demiurge neither establishes nor rejects material sacrifices; such things are irrelevant to the realm of pure spirit.

The complaint that Israel's loyalty evaporates like morning dew (6:4) might also describe the Demiurge's own ephemeral power. The material world he has constructed is itself transient — a "morning cloud" that will dissipate when the light of gnosis fully dawns. The Demiurge's frustration with his creation's instability unknowingly testifies to the fundamental impermanence of the material order.
