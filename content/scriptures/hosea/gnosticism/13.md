Hosea 13 is the book's darkest chapter — a death sentence pronounced by a god who compares himself to a lion, leopard, and bear (13:7-8). The Gnostic reader recognizes in these predatory images the true nature of the Demiurge: not a shepherd who protects but a predator who devours. The archontic god reveals himself most clearly in moments of wrath, when the mask of benevolence drops and raw power is displayed.

The disputed verse 13:14 — concerning death and Sheol — is read by some Gnostics as the Demiurge summoning death as an ally rather than an enemy. Death serves the archontic system by recycling souls through the material realm, and the threat to unleash death's "plagues" reveals the Demiurge's willingness to destroy what he cannot control. But Paul's resurrection reading (1 Corinthians 15:55) introduces a counter-voice: the true God defies death, breaking the cycle of material imprisonment.

The violence of the chapter's conclusion reveals what Gnostic texts call the Demiurge's *orge* (wrath) — the destructive rage of a creator whose creation has escaped his control. The pneumatic reader is not terrified by this wrath but sees through it to the impotence it conceals. The Demiurge destroys because he cannot persuade, punishes because he cannot transform. His violence is the admission of his own limitation.
