## Overview

The Letter of Peter to Philip (NHC VIII,2) is a composite text that begins with a brief letter from Peter to Philip, urging him to rejoin the assembled apostles, and then transitions into a Gnostic revelation dialogue in which the risen Christ appears on the Mount of Olives and instructs the disciples about the Pleroma (divine fullness), the deficiency caused by the Mother's disobedience, the creation of the material world by the archons, and the meaning of the apostles' sufferings. The text reflects a concern for apostolic unity — Peter's letter calls Philip back to the group — and provides a Gnostic interpretation of apostolic suffering: the disciples suffer because they are strangers in a world created by ignorant powers, but their suffering participates in and imitates the Savior's own self-giving. A second copy of this text was found in Codex Tchacos, confirming its importance in Gnostic circles.

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: Provides a Gnostic interpretation of apostolic authority, suffering, and mission
- **Academic**: Studied for its composite genre (epistle + revelation dialogue), its Christology, and its second copy in Codex Tchacos
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to traditions of post-mortem instruction by a master and the meaning of suffering for the spiritual teacher
- **Christian**: Engages with canonical themes of apostolic unity, mission, and the meaning of suffering while developing them in a Gnostic direction
- **Jewish**: Reflects Jewish concepts of divine Wisdom (Sophia/Hokhmah) and the motif of prophetic suffering
- **Interfaith**: Addresses the universal question of why spiritual people suffer and whether suffering can have redemptive significance

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Apostolic Unity | Peter's letter to Philip emphasizes the importance of the apostles remaining together |
| Pleroma and Deficiency | The cosmos is a place of deficiency caused by the Mother's error; the Pleroma is the fullness to which all must return |
| The Meaning of Suffering | Apostolic suffering is not meaningless but participates in the cosmic drama of redemption |
| Gnostic Cosmogony | The risen Christ reveals the Gnostic creation account to the assembled apostles |
