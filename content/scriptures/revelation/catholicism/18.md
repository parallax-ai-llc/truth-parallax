## Babylon's Fall and Catholic Social Teaching

Revelation 18 is one of the most important texts for Catholic social doctrine's critique of economic injustice. The chapter's sustained dirge over Babylon's commercial empire — its luxury goods, its exploitation of labor, its trafficking in "human souls" (18:13) — resonates with papal encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* (1891) to *Fratelli Tutti* (2020). Catholic interpretation reads Babylon not as a single future city but as a perennial symbol of economic systems that commodify human persons and prioritize profit over dignity.

## The Prophetic Call: "Come Out of Her"

The command to "come out of her, my people" (18:4) is read in Catholic tradition not as a call to physical separation but as a summons to interior detachment from the values of a consumerist culture. Catholic religious life — with its vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience — has been understood as a prophetic response to this call, living as a sign of contradiction within the world. The Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church applies this prophetic stance to all Christians who must resist the "structures of sin" embedded in unjust economic arrangements.

## The Silence of Judgment

The comprehensive silence that falls over Babylon — no more music, craftsmanship, light, or celebration (18:22-23) — represents the total cessation of cultural life. Catholic interpretation reads this not as an endorsement of cultural destruction but as a warning: culture and commerce, when detached from justice and oriented solely toward luxury and exploitation, are ultimately self-annihilating. The heavenly rejoicing (18:20) celebrates the vindication of justice, not the destruction of culture per se.
