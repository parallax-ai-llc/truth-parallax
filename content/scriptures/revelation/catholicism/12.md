## The Woman Clothed with the Sun

Revelation 12 is one of the most important chapters for Catholic theology, as the Woman clothed with the sun (12:1) is read as both the Church and the Blessed Virgin Mary. This dual typology is affirmed by the Second Vatican Council's *Lumen Gentium* (chapter 8): Mary is both a member of the Church and its preeminent type. The imagery — crowned with twelve stars, standing on the moon — has directly shaped the Catholic iconography of the Immaculate Conception and the Assumption, and the liturgy assigns Revelation 12 as the first reading for the Feast of the Assumption (August 15).

## The Dragon and the Male Child

The great red dragon (12:3) is identified as "the ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan" (12:9), connecting Revelation to Genesis 3:15 — the *protoevangelium*, in which the woman's seed will crush the serpent's head. Catholic theology reads the male child "caught up to God and to his throne" (12:5) as Christ, whose ascension inaugurates the definitive defeat of Satan. The war in heaven (12:7-9), led by Michael the Archangel, is commemorated on the Feast of Saints Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael (September 29).

## The Persecution of the Woman

The dragon's pursuit of the woman into the wilderness (12:13-17) is read as the persecution of both Mary and the Church. The woman's "other offspring" (12:17) — those who keep the commandments of God and hold to the testimony of Jesus — are the faithful members of the Church in every age. Catholic theology sees in this chapter the cosmic dimension of the Church's earthly struggles: the battle is ultimately spiritual, and the victory has already been won by the blood of the Lamb.
