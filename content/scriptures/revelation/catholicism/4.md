## The Heavenly Liturgy

Catholic interpretation of Revelation 4 centers on the liturgical character of the throne-room vision. Scott Hahn and other Catholic scholars have argued that the structure of Revelation mirrors the structure of the Mass: the Liturgy of the Word (the scroll and its opening) and the Liturgy of the Eucharist (the Lamb's sacrifice and the heavenly banquet). The elders casting their crowns before the throne (4:10) has been seen as the prototype of the offertory — the faithful offering back to God what they have received.

## The Trisagion and Liturgical Continuity

The four living creatures' ceaseless cry — "Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty" (4:8) — is the *Sanctus* of the Mass, directly linking earthly worship to heavenly reality. Catholic liturgical theology holds that when the congregation sings the *Sanctus*, they join the angelic and celestial worship described in this chapter. The Second Vatican Council's *Sacrosanctum Concilium* affirmed that the earthly liturgy is a participation in the heavenly liturgy "celebrated in the holy city of Jerusalem toward which we journey as pilgrims."

## Theological Significance

The twenty-four elders (4:4) have been interpreted in Catholic tradition as representing the twelve patriarchs of Israel and the twelve apostles — the complete people of God spanning both covenants. Their priestly function (white garments, golden crowns, prostration, hymns) affirms the Catholic understanding of the universal call to worship and the ordained priesthood's role in mediating that worship.
