## The Great Prostitute

Catholic interpretation of Revelation 17 has undergone significant development. While the Reformers uniformly identified the prostitute with the Catholic Church and the papacy, Catholic scholarship reads Babylon as a symbol of imperial Rome in its original context and, more broadly, as any civilization that sets itself up in opposition to God through idolatry, oppression, and seduction. Catholic commentators note that the text itself invites multiple layers of meaning — the angel says "this calls for a mind with wisdom" (17:9).

## The Scarlet Beast and the Seven Hills

The seven heads identified as seven mountains (17:9) unmistakably evoke Rome — built on seven hills — and Catholic scholars acknowledge this identification freely. However, Catholic interpretation insists that the critique is directed at pagan Rome, the persecutor of the early Church, not at the Church itself. The beast "was, and is not, and is about to come" (17:8) parodies Christ's eternal existence and has been connected to the Nero *redivivus* myth — the popular first-century expectation that Nero would return from the dead.

## Theological Significance

Catholic moral theology reads this chapter as a warning against the seduction of worldly power and luxury — a warning applicable to the Church herself when she becomes too closely allied with political power. Pope Francis has spoken of the danger of a "worldly Church" that loses its prophetic edge. The destruction of the prostitute by the very beast she rides (17:16) illustrates the self-consuming nature of alliances built on mutual exploitation rather than genuine communion.
