## The Lamb on Mount Zion

Revelation 14 opens with the Lamb and the 144,000 on Mount Zion — a vision of the redeemed community contrasting with the beast's followers in chapter 13. Catholic interpretation emphasizes that these are those "redeemed from the earth" (14:3) who follow the Lamb wherever he goes. The description of the 144,000 as those "who have not defiled themselves with women" (14:4) has been read in Catholic tradition as both a commendation of consecrated virginity and a metaphor for spiritual fidelity — the Church as the chaste bride of Christ.

## The Three Angels

The first angel proclaims "an eternal gospel" (14:6-7) — a call to worship the Creator. Catholic theology sees this as affirming the universality of the gospel invitation and the natural knowledge of God accessible to all people (cf. Romans 1:19-20, CCC 31-35). The second angel announces Babylon's fall (14:8). The third angel warns of eternal consequences for those who worship the beast — a passage that Catholic teaching cites in affirming the reality and eternity of hell (CCC 1033-1037).

## The Harvest

The dual harvest (14:14-20) — grain and grapes — echoes Jesus' parables of the wheat and tares and the vine. Catholic interpretation reads the "Son of Man" on the cloud (14:14) as Christ at the final judgment, and the winepress of God's wrath as the definitive separation of good and evil. The eucharistic overtones of bread (grain) and wine (grapes) are noted by Catholic liturgical commentators, who see in this chapter the eschatological dimension of every celebration of the Eucharist.
