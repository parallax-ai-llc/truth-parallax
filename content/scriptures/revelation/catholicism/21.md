## The New Heaven and New Earth

Revelation 21 presents the consummation of salvation history in the descent of the New Jerusalem. Catholic theology reads this chapter as the fulfillment of the entire biblical narrative — the restoration of the communion between God and humanity that was fractured in Genesis 3. The declaration "Behold, the dwelling of God is with man" (21:3) echoes the covenant formula and finds its ultimate realization in the beatific vision: the direct, unmediated experience of God that is the core of Catholic eschatological hope.

## The Beatific Vision

The absence of a temple in the New Jerusalem — "for its temple is the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb" (21:22) — represents what Catholic theology calls the beatific vision (CCC 1023-1029): the blessed see God "face to face" (1 Corinthians 13:12). All mediation — sacramental, institutional, liturgical — reaches its fulfillment when God's presence becomes immediate. The city needs no sun or moon, "for the glory of God gives it light, and its lamp is the Lamb" (21:23).

## The City as Bride

The New Jerusalem is simultaneously a city and a bride (21:2, 9-10), and Catholic ecclesiology reads this as the Church in her eschatological perfection — purified, glorified, and united with Christ. The city's dimensions (a perfect cube echoing the Holy of Holies), its precious foundations, and its open gates suggest both the perfection of divine order and the radical inclusivity of salvation — "the nations will walk by its light, and the kings of the earth will bring their glory into it" (21:24).
