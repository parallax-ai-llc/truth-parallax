## The Victorious Saints and the Song of the Lamb

Revelation 15 serves as a liturgical prelude to the bowl judgments. The saints who have conquered the beast stand on a sea of glass mingled with fire, singing "the song of Moses, the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb" (15:3). Catholic interpretation sees this as a profound statement of liturgical continuity: the song of Israel's deliverance at the Red Sea is united with the song of the Lamb's redemption, affirming the unity of the two Testaments and the single economy of salvation.

## The Heavenly Liturgy Continues

The opening of the heavenly temple and the emergence of the seven angels with golden bowls (15:5-8) extend the liturgical framework that pervades Revelation. Catholic commentators note the temple filled with smoke from God's glory (15:8), echoing both the Sinai theophany (Exodus 19:18) and Solomon's temple dedication (1 Kings 8:10-11). The incense used in Catholic worship participates in this same reality, making visible the invisible glory of God.

## Theological Significance

The declaration that God's "righteous acts have been revealed" (15:4) establishes that the coming judgments are not arbitrary but flow from divine justice. Catholic moral theology reads the bowl judgments within the framework of God's patience finally giving way to definitive justice — the same trajectory found in the prophetic tradition of the Old Testament.
