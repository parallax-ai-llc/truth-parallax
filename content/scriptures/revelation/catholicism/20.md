## The Millennium: Augustinian Interpretation

Catholic theology has followed St. Augustine's interpretation of the millennium since the 5th century. In *The City of God* (Book XX), Augustine argued that the thousand years symbolize the present age of the Church — the period between Christ's first and second comings. Satan is "bound" in the sense that his power to deceive the nations is restrained by the proclamation of the gospel, though he retains the ability to tempt individuals. The first resurrection (20:4-6) is interpreted as spiritual regeneration through baptism, and the "thousand years" is not a literal chronological period but a symbol of completeness.

## The Great White Throne Judgment

The final judgment scene (20:11-15) affirms Catholic eschatological teaching on the general judgment — the moment when all human actions are definitively assessed. Catholic theology distinguishes between the particular judgment (at the moment of death) and the general judgment described here. The judgment "by what was written in the books, according to what they had done" (20:12) is read in harmony with Catholic teaching on the role of works in salvation — not as earning salvation through merit but as the visible expression of a living faith animated by charity.

## The Book of Life

The "book of life" (20:15) represents God's eternal knowledge of the elect. Catholic theology affirms both God's sovereign predestination and the genuine freedom of human response — the mystery of grace and free will that the Council of Trent carefully articulated against both Pelagianism and strict double predestination.
