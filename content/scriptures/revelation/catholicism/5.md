## The Lamb and the Scroll

Revelation 5 is central to Catholic sacramental theology. The Lamb "standing as though slain" (5:6) is read as the enduring sacrificial reality made present in the Eucharist — the risen Christ who perpetually bears the marks of his sacrifice. The Council of Trent affirmed that the Mass makes present the one sacrifice of Calvary, and Catholic commentators see in Revelation 5 the heavenly archetype of this liturgical reality.

## The Heavenly Sacrifice

The incense identified as "the prayers of the saints" (5:8) affirms the Catholic practice of intercessory prayer, the communion of saints, and the use of incense in the liturgy. The elders who hold the bowls of incense function as heavenly priests — a reality that Catholic theology sees reflected in the earthly priesthood. The Lamb's reception of the sealed scroll is interpreted as Christ's reception of authority to execute God's plan of salvation, which unfolds through the sacramental life of the Church.

## Universal Redemption

The new song declares that the Lamb ransomed people "from every tribe and language and people and nation" (5:9), a text that Catholic teaching cites in support of the universal scope of Christ's redemptive work. The declaration that the redeemed are made "a kingdom and priests to our God" (5:10) affirms the baptismal priesthood of all the faithful, while the hierarchical structure of the heavenly worship (Lamb, elders, living creatures, angels) reflects the Catholic understanding of ordered ministry within the one Body of Christ.
