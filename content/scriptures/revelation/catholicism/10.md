## The Angel and the Little Scroll

Revelation 10 provides an interlude of prophetic recommissioning. The mighty angel clothed in a cloud, with a rainbow crown and a face like the sun, descends with a little scroll. Catholic interpretation sees this figure as a powerful angelic mediator — not Christ himself, since the angel swears by "him who lives forever and ever, who created heaven and what is in it" (10:6), distinguishing him from the Creator.

## The Sealed Thunders and the Limits of Revelation

The seven thunders speak, but John is forbidden to record their message (10:4). Catholic theology reads this as affirming that divine revelation, while sufficient for salvation, does not disclose everything. The *Catechism* teaches that "no new public revelation is to be expected before the glorious manifestation of our Lord Jesus Christ" (CCC 66), but acknowledges that the full depth of what has been revealed continues to be explored by the Church through the centuries.

## The Bittersweet Commission

John's eating of the scroll — sweet in the mouth, bitter in the stomach (10:9-10) — echoes Ezekiel's prophetic commission. Catholic interpretation applies this to the prophetic vocation of the entire Church: the word of God is a source of joy and nourishment, but faithfully proclaiming it in a hostile world brings suffering. The Church's prophetic mission, exercised through the Magisterium and the witness of all the baptized, requires both the sweetness of contemplation and the bitterness of counter-cultural testimony.
