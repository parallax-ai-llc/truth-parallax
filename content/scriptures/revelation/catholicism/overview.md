## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Revelation is guided by the Magisterium, the Church Fathers, and the liturgical tradition. The Catholic Church has consistently resisted both literalist apocalypticism and dismissive allegorization, instead reading Revelation as a profoundly liturgical and ecclesial text. The heavenly worship scenes, the role of the saints, and the imagery of the Woman clothed with the sun have all shaped Catholic theology and devotion in distinctive ways.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Foundations
The earliest Catholic commentaries include those of **Victorinus of Pettau** (3rd century), the first known Latin commentary on Revelation, and **St. Jerome**, who revised Victorinus's work. **St. Augustine** interpreted the millennium as the present age of the Church, establishing the amillennial reading that became dominant in Catholic theology.

### Liturgical Reading
Catholic scholars have noted the pervasive liturgical imagery in Revelation: altars, incense, hymns, white robes, the Lamb, and heavenly worship. Scott Hahn's *The Lamb's Supper* argues that Revelation is structured around the Mass, with the heavenly liturgy providing the archetype for earthly worship.

### Marian Typology
The Woman of Revelation 12 has been read as both the Church and the Blessed Virgin Mary. This dual typology informs Marian dogmas and devotional practice, including the iconography of the Immaculate Conception (the woman crowned with stars, standing on the moon).

## Theological Emphases

- **The Church as the New Israel**: Revelation's imagery of the 144,000, the twelve tribes, and the New Jerusalem are read ecclesially
- **The Communion of Saints**: The martyrs under the altar (6:9-11) and the great multitude (7:9-17) reflect Catholic belief in the intercession of the saints
- **Sacramental Vision**: The material elements of Revelation — water, blood, bread, incense — point to the sacramental life
- **Social Teaching**: Babylon's condemnation for economic exploitation (chapter 18) resonates with Catholic social doctrine
