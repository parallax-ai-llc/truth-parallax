## The Two Witnesses

Catholic interpretation of the two witnesses (11:3-12) has varied across the centuries. Patristic writers often identified them as Enoch and Elijah — the two Old Testament figures who did not die — expected to return before the end. The *Catechism* notes the expectation of Elijah's return (CCC 2582). Other Catholic commentators read the witnesses as symbols of the Church's prophetic testimony, with their death and resurrection mirroring the pattern of Christ and the persecuted Church.

## The Measuring of the Temple

The command to measure the temple but not the outer court (11:1-2) is read in Catholic ecclesiology as a symbol of God's preservation of the Church's inner life — her sacramental reality, doctrine, and spiritual core — even when her outward, institutional form is "trampled by the nations." The forty-two months of Gentile domination corresponds to the period of tribulation that the Church endures in every age.

## The Seventh Trumpet and the Ark

The seventh trumpet (11:15-19) announces the kingdom of God and opens the heavenly temple, revealing the Ark of the Covenant (11:19). This detail is profoundly significant for Catholic theology. The original Ark, lost since the Babylonian exile, appears in heaven — and Catholic Marian theology notes that this vision immediately precedes Revelation 12, where the Woman clothed with the sun appears. The theological connection between the Ark (which contained the word of God, the manna, and Aaron's staff) and Mary (who bore the incarnate Word) is a cornerstone of Catholic Marian typology.
