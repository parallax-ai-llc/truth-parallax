## The Vision of the Risen Christ

Revelation opens with John's vision of the glorified Christ walking among seven golden lampstands. Catholic interpretation reads this inaugural vision through the lens of liturgy and ecclesiology. The seven lampstands represent seven churches — and by extension, the universal Church in its local manifestations. Christ's priestly garment (1:13), the golden sash, and his position among the lampstands evoke the high priest moving among the menorah in the temple, a connection that Catholic liturgical theology finds deeply significant.

## Liturgical and Sacramental Reading

The opening doxology (1:5-6) — "To him who loves us and has freed us from our sins by his blood and made us a kingdom, priests to his God and Father" — is read in Catholic tradition as affirming the baptismal priesthood of all believers and the sacrificial character of Christ's redemptive work, made present in the Eucharist. The reference to Christ as "the faithful witness" (*ho martys ho pistos*, 1:5) connects witness with martyrdom, a theme that pervades Catholic hagiography.

## Theological Significance

The self-designation "Alpha and Omega" (1:8) affirms Christ's divinity in terms the Magisterium has consistently upheld. Pope Benedict XVI noted that Revelation's opening chapter establishes the Christological center of all Christian hope: the same Jesus who was crucified is now the Lord of history, present in his Church, and coming again in glory.
