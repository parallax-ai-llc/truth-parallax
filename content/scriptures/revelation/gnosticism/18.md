## The Fall of the Material World

Revelation 18's dirge over Babylon was read by Gnostic interpreters as the lament of those who are invested in the material world — the hylics and psychics who have mistaken the Demiurge's creation for ultimate reality. The merchants, kings, and seafarers who weep over Babylon's fall (18:9-19) represent those whose identity is entirely bound up with the material order. They cannot imagine existence beyond the Demiurge's cosmos and therefore experience its dissolution as catastrophe rather than liberation.

## "Come Out of Her, My People"

The command to "come out of her" (18:4) is the Gnostic call to awakening in its most concentrated form. "Coming out" of Babylon means recognizing the material world for what it is — a prison constructed by the Demiurge — and detaching from it through *gnosis*. This is not physical emigration but spiritual liberation: the pneumatic remains in the material world bodily but is no longer of it spiritually. The Gnostic *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 56) echoes this: "Whoever has come to know the world has found a corpse."

## The Catalog of Goods

The list of Babylon's merchandise (18:12-13), climaxing in "bodies — and human souls," was read by Gnostic interpreters as a catalog of the Demiurge's instruments of entrapment. Each luxury item represents a lure designed to bind the divine spark more tightly to the material world. The inclusion of "human souls" as a commodity reveals the ultimate purpose of the material system: the trafficking of divine sparks, the exploitation of the very substance of the Pleroma for the Demiurge's self-aggrandizement.
