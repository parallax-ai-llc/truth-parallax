## The Victorious on the Sea of Glass

The victorious saints standing on a "sea of glass mingled with fire" (15:2) were read by Gnostic interpreters as pneumatics who have successfully crossed the boundary between the material and spiritual realms. The sea of glass represents the barrier between the archontic cosmos and the Pleroma — crystallized, impassable to the uninitiated, but crossed by those who possess the *gnosis* of the Lamb. The fire mingled with the glass represents the purifying energy that burns away the last vestiges of material attachment.

## The Song of Liberation

The "song of Moses and the song of the Lamb" (15:3) was reinterpreted as the hymn of pneumatic liberation — sung not after deliverance from a physical oppressor (Pharaoh) but after deliverance from the cosmic oppressor (the Demiurge). Several Gnostic texts contain hymns of liberation — the *Hymn of the Pearl* in the *Acts of Thomas* celebrates the soul's return to the Pleroma after its sojourn in the material world. The song's declaration that "all nations will come and worship before you" (15:4) was read as the eventual recognition by all pneumatic beings of the true God beyond the Demiurge.

## The Seven Plagues as Cosmic Dissolution

The seven final plagues (15:6-8) were welcomed in Gnostic reading as the instruments of the material cosmos's dissolution. The temple filled with smoke from God's glory (15:8) — so that no one can enter — represents the momentary inaccessibility of the Pleroma during the violent process of cosmic dismantling. The material world must be fully dissolved before the Pleroma can be fully revealed.
