## The New Jerusalem as the Pleroma

The descent of the New Jerusalem (21:2) was read by Gnostic interpreters as the final revelation of the Pleroma — the divine fullness that has always existed above and beyond the Demiurge's cosmos but is now made manifest after the material world's dissolution. The city is not a renewed material creation (the orthodox reading) but a purely spiritual reality — the true home of the pneumatic souls that had been temporarily exiled in matter.

## The Absence of the Temple

The declaration that "I saw no temple in the city" (21:22) was embraced by Gnostic readers as confirmation that all mediation — material, ritual, institutional — is eliminated in the final state. The temple belongs to the Demiurge's order, where God is accessed only indirectly through sacrifice, priesthood, and sacrament. In the Pleroma, the divine is immediately present — no curtains, no holy of holies, no archontic barriers between the pneumatic and the true God.

## The Bridal Chamber

The New Jerusalem as "a bride adorned for her husband" (21:2) connected to the Valentinian sacrament of the *bridal chamber* (*nymphon*), the highest sacrament in some Gnostic systems. The *Gospel of Philip* describes the bridal chamber as the place where the pneumatic soul is reunited with its heavenly counterpart (*syzygy*) — the Aeon from whom it was separated at the time of Sophia's fall. The descent of the New Jerusalem represents the cosmic bridal chamber: the entire pneumatic community reunited with the Pleroma in an act of restoration that heals the primordial rupture.
