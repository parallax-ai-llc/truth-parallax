## The Locust Plague and Joel

Revelation 9's locust vision is deeply indebted to Joel 1-2, which describes a devastating locust plague as the Day of the Lord. Joel's locusts are compared to a marching army with the appearance of horses (Joel 2:4) — exactly the imagery Revelation develops. Jewish scholars note that Joel itself may be drawing on actual locust swarms as a prophetic symbol, while Revelation transforms the natural phenomenon into a supernatural assault from the abyss.

## Abaddon

The name of the locust king — "Abaddon" (9:11) — is a Hebrew word meaning "destruction" or "place of destruction." In the Hebrew Bible, *Abaddon* appears as a synonym for Sheol, the realm of the dead (Job 26:6, 28:22; Psalm 88:11; Proverbs 15:11, 27:20). Revelation's personification of Abaddon as a demonic king represents a development of the concept found in Second Temple Jewish literature, where the underworld becomes increasingly populated with malevolent spiritual beings.

## The Euphrates and Eastern Threats

The Euphrates River (9:14) serves in Jewish tradition as the boundary of the promised land (Genesis 15:18; Deuteronomy 1:7) and the frontier beyond which threatening empires — Assyria, Babylon, Persia — originated. The release of destructive forces from the Euphrates would have resonated with Jewish historical memory of invasion and exile. Jewish scholars note that this imagery was readily available to a first-century author confronting the power of the Parthian Empire, Rome's rival across the Euphrates.
