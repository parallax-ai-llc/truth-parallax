## The Song of Moses

Revelation 15's "song of Moses, the servant of God" (15:3) explicitly connects this vision to the Song of the Sea (Exodus 15:1-18), sung by Israel after crossing the Red Sea. This song is recited daily in Jewish morning prayers (*Pesukei d'Zimrah*) and holds a central place in the Passover liturgy. Jewish scholars note that Revelation's description of the victorious saints standing on a "sea of glass mingled with fire" directly evokes the Red Sea crossing — the paradigmatic act of divine salvation in Jewish memory.

## The Tabernacle of Testimony

The opening of "the temple of the tabernacle of testimony" (15:5) connects to the wilderness tabernacle (*mishkan ha-edut*) described in Exodus 38:21. The tabernacle housed the Ark of the Covenant, containing the tablets of the testimony — the Decalogue. Jewish scholars observe that Revelation consistently uses temple and tabernacle imagery to frame its visions, reflecting the profound influence of the Israelite cult on early Christian theological imagination.

## Seven Plagues

The seven plagues echo the ten plagues of Egypt, and Jewish tradition extensively developed the plague narrative. The Passover Haggadah attributes the plagues to God's direct action, and the midrashim multiply and elaborate the plague traditions. Revelation's use of this material places its judgment narrative firmly within the Jewish exodus typology — the pattern of divine judgment against the oppressor followed by the liberation of God's people.
