## The Silence and Temple Liturgy

The half-hour of silence in heaven (8:1) has been connected by scholars to the temple liturgy. During the daily *tamid* offering in the Jerusalem Temple, there was a moment of silence while the incense was offered in the Holy Place and the people prayed outside (cf. Luke 1:10). The angel's offering of incense on the golden altar (8:3-4) directly mirrors this temple practice, suggesting that Revelation envisions the heavenly worship as the archetype of the Jerusalem Temple service.

## The Trumpets and the Shofar

The seven trumpets evoke the shofar, the ram's horn that plays a central role in Jewish liturgy — particularly on Rosh Hashanah (the Day of Judgment) and at the conclusion of Yom Kippur. The shofar blast signals divine intervention, the call to repentance, and the inauguration of God's sovereign rule. Jewish scholars note that Revelation's trumpet sequence draws on this rich liturgical symbolism, transforming the shofar's call to repentance into a series of judgment events.

## Egyptian Plague Parallels

The first four trumpets (8:7-12) — hail and fire, sea turned to blood, bitter waters, darkened luminaries — recapitulate the Egyptian plagues of Exodus 7-10. This is a standard feature of Jewish apocalyptic literature, which frequently models future divine intervention on the paradigmatic act of deliverance at the Exodus. The Passover Haggadah's elaboration of the plagues and the principle of *kal va-homer* (lesser to greater) suggest that the final redemption will surpass the original.
