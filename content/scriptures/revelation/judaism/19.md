## The Hallelujah and the Psalms

The fourfold "Hallelujah" of Revelation 19:1-6 is the only occurrence of this Hebrew word in the New Testament. *Hallelu-Yah* ("Praise the Lord") is the characteristic exclamation of the *Hallel* Psalms (Psalms 113-118), which are recited during the major Jewish festivals — Passover, Shavuot, and Sukkot. Jewish scholars note that the doxological language of Revelation 19 is saturated with Psalmic vocabulary, demonstrating the author's deep immersion in the liturgical tradition of Second Temple Judaism.

## The Messianic Warrior

The rider on the white horse (19:11-16) draws on the tradition of the warrior messiah found in Isaiah 63:1-6 (the divine warrior treading the winepress), Psalm 2 (the king who rules the nations with a rod of iron), and the Psalms of Solomon 17 (the Davidic messiah who destroys the wicked). Jewish scholars observe that while mainstream Judaism did not ultimately adopt the christological identification, the imagery itself is thoroughly Jewish. The "sharp sword" from his mouth (19:15) echoes Isaiah 11:4 ("he shall strike the earth with the rod of his mouth") and 49:2 ("he made my mouth like a sharp sword").

## The Marriage Supper

The "marriage supper of the Lamb" (19:9) draws on the Jewish tradition of the messianic banquet — described in Isaiah 25:6-8 as a feast "of rich food, a feast of well-aged wine" that God prepares for all peoples on Mount Zion. The Talmud (Bava Batra 75a) and other rabbinic sources describe the eschatological feast at which the righteous will feast on Leviathan and drink wine preserved since creation.
