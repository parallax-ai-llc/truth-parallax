## The Seven Churches and Jewish-Christian Relations

Revelation 2 is of particular interest to Jewish scholars because of its references to the "synagogue of Satan" (2:9) and the teaching of "Balaam" (2:14). These passages illuminate the fraught relationship between the emerging Christian movement and the Jewish communities of Asia Minor in the late first century.

## The "Synagogue of Satan"

The reference in the Smyrna letter to "those who say they are Jews and are not, but are a synagogue of Satan" (2:9) has been the subject of extensive scholarly analysis. Jewish historians such as Daniel Boyarin argue that this reflects an intra-communal conflict — a dispute between Jewish followers of Jesus and other Jews about who constitutes the true Israel, not an external attack on Judaism as a whole. The language is polemical and reflects the pain of a community experiencing social marginalization, possibly including exclusion from synagogue worship (*birkat ha-minim*).

## Balaam and the Question of Boundaries

The reference to the "teaching of Balaam" in Pergamum (2:14) — eating food sacrificed to idols and sexual immorality — echoes the Numbers 25 narrative and points to the central halakhic question facing the early Jesus movement: how should Gentile believers relate to pagan culture? This was the same question addressed at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15) and debated extensively in Pauline correspondence. Jewish scholars note that the Revelation community's strict position aligns more closely with the James faction than with Pauline accommodation.
