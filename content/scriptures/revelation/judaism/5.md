## The Sealed Scroll and Jewish Messianic Expectation

The vision of the sealed scroll in Revelation 5 draws on Daniel 12:4 ("shut up the words, and seal the book, until the time of the end") and the broader Jewish tradition of sealed prophetic texts that will be opened in the messianic age. Jewish apocalypses such as 1 Enoch and 4 Ezra similarly feature sealed writings that contain the mysteries of divine justice and the eschatological future.

## The Lion of Judah

The title "Lion of the tribe of Judah" (5:5) derives from Jacob's blessing in Genesis 49:9-10 — "Judah is a lion's whelp... the scepter shall not depart from Judah." This messianic interpretation of Genesis 49 was widespread in Second Temple Judaism, as evidenced by the *Testament of Judah* and the Qumran texts. Jewish scholars note that Revelation's transformation of the Lion into a Lamb represents a radical reinterpretation of messianic expectation — the warrior-king becomes a sacrificial victim — that would have been deeply counterintuitive within mainstream Jewish messianism.

## The New Song

The "new song" (5:9) echoes multiple Psalms (33:3, 96:1, 149:1) that celebrate God's new acts of salvation. The declaration that the Lamb has "ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation" (5:9) universalizes the exodus redemption. Jewish scholars note the tension between this universal vision and the particular election of Israel — a tension that was itself debated within Second Temple Judaism, as universalist and particularist strands coexisted in prophetic and apocalyptic literature.
