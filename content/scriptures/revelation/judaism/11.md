## The Two Witnesses and Jewish Eschatological Expectation

The two witnesses of Revelation 11 connect to the Jewish expectation of the return of Moses and Elijah before the messianic age. Malachi 4:5-6 (Hebrew 3:23-24) promises that God will send Elijah "before the great and awesome day of the Lord." The Passover Seder includes a cup for Elijah and the opening of the door in anticipation of his arrival. The witnesses' powers — to shut the sky (1 Kings 17:1), turn water to blood (Exodus 7:17), and strike the earth with plagues — identify them with Elijah and Moses respectively.

## The Temple Measurement

The command to measure the temple (11:1-2) draws on Ezekiel 40-48, where the prophet is given a detailed tour and measurement of the eschatological temple. The Qumran community produced the *Temple Scroll* (11QT), which similarly describes an ideal temple with precise measurements. Jewish scholars note that the instruction to exclude the outer court and the reference to the holy city being trampled by the nations for forty-two months likely reflects the post-70 CE reality of the destroyed Temple and the Jewish-Christian community's theological response to that catastrophe.

## The Seventh Trumpet

The seventh trumpet's announcement — "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord" (11:15) — echoes the Jewish daily prayer *Aleinu*, which anticipates the day when "the Lord shall be king over all the earth; on that day the Lord shall be one and His name one" (Zechariah 14:9). Jewish scholars note that this eschatological vision of universal divine sovereignty is shared between Judaism and early Christianity, even as the two traditions diverge on its christological content.
