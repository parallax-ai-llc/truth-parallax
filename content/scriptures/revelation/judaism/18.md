## The Dirge over Babylon

Revelation 18 is modeled closely on Ezekiel 26-27 (the oracle against Tyre) and Jeremiah 50-51 (the oracle against Babylon). The literary structure — the announcement of doom, the mourning of those who profited from the city, the catalog of luxury goods, and the symbolic act of destruction — follows these prophetic precedents closely. Jewish scholars note that Revelation's author demonstrates a sophisticated command of the Hebrew prophetic tradition, weaving together multiple texts into a sustained composition.

## The Catalog of Goods

The list of Babylon's merchandise (18:12-13) closely parallels Ezekiel 27:12-25's catalog of Tyre's trade goods. The climactic mention of "bodies — and human souls" (18:13) echoes Ezekiel 27:13 ("they traded in human beings") and connects to the Jewish prophetic tradition's consistent condemnation of those who commodify human life. Jewish law extensively regulates and ultimately restricts slavery, and the prophets consistently defend the dignity of the poor and vulnerable against economic exploitation.

## "Come Out of Her"

The command to "come out of her, my people" (18:4) directly quotes Jeremiah 51:45 ("Come out of her, my people! Let every one save his life from the fierce anger of the Lord"). This exodus call — the summons to leave the empire of oppression — is one of the most fundamental patterns in Jewish theology, from Abraham's departure from Ur to the Exodus from Egypt to the return from Babylonian exile. Revelation transposes this pattern into an eschatological key.
