## Babylon in Jewish Memory

Revelation 17's portrait of Babylon draws on centuries of Jewish literary tradition. The original Babylon destroyed Solomon's Temple in 586 BCE and exiled the Jewish elite — a trauma that shaped Jewish theology, liturgy, and literature permanently. Psalm 137 ("By the rivers of Babylon") and the prophetic oracles against Babylon (Isaiah 13-14, 21; Jeremiah 50-51) provide the literary templates that Revelation adapts. After 70 CE, "Babylon" became a Jewish code name for Rome — the new empire that destroyed the Second Temple — as evidenced in the Sibylline Oracles, 4 Ezra, 2 Baruch, and later in rabbinic literature.

## The Prostitute and Prophetic Precedent

The personification of a city as a seductive woman draws on the prophetic tradition of Ezekiel 16 and 23 (Jerusalem as an unfaithful wife), Isaiah 23 (Tyre as a prostitute), and Nahum 3:4 (Nineveh). Jewish scholars note that this is not misogynistic imagery per se but a deployment of the prophetic marriage metaphor: the covenant relationship between God and his people is modeled on marriage, and infidelity to God is figured as sexual betrayal.

## The Seven Hills

The identification of the seven heads as seven mountains (17:9) unmistakably points to Rome, the city built on seven hills (*urbs septicollis*). Jewish readers in the first century would have recognized this immediately, as would readers of other contemporary anti-Roman Jewish texts. The beast-and-prostitute complex represents the Jewish theological critique of Roman imperial power — a critique shared by the emerging Christian community.
