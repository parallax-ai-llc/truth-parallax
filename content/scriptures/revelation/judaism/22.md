## The River and the Tree

Revelation 22's river of the water of life (22:1) draws directly on Ezekiel 47:1-12, where a river flows from the threshold of the eschatological temple, growing deeper as it goes, making the Dead Sea fresh, and supporting trees whose fruit provides food and whose leaves provide healing. Jewish scholars note that Revelation adapts this vision by locating the river's source at the throne of God rather than the temple — consistent with the absence of a temple in the New Jerusalem (21:22).

## The Tree of Life

The tree of life bearing twelve kinds of fruit (22:2) connects to both Ezekiel 47:12 and the tree of life in Genesis 2:9. In Jewish tradition, the tree of life became a symbol of the Torah itself — the *etz chayyim* ("tree of life") is the name given to the wooden rollers of the Torah scroll, and the liturgical prayer declares: "It is a tree of life to those who hold fast to it." Revelation's vision of restored access to the tree of life reverses the exclusion from Eden in Genesis 3:22-24, a motif that Jewish mystical tradition also developed extensively.

## The Healing of the Nations

The leaves of the tree are "for the healing of the nations" (22:2), echoing Ezekiel 47:12 and reflecting the universalist strand of Jewish eschatological hope. Isaiah 2:2-4's vision of all nations streaming to Zion and Zechariah 14:16's expectation that the nations will come to Jerusalem to celebrate the Feast of Tabernacles express the same hope for the eschatological inclusion of the Gentiles. Jewish scholars note that this universalist vision coexists in tension with particularist elements throughout both the Hebrew Bible and Revelation.
