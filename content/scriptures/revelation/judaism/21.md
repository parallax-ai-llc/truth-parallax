## The New Jerusalem and Jewish Restoration Theology

Revelation 21's vision of the New Jerusalem descending from heaven draws extensively on Ezekiel 40-48 (the vision of the new temple and the restored city), Isaiah 60 (the glorified Zion illuminated by God's light), and Isaiah 65:17-25 (the new heavens and new earth). These prophetic texts, composed during and after the Babylonian exile, shaped Jewish hopes for national and cosmic restoration for centuries.

## The Twelve Gates and Twelve Foundations

The twelve gates named for the twelve tribes (21:12) echo Ezekiel 48:30-35, which assigns a gate to each tribe in the restored Jerusalem. Jewish scholars note that Revelation adds twelve foundations named for the twelve apostles (21:14), layering a Christian element onto the Jewish template. This dual structure — tribal gates and apostolic foundations — represents the author's claim that the Christian community constitutes the fulfillment of Israel's restoration hopes.

## The Absence of the Temple

The startling declaration that "I saw no temple in the city" (21:22) stands in sharp contrast to Ezekiel 40-48, which devotes nine chapters to the detailed description of the new temple. Jewish scholars observe that this represents a radical theological move: the temple is no longer needed because God's presence is immediate and universal. While mainstream Jewish theology continued to hope for the rebuilding of the Temple (as expressed in the daily *Amidah*), some Jewish mystical traditions similarly envisioned an eschatological state in which the mediation of the temple would be superseded by direct divine presence.
