## The Throne-Room Vision and Merkavah Mysticism

Revelation 4 presents a vision of the divine throne that is deeply indebted to Ezekiel 1 and the tradition of merkavah (chariot) mysticism that developed in Judaism from the Second Temple period through the rabbinic era. The four living creatures (4:6-8), the throne surrounded by fire and crystal, and the elders prostrating in worship all parallel the merkavah visions in Ezekiel, 1 Enoch, and the later *Hekhalot* literature.

## Connections to Hekhalot Literature

The *Hekhalot* (heavenly palaces) texts, which describe the mystic's ascent through seven heavens to behold the divine throne, share Revelation's basic structure: the seer ascends, encounters angelic beings, beholds the throne, and hears celestial hymns. Jewish scholars such as David Halperin and Rachel Elior have explored the connections between these Jewish mystical traditions and the apocalyptic visions of early Christianity, suggesting a shared matrix of visionary practice.

## The Trisagion

The four creatures' hymn — "Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty" (4:8) — derives from Isaiah 6:3, the *Kedushah* that remains central to Jewish liturgy. In the *Amidah* (standing prayer), the *Kedushah* is recited during the reader's repetition, and the congregation joins the angelic praise. Jewish scholars note that Revelation's appropriation of this quintessentially Jewish liturgical element demonstrates the deep roots of early Christian worship in synagogue practice.
