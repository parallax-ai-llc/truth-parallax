## Gog and Magog

The brief release of Satan and the rebellion of "Gog and Magog" (20:8) derives from Ezekiel 38-39, which prophesies a great eschatological invasion led by Gog from the land of Magog against the restored Israel. In Jewish apocalyptic tradition, the war of Gog and Magog became a standard feature of the messianic scenario — a final battle preceding the establishment of God's kingdom. The Targum to Song of Songs lists the war of Gog and Magog among the events of the messianic era, and the *Amidah*'s blessing for the rebuilding of Jerusalem anticipates the defeat of these forces.

## The Final Judgment

The great white throne judgment (20:11-15) resonates with Jewish traditions about the Day of Judgment (*Yom ha-Din*). The opening of "books" (20:12) corresponds to the Jewish imagery of divine record-keeping, prominently featured in the High Holy Days liturgy: "On Rosh Hashanah it is inscribed, and on Yom Kippur it is sealed — who shall live and who shall die." The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 16b) teaches that three books are opened on Rosh Hashanah: one for the completely righteous, one for the completely wicked, and one for those in between.

## The Book of Life

The "book of life" (20:15) appears in Exodus 32:32-33 (Moses asks God to blot him from the book if Israel is not forgiven) and Psalm 69:28 ("Let them be blotted out of the book of the living"). This concept is central to Jewish liturgy during the High Holy Days, when the greeting *l'shanah tovah tikatevu* ("may you be inscribed for a good year") expresses the hope of being written in the Book of Life.
