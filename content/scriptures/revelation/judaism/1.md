## Jewish Apocalyptic Roots

Revelation 1 is saturated with imagery drawn from the Hebrew Bible. The "Son of Man" figure (1:13) derives directly from Daniel 7:13-14, where "one like a son of man" approaches the Ancient of Days and receives dominion over all nations. Jewish scholars note that this figure was interpreted diversely in Second Temple Judaism — as the angelic leader Michael, as a corporate symbol for Israel, or as an individual messianic figure — before Christian tradition identified him exclusively with Jesus.

## The Menorah and Temple Imagery

The seven golden lampstands (1:12) correspond to the menorah of the Jerusalem Temple, one of the most potent symbols of Jewish worship. The vision of a glorious figure walking among the lampstands echoes the priestly function within the Temple. Jewish scholars observe that Revelation's author, writing in the aftermath of the Temple's destruction (70 CE), transposes Temple imagery into a heavenly key — a strategy also found in contemporary Jewish apocalypses such as 4 Ezra and 2 Baruch, which grappled with the theological crisis of the Temple's loss.

## The Alpha and Omega

The self-designation "the first and the last" (1:17) draws on Isaiah 44:6 and 48:12, where YHWH alone claims this title. Jewish scholars note that the application of this divine attribute to a human figure would have been deeply controversial in a first-century Jewish context, representing one of the key points at which the Revelation community parted ways with the broader Jewish world.
