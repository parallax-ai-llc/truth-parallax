## The 144,000 and the Great Multitude

Revelation 7 provides an interlude between the sixth and seventh seals, presenting two groups: the 144,000 sealed from the twelve tribes of Israel (7:1-8) and the innumerable great multitude from every nation (7:9-17). The relationship between these two groups is one of the most debated questions in Protestant eschatology.

## Dispensationalist and Reformed Readings

Dispensationalist interpreters typically distinguish the two groups sharply: the 144,000 are ethnic Jews who come to faith during the tribulation and serve as evangelists, while the great multitude represents Gentile tribulation converts. Reformed and amillennial interpreters generally identify the two groups as the same reality seen from two perspectives — the church militant (numbered, organized for spiritual battle) and the church triumphant (innumerable, worshipping in heaven). The list of tribes is unusual — omitting Dan and including Manasseh — which interpreters have explained variously as symbolic restructuring, judgment on Dan for idolatry, or simply reflecting first-century variation.

## Theological Significance

The promise that God "will wipe away every tear from their eyes" (7:17) anticipates the climactic vision of Revelation 21:4. Protestant theology sees this passage as a profound assurance of God's care for his persecuted people and a call to endurance. The multitude's cry — "Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!" — affirms the Protestant emphasis on salvation as God's sovereign act, not human achievement.
