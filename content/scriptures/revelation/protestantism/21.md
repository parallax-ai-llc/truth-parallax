## The New Heaven and New Earth

Revelation 21 presents the consummation of God's redemptive plan: a new heaven and a new earth, with the New Jerusalem descending from heaven "prepared as a bride adorned for her husband" (21:2). Protestant interpreters debate whether this represents a completely new creation replacing the old or a renewed and transformed creation — the Greek *kainos* (new in quality) rather than *neos* (new in time) supports the renewal reading. The declaration "Behold, I am making all things new" (21:5) — not "making all new things" — further suggests transformation rather than replacement.

## God Dwelling with Humanity

The central promise — "Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people" (21:3) — fulfills the entire biblical trajectory from Eden to the tabernacle to the temple to the incarnation. Protestant theology sees this as the ultimate realization of the covenant formula. The catalog of things that "will be no more" — death, mourning, crying, pain — represents the complete reversal of the curse of Genesis 3.

## The New Jerusalem

The city's dimensions — a perfect cube of 12,000 stadia (about 1,400 miles) on each side — echo the Holy of Holies in Solomon's temple, which was also a perfect cube. The twelve gates named for the twelve tribes and the twelve foundations named for the twelve apostles unite the Old and New Testament people of God. Protestant commentators emphasize the absence of a temple in the city — "for its temple is the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb" (21:22) — as the fulfillment of all worship: mediation is no longer needed because God's presence is direct and immediate.
