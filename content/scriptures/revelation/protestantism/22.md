## The River of Life and the Tree of Life

Revelation 22 completes the biblical narrative by returning to Eden's imagery — but transformed and glorified. The river of the water of life flows from the throne of God and the Lamb, and the tree of life yields twelve kinds of fruit, with leaves "for the healing of the nations" (22:2). Protestant interpreters see this as the reversal of Genesis 3: the tree of life that was guarded by cherubim is now freely accessible, the curse is lifted, and God's servants see his face. The entire biblical storyline — from garden to city, from exclusion to access — reaches its resolution.

## "Surely I Am Coming Soon"

The closing dialogue between Christ and the church — "Surely I am coming soon" / "Amen. Come, Lord Jesus!" (22:20) — encapsulates the eschatological hope that has sustained Protestant Christianity through centuries of persecution and expectation. The Aramaic *Maranatha* ("Come, O Lord") preserves one of the earliest Christian prayers, connecting Revelation's conclusion to the earliest apostolic worship.

## The Canonical Warning

The warning against adding to or taking from "the words of the prophecy of this book" (22:18-19) has been read by some Protestants as applying to the entire biblical canon, though scholars note it specifically refers to Revelation itself. This passage has nonetheless been influential in Protestant arguments for the closed canon and the sufficiency of Scripture. The book closes with the most elemental Christian hope: the promise of Christ's return and the prayer for his coming — a fitting conclusion to the Protestant reading of Revelation as a book fundamentally about the triumph of the Lamb.
