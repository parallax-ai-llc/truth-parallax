## The Hallelujah Chorus and the Marriage Supper

Revelation 19 opens with heaven's fourfold "Hallelujah" — the only occurrences of this Hebrew word in the New Testament. The heavenly multitude praises God for his righteous judgment of Babylon and announces the "marriage supper of the Lamb" (19:7-9). Protestant interpreters see the marriage metaphor as the culmination of the covenant relationship between Christ and his church, drawing on Ephesians 5:25-32 and the Old Testament image of God as husband of Israel. The bride's fine linen, "bright and pure," represents "the righteous deeds of the saints" (19:8) — a text that Protestant commentators carefully distinguish from works-righteousness, noting that these deeds are themselves gifts of grace.

## The Rider on the White Horse

The climactic vision of Christ as the warrior King (19:11-21) is one of the most powerful christological passages in Scripture. His name is "Faithful and True," "The Word of God," and "King of kings and Lord of lords." Protestant interpreters note that his weapon is a sword proceeding from his mouth (19:15) — the word of God, not a military instrument. This reinforces Revelation's consistent theme that Christ conquers through truth and sacrificial love, not through violence. His robe is "dipped in blood" — likely his own sacrificial blood, not that of his enemies — a reading supported by the allusion to Isaiah 63 and the pattern established by the Lamb imagery.

## The Defeat of the Beast

The chapter concludes with the beast and false prophet captured and thrown into the lake of fire, and their armies destroyed by the sword from Christ's mouth. Protestant theology sees this as the decisive end of all anti-Christian political and religious power — not a military battle but a divine decree that unmasks and destroys pretension.
