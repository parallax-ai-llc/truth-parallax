## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Revelation is shaped by the Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* and has generated more eschatological debate than perhaps any other biblical book. From the Reformers' identification of the papacy with Babylon to modern dispensationalist rapture theology, Revelation has served as a lens through which Protestants read current events, church history, and the future of God's kingdom.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Historicist
The dominant Reformation-era approach. Luther, Calvin, and later commentators read Revelation as a symbolic timeline of church history, with the pope frequently identified as the Antichrist and Babylon as Rome. This view has largely fallen out of academic favor but persists in some traditions.

### Futurist
Dominant in contemporary evangelicalism, especially through dispensationalism. The bulk of Revelation (chapters 4–22) describes events yet to occur: a literal seven-year tribulation, the rapture of the church, the rise of the Antichrist, and a literal thousand-year reign of Christ. Key proponents include J.N. Darby, C.I. Scofield, and the *Left Behind* series.

### Idealist
Revelation presents timeless spiritual truths about the conflict between good and evil, applicable to every era. This view is common among mainline Protestants and some Reformed interpreters.

### Preterist
Most of Revelation was fulfilled in the first century, particularly in the fall of Jerusalem (70 AD) and the Roman Empire. Partial preterism retains a future second coming and final judgment.

## Theological Emphases

- **The Sovereignty of God**: Despite the chaos of history, God remains on the throne (chapters 4–5)
- **Christus Victor**: Christ conquers not through military might but through sacrificial death (the Lamb)
- **Perseverance of the Saints**: The letters to the seven churches call believers to endure
- **Sola Scriptura**: Revelation's self-attestation as prophecy (1:3; 22:18-19) is taken as a model of scriptural authority
