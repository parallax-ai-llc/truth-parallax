## The Millennium

Revelation 20 is the most debated chapter in Protestant eschatology, as it is the only biblical text that explicitly mentions a thousand-year reign of Christ. Three major positions have defined Protestant thought:

| View | Description | Key Proponents |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| Premillennialism | Christ returns before a literal 1,000-year earthly reign | J.N. Darby, George Eldon Ladd |
| Amillennialism | The 1,000 years symbolize the present church age | Augustine, B.B. Warfield, G.K. Beale |
| Postmillennialism | The gospel will progressively Christianize the world before Christ's return | Jonathan Edwards, R.J. Rushdoony |

Dispensational premillennialists further distinguish between the rapture (before a seven-year tribulation) and the second coming (before the millennium), while historic premillennialists place the rapture and second coming together.

## Satan Bound and Released

Satan is bound for a thousand years (20:1-3), then released briefly to deceive the nations one final time before being thrown into the lake of fire (20:7-10). Amillennialists read the binding as Christ's victory at the cross, which limits but does not eliminate Satan's activity during the present age. Premillennialists insist on a future, complete binding. The brief release and final rebellion of Gog and Magog (drawing on Ezekiel 38-39) demonstrates that even after a thousand years, human rebellion persists apart from divine grace.

## The Great White Throne Judgment

The chapter concludes with the final judgment before the great white throne (20:11-15). The dead are judged "according to what they had done, as recorded in the books," and anyone whose name is not found in the book of life is thrown into the lake of fire. Protestant interpreters reconcile judgment "by works" with justification by faith alone by reading the works as evidence of genuine faith, not its ground — the book of life, not the books of deeds, determines eternal destiny.
