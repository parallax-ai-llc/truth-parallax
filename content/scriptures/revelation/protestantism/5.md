## The Scroll and the Lamb

Revelation 5 presents the dramatic scene of the sealed scroll and the Lamb who alone is worthy to open it. This chapter is central to Protestant Christology and soteriology. John weeps because no one is found worthy to open the scroll — interpreted as the scroll of God's redemptive plan, or the title deed to creation. The announcement of the "Lion of the tribe of Judah" who then appears as a "Lamb standing as though slain" (5:5-6) is one of the most theologically significant visual juxtapositions in all of Scripture.

## The Paradox of the Conquering Lamb

Protestant commentators emphasize the radical paradox: the expected conquering Lion is revealed as a sacrificial Lamb. This "rebirth of images" (Austin Farrer's phrase) transforms the concept of divine power. G.K. Beale and other Reformed scholars argue that this establishes the pattern for all of Revelation — victory comes through faithful witness and sacrificial death, not through military conquest. The Lamb's seven horns (complete power) and seven eyes (complete knowledge, identified as the seven spirits of God) affirm his full divine authority.

## The New Song and Universal Worship

The "new song" (5:9-10) celebrates the Lamb's worthiness based on his redemptive work — "you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation." Protestant theology sees this as affirming both particular atonement (the Lamb's deliberate purchase) and its universal scope (every tribe and nation). The chapter climaxes with every creature in heaven, on earth, under the earth, and in the sea offering worship to both the enthroned God and the Lamb — a doxology that affirms Christ's full deity.
