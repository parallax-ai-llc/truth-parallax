## The Great Prostitute and the Beast

Revelation 17 presents the vision of "Babylon the great, mother of prostitutes and of earth's abominations" (17:5), seated on a scarlet beast with seven heads and ten horns. This chapter has been among the most politically charged in Protestant interpretive history. The Reformers uniformly identified the prostitute with the Roman Catholic Church and the beast with the papacy — an interpretation that shaped centuries of Protestant identity and anti-Catholic polemic.

## The Seven Heads and Ten Horns

The angel interprets the seven heads as both seven mountains (evoking Rome's seven hills) and seven kings (17:9-10). Protestant historicists read the kings as successive Roman emperors or world empires. Futurists see a revived Roman Empire or end-times political confederation. The ten horns are ten kings who will give their authority to the beast for "one hour" before turning on the prostitute and destroying her (17:16-17). This self-destructive dynamic — evil ultimately consuming itself — is a key theological point: God uses the internal contradictions of evil systems to accomplish his purposes.

## Theological Significance

Modern Protestant scholarship has increasingly moved away from sectarian identifications, instead reading Babylon as a symbol of all corrupt religio-political systems that seduce through luxury, oppress through violence, and demand ultimate allegiance. The call to "come out of her" (18:4) is read as a summons to holiness and cultural discernment in every age, not merely an anti-Catholic slogan.
