## The Seven Bowls of Wrath

Revelation 16 describes the pouring out of the seven bowls, the most intense and comprehensive judgment sequence in the book. Unlike the seals and trumpets, which affect a third of creation, the bowls affect the whole earth. Protestant interpreters note the deliberate parallels with the Egyptian plagues (sores, water to blood, darkness, frogs), suggesting that God's final judgments recapitulate and intensify the exodus pattern.

## Armageddon

The sixth bowl (16:12-16) dries up the Euphrates River and introduces the gathering at "Armageddon" (*Har Magedon*, the mountain of Megiddo). This is the only occurrence of the word in Scripture. Protestant futurists anticipate a literal military confrontation in the Jezreel Valley of Israel. Idealists read Armageddon as a symbol for the final confrontation between God and all forces of evil, not a geographic prediction. Preterists connect it to the Roman-Jewish war. The word itself may be symbolic — Megiddo was a plain, not a mountain, suggesting a metaphorical or composite reference drawing on Israel's history of decisive battles there (Judges 5; 2 Kings 23).

## The Great Earthquake and Babylon's Fall

The seventh bowl (16:17-21) brings the climactic declaration "It is done!" followed by a catastrophic earthquake and hundred-pound hailstones. "Great Babylon" is remembered before God to receive the cup of his wrath, anticipating the detailed portrait of Babylon's destruction in chapters 17-18. Protestant commentators see this as the decisive moment of divine judgment against all corrupt human systems that oppose God's kingdom.
