## The Angel and the Little Scroll

Revelation 10 provides an interlude between the sixth and seventh trumpets, paralleling the interlude in chapter 7. A mighty angel descends from heaven wrapped in a cloud, with a rainbow above his head, his face like the sun, and his legs like pillars of fire. Protestant interpreters debate whether this is Christ himself (given the solar imagery and divine attributes) or a powerful angel acting as Christ's representative.

## The Seven Thunders Sealed

When the seven thunders speak, John is told to "seal up what the seven thunders have said, and do not write it down" (10:4). This unique moment of concealment within a book of revelation has fascinated Protestant commentators. It serves as a reminder that God's purposes are not exhaustively disclosed — even in a book called "Revelation," there are mysteries that remain hidden. This provides a check against the interpretive overconfidence that has sometimes characterized Protestant apocalyptic speculation.

## The Bittersweet Scroll

John is commanded to take the little scroll from the angel and eat it (10:8-11), echoing Ezekiel 2:8-3:3. The scroll is sweet in the mouth but bitter in the stomach. Protestant interpreters read this as representing the prophetic vocation: the word of God is sweet to receive but its message of judgment is bitter to proclaim. The commission "You must again prophesy about many peoples and nations and languages and kings" (10:11) establishes the universal scope of the remaining prophecy.
