## The Lamb and the 144,000 on Mount Zion

Revelation 14 opens with a contrasting vision to the beasts of chapter 13: the Lamb standing on Mount Zion with the 144,000 who bear his name on their foreheads (rather than the mark of the beast). Protestant interpreters debate whether these are the same 144,000 from chapter 7 or a distinct group. Their description as those "who have not defiled themselves with women" (14:4) has been read literally by some (celibate male evangelists) and figuratively by most (spiritual purity, faithful witness, refusal to participate in idolatrous compromise — "harlotry" being a standard Old Testament metaphor for idolatry).

## The Three Angels' Announcements

Three angels make successive proclamations: the "eternal gospel" calling all people to fear God and worship the Creator (14:6-7); the announcement of Babylon's fall (14:8); and a warning of eternal torment for those who receive the mark of the beast (14:9-11). Protestant interpreters note that the "eternal gospel" here is not a different gospel but the original and enduring call to worship the true God. The warning of torment "forever and ever" (14:11) is a key text in Protestant debates about the nature of hell — whether eternal conscious torment, annihilationism, or conditional immortality is the correct reading.

## The Harvest and the Winepress

The chapter concludes with dual harvest imagery: a grain harvest (14:14-16) and a grape harvest with a winepress of divine wrath (14:17-20). Protestant commentators generally read the grain harvest as the gathering of the redeemed and the grape harvest as judgment on the wicked. The blood flowing from the winepress "as high as a horse's bridle, for 1,600 stadia" (14:20) is typically read as hyperbolic imagery for the completeness of divine judgment.
