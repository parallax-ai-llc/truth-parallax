## Prelude to the Bowl Judgments

Revelation 15 serves as a brief but theologically dense introduction to the seven bowl judgments. The victorious saints stand on a sea of glass mingled with fire, singing "the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb" (15:3). Protestant interpreters see this fusion of Mosaic and christological imagery as affirming the unity of the Old and New Testaments — the same God who delivered Israel through the Red Sea delivers the church through the blood of the Lamb.

## The Song of Moses and the Lamb

The song declares God's works "great and amazing," his ways "just and true," and asks "Who will not fear, O Lord, and glorify your name?" (15:3-4). Protestant commentators note that the song celebrates not God's wrath in isolation but his justice and holiness — the bowl judgments that follow are not arbitrary but righteous. The declaration that "all nations will come and worship before you" (15:4) has been debated: is this a universal promise of eventual conversion or a recognition of God's sovereignty that even his enemies will be compelled to acknowledge?

## The Heavenly Temple Opened

The chapter concludes with the opening of "the temple of the tabernacle of testimony in heaven" (15:5) and seven angels receiving seven golden bowls full of the wrath of God. The temple fills with smoke from God's glory and power, and "no one could enter the temple" until the plagues are completed (15:8). Protestant interpreters read this as indicating that the time for intercession has passed — a solemn moment of irreversible judgment.
