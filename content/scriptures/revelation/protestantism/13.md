## The Two Beasts

Revelation 13 is arguably the most culturally influential chapter in the entire book, introducing the beast from the sea, the beast from the earth (later called the false prophet), and the infamous number 666. Protestant interpretive history is rich and varied on these figures.

## The Beast from the Sea

The beast with seven heads and ten horns (13:1-10) draws on Daniel 7's four beasts, combining their features into a single monstrous entity. Protestant historicists identified the beast with the Roman Empire, the papacy, or successive world empires. Futurists anticipate a literal future world ruler — the Antichrist — who demands universal allegiance. Idealists read the beast as representing all forms of totalitarian political power that oppose God and persecute his people. The beast's fatal wound that was healed (13:3) has been interpreted as a parody of Christ's resurrection.

## The Beast from the Earth and the Mark

The second beast (13:11-18) promotes worship of the first beast and performs deceptive signs. The "mark of the beast" — required for buying and selling — has generated centuries of speculation. The number 666, likely calculated through *gematria* (assigning numerical values to letters), most commonly yields "Nero Caesar" in Hebrew (*Neron Qesar*). Protestant futurists anticipate a literal economic control system; idealists see a symbol of all systems that demand ultimate allegiance to anything other than God. The exhortation "let the one who has understanding calculate" (13:18) invites careful discernment rather than wild speculation.
