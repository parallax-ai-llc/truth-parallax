## The Fall of Babylon

Revelation 18 is a sustained prophetic dirge over Babylon's destruction, modeled on the Old Testament oracles against Tyre (Ezekiel 26-27) and historical Babylon (Isaiah 13-14; Jeremiah 50-51). Protestant interpreters note the distinctly economic character of this judgment: Babylon is condemned not only for idolatry and persecution but for systemic greed, exploitation, and the commodification of human lives ("slaves, that is, human souls" — 18:13).

## The Three Groups of Mourners

Three groups lament Babylon's fall: kings who shared her luxury (18:9-10), merchants who grew rich from her trade (18:11-17), and seafarers whose commerce depended on her (18:17-19). Protestant commentators have applied this critique to every form of economic system that prioritizes profit over human dignity — from the slave trade (which abolitionist Protestants like William Wilberforce connected to this passage) to modern global capitalism. The catalog of luxury goods (18:12-13) culminates shockingly in "bodies — and human souls," exposing the dehumanizing logic of unbridled commerce.

## Rejoicing in Heaven

In contrast to the earthly mourning, heaven rejoices: "Rejoice over her, O heaven, and you saints and apostles and prophets, for God has given judgment for you against her" (18:20). A mighty angel hurls a millstone into the sea, declaring Babylon will be found no more. Protestant theology reads this as the vindication of the martyrs' prayer in 6:10 — divine justice has been served. The silence that descends on Babylon — no more music, no more craftsmen, no more light, no more marriage celebrations — is a devastating portrait of cultural annihilation.
