## The Risen Christ Among the Lampstands

Revelation opens with John's account of his prophetic commission while exiled on the island of Patmos. Protestant interpreters emphasize that this is a revelation *of* Jesus Christ, not merely *about* future events — the person of Christ is the hermeneutical key to the entire book. The title "Alpha and Omega" (1:8) establishes Christ's full deity, a point stressed by Reformed and evangelical commentators against subordinationist readings.

## The Seven Churches and Lampstands

The vision of the risen Christ walking among seven golden lampstands (1:12-16) introduces the letters to the seven churches of Asia Minor (chapters 2-3). Protestant commentators debate whether these churches represent historical congregations only, successive ages of church history (the historicist view), or perennial spiritual conditions applicable to all churches in every era. The description of Christ — white hair, eyes like fire, a sharp two-edged sword from his mouth — draws on Daniel 7:9-14 and establishes Christ as both judge and redeemer.

## Theological Significance

The opening chapter establishes the prophetic authority of the book. The blessing on those who read, hear, and keep its words (1:3) is the first of seven beatitudes in Revelation. Protestant theology takes this as affirming Revelation's full canonical authority, despite Luther's early doubts and Calvin's decision not to write a commentary on it. The Trinitarian greeting (1:4-5) — from the Father, the seven spirits, and Jesus Christ — frames the entire book within orthodox Trinitarian theology.
