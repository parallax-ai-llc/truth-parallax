## The Two Witnesses

Revelation 11 introduces two of the most debated figures in Protestant eschatology: the two witnesses who prophesy for 1,260 days, are killed by the beast, lie dead in the streets for three and a half days, and are then resurrected and taken up to heaven. Protestant interpreters have proposed various identifications: Moses and Elijah (based on the plagues and fire imagery), Enoch and Elijah (the two who did not die), Peter and Paul, or symbolic representations of the Law and the Prophets, the Old and New Testaments, or the witnessing church itself.

## The Measuring of the Temple

John is told to measure the temple but leave out the outer court, "for it is given over to the nations, and they will trample the holy city for forty-two months" (11:1-2). Dispensationalists read this as a literal rebuilt temple in Jerusalem during the tribulation. Reformed interpreters see the temple as a symbol of the church — the measured inner court represents God's spiritual protection of his people, while the outer court's exposure to the nations signifies the church's outward vulnerability to persecution.

## The Seventh Trumpet

The seventh trumpet (11:15-19) announces: "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, and he shall reign forever and ever." This climactic declaration is one of the most celebrated texts in Protestant theology, famously set to music in Handel's *Messiah*. Protestant interpreters see it as either a proleptic announcement of what will be fully realized at Christ's return or as a present spiritual reality already operative through the church's proclamation.
