## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church's relationship with Revelation is distinctive among Christian traditions. While the book is accepted as canonical, it is the only New Testament book not read in the Orthodox liturgical lectionary. This liturgical absence reflects a longstanding patristic caution about the text's symbolic complexity and its potential for misuse by apocalyptic movements. Orthodox interpretation emphasizes the theological content — the sovereignty of God, the victory of the Lamb, the transfiguration of creation — while resisting speculative timelines and date-setting.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Patristic Reserve
The Orthodox tradition inherited hesitation about Revelation from several Eastern Fathers. Eusebius of Caesarea reported doubts about its authorship, and it was slow to gain acceptance in the Eastern canon. **St. Andrew of Caesarea** (6th–7th century) produced the definitive Orthodox commentary, reading the text as a spiritual allegory of the Church's ongoing struggle and ultimate triumph.

### Liturgical Absence, Theological Presence
Although not read at the Divine Liturgy, Revelation's imagery pervades Orthodox hymnography and iconography. The Paschal troparion ("Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death") echoes Revelation's vision of the Lamb's victory. The iconography of the *Pantocrator* (Christ the Almighty) draws on Revelation's throne-room vision.

### Eschatological Reserve
Orthodox theology resists detailed eschatological speculation. The *eschaton* is understood as already breaking into the present through the liturgy and sacraments, not as a program of future events to be decoded.

## Theological Emphases

- **Theosis**: The New Jerusalem (chapters 21–22) represents the ultimate deification of humanity and creation
- **Christus Victor**: Christ conquers death and the devil — the central Paschal theme
- **Cosmic Transfiguration**: All creation is destined for renewal, not destruction
- **The Saints and Martyrs**: The "great cloud of witnesses" in Revelation affirms the Orthodox veneration of saints
