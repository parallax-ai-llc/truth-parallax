## The Opening of the Seals

Orthodox interpretation of the seal judgments in Revelation 6 reflects the tradition's characteristic eschatological reserve. St. Andrew of Caesarea reads the four horsemen not as specific future events but as manifestations of the consequences of human sin throughout history — conquest, war, famine, and death are the perennial fruits of humanity's rebellion against God. This reading resists the temptation to map the horsemen onto particular historical events or political figures.

## The Martyrs Under the Altar

The fifth seal (6:9-11) reveals the souls of the martyrs crying for justice. This vision is profoundly significant for the Orthodox Church, which was shaped by centuries of martyrdom under Roman persecution, Islamic rule, and communist oppression. The veneration of martyrs — their icons, relics, and feast days — finds scriptural foundation in this passage. The Orthodox funeral service and memorial prayers (*mnimosyna*) affirm the conscious existence of the departed and the efficacy of prayer for the dead and by the dead.

## The Sixth Seal

The cosmic upheaval of the sixth seal (6:12-17) draws on prophetic imagery from Isaiah, Joel, and Amos. Orthodox interpretation, following St. Andrew, reads these phenomena as both symbolic of political collapse and indicative of the cosmic dimension of divine judgment. The created order itself responds to human sin and divine justice. The question "Who can stand?" (6:17) is answered in chapter 7 with those who have been sealed — a response that Orthodox theology connects to baptismal chrismation, the seal of the Holy Spirit.
