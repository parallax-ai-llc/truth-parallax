## The New Heaven and New Earth

Revelation 21 presents the consummation of the Orthodox vision of *theosis* — the deification of humanity and the transfiguration of all creation. The new heaven and new earth are not a replacement of the old but its transformation — the fulfillment of the promise anticipated in Christ's Transfiguration on Mount Tabor, where his humanity shone with uncreated light. The Greek word *kainos* (new in quality, renewed) rather than *neos* (new in time, different) supports the Orthodox understanding that creation is destined for glorification, not annihilation.

## The Dwelling of God with Humanity

The declaration "Behold, the dwelling of God is with man" (21:3) fulfills the entire trajectory of salvation history as Orthodox theology understands it: from the divine *kenosis* (self-emptying) in the incarnation to the ultimate *theosis* of humanity and cosmos. The absence of a temple (21:22) signifies that all mediation — icons, sacraments, liturgy — will give way to the direct, unmediated experience of God. The city illuminated by divine glory (21:23) echoes the uncreated light of Tabor and the theology of St. Gregory Palamas.

## The Open Gates

The New Jerusalem's gates that are never shut (21:25) and the nations walking by its light (21:24) express the radical openness of the eschatological community. Orthodox theology sees in this vision the ultimate fulfillment of the Church's universal mission — not the exclusion of the many but the ingathering of all peoples into the divine life. The tears wiped away, the death abolished, the mourning and pain eliminated (21:4) — this is the Orthodox hope of *apokatastasis*, the restoration of all things in Christ.
