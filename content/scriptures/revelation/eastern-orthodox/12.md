## The Woman and the Dragon

Revelation 12 is interpreted in Orthodox theology through a multi-layered typology. The Woman clothed with the sun represents both the Church and the Theotokos (Mother of God). Orthodox iconography frequently depicts the Theotokos with cosmic imagery drawn from this chapter — surrounded by light, crowned with stars, standing on the crescent moon. The Akathist Hymn to the Theotokos celebrates Mary as the one through whom "the light that never sets has shone upon the world," echoing the radiant imagery of Revelation 12.

## The Great Red Dragon

The dragon identified as "the ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan" (12:9) connects Revelation to Genesis 3 and the Orthodox understanding of the cosmic scope of evil. The dragon's attempt to devour the male child (Christ) at birth connects to the Nativity narrative and the flight to Egypt. Orthodox theology reads the entire drama of Revelation 12 as a cosmic retelling of the salvation narrative — from Christ's incarnation through his ascension, the Church's persecution, and her ultimate deliverance.

## War in Heaven

The war between Michael and the dragon (12:7-9) is commemorated in the Orthodox Church on the Feast of the Archangels (November 8). The defeat of Satan "by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony" (12:11) establishes the Paschal pattern that defines Orthodox spirituality: victory comes through suffering, life through death, exaltation through humility. The woman's flight to the wilderness (12:14) represents the Church in her earthly sojourn — sustained by God but not yet fully glorified.
