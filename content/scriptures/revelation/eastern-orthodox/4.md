## The Heavenly Throne Room

Revelation 4 presents the vision of God's throne that lies at the theological heart of Revelation. Orthodox interpretation, shaped by the theology of divine energies articulated by St. Gregory Palamas, reads this chapter as a disclosure of God's uncreated glory — not the divine essence (which remains unknowable), but the energies through which God makes himself present to creation. The rainbow around the throne (4:3), the lightning, and the sea of glass all manifest divine glory in ways accessible to creaturely perception.

## The Trisagion and Orthodox Worship

The four living creatures' ceaseless hymn — "Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty" (4:8) — is the *Trisagion* that forms the heart of Orthodox worship. The Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom includes the *Thrice-Holy Hymn* ("Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal, have mercy on us"), and the *Sanctus* in the Eucharistic canon directly echoes this heavenly worship. Orthodox theology holds that when the Church celebrates the Divine Liturgy, the veil between heaven and earth thins, and the congregation participates in the very worship described here.

## The Twenty-Four Elders

St. Andrew of Caesarea identifies the twenty-four elders as representatives of the totality of the redeemed — the saints of both the Old and New Covenants united in worship before God's throne. Their casting of crowns (4:10) represents the offering of all human achievement back to God, the source of every good gift. This act of worship is the archetype of the offering made at every Divine Liturgy, where the gifts of bread and wine — representing all of human labor and creation — are offered to God and returned transformed.
