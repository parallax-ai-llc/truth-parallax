## The Two Witnesses

Orthodox interpretation of the two witnesses (11:3-12) has historically favored the identification with Enoch and Elijah — the two Old Testament figures taken up to heaven without dying — who are expected to return before the end. St. Andrew of Caesarea discusses both this identification and the broader symbolic reading (the witnesses as the Law and the Prophets, or as the witnessing Church). The witnesses' power to shut the sky and turn water to blood connects them to Elijah and Moses, and their death and resurrection after three and a half days mirrors the pattern of Christ.

## The Measuring of the Temple

The command to measure the temple while leaving the outer court to the nations (11:1-2) is read in Orthodox ecclesiology as a symbol of God's preservation of the Church's essential identity — her faith, sacraments, and apostolic continuity — even during periods of external persecution and compromise. The forty-two months of Gentile domination corresponds to the period of tribulation, which Orthodox theology interprets as a present and ongoing reality rather than a single future period.

## The Seventh Trumpet

The seventh trumpet's proclamation — "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ" (11:15) — expresses the eschatological hope at the heart of Orthodox faith. This kingdom is already present in the Church's liturgical life, where heaven and earth intersect, and will be fully realized at Christ's second coming. The opening of the heavenly temple and the appearance of the Ark of the Covenant (11:19) connect the Christian mystery to the Mosaic covenant, affirming the continuity of God's saving plan.
