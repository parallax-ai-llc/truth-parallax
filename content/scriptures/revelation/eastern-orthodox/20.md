## The Millennium in Orthodox Theology

Orthodox theology follows the Augustinian interpretation of the millennium: the thousand years represent the present age of the Church, the period between Christ's first and second comings. The Second Ecumenical Council (Constantinople, 381) effectively ended chiliasm (literal millennialism) as a respectable theological position in the East, and the Nicene Creed's affirmation that Christ "will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead, whose kingdom shall have no end" points to an eternal kingdom, not a temporary millennial reign.

## Satan Bound

The binding of Satan (20:1-3) is interpreted as the restriction of Satan's power achieved through Christ's death, resurrection, and ascension. Orthodox theology does not teach that Satan is inactive during the present age — spiritual warfare is a constant reality — but that his ability to "deceive the nations" on a universal scale is restrained by the proclamation of the gospel and the power of the sacraments. His brief release at the end (20:7-10) represents a final intensification of evil before the parousia.

## The Great White Throne

The final judgment (20:11-15) is depicted in the Orthodox iconographic tradition in the icon of the Last Judgment, which occupies the western wall of many Orthodox churches. The faithful see this icon as they exit the church, reminding them that they go out into the world under the judgment and mercy of God. Orthodox eschatology holds together the reality of judgment with the hope of divine mercy — a tension maintained by the paradoxical witness of the Fathers, who warned of hell while hoping for universal restoration.
