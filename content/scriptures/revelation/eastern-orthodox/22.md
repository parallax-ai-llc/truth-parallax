## The River and the Tree of Life

Revelation 22 completes the scriptural narrative with the vision of the river of the water of life flowing from the throne of God and the Lamb, and the tree of life bearing twelve fruits with leaves "for the healing of the nations" (22:2). Orthodox theology reads this as the eschatological fulfillment of baptism (the water of life), the Eucharist (the fruit of the tree), and the Church's ministry of healing. The lifting of the curse (22:3) reverses the consequences of the Fall described in Genesis 3, completing the cosmic drama.

## "They Will See His Face"

The promise "they will see his face, and his name will be on their foreheads" (22:4) is the ultimate expression of *theosis* — the direct vision of God that the Fathers describe as the purpose of human existence. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that in the age to come, humanity will be united to God by grace in a manner analogous to the hypostatic union of divine and human natures in Christ. This vision of God is not a static contemplation but an eternal, ever-deepening participation in the inexhaustible divine life.

## "Come, Lord Jesus"

The closing prayer — "Amen. Come, Lord Jesus!" (22:20) — preserves the Aramaic *Maranatha* of the earliest Christian communities. In Orthodox worship, every celebration of the Divine Liturgy is an anticipation of and prayer for the parousia. The Paschal greeting — "Christ is risen! / Truly he is risen!" — is the Church's answer to this prayer, affirming that the Lord who is to come has already come, has already conquered, and will come again to make all things new.
