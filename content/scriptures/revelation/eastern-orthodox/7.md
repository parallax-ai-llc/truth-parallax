## The Sealed and the Triumphant

Revelation 7's two visions — the sealing of the 144,000 and the worship of the great multitude — are read in Orthodox theology as complementary images of the one Church in her earthly struggle and heavenly glory. The sealing (7:3) corresponds to the sacramental seal of chrismation (*myron*), by which the baptized receive the "seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit" and are marked as belonging to God.

## The Great Multitude

The great multitude "from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and languages" (7:9) represents the universal scope of salvation that the Orthodox Church proclaims. Their white robes, washed in the blood of the Lamb (7:14), signify both baptismal purity and the purification wrought by martyrdom and suffering. The palm branches evoke the Feast of Tabernacles — the great pilgrimage feast of ingathering — and anticipate the eschatological harvest of all peoples into the Kingdom.

## Tears Wiped Away

The promise that "God will wipe away every tear from their eyes" (7:17) is among the most consoling passages in Scripture and features in Orthodox funeral and memorial services. Orthodox eschatological hope is not primarily about predicting future events but about trusting in God's ultimate restoration of all things — the *apokatastasis* that some Fathers (notably St. Gregory of Nyssa) dared to hope might encompass all creation.
