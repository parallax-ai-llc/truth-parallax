## The Great Prostitute

Orthodox interpretation of Revelation 17 reads Babylon the Great as a symbol of worldly power that seduces through luxury and oppresses through violence. St. Andrew of Caesarea identifies the original referent as imperial Rome — the persecutor of the early Church — while extending the symbol to all subsequent empires and civilizations that demand ultimate allegiance. Orthodox Christianity's experience under successive empires — Roman, Byzantine (when the Church was tempted by imperial co-optation), Ottoman, and Soviet — provides a rich context for reading this chapter.

## The Scarlet Beast

The beast with seven heads and ten horns represents the totalitarian political power that carries the prostitute. Orthodox theology notes the symbiotic and ultimately self-destructive relationship between worldly power and corrupt religious or ideological systems: the beast eventually turns on the prostitute and destroys her (17:16). This pattern — the alliance of convenience between political and ideological power that eventually collapses — has been observed repeatedly in Orthodox historical experience.

## Theological Significance

The angel's interpretation (17:7-18) invites discernment, not speculation. Orthodox theology reads this chapter as a call to recognize and resist the seductions of worldly power in every age. The Church's prophetic vocation is to stand apart from the prostitute's allure — to be in the world but not of it — maintaining the eschatological horizon that relativizes all earthly kingdoms.
