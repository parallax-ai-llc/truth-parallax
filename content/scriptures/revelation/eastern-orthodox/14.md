## The Lamb on Mount Zion

Revelation 14 opens with the consoling vision of the Lamb and the 144,000 on Mount Zion — a deliberate contrast to the beasts of chapter 13. Orthodox interpretation reads the 144,000 as the company of the redeemed who have maintained their spiritual integrity. Their description as "virgins" (14:4) is understood both literally — honoring monasticism and the consecrated life — and spiritually, as those who have preserved their baptismal purity and refused spiritual adultery with the beast.

## The Three Angelic Proclamations

The first angel's proclamation of "an eternal gospel" (14:6-7) — calling all people to "fear God and give him glory" — affirms the universal scope of divine revelation. The second angel's announcement of Babylon's fall (14:8) anticipates the detailed judgment of chapters 17-18. The third angel's warning of torment for beast-worshippers (14:9-11) raises the question of eternal punishment. Orthodox theology maintains a creative tension between the affirmation of eternal consequences and the hope, expressed by Fathers such as St. Isaac the Syrian and St. Gregory of Nyssa, that God's love ultimately prevails.

## The Harvest

The grain and grape harvests (14:14-20) represent the final separation of the righteous and the wicked. Orthodox eschatology holds that this separation, while definitive at the last judgment, is already being worked out in the choices of every human life. The "Son of Man" seated on the cloud (14:14) is Christ at the parousia, a figure depicted in the Orthodox icon of the Last Judgment (*Deisis*) that adorns the western wall of many Orthodox churches.
