## The Fall of Babylon

Revelation 18 presents a sustained prophetic lament over Babylon's destruction, modeled on the Old Testament oracles against Tyre and Babylon. Orthodox interpretation reads this chapter as a warning about the spiritual danger of wealth, luxury, and the commodification of human life. The monastic tradition — with its vows of poverty, obedience, and chastity — represents the most radical Orthodox response to the allure of Babylonian values.

## "Come Out of Her, My People"

The call to "come out of her" (18:4) has been applied in Orthodox spiritual theology to the inner *exodus* from attachment to worldly goods and values. The Desert Fathers' withdrawal to the wilderness, the hesychast tradition of inner stillness, and the ongoing practice of fasting and asceticism all represent forms of this spiritual departure. The faithful remain physically in the world but spiritually detached from its distorted values.

## Justice and the Saints

The heavenly rejoicing over Babylon's fall (18:20) and the martyrs' vindication affirm that justice is integral to God's nature. Orthodox theology holds together divine mercy and divine justice, refusing to reduce God's character to either alone. The silence that falls over Babylon — no more music, light, or human community (18:22-23) — represents the terrible consequence of a civilization built on exploitation: not transformation but dissolution.
