## The Vision of the Glorified Christ

Orthodox interpretation of Revelation 1 is shaped primarily by St. Andrew of Caesarea's commentary, the definitive Orthodox treatment of the Apocalypse. The vision of the risen Christ among the seven lampstands is read as a theophany — a manifestation of divine glory that connects to the Old Testament theophanies of Sinai, the burning bush, and Ezekiel's chariot vision. The description of Christ with white hair, eyes like fire, and a voice like many waters draws on Daniel 7:9-14 and establishes Christ as the *Pantocrator* — the Almighty who holds all things in his hands.

## Liturgical Absence, Theological Presence

Although Revelation is not read in the Orthodox liturgical lectionary, the imagery of chapter 1 pervades Orthodox iconography and hymnography. The icon of Christ Pantocrator — depicting Christ with a book in one hand and the other raised in blessing — draws on this chapter's portrait of the glorified Lord. The seven stars in his right hand (1:16) and the seven lampstands are frequently represented in church frescoes and manuscript illumination.

## Patristic Interpretation

St. Andrew of Caesarea interprets the seven lampstands as the seven churches and, by extension, the fullness of the Church in every age. The command "Write what you see" (1:19) establishes the prophetic authority of the text, though Orthodox tradition reads Revelation with greater caution than other biblical books, emphasizing its symbolic rather than predictive character. The title "Alpha and Omega" affirms Christ's co-eternity with the Father — a truth proclaimed in the Nicene Creed and defended by the Cappadocian Fathers.
