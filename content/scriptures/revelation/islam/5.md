## The Lamb and Islamic Christology

Revelation 5 presents the theological claim most fundamentally incompatible with Islam: a sacrificial figure (the Lamb) who shares the divine throne and receives the worship due to God alone. The elders and living creatures fall before the Lamb (5:8), and "every creature" offers praise "to him who sits on the throne and to the Lamb" (5:13). Islamic theology categorically rejects this as *shirk* — the association of a partner with God in worship.

## The Crucifixion Question

The Lamb "standing as though slain" (5:6) presupposes the crucifixion of Jesus, which the Quran explicitly denies: "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but it was made to appear so to them" (4:157). Islamic theology holds that God rescued Isa from the cross — he was raised to heaven alive. The entire theological edifice of Revelation 5 — the atoning sacrifice, the worthiness earned through death, the opening of history's sealed purposes through blood — is therefore built on a premise that Islam rejects.

## Mediation and Direct Access

Revelation 5's theology of mediation — only the Lamb can open the scroll, only through his blood are people ransomed — contrasts with the Islamic emphasis on direct access to God without intermediary. While Islam recognizes prophetic intercession (*shafa'ah*) on the Day of Judgment, this is intercessory prayer by a servant to his Lord, not the sovereign action of a divine figure who shares God's throne. The Quran reminds: "To Allah belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth. And sufficient is Allah as Disposer of affairs" (4:132).
