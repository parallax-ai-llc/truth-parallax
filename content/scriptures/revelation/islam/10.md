## The Angel and Prophetic Revelation

Revelation 10's vision of a mighty angel descending with a scroll corresponds broadly to the Islamic concept of angelic mediation of divine revelation (*wahy*). In Islam, Jibril (Gabriel) served as the primary intermediary between Allah and the Prophet Muhammad, transmitting the Quran over twenty-three years. The scroll that John is commanded to eat and internalize echoes the Islamic understanding that revelation transforms the prophet who receives it — the Quran describes its own descent into Muhammad's heart (26:193-194).

## Sealed Knowledge and *Ilm al-Ghayb*

The sealing of the seven thunders' message (10:4) connects to the Islamic concept of *ilm al-ghayb* — knowledge of the unseen, which belongs exclusively to God. The Quran states: "And with Him are the keys of the unseen; none knows them except Him" (6:59). Islamic theology is suspicious of any claim to comprehensive eschatological knowledge; the Quran reminds that even the Prophet does not know when the Hour will come (7:187). The sealed thunders, from an Islamic perspective, rightly acknowledge the limits of prophetic disclosure.

## Sweet and Bitter

The bittersweet scroll (10:9-10) resonates with the Islamic understanding of prophethood as a calling that brings both spiritual joy and earthly suffering. The Prophet Muhammad's mission brought the sweetness of divine intimacy and the bitterness of persecution, exile, and warfare. The Quran acknowledges this dual reality: "We know that your breast is constrained by what they say" (15:97).
