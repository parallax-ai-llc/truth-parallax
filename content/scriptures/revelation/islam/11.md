## The Two Witnesses and Islamic Prophetology

The two witnesses of Revelation 11 — who prophesy, perform miracles, are killed, and rise from the dead — have been compared by some scholars to the Islamic eschatological expectation of the return of Isa (Jesus) and the appearance of the Mahdi. Islamic hadith literature describes Isa descending near a white minaret in Damascus, breaking the cross, killing the swine, and establishing justice. The Mahdi, while not mentioned in the Quran, appears prominently in hadith as a righteous leader who fills the earth with justice before the Day of Judgment.

## Points of Convergence and Divergence

Both Revelation 11 and Islamic eschatology envision prophetic witnesses who confront the forces of evil in the last days, suffer opposition, and are ultimately vindicated by God. However, Islam rejects the notion that Isa was killed and resurrected — a pattern central to the two witnesses' narrative. In Islamic teaching, Isa was raised to heaven alive (Quran 4:158) and will return in the end times, but as a servant of God, not as a divine figure exercising sovereign authority.

## The Kingdom of God

The seventh trumpet's announcement — "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord" (11:15) — resonates with the Islamic belief in God's absolute sovereignty (*hakimiyyah*) over all creation. The Quran declares: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth" (3:189). However, Islam sees this sovereignty as eternally established, not as something that must be wrested from opposing powers through eschatological struggle. God's kingdom is not "becoming" — it simply is, and the Day of Judgment reveals what has always been true.
