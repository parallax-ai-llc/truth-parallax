## The Lamb on Mount Zion

Revelation 14's vision of the Lamb on Mount Zion with the 144,000 raises the same christological objections from an Islamic perspective as chapter 5: a human figure occupying a position of divine authority and receiving devotion. The Islamic understanding of Isa's role in the end times places him as a servant and prophet who submits to Allah, not as a sovereign figure who commands the allegiance of the redeemed.

## The Eternal Gospel and the Universal Call

The angel's proclamation of "an eternal gospel" — "Fear God and give him glory, for the hour of his judgment has come, and worship him who made heaven and earth" (14:6-7) — is remarkably consonant with the Islamic message. This basic call to monotheism — fear God, glorify God, worship the Creator — is the essence of the Quranic *da'wah* (invitation). Muslim scholars have noted that this "eternal gospel," stripped of its christological context, is essentially the message of every prophet from Adam to Muhammad: worship the one God alone.

## The Harvest and the Day of Judgment

The grain and grape harvests (14:14-20) as images of final judgment parallel the Quranic Day of Judgment, where humanity is separated into the people of the right hand (righteous) and the people of the left hand (wicked): "Then as for he who is given his record in his right hand, he will be judged with an easy account. But as for he who is given his record behind his back, he will cry out for destruction" (84:7-11). Both traditions affirm a definitive divine separation of the just and the unjust.
