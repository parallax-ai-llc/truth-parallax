## The New Jerusalem and Jannah

Revelation 21's vision of the New Jerusalem — a city of gold and precious stones, illuminated by divine glory, free from death and suffering — invites comparison with the Quranic descriptions of Jannah (Paradise). The Quran describes gardens with flowing rivers (47:15), mansions (39:20), and "what no eye has seen, no ear has heard, and what has never crossed the human mind" (hadith, Sahih Muslim). Both traditions envision the eschatological reward as a realm of unimaginable beauty and joy.

## Key Differences

Several fundamental differences emerge. The New Jerusalem descends to a renewed earth, suggesting the redemption of the material world; Islamic eschatology envisions Jannah as a separate, already-existing realm that the righteous enter after judgment. Revelation's promise "the dwelling of God is with man" (21:3) implies a direct, unmediated divine presence that transcends anything in Islamic descriptions of paradise. While the Quran does promise the beatific vision — "faces that Day will be radiant, looking at their Lord" (75:22-23) — the nature of this vision is debated among Islamic theologians, with the Ash'ari school affirming it and the Mu'tazili school denying it.

## No Temple

Revelation's declaration that there is "no temple" in the New Jerusalem (21:22) resonates with an aspect of Islamic theology: in Islam, the entire earth is a mosque (*masjid*), and the physical structures of worship — while important — are not ultimately necessary for communion with God. The eschatological removal of mediating structures in favor of direct divine presence is a vision that, at its deepest level, both traditions share.
