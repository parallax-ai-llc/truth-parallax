## Islamic Assessment of Revelation 1

Revelation 1 presents the glorified Jesus Christ in language of divine majesty — "the Alpha and the Omega" (1:8), the "Son of Man" with eyes like fire and a voice like many waters. From an Islamic perspective, this chapter epitomizes the fundamental theological divergence between Christianity and Islam: the elevation of a human prophet to divine status. Islam honors Isa ibn Maryam (Jesus son of Mary) as one of the greatest prophets (*ulu al-azm*), born of a virgin, worker of miracles by God's permission, and the Messiah — but categorically rejects his divinity.

## Tawhid and the Self-Designation "Alpha and Omega"

The claim "I am the Alpha and the Omega" (1:8) attributes to Jesus what the Quran reserves exclusively for Allah: absolute priority and finality. The Quran declares, "He is the First and the Last, the Manifest and the Hidden, and He has knowledge of all things" (57:3). Islamic theology (*kalam*) regards the application of such divine attributes to any creature — however exalted — as *shirk* (associating partners with God), the gravest sin in Islam.

## The Prophetic Vision

Islamic tradition does affirm the reality of prophetic visions. Muhammad's Night Journey (*Isra' wa Mi'raj*) describes an ascent through the heavens and an encounter with divine majesty. However, in the Islamic account, the Prophet remains entirely human throughout — he sees signs of God's greatness but is never confused with God himself. The Islamic reading of Revelation 1 thus acknowledges the visionary genre while rejecting its christological content.
