## The Woman and the Dragon

Revelation 12's cosmic drama of the Woman, the dragon, and the male child invites comparison with several Islamic themes. The great red dragon, identified as "the ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan" (12:9), corresponds to Iblis in Islamic theology — the jinn who refused to bow to Adam and was cast down, vowing to lead humanity astray (Quran 7:11-18, 15:28-42). Both traditions affirm a cosmic adversary who opposes God's purposes and persecutes the faithful.

## Maryam in Islamic Tradition

While Christian tradition identifies the Woman with Mary (among other interpretations), Islam holds Maryam (Mary) in the highest esteem — she is the only woman mentioned by name in the Quran, and an entire surah (19, Maryam) is dedicated to her. The Quran describes her as chosen and purified above all women (3:42) and recounts the miraculous birth of Isa. However, Islam does not ascribe to Maryam the cosmic role that Revelation 12 envisions for the Woman — clothed with the sun, crowned with stars, giving birth to the ruler of the nations.

## The Casting Down of Iblis

The war in heaven and Satan's expulsion (12:7-9) parallels the Quranic account of Iblis's expulsion from the divine presence after his refusal to prostrate before Adam (Quran 7:13, 15:34-35, 38:77). In both accounts, the adversary is cast down and subsequently directs his malice against humanity. However, in Islamic theology, Iblis is a jinn (not a fallen angel — angels in Islam have no free will to rebel), and his opposition is directed against all of humanity, not specifically against a messianic community.
