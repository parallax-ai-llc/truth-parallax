## The Fall of Babylon and Quranic Warnings

Revelation 18's detailed account of Babylon's economic corruption and catastrophic fall parallels the Quranic narratives of destroyed civilizations. The Quran repeatedly describes prosperous communities that were destroyed because of their arrogance, injustice, and rejection of God's messengers: the people of 'Ad built impressive structures yet were annihilated by wind (69:6-7); Thamud carved homes in mountains yet were destroyed by a blast (91:14); Pharaoh's wealth and power could not save him from drowning (10:90-92).

## Economic Justice

Babylon's condemnation for trafficking in "bodies — and human souls" (18:13) resonates with the Quranic emphasis on economic justice. The Quran condemns those who "devour the wealth of orphans" (4:10), engage in usury (*riba*, 2:275), and hoard wealth without distributing it (9:34). The Prophet Muhammad declared: "He is not a believer whose stomach is filled while his neighbor goes hungry." Both Revelation 18 and Islamic social ethics condemn economic systems that commodify human beings and concentrate wealth through exploitation.

## "Come Out of Her"

The call to "come out of her, my people" (18:4) echoes the broader Quranic theme of *hijra* — emigration from a land of corruption to preserve one's faith. The Prophet Muhammad's *hijra* from Mecca to Medina established the principle that believers may be called to leave behind comfort and familiar surroundings in order to maintain their integrity before God. Both traditions affirm that the faithful must be willing to separate from corrupt systems rather than be complicit in their sins.
