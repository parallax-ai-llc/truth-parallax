## The Throne Vision and the Arsh

Revelation 4's throne-room vision presents striking parallels with Islamic descriptions of God's throne (*Arsh*). The Quran describes God as "Lord of the mighty Throne" (9:129), and the *Ayat al-Kursi* (Throne Verse, 2:255) declares: "His Kursi extends over the heavens and the earth, and their preservation does not tire Him." The hadith literature provides elaborate descriptions of the Throne, borne by immense angels, surrounded by light — imagery that resonates with Revelation's portrait of the throne surrounded by lightning, thunder, and worshipping beings.

## Angels and Worship

The four living creatures who cry "Holy, holy, holy" (4:8) correspond functionally to the angels who perpetually glorify God in Islamic tradition. The Quran describes angels who "celebrate the praises of their Lord" (42:5) without ceasing. The hadith (Sahih Muslim) describes angels who prostrate and glorify God perpetually, never tiring. Islamic theology would affirm the reality of angelic worship around God's throne while rejecting any suggestion that a human figure (the Lamb) shares that throne.

## Key Divergence: The Enthroned Figure

The critical Islamic objection to Revelation 4 concerns the relationship between the enthroned God and the Lamb introduced in chapter 5. Islam affirms God's absolute transcendence (*tanzih*) — God is beyond all comparison and sharing of His sovereignty. The Quran declares: "There is nothing like unto Him" (42:11). Any vision that places a created being alongside God on the throne contradicts *tawhid* in its most fundamental expression.
