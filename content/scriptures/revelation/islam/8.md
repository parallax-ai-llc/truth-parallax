## The Trumpets and the Trumpet of Israfil

The seven trumpets of Revelation 8 invite comparison with the Islamic tradition of the trumpet (*sur*) blown by the angel Israfil. The Quran references the trumpet in multiple passages: "And the trumpet will be blown, and whoever is in the heavens and whoever is on the earth will fall dead except whom Allah wills. Then it will be blown again, and at once they will be standing, looking on" (39:68). While Revelation describes seven sequential trumpet blasts with specific judgments attached, Islamic tradition speaks of two primary blasts — one of destruction and one of resurrection.

## Angelic Agency

Both Revelation and Islamic tradition present angels as instruments of divine judgment. The seven angels with seven trumpets parallel the Islamic understanding of angels assigned specific cosmic functions — Israfil with the trumpet, Azrael (Izra'il) with death, Jibril (Gabriel) with revelation, and Mika'il (Michael) with provision. Islamic theology emphasizes that angels act solely by God's command, possessing no independent will — a point consistent with Revelation's portrayal of the angels receiving their instructions from the heavenly court.

## The Partial Judgments

The trumpet judgments' destruction of "a third" of creation (8:7-12) has no direct Quranic parallel. Islamic eschatological teaching tends to describe the end as total and sudden rather than graduated. However, the concept of divine warning through partial calamity is present in the Quran: "We will surely let them taste the nearer punishment short of the greater punishment that perhaps they will repent" (32:21).
