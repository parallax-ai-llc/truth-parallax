## The Song of Praise

Revelation 15's song — "Great and amazing are your deeds, O Lord God the Almighty! Just and true are your ways, O King of the nations!" (15:3) — expresses a theology of divine sovereignty and justice that is broadly compatible with Islamic praise. The Quranic *tasbih* (glorification) declares: "Glory be to Allah, Lord of the Throne, above what they describe" (21:22). The affirmation of God's greatness, justice, and sovereignty over all nations is a cornerstone of Islamic theology.

## The Song of Moses

The explicit connection to "the song of Moses" (15:3) links this passage to the Exodus narrative, which Islam also affirms. The Quran recounts the story of Musa (Moses) extensively — he is mentioned more than any other prophet — and describes the Israelites' deliverance from Pharaoh as a paradigmatic act of divine salvation. However, Islam does not preserve the specific Song of the Sea (Exodus 15) in its liturgy; the Quranic account emphasizes the theological lesson rather than the poetic expression.

## Divine Justice

The declaration that God's "righteous acts have been revealed" (15:4) and the coming plague judgments reflect a theology of divine retributive justice. Islamic theology affirms that God is *al-Adl* (the Just) and *al-Hakam* (the Judge), and that justice is integral to His nature. The Quran warns: "And your Lord does injustice to no one" (18:49). Both Revelation and the Quran present divine judgment as the vindication of justice, not arbitrary wrath.
