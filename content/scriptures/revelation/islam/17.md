## Babylon as Worldly Corruption

Revelation 17's portrait of Babylon — the great prostitute drunk with the blood of the saints, seated on a beast with seven heads — presents a critique of corrupt worldly power that resonates with Islamic theology. The Quran consistently warns against the seductions of *dunya* (the worldly life): "The life of this world is nothing but amusement and diversion, and the home of the Hereafter — that is the true life, if only they knew" (29:64). Babylon's intoxicating luxury and ultimate destruction serve as a cautionary tale compatible with Islamic moral teaching.

## Imperial Oppression

The beast's persecution of the saints (17:6) resonates with the Islamic memory of persecution under the Meccan Quraysh and the broader prophetic narrative of oppressive powers opposing God's messengers. The Quran recounts how Pharaoh, the people of 'Ad and Thamud, and other tyrants rejected their prophets and were destroyed: "And how many a city did We destroy while it was committing wrong" (22:45). Revelation's portrait of Babylon's self-destruction (the beast turns on the prostitute, 17:16) illustrates the Quranic principle that oppressive systems contain the seeds of their own demise.

## Islamic Disinterest in Specific Identifications

Muslim scholars have generally not engaged in identifying the specific historical referent of Babylon (Rome, a future city, etc.), since the text is not accepted as authoritative. The moral lesson, however, is universally applicable: the corruption, self-deception, and ultimate collapse of worldly power that sets itself against divine truth.
