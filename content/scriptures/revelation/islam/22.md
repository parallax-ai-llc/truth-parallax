## The River and Tree of Life

Revelation 22's river of the water of life (22:1) and tree of life (22:2) invite comparison with the Quranic rivers of paradise and the tree of *Tuba*. The Quran describes paradise as containing "rivers of water unaltered, rivers of milk, rivers of wine, and rivers of purified honey" (47:15). The hadith describe the river *Kawthar* — a river in paradise granted specifically to the Prophet Muhammad, whiter than milk and sweeter than honey (Sahih Bukhari). The tree of *Tuba* in paradise is described in hadith as a tree so vast that a rider could travel in its shade for a hundred years.

## The Healing of the Nations

The tree whose leaves are "for the healing of the nations" (22:2) resonates with the Quranic concept of the Quran itself as a healing (*shifa'*): "We send down of the Quran that which is a healing and a mercy for the believers" (17:82). Both traditions envision the eschatological state as one of comprehensive healing — not merely physical but spiritual, communal, and cosmic.

## "Surely I Am Coming Soon"

Revelation's concluding dialogue — "Surely I am coming soon" / "Amen. Come, Lord Jesus!" (22:20) — expresses an eschatological longing that has parallels in Islamic devotional life. Muslims regularly pray for the coming of the final hour and the establishment of divine justice. However, the Islamic prayer is directed to Allah alone, not to a returning Christ-figure, and the emphasis is on submission to God's timing: "They ask you about the Hour: when is its arrival? Say, 'Its knowledge is only with my Lord. None will reveal its time except Him'" (7:187). Both traditions live in eschatological hope, but the object and structure of that hope differ fundamentally.
