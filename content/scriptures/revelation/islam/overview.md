## Overview

Revelation is not recognized as scripture in Islam. The Quran acknowledges earlier revelations — the Torah (*Tawrat*), the Psalms (*Zabur*), and the Gospel (*Injil*) — but considers their present textual forms to have been subject to *tahrif* (alteration). Revelation, as a distinctly Christian apocalyptic text, falls outside even these acknowledged earlier scriptures. Nevertheless, Islamic eschatology shares significant thematic parallels with Revelation, and Muslim scholars have engaged with the text comparatively to highlight both convergences and fundamental divergences.

## Quranic Eschatological Parallels

The Quran contains extensive eschatological material that resonates with Revelation's themes:

| Revelation Theme | Quranic Parallel | Key References |
|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Day of Judgment | Yawm al-Qiyamah (Day of Resurrection) | Al-Qiyamah 75, Al-Infitar 82 |
| Destruction of the world | Cosmic upheaval and dissolution | At-Takwir 81:1-14, Al-Inshiqaq 84:1-5 |
| Paradise / New Jerusalem | Jannah (Garden of Paradise) | Ar-Rahman 55:46-78, Al-Waqi'ah 56:10-40 |
| The Beast | Dabbat al-Ard (Beast of the Earth) | An-Naml 27:82 |
| Final battle | The descent of Isa (Jesus) and defeat of the Dajjal | Hadith collections (Sahih Muslim, Sunan Abu Dawud) |
| Eternal punishment | Jahannam (Hellfire) | Al-Mulk 67:6-11, An-Naba 78:21-30 |

## Key Theological Divergences

### Christology
Revelation's central figure is the exalted Christ — the Lamb, the Alpha and Omega, the King of Kings. Islam honors Isa ibn Maryam (Jesus son of Mary) as a prophet and the Messiah but categorically rejects his divinity, crucifixion (Quran 4:157), and the Trinitarian theology that pervades Revelation.

### The Nature of Apocalyptic Knowledge
Islam teaches that knowledge of the Hour (*al-Sa'ah*) belongs to God alone (Quran 7:187, 31:34). The detailed symbolic sequences of Revelation — seals, trumpets, bowls — have no Quranic counterpart. Islamic eschatology is conveyed primarily through hadith, listing "signs of the Hour" rather than an unfolding visionary narrative.

### Intercession and Mediation
Revelation's portrayal of saints, martyrs, and angelic mediators contrasts with the Islamic emphasis on direct relationship with God, though Islam does recognize prophetic intercession (*shafa'ah*) on the Day of Judgment.

## Major Commentators on Eschatology

| Scholar | Era | Contribution |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| Ibn Kathir | 14th century | *Al-Nihayah fi al-Fitan wa al-Malahim* — systematic compilation of eschatological hadith |
| Al-Qurtubi | 13th century | *Al-Tadhkirah* — comprehensive treatment of death and the afterlife |
| Al-Ghazali | 11th–12th century | *Ihya Ulum al-Din* — spiritual dimensions of eschatology |
| Sayyid Qutb | 20th century | Modern interpretations of Quranic judgment passages |
