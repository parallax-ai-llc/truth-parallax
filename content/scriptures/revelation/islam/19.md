## The Rider on the White Horse

Revelation 19's vision of the rider on the white horse — named "Faithful and True," "The Word of God," and "King of kings" — invites comparison with the Islamic eschatological expectation of the return of Isa ibn Maryam. Islamic hadith describe Isa descending from heaven, wearing two yellow garments, supported by two angels, and leading the faithful against the Dajjal. Both traditions envision a divinely appointed figure who defeats the forces of evil in the end times.

## The Fundamental Divergence

The critical difference lies in the rider's identity and status. In Revelation, the rider is the divine Christ — the Word of God incarnate, King of kings, bearing names that are exclusively divine attributes. In Islam, Isa returns as a human prophet and servant of God who prays behind the Mahdi, breaks the cross (rejecting the Christian doctrine of crucifixion), and lives as a Muslim. The Islamic Isa is subordinate to God in every way; the Revelation rider shares God's throne and receives universal worship.

## The Marriage Supper

The "marriage supper of the Lamb" (19:9) has no direct parallel in Islamic eschatology. Islam describes the rewards of paradise — gardens, rivers, companionship — but does not employ the marriage metaphor between God (or a divine figure) and the community of believers. The intimacy implied by the marriage imagery — union between the divine and the human — is precisely the kind of theological claim that Islam considers incompatible with God's absolute transcendence.
