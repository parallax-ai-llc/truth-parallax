## The Final Judgment and Islamic Eschatology

Revelation 20's final judgment scene (20:11-15) — the dead raised, the books opened, judgment according to deeds — closely parallels the Islamic Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*). The Quran describes the resurrection in vivid terms: "Does man think that We will not assemble his bones? Yes. We are able to proportion even his fingertips" (75:3-4). The opening of the books corresponds to the Quranic *kitab* (record of deeds): "And the record will be placed open, and you will see the criminals fearful of that within it" (18:49).

## The Millennium

The concept of a thousand-year messianic reign (20:1-6) has no direct parallel in Quranic eschatology. Islamic teaching describes a period of justice and prosperity following the return of Isa and the defeat of the Dajjal, but this is not quantified as a millennium. The hadith describe Isa living for a period (variously reported as forty years), during which peace and justice prevail, before his natural death and the subsequent final events. Islamic scholars have not developed millennial theology comparable to the Christian debates between pre-, post-, and amillennialism.

## The Book of Life and the Book of Deeds

Revelation's "book of life" (20:15) corresponds to the Islamic concept of divine foreknowledge and predestination (*qadar*), while the "books" of deeds correspond to the record maintained by the recording angels (*kiraman katibin*, Quran 82:10-12). Islamic theology holds together divine sovereignty and human accountability in a manner that parallels the tension in Revelation between the "book of life" (divine election) and judgment "according to what they had done" (human responsibility).
