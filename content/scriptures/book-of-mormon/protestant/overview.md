## Overview

Protestant engagement with the Book of Mormon is shaped by the foundational Reformation principle of *sola scriptura* — the belief that the Bible alone is the sufficient and final authority for Christian faith and practice. The claim that the Book of Mormon is "Another Testament of Jesus Christ" raises fundamental questions about the canon, the nature of revelation, and the sufficiency of Scripture.

## Key Interpretive Issues

### Biblical Sufficiency
Most Protestant theologians hold that the biblical canon is closed and that God's special revelation is complete in the sixty-six books of the Old and New Testaments. The introduction of additional scripture is viewed as contradicting this principle.

### Theological Evaluation
Protestant scholars examine Book of Mormon theology against biblical orthodoxy. Points of convergence (monotheism, the centrality of Christ, the necessity of repentance) and divergence (additional priesthood offices, pre-Columbian Christianity, the nature of God) are carefully analyzed.

### Historical Assessment
Protestant critics have raised questions about the Book of Mormon's historical claims, including the absence of archaeological evidence for large civilizations described in the text, linguistic concerns about "reformed Egyptian," and anachronistic elements.

## Theological Emphases

- **Canon closure**: The sufficiency of the biblical canon
- **Justification**: The Protestant understanding of salvation by grace through faith
- **Christology**: Whether Book of Mormon Christology aligns with Nicene orthodoxy
- **Evidence**: The role of historical and archaeological evidence in evaluating truth claims
