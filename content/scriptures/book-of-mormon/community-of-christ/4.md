## Enos/Jarom/Omni

This section of the Book of Mormon covers the brief records of Enos (his wrestle before God in prayer), Jarom (a condensed history), and Omni (the transition of record-keeping and the discovery of the people of Zarahemla).

Enos's prayer narrative is valued in Community of Christ as a profound exploration of contemplative spirituality. The image of Enos wrestling before God — not simply reciting petitions but engaging in a sustained, whole-person encounter with the divine — resonates with the denomination's appreciation for diverse spiritual practices, including contemplative prayer, silence, and spiritual direction. Community of Christ Doctrine and Covenants 163:2 invites members to "open your hearts and feel the yearnings of your brothers and sisters who are lonely, despised, fearful, neglected, unloved," and Enos's progressive expansion of concern — from himself, to his people, to even his enemies — models exactly this widening circle of compassion.

The fact that Enos prays for the Lamanites, the traditional enemies of his people, is particularly significant for Community of Christ's peace theology. This movement from self-concern to concern for the other — including the hostile other — exemplifies the "pursuit of peace (Shalom)" that is one of Community of Christ's Enduring Principles. Rather than reading the Nephite-Lamanite division as a fixed moral taxonomy, Community of Christ interprets Enos's prayer as evidence that the text itself subverts rigid us-versus-them categories when it is read at its best.

Jarom and Omni, with their brevity and multiple authors of varying spiritual commitment, are read in Community of Christ as honest testimony to the unevenness of faith within any community. Not every record-keeper is a prophet; not every generation produces spiritual giants. This acknowledgment of spiritual diversity within the sacred record resonates with Community of Christ's open and inclusive congregational life, where members hold diverse views and varying levels of certainty. The discovery of the people of Zarahemla — a separate community with its own history — also illustrates the Community of Christ principle that God is at work among many peoples and that covenant communities may take forms we do not expect.
