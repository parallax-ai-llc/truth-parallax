## Mosiah

This section of the Book of Mormon covers the reign of King Benjamin and his transformative covenant speech, the mission of the sons of Mosiah to the Lamanites, and the transition from monarchy to a system of judges.

King Benjamin's speech (Mosiah 2-5) is among the passages in the Book of Mormon that Community of Christ finds most compelling and most directly relevant to its identity. Benjamin's teaching that "when ye are in the service of your fellow beings ye are only in the service of your God" (Mosiah 2:17) is a cornerstone text for Community of Christ's theology of responsible discipleship. The denomination has built its understanding of ministry around exactly this principle — that authentic worship expresses itself in concrete service to others, particularly the marginalized and vulnerable. Community of Christ Doctrine and Covenants 163:9 echoes Benjamin's call when it instructs: "Become a people of the Temple — those who see violence but proclaim peace, who feel conflict yet extend the hand of reconciliation."

Benjamin's graphic description of the poor — "perhaps thou shalt say: The man has brought upon himself his misery" (Mosiah 4:17) — and his sharp rebuke of those who rationalize indifference to suffering provides Community of Christ with a scriptural mandate for its social justice commitments. The denomination reads this passage as directly applicable to contemporary issues of poverty, economic inequality, and systemic injustice. Benjamin's insistence that "we are all beggars" before God (Mosiah 4:19) levels all human distinctions of status and worth — a teaching that Community of Christ connects to its Enduring Principle of the "worth of all persons."

The transition from monarchy to a system of judges described later in Mosiah is read by Community of Christ as an early narrative exploration of the tension between concentrated authority and shared governance. The denomination, which has moved toward increasingly democratic and collaborative structures of leadership, finds in Mosiah's concern about tyrannical kings a resonance with its own commitment to common consent, prophetic counsel tested by community discernment, and the diffusion of power. The conversion stories in Mosiah — including Alma's transformation and the sons of Mosiah's dramatic change of heart — are valued as testimonies to the transformative power of grace, read not as validations of a particular institutional structure but as invitations to ongoing personal and communal renewal.
