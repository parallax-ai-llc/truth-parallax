## 2 Nephi

This section of the Book of Mormon covers Nephi's teachings on the Atonement, Isaiah's prophecies applied to the latter days, Lehi's final blessings, and the foundational declaration that 'men are that they might have joy'.

Community of Christ finds in 2 Nephi rich theological resources that align with its progressive and justice-oriented identity. The declaration that "men are, that they might have joy" (2 Nephi 2:25) is read not as a narrow soteriological formula but as an affirmation of the inherent goodness of creation and human experience — consistent with the denomination's emphasis on the worth of all persons and the sacredness of embodied life. Community of Christ theologians have connected this verse to the church's commitment to promoting conditions of joy for all people, including through advocacy for social justice, economic equality, and human dignity.

The extensive Isaiah quotations in 2 Nephi are approached by Community of Christ readers with the tools of modern biblical scholarship. Rather than reading these passages as encoded prophecies about specific latter-day events, Community of Christ's non-literalist hermeneutic invites engagement with Isaiah's themes of justice for the oppressed, critique of religious hypocrisy, and the prophetic vision of a peaceable world. This reading connects naturally to the denomination's Enduring Principles, particularly the "pursuit of peace (Shalom)" — the holistic biblical vision of a world where all relationships are set right. Community of Christ Doctrine and Covenants 163:4 calls the church to "become a people of the temple — those who see violence but proclaim peace," language that echoes Isaiah's vision of swords beaten into plowshares.

Nephi's "doctrine of Christ" in 2 Nephi 31 — faith, repentance, baptism, the Holy Ghost — is affirmed by Community of Christ as describing the basic pattern of Christian discipleship, but it is read within the denomination's understanding that forms and practices evolve as the Spirit leads. Community of Christ does not treat these passages as establishing fixed, unchangeable ordinances but as articulating an ongoing spiritual reality: the continuous process of turning toward Christ, entering covenant community, and being empowered by the Spirit for lives of service. This interpretive approach reflects the denomination's conviction, expressed in Community of Christ Doctrine and Covenants 164, that "Scripture is not to be worshiped or idolized" but engaged as a living resource for faithful living.
