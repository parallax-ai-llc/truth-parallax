## 1 Nephi

This section of the Book of Mormon covers Lehi's vision and departure from Jerusalem, the journey through the wilderness, the acquisition of the brass plates, and the family's voyage to the promised land under the guidance of the Liahona.

Community of Christ approaches 1 Nephi with openness about its nature — the denomination does not require members to affirm the narrative as literal ancient history. As expressed in the church's 2007 statement on scripture, the Book of Mormon is valued as scripture that "has been and is used by God to bring people closer to Christ," regardless of the historical questions surrounding its origins. This posture of theological openness allows Community of Christ members to engage the text devotionally without the burden of defending specific historical claims.

Lehi's call to leave Jerusalem and journey to a new land resonates with Community of Christ's understanding of vocation and responsive discipleship. The narrative's emphasis on following divine guidance even when the destination is unknown speaks to the denomination's principle of continuing revelation — the conviction, articulated in Community of Christ Doctrine and Covenants 162:2, that "the Spirit of the Restoration is not locked in one moment of time" but continues to unfold. Lehi's willingness to leave behind comfort and certainty models what Community of Christ calls "responsible discipleship": acting faithfully in response to the best light available.

The family conflicts depicted in 1 Nephi — Laman and Lemuel's resistance, the tensions between brothers — are read in Community of Christ not primarily as moral object lessons about obedience and rebellion, but as honest depictions of how communities struggle with change and uncertainty. The denomination's commitment to the worth of all persons means that even antagonists in the narrative are understood as full human beings whose fears and objections deserve empathetic reading. Community of Christ scholars have encouraged reading these family dynamics in dialogue with contemporary pastoral theology and conflict resolution, finding in them resources for navigating disagreement within faith communities without demonizing those who dissent.
