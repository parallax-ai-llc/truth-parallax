## Jacob

This section of the Book of Mormon covers Jacob's temple sermon on chastity and pride, the allegory of the olive tree (Jacob 5), and Sherem the anti-Christ — the first of several such figures in the Book of Mormon.

Community of Christ engages Jacob's temple sermon on chastity and pride (Jacob 2-3) with careful attention to both its ethical insights and its cultural context. The sermon's condemnation of economic pride and the exploitation of the vulnerable aligns powerfully with Community of Christ's Enduring Principle of "worth of all persons" and the denomination's consistent advocacy against economic inequality. However, Community of Christ readers also bring a critical lens to passages that have historically been used to restrict or control — recognizing that the text's teachings on sexuality and gender reflect ancient patriarchal assumptions that must be read in dialogue with the church's contemporary commitment to the full dignity and equality of all persons, including LGBTQ+ members, as affirmed in Community of Christ Doctrine and Covenants 164.

The allegory of the olive tree (Jacob 5) is one of the passages in the Book of Mormon that Community of Christ theologians find most theologically generative. Read through the denomination's lens of continuing revelation and God's expansive love, the allegory speaks to God's persistent, patient labor to nurture and reclaim all branches of the human family — not just a single chosen lineage. The image of the Lord of the vineyard weeping over fruitless branches and working tirelessly to graft and re-graft suggests a God whose salvific intent is universal and whose methods are adaptive rather than rigid. This reading supports Community of Christ's understanding that God's covenant community is always expanding, always being re-formed, and that no one is permanently outside the reach of divine grace.

The Sherem episode (Jacob 7) is read in Community of Christ with particular nuance. While the text presents Sherem as a dangerous opponent of prophetic truth, Community of Christ's commitment to open theological inquiry means that dissent and questioning are not automatically equated with spiritual failure. The denomination encourages members to engage difficult questions honestly, trusting that authentic faith can withstand scrutiny. Sherem's story thus becomes an occasion for reflecting on how faith communities handle internal disagreement — a question Community of Christ has navigated extensively as it has embraced progressive positions on gender, sexuality, and scriptural interpretation.
