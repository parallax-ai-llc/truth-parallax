## Overview

Historical-critical study of the Book of Mormon applies the same methods used for other ancient texts — archaeology, linguistics, genetics, source criticism, and historical analysis — to evaluate its claims of ancient American authorship and historicity. This approach is practiced by both LDS and non-LDS scholars, sometimes reaching different conclusions from the same evidence.

## Key Methodological Areas

### Archaeology
Despite decades of search, no archaeological site, artifact, or inscription has been definitively linked to Book of Mormon peoples, places, or events. LDS scholars propose limited geographical models (particularly in Mesoamerica) while critics regard the absence of corroborating evidence as significant.

### Linguistics
Claims about "reformed Egyptian" as a writing system, the presence of Hebrew linguistic patterns in the English text, and the relationship between Book of Mormon names and ancient Near Eastern languages remain actively debated.

### Genetics
DNA studies of Native American populations have not confirmed Near Eastern genetic origins as described in the Book of Mormon narrative. LDS scholars have responded by arguing for limited geographic models and small founding populations.

### Textual Criticism
Royal Skousen's critical text project represents the most rigorous textual-critical work on the Book of Mormon, tracing changes from the original manuscript through the printer's manuscript to published editions.

## Key Debates

| Issue | Supporter Position | Critic Position |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Geography | Limited Mesoamerican model | No identifiable location |
| Archaeology | Correlations exist but are debated | No confirmed correlation |
| Linguistics | Hebrew/Egyptian parallels | 19th-century English composition |
| Genetics | Small population, genetic drift | No Near Eastern DNA in pre-Columbian Americas |
