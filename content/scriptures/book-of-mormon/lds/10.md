## 4 Nephi

This section of the Book of Mormon covers the brief record of nearly two hundred years of peace following Christ's visit, followed by the gradual return to wickedness and division.

Fourth Nephi covers nearly three hundred years in a single brief chapter, yet for Latter-day Saints it contains the most complete description of a Zion society anywhere in scripture. The record that "there were no contentions and disputations among them, and every man did deal justly one with another" and that "they had all things common among them" (4 Nephi 1:2-3) describes the consecrated, egalitarian community that is the ultimate goal of the LDS law of consecration first revealed in Doctrine and Covenants 42. General Authorities have consistently taught that the Zion described in 4 Nephi is not merely a historical curiosity but a prophetic blueprint for the millennial society Latter-day Saints are working to establish.

The passage's description of the gradual apostasy that followed — the reintroduction of class distinctions, the rise of competing churches, and the eventual persecution of the true disciples of Christ — is read by Latter-day Saints as a condensed parallel to the Great Apostasy that followed the death of the original apostles in the Old World. The pattern reinforces the LDS understanding that priesthood authority and gospel fulness cannot be maintained without continuous prophetic leadership, and that apostasy is the inevitable result when people reject living prophets.

The brevity of 4 Nephi is itself significant in LDS interpretation. Mormon, as editor, gave hundreds of pages to periods of conflict but only a few verses to centuries of peace, suggesting that righteousness produces a kind of narrative simplicity — there is little drama when people live the gospel. This editorial choice is sometimes cited in LDS teaching as evidence that contention is not inherent to human experience but is rather the result of departing from covenant relationships. The text's final verses, describing the hiding of sacred records as wickedness returns, connect directly to the Book of Mormon's own coming forth in the latter days through Joseph Smith.
