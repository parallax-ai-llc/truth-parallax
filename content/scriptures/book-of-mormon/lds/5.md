## Words of Mormon

This section of the Book of Mormon covers Mormon's editorial bridge connecting the small plates of Nephi to his abridgment of the large plates, explaining his purposes as editor and compiler.

Latter-day Saints find in the Words of Mormon a remarkable window into Mormon's editorial process and, more importantly, evidence of divine providence in the preservation of scripture. Mormon explains that he discovered the small plates of Nephi while abridging the large plates and felt prompted by the Spirit to include them alongside his abridgment "for a wise purpose" he did not fully understand (Words of Mormon 1:7). LDS readers understand this "wise purpose" in light of Doctrine and Covenants 10, which reveals that God foresaw Martin Harris's loss of the 116 manuscript pages and prepared the small plates centuries in advance as a replacement record.

This editorial bridge is significant for LDS apologetics because it demonstrates what Grant Hardy has called the "multiple narrative voices" of the Book of Mormon — evidence that the text presents itself as a complex compilation rather than a simple dictation. The fact that Mormon, writing around 385 AD, includes material originally composed by Nephi around 570 BC, with an explanatory note about his editorial decisions, is consistent with the LDS claim that the Book of Mormon is an authentic ancient record processed by a late compiler.

For Latter-day Saint theology, Mormon's editorial aside also reinforces the principle that God works through human agency while directing the overall outcome. Mormon did not know why the Spirit prompted him to include the small plates, yet his obedience ensured that the lost material could be replaced fourteen hundred years later. This is frequently taught in LDS settings as an illustration of how individual faithfulness serves purposes far beyond what the individual can foresee — a principle deeply connected to the LDS understanding of foreordination and the plan of salvation.
