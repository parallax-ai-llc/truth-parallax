## Mosiah

This section of the Book of Mormon covers the reign of King Benjamin and his transformative covenant speech, the mission of the sons of Mosiah to the Lamanites, and the transition from monarchy to a system of judges.

King Benjamin's covenant speech (Mosiah 2-5) is one of the most frequently cited and doctrinally rich passages in all of Latter-day Saint scripture. LDS scholars, particularly John W. Welch, have demonstrated that the speech follows the structure of ancient Near Eastern covenant renewal ceremonies — a finding that supports Book of Mormon historicity. Benjamin's teaching that the natural man is "an enemy to God" unless he "yields to the enticings of the Holy Spirit" (Mosiah 3:19) is foundational to the LDS understanding of the Atonement and the process of sanctification. This verse appears repeatedly in General Conference addresses, seminary curriculum, and missionary discussions.

The account of the people's response — falling to the earth, crying out for the "atoning blood of Christ" (Mosiah 4:2), and then covenanting to take upon them the name of Christ — provides Latter-day Saints with a scriptural model of the covenant relationship that is renewed each week through the sacrament. The parallel with temple covenants is widely recognized in LDS devotional literature.

The mission of the sons of Mosiah to the Lamanites (Mosiah 27-28) establishes the missionary paradigm that shapes modern LDS practice: young men and women set apart by priesthood authority to preach repentance among those who do not yet know the gospel. Alma the Younger's dramatic conversion, struck down by an angel and experiencing spiritual rebirth, is taught as the premier Book of Mormon example of the mighty change of heart (Alma 5:14) that the gospel enables. The subsequent transition from monarchy to a system of judges is read through the LDS lens of righteous governance, with Mosiah's warning that if a king becomes wicked the sins rest upon the king providing an argument for accountability structures that Latter-day Saints see reflected in the church's system of common consent (D&C 26).
