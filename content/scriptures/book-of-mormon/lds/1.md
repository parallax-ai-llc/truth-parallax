## 1 Nephi

This section of the Book of Mormon covers Lehi's vision and departure from Jerusalem, the journey through the wilderness, the acquisition of the brass plates, and the family's voyage to the promised land under the guidance of the Liahona.

Latter-day Saints understand Lehi's departure from Jerusalem as a literal, historical event directed by God — a pattern of prophetic obedience that recurs throughout the Restoration. Lehi's initial vision of the destruction of Jerusalem parallels the prophetic call narratives in the Old Testament, and LDS scholars such as Hugh Nibley have drawn extensive comparisons between Lehi's journey and ancient Near Eastern travel accounts in *Lehi in the Desert* (1950). The brass plates, obtained at great cost by Nephi, prefigure the LDS emphasis on scripture preservation and the importance of maintaining sacred records across generations.

The Liahona — a divinely prepared compass that functioned according to faith and diligence — is frequently cited in General Conference as a type for personal revelation. Elder David A. Bednar has compared the Liahona to the guidance available through the Holy Ghost, while President Russell M. Nelson has taught that the words of living prophets serve a similar directional function for modern Saints. This typological reading is central to how Latter-day Saints engage 1 Nephi: every element of the narrative is understood to teach principles applicable to covenant life today.

Nephi's account also establishes foundational LDS doctrines about obedience and agency. His declaration "I will go and do the things which the Lord hath commanded" (1 Nephi 3:7) is among the most memorized and cited passages in the LDS tradition, taught in Primary, seminary, and missionary discussions as the paradigmatic statement of faith-driven action. The contrast between Nephi and his murmuring brothers Laman and Lemuel is read through the lens of the plan of salvation — as an illustration of how individuals respond differently to divine instruction, with eternal consequences. Doctrine and Covenants 9:7-8 further illuminates the Liahona principle by explaining that personal revelation requires both study and faith.
