## Alma

This section of the Book of Mormon covers the longest book in the Book of Mormon, covering Alma's conversion, the Anti-Nephi-Lehies, Captain Moroni's military leadership, and extensive doctrinal discourses on faith, the Atonement, and resurrection.

The book of Alma, the longest in the Book of Mormon, is a theological and narrative centerpiece for Latter-day Saints. Alma's discourse on faith as a seed (Alma 32) is one of the most widely taught passages in the church, providing a practical epistemological framework for spiritual knowledge that is cited in missionary discussions, Gospel Doctrine classes, and General Conference. Elder Jeffrey R. Holland has called it "one of the greatest sermons on faith ever delivered." The passage's empirical metaphor — plant the word, nourish it, observe whether it produces good fruit — resonates with the LDS conviction that religious truth can be personally verified through experience and the witness of the Holy Ghost.

The Anti-Nephi-Lehies, Lamanite converts who buried their weapons and chose death rather than break their covenant of nonviolence (Alma 24), are held up in LDS teaching as a supreme example of covenant faithfulness. Their story illustrates the LDS doctrine that covenants made with God carry binding moral force, a principle that connects directly to the temple covenants Latter-day Saints make today. The stripling warriors (Alma 53, 56-58), sons of these same converts, are among the most celebrated figures in LDS youth programs — their faith that "God would deliver them" because their mothers had taught them to trust in God is a foundational text for LDS teachings about parental influence and the power of righteous upbringing.

Captain Moroni's "Title of Liberty" (Alma 46) — "In memory of our God, our religion, and freedom, and our peace, our wives, and our children" — is widely recognized in LDS culture as a statement of righteous patriotism and the duty to defend faith and family. The extended war chapters (Alma 43-63) are read not merely as military history but as a study in the spiritual conditions that lead to victory or defeat, with applications to the personal spiritual warfare every Latter-day Saint faces. Hugh Nibley's *Since Cumorah* provides extensive analysis of the military strategies described in these chapters, arguing for their consistency with ancient patterns of warfare.
