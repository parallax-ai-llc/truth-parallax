## 3 Nephi

This section of the Book of Mormon covers the climactic appearance of the resurrected Jesus Christ to the Nephites, his teaching of the Sermon on the Mount, the establishment of his church, and the period of peace that followed.

Third Nephi is regarded by Latter-day Saints as the spiritual climax of the entire Book of Mormon — what President Ezra Taft Benson called "the fifth gospel." The appearance of the resurrected Jesus Christ to the Nephites at the temple in Bountiful (3 Nephi 11) is the event toward which the entire preceding narrative builds and from which the remainder flows. Christ's invitation for each person present to come forward individually and feel the prints in his hands, feet, and side establishes what Latter-day Saints understand as the deeply personal nature of Christ's Atonement — not an abstract theological concept but an intimate, tangible encounter.

Christ's delivery of the Sermon at the temple (3 Nephi 12-14), paralleling the Sermon on the Mount, is read by LDS scholars as evidence that Christ teaches the same gospel in every dispensation. The differences between the Matthean and Nephite versions of the sermon are analyzed by scholars such as John W. Welch in *The Sermon at the Temple and the Sermon on the Mount* (1990), who argues that the 3 Nephi version reflects a temple-covenant context that illuminates the original meaning of Jesus's teachings. The institution of the sacrament among the Nephites (3 Nephi 18) directly parallels the ordinance performed weekly in LDS sacrament meetings, reinforcing the continuity between Christ's ancient church and the restored church.

Christ's establishment of twelve disciples, his bestowal of priesthood authority, and his institution of baptism and the sacrament among the Nephites constitute, in LDS theology, a full dispensation of the gospel — proof that the resurrected Christ organized his church in the Americas just as he had in Palestine. The period of peace that followed (described in 4 Nephi) represents the Zion ideal that Latter-day Saints aspire to build: a society with "no contention in the land, because of the love of God which did dwell in the hearts of the people" (4 Nephi 1:15). This vision directly informs the LDS concept of Zion as articulated in Doctrine and Covenants 97:21.
