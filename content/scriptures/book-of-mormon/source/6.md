title: "Book of Mormon Section 6: Alma, Part One"

The book of Alma, the longest book in the Book of Mormon, begins with the account of Alma the Younger, who in his youth was numbered among the unbelievers and went about seeking to destroy the church of God. But an angel appeared to him and his companions with a voice of thunder, and Alma was struck dumb and fell to the earth.

For three days and three nights, Alma was racked with the pains of a damned soul, tormented by all his sins. In this extremity he remembered what his father had taught concerning Jesus Christ, who should come to atone for the sins of the world. He cried within his heart: O Jesus, thou Son of God, have mercy on me, who am in the gall of bitterness and encircled about by the everlasting chains of death.

And when he thought this, he could remember his pains no more; he was harrowed up by the memory of his sins no more. There could be nothing so exquisite and so bitter as were his pains, and nothing so exquisite and sweet as was his joy. He saw God sitting upon His throne, surrounded with numberless concourses of angels, and his soul did long to be there.

Alma became a mighty preacher and the first chief judge of the Nephites. He taught concerning faith, declaring that faith is not to have a perfect knowledge of things, but if ye will awake and arouse your faculties, even to an experiment upon my words, and exercise a particle of faith, even if ye can no more than desire to believe, let this desire work in you until ye believe in a manner that ye can give place for a portion of my words.

He compared the word to a seed planted in the heart, teaching that if it is a good seed and ye do not cast it out by your unbelief, it will begin to swell within your breasts, and when you feel these swelling motions, ye will begin to say within yourselves: it must needs be that this is a good seed, for it beginneth to enlarge my soul and to enlighten my understanding.
