Alma continued his ministry among the Nephites, teaching the doctrine of the resurrection and the justice and mercy of God. He declared that there must be an atonement made, or else all mankind must unavoidably perish. The plan of redemption requires that men repent, for the Lord cannot look upon sin with the least degree of allowance, yet His mercy claimeth the penitent through the atoning sacrifice.

The great wars of the Nephites and Lamanites occupy much of the book of Alma. Captain Moroni, a man of perfect understanding and a man who did not delight in bloodshed, rose to defend his people against the Lamanite armies and the internal conspiracies of the king-men. He raised the title of liberty upon his coat, writing upon it: In memory of our God, our religion, and freedom, and our peace, our wives, and our children.

Moroni fortified the Nephite cities, building walls of stone, banks of earth, and towers from which the defenders could cast stones upon their enemies. He was a man whose heart swelled with thanksgiving to God for the many privileges and blessings which He bestowed upon His people, and his soul was filled with joy in the liberty and freedom of his country.

The account of the two thousand stripling warriors is preserved in Alma. These young men, sons of the people of Ammon who had made a covenant never to shed blood again, entered into a covenant to fight for the liberty of the Nephites. Though they were young and had never fought before, they were exceedingly valiant for courage and for strength, and they attributed their preservation in battle to the faith taught them by their mothers.

They said: We do not doubt our mothers knew it. This simple declaration of trust in maternal faith has resonated through generations of readers, testifying to the power of a believing household to instill courage and conviction in the rising generation.
