The words of Jacob, the brother of Nephi, which he spake unto the people of Nephi. Jacob, having been consecrated a priest and teacher of the people by the hand of Nephi, took upon himself the responsibility of teaching the word of God with all diligence.

Jacob taught the allegory of the olive tree, received from the prophet Zenos, which portrays the history of God's dealings with the house of Israel. The lord of the vineyard planted a tame olive tree and nourished it for a long time. When it began to decay, he pruned it and dunged it and labored upon it. He took the young and tender branches and grafted them into wild olive trees throughout the vineyard, and he grafted wild branches into the mother tree.

After a long time, the lord of the vineyard returned and found that the trees had produced both good fruit and corrupt fruit. He labored with his servant, grafting natural branches back into the natural trees and pruning away the corrupt branches. The lord of the vineyard wept and said unto his servant: What could I have done more for my vineyard?

This allegory teaches that God has scattered Israel among the nations of the earth but has not forgotten His covenant people. In the last days, He will gather them again, restoring the natural branches to the mother tree. The Lord labors for the salvation of His people, and His work continues throughout the ages.

Jacob also confronted the pride and wickedness that had begun to appear among the Nephites. He warned against the love of riches, the persecution of the poor, and the practice of taking many wives and concubines. He taught that all are alike unto God, whether black or white, bond or free, male or female, and that all who come unto Him through the Holy Messiah shall be saved.
