## Overview

The Book of Mormon is a sacred text of the Latter-day Saint movement, first published in 1830 by Joseph Smith, who described it as a translation of ancient golden plates inscribed in "reformed Egyptian" by prophets in the ancient Americas. Subtitled "Another Testament of Jesus Christ," it narrates the history of several groups who migrated from the ancient Near East to the Americas and records Christ's post-resurrection ministry among them.

## Structure

The Book of Mormon comprises fifteen books (named after their principal authors or editors), organized as follows:

1. **Small Plates of Nephi** (1 Nephi – Omni): The spiritual account from Lehi's departure from Jerusalem to Omni
2. **Words of Mormon**: A brief editorial bridge
3. **Large Plates Abridgment** (Mosiah – 4 Nephi): Mormon's abridgment of the larger historical record
4. **Mormon and Moroni**: Mormon's own record and his son Moroni's concluding writings
5. **Book of Ether**: Moroni's abridgment of the Jaredite record
6. **Supporting Materials**: Title page, testimonies, and doctrinal summary

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Book of Mormon holds distinct significance across multiple interpretive frameworks:

- **LDS (Latter-day Saint)**: The keystone of their religion, a divine witness of Jesus Christ alongside the Bible
- **Community of Christ**: An inspired text used devotionally, though with less emphasis on historicity
- **Academic**: Studied through literary, historical, and sociological methods
- **Protestant**: Evaluated against biblical sufficiency and historical evidence
- **Comparative**: Analyzed alongside other restoration scriptures and new religious movements
- **Historical-Critical**: Examined through archaeological, linguistic, and textual-critical methods

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Christ in the Americas | Jesus Christ's post-resurrection ministry to the Nephites |
| Covenant | God's promises to the house of Israel, extended to the Americas |
| Apostasy and Restoration | Cycles of faithfulness and falling away |
| Agency | The centrality of moral choice and accountability |
| Gathering of Israel | The eventual reunification of God's covenant people |
