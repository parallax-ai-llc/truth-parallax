## Overview

The Testament of Solomon is a pseudepigraphical text attributed to King Solomon, recounting how he received a magical ring from the archangel Michael that gave him power to summon, interrogate, and command demons, whom he then compelled to assist in building the Temple in Jerusalem. The text is preserved in Greek and is generally dated to between the first and fifth centuries CE, with most scholars favoring the third or fourth century. It reflects a Christian redaction of older Jewish traditions about Solomon's legendary wisdom and mastery over demons.

The work is cast as Solomon's first-person testament or autobiography, following the literary convention of the "testaments" genre. Solomon summons demons one by one, questions them about their names, activities, celestial counterparts, and the means by which they can be thwarted — typically through the invocation of specific angels or divine names. This interrogation format makes the Testament one of the earliest and most important sources for the demonological traditions that profoundly influenced medieval and early modern magic, exorcism, and angelology.

The text concludes with Solomon's own downfall: his love for a Shunammite woman leads him to idolatry, resulting in the loss of his divine gift and the division of his kingdom. This cautionary ending transforms the work from a mere catalogue of demons into a moral narrative about the dangers of spiritual pride and sexual temptation.

## Structure

The Testament of Solomon comprises 26 chapters organized around Solomon's encounters with various demons:

1. **Introduction and the Ring** (Chapters 1-2): Solomon receives the divine ring from Michael; the first demon (Ornias) is subdued
2. **The Major Demon Interrogations** (Chapters 3-15): Solomon summons and interrogates a series of demons, learning their names, activities, and weaknesses
3. **The Temple Construction** (Chapters 16-20): Demons are compelled to labor in building the Temple; various demonic powers and cosmic forces are catalogued
4. **The Seven Planetary Spirits** (Chapters 21-23): The demons of the seven planets and their influences
5. **Solomon's Fall** (Chapters 24-26): Solomon's love for the Shunammite woman, his idolatry, loss of power, and repentance

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: A key primary source for the study of ancient demonology, magic, and the intersection of Jewish, Christian, and Greco-Roman traditions
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels with Islamic Solomonic traditions, Mesopotamian exorcism texts, and cross-cultural demonological literature
- **Protestant**: Illuminates the demonological and angelological assumptions of the New Testament world
- **Catholic**: Significant for understanding the development of exorcism traditions and the theology of spiritual warfare
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Resonates with Orthodox traditions of exorcism, the power of the cross, and the invocation of angels against demonic forces
- **Interfaith**: A bridge text connecting Jewish Solomonic traditions, Christian demonology, and Islamic jinn lore

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Solomonic Authority | Solomon's divinely granted power over demons through the magical ring |
| Demonology | A systematic catalogue of demons, their names, activities, and hierarchies |
| Exorcism and Binding | The techniques and divine names used to subdue and control demonic forces |
| Temple Building | The construction of the Jerusalem Temple with the forced labor of demons |
| Angelic Counterparts | Each demon is opposed by a specific angel or divine power |
| Solomon's Fall | The moral narrative of Solomon's downfall through idolatry and lust |
