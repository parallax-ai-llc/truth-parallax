Psalm 5 continues the morning-evening cycle begun in Psalms 3-4, opening with a plea for God's attention at daybreak: "O Lord, in the morning you hear my voice; in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" (5:3). Protestant devotional theology has drawn on this verse to commend the discipline of morning prayer. The word translated "prepare" (*'arak*) is a sacrificial term — the worshiper arranges prayers as a priest arranges offerings on the altar.

The psalm's middle section (5:4-6) contains some of the strongest language in the Psalter about God's hatred of evil: "You hate all evildoers" (5:5), "the Lord abhors the bloodthirsty and deceitful man" (5:6). Protestant theology holds these statements in tension with God's love, seeing them as expressions of divine holiness that make the gospel necessary. Luther read this psalm as describing the stark divide between those under grace and those under wrath.

The closing petition — "Lead me, O Lord, in your righteousness because of my enemies; make your way straight before me" (5:8) — is a prayer for divine guidance that resonates with the Protestant emphasis on Scripture as the believer's guide. Verse 12 wraps the psalm with assurance: "For you bless the righteous, O Lord; you cover him with favor as with a shield." This language of divine covering connects to the New Testament doctrine of imputed righteousness, where God's favor surrounds the believer not because of personal merit but through Christ's righteousness.
