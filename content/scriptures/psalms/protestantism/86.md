## A Prayer of David for Mercy

Psalm 86 is the only psalm in Book III attributed to David, and it serves as an intensely personal prayer woven from phrases found throughout the Psalter and the Torah. David opens by declaring his neediness: "Incline your ear, O LORD, and answer me, for I am poor and needy" (v. 1). Protestant theology has consistently taught that poverty of spirit — the recognition of one's utter dependence on God — is the prerequisite for receiving divine grace. Luther emphasized that God's ear is inclined toward the humble, not the self-sufficient, and that this spiritual poverty is not a condition to be overcome but a posture to be maintained throughout the Christian life.

The theological center of the psalm is David's confession of God's character: "For you, O Lord, are good and forgiving, abounding in steadfast love to all who call upon you" (v. 5). This echoes the great revelation of Exodus 34:6, which Protestant interpreters consider the foundational self-disclosure of God's character. David's petition for an "undivided heart" in verse 11 — "Teach me your way, O LORD, that I may walk in your truth; unite my heart to fear your name" — acknowledges the divided loyalties that plague every believer. Calvin saw this as a prayer against the heart's natural tendency toward idolatry, recognizing that only God can produce the singular devotion that the first commandment requires.

Verse 9 expresses a remarkable universalism: "All the nations you have made shall come and worship before you, O Lord, and shall glorify your name." This expectation that all nations will worship the LORD is grounded not in religious pluralism but in God's sovereign creative authority — He made them, and they will ultimately acknowledge their Maker. The psalm closes with a plea for a "sign of your favor" (v. 17), and Protestant interpreters have found the ultimate sign of divine favor in the resurrection of Christ, which demonstrates God's power to deliver "from the depths of Sheol" (v. 13).
