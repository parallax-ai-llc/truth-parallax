## God the Righteous Judge

Psalm 75 presents a divine oracle framed by human praise, with God himself speaking in verses 2-5. The psalm opens with thanksgiving — "We give thanks to you, O God; we give thanks, for your name is near. We recount your wondrous deeds" (v. 1) — and then God declares: "At the set time that I appoint I will judge with equity. When the earth totters, and all its inhabitants, it is I who keep steady its pillars" (vv. 2-3). Protestant theology has found in these verses a powerful affirmation of divine sovereignty over history's timing. God judges, but He judges at His "set time," not according to human impatience.

God's warning to the arrogant — "Do not lift up your horn on high, or speak with haughty neck" (v. 5) — addresses the fundamental sin of pride. Calvin read the "horn" metaphor as representing self-exalted power and noted that the psalm demolishes all human claims to autonomous authority. Verse 6 reinforces this: "For not from the east or from the west and not from the wilderness comes lifting up, but it is God who executes judgment, putting down one and lifting up another." Protestant interpreters have seen here a complete theology of divine providence over human affairs — all exaltation and humiliation flow from God's sovereign will.

The image of the cup in verse 8 — "For in the hand of the LORD there is a cup with foaming wine, well mixed, and he pours out from it, and all the wicked of the earth shall drain it down to the dregs" — is one of Scripture's most vivid judgment metaphors, echoed in Revelation 14:10. Protestant interpreters note that Jesus himself took this cup of judgment in Gethsemane (Matthew 26:39), drinking what the wicked deserved so that the righteous might be spared. The psalm concludes with the psalmist's resolve: "But I will declare it forever; I will sing praises to the God of Jacob" (v. 9), affirming that the proper response to God's just rule is perpetual worship.
