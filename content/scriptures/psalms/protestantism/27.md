Psalm 27 opens with one of Scripture's most powerful declarations of faith: "The Lord is my light and my salvation — whom shall I fear?" (27:1). Protestant theology sees this as a paradigmatic expression of justification by faith: when God is one's light and fortress, all human threats lose their power. Luther found great comfort in this psalm during his own spiritual struggles.

The central petition — "One thing I ask from the LORD, this only do I seek: that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life" (27:4) — expresses the Reformed priority of communion with God as the highest good. This is not mere temple attendance but the desire for intimate fellowship with the living God, a theme that resonates with the Protestant emphasis on personal relationship with Christ.

The psalm's shift in tone at verse 7 moves from confident declaration to urgent petition, reflecting the real tension of faith. David cries, "Do not hide your face from me" (27:9). Protestant interpreters value this honesty — faith does not eliminate seasons of felt absence. The closing exhortation, "Wait for the LORD; be strong and take heart and wait for the LORD" (27:14), captures the patient trust that characterizes genuine Protestant spirituality.
