Psalm 3 is the first psalm with a historical superscription, linking it to David's flight from his son Absalom (2 Samuel 15-18). Protestant commentators value these superscriptions as authentic historical context, reading the psalm as a genuine prayer composed amid mortal danger. The opening cry — "O Lord, how many are my foes!" (3:1) — captures the desperation of a king betrayed by his own son and abandoned by much of his kingdom.

The turning point comes in verse 3: "But you, O Lord, are a shield about me, my glory, and the lifter of my head." This adversative "but" is characteristic of psalmic faith — the psalmist acknowledges the reality of danger, then pivots to affirm God's protective presence. Verse 5, "I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the Lord sustained me," has been treasured in Protestant devotion as a testimony to the peace that faith brings even in extremity. Spurgeon called this "the sweetest sleep that ever a man had."

The psalm's conclusion — "Salvation belongs to the Lord; your blessing be on your people" (3:8) — states a core Protestant conviction: salvation is entirely God's work, not human achievement. The Hebrew *yeshu'ah* (salvation) foreshadows the name of Jesus Himself. This psalm models the movement from desperate petition to confident trust that characterizes authentic biblical prayer, a pattern Protestant pastoral theology commends to all believers facing opposition and fear.
