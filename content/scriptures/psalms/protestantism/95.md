## Worship and Warning

Psalm 95 is divided sharply between joyful summons to worship (vv. 1-7a) and solemn divine warning (vv. 7b-11). The opening call is exuberant: "Oh come, let us sing to the LORD; let us make a joyful noise to the rock of our salvation!" (v. 1). The reasons for worship are both cosmic and covenantal: "For the LORD is a great God, and a great King above all gods. In his hand are the depths of the earth; the heights of the mountains are his also" (vv. 3-4). Protestant worship theology has drawn from these verses the conviction that praise should be grounded in theological content — who God is and what He has done — not merely in emotional experience.

The transition at verse 7 is abrupt and arresting: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts, as at Meribah, as on the day of Massah in the wilderness" (vv. 7b-8). The reference to Israel's rebellion at Meribah (Exodus 17) transforms the psalm from invitation to admonition. The writer of Hebrews devotes extensive attention to this passage (Hebrews 3:7-4:13), applying it to new covenant believers and warning that the danger of hardened hearts persists in the church. Calvin read this as evidence that genuine worship always involves the risk of rejection — hearing God's voice creates a moment of decision, and refusal leads to judicial hardening.

God's oath — "Therefore I swore in my wrath, 'They shall not enter my rest'" (v. 11) — introduces the crucial biblical concept of divine rest. Protestant interpreters have understood this "rest" as encompassing both the earthly promised land and the eschatological rest that remains for God's people (Hebrews 4:9). Luther emphasized the word "today" as expressing the urgency of the gospel: every encounter with God's word is a fresh "today" in which faith or unbelief is chosen. The psalm thus serves as a permanent liturgical warning — worship that does not produce obedience leads not to rest but to exclusion.
