## Waiting in Silence for God Alone

Psalm 62 is distinguished by its radical exclusivity: "For God alone my soul waits in silence; from him comes my salvation" (v. 1). The word "alone" (ak) appears six times in this psalm, creating an emphatic pattern that Protestant interpreters have found theologically significant. Calvin read this psalm as a rebuke to all forms of divided loyalty -- trusting partly in God and partly in human resources, wealth, or social position. The sola emphasis of the Reformation finds a natural home in this psalm's insistence that salvation, hope, and refuge come from God alone.

The psalm contains a searching indictment of misplaced trust: "Those of low estate are but a breath; those of high estate are a delusion; in the balances they go up; they are together lighter than a breath" (v. 9). This democratic demolition of human pretension -- neither common people nor nobles can bear the weight of ultimate trust -- was revolutionary in the hierarchical world of the ancient Near East and continues to challenge contemporary idolatries of status and wealth. Verse 10 adds: "Put no trust in extortion; set no vain hopes on robbery; if riches increase, set not your heart on them."

The psalm's conclusion articulates two foundational attributes: "Once God has spoken; twice have I heard this: that power belongs to God, and that to you, O Lord, belongs steadfast love" (vv. 11-12). Protestant theology has found in this pair -- power and love -- a summary of the gospel: God has both the ability and the desire to save. Kidner observes that the psalm moves from personal testimony ("my soul waits") to congregational exhortation ("Trust in him at all times, O people," v. 8), modeling the way individual faith overflows into communal encouragement.
