## A Prayer Against a Confederacy of Enemies

Psalm 83 is the final psalm of Asaph and presents an urgent plea against a coalition of nations conspiring to destroy Israel: "O God, do not keep silence; do not hold your peace or be still, O God! For behold, your enemies make an uproar; those who hate you have raised their heads" (vv. 1-2). The conspiracy is explicitly anti-covenantal: "They say, 'Come, let us wipe them out as a nation; let the name of Israel be remembered no more!'" (v. 4). Protestant interpreters have understood this coalition — which includes Edom, Moab, Ammon, Philistia, Tyre, and Assyria (vv. 6-8) — as representative of the world's persistent hostility toward God's covenant people. Calvin noted that the church in every age faces analogous conspiracies, and that this psalm teaches believers to bring geopolitical threats before God rather than relying solely on political maneuvering.

The psalmist appeals to historical precedents of divine deliverance: "Do to them as you did to Midian, as to Sisera and Jabin at the river Kishon" (v. 9). These references to Judges 4-8 recall decisive moments when God overthrew vastly superior forces through unlikely instruments — Deborah, Barak, and Gideon. Luther drew encouragement from such precedents, particularly during the Reformation's most vulnerable years when the evangelical movement faced threats from both papal and imperial powers. The principle of sola fide operates implicitly: God's people trust not in their own military capacity but in God's proven pattern of delivering the helpless.

The psalm's conclusion reveals a surprising evangelistic purpose behind the imprecation: "Fill their faces with shame, that they may seek your name, O LORD... Let them know that you alone, whose name is the LORD, are the Most High over all the earth" (vv. 16, 18). The ultimate goal is not annihilation but recognition — that the hostile nations would come to acknowledge the LORD's supreme sovereignty. Protestant missiology has found in this concluding verse a theology of divine judgment as a means of bringing the nations to repentance, consistent with the broader biblical vision of universal acknowledgment of God's reign.
