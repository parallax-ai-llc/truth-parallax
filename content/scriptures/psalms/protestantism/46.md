Psalm 46 inspired Martin Luther's great Reformation hymn *Ein feste Burg ist unser Gott* — "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God." The psalm's opening declaration, "God is our refuge and strength, an ever-present help in trouble" (46:1), became the battle cry of the Protestant Reformation. Luther saw in this psalm the assurance that no earthly power could prevail against the church built on God's Word.

The psalm envisions cosmic upheaval — mountains falling into the sea, waters roaring and foaming (46:2-3) — yet declares that "the city of God" remains secure because "God is within her" (46:4-5). Protestant theology interprets this city not as earthly Jerusalem but as the Church, the community of believers sustained by God's indwelling presence through the Holy Spirit.

The command "Be still, and know that I am God" (46:10) is one of the most quoted verses in Protestant devotion. It calls believers to cease striving and acknowledge God's sovereignty over all nations and all history. Protestant interpreters read this not as mere relaxation but as the active surrender of control — the recognition that God's purposes will prevail regardless of human efforts either for or against them.
