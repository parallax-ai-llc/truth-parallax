Psalm 146 begins the final Hallel sequence (Psalms 146-150), each psalm opening and closing with "Praise the Lord!" (*Hallelujah*). This psalm's distinctive contribution is its sharp warning against misplaced trust: "Put not your trust in princes, in a son of man, in whom there is no salvation. When his breath departs, he returns to the earth; on that very day his plans perish" (146:3-4). Protestant political theology has drawn heavily on these verses, insisting that no human leader or institution can provide ultimate security. Luther invoked this psalm against both papal and imperial claims to absolute authority, and Reformed tradition has used it to ground resistance to tyranny.

The psalm contrasts human frailty with divine reliability: "Blessed is he whose help is the God of Jacob, whose hope is in the Lord his God, who made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that is in them, who keeps faith forever" (146:5-6). The appeal to God as Creator establishes the broadest possible basis for trust — the one who made everything sustains everything. Calvin emphasized "who keeps faith forever" as the distinguishing mark of divine reliability: human promises expire with human breath, but God's covenant endures eternally.

The catalog of God's activities in verses 7-9 reads like a manifesto of divine justice: executing justice for the oppressed, giving food to the hungry, setting prisoners free, opening the eyes of the blind, lifting up the bowed down, watching over sojourners, upholding the widow and the fatherless. Protestant social ethics finds in this list not optional charitable work but a description of God's own priorities, which the church is called to reflect. Jesus' ministry visibly enacts each item on this list, confirming for Protestant Christology that He is the God of Psalm 146 in human form. The psalm's final declaration — "The Lord will reign forever, your God, O Zion, to all generations" (146:10) — grounds these ethical commitments in the permanence of God's kingdom.
