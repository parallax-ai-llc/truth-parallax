Psalm 148 is the cosmic praise psalm, summoning every level of creation — from angels to sea creatures, from weather to trees, from kings to children — to praise the Lord. The psalm moves from heaven to earth, from the heights (148:1-6) to the depths (148:7-14), encompassing the totality of the created order. Protestant interpreters see in this comprehensive summons an expression of the theological principle that all creation exists for God's glory. Calvin's famous statement that the universe is "the theater of God's glory" finds its scriptural basis in psalms like this one — every creature is both audience and performer in the drama of divine praise.

The heavenly section (148:1-6) calls on angels, sun, moon, stars, and the waters above the heavens to praise God, grounding their obligation in their origin: "For he commanded and they were created. And he established them forever and ever; he gave a decree, and it shall not pass away" (148:5-6). Protestant theology's doctrine of creation ex nihilo — God spoke and everything came into being — is affirmed here. The stability of creation rests not on its own properties but on God's decree, a point that Reformed theology emphasizes against any view that grants nature autonomous existence.

The earthly section (148:7-14) is remarkably democratic in its summons: sea creatures, fire and hail, mountains and hills, fruit trees, wild animals, creeping things, kings and peoples, young men and young women, old men and children. No hierarchy exempts anyone from the obligation to praise. The psalm's conclusion — "He has raised up a horn for his people, praise for all his saints, for the people of Israel who are near to him" (148:14) — identifies Israel as the priestly people who lead creation's worship. Protestant ecclesiology extends this role to the church, which exists not for its own sake but to articulate the praise that all creation owes its Maker.
