Psalm 17 is a prayer of David in which he appeals to God's justice on the basis of his integrity in a specific matter. The opening — "Hear a just cause, O Lord; attend to my cry!" (17:1) — is not a claim of sinless perfection but a protest of innocence against specific false accusations. Protestant interpreters, following Calvin, distinguish between self-righteousness and legitimate appeals for vindication when one is genuinely wronged. The psalm models how believers may approach God with confidence when their conscience is clear.

The prayer's central petition employs some of Scripture's most intimate language: "Keep me as the apple of your eye; hide me in the shadow of your wings" (17:8). The "apple of the eye" (*ishon bat-ayin*, literally "the little man of the daughter of the eye" — the pupil) speaks of God's tender watchfulness, while the wing imagery recalls the protective covering of the cherubim over the ark and of a mother bird over her young. Protestant devotion has treasured these metaphors as expressing the personal, affectionate character of God's care.

The psalm's final verse is one of the Psalter's great declarations of hope: "As for me, I shall behold your face in righteousness; when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness" (17:15). Protestant eschatology reads this as anticipating the beatific vision — seeing God "face to face" (1 Corinthians 13:12) — and connects "when I awake" to the resurrection. The ultimate satisfaction of the human soul is not found in any earthly good but in being conformed to God's likeness, a hope that John confirms: "We shall be like him, because we shall see him as he is" (1 John 3:2).
