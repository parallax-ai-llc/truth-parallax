Psalm 120 is the first of the Songs of Ascents (Psalms 120-134), a collection traditionally associated with pilgrimage to Jerusalem. Protestant interpreters understand this collection as a spiritual journey from alienation to communion with God, and Psalm 120 begins that journey at its lowest point — exile among hostile, deceitful people. "Woe to me, that I sojourn in Meshech, that I dwell among the tents of Kedar!" (120:5). Luther read this psalm as expressing the believer's fundamental alienation from a world governed by falsehood, a theme deeply consonant with the Reformation's sense of standing against the established order for the sake of truth.

The psalm's central complaint is about lying speech: "Deliver me, O Lord, from lying lips, from a deceitful tongue" (120:2). The psalmist's anguish is not primarily physical but moral — he lives among people whose words cannot be trusted. Calvin connected this to the ninth commandment and the broader Reformed emphasis on truthfulness as foundational to community. The rhetorical question of verse 3 — "What shall be given to you, and what more shall be done to you, you deceitful tongue?" — anticipates divine judgment against falsehood, a theme that Protestant theology traces through to Christ's declaration that "the truth will set you free" (John 8:32).

The psalm closes with a poignant contrast: "I am for peace, but when I speak, they are for war!" (120:7). The pilgrim's longing for peace amid hostility sets the tone for the entire Ascents collection. Protestant spirituality recognizes this tension as the permanent condition of the church in the world — the community of faith seeking shalom in a culture of conflict. The journey that begins here will find its resolution only in Psalm 134, within the sanctuary of God's presence. Until then, the pilgrim walks by faith, sustained by prayer and the hope of arrival.
