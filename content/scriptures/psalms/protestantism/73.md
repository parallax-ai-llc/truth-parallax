## The Prosperity of the Wicked and the Nearness of God

Psalm 73 opens Book III of the Psalter and is among the most philosophically profound psalms, wrestling with the apparent prosperity of the wicked. Asaph confesses, "My feet had almost stumbled, my steps had nearly slipped. For I was envious of the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked" (vv. 2-3). What follows is a vivid catalogue of the wicked's advantages: no pangs of death, sound bodies, freedom from human troubles (vv. 4-5), pride and violence, overflowing wealth and mockers who say "How can God know?" (vv. 6-11). Protestant interpreters value this psalm's radical honesty about the intellectual crisis that the problem of evil creates for faith.

The turning point comes in verse 17: "until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end." In the sanctuary — the place of worship and divine revelation — the psalmist's perspective is transformed. He sees that the wicked are set on "slippery ground" (v. 18) and will be swept away "like a dream when one awakes" (v. 20). Calvin emphasized that worship, not philosophical argument, resolved the psalmist's crisis. The Protestant tradition has drawn from this the principle that theodicy questions find their answer not in logic alone but in encounter with God.

The psalm's climax is one of the Psalter's greatest confessions of faith: "Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you. My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever" (vv. 25-26). Protestant interpreters have found here a mature faith that transcends the prosperity question entirely — not because the question is answered but because God himself becomes the answer. The closing verse, "But for me it is good to be near God" (v. 28), summarizes the entire psalm's journey from envious distance to satisfied nearness.
