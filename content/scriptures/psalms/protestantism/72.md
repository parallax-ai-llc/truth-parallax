## The Reign of the Righteous King

Psalm 72, attributed to Solomon, describes an ideal king whose reign is characterized by justice, compassion, and universal dominion. The opening prayer — "Give the king your justice, O God, and your righteousness to the royal son!" (v. 1) — was historically applied to Solomon's enthronement but, Protestant interpreters insist, far exceeds what any human king could fulfill. The description of a king who "judges your people with righteousness and your poor with justice" (v. 2) and whose reign extends "from sea to sea, and from the River to the ends of the earth" (v. 8) points irresistibly to Christ.

The psalm's social vision is remarkable: "He delivers the needy when he calls, the poor and him who has no helper. He has pity on the weak and the needy, and saves the lives of the needy. From oppression and violence he redeems their life, and precious is their blood in his sight" (vv. 12-14). Protestant social theology has drawn extensively on these verses, arguing that Christ's kingdom is characterized by active concern for the vulnerable. The Reformers noted that this psalm provides the standard by which all earthly governance should be measured.

The psalm concludes with a doxology: "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Israel, who alone does wondrous things. Blessed be his glorious name forever; may the whole earth be filled with his glory! Amen and Amen!" (vv. 18-19). This also marks the end of Book II of the Psalter. Protestant interpreters see in the psalm's Messianic vision a preview of Revelation 11:15 — "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, and he shall reign forever and ever." Isaac Watts paraphrased this psalm in his famous hymn "Jesus Shall Reign Where'er the Sun," which became an anthem of the global missionary movement.
