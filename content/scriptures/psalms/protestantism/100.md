## A Psalm of Joyful Thanksgiving

Psalm 100 is perhaps the most universally known call to worship in Scripture, and its brevity belies its theological depth. The psalm commands "all the earth" to "make a joyful noise to the LORD" (v. 1) and to "serve the LORD with gladness" (v. 2). Protestant worship theology has drawn from these opening verses the conviction that worship is not a somber duty but a glad service — the word "serve" (abad) is the same word used for both worship and labor, suggesting that all of life, rightly oriented, is worship. Luther insisted that the joy commanded here is not optional emotional decoration but an essential mark of genuine faith: a heart that truly knows God cannot help but rejoice.

The psalm's central declaration is both ontological and covenantal: "Know that the LORD, he is God! It is he who made us, and we are his; we are his people, and the sheep of his pasture" (v. 3). The statement "he made us, and we are his" grounds human identity in divine creation and ownership — we belong to God by right of creation, and doubly so by right of redemption. Calvin expanded this into a comprehensive theology of human purpose: to "know" that the LORD is God is the beginning of wisdom, and this knowledge produces not anxiety but security, for we are "the sheep of his pasture," cared for by a shepherd who knows and owns his flock (cf. John 10:14).

The psalm concludes with an eternal affirmation: "For the LORD is good; his steadfast love endures forever, and his faithfulness to all generations" (v. 5). These three attributes — goodness, steadfast love (hesed), and faithfulness — constitute the covenant character of God. Protestant theology has consistently returned to this verse as a summary of the gospel's foundation: God is not fickle but faithful, not harsh but good, not temporary in his affections but enduring in his covenant love "to all generations." The psalm thus serves as both the climax of the enthronement psalms and a perpetual invitation to enter God's presence with confidence.
