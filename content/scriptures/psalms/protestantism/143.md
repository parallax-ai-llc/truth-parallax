Psalm 143 is the last of the seven penitential psalms recognized in Christian tradition, and its opening petition reveals the Protestant understanding of justification with exceptional clarity: "Hear my prayer, O Lord; give ear to my pleas for mercy! In your faithfulness answer me, in your righteousness! Enter not into judgment with your servant, for no one living is righteous before you" (143:1-2). Paul echoes this verse in Romans 3:20 and Galatians 2:16, establishing it as a cornerstone of the doctrine of justification by faith alone. Luther considered verse 2 the definitive Old Testament statement against works-righteousness: if no living person is righteous before God, then righteousness must come from outside the self — from Christ alone.

The psalmist's description of spiritual desolation is vivid: "The enemy has pursued my soul; he has crushed my life to the ground; he has made me sit in darkness like those long dead. Therefore my spirit faints within me; my heart within me is appalled" (143:3-4). Yet even in this extremity, the psalmist turns to memory and meditation: "I remember the days of old; I meditate on all that you have done" (143:5). Calvin saw here the discipline of recollection — in the darkest moments, the believer rehearses God's past faithfulness as the ground of present hope. This practice of remembering God's acts is central to Protestant spirituality.

The psalm's most distinctive petition comes in verse 10: "Teach me to do your will, for you are my God! Let your good Spirit lead me on level ground!" This is one of the clearest Old Testament references to the Holy Spirit's guiding work, and Protestant pneumatology draws on it extensively. The believer does not merely need forgiveness (verse 2) but also transformation — the Spirit must teach what the will cannot discover on its own. The psalm thus encompasses the full Protestant ordo salutis: justification (verse 2), sanctification (verse 10), and perseverance through divine faithfulness (verse 12).
