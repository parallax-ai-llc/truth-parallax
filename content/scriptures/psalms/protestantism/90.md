## The Eternity of God and the Brevity of Life

Psalm 90 opens Book IV of the Psalter and is attributed to Moses, making it the oldest psalm in the collection. Its opening declaration is among Scripture's most sublime: "Lord, you have been our dwelling place in all generations. Before the mountains were brought forth, or ever you had formed the earth and the world, from everlasting to everlasting you are God" (vv. 1-2). Protestant theology has drawn from these verses a foundational doctrine of God's eternal self-existence — He is not a being among beings but the ground of all existence. Calvin emphasized that God as "dwelling place" means that even before the tabernacle and temple, God himself was the shelter of his people, a truth that no earthly destruction can negate.

The psalm's meditation on human mortality is unflinching: "You return man to dust and say, 'Return, O children of man!' For a thousand years in your sight are but as yesterday when it is past, or as a watch in the night" (vv. 3-4). Human life is compared to grass that "flourishes in the morning" and "fades and withers" by evening (v. 6). Luther preached frequently on these verses, connecting human frailty to the consequences of the fall — mortality is not natural but judicial, the result of God's wrath against sin: "For we are brought to an end by your anger; by your wrath we are dismayed" (v. 7). This sober assessment of the human condition undergirds the Protestant insistence on the necessity of grace: creatures so transient and sin-stained cannot save themselves.

The psalm's closing petitions transform despair into prayer: "So teach us to number our days that we may get a heart of wisdom" (v. 12). This is not mere philosophical reflection on mortality but a request for divine instruction that leads to practical wisdom. The final verses plead for God's compensating joy: "Make us glad for as many days as you have afflicted us... Let the favor of the Lord our God be upon us, and establish the work of our hands" (vv. 15, 17). Protestant interpreters find here a theology of vocation — human work is meaningful not because it endures eternally on its own merits, but because God condescends to "establish" it, giving lasting significance to what would otherwise perish with the worker.
