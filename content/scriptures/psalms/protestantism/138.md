Psalm 138 opens a final Davidic collection (Psalms 138-145) with an emphatic declaration of gratitude: "I give you thanks, O Lord, with my whole heart; before the gods I sing your praise" (138:1). The phrase "before the gods" has been interpreted by Protestant commentators as David's defiant worship in the presence of rival powers — whether angelic beings, pagan deities, or earthly rulers. The "whole heart" signals the undivided devotion that Protestant theology sees as the proper response to justification by grace: because God has given Himself wholly, the believer responds with total commitment.

Verse 2 contains a remarkable statement: "You have exalted above all things your name and your word." Protestant theology, grounded in sola scriptura, finds this verse profoundly significant — God Himself elevates His Word to the highest place. Calvin drew from this the principle that Scripture's authority derives from God's own commitment to His promises. When David adds, "On the day I called, you answered me; my strength of soul you increased" (138:3), the connection between God's Word and answered prayer is established: prayer grounded in God's promises receives God's response.

The psalm envisions a universal scope: "All the kings of the earth shall give you thanks, O Lord, for they have heard the words of your mouth" (138:4). This missionary vision — kings submitting to God's Word — anticipates the Great Commission. The psalm closes with confident trust: "The Lord will fulfill his purpose for me; your steadfast love, O Lord, endures forever. Do not forsake the work of your hands" (138:8). The phrase "the work of your hands" identifies the believer as God's creation and project — a theology of perseverance grounded not in human faithfulness but in God's commitment to complete what He has begun (Philippians 1:6).
