Psalm 9 begins an acrostic pair (Psalms 9-10) and opens with exuberant praise: "I will give thanks to the Lord with my whole heart; I will recount all of your wonderful deeds" (9:1). Protestant commentators note the totality of this praise — "with my whole heart" — as a model for authentic worship. The psalm celebrates God's just judgment against the nations who oppose His people, a theme that runs through the entire Psalter and finds its culmination in Revelation.

The psalm's central theological affirmation is found in verse 7-8: "The Lord sits enthroned forever; he has established his throne for judgment, and he judges the world with righteousness." This portrait of God as the ultimate Judge who executes perfect justice is foundational to Protestant eschatology. The present experience of injustice does not negate God's sovereignty; rather, it points forward to the final reckoning. Verse 9 adds pastoral warmth: "The Lord is a stronghold for the oppressed, a stronghold in times of trouble."

The psalm also introduces the important theme of God's care for the afflicted and needy. "For he who avenges blood is mindful of them; he does not forget the cry of the afflicted" (9:12). Protestant social ethics has drawn on texts like these to argue that justice for the oppressed is not optional but central to God's character and therefore to the church's mission. The psalm's closing petition — "Arise, O Lord! Let not man prevail" (9:19) — expresses the longing for God's justice that sustains believers living between the times of Christ's first and second coming.
