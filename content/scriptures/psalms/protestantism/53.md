## Universal Corruption and Divine Deliverance

Psalm 53 is a close parallel to Psalm 14, with the significant substitution of Elohim for Yahweh and a different conclusion at verse 5. Protestant exegesis has given this psalm outsized theological importance because Paul quotes it in Romans 3:10-12 to establish the universal sinfulness of humanity. The opening declaration, "The fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (v. 1), is not merely a statement about theoretical atheism but about practical godlessness -- living as though God is irrelevant. Luther read this as a description of the natural human heart apart from grace.

The psalm's survey of humanity -- "God looks down from heaven on the children of man to see if there are any who understand, who seek after God" (v. 2) -- yields a devastating verdict: "They have all fallen away; together they have become corrupt; there is none who does good, not even one" (v. 3). This passage became a cornerstone of the Reformed doctrine of total depravity. The Westminster divines and the Canons of Dort both drew on this text to argue that apart from regenerating grace, no human being seeks God or performs genuinely good works.

The brief psalm concludes with a longing for salvation: "Oh, that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion!" (v. 6). Protestant interpreters hear in this cry a prophetic anticipation of Christ, the deliverer who would indeed come from Zion. Spurgeon preached that this verse bridges the entire Old Testament -- it is the cry of every believing heart before the Incarnation, and its answer is the gospel itself.
