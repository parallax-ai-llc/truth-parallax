Psalm 115 opens with what became a rallying cry of the Reformation: "Not to us, O Lord, not to us, but to your name give glory, for the sake of your steadfast love and your faithfulness!" (115:1). This verse encapsulates soli Deo gloria — to God alone be the glory — one of the five solas that define Protestant theology. Calvin made this principle the organizing center of his entire theological system: all human endeavor, worship, and salvation must direct glory away from creatures and toward the Creator. The psalm's opening is thus not merely liturgical humility but a fundamental theological commitment.

The extended polemic against idols in verses 4-8 is among the most pointed in Scripture. The idols "have mouths, but do not speak; eyes, but do not see" (115:5) — a devastating critique that Protestant reformers applied broadly. Luther used these verses to critique not only pagan idolatry but any religious practice that substitutes human constructions for the living God. The psalm's devastating conclusion — "Those who make them become like them; so do all who trust in them" (115:8) — articulates a principle of spiritual formation: we become what we worship. This insight has been central to Protestant pastoral theology and preaching.

The psalm's second half turns from polemic to exhortation, calling Israel, the house of Aaron, and all who fear the Lord to trust in God, "their help and their shield" (115:9-11). The threefold structure suggests that covenant faithfulness encompasses the whole community — laity, clergy, and converts alike. The blessing formula of verses 12-15 affirms that God's favor extends to "both small and great," demolishing any hierarchy of spiritual privilege. The closing verses (115:16-18) distinguish heaven as God's domain and earth as humanity's stewardship, a distinction that grounds Protestant vocation theology: the earth is entrusted to us for God's glory.
