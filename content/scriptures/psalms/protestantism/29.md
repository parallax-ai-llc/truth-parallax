Psalm 29 is a magnificent hymn celebrating God's sovereign power displayed in a thunderstorm. The sevenfold repetition of "the voice of the LORD" (29:3-9) demonstrates what Protestant theology calls God's general revelation — his power and divine nature clearly perceived through creation (Romans 1:20).

The psalm commands heavenly beings to "ascribe to the LORD glory and strength" (29:1-2). Protestant interpreters note this cosmic scope of worship: God's sovereignty extends over all created powers, angelic and natural. The storm imagery — breaking cedars, shaking the wilderness, stripping forests bare (29:5-9) — reveals a God who is not domesticated or sentimental but awesomely powerful.

Yet this same God of terrifying power "sits enthroned over the flood" and "gives strength to his people" and "blesses his people with peace" (29:10-11). Protestant theology treasures this juxtaposition: the God of omnipotence is also the God of pastoral care. His sovereign power is exercised for the blessing of his covenant people, a theme that undergirds the Reformed doctrine of providence.
