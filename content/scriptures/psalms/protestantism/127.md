Psalm 127, attributed to Solomon, opens with one of the most quoted statements in Protestant theology: "Unless the Lord builds the house, those who build it labor in vain. Unless the Lord watches over the city, the watchman stays awake in vain" (127:1). This verse has shaped the Protestant understanding of vocation, providence, and the limits of human effort. Calvin used it to argue that all legitimate work — building, guarding, commerce — depends on God's sovereign blessing for its success. The psalm does not discourage labor but reframes it: human effort without divine blessing is futile, but human effort under divine blessing is fruitful.

Verse 2 deepens the point: "It is in vain that you rise up early and go late to rest, eating the bread of anxious toil; for he gives to his beloved sleep." Luther found this verse liberating, teaching that the compulsive overwork driven by anxiety is itself a form of unbelief. The gospel frees believers to work diligently and then rest, trusting that outcomes belong to God. The phrase "he gives to his beloved sleep" has been interpreted by Protestant commentators as God's provision coming even in rest — a radical inversion of the world's assumption that only ceaseless striving produces results.

The psalm's second half (127:3-5) celebrates children as "a heritage from the Lord" and compares sons to "arrows in the hand of a warrior." Protestant family theology has drawn heavily on these verses, affirming the goodness of procreation and the raising of children as a divine vocation. The image of arrows suggests purpose and direction — children are not possessions but projectiles launched into the future by faithful parents. The concluding image of the man who "shall not be put to shame when he speaks with his enemies in the gate" (127:5) envisions the blessed household as a source of strength and influence in the community.
