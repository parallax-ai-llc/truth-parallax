## Judgment on Unjust Rulers

Psalm 58 is among the most challenging imprecatory psalms, opening with a direct confrontation of corrupt rulers: "Do you indeed decree what is right, you gods? Do you judge the children of man uprightly?" (v. 1). The term "gods" (elim) likely refers to human judges who hold divine-like authority. Protestant interpreters have read this as a prophetic indictment of all who abuse delegated power. Calvin, who devoted considerable attention to the responsibilities of magistrates, found in this psalm a divine standard against which all earthly rulers are measured.

The psalm's graphic depictions of the wicked -- born astray from the womb (v. 3), deaf as adders (v. 4), worthy of being dissolved like a snail (v. 8) -- have troubled modern readers. Protestant hermeneutics has approached these imprecations in several ways. Some interpreters, following C. S. Lewis, acknowledge the raw honesty of the psalmist's emotion while noting that Scripture records but does not necessarily approve every sentiment expressed. Others, following Bonhoeffer, argue that these curses are properly placed in God's hands rather than acted upon by humans, and ultimately find their fulfillment in Christ's judgment of all wickedness.

The psalm concludes with assurance that "the righteous shall rejoice when he sees the vengeance... Mankind will say, 'Surely there is a reward for the righteous; surely there is a God who judges on earth'" (vv. 10-11). Protestant theology maintains that the longing for justice expressed here is legitimate and will be satisfied at the final judgment. The psalm thus serves as a protest against moral relativism -- it insists that injustice matters to God and that there will be a definitive reckoning.
