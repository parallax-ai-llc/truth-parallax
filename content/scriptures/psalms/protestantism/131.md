Psalm 131 is one of the shortest and most tender psalms, a Davidic Song of Ascents that models spiritual humility. "O Lord, my heart is not lifted up; my eyes are not raised too high; I do not occupy myself with things too great and too marvelous for me" (131:1). Protestant interpreters have treasured this verse as an antidote to theological pride. Calvin, despite his rigorous intellect, commended this psalm's disposition, warning that the desire to comprehend what God has not revealed leads to speculation and error. Sola scriptura implies not only reliance on Scripture but restraint before its mysteries.

The psalm's central image is striking: "But I have calmed and quieted my soul, like a weaned child with its mother; like a weaned child is my soul within me" (131:2). The weaned child is significant — not an infant demanding milk but a child who has moved past desperate need to quiet contentment in the mother's presence. Luther saw here the mature faith that rests in God without constantly demanding answers or signs. The image challenges the activism and restlessness that can characterize Protestant piety, calling believers to a posture of simple trust.

The psalm concludes with a communal exhortation: "O Israel, hope in the Lord from this time forth and forevermore" (131:3). The movement from personal testimony to corporate call mirrors the pattern of Psalm 130, suggesting that individual humility is the foundation of communal hope. Protestant spirituality at its best holds together intellectual rigor and childlike trust, doctrinal precision and devotional simplicity. This tiny psalm, just three verses, teaches what volumes of systematic theology sometimes obscure: that the heart of faith is resting quietly in God.
