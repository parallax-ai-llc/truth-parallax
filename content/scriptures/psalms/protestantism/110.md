## The LORD's Anointed King and Priest

Psalm 110 is the most frequently quoted Old Testament text in the New Testament and stands at the heart of Protestant Christology. Its opening oracle — "The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool'" (v. 1) — was cited by Jesus himself to demonstrate that the Messiah is more than David's son (Matthew 22:41-46). Peter proclaimed its fulfillment at Pentecost (Acts 2:34-35), and the writer of Hebrews builds an extended argument upon it (Hebrews 1:13). Calvin considered this verse the clearest Old Testament testimony to Christ's divine nature — David calls his own descendant "my Lord," an acknowledgment that transcends mere human kingship and points to one who shares the divine throne.

Verse 4 introduces the second great oracle: "The LORD has sworn and will not change his mind, 'You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek.'" This declaration fuses kingship and priesthood in a single figure — something the Mosaic law strictly separated between the tribes of Judah and Levi. Hebrews 5-7 develops this extensively, arguing that Christ's priesthood supersedes the Levitical order precisely because it is of the Melchizedekian type: without genealogical succession, without end, and confirmed by divine oath. Lutheran and Reformed theology alike have drawn from this verse the finality of Christ's priesthood — there is no further priestly mediation needed beyond what Christ has accomplished, a truth that undergirds the Reformation's rejection of sacerdotal systems.

The psalm portrays the Messiah's victorious reign with vivid martial imagery: "The Lord is at your right hand; he will shatter kings on the day of his wrath. He will execute judgment among the nations" (vv. 5-6). Protestant interpreters distinguish between Christ's present spiritual reign — seated at God's right hand, ruling through the gospel — and his future consummative victory when all enemies are placed under his feet (1 Corinthians 15:25). The psalm's final image — "He will drink from the brook by the way; therefore he will lift up his head" (v. 7) — has been variously interpreted, but many Protestant commentators read it as a picture of the Messiah refreshed in the midst of battle, combining humble dependence with triumphant exaltation, a pattern that defines Christ's entire ministry from incarnation to ascension.
