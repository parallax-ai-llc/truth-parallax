Psalm 31 contains the words Jesus quoted from the cross: "Into your hands I commit my spirit" (31:5; Luke 23:46). For Protestant theology, this christological connection transforms the entire psalm into a meditation on Christ's suffering and trust in the Father. David's experience of distress becomes a prophetic template for the Messiah's passion.

The psalm describes intense suffering — David's strength fails, his bones waste away, he is forgotten like the dead (31:10-12). Protestant pastors have long used these verses to minister to those in deep depression or crisis, showing that even the greatest saints experienced anguish. The Reformation's emphasis on the "theology of the cross" finds rich expression here: God meets his people in their suffering, not only in triumph.

Despite overwhelming circumstances, David declares, "But I trust in you, LORD; I say, 'You are my God.' My times are in your hands" (31:14-15). This confession of faith amid adversity embodies the Protestant principle of *fiducia* — personal trust as the essence of saving faith. The closing exhortation, "Be strong and take heart, all you who hope in the LORD" (31:24), calls believers to courageous faith grounded in God's unfailing love.
