## Deliverance from Bloodthirsty Enemies

Psalm 59 is set in the perilous moment when Saul sent men to watch David's house in order to kill him (1 Samuel 19:11). The psalm opens with urgent pleas -- "Deliver me from my enemies, O my God; protect me from those who rise up against me" (v. 1) -- and the vivid image of enemies prowling like dogs around the city (vv. 6, 14). Protestant interpreters appreciate the psalm's concrete historical setting as evidence that biblical prayer arises from real danger, not theoretical piety.

The psalm contains a remarkable theological argument: David asks God to act not only for David's sake but for the sake of the nations. "Awake to punish all the nations" (v. 5) and "Do not kill them, lest my people forget; make them totter by your power and bring them down, O Lord, our shield" (v. 11) -- here David asks that God's judgment be visible and memorable, serving as a testimony to divine sovereignty. The Reformers saw in this a principle: God's dealings with individuals are meant to instruct the watching world.

The psalm's refrain -- "But you, O LORD, laugh at them; you hold all the nations in derision" (v. 8) -- echoes Psalm 2 and reinforces the Protestant conviction of God's absolute sovereignty over human scheming. The psalm concludes with the declaration, "O my Strength, I will sing praises to you, for you, O God, are my fortress, the God who shows me steadfast love" (v. 17). The progression from terror to praise, from vulnerability to confidence in God as fortress, represents the experiential shape of Protestant faith: honest about danger, resolute in trust, and ultimately issuing in worship.
