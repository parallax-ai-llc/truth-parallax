Psalm 118 is the climactic psalm of the Egyptian Hallel and one of the most Christologically significant psalms in the Protestant tradition. Luther called it "my own beloved psalm" and said he valued it above "all the wealth, honor, and power of the pope, the Turk, and the emperor." The psalm's refrain — "His steadfast love endures forever" (118:1-4) — frames a dramatic narrative of distress, divine intervention, and triumphant entry into God's presence. The movement from desperate prayer ("Out of my distress I called on the Lord," 118:5) to confident proclamation ("The Lord is on my side; I will not fear," 118:6) mirrors the believer's experience of justification by faith.

The most quoted verse — "The stone that the builders rejected has become the cornerstone" (118:22) — is applied to Christ by Jesus Himself (Matthew 21:42), by Peter (Acts 4:11; 1 Peter 2:7), and by Paul (Ephesians 2:20). Protestant Christology reads this as a prophecy of the Crucifixion and Resurrection: the one rejected by the religious establishment becomes the foundation of God's new temple, the church. Calvin saw in this verse a rebuke of all human authority that presumes to judge what God has chosen. The following verse — "This is the Lord's doing; it is marvelous in our eyes" (118:23) — reminds the church that the gospel reversal is entirely God's work, not human achievement.

The processional cry of verse 25 — "Save us, we pray, O Lord! O Lord, we pray, give us success!" — is the Hebrew *Hosanna* shouted by the crowds at Jesus' triumphal entry (Matthew 21:9). Verse 26 — "Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord" — became a messianic title in Protestant understanding, identifying Jesus as the one anticipated by the entire Psalter. The psalm's closing section (118:27-29) speaks of binding "the festal sacrifice" and giving thanks at the altar, imagery that Protestant interpreters connect to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice. The psalm thus spans the entire arc of redemptive history, from Israel's worship to Christ's passion to the church's eternal praise.
