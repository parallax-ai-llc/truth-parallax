Psalm 112 is the companion acrostic to Psalm 111, mirroring its structure precisely. Where Psalm 111 describes God's character, Psalm 112 describes the character of the person who fears God — a deliberate literary pairing that Protestant interpreters find deeply instructive. The righteous person reflects the attributes of God Himself: gracious, merciful, generous. Calvin noted that this parallelism teaches that sanctification is a process of being conformed to God's image, not an independent human achievement. The believer's righteousness in verse 3 ("his righteousness endures forever") echoes the same phrase applied to God in 111:3.

The psalm's portrait of the righteous person emphasizes generosity and justice: "He has distributed freely; he has given to the poor; his righteousness endures forever" (112:9). Paul quotes this verse directly in 2 Corinthians 9:9 to encourage generous giving, a connection Protestant preachers have emphasized throughout the centuries. The righteous person is "not afraid of bad news" (112:7) because "his heart is firm, trusting in the Lord." Luther found great comfort in this verse during his many trials, teaching that steadfastness in adversity is the fruit of faith rather than temperament or willpower.

The contrast with the wicked in verse 10 — "The wicked man sees it and is angry; he gnashes his teeth and melts away; the desire of the wicked will perish" — serves as a sober warning. Protestant theology reads this not as triumphalism but as a statement about the ultimate futility of life lived in opposition to God. The wicked person's anger at the prosperity of the righteous reveals the self-defeating nature of envy and rebellion. The psalm thus functions as both encouragement to believers and an evangelistic appeal to those outside the covenant.
