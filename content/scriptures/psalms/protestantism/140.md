Psalm 140 is a Davidic prayer for deliverance from violent and deceitful enemies, opening with urgency: "Deliver me, O Lord, from evil men; preserve me from violent men, who plan evil things in their heart and stir up wars continually" (140:1-2). The psalm's vivid description of the wicked — tongues sharp as serpents, venom of asps under their lips (140:3) — is quoted by Paul in Romans 3:13 as part of his indictment of universal human sinfulness. Protestant theology thus reads this psalm not merely as David's personal prayer but as a diagnosis of the human condition apart from grace.

The central petition (140:6-7) reveals the psalmist's confidence: "I say to the Lord, You are my God; give ear to the voice of my pleas for mercy, O Lord! O Lord, my Lord, the strength of my salvation, you have covered my head in the day of battle." Luther treasured the metaphor of God covering the believer's head — a picture of divine protection that does not remove the battle but shields the warrior within it. Reformed theology connects this to the spiritual warfare described in Ephesians 6, where God provides armor for the conflict that the Christian cannot avoid.

The psalm's imprecatory section (140:9-11) calls for the schemes of the wicked to recoil upon themselves: "Let the mischief of their lips overwhelm them! Let burning coals fall upon them!" Protestant interpreters understand these prayers as appeals to divine justice, not expressions of personal hatred. Calvin argued that the psalmist's desire is for the vindication of God's righteousness, not the satisfaction of private revenge. The psalm closes with assurance: "I know that the Lord will maintain the cause of the afflicted and will execute justice for the needy" (140:12). This confidence in God's justice enables the believer to leave vengeance to God and to pray even for those who persecute (Matthew 5:44).
