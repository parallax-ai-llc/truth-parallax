Psalm 43 is closely connected to Psalm 42 — many scholars treat them as a single composition. The same refrain appears here: "Why, my soul, are you downcast?" (43:5). Protestant interpreters read this brief psalm as the resolution of the spiritual crisis begun in Psalm 42, as the psalmist moves from lament toward renewed hope.

The central petition is striking: "Send me your light and your faithful care, let them lead me; let them bring me to your holy mountain, to the place where you dwell" (43:3). Protestant theology sees in "light" and "truth" a foreshadowing of Christ, who declared himself the light of the world and the truth (John 8:12; 14:6). The psalmist's longing for God's holy mountain anticipates the believer's access to God through Christ.

The psalm concludes with the psalmist resolving to praise God "with the lyre" and exhorting his own soul to hope (43:4-5). Protestant spirituality emphasizes this discipline of directed praise — choosing to worship even when emotions resist, trusting that God will yet prove faithful.
