## Sing a New Song to the LORD

Psalm 96 is a missionary psalm, calling all the earth to worship the LORD with a "new song" (v. 1). The command to "sing to the LORD, all the earth" (v. 1) breaks through any parochial understanding of Israel's faith. The psalm demands not merely private worship but public proclamation: "Declare his glory among the nations, his marvelous works among all the peoples!" (v. 3). Protestant missiology has found in this psalm a mandate for world evangelization rooted in the Old Testament itself. Calvin argued that the "new song" anticipates the gospel proclamation — new because it extends God's salvation beyond Israel to every nation.

The psalm offers a devastating critique of idolatry: "For great is the LORD, and greatly to be praised; he is to be feared above all gods. For all the gods of the peoples are worthless idols, but the LORD made the heavens" (vv. 4-5). The Hebrew word translated "worthless idols" (elilim) is a deliberate pun on elohim (gods) — the so-called gods are in reality "nothings." Luther wielded this polemic against what he considered the idolatries of medieval Catholicism, but the principle extends to every age: whatever competes with the LORD for ultimate allegiance is exposed as a manufactured nothing when measured against the Creator of the heavens.

The psalm culminates in an eschatological vision of cosmic joy at the LORD's coming judgment: "Let the heavens be glad, and let the earth rejoice; let the sea roar, and all that fills it; let the field exult, and everything in it! Then shall all the trees of the forest sing for joy before the LORD, for he comes, for he comes to judge the earth" (vv. 11-13). Protestant interpreters note the remarkable detail that creation itself rejoices at the coming judgment — because God's judgment is not arbitrary destruction but the restoration of right order: "He will judge the world in righteousness, and the peoples in his faithfulness" (v. 13). Judgment is good news for a creation groaning under the weight of injustice and corruption.
