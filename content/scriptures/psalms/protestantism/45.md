Psalm 45 is a royal wedding psalm that Protestant theology reads as profoundly messianic. The king is addressed as divine: "Your throne, O God, will last for ever and ever" (45:6). The book of Hebrews (1:8-9) applies this verse directly to Christ, making Psalm 45 a key text for the Protestant doctrine of Christ's deity and eternal kingship.

The king is described in terms of beauty, righteousness, and martial valor: "In your majesty ride forth victoriously in the cause of truth, humility and justice" (45:4). Protestant Christology sees here a portrait of Christ as prophet, priest, and king — the one who conquers not by worldly power but by truth and righteousness. His garments are fragrant with myrrh and aloes (45:8), imagery that connects to both the anointing of kingship and the suffering of the cross.

The bride of the king — "all glorious within" and adorned in gold (45:13) — is interpreted by Protestant ecclesiology as a type of the Church, the bride of Christ (Ephesians 5:25-27; Revelation 19:7-8). The psalm's vision of the king's name being remembered through all generations (45:17) is fulfilled in the universal proclamation of Christ's name across every nation and century.
