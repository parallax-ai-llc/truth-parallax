## Zion, the City of God

Psalm 87 is a brief but theologically explosive poem celebrating Zion as the spiritual birthplace of people from every nation. It opens with God's foundational commitment: "On the holy mount stands the city he founded; the LORD loves the gates of Zion more than all the dwelling places of Jacob" (vv. 1-2). Protestant interpreters note that God's love for Zion is not merely architectural affection but covenantal commitment — Zion represents the place where God meets his people, which in the new covenant is the church, "the Jerusalem above" (Galatians 4:26).

The psalm's revolutionary core appears in verses 4-6, where God enrolls the most unlikely nations as citizens of Zion: "Among those who know me I mention Rahab and Babylon; behold, Philistia and Tyre, with Cush — 'This one was born there,' they say." These are Israel's historic enemies — Egypt (Rahab), Babylon, Philistia — yet God registers them as native-born citizens of his holy city. Calvin marveled at this passage, seeing in it an anticipation of Ephesians 2:19, where Gentile believers are "no longer strangers and aliens, but fellow citizens with the saints." The Reformation's emphasis on justification by faith alone — apart from ethnic identity or legal observance — finds an Old Testament precursor in this radical inclusivity.

The closing verse, "Singers and dancers alike say, 'All my springs are in you'" (v. 7), envisions a joyful worship that transcends national boundaries. Protestant missiology has drawn on Psalm 87 as evidence that God's saving purpose was never limited to ethnic Israel but always aimed at gathering a people from every nation. The psalm thus anticipates the Pentecost vision of Acts 2, where people from every nation hear the gospel in their own tongue and are "born" as citizens of the new Zion through faith in Christ.
