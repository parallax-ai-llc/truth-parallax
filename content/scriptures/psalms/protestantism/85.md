## A Prayer for Revival and Restoration

Psalm 85 moves from grateful remembrance of past restoration to urgent petition for present renewal. The opening verses recall God's prior mercy: "LORD, you were favorable to your land; you restored the fortunes of Jacob. You forgave the iniquity of your people; you covered all their sin" (vv. 1-2). Protestant theology has found in the language of forgiveness and covering a clear anticipation of justification — God's act of covering sin that finds its ultimate expression in the imputed righteousness of Christ. Calvin noted that the Hebrew word for "covered" (kasah) in verse 2 conveys complete concealment, pointing toward the Pauline declaration that God "will not count their sin against them" (Romans 4:8, citing Psalm 32:1).

The heart of the psalm is a plea for revival: "Will you not revive us again, that your people may rejoice in you?" (v. 6). This verse has served as a rallying cry throughout Protestant revival movements, from the Great Awakening to the Welsh Revival. The theological premise is that spiritual vitality is God's gift, not a human achievement — "revive us again" acknowledges total dependence on divine initiative. Luther read this as a corrective against works-righteousness: the life of faith must be continually renewed by God's Spirit, not sustained by human effort.

Verses 10-13 contain one of the Old Testament's most magnificent theological statements: "Steadfast love and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other. Faithfulness springs up from the ground, and righteousness looks down from the sky" (vv. 10-11). Protestant interpreters have seen in this meeting of mercy and justice a prophetic description of the cross, where God's righteous demands and his merciful love are simultaneously satisfied. The four attributes that seemed irreconcilable — love, truth, righteousness, and peace — are harmonized in the atonement of Christ, where God is both "just and the justifier" (Romans 3:26).
