Psalm 38 is one of the seven traditional Penitential Psalms, expressing deep anguish under God's discipline. "LORD, do not rebuke me in your anger or discipline me in your wrath" (38:1). Protestant theology reads this as illustrating the painful but necessary process of sanctification — God disciplines those he loves (Hebrews 12:6).

David's description of his suffering is vivid and unsparing: his wounds fester because of his folly, he is bowed down and brought low, his friends stand aloof from his affliction (38:5-11). Protestant interpreters value the psalm's honesty about the consequences of sin. Unlike cheap grace that minimizes transgression, David fully acknowledges that his suffering flows from his own sinfulness: "My guilt has overwhelmed me like a burden too heavy to bear" (38:4).

Yet the psalm is not despair but prayer. David turns to God precisely in his lowest moment: "LORD, I wait for you; you will answer, Lord my God" (38:15). Protestant soteriology sees here the essential movement of repentance — from self-awareness of sin, through honest confession, to confident hope in God's mercy. The sinner's only hope is not self-reformation but divine grace: "Come quickly to help me, my Lord and my Savior" (38:22).
