Psalm 149 calls for a "new song" to the Lord, combining jubilant worship with militant imagery in a way that has both inspired and troubled interpreters. "Let Israel be glad in his Maker; let the children of Zion rejoice in their King!" (149:2). The psalm envisions worship as total embodied celebration — singing, dancing, making music with tambourine and lyre (149:3). Protestant worship traditions have varied widely in their application of these verses, from the austere simplicity of Reformed psalmody to the exuberant physicality of charismatic praise, but all agree that worship should express genuine joy in God.

The psalm's most challenging section describes the saints with "the high praises of God in their throats and two-edged swords in their hands, to execute vengeance on the nations" (149:6-7). Protestant interpreters have generally resisted literal militaristic readings, instead following the trajectory set by Hebrews 4:12, which identifies God's Word as the two-edged sword. Calvin argued that the ultimate vengeance and binding of kings described here is accomplished through the preaching of the gospel, which conquers hearts and overthrows spiritual principalities. Luther similarly spiritualized the warfare imagery, connecting it to the church's proclamation that overthrows falsehood and establishes truth.

The psalm's closing verse — "This is glory for all his saints" (149:9) — identifies participation in God's redemptive purposes as the highest honor the believer can receive. Protestant theology reads this not as human self-glorification but as the privilege of being instruments in God's hands. The "new song" of verse 1 points eschatologically to the new creation, where the tension between praise and warfare will be resolved in the eternal worship of the Lamb. Until that day, the church sings and serves, trusting that its praise and proclamation participate in God's victory over evil.
