Psalm 28 moves from desperate supplication to joyful praise, illustrating the Protestant understanding of prayer as genuine dialogue with a responsive God. David cries out to the Lord as "my rock" (28:1), a title Protestant theology connects to Christ as the cornerstone and foundation of faith (1 Corinthians 10:4).

The psalmist asks God not to "drag me away with the wicked" (28:3) — those whose words are friendly but whose hearts harbor malice. Protestant moral theology emphasizes this contrast between outward appearance and inward reality, a theme central to the Reformation's critique of religious hypocrisy. True faith is a matter of the heart, not mere external conformity.

The closing verses (28:6-9) erupt in thanksgiving: "The LORD is my strength and my shield; my heart trusts in him, and he helps me" (28:7). The final verse's pastoral imagery — "be their shepherd and carry them forever" (28:9) — is read christologically by Protestant interpreters as anticipating Jesus the Good Shepherd of John 10.
