## Overview

The Psalms have been central to Protestant worship and theology since the Reformation. Martin Luther called the Psalter "a little Bible" (*eine kleine Biblia*) that contains the whole of Scripture in miniature. John Calvin devoted more commentary to the Psalms than to any other biblical book, describing them as "an anatomy of all parts of the soul." Protestant interpretation emphasizes the Psalms as the divinely inspired prayer book, read through the lens of covenant theology and the finished work of Christ.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Sola Scriptura
The Psalms are interpreted as the inerrant, inspired Word of God. Protestant exegesis attends closely to the original Hebrew text, historical context, and the psalm's place in the canonical narrative — from creation through covenant to consummation.

### Christological Reading
Following the New Testament's own use of the Psalms (e.g., Psalm 2 in Acts 4, Psalm 22 in the Passion narratives, Psalm 110 in Hebrews), Protestant interpreters read the Psalter as fundamentally Christological. The royal psalms anticipate Christ as the Davidic King; the lament psalms find their deepest fulfillment in the suffering of Christ; the praise psalms celebrate the God revealed fully in Jesus.

### Covenant Theology
The Psalms are situated within the unfolding covenant narrative — Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, and New. The Psalter's five-book structure is often seen as mirroring the Pentateuch, embedding Israel's prayer within the framework of Torah and covenant.

## Theological Emphases

- **Justification by faith**: Psalms like 32 and 51 are foundational texts for the Protestant doctrine of justification (cf. Romans 4:6-8)
- **Providence and sovereignty**: The enthronement psalms (93-99) declare God's sovereign rule over all creation and history
- **Pastoral comfort**: The Psalms as the believer's companion in suffering, doubt, and praise — Psalm 23 as the paradigmatic text of pastoral care
- **Corporate worship**: The Psalms as the model and content of congregational singing — from Genevan Psalter to modern hymnody
