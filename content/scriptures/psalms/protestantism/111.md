Psalm 111 is an acrostic hymn of praise, each half-verse beginning with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet, celebrating the mighty works of God. Protestant interpreters have long valued this psalm for its insistence that God's character is revealed through His deeds in history. Calvin emphasized that the "works of the Lord" (111:2) are not abstract attributes but concrete acts of redemption — the Exodus, the giving of the covenant, the provision of food — through which believers come to know the God they worship. The psalm's structure itself teaches that praise should be orderly and comprehensive, leaving no aspect of God's goodness unacknowledged.

The central theological claim appears in verses 5-9, where the psalmist links God's provision ("He gives food to those who fear him") to His covenant faithfulness. The declaration that "he remembers his covenant forever" (111:5) is foundational to Reformed covenant theology. God's redemptive acts are not sporadic kindnesses but expressions of an eternal, unbreakable commitment to His people. Luther saw in verse 9 — "He sent redemption to his people; he has commanded his covenant forever" — a prefigurement of the gospel: redemption is God's initiative, commanded and established by divine authority, not earned by human merit.

The psalm's concluding verse — "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom; all those who practice it have a good understanding. His praise endures forever!" (111:10) — is one of the great wisdom statements of Scripture. Protestant theology insists that true knowledge begins with reverent submission to God's self-revelation in Scripture. This is not servile terror but the awe-filled recognition of God's holiness and grace that sola scriptura demands. Wisdom, in the Protestant reading, is never detached from doxology: understanding leads to praise, and praise deepens understanding.
