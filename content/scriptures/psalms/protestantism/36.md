Psalm 36 opens with a penetrating analysis of human wickedness — "Transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart; there is no fear of God before his eyes" (36:1). Paul quotes this in Romans 3:18 as part of his indictment of universal human sinfulness, making this verse central to the Protestant doctrine of total depravity.

The psalm then pivots dramatically to celebrate God's character: "Your steadfast love, O LORD, extends to the heavens, your faithfulness to the clouds" (36:5). Protestant theology reads this contrast as the heart of the gospel — against the backdrop of human depravity, God's love shines all the brighter. His righteousness is like the mighty mountains, his judgments like the great deep (36:6).

Verse 9 contains a luminous theological declaration: "For with you is the fountain of life; in your light do we see light." Protestant theologians from Calvin onward have seen here both the doctrine of illumination — that true knowledge comes only through God's self-revelation — and the affirmation that all life flows from God as its source. Apart from his light, humanity gropes in the darkness of sin and ignorance.
