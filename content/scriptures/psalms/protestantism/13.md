Psalm 13 is the quintessential lament psalm, packed with emotional intensity in just six verses. The fourfold repetition of "How long?" (13:1-2) gives voice to every believer who has experienced God's apparent absence. Protestant pastoral theology has drawn deeply on this psalm. Calvin wrote that David "neitherering in moderation through restraint nor gave way to despair," modeling a faith that is honest about suffering without abandoning trust.

The psalm's structure is a model of biblical prayer: lament (13:1-2), petition (13:3-4), and trust (13:5-6). The petition is urgent — "Consider and answer me, O Lord my God; light up my eyes, lest I sleep the sleep of death" (13:3) — and the stated reason is both personal ("lest my enemy say, 'I have prevailed'") and theological (God's honor is at stake). Protestant prayer theology teaches that we may appeal to God's own glory as grounds for our petitions.

The remarkable turn in verses 5-6 — "But I have trusted in your steadfast love; my heart shall rejoice in your salvation. I will sing to the Lord, because he has dealt bountifully with me" — occurs without any reported change in circumstances. The psalmist chooses to trust and praise before deliverance comes. This is what Protestant theologians call the "sacrifice of praise" — worship offered not from the mountaintop of answered prayer but from the valley of ongoing trial. Spurgeon noted that the psalm moves from "How long?" to "I will sing" in the space of six verses, demonstrating faith's transformative power.
