Psalm 128 is the companion to Psalm 127, extending the theme of the blessed household. "Blessed is everyone who fears the Lord, who walks in his ways!" (128:1). The psalm then paints a domestic portrait: "You shall eat the fruit of the labor of your hands; you shall be blessed, and it shall be well with you" (128:2). Protestant interpreters have read this as an affirmation of the dignity of ordinary work — the fruit of one's own labor, honestly earned, is a sign of God's blessing. Luther's doctrine of vocation finds strong support here: the common tasks of daily life are sacred callings when performed in the fear of the Lord.

The psalm's central image — "Your wife will be like a fruitful vine within your house; your children will be like olive shoots around your table" (128:3) — has shaped Protestant family piety for centuries. Calvin was careful to note that this blessing is not automatic but conditional on fearing the Lord and walking in His ways. The vine and olive shoot imagery connects domestic life to the broader biblical metaphor of Israel as God's vineyard and orchard. The family table, in Protestant understanding, is a place of covenant nurture where faith is transmitted from generation to generation.

The psalm concludes by linking domestic blessing to Zion's welfare: "May you see the prosperity of Jerusalem all the days of your life! May you see your children's children! Peace be upon Israel!" (128:5-6). This expansion from household to city to nation teaches that the well-being of the family and the well-being of the community of faith are inseparable. Protestant social ethics has drawn on this connection, arguing that strong families are the foundation of a healthy church and society. The psalm's final word — "Peace be upon Israel!" — echoes the benediction of Psalm 125, weaving the Songs of Ascents into a unified vision of shalom.
