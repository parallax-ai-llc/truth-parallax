Psalm 134 is the final Song of Ascents, bringing the pilgrimage collection to its quiet, nighttime conclusion. "Come, bless the Lord, all you servants of the Lord, who stand by night in the house of the Lord! Lift up your hands to the holy place and bless the Lord!" (134:1-2). The psalm addresses the temple servants who maintained the night watch, worshiping while the city slept. Protestant interpreters find in this image a call to ceaseless praise that does not depend on public visibility or congregational energy. Calvin noted that true worship continues even when no one is watching — the night watch is worship offered purely to God, without an audience.

The psalm's brevity — just three verses — belies its structural significance. As the last of fifteen Songs of Ascents, it represents the journey's arrival at its destination: the house of the Lord, face to face with God's presence. The pilgrimage that began in the alienation of Psalm 120 reaches its fulfillment in the sanctuary. Protestant worship theology values this trajectory: the Christian life is a pilgrimage from exile to home, from lament to praise, from the world's hostility to God's peace.

The final verse is a priestly benediction: "May the Lord bless you from Zion, he who made heaven and earth!" (134:3). The psalm that calls worshipers to bless God concludes with God blessing the worshipers — a beautiful exchange that lies at the heart of Protestant liturgy. The Creator of heaven and earth directs His personal blessing toward those who lift their hands in the night. Luther saw in this closing benediction the gospel promise that God meets us in our worship with His grace, turning human praise into an occasion for divine generosity.
