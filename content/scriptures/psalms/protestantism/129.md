Psalm 129 recounts Israel's long history of suffering with remarkable candor: "Greatly have they afflicted me from my youth — let Israel now say — greatly have they afflicted me from my youth, yet they have not prevailed against me" (129:1-2). The repetition emphasizes both the severity and the persistence of affliction, while the concluding clause affirms God's preserving power. Protestant interpreters from the Reformation onward have identified with this psalm's testimony, seeing in it the experience of the true church persecuted by worldly powers. Luther applied it directly to the church's struggle against papal authority, finding comfort in the assurance that affliction does not mean defeat.

The vivid imagery of verse 3 — "The plowers plowed upon my back; they made long their furrows" — depicts cruelty so severe it carves the body like plowed earth. Yet verse 4 immediately counters: "The Lord is righteous; he has cut the cords of the wicked." God's righteousness is not an abstract attribute but an active intervention — He severs the bonds of oppression. Calvin emphasized that God's timing in deliverance is sovereign; the cords may hold for a season, but they will be cut. This assurance sustained Reformed communities through centuries of persecution across Europe.

The psalm's second half (129:5-8) calls for the shame and futility of Zion's enemies, comparing them to grass on rooftops that withers before it can be harvested. The image is one of superficial growth without deep roots — a powerful metaphor for opposition to God's purposes. Protestant theology reads this not as personal vindictiveness but as confidence in the ultimate failure of all that opposes God's kingdom. The withholding of blessing in verse 8 — passersby do not say "The blessing of the Lord be upon you!" — indicates exclusion from the covenant community, a solemn warning against persecuting God's people.
