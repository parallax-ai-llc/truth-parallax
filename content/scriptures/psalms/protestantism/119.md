Psalm 119 is the longest chapter in the Bible — 176 verses arranged in twenty-two eight-verse stanzas, each corresponding to a letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Nearly every verse contains a synonym for God's revealed Word: law, statutes, precepts, commandments, rules, testimonies, word, and promise. For Protestant theology, this psalm is the scriptural foundation for sola scriptura. It does not merely recommend Bible reading; it models a life entirely oriented around, sustained by, and transformed through God's written revelation. Luther considered this psalm indispensable for understanding the Christian's relationship to Scripture, and Calvin preached extensively on its stanzas as a guide to the devotional life.

The psalm's emotional range is remarkable. The psalmist exults — "Oh how I love your law! It is my meditation all the day" (119:97) — and laments — "My soul clings to the dust; give me life according to your word!" (119:25). This combination of delight and desperation reflects the Protestant conviction that the Christian life is simultaneously one of joy and struggle, what Luther described as *simul justus et peccator*. The psalmist does not pretend to perfect obedience but continually returns to God's Word as the source of renewal: "Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path" (119:105). This verse, perhaps the most famous in the psalm, has shaped Protestant spirituality's insistence that Scripture provides sufficient guidance for faith and life.

Throughout the psalm, the Word of God functions in multiple ways: it teaches (119:12), sustains in suffering (119:50), guards against sin (119:11), provides freedom (119:45), and gives understanding surpassing that of human teachers (119:99). Protestant hermeneutics finds here a theology of Scripture's sufficiency — the Word is not one resource among many but the primary means of grace. The psalm also emphasizes the internal work of the Spirit in illuminating the Word: "Open my eyes, that I may behold wondrous things out of your law" (119:18). Reformed theology's insistence on the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit as necessary for understanding Scripture finds its devotional expression in prayers like this.

The psalm's sheer length and repetitive structure serve a pedagogical purpose. By circling back to the same themes through twenty-two stanzas, it teaches the discipline of sustained meditation on Scripture — not a single dramatic encounter but a lifelong habit of returning to the Word. The closing verse — "I have gone astray like a lost sheep; seek your servant, for I do not forget your commandments" (119:176) — ends not in triumph but in humble dependence. The lover of God's Word remains a wandering sheep in need of the Good Shepherd, a tension that Protestant theology holds as essential to authentic faith.
