Psalm 8 is one of the most theologically rich psalms in the Psalter, celebrating both God's transcendent majesty and humanity's surprising dignity. The refrain that opens and closes the psalm — "O Lord, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth!" (8:1, 9) — establishes the frame: all of creation testifies to God's glory. Protestant worship has drawn extensively on this psalm; hymns from Isaac Watts's paraphrases to modern praise songs echo its themes.

The central question — "What is man that you are mindful of him?" (8:4) — is one of the Bible's most profound. Against the backdrop of the heavens, moon, and stars, humanity appears insignificant, yet God has crowned human beings "with glory and honor" and given them "dominion over the works of your hands" (8:5-6). Protestant theology reads this as affirming the *imago Dei* — humankind made in God's image (Genesis 1:26-28) — and the cultural mandate to steward creation responsibly. The Reformers rejected any view of fallen humanity that denied this original dignity.

The New Testament application is decisive: Hebrews 2:6-9 quotes Psalm 8:4-6 and applies it to Jesus Christ, who was "made a little lower than the angels" through the incarnation and then "crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death." Protestant Christology thus reads Psalm 8 as ultimately fulfilled in Christ, the true human who perfectly exercises the dominion that Adam forfeited. The psalm's vision of creation ordered under human stewardship points forward to the new creation where Christ reigns over all things (1 Corinthians 15:27).
