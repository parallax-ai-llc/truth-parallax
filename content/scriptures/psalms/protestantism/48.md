Psalm 48 celebrates Zion as "the city of our God, his holy mountain... the joy of the whole earth" (48:1-2). Protestant theology reads Zion typologically — not as an endorsement of any earthly city's supremacy but as pointing to the heavenly Jerusalem and the Church as God's dwelling place among his people (Hebrews 12:22; Revelation 21:2).

The psalm describes God's defense of his city against hostile kings: "They saw her and were astounded; they fled in terror" (48:5). Protestant interpreters see in this God's faithfulness to preserve his Church against all opposition — a theme Jesus echoed when he declared that "the gates of Hades will not overcome it" (Matthew 16:18).

The closing meditation — "Walk about Zion, go around her, count her towers... that you may tell it to the next generation" (48:12-13) — emphasizes the importance of corporate memory and intergenerational faith. Protestant ecclesiology values the visible, historical continuity of the Church while insisting that the Church's true security rests not in institutional structures but in the God who "will be our guide even to the end" (48:14).
