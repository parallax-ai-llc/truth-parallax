Psalm 122 expresses the pilgrim's joy upon arriving at Jerusalem: "I was glad when they said to me, 'Let us go to the house of the Lord!'" (122:1). This verse has become a fixture in Protestant calls to worship, capturing the delight that should characterize the gathered community. Luther emphasized that gladness in worship is not optional piety but the proper response to God's invitation. The psalm shifts from anticipation (verse 1) to arrival (verse 2) — "Our feet have been standing within your gates, O Jerusalem!" — conveying the thrill of presence in the place where God has chosen to dwell.

The description of Jerusalem as "bound firmly together" (122:3) has been read by Protestant ecclesiology as a metaphor for the church's unity. The city where "the tribes go up" (122:4) prefigures the gathering of all God's people in worship, transcending tribal and national divisions. Calvin noted that the "thrones for judgment, the thrones of the house of David" (122:5) unite worship and justice — the city of praise is also the city of righteous governance. Protestant political theology has drawn on this connection, insisting that true justice flows from true worship.

The psalm's closing prayer — "Pray for the peace of Jerusalem!" (122:6) — has been interpreted in the Protestant tradition both literally and typologically. While affirming God's ongoing purposes for the Jewish people, Protestant interpreters also understand "Jerusalem" as pointing to the church, the eschatological city of God. The prayer for peace (*shalom*) encompasses prosperity, wholeness, and security: "Peace be within your walls and security within your towers!" (122:7). The psalm models intercessory prayer for the community of faith, reminding believers that the flourishing of the church is a matter for earnest petition, not passive assumption.
