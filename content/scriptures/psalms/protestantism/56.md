## Trust in God Against Human Adversaries

Psalm 56, attributed to David when the Philistines seized him in Gath (1 Samuel 21:10-15), is a masterclass in the movement from fear to faith. The psalmist honestly confesses, "When I am afraid, I put my trust in you" (v. 3) -- not denying fear but redirecting it. Protestant theology has embraced this verse as demonstrating that courage is not the absence of fear but the decision to trust God in spite of it. Luther, who faced mortal threats for his reforms, found great consolation in this psalm's honest wrestling with danger.

The repeated refrain "In God, whose word I praise, in God I trust; I shall not be afraid. What can flesh do to me?" (v. 4, echoed in vv. 10-11) has been central to the Protestant understanding of faith's relationship to Scripture. The phrase "whose word I praise" anchors trust in God's revealed promises -- a distinctly sola Scriptura emphasis. The believer does not trust blindly but trusts the God who has spoken, whose word is reliable and praiseworthy.

Verse 8 contains one of the Psalter's most tender images: "You have kept count of my tossings; put my tears in your bottle. Are they not in your book?" The image of God collecting human tears in a bottle and recording them in a book assures the sufferer that no grief goes unnoticed. Spurgeon preached powerfully on this verse, declaring that the God who numbers the hairs of our heads also numbers our tears. The psalm concludes with thanksgiving for deliverance and a commitment to walk "before God in the light of the living" (v. 13), a phrase Protestant interpreters connect to the new life available through faith in Christ.
