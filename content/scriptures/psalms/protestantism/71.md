## A Lifelong Trust from Youth to Old Age

Psalm 71 is the prayer of an aging believer who draws on a lifetime of experience with God. The psalmist recalls, "Upon you I have leaned from before my birth; you are he who took me from my mother's womb" (v. 6), and pleads, "Do not cast me off in the time of old age; forsake me not when my strength is spent" (v. 9). Protestant interpreters have valued this psalm for its unflinching portrayal of the vulnerabilities of aging — declining strength, encircling enemies, the temptation to despair — and for its resolute answer in continued trust.

The psalm's testimony is powerful: "O God, from my youth you have taught me, and I still proclaim your wondrous deeds. So even to old age and gray hairs, O God, do not forsake me, until I proclaim your might to another generation, your power to all those to come" (vv. 17-18). Protestant theology finds here a model of intergenerational faithfulness — the older believer's responsibility to transmit testimony to the young. Spurgeon, preaching on this psalm, urged aging Christians not to retire from active witness but to regard their accumulated experience as a treasury of evidence for God's faithfulness.

The psalm repeatedly returns to hope: "But I will hope continually and will praise you yet more and more" (v. 14). The phrase "yet more and more" suggests that praise is not diminished by age but deepened by it. The psalmist's commitment to declare God's "righteous acts" and "deeds of salvation all the day" (v. 15) reflects the Protestant conviction that the entirety of life — including its final season — is lived *coram Deo*, before the face of God, and is therefore charged with purpose and praise.
