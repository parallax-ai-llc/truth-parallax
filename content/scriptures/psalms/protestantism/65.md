## God's Abundant Provision in Creation

Psalm 65 is a magnificent thanksgiving hymn that moves from the temple to the cosmos. It opens with praise in Zion (v. 1) and an affirmation that God is the one "who atones for our transgressions" (v. 3, ESV margin). Protestant interpreters have noted this juxtaposition of atonement and creation as theologically significant: the God who forgives sin is the same God who waters the earth. The Reformers insisted that creation and redemption are not separate divine projects but unified expressions of God's sovereign grace.

The psalm's central section celebrates God's power in nature: "You still the roaring of the seas, the roaring of their waves, the tumult of the peoples" (v. 7). Calvin read this as affirming God's ongoing providential governance -- not merely creating the world and stepping back, but actively sustaining and directing it moment by moment. The parallel between natural chaos (seas, waves) and human chaos (tumult of peoples) suggests that God's sovereignty extends equally over both.

The closing verses offer a rapturous description of agricultural bounty: "You crown the year with your bounty; your wagon tracks overflow with abundance. The pastures of the wilderness overflow, the hills gird themselves with joy, the meadows clothe themselves with flocks, the valleys deck themselves with grain, they shout and sing together for joy" (vv. 11-13). Protestant harvest festivals and Thanksgiving celebrations draw deeply on this psalm's theology of gratitude. The personification of nature -- hills girding with joy, valleys shouting -- expresses the conviction that creation itself participates in praise to its Maker, an idea Paul develops in Romans 8:19-22.
