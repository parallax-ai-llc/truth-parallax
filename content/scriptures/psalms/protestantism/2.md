Psalm 2 is the first explicitly messianic psalm and is quoted more frequently in the New Testament than any other Old Testament passage. The early church applied it directly to Jesus Christ: Acts 4:25-26 cites verses 1-2 in reference to the conspiracy of Herod and Pilate, while Acts 13:33 and Hebrews 1:5 identify verse 7 — "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" — as fulfilled in Christ's resurrection and divine sonship. Protestant theology treats this psalm as foundational to Christology.

The psalm's four stanzas present a dramatic confrontation. The nations rage and conspire against the Lord and His Anointed (2:1-3), but God responds with sovereign laughter from heaven (2:4-6), enthroning His King on Zion despite all opposition. The decree of verse 7 establishes the filial relationship between the Father and the Son — a text central to Nicene orthodoxy and to the Protestant affirmation of Christ's eternal Sonship. Calvin saw here both the historical Davidic kingship and its ultimate fulfillment in Christ.

The psalm concludes with both warning and invitation. "Kiss the Son, lest he be angry" (2:12) calls the nations to submit to Messiah's rule, while "blessed are all who take refuge in him" (2:12b) extends the promise of Psalm 1 into the messianic horizon. Protestant preaching has consistently held that this psalm reveals the futility of human rebellion against God's sovereign purposes and the certainty of Christ's ultimate triumph over every opposing power.
