## The Holy King

Psalm 99 is structured around a threefold refrain — "he is holy!" (vv. 3, 5, 9) — creating a trisagion that echoes the seraphim's cry in Isaiah 6:3. The psalm opens with trembling: "The LORD reigns; let the peoples tremble! He sits enthroned upon the cherubim; let the earth quake!" (v. 1). Protestant theology has insisted that God's holiness is not merely one attribute among many but the attribute that qualifies all others — his love is holy love, his justice is holy justice, his mercy is holy mercy. Calvin argued that the trembling demanded here is not servile fear but reverential awe before a God whose moral perfection exceeds all human comprehension.

The psalm uniquely combines holiness with justice and equity: "The King in his might loves justice. You have established equity; you have executed justice and righteousness in Jacob" (v. 4). God's holiness is not abstract or otherworldly but actively engaged in establishing just social order. Luther drew from this the principle that godly governance reflects divine character — rulers are called to mirror God's commitment to equity, and the Reformation itself was framed as a recovery of spiritual justice against ecclesiastical corruption. The psalm names three great mediators — Moses, Aaron, and Samuel (vv. 6-7) — who called upon God and received answers, demonstrating that the holy God is also the accessible God.

The psalm's final verse holds together what seems irreconcilable: "O LORD our God, you answered them; you were a forgiving God to them, but an avenger of their wrongdoings" (v. 8). God simultaneously forgives and avenges — mercy and holiness coexist without compromise. Protestant atonement theology sees in this tension the necessity of the cross: God cannot simply overlook sin (he is the avenger) yet he freely pardons sinners (he is the forgiving God). These two truths meet at Calvary, where God's holy wrath against sin and his merciful forgiveness of sinners are both fully expressed in the sacrifice of Christ.
