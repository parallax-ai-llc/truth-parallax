## Give Thanks for God's Deliverance

Psalm 107 opens Book V of the Psalter with a resounding call to gratitude: "Oh give thanks to the LORD, for he is good, for his steadfast love endures forever! Let the redeemed of the LORD say so, whom he has redeemed from trouble" (vv. 1-2). The psalm then presents four vivid portraits of human distress and divine deliverance: wanderers in the desert (vv. 4-9), prisoners in darkness (vv. 10-16), the sick brought near to death (vv. 17-22), and sailors in a storm (vv. 23-32). Each scenario follows the same pattern: distress, crying out to the LORD, divine rescue, and a call to thanksgiving. Protestant interpreters have seen in this fourfold structure a comprehensive picture of the human condition — lostness, bondage, sickness, and chaos — and the corresponding comprehensiveness of God's salvation.

The refrain that unites all four sections is powerful in its simplicity: "Then they cried to the LORD in their trouble, and he delivered them from their distress" (vv. 6, 13, 19, 28). Luther found in this repeated pattern the essence of prayer — it is the cry of the helpless to the all-sufficient God. Sola fide is implicit in the structure: the distressed do nothing to earn their deliverance; they simply cry out, and God acts. Each section also includes the exhortation: "Let them thank the LORD for his steadfast love, for his wondrous works to the children of man!" (vv. 8, 15, 21, 31), establishing gratitude as the fundamental posture of the redeemed.

The psalm concludes with a wisdom reflection: "Whoever is wise, let him attend to these things; let them consider the steadfast love of the LORD" (v. 43). Calvin noted that this closing verse transforms the psalm from liturgical recitation into theological education — the wise person studies God's patterns of deliverance and draws from them a deepened understanding of divine character. The steadfast love (hesed) of God is not an abstraction but a pattern observable in history, in the lives of delivered wanderers, freed prisoners, healed sufferers, and rescued sailors. Protestant theology insists that this same pattern continues in the church through the gospel.
