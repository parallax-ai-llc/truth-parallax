Psalm 135 is a hymn of praise that celebrates God's sovereignty over creation, history, and the nations, drawing extensively on earlier scriptural texts. The psalm opens with a call to praise directed at "you servants of the Lord, you who stand in the house of the Lord" (135:2), echoing Psalm 134's night watch. Protestant interpreters value the psalm's deeply scriptural character — it weaves together language from Exodus, Deuteronomy, and earlier psalms, modeling the kind of Scripture-saturated worship that the Reformation sought to recover.

The theological center is verse 6: "Whatever the Lord pleases, he does, in heaven and on earth, in the seas and all deeps." This is one of the clearest statements of divine sovereignty in Scripture, and Reformed theology has built extensively on it. Calvin cited this verse as evidence that God's will is the ultimate cause of all things, not arbitrary but purposeful and good. The psalm grounds this sovereignty in creation (135:7 — God makes lightning, wind, and rain) and redemption (135:8-12 — the Exodus plagues, the defeat of Canaanite kings, and the gift of the land). God's power is not abstract omnipotence but saving power exercised on behalf of His chosen people.

The psalm's polemic against idols (135:15-18) closely parallels Psalm 115:4-8, reinforcing the contrast between the sovereign Lord and the impotent creations of human hands. "The idols of the nations are silver and gold, the work of human hands" (135:15). Protestant theology extends this critique beyond ancient paganism to any system — philosophical, political, or religious — that substitutes human constructions for the living God. The psalm concludes with a fourfold call to praise from the house of Israel, the house of Aaron, the house of Levi, and all who fear the Lord (135:19-20), encompassing the entire covenant community in its summons to worship.
