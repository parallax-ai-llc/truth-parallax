Psalm 150 is the grand doxology of the entire Psalter, a psalm composed entirely of praise without petition, confession, or lament. Its structure answers three questions: where to praise ("in his sanctuary... in his mighty heavens," 150:1), why to praise ("for his mighty deeds... according to his excellent greatness," 150:2), and how to praise (with every instrument and with dance, 150:3-5). The final verse — "Let everything that has breath praise the Lord! Praise the Lord!" (150:6) — serves as the climactic conclusion not only to this psalm but to the entire Book of Psalms. Protestant interpreters see in this verse the ultimate purpose of all creation: every breathing creature exists to glorify God.

The catalog of instruments — trumpet, lute, harp, tambourine, strings, pipe, cymbals — represents the full range of musical expression available in ancient Israel. Protestant traditions have debated the proper application of this list, with some Reformed churches historically limiting worship to unaccompanied psalmody and others embracing instrumental diversity. Calvin acknowledged the psalm's exuberance while cautioning that outward forms must serve inward devotion. Luther, by contrast, enthusiastically endorsed musical variety in worship, composing hymns and encouraging congregational singing as expressions of the gospel's joy.

The psalm's theological significance transcends its liturgical function. The Psalter begins with the solitary righteous person meditating on God's law (Psalm 1) and ends with the entire creation erupting in praise. This arc — from individual obedience to cosmic worship — mirrors the Protestant understanding of redemptive history: God's saving purposes begin with His call to individual faith and culminate in the renewal of all things. The repeated *Hallelujah* — thirteen times in five psalms — creates a crescendo that Protestant eschatology connects to the "great multitude" of Revelation 19:1-6, whose "Hallelujah!" echoes through eternity. The Psalter does not end with a prayer or a promise but with pure praise, teaching the church that doxology is the final word.
