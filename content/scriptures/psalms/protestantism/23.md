Psalm 23 is the most beloved psalm in the English-speaking world and occupies a central place in Protestant devotion, pastoral care, and worship. Its opening declaration — "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want" (23:1) — expresses the intimate, personal relationship with God that stands at the heart of Protestant faith. The shift from "he" in psalm titles to "my" is decisive: this is not abstract theology but personal testimony. Jesus identified Himself as "the good shepherd" (John 10:11), and Protestant Christology reads Psalm 23 as ultimately describing the believer's relationship with Christ.

The psalm moves through three scenes: green pastures and still waters (23:2-3), the valley of the shadow of death (23:4), and a banquet table in the presence of enemies (23:5). The pastoral imagery of the first section — restoration, guidance "in paths of righteousness for his name's sake" — has shaped Protestant spirituality profoundly. The valley of the shadow of death is the psalm's most dramatic moment: "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me; your rod and your staff, they comfort me" (23:4). Note the shift from third person ("he leads") to second person ("you are with me") — in the darkest moment, the relationship becomes most intimate.

The banquet scene (23:5-6) shifts the metaphor from shepherd to host: "You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies; you anoint my head with oil; my cup overflows." Protestant interpreters see eucharistic overtones here — the Lord's Table as a foretaste of the messianic banquet. The psalm's final declaration — "Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life, and I shall dwell in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6) — has comforted countless believers at life's end. The Hebrew for "follow" (*radaph*) actually means "pursue" — God's goodness actively chases the believer through life and into eternity.
