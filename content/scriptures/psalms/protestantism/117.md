Psalm 117 is the shortest chapter in the Bible — just two verses — yet its theological reach is immense. "Praise the Lord, all nations! Extol him, all peoples!" (117:1). This universal summons shatters any notion that Israel's God is merely a tribal deity. Paul quotes this psalm in Romans 15:11 as part of his argument that the inclusion of the Gentiles was always God's plan, not an afterthought. Protestant theology, with its emphasis on the sufficiency of Scripture, finds in this tiny psalm a comprehensive summary of the gospel's global scope: every nation and every people is called into the orbit of divine praise.

The reason for praise is given in verse 2: "For great is his steadfast love toward us, and the faithfulness of the Lord endures forever." The two Hebrew terms — *hesed* (steadfast love, covenant loyalty) and *emet* (truth, faithfulness) — are the twin pillars of God's covenantal character. Luther saw in this pairing the entire content of the gospel: God's love is not fickle sentiment but enduring commitment, and His faithfulness guarantees that every promise finds its "Yes" in Christ (2 Corinthians 1:20). Calvin observed that the psalm's brevity is itself instructive — the essentials of faith can be stated simply, without elaborate human additions, a principle consonant with sola scriptura and the Reformation's suspicion of theological accretion.
