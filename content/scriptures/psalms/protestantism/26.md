Psalm 26 is a psalm of David in which the psalmist appeals to God on the basis of his integrity and faithfulness. Protestant interpreters read this not as self-righteous boasting but as covenantal confidence — David walks in God's truth (26:3) because he has first trusted in the Lord (26:1). The Protestant emphasis on *sola fide* does not negate the reality that genuine faith produces a life of observable integrity.

The psalmist declares, "I wash my hands in innocence and go around your altar, O LORD" (26:6). Protestant theologians note that this ceremonial language points beyond ritual purity to the cleansed conscience that comes through faith. Calvin observed that David's confidence rests not in his own merit but in his sincere devotion to God's covenant.

Verses 9-10 reveal the stakes: David asks not to be gathered with sinners or the bloodthirsty. The Protestant reading emphasizes the doctrine of separation — believers are called to live distinctly in a fallen world. The psalm closes with David standing on "level ground" (26:12), a metaphor for the stable foundation of a life built on trust in God rather than human schemes.
