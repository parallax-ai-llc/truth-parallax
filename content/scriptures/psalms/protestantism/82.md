## God Judges the Unjust Rulers

Psalm 82 is one of the most theologically provocative psalms, depicting God presiding over a divine council: "God has taken his place in the great assembly; in the midst of the gods he holds judgment" (v. 1). Protestant interpreters have debated whether "the gods" (elohim) refers to angelic beings, human judges, or pagan deities. Calvin favored the interpretation that these are human judges and magistrates who bear the title "gods" because they exercise delegated divine authority (cf. John 10:34-36, where Jesus cites this psalm). Luther similarly understood the psalm as addressing earthly rulers who have been entrusted with the responsibility of executing justice on God's behalf.

God's indictment is devastating: "How long will you judge unjustly and show partiality to the wicked?" (v. 2). The divine charge demands justice for the vulnerable: "Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute. Rescue the weak and the needy; deliver them from the hand of the wicked" (vv. 3-4). Protestant social ethics has drawn heavily on these verses, insisting that civil magistrates bear a God-given mandate to protect the powerless. The Reformation's emphasis on the proper ordering of civil government — articulated in documents like the Augsburg Confession and Calvin's Institutes — finds scriptural grounding in this psalm's vision of accountable governance.

The psalm concludes with a sentence of mortality against the unjust rulers: "I said, 'You are gods, sons of the Most High, all of you; nevertheless, like men you shall die, and fall like any prince'" (vv. 6-7). No title or office exempts anyone from divine judgment. The final verse is a prayer: "Arise, O God, judge the earth; for you shall inherit all the nations!" (v. 8). Protestant eschatology reads this as a cry for the ultimate Judge — Christ himself — to bring perfect justice where human rulers have failed, establishing a kingdom where partiality and oppression are no more.
