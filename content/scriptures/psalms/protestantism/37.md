Psalm 37 is a wisdom psalm addressing the perennial problem of theodicy — why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer? David's counsel begins with a negative command: "Do not fret because of those who are evil" (37:1). Protestant theology reads this as a call to resist envy and anxiety, trusting instead in God's sovereign timing.

The psalm's positive commands are foundational to Protestant ethics: "Trust in the LORD and do good; dwell in the land and enjoy safe pasture. Take delight in the LORD, and he will give you the desires of your heart. Commit your way to the LORD; trust in him and he will do this" (37:3-5). These verses do not promise prosperity theology but describe the reorientation of desires that comes through genuine faith — when one delights in God, one's desires align with God's purposes.

The psalm repeatedly contrasts the temporary success of the wicked with the enduring inheritance of the righteous (37:9-11, 22, 29). Protestant eschatology reads these promises in light of Christ's teaching: "Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5, quoting 37:11). The ultimate resolution of theodicy awaits the final judgment, but the psalmist assures believers that God is not indifferent to injustice.
