## A Royal Pledge of Integrity

Psalm 101 is a royal psalm in which David pledges to govern with integrity, beginning with personal devotion: "I will sing of steadfast love and justice; to you, O LORD, I will make music. I will ponder the way that is blameless. Oh when will you come to me? I will walk with integrity of heart within my house" (vv. 1-2). Protestant interpreters have noted that David's commitment to public justice begins with private integrity — "within my house" precedes public governance. Luther emphasized that a leader's moral authority flows from personal character, not from the office itself, a principle that animated the Reformation's challenge to ecclesiastical leaders whose private lives contradicted their public roles.

David's pledges are remarkably specific: "I will not set before my eyes anything that is worthless" (v. 3); "Perverseness of heart shall be far from me; I will know nothing of evil" (v. 4); "Whoever slanders his neighbor secretly I will destroy" (v. 5). Calvin read these vows as a description of the ideal magistrate — one who actively opposes falsehood, refuses moral compromise, and protects the reputation of citizens against slander. The Reformed tradition's emphasis on godly governance drew heavily on Psalm 101, holding that civil authority is a divine calling requiring moral seriousness.

The psalm concludes with a vision of a righteous administration: "I will look with favor on the faithful in the land, that they may dwell with me; he who walks in the way that is blameless shall minister to me" (v. 6). David surrounds himself with the faithful and excludes the deceitful. Protestant ecclesiology has applied this principle to church leadership — those who serve in spiritual office should be characterized by integrity, not merely competence. The psalm's final verse — "Morning by morning I will destroy all the wicked in the land, cutting off all the evildoers from the city of the LORD" (v. 8) — expresses the king's daily commitment to justice, a standard no merely human ruler can perfectly sustain, pointing to the need for the perfect King.
