Psalm 125 offers one of the most comforting images in the Psalter: "Those who trust in the Lord are like Mount Zion, which cannot be moved, but abides forever. As the mountains surround Jerusalem, so the Lord surrounds his people, from this time forth and forevermore" (125:1-2). The double metaphor — believers are as stable as Zion, and God encircles them as mountains encircle the city — has been foundational to the Reformed doctrine of the perseverance of the saints. Calvin drew extensively on this psalm to argue that those whom God has chosen are kept by His power, immovable despite all assaults.

Verse 3 introduces a nuance: "For the scepter of wickedness shall not rest on the land allotted to the righteous, lest the righteous stretch out their hands to do wrong." God limits the duration and intensity of oppression so that His people are not pushed beyond endurance into sin. This principle — that God calibrates trials to the capacity of His people — is echoed by Paul in 1 Corinthians 10:13 and has been a mainstay of Protestant pastoral counsel. Luther preached that believers may be afflicted but never abandoned, tested but never destroyed.

The psalm's closing prayer — "Do good, O Lord, to those who are good, and to those who are upright in their hearts!" (125:4) — is balanced by the warning that "those who turn aside to their crooked ways, the Lord will lead away with evildoers" (125:5). Protestant interpreters read this not as salvation by works but as the inseparability of faith and obedience. The final benediction, "Peace be upon Israel!" (125:5), extends the promise of shalom to the whole covenant community, a fitting close for a psalm about corporate security under God's sovereign care.
