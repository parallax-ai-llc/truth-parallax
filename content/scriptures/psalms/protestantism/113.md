Psalm 113 opens the Egyptian Hallel (Psalms 113-118), the collection of praise psalms sung during the Jewish Passover and thus almost certainly sung by Jesus and His disciples at the Last Supper (Matthew 26:30). Protestant interpreters find this liturgical context profoundly significant: the psalms of Israel's deliverance from Egypt frame the institution of the Lord's Supper, connecting old covenant redemption with new. The psalm's opening imperative — "Praise the Lord! Praise, O servants of the Lord, praise the name of the Lord!" (113:1) — establishes that worship is the proper vocation of God's people across all time and space, "from the rising of the sun to its setting" (113:3).

The theological center of the psalm is the paradox of verses 5-6: "Who is like the Lord our God, who is seated on high, who looks far down on the heavens and the earth?" God's transcendence does not produce distance but attentiveness. Calvin marveled at this: the God who dwells above the heavens stoops to regard the lowliest creatures on earth. This condescension reaches its climax in the Incarnation, which Protestant Christology sees as the ultimate fulfillment of what this psalm describes.

Verses 7-9 describe God's characteristic action: raising the poor from the dust, lifting the needy from the ash heap, and giving the barren woman a home with children. Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55) draws directly on this psalm, and Protestant interpreters from Luther onward have read Psalm 113 as a charter for God's preferential concern for the marginalized. This is not works-righteousness but pure grace — God exalts those who have nothing to commend themselves, seating them "with princes, with the princes of his people" (113:8). The gospel of sola gratia is embedded in this ancient hymn.
