## The God of Vengeance

Psalm 94 opens with a startling invocation: "O LORD, God of vengeance, O God of vengeance, shine forth! Rise up, O judge of the earth; repay to the proud what they deserve!" (vv. 1-2). Protestant interpreters have been careful to distinguish divine vengeance from human vindictiveness. God's vengeance is the expression of perfect justice — the necessary response of a holy God to persistent, unrepentant evil. Calvin emphasized that the psalmist does not take justice into his own hands but appeals to God as "judge of the earth," thereby affirming that ultimate justice belongs to God alone (cf. Romans 12:19). The double invocation underscores the urgency: evil has become so brazen that the righteous can only cry for divine intervention.

The psalm catalogs the crimes of the wicked with specificity: "They crush your people, O LORD, and afflict your heritage. They kill the widow and the sojourner, and murder the fatherless" (vv. 5-6). The victims are precisely those whom covenant law most protects — the vulnerable members of society. The oppressors compound their crimes with theological error: "And they say, 'The LORD does not see; the God of Jacob does not perceive'" (v. 7). Luther connected this practical atheism to the folly described in Psalm 14:1 — denying not God's existence but his attentiveness. The psalmist's response is withering: "He who planted the ear, does he not hear? He who formed the eye, does he not see?" (v. 9).

The psalm shifts to comfort in its second half, introducing a theology of divine discipline: "Blessed is the man whom you discipline, O LORD, and whom you teach out of your law" (v. 12). Protestant theology distinguishes sharply between punitive judgment (reserved for the unrepentant) and fatherly discipline (given to God's children for their growth). The psalm concludes with personal testimony: "When I thought, 'My foot slips,' your steadfast love, O LORD, held me up. When the cares of my heart are many, your consolations cheer my soul" (vv. 18-19). The final verse affirms that God "will bring back on them their iniquity and wipe them out for their wickedness" (v. 23) — justice delayed is not justice denied.
