Psalm 126 celebrates the restoration of Zion with language so exuberant it seems dreamlike: "When the Lord restored the fortunes of Zion, we were like those who dream. Then our mouth was filled with laughter, and our tongue with shouts of joy" (126:1-2). Protestant interpreters identify this with the return from Babylonian exile but read it typologically as well — every act of divine restoration recapitulates the pattern of exile and return, captivity and freedom. Luther saw in this psalm the experience of the gospel itself: the believer delivered from spiritual bondage scarcely believes the reality of grace.

The psalmist's prayer in verse 4 — "Restore our fortunes, O Lord, like streams in the Negeb!" — suggests that the restoration is not yet complete. The Negeb's dry riverbeds that suddenly fill with water after rain provide a vivid image of sudden, dramatic divine intervention. Calvin noted that this prayer teaches the church to combine gratitude for past mercies with urgent petition for present needs. The Reformation itself, in this reading, was a Negeb moment — a dry spiritual landscape suddenly flooded with the waters of recovered gospel truth.

The psalm's closing proverb — "Those who sow in tears shall reap with shouts of joy! He who goes out weeping, bearing the seed for sowing, shall come home with shouts of joy, bringing his sheaves with him" (126:5-6) — has been one of the most quoted passages in Protestant missionary and pastoral literature. The agricultural metaphor teaches that faithful labor, even when accompanied by suffering and grief, will yield a harvest. This is not naive optimism but covenantal promise: God guarantees the harvest to those who sow in faith. The tears are real, but they are not the final word.
