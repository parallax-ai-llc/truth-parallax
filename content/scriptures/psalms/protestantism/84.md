## Longing for the House of God

Psalm 84 is among the most beloved psalms, expressing an intense longing for God's presence in worship: "How lovely is your dwelling place, O LORD of hosts! My soul longs, yes, faints for the courts of the LORD; my heart and flesh sing for joy to the living God" (vv. 1-2). The psalmist's yearning is not for the physical structure but for the God who inhabits it. Calvin emphasized that this distinction protects against superstition — the temple's value lies entirely in God's covenantal promise to meet his people there. In the new covenant, Protestant theology extends this principle: believers gather not because a building is sacred but because Christ has promised his presence "where two or three are gathered" (Matthew 18:20).

The pilgrimage imagery of verses 5-7 has deeply shaped Protestant devotional life: "Blessed are those whose strength is in you, in whose heart are the highways to Zion. As they go through the Valley of Baca they make it a place of springs; the early rain also covers it with pools" (vv. 5-6). The "Valley of Baca" (the valley of weeping) is transformed by the pilgrim's faith into a place of refreshment. Luther preached powerfully from this image, teaching that the Christian life is a pilgrimage through suffering, but that faith transforms the valley of tears into a source of spiritual renewal. The journey itself becomes a means of grace — "they go from strength to strength; each one appears before God in Zion" (v. 7).

The psalm's famous declaration — "For a day in your courts is better than a thousand elsewhere. I would rather be a doorkeeper in the house of my God than dwell in the tents of wickedness" (v. 10) — encapsulates the Protestant conviction that communion with God surpasses all earthly goods. The closing verse, "O LORD of hosts, blessed is the one who trusts in you!" (v. 12), grounds this blessedness not in location or ritual but in personal trust — sola fide applied to the life of worship.
