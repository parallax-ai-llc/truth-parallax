## A Missionary Prayer for All Nations

Psalm 67, echoing the Aaronic blessing of Numbers 6:24-26, has been called "the missionary psalm" in Protestant tradition. Its opening — "May God be gracious to us and bless us and make his face to shine upon us" (v. 1) — immediately extends beyond Israel: "that your way may be known on earth, your saving power among all nations" (v. 2). Protestant missions theology has drawn deeply on this text, finding in it the principle that God blesses His people not for their sake alone but as a conduit of blessing to the world.

The psalm's refrain — "Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!" (vv. 3, 5) — envisions universal worship that transcends ethnic and national boundaries. The Reformers saw this as anticipating the Great Commission, and William Carey, the father of modern Protestant missions, cited this psalm as evidence of God's intention to be worshiped by every tongue and tribe. The psalm's brevity belies its enormous theological scope — in seven verses it encompasses the entire divine purpose for humanity.

The connection between divine blessing and agricultural abundance — "The earth has yielded its increase; God, our God, shall bless us" (v. 6) — reflects the biblical conviction that material provision and spiritual mission are intertwined. Protestant interpreters have noted that the psalm sees harvest not as an end in itself but as a sign of God's favor meant to provoke the nations to seek Him. The concluding prayer, "May God bless us, and let all the ends of the earth fear him" (v. 7), summarizes the psalm's vision: blessing received, blessing extended, and universal reverence as the final goal.
