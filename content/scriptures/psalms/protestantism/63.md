## Thirsting for God in a Dry Land

Psalm 63, set in the "wilderness of Judah," opens with one of the Psalter's most passionate expressions of longing: "O God, you are my God; earnestly I seek you; my soul thirsts for you; my flesh faints for you, as in a dry and weary land where there is no water" (v. 1). Protestant mystics and devotional writers have treasured this psalm as expressing the essence of genuine piety -- a visceral, all-consuming desire for God himself, not merely for God's gifts. Spurgeon called this "the pearl of the Psalms" and devoted extensive commentary to its devotional richness.

The psalmist declares that God's "steadfast love is better than life" (v. 3) -- a startling claim that ranks divine love above physical existence itself. Protestant preachers have used this verse to call believers beyond superficial religion to radical devotion. The satisfaction imagery that follows -- "My soul will be satisfied as with fat and rich food" (v. 5) -- portrays communion with God as a feast that fills every hunger. The nighttime meditation described in verse 6, "when I remember you upon my bed, and meditate on you in the watches of the night," models the kind of sustained, personal devotion that Protestant spirituality prizes.

The psalm's final verses express confidence that "the king shall rejoice in God" while "the mouths of liars will be stopped" (v. 11). Protestant interpreters hear in this both David's personal faith and a Messianic anticipation. The entire psalm traces a journey from desperation (wilderness thirst) to satisfaction (divine feast) to security (God's protective right hand, v. 8), modeling the Christian experience of finding fullness in God amid barren circumstances.
