## Remembering God's Deeds in Distress

Psalm 77 is a deeply personal lament in which the psalmist wrestles with the apparent silence of God. The opening verses are raw: "I cry aloud to God, aloud to God, and he will hear me. In the day of my trouble I seek the Lord; in the night my hand is stretched out without wearying; my soul refuses to be comforted" (vv. 1-2). Protestant pastoral theology has long valued this psalm for its honest depiction of spiritual desolation. Luther, who experienced severe bouts of Anfechtung (spiritual trial), identified with the psalmist's agonizing questions: "Will the Lord spurn forever, and never again be favorable? Has his steadfast love forever ceased?" (vv. 7-8). The Reformation tradition insists that such honest questioning is not faithlessness but a form of faith struggling toward the light.

The turning point comes in verse 10: "Then I said, 'I will appeal to this, to the years of the right hand of the Most High.'" The psalmist deliberately chooses to remember God's past acts of deliverance. Verses 11-15 rehearse the mighty works: "I will remember the deeds of the LORD; yes, I will remember your wonders of old. I will ponder all your work, and meditate on your mighty deeds." Calvin noted that this deliberate act of remembrance is the essence of faith — choosing to trust God's revealed character over present circumstances. This aligns with the sola scriptura principle: the believer anchors faith not in subjective feeling but in God's objective, recorded acts of redemption.

The psalm closes with a magnificent recollection of the Exodus: "Your way was through the sea, your path through the great waters; yet your footprints were unseen. You led your people like a flock by the hand of Moses and Aaron" (vv. 19-20). Protestant interpreters find here a paradigm for all Christian experience — God's deliverance is real even when his "footprints" remain invisible. The path through the sea is simultaneously a historical event and a typological foreshadowing of baptism (1 Corinthians 10:1-2), through which believers pass from bondage into the freedom of the new covenant.
