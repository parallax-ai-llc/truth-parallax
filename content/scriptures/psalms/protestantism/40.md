Psalm 40 begins with testimony of answered prayer — "I waited patiently for the LORD; he turned to me and heard my cry" (40:1) — before moving to a profound statement about the nature of true worship. Protestant theology gives special attention to verses 6-8: "Sacrifice and offering you did not desire — but my ears you have opened — burnt offerings and sin offerings you did not require. Then I said, 'Here I am, I have come — it is written about me in the scroll.'"

The book of Hebrews (10:5-10) applies these verses directly to Christ, reading them as the Son's willing obedience to the Father's redemptive plan. Protestant Christology sees Psalm 40 as a messianic psalm in which David's experience prefigures Christ's perfect obedience. The shift from external sacrifice to heart-obedience anticipates the Reformation's emphasis on inward faith over outward ritual.

The psalm's second half (40:11-17) returns to urgent petition, revealing that the life of faith includes ongoing struggle even after great deliverances. David does not rest on past experiences but continues to cry out for present help: "Do not withhold your mercy from me, LORD" (40:11). This pattern of gratitude and renewed dependence characterizes authentic Protestant spirituality.
