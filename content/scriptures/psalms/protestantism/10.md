Psalm 10 is the companion to Psalm 9, completing the acrostic pattern, and opens with one of Scripture's most raw questions: "Why, O Lord, do you stand far away? Why do you hide yourself in times of trouble?" (10:1). Protestant theology has never shied away from this question. The lament psalms validate honest wrestling with God's apparent silence, and Reformed thinkers from Calvin to modern pastors have commended this psalm to sufferers as a model of faithful complaint.

The extended portrait of the wicked in verses 2-11 is among the most detailed in the Psalter. The wicked man "boasts of the desires of his soul" (10:3), "says in his heart, 'God has forgotten'" (10:11), and "lurks in ambush like a lion" to prey on the helpless (10:9). Protestant interpreters see here a theology of practical atheism — the wicked person may not deny God's existence theoretically but lives as if God does not see or act. This analysis of sin as functional godlessness has been influential in Protestant apologetics and pastoral counseling.

The psalm's resolution comes in verses 14-18, where the psalmist reaffirms that God indeed sees: "But you do see, for you note mischief and vexation" (10:14). The closing verses declare that "the Lord is king forever and ever" (10:16) and that He hears the desire of the afflicted, doing justice "to the fatherless and the oppressed, so that man who is of the earth may strike terror no more" (10:18). Protestant eschatology holds that this promise finds partial fulfillment in God's providential governance of history and ultimate fulfillment in Christ's return.
