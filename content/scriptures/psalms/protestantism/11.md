Psalm 11 opens with a bold declaration of trust against counsel to flee: "In the Lord I put my trust; how can you say to my soul, 'Flee like a bird to your mountain'?" (11:1). Protestant interpreters have read this as David's response to well-meaning but faithless advisors who urged him to abandon his post when danger loomed. Luther saw here the temptation every believer faces to rely on human strategies rather than standing firm in faith.

The psalm's central image — "The Lord is in his holy temple; the Lord's throne is in heaven; his eyes see, his eyelids test the children of man" (11:4) — establishes divine omniscience as the ground of the believer's courage. God is not absent from the chaos of earthly events; He observes and tests. The phrase "the Lord tests the righteous" (11:5) introduces the Protestant understanding of sanctification through trial, a theme developed extensively in James 1:2-4 and 1 Peter 1:6-7. Suffering is not evidence of God's absence but of His refining purpose.

The psalm concludes with both judgment and hope: God rains "fire and sulfur and a scorching wind" on the wicked (11:6), echoing the destruction of Sodom, while "the upright shall behold his face" (11:7). This final phrase — beholding God's face — is the deepest longing of biblical faith and the ultimate promise of the gospel. The Westminster Shorter Catechism's first answer — that man's chief end is "to glorify God and enjoy Him forever" — finds its scriptural warrant in verses like this, where seeing God face to face is the consummation of all hope.
