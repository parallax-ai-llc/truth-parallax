## God's Triumphal March

Psalm 68 is one of the most complex and exultant psalms, tracing God's march from Sinai to Zion as a divine warrior. Its opening — "God shall arise, his enemies shall be scattered" (v. 1), echoing Numbers 10:35 — sets a tone of irresistible divine advance. Protestant interpreters have read this psalm as a panoramic survey of redemptive history, from the Exodus through the conquest to the establishment of the temple, all pointing forward to Christ's ultimate triumph.

Verse 18 — "You ascended on high, leading a host of captives in your train and receiving gifts among men" — is quoted by Paul in Ephesians 4:8 as fulfilled in Christ's ascension. This Christological reading is central to Protestant exegesis of the psalm. The Reformers saw in God's ascent to Zion a type of Christ's ascension to heaven, where He received the gift of the Spirit to distribute to His Church. This verse thus connects the psalm to Pentecost and to the ongoing distribution of spiritual gifts within the body of Christ.

The psalm also celebrates God as "father of the fatherless and protector of widows" (v. 5), who "settles the solitary in a home" and "leads out the prisoners to prosperity" (v. 6). Protestant social ethics has drawn on these verses to argue that care for the vulnerable is not peripheral but central to God's character and therefore to the Church's mission. The psalm's concluding doxology — "Awesome is God from his sanctuary... he is the one who gives power and strength to his people. Blessed be God!" (vv. 35) — captures the Protestant conviction that divine power and human empowerment are not in tension but flow naturally from God's sovereign goodness.
