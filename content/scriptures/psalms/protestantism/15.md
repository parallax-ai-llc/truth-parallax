Psalm 15 poses the foundational question of all religion: "O Lord, who shall sojourn in your tent? Who shall dwell on your holy hill?" (15:1). The answer provides an ethical profile of the person fit for God's presence. Protestant interpreters have read this psalm through the lens of the gospel: while the standard described is real and binding, no one meets it perfectly apart from Christ. The psalm thus simultaneously reveals God's holy requirements and drives the reader to seek grace.

The character described in verses 2-5 encompasses speech, relationships, and commerce. The righteous person "walks blamelessly and does what is right and speaks truth in his heart" (15:2), "does not slander with his tongue" (15:3), "honors those who fear the Lord" (15:4), and "does not put out his money at interest" against the poor (15:5). Protestant ethics has drawn on this psalm to insist that genuine faith produces observable moral fruit — not as the ground of salvation but as its evidence, consistent with James 2:17.

The psalm's closing promise — "He who does these things shall never be moved" (15:5b) — offers the assurance of stability and permanence in God's presence. Protestant theology connects this immovability to the believer's union with Christ: because Christ perfectly fulfills the requirements of Psalm 15, those united to Him by faith share in His standing before the Father. The psalm thus functions as both moral instruction and gospel pointer, revealing what God requires and Who alone truly provides it.
