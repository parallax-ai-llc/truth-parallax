Psalm 121 is among the most cherished psalms in Protestant worship, a Song of Ascents that answers the pilgrim's anxiety with unwavering assurance of divine protection. The opening question — "I lift up my eyes to the hills. From where does my help come?" (121:1) — has been read by Protestant interpreters not as gazing toward the hills for help but as looking past them, beyond earthly security, to their true source. The answer is immediate and definitive: "My help comes from the Lord, who made heaven and earth" (121:2). The Creator of all things is personally invested in the safety of the individual believer.

The psalm's dominant word is "keep" (*shamar*), repeated six times in eight verses. "He who keeps Israel will neither slumber nor sleep" (121:4). Calvin found this repetition deeply pastoral: God's vigilance is total, ceaseless, and personal. The shift from the pilgrim's self-assurance in verse 2 to the priestly blessing of verses 3-8 suggests a liturgical exchange — the worshiper declares faith, and a minister responds with God's promise. Protestant worship has preserved this dialogical structure, recognizing that assurance comes not from self-generated confidence but from hearing God's Word spoken over us.

The psalm's specificity is striking: God keeps you from "all evil" (121:7), preserves "your going out and your coming in" (121:8), guards you by day from the sun and by night from the moon (121:6). This comprehensive coverage — temporal, spatial, cosmic — leaves no gap in divine protection. Reformed theology cautions against reading this as a guarantee of physical immunity from harm, pointing instead to the deeper keeping of the soul: "He will keep your life" (121:7). The psalm's final phrase — "from this time forth and forevermore" — extends God's keeping beyond death itself, a promise that undergirds the Protestant doctrine of the perseverance of the saints.
