## The LORD's Salvation Revealed to the Nations

Psalm 98 opens with the same exhortation as Psalm 96 — "Oh sing to the LORD a new song, for he has done marvelous things!" — but immediately specifies the cause: "His right hand and his holy arm have worked salvation for him" (v. 1). The salvation is accomplished by God alone, without human assistance — "his right hand and his holy arm" emphasize divine self-sufficiency. Protestant soteriology finds here a vivid Old Testament expression of sola gratia: salvation is God's work from beginning to end. Calvin emphasized that the "new song" corresponds to a new act of salvation, and that the ultimate new song celebrates the definitive salvation accomplished in Christ.

The psalm emphasizes the public and universal character of God's saving work: "The LORD has made known his salvation; he has revealed his righteousness in the sight of the nations. He has remembered his steadfast love and faithfulness to the house of Israel. All the ends of the earth have seen the salvation of our God" (vv. 2-3). The movement from particular (Israel) to universal (all the ends of the earth) traces the trajectory of redemptive history from covenant to gospel. Luther found in these verses the theological basis for translating the Bible into the vernacular — if God intends all nations to see his salvation, then his word must be accessible to all peoples in their own languages.

The psalm's closing verses summon all creation to praise: "Let the sea roar, and all that fills it; the world and those who dwell in it! Let the rivers clap their hands; let the hills sing for joy together before the LORD, for he comes to judge the earth" (vv. 7-9). The personification of rivers and hills as worshipers expresses a vision of cosmic redemption — not just human salvation but the renewal of all creation. Protestant interpreters connect this to Paul's teaching in Romans 8:19-22 that creation itself "waits with eager longing" for the revelation of God's children, when it too will be "set free from its bondage to corruption."
