Psalm 16 is a *miktam* of David and one of the most important messianic psalms in Protestant theology. Peter's sermon at Pentecost (Acts 2:25-31) and Paul's preaching at Pisidian Antioch (Acts 13:35-37) both cite verses 9-11 as prophecy fulfilled in Christ's resurrection. The argument is that David himself died and was buried, so the promise "you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" (16:10) must refer to David's greater Son.

The psalm begins with trust — "Preserve me, O God, for in you I take refuge" (16:1) — and moves through a profound confession of satisfaction in God alone. Verse 2, "I say to the Lord, 'You are my Lord; I have no good apart from you,'" has been central to Reformed devotion. Verses 5-6 employ the language of inheritance: "The Lord is my chosen portion and my cup; you hold my lot. The lines have fallen for me in pleasant places; indeed, I have a beautiful inheritance." Where Israel's tribes received territorial allotments, the psalmist's inheritance is God Himself.

The climactic declaration in verses 9-11 expresses a joy that transcends death: "Therefore my heart is glad, and my whole being rejoices; my flesh also dwells secure... You make known to me the path of life; in your presence there is fullness of joy; at your right hand are pleasures forevermore." Protestant theology reads this as the earliest clear testimony to resurrection hope in the Psalter. The "fullness of joy" and "pleasures forevermore" at God's right hand describe not merely temporal blessing but eternal life — the hope that sustains believers through every earthly trial.
