## Betrayal by a Close Companion

Psalm 55 is one of the most emotionally raw prayers in the Psalter, expressing the anguish of betrayal by a trusted friend. The psalmist's opening cry -- "Give ear to my prayer, O God, and hide not yourself from my plea" (v. 1) -- is followed by a vivid description of inner turmoil: "My heart is in anguish within me; the terrors of death have fallen upon me" (v. 4). Protestant interpreters have valued this psalm for its honesty about suffering, seeing in it evidence that faith does not preclude emotional devastation.

The psalm's most piercing verses describe the betrayal: "For it is not an enemy who taunts me -- then I could bear it... But it is you, a man, my equal, my companion, my familiar friend. We used to take sweet counsel together; within God's house we walked in the throng" (vv. 12-14). Protestant commentators from Luther to Bonhoeffer have seen here a type of Judas's betrayal of Christ, and more broadly, the particular agony that comes when trust within the covenant community is violated. The Reformers knew this pain personally, having experienced betrayal by former allies.

Verse 22 -- "Cast your burden on the LORD, and he will sustain you; he will never permit the righteous to be moved" -- is one of the most quoted verses in Protestant devotional literature. Peter echoes it in 1 Peter 5:7, and Protestant preachers have consistently pointed to this verse as the practical remedy for anxiety. Spurgeon called it "the casting-care verse" and urged believers to treat their worries as burdens to be literally transferred to God. The psalm's theological logic is clear: because God is faithful and sovereign (v. 19), the believer can entrust even the deepest relational wounds to divine care.
