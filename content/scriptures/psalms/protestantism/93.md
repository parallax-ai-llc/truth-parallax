## The LORD Reigns

Psalm 93 inaugurates the "enthronement psalms" (93-99), which celebrate the LORD's universal kingship. Its opening declaration is majestic in its simplicity: "The LORD reigns; he is robed in majesty; the LORD is robed; he has put on strength as his belt. Yes, the world is established; it shall never be moved" (v. 1). Protestant theology has insisted that God's reign is not a future aspiration but a present reality — He reigns now, over all things, regardless of appearances to the contrary. Calvin saw in this affirmation the antidote to anxiety: if the LORD reigns, then no political upheaval, no ecclesiastical corruption, and no personal catastrophe falls outside his sovereign governance.

The psalm depicts the chaotic waters rising against God's established order: "The floods have lifted up, O LORD, the floods have lifted up their voice; the floods lift up their roaring. Mightier than the thunders of many waters, mightier than the waves of the sea, the LORD on high is mighty!" (vv. 3-4). Ancient Near Eastern mythology personified the sea as a chaotic divine force, but this psalm demythologizes the waters — they are merely creatures, and the LORD is incomparably mightier. Luther drew from this imagery a profound comfort for the embattled church: the floods of opposition may roar, but they cannot topple the throne of God.

The psalm concludes by linking cosmic sovereignty to ethical holiness: "Your decrees are very trustworthy; holiness befits your house, O LORD, forevermore" (v. 5). Protestant interpreters have emphasized this connection — God's power is not arbitrary but governed by his decrees, which are "very trustworthy." The sola scriptura principle finds resonance here: God's word (his decrees) is the reliable expression of his sovereign will, and the holiness of his dwelling reflects the moral character of his rule. Power and purity are united in the God who reigns.
