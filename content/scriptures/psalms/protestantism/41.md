Psalm 41 opens with a beatitude: "Blessed are those who have regard for the weak; the LORD delivers them in times of trouble" (41:1). Protestant social ethics draws on this verse to emphasize that genuine faith produces compassion for the vulnerable — a theme James develops when he insists that faith without works is dead (James 2:17).

The psalm's most poignant verse describes betrayal by a close friend: "Even my close friend, someone I trusted, one who shared my bread, has turned against me" (41:9). Jesus applies this verse directly to Judas's betrayal at the Last Supper (John 13:18), giving Psalm 41 a prominent place in Protestant Passion theology. David's experience of intimate treachery becomes a prophetic foreshadowing of Christ's suffering.

Psalm 41 concludes Book I of the Psalter with a doxology: "Praise be to the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting. Amen and Amen" (41:13). Protestant interpreters note that despite all the lament, suffering, and betrayal recorded in Psalms 1-41, the first book closes with praise — affirming that worship, not complaint, has the final word in the life of faith.
