Psalm 145 is David's final psalm in the Psalter, an acrostic hymn that serves as the bridge between the Davidic collection and the concluding doxology of Psalms 146-150. Its opening commitment — "I will extol you, my God and King, and bless your name forever and ever. Every day I will bless you and praise your name forever and ever" (145:1-2) — establishes praise as a daily discipline, not an occasional impulse. Protestant worship theology draws on this psalm's emphasis on regularity and comprehensiveness: praise should be daily, perpetual, and encompassing all of God's attributes.

The psalm's theological richness is concentrated in verses 8-9: "The Lord is gracious and merciful, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love. The Lord is good to all, and his mercy is over all that he has made." These verses, echoing the great self-revelation of Exodus 34:6-7, present God's character as fundamentally gracious. Calvin used this passage to argue that God's severity in judgment is always subordinate to His mercy — wrath is His "strange work," while grace is His proper work. The universal scope of God's goodness ("good to all") has been significant in Protestant discussions of common grace and general revelation.

The kingdom theme dominates verses 10-13: "Your kingdom is an everlasting kingdom, and your dominion endures throughout all generations" (145:13). Protestant eschatology reads this as pointing to Christ's eternal reign, inaugurated at the Resurrection and consummated at His return. The psalm's closing section (145:14-21) catalogs God's care for the fallen, the hungry, the oppressed, and all who call upon Him "in truth" (145:18). Luther found in verse 16 — "You open your hand; you satisfy the desire of every living thing" — the basis for daily trust in God's provision, connecting it to the Lord's Prayer petition for daily bread. The psalm ends as it began, with praise, establishing the pattern for the Psalter's final crescendo.
