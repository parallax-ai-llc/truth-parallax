Psalm 21 is the companion to Psalm 20, forming a petition-and-thanksgiving pair. Where Psalm 20 prayed for victory before battle, Psalm 21 celebrates the victory God has granted. The opening — "O Lord, in your strength the king rejoices, and in your salvation how greatly he exults!" (21:1) — attributes the king's triumph entirely to God's power, a principle central to the Protestant doctrine of *soli Deo gloria*.

The blessings catalogued in verses 2-7 encompass both material prosperity and spiritual depth. God has granted the king "his heart's desire" (21:2), set "a crown of fine gold upon his head" (21:3), and given him "length of days forever and ever" (21:4). Protestant messianic interpretation notes that while Davidic kings received temporal blessings, the promise of eternal life in verse 4 transcends any earthly monarchy and points to Christ, who alone reigns forever. Verse 6 — "you make him most blessed forever; you make him glad with the joy of your presence" — describes a blessedness that no mortal king fully experienced.

The psalm's second half (21:8-12) turns to the fate of the king's enemies, described in vivid language of fire and destruction. Protestant interpreters understand this as both a historical description of military victory and a prophetic picture of Christ's final triumph over all opposition. The closing verse — "Be exalted, O Lord, in your strength! We will sing and praise your power" (21:13) — returns to the theme of divine strength, ensuring that the psalm ends as it began: with God, not the king, receiving all glory for the victory won.
