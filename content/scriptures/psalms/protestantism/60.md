## Prayer After Military Defeat

Psalm 60 is a national lament following a military defeat, attributed to David during campaigns against Aram-Naharaim, Aram-Zobah, and Edom (cf. 2 Samuel 8). The psalm opens with the startling confession, "O God, you have rejected us, broken our defenses; you have been angry; oh, restore us" (v. 1). Protestant theology values this psalm's honesty about divine discipline -- it does not deny that God sometimes allows His people to suffer defeat, and it does not pretend such experiences are painless.

The image of God making the land "to quake" and giving His people "wine to drink that made us stagger" (v. 3) speaks to the disorienting experience of divine judgment. Yet the psalm immediately pivots: "You have set up a banner for those who fear you, that they may flee to it from the bow" (v. 4). Calvin found here the principle that even in judgment, God provides a rallying point for the faithful -- a banner under which they can regroup. Protestant interpreters see this banner as a type of Christ, who is the rallying point for all who are broken and scattered.

The oracle in verses 6-8, where God claims sovereignty over Shechem, Succoth, Gilead, Manasseh, Ephraim, Moab, Edom, and Philistia, is a powerful declaration that the land belongs to God regardless of military outcomes. The psalm concludes with both a plea -- "Oh, grant us help against the foe" (v. 11) -- and a confession of faith: "With God we shall do valiantly, for it is he who will tread down our foes" (v. 12). This combination of honest petition and resolute confidence characterizes the Protestant approach to national and corporate prayer in times of crisis.
