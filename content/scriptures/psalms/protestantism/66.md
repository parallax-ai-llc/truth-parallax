## Jubilant Praise for God's Mighty Deeds

Psalm 66 begins with a universal call — "Shout for joy to God, all the earth!" (v. 1) — and moves from corporate remembrance of the Exodus to personal testimony of answered prayer. This dual structure has been significant for Protestant worship, which values both the communal rehearsal of salvation history and the individual believer's witness. The psalm's recollection of the Red Sea crossing — "He turned the sea into dry land; they passed through the river on foot" (v. 6) — grounds praise in historical event, not abstract theology.

The psalm's middle section acknowledges that God tests His people: "For you, O God, have tested us; you have tried us as silver is tried. You brought us into the net; you laid a crushing burden on our backs" (vv. 10-11). Protestant interpreters have valued this frank acknowledgment that divine testing is purposeful, not arbitrary. The metaphor of refining silver implies that suffering removes impurities and produces something precious. The Puritans drew extensively on this imagery in their theology of sanctification through trials.

The psalm shifts to first-person singular in verse 13: "I will come into your house with burnt offerings; I will perform my vows to you." The psalmist then testifies, "Come and hear, all you who fear God, and I will tell what he has done for my soul" (v. 16). This public testimony — declaring God's specific acts of deliverance — is central to Protestant piety. The psalm concludes with a principle: "If I had cherished iniquity in my heart, the Lord would not have listened" (v. 18), underscoring the Protestant conviction that genuine faith produces moral transformation, and unconfessed sin hinders prayer.
