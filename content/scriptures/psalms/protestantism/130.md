Psalm 130, known by its Latin incipit *De Profundis*, is one of the seven penitential psalms and one of the most theologically rich texts in the entire Psalter. Luther ranked it among his favorite psalms and called it a "Pauline psalm" because of its clear articulation of grace. The opening cry — "Out of the depths I cry to you, O Lord! Lord, hear my voice!" (130:1-2) — expresses the human condition before God with raw honesty. The "depths" are not merely emotional distress but the abyss of sin and guilt from which no human effort can extract the soul. Protestant theology finds in this cry the starting point of all genuine faith: the acknowledgment of helplessness.

Verse 3 asks the decisive question: "If you, O Lord, should mark iniquities, O Lord, who could stand?" The implied answer — no one — demolishes every system of self-justification. But verse 4 delivers the gospel: "But with you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared." Luther noted the surprising connection between forgiveness and fear: it is precisely because God forgives that He is rightly feared. Cheap grace produces presumption; costly grace — forgiveness freely given at infinite cost — produces reverent awe. This insight stands at the heart of Reformation soteriology and the doctrine of justification by faith alone.

The psalm's second half turns from confession to hope: "I wait for the Lord, my soul waits, and in his word I hope; my soul waits for the Lord more than watchmen for the morning" (130:5-6). The waiting is not passive but active, anchored in God's Word — a thoroughly Protestant posture. The psalm concludes with a call to the entire community: "O Israel, hope in the Lord! For with the Lord there is steadfast love, and with him is plentiful redemption. And he will redeem Israel from all his iniquities" (130:7-8). The movement from individual confession to corporate hope mirrors the Protestant conviction that personal faith always finds its home in the gathered church.
