Psalm 7 is a *shiggaion* — a term of uncertain meaning, possibly indicating an impassioned or wandering song — attributed to David in connection with "Cush, a Benjaminite." Protestant scholars have debated this figure's identity, with many linking it to Saul's persecution of David. The psalm is a vigorous appeal for divine justice, opening with the urgent cry: "O Lord my God, in you do I take refuge; save me from all my pursuers" (7:1).

David's oath of innocence in verses 3-5 is striking: he invites God's judgment on himself if he has committed the wrongs his accuser alleges. This self-imprecation is not a claim of sinless perfection but an assertion of innocence on the specific charge. Protestant theology distinguishes between general sinfulness (which David confesses elsewhere, notably in Psalm 51) and innocence in a particular matter. Calvin noted that believers may rightly appeal to God's justice when falsely accused, without contradicting the doctrine of total depravity.

The psalm's theological center is the portrait of God as righteous Judge (7:11-13). God "feels indignation every day" toward the wicked and has prepared instruments of judgment. Yet verses 14-16 reveal that the wicked ultimately destroy themselves: "He who digs a pit will fall into it" (7:15). This principle of self-recoiling evil is a major biblical theme that Protestant ethics uses to explain how divine justice operates within history. The psalm closes with praise — "I will give to the Lord the thanks due to his righteousness" (7:17) — affirming that trust in God's justice, not personal vengeance, is the proper response to injustice.
