## Israel's Persistent Rebellion and God's Persistent Mercy

Psalm 106 forms a deliberate counterpart to Psalm 105. Where Psalm 105 celebrates God's faithfulness, Psalm 106 confesses Israel's faithlessness. The psalm opens with praise — "Praise the LORD! Oh give thanks to the LORD, for he is good, for his steadfast love endures forever!" (v. 1) — but immediately shifts to confession: "Both we and our fathers have sinned; we have committed iniquity; we have done wickedness" (v. 6). Protestant interpreters have valued this psalm's corporate confession, recognizing that the church's sins are not merely individual but generational. Calvin emphasized that the psalmist includes himself — "we and our fathers" — acknowledging solidarity with the sinful history he recounts.

The catalog of Israel's rebellions is extensive and damning: complaining at the Red Sea (v. 7), craving meat in the wilderness (v. 14), envying Moses and Aaron (v. 16), fashioning the golden calf (v. 19), despising the promised land (v. 24), joining in Baal worship at Peor (v. 28), and sacrificing their children to Canaanite idols (vv. 37-38). Luther drew from this relentless catalog the doctrine of total depravity — not that humans are incapable of any good, but that sin pervades every aspect of human nature and recurs despite the most dramatic divine interventions. Even the parting of the Red Sea could not secure lasting faithfulness.

Yet the psalm's refrain is mercy: "Many times he delivered them, but they were rebellious in their purposes and were brought low through their iniquity. Nevertheless, he looked upon their distress, when he heard their cry. For their sake he remembered his covenant, and relented according to the abundance of his steadfast love" (vv. 43-45). The word "nevertheless" is the gospel in miniature — despite everything, God remembers his covenant. Protestant soteriology finds here the pattern of grace that culminates in Christ: human sin is persistent and comprehensive, but God's covenant love is more persistent still. The psalm closes with a doxology — "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting!" (v. 48) — sealing Book IV of the Psalter with praise.
