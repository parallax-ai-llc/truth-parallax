Psalm 22 is the supreme messianic psalm of suffering and the most extensively quoted psalm in the New Testament Passion narratives. Jesus Himself cried its opening words from the cross: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1; Matthew 27:46). Protestant Christology reads the entire psalm as a prophetic portrait of the crucifixion, finding specific fulfillments in the mockery of bystanders (22:7-8; Matthew 27:39-43), the piercing of hands and feet (22:16), the division of garments by lot (22:18; John 19:23-24), and the extreme dehydration of the sufferer (22:15).

The first half of the psalm (22:1-21) plumbs the depths of messianic suffering with an intensity unmatched in the Old Testament. The cry of forsakenness in verse 1 has been the subject of extensive Protestant theological reflection. Luther understood it as Christ truly experiencing the full weight of divine wrath against sin — not merely feeling forsaken but being forsaken in His substitutionary role. This interpretation is central to the penal substitutionary theory of atonement that defines much of Protestant soteriology. The vivid description of physical torment — "I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint" (22:14) — corresponds precisely to the agonies of crucifixion centuries before that method of execution was invented.

The dramatic turn at verse 22 — "I will tell of your name to my brothers; in the midst of the congregation I will praise you" — shifts from suffering to triumphant praise. Hebrews 2:12 quotes this verse as Christ's own words after the resurrection. The psalm expands outward from individual deliverance to universal worship: "All the ends of the earth shall remember and turn to the Lord" (22:27). The final verse — "they shall come and proclaim his righteousness to a people yet unborn, that he has done it" (22:31) — echoes Christ's cry "It is finished" (John 19:30). Protestant theology sees in Psalm 22 the complete arc of redemption: from the depths of substitutionary suffering to the heights of resurrection glory to the worldwide proclamation of the gospel.
