Psalm 133 celebrates the beauty of communal harmony with two vivid similes: "Behold, how good and pleasant it is when brothers dwell in unity! It is like the precious oil on the head, running down on the beard, on the beard of Aaron, running down on the collar of his robes! It is like the dew of Hermon, which falls on the mountains of Zion!" (133:1-3). Protestant interpreters have been drawn to this psalm's vision of unity, especially given the Reformation's painful experience of division. Calvin acknowledged the irony of a movement that fractured Christendom appealing to this psalm, yet he insisted that true unity must be grounded in truth, not institutional conformity.

The oil imagery connects unity to priestly consecration — the anointing oil of Exodus 30:22-33, poured on Aaron's head, signified his being set apart for God's service. Protestant readers see here a picture of the church as a priestly community (1 Peter 2:9), where the anointing of the Spirit flows from Christ, the great High Priest, to every member of His body. The abundance of the oil — running down the beard, down the robes — suggests that Spirit-given unity is lavish, not grudging. Luther emphasized that this unity is God's gift, not a human achievement, descending from above like the anointing oil.

The dew of Hermon, falling on Zion's mountains, adds a second image of blessing descending from on high. Hermon's snowcapped peak was the source of moisture for the entire region — its dew was life-giving in an arid land. The psalm concludes with the theological grounding: "For there the Lord has commanded the blessing, life forevermore" (133:3). The word "commanded" indicates sovereign decree: where unity exists among God's people, blessing is not merely possible but divinely mandated. Protestant ecclesiology holds this psalm as both an aspiration and a judgment — the church's divisions grieve the God who commands blessing on unity.
