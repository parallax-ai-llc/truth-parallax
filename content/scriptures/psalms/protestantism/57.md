## Refuge Under God's Wings

Psalm 57 arises from David's experience fleeing from Saul in a cave (1 Samuel 22:1 or 24:3). The opening plea -- "Be merciful to me, O God, be merciful to me, for in you my soul takes refuge; in the shadow of your wings I will take refuge, till the storms of destruction pass by" (v. 1) -- uses the maternal image of a bird sheltering its young. Protestant interpreters note that Jesus himself adopts this image in Matthew 23:37, suggesting a deep typological continuity between the Psalms and Christ's own self-understanding.

The psalm's central verse declares, "God will send out his steadfast love and his faithfulness" (v. 3). Calvin highlighted that God's love and faithfulness are portrayed almost as emissaries dispatched to the believer's aid -- they are not passive attributes but active divine interventions. Even while surrounded by enemies described as lions whose "teeth are spears and arrows, their tongues sharp swords" (v. 4), the psalmist maintains confidence that God's character is more determinative than human hostility.

The refrain "Be exalted, O God, above the heavens! Let your glory be over all the earth!" (vv. 5, 11) transforms a prayer of desperation into a hymn of cosmic praise. Protestant worship has drawn heavily on this movement -- the idea that personal suffering, rightly processed through faith, leads not to self-pity but to doxology. The psalmist's heart is described as "steadfast" (v. 7), and from that steadfastness flows music: "I will sing and make melody! Awake, my glory! Awake, O harp and lyre! I will awake the dawn!" (vv. 7-8). This exuberant praise from the depths of a cave has inspired centuries of Protestant hymnody.
