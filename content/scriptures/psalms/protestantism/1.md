Psalm 1 serves as the gateway to the entire Psalter, establishing the fundamental contrast between the righteous and the wicked that runs through all 150 psalms. Protestant interpreters from Luther onward have seen this psalm as a summary of the gospel itself: blessing comes not from human striving but from delighting in God's Word. The threefold progression of sin in verse 1 — walking, standing, sitting — illustrates how compromise with evil deepens incrementally, a theme Charles Spurgeon explored extensively in *The Treasury of David*.

The image of the tree planted by streams of water (1:3) is central to Protestant devotional life. The righteous person who meditates on God's law "day and night" (1:2) draws sustenance from Scripture itself. Reformed theologians emphasize that this "law" (*torah*) encompasses all of God's revealed instruction, not merely legal statutes. The fruitfulness described — "yields its fruit in its season" (1:3) — is understood as the natural outworking of a life rooted in grace, not a legalistic checklist.

The stark contrast of verse 4 — "the wicked are not so" — introduces the doctrine of two destinies. The chaff metaphor underscores the insubstantiality of a life lived apart from God. Verse 5 declares that the wicked "will not stand in the judgment," a text Protestant preachers have long connected to the final judgment and the necessity of justification by faith. Verse 6 summarizes the entire psalm: the Lord "knows" the way of the righteous — an intimate, covenantal knowing — while the way of the wicked "will perish."
