## The Glory of God in Creation

Psalm 104 is the great creation hymn of the Psalter, celebrating God's ongoing providential care for the natural world. It opens with majestic imagery: "Bless the LORD, O my soul! O LORD my God, you are very great! You are clothed with splendor and majesty, covering yourself with light as with a garment, stretching out the heavens like a tent" (vv. 1-2). Protestant natural theology has drawn on this psalm to articulate the doctrine of general revelation — the created order speaks of its Creator (cf. Romans 1:20). Calvin's famous description of creation as a "theater of God's glory" finds its scriptural basis largely in this psalm.

The psalm traces God's creative and sustaining activity with remarkable detail — from the foundations of the earth (v. 5) to the watering of mountains (v. 13), from the provision of grass for cattle to wine that "gladdens the heart of man" (vv. 14-15). Every creature depends on God: "These all look to you, to give them their food in due season. When you give it to them, they gather it up; when you open your hand, they are filled with good things" (vv. 27-28). Luther preached from these verses the doctrine of divine preservation — God did not merely create the world and step back but actively sustains every living thing moment by moment. The Reformed tradition's doctrine of providence finds extensive support here.

Verse 30 introduces the Spirit's creative role: "When you send forth your Spirit, they are created, and you renew the face of the ground." Protestant pneumatology has connected this verse to the Spirit's work in regeneration — the same Spirit who renews the natural world also brings spiritual new creation in the hearts of believers. The psalm's conclusion brings a jarring note: "Let sinners be consumed from the earth, and let the wicked be no more!" (v. 35). The intrusion of human sin into the harmony of creation is a discordant note that demands resolution. Protestant theology reads this as an anticipation of the new creation (Revelation 21-22), where the beauty celebrated in Psalm 104 will be fully restored without the deforming presence of sin.
