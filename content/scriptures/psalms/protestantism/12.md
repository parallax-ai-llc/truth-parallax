Psalm 12 contrasts the deceitful speech of the wicked with the pure words of God, a theme that resonated powerfully with the Reformers and their emphasis on *sola Scriptura*. The psalm opens with a lament: "Save, O Lord, for the godly one is gone; for the faithful have vanished from among the children of man" (12:1). This sense of living in a time of moral collapse and verbal corruption has struck Protestant readers in every generation as immediately relevant.

The centerpiece of the psalm is verse 6: "The words of the Lord are pure words, like silver refined in a furnace on the ground, purified seven times." Protestant theology has made this verse a touchstone for the doctrine of biblical inerrancy and the trustworthiness of Scripture. The image of sevenfold refining suggests absolute purity — God's words contain no admixture of error or deceit. Against the "flattering lips" and "double heart" of the wicked (12:2), God's Word stands as the one reliable foundation for faith and life.

The psalm also addresses the arrogance of those who say, "With our tongue we will prevail, our lips are with us; who is master over us?" (12:4). Protestant social commentary has seen in this verse the spirit of every age that exalts human autonomy and rejects divine authority. God's response is swift and decisive: "Because the poor are plundered, because the needy groan, I will now arise" (12:5). The assurance of verse 7 — "You, O Lord, will keep them; you will guard us from this generation forever" — extends the promise of protection to all who trust in God's pure Word rather than human flattery.
