## An Urgent Plea for Help

Psalm 70 is a brief, urgent prayer that closely parallels Psalm 40:13-17. Its opening and closing frame the psalm with the word "hasten" — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me! O LORD, make haste to help me!" (v. 1) and "You are my help and my deliverer; O LORD, do not delay!" (v. 5). Protestant interpreters have noted that this psalm's very brevity conveys its urgency — there is no time for elaborate composition when danger is immediate.

The psalm asks that those who seek the psalmist's life be "put to shame and confusion" (v. 2) while those who seek God may "rejoice and be glad" (v. 4). This sharp binary — shame for the wicked, joy for the righteous — reflects the Psalter's consistent moral framework that Protestant theology affirms. Calvin observed that the psalm teaches believers to bring even their most desperate and inarticulate cries to God, trusting that brevity does not diminish prayer's effectiveness.

The psalmist's self-description — "I am poor and needy" (v. 5) — is not merely a statement of material condition but a spiritual posture. Protestant theology, particularly in its Reformed expression, has emphasized that awareness of spiritual poverty is the prerequisite for receiving grace. The Beatitudes' "Blessed are the poor in spirit" (Matthew 5:3) echoes this psalmist's stance. Spurgeon preached that this tiny psalm contains the essence of all prayer: acknowledgment of need, appeal to God, and confidence in His timing.
