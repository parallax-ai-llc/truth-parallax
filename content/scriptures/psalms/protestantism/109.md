## A Prayer Against a Treacherous Enemy

Psalm 109 is among the most challenging imprecatory psalms, containing some of the most severe curses in Scripture. The psalm opens with a plea: "Be not silent, O God of my praise! For wicked and deceitful mouths are opened against me, speaking against me with lying tongues" (vv. 1-2). The psalmist's anguish is compounded by betrayal: "In return for my love they accuse me, but I give myself to prayer. So they reward me evil for good, and hatred for my love" (vv. 4-5). Protestant interpreters have recognized in these words a typological foreshadowing of Christ's experience — betrayed by one he loved, rewarded with evil for his good. Peter applies verse 8 directly to Judas in Acts 1:20: "Let another take his office."

The extended imprecation of verses 6-20 is staggering in its severity, calling for the enemy's children to become orphans, his wife a widow, and his posterity cut off (vv. 9-13). Calvin addressed the moral difficulty directly, arguing that these are not expressions of personal malice but prophetic pronouncements of divine justice spoken under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit. The Reformation hermeneutical principle applies: these curses must be read in their canonical context, where they function as covenant sanctions against those who oppose God's anointed. Protestant theology maintains the distinction between personal vengeance (forbidden in Romans 12:19) and prophetic announcement of divine judgment.

The psalm's final section returns to personal lament and trust: "For I am poor and needy, and my heart is stricken within me... Help me, O LORD my God! Save me according to your steadfast love!" (vv. 22, 26). The psalmist's confidence rests not on the justice of his cause alone but on God's covenant love. The closing verse affirms God's advocacy for the vulnerable: "For he stands at the right hand of the needy one, to save him from those who condemn his soul to death" (v. 31). Luther drew comfort from this image — while accusers stand to condemn, God himself stands at the right hand of the afflicted as their defender, a truth fulfilled in Christ's ongoing intercession (Romans 8:34).
