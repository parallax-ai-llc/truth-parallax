## The LORD Reigns in Righteousness

Psalm 97 continues the enthronement theme with a declaration that triggers universal response: "The LORD reigns, let the earth rejoice; let the many coastlands be glad!" (v. 1). The theophanic imagery that follows — clouds, darkness, fire, lightning, melting mountains (vv. 2-5) — echoes the Sinai revelation and establishes God's awesome holiness. Protestant interpreters have noted the deliberate pairing of sovereignty and mystery: "Clouds and thick darkness are all around him; righteousness and justice are the foundation of his throne" (v. 2). Calvin taught that while God's ways may be hidden in clouds, his character is not — righteousness and justice form the unshakable foundation of all divine action.

The psalm pronounces judgment on idolatry: "All worshipers of images are put to shame, who make their boast in worthless idols; worship him, all you gods!" (v. 7). The writer of Hebrews applies this verse to Christ's superiority over angels (Hebrews 1:6), reading "worship him, all you gods" as a command for angelic worship of the incarnate Son. This Christological reading is central to Protestant interpretation — the psalm's enthronement language finds its ultimate fulfillment in the exaltation of Christ, before whom every knee bows (Philippians 2:10-11).

The psalm concludes with practical exhortations grounded in theology: "O you who love the LORD, hate evil! He preserves the lives of his saints; he delivers them from the hand of the wicked" (v. 10). Lutheran and Reformed ethics alike have drawn on this verse to insist that love for God necessarily produces hatred of evil — passive tolerance of wickedness is incompatible with genuine devotion. The final verse — "Light is sown for the righteous, and joy for the upright in heart" (v. 11) — presents righteousness as a seed that will inevitably produce a harvest of light and gladness, a promise that sustains believers through seasons when the harvest is not yet visible.
