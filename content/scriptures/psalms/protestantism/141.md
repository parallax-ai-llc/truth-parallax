Psalm 141 is an evening prayer of David that opens with one of the most beautiful liturgical images in the Psalter: "Let my prayer be counted as incense before you, and the lifting up of my hands as the evening sacrifice!" (141:2). Protestant interpreters have read this verse as evidence that the sacrificial system pointed beyond itself to the spiritual worship of prayer and praise. The Reformation's rejection of the Mass as a repeated sacrifice found support in texts like this, where prayer itself is the true offering. Calvin saw in this verse the fulfillment of the ceremonial law: the believer's prayers ascend to God as fragrant incense, without need for mediating rituals.

The psalm's central petition concerns the discipline of speech and desire: "Set a guard, O Lord, over my mouth; keep watch over the door of my lips! Do not let my heart incline to any evil" (141:3-4). Luther emphasized that the psalmist recognizes his inability to control his own tongue and desires without divine help — a candid acknowledgment that sanctification is the Spirit's work, not human willpower. The prayer not to be attracted to "the delicacies" of evildoers (141:4) reveals the seductive nature of sin: wickedness often presents itself as a feast, not a poison.

Verse 5 offers a striking paradox: "Let a righteous man strike me — it is a kindness; let him rebuke me — it is oil for my head." Protestant spirituality values this openness to correction, connecting it to the Reformation's emphasis on mutual accountability within the community of faith. The psalm's later verses (141:6-10) are textually difficult but clearly express trust that the wicked will fall into their own traps while the psalmist's "eyes are toward you, O God, my Lord; in you I seek refuge" (141:8). The psalm thus models a complete spiritual posture: prayer ascending, lips guarded, correction welcomed, and eyes fixed on God.
