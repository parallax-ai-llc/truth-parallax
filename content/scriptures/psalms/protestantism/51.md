## A Penitent Heart Before God

Psalm 51 stands as the supreme penitential psalm in Protestant devotion, attributed to David after Nathan confronted him over his sin with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 12). The superscription grounds this psalm in concrete historical sin, and Protestant interpreters from Luther onward have insisted that its power lies precisely in this specificity. David does not offer vague regret but a total confession: "Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight" (v. 4). Luther saw in this verse the essence of evangelical repentance -- sin is ultimately an offense against God's holiness, not merely a social transgression.

The psalm's theology of grace is foundational to Protestant soteriology. David does not appeal to his own merit or promise future obedience as a bargaining chip; he throws himself entirely on God's "steadfast love" and "abundant mercy" (v. 1). The plea "Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me" (v. 10) became a key text for the Reformers' insistence that regeneration is God's sovereign work, not a human achievement. Calvin emphasized that the verb "create" (bara) is used exclusively of divine action in Scripture, demonstrating that moral renewal comes from God alone.

Verse 5 -- "Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity, and in sin did my mother conceive me" -- has served as a central proof-text for the doctrine of original sin in Protestant theology. The Westminster Confession cites this verse in its treatment of the fall. Equally significant is verse 11, "Cast me not away from your presence, and take not your Holy Spirit from me," which Protestant commentators read as expressing the believer's utter dependence on the Spirit's indwelling. Spurgeon devoted some of his most moving exposition to this verse, warning that the loss of God's presence is more terrible than any earthly calamity. The psalm's conclusion -- that God desires not sacrifice but "a broken and contrite heart" (v. 17) -- anticipates the Protestant emphasis on inward faith over external ritual.
