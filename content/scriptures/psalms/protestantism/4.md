Psalm 4, paired with Psalm 3 as an evening counterpart to a morning prayer, has long been used in Protestant evening devotions. David's opening petition — "Answer me when I call, O God of my righteousness" (4:1) — appeals not to his own merit but to God's righteous character. The phrase "you have given me relief when I was in distress" (4:1b) establishes a pattern of remembering past deliverances as fuel for present faith, a practice central to Reformed spirituality.

The central challenge comes in verses 2-3: David rebukes those who love "vain words" and seek "lies," then declares that "the Lord has set apart the godly for himself" (4:3). Protestant interpreters read this as the doctrine of election applied pastorally — the believer's confidence rests not in human loyalty but in God's sovereign setting-apart. Verse 4, "Be angry, and do not sin; ponder in your own hearts on your beds, and be silent," is quoted by Paul in Ephesians 4:26, connecting Old Testament wisdom to New Testament ethics.

The psalm's climax contrasts worldly and spiritual joy: "You have put more gladness in my heart than they have when their grain and wine abound" (4:7). This verse has been central to the Protestant teaching that true contentment comes from God's presence rather than material prosperity. The closing verse — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep, for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety" (4:8) — has been a beloved bedtime prayer across Protestant traditions for centuries.
