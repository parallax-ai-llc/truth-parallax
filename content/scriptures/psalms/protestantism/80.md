## Restore Us, O God

Psalm 80 is structured around a repeated refrain that intensifies with each repetition: "Restore us, O God; let your face shine, that we may be saved!" (vv. 3, 7, 19). The psalm addresses God as "Shepherd of Israel" who leads "Joseph like a flock" and is enthroned "upon the cherubim" (v. 1). Protestant theology has noted the progressive intensification of the divine name in each refrain — from "God" (Elohim, v. 3) to "God of hosts" (v. 7) to "LORD God of hosts" (v. 19) — as reflecting an escalating urgency in prayer. Calvin saw this pattern as instructive for believers: persistent prayer grows bolder, not weaker, in the face of God's apparent delay.

The extended vine metaphor in verses 8-16 provides one of Scripture's richest images of God's covenant people: "You brought a vine out of Egypt; you drove out the nations and planted it. You cleared the ground for it; it took deep root and filled the land" (vv. 8-9). Yet now the vine's walls are broken, and "the boar from the forest ravages it" (v. 13). Jesus draws directly on this imagery in John 15, declaring himself the true vine — thereby claiming to fulfill what Israel as the vine could not. Protestant interpreters thus read Psalm 80 as pointing beyond national restoration to the person of Christ, in whom the vine metaphor finds its ultimate meaning.

Verse 17 introduces a striking petition: "But let your hand be on the man of your right hand, the son of man whom you have made strong for yourself!" The phrase "son of man" anticipates both Daniel 7:13 and Jesus' favorite self-designation. Reformed interpreters have understood this as a messianic plea — the nation's restoration depends not on collective repentance alone but on the coming of one particular "man" whom God strengthens. The psalm thus moves from national lament to messianic hope, pointing to the one through whom God's face ultimately shines upon his people.
