## A Festival Call to Obedience

Psalm 81 opens with an exuberant summons to worship: "Sing aloud to God our strength; shout for joy to the God of Jacob! Raise a song; sound the tambourine, the sweet lyre with the harp" (vv. 1-2). The psalm is set in the context of a festival — likely the Feast of Tabernacles — with its call to blow the trumpet "at the new moon, at the full moon, on our feast day" (v. 3). Protestant theology values this psalm for its integration of joyful worship with covenantal obedience. Luther emphasized that true worship is never merely ceremonial but flows from a heart that recognizes God as "our strength," the one who delivered Israel "from the burden" and whose hands "were freed from the basket" of Egyptian slavery (v. 6).

The psalm pivots dramatically in verse 8, where God himself speaks: "Hear, O my people, while I admonish you! O Israel, if you would but listen to me! There shall be no strange god among you; you shall not bow down to a foreign god. I am the LORD your God, who brought you up out of the land of Egypt" (vv. 8-10). Calvin noted that the first commandment is here restated within a liturgical context — worship that does not lead to exclusive devotion is hollow. The Reformation principle of solus Christus finds its Old Testament root in this demand for undivided allegiance. God's promise, "Open your mouth wide, and I will fill it" (v. 10), presents divine provision as contingent not on human effort but on receptive faith.

The psalm's tragic conclusion reveals what Israel chose instead: "But my people did not listen to my voice; Israel would not submit to me. So I gave them over to their stubborn hearts, to follow their own counsels" (vv. 11-12). This judicial hardening anticipates Paul's argument in Romans 1:24-28. Yet the psalm ends on a note of yearning: "Oh, that my people would listen to me... I would feed you with the finest of the wheat, and with honey from the rock I would satisfy you" (vv. 13, 16). Protestant interpreters hear in God's voice not cold sovereignty but a genuine lament over his people's refusal — a divine desire to bless that human stubbornness has thwarted.
