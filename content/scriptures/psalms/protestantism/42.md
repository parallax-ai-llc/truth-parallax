Psalm 42 opens with one of Scripture's most beloved images of spiritual longing: "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God" (42:1). Protestant devotional theology has treasured this psalm as expressing the believer's deep hunger for God's presence, particularly during seasons of spiritual dryness or felt absence.

The psalmist — a Son of Korah, exiled from the temple — wrestles with depression and divine silence: "My tears have been my food day and night, while people say to me all day long, 'Where is your God?'" (42:3). Protestant pastoral care draws on this psalm to normalize spiritual struggle. The threefold refrain — "Why, my soul, are you downcast? Put your hope in God" (42:5, 11; 43:5) — models the practice of preaching truth to oneself rather than passively accepting despair.

The psalm's imagery of "deep calls to deep in the roar of your waterfalls" (42:7) has been interpreted by Protestant mystics and theologians alike as describing the overwhelming nature of both suffering and divine encounter. Calvin saw this verse as expressing the soul's sense of being engulfed by wave upon wave of trial, yet even in the depths, the psalmist remembers God's steadfast love (42:8).
