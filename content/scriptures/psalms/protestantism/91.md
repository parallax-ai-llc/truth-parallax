## The Shelter of the Most High

Psalm 91 is one of the Psalter's most powerful expressions of divine protection: "He who dwells in the shelter of the Most High will abide in the shadow of the Almighty. I will say to the LORD, 'My refuge and my fortress, my God, in whom I trust'" (vv. 1-2). The psalm employs four divine names — Most High (Elyon), Almighty (Shaddai), LORD (Yahweh), and God (Elohim) — suggesting a comprehensive declaration of trust in every aspect of God's character. Protestant interpreters have emphasized that the protection described is covenantal and conditional — it belongs to the one who "dwells" and "abides," not to every person indiscriminately.

The sweeping promises of protection — deliverance from the snare, the pestilence, the terror of night, the arrow by day (vv. 3-6) — culminate in the famous declaration: "A thousand may fall at your side, ten thousand at your right hand, but it will not come near you" (v. 7). Calvin warned against reading these promises as a guarantee of physical immunity, interpreting them instead as expressions of God's sovereign care over the ultimate well-being of his people. The Reformation understanding is that God's protection operates within the larger narrative of his redemptive purposes — sometimes through miraculous deliverance, sometimes through sustaining grace in suffering.

Verses 11-12 are famously quoted by Satan in the temptation of Christ: "For he will command his angels concerning you to guard you in all your ways. On their hands they will bear you up, lest you strike your foot against a stone" (Matthew 4:6). Jesus' response — refusing to test God by leaping from the temple — provides the definitive Protestant hermeneutic for this psalm. Scripture must interpret Scripture; individual promises cannot be extracted from their canonical context and weaponized as demands upon God. The psalm concludes with God himself speaking: "Because he holds fast to me in love, I will deliver him... With long life I will satisfy him and show him my salvation" (vv. 14-16), grounding all promises in the relational bond of covenant love.
