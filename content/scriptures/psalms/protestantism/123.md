Psalm 123 is a brief but intense prayer for mercy, using the image of servants watching their master's hand to describe the believer's posture before God. "Behold, as the eyes of servants look to the hand of their master, as the eyes of a maidservant to the hand of her mistress, so our eyes look to the Lord our God, till he has mercy upon us" (123:2). Protestant interpreters find in this analogy not servile fear but attentive dependence — the servant watches the master's hand for direction, provision, and signal. Calvin emphasized that this watchfulness is the essence of prayer: not anxious pleading but patient, expectant trust in God's timing.

The psalm's occasion is social contempt: "Have mercy upon us, O Lord, have mercy upon us, for we have had more than enough of contempt. Our soul has had more than enough of the scorn of those who are at ease, of the contempt of the proud" (123:3-4). Luther knew this experience intimately, facing ridicule from powerful ecclesiastical authorities, and he found in this psalm the proper response — not retaliation but appeal to God. The repeated cry for mercy acknowledges that the afflicted have no resource in themselves; their only hope is the sovereign God "enthroned in the heavens" (123:1).

The psalm's brevity is itself eloquent. When suffering under contempt, elaborate prayers feel impossible. This four-verse psalm teaches that God hears the briefest, most desperate cries. Protestant spirituality values this directness, rejecting the notion that lengthy or formulaic prayers are more effective. The upward gaze — from servant to master, from earth to heaven — models the orientation of the Christian life: away from the scorn of the proud and toward the mercy of God.
