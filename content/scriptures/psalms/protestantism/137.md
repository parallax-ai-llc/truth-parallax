Psalm 137 is one of the most emotionally powerful and theologically challenging psalms in the Psalter. Its opening — "By the waters of Babylon, there we sat down and wept, when we remembered Zion" (137:1) — captures the anguish of exile with devastating simplicity. The captors' demand for entertainment — "Sing us one of the songs of Zion!" (137:3) — adds cruelty to displacement. Protestant interpreters have read this psalm as expressing the church's experience in a hostile world: the songs of faith become impossible when the context of worship is destroyed. Luther connected this experience to the Reformation's own sense of spiritual exile from a corrupted church.

The psalmist's vow of remembrance in verses 5-6 is the psalm's theological core: "If I forget you, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget its skill! Let my tongue stick to the roof of my mouth, if I do not remember you, if I do not set Jerusalem above my highest joy!" The willingness to sacrifice one's most treasured abilities — the musician's hand, the singer's tongue — rather than forget Zion expresses a total devotion that Protestant interpreters see as a model for the believer's commitment to the worship of God. Calvin noted that memory is itself a spiritual discipline: to remember Jerusalem is to refuse to be assimilated by Babylon.

The psalm's closing verses (137:7-9), including the notorious blessing on whoever dashes Babylon's infants against the rock, have troubled Christians throughout history. Protestant interpreters handle this text with care. Calvin insisted that the psalm expresses divinely sanctioned judgment against Babylon prophesied elsewhere in Scripture (Isaiah 13:16), not a license for personal vengeance. Many Protestant commentators read the infants typologically as representing the earliest stirrings of sin that must be destroyed before they mature. Whatever interpretive approach is taken, the psalm's raw honesty before God — bringing even the most violent emotions into prayer rather than acting on them — has been valued as a hallmark of biblical faith.
