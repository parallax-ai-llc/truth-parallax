## God's Faithfulness and Israel's Rebellion

Psalm 78 is the longest historical psalm, spanning Israel's story from the Exodus to the election of David. It opens as didactic wisdom: "Give ear, O my people, to my teaching; incline your ears to the words of my mouth! I will open my mouth in a parable; I will utter dark sayings from of old" (vv. 1-2). Matthew 13:35 applies this verse to Jesus' parabolic teaching, and Protestant interpreters have therefore seen the psalm as fundamentally Christological in its trajectory. The purpose of recounting history is explicitly stated: "that they should set their hope in God and not forget the works of God, but keep his commandments" (v. 7). Sola scriptura finds a natural home here — the written record of God's deeds serves as the foundation for faith across generations.

The bulk of the psalm recounts a devastating cycle of divine mercy and human rebellion. God provided manna and water in the wilderness (vv. 23-29), yet "they did not stop their craving" (v. 30). He delivered plagues against Egypt (vv. 43-51), yet Israel "tested and rebelled against the Most High God" (v. 56). Calvin drew from this pattern a profound lesson about total depravity — even those who witness God's mightiest acts are incapable of sustained faithfulness apart from transforming grace. Luther similarly noted that the psalm dismantles any notion of human merit; Israel's persistent unfaithfulness demonstrates that covenant relationship depends entirely on God's patience, not human performance.

The psalm reaches its climax in God's sovereign election: "He chose the tribe of Judah, Mount Zion, which he loved. He built his sanctuary like the high heavens... He chose David his servant and took him from the sheepfolds" (vv. 68-70). Despite the catastrophic failure of the northern tribes and the loss of the ark (vv. 60-64), God's purposes are not thwarted. He raises up David to "shepherd them with upright heart" (v. 72). Protestant covenant theology sees in this election a preview of Christ, the greater David, who shepherds God's people with perfect integrity — accomplishing what neither Israel nor David ultimately could.
