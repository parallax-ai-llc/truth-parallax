Psalm 136, known in Jewish tradition as the "Great Hallel," is one of the most distinctive psalms in the Psalter, with every one of its twenty-six verses ending with the refrain "for his steadfast love endures forever." This antiphonal structure — a cantor or choir stating God's acts, the congregation responding with the refrain — has profoundly shaped Protestant liturgical practice. The repetition is not redundant but pedagogical: it teaches the community to interpret every event in salvation history through the lens of God's *hesed*, His covenant love that never fails.

The psalm surveys the full sweep of God's works: creation (136:4-9), the Exodus and Red Sea deliverance (136:10-15), the wilderness journey (136:16), the conquest of Canaan (136:17-22), and ongoing providence (136:23-25). Each mighty act is attributed to the same motivation — steadfast love. Calvin emphasized that this comprehensive scope teaches believers to see God's love not only in dramatic interventions but in daily provision: "He gives food to all flesh, for his steadfast love endures forever" (136:25). Protestant theology insists that creation, redemption, and sustenance are all expressions of a single covenantal commitment.

The psalm's opening triad — "Give thanks to the Lord, for he is good" (136:1), "Give thanks to the God of gods" (136:2), "Give thanks to the Lord of lords" (136:3) — establishes God's supremacy in goodness and authority. Luther found in this psalm a cure for ingratitude, the sin he considered the root of all unbelief. By rehearsing God's acts one by one, each followed by the confession of enduring love, the psalm trains the heart in gratitude. The Protestant practice of recounting God's mercies in prayer and testimony finds its scriptural model here: remember what God has done, and you will trust what God will do.
