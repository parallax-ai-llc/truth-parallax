Psalm 20 is a royal psalm, a congregational prayer offered for the king before battle. The people petition God to "answer you in the day of trouble" and "send you help from the sanctuary" (20:1-2), invoking blessings on the king's plans and offerings. Protestant interpreters read this psalm on two levels: historically, it reflects Israel's liturgical life surrounding the Davidic monarchy; typologically, it points to Christ the King and the church's prayers for His kingdom to advance.

The theological heart of the psalm is verse 7: "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God." This declaration has become a watchword for Protestant faith. The contrast between military technology and divine reliance reflects a principle that runs throughout Scripture — God's people prevail not by superior resources but by dependence on the Lord. Calvin saw this verse as applicable to every area of life where believers are tempted to place confidence in human means rather than God's provision.

The psalm's closing cry — "O Lord, save the king! May he answer us when we call" (20:9) — is both a prayer for the earthly monarch and, in Protestant reading, a prayer for Christ's saving reign. The interplay between divine initiative and human prayer modeled here is central to Protestant theology of prayer: God has ordained the end (victory) but also the means (prayer). The congregation's intercession is not empty ritual but genuine participation in God's purposes, a principle that undergirds Protestant prayer meetings and intercessory ministries.
