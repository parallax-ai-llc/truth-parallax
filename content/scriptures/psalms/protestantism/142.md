Psalm 142 is identified in its superscription as a prayer of David "when he was in the cave," likely the cave of Adullam (1 Samuel 22) or En-gedi (1 Samuel 24). Protestant interpreters value this historical context because it grounds the psalm in concrete human experience — David is not theologizing in comfort but crying out from a place of physical danger and profound isolation. "I cry out to the Lord with my voice; with my voice to the Lord I plead for mercy" (142:1). The doubled reference to "voice" emphasizes that this is audible, urgent prayer, not quiet meditation.

The psalm's most poignant verse is the confession of abandonment: "Look to the right and see: there is none who takes notice of me; no refuge remains to me; no one cares for my soul" (142:4). Luther found this verse especially moving, recognizing in it the experience of every believer who has felt utterly alone. Protestant pastoral theology teaches that such moments of perceived abandonment, while agonizing, drive the soul to its only true refuge. The very next verse provides the turn: "I cry to you, O Lord; I say, 'You are my refuge, my portion in the land of the living'" (142:5). When human support fails completely, God alone remains.

The psalm concludes with a petition that looks beyond the cave to future praise: "Bring me out of prison, that I may give thanks to your name! The righteous will surround me, for you will deal bountifully with me" (142:7). The movement from isolation to community, from imprisonment to thanksgiving, mirrors the gospel pattern of death and resurrection. Protestant hope is anchored not in the absence of suffering but in God's faithfulness to bring His people through suffering into praise. The "righteous" who will gather around the delivered psalmist anticipate the church — the community that forms around the testimony of God's rescue.
