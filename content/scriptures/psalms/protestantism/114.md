Psalm 114 is a compact masterpiece of Hebrew poetry celebrating the Exodus with vivid, dramatic imagery. Protestant interpreters have treasured this psalm for its bold depiction of creation responding to the presence of Israel's God. The sea "looked and fled," the Jordan "turned back," the mountains "skipped like rams" (114:3-4). Luther delighted in this personification, arguing that it teaches a theology of nature: the physical world recognizes and responds to its Creator even when human beings do not. Calvin similarly noted that if mountains tremble before God, how much more should sinful humanity.

The psalm's opening verses identify the Exodus as the constitutive event of Israel's identity: "When Israel went out from Egypt, the house of Jacob from a people of strange language, Judah became his sanctuary, Israel his dominion" (114:1-2). Protestant covenant theology reads this as the paradigmatic act of redemption — God choosing a people, delivering them from bondage, and dwelling among them. The Reformation's emphasis on sola gratia finds powerful expression here: Israel did not earn deliverance but received it as a sovereign act of divine initiative.

The psalm's closing question — "Tremble, O earth, at the presence of the Lord, at the presence of the God of Jacob, who turns the rock into a pool of water, the flint into a spring of water" (114:7-8) — issues a universal summons. The God who parts seas and splits rocks is not a tribal deity but the Lord of all creation. Paul's identification of the rock with Christ (1 Corinthians 10:4) has led Protestant exegetes to read this psalm Christologically: the water from the rock prefigures the living water that Christ offers to all who believe.
