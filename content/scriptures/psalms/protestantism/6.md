Psalm 6 is the first of the seven Penitential Psalms (6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143), a collection that has shaped Protestant understanding of repentance and divine mercy since the Reformation. Luther's own spiritual breakthrough was deeply influenced by meditating on the Penitential Psalms, and he published a commentary on them in 1517, the year of the Ninety-Five Theses. The opening cry — "O Lord, rebuke me not in your anger" (6:1) — expresses the sinner's terror before divine judgment.

The psalm's central section (6:2-7) depicts suffering that is simultaneously physical, emotional, and spiritual. "My bones are troubled" (6:2), "my soul also is greatly troubled" (6:3), "I drench my couch with my weeping" (6:6) — this language of total anguish resonated deeply with Luther's own experience of *Anfechtung* (spiritual trial). Protestant pastoral theology has drawn on Psalm 6 to validate the reality of spiritual suffering while pointing sufferers toward the God who hears.

The dramatic turn in verse 8 — "Depart from me, all you workers of evil, for the Lord has heard the sound of my weeping" (6:8) — models the faith-shift that occurs within prayer itself. The psalmist moves from desperate plea to confident assurance without any change in external circumstances. Jesus quotes this very phrase in Matthew 7:23, giving it eschatological weight. Protestant interpreters see in this psalm the pattern of justification by faith: the sinner cries out in helplessness, and God responds with mercy, not based on the petitioner's worthiness but on His own steadfast love (6:4).
