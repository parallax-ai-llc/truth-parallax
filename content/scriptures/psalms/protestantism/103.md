## Bless the LORD, O My Soul

Psalm 103 is one of the Psalter's supreme expressions of praise, and its opening self-exhortation has become foundational for Protestant devotional life: "Bless the LORD, O my soul, and all that is within me, bless his holy name!" (v. 1). The psalmist commands himself to worship — worship is a deliberate act of the will, not merely a spontaneous emotion. David then enumerates God's benefits: "who forgives all your iniquity, who heals all your diseases, who redeems your life from the pit, who crowns you with steadfast love and mercy" (vv. 3-4). Luther noted the order — forgiveness precedes healing, because sin is the deeper disease from which all other afflictions flow.

The psalm's theology of forgiveness is breathtaking in its scope: "He does not deal with us according to our sins, nor repay us according to our iniquities. For as high as the heavens are above the earth, so great is his steadfast love toward those who fear him; as far as the east is from the west, so far does he remove our transgressions from us" (vv. 10-12). Protestant theology has found in these verses a clear articulation of the grace that the Reformation recovered — God's dealing with his people is not proportional to their sin but proportional to his love. Calvin marveled at the spatial metaphors: the immeasurable distance between heaven and earth, the infinite gap between east and west — these express a forgiveness that no human arithmetic can calculate.

The psalm's most tender image is paternal: "As a father shows compassion to his children, so the LORD shows compassion to those who fear him. For he knows our frame; he remembers that we are dust" (vv. 13-14). God's compassion is informed by intimate knowledge of human frailty — He does not hold us to a standard disconnected from our creaturely limitations. The psalm concludes by expanding from personal praise to cosmic worship: "Bless the LORD, all his works, in all places of his dominion. Bless the LORD, O my soul!" (v. 22). The personal and the universal are joined — one soul's praise participates in the worship of all creation, and all creation's worship returns to the individual heart.
