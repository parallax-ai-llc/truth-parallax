Psalm 34, composed when David feigned madness before Abimelech, is an acrostic psalm of thanksgiving. Its most famous invitation — "Taste and see that the LORD is good; blessed is the one who takes refuge in him" (34:8) — is read by Protestant interpreters as calling for experiential faith. Knowing God is not merely intellectual assent but personal encounter.

The psalm contains practical wisdom teaching: "Come, my children, listen to me; I will teach you the fear of the LORD" (34:11). Protestant pedagogy has drawn on these verses for catechetical instruction, teaching that the fear of the Lord involves both reverence and practical righteousness — keeping one's tongue from evil and pursuing peace (34:13-14).

Verse 18 offers one of Scripture's most tender promises: "The LORD is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit." Protestant pastoral theology elevates this assurance, insisting that God's nearness is not contingent on human strength or spiritual performance. The psalm closes with the affirmation that "the LORD delivers them from them all" (34:19) — not that the righteous avoid affliction, but that God is faithful through every trial.
