Psalm 132 is the longest of the Songs of Ascents and the most theologically dense, recounting David's zeal to find a dwelling place for the ark of God and God's reciprocal covenant with the house of David. The psalm's opening petition — "Remember, O Lord, in David's favor, all the hardships he endured" (132:1) — appeals not to David's merit but to his devotion, specifically his oath not to rest until he had found "a dwelling place for the Mighty One of Jacob" (132:5). Protestant interpreters see in David's zeal a model of the believer's desire for God's presence, while insisting that the fulfillment of this longing comes through Christ, the true temple (John 2:19-21).

The Davidic covenant promise in verses 11-12 is central to Protestant messianic theology: "The Lord swore to David a sure oath from which he will not turn back: 'One of the sons of your body I will set on your throne.'" Calvin identified this as an unconditional divine commitment that finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ, the eternal Son of David. The conditional element — "If your sons keep my covenant and my testimonies that I shall teach them" (132:12) — applies to the historical Davidic line, whose failures necessitated the coming of the one king who would keep the covenant perfectly.

The psalm's closing section (132:13-18) describes God's chosen dwelling in Zion with extravagant promises: abundant provision, satisfied poor, clothed priests, and a horn sprouting for David. The imagery of the lamp prepared for God's anointed (132:17) is read Christologically by Protestant interpreters — Christ is the light that will never be extinguished. Luther saw the entire psalm as a prophecy of the gospel: God's determination to dwell among His people, fulfilled not in a building but in the incarnate Christ and, by the Spirit, in the church. The enemies who will be "clothed with shame" (132:18) while the anointed one's crown "will shine" represent the final triumph of God's kingdom.
