Psalm 24 forms a trilogy with Psalms 22 and 23 that Protestant theology has long read as a sequence of Christ's work: Psalm 22 portrays the Good Shepherd who dies for the sheep (John 10:11), Psalm 23 the Great Shepherd who tends His flock (Hebrews 13:20), and Psalm 24 the Chief Shepherd who appears in glory (1 Peter 5:4). The psalm opens with a majestic declaration of divine ownership: "The earth is the Lord's and the fullness thereof, the world and those who dwell therein" (24:1), quoted by Paul in 1 Corinthians 10:26.

The entrance liturgy in verses 3-6 echoes Psalm 15's question — "Who shall ascend the hill of the Lord?" — and answers: "He who has clean hands and a pure heart, who does not lift up his soul to what is false" (24:4). Protestant interpretation applies this both ethically (as the standard for worship) and christologically (as a description of Christ, the only one who perfectly meets these requirements). Through union with Christ, believers are granted access to God's presence that their own moral record could never secure.

The psalm's climax (24:7-10) is a dramatic antiphonal exchange: "Lift up your heads, O gates! And be lifted up, O ancient doors, that the King of glory may come in. Who is this King of glory? The Lord, strong and mighty, the Lord, mighty in battle!" Protestant liturgy has associated this passage with Christ's ascension into heaven, the victorious King returning to the Father's right hand after conquering sin and death. The repeated question "Who is this King of glory?" and the answer "The Lord of hosts, he is the King of glory!" (24:10) have been set to music in countless Protestant compositions, from Handel to modern worship, celebrating Christ's triumphal enthronement.
