## Suffering for God's Sake

Psalm 69 is the second most quoted psalm in the New Testament (after Psalm 22), making it indispensable for Protestant Christological interpretation. The opening cry — "Save me, O God! For the waters have come up to my neck" (v. 1) — depicts drowning in affliction. The psalmist suffers not for wrongdoing but for zeal: "For zeal for your house has consumed me, and the reproaches of those who reproach you have fallen on me" (v. 9). John 2:17 applies the first half of this verse to Jesus cleansing the temple, and Romans 15:3 applies the second half to Christ bearing reproach.

Protestant interpreters have found in this psalm a detailed prophetic portrait of Christ's passion. The sufferer is alienated from his own family (v. 8), mocked by those sitting in the gate (v. 12), offered vinegar for his thirst (v. 21, fulfilled in John 19:28-29), and prays for deliverance from the "mire" and "deep waters" (vv. 14-15). The imprecatory section (vv. 22-28), including "Let their table become a snare before them" (v. 22, cited in Romans 11:9), has been read by Protestant commentators as prophetic judgment on those who reject the Messiah.

The psalm's conclusion turns to hope: "Let heaven and earth praise him, the seas and everything that moves in them. For God will save Zion" (vv. 34-35). Protestant theology reads this as the vindication that follows the cross — resurrection, ascension, and the salvation of God's people. The psalm thus encapsulates the entire gospel narrative: innocent suffering, unjust persecution, divine vindication, and cosmic praise.
