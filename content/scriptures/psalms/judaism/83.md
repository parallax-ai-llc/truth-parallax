## Jewish Reading of Psalm 83

Psalm 83 is read in Jewish tradition within the framework of the Psalter's five-book division, which mirrors the five books of the Torah. The rabbinic commentators — Rashi, Ibn Ezra, Radak, and the Midrash Tehillim — offer layered interpretations that attend to *peshat* (plain sense), *remez* (allusion), *derash* (homiletical meaning), and *sod* (mystical meaning).

## Key Themes

### Rabbinic Commentary

The Midrash Tehillim on this psalm draws connections between the psalmist's words and the broader narrative of Torah. The themes of a prayer against enemies are woven into discussions of halakhah (law) and aggadah (narrative), demonstrating the rabbinic conviction that every verse of Scripture yields multiple layers of meaning.

### Liturgical Setting

This psalm finds its place in Jewish worship through the daily, Shabbat, or festival liturgy. The Psalms are chanted as part of *Pesukei de-Zimra* (Verses of Praise) in the morning service, and specific psalms are assigned to particular days and occasions, embedding them in the rhythm of Jewish sacred time.

### Ethical Teaching

Jewish ethical literature (*mussar*) draws on the Psalms for moral instruction. The themes of imprecation, enemies, national prayer found in this psalm inform the cultivation of *middot* (character traits) that stand at the heart of Jewish moral formation.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah connection**: The psalm resonates with Torah narratives and commandments
- **Rabbinic interpretation**: Multiple layers of meaning in the tradition
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes Jewish daily and festival worship
- **Ethical formation**: Practical guidance for righteous living
