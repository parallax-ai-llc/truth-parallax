Psalm 4 is traditionally read as an evening psalm, paired with Psalm 3 as its morning counterpart. Verse 8 — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep, for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety" — is incorporated into the *Kriat Shema al HaMitah* (the bedtime Shema), the prayers said before sleep. The juxtaposition of Psalms 3 and 4 creates a liturgical frame for the Jewish day: trust in God upon waking (Psalm 3:5) and trust in God before sleeping (Psalm 4:8).

The Talmud (Berakhot 4b-5a) discusses verse 4, "Tremble and do not sin; ponder in your own hearts upon your beds, and be silent, Selah," in the context of the nightly *cheshbon ha-nefesh* (accounting of the soul). Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish interprets this as instruction to examine one's actions each night, reflecting on whether one has sinned during the day and resolving to do better. This practice of nightly self-examination became central to the Mussar movement founded by Rabbi Israel Salanter in the nineteenth century.

The contrast between those who seek material prosperity and the psalmist's spiritual joy (4:6-7) — "You have put more gladness in my heart than they have when their grain and wine abound" — reflects the Jewish concept of *simchah shel mitzvah* (the joy of fulfilling commandments). The Vilna Gaon taught that the most precious thing in life is the joy that comes from serving God, not from wealth or pleasure. This verse has been used in Jewish ethical literature to counsel contentment with one's portion, echoing the Mishnah (Avot 4:1): "Who is rich? One who is happy with his lot."
