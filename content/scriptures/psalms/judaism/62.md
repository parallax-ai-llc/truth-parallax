## Silence and Trust Before God Alone

Psalm 62's opening — "For God alone my soul waits in silence" (v. 1) — uses the rare Hebrew word *dumiyyah* (silence/stillness), which Rashi connects to resigned acceptance and trust. The word appears only in the Psalms (cf. 39:2, 65:1) and conveys not merely the absence of speech but a positive spiritual state — the quieting of all competing claims upon the soul. The *mussar* movement, particularly Rabbi Yisrael Salanter, emphasized this kind of inner silence as essential to moral and spiritual growth, teaching that *bitachon* requires stilling the anxious voice of the *yetzer hara*.

The psalm's social critique is sharp: "Those of low estate are but a breath; those of high estate are a delusion" (v. 9). Ibn Ezra observes that this equalizing judgment abolishes all social pretension before God — neither birth nor wealth nor reputation creates genuine security. This resonates with the Jewish liturgy for Yom Kippur, when every Jew, regardless of status, stands equally before the divine Judge. The additional warning, "if riches increase, set not your heart on them" (v. 10), is echoed in Pirkei Avot (6:9): when a person dies, "neither silver nor gold nor precious stones accompany him, but only Torah and good deeds."

The psalm's conclusion — "power belongs to God, and... steadfast love" (vv. 11-12) — presents what Jewish theology calls the balance between *middat ha-din* (the attribute of justice/power) and *middat ha-rachamim* (the attribute of mercy/love). The Midrash teaches that God created the world initially with justice alone but saw it could not endure, so He added mercy. This psalm's pairing of the two attributes reflects that foundational theological conviction. The assertion that God "repays each according to his work" (v. 12) reinforces the Jewish belief in divine recompense, a cornerstone of the *Ani Ma'amin* (Thirteen Principles of Faith).
