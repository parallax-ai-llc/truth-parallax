## God's March from Sinai to Zion

Psalm 68 is among the most important psalms for the festival of Shavuot (Pentecost), as it describes God's theophany at Sinai and His procession to Zion. The opening verse — "God shall arise, his enemies shall be scattered" (v. 1) — is recited in the synagogue when the Torah scroll is removed from the Ark for public reading, connecting the revelation at Sinai with the ongoing encounter with Torah in communal worship. The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) elaborates on verse 8 ("the earth trembled, the heavens poured down rain") as describing the cosmic response to God's descent on Sinai to give the Torah.

Verse 18 — "The chariots of God are twice ten thousand, thousands upon thousands; the Lord is among them; Sinai is in the sanctuary" — is understood in rabbinic tradition as describing the angelic hosts that accompanied the giving of the Torah. The Midrash Tehillim teaches that God brought 22,000 angels to Sinai, and that these same angelic hosts now dwell in the Temple sanctuary. This verse links Sinai and Zion as complementary locations of divine revelation — the Torah given at Sinai is housed in and transmitted from the Temple in Zion.

The psalm's social vision — "father of the fatherless and protector of widows" (v. 5), "he sets the solitary in a home" and "leads out the prisoners to prosperity" (v. 6) — reflects the Torah's extensive legislation protecting vulnerable populations. The commandments to care for orphans, widows, and strangers (Deuteronomy 10:18, 24:17-21) are grounded in the character of the God described in this psalm. Jewish communal institutions — *tzedakah* funds, *gemilut chasadim* societies, and the network of care for the poor — embody this psalm's theological vision of a God whose power is expressed through justice for the weak.
