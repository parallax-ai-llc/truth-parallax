## Praise Rising from the Cave

Psalm 57 is attributed to David hiding from Saul in a cave, and the Midrash Tehillim draws a powerful lesson from the setting: David composed praise from the darkest, most confined circumstances. The rabbis teach that the deepest praise comes not from comfort but from constriction (*metzar*) — the same root as *Mitzrayim* (Egypt). Just as Israel's salvation began in the narrowest place of bondage, David's most expansive praise arose from the narrowness of a cave. The plea for mercy (v. 1) and the confidence in God's faithfulness (v. 3) coexist in Jewish prayer, which does not require the resolution of danger before thanksgiving begins.

Rashi interprets the "shadow of your wings" (v. 1) as referring to the divine *Shechinah* (Presence), which accompanied David even in the cave. This connects to the broader rabbinic teaching that the *Shechinah* descends to be with those who suffer — "In all their affliction He was afflicted" (Isaiah 63:9). The psalm's assertion that God "sends out his steadfast love and his faithfulness" (v. 3) portrays *chesed* and *emet* as active divine agents, a personification that the Midrash develops into a rich theology of God's attributes working on behalf of the righteous.

The declaration "I will awake the dawn!" (v. 8) is interpreted in the Talmud (Berakhot 3b) as David's claim that he awakened the dawn rather than the dawn awakening him — he rose before daylight for prayer and praise. This became the basis for the custom of the pious (*chasidim*) to rise before dawn for the early morning prayers. The refrain "Be exalted, O God, above the heavens! Let your glory be over all the earth!" (vv. 5, 11) expresses the universalism present within Jewish monotheism — God's glory is not confined to Israel but extends over all creation.
