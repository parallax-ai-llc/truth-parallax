Psalm 40 opens with testimony of patient waiting: "I waited patiently for the LORD; he turned to me and heard my cry" (40:1). The Hebrew *kavoh kiviti* — an emphatic doubling — emphasizes the intensity of this waiting. Jewish theology of prayer recognizes that divine response often requires sustained patience. The Talmud (Berakhot 32b) teaches that the early pious ones (*chasidim rishonim*) would wait an hour before praying, to direct their hearts toward God.

The remarkable declaration — "Sacrifice and offering you did not desire, but my ears you have opened" (40:6) — is understood in Jewish tradition not as abolishing the sacrificial system but as prioritizing inner obedience over external ritual. The Prophets consistently delivered this message: "Does the LORD delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices as much as in obeying the LORD?" (1 Samuel 15:22). Jewish post-Temple theology extended this principle, teaching that prayer, Torah study, and acts of kindness replace sacrifices (Berakhot 26b; Avot d'Rabbi Natan 4:5).

The psalmist's declaration, "I delight to do your will, O my God; your Torah is within my heart" (40:8), expresses the ideal of *Torah lishmah* — Torah studied and observed for its own sake, out of love for God. This concept, elaborated by the Rambam and later Jewish thinkers, represents the highest level of religious devotion: not obedience from fear of punishment or hope of reward, but service flowing from a heart transformed by Torah.
