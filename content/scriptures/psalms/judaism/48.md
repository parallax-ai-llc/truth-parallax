Psalm 48 is a Song of Zion celebrating Jerusalem as "the city of our God, his holy mountain, beautiful in its loftiness, the joy of the whole earth" (48:1-2). Jewish love for Jerusalem is expressed in this psalm with an intensity that has sustained Jewish devotion through two millennia of exile. The Talmud (Kiddushin 49b) declares that "ten measures of beauty descended to the world; nine were taken by Jerusalem."

The psalm's description of hostile kings who "saw her and were astounded; they fled in terror" (48:5) is connected by Rashi and other commentators to the invasion of Sennacherib, when the Assyrian army was miraculously defeated at the gates of Jerusalem (2 Kings 18-19). This historical memory reinforces the theological conviction that Jerusalem's security rests not in military might but in God's presence within her.

The instruction to "walk about Zion, go around her, count her towers" (48:12) is fulfilled in Jewish practice by those who circle the Old City walls and by pilgrims who visit the *Kotel* (Western Wall) — the last remaining structure from the Temple complex. The psalm's closing affirmation — "this God is our God for ever and ever; he will be our guide even to the end" (48:14) — is recited at funerals, connecting God's guidance of Israel with his faithfulness beyond death.
