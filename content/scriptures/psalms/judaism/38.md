Psalm 38 is a penitential psalm whose raw honesty about sin's consequences makes it a powerful text for Jewish reflection during the *Yamim Noraim* (Days of Awe). David's opening plea — "LORD, do not rebuke me in your anger" (38:1) — echoes the language of Psalm 6 and expresses the penitent's awareness that divine justice would be devastating if applied without mercy. The Jewish liturgy of Yom Kippur is built on this tension between justice (*din*) and mercy (*rachamim*).

The vivid physical suffering described — "My wounds fester and are loathsome because of my sinful folly" (38:5) — illustrates the rabbinic teaching that sin affects the whole person. The Talmud (Nedarim 41a) states, "There is no suffering without sin," though this principle is qualified elsewhere by the concept of "afflictions of love" (*yissurin shel ahavah*) — suffering that comes not as punishment but as purification (Berakhot 5a).

The psalm's conclusion — "Come quickly to help me, my Lord and my Savior" (38:22) — uses the divine name in conjunction with *teshuvati* (my salvation). Jewish theology grounds all hope for deliverance in HaShem alone. No human mediator, no ritual system, no institutional authority can substitute for the direct relationship between the penitent soul and the God who hears prayer. This psalm models the *teshuvah* that stands at the heart of Jewish spiritual life.
