Psalm 23 is among the most beloved texts in Tehillim and is recited at Jewish funerals, during the Shabbat meal, and in times of personal distress. The shepherd metaphor has deep roots in Jewish consciousness: Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, and David were all shepherds, and the Midrash teaches that God tested Moses' fitness for leadership by observing how he tended his flock. The declaration "The Lord is my shepherd" (23:1) thus invokes the entire history of pastoral leadership in Israel while affirming that God is the ultimate Shepherd of His people.

The phrase "He leads me in paths of righteousness for his name's sake" (23:3) is understood by Rashi as meaning that God guides the righteous for the honor of His own name — Israel's well-being is connected to God's reputation among the nations. The "valley of the shadow of death" (*gei tzalmavet*, 23:4) is interpreted by the Targum as the exile among the nations, through which God accompanies Israel with His protective presence. The "rod and staff" symbolize Torah and mitzvot, the instruments by which God guides and corrects His people even in the darkest circumstances.

The table prepared "in the presence of my enemies" (23:5) is associated by the Midrash with the messianic banquet — the festive meal that God will prepare for the righteous in the World to Come, while the nations who persecuted Israel look on. The anointing with oil recalls the consecration of kings and priests, suggesting that every Jew is anointed for sacred service. The psalm's final verse — "I shall dwell in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6) — is recited at the Shabbat table because Shabbat itself is a foretaste of the *olam ha-ba*, the eternal world where the righteous dwell in God's presence. The word "forever" (*l'orekh yamim*) literally means "for length of days," expressing the boundless duration of the soul's communion with God.
