Psalm 9, the beginning of an acrostic that continues into Psalm 10, opens with wholehearted thanksgiving: "I will give thanks to the Lord with my whole heart; I will recount all of your wonderful deeds" (9:1). The Midrash Tehillim notes that "with my whole heart" (*b'khol libi*) echoes the Shema's command to love God "with all your heart" (*b'khol l'vavkha*, Deuteronomy 6:5), connecting thanksgiving to the fundamental obligation of love. The acrostic structure — each verse beginning with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet — is itself a form of praise, suggesting that God's praises require the full range of human language.

The psalm's affirmation that God "judges the world with righteousness" and "judges the peoples with uprightness" (9:8) is foundational to the Jewish understanding of divine justice. The Rosh Hashanah liturgy declares God as *Melekh ha-Mishpat* (King of Judgment), and the High Holy Days are structured around the belief that God evaluates every human action. Yet Jewish theology tempers judgment with mercy: the Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 17b) teaches that God wraps Himself in a tallit and shows Moses the order of prayer, including the thirteen attributes of mercy (Exodus 34:6-7), demonstrating that mercy accompanies and tempers judgment.

The psalm's concern for the oppressed — "The Lord is a stronghold for the oppressed" (9:9) — connects to the prophetic tradition's emphasis on social justice. The Hebrew word for "oppressed" (*dakh*) literally means "crushed," and Jewish legal traditions including the laws of charity (*tzedakah*), the sabbatical year (*shemittah*), and the jubilee (*yovel*) are designed to prevent and remedy the crushing of the vulnerable. The Talmud teaches that *tzedakah* (righteousness/charity) is equal in weight to all the other commandments combined (Bava Batra 9a), reflecting the psalm's vision of a God who acts on behalf of the downtrodden.
