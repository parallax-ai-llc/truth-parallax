## Tefillah le-Moshe: The Prayer of Moses

Jewish tradition attributes Psalm 90 to Moses, making it the oldest psalm in the Psalter. The Midrash Tehillim teaches that Moses composed the eleven psalms from 90 to 100. This psalm is recited in some communities at funeral services and on the eve of Yom Kippur.

## Key Themes

### God as Eternal Dwelling (90:1)

"Lord, You have been our dwelling place (*ma'on*) in every generation." Rashi connects *ma'on* to the idea that God is the place (*makom*) of the world, but the world is not His place — a teaching found in Genesis Rabbah 68:9. God transcends space and time while providing refuge for every generation.

### "A thousand years in Your sight" (90:4)

This verse became central to rabbinic calculations of cosmic history. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 97a) records the tradition of a six-thousand-year cosmic week, with each "day" corresponding to a thousand years, followed by a cosmic Sabbath. This verse provides the scriptural basis for that schema.

### "Teach us to number our days" (90:12)

The concept of *cheshbon ha-nefesh* (accounting of the soul) draws on this verse. The Mishnah (Avot 2:10) teaches: "Repent one day before your death" — since no one knows the day of death, one must repent every day. This psalm's meditation on mortality serves the same purpose.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah connection**: The psalm resonates with Torah narratives and commandments
- **Rabbinic interpretation**: Multiple layers of meaning in the tradition
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes Jewish daily and festival worship
- **Ethical formation**: Practical guidance for righteous living
