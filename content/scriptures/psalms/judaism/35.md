Psalm 35 is a psalm of David calling upon HaShem as divine warrior: "Contend, LORD, with those who contend with me" (35:1). Jewish tradition reads this not as a personal vendetta but as an appeal to the *Dayan Emet* — the True Judge — to administer justice that humans cannot. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) records that when Beruriah counseled her husband Rabbi Meir to pray for sinners' repentance rather than their destruction, she drew on the principle that righteous indignation should be directed at sin, not sinners.

The psalmist's account of ingratitude — "I put on sackcloth and humbled myself with fasting... yet when I stumbled, they gathered in glee" (35:13, 15) — resonates with the Jewish experience of being repaid with hatred for contributions to the societies in which Jews lived. The Midrash applies such passages to Israel's relationship with hostile nations, finding in David's experience a paradigm for the nation's suffering.

David's petition for vindication — "may those who delight in my vindication shout for joy and gladness" (35:27) — expresses the Jewish understanding that God's justice, when finally revealed, will bring joy not only to the vindicated but to all who love righteousness. This eschatological hope sustains Jewish faith through periods of injustice, maintaining that God's *mishpat* (justice) will ultimately prevail over human cruelty.
