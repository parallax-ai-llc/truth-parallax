Psalm 11 opens with David's declaration of trust against the counsel to flee: "In the Lord I put my trust; how can you say to my soul, 'Flee like a bird to your mountain'?" (11:1). Rashi identifies the advisors as those who counseled David to flee from Saul, and Ibn Ezra understands the "bird" metaphor as suggesting cowardice — abandoning one's divine mission out of fear. Jewish tradition values *bitachon* (trust in God) as one of the highest spiritual virtues, and this psalm has been cited by Jewish thinkers from the medieval *Chovot HaLevavot* (Duties of the Heart) to modern commentators as a model of unwavering faith.

The statement "The Lord tests the righteous" (11:5) is important in Jewish theodicy. The Midrash (Bereishit Rabbah 55:1) compares God's testing to a potter who tests the strong vessels, not the weak ones — God tests the righteous because they can bear it and grow from the experience. This understanding of suffering as divine testing pervades the book of Job and has been central to Jewish responses to catastrophe throughout history. The Hasidic master Rabbi Nachman of Breslov taught that every test is an opportunity for spiritual elevation.

The concluding promise — "the upright shall behold his face" (11:7) — is understood in Jewish mystical tradition as referring to the revelation of the *Shekhinah* (divine presence). The Zohar teaches that the righteous merit the vision of the Shekhinah both in this world, through spiritual attainment, and in the World to Come. The Talmud (Berakhot 17a) describes the World to Come as a state where "the righteous sit with their crowns on their heads and enjoy the radiance of the Shekhinah," and this psalm verse is among the texts supporting that vision.
