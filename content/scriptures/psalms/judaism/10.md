Psalm 10, the continuation of the acrostic begun in Psalm 9, opens with one of Tanakh's most direct confrontations with divine hiddenness: "Why, O Lord, do you stand far away? Why do you hide yourself in times of trouble?" (10:1). This question is at the heart of Jewish theological reflection on *hester panim* (the hiding of God's face), a concept developed extensively in rabbinic and mystical literature. The Talmud (Chagigah 5b) teaches that even when God hides His face, He weeps in the inner chambers — divine absence is not indifference but a mysterious mode of presence.

The detailed description of the wicked in verses 2-11 — arrogant, self-sufficient, predatory, claiming "God has forgotten" (10:11) — has been applied in Jewish ethical literature to the phenomenon of power without accountability. Maimonides, in his *Guide for the Perplexed* (III:51), argues that the wicked person's denial of divine providence stems from intellectual failure — an inability or unwillingness to perceive God's governance of the world. The Chasidic masters, by contrast, emphasized the moral dimension: the wicked deny God not because they cannot see Him but because seeing Him would require changing their behavior.

The psalm's resolution affirms God's kingship — "The Lord is king forever and ever" (10:16) — and His special care for "the fatherless and the oppressed" (10:18). Jewish law's meticulous protections for orphans, widows, and strangers (*gerim*) are grounded in texts like these. The Shulchan Aruch devotes extensive sections to the obligations toward orphans, and the rabbis taught that one who raises an orphan is considered as though he gave birth to that child (Sanhedrin 19b). The psalm's theodicy resolves not in philosophical explanation but in the affirmation of God's active justice and the community's obligation to embody it.
