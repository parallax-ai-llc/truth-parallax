Psalm 37 is an acrostic wisdom psalm that addresses the perennial question of *tzaddik v'ra lo, rasha v'tov lo* — why do the righteous suffer while the wicked prosper? This question occupies a central place in Jewish theology, from the book of Job to the Talmud (Berakhot 7a), where even Moses is said to have asked God this question. David's response is practical wisdom: "Do not fret because of those who are evil" (37:1).

The psalm's ethical instruction — "Trust in the LORD and do good; dwell in the land and cultivate faithfulness" (37:3) — embodies the Jewish integration of faith and action. *Bitachon* (trust) and *ma'asim tovim* (good deeds) are inseparable in Jewish thought. The Talmud (Kiddushin 40b) teaches that study is greater than action because study leads to action — but action remains the goal. The psalmist models this integration: trust God, and do good.

The promise that "the meek will inherit the land" (37:11) — *va'anavim yirshu aretz* — carries profound significance for Jewish theology of the Land of Israel. The righteous remnant, not the powerful, will ultimately possess Eretz Yisrael. This verse sustained Jewish hope during centuries of exile: the land belongs by divine promise to those who embody humility and righteousness, regardless of what empires rise and fall.
