## Teshuvah: The Way of Return

Psalm 51 is the quintessential psalm of *teshuvah* (repentance/return) in Jewish tradition. The superscription links it to David's sin with Bathsheba, and the Talmud (Avodah Zarah 4b-5a) suggests that David's sin and repentance were divinely orchestrated to teach Israel that *teshuvah* is always possible — even for the greatest sinners. Rashi comments on verse 3 ("wash me thoroughly from my iniquity") that David does not ask for a partial cleansing but for the total removal of sin, modeling the completeness that *teshuvah* requires.

The Midrash Tehillim elaborates extensively on verse 4, "Against you, you only, have I sinned," noting the paradox: David sinned against Bathsheba, Uriah, and the nation, yet he says "against you only." The midrashic resolution is that all sin is ultimately a breach in the relationship with HaShem, and *teshuvah* addresses that primary rupture. Verse 5 ("in sin did my mother conceive me") is interpreted by most Jewish commentators not as a doctrine of inherited sin but as a poetic expression of the human condition — the *yetzer hara* (evil inclination) is present from birth, but so is the capacity to choose good.

This psalm is recited as part of the *Selichot* (penitential prayers) leading up to Yom Kippur and during the High Holy Days. The Radak (Rabbi David Kimchi) emphasizes that verse 17 — "The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit" — teaches that internal transformation is more fundamental than ritual performance, a principle echoed throughout the Prophets (cf. Isaiah 1:11-17, Micah 6:6-8). Yet this is not a rejection of ritual but a prioritization: the broken heart makes ritual meaningful. The psalm's concluding prayer for Jerusalem's restoration (vv. 18-19) connects personal *teshuvah* to communal and national redemption — a characteristically Jewish integration.
