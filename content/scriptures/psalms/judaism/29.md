Psalm 29 is recited in the Shabbat morning liturgy as the Torah scroll is returned to the Ark, and its seven-fold "voice of the LORD" (*kol Adonai*) is one of the most powerful poetic structures in Tehillim. The Talmud (Zevachim 116a) records a tradition that this psalm describes the giving of Torah at Sinai, when God's voice shook the earth and the nations trembled.

The Midrash (Shemot Rabbah 29:9) teaches that the "voice of the LORD" at Sinai divided into seventy languages, so that all nations could hear. This tradition reflects the universalist dimension of Jewish theology: Torah, while given to Israel, contains wisdom for all humanity. The psalm's opening call to the "heavenly beings" (*bnei elim*) to acknowledge God's glory (29:1-2) extends worship beyond Israel to the entire cosmic order.

The psalm's conclusion — "The LORD gives strength to his people; the LORD blesses his people with peace" (29:11) — is recited at the end of the Priestly Blessing (*Birkat Kohanim*) and throughout Jewish liturgy. The juxtaposition of divine power (the storm) and divine peace (*shalom*) is characteristically Jewish: the same God whose voice shatters cedars (29:5) bestows *shalom* upon his people. True peace is not the absence of power but the presence of a God who uses his power for blessing.
