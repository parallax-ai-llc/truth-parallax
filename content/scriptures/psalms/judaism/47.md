Psalm 47 is recited seven times before the blowing of the *shofar* on Rosh Hashanah, making it one of the most liturgically significant psalms in the Jewish year. The psalm's declaration "God has ascended amid shouts of joy, the LORD amid the sounding of trumpets (*shofar*)" (47:5) connects directly to the central ritual of Rosh Hashanah — the sounding of the *shofar* that proclaims God's kingship over all creation.

The psalm's universal scope — "God reigns over the nations; God is seated on his holy throne" (47:8) — expresses the Rosh Hashanah theme of *Malkhuyot* (God's Sovereignty). The liturgy of Rosh Hashanah celebrates God as King not only of Israel but of the entire world. The *Aleinu* prayer, which concludes every Jewish service, draws on this universalist vision: "On that day the LORD will be one and his name one" (Zechariah 14:9).

The gathering of "the nobles of the nations" as "the people of the God of Abraham" (47:9) anticipates the prophetic vision of the nations streaming to Zion (Isaiah 2:2-3). Jewish eschatology affirms that the messianic age will bring not the destruction of the nations but their recognition of the one God. The Rambam (Hilkhot Melakhim 11-12) envisions the Messianic King establishing a world of justice and knowledge of God — precisely the universal reign this psalm celebrates.
