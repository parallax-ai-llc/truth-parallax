## Overview

In Judaism, the Book of Psalms (*Sefer Tehillim*, "Book of Praises") occupies a singular place in the Ketuvim (Writings) section of the Tanakh. Traditionally attributed primarily to King David, the Psalms serve as the foundation of Jewish liturgical prayer, personal devotion, and communal worship. The recitation of Tehillim is considered a powerful spiritual practice — recited at times of illness, danger, mourning, and celebration.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Rabbinic Interpretation
The Talmud and Midrash Tehillim (Shocher Tov) provide extensive commentary:
- **David as author and prophet**: The Talmud (Bava Batra 14b-15a) discusses the compilation of the Psalms
- **Ten expressions of praise**: The Midrash identifies ten Hebrew terms for song used in the Psalms
- **Correspondence with Torah**: The fivefold division mirrors the five books of Moses

### Medieval Commentators
- **Rashi**: Peshat-oriented commentary with midrashic elements
- **Ibn Ezra**: Grammatical-philological analysis
- **Radak (David Kimchi)**: Systematic verse-by-verse commentary, widely studied
- **Meiri**: Philosophical-ethical interpretation

### Kabbalistic Reading
The Zohar and Lurianic Kabbalah assign mystical significance to the Psalms:
- Specific psalms correspond to the *sefirot*
- Recitation of Tehillim effects *tikkun* (cosmic repair)
- The letters and words contain hidden divine names

## Liturgical Use

| Context | Psalms Used |
|---------|-------------|
| Daily Shacharit (Morning) | Pesukei d'Zimra: Psalms 145-150 |
| Kabbalat Shabbat (Friday evening) | Psalms 95-99, 29 |
| Hallel (Festivals) | Psalms 113-118 |
| Mourning/Shiva | Selected Tehillim recited continuously |
| Monthly (Rosh Chodesh) | Division into 30 daily portions |

## Theological Emphases

- **Prayer as service of the heart**: The Psalms model authentic conversation with God
- **Divine kingship**: God as Melekh (King) over Israel and all nations
- **Covenant faithfulness**: God's *chesed* (lovingkindness) as the foundation of hope
- **The righteous and the wicked**: Wisdom psalms exploring theodicy and moral order
