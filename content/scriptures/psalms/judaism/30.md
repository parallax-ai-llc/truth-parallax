Psalm 30 bears the superscription "A Song at the Dedication of the House" and is traditionally recited during Chanukah, connecting David's personal thanksgiving to the rededication of the Temple by the Maccabees. The psalm is also recited daily as part of *Pesukei d'Zimra* (Verses of Praise) in the morning service, making it one of the most frequently chanted psalms in Jewish liturgy.

The celebrated verse "Weeping may tarry for the night, but joy comes in the morning" (30:5) — *ba'erev yalin bekhi, v'laboker rinah* — has become a foundational expression of Jewish resilience. Through destruction, exile, persecution, and the Shoah, the Jewish people have clung to this promise. The "night" of suffering is real but temporary; the "morning" of redemption is certain.

David's confession, "When I felt secure, I said, 'I will never be shaken'" (30:6), is read by the Malbim as warning against spiritual complacency. The rabbis taught that one should not trust in one's own merit but in God's mercy. This mirrors the broader Jewish dialectic between *bitachon* (trust in God) and *hishtadlut* (human effort) — one must work diligently while recognizing that all outcomes rest in HaShem's hands.
