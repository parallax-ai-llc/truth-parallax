Psalm 44 is a communal lament of devastating honesty. The people recount God's past deliverances (44:1-8) but then confront a present reality of defeat and humiliation: "You gave us up to be devoured like sheep" (44:11). Most significantly, the community insists on its innocence: "All this came upon us, though we had not forgotten you; we had not been false to your covenant" (44:17). This is not the theology of Deuteronomy, where suffering follows sin; this is the mystery of innocent suffering.

Jewish theology after the Shoah has returned to Psalm 44 with renewed intensity. The verse "For your sake we are killed all day long; we are considered as sheep to be slaughtered" (44:22) was invoked by rabbis and scholars struggling to comprehend the Holocaust. Theologians like Emil Fackenheim and Irving Greenberg grappled with how Jewish faith could persist when a psalm written millennia ago so precisely described the experience of the gas chambers.

The psalm's bold demand — "Awake, Lord! Why do you sleep? Rise up! Do not reject us forever!" (44:23) — is not blasphemy but *chutzpah klapei shamaya* — audacious prayer directed toward heaven. The Talmud (Berakhot 32a) records that Moses, Abraham, and other biblical figures argued with God, and their boldness was counted as righteousness. This psalm teaches that faith is not passive acceptance but active engagement with God, even when God seems absent.
