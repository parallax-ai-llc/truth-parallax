## The Demand for True Justice

Psalm 58 opens by confronting unjust judges: "Do you indeed decree what is right, you gods?" (v. 1). The word *elim* (gods/powers) is understood by most Jewish commentators as referring to human judges, who are called *elohim* (Exodus 21:6, 22:8-9) because they exercise divine-like authority over human affairs. The Talmud (Shabbat 10a) teaches that a judge who renders true judgment becomes "a partner with God in the work of creation," while a corrupt judge destroys the world. This psalm serves as a prophetic indictment of judicial corruption, a concern permeating Jewish legal tradition.

Rashi and the Radak interpret the metaphors of deaf adders (v. 4) and dissolved snails (v. 8) as vivid illustrations of the wicked's willful refusal to heed moral instruction. The serpent imagery connects to the *yetzer hara* (evil inclination), which the rabbis sometimes depict as a serpent. The wicked are "estranged from the womb" (v. 3) — a statement the Midrash interprets not as predestination but as the observation that some people choose wickedness so early and so consistently that they seem born to it.

The psalm's concluding assertion — "Surely there is a reward for the righteous; surely there is a God who judges on earth" (v. 11) — is a foundational statement of Jewish faith in divine justice. The *Mishnah Avot* (2:16) teaches: "Know what is above you — a seeing eye, a hearing ear, and all your deeds written in a book." This psalm reinforces that conviction. Jewish theodicy, while acknowledging the mystery of suffering, maintains that justice ultimately prevails — if not in this world, then in the *Olam HaBa* (World to Come).
