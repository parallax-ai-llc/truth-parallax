Psalm 31 contains the phrase "Into your hands I commit my spirit" (31:5) — *b'yadkha afkid ruchi* — which is recited as part of the *Kriat Shema al HaMitah*, the bedtime prayer. Every night, the observant Jew entrusts their soul to God before sleep, echoing David's act of radical trust. This practice reflects the Talmudic teaching (Berakhot 57b) that sleep is one-sixtieth of death, and waking is a daily resurrection.

The psalm's extensive description of suffering — persecution by enemies, abandonment by friends, physical decline (31:9-13) — gives voice to Jewish experiences of exile and oppression. The Midrash applies these verses to the suffering of Israel among the nations. Yet David's response is not despair but declaration: "But I trust in you, LORD; I say, 'You are my God.' My times are in your hands" (31:14-15). This confession of *emunah* in the midst of suffering is paradigmatic for Jewish faith.

The psalm concludes with a call to "love the LORD, all his faithful people" and to "be strong and take heart" (31:23-24). Rashi interprets this as David encouraging the righteous who suffer: their faithfulness is not unseen, and God "preserves the faithful" even when the present moment offers no visible evidence of his care. This theology of hidden providence sustains Jewish faith through history's darkest chapters.
