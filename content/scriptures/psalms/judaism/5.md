Psalm 5 is a morning prayer that Radak connects to the daily *tamid* (perpetual) offering in the Temple. The verse "in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" (5:3) uses the verb *arokh*, which specifically describes the priestly arrangement of the offerings on the altar. After the Temple's destruction, Jewish liturgy understood prayer as replacing sacrifice, and the *Shacharit* (morning) service corresponds to the morning *tamid*. This psalm thus bridges the Temple era and the synagogue era, maintaining continuity of worship through changed circumstances.

The psalm's strong language about God's hatred of evil (5:4-6) is discussed in rabbinic literature with characteristic nuance. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) records that Rabbi Meir's wife Bruria corrected him when he prayed for the destruction of wicked neighbors, citing Psalm 104:35: "Let sins cease from the land" — not sinners, but sins. Jewish theology generally prefers to pray for repentance rather than destruction, reading the psalm's harsh language as describing God's stance toward unrepented evil rather than toward the persons themselves.

Verse 8, "But I, through the abundance of your steadfast love, will enter your house," has been connected to the daily act of entering the synagogue for prayer. The custom of pausing at the doorway of the synagogue and reciting a verse of Torah before entering reflects the psalmist's awareness that access to God's presence is a gift of *chesed* (lovingkindness), not a right. Metzudat David notes that the psalmist contrasts his own entrance through God's love with the exclusion of the wicked — privilege and responsibility are inseparable in Jewish worship.
