## From Fear to Faithfulness

Psalm 56 is linked to David's dangerous sojourn among the Philistines (1 Samuel 21:10-15). Rashi notes that the superscription's phrase "a dove on far-off terebinths" (*yonat elem rechokim*) may describe David himself — like a silent dove far from home, surrounded by enemies. The Midrash elaborates that David behaved as a madman before Achish of Gath, an act of cunning survival that this psalm contextualizes within an inner life of profound trust in God.

The psalm's most celebrated verse — "You have kept count of my tossings; put my tears in your bottle" (v. 8) — has deeply influenced Jewish understanding of divine compassion. The Talmud (Berakhot 32b) teaches that "the gates of tears are never shut," meaning that prayers accompanied by tears always reach heaven. The image of God preserving tears in a bottle (*nod*) suggests that no suffering is wasted — it is collected, remembered, and ultimately transformed. The *mussar* tradition has emphasized this verse as evidence that God's knowledge of individual suffering is not distant or abstract but intimate and preserving.

The repeated refrain — "In God I trust; I shall not be afraid. What can flesh do to me?" (v. 4, cf. vv. 10-11) — articulates the principle of *bitachon* that permeates Jewish ethical literature. Ibn Ezra distinguishes between *emunah* (faith/belief) and *bitachon* (active trust) — the latter involves not merely believing that God exists but conducting one's life as though God's power is more determinative than any human threat. The psalm's movement from fear (v. 3) through trust (v. 4) to testimony (v. 13) traces the path of spiritual maturation that the rabbis commend to every Jew.
