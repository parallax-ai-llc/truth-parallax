Psalm 3, titled "A Psalm of David when he fled from Absalom his son," is the first psalm with a historical superscription, linking Tehillim to the narrative books of Tanakh. The Talmud (Berakhot 7b) asks why David composed a psalm during such a crisis and answers that the rebellion confirmed the prophecy of Nathan (2 Samuel 12:11) — David found comfort in knowing that the tragedy was divine discipline from a loving God, not random catastrophe. The Midrash notes that despite being pursued by his own son, David composed a *mizmor* (song), not a *kinah* (elegy), because he trusted in divine deliverance.

Verse 5, "I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the Lord sustained me," is recited as part of the *Modeh Ani* prayer said upon waking each morning. Jewish liturgy treats every morning awakening as a small resurrection — the Talmud (Berakhot 57b) says that sleep is one-sixtieth of death, and waking is a foretaste of the resurrection of the dead. The verse connects the personal experience of David's imperiled sleep to the daily experience of every Jew who wakes and gives thanks.

The declaration "Salvation belongs to the Lord; your blessing be on your people" (3:8) is a cornerstone of Jewish theology. The Hebrew *la-YHVH ha-yeshu'ah* places the emphasis on God as the sole source of salvation. Metzudat David explains that David prays not only for himself but for all Israel — even in personal extremity, the righteous person's concern extends to the community. This communal dimension of prayer is fundamental to Jewish liturgical practice, where the *minyan* (quorum of ten) enables the fullest expression of communal worship.
