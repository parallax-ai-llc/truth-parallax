## Divine Protection Against Night Dangers

Psalm 59 is attributed to David when Saul sent men to watch his house by night (1 Samuel 19:11). The Midrash Tehillim emphasizes the nocturnal setting — the enemies circled like dogs in the darkness — and draws from it a lesson about the unseen spiritual dangers that threaten the righteous. The evening recitation of the *Shema* includes prayers for divine protection during the night, and this psalm's imagery of enemies prowling at dusk (vv. 6, 14) resonates with those liturgical prayers.

Rashi's commentary on verse 11 — "Do not kill them, lest my people forget" — highlights David's remarkable request that God not annihilate the wicked entirely but scatter them as a visible reminder of divine judgment. The Radak explains that complete destruction might lead Israel to forget the lessons of God's justice, while ongoing visibility of the humbled wicked serves as a perpetual witness. This interpretation reveals a characteristically Jewish concern with memory and education — historical events must be preserved in communal memory for the instruction of future generations.

The psalm's refrain — "O my Strength, I will watch for you, for you, O God, are my fortress" (v. 9) — expresses the concept of *shemirah* (watchfulness/guarding) that pervades Jewish spiritual life. The watchman's posture — alert, expectant, positioned on the walls — becomes a metaphor for the spiritual vigilance that Jewish tradition demands. The conclusion, celebrating God's "steadfast love" (v. 17), connects divine protection to *chesed* — the covenantal loyalty that binds God to Israel. Jewish morning prayers begin with an acknowledgment of God's *chesed*, and this psalm provides scriptural basis for that liturgical practice.
