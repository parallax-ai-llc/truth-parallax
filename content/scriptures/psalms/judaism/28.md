Psalm 28 opens with David crying out to HaShem as "my rock" (28:1) — *tzuri* in Hebrew, a name for God that emphasizes his reliability and permanence. The Midrash connects this to the rock in the wilderness from which water flowed (Exodus 17:6), teaching that just as God sustained Israel in the desert, he sustains the faithful in every generation.

David's plea that God not remain silent (28:1) addresses a profound concern in Jewish theology: the hiddenness of God (*hester panim*). The concept of God "hiding his face" appears throughout Tanakh and is extensively discussed in rabbinic literature. The psalmist's fear that divine silence equals divine absence is met by the psalm's own resolution: God does respond, and the psalmist erupts in praise (28:6-7).

The concluding petition — "Save your people and bless your inheritance; be their shepherd and carry them forever" (28:9) — is a prayer for all Israel, not merely for the individual. Jewish prayer consistently moves from personal petition to communal concern. This verse is incorporated into the daily liturgy and reflects the rabbinic principle that one who prays for the needs of the community is answered first (Bava Kamma 92a).
