## Trusting God's Name in Betrayal

Psalm 54's opening plea — "O God, save me by your name" (v. 1) — invokes the power of the divine Name, which holds immense theological significance in Judaism. The *Shem HaMeforash* (the explicit Name of God) was pronounced only by the High Priest on Yom Kippur; in daily life, Jews use substitutes (*Adonai*, *HaShem*) out of reverence. Rashi comments that David appeals to God's Name because the Name represents the totality of God's covenantal commitment to Israel — it is not merely a word but a relationship.

The superscription connects this psalm to the Ziphites' betrayal of David to Saul (1 Samuel 23:19). The Midrash Tehillim notes that the Ziphites were fellow Judahites — David's own kinsmen — making their betrayal especially painful. The rabbis draw a moral lesson: betrayal by one's own community is more devastating than attack by outsiders, because it violates the bonds of *ahavat Yisrael* (love of fellow Jews) that are fundamental to communal life.

The psalm's movement from plea (vv. 1-3) to confidence (v. 4, "God is my helper") to thanksgiving (vv. 6-7) models the pattern of Jewish prayer. The Amidah (standing prayer) follows a similar structure: praise, petition, and thanksgiving. The Radak observes that verse 7 — "he has delivered me from every trouble" — uses the past tense even though David is still in danger, demonstrating the depth of *bitachon* (trust) that anticipates God's salvation as already accomplished. This anticipatory confidence is a hallmark of mature Jewish faith.
