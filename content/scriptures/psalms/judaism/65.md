## God of Atonement and Harvest

Psalm 65 opens with the declaration that God is the one "who atones for our transgressions" (v. 3) — a statement that connects directly to the Jewish liturgical cycle. The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 17b) teaches that God "passes over transgression" for those who themselves pass over offenses committed against them, establishing a reciprocal relationship between divine and human forgiveness. This psalm's conjunction of atonement (v. 3) with agricultural blessing (vv. 9-13) reflects the Jewish calendar's integration of *teshuvah* (repentance) in Tishrei with the autumn harvest, recognizing that spiritual and material renewal are interconnected.

The psalm's creation theology — God "stills the roaring of the seas" and makes "the gateways of the morning and evening shout for joy" (vv. 7-8) — resonates with the Jewish morning prayers (*Shacharit*), which include blessings praising God as Creator of light and darkness, who "renews the work of creation every day." The Radak comments that the psalm moves from temple (v. 1) to cosmos (vv. 5-8) to fields (vv. 9-13), demonstrating that the same God who forgives sin also governs nature and provides sustenance — a unified theological vision.

The closing agricultural imagery — "the valleys deck themselves with grain, they shout and sing together for joy" (v. 13) — has been associated with the *Bikkurim* (first fruits) offerings described in Deuteronomy 26. The Jewish blessing after meals (*Birkat HaMazon*) thanks God for the land and its produce, and this psalm provides scriptural foundation for that practice. The personification of nature rejoicing reflects the Kabbalistic teaching that all creation possesses a spiritual dimension that praises its Creator, a theme developed extensively in the Perek Shirah (Chapter of Song), where every element of creation sings its own praise to God.
