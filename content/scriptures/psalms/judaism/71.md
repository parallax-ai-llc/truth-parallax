## The Elder's Prayer Through Life's Final Season

Psalm 71 has been particularly treasured by elderly Jews who find in its words a script for the fears and hopes of advanced age. The plea "Do not cast me off in the time of old age; forsake me not when my strength is spent" (v. 9) addresses the universal anxiety of declining capacity with unflinching honesty. The Talmud (Kiddushin 33a) mandates respect for the elderly — "You shall stand before the gray-headed and honor the face of an old man" (Leviticus 19:32) — and this psalm provides the spiritual counterpart to that halakhic requirement.

The psalmist's testimony spans an entire lifetime: "Upon you I have leaned from before my birth" (v. 6) and "from my youth you have taught me" (v. 17). Rashi reads this as David's retrospective over decades of divine guidance — from his youth as a shepherd through his anointing, his flight from Saul, and his troubled reign. The commitment to "proclaim your might to another generation" (v. 18) expresses the fundamental Jewish obligation of *mesorah* (transmission of tradition). The Passover Seder, with its emphasis on telling the story to children, embodies this psalm's intergenerational vision.

The psalm's remarkable declaration — "I will hope continually and will praise you yet more and more" (v. 14) — suggests that aging, rather than diminishing the capacity for praise, deepens it. The *mussar* tradition teaches that *tikvah* (hope) is not naive optimism but a disciplined spiritual practice — the decision to orient oneself toward God's future promises despite present difficulties. The Chofetz Chaim, who lived to nearly ninety-five, embodied this psalm's vision of an elder whose accumulated experience becomes the raw material for ever-increasing praise and the instruction of future generations.
