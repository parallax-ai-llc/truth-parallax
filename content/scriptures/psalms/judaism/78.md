## A Maskil of Asaph: Teaching Through History

Psalm 78 is classified as a *maskil* (instructive psalm) of Asaph, one of the Levitical musicians. In Jewish tradition, this psalm serves as a paradigmatic example of *aggadah* — narrative theology that teaches through storytelling. The Midrash Tehillim draws extensively on this psalm's historical recitation.

## Key Themes

### The Obligation to Teach (78:1-8)

The opening verses establish a fundamental mitzvah: the transmission of Torah from parents to children. This resonates with Deuteronomy 6:7 ("You shall teach them diligently to your children") and forms the basis for the Jewish educational tradition. The Passover Seder, with its mandate to tell the story to each generation, enacts this psalm's opening commandment.

### Israel's Failures and God's Mercy (78:9-64)

The psalm's unflinching recounting of Israel's rebellions is characteristic of Jewish self-examination (*cheshbon ha-nefesh*). The rabbis taught that honest acknowledgment of past failures is essential to *teshuvah* (repentance). The psalm's pattern — rebellion, judgment, crying out, deliverance — mirrors the cycle found in the book of Judges.

### The Election of Judah and David (78:65-72)

The psalm's climax — God's choice of Judah over Ephraim, Zion over Shiloh, David as shepherd-king — is read in rabbinic tradition as affirming divine sovereignty over the course of history. The Talmud (Zevachim 118b) discusses the transition from Shiloh to Jerusalem as a permanent elevation of Zion's sanctity.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah connection**: The psalm resonates with Torah narratives and commandments
- **Rabbinic interpretation**: Multiple layers of meaning in the tradition
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes Jewish daily and festival worship
- **Ethical formation**: Practical guidance for righteous living
