## Suffering for Righteousness' Sake

Psalm 69 is one of the Psalter's most intense laments, depicting suffering that results not from sin but from devotion to God. The cry "Save me, O God! For the waters have come up to my neck" (v. 1) uses flood imagery that the Midrash connects to the overwhelming troubles that engulf the righteous in a hostile world. The psalmist suffers "for your sake" (v. 7) and because "zeal for your house has consumed me" (v. 9) — a statement the rabbis associated with those who defend God's honor at personal cost. The Talmud identifies such individuals as *kanna'im* (zealots in the positive sense), whose passion for divine truth provokes persecution.

Rashi interprets the psalmist's alienation from his own brothers (v. 8) as the cost of speaking unpopular truth. The Midrash Tehillim applies this psalm to David specifically but also to the righteous in every generation who suffer ostracism for their fidelity to Torah. The offering of "vinegar to drink" (v. 21) is read as a metaphor for the bitterness that the world offers in exchange for righteousness. The extensive imprecatory section (vv. 22-28) has prompted careful rabbinic discussion about the legitimacy of praying for the downfall of the wicked. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) records that Beruriah rebuked her husband Rabbi Meir for praying for the death of sinners, arguing that one should pray for the death of sin rather than of sinners.

The psalm's conclusion turns to hope: "For God will save Zion and build up the cities of Judah" (v. 35). This verse has sustained Jewish hope for national restoration throughout the centuries of exile. The *Amidah* prayer includes petitions for the rebuilding of Jerusalem and the restoration of the Temple that echo this psalm's language. The final verse — "the offspring of his servants shall inherit it, and those who love his name shall dwell in it" (v. 36) — expresses the covenantal promise that God's faithful people will ultimately dwell in the renewed Land of Israel.
