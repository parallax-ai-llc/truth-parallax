## The Fool Who Denies God

Psalm 53, a near-parallel of Psalm 14, presents the *naval* (fool) who denies God. Jewish commentary distinguishes this from intellectual doubt — the *naval* is not a philosopher but a moral degenerate whose denial of God serves to justify sinful behavior. Rashi connects this to Deuteronomy 32:6, where *naval* connotes ingratitude — the fool denies not merely God's existence but God's claims upon him. The Midrash Tehillim suggests that every generation produces such fools, and that the psalm speaks to an enduring human tendency rather than a single historical moment.

The rabbis noted the difference between Psalms 14 and 53: the latter uses *Elohim* exclusively where the former uses *YHVH*, and verse 5 differs significantly. The Midrash explains that the *Elohim* usage emphasizes God's attribute of justice (*middat ha-din*), while the variant in verse 5 — referring to God scattering the bones of those who encamp against the righteous — speaks of military defeat. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) records the principle that the righteous should pray for the repentance of the wicked, not their destruction, yet this psalm acknowledges that persistent denial of God leads to ruin.

The concluding cry — "Oh, that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion!" (v. 6) — is a prayer for the Messianic redemption (*ge'ulah*) that will transform the entire moral order. Jewish eschatology understands this as a longing for the time when "God restores the fortunes of his people" — the ingathering of exiles, the rebuilding of the Temple, and the universal recognition of God's sovereignty. The psalm's brevity belies its scope: in six verses it encompasses the human problem (denial of God), its diagnosis (moral corruption), and its remedy (divine salvation from Zion).
