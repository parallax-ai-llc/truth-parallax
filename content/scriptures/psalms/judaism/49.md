Psalm 49 is a wisdom meditation on mortality and wealth that addresses "both low and high, rich and poor alike" (49:2). The psalm's universal address distinguishes it from most psalms; this is wisdom for all humanity, not only for Israel. The Hebrew *chokhmah* (wisdom) and *tevunah* (understanding) in verse 3 identify this as a philosophical meditation in the tradition of Proverbs and Ecclesiastes.

The central teaching — "no one can redeem the life of another or give to God a ransom for them" (49:7) — confronts the illusion that wealth can purchase immortality. The Midrash (Kohelet Rabbah 5:11) observes that a person enters the world with clenched fists (grasping) and leaves with open hands (empty). Jewish ethical teaching on *tzedakah* finds support here: since wealth cannot be taken beyond the grave, it should be used for righteous purposes during one's lifetime.

Verse 15 — "But God will redeem my soul from the power of Sheol; he will surely take me" — is one of the more debated verses in Jewish theology. While Judaism does affirm *olam ha-ba* (the world to come) and *techiyat ha-metim* (resurrection of the dead), the precise nature of afterlife remains less defined than in Christianity or Islam. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 90a-b) establishes resurrection as a fundamental Jewish belief, and this verse is among the biblical texts cited in its support.
