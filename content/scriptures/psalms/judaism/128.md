## Overview

Psalm 128 — *Blessings of Fearing the Lord* — comprises 6 verses. In the Jewish tradition, the book of *Tehillim* (Psalms) holds a unique place as both Scripture and liturgy, prayer book and poetry. Jewish interpretation of Psalm 128 draws on the Talmud, Midrash Tehillim, and the classical medieval commentators.

Psalm 128 is one of the *Shir HaMa'alot* (Songs of Ascent, Psalms 120-134). The Talmud (Sukkah 51b) describes how the Levites stood on fifteen steps between the Court of Israel and the Court of Women, corresponding to these fifteen psalms, and sang them during Sukkot. In Jewish liturgy, Psalm 126 (*Shir HaMa'alot*) is traditionally recited before *Birkat HaMazon* (Grace After Meals) on Shabbat and festivals.

## Classical Commentators

**Rashi** (1040-1105) provides concise *peshat* (plain sense) commentary on Psalm 128, often drawing on Midrash Tehillim and the Talmud. His interpretations anchor the psalm in its historical context while revealing layers of meaning relevant to Jewish life.

**Ibn Ezra** (1089-1167) brings his characteristic grammatical precision and literary sensitivity, examining the Hebrew word choices and poetic structures of Psalm 128.

**Radak** (David Kimchi, 1160-1235) offers a systematic commentary that balances philological analysis with theological reflection, often synthesizing earlier interpretive traditions.

## Midrash Tehillim

*Midrash Tehillim* (Midrash on Psalms) provides aggadic interpretations of Psalm 128, linking its themes to broader narratives in Torah and to eschatological hopes. The midrash often reads the psalms as David's personal spiritual diary while simultaneously seeing them as the prayers of all Israel.

## Liturgical Context

In the *siddur* (prayer book), psalms permeate Jewish daily, Shabbat, and festival worship. The *Tehillim* are recited for healing, mourning, celebration, and personal devotion. Jewish communities worldwide maintain the practice of reciting the entire Psalter in cycles, particularly in times of communal need.

## Theological Significance

Psalm 128 reflects core themes of Jewish theology: God's sovereignty, the covenant relationship between God and Israel, the centrality of Torah, the longing for redemption, and the practice of praise as spiritual discipline.
