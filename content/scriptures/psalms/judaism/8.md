Psalm 8 is a hymn to God's majesty as revealed through creation and through the surprising dignity granted to humankind. The Midrash Tehillim relates that David composed this psalm while gazing at the night sky and contemplating the vast disparity between the infinite heavens and finite humanity. The question "What is man (*enosh*) that you are mindful of him?" (8:4) uses the word *enosh*, which carries connotations of frailty and mortality, heightening the paradox that God should attend to such fragile creatures.

The declaration that God has made humanity "a little lower than *elohim*" (8:5) has generated extensive rabbinic discussion. While *elohim* can mean "God" or "angels," the Targum and many rabbinic authorities translate it as "angels," maintaining a firm distinction between Creator and creature. Yet the Midrash also interprets the verse as affirming humanity's extraordinary status — created in the divine image (*tzelem Elohim*), the human being stands at the pinnacle of creation, charged with dominion over the works of God's hands (8:6). This dominion is understood in Jewish ethics as a responsibility, not a license — the concept of *bal tashchit* (do not destroy) limits human authority over nature.

The psalm's frame — opening and closing with "O Lord, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth!" (8:1, 9) — places human dignity within the context of divine sovereignty. Rabbi Joseph B. Soloveitchik in *The Lonely Man of Faith* explored the tension between humanity's creative mandate (Genesis 1) and its existential vulnerability (Genesis 2-3), a tension that Psalm 8 captures perfectly. Jewish thought holds both truths simultaneously: humanity is crowned with glory *and* is dust and ashes, and authentic religious life requires awareness of both.
