## National Crisis and Covenantal Hope

Psalm 60 arises from military defeat and national humiliation, and Jewish tradition has applied it to the recurring experience of exile and catastrophe that has marked Jewish history. The opening lament — "O God, you have rejected us, broken our defenses" (v. 1) — has been prayed by Jewish communities after the destruction of both Temples, during the Crusades, and in the wake of pogroms and the Shoah. The Midrash Tehillim reads the "wine to drink that made us stagger" (v. 3) as a reference to the disorienting suffering that challenges faith but does not destroy it.

The divine oracle (vv. 6-8) claims sovereignty over the entire Land of Israel and its neighbors. Rashi interprets this passage as God affirming His ownership of the land and His authority to distribute it according to His will — a key concept in Jewish theology of *Eretz Yisrael*. The claim that "Gilead is mine, and Manasseh is mine; Ephraim is my helmet, Judah is my scepter" expresses the indivisible connection between HaShem and the land He promised to Abraham's descendants. This theological claim has sustained Jewish hope for return through nearly two millennia of exile.

The concluding confession — "With God we shall do valiantly, for it is he who will tread down our foes" (v. 12) — combines human agency with divine power in a way characteristic of Jewish theology. The Talmud teaches *hishtadlut* — human effort and initiative — alongside *bitachon* — trust in divine outcome. This psalm models their integration: Israel is not passive (they act "valiantly") but recognizes that ultimate victory belongs to God. The psalm has been recited in Jewish communities facing collective danger as an expression of faith that national calamity is temporary but God's covenant is permanent.
