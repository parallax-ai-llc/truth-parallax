Psalm 16 is a *miktam* of David, a term the Talmud (Sotah 10b) connects to the word *makhtam* (a jewel or branding), suggesting the psalm is a precious or indelible composition. The declaration "I say to the Lord, 'You are my Lord; I have no good apart from you'" (16:2) expresses the concept of *devekut* (cleaving to God) that the Chasidic movement made central to Jewish spirituality. The Baal Shem Tov taught that true *devekut* means recognizing God as the source of all good and finding one's identity in relationship with the Divine.

The concept of God as one's "portion" and "cup" (16:5) has deep resonance in Jewish life. The Levites, who received no territorial inheritance, had God as their portion (Numbers 18:20), and this psalm extends that spiritual inheritance to every person who trusts in the Lord. The Mishnah (Avot 6:4) describes the way of Torah as "bread with salt you shall eat, water by measure you shall drink" — material simplicity compensated by the spiritual richness of God's companionship. The "pleasant places" and "beautiful inheritance" of verse 6 are understood as the spiritual blessings of Torah, community, and divine relationship.

Verse 10 — "For you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" — is interpreted in Jewish tradition with varied emphases. The *peshat* (plain sense) refers to God preserving David from premature death. Some rabbinic commentators, including the Radak, see hints of the resurrection of the dead (*techiyat ha-metim*), a doctrine affirmed as a fundamental principle of faith by Maimonides (Thirteen Principles, #13). The verse's promise of "the path of life" and "fullness of joy" in God's presence (16:11) anticipates the rabbinic teaching about the *olam ha-ba*, where the righteous enjoy the radiance of the *Shekhinah*.
