## The Pain of a Broken Friendship

Psalm 55 is traditionally associated with David's anguish over the betrayal of Ahithophel, his trusted counselor who sided with Absalom's rebellion (2 Samuel 15-17). The Talmud (Sanhedrin 106b) identifies the "companion" and "familiar friend" of verses 13-14 with Ahithophel, noting that they had studied Torah together. The rabbis found this especially instructive: even shared Torah study does not guarantee loyalty, and the violation of trust between study partners is among the most painful human experiences.

The psalm's emotional range — fear, trembling, horror, the wish to flee on dove's wings (vv. 4-8) — is valued in Jewish tradition for its psychological honesty. The Midrash Tehillim notes that David did not suppress his emotions but brought them raw before God. This models the Jewish understanding of prayer (*tefillah*) as genuine dialogue with the divine — not a performance of piety but an outpouring of the actual heart. Rashi comments on the three-fold prayer pattern (v. 17, "Evening and morning and at noon I utter my complaint") that David established a rhythm of prayer that became the basis for the three daily services (*Shacharit*, *Minchah*, *Ma'ariv*).

Verse 22 — "Cast your burden on the LORD, and he will sustain you" — has become a key text in Jewish *bitachon* (trust) literature. The *Chovot HaLevavot* (Duties of the Heart) by Bahya ibn Paquda draws on this verse in its extended treatment of trust in God (*sha'ar ha-bitachon*), teaching that true trust involves not the absence of effort but the redirection of anxiety from outcomes to relationship with God. The Hebrew word for "burden" (*yehab*) occurs only here in the Bible, leading the rabbis to derive additional meaning from its rare usage — every unique burden finds its answer in God's unique care.
