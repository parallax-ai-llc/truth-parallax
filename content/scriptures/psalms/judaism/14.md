Psalm 14 opens with the declaration "The fool (*naval*) says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (14:1). In Jewish wisdom tradition, the *naval* is not merely unintelligent but morally corrupt — the same term used of Nabal in 1 Samuel 25:25, whose name matched his character. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 99b-100a) discusses the various forms of denial of God, distinguishing between theoretical atheism (rare in the ancient world) and practical denial — living as though God does not observe human conduct. The Rambam (Maimonides) in his *Mishneh Torah* lists denial of divine providence as one of the categories of heresy.

The universal indictment — "They have all turned aside; together they have become corrupt; there is none who does good, not even one" (14:3) — is understood in Jewish tradition as a description of particular generations (such as the generation of the Flood or the generation of the Tower of Babel) rather than an absolute statement about all humanity at all times. Jewish anthropology affirms both the *yetzer ha-ra* (evil inclination) and the *yetzer ha-tov* (good inclination), and while sin is universal, the capacity for righteousness is never entirely extinguished. The righteous in every generation — the *tzaddikim* — demonstrate that human goodness, aided by divine grace, remains possible.

The messianic yearning of verse 7 — "Oh, that salvation would come out of Zion! When the Lord restores the fortunes of his people" — is recited in the Jewish liturgy with intense longing. The *Amidah* prayer includes petitions for the restoration of Jerusalem, the rebuilding of the Temple, and the coming of the Messiah — all themes anticipated in this verse. Jewish eschatology envisions not merely individual salvation but the redemption of all Israel and indeed all humanity, a cosmic restoration that will fulfill the psalm's yearning for God to act from Zion.
