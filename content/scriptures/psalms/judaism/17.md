Psalm 17, titled "A Prayer of David," is one of five psalms explicitly designated as *tefillah* (prayer) rather than *mizmor* (song). Rashi and Ibn Ezra associate this psalm with David's persecution by Saul, and the assertion of innocence in verses 3-5 — "you have tested me, and you will find nothing" — is understood as David's claim that he is guiltless of the specific charges his enemies bring against him, not as a claim of absolute sinlessness. Jewish tradition values the ability to stand before God with a clear conscience on a particular matter while acknowledging one's general imperfection.

The petition "Keep me as the apple of your eye" (17:8) uses the rare Hebrew phrase *ishon bat-ayin* (literally "the little man of the daughter of the eye"), referring to the pupil, which reflects the face of the one gazing. The Midrash draws from this a profound teaching: when God looks at the righteous, He sees His own image reflected in them, just as a person sees their reflection in another's pupil. This mutuality of gaze — God seeing Himself in the righteous, the righteous seeing God — is at the heart of the covenant relationship.

The final verse — "As for me, I shall behold your face in righteousness; when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness" (17:15) — is interpreted by the Metzudat David as referring to the morning, when David rises from sleep and is satisfied by God's renewed presence. The Zohar, however, reads it eschatologically: the "awakening" is the resurrection, and the "satisfaction with your likeness" (*temunah*) is the revelation of the divine glory that awaits the righteous in the World to Come. The word *temunah* (form, likeness) is rare and significant — it is the same word used in Numbers 12:8, where Moses is said to behold God's *temunah*, suggesting that the psalmist aspires to a Mosaic level of divine encounter.
