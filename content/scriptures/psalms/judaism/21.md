Psalm 21, the thanksgiving companion to Psalm 20's petition, celebrates the blessings God has bestowed upon the king. The Midrash Tehillim reads this psalm as referring to both David and the future Messiah, noting that the blessings described — "length of days forever and ever" (21:4), "splendor and majesty" (21:5) — transcend what any mortal king experienced. Jewish messianic interpretation sees in this psalm a description of the Messiah's reign of peace and justice that will inaugurate the final redemption.

The verse "You have given him his heart's desire" (21:2) is connected by the Talmud (Megillah 15b) to the principle that God fulfills the desires of the righteous. However, Jewish theology qualifies this: the righteous desire only what God desires, so the "fulfillment" is not a blank check but a convergence of wills. The Chasidic masters taught that the highest spiritual attainment is *bitul ha-ratzon* (nullification of one's own will), whereby one's desires become aligned with God's. The king's "heart's desire" is thus understood as a holy desire — for justice, peace, and the flourishing of God's people.

The psalm's vision of the king's enemies defeated by divine fire (21:9-12) has been applied in Jewish eschatology to the ultimate defeat of the forces that oppose God's purposes. The Targum interprets these verses as describing the downfall of the nations that persecute Israel in the end times. However, mainstream Jewish theology emphasizes that the messianic victory is fundamentally about the establishment of justice and the knowledge of God, not merely the punishment of enemies. The psalm's closing doxology — "Be exalted, O Lord, in your strength! We will sing and praise your power" (21:13) — voices the communal hope that animated Jewish worship throughout the centuries of exile.
