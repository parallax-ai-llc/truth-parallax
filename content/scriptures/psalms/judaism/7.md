Psalm 7 is titled a *shiggaion* of David, a rare term that the Talmud (Moed Katan 16b) relates to *shegagah* (inadvertent error), suggesting the psalm concerns David's unwitting involvement in some conflict. The historical reference to "Cush, a Benjaminite" has been debated extensively: the Talmud (Moed Katan 16b) identifies Cush as a reference to Saul, who was from the tribe of Benjamin, with "Cush" (meaning "Cushite/Ethiopian") being an ironic epithet for Saul's handsome appearance, just as a beautiful person might paradoxically be called by an unflattering name.

David's self-imprecation (7:3-5) — inviting divine punishment if he has committed the alleged wrongs — is an example of the biblical oath of innocence, discussed in the Talmud's treatment of oaths (*Shevuot*). Jewish law takes oaths with extreme seriousness; the rabbis taught that false oaths shake the very foundations of the world. David's willingness to stake his life on his innocence in this specific matter is understood as the mark of genuine integrity, not as a claim to general sinlessness.

The principle of self-recoiling evil (7:14-16) — "he who digs a pit will fall into it" — became a major theme in Jewish wisdom literature and ethical teaching. The Midrash applies this principle to Haman, who was hanged on the very gallows he prepared for Mordecai (Esther 7:10). Rabbinic theology sees this not merely as poetic justice but as a fundamental feature of the moral order: *midah k'neged midah* (measure for measure) is one of the principles by which God governs the world. The wicked are ultimately undone by the very instruments they fashion for others.
