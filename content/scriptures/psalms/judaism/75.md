## God's Appointed Time of Judgment

Psalm 75 opens with thanksgiving and immediately introduces a divine oracle: "At the set time that I appoint I will judge with equity" (v. 2). This concept of *zman* (appointed time) is deeply embedded in Jewish theology and liturgy. The festivals are called *mo'adim* (appointed times), and the Jewish calendar structures all of life around divinely ordained seasons. The psalm's assertion that God judges at His own appointed time, not according to human schedules, provides theological grounding for the Jewish virtue of patience (*savlanut*) and trust in divine providence.

The psalm's warning against pride — "Do not lift up your horn on high, or speak with haughty neck" (v. 5) — echoes Proverbs' consistent warnings against *ga'avah* (pride), which the rabbis identified as among the most destructive vices. The Talmud (Sotah 5a) declares that God says of the arrogant, "He and I cannot dwell together." Verse 6's assertion that exaltation comes from God alone — "not from the east or from the west" — is interpreted by the Midrash as teaching that human advancement is ultimately determined by divine decree, not by human ambition or geographic advantage.

The cup of foaming wine (v. 8), drained to the dregs by the wicked, is a powerful prophetic image that appears also in Isaiah 51:17 and Jeremiah 25:15. The Midrash Tehillim distinguishes several cups: the cup of consolation given to mourners, the cup of salvation lifted at the Seder, and the cup of judgment described here. Jewish eschatology connects these images: at the redemption, the righteous will drink the cup of salvation (Psalm 116:13) while the wicked drain the cup of judgment. The psalm's concluding promise — "the horns of the righteous shall be exalted" (v. 10) — expresses the Jewish conviction that humility before God leads to ultimate exaltation, while self-exaltation leads to humiliation.
