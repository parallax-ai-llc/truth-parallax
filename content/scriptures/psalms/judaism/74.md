## Mourning the Destruction of the Temple

Psalm 74 is traditionally associated with the destruction of the First Temple by Nebuchadnezzar in 586 BCE, and Jewish tradition has applied it equally to the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE. This psalm is recited on Tisha B'Av, the annual fast day commemorating both destructions, and its anguished questions — "O God, why do you cast us off forever?" (v. 1) — give liturgical voice to the deepest trauma in Jewish historical memory. The *Churban* (Destruction) is not merely a historical event in Jewish consciousness but an ongoing spiritual condition that awaits eschatological repair.

The description of enemies hacking at the Temple's woodwork (vv. 5-6) and setting fire to "the dwelling place of your name" (v. 7) is read with excruciating specificity by Jewish commentators. Rashi identifies each detail with particular acts of desecration recorded in rabbinic literature. The cry "We do not see our signs; there is no longer any prophet" (v. 9) expresses the post-prophetic condition that has characterized Judaism since the Second Temple period — the absence of direct prophetic communication, compensated for by the interpretive tradition of the rabbis.

The psalm's appeal to creation (vv. 13-17) and covenant (v. 20) constitutes a powerful theological argument: the God who conquered chaos and established the natural order surely has the power and the obligation to restore His desecrated sanctuary. The Midrash reads these verses as Israel reminding God, as it were, of His own commitments. The rabbinic concept of *chutzpah k'lapei shamaya* (boldness toward heaven) is exemplified in this psalm — the faithful protest to God from within the covenant, not against it. The concluding plea, "Arise, O God, defend your cause!" (v. 22), is recited on Tisha B'Av afternoon when, according to tradition, the mourning begins to ease and hope for restoration emerges.
