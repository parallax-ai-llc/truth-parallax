Psalm 6 is recognized in Jewish tradition as one of the seven psalms of penitence and is recited in certain communities during the *Tachanun* (supplication) prayers following the *Amidah*. The opening plea — "O Lord, rebuke me not in your anger" (6:1) — does not deny that divine correction is deserved but asks that it be tempered with mercy. Rashi explains that David accepts God's right to discipline but asks not to be disciplined while God's anger is at its peak, recognizing that correction given in love is bearable while correction given in wrath is not.

The psalm's emotional intensity — "my eye wastes away because of grief" (6:7) — is valued in Jewish tradition rather than suppressed. The Talmud (Bava Metzia 59a) teaches that "the gates of tears are never locked," meaning that heartfelt weeping always reaches God. The Zohar interprets tears as having the power to break through all heavenly barriers, and the Kotzker Rebbe taught that "there is nothing so whole as a broken heart." Jewish prayer encourages authentic emotion rather than stoic composure.

The turning point in verse 8 — "The Lord has heard the sound of my weeping" — is cited in the Talmud (Berakhot 32b) in a discussion about the efficacy of prayer. The rabbis debate whether prayer changes God's decree or changes the person praying; either way, the psalm affirms that prayer is genuinely heard. The shift from lament to confidence within the psalm mirrors the structure of the *Amidah* itself, which moves from praise through petition to thanksgiving, enacting within the prayer the very transformation the psalmist experiences.
