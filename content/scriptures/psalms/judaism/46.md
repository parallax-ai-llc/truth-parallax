Psalm 46 — "God is our refuge and strength, an ever-present help in trouble" (46:1) — has sustained Jewish communities through centuries of persecution and upheaval. The psalm envisions cosmic chaos — "though the earth give way and the mountains fall into the heart of the sea" (46:2) — and declares that God's presence is sufficient even then. Jewish theology calls this *emunah peshutah* — simple, unshakeable faith that persists regardless of external circumstances.

The "river whose streams make glad the city of God" (46:4) is traditionally identified with the waters of Shiloach that flowed through Jerusalem (Isaiah 8:6). The Talmud (Sukkah 48b) connects this to the water-drawing ceremony (*Simchat Beit HaShoevah*) during Sukkot, one of the most joyous occasions in Temple worship. The "streams" that gladden God's city are understood as Torah, which flows from Zion and brings life to the world.

"Be still, and know that I am God" (46:10) — *harpu ud'u ki anochi Elohim* — commands a cessation of human striving that resonates with the Shabbat commandment. The root *r-p-h* means "to let go, to release." Jewish Shabbat theology teaches that ceasing from creative work one day in seven is an act of profound trust — an acknowledgment that the world belongs to God and does not depend on our constant effort to sustain it. Psalm 46 extends this Shabbat principle to every dimension of life.
