Psalm 32 is one of the great penitential psalms, and its opening words — "Blessed is the one whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered" (32:1) — articulate the theology of *teshuvah* (repentance and return) that is central to Judaism. The Talmud (Yoma 86b) teaches that genuine repentance transforms deliberate sins into merits, turning the penitent's very failures into sources of spiritual growth.

David's account of the torment of concealed sin — "When I kept silent, my bones wasted away" (32:3) — illustrates the rabbinic teaching that sin creates a barrier between the soul and God. The Zohar explains that unconfessed sin constricts the flow of divine blessing, causing spiritual and even physical suffering. Confession (*vidui*), which Judaism practices especially on Yom Kippur, breaks through this barrier and restores the soul's connection to HaShem.

The verse "I acknowledged my sin to you and did not cover up my iniquity... and you forgave the guilt of my sin" (32:5) demonstrates that in Jewish theology, forgiveness comes through sincere confession and change, not through external rituals alone. The Rambam (Maimonides) codified the three essential components of *teshuvah*: recognition of sin, verbal confession, and genuine resolve not to repeat the offense. This psalm models all three.
