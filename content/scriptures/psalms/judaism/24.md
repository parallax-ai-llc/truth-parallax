Psalm 24 is the psalm designated for Yom Rishon (Sunday) in the daily psalm cycle of the Levitical Temple service, according to the Mishnah (Tamid 7:4). It was chosen for Sunday because the first day commemorates creation — "The earth is the Lord's and the fullness thereof, the world and those who dwell therein, for he has founded it upon the seas" (24:1-2). This cosmic declaration of divine ownership is the foundation of Jewish environmental ethics and economic teaching: since everything belongs to God, human use of the world's resources requires blessing and gratitude. The *berakhah* (blessing) recited before eating or enjoying any part of creation acknowledges this divine ownership.

The entrance liturgy (24:3-6) — "Who shall ascend the hill of the Lord?" — was connected by the rabbis to the qualifications for entering the Temple. The requirement of "clean hands and a pure heart" was elaborated in the laws of ritual purity (*taharah*) that governed Temple access. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) counts the qualities listed here among David's distillation of the commandments. After the Temple's destruction, the psalm's entrance requirements were applied metaphorically to the conditions for effective prayer — the synagogue being the *mikdash me'at* (small sanctuary) that replaced the Temple.

The antiphonal dialogue "Lift up your heads, O gates!" (24:7-10) was associated by the Talmud (Shabbat 30a) with Solomon's dedication of the First Temple, when the gates refused to open until Solomon invoked the merit of his father David. The Midrash also connects this passage to the Ark of the Covenant's entrance into the Temple. The repeated question "Who is this King of glory?" and its answer — "The Lord of hosts, he is the King of glory" (24:10) — is chanted with particular fervor when the Torah scroll is returned to the Holy Ark during synagogue services, reenacting the psalm's liturgical drama.
