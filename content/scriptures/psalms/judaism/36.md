Psalm 36 opens with a penetrating description of the wicked person: "Transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart; there is no fear of God before his eyes" (36:1). The Hebrew *n'um pesha* — "an oracle of transgression" — suggests that sin itself speaks within the human heart like a false prophet. The *yetzer hara* (evil inclination) whispers its counsel, and without the fear of God (*yirat Shamayim*), the person follows it uncritically.

The psalm's celebration of divine attributes — "Your steadfast love (*chesed*), O LORD, extends to the heavens" (36:5) — is central to Jewish theology of God's character. The thirteen attributes of mercy (*middot harachamim*) revealed to Moses at Sinai (Exodus 34:6-7) find poetic expression here. Jewish liturgy, especially during the High Holy Days, appeals to these attributes as the ground of hope for forgiveness.

Verse 9 — "For with you is the fountain of life; in your light do we see light" — has inspired extensive Jewish philosophical and mystical reflection. Maimonides understood this as affirming that God is the source of all existence and knowledge. The Kabbalistic tradition reads "light" as referring to the *Ohr Ein Sof* — the infinite light of God that pervades creation while remaining transcendent. Torah itself is called "light" (Proverbs 6:23), and it is through Torah that Israel sees the ultimate light.
