## Overview

Psalm 111 — *Praise for God's Works* — comprises 10 verses. In the Jewish tradition, the book of *Tehillim* (Psalms) holds a unique place as both Scripture and liturgy, prayer book and poetry. Jewish interpretation of Psalm 111 draws on the Talmud, Midrash Tehillim, and the classical medieval commentators.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

### Classical Commentators

**Rashi** (1040-1105) provides concise *peshat* (plain sense) commentary on Psalm 111, often drawing on Midrash Tehillim and the Talmud. His interpretations anchor the psalm in its historical context while revealing layers of meaning relevant to Jewish life.

**Ibn Ezra** (1089-1167) brings his characteristic grammatical precision and literary sensitivity, examining the Hebrew word choices and poetic structures of Psalm 111.

**Radak** (David Kimchi, 1160-1235) offers a systematic commentary that balances philological analysis with theological reflection, often synthesizing earlier interpretive traditions.

### Midrash Tehillim

*Midrash Tehillim* (Midrash on Psalms) provides aggadic interpretations of Psalm 111, linking its themes to broader narratives in Torah and to eschatological hopes. The midrash often reads the psalms as David's personal spiritual diary while simultaneously seeing them as the prayers of all Israel.

## Liturgical Context

In the *siddur* (prayer book), psalms permeate Jewish daily, Shabbat, and festival worship. The *Tehillim* are recited for healing, mourning, celebration, and personal devotion. Jewish communities worldwide maintain the practice of reciting the entire Psalter in cycles, particularly in times of communal need.

## Theological Significance

Psalm 111 reflects core themes of Jewish theology: God's sovereignty, the covenant relationship between God and Israel, the centrality of Torah, the longing for redemption, and the practice of praise as spiritual discipline.
