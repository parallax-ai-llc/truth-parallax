## God Exposes Hidden Conspiracies

Psalm 64 confronts the danger of hidden malice — enemies who "whet their tongues like swords" and "aim bitter words like arrows, shooting from ambush at the blameless" (vv. 3-4). Jewish ethical literature treats the weaponization of speech as among the gravest sins. The Chofetz Chaim, in his compendium on the laws of *lashon hara*, cites the principle embodied in this psalm: that God holds secret slanderers accountable even when no human court can reach them. The ambush imagery — attacking "suddenly and without fear" (v. 4) — depicts the coward who harms others from behind the protection of anonymity or social standing.

The psalm's central principle of reversal — "God shoots his arrow at them" and "their own tongues are turned against them" (vv. 7-8) — reflects a recurring pattern in Jewish history and theology. The Midrash identifies this principle with the *midah k'neged midah* (measure for measure) justice that God employs. Just as Haman was hanged on the gallows he built for Mordechai, the conspiring tongue falls upon its own speaker. The Radak comments that this reversal is not arbitrary but deeply just — the punishment arises organically from the nature of the sin itself.

The psalm's conclusion invites the righteous to "rejoice in the LORD and take refuge in him" (v. 10). Rashi emphasizes that the rejoicing is not in the enemies' suffering per se but in the vindication of divine justice. Jewish tradition is careful to distinguish between *simchah* (joy) in God's righteousness and *schadenfreude* (joy in another's misfortune) — the latter is prohibited by Proverbs 24:17. The joy celebrated here is ultimately theological: it is the confirmation that the moral order is real and that the God of Israel is sovereign over it.
