Psalm 27 holds a unique place in Jewish liturgical life: it is recited daily from the first of Elul through Shemini Atzeret — the season of repentance and return that encompasses Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. The psalm's opening declaration, "The LORD is my light and my salvation" (27:1), is traditionally associated with Rosh Hashanah ("my light") and Yom Kippur ("my salvation"), while "whom shall I fear" refers to the Days of Awe that fall between them.

The central verse — "One thing I ask from the LORD, this only do I seek: that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life, to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD and to seek him in his Temple" (27:4) — expresses the longing for God's presence that intensifies during the *Yamim Noraim* (Days of Awe). The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 21:4) teaches that David's "one thing" refers to Torah study, which is the primary means by which Israel dwells in God's presence even after the Temple's destruction.

The closing verse, "Wait for the LORD; be strong and let your heart take courage; wait for the LORD" (27:14), is repeated for emphasis — a pattern the rabbis interpret as encouraging persistence in prayer. The Talmud (Berakhot 32b) teaches that a person's prayer requires strengthening, and the doubled exhortation to "wait" teaches that one should pray and pray again, never despairing of HaShem's response.
