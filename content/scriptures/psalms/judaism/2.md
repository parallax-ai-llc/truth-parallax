Psalm 2 is understood in Jewish tradition primarily as referring to King David and the Davidic dynasty, with messianic implications developed in rabbinic and medieval literature. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) pairs it with Psalm 1 as a single unit: Psalm 1 begins with *ashrei* (blessed) and Psalm 2 ends with *ashrei*, forming an inclusio. The raging of the nations (2:1) is read by Rashi as referring to the wars David fought against surrounding kingdoms, while Radak (Rabbi David Kimchi) extends it to the future messianic era when the nations will resist God's appointed king.

The divine decree "You are my son; today I have begotten you" (2:7) is interpreted in Jewish tradition not as ontological divine sonship but as the installation of the king in a special covenantal relationship with God. The Targum translates "son" as "beloved as a son," maintaining strict monotheism. Rashi reads the "begetting" as the anointing that establishes the king's authority. The Midrash Tehillim applies this verse to the Messiah, envisioning a future king who will receive the nations as his inheritance (2:8), but this inheritance is understood as messianic sovereignty within the framework of God's universal kingship, not as divine identity.

The closing exhortation to "serve the Lord with fear, and rejoice with trembling" (2:11) has been foundational in Jewish liturgical theology. The Talmud (Berakhot 30b) cites this verse to establish that prayer must be approached with both joy and awe — neither somber dread nor casual levity. This balance of *yirah* (awe/fear) and *simchah* (joy) characterizes the ideal mode of Jewish worship, reflected in the liturgical structure itself, which moves between solemn petition and exuberant praise. The psalm's final blessing upon those who "take refuge in Him" (2:12) uses the term *chosei vo*, a designation for the righteous who trust in God.
