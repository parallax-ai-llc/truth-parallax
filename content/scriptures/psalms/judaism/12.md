Psalm 12 contrasts the deceitful speech of the wicked with the pure words of God, a theme of great importance in Jewish ethics. The lament "the faithful have vanished from among the children of man" (12:1) echoes the concern of the prophet Micah (7:2) and has been applied in rabbinic literature to generations that feel spiritually diminished. The Talmud (Sotah 49a) lists the signs of the "footsteps of the Messiah" era, including the decline of truth and faithfulness — yet Jewish tradition insists that even a few righteous individuals can sustain the world.

The centerpiece — "The words of the Lord are pure words, like silver refined in a furnace on the ground, purified seven times" (12:6) — is a key prooftext for the Jewish doctrine of the perfection of Torah. The Midrash interprets the sevenfold purification as corresponding to the seven days of creation, suggesting that God's words are as perfect and intentional as the created order itself. The Talmud (Eruvin 21b) draws on this verse to teach that every letter, crown, and ornamentation in the Torah scroll carries meaning — nothing is superfluous, nothing accidental.

The problem of deceptive speech addressed in verses 2-4 — "flattering lips" and "a double heart" (*lev va-lev*, literally "a heart and a heart") — is treated extensively in the Talmudic tractate *Arakhin* (15b-16a), which devotes significant attention to the sin of *lashon hara* (evil speech). The Chofetz Chaim (Rabbi Israel Meir Kagan) built an entire ethical system around the laws of speech, drawing on psalms like this one. In Jewish ethics, speech is not merely communication but a creative force — God created the world through speech, and human words carry a corresponding power to build or destroy.
