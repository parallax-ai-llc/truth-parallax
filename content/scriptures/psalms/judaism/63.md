## Longing for God as the Soul's Deepest Need

Psalm 63's opening — "O God, you are my God; earnestly I seek you; my soul thirsts for you" (v. 1) — has been a central text in Jewish *devekut* (cleaving to God) theology. The Baal Shem Tov, founder of Hasidism, taught that the soul's thirst for God is its most natural and essential impulse, often buried beneath worldly distractions but never extinguished. The "wilderness of Judah" setting connects this longing to physical deprivation — when external comforts are stripped away, the soul's true desire for God becomes visible.

The declaration "your steadfast love is better than life" (v. 3) has provoked extensive rabbinic reflection. The Talmud (Berakhot 61b) records that Rabbi Akiva, who was tortured to death by the Romans while reciting the *Shema*, exemplified this verse — he affirmed God's love as more valuable than life itself. The Midrash Tehillim comments that David, who wrote this psalm in the wilderness while fleeing for his life, could speak of God's love surpassing life precisely because he was risking his life. The verse became a touchstone for Jewish martyrology, invoked by countless Jews who chose death over apostasy (*kiddush HaShem*).

The psalm's description of worship — "My lips will glorify you. Thus I will bless you as long as I live; in your name I will lift up my hands" (vv. 3-4) — describes both the formal gestures of Jewish prayer (lifting hands, blessing with lips) and the internal attitude that animates them. The nocturnal meditation of verse 6 connects to the custom of reciting psalms and Torah during the night, a practice particularly valued in Kabbalistic tradition. The Zohar teaches that the hours between midnight and dawn are especially propitious for communion with the divine, and this psalm's imagery of meditation "in the watches of the night" provides its scriptural basis.
