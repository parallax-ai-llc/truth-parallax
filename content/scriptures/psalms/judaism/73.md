## Wrestling with the Prosperity of the Wicked

Psalm 73 engages the most persistent question in Jewish theology: why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer? This question pervades the Talmud, particularly in Berakhot 7a, where Moses himself is depicted as asking it. Asaph's confession — "my feet had almost stumbled... for I was envious of the arrogant" (vv. 2-3) — is refreshingly honest, and the rabbis valued this honesty as itself a form of piety. The Midrash Tehillim comments that Asaph's willingness to admit his near-fall teaches that pretending to be undisturbed by injustice is less righteous than honestly struggling with it.

The turning point — "until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end" (v. 17) — has been interpreted in multiple ways. Rashi reads the "sanctuary" (*mikdash*) literally as the Temple, where the psalmist received prophetic insight. The Radak broadens this to any encounter with divine wisdom — through Torah study, through communion with the wise, or through prayer. The Talmud's resolution of the theodicy problem similarly points beyond this-worldly appearances to the *Olam HaBa* (World to Come), where true recompense will be revealed: the wicked's prosperity is temporary and their "end" (v. 17) is destruction, while the righteous receive their true reward in the life beyond.

The psalm's climactic declaration — "Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you" (v. 25) — has been central to Jewish mystical theology. The Zohar reads this verse as an expression of *devekut* — the soul's cleaving to God that transcends all earthly attachment. Rabbi Nachman of Breslov taught that this verse describes the moment when the soul breaks through all illusions and recognizes that God alone is real, that all other desires are shadows. The final verse — "it is good to be near God" (v. 28) — is a remarkable conclusion to the theodicy struggle: the psalm does not answer the intellectual question but dissolves it in the experience of divine nearness, which renders the question irrelevant.
