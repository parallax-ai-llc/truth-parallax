## A Psalm of David: The King and Priest

Psalm 110 is one of the most contested psalms in Jewish-Christian dialogue. While Christian tradition reads it as messianic prophecy of Christ, Jewish interpretation has developed alternative readings that emphasize different theological concerns.

## Key Themes

### "The LORD says to my lord" (110:1)

Rashi interprets this psalm as referring to Abraham. "The LORD said to my master Abraham" — God promised Abraham victory over the kings (Genesis 14). This reading avoids the Christological interpretation while finding a coherent peshat (plain sense). Ibn Ezra, however, reads it as David speaking of himself or as a psalm composed for a Davidic king.

### The Priesthood of Melchizedek (110:4)

Jewish commentators note that Melchizedek was a Canaanite priest-king of Salem (Jerusalem). The Talmud (Nedarim 32b) identifies Melchizedek with Shem, son of Noah, and teaches that the priesthood was transferred from him to Abraham's descendants because Melchizedek blessed Abraham before blessing God (Genesis 14:19-20).

### Historical Context

The Midrash Tehillim offers multiple historical settings for this psalm: Abraham's victory over the four kings, David's military campaigns, or the future messianic king. The plurality of interpretations is characteristic of rabbinic exegesis, which values multiple valid readings.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah connection**: The psalm resonates with Torah narratives and commandments
- **Rabbinic interpretation**: Multiple layers of meaning in the tradition
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes Jewish daily and festival worship
- **Ethical formation**: Practical guidance for righteous living
