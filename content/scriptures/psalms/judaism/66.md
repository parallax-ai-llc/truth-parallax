## National Remembrance and Personal Testimony

Psalm 66 begins with a universal call to praise and then focuses on Israel's foundational memory — the Exodus. "He turned the sea into dry land; they passed through the river on foot" (v. 6) is the defining event of Jewish collective memory, relived annually at the Passover Seder. The Haggadah instructs that "in every generation, one must see oneself as if they personally went out of Egypt," and this psalm models that identification — the psalmist does not say "they crossed" in distant third person but "there we rejoiced in him" (v. 6), collapsing the temporal distance between past event and present experience.

The psalm's acknowledgment of testing — "You have tried us as silver is tried" (v. 10) — is read in the *mussar* tradition as affirming the purposefulness of suffering. Rabbi Akiva taught (Berakhot 60b) that "everything the Merciful One does, He does for good," and this psalm's refining imagery supports that conviction. The silver metaphor implies that the testing is not punitive but purificatory — it removes impurities to reveal something precious. The progression from being "brought into the net" and bearing "a crushing burden" (v. 11) to being brought "out to a place of abundance" (v. 12) traces the arc of Jewish historical experience: suffering followed by redemption.

The shift to personal testimony — "Come and hear, all you who fear God, and I will tell what he has done for my soul" (v. 16) — models the Jewish practice of sharing individual stories within the communal context. The Talmud values personal testimony alongside communal tradition, and the custom of sharing *divrei Torah* (words of Torah) at celebrations reflects this psalm's pattern. The psalm's concluding principle — "If I had cherished iniquity in my heart, the Lord would not have listened" (v. 18) — teaches that the authenticity of prayer depends on moral sincerity, a principle enshrined in the requirement of *teshuvah* before Yom Kippur.
