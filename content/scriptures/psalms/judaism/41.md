Psalm 41 opens with a beatitude that has shaped Jewish ethics of *tzedakah* (charitable justice): "Blessed is the one who considers the poor (*dal*)" (41:1). The Hebrew *maskil el dal* implies not merely giving to the poor but understanding their situation with wisdom and empathy. Maimonides' famous ladder of *tzedakah* (Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Matanot Aniyim 10:7-14) embodies this psalm's ideal: the highest form of charity enables the poor to become self-sufficient.

The verse about betrayal — "Even my close friend, someone I trusted, one who shared my bread, has turned against me" (41:9) — resonates powerfully in Jewish tradition, where sharing bread (*lechem*) creates bonds of sacred fellowship. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 103b) connects this verse to the figure of Ahithophel, David's trusted counselor who betrayed him. The violation of table fellowship is understood as among the gravest of betrayals.

Psalm 41 concludes Book I of the Psalter with a doxology: "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting! Amen and Amen" (41:13). The double "Amen" is significant in Jewish liturgical practice — it affirms and seals what has been said with emphatic agreement. The Talmud (Shabbat 119b) teaches that one who responds "Amen" with full intention (*kavanah*) opens the gates of paradise. This doxology models the Jewish conviction that all speech about God must culminate in blessing.
