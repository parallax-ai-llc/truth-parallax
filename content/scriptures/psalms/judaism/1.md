Psalm 1 opens the Sefer Tehillim (Book of Psalms) and establishes Torah study as the foundation of the blessed life. The Talmud (Berakhot 9b-10a) notes that Psalms 1 and 2 form a single literary unit, together serving as the prologue to the entire Psalter. The opening word *ashrei* ("happy" or "blessed") appears twenty-six times in the Psalter — the same numerical value as the Tetragrammaton (YHVH) — suggesting that true happiness is found only in connection with God. Rashi interprets the threefold negative of verse 1 (not walking, not standing, not sitting with the wicked) as stages of increasing involvement in sin: first one walks past evil casually, then lingers, then takes up permanent residence.

The phrase "his delight is in the Torah of the Lord, and on His Torah he meditates day and night" (1:2) is the scriptural warrant for the centrality of Torah study in Jewish life. The Midrash Tehillim compares Torah study to water, wine, oil, and honey — it nourishes, gladdens, anoints, and sweetens. The Talmud (Avodah Zarah 3b) interprets "day and night" not as a literal requirement to study every moment but as a principle that one should never entirely cease from Torah learning. The Vilna Gaon taught that every moment of Torah study sustains the world itself.

The image of the tree planted by streams of water (1:3) is interpreted by the Midrash as referring to the person who studies Torah and teaches it to others — the streams (*palgei mayim*) represent the channels through which Torah flows from master to student. Ibn Ezra notes that the tree is "planted" (*shatul*), not "growing wild" — it has been deliberately placed, suggesting divine providence in the righteous person's life. The contrast with the chaff (1:4) is absolute: while the righteous have substance, permanence, and fruitfulness, the wicked are empty husks blown about by circumstance. The psalm's closing verse — "the Lord knows the way of the righteous" (1:6) — uses *yodea* in the sense of intimate covenantal knowledge, the same verb used of marriage.
