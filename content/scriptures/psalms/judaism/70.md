## The Brevity of Urgent Prayer

Psalm 70, nearly identical to the closing verses of Psalm 40, is a concentrated cry for help that the rabbis valued for its demonstration that effective prayer need not be lengthy. The Talmud (Berakhot 61a) discusses the relationship between prayer length and sincerity, concluding that brevity born of urgency is superior to length born of habit. This psalm's five verses strip prayer to its essentials: acknowledgment of need, petition for deliverance, and expression of trust.

The plea that those who seek God may "rejoice and be glad" while enemies are put to shame (vv. 2-4) reflects the Jewish binary of *tzaddikim* (righteous) and *resha'im* (wicked) that structures much of the Psalter and of Jewish ethical thought. Rashi notes that the psalm does not ask for the enemies' destruction but for their "shame" — a distinction that preserves the possibility of *teshuvah*. The wicked may yet repent and join the righteous; their current shame is a goad toward repentance, not a final sentence.

The self-description "I am poor and needy" (v. 5) — *ani v'evyon* in Hebrew — identifies the psalmist with the *aniyei Yisrael* (the poor of Israel), who occupy a special place in Jewish theology. The Torah commands special concern for the *ani* (poor person), and the prophets consistently defend the rights of the needy against exploitation. By claiming this identity, the psalmist appeals not to personal merit but to God's covenantal commitment to protect the vulnerable. The concluding plea — "O LORD, do not delay!" — expresses the tension between patience (*savlanut*) and urgency that characterizes authentic Jewish prayer.
