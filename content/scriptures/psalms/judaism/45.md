Psalm 45 is a royal wedding psalm that Jewish tradition interprets both historically and eschatologically. The king addressed in superlative terms — "You are the most excellent of men; grace is poured upon your lips" (45:2) — is understood by some commentators as Solomon on his wedding day, while others read the psalm as describing the Messianic King (*Melekh HaMashiach*) and his relationship with Israel.

The verse "Your throne, O God, will last for ever and ever" (45:6) presents a translation challenge. Jewish commentators generally read *elohim* here not as addressing the king as divine but as meaning "Your throne is [established by] God forever" or "Your divine throne endures forever." This preserves the strict monotheism of Jewish theology while acknowledging the extraordinary honor bestowed on the anointed king as God's earthly representative.

The bride who is told to "forget your people and your father's house" (45:10) is interpreted in Midrashic tradition as a convert to Judaism, or allegorically as Israel entering into covenant relationship with God. The Targum reads the entire psalm as describing the relationship between the Torah scholar (*talmid chacham*) and the Torah itself — the king is the scholar, and the bride is the Torah that he lovingly studies and serves. This interpretation reflects the rabbinic tendency to spiritualize royal imagery in terms of Torah devotion.
