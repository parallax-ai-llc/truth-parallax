Psalm 42 opens with one of Tehillim's most evocative images: "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God" (42:1). This psalm is attributed to the Sons of Korah — Levites who served in the Temple — and expresses the anguish of exile from the place of worship. Jewish tradition has applied this longing to the centuries of exile from Jerusalem, when the Jewish people yearned for the restoration of Temple worship.

The taunt "Where is your God?" (42:3, 10) echoes through Jewish history — from the Babylonian exile to the medieval disputations to the questions raised by the Shoah. Jewish theology does not dismiss this challenge but engages it with honesty and courage. The psalmist's response is not a theological argument but a spiritual discipline: "Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him" (42:5, 11). Faith is maintained not by answering every question but by continuing to hope and praise.

The psalm's imagery of "deep calls to deep" (42:7) is interpreted in the Zohar as describing the dialogue between the human soul (*neshamah*) and its divine source. Every soul contains a spark of the divine, and this spark calls out to its origin even when the conscious mind experiences only darkness and confusion. The mystical tradition teaches that this deep calling is the essence of all genuine prayer.
