Psalm 15 asks "Lord, who shall sojourn in your tent? Who shall dwell on your holy hill?" (15:1) and provides an ethical answer that the Talmud treats as a summary of the entire Torah. The Talmud (Makkot 24a) records that David reduced the 613 commandments to the eleven principles of Psalm 15, just as Isaiah reduced them to six (Isaiah 33:15-16) and Micah to three (Micah 6:8). This process of distillation does not diminish the commandments but reveals their ethical core. The eleven qualities listed are not ritual requirements but moral virtues, emphasizing that access to God depends on righteous character.

The specific ethical requirements — truthfulness (15:2), not slandering (15:3), honoring those who fear God (15:4), keeping one's oath even to one's own hurt (15:4) — are elaborated extensively in the *Shulchan Aruch* (Code of Jewish Law). The prohibition against lending at interest (15:5) was applied in Jewish law specifically to loans between Jews, a provision designed to maintain communal solidarity. The Rambam explains that charging interest to a fellow Jew is tantamount to treating a brother as a stranger, undermining the covenant community's bonds of mutual responsibility.

The psalm's conclusion — "He who does these things shall never be moved" (15:5b) — promises stability and permanence to the ethically upright. The Midrash connects this to the *olam ha-ba* (World to Come), where the righteous dwell permanently in God's presence. Rabbeinu Yonah, in his *Sha'arei Teshuvah* (Gates of Repentance), uses Psalm 15 as a framework for examining one's moral conduct, treating each verse as a standard against which to measure one's behavior. The psalm thus functions in Jewish tradition as both an aspiration and a practical ethical checklist.
