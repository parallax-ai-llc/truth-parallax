Psalm 22, one of the longest and most emotionally intense psalms in Tehillim, opens with the anguished cry "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1). Jewish tradition reads this psalm primarily as David's prayer during extreme persecution, while also seeing it as the voice of the Jewish people in exile. The Midrash Tehillim applies the psalm to Queen Esther, who approached King Ahasuerus unbidden and felt abandoned by God at the moment of greatest danger. The psalm's oscillation between despair and trust mirrors Israel's historical experience of alternating persecution and deliverance.

The description of suffering in verses 6-18 — "I am a worm and not a man, scorned by mankind and despised by the people" (22:6), "they have pierced my hands and my feet" (22:16) — is read by Jewish commentators in the context of Davidic persecution. The controversial verse 16, which contains the textually disputed word *ka'ari* ("like a lion") versus *karu* ("they pierced"), is understood in the Masoretic tradition as "like a lion — my hands and my feet," describing enemies that maul like predators. Rashi reads the entire section as describing the attacks of David's enemies using animal metaphors (dogs, bulls, lions).

The psalm's dramatic turn at verse 22 — from suffering to praise — and its expansion to universal worship (22:27, "All the ends of the earth shall remember and turn to the Lord") are read in Jewish messianic tradition as anticipating the era when all nations will acknowledge the God of Israel. The final verse (22:31) — that future generations "shall come and proclaim his righteousness to a people yet unborn, that he has done it" — expresses the Jewish commitment to *l'dor v'dor* (from generation to generation) transmission of faith. Each generation receives the testimony of God's faithfulness and passes it forward, forming an unbroken chain of witness.
