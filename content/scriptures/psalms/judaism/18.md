Psalm 18, the longest psalm attributed to David, appears also in 2 Samuel 22 with minor textual variations that have been the subject of extensive rabbinic and scholarly analysis. The Talmud (Pesachim 117a) assigns this psalm to the occasion when God delivered David from all his enemies and from Saul, placing it at the culmination of David's military career. The opening verse's cascade of divine metaphors — "my rock, my fortress, my deliverer, my God, my rock, my shield, my horn of salvation, my stronghold" (18:2) — is cited by the Midrash as evidence that a single metaphor can never capture God's fullness.

The theophany of verses 7-15 draws on Sinai imagery — earthquake, fire, smoke, darkness, cherubim — and has been connected in Jewish liturgical poetry (*piyyutim*) to the great revelations of Israel's history. The Midrash Tehillim observes that God descended to save one individual (David) with the same cosmic display He used at Sinai for the entire nation, teaching that every individual is worthy of divine intervention. This principle — the infinite value of each human life — is enshrined in the Mishnah (Sanhedrin 4:5): "Whoever saves a single life is considered as if he saved an entire world."

The Davidic covenant affirmation in the final verse — "He shows steadfast love to his anointed, to David and his offspring forever" (18:50) — is foundational to Jewish messianic hope. The *Shemoneh Esrei* (Amidah) includes a blessing for the "offspring of David" (*tzemach David*), praying daily for the messianic restoration. Jewish messianism, unlike some other traditions' interpretations, awaits a human king descended from David who will rebuild the Temple, gather the exiles, and inaugurate an era of universal peace. Psalm 18's military victories are thus read as typological anticipations of the Messiah's future deliverance of Israel.
