## The Messianic King's Reign of Justice

Psalm 72 is the primary biblical text for the Jewish understanding of the Messianic king. While its superscription attributes it to Solomon, the rabbis read it as a prophecy extending far beyond Solomon's historical reign. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98b) cites this psalm in its discussions of the Messiah's characteristics: he will "judge your people with righteousness and your poor with justice" (v. 2), establishing the social order that Jewish eschatology envisions. Rashi comments that the Messianic king's primary qualification is not military prowess but judicial righteousness — the ability to discern truth and defend the vulnerable.

The psalm's social vision is extraordinary: "He delivers the needy when he calls, the poor and him who has no helper... precious is their blood in his sight" (vv. 12, 14). This describes a kingdom where the most vulnerable members receive the king's primary attention — a vision that has shaped Jewish political ethics throughout history. The Rambam (Maimonides), in his *Mishneh Torah* (Laws of Kings, chapters 11-12), draws on this psalm's portrait to describe the Messianic age: a time of justice, abundance, and universal knowledge of God, not a supernatural transformation of nature but the perfection of the social order under divinely guided leadership.

The doxology concluding Book II of the Psalter — "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Israel, who alone does wondrous things. May the whole earth be filled with his glory!" (vv. 18-19) — is echoed in the *Kedushah* of the *Amidah* and in the daily liturgy. The phrase "the whole earth filled with his glory" (cf. Isaiah 6:3) expresses the eschatological hope that pervades Jewish worship — the conviction that God's kingdom will ultimately encompass all creation. The psalm's conclusion, "The prayers of David, the son of Jesse, are ended" (v. 20), marks a literary milestone in the Psalter and has prompted extensive midrashic reflection on the relationship between David's prayers and the ongoing prayer life of the Jewish people.
