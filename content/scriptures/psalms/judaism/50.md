Psalm 50 is attributed to Asaph, the Levitical musician, and presents God as both covenant lord and cosmic judge. The theophanic opening — "The Mighty One, God, the LORD (*El, Elohim, Adonai*), speaks and summons the earth from the rising of the sun to where it sets" (50:1) — uses three divine names, which the Midrash interprets as expressing God's roles as judge, lawgiver, and merciful redeemer.

God's declaration — "I have no need of a bull from your stall or of goats from your pens, for every animal of the forest is mine" (50:9-10) — must be understood carefully within Jewish theology. The sacrificial system was divinely ordained, so God is not rejecting his own commands but insisting that sacrifice without proper *kavanah* (intention) is meaningless. The prophetic critique of sacrifice (Isaiah 1:11-17, Amos 5:21-24) does not abolish the Temple system but demands that ritual be accompanied by moral integrity.

The psalm's closing verse — "Those who sacrifice thank offerings honor me, and to the blameless I will show my salvation" (50:23) — is significant for post-Temple Judaism. With the Temple's destruction in 70 CE, the "sacrifice of thanksgiving" was reinterpreted as verbal praise and prayer. The Talmud (Berakhot 26b) teaches that the three daily prayers correspond to the daily sacrifices, and the *todah* (thank offering) finds its continuation in the *berakha* (blessing) recited over God's gifts. This psalm thus bridges Temple worship and the prayer-centered Judaism of the post-Temple era.
