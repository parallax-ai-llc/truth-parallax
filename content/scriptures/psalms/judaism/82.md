## God Judges the Judges

Psalm 82 occupies a unique place in Jewish theological reflection. The psalm's depiction of God standing in the "divine assembly" (*adat El*) and judging the "gods" (*elohim*) has generated extensive rabbinic discussion about justice, authority, and the nature of divine governance.

## Key Themes

### The Divine Council (82:1)

Rashi interprets the "gods" as human judges who are called *elohim* because they exercise divine authority. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 6b-7a) teaches that when judges sit in righteousness, the Shekhinah dwells among them, but when they pervert justice, they are held accountable as though they were false gods.

### Justice for the Vulnerable (82:2-4)

The psalm's demand to "give justice to the weak and the fatherless" resonates throughout Jewish legal tradition. The Torah's repeated emphasis on protecting the widow, orphan, and stranger (*ger*) finds its poetic expression here. Maimonides codified these obligations in the *Mishneh Torah*, Laws of Gifts to the Poor.

### "You are gods" (82:6)

This verse has been variously interpreted. Rashi reads it as God addressing Israel at Sinai — "I said you are godlike" — a status forfeited through the sin of the Golden Calf. The Midrash Tehillim connects this to the teaching that Israel was offered immortality at Sinai but lost it through disobedience.

## Theological Significance

- **Torah connection**: The psalm resonates with Torah narratives and commandments
- **Rabbinic interpretation**: Multiple layers of meaning in the tradition
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes Jewish daily and festival worship
- **Ethical formation**: Practical guidance for righteous living
