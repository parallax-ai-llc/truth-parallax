## Yearning for the Rock of Ages

Psalm 61's plea — "lead me to the rock that is higher than I" (v. 2) — is read in Jewish tradition as expressing the longing for stability and divine protection that transcends human capability. Rashi interprets the "rock" as the *Beit HaMikdash* (Holy Temple), the place of God's dwelling on earth, while the Radak sees it more broadly as God Himself, the *Tzur Yisrael* (Rock of Israel). The phrase "from the end of the earth I call to you" (v. 2) has spoken powerfully to diaspora Jews throughout history, expressing the experience of spiritual distance from the Holy Land and the Temple.

The prayer for the king's long life (vv. 6-7) is interpreted in the Midrash as referring ultimately to the Messianic king, whose reign will be "forever" — a description no ordinary human monarch can fulfill. The rabbis taught that every righteous king of Israel was a partial realization of the Messianic promise, but the psalm's language stretches beyond any historical king to the *Melech HaMashiach* (King Messiah) whose rule will establish permanent justice and peace. The request that "steadfast love and faithfulness" (*chesed* and *emet*) guard the king reflects the Jewish understanding that true sovereignty is exercised through covenantal virtues, not through force.

The psalmist's vow — "I will ever sing praises to your name, as I perform my vows day after day" (v. 8) — connects praise to daily faithful living. The *mussar* tradition emphasizes that authentic praise is inseparable from moral conduct; the Psalms are not merely sung but lived. The Sfas Emes (Rabbi Yehudah Aryeh Leib Alter) taught that performing one's vows "day after day" describes the ongoing, never-completed work of spiritual growth — each day presents new opportunities for faithful response to God's gifts.
