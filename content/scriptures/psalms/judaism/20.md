Psalm 20 is a congregational prayer for the king's success that has been incorporated into the daily liturgy. It is recited after the *Amidah* in many *Nusach Ashkenaz* communities on weekdays, and its themes of communal intercession and trust in God rather than military power have made it a prayer for all seasons of Jewish life. The Talmud (Berakhot 12b) notes that certain communities added extra psalms to the daily service, and Psalm 20's intercessory character made it a natural choice.

The declaration "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we make mention of the name of the Lord our God" (20:7) has been a watchword of Jewish faith throughout history. The Hebrew *nazkir* ("we make mention" or "we invoke") suggests that the very act of pronouncing God's name is itself a source of strength. Jewish tradition treats the divine Name with extreme reverence — the Tetragrammaton is not pronounced as written, and the Talmud discusses the awesome power contained in its letters. To "invoke the name" is not a casual act but a deliberate choice to ground one's security in divine reality rather than human resources.

The psalm's communal character — "May the Lord answer you" (20:1, second person), shifting to "we will shout for joy" (20:5, first person) — reflects the Jewish principle that prayer is most powerful when offered by the community (*tzibbur*). The Talmud (Berakhot 8a) teaches that God does not reject the prayer of the congregation, and the *minyan* (quorum of ten) is required for the most sacred liturgical acts. Psalm 20 models this communal prayer at its best: the community intercedes for its leader, and leader and community together celebrate the anticipated victory that God will grant.
