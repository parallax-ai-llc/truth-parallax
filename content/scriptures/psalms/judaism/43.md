Psalm 43 continues the prayer begun in Psalm 42 and shares its refrain: "Why, my soul, are you downcast?" (43:5). Many Jewish commentators, including Ibn Ezra and Radak, treat Psalms 42-43 as a single composition. The absence of a superscription for Psalm 43 — unusual in Book II — supports this reading.

The petition "Send me your light and your truth, let them lead me" (43:3) is read in Jewish tradition as a prayer for Torah illumination. "Light" (*or*) and "truth" (*emet*) are both names for Torah in rabbinic literature. The Midrash (Bereishit Rabbah 1:6) identifies the primordial light of Genesis 1:3 with the light of Torah, which God stored away for the righteous in the world to come. The psalmist prays that this light would guide him back to God's presence.

The resolution — "Then I will go to the altar of God, to God, my joy and my delight" (43:4) — expresses the longing for restored worship that characterized the Jewish experience during both the Babylonian exile and the post-70 CE diaspora. The daily prayers (*tefillot*) that replaced Temple sacrifice were understood as temporary substitutes, sustained by the hope that one day Israel would again approach the altar with joy.
