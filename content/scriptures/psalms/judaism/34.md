Psalm 34 is an acrostic psalm composed when David "feigned madness before Abimelech" (the superscription refers to the incident with Achish king of Gath in 1 Samuel 21). The Talmud (Berakhot 33a) praises David's cleverness in this situation, noting that even apparent humiliation can serve divine purposes. The acrostic structure — each verse beginning with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet — reflects the Jewish love of literary craft as a form of worship.

The invitation "Taste and see that the LORD is good" (34:8) — *ta'amu u'r'u ki tov Adonai* — is understood in Jewish tradition as calling for experiential knowledge of God through the performance of *mitzvot*. One does not merely believe in God's goodness abstractly but encounters it through Shabbat observance, Torah study, acts of *chesed* (lovingkindness), and prayer. The "taste" is the lived experience of the Torah-observant life.

The psalm's wisdom instruction on "the fear of the LORD" (34:11-14) — including "keep your tongue from evil" and "seek peace and pursue it" — is cited extensively in rabbinic ethical literature. Chofetz Chaim (Rabbi Yisrael Meir Kagan) built his influential teachings on *lashon hara* (evil speech) partly on verse 13: "Keep your tongue from evil and your lips from telling lies." This psalm thus shapes practical Jewish ethics across the centuries.
