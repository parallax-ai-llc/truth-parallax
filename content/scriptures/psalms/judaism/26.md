Psalm 26 is a psalm of David in which the speaker appeals to HaShem on the basis of his *temimut* (integrity). The Talmud (Makkot 24a) counts walking in integrity among the fundamental principles by which one may approach God. David's declaration, "I have trusted in the LORD without wavering" (26:1), expresses the quality of *emunah* — steadfast faithfulness — that Jewish tradition regards as the foundation of the righteous life.

The ritual imagery — "I wash my hands in innocence and go around your altar, O LORD" (26:6) — connects this psalm to the Temple service. The *kohanim* (priests) washed their hands and feet at the *kiyor* (laver) before approaching the altar. Jewish liturgical practice preserves this connection: the ritual handwashing (*netilat yadayim*) before meals and prayer echoes the psalmist's gesture of purification before worship.

The psalm's concluding declaration, "My feet stand on level ground; in the great congregation I will praise the LORD" (26:12), affirms the communal dimension of Jewish worship. Judaism has always understood that the individual's integrity finds its fullest expression within the community (*tzibur*). The righteous person does not withdraw from the congregation but stands within it, adding personal holiness to the collective praise of Israel.
