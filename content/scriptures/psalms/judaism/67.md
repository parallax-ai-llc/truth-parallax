## The Menorah Psalm

Psalm 67 holds a unique place in Jewish tradition as the "Menorah Psalm" — it is traditionally written in the shape of a seven-branched menorah, with its seven verses corresponding to the seven branches. This visual arrangement, found in siddurim (prayer books) and in amulets, reflects the Kabbalistic teaching that this psalm contains mystical power connected to divine light. The opening verse — "May God be gracious to us and bless us and make his face to shine upon us" (v. 1) — directly echoes the Priestly Blessing (*Birkat Kohanim*, Numbers 6:24-26), which is recited daily in the Land of Israel and on festivals in the diaspora.

The refrain "Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!" (vv. 3, 5) expresses the universalist strand within Jewish monotheism. While Judaism does not proselytize, it has always maintained that the nations will ultimately acknowledge the God of Israel. The Aleinu prayer, recited at the conclusion of every service, expresses this same hope: "On that day the LORD shall be one and His Name one" (Zechariah 14:9). This psalm provides the scriptural basis for that eschatological vision of universal divine recognition.

The harvest imagery — "The earth has yielded its increase; God, our God, shall bless us" (v. 6) — connects this psalm to the agricultural festivals of Sukkot (Tabernacles) and Shavuot (Weeks). The Talmud (Ta'anit 6b) discusses the relationship between Israel's faithfulness and the land's productivity, reflecting the Deuteronomic covenant theology that underlies this psalm. The final verse's prayer that "all the ends of the earth" would fear God (v. 7) is recited during the *Sefirat HaOmer* (counting of the Omer), connecting the harvest period between Passover and Shavuot to the hope for universal divine recognition.
