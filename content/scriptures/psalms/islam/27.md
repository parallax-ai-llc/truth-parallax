Psalm 27 opens with "The Lord is my light and my salvation" (27:1), language that connects profoundly to the Quranic verse known as *Ayat al-Nur* (the Light Verse): "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth" (24:35). Islamic theology identifies Allah as the source of all light — physical, intellectual, and spiritual. The psalm's metaphor of divine light as salvation echoes the Quran's teaching that Allah brings believers "out of darkness into light" (2:257).

The psalmist's desire to "dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life" (27:4) expresses a longing that Islam channels into the five daily prayers and the spiritual aspiration to be constantly in a state of *dhikr* (remembrance of God). The Quran declares that "in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest" (13:28), and the Sufi tradition in particular has developed practices of continuous God-consciousness that embody this psalm's desire for perpetual divine presence.

The closing counsel to "wait for the LORD; be strong and take heart" (27:14) exemplifies the Islamic virtue of *sabr* (patient perseverance), which the Quran praises extensively. "Indeed, Allah is with the patient" (2:153). *Sabr* in Islamic theology is not passive resignation but active trust in Allah's wisdom and timing, maintaining faith and righteous conduct even when circumstances are difficult. This is precisely the posture Psalm 27 commends.
