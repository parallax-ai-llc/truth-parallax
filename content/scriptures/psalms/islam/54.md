## Invoking the Divine Name for Protection

Psalm 54's opening plea — "O God, save me by your name" (v. 1) — resonates with the Islamic practice of invoking Allah's names (*al-asma al-husna*) for protection and guidance. The *Bismillah* ("In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful") opens every surah of the Quran except one, and Muslims begin every significant action with this invocation. The psalmist's appeal to God's name reflects a shared Abrahamic conviction that the divine Name carries real power and that invoking it establishes a connection between the servant and the Lord.

The psalm's description of enemies who "do not set God before themselves" (v. 3) identifies the root cause of human wickedness in Islamic terms: *ghaflah* (heedlessness of God). The Quran warns repeatedly against heedlessness — "And do not be like those who forgot Allah, so He made them forget themselves" (Surah al-Hashr 59:19). The enemies' violence arises not from mere personal malice but from a failure to maintain consciousness of God (*taqwa*), which Islamic ethics identifies as the foundation of all moral behavior.

The psalm's movement from plea to thanksgiving — "With a freewill offering I will sacrifice to you" (v. 6) and "he has delivered me from every trouble" (v. 7) — models the Islamic virtue of *shukr* (gratitude). The Quran asks, "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you" (Surah Ibrahim 14:7), establishing gratitude as both a response to divine favor and a means of attracting further blessing. The psalm's pattern of crying to God in distress and thanking God for deliverance mirrors the daily life of the Muslim, who turns to Allah in both trial and triumph.
