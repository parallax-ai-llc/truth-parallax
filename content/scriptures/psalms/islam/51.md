## Tawbah: The Return to Allah

Psalm 51's theme of repentance resonates deeply with the Islamic concept of *tawbah* (repentance/turning back to God). The Quran presents Prophet Dawud (David) as a righteous servant who, when he erred, turned immediately to Allah in repentance: "And Dawud became certain that We had tried him, and he asked forgiveness of his Lord and fell down bowing in prostration and turned in repentance" (Surah Sad 38:24). The psalm's cry for mercy — "Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love" (v. 1) — echoes the Quranic refrain that Allah is *al-Tawwab* (the Acceptor of Repentance) and *al-Rahim* (the Most Merciful).

Islamic theology diverges from the psalm on several points. The assertion "in sin did my mother conceive me" (v. 5) is incompatible with the Islamic doctrine that every child is born in a state of *fitrah* (natural purity and inclination toward God). The Quran teaches that "no bearer of burdens will bear the burden of another" (Surah al-An'am 6:164), rejecting any concept of inherited sin. Muslim scholars approach this verse as an example of *tahrif* (alteration) in the biblical text, or as a poetic exaggeration that should not be taken as doctrinal.

However, the psalm's central insight — that God desires inner transformation over external ritual — finds strong Quranic support. The Quran declares, "Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety from you" (Surah al-Hajj 22:37). The psalm's plea for a "clean heart" (v. 10) aligns with the hadith in which the Prophet Muhammad said, "Verily, Allah does not look at your appearance or your wealth, but He looks at your hearts and your deeds" (Sahih Muslim). The psalm thus preserves traces of authentic prophetic teaching about the priority of inner sincerity over outward observance.
