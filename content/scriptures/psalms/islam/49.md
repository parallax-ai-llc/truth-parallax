Psalm 49's wisdom teaching on wealth and death — "Those who trust in their wealth and boast of their great riches cannot redeem anyone" (49:6-7) — powerfully echoes the Quranic warning: "Wealth and children are but adornment of the worldly life. But the enduring good deeds are better to your Lord for reward and better for hope" (18:46). Islamic theology consistently subordinates material wealth to spiritual worth, and *zakat* (obligatory charity) institutionalizes this principle.

The psalm's observation that "the wise die, the foolish and the senseless also perish, leaving their wealth to others" (49:10) parallels the Quranic parable of the two men and their gardens (18:32-44), in which the wealthy man who boasted of his possessions lost everything, while the faithful man who trusted in Allah was vindicated. Islamic homiletic literature (*wa'z*) draws extensively on such narratives to warn against attachment to *dunya*.

The hope expressed in verse 15 — "God will redeem me from the realm of the dead" — anticipates the Islamic doctrine of resurrection (*ba'th*) and the hereafter. The Quran is emphatic: "Does man think that he will be left neglected? Was he not a drop of ejected semen? Then he was a clinging clot, and Allah created him and proportioned him. And made of him two mates, the male and the female. Is not that Creator able to give life to the dead?" (75:36-40). The psalm's tentative hope becomes full certainty in Quranic revelation.
