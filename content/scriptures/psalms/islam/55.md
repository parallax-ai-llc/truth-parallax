## The Trial of Betrayal and Trust in Allah

Psalm 55's theme of betrayal by a trusted companion resonates with the prophetic experience in Islamic tradition. Prophet Muhammad himself experienced betrayal — by members of his own Quraysh tribe, by supposed allies among the hypocrites (*munafiqun*) of Medina, and by those who violated the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah. The psalm's anguished description of a trusted friend turned traitor (vv. 12-14) speaks to the particular pain of betrayal from within one's own community, a pain that the Prophet experienced deeply and addressed in the Quran's critique of hypocrisy (Surah al-Munafiqun 63).

The psalm's emotional rawness — "Fear and trembling come upon me, and horror overwhelms me" (v. 5) — is compatible with the Quranic depiction of prophets as fully human beings who experience the full range of human emotion. The Quran records moments of distress among the prophets: Yunus (Jonah) calling out from the belly of the whale (Surah al-Anbiya 21:87), Yaqub (Jacob) weeping until he lost his sight (Surah Yusuf 12:84). The psalm's honesty about suffering does not diminish the prophet's status but demonstrates the authentic humanity that makes prophetic example relevant to all believers.

The exhortation "Cast your burden on the LORD" (v. 22) directly parallels the Quranic and prophetic teaching on *tawakkul* (reliance on Allah). The Prophet Muhammad taught, "If you were to rely upon Allah with the reliance He is due, you would be provided as the birds are provided — they go out hungry in the morning and return full in the evening" (Tirmidhi). The psalm's counsel to entrust one's burdens to God reflects this same principle: active trust that releases anxiety not through denial but through confidence in divine sufficiency and care.
