Psalm 46 declares "God is our refuge and strength" (46:1), a confession that resonates with the Quranic teaching on *tawakkul* — placing one's complete reliance on Allah. The Quran promises: "And whoever puts their trust in Allah, He will be enough for them" (65:3). Islamic theology teaches that true security comes not from material defenses but from the relationship between the servant and Allah.

The psalm's vision of cosmic upheaval — "though the earth give way and the mountains fall into the heart of the sea" (46:2) — parallels Quranic descriptions of the Day of Judgment: "When the earth is shaken with its final earthquake" (99:1). Yet the psalm affirms that God's people remain secure even amid universal dissolution. This eschatological confidence mirrors the Quranic assurance that "whoever comes to Allah as a believer, having done righteous deeds — for those will be the highest degrees" (20:75).

"Be still, and know that I am God" (46:10) resonates with the Islamic practice of *muraqabah* — the contemplative awareness of Allah's presence that is cultivated through meditation and dhikr. The Sufi tradition, particularly the *Naqshbandi* order, emphasizes silent contemplation (*tafakkur*) as a path to knowledge of Allah. The psalm's command to stillness affirms what Islamic mystics have always taught: the deepest knowledge of God comes not through activity but through the quieting of the self before the Divine.
