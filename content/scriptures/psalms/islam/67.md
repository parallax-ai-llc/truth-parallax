## God's Blessing Extended to All Nations

Psalm 67's universal vision — "that your way may be known on earth, your saving power among all nations" (v. 2) — resonates with Islam's self-understanding as a universal religion. The Quran declares Muhammad to be "a mercy to all the worlds" (Surah al-Anbiya 21:107), not merely to the Arabs. While the psalm expresses this universalism within the framework of Israelite religion, Islamic theology sees the same divine impulse — the desire for all nations to know and worship God — reaching its fulfillment in the final revelation given to the last Prophet.

The refrain "Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!" (vv. 3, 5) envisions universal worship that Islam actualizes in the annual Hajj, where millions from every nation gather in Mecca to worship the one God. The Prophet Muhammad's farewell sermon declared, "All mankind is from Adam, and an Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab." The psalm's vision of all peoples united in praise transcends the ethnic boundaries that the Quran itself criticizes: "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another" (Surah al-Hujurat 49:13).

The connection between divine blessing and earthly harvest (v. 6) reflects the Quranic teaching that the earth's productivity is a sign of God's mercy and a trust (*amanah*) given to humanity. The Quran instructs, "Eat and drink from the provision of Allah, and do not commit abuse on the earth, spreading corruption" (Surah al-Baqarah 2:60). The psalm's integration of agricultural blessing with universal worship echoes the Islamic environmental ethic that views humanity as God's steward (*khalifah*) on earth, responsible for sharing divine bounty justly among all peoples.
