Psalm 43 petitions God to "send me your light and your faithful care" (43:3), a prayer that Islamic theology understands as requesting *hidayah* (divine guidance). The opening of every *salah* (prayer) includes the recitation of *al-Fatiha*, which contains the fundamental petition: "Guide us to the straight path" (1:6). The psalmist's prayer for light and truth parallels this daily Islamic supplication for guidance.

The psalm's desire to come "to the altar of God, to God, my joy and my delight" (43:4) expresses the longing for divine proximity (*qurb*) that Islamic worship is designed to fulfill. The Quran declares: "Prostrate and draw near [to Allah]" (96:19). The moment of *sujud* (prostration) in prayer is understood as the closest a servant comes to Allah — the physical act of placing one's forehead on the ground enacting the spiritual surrender the psalmist describes.

The resolution "I will yet praise him, my Savior and my God" (43:5) embodies the Islamic principle that praise of Allah (*hamd*) is appropriate in every circumstance. The Prophet Muhammad praised Allah in both ease and difficulty, teaching his followers to say *Alhamdulillah* (praise be to Allah) in every situation. This psalm's movement from despair to determined praise models the Islamic ideal of *rida* — contentment with Allah's decree.
