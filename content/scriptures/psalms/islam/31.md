Psalm 31's declaration "Into your hands I commit my spirit" (31:5) expresses the total *tawakkul* (reliance on God) that Islam regards as the hallmark of true faith. The Quran teaches: "And whoever relies upon Allah — then He is sufficient for him" (65:3). This surrender is not fatalism but the active trust of one who has done their best and entrusts the outcome to Allah's wisdom.

The psalmist's suffering — "I am the utter contempt of my neighbors" (31:11) — mirrors the experience of the Prophet Muhammad during the early years of his mission in Makkah, when he faced ridicule, persecution, and social ostracism. The Quran comforts him: "We know that your heart is distressed by what they say. So glorify your Lord with praise and be among those who prostrate" (15:97-98). The prophets' suffering is a recurring theme in both the Psalms and the Quran.

The confession "My times are in your hands" (31:15) aligns with the Islamic concept of *qadr* (divine decree). Everything that occurs is within Allah's knowledge and permission. The Prophet Muhammad taught that the pens have been lifted and the pages have dried (Tirmidhi) — all things are decreed. Yet this does not negate human responsibility; rather, it provides the believer with serenity in the face of suffering, knowing that nothing befalls them except what Allah has written for them (9:51).
