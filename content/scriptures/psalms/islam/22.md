Psalm 22 is a psalm that Islamic and Christian traditions read very differently. The opening cry — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1) — is attributed in the Gospel narratives to Jesus on the cross, but Islamic theology categorically rejects the crucifixion of Isa (Jesus): "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them" (Quran 4:157). Muslim scholars understand the psalm as David's own prayer during extreme suffering, consistent with the prophetic experience of trial. The Quran teaches that all prophets faced persecution but were ultimately delivered by God.

The psalm's description of suffering — mockery, physical torment, abandonment — resonates with the experiences of the Prophet Muhammad and other prophets as described in Islamic sources. The Prophet endured years of persecution in Makkah, the stoning at Ta'if, the death of beloved family members, and military attacks. The Quran acknowledges: "Messengers were denied before you, but they were patient over the denial and persecution" (6:34). The suffering described in Psalm 22 is thus read as part of the universal prophetic experience, not as unique to any single figure.

The psalm's triumphant second half (22:22-31), with its expansion from individual deliverance to universal worship, aligns with the Islamic vision of *dawah* (calling all peoples to worship the one God). The psalm's declaration that "all the ends of the earth shall remember and turn to the Lord" (22:27) corresponds to the Quran's description of Islam's universal mission: "And We have not sent you except as a mercy to the worlds" (21:107). The progression from suffering to vindication to worldwide acknowledgment of God's sovereignty mirrors the arc of the Prophet Muhammad's own career — from persecution to victory to the spread of Islam across nations.
