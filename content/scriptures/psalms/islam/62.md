## Silence and Surrender Before God Alone

Psalm 62's insistence on trusting "God alone" (v. 1) resonates with the absolute monotheism (*tawhid*) of Islam. The psalm's emphatic repetition of "alone" mirrors the Quranic declaration "La ilaha illa Allah" (There is no god but Allah) — the foundational statement of Islamic faith that excludes all rivals to divine sovereignty. The psalm's critique of trusting in human power, wealth, or social standing (vv. 9-10) directly aligns with the Quranic warning: "O mankind, indeed the promise of Allah is truth, so let not the worldly life delude you" (Surah Fatir 35:5).

The psalm's waiting in silence (v. 1) connects to the Islamic spiritual practice of *muraqabah* (watchful meditation/awareness of God). Sufi tradition has developed this into a formal contemplative practice, but its roots lie in the Quran's instruction to remember Allah with devotion and humility. The silence described in this psalm is not emptiness but receptivity — the soul stilling itself to receive what God alone can give. This resonates with the hadith qudsi in which Allah says, "I am as My servant thinks of Me, and I am with him when he remembers Me" (Sahih al-Bukhari).

The concluding revelation that "power belongs to God, and... steadfast love" (vv. 11-12) presents two divine attributes that Islam affirms through the names *al-Qawiyy* (the All-Powerful) and *al-Wadud* (the Loving). Islamic theology holds these attributes in perfect unity within the divine nature — God's power is never exercised apart from wisdom and mercy, and God's mercy is never impotent. The psalm's assertion that God "repays each according to his work" (v. 12) aligns with the Quranic teaching: "Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it" (Surah az-Zalzalah 99:7-8).
