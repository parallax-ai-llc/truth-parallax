Psalm 34's invitation to "taste and see that the LORD is good" (34:8) resonates with the Islamic concept of *dhawq* — the direct spiritual experience of divine reality that Sufi masters have described as the goal of the spiritual path. While Islamic theology insists on the transcendence of Allah, it also affirms that believers can experience His mercy, beauty, and nearness through worship, remembrance, and righteous living.

The psalm's wisdom teaching — "The LORD is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit" (34:18) — mirrors the *hadith qudsi* (sacred tradition) in which Allah declares: "I am near to the thought of My servant as he thinks about Me, and I am with him as he remembers Me" (Bukhari). Allah's nearness to the suffering is a central theme in Islamic pastoral theology. The Quran affirms: "We are nearer to him than his jugular vein" (50:16).

David's instruction to "fear the LORD" (34:11) corresponds to *taqwa* — the God-consciousness that the Quran identifies as the most valuable quality a person can possess: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous (*atqakum*) of you" (49:13). *Taqwa* is not terror but reverential awareness — the constant consciousness of Allah's presence that shapes every thought, word, and deed toward righteousness.
