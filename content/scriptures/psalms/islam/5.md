Psalm 5's opening as a morning prayer corresponds to the *Fajr* prayer, which the Quran singles out as especially significant: "Establish prayer at the decline of the sun to the darkness of the night and the Quran at dawn. Indeed, the recitation at dawn is ever witnessed" (Quran 17:78). The psalm's declaration "in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" (5:3) aligns with the Islamic understanding that the early morning hours are a time of special divine proximity, when the sincere worshiper's prayers are most likely to be answered.

The psalm's strong language about God's hatred of falsehood and evildoers (5:4-6) accords with the Quranic description of Allah's relationship to evil. The Quran declares: "Indeed, Allah does not love the transgressors" (2:190), "Allah does not love the corrupters" (28:77), and "Allah does not love everyone self-deluded and boastful" (31:18). Islamic theology affirms that God's nature is utterly opposed to falsehood, injustice, and corruption, while always extending the opportunity for repentance (*tawbah*) to those who turn back to Him.

The petition "Lead me, O Lord, in your righteousness" (5:8) echoes the central request of Surah al-Fatihah: "*Ihdina al-sirat al-mustaqim*" — "Guide us to the straight path." This prayer for guidance is the most frequently recited supplication in Islam, repeated at least seventeen times daily in the obligatory prayers. The Islamic understanding of *hidayah* (guidance) encompasses both the general guidance given to all creation and the specific guidance of revelation and prophetic teaching, corresponding to the psalm's request for divine direction through a world filled with deceit and danger.
