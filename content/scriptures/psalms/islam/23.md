Psalm 23's imagery of divine provision and guidance resonates deeply with the Islamic understanding of Allah as *al-Razzaq* (the Provider), *al-Hadi* (the Guide), and *al-Wakil* (the Trustee). While the specific shepherd metaphor is less prominent in Islamic theology — which generally avoids imagery that might suggest similarity between God and creatures — the substance of the psalm's theology is fully embraced. The Quran declares: "And there is no creature on earth but that upon Allah is its provision, and He knows its place of dwelling and place of storage" (11:6).

The "valley of the shadow of death" (23:4) through which the shepherd accompanies the trusting soul corresponds to the Islamic concept of the *barzakh* (the intermediate realm between death and resurrection) and the testing of the grave. Islamic eschatology teaches that the righteous soul is comforted and protected after death, while the wicked experience distress. The Prophet Muhammad taught specific supplications for protection from the "punishment of the grave" and assured the faithful that the angels of mercy attend the righteous at death: "The angel of death comes to the dying person, sits at his head, and says, 'O good soul, come out to God's forgiveness and pleasure'" (*Musnad Ahmad*).

The banquet imagery — "You prepare a table before me" (23:5) — is understood in Islamic eschatology as pointing to the banquets of Paradise. The Quran describes the bounties of *Jannah* in vivid detail: "They will be served with plates of gold and cups. And therein is whatever the souls desire and what delights the eyes" (43:71). The psalm's promise of dwelling "in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6) corresponds to the Quranic description of Paradise as the eternal abode: "But those who feared their Lord will have gardens beneath which rivers flow, abiding eternally therein — a reward from Allah, and what is with Allah is best for the righteous" (3:198).
