Psalm 4 as an evening prayer of trust and contentment resonates with Islamic devotional practice, which prescribes specific prayers and supplications for the nighttime. The *Isha* prayer, the last of the five daily prayers, and the night vigil (*qiyam al-layl*) recommended in the Quran (73:1-4) establish a rhythm of evening worship that corresponds to the psalm's themes. The closing verse — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep, for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety" (4:8) — closely parallels the *du'a* before sleeping: "O Allah, in Your name I die and I live."

The psalm's counsel to "be angry, and do not sin; ponder in your own hearts on your beds, and be silent" (4:4) aligns with the prophetic teaching on anger management. The Prophet Muhammad said, "The strong man is not the one who can wrestle, but the strong man is the one who controls himself at the time of anger" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*). Islam teaches that anger is a natural emotion but must be governed by reason and piety. The advice to reflect silently on one's bed echoes the Islamic practice of *muhasabah* (self-accounting), in which the believer reviews the day's actions before sleeping.

The declaration "You have put more gladness in my heart than they have when their grain and wine abound" (4:7) expresses a contentment that Islam calls *qana'ah* — satisfaction with God's provision. The Prophet Muhammad taught: "Richness is not having many possessions, but richness is the richness of the soul" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*). This inner contentment, rooted in trust in God's decree (*qadr*), is considered a great spiritual virtue in Islamic ethics. The mention of wine in the original psalm text is noted but not embraced, as Islamic law prohibits intoxicants; the spiritual principle of contentment beyond material wealth, however, is fully affirmed.
