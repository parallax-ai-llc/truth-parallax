## A Lifetime of Trust in the Creator

Psalm 71's testimony of lifelong divine care — "Upon you I have leaned from before my birth" (v. 6) — resonates with the Islamic teaching that Allah's care for each person begins before birth. The Quran describes the stages of human creation in the womb (Surah al-Mu'minun 23:12-14), and a hadith records that Allah sends an angel to breathe the soul into the fetus and record its provision, lifespan, deeds, and destiny. The psalmist's awareness of God's involvement from the womb reflects this Islamic understanding that divine care is present from the very beginning of human existence.

The plea "Do not cast me off in the time of old age" (v. 9) addresses a concern that Islamic ethics takes seriously. The Quran commands kindness to parents, especially in their old age: "And your Lord has decreed that you not worship except Him, and to parents, good treatment. Whether one or both of them reach old age while with you, say not to them 'uff' and do not repel them, but speak to them a noble word" (Surah al-Isra 17:23). The honoring of elders is not merely a social convention in Islam but a divine command, reflecting the psalm's conviction that every stage of life is dignified by God's attention.

The commitment to "proclaim your might to another generation" (v. 18) corresponds to the Islamic responsibility of transmitting knowledge of God to the next generation. The Prophet Muhammad said, "Convey from me even if it is a single verse" (Sahih al-Bukhari), establishing the obligation of every Muslim to pass on what they know of divine truth. The psalm's elderly believer, determined to praise God "yet more and more" (v. 14), models the Islamic conviction that gratitude deepens with accumulated experience — the longer one lives, the more evidence of divine mercy one has witnessed, and the more reason one has for praise.
