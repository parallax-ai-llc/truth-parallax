Psalm 36 opens with a description of the wicked person who has "no fear of God before his eyes" (36:1). In Islamic terminology, this describes the person devoid of *taqwa* — the God-consciousness that is the foundation of all moral life. The Quran warns that those who forget Allah are caused by Him to forget themselves (59:19): the loss of God-awareness leads inevitably to moral self-destruction.

The psalm's celebration of divine attributes — steadfast love reaching to the heavens, faithfulness to the clouds, righteousness like the great mountains (36:5-6) — echoes the Quranic practice of meditating on the *Asma al-Husna* (Beautiful Names of Allah). Each attribute the psalmist names corresponds to divine names in Islamic tradition: love (*al-Wadud*), faithfulness (*al-Mu'min*), righteousness (*al-Barr*). The contemplation of God's names is a form of worship in both traditions.

Verse 9 — "In your light do we see light" — connects to the Quran's *Ayat al-Nur*: "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth" (24:35). Islamic philosophy distinguishes between Allah's essential light (His self-knowledge) and the light He bestows upon creation (guidance, knowledge, faith). The psalmist's insight that all human illumination derives from divine light is fully affirmed in Islamic epistemology: reason, revelation, and intuition are all gifts from the One who is *al-Nur* (the Light).
