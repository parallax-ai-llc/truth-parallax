Psalm 41's opening beatitude — "Blessed are those who have regard for the weak" (41:1) — aligns with one of Islam's most fundamental ethical obligations: care for the poor and vulnerable. *Zakat* (obligatory charity) is one of the Five Pillars of Islam, and *sadaqah* (voluntary charity) is praised throughout the Quran: "Those who spend their wealth in the way of Allah and then do not follow up what they have spent with reminders or injury will have their reward with their Lord" (2:262).

The psalm's description of betrayal by "my close friend, one who shared my bread" (41:9) echoes the Quranic accounts of broken trust and treachery. The Quran regards betrayal of trust as among the gravest sins: "O you who have believed, do not betray Allah and the Messenger or betray your trusts while you know" (8:27). Islamic ethics place enormous weight on *amanah* (trustworthiness); the violation of shared bread — which creates a sacred bond in both Arab and Israelite culture — is particularly egregious.

The doxology closing Book I — "Praise be to the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting" (41:13) — expresses the *tasbih* (glorification) that Islam regards as the fundamental posture of all creation. The Quran declares: "Whatever is in the heavens and the earth glorifies Allah" (59:1). The eternal praise that the psalmist offers is not exceptional but participatory — joining the cosmic chorus of glorification that pervades all existence.
