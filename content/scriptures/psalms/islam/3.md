Psalm 3's themes of trust in God amid persecution resonate with the experience of Prophet Dawud as described in Islamic tradition. The Quran identifies Dawud as both prophet and king (Quran 38:17-26), and the *hadith* literature describes him as a model of worship, fasting, and prayer. His flight from Absalom, while not narrated in the Quran, is consistent with the Islamic understanding that prophets face severe trials as tests of their faith — a theme exemplified supremely by Ibrahim (Abraham), Yusuf (Joseph), and Muhammad himself during the *hijrah* (migration from Makkah to Madinah).

The declaration "I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the Lord sustained me" (3:5) aligns with the Islamic *du'a* (supplication) recited upon waking: "All praise is for Allah, who gave us life after having taken it from us, and unto Him is the resurrection" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*). Islam treats sleep as a minor form of death and waking as a daily resurrection, a perspective remarkably similar to the psalm's theology. The Prophet Muhammad taught specific *adhkar* (remembrances of God) for both sleeping and waking, sanctifying these transitions as acts of worship.

The affirmation "Salvation belongs to the Lord" (3:8) resonates with the Quranic principle that "there is no power and no strength except with Allah" (*la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah*), a phrase the Prophet Muhammad called one of the "treasures of Paradise." Islamic theology insists that all deliverance, protection, and sustenance come from Allah alone, and the attribution of saving power to any other source is a form of *shirk* (associating partners with God). The psalm's clear attribution of salvation to God alone is fully consistent with the Islamic emphasis on *tawhid* in all its dimensions — oneness of God's being, attributes, and right to worship.
