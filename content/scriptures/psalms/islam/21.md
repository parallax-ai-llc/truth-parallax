Psalm 21's celebration of the king's victory as a gift from God aligns with the fundamental Islamic principle that all success comes from Allah. The Quran's commentary on the Battle of Badr makes this explicit: "And already had Allah given you victory at Badr while you were few in number" (3:123). The attribution of victory to divine agency rather than human prowess — "O Lord, in your strength the king rejoices" (21:1) — is exactly the Islamic posture after any success: "All praise is due to Allah" (*Alhamdulillah*).

The blessings enumerated in the psalm — "his heart's desire" (21:2), "length of days" (21:4), "splendor and majesty" (21:5) — are understood in Islamic perspective as divine favor (*ni'mah*) that requires gratitude (*shukr*). The Quran warns that blessings entail responsibility: "Then you will surely be asked that Day about pleasure" (102:8). The Islamic concept of *ni'mah* includes both worldly blessings and spiritual gifts, and the appropriate response to both is gratitude expressed through worship, generosity, and righteous conduct.

The psalm's description of the king's enemies consumed by fire (21:9-12) is understood in Islamic eschatology through the Quranic descriptions of the fate of those who oppose God's purposes. The Quran describes the Fire in vivid terms as the consequence for those who persist in *kufr* (disbelief) and *zhulm* (oppression). However, Islamic theology also emphasizes that God is never unjust: "Indeed, Allah does not do injustice, even as much as an atom's weight" (4:40). Divine punishment is always proportionate and just, a principle that the psalm's context of retributive justice assumes but does not explicitly state.
