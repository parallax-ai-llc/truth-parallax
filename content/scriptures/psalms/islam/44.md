Psalm 44 raises the agonizing question of communal suffering despite faithfulness: "All this came upon us, though we had not forgotten you" (44:17). Islamic theology addresses this through the concept of *ibtila* (divine testing). The Quran states: "Do people think that they will be left to say, 'We believe,' and they will not be tested?" (29:2). Suffering is not necessarily punishment; it may be purification, elevation in rank, or an opportunity to demonstrate the sincerity of faith.

The verse "For your sake we face death all day long" (44:22) describes the reality of *shahada* (martyrdom/witness) that Islamic tradition honors deeply. The Quran assures: "Never think of those who have been killed in the cause of Allah as dead. Rather, they are alive with their Lord, receiving provision" (3:169). Islamic theology transforms the apparent tragedy of faithful suffering into triumph — the martyr's death is not defeat but the highest form of victory.

The bold demand "Awake, Lord! Why do you sleep?" (44:23) is understood in Islamic theology not literally — Allah neither slumbers nor sleeps (*Ayat al-Kursi*, 2:255) — but as the permissible urgency of *dua*. The Prophet Muhammad taught that persistent, even insistent prayer is pleasing to Allah (Tirmidhi). The psalm models the Islamic conviction that the servant may approach the Lord with bold petition, confident that Allah's apparent silence is not indifference but divine wisdom.
