Psalm 10's confrontation with the prosperity of the wicked addresses a question that the Quran also treats extensively. The psalmist's cry — "Why, O Lord, do you stand far away?" (10:1) — expresses a perplexity that Islamic theology addresses through the concept of *hikmah* (divine wisdom). Allah's apparent delay in punishing the wicked is understood not as absence or indifference but as a manifestation of His wisdom and patience (*hilm*). The Quran warns: "And let not those who disbelieve ever think that our extending their time of enjoyment is better for them. We only extend it for them so that they may increase in sin" (3:178).

The portrait of the wicked who says "God has forgotten" (10:11) corresponds to the Quranic description of those who are *ghafilun* (heedless) — people who live as though the Day of Judgment will never come. The Quran addresses this self-deception directly: "They forgot Allah, so He forgot them" (9:67). In Islamic theology, this "forgetting" by God is not literal (since Allah is *al-Alim*, the All-Knowing) but describes the withdrawal of divine protection and guidance from those who persistently reject it.

The psalm's affirmation that God is "king forever and ever" (10:16) and does justice to "the fatherless and the oppressed" (10:18) accords with the Islamic understanding of divine sovereignty as both ultimate and active. Allah is *al-Malik* (the King) and *al-Waliyy* (the Protector), especially of the vulnerable. The Prophet Muhammad said: "I and the one who cares for an orphan will be together in Paradise like this" — and he held his two fingers together (*Sahih al-Bukhari*). The protection of orphans is so central to Islamic ethics that the Quran addresses it more frequently than almost any other social obligation.
