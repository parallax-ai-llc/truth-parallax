Psalm 42's image of the soul thirsting for God — "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you" (42:1) — resonates with the Sufi concept of *shawq* (spiritual longing) for Allah. The great Sufi poet Rumi wrote extensively of this divine thirst, and Rabia al-Adawiyya's prayers expressed a love for Allah that mirrors the psalmist's desperate yearning. Islamic mysticism recognizes that the soul's deepest desire is for union with its Creator.

The psalmist's complaint — "My tears have been my food day and night, while people say to me all day long, 'Where is your God?'" (42:3) — mirrors the experience of Muslims facing skepticism and persecution. The early Muslim community in Makkah endured precisely this taunt from the Quraysh, who mocked the believers' faith in an unseen God. The Quran responds: "They will say, 'When is this promise, if you are truthful?' Say, 'The knowledge is only with Allah'" (67:25-26).

The refrain "Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him" (42:5, 11) models the Islamic practice of *husn al-dhann billah* — maintaining a good opinion of Allah regardless of circumstances. The Prophet Muhammad taught that Allah says, "I am as My servant thinks of Me" (Bukhari). When the believer maintains hope and expectation of divine mercy, even in darkness, that expectation becomes the channel through which mercy flows.
