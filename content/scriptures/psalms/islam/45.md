Psalm 45 is a royal psalm praising the king in terms of extraordinary beauty and righteousness. Islamic tradition, which venerates Dawud as both prophet and king, reads this psalm as celebrating the ideal of just governance that the Quran demands of those in authority: "O Dawud, indeed We have made you a successor (*khalifah*) upon the earth, so judge between the people in truth" (38:26).

The psalm's description of the king riding forth "in the cause of truth, humility and justice" (45:4) embodies the Islamic concept of *adl* (justice) as the fundamental obligation of the ruler. The Quran declares: "Indeed, Allah orders justice and good conduct" (16:90). Islamic political theology, from the Rightly Guided Caliphs to later jurists, has consistently held that a ruler's legitimacy depends on the maintenance of justice.

The bridal imagery of the psalm's second half — the queen in gold, the maidens attending her (45:9-15) — is read in Islamic tradition as metaphorical rather than messianic. The beauty described is understood as the beauty of righteousness and divine favor that attends just governance. Islamic aesthetics, rooted in the *hadith* "Allah is beautiful and loves beauty" (Muslim), affirm that beauty in its truest sense is a reflection of divine attributes, not mere physical adornment.
