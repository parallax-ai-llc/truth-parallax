Psalm 12's contrast between corrupt human speech and the pure words of God resonates powerfully with the Islamic understanding of the Quran as the uncorrupted, final revelation. The psalm's declaration that God's words are "like silver refined in a furnace, purified seven times" (12:6) finds its Islamic counterpart in the Quranic assertion: "Indeed, it is We who sent down the message, and indeed, We will be its guardian" (15:9). While Islamic theology holds that previous scriptures including the *Zabur* underwent *tahrif* (alteration), the Quran is divinely protected from any corruption.

The psalm's lament over "flattering lips" and "a double heart" (12:2) corresponds to the Quranic condemnation of *nifaq* (hypocrisy). The *munafiqun* (hypocrites) of Madinah — those who professed Islam outwardly while harboring disbelief — are among the most severely condemned figures in the Quran. An entire surah (Surah 63, *al-Munafiqun*) is devoted to them, and the Quran warns: "Indeed, the hypocrites will be in the lowest depths of the Fire" (4:145). The psalm's description of those who speak with "a double heart" — saying one thing while meaning another — precisely matches the Quranic portrait of hypocrisy.

God's promise to arise "because the poor are plundered" and "the needy groan" (12:5) aligns with the Quranic guarantee that Allah will vindicate the oppressed. The Quran warns exploiters of the poor: "And let those who hoard what Allah has bestowed upon them of His bounty not think that it is better for them. Rather, it is worse for them" (3:180). The Islamic economic system's prohibition of *riba* (usury/interest), requirement of *zakat* (obligatory charity), and condemnation of hoarding are institutional expressions of the divine concern for the needy that this psalm voices.
