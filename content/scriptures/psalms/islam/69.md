## The Prophetic Cry from the Depths

Psalm 69's depiction of overwhelming suffering — "the waters have come up to my neck... I have come into deep waters" (vv. 1-2) — speaks to the prophetic experience of persecution that the Quran documents extensively. Every prophet faced opposition: "And We had certainly sent Nuh to his people, and he remained among them a thousand years minus fifty years, and the flood seized them while they were wrongdoers" (Surah al-Ankabut 29:14). The psalmist's suffering "for God's sake" (v. 7) and because of "zeal for your house" (v. 9) reflects the pattern of prophetic suffering that results from faithfulness to divine mission.

The alienation from family described in verse 8 — "I have become a stranger to my brothers" — mirrors the experience of Prophet Muhammad, who was rejected by his own Quraysh clan. The Quran records this pain: "So perhaps you would kill yourself with grief that they will not be believers" (Surah ash-Shu'ara 26:3). Islamic tradition honors the grief that prophets bear as part of their calling, recognizing that the greatest suffering comes not from external enemies but from the rejection of those closest to the prophet — his own people.

Muslim scholars note that the imprecatory passages (vv. 22-28) must be evaluated carefully. Islamic teaching distinguishes between legitimate *du'a* against oppressors who refuse all appeals to justice and personal vindictiveness, which is prohibited. The Quran permits the oppressed to speak against their oppressor: "Allah does not like the public mention of evil except by one who has been wronged" (Surah an-Nisa 4:148). The psalm's concluding hope for Zion's salvation (v. 35) is understood within prophetic continuity — God's ultimate plan for justice and peace that Islam sees fulfilled in the guidance sent through all the prophets.
