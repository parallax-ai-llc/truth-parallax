## Remembering God's Mighty Acts

Psalm 66's recollection of the Red Sea crossing — "He turned the sea into dry land" (v. 6) — recalls an event that the Quran narrates in detail. The drowning of Pharaoh and the salvation of Bani Isra'il (the Children of Israel) through the parted sea is one of the Quran's most frequently retold narratives (Surah ash-Shu'ara 26:60-67, Surah al-Baqarah 2:50). The Quran presents this event as a supreme demonstration of Allah's power and mercy, and Muslim scholars recognize the psalm's remembrance of this event as preserving an authentic element of the original *Zabur*.

The psalm's teaching that God tests His people — "You have tried us as silver is tried" (v. 10) — aligns with the Quranic principle that testing (*ibtila'*) is a fundamental feature of human existence. "Do the people think that they will be left to say, 'We believe,' and they will not be tested?" (Surah al-Ankabut 29:2). Islamic theology views trials not as arbitrary suffering but as opportunities for spiritual purification and demonstration of faith. The silver-refining metaphor echoes this understanding: testing removes impurities and reveals the true quality of the believer's faith.

The psalm's principle that "if I had cherished iniquity in my heart, the Lord would not have listened" (v. 18) directly parallels the Islamic teaching on the conditions of accepted prayer (*du'a*). The Prophet Muhammad taught that Allah does not accept the prayer of one whose heart is heedless and whose income is unlawful (Tirmidhi). Sincerity of heart (*ikhlas*) is the foundational condition for prayer in Islam, and this psalm's insistence on moral integrity as a prerequisite for effective petition reflects prophetic teaching that transcends any single religious tradition.
