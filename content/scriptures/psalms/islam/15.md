Psalm 15's ethical profile of the person fit for God's presence resonates with the Islamic concept of *akhlaq* (moral character), which the Prophet Muhammad identified as the essence of his mission: "I was sent to perfect noble character" (*Muwatta Malik*). The qualities listed — truthfulness, justice, faithfulness, integrity — correspond to virtues celebrated in the Quran and Sunnah. The Quran declares: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (49:13), subordinating all distinctions of wealth, lineage, and status to moral character.

The psalm's integration of worship and ethics — the same person who dwells on God's holy hill must live justly — aligns with the Islamic principle that *ibadah* (worship) and *mu'amalat* (interpersonal dealings) are inseparable. The Prophet Muhammad said: "By Allah, he does not believe — he whose neighbor is not safe from his evil" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*), and "The bankrupt person of my ummah is one who comes on the Day of Resurrection with prayer, fasting, and charity, but also with insults, slander, and oppression against others" (*Sahih Muslim*). Ritual worship without ethical conduct is spiritually worthless in Islamic teaching.

The prohibition of lending at interest (15:5) is particularly significant in Islamic perspective, as the prohibition of *riba* (usury/interest) is one of the most emphatically enforced economic regulations in Islamic law. The Quran warns: "Those who consume interest cannot stand except as one stands who is being beaten by Satan into insanity" (2:275). Islamic finance has developed elaborate systems of profit-sharing (*mudarabah*), partnership (*musharakah*), and cost-plus financing (*murabahah*) to honor this prohibition while enabling economic activity, confirming that the psalm's ethical demand is not merely theoretical but practically enforceable.
