Psalm 18's royal thanksgiving for divine deliverance from enemies resonates with the Quranic accounts of how Allah rescued His prophets from danger. The Quran recounts how Allah delivered Musa from Fir'awn, Ibrahim from the fire of Nimrod, Yunus from the belly of the whale, and Muhammad from the plots of the Quraysh. The psalmist's cascade of divine titles — "my rock, my fortress, my deliverer" (18:2) — corresponds to Allah's ninety-nine Beautiful Names (*al-asma al-husna*), including *al-Mani'* (the Preventer of harm), *al-Hafiz* (the Preserver), and *al-Nasir* (the Helper).

The theophany in verses 7-15, where God descends with cosmic upheaval to rescue one individual, has parallels in the Quran's descriptions of divine intervention in history. The Battle of Badr (624 CE), the first major military victory of Islam, was understood by the early Muslims as direct divine intervention: "And you did not kill them, but it was Allah who killed them. And you did not throw when you threw, but it was Allah who threw" (8:17). The Quran also describes angelic reinforcements sent to aid the believers: "Yes, if you remain patient and conscious of Allah and the enemy come upon you in rage, your Lord will reinforce you with five thousand angels" (3:125).

The psalm's principle that God deals with people according to their character (18:25-26) aligns with the Islamic concept of *jaza'* (recompense). The Quran states: "Is the reward of goodness anything but goodness?" (55:60) and "Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it" (99:7-8). Islamic theology affirms that God's dealings with humanity are perfectly just and correspond to each person's intentions and actions, while always leaving room for divine mercy that exceeds what justice strictly requires.
