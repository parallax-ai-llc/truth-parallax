Psalm 30 is a thanksgiving psalm that resonates with the Islamic emphasis on *shukr* (gratitude). The Quran repeatedly pairs faith with gratitude: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you [in favor]" (14:7). Dawud's praise for deliverance — "You brought me up from the realm of the dead" (30:3) — exemplifies the response Islam expects from those who have experienced Allah's mercy: acknowledgment and praise.

The pivotal verse — "Weeping may stay for the night, but rejoicing comes in the morning" (30:5) — expresses a truth that Islamic theology grounds in the concept of *yusr ba'd al-'usr* (ease after hardship). The Quran states emphatically: "Indeed, with hardship comes ease. Indeed, with hardship comes ease" (94:5-6). The repetition underscores the certainty of divine relief. No trial is permanent; Allah's mercy always prevails over difficulty.

David's confession that prosperity bred complacency (30:6) serves as a warning against *ghurur* (self-delusion), which the Quran identifies as a tool of Shaytan: "O humanity, what has deceived you concerning your Lord, the Most Generous?" (82:6). Islamic ethics teach that wealth, health, and security are tests (*ibtila*) no less than poverty and suffering. The believer must maintain gratitude and humility in prosperity, recognizing that all blessings come from Allah and may be withdrawn.
