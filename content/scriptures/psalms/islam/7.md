Psalm 7's appeal for divine justice resonates with the Quranic emphasis on *'adl* (justice) as a fundamental divine attribute and human obligation. The Quran commands: "O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah, even if it be against yourselves" (4:135). The psalmist's willingness to be judged by God if guilty (7:3-5) aligns with the Islamic emphasis on personal accountability, especially as it will be manifested on the Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*).

The portrait of God as righteous Judge (7:11) is central to Islamic eschatology. The Quran describes the Day of Judgment in extensive detail — the scales of justice (*al-mizan*), the record of deeds, the testimony of one's own limbs — emphasizing that divine judgment is absolutely precise and fair. "And We place the scales of justice for the Day of Resurrection, so no soul will be treated unjustly at all. And if there is even the weight of a mustard seed, We will bring it forth" (Quran 21:47). The psalm's confidence that God "judges the peoples with equity" (7:8) reflects the same theology.

The principle of self-recoiling evil (7:14-16) — the wicked falling into their own pit — is well established in the Quran. The story of Fir'awn (Pharaoh) illustrates this perfectly: he who drowned the children of Israel was himself drowned in the sea (Quran 28:40). The Quran declares: "And the evil plot does not encompass except its own people" (35:43). Islamic theology understands this not as karmic automatism but as divine justice — Allah ensures that oppression ultimately rebounds on the oppressor, whether in this life or the next.
