Psalm 24's opening declaration — "The earth is the Lord's and the fullness thereof" (24:1) — expresses the Islamic principle of divine sovereignty (*mulk*) over all creation. The Quran repeatedly affirms: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth" (3:189), and "His is the dominion of the heavens and the earth. He gives life and causes death" (57:2). This cosmic ownership is the foundation of Islamic economic ethics: all wealth belongs ultimately to Allah, and human possession is a trust (*amanah*) that entails obligations of charity, stewardship, and justice.

The entrance requirements — "clean hands and a pure heart" (24:4) — correspond to the Islamic prerequisites for worship: *taharah* (ritual purity, achieved through *wudu* or *ghusl*) and *ikhlas* (sincerity of intention). The Prophet Muhammad said: "Allah does not look at your forms or your wealth, but He looks at your hearts and your deeds" (*Sahih Muslim*). Islamic worship requires both external purification (symbolizing readiness) and internal sincerity (ensuring that worship is directed to God alone). The psalm's combination of external and internal qualifications mirrors this Islamic dual requirement.

The dramatic dialogue — "Lift up your heads, O gates! Who is this King of glory?" (24:7-8) — celebrates divine sovereignty in language that Islamic theology affirms with the reservation that God's glory is beyond all human comprehension. The Quran describes God's majesty in transcendent terms: "Allah — there is no deity except Him, the Ever-Living, the Sustainer of existence. Neither drowsiness overtakes Him nor sleep. To Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth" (2:255, *Ayat al-Kursi*). The "King of glory" in Islamic understanding is Allah alone, the sovereign over all creation, whose majesty no gate, no temple, and no human conception can contain.
