Psalm 47's celebration of God's universal sovereignty — "God is the King of all the earth" (47:7) — expresses the Islamic principle of *tawhid* (divine oneness) in its political dimension. Allah is *al-Malik* (the King) and *Malik al-Mulk* (Owner of all sovereignty). No human authority is ultimate; all power is delegated from Allah and will be reclaimed on the Day of Judgment, when "the sovereignty that Day will be entirely for Allah" (22:56).

The psalm's reference to God ascending "amid the sounding of trumpets (*shofar*)" (47:5) has an Islamic parallel in the trumpet blast that will announce the Day of Judgment: "And the Horn (*sur*) will be blown, and whoever is in the heavens and whoever is on the earth will fall dead except whom Allah wills. Then it will be blown again, and at once they will be standing, looking on" (39:68). Both traditions connect the trumpet with divine sovereignty and judgment.

The vision of "the nobles of the nations" assembling as "the people of the God of Abraham" (47:9) resonates with Islam's understanding of itself as the continuation of the Abrahamic faith. The Quran declares: "Abraham was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was a *hanif* (pure monotheist), a Muslim" (3:67). The gathering of all nations under the God of Abraham is the eschatological hope shared by the Psalms and the Quran.
