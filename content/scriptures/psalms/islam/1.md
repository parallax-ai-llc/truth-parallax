Islamic engagement with the Psalms (*Zabur*) is shaped by the Quranic affirmation that God gave Dawud (David) the *Zabur* (Quran 4:163, 17:55) and the principle of *tahrif* — that earlier scriptures underwent alteration over time. Muslim scholars approach individual psalms with discernment, affirming what accords with the Quran and Sunnah while recognizing the text's complex transmission history. Psalm 1's fundamental contrast between the righteous path and the path of destruction resonates deeply with the Quranic concept of *sirat al-mustaqim* (the straight path), invoked in every *rak'ah* of the daily prayers through Surah al-Fatihah.

The image of the righteous person as a tree planted by water (1:3) parallels the Quranic parable in Surah Ibrahim 14:24-26, where "a good word" is compared to "a good tree, whose root is firmly fixed and whose branches reach to the sky, yielding its fruit at all times." Both texts link spiritual fruitfulness to rootedness in divine truth. The psalm's emphasis on meditating on God's instruction "day and night" (1:2) aligns with the Quranic injunction to recite and reflect on God's revelations regularly, as the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) recited Quran during the night prayers (*tahajjud*).

The contrast between the righteous and the wicked — the latter compared to chaff driven by wind (1:4) — echoes the Quranic description of those who reject divine guidance: "As for the disbelievers, their deeds are like a mirage in a desert" (Quran 24:39). The psalm's assertion that "the Lord knows the way of the righteous" (1:6) aligns with the Islamic teaching that Allah is *al-Alim* (the All-Knowing), fully aware of every human deed and intention. The concept of *taqwa* (God-consciousness) provides the Islamic framework for understanding the psalm's call to avoid the way of the wicked and cleave to the path of righteousness.
