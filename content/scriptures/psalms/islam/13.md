Psalm 13's cry of "How long?" (13:1-2) raises the question of God's apparent delay in responding to the believer's suffering. Islamic theology addresses this through the twin concepts of *sabr* (patience) and *tawakkul* (trust in God). The Quran promises: "Indeed, with hardship comes ease. Indeed, with hardship comes ease" (94:5-6) — the repetition emphasizing the certainty of relief. The psalmist's experience of prolonged suffering followed by renewed trust mirrors the Islamic teaching that patient endurance in trial is one of the highest spiritual stations.

The petition "Light up my eyes, lest I sleep the sleep of death" (13:3) resonates with the Islamic concept of spiritual blindness versus spiritual sight. The Quran declares: "Indeed, it is not the eyes that are blind, but blind are the hearts which are within the breasts" (22:46). Islamic spirituality seeks *basirah* (spiritual insight) — the inner light that enables the believer to perceive God's wisdom even in circumstances of apparent darkness. The psalm's plea for illumination corresponds to the Quranic *du'a*: "My Lord, increase me in knowledge" (20:114).

The psalm's turn from despair to trust — "But I have trusted in your steadfast love" (13:5) — models what Islamic psychology calls *husn al-zhann billah* (having a good opinion of God). A *hadith qudsi* records: "I am as My servant thinks of Me" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*), teaching that the believer's positive expectation of God's mercy is itself a form of worship. The closing praise — "I will sing to the Lord, because he has dealt bountifully with me" (13:6) — reflects the Islamic practice of praising God in all circumstances, as the Prophet Muhammad said: "How wonderful is the affair of the believer, for his affairs are all good" (*Sahih Muslim*).
