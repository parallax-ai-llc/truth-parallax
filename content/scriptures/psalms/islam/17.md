Psalm 17's prayer for divine protection and vindication resonates with the Islamic practice of *du'a* (supplication), which the Prophet Muhammad called "the essence of worship" (*al-Tirmidhi*). The psalmist's approach — presenting his case before God with integrity and urgency — models the kind of earnest, heartfelt supplication that Islam prizes. The Quran teaches: "Call upon Me; I will respond to you" (40:60), and the prophetic tradition encourages believers to make specific, passionate appeals to God in their own words.

The petition "Keep me as the apple of your eye; hide me in the shadow of your wings" (17:8) employs metaphorical language for divine protection that Islamic theology affirms while carefully avoiding anthropomorphism. The Quran uses metaphors of divine closeness — "We are closer to him than his jugular vein" (50:16) — but Islamic *aqeedah* (creedal theology) insists that such language does not imply physical similarity between God and creation. Allah protects the believer in ways that transcend human metaphors, though human language necessarily employs them.

The concluding hope — "when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness" (17:15) — is understood in Islamic eschatology through the concept of the *ru'yah* (vision of God in Paradise). Sunni theology affirms, based on hadith and Quranic evidence, that the believers will see Allah in the Hereafter, and this vision will be the supreme joy of Paradise. The Prophet Muhammad said: "When the people of Paradise enter Paradise, Allah will say, 'Do you want anything more?' They will say, 'Have You not brightened our faces, admitted us to Paradise, and saved us from the Fire?' Then the veil will be lifted, and they will not have been given anything more beloved to them than looking at their Lord" (*Sahih Muslim*).
