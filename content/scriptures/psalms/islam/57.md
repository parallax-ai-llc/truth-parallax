## Seeking Refuge in the Merciful God

Psalm 57's opening plea for mercy — "Be merciful to me, O God, be merciful to me" (v. 1) — echoes the fundamental Islamic prayer for divine mercy that opens every surah of the Quran: *Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim* (In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful). The concept of seeking refuge in God is formalized in Islam through the *isti'adhah* — the prayer "I seek refuge in Allah from the accursed Satan" — recited before Quran reading and in moments of spiritual vulnerability. This psalm's request for shelter "in the shadow of your wings" (v. 1) expresses the same theology of divine protection.

The psalm's cosmic praise — "Be exalted, O God, above the heavens!" (vv. 5, 11) — directly aligns with the Islamic declaration of *takbir*: *Allahu Akbar* (God is Greater). This exclamation, repeated throughout the daily prayers and in moments of awe, affirms God's transcendence above all creation. The psalm's movement from personal distress to cosmic praise mirrors the Muslim's experience in *salah* (prayer): beginning with personal need and expanding into awareness of God's infinite greatness that dwarfs all earthly concerns.

The declaration "I will awake the dawn!" (v. 8) resonates with the Islamic practice of *Fajr* — the pre-dawn prayer that is among the most spiritually significant of the five daily prayers. The Prophet Muhammad taught that the *Fajr* prayer is particularly beloved to Allah, and the Muslim who rises before dawn to pray enacts this psalm's determination to greet the day with praise. The steadfast heart described in verse 7 corresponds to the Quranic concept of *qalb salim* (the sound heart) — the heart that is firm in its orientation toward God despite external pressures.
