Psalm 9's themes of divine justice and the protection of the oppressed resonate strongly with Quranic teachings. The psalm's opening — whole-hearted thanksgiving and recounting of God's wonderful deeds (9:1) — corresponds to the Islamic practice of *shukr* (gratitude), which the Quran frequently enjoins: "And remember when your Lord proclaimed, 'If you are grateful, I will surely increase you'" (14:7). The Islamic concept of *dhikr* (remembrance of God) encompasses both praise and the recounting of God's blessings, mirroring the psalm's two-fold opening.

The affirmation that God "has established his throne for judgment" and "judges the world with righteousness" (9:7-8) is central to Islamic theology. The Quran presents Allah as *al-Hakam* (the Judge) and *al-'Adl* (the Just), whose judgment is infallible and whose justice encompasses all of creation. The Day of Judgment is one of the six articles of Islamic faith, and the Quran returns to it more frequently than to any other eschatological theme. The psalm's assurance that justice will ultimately prevail — even when present circumstances suggest otherwise — corresponds to the Islamic conviction that the apparent prosperity of the wicked is temporary and illusory.

The psalm's particular concern for the oppressed (9:9, 12, 18) aligns with the Quranic mandate for social justice. The Quran repeatedly commands the defense of the weak: "And what is wrong with you that you fight not in the cause of Allah and of those weak, ill-treated and oppressed among men, women, and children?" (4:75). Islamic law's system of *zakat* (obligatory charity), *waqf* (charitable endowment), and *sadaqah* (voluntary charity) institutionalize the divine concern for the vulnerable that both the Quran and this psalm express.
