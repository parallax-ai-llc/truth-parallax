Psalm 32's opening beatitude — "Blessed is the one whose transgressions are forgiven" (32:1) — resonates with the Quranic emphasis on Allah as *al-Ghaffar* (the Perpetual Forgiver) and *al-Ghafur* (the Most Forgiving). The Quran declares: "Say, O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins" (39:53). Islamic theology places divine forgiveness at the center of the God-human relationship.

David's description of the torment of concealed sin — "When I kept silent, my bones wasted away" (32:3) — illustrates the Islamic understanding that sin creates a spiritual burden that can only be relieved through *tawbah* (repentance). The Quran uses the term *tawbah* — literally "turning" — to describe the return to Allah that restores spiritual health. The Prophet Muhammad said that Allah is more delighted by the repentance of His servant than a person who finds their lost camel in the desert (Bukhari).

The psalm's assurance of forgiveness upon confession — "I acknowledged my sin... and you forgave the guilt of my sin" (32:5) — confirms the Islamic teaching that *tawbah* requires three elements: genuine remorse (*nadam*), cessation of the sinful act, and firm resolve not to return to it. When these conditions are met, Allah's forgiveness is certain. Islam does not require priestly mediation or sacramental absolution; the servant turns directly to Allah, and Allah forgives — a directness this psalm beautifully models.
