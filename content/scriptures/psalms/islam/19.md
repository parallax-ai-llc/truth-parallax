Psalm 19's movement from natural revelation to revealed law is remarkably parallel to the Quranic epistemology that recognizes both *ayat kawniyyah* (cosmic signs) and *ayat qur'aniyyah* (Quranic verses) as sources of divine knowledge. The psalm's opening — "The heavens declare the glory of God" (19:1) — corresponds directly to numerous Quranic passages: "Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth and the alternation of the night and the day are signs for those of understanding" (3:190). The Quran repeatedly invites contemplation of the natural world as evidence of God's existence, power, and wisdom.

The psalm's transition from cosmic revelation to the perfection of divine law (19:7-9) mirrors the Islamic understanding that nature points to God but that explicit revelation is needed for moral guidance. The Quran is called *furqan* (the criterion, that which distinguishes right from wrong), and the *Shariah* (divine law) provides the detailed guidance that natural observation alone cannot supply. The six attributes of the Torah in Psalm 19 — perfect, sure, right, pure, clean, true — would be readily affirmed by Muslim scholars as descriptions of authentic divine revelation, with the understanding that the Quran is the final, uncorrupted expression of that revelation.

The personal prayer concluding the psalm — "Let the words of my mouth and the meditation of my heart be acceptable in your sight" (19:14) — resonates with the Islamic concept of *ikhlas* (sincerity), which the Prophet Muhammad identified as the condition for every act of worship to be accepted by God. The Quran warns: "So woe to those who pray but who are heedless of their prayer — those who make show of their deeds" (107:4-6). The psalm's desire for inner and outer alignment — that both speech and thought be acceptable to God — is the essence of *ikhlas*, without which no prayer or deed has value.
