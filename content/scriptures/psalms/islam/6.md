Psalm 6's cry for mercy in suffering resonates with the Islamic understanding of divine mercy (*rahmah*) as God's most prominent attribute. Every surah of the Quran except one begins with "In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious (*al-Rahman*), the Most Merciful (*al-Rahim*)," and a *hadith qudsi* records Allah saying: "My mercy prevails over My wrath" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*). The psalmist's plea "O Lord, rebuke me not in your anger" (6:1) reflects a posture that Islam encourages — appealing to God's mercy while acknowledging that His correction is just.

The psalm's description of physical and spiritual suffering intertwined — "my bones are troubled" (6:2), "my soul also is greatly troubled" (6:3) — aligns with the Islamic holistic understanding of the human person. Islam does not separate body and soul as sharply as some philosophies do; illness, suffering, and recovery are understood as affecting the whole person. The Prophet Muhammad taught that illness expiates sins: "No fatigue, disease, sorrow, sadness, hurt, or distress befalls a Muslim, even if it were the prick of a thorn, except that Allah expiates some of his sins thereby" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*). Suffering in the Islamic framework is thus potentially redemptive.

The turning point — "The Lord has heard the sound of my weeping" (6:8) — affirms a principle central to Islamic prayer: God hears the sincere supplicant. The Quran promises: "And when My servants ask you concerning Me, indeed I am near. I respond to the invocation of the supplicant when he calls upon Me" (2:186). The psalm's movement from anguish to assurance mirrors the Islamic teaching that *du'a* (supplication) is never wasted — it either brings what was requested, averts a harm, or is stored as reward in the Hereafter.
