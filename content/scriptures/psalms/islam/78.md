## The Zabur and Sacred History

Islamic tradition recognizes the Psalms (*Zabur*) as revelation given to Prophet Dawud (David), though Muslims hold that the present text has been subject to *tahrif* (alteration). Psalm 78's recounting of Israel's history — the Exodus, the wilderness, the covenant — resonates with Quranic narratives, which serve as the authoritative standard.

## Quranic Parallels

### The Exodus Narrative

The Quran recounts the Exodus story extensively (Quran 2:49-73, 7:103-162, 20:9-98, 26:10-68). Many events in Psalm 78 — the plagues, the sea crossing, manna and quail, the golden calf — appear in the Quran but with distinct theological framing. The Quran emphasizes *tawhid* (God's oneness) and the prophetic mission of Musa (Moses).

### Ingratitude and Rebellion

The Quran's portrayal of Bani Isra'il's (Children of Israel's) rebellion mirrors Psalm 78's account but serves a specifically Quranic purpose: warning the Muslim *ummah* against repeating these patterns. "And We gave you the shade of clouds and sent down to you manna and quail... but they did not wrong Us; rather, they wronged themselves" (Quran 2:57).

### Divine Patience

Both Psalm 78 and the Quran emphasize God's patience (*hilm*) with rebellious humanity. However, the Quran frames this within the broader prophetic narrative — each prophet calls to the same *tawhid*, and each generation's response serves as a lesson for those who follow.

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All themes are read through the lens of God's absolute oneness
- **Prophetic continuity**: The psalm's context within the chain of prophets
- **Quranic authority**: The Quran serves as the criterion for evaluating biblical texts
- **Ethical guidance**: Moral teachings that accord with Islamic values
