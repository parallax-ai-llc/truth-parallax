Psalm 20's congregational prayer for the king before battle resonates with the Islamic understanding of communal supplication (*du'a jama'i*) and reliance on God in times of conflict. The Prophet Muhammad himself made extensive *du'a* before the Battle of Badr, raising his hands and pleading with Allah until his companion Abu Bakr said: "O Messenger of Allah, your Lord will fulfill what He has promised you." The psalm models the same posture: a community turning to God rather than relying on their own strength.

The decisive verse — "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God" (20:7) — expresses a principle central to the Islamic doctrine of *tawakkul*. The Quran teaches: "And prepare against them whatever you are able of power and of steeds of war... But if they incline to peace, then incline to it and rely upon Allah" (8:60-61). The Islamic approach is neither fatalistic passivism (ignoring practical means) nor secular self-reliance (trusting only in means). Rather, it is *tawakkul* after *tawfiq*: taking all practical measures, then placing ultimate trust in God's decree.

The psalm's intercessory character — the community praying for its leader's success — corresponds to the Islamic practice of praying for those in authority. The Prophet Muhammad taught: "The best of your rulers are those whom you love and who love you, who invoke God's blessings upon you and you invoke His blessings upon them" (*Sahih Muslim*). Friday sermons (*khutbah*) traditionally include prayers for Muslim leaders and for the welfare of the Muslim community, extending the psalm's model of corporate intercession into weekly liturgical practice.
