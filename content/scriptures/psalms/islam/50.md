Psalm 50 presents God as cosmic judge, summoning heaven and earth — "The Mighty One, God, the LORD, speaks and summons the earth" (50:1). This theophanic imagery corresponds to the Quranic Day of Judgment, when Allah will gather all creation for reckoning: "And the earth will shine with the light of its Lord, and the record will be placed, and the prophets and the witnesses will be brought" (39:69). Both texts envision a divine court of ultimate accountability.

God's declaration — "I have no need of a bull from your stall" (50:9) — affirms the Islamic principle that ritual worship serves the worshipper, not Allah. The Quran states this explicitly regarding sacrifice: "Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety from you" (22:37). Allah is *al-Ghani* (the Self-Sufficient); He requires nothing from creation. Worship is for human benefit — the cultivation of *taqwa* and the alignment of the human will with the divine.

The psalm's demand for integrity — matching religious profession with moral practice — is central to Islamic ethics. The Quran harshly condemns those who preach what they do not practice: "O you who have believed, why do you say what you do not do? Great is hatred in the sight of Allah that you say what you do not do" (61:2-3). Psalm 50's insistence that covenant relationship requires ethical consistency, not merely ritual observance, is a message Islam emphatically affirms.
