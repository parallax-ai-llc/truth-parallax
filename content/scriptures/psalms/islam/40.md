Psalm 40 opens with patient waiting — "I waited patiently for the LORD" (40:1) — which exemplifies the Islamic virtue of *sabr* (patience). The Quran promises: "Indeed, the patient will be given their reward without account" (39:10). Islamic theology distinguishes several types of *sabr*: patience in affliction, patience in obedience, and patience in restraining from sin. This psalm demonstrates the first: steadfast trust during a period of unanswered prayer.

The declaration that God desires obedience over sacrifice — "Sacrifice and offering you did not desire" (40:6) — resonates deeply with Islamic theology. The Quran states regarding animal sacrifice: "Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety (*taqwa*) from you" (22:37). Islam values the rituals of *ibadah* (worship) but insists that their purpose is the cultivation of inner righteousness, not the mechanical performance of external acts.

The psalmist's testimony — "I proclaim your saving acts in the great assembly; I do not seal my lips" (40:9) — corresponds to the Islamic duty of *da'wah* (invitation to God's path). The Quran commands: "Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good instruction" (16:125). Those who have experienced Allah's mercy are obligated to share that experience with others, making gratitude and proclamation inseparable aspects of the faithful life.
