Psalm 2's depiction of nations raging against God's purposes resonates with the Quranic theme of human rebellion against divine authority. The Quran repeatedly describes how nations rejected the prophets sent to them — from the people of Nuh (Noah) to the Quraysh of Makkah — and faced divine consequences. The psalm's portrayal of God "who sits in the heavens" laughing at human defiance (2:4) accords with the Islamic understanding of divine sovereignty (*qadr*): no human scheme can frustrate Allah's decree, and opposition to God's will is ultimately futile.

The declaration "You are my son; today I have begotten you" (2:7) is a point where Islamic theology diverges sharply from certain other interpretations. Islam categorically rejects any notion of divine sonship in an ontological sense. The Quran states explicitly: "He begets not, nor is He begotten" (Quran 112:3). Muslim scholars who engage with the psalm text understand the "sonship" language as metaphorical — a manner of speaking about the king's special commission by God, consistent with ancient Near Eastern royal ideology but never implying that God has literal offspring. The Quran's insistence on *tawhid* (absolute divine oneness) is the lens through which all such language must be evaluated.

The psalm's call to "serve the Lord with fear" and "take refuge in him" (2:11-12) aligns with the Islamic posture of *ibadah* (worship/servitude) and *tawakkul* (reliance on God). Every Muslim is an *abd* (servant) of Allah, and the highest spiritual station is complete submission to and trust in God's will. The warning to earthly rulers that God's decree cannot be overturned has particular resonance in Islamic political theology, which historically emphasized that all earthly authority is delegated by God and accountable to His law (*shariah*).
