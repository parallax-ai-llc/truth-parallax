## Overcoming Fear Through Trust in God

Psalm 56's confession "When I am afraid, I put my trust in you" (v. 3) articulates a spiritual pattern that Islam affirms wholeheartedly. The Quran addresses fear directly: "Those who believed and whose hearts are assured by the remembrance of Allah — verily, in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest" (Surah ar-Ra'd 13:28). The Islamic remedy for fear is *dhikr* (remembrance of God) — the constant recollection of Allah's presence, power, and mercy that transforms the believer's relationship to threatening circumstances.

The image of God collecting tears in a bottle (v. 8) conveys a tenderness of divine care that resonates with the Quranic description of Allah as *al-Latif* (the Subtle, the Kind) and *al-Khabir* (the All-Aware). Islamic theology affirms that Allah's knowledge extends to the most minute details of each person's experience, including every tear shed. The hadith records that the Prophet Muhammad wept at the death of his son Ibrahim and said, "The eye weeps and the heart grieves, but we do not say except what pleases our Lord" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Grief and tears are not signs of weak faith but of authentic human experience held within the framework of divine trust.

The refrain "What can flesh do to me?" (v. 4) resonates with the Quranic teaching that no harm can befall a person except by Allah's permission (Surah at-Taghabun 64:11). This does not deny the reality of human threat but relativizes it within the larger framework of divine sovereignty (*qadr*). The Muslim finds courage not in denial of danger but in the conviction that Allah's decree encompasses all events, and that the believer's ultimate destiny is in Allah's hands alone.
